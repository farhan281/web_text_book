Oath Not to Delay: The Meaning of Ta'khir
Ruling on oath not to delay vs. leaving to collect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the Hanafi legal distinction between active delay (ta'khir/ta'jil) and passive forbearance (tark al-taqadi), and apply this distinction to determine whether an oath is violated.

Core Idea
Not demanding payment is not the same as delaying it – an oath against delay is not broken by mere forbearance.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a subtle point in oaths related to debts. A person swears not to delay a right for a month, but then simply refrains from demanding payment. Does he break his oath? The Hanafi answer is no, because 'delay' (ta'khir) legally means a formal agreement to postpone (ta'jil), not merely leaving off demand. Understanding this terminological precision is key to applying oath rulings correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath condition
The oath is 'not to delay (la yu'akhkhir) the right for a month'. Determine what the oath prohibits: active postponement, not passive inaction.
Focus on the verb yu'akhkhir (to delay) – it denotes an action, not a state.
2
Define the key legal terms
The author defines ta'khir = ta'jil (formal rescheduling). Tark al-taqadi (leaving off demanding) is explicitly excluded. Understand the difference: ta'khir requires a bilateral agreement or at least an active decision; tark al-taqadi is simply not pursuing the right.
This is a rational (aqlī) proof based on linguistic precision.
3
Apply the definition to the facts
The creditor did not actively agree to postpone, nor did he do any act of rescheduling. He merely remained silent. Since this is tark al-taqadi, not ta'khir, the oath's condition is not triggered.
The oath's violation requires an act of delay, not omission.
4
State the ruling
The ruling: lam yahnath (he does not break his oath). The oath remains intact; no sin or expiation is required.

The reason is that 'delay' (ta'khir) legally means a formal agreement to postpone (ta'jil), not merely the absence of demand. Purely passive forbearance does not constitute a delay in legal terms.

Unilateral vs Bilateral Contracts in Oaths: The Role of Acceptance
Rulings on contracts and oath-breaking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which contracts break an oath upon mere offer and which require acceptance, and state the intra-Hanafi dispute on khiyar.

Core Idea
A gift breaks an oath at offer; a sale needs acceptance—because compensation is bilateral.

When a person swears not to perform a certain transaction, the moment the oath is broken depends on the nature of that contract. Some contracts transfer ownership from one side only, while others require both parties' consent. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on oath-breaking for unilateral acts like gifts and charity, bilateral contracts like sale and lease, and the disputed case of a sale with an option (khiyar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of contract in the oath
Read the passage and note the contracts listed: gift (hibah), charity (sadaqah), lending (i'arah), sale (bay'), lease (ijarah), kitaba, and loan (qard). Classify each as unilateral (one-sided) or bilateral (exchange of compensations).
Gift and charity are unilateral; sale and lease are bilateral. Lending is considered unilateral by Hanafis for oath purposes.
2
Apply the rule for unilateral contracts
For unilateral contracts like gift, charity, and 'ariyah, the oath is broken by the mere offer or statement (e.g., 'I have lent you') even if the other party does not accept. The reason: ownership transfers from one side only, so the act of the oath-taker alone completes the contract's purpose.
The text says: 'لأن الملك هنا من جانب واحد'.
3
Apply the rule for bilateral contracts
For bilateral contracts like sale, lease, and kitaba, the oath is not broken until the other party accepts. The contract's purpose is to exchange two compensations ('iwadayn), which requires both offer and acceptance.
Contrast: 'بخلاف ما إذا حلف لا يبيع ... فإنه لا يحنث حتى يقبل الآخر'.
4
Analyze the khiyar dispute
If the oath was not to sell, and a sale is made with an option (khiyar) for buyer or seller, Muhammad holds the oath is broken because the sale contract has occurred (العقد وجب). Abu Yusuf holds it is not broken because the contract is not final due to the option. No tarjih is given in this passage.
Later Hanafi works may prefer Abu Yusuf's view.

Commentary
This passage discusses the role of acceptance in oaths involving various contracts. For contracts that involve exchange of two compensations (sale, lease, kitaba), the oath is not broken until the other party accepts, because the contract is bilateral and requires acceptance to be complete. The passage then addresses a specific case: a sale with khiyar (option). Muhammad says the oath is broken as soon as the sale is made (even with option), because the sale has occurred. Abu Yusuf says it is not broken because the contract is not final. Ruling when two claim purchase of the same slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two claimants each prove purchase of the same slave from the same seller and explain the legal reasoning behind the option given.

Core Idea
When two buyers both prove sale of the same slave, each may take half at half price or abandon the claim.

What happens when two people each present a valid contract of sale for the same item? This lesson explores the Hanafi solution to conflicting evidence in a sale of a slave, balancing the rights of both claimants and the seller. The ruling grants each claimant a choice, demonstrating the school's concern for equity and practicality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Two claimants each allege that they purchased the same slave from the same seller, and both present evidence (bayyinah).
The evidence is assumed equally strong; no one is preferred.
2
Recognize the conflict
The slave is a single, indivisible item. A valid sale to one claimant logically excludes the other, yet both proofs are equally sound.
3
Analyze the ruling
Each claimant is given a khiyar (option): either accept half the slave for half the price, or withdraw the claim entirely.
The option prevents forced partnership.
4
Understand the rationale
Because the evidence equitably supports both claims, the sale is deemed valid for half each; but the claimant may reject the half and avoid an unwanted commitment.
This balances the strength of evidence with the claimant's consent.
5
Apply the rule
If a claimant chooses to take half, he pays half the agreed price. If he chooses to leave, he forfeits the claim, and the other claimant (if accepting) may take the whole? (Not explicitly addressed, but the ruling ensures no one is forced into a sale.)
The text only covers the case where both have evidence; further scenarios require qiyas.

The option allows the claimants to avoid being forced into a partnership if they do not desire it.

Two Claimants for Purchase of Same Slave
Ruling on conflicting purchase claims for the same slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling when two claimants each present evidence of purchase of the same item from the same seller.

Core Idea
When two buyers each prove purchase of the same slave, each may take half or leave it; earlier date or possession decides.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a tricky judicial scenario: two buyers each claim to have purchased the same slave from the same seller, and both bring valid evidence. How does a judge resolve this? The Hanafi school provides a structured solution that balances the claims without preferring one over the other arbitrarily. Let's work through the ruling step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Recognize that two claimants each assert purchase from the same seller, both with evidence (bayyinah). The slave is a single indivisible item.
2
Understand the default option rule
Since both have equal proof, the judge does not award the whole to either. Instead, each claimant has an option: take half the slave for half the price, or leave it entirely.
3
Apply priority factors: date and possession
If both provide dates, the earlier date wins. If neither provides a date, the one with physical possession (qabd) is preferred. If only one dates, that one wins.
4
Distinguish from the two-seller scenario
If the claimants allege purchase from two different sellers, the rule differs: when only one has a date, the slave is split equally. Understand why the source of ownership matters.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete case
Take a hypothetical with given details (dates, possession, refusal) and determine the outcome using the principles above.

Form of Oath in a Claim of Sale
Ruling on the form of oath for a sale claim with istihsan justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct form of oath in a claim of sale and explain the istihsan reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
In a sale claim, the defendant swears by Allah that no current sale exists, not that he never sold — because the sale may have been cancelled or the item returned.

When a buyer claims he purchased an item and the seller denies, the court requires an oath. But what exactly should the defendant swear? This lesson explains the precise wording mandated by the Hanafi school and the rationale (istihsan) that prevents a false oath when the transaction might have been undone after the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim and Denial
The plaintiff claims to have bought a specific item (here, a slave) for a price, and the defendant denies this.
The denial is not about the existence of a sale at any time, but about a binding contract at present.
2
State the Correct Oath Form
The defendant is made to swear: 'By Allah, there is no current sale between the two of you regarding it.' This negates a present enforceable contract.
The oath is phrased in the present tense.
3
Identify the Incorrect Form
The defendant is not made to swear: 'By Allah, I did not sell.' This would negate the historical act of selling.
This form is avoided because it could be false even if the sale happened but was later rescinded.
4
Understand the Rationale (Istihsan)
The ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) to prevent a false oath. A sale may have occurred but then been cancelled (iqalah) or the item returned due to a defect. Swearing 'I did not sell' would be a lie in such cases.
The oath must be limited to the present state to avoid perjury.
5
Apply the Rule
The correct oath protects the defendant from a false oath while still fulfilling the plaintiff's right to a denial under oath.
This ruling applies to claims of sale; other claims have their own oath forms.

Dividing Property with Conflicting Evidence: Abu Hanifa's Ruling
Ruling on proportional division with conflicting evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for dividing property when two claimants present equal evidence, calculating each claimant's share proportionally.

Core Idea
Equal evidence means proportional division: the larger claim gets the undisputed portion plus half the disputed portion.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling on conflicting claims to a single property. Both claimants present valid evidence, but one claims the whole, the other only half. How should the property be divided? Imam Abu Hanifa provides a specific proportional solution that balances the strength of each claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify claims
Determine what each claimant asserts: Claimant A claims the whole (1), Claimant B claims half (1/2).
2
Determine disputed portion
The disputed portion is the half claimed by B, since A also claims it. The other half is undisputed (only A claims it).
3
Divide disputed equally
Since both have equal evidence for the disputed half, it is split equally between them: each gets 1/4 of the total property.
This applies the principle of ta'adul (equal strength of evidence).
4
Award undisputed portion
The undisputed half (not claimed by B) goes entirely to A: another 1/2.
5
Calculate final shares
A's total = 1/2 (undisputed) + 1/4 (from disputed) = 3/4. B's total = 1/4.
The combined shares equal 1.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Dividing a Claimed House
Ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on dividing a house when two claimants present evidence, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
When claimants differ, the division depends on whether the uncontested portion is awarded entirely to the one claiming more.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents an intra-Hanafi dispute over dividing a house when one claimant claims the entire house and another claims half, both with evidence. The matn states Abu Hanifa's ruling, and the sharh explains the Sahibayn's objection. You will learn the reasoning behind each position and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Recognize that we have a house in possession of one man (or in both, later), with two claimants: one claims the whole, the other claims half. Both present evidence.
The scenario assumes the house is initially in one man's possession; later a variant is discussed.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and his rationale
Abu Hanifa gives the claimant of the whole 3/4 and the claimant of half 1/4. His reasoning: the half not claimed by the second claimant goes entirely to the first; the contested half is split equally.
This respects the uncontested portion as fully belonging to the first claimant.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and their analogy
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad divide into thirds: claimant of whole gets 2/3, claimant of half gets 1/3. They analogize to 'awl in inheritance, treating the claims as shares: whole claims 2 shares, half claims 1 share, total 3, hence proportional reduction.
'Awl is typically used in inheritance when total shares exceed the base.
4
Compare the two: thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the claimant of whole receives 3/4 (75%); under Sahibayn, 2/3 (66.67%). The difference is 1/12 of the house.
The tarjih in later Hanafi works often favors Abu Hanifa.

Qada al-Tark vs. Qada al-Ilzam in Property Disputes
Explanation of qada al-tark and qada al-ilzam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qada al-tark and qada al-ilzam and apply them to a scenario where possession is divided.

Core Idea
The acceptance of evidence depends on possession: in qada al-tark, evidence is accepted without opponent's acknowledgment.

The passage continues with a variant where the house is in possession of both claimants. It introduces two types of judicial rulings: qada al-tark and qada al-ilzam. This lesson focuses on the distinction between these two concepts and how they apply when each party holds part of the property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the variant scenario
Now the house is in both claimants' possession: each has half. The judge rules on the claim of the whole claimant.
Possession here means physical control, not ownership.
2
Understand the two types of qada
Qada al-tark (judgment of abandonment) and qada al-ilzam (judgment of enforcement). In qada al-tark, the judge accepts evidence and awards possession without requiring the opponent to concede. In qada al-ilzam, the evidence is only accepted if the opponent acknowledges its validity.
The key difference is whether the opponent's acceptance is needed for the evidence to be heard.
3
Apply to the case at hand
The claimant of the whole has evidence. Half of the house is in his own hand; the other half is in the other's hand. His evidence is not accepted for the half he possesses (since he already has it), but it is accepted for the half in the other's hand via qada al-tark. So the judge awards him the half in the other's hand.
The claimant of half does not get additional share because he already possesses half and his evidence is not needed.
4
Distinguish from qada al-ilzam
If qada al-ilzam were used, the evidence of the whole claimant would only be heard if the half-claimant conceded its validity. Here, since the half-claimant may not concede, qada al-tark is applied to avoid this hurdle.
This ensures justice when the opponent's concession cannot be obtained.

Calculating Co-owner Shares: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on pricing distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the distribution of sale proceeds among co-owners according to the methods of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain the difference in their divisors.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa divides by 12; the Sahibayn divide by 13 – same shares, different amounts.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah presents a calculation dispute within the Hanafi school. A house owned by three partners with agreed shares (7/12, 3/12, 2/12) is sold for 1000 dirhams. Abu Hanifa divides the price by the sum of shares (12), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) divide by 13. Understanding this difference sharpens your precision in fiqh arithmetic and shows that even foundational imams can differ in method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ownership shares
Note the shares: Kamil owns 7/12, Layth owns 3/12, Nasr owns 2/12. The total is 12 parts.
These shares are agreed upon by both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Apply Abu Hanifa's method
Divide the total price (1000) by 12, giving 83⅓ dirhams per part. Multiply each owner's share by this quotient.
Kamil: 7×83⅓ = 583⅓; Layth: 3×83⅓ = 250; Nasr: 2×83⅓ = 166⅔.
3
Apply the Sahibayn's method
Divide the price (1000) by 13, giving approximately 76.923 dirhams per part. Multiply by each share.
Kamil: 7×76.923 ≈ 538.46; Layth: 3×76.923 ≈ 230.77; Nasr: 2×76.923 ≈ 153.85.
4
Compare the results
Under Abu Hanifa, the larger shareholder (Kamil) receives more; under the Sahibayn, the distribution is more even. Both methods sum to 1000.
The practical difference is significant: Kamil gets about 45 dirhams more under Abu Hanifa.
5
Recognize the legal basis
The text does not explain why the Sahibayn use 13. It may be due to a legal principle (e.g., reserving a share for the house) or a different interpretation of ownership. Both views are recorded as valid.
This shows that intra-madhhab differences can be purely methodological.

When buyer and seller disagree on price, evidence proving the increase prevails; if none, buyer accepts seller's claim or sale is rescinded.

In any sale, disagreements can arise over the agreed price or the quantity of the item. Islamic law provides clear procedures for a judge to resolve such disputes fairly. This lesson explains the Hanafi rulings based on who provides evidence and what kind of evidence is given priority. Understanding these rules will help you navigate sale disputes with legal clarity.

1
Identify the dispute scenario

The buyer cannot force the seller to accept the buyer's claim; the contract is voidable if not accepted.

Core Idea
Slaves and jewels cannot be forcibly partitioned because their intrinsic disparity (tafāwut) makes equal division impossible.

The law of partition (qismah) aims to divide jointly owned property equitably. But what if the property's value cannot be measured objectively? The exceptions show the issue is not prohibition but the impossibility of compulsion.

This difference affects practical transactions in joint ownership.

When co-owners cannot agree on dividing joint property, a judge appoints an official divider (qasim) to oversee the process. This lesson unpacks the three essential duties the divider must perform: depicting the shares (taswir), equating them in value (ta'dil), and estimating the property (dar'). Knowing the value/mesure prevents later disputes.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Quran, along with an intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar, regarding transactions entered under coercion. The core ruling is that if a person is coerced (ikrah) to sell, buy, lease, or confess—under threat of death, severe beating, or lengthy imprisonment—the transaction is valid but the coerced party has the option (khiyar) to either affirm or rescind it. The proof is from Quran 4:29, which requires mutual consent for trade. The commentator then addresses a difference: Zufar holds that a coerced sale does not transfer ownership at all because it is suspended on approval, but the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad) is that ownership transfers upon delivery (qabd), because the pillar of the contract is sound; the only defect is the lack of consent, which makes it similar to an invalid condition. Thus, if the buyer takes possession and then performs an act that cannot be undone (like emancipating a slave or making a tadbir), the transaction is finalized and the buyer owes the value. If the act can be undone (like reselling), the original seller can reclaim the item. The second part discusses coercion in confession: if coerced to confess to a specific amount, confessing less is invalid; confessing more makes the extra binding. Finally, the commentator explains that receipt of the price willingly indicates approval, but receipt under coercion does not. The passage ends by distinguishing coerced transactions from other invalid sales where the right of a third party may prevent rescission.

Voluntary vs Coerced Acceptance in Duress Sales
Explains rulings on duress in sale contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on duress in sale contracts, distinguish the legal effect of voluntary and coerced acceptance of price or delivery, and determine liability for the sold item if it perishes.

Core Idea
Voluntary acceptance ratifies the coerced sale; coerced acceptance does not, and the price must be returned; liability for perish follows the buyer if not under duress.

This passage from Sharh al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses the crucial distinction between voluntary and coerced acts in a sale made under duress. The key is whether the coerced party ratifies the contract through voluntary conduct, such as accepting the price or delivering the item. Understanding this subtlety is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi rules on duress in transactions.

Voluntary delivery of the sold item has the same effect.

The sale remains invalid.

Duress in Acknowledgment: Scope of Compulsion
Explains rulings on duress in acknowledgment (iqrar).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that an acknowledgment made under duress is void only to the extent of the compulsion, and determine when an acknowledgment exceeding the compelled amount or type is binding.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment coerced for a specific amount is void for that amount, but any excess or different type acknowledged voluntarily is binding.

This passage from the same commentary examines the effect of duress on acknowledgments (iqrar). The key principle is that the compulsion is limited to the specific amount or type demanded; any acknowledgment beyond that scope is considered a voluntary act and thus binding. This nuance is critical for correctly weighting the legal effect of statements made under pressure.

Partial acknowledgment is still under duress.

The part exceeding compulsion is voluntary.
4

The type is part of the scope of compulsion.

Generalize: any acknowledgment that does not fall within the scope of duress is treated as voluntary and binding.
This protects the coerced party while holding them accountable for voluntary statements.

The author distinguishes this case from other invalid sales (buyu' fasidah) where the buyer's subsequent disposition (like selling) does not nullify the sale; the reasoning is that in those sales the invalidity is due to a right of the Shari'ah, and a second buyer's right (like a slave's right) may take precedence due to need. But in this duress case, the right to return belongs to the coerced seller, and both parties' rights are equal, so the first buyer's right does not prevail. The passage then discusses duress in acknowledgment (iqrar): if compelled to acknowledge a debt of 1000 dirhams, an acknowledgment of 500 is void; if compelled to acknowledge 1000 but acknowledges 2000, only the extra 1000 (which was not compelled) is binding. Similarly, if compelled to acknowledge dirhams but acknowledges dinars or another type, it is binding because it was not under duress. The commentary then restates and expands on the original matn: voluntary acceptance of price or delivery of the sold item are signs of ratification. Finally, it addresses the situation where the sold item perishes in the buyer's hand while not under duress: the buyer is liable for its value. The coerced seller may claim the value from either the buyer or the coercer; if he claims from the coercer, the coercer can recourse against the buyer; if he claims from the buyer, the buyer has no recourse against the coercer.

Classification of 'Ushri and Kharaji Lands with the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Revived Dead Land
Explains rulings on conquered land, revived dead land, and water-based classification with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for classifying agricultural lands as 'ushri or kharaji, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on revived dead land, and determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the two positions.

Core Idea
Land conquered by force is kharaji; land irrigated by rain or springs is 'ushri; revived dead land's classification is disputed between proximity (Abu Yusuf) and water source (Muhammad).

This lesson from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses the legal classification of agricultural lands in the Hanafi school. Two main categories exist: 'ushri (tithe land) and kharaji (land tax land). The default rule for conquered land is kharaji, but an exception exists for lands irrigated by natural sources like rain and springs, which remain 'ushri. A key intra-Hanafi dispute arises regarding revived dead land: Abu Yusuf ties it to the surrounding area's classification, while Muhammad al-Shaybani looks at the water source. Understanding this dispute is essential for applying the correct tax obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule for conquered land
State the rule: every land conquered by force (عنوة) and left with its original inhabitants is kharaji land. This is because kharaj is a tax on non-Muslims.
This rule applies only if the land is reached by rivers (the water source for kharaj).
2
Apply the irrigation exception
If the land is irrigated only by rain or springs (natural sources not controlled by humans), it is 'ushri. The hadith 'What the sky waters: in it is the tithe' and Quranic verse (39:21) equate spring water to rain water.
This exception overrides the conquest rule for such lands.
3
Understand Abu Yusuf's position on revived dead land
Abu Yusuf holds that revived dead land takes the classification of its surrounding area (حيز). If it borders kharaj land, it is kharaji; if borders 'ushr land, it is 'ushri. An exception is Basra, which is 'ushri by ijmā' despite being in a kharaj area.
His reasoning is based on the general rule of proximity; he abandons qiyas for Basra due to Companion consensus.
4
Understand Muhammad al-Shaybani's position on revived dead land
Muhammad classifies revived land based on the water source used for irrigation. If revived by a well, spring, or a great river like the Tigris or Euphrates (which are public domain), the land is 'ushri. This aligns with the hadith on natural water.
For him, the nature of water (public vs. private) is decisive, not location.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Yusuf, a Muslim who revives land near a kharaj area pays kharaj even if he digs a well. Under Muhammad, if he digs a well, the land is 'ushri. The mufta bihi position often follows Muhammad for water sources, but both views are transmitted.
Additionally, a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) reviving land always pays kharaj, regardless of location.

The main intra-Hanafi dispute concerns the revival of dead land (إحياء الموات). Abu Yusuf holds that the revived land takes the classification of the surrounding area (حيز). So if it borders kharaj land, it becomes kharaji; if it borders 'ushr land, it becomes 'ushri. An exception is Basra: by qiyas it should be kharaji, but due to the consensus (ijma) of the Companions who imposed 'ushr on it, Abu Yusuf abandons qiyas and accepts it as 'ushri.

Muhammad al-Shaybani, on the other hand, classifies the revived land based on the source of irrigation water. If the land is irrigated by a well, spring, or a great river like the Tigris or Euphrates—which no one owns—it is 'ushri. This is because such water is akin to rain water (public domain). The Hidayah clarifies: 'ushri water is from rain, wells, springs, and seas; kharaji water is from rivers dug by the Persians (artificial canals). For the great rivers (Sayhun, Jayhun, Tigris, Euphrates), Muhammad says they are 'ushri, Abu Yusuf says kharaji.

Additionally, a dhimmi who revives dead land must pay kharaj even if the land is in an 'ushr area, because kharaj is a form of humiliation for non-Muslims.

The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is not explicitly stated here, but later Hanafi works generally follow the view of Abu Yusuf for proximity and Muhammad for water sources in certain contexts. This passage illustrates the Hanafi method of combining textual evidence (hadith, Quran), qiyas, and ijma, and shows how the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ within the school.

Key Terms
العشر (al-'ushr): The tithe (10% or 5%) due on agricultural produce from 'ushri land.
الخراج (al-kharaj): Annual land tax imposed on kharaji land, originally on non-Muslim inhabitants.
إحياء الموات (ihya' al-mawat): Reviving dead (unowned/unused) land by cultivation, building, or irrigation.
الحيز (al-hayz): Proximity or contiguous area; the surrounding region of a land.

Accepting the Statement of a Fasiq in Transactions
Ruling on accepting a fasiq's statement in business dealings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a fasiq's statement in mu'amalat and distinguish it from the rule in testimony.

Core Idea
In transactions, a fasiq's word is accepted; in testimony, it is not.

This lesson addresses a key distinction in Hanafi law: moral character matters in testimony but not in contracts. The ruling is straightforward: a fasiq's statement is accepted in all financial transactions, such as agency, partnership, and trade permissions. Understanding this helps you see how Hanafi fiqh prioritizes the form and function of contracts over the personal piety of the parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that the ruling applies to mu'amalat (financial transactions), not to everything a fasiq says.
Mu'amalat includes contracts like sale, agency, partnership, etc.
2
State the ruling
The text says: 'The statement of the fasiq is accepted in transactions.' Memorize this clear rule.
The ruling is mubah (permissible) — it is allowed, not obligatory.
3
Recognize the exception
This rule does NOT apply to testimony (shahada). In testimony, 'adalah (moral uprightness) is required.
The contrast is crucial; many students confuse the two contexts.
4
Apply to examples
The passage lists wakalat (agency), mudaraba (silent partnership), and idhn fi al-tijarat (permission to trade). In all these, a fasiq's word is sufficient.
Think of a scenario: a fasiq says he is your agent — you can rely on it.
5
Contrast with other schools
Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'i) may require 'adalah even in contracts. The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both positions are valid.

Accepting Statements of Minors and Slaves
Ruling with conditions on accepting statements from minors and slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which the Hanafi school accepts the statement of a child or slave in matters of gifts and permissions, and apply this condition to factual scenarios.

Core Idea
A child's word is accepted if the recipient has a strong presumption of truthfulness.

This passage from the Book of Prohibition and Permission addresses a practical issue in daily transactions: can you rely on the word of a child or a slave when they claim to bring a gift or have permission? The Hanafi school answers with a nuanced condition based on customary likelihood and the trustworthiness of the informant. Let's unpack the ruling and its application through the examples given by the early Hanafi masters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The matn states: 'It is permissible to accept in gift and permission the statement of a child and a slave.' Note that the ruling covers both gifts (hibah) and permissions (idhn) – two distinct contexts.
The ruling is a permission (jawaz), not a requirement.
2
Isolate the condition
The commentator immediately adds: 'This is if his opinion is that they are truthful and trustworthy.' The condition is ghalabat al-ra'y – a strong presumption of truthfulness based on circumstances.
If no such presumption exists, accepting their statement is not allowed.
3
Apply the condition to concrete examples
Two illustrative cases show how the condition works. First, al-Mabsut: a child with coins – if he asks for household items, presumption of truth; if for sweets, presumption of lying. Second, al-Jami' al-Saghir: a slave girl claiming to be sent as a gift – accepted because the unlikelyhood of the girl running away is low.
The examples reveal that 'urf (custom) and apparent circumstances inform ghalabat al-ra'y.
