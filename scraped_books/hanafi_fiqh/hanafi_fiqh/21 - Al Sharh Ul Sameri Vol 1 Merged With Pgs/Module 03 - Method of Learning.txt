Author's Evidentiary Hierarchy for Fiqh Rulings
Author explains his method of citing hadith for rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the author's priority order of evidence and explain how the source's rank indicates the relative strength of a fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
The strength of a ruling depends on its evidence source: Quran > Sahihain > other Sihah Sittah > lesser hadith works > sayings of Companions > Successors.

This lesson introduces a key methodological principle from al-Sharh al-Thamiri: the hierarchical order of evidence used by the author to support each ruling. Understanding this hierarchy allows you to assess the certainty and persuasiveness of a ruling, especially when engaging with scholars from other madhhabs. The author designed this system to respond to student requests for textual proof and to maintain unity in diverse mosque communities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Recognize why the author developed this hierarchy: students in multi-madhhab mosques faced demands from Shafi'is, Malikis, and Hanbalis for textual proof (Quran/hadith) rather than rational arguments alone.
The author wanted to equip students with concrete evidence for each ruling.
2
List the Twelve Sources in Order
Write down the author's exact priority order: (1) Quranic verse, (2) Sahih al-Bukhari, (3) Sahih Muslim, (4) Sunan Abi Dawud, (5) Sunan al-Tirmidhi, (6) Sunan al-Nasa'i, (7) Sunan Ibn Majah, (8) Sunan al-Dar Qutni, (9) Sunan al-Bayhaqi, (10) Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, (11) Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, (12) saying of Companion or Successor (if none, leave blank).
The first seven are the Sihah Sittah plus Quran; the rest are supplementary.
3
Understand the Implied Strength Ranking
The author states that ruling strength decreases as you go down the list: Quran strongest, then Sihah Sittah, then other hadith works, then sayings of Companions/Successors. This helps you know how strongly to advocate for a ruling in debate.
Lower-ranked evidence does not mean the ruling is invalid; it is simply less authoritative.
4
Apply the Hierarchy
When studying a ruling, first check the cited source. If it is a Quranic verse, recognize it as the highest level. If it is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, understand it is based on Companion/Successor sayings and should be presented with appropriate caution in inter-madhhab discussions.
This prevents unnecessary argumentation and promotes unity.

Therefore, this humble one was also troubled and thought constantly: if there is an opportunity, I should quote the complete hadith for every issue of al-Quduri, along with the chapter, page, and hadith numbers, so that students may have ease and satisfy those of other schools. If anyone wants to see the original book, they can refer to it. By writing the number of the hadith, chapter, and book, students will also know the level of the issue: if proven by a verse, it is strong; if proven by a hadith from the six authentic books, it is of a lower grade; if from Dar Qutni or Sunan al-Bayhaqi, it is of a lower grade; if from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba or Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq as a saying of a Companion or Successor, it is of an even lower grade. Commentary
This passage is a methodological preface (front matter) from al-Sharh al-Thamiri, where the author explains his approach to citing evidence for the rulings he discusses. He responds to students who complained that rational arguments alone are insufficient when debating with followers of other madhhabs (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) who demand textual proof from the Quran and hadith. The author states that he will provide complete hadith citations with references, following a strict priority order to show the strength of each ruling. The priority order is: (1) Quranic verse, (2) hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari, (3) Sahih Muslim, (4) Sunan Abi Dawud, (5) Sunan al-Tirmidhi, (6) Sunan al-Nasa'i, (7) Sunan Ibn Majah (these six are the authentic books), then (8) Sunan al-Dar Qutni, (9) Sunan al-Bayhaqi, (10) Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, (11) Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba. If none of these contain a hadith, he cites the saying of a Companion (qawl sahabi) or a Successor (qawl tabi'i). If even that is unavailable, he leaves the space blank and appeals to scholars to inform him if they find evidence. He also mentions that he includes the positions of Imams Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali with their own proofs in the same order. The author stresses that he does not skip directly to a Companion's saying without searching for a hadith first. This methodology ensures that students understand the weight of each ruling: those proven by the Quran are strongest, followed by the six books, then lesser hadith collections, then sayings of Companions/Successors. The author also notes the geographical differences regarding hadith narrators. This passage is essential for understanding the epistemological hierarchy of evidence used throughout the book.

Key Terms
صحاح ستہ (sihah sittah): The six authentic hadith collections: Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Sunan Abi Dawud, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan al-Nasa'i, Sunan Ibn Majah.
قول صحابی (qawl sahabi): The saying or legal opinion of a Companion of the Prophet ﷺ. Used as evidence when no clear Quran or hadith is found.
قول تابعی (qawl tabi'i): The saying of a Successor (a Muslim who met a Companion). Used as lower-level evidence.
مصنف (musannaf): A collection of hadith arranged by topic (rather than by narrator). Refers specifically to Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba in this context.

What is Hanafiyyah? The School of Three Imams and Its Sources
Explaining the meaning of Hanafi madhhab and its foundational sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the meaning of Hanafiyyah as the collective school of the three Imams and identify the six sources of Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Hanafiyyah is the collective school of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani — not just one Imam.

The Hanafi madhhab is often misunderstood as only the opinions of Imam Abu Hanifa. This passage clarifies that the school includes the views of his two primary students, the Sahibayn. It also outlines the six sources from which Hanafi rulings are derived, addressing common misconceptions about reliance on reason.

Guided Path
1
Define Hanafiyyah
Understand that the term 'Hanafiyyah' refers to the collective fiqh of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. A fatwa based on any of their views is considered Hanafi.
The author emphasizes that acting on the Sahibayn's views does not exit one from the madhhab.
2
Identify the sources of Hanafi fiqh
The passage lists six sources: Quran, Hadith, statements of Companions (qawl sahabi), fatwas of Successors (fatwa tabi'i), ijma (consensus), and qiyas (analogy). Each ruling in al-Quduri is traced to one of these.
This refutes the claim that Hanafis rely solely on rational evidence.
3
Analyze the precautionary approach of Imam Abu Hanifa
Imam Abu Hanifa's positions often show caution because he codified fiqh first and faced scrutiny. His students sometimes differed when strong hadiths supported other views, aligning with other schools.
This explains differences within the madhhab without diminishing Imam Abu Hanifa's excellence.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Hanafiyyah is the name of the collective of the three Imams. My respected teacher used to say that Hanafiyyah is not only the name of the school of Imam Abu Hanifa, but it is the name of the collective of the schools of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them. If one acts upon the school of any one of them, it will be considered acting upon the Hanafi school. And if a fatwa is given according to the school of Imam Muhammad or Imam Abu Yusuf, it will not be considered outside the Hanafi school. This is why in important Hanafi books like al-Quduri and al-Hidayah, the schools of these two Imams are recorded, and when necessary, fatwa is given according to them. The school of Imam Abu Hanifa is based on precaution. Imam Abu Hanifa was a very pious and God-fearing man. Therefore, he always gave fatwa based on precaution and adopted that school. Another matter is that at that time fiqh had not yet been codified. Imam Abu Hanifa is the first Imam who codified fiqh and usul al-fiqh. Therefore, if he had adopted a different approach besides precaution, every person would have raised a finger. So he adopted a precautionary school, even if it required the fatwa of a Tabi'i. But his distinguished students, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, sometimes adopted another school in light of Hadith, and with an open heart they recorded the school with evidences. Now the readers have a choice to adopt the school of the Great Imam or the school of his distinguished students. In both cases, the excellence goes to the Great Imam. In the last century, the credit for highlighting and spreading the school of Imam Azam lies with the Deobandi school of thought. They also adopted a precautionary approach and generally gave fatwa based on precaution like Imam Azam. Therefore, some readers raised an objection and said that the Hanafi school differs from Hadith. But they may not have considered that in those issues where they do not find Hadith, the school of the two important pillars of the Hanafis differs from Imam Azam. And for the school they have chosen, there are 100% authentic Hadiths. It is another matter that on such occasions, the school of the two companions (Sahibayn) coincides with Imam Shafi'i and Imam Malik. In the book under review, 'Al-Sharh al-Numayri', you will see throughout that wherever the two companions differed from Imam Azam, Imam Azam has the statement of a Sahabi or fatwa of a Tabi'i, while the two companions have Hadiths. But Imam Azam's school is based on precaution. (1) My humble opinion is that those who raised the objection only considered the school of Imam Azam, and because they did not find Hadith in some places, they strengthened their objection against the entire Hanafi school. They did not pay attention to the difference of his distinguished students Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad and their strong Hadiths, and did not consider that they too are two important pillars of the Hanafi school. And the publication of all issues is indebted to their pens. (2) They also did not consider that the school of Imam Azam is based on precaution, and he is the first codifier of fiqh, due to which he had to adopt a precautionary approach. (3) It is not correct to assume that the Hanafi school is based on rational evidence. Rather, later scholars used rational evidence to explain wisdom. Otherwise, the entire al-Quduri, this humble one has examined every issue: it is derived from either a verse, Hadith, statement of a Sahabi, or fatwa of a Tabi'i. Or from one of these four, a principle is established and from the principle the issue is extracted. Issues are not based solely on rational evidence. And where only rational evidence is mentioned, this humble one has written the principle, and the principles are derived from Hadith, so those issues are also derived from Hadith. Only in the Book of Iman and the Book of Qada, some issues are based on the idioms of that time. Therefore, there the issue is written under idioms. Those issues have no Hadith or Quran. Obviously, these issues are based on idioms. So where would Hadith or verses be found for these issues? The schools of the Hanafis, indeed all the noble Imams, are derived from (1) Quran, (2) Hadith, (3) statement of a Sahabi, (4) fatwa of a Tabi'i, (5) Ijma, and (6) Qiyas. Therefore, it is not correct to point fingers at the noble Imams. The most humble servant: Thamir al-Din Qasimi.

Commentary
This passage is a front-matter introduction explaining the nature and sources of the Hanafi madhhab. The author clarifies that the Hanafi school is not limited to the views of Imam Abu Hanifa alone, but includes the positions of his two main disciples, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. The passage concludes by listing the six sources of Hanafi fiqh: Quran, hadith, qawl sahabi, fatwa tabi'i, ijma, and qiyas, asserting that criticism of the Imams is unfounded.

Hanafi Fiqh: Rooted in Revelation, Not Pure Reason
Author refutes the charge of excessive rationalism in Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six primary sources of Hanafi fiqh and explain why rational arguments are only secondary explanatory tools.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh derives directly from Quran, Sunnah, and scholarly consensus, not from pure rational speculation.

This passage addresses a common misconception: that the Hanafi school relies excessively on rational reasoning. The author, Thamir al-Din al-Qasimi, firmly rebuts this by demonstrating that every ruling in a foundational text like Al-Quduri is traced back to scriptural or consensus-based evidence. As you work through this lesson, you will learn to identify the actual sources of Hanafi law and understand the limited role of rationality within it.

Guided Path

Read the opening lines where the author rejects the notion that the Hanafi madhhab is based on rational proof. Note the specific claim being refuted.

List the Stated Sources
The author enumerates the sources from which Hanafi rulings are derived. Extract the six sources mentioned in the passage: Quran, Hadith, saying of a Companion, fatwa of a Tab'i, consensus (ijma), and qiyas (analogy).
These are the classical uṣūl al-fiqh sources accepted by all Sunnī schools, though with differences in detail.

Understand the Role of Rational Proof
The author explains that later scholars sometimes used rational arguments (dalīl 'aqlī) to explain the wisdom behind rulings, but the derivation itself is from texts. Note that rational proof is secondary, not fundamental.

Examine the Exceptions

Customary usage is a recognized secondary source in Hanafi fiqh, but only in limited contexts.
5
Synthesize the Defense
Combine all points to articulate why criticizing the Hanafi school for being 'rationalist' is unfounded. Recognize that the school's methodology is firmly rooted in revelation and scholarly consensus.
This defense applies to all imams, not just Hanafis, as the author explicitly extends it.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological defense of the Hanafi school by the author, Thamir al-Din al-Qasimi. He refutes the misconception that the Hanafi madhhab relies primarily on rational arguments (dalil aqli). The author insists that every ruling in the Quduri (a foundational Hanafi text) is derived from scriptural sources: either a verse of the Quran, a hadith, a saying of a Companion, or a saying of a Tabi'un. Alternatively, principles are established from these sources and rulings extracted. Rational arguments are only used by later scholars to explain the wisdom behind rulings, not as the basis. The author notes exceptions: in Kitab al-Iman and Kitab al-Qada', some rulings depend on the customary idioms of that era, so they lack direct scriptural evidence. Nonetheless, he affirms that all imams' schools are based on six sources: Quran, Hadith, qawl Sahaba, fatwa Tabi'een, ijma (consensus), and qiyas (analogy). Hence, criticizing the imams is unjustified.

Key Terms

مسلک (mashlak): School or madhhab; the specific legal methodology and rulings of a particular imam.
اصول (usul): Principles of jurisprudence (usul al-fiqh) from which specific rulings are derived.
استنباط (istinbat): Deduction or derivation of a legal ruling from its source (Quran, Hadith, etc.).
محاورات (muhawarat): Current idioms, customary expressions, or prevalent usage in a particular time and place.
