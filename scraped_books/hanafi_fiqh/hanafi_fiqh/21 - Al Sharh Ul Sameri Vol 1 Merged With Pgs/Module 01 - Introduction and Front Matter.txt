Introducing Al-Sharh al-Thamiri and the Hanafi Tradition
Front matter with title, contents, and publication details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the title, author, and fiqh chapters covered in Volume 1 of Al-Sharh al-Thamiri.

Core Idea
Al-Sharh al-Thamiri is a modern commentary on the foundational Hanafi matn, Al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri.

Every journey in Hanafi fiqh begins with a matn (text) and its commentary. This lesson orients you to one such pair: Al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri and its commentary Al-Sharh al-Thamiri by Maulana Samiruddin Qasimi. Understanding what a commentary does and which chapters it covers is the first step in systematic learning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the title and its components
Notice the phrase 'Al-Sharh al-Thamiri ala al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri'. 'Sharh' means commentary, 'Thamiri' means precious, 'ala' means upon, 'Mukhtasar' means concise, 'Quduri' refers to the author Imam al-Quduri. So the title tells you: this is a commentary on a concise text by Imam al-Quduri.
The commentator is Maulana Samiruddin Qasimi, not the original author.
2
Recognize the original text and its author
Al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri was written by Imam Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri (d. 428 AH). It is one of the most widely taught primers in the Hanafi school, covering the major topics of fiqh.
This matn is often the first fiqh text a Hanafi student memorizes.
3
Note the scope of Volume 1
The front matter lists the five books (kutub) included in this volume: Tahara, Salah, Zakat, Sawm, and Hajj. These are the ibadat (acts of worship) chapters in fiqh.
Subsequent volumes likely cover muamalat (transactions) and other topics.
4
Understand the publication context
Published by Khatme Nubuwwat Academy in London, this edition includes a copyright notice transferring rights to Mr. Abdur Rahman Yaqub Bawa. Knowing the publisher and edition helps in citing and locating the correct version.

Features of Al-Sharh al-Thamiri: A Pedagogical Overview
List of twelve features explaining the commentary's methodology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the twelve distinctive features of Al-Sharh al-Thamiri and explain how each serves the goal of accessible Hanafi fiqh instruction.

Core Idea
Al-Sharh al-Thamiri combines numbered rulings, dual proofs, hadith sourcing, usul principles, and a calculator for inheritance to make Hanafi fiqh beginner-friendly.

Al-Sharh al-Thamiri is a contemporary Urdu commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, a foundational Hanafi fiqh text. Its author, Maulana Umaruddin Qasimi, lists twelve features that distinguish this commentary. These features reflect a pedagogical strategy aimed at making fiqh accessible without sacrificing scholarly rigor. Understanding these features helps a student appreciate the methodology behind the commentary and use it effectively.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pedagogical goal
The author wants to make fiqh easy to understand and extract. Scan the features and note which ones directly aid comprehension (e.g., numbering, simple language, concise explanations).
Features 1, 2, 8, 9, 10 serve this goal.
2
Categorize by evidential support
Features 3, 4, 11 deal with proofs: rational and transmitted evidence, hadith collections, and sourcing from major works. Group these together to see the emphasis on evidence-based fiqh.
Note the Hanafi reliance on both aqli (qiyas, istihsan) and naqli (Quran, Sunnah, ijma) proofs.
3
Analyze the usul integration
Feature 5 mentions stating the usul (principle) under which each ruling fits. This links fiqh to its roots in usul al-fiqh, a hallmark of Hanafi scholarship.
4
Examine the linguistic and lexical aids
Features 2 (idiomatic translation) and 6 (lexical research on difficult words) show attention to language. Consider how this helps non-Arabic readers.
5
Evaluate the inheritance calculator
Feature 12 introduces a calculator method for inheritance. Consider the pedagogical benefit: reducing computational errors while keeping the fiqh rules intact. Discuss potential misuse.
This is not a religious innovation but a tool to apply established faraid rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the twelve pedagogical features of Al-Sharh al-Thamiri and describe how they facilitate learning Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A well-structured commentary combines proof, principle, and clarity to make fiqh accessible.

This passage introduces the methodology of a modern Hanafi commentary on al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri. The author lists twelve distinct features that make this commentary student-friendly: from numbering each ruling to providing rational and transmitted proofs, citing hadiths, explaining underlying principles, and even using a calculator for inheritance. Understanding these features gives you insight into how a scholarly work can be designed for effective learning.

Guided Path
1
Count the twelve features
Read through the list of features (numbered 1 to 12, though note a small numbering error where (1) appears twice). Make a mental or written list of each feature.
The features are: numbering rulings, idiomatic translation, rational and transmitted proofs, hadith collection, usul identification, vocabulary research, imam differences, simple explanation of complex issues, conciseness, easy language, authoritative sources, calculator method for inheritance.
2
Categorize the features into pedagogical themes
Group the features by purpose: those that aid understanding (e.g., translation, simple language), those that provide evidence (proofs, hadiths, usul), and those that enhance usability (numbering, calculator).
For example, features 1,2,8,10 focus on clarity; 3,4,5,11 focus on evidence; 7,9,12 focus on brevity and innovation.
3
Identify the intended learning outcome
For each feature, ask: 'How does this help the student learn fiqh more effectively?' For instance, numbering allows quick reference, while citing usul ties rulings to legal principles.
The overarching goal is to make fiqh accessible without sacrificing depth.
4
Relate the features to the table of contents
Scan the table of contents provided. Notice how the book is organized by chapters (e.g., Kitab al-Taharah) and that each chapter contains numbered issues. This shows how the features are implemented throughout the book.
The table of contents gives a bird's-eye view of the fiqh topics covered, from taharah to later sections.
5
Evaluate the innovation of the calculator method
Reflect on the twelfth feature: using a calculator for inheritance. Consider its benefits (speed, accuracy) and how it aligns with traditional fiqh methodology (it does not change the rules, just the calculation method).
This feature shows the author's concern for modern pedagogical tools.

Navigating the Fihrist of Al Sharh Ul Sameri
Table of contents with chapter titles and reference numbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the structure of a Hanafi fiqh fihrist and locate specific chapters by their issue number ranges and page references.

Core Idea
The fihrist is your roadmap to the book's rulings.

A table of contents (fihrist) is more than a list — it is the key to efficiently finding rulings in a classical Hanafi text. This lesson teaches you how to read the fihrist of Al Sharh Ul Sameri, identifying how chapters (abwāb) are organized with issue number ranges and page references. Even with imperfect OCR, you will learn to navigate the book's structure, distinguishing major divisions like purification (ṭahārah), prayer (ṣalāh), and obligatory charity (zakāh).

Guided Path
1
Identify the three columns
The fihrist has three main columns: a numbering column (chapter or issue number), a topic column (the chapter title in Arabic or Urdu), and a page column (either a page range or a single page number). On page 8, the third column appears to be a page range; on page 9, it is a single page number. This inconsistency may be due to OCR or layout, but the pattern is clear: the topic and its place in the book.
Some entries have unclear numbers due to OCR corruption (e.g., ΥΛ, YEEEE). Focus on the overall structure.
2
Locate the major book divisions
The fihrist shows the book is divided into three major sections: Purification (ṭahārah), Prayer (ṣalāh), and Zakāh. The purification section begins with 'Issues of Wells' (کنویں کے مسائل) and continues through chapters on tayammum, wiping over khuffs, menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and impurities. The prayer section starts with 'Book of Prayer' (كتاب الصلوة) and includes adhan, conditions of prayer, description of prayer, witr, congregation, disliked acts, missed prayers, prostrations, etc. The zakāh section begins with 'Book of Zakāh' (كتاب الزكوة) and details zakat on camels, sheep, silver, gold, trade goods, crops, and who may receive charity.
The fihrist uses the standard Hanafi sequence: ṭahārah → ṣalāh → zakāh.
3
Note the issue number ranges
Each chapter is assigned a range of 'issue numbers' (مسئلہ نمبر). For example, 'Issues of Wells' covers issues 47 to 58. These issue numbers refer to the numbered rulings within the book. The ranges help you see how much content a chapter contains and allow quick cross-referencing.
Some ranges appear corrupted (e.g., 'from 59 to 6' is likely an OCR error). Use the overall logik to infer correct ranges.
4
Understand the page reference system
On page 8, the page column gives a page range (e.g., '24' for wells, '60' for tayammum). On page 9, the page column gives a single page number for the start of each chapter (e.g., page 216 for prostrations of recitation, page 221 for traveler's prayer). This dual system suggests the fihrist may have two columns: one for the starting page and one for the range, but OCR merged them. When using the book, rely on the page numbers to locate the first issue of each chapter.
Always verify page numbers against the actual book if possible.

The Evidentiary Hierarchy in Sharh al-Thamiri
Front matter: taqriz and methodological description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three-tier hierarchy of evidence used in Sharh al-Thamiri (Quran, hadith, qawl sahabi) and explain the role of qawl sahabi as a source in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A Hanafi commentary proves every ruling with Quran, then hadith, then qawl sahabi — this hierarchy is the bedrock of evidence-based fiqh.

This passage presents the commendation (taqriz) of a renowned Hanafi scholar endorsing a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author's method is described: for every fiqh issue, he first seeks a Quranic verse, then a hadith from the six authentic books, and failing that, a saying of a Companion (qawl sahabi) or Follower. This hierarchy reflects the Hanafi school's approach to evidence, where the strength of a ruling is tied to its textual basis. In this lesson, we will explore this methodology and focus on qawl sahabi as a legitimate source when primary texts are absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three-Tier Hierarchy
List the three sources in order of priority as described in the taqriz: Quranic verse, then hadith from Sihah Sittah, then qawl sahabi or qawl tabi'i.
2
Understand the Priority Reasoning
Why is the Quran first? Because it is the primary and most authoritative source. Hadith comes next, and only when neither is available does the author resort to sayings of Companions. This order ensures maximum authenticity.
In Hanafi usul, the Quran is qat'i al-thubut; ahad hadith are zanni, but still preferred over qawl sahabi.
3
Define Qawl Sahabi in Hanafi Fiqh
A qawl sahabi is a legal opinion or statement from a Companion of the Prophet. In the Hanafi school, it is considered a source (hujja) under certain conditions, especially if it is widely known and not contradicted.
Other schools differ on the authority of qawl sahabi; Hanafis accept it as a secondary source.
4
Apply the Hierarchy to a Sample Issue
Consider a hypothetical fiqh issue. First check if a Quranic verse addresses it. If not, search for a hadith in the Sihah Sittah. If still not found, look for a qawl sahabi in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah or similar collections.
This method ensures that every ruling in the commentary is grounded in textual evidence.
5
Recognize the Broader Significance
The taqriz praises this work for making the evidentiary basis clear. This helps students distinguish rulings by their level of proof and understand the hierarchy of sources.
Such methodological transparency is valued in the Hanafi tradition for training future jurists.

The Hanafi Scholar's Hierarchy of Evidence
Author explains his method for sourcing hadith and other authorities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the author's hierarchy of proofs and apply it to determine the source of a fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
Search hadith first from Sihah Sittah, then other collections, then qawl sahabi, then qawl tabi'i.

In the preface to Al-Sharh al-Thameeri, the author outlines a meticulous method for sourcing legal proofs. He prioritizes authentic hadith from the six canonical collections, then moves to other hadith works, then to sayings of Companions, and finally to sayings of Successors. This hierarchy reflects the Hanafi commitment to the strongest evidence first. Understanding this method helps you appreciate how Hanafi scholars construct their rulings with care.

Commentary
This passage from the preface of Al-Sharh al-Thameeri explains the author's meticulous methodology for citing sources in his fiqh work. He begins by listing the six authentic hadith collections (Sihah Sittah) with their details. He then outlines a hierarchy: first he searches for hadith in these six books; if not found, he turns to other well-known collections like Dar Qutni, Bayhaqi, Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba. If still unsuccessful, he resorts to sayings of Companions (qawl sahabi) or Successors (qawl tabi'i). If even those are unavailable, he leaves the proof blank and requests scholars to inform him of any missing proofs. He emphasizes that he did not simply skip to a Companion's saying without searching for hadith; every citation reflects an exhaustive effort. Furthermore, he includes the positions of Imam al-Shafi'i, Imam Malik, and Imam Ahmad in his work, presenting their proofs in the same hierarchical order. He notes that the author of Al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) also treated these imams with great respect, and he follows that tradition. Distinguishing Hadith from Athar in Hanafi Fiqh
Author's note on labeling Hadith vs Athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a prophetic hadith and an athar (Companion or Successor saying) and explain the significance of this distinction in Hanafi evidence hierarchy.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh relies on athar from Companions as a source; labeling them correctly ensures proper evidence weight.

In his preface, the author explicitly states he will label prophetic reports as 'Hadith' and reports from Companions or Successors as 'Athar'. This simple terminological distinction reflects a deeper principle in Hanafi usul: the proofs are ranked, and the category of the report affects its legal weight.

Guided Path
1
Define the two terms as used in this book
Hadith = words/actions/approvals of the Prophet (ﷺ). Athar = sayings/actions of Companions or Successors. This is the author's explicit definition in feature 9.
Note that in other contexts, 'athar' can be synonymous with 'hadith', but here it is specifically used for non-prophetic reports.
2
Understand why this distinction matters in Hanafi fiqh
Hanafis consider the practice of the Companions (athar) as evidence, but it is not on par with prophetic hadith. When a Companion's statement contradicts qiyas, the Hanafi may prefer the Companion's statement (qawl al-sahabi) under certain conditions.
This is part of the Hanafi usul principle that the Companions are reliable transmitters and their ijtihad carries weight.
3
Identify how to use the labeling in study
When reading Al-Sharh al-Thamiri, seeing 'Hadith' indicates a direct proof from the Prophet; seeing 'Athar' indicates a Companion/Tabi'i view, which may be used to support or explain the ruling.
The author's labeling allows the student to quickly assess the strength of the evidence.

Definition and Subject Matter of Fiqh
Definition of fiqh linguistically and terminologically with its subject matter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the classical Hanafi definition of fiqh and identify its subject matter, distinguishing between fundamental and subsidiary rulings.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of subsidiary Shari'ah rulings from detailed evidences, applied only to the actions of a mukallaf.

Before studying specific rulings, a faqih must understand what fiqh is and what it covers. This lesson unpacks the classical Hanafi definition, showing that fiqh is a science of practical rulings derived from detailed proofs, and that it concerns only the actions of a legally responsible person (mukallaf). Understanding this foundation prevents confusion between theology and fiqh, and clarifies why minors and the insane are not addressed by fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify lexical vs terminological meaning
First, note that linguistically, fiqh means understanding and clarity. Terminologically, it is a defined science with specific scope. This distinction is crucial: the technical meaning is narrower than the linguistic one.
The term 'faqih' in Arabic originally meant a person with deep understanding, but in Shari'ah it refers specifically to one who knows the detailed rulings.
2
Break down the terminological definition
The definition 'Knowledge of subsidiary Shari'ah rulings from their detailed evidences' has four key parts: 'knowledge' (a acquired discipline), 'subsidiary rulings' (practical, not belief), 'Shari'ah' (from revelation), and 'from detailed evidences' (not general principles). Examine each part.
The phrase 'subsidiary' (far'iyyah) contrasts with 'fundamental' (asliyyah) rulings which are beliefs.
3
Distinguish fundamental from subsidiary rulings
The text explains that ahkam are of two types: fundamental (e.g., tawhid, prophethood) and subsidiary (e.g., prayer, fasting, transactions). Fiqh deals only with subsidiary rulings. This excludes theology (kalam) from fiqh.
This distinction is critical: a student must not confuse fiqh with 'ilm al-kalam.
4
Understand 'from detailed evidences'
Fiqh knowledge must be accompanied by its specific proof. For example, stating that Hajj is obligatory must be backed by the Quranic verse. This means fiqh is not mere opinion but evidence-based derivation.
The Hanafi school emphasizes this: a faqih must know the dalil for each ruling, even if he does not quote it in every fatwa.
5
Recognize the subject matter: actions of a mukallaf
The text states that fiqh discusses the actions of a mukallaf (mature, sane person) and categorizes them into ten legal values (fard, wajib, sunnah, haram, etc.). Actions of minors and the insane are not addressed because they are not legally responsible.
This restriction is a key Hanafi principle: taklif (legal responsibility) requires sanity and puberty.

Al-Sharh al-Thamiri, Part 1, 21. Science of Fiqh. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Science of Fiqh. Lexical meaning of fiqh: The lexical meaning of fiqh is to open and clarify something. A faqih is called that scholar who clarifies the legal rulings and traces their realities and clarifies difficult and complex issues. Terminological meaning: In the terminology of the people of Shari‘ah, the famous definition of fiqh is: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary Shari‘ah rulings from their detailed evidences.' Translation: Fiqh is called that knowledge of subsidiary Shari‘ah rulings which is acquired from the detailed evidences of the rulings. Explanation of each component: al-ahkam al-shar‘iyyah al-far‘iyyah: Rulings are of two types: fundamental and subsidiary. Fundamental rulings: Belief rulings are called fundamental rulings, such as the oneness of the Sacred Lord, prophethood, the coming of the Prophet, knowledge of the Hereafter; all these are belief rulings and are called fundamental rulings. The science of fiqh does not conflict with this because it deals with subsidiary rulings. Subsidiary rulings: The rulings that pertain to action, such as the rulings of prayer, fasting, Hajj, buying and selling, etc. In the science of fiqh, these rulings are stated. From their detailed evidences: The meaning of this phrase is that the detailed evidences of the rulings should also be known. For example, if it is said that Hajj is obligatory, then the verse 'And complete the Hajj and the Umrah for Allah' (al-Baqarah 2:196) should be known as its evidence. Or if the ruling that prayer and zakat are obligatory is stated, then the evidence 'Establish prayer and give zakat and bow with those who bow' (al-Baqarah 2:43) should also be known. The conclusion is that applying the ruling of permissibility, prohibition, dislike, obligation, etc., to every detail of human actions, and stating the evidence for each, is the science of fiqh. The Knowledge of Fiqh.
Linguistic Meaning of Fiqh: The linguistic meaning of fiqh is to open and clarify a thing. A faqih is that scholar who clarifies the Shar'i rulings and traces their realities and clarifies ambiguous and complex issues.
Terminological Meaning: In the terminology of the people of Shar', the famous definition of fiqh is: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary Shar'i rulings from their detailed evidences.' Translation: Fiqh is called that knowledge of subsidiary Shar'i rulings which is obtained from the detailed evidences of the rulings.
Explanation of Each Component:
Al-ahkam al-shar'iyyah al-far'iyyah: Rulings are of two types: primary and subsidiary. Primary rulings: Rulings of belief are called primary rulings, such as the oneness of Allah, prophethood, the coming of a messenger, knowledge of the hereafter; all these are beliefs and are called primary rulings. The science of fiqh does not conflict with this because it deals with subsidiary rulings. Subsidiary rulings: Those rulings that are related to action, such as rulings of prayer, fasting, Hajj, buying and selling, etc. These are the rulings expounded in the science of fiqh.
Their detailed evidences: The meaning of this phrase is that the detailed evidences of the rulings must also be known. For example, if someone says Hajj is fard, then the verse should also be known that its evidence is present: 'And complete the Hajj and 'Umrah for Allah' (Qur'an 2:196). Or if one states that prayer and zakat are fard, then the evidence should also be known that the verse 'Establish prayer and give zakat and bow with those who bow' (Qur'an 2:43) is its evidence. In summary, applying rulings like permissibility, prohibition, dislike, obligation, etc. to every detail of human actions and stating the evidence for each of these is the science of fiqh.
Subject of the Science of Fiqh: The action and deed of a legally responsible person (mukallaf) regarding which it is discussed whether it is: (1) fard, (2) wajib, (3) sunnah mu'akkadah, (4) sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah, (5) nafl, (6) mustahabb, (7) haram, (8) makruh tahrimi, (9) makruh tanzihi, (10) mubah.
Categorizing the actions of a legally responsible person into the above categories and applying rulings is the subject of fiqh. The restriction 'mukallaf' is applied because the Shari'ah neither applies rulings to the actions of a minor child or an insane person nor discusses them; it only discusses the actions of a mukallaf.
Purpose and Goal: The purpose of the science of fiqh is achieving the prosperity of both worlds (this world and the Hereafter), for the faqih attains high ranks by benefiting Allah's creation in this world, and in the Hereafter he will intercede for whomever he wishes and will be honored with the vision of his Lord. Or, by learning fiqh, he develops the power and disposition to act in accordance with the Shar'i rulings—that is its purpose.
Greatness of the Science of Fiqh: What greater greatness could there be than that the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him) praised the faqih in very lofty words? He said: 'Ibn 'Abbas reported that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: Whomsoever Allah intends good for, He gives him understanding of the religion (fiqh fi al-din).' (Tirmidhi, Chapter: When Allah intends good for a servant, He gives him fiqh in the religion, p. 601, no. 2645; Musnad Ahmad, Musnad Abi Hurayrah, vol. 2, p. 465, no. 7153). From this hadith, it is understood that Allah grants the knowledge of fiqh fi al-din to the servant concerning whom He decrees good.
In another hadith: 'Ibn 'Abbas said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: A single faqih is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers.' (Tirmidhi, Chapter: On the virtue of fiqh over worship, p. 608, no. 268). This hadith shows that a faqih outweighs a thousand worshippers.
Therefore, learning fiqh and acting upon it is a great virtue.

Introduction of the Book. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. I begin with the name of Allah who is very kind and most merciful. The reason for writing Bismillah first is: (a) The first revelation to the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded reading in the name of Allah: 'Read in the name of your Lord who created' (Surah al-'Alaq 96:1). (b) Prophet Sulayman (peace be upon him) wrote to Bilqis beginning with Bismillah: 'Indeed, it is from Sulayman, and indeed it is in the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful' (Surah al-Naml 27:30). (c) The Qur'an begins with Bismillah. (d) All letters and sermons of the Prophet begin with Bismillah. Therefore the author begins the book with Bismillah. (e) There is a hadith: From Abu Huraira, he said: The Messenger of Allah said: 'Every speech that does not begin with praise of Allah is deficient' (Abu Dawud, Bab al-Hadi fi al-Kalam, vol. 2, p. 1317, no. 4840; Ibn Majah, Bab Khutbat al-Nikah, p. 272, no. 1894). Every speech that does not begin with praise is deficient, and Bismillah is part of praise, so beginning with Bismillah is established. It is recommended to mention Allah before any action, but it is not necessary that it be specifically Bismillah; tasbih or taqdis can also be used. However, the best way is with Bismillah because the Qur'an begins with it. Bismillah: The 'ba' is a preposition with many meanings. Here it denotes attachment (al-ilaq) - i.e., to attach an action to it - or it denotes seeking help - i.e., I begin with the help of Allah's name. Since 'ba' is a preposition, a verb must be assumed to be its object. It is best to assume 'abtadi' (I begin) so that the meaning is 'I begin with the name of Allah.' The 'ba' becomes attached, and 'ism' is subordinate to 'Allah', so the word 'Allah' is given precedence, which is the original intent. Allah: The names of Allah are attributive, and this is the essential name. 'Allah' is derived from 'ilah' which means 'worshipped', and with the definite article it becomes 'the specific worshipped', i.e., Allah. There are no other deities, but polytheists have fabricated them in their belief; separating from that, the specific deity is named Allah. Al-Rahman: On the pattern of fa'lan, derived from rahmah (mercy). Rahmah literally means tenderness of heart. Tenderness in Allah is impossible because He is free from heart and emotion. Therefore, when mercy is attributed to Allah, it means granting favor and kindness. Al-Rahman is an intensive form; it means 'He whose mercy encompasses everything', extremely merciful. Al-Rahim: On the pattern of fa'il, also derived from rahmah and is intensive, but less intensive than al-Rahman because al-Rahman has more letters (five vs. four). Therefore al-Rahman indicates greater intensity. In the Prophet's supplication it comes: 'O Rahman of this world and Rahim of the Hereafter,' which indicates that Allah's mercy is more extensive in this world than in the Hereafter, because in this world mercy is shown to both believers and disbelievers, but in the Hereafter only to believers.

Commentary
This is the author's introduction (khutbat al-kitab), a standard feature in Islamic scholarly works. It begins with the Basmalah and justifies this practice with four proofs: (1) the first revelation commanded reading in Allah's name, (2) Prophet Sulayman's letter to the Queen of Sheba began with Bismillah, (3) the Quran begins with Bismillah, and (4) all the Prophet's letters and sermons begin with Bismillah. The author also cites a hadith stating that any speech that does not begin with praise of Allah is deficient; Bismillah is considered part of praise. Then the author provides a detailed linguistic and theological analysis of each word of the Basmalah. For 'Bismillah', he explains that the 'ba' is a preposition meaning attachment or seeking help, and a verb like 'abtadi' (I begin) is implied. For 'Allah', he states it is the essential name derived from 'ilah' (deity) with the definite article, meaning the only true deity. For 'Al-Rahman', he explains it is an intensive form from 'rahmah' (mercy), but since mercy in Allah's case means favor and kindness (not emotional tenderness), it signifies His mercy encompassing all creation in this world. For 'Al-Rahim', it is also intensive but less so than 'Al-Rahman' because of fewer letters; the Prophet's supplication 'O Rahman of this world and Rahim of the Hereafter' shows that Allah's mercy in this world extends to both believers and disbelievers, whereas in the Hereafter it is only for believers. This sets the tone for the book as a work of fiqh grounded in textual evidence and careful linguistic analysis, a hallmark of Hanafi scholarship.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. I begin with the name of Allah, who is very merciful and extremely compassionate. The reason for writing Bismillah first: (a) The first revelation to the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded recitation in the name of Allah: 'Read in the name of your Lord who created' (Surah Al-Alaq 96:1). (b) Prophet Solomon (peace be upon him) wrote to Bilqis starting with Bismillah: 'It is from Solomon and it is in the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful' (Surah An-Naml 27:30). (c) The Quran begins with Bismillah. (d) All of the Prophet's letters and sermons began with Bismillah. Hence the author began his book with Bismillah. (e) There is a hadith: 'Every speech that does not begin with the praise of Allah is deficient.' Since Bismillah is part of praise, it establishes beginning with Bismillah. It is commendable to mention Allah before any action, but it is not obligatory to use Bismillah specifically; tasbih or taqdis may also be used. However, the best method is with Bismillah because the Quran begins with it. The 'ba' in Bismillah is a preposition with many meanings. Here it indicates attachment, i.e., connecting with an action, or it indicates seeking help: 'I begin with the help of Allah's name.' Since 'ba' is a preposition, an omitted verb must be assumed. It is best to assume 'abtadi' (I begin) so that the meaning is 'I begin with the name of Allah.' Then the word 'Allah' is given precedence, which is the intended purpose. 'Allah' is a proper name derived from 'ilah' (deity) with the definite article, meaning the sole deity. Other so-called deities are only in the belief of polytheists. 'Ar-Rahman' is on the pattern of fa'lan, derived from 'rahmah' (mercy). Mercy in Allah means bestowing favor and kindness. It is a hyperbolic form, meaning 'the One whose mercy encompasses everything.' 'Ar-Raheem' is on the pattern of fa'eel, also derived from mercy but with less intensity than Rahman. The wisdom of presenting Rahman before Raheem may be that Rahman relates to this world, which comes first. (1) All praise is due to Allah, the Lord of all worlds. The reason for presenting Alhamdulillah first: The Quran begins with 'Alhamdulillah' and there is a hadith: 'Every important matter that does not begin with praise is deficient.' Hamd means describing the praised one's voluntary virtues, whether in response to blessings or not. Since Allah's blessings are continuous, all our praise is in response to blessings. The definite article in 'al-hamd' indicates generality: all praise belongs to Allah. 'Rabbi al-'alameen': 'Rabb' means to nurture gradually. The word 'Rabb' when used alone refers only to Allah; with a possessive it can refer to others, e.g., 'your king' in the Quran. 'Al-'alameen' is the plural of 'alam' (world), derived from 'alamah' (sign), because all things are signs of the Creator. It is a noun of instrument. (2) 'And the good end is for the Muttaqeen.' 'Taqwa' is derived from 'wiqayah' (to protect oneself); it means not persisting in sins and not being prideful in worship. Some say it is complete following of the Prophet and his Companions. The people of taqwa are successful in this world and the next. (3) 'And prayers and peace be upon His Messenger Muhammad, and upon his family and all his Companions.' 'Salah' means supplication; when attributed to Allah it means mercy, and to angels it means seeking forgiveness.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction (khutbat al-kitab) where he explains the significance of opening with Bismillah and Alhamdulillah. He provides multiple proofs: Quranic verses, prophetic practice, and a hadith emphasizing that every important matter should begin with praise. He then offers a detailed linguistic analysis of each word: the ba in bismillah indicates attachment or seeking help, with an omitted verb 'I begin'; Allah is the proper name of the unique deity; Ar-Rahman and Ar-Raheem both derive from mercy but differ in intensity—Rahman is more comprehensive, encompassing all creation in this world, while Raheem is specific to believers in the hereafter. The author also explains the meaning of hamd (praise) as describing voluntary virtues, and notes that the definite article in 'al-hamd' indicates all praise belongs to Allah. He defines 'Rabb' as the nourisher and 'alameen' as worlds, and explains taqwa as piety. Finally, he ends with salawat upon the Prophet, his family, and companions. The commentary follows the author's order and relies solely on the provided text.

Key Terms
بسم الله (Bismillah): The phrase 'In the name of Allah' used to begin actions, with 'ba' indicating attachment or seeking help.
الحمد لله (Alhamdulillah): All praise belongs to Allah; describing His voluntary virtues, whether in response to blessings or not.
الرحمن (Ar-Rahman): The Most Gracious, a hyperbolic form indicating mercy encompassing all creation in this world.
الرحيم (Ar-Raheem): The Most Merciful, also hyperbolic but with less intensity than Ar-Rahman, often specific to believers in the hereafter.
رب العالمين (Rabb al-'Alameen): Lord of all worlds; 'Rabb' means nourisher and sustainer, and 'alameen' is plural of alam (world), derived from 'alamah (sign).
التقوى (At-Taqwa): Piety: protecting oneself from sins by not persisting in them and not being prideful in worship; complete following of the Prophet and his Companions.
الصلاة (As-Salah): Prayer or blessing; from Allah it means mercy, from angels it means seeking forgiveness.
