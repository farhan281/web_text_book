Zakat Given to Apparent Poor but Later Found Ineligible
Ruling on validity of zakat when recipient is later found to be wealthy, Hashimi, or a kafir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of zakat when the recipient is later discovered to be ineligible, and explain the condition of good faith investigation (tahqiq).

Core Idea
Good faith belief based on due diligence makes zakat valid even if recipient is later found ineligible.

Imagine you give your zakat to someone who appears poor, but later you discover they are actually wealthy, or even a Hashimi or a kafir. Does your zakat count? According to the Hanafi school, if you had a reasonable assumption based on investigation, your obligation is fulfilled. This lesson explains the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, along with the underlying evidence from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is giving zakat to a person believed to be poor. The ruling is that the zakat is valid and does not need to be repeated if the recipient later turns out to be ineligible (wealthy, Hashimi, kafir, or a direct ascendant/descendant).
This ruling is from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad; Abu Yusuf may differ.
2
Recognize the Condition of Investigation (Tahqiq)
The key condition is that the payer had a reasonable belief based on apparent circumstances or investigation. If one gave without any investigation and later discovered ineligibility, the ruling may differ.
The passage emphasizes giving 'assuming he is poor' and 'after investigation'.
3
Apply the Ruling to Specific Cases
The passage lists five cases: recipient is wealthy, Hashimi, kafir, father, or son. In all these, if given in good faith, the zakat is valid.
The prohibition of zakat for Hashimis is absolute, but ignorance at the time excuses.
4
Examine the Evidence (Dalil)
The footnote cites a hadith that charities are the 'dirt of people' and not permissible for the Prophet's family. This supports the ruling that if given in error, it is still valid because the recipient was not at fault.
The evidence from the Sunnah confirms the principle of good faith.
5
Contrast with Other Possible Views
Some other schools or even a literal reading might require repeating the zakat if the recipient is ineligible. The Hanafi school prioritizes the payer's intention and effort.
This dispute is rooted in different understanding of the condition of the recipient.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad regarding cases where zakat is given to someone believed to be a legitimate recipient but later found to be ineligible. The imams rule that if the payer had a genuine belief (based on investigation or apparent circumstances) that the recipient was poor, and it later turns out that the recipient is wealthy, a Hashimi, a disbeliever, or even a direct ascendant or descendant (father or son), the zakat obligation is discharged and does not need to be repeated. The key factor is the payer's due diligence and good faith. The Hanafi school does not require recalling the zakat or re-giving it. The footnote reiterates the prohibition of zakat for the Prophet's family, underscoring that the exception here is only due to ignorance at the time of payment.

تحقیق (tahqiq): Investigation or due diligence to determine eligibility.

Prohibition of Zakat to Banu Hashim and Their Clients
Ruling and proofs for prohibiting zakat to Banu Hashim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibition of zakat for Banu Hashim and their freed slaves, and identify the hadith evidence for this ruling.

Core Idea
Zakat is forbidden to Banu Hashim and their freed slaves because it is the filth of people.

Zakat is a pillar of Islam, but not everyone may receive it. This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling that Banu Hashim—the Prophet's clan—and their freed slaves are prohibited from taking zakat. We'll examine the key hadith proofs and understand who exactly is included in this prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Prohibited Recipients
Read the passage to list exactly who cannot receive zakat. Note the families and the inclusion of freed slaves.
Hanafis include five families: Ali, Abbas, Ja'far, Aqil, and al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib.
2
Locate the Primary Proof
Find the hadith from Abdullah ibn Nawfal. Note the key phrase: 'These charities are nothing but the impurities of people, and they are not permissible for Muhammad nor for the family of Muhammad.'
This hadith is from Muslim and Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the Legal Reasoning (Wajh al-Dalalah)
Explain how the hadith proves prohibition. The word 'لا تحل' indicates clear prohibition, and 'أوساخ' (filth) shows why it is unsuitable for the Prophet's family.
The ruling is derived from the explicit negation.
4
Extend to Freed Slaves
Find the hadith from Abu Rafi' that states 'the clients of a people are from among themselves.' This extends the prohibition to the freed slaves of Banu Hashim.
Freed slaves are legally considered part of their patron's clan.
5
Note the Concession and Ikhtilaf
Read the note about desperate times (ibtira) allowing some later muftis to permit zakat to Banu Hashim. Also note that the Shafi'i and Maliki schools generally agree but differ on other forms of charity.
The original ruling remains haram; concession is only for extreme necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It states that zakat must not be given to Banu Hashim, which includes the families of Ali, Abbas, Ja'far, Aqil, and al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib, as well as their freed slaves. The proof is the hadith from Abdullah ibn Nawfal that zakat is the filth of people and not permissible for Muhammad and his family. Another hadith from Abu Rafi' establishes that freed slaves hold the same status as their patrons, so the prohibition extends to them. The commentary notes that in desperate times, some later jurists have allowed giving zakat to Banu Hashim as a concession. The passage also references the case of Barirah to show that charity can change status when transferred.

Key Terms
بني هاشم (Banu Hashim): The clan of the Prophet Muhammad including the families of Ali, Abbas, Ja'far, Aqil, and al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib.
موالي (mawali): Freed slaves of Banu Hashim who are treated legally as part of the clan.

Mistaken Zakat Giving: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Repayment
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mistaken zakat repayment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi mufta bihi ruling on whether zakat must be repaid if given mistakenly to an undeserving person, and explain the evidence used by Abu Hanifa/Muhammad vs Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Genuine belief in deservingness suffices for zakat; repayment is not required according to the relied-upon Hanafi view.

Zakat must reach its rightful recipients, but what if you give to someone you honestly believe is poor, only to discover later they are rich? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on this very question. This lesson unpacks the positions of the three Hanafi masters and the evidence they cite, so you can confidently apply the school's established ruling.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling when zakat is given to someone mistakenly thought to be deserving. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the giver had a genuine belief that the recipient was poor (or otherwise deserving), and later it becomes apparent that he was not, the zakat is discharged and no repayment is required. They rely on the hadith of Ma'n ibn Yazid where the Prophet said the father's intention suffices, and on the athar of al-Hasan. Abu Yusuf disagrees, requiring repayment because the zakat did not actually reach a deserving person. He cites the athar of Ibrahim. The mufta bihi position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as stated explicitly: 'حنفیہ کے نزدیک دوبارہ دینے کی ضرورت نہیں'. The passage also includes a separate ruling by consensus: if the recipient turns out to be one's own slave or mukatib, the zakat is invalid because ownership reverts to the giver. This is agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.

Mistaken Payment of Zakat: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Presents the ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on mistaken zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on mistaken zakat payment, identify the evidence for each, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Intention alone may suffice for zakat despite mistaken recipient, but Abu Yusuf requires actual delivery.

When a person gives zakat to someone they believe is poor, but later discovers the recipient is rich, Hashimi, a disbeliever, or even a close relative, a dispute arises within the Hanafi school. Imam Abu Hanifa holds the zakat valid due to the payer's intention, while Imam Abu Yusuf requires repetition. This lesson examines the proofs for both views and the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the common scenario: zakat paid to someone thought to be a valid recipient, but later found to be unqualified. Notice the categories mentioned: rich, Hashimi, kafir, father, son.
The payer's mistake is in the identity, not in the intended act.
2
Identify the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Imam Muhammad narrates Abu Hanifa's ruling: no repetition (la i'adah 'alayh). The zakat is valid and fulfilled.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
Identify the opposing position (Abu Yusuf)
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that repetition is required (wa 'alayh al-i'adah). The zakat is not valid because the intended recipient was not actually a deserving poor person.
The disagreement rests on whether intention or actual delivery is decisive.
4
Analyze the evidence for each
Abu Hanifa uses the hadith of Ma'n ibn Yazid: the Prophet accepted the charity based on the father's intention despite the son receiving it. Also an athar from al-Hasan. Abu Yusuf uses an athar from Ibrahim that zakat to a rich person unknowingly does not suffice.
Both sides rely on transmitted reports (athar). The hadith is from Bukhari, thus strong.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the payer owes nothing further; under Abu Yusuf, the payer must give zakat again to a valid recipient.
This affects how a mufti advises a person who discovers the mistake.

Commentary
This passage presents a intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of zakat when the payer mistakenly gives it to an unqualified recipient. The matn states the ruling of Imam Muhammad (which represents Imam Abu Hanifa's view): if one pays zakat to a person he thinks is poor, but later discovers he is rich, a Hashimi, a kafir, or (if he paid in darkness) that the recipient is his father or son, the zakat is valid and no repetition is required. For Abu Hanifa's view, it cites the hadith of Ma'n ibn Yazid, where a father's charity given to his own son by mistake was accepted by the Prophet based on the father's intention. It also cites an athar from al-Hasan that if a rich person is given zakat unknowingly, it suffices. For Abu Yusuf's view, it cites an athar from Ibrahim that if zakat is given to a rich person unknowingly, it does not suffice. However, the primary ruling in the matn is that of Imam Abu Hanifa. The key issue is whether the payer's intention (niyyah) suffices for validity despite the mistake, or whether actual delivery to a deserving recipient is required. The hadith supports the former, while the athar from Ibrahim supports the latter.

Zakat to One's Own Slave or Mukatab is Invalid
States the ruling that zakat to one's own slave or mukatab is invalid by consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on giving zakat to one's own slave or mukatab and explain why it is invalid.

Core Idea
Giving zakat to your own slave is like putting money in your own pocket — no transfer of ownership.

This lesson addresses a critical condition for zakat: the recipient must be a valid owner. If you give zakat to your own slave or mukatab (a slave who has contracted freedom), the zakat is invalid. The reason lies in Hanafi principles of ownership: a slave's property belongs to the master, so no actual transfer occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat to one's own slave or mukatab. The ruling is that it is invalid (batil) by consensus of all Hanafi imams.
Note that 'mukatab' is specifically included despite his contract for freedom.
2
Understand the reasoning
The commentary explains that the slave's property is the master's property. Paying zakat to the slave is like moving money from one pocket to another — ownership does not leave the master.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that a slave cannot own property independently.
3
Recognize the consensus
The text says 'in the view of all of them' and 'by consensus', indicating that this is not a point of ikhtilaf among the Hanafi school.
Cross-madhhab: this ruling is also agreed upon by other schools for similar reasons.
4
Apply the principle to new cases
Consider a person who gives zakat to his own adult son who is still under his financial guardianship? Normally not an issue because the son owns property. But if the son is a slave or mukatab, it applies.
The key is whether the recipient has independent ownership from the giver.

While local distribution is strongly preferred, the school recognizes valid reasons—such as supporting needy relatives or a more impoverished community—that make transfer not only permissible but also rewarding. A hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) underscores the merit of giving to relatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Ruling
The passage states that each community's sadaqah should be distributed among themselves. This establishes the default: zakah is best given locally.
The term 'sadaqah' here means obligatory zakah.
2
Recognize the Two Exceptions
Transfer to another city is allowed when (1) the recipients are the donor's relatives, or (2) the people of that city are more needy than the local poor.
In these cases, the transfer is not only permissible but recommended and carries extra reward.
3
Understand the Hukm for Non-Exceptional Transfer
If zakah is transferred without a valid reason (not relatives, not more needy), the payment is still valid because the recipients are poor and eligible. However, doing so is makruh tanzih (disliked).
Makruh tanzih means it is discouraged but not sinful; one should avoid it.
4
Analyze the Dalil
The hadith 'He has two rewards: the reward of kinship and the reward of sadaqah' provides evidence for the merit of giving to relatives. It implies that transferring to relatives is praiseworthy and encouraged.
The hadith is ahad (single-narrator) and zanni in transmission, but it is accepted for fadail and non-obligatory rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the permissibility of transferring zakah from one city to another. The author states that as a general rule, zakah should be distributed among the people of the same locality. However, there are exceptions: if one has relatives in another city, or if the people of another city are more needy, then transferring zakah there is permissible and even recommended. The hadith quoted indicates that giving zakah to relatives carries a double reward: one for maintaining family ties and one for the sadaqah itself. The Urdu commentary explains that if one transfers zakah to another city without these reasons (i.e., to people who are not more needy and not relatives), the zakah is still valid because the recipients are poor, but doing so is makruh (disliked). The footnote repeats the double reward. Thus, the preferred practice is local distribution, but the rule allows flexibility for genuine needs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib only on those with surplus wealth equal to nisab, not on every Muslim.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on Sadaqat al-Fitr. You will learn the precise conditions that trigger its obligation and the textual proofs from the Sunnah. We will also compare the Shafi'i position to sharpen your understanding of the ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Sadaqat al-Fitr is classified as wajib in the Hanafi school. Note the distinction: wajib (obligatory but from zanni evidence) vs fard (from qat'i evidence).
2
List the conditions for obligation
The passage lists: free Muslim, owner of nisab, and the nisab must be surplus beyond basic needs (hajat asliyyah).
Hajat asliyyah includes dwelling, clothes, furniture, horse, weapon, servant, and yearly provisions.
3

Two hadiths from Bukhari are cited: 'Take sadaqah from their rich and return to their poor' (no. 1496) and 'No sadaqah except from surplus wealth' (no. 2750). Both indicate obligation is on the wealthy only.

4

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that every Muslim, rich or poor, must pay Sadaqat al-Fitr. His evidence is a hadith commanding payment for young and old, free and slave.
The Hanafi response interprets that hadith as general but restricted by the other hadiths or as a recommendation for the poor.
5

The Hanafi position is that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib only upon those who possess nisab beyond basic needs. This is the relied-upon view for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage establishes the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins by stating that it is wajib upon every free Muslim who owns nisab beyond his basic needs. The Urdu commentary clarifies that basic needs include dwelling, clothing, furniture, horse for jihad, weapons, and a servant. One must be free from these and also have enough provisions for the year; then if surplus wealth equals nisab, Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes obligatory. Evidence is provided from two hadiths: one about taking sadaqah from the rich to give to the poor, and another that no sadaqah is due except from surplus wealth ('an zahr ghina). The commentary also quotes Sa'id ibn Jubayr's statement that a person with a house, servant, and horse is not considered rich for zakah purposes. Then the passage presents the view of Imam al-Shafi'i, who considers anyone with more than one day's food as obligated. His evidence is a hadith commanding the payment of Sadaqat al-Fitr for everyone, rich and poor. The Hanafi position is that the payer must be rich (possessing nisab beyond basic needs), while the Shafi'i view is that everyone must pay, regardless of wealth, but the rich pay in purification and the poor receive a greater return. The passage thus sets up an inter-madhhab dispute, though the commentary primarily explains the Hanafi position.

Rulings on Sadaqat al-Fitr: Obligation, Conditions, and Dependents
States Hanafi rulings on Sadaqat al-Fitr with proofs and Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on Sadaqat al-Fitr: who must pay, under what conditions, and for whom, and explain the disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib only on the wealthy with surplus nisab after basic needs; not on the poor.

This lesson from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri presents the complete Hanafi fiqh of Sadaqat al-Fitr: its obligation, the conditions of wealth, the definition of basic needs, and the precise list of dependents one must and must not pay for. It also addresses the Shafi'i counterargument and provides textual proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm level
Sadaqat al-Fitr is classified as wajib in the Hanafi school. Understand the distinction between wajib and fard in this context.
Wajib means obligation proven by zanni evidence; fard is for qat'i evidence.
2
List the conditions for obligation
The person must be free, Muslim, and possess nisab (200 dirhams or equivalent) surplus to basic needs (hajat asliyyah). Basic needs include dwelling, clothes, furnishings, horse, weapon, and slaves for service.
These are items necessary for daily life and jihad; they are not counted toward nisab.
3
Explain who pays for whom
One pays for himself, his minor children, and his slaves for service. He does not pay for his wife or adult children, even if they are under his support.
Wife's maintenance is due to confinement, not guardianship; adult children have separate wealth.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proofs
Main proof is the hadith of Ibn Umar using the word 'farada' along with the principle that charity is only from surplus wealth. The effect of Sa'id ibn Jubayr defines basic needs.
Hadith ahad means zanni al-thubut, supporting the wajib classification.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds it is wajib on anyone with more than one day's food. The Hanafi response: the Shafi'i hadith encourages but does not obligate the poor.
Practical difference: poor are sinful for not paying in Shafi'i, not in Hanafi.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri provides the Hanafi rulings on Sadaqat al-Fitr (Zakat al-Fitr), including its obligation, conditions, who must pay, and who is exempt, along with proofs and a response to Imam al-Shafi'i. The passage opens with the core ruling: Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib upon every free Muslim who possesses nisab (minimum wealth threshold) surplus to basic needs. Basic needs include one's dwelling, clothing, household furnishings, horse for jihad, weapons, and slaves for service. These are considered necessities of life; only wealth beyond these and one year's sustenance triggers the obligation. The Hanafi proof comes from the hadith 'Take charity from their rich and return it to their poor' and the principle that charity is only from surplus wealth. Further, the effect from Sa'id ibn Jubayr states that one who has a house, a servant, and a horse is not considered wealthy—such a person may receive zakat. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that Sadaqat al-Fitr is obligatory on anyone who possesses more than one day's food, citing a hadith that includes the poor. The Hanafi respond that the word 'farada' in the hadith of Ibn Umar indicates obligation, but it is applied to the wealthy as per other proofs; the poor person, even if he pays, will receive a greater reward from Allah, but it is not wajib on him. The passage then details who must pay: one pays for himself, his minor children, and his slaves for service—those whom he fully supports. The proof is the hadith commanding Sadaqat al-Fitr for those whom one supports (mamunun). Finally, the passage clarifies that one does not pay for his wife or adult children, even if they are under his financial support. The wife's maintenance is due because of her confinement (ihtibas) in the husband's home, not because of guardianship (kafalah); thus her wealth is separate. Similarly, an adult child's wealth is separate from the father's, so the father is not obligated to pay for him, even if the father must support him due to need. This ruling protects the independence of the wife's and adult child's property and prevents double obligation.

Pay Fitrana for yourself, your minor children, and your service slaves – those you fully support.

Ruling on Fitrana for Wife and Adult Children
Ruling with proof excluding wife and adult children from Fitrana obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on Fitrana obligation for wife and adult children and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) based on separate property and the nature of nafaqah.

Core Idea
Fitrana is not wajib on a husband/father for his wife/adult child because they have separate legal personality – only the individual with nisab must pay.

This lesson tackles a common confusion: if you support your wife or adult children, must you pay Fitrana for them? The Hanafi school draws a clear line. While you may be responsible for their daily maintenance, Fitrana belongs to the person who owns nisab. The source passage explains that your obligation is based on legal guardianship (kafala), not mere financial support. Let's examine the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is paying Fitrana for one's wife or adult children. The ruling is that it is not obligatory (wajib) for the husband/father; if done voluntarily, it is valid.
The obligation lies with the wife/adult children themselves if they have nisab.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage provides a reasoning-based dalil: the wife's maintenance is due to confinement (ihtibas) in the husband's house, not due to guardianship (kafala). The adult child's property is separate from the father's. This is supported by the general Hanafi principle that Fitrana is tied to personal ownership of nisab.
The dalil is a qawl sahabi (practice of Ibn Umar) as per the analytical toolbox, though not quoted verbatim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: Fitrana is a religious obligation that requires the person to have nisab and be legally responsible. Since the wife's property is separate and the husband's nafaqah is not based on guardianship, the husband does not become legally responsible for her Fitrana. Similarly, the adult child's property is separate, so the father is not responsible.
The key distinction is between nafaqah (maintenance) and kafala (legal guardianship).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that the husband must pay Fitrana for his wife. Their basis is that nafaqah includes all necessities, and Fitrana is a necessity.
The Hanafi school differs based on the specific nature of nafaqah and property independence.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view within the school, based on zahir al-riwayah. The reasoning of separate property and confinement-based nafaqah is considered sound.
There is no intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on this issue.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fitranah for a non-Muslim slave, explain the evidence, and identify the practical difference with the Shafi'i school, and also state the required quantity.

Core Idea
A master must pay fitranah for his non-Muslim slave—the absolute hadith overrides the restricted one.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding Sadaqat al-Fitr. While both schools agree that fitranah is wajib on behalf of Muslim dependents, they diverge on non-Muslim slaves. The Hanafi school relies on general hadith evidence to include all slaves, while the Shafi'i school restricts the obligation to Muslim slaves. We will also explore the quantity of fitranah: half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates, raisins, or barley.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
Recognize that the question is whether a Muslim master must pay fitranah for his non-Muslim slave. The Hanafi position says yes; the Shafi'i says no.
The matn states 'The Muslim pays fitranah on behalf of his non-Muslim slave.'
2
Examine the Hanafi evidence
Look at the general hadith of Ibn Umar (Bukhari) that says 'al-mamluk' without restriction, and the hadith of Ibn Abbas (Darqutni) that explicitly includes 'Jew or Christian'.
These texts are absolute (mutlaq) and support wajib for all slaves.
3
Examine the Shafi'i evidence and Hanafi response
The Shafi'is cite a hadith with the restriction 'among the Muslims' (Bukhari 1504) and argue that charity is worship, so a non-Muslim cannot be its subject. The Hanafis respond that the general hadiths override the restricted one.
The Hanafi response is based on the principle that amm (general) remains binding unless proven otherwise.
4

The practical difference: in Hanafi fiqh, the master pays; in Shafi'i, he does not. Then note that the quantity of fitranah is half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates/barley/raisins, proven by hadith.

Sadaqat al-Fitr is an obligation tied to the moment of Fajr Sadiq on Eid al-Fitr. This lesson clarifies two key exemptions: death before that moment, and conversion or birth after it. It covers three key timings: the recommended time (mustahabb) on Eid day before the prayer, the permissible time (jaa'iz) before Eid day, and the obligation that remains even if delayed past Eid. Understanding these distinctions helps ensure the act is performed correctly and the obligation fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three timing rulings
From the passage, extract three distinct rulings: (1) mustahabb to pay on Eid day before prayer, (2) ja'iz to pay earlier, (3) wajib to pay if delayed. These are separate hukm levels for the same act.
The mustahabb ruling is based on a hadith; the permissibility on athar; the delay ruling on qiyas to other wajibat.
2
Examine the evidence for the recommended time
The hadith from Ibn Umar shows the Prophet (saw) commanded Zakat al-Fitr to be given before people go to prayer. This indicates the preferred time is just before Eid prayer.
The command is not obligatory for the timing, as the ruling is mustahabb, not wajib.
3
Understand the permissibility of early payment
The commentary cites an athar that Ibn Umar used to give Sadaqat al-Fitr a day or two before Eid. The reason is that the underlying cause (sabab) – wealth ownership – already exists, so early payment is valid, similar to giving Zakat early.
This shows the cause (sabab) is the existence of wealth, not the arrival of Eid day itself, though the time of wajib is Subh Sadiq of Eid.
4
Analyze the delay ruling
If Sadaqat al-Fitr is not paid on Eid day, the obligation does not drop; it must still be given. The author analogizes it to prayer: once wajib, it remains until fulfilled.
This emphasizes that the obligation is not tied to the specific day; delay does not absolve.
5
Distinguish the levels of hukm
The three timings correspond to different hukm levels: mustahabb (recommended), jaa'iz (permissible), and wajib (obligatory if delayed). Note that the overall obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr itself is wajib; only the timing preferences vary.
Confusing these levels is a common error. Mustahabb is not wajib; and early payment is not better than the recommended timing.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins by stating that it is mustahabb (recommended) to give the charity on the day of Eid before going out to the musalla (place of prayer). This is based on a hadith from Ibn Umar where the Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered giving zakat al-fitr before people go out for the Eid prayer. The commentator explains that giving it earlier (before Eid day) is permissible because the underlying cause for obligation is possessing wealth (maldari), which already exists before Eid. Therefore, if one gives it before dawn on Eid, or even days earlier, the payment is valid, just as one can give zakat on wealth before its annual cycle is complete. This is supported by the practice of Ibn Umar, who would give it one or two days before Eid. The second ruling (14) states that if one delays giving it beyond Eid day, the obligation does not fall away; it remains due and must be paid later. This is analogous to prayer: once prayer becomes obligatory, it is not dropped. The commentator notes that since the amount is small (one sa' of whole dates or half sa' of wheat), deferring it is not a significant burden.
