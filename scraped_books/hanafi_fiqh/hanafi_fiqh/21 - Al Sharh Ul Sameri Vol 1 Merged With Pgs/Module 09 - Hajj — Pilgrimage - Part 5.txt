Ruling on Leaving Four Shawts of Tawaf al-Ziyarah
Consequence of omitting four shawts of Tawaf al-Ziyarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the consequence of leaving four shawts of Tawaf al-Ziyarah and identify the evidence (dalil) used by the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Leaving four shawts of Tawaf al-Ziyarah keeps one in ihram forever until completion.

This lesson examines a critical ruling in Hajj: what happens when a pilgrim omits a significant portion of Tawaf al-Ziyarah. The Hanafi school holds that if four or more shawts are left, the pilgrim remains in ihram indefinitely until they are performed. The proof is an athar from the Madinan fuqaha reported by Abu al-Zinad, as found in Sunan al-Bayhaqi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its consequence
What is the act? Leaving four shawts of Tawaf al-Ziyarah. Consequence: the pilgrim remains a muhrim forever (abadan) until he completes those shawts.
Note that 'forever' means indefinitely until the condition is fulfilled, not literal eternity.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar (report from the early Muslims) from the Madinan fuqaha, narrated by Abu al-Zinad, recorded in Sunan al-Bayhaqi.
The athar equates forgetting the entire Tawaf al-Ifadah with leaving the majority of it.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
How does the athar prove the ruling? It states that whoever forgets the Ifadah tawaf remains in ihram until he returns and performs tawaf. Since leaving four shawts is the majority, it is treated like leaving the entire tawaf.
The textual indication (dalala) is indirect but sufficient for the Hanafi school.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow the pilgrim to complete the tawaf later without remaining in ihram continuously. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is stricter, requiring continuous ihram until the tawaf is made up.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that the pilgrim must complete the omitted shawts; until then, he remains in ihram and all ihram prohibitions apply.
If marital relations occur, a camel sacrifice (dam) is required as per the athar.

Commentary
This ruling addresses the severe case where the pilgrim omits more than half of Tawaf al-Ziyarah (four or more shawts). Since the majority of the tawaf is left undone, it is as if he did not perform the tawaf at all. Consequently, he remains in the state of ihram permanently until he completes those shawts. This means he is not allowed to have marital relations with his wife until he does so. The proof is an athar from the fuqaha of Madinah reported by Abu al-Zinad: 'Whoever forgets the Ifadah tawaf until he returns to his homeland, he remains forbidden (haram) until he returns to the House and performs tawaf, and if he has intercourse with his wife, he must sacrifice a camel.' Core Idea
Omitting three shawts of Tawaf al-Sadr requires sadaqah, not a full sacrifice.

Tawaf al-Sadr is a wajib act of Hajj. If a pilgrim omits some of its shawts, the compensation depends on the number omitted. This lesson focuses on the ruling when three or fewer shawts are left, which requires a simple sadaqah rather than a larger penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal nature
Recognize that Tawaf al-Sadr is wajib, not fard. This determines the level of compensation.
Tawaf al-Sadr is also called farewell tawaf.
2
Determine the number omitted
The ruling applies to three or fewer shawts omitted. Four or more would have a different ruling.
Majority of the tawaf is considered performed if only a few shawts are missed.
3
State the required compensation
Sadaqah is wajib. This is a fixed charity: half a sa' of wheat or equivalent.
This is milder than a dam (sacrifice) required for omitting parts of the obligatory Tawaf al-Ziyarah.

Omitting all or four shawts of a wajib tawaf requires a sheep as penalty.

Tawaf al-Sadr is a wajib (obligatory) component of Hajj. This lesson addresses what happens if a pilgrim omits all of it or a significant portion—specifically, four out of seven shawts. The Hanafi school prescribes a sacrificial sheep (dam) as the penalty, based on a general principle from the Companion Ibn Abbas. 2
Determine what constitutes omission
The text covers two scenarios: leaving all seven shawts OR leaving four shawts (the majority). In the latter case, the ruling treats it like complete omission.
The logic: if the majority is missing, the act is considered effectively omitted.
3
State the penalty
For both cases, the penalty is 'dam'—a sheep. This is not a camel or charity.
The word 'dam' in this context is specified to a sheep by Hanafi jurists.

Locate the proof
The ruling is based on the athar of Ibn Abbas: 'Whoever forgets something of his rites or leaves it, let him spill blood.' This general principle covers omission of any wajib rite.
The athar is transmitted with a zanni chain but accepted as evidence in this context.
5

The word 'تركه' (leaves it) in the athar applies to both complete and partial omission when the majority is left. Thus, the ruling for four shawts follows the same principle.

Consequence of leaving Sa'i completely

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting Sa'i, explain why it is wajib, and apply the compensation rule.

Core Idea
Sa'i is wajib; omitting it requires a sheep but does not invalidate Hajj.

In this passage, the author addresses a common question: what happens if a pilgrim completely leaves out the Sa'i between Safa and Marwah? The answer clarifies the Hanafi classification of Sa'i as wajib, not rukn, and the consequence—a sheep sacrifice, while the Hajj remains valid. This distinction is crucial for understanding the Hanafi hierarchy of obligations in Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is omitting the Sa'i between Safa and Marwah entirely. The ruling is that a sheep is due, but the Hajj is complete.
The passage explicitly states 'فعليه شاة' (upon him is a sheep) and 'وحجه تام' (his Hajj is complete).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the athar of Ibn Abbas and Hasan al-Basri, which states that a sheep is due for leaving the Sa'i.
This is a Qawl Sahabi, which is zanni in transmission and indication.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit phrase 'عليه دم' directly indicates that a blood sacrifice (sheep) is required, implying that the act is not a pillar but an obligation that can be compensated.
The dalalah is zanni because it is a companion's statement, not a direct revelation.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school considers Sa'i a rukn (pillar), so omission would invalidate the Hajj entirely, requiring the pilgrim to repeat the Sa'i or the whole Hajj.
This difference stems from the classification of Sa'i in each school's usul.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that Sa'i is wajib, not rukn, based on the athar and the overall evidence. Compensation suffices, and the Hajj remains valid.
This is the relied-upon view in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Departing Arafat Before the Imam: Penalty
Penalty for departing Arafat before the Imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for departing Arafat before the Imam and identify the proof from the Prophet's practice.

Core Idea
Departing Arafat before sunset triggers a wajib dam — the Prophet's example sets the sunnah timing.

This lesson addresses a specific timing violation in Hajj: leaving Arafat before the Imam departs after sunset. The text establishes the ruling with a clear penalty. We will examine the ruling's wujub level and its proof from the hadith of Jabir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is departing (ifada) from Arafat before the Imam. The ruling is that a dam (sheep) is wajib upon the pilgrim.
The Imam here refers to the leader of the Hajj caravan, typically the ruler or his representative.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is from the Prophet's (ﷺ) practice as reported in Sahih Muslim: he remained standing at Arafat until after sunset.
The hadith of Jabir indicates the Prophet did not depart until the sun had completely set.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's continuous practice (fi'l) of staying until sunset indicates that departing earlier is a violation. In Hanafi usul, the Prophet's action (fi'l) conveys obligation (wujub) unless proven otherwise.
The hadith is ahad but sahih; the Hanafi school accepts ahad hadith as proof for fiqh rulings.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider standing until sunset as sunnah, not wajib, so no compensation is required for early departure.
This difference arises from different usul principles regarding the indication of the Prophet's action.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is that a dam is wajib and is the mufta bihi. The tarjih is based on the strength of the dalil and the established practice of the Companions.
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon in fatwa.

Commentary
The proper time for standing at Arafat (wufoof) extends from after noon on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah until dawn of the 10th, but the sunnah is to depart after sunset with the Imam. The proof is from the practice of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam) as reported in Sahih Muslim: 'We entered upon Jabir ibn Abdullah... and he did not cease standing until the sun set and the yellowness disappeared a little until the disc disappeared.' This shows the Prophet waited until after sunset to depart, and departing earlier is a violation requiring compensation.

Ruling on Leaving the Stoning (Ramy) of All Days
States ruling and proof for dam on omitting ramy of all or one day.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting ramy al-jimar and explain the application of tadakhul to determine the required dam.

Core Idea
Ramy is wajib daily; omitting any day's ramy requires one dam, and all days combined also require only one dam due to tadakhul.

What happens if a pilgrim omits the stoning of the jamarat during Hajj? This passage from Sharh al-Sameri provides the Hanafi ruling: ramy is wajib each day, and its omission—whether for one day or all three—necessitates a single dam. The author also introduces the principle of tadakhul, where multiple similar violations are covered by one expiation. Let's examine the ruling, its evidence, and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being discussed (ramy al-jimar) and its legal status in the Hanafi school (wajib for each day of Tashriq).
Ramy is not a rukn of Hajj but a wajib act.
2
Understand the consequence of omission
Note that omitting ramy—whether for one day or all days—requires a dam (sacrifice) as compensation. The Hajj remains valid.
A dam is an animal sacrifice; if unable, alternatives exist.
3
Apply the principle of tadakhul
When multiple omissions of the same type occur (here, all three days of ramy), they overlap, and only one dam is needed. For omission of a single day, one dam is also required.
Tadakhul applies because the violations are homogeneous and continuous.
4
Examine the dalil (evidence)
The author cites two athar: (1) Aban ibn Uthman emphasizing the importance of ramy, and (2) Ata ibn Abi Rabah explicitly stating that forgetting one or all jamarat until after Tashriq requires one dam.
These are zanni al-thubut but still authoritative for wajib rulings.
5
Consider the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school considers ramy sunnah, so no dam is required for omission. The Hanbali school aligns with the Hanafi view. This ikhtilaf affects practical fatwa.
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the position stated here.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether delaying Tawaf al-Ziyarah obligates a dam, and explain the evidence each side uses.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: dam wajib. Sahibayn: no dam. The hadith 'la haraj' vs. Madinan practice.

Tawaf al-Ziyarah is the essential pillar of Hajj performed after Mina. A question arises: if a pilgrim delays it beyond the days of Nahr, must they offer a blood sacrifice (dam)? This lesson examines an internal Hanafi disagreement: Imam Abu Hanifa says yes; his two foremost students, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, say no. Understanding their proofs sharpens your ability to weigh evidence in fiqh disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The issue is whether delaying Tawaf al-Ziyarah from its normal time (days of Nahr) obligates a dam. Note that the act itself (tawaf) is still required, but the penalty for delay is contested.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf, not between schools.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and evidence
Abu Hanifa holds that a dam is wajib. His proof is the practice of the Madinan jurists (athar of Abu al-Zinad) that if one forgets tawaf and has marital relations, a camel must be sacrificed. This implies that delaying tawaf without excuse incurs a penalty.
The athar is from Sunan al-Bayhaqi.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and evidence
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that no dam is required. Their proof is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: the Prophet (peace be upon him) repeatedly said 'la haraj' (no harm) when asked about advancing or delaying Hajj rituals, including tawaf.
The hadith is in Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to the evidence
The response (implicit here) is that the hadith of 'la haraj' applies to mere sequence changes, not to fundamental pillars like tawaf al-ziyarah. Moreover, the athar from Madinah shows that omitting tawaf until intercourse occurs necessitates expiation.
Abu Hanifa distinguishes between ordinary order changes and missing a rukn.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, a pilgrim who delays tawaf must offer a dam (sheep); according to the Sahibayn, no extra sacrifice is needed. Later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn's view for ease.
The mufta bihi in many later texts is the Sahibayn's view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the ruling on delaying Tawaf al-Ziyarah (the essential tawaf of Hajj) from the days of Nahr (10th-13th Dhul Hijjah). The matn states the position of Imam Abu Hanifa: delaying this tawaf obligates a dam (blood sacrifice). The commentator then presents the view of the two Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani): no dam is required. The Sahibayn base their view on the famous hadith of Ibn Abbas, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) repeatedly said 'la haraj' (no harm) when asked about performing Hajj rituals out of order. This hadith indicates that any sequence of actions is permissible without penalty. The commentator also adds the version in Muslim: every order of advance or delay brings the response 'af'ala wa la haraj' (do it and there is no harm). The Sahibayn thus extend this principle to all actions of Hajj, including shaving (halq) and tawaf. Abu Hanifa, on the other hand, relies on the practice of the people of Madinah narrated by Abu al-Zinad: if someone forgets tawaf al-ziyarah and then engages in marital relations, they must sacrifice a full camel (badanah). This implies that delaying tawaf until after leaving Mina and having intercourse with one's spouse (which would be forbidden if still in Ihram) necessitates a heavy penalty. The commentator notes that this is only for forgetfulness; the case of intentional delay is analogous. Thus, Abu Hanifa distinguishes between actions that are merely out of order (as in the hadith) and the critical tawaf al-ziyarah, whose omission or delay without valid excuse carries a penalty. The practical difference: according to Abu Hanifa, a pilgrim who delays tawaf al-ziyarah beyond the days of tashriq must offer a dam; according to the Sahibayn, no additional sacrifice is required. The latter view is considered more lenient and likely preferred in later fatwa practice (mufta bihi), but the text does not explicitly state a tarjih.

دم (Dam): A blood sacrifice, usually a sheep or goat, offered as a penalty (jaza') for violating an obligation or prohibition during Hajj or Umrah.

Prohibition of Hunting for a Muhrim and the Penalty
Ruling on hunting prohibition and penalty with expansion proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on hunting for a Muhrim, identify the evidence, and apply the penalty rules to cases of direct killing, indicating, and accidental killing.

Core Idea
Hunting land game is haram in Ihram; the penalty applies even for accidental killing or merely directing another hunter.

This lesson examines the prohibition of hunting (sayd) for a person in the state of Ihram. The Hanafi school holds that any land animal that is permissible to eat outside Ihram is forbidden to hunt during Ihram, and if killed, a penalty (jaza') is wajib. The ruling is supported by clear Quranic verses and hadith. Additionally, the school expands liability to include those who indicate the game to another, and makes no distinction between intentional and forgetful acts.

What exactly is prohibited? Hunting land game. What is the ruling? Haram. What is the penalty? Wajib.
2

The prohibition is derived from Surah al-Ma'idah 5:94 and 5:96. 5:94 also mentions the penalty for intentional killing.
3

The explicit command 'la taqtulu al-sayd' indicates haram. The phrase 'fajaza'un mithlu ma qatala min al-na'am' shows that a penalty is due.
4
Identify the expansion: forgetfulness and indication
The matn states that intentional and forgetful, and the one who indicates the game, are all equal in liability. This is based on hadith and athar.
5

The hadith of Abu Qatada shows that pointing to game is treated as killing. The athar of Ibn Mas'ud on accidental killing of a jerboa demonstrates that even forgetful killing requires penalty.

Commentary
This passage presents the legal rules regarding hunting (sayd) during the state of Ihram for Hajj or Umrah. It is a ruling_with_proof lesson. The commentator first gives an 'essential note' summarizing the basic prohibition: land game is haram, sea game is halal. This is based on two clear Quranic verses (5:94 and 5:96). Then the discussion moves to the penalty (jaza') for violating the prohibition. The matn (Arabic phrase) in [750] (37) states: 'If a Muhrim kills game or indicates it to someone who kills it, then the penalty is upon him, and in this regard the intentional and forgetful, the beginner and the one who repeats, are all equal.' The commentator expands this with various proofs. The key points: 1) The penalty applies whether one directly kills or merely indicates the game to another (even a non-Muhrim). 2) Intention does not affect liability; even forgetful killing requires penalty. 3) The penalty is the equivalent of the animal (mithl) from livestock, as judged by two just men, or alternatively feeding the poor, or fasting according to the value. 4) Five specific animals (crow, kite, scorpion, mouse, vicious dog) are excluded and may be killed even in Ihram, based on the hadith of Aisha. The commentator also brings the hadith of Abu Qatada: when the Companions asked about eating game hunted by a non-Muhrim, the Prophet inquired whether any of them had indicated or directed the hunting, implying that if they had, they would be liable. This establishes that directing the hunter (isharah) is equivalent to killing oneself. The athar from al-Hasan and 'Ata' confirms this. The athar of Ibn Mas'ud on the accidental killing of a jerboa shows that even accidental killing (without intent) incurs the penalty. Thus, the rule is comprehensive: no exception for forgetfulness or accident, and no exception for one who is a first-time Muhrim or a repeat offender. The mufta bihi (relied-upon ruling) in the Hanafi school is as stated: all are equal in liability.

Key Terms
صيد (Sayd): Game animals, specifically land animals that are hunted. In this context, it refers to animals that are halal to eat outside Ihram, but prohibited to hunt during Ihram.
محرم (Muhrim): A person who has entered the state of Ihram for Hajj or Umrah, thus bound by its prohibitions.
جزاء (Jaza'): The penalty or compensation for killing game while in Ihram. It can be the equivalent animal (mithl), feeding the poor, or fasting.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on valuing game killed in ihram, explain the derivation from Quran 5:95 and the hadith of Ka‘b ibn ‘Ujrah, and distinguish it from the physical similarity method.

Core Idea
The penalty for game in ihram is based on market value at the place of killing, not physical similarity.

When a pilgrim kills game while in ihram, a penalty must be paid. The Hanafi school, following Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, determines this penalty by assessing the market value of the game at the location of the killing. This lesson walks through that process, the Quranic proof, the role of two just appraisers, and how this view differs from the physical similarity method held by Imam Muhammad and Imam Shafi‘i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi penalty method
State that according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the penalty is the market value of the game at the place where it was killed (or nearest settlement if in the desert).
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Understand the role of two just men
Two just, expert appraisers (dhawi ‘adl) must determine the value. Their judgment is required by the Quran.
They are appraisers, not deciders of physical similarity.
3
Analyze the Quranic proof
Read verse 5:95 and identify the phrase 'fajaza'un mithlu ma qatala min an-na‘mi'. Explain how Hanafis interpret 'mithl' as equivalence in value (mithl ma‘nawi) rather than physical form.
The subsequent options (hadi, food, fasting) support value-based penalty.
4
Compare with the opposing view
Imam Muhammad and Imam Shafi‘i hold that the penalty must be an animal physically similar to the game (e.g., deer → goat). Note the difference.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved in favor of Shaikhain.
5
List the three options after valuation
Once value is assessed, the offender may: (1) buy a hadi and slaughter in the Haram, (2) buy food (wheat) and give half a sa‘ per poor person, or (3) fast one day per half sa‘ (or one sa‘ of barley).
These options are from the verse's implication.

The need for two just men is greater when value assessment is required, and the Quran indicated this by the condition of dhawi 'adl. The offender has three choices after valuation: sacrifice, charity, or fasting — each linked to the value assessed.

When a person in ihram kills or damages game, a penalty is assessed by two just appraisers. The offender is not locked into a single method; rather, Islamic law provides flexibility. This passage from the Hanafi fiqh text details the three options — hadi (sacrifice), food charity, or fasting — and ties them directly to the valuation process. Leftover Food Less Than Half Sa': Charity or Fast
Ruling when after valuation the food amount is less than half a sa'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for leftover food less than half a sa' in the Hajj penalty and distinguish between the two valid options.

Core Idea
Less than half a sa' of leftover food gives the offender a choice: charity or a day of fasting.

In the detailed Hanafi system of penalties for hunting during Hajj (jinaayat al-hajj), the valuation of the hunted game is converted into food. After distributing the required half-sa' portions to the poor, a remainder of less than half a sa' may be left. This lesson presents the precise ruling for such fractional leftovers, ensuring no penalty is wasted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The offender has committed a hunting violation, the value of the game has been assessed, and the required amount of food (wheat, etc.) has been distributed in multiples of half a sa' to the poor. Now a remainder of less than half a sa' remains.
If the remainder were exactly half a sa', it would go to one poor person; if more, additional recipients would be included.
2
State the ruling
According to the text, the offender has a choice (mukhayyar): either give that leftover as charity to one poor person, or fast one complete day as compensation.
This option is specific to the Hanafi school; other schools may have different rules.
3
Understand the rationale
The ruling prevents the leftover small amount from being negligible or wasted. By allowing charity or fasting, the penalty is fulfilled practically. The choice accommodates the offender's convenience.
Fasting is measured as one full day, not partial, because fasting cannot be fractional.
4
Apply the ruling
In practice, if an offender ends up with, e.g., 0.3 sa' leftover, he may give that amount as charity or observe one fast. He cannot ignore it.
This is a sub-rule of the broader penalty system for hunting violations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three expiation options for a hunter in ihram after valuation and apply the rule that the hunter may choose any option regardless of the value amount.

Core Idea
After valuation, the hunter may choose: sacrifice, feed the poor, or fast — each option is equally available.

When a hunter in ihram kills a permissible animal, expiation is required. The Quran commands a 'recompense equivalent' (jaza'un mithl). The Hanafi school, following the practice of Ibn Abbas, interprets this as a monetary valuation, after which the hunter has three distinct choices. In this lesson, we will examine those options and the evidence supporting them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the expiation after killing a hunted animal while in ihram. The ruling is that expiation is wajib (obligatory).
Wajib here means obligatory; the hunter must choose one of the three options.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary dalil is Quran 5:95: 'a recompense equivalent to what he killed of grazing livestock'. The Hanafis rely on the interpretation of Ibn Abbas, who explains that the 'equivalent' is determined by valuation (taqwim).
The hadith from Bayhaqi is cited to show the sequential understanding: if one can afford the animal, that is preferred, but the valuation opens options.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The key phrase is 'jaza'un mithl' (recompense equivalent). Hanafis understand 'mithl' as value rather than physical similarity, based on the Companions' practice. This leads to valuation by two just experts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing harmful animals while in ihram and identify the hadith proof and its wajh al-dalalah.

Core Idea
A Muhrim may kill harmful animals without penalty because the Prophet ﷺ exempted them in clear hadith.

In ihram, hunting and killing game (sayd) is prohibited and incurs a penalty. However, certain animals are inherently harmful and their killing is permitted even in ihram. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling based on the hadith of Aisha (ra) and the extension to all harmful animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is killing harmful animals while in ihram. The ruling is that it is permissible (mubah) and incurs no penalty (jaza').
The ruling is an exception to the general prohibition of killing game in ihram.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The evidence is the hadith from Aisha (ra): the Prophet ﷺ listed five 'fasiq' animals that are killed even in the sanctuary. This hadith is found in Bukhari and Muslim.
The list includes crow, kite, scorpion, rat, and predatory dog.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning from the text)
The command to kill indicates permissibility (al-amr yufidu al-ibahah in this context). The word 'fasiq' indicates these animals are harmful, justifying the exception to the general prohibition.
Hanafi usul: The imperative form here is interpreted as ibahah (permission) not wujub (obligation) because the context shows it is a concession.
4
Apply the illa (effective cause) to other animals
The illa is harmfulness (dharar or idha). Therefore, any animal that is naturally harmful (mu'dhi) is included. 'Predatory dog' (kalb 'aqur) covers all beasts of prey like lions, wolves, leopards.
Imam Malik's statement supports this extension: 'every animal that attacks people is kalb 'aqur'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical outcome)
The practical consequence: A Muhrim can kill any harmful animal without worry of penalty. This includes scorpions, snakes, rats, crows, etc. Domesticated dogs are not included because they are not naturally harmful.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling from the matn: a person in ihram may kill certain animals without incurring any penalty (jaza'). The list includes: crow, kite, wolf, snake, scorpion, rat, dog, and any predatory animal ('aqur). The commentary explains the reasoning: these animals are inherently harmful (mu'dhi) by nature, so their killing is permitted even in ihram. The primary proof is the hadith of Aisha (ra) in Bukhari and Muslim listing five 'fasiq' animals that are killed in the sanctuary. The term 'kalb 'aqur' is interpreted by Imam Malik as including all predatory beasts because they attack and cause fear. Domesticated dogs are not included because they are not harmful in the same way. Therefore, killing any animal that is naturally harmful does not require fidyah.

محرم (muhrim): A person who has entered the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah.
الكلب العقور (al-kalb al-'aqur): A predatory dog that attacks and harms people; interpreted broadly to include all beasts of prey.

No Penalty for Killing Mosquitoes, Fleas, and Ticks in Ihram
Ruling with proof: no penalty for killing hawamm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing small harmful insects (hawamm) in ihram and identify the proof from the narration of Aisha.

Core Idea
Killing naturally harmful insects in ihram incurs no penalty — the exception is lice.

A muhrim (pilgrim in ihram) is forbidden from hunting but may kill certain harmful insects. This lesson clarifies which insects are exempt and the reasoning behind the Hanafi position, supported by a narration from Aisha (ra).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the matn: 'There is nothing in killing mosquitoes, fleas, and ticks.' The ruling is that no penalty (fidyah) is required.
The act is killing these specific insects while in ihram.
2
Understand the reasoning
The commentary gives two reasons: these are not considered game (sayd), and they are naturally harmful (mu'dhi). Hence, killing them does not violate the prohibition on hunting.
The principle: that which is not game and is harmful is permissible to kill.
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The passage cites an athar from Aisha: 'The Muhrim kills all insects (hawamm) except the louse, for it comes from him.' This narration explicitly allows killing all hawamm except lice.
The proof is a qawl sahabi, considered a valid source in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Distinguish lice from other insects
Lice are excluded because they originate from the human body, unlike mosquitoes, fleas, and ticks. Killing lice requires charity (fidyah).
This distinction is crucial for correct application.

Killing a Louse in Ihram: The Ruling of Sadaqah
A ruling with proof for the sadaqah when killing a louse in ihram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing a louse during ihram and identify the evidence from the narration of Abdullah ibn Umar.

Core Idea
Killing a louse in ihram requires sadaqah because it originates from prohibited filth removal.

In ihram, many normal actions become restricted. While killing harmful insects is generally allowed, lice are treated specially because they are generated from the pilgrim's own body. This lesson explains the ruling and the proof from the narration of Abdullah ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Killing a louse (qamlah) during ihram. What is the ruling? The matn states that one must give charity (sadaqah) as he wishes.
2
Locate the dalil
What evidence is cited? A narration from Malik via Abdullah ibn Umar: 'Do not kill a louse... if the Muhrim throws it from his skin or garment, let him feed a handful of food.' This is a qawl sahabi.
The dalil is a statement of a Companion, considered zanni al-thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The imperative 'let him feed' indicates a requirement for compensation. The amount specified (a handful) is interpreted as the minimum, but the matn says 'as he wishes', allowing flexibility.
Hanafis hold that the command indicates recommendation, not obligation, due to the zanni nature of the evidence.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there a difference of opinion? The Maliki school considers feeding a handful obligatory, while Hanafis see it as recommended (some say wajib).
The difference arises from the weight given to the narration.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon? The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is that sadaqah is recommended but not obligatory, though some earlier texts call it wajib.
The later fatwa favors recommendation due to the weakness of the evidence.

Thus, lice are treated differently from other insects because they are not considered general harmful insects (hawamm) but rather something that originates from the person's own body.

Killing a Locust in Ihram: Charity and Proof
Ruling with proof from a companion's verdict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing a locust in ihram and identify the proof from the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Core Idea
Killing a locust requires a voluntary charity; a date suffices.

A locust (jaradah) is a special case in ihram violations. Unlike larger game, its penalty is not a full fidyah but a simple charity. The matn and its commentary anchor this ruling in a precedent from Caliph Umar, showing how a qawl sahabi can serve as evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is killing a locust while in ihram. The ruling is that one gives charity (sadaqah) as he wishes, with a date being a sufficient minimum.
The Arabic uses an imperative ('تصدق') softened by 'بما شاء', indicating recommendation.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is a narration from Zaid ibn Aslam: a man came to Umar and confessed killing locusts with his whip. Umar instructed: 'Feed a handful of food.'
This is a qawl sahabi, considered a valid dalil in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the istidlal
Umar's command ('اطعم') shows a penalty of food. The amount is unspecified ('قبضة من طعام'), and the additional narration stating 'a date is better than a locust' clarifies that a date suffices.
The second narration indicates the value: a date is better than a locust, so giving a date is adequate.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The Hanafi school treats locusts as a minor violation. Other schools may require a full fidyah. The practical difference is the amount and type of penalty.

Penalty for Killing Inedible or Predatory Animals
Ruling with proof from Hadith on non-edible game compensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for killing non-edible game animals in ihram and apply the limit that its value does not exceed a goat.

Core Idea
Non-edible game animals require compensation, but value capped at a goat.

This lesson examines the ruling for a muhrim who kills a game animal whose meat is not eaten, such as predators or rodents. The Hanafi school requires compensation (jaza'), but the value is limited to that of a goat, as established by a hadith. Understanding this limit prevents overestimation of the penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is killing a non-edible game animal while in ihram. The ruling is that compensation (jaza') is wajib.
2
Note the value cap
The compensation value cannot exceed the price of a goat (shah).
This applies even if the animal is large like a hyena.
3
Examine the Hadith proof
The Prophet (s) assigned specific compensations: hyena: ram, gazelle: sheep, rabbit: young goat, jerboa: kid. This scale indicates a limit for non-edible animals.
The hadith is from Jabir in Darqutni.
4
Extract the principle
Non-edible game still requires jaza', but the value is lower and capped at a goat.
5
Apply to examples
For a jerboa, compensation is a kid (young goat); for a rabbit, a young goat (anaq). Both are non-edible.
These examples from the hadith confirm the principle.

Ruling on expiation for killing a locust in ihram (Hanafi)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the expiation required for killing a locust in ihram and identify the evidence from the Sahaba.

Core Idea
Killing a locust in ihram requires a handful of food as expiation.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling for a muhrim who kills a locust. While locusts are considered game, their small value results in a minimal expiation. The Hanafi school relies on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab to determine the amount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is killing a locust while in ihram. The ruling (hukm) is that expiation (fidyah) is wajib.
Even though locusts are small, they are considered game (sayd).
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the athar from Zayd ibn Aslam that Umar ibn al-Khattab commanded a muhrim who killed locusts: 'Feed a handful of food.'
This is a qawl sahabi, which is a valid source in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'at'im qabdatan min ta'am' (feed a handful of food) is explicit and indicates the expiation is a small amount of food.
The second athar that 'a date is better than a locust' shows that a date is sufficient.
4
Apply the ruling
The mufta bihi position is to give a handful of food (e.g., dates, wheat, etc.) or one date as expiation for one locust.
If multiple locusts are killed, a similar calculation applies.

Killing Non-Edible Predators in Ihram
Ruling on expiation for killing non-edible predators in ihram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on expiation for killing non-edible predators in ihram and identify the dalil that limits the value to a sheep.

Core Idea
Non-edible game in ihram requires expiation capped at a sheep.

The ruling on hunting during ihram distinguishes between edible and non-edible game. For predators and other animals whose meat is not eaten, the expiation (jaza') is still required but its value is limited. This lesson explores the textual basis for this cap and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is killing a non-edible predator (or similar animal) while in ihram. The ruling is that expiation is wajib.
Even if the animal is not a predator but its meat is not halal, it is still game.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith from Jabir (Darqutni) that specifies expiation amounts for various non-edible animals.
The hadith lists: hyena (ram), deer (sheep), rabbit (young goat), jerboa (young sheep).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith establishes that non-edible game has expiation values at or below a sheep. The maximum mentioned is a ram (for hyena) which is equivalent to a sheep. Hence the value does not exceed a sheep.
The principle: since the meat is not eaten, the value is less than that of edible game.
4
Confirm the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that expiation is wajib and the value is capped at a sheep. This is from the zahir al-riwayah.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: none; all three imams agree on the cap.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may not cap the value, but the Hanafis follow the explicit hadith.

Muhrim Killing a Predatory Animal in Self-Defense
Ruling on muhrim killing an attacking predator with no penalty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a muhrim may kill an attacking predatory animal without any penalty and explain the proof from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
No penalty for a muhrim who kills an attacking predator.

What happens if a person in ihram is attacked by a tiger or wolf? This lesson clarifies that self-defense overrides the normal prohibitions on hunting during ihram. The ruling is based on a well-known hadith about five inherently harmful animals, and extended to any attacking predator.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a muhrim killing a predatory animal that is attacking him. The ruling is that nothing is required of him—no penalty, no compensation.
The term 'nothing' means neither fidyah nor dam nor sadaqah.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith narrated by Aisha: 'Five animals, all of them are fasid, they are killed in the Haram.' This establishes that even in the sacred territory, these animals may be killed. If they can be killed in the Haram, then outside the Haram, and especially when attacking, it is even more permissible.
The five animals are: crow, kite, scorpion, mouse, and rabid dog.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative form 'yughtalu' (are killed) indicates permissibility. The hadith explicitly exempts these animals from the prohibition of hunting in ihram. By analogy (qiyas auwla), any predatory animal that attacks a muhrim is more deserving of being killed than merely suspected of harm.
The principle of 'first al-khitab' and qiyas auwla makes the ruling qat'i.
4
Confirm with the athar of Ata
The statement of the tabi'i Ata: 'Every enemy that attacks you, kill it even if you are in ihram.' This serves as additional support (shahid) from the practice of the early generations.
Although a sahabi qawl would be stronger, the athar is cited as corroboration.

Compulsion does not remove the obligation of compensation for killing game in ihram.

In this passage, we encounter a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: necessity does not eliminate penalty for violating ihram prohibitions. The author addresses the case of a muhrim forced to eat game meat who kills game. The ruling is clear: compensation (jaza') is due. The proof is drawn by analogy from the Quranic verse on fidyah for shaving the head due to illness.

The verse shows that even a permissible act (shaving) done under compulsion requires compensation. By qiyas, a prohibited act (killing game) done under compulsion also requires compensation. The common element is the existence of a prohibition that is violated under necessity.
The analogy strengthens the ruling: if compulsion does not exempt in a lighter case, it certainly does not in a graver one.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A qarin violates two ihrams with one act, so must pay two dam.

This lesson clarifies a distinct Hanafi fiqh ruling regarding hajj. When a person combines hajj and umrah in a single ihram (qarin), any violation that would cost a mufrid one dam costs the qarin two. The text explains that this doubled penalty arises because the qarin simultaneously holds two ihrams – one for hajj and one for umrah – and a single violation disrespects both.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
First, recognize the act: a qarin commits an ihram violation (e.g., hunting, cutting hair, removing nails). The Hanafi ruling is that he must offer two dam – one for his hajj ihram and one for his umrah ihram.
The ruling applies only to violations that would require a single dam from a mufrid.
2
Understand the key distinction
Compare with a mufrid (hajj only). For the identical violation, a mufrid owes one dam. The qarin owes two. This doubling is the distinctive legal consequence of combining the two pilgrimages.
The qarin is considered to have two independent ihrams from the moment he enters the state.
3
Grasp the reasoning
The text states that the qarin has tied two ihrams simultaneously. Therefore, a single forbidden act violates both ihrams. The penalty must compensate for each desecration separately.
This is a qiyas principle: each ihram gives rise to its own obligation.

The passage cites an athar from Imam Malik in the Muwatta: if a qarin misses hajj, he must sacrifice two animals – one for the combination and one for the missed hajj. This supports the broader Hanafi rule that a qarin incurs double penalties.
The evidence is a precedent (athar) demonstrating the double sacrifice, even though the specific context is missing hajj.

Practice: Suppose a qarin cuts his hair before the conclusion of his rituals. The mufrid penalty is a dam. Hence the qarin owes two dam. If the same qarin kills a locust, again two dam.
The number of violations does not matter; each violation triggers the double penalty.

Core Idea
A qarin who passes the miqat without ihram owes one dam, because the violation preceded qiran status.

Previously, you learned that passing the miqat without ihram incurs a penalty of one dam. But what if a person later becomes a qarin (combines hajj and umrah)? Does he owe two dam? This lesson clarifies an exception: only one dam is due if he was not in ihram at the time of crossing the miqat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is passing the miqat without any ihram. The ruling: one dam is wajib (obligatory) as a penalty.
2
Recognize the Exception's Condition
The exception applies when the person later enters ihram for both hajj and umrah, becoming a qarin after passing the miqat.
The key is that at the moment of passing the miqat, he was not a qarin.
3
Trace the Rationale (Illah)
The reason for one dam: the penalty is for passing the miqat without ihram. Since the qiran status occurred later, it does not double the penalty.
The illah is the timing of the violation relative to assuming ihram.
4
Contrast with the General Rule
The general rule: a qarin who passes the miqat without ihram would owe two dam (one for each pilgrimage) but this exception shows that if he was not in ihram at the miqat, only one is due.
This prevents double counting for a single violation.

الا ان يتجاوز الميقات من غیر احرام ثم يحرم بالعمرة والحج فيلزمه دم واحد [۷۷۳] (۲۰) مگر یہ کہ میقات سے بغیر احرام کے گزر جائے پھر عمرہ اور حج کا احرام باندھے تو اس کو ایک ہی دم لازم ہوگا۔ اس صورت میں ایک ہی دم اس لئے لازم ہے کہ میقات سے گزرتے وقت وہ قارن نہیں تھا۔ قارن تو بعد میں بنا ہے اور بعد میں حج اور عمرہ کا احرام باندھا ہے اس لئے میقات سے بغیر احرام کے گزرنے کا ایک دم لازم ہوگا۔
Except if he passes the miqat without ihram, then he enters ihram for umrah and hajj, then one dam is incumbent upon him. (60) Except that he passes the miqat without ihram, then ties ihram for umrah and hajj, then one dam will be necessary. In this case, only one dam is necessary because when he passed the miqat he was not a qarin. He became a qarin later and tied ihram for hajj and umrah later. Therefore, the one dam is for passing the miqat without ihram.

Commentary
This is an exception to the previous ruling. If a person passes the miqat without being in ihram, and later enters ihram for both hajj and umrah (thus becoming a qarin after the miqat), he only owes one dam. The reason is that the penalty for passing the miqat without ihram is a single dam, and the later ihram for qiran does not double it because the violation occurred before the qiran status. The ruling distinguishes between the time of the violation and the time of assuming the ihram.

Key Terms
ميقات (miqat): The designated boundary for entering ihram; passing it without ihram incurs a penalty.
دم (dam): A penalty requiring slaughter of a sheep or goat; wajib for certain violations in hajj.
قارن (qarin): One who performs hajj and umrah together with a single ihram.
