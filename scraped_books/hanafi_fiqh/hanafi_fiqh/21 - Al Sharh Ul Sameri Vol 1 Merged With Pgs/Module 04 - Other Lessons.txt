The Opening Praise and Its Proof
Explaining the basmalah and hamd formula with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on beginning important matters with al-hamd and explain why it is sunnah based on the hadith.

Core Idea
Beginning important matters with hamd is sunnah, not wajib, because the hadith says 'aqta' (defective), not batil.

In this opening passage of Al-Sharh al-Sameri, the author explains the basmalah and hamd formula. The key legal point is the ruling on starting any significant task with praise of Allah. We will examine the hadith evidence and understand why the Hanafi school considers it a confirmed sunnah rather than an obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is beginning an important matter with al-hamd. The ruling is sunnah muakkadah (confirmed sunnah).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Ibn Majah: 'Every important matter that does not begin with praise is defective (aqta').'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'aqta' indicates deficiency, not invalidity. In Hanafi usul, the absence of a requirement leads to deficiency (sunnah) unless the text explicitly states invalidity.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no dispute among the four schools that it is recommended; all consider it mustahabb (sunnah).
5
Evaluate the ruling
Because the hadith is ahad and the word 'aqta' implies deficiency, the Hanafis classify it as sunnah muakkadah, not wajib.

The book's introduction: (1) Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds, (2) and the good end is for the righteous, (3) and blessings and peace be upon His Messenger Muhammad and his family and all his companions. Perhaps the wisdom of mentioning ar-Rahman first is that his mercy pertains to the people of the world, and the world is foremost. Therefore in Bismillahir Rahmanir Raheem, ar-Rahman is placed first. (1) All praises are for Allah, the Cherisher of all the worlds. The reason for placing al-Hamdulillah first: In the Qur'an, al-Hamdulillah is at the very beginning. There is a hadith from Abu Hurairah that the Prophet said: 'Every important matter that does not begin with praise is defective.' (Ibn Majah, chapter on the marriage sermon, p.272, no.1894) Translation: Any important task that is not started with praise is incomplete. This hadith indicates that one should begin the book with al-Hamdulillah. Al-Hamd means verbal expression of the praised one's voluntary virtues, whether in return for a blessing or not. Since blessings from Allah are constantly descending, any praise we utter will be in return for a blessing. The 'al' in 'al-Hamdu' is for istighraq (comprehensiveness), meaning that all praises that can exist in the world are for Allah Almighty; this is the view of the majority. Sometimes the 'al' is for genus, meaning the essence and reality of praise is specific to Allah. Sometimes it is for covenant and reference to a specific thing, i.e. the specific praises mentioned in the Qur'an and Hadith are for Allah. Rabbul 'Alamin: Rabb means to nourish something gradually. Allah provides sustenance to all worlds gradually and nourishes them, therefore He is called Rabb. When the word Rabb is used alone, it is only for Allah; when used in idafah (construct), it can be used for others, as in the Quranic verse 'Remember me in the presence of your lord, but Satan caused him to forget to mention it to his lord' (Yusuf 12:42) – here Rabb is used in idafah and refers to the king of the prisoners. Al-'Alamin: 'Alamin is the plural, singular is 'alam. It is derived from 'alamah (sign). Since all things in the world are signs of the Creator, this world is called 'alam. It is on the pattern of an active participle but is a noun of instrument, like khatam and tali' (a tool for stamping).

Commentary
This passage forms the author's introduction (khutbah al-kitab). The commentary begins by explaining why 'Rahman' precedes 'Raheem' in the basmalah: mercy to the world's creation takes precedence. The first phrase 'Al-Hamdu lillahi Rabbil 'Alamin' is proven by two evidences: the Qur'an itself begins with it, and a hadith from Ibn Majah (no. 1894) states that any important matter not begun with praise is 'aqta' (defective). The author clarifies that 'hamd' is verbal praise for voluntary virtuous qualities, whether in response to a blessing or not. The definite article 'al' is analyzed as istighraq (comprehensiveness), meaning all possible praises belong exclusively to Allah. Alternative interpretations (genus, covenant) are noted. The word 'Rabb' is defined as the gradual nurturer, and its usage with or without idafah is explained with a Quranic example. 'Alamin' is derived from 'alamah (sign), highlighting that the universe is a sign of the Creator. This establishes the linguistic and theological foundation for the book's opening.

Key Terms
الحمد (al-hamd): Verbal praise for the voluntary virtues of the praised one, whether in return for a blessing or not.
رب العالمين (rabb al-ʿālamīn): The One who gradually nourishes and sustains all the worlds.
العالمين (al-ʿālamīn): Plural of ʿalam; everything that is a sign of the Creator; the universe.

Defining Taqwa: The Good End for the Righteous
Defining taqwa and its active participle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define taqwa and identify its two essential components: avoiding persistence in sins and avoiding conceit in worship.

Core Idea
Taqwa is guarding oneself: not persisting in sins and not being proud of worship.

This passage explains the second phrase of the khutbah, focusing on the meaning of taqwa. The author provides both a linguistic and legal definition, emphasizing that taqwa is not simply fear but an active state of God-consciousness that manifests in action. Mastering this definition is foundational for understanding many fiqh rulings related to intention and sincerity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term
The passage defines the term 'taqwa'. Locate the Arabic word and its linguistic root (wiqaayah).
Linguistic derivation helps understand the core meaning.
2
Extract the first component
Find the first element: not persisting in sins. This means avoiding sin and repenting if one slips.
Persistence (israr) is key; occasional sin with repentance does not negate taqwa.
3
Extract the second component
The second element: not being conceited about worship. This guards against pride (ujb) in one's acts of worship.
Pride can nullify the reward; humility is essential.
4
Note the alternative definition
Some scholars define taqwa as complete following of the Prophet and Companions. This encompasses both components.
This definition links taqwa to prophetic example.
5
Understand the outcome
The phrase promises success in this world and the Hereafter for the muttaqin (those with taqwa).
This motivates the believer to cultivate taqwa.

Commentary
This passage explains the second phrase of the khutbah: 'Wa al-'āqibatu lil-muttaqīn' (And the good end is for the righteous). The author defines taqwa linguistically as derived from wiqaayah (protection), meaning to guard oneself. In a legal sense, taqwa consists of two elements: not persisting in sins, and not becoming proud of one's acts of worship. An alternative definition is given: complete adherence to the Prophet and his Companions. The active participle 'muttaqī' and its plural 'muttaqūn' are derived from this root. The overall meaning is that those who possess taqwa will ultimately be successful in both this life and the Hereafter. This phrase serves as a reminder of the ultimate reward for righteousness.

The Obligation of Sending Blessings and Peace upon the Prophet
Explaining the salawat and salam on the Prophet

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sending salat and salam upon the Prophet, explain the Quranic and hadith proofs, and define the key terms salat, salam, rasul, nabi, al, and ashab.

Core Idea
Sending blessings on the Prophet is wajib; the Quran commands it, and Allah Himself does it.

When we mention the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in speech or writing, the Hanafi school teaches that it is obligatory (wajib) to send blessings and peace upon him. This lesson examines the Quranic verse and prophetic hadith that establish this obligation, and clarifies the meanings of the key terms used in the traditional formula.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is sending salat (blessings) and salam (peace) upon the Prophet, his family, and his companions. According to the Hanafi school, this is wajib (obligatory) when the Prophet is mentioned.
Some scholars consider it sunnah, but the Hanafi position based on the imperative is wujub.
2
Locate the primary proof from the Quran
The author cites Quran 33:56: 'Indeed, Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who believe, send blessings upon him and greet him with a greeting of peace.' This verse contains two imperatives: 'sallū' (send blessings) and 'sallimū' (send peace).
The imperative form (amr) in Hanafi usul indicates obligation (wujub) unless there is a contrary indicator.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof yields the ruling)
In Hanafi usul, al-amr yufidu al-wujub (the imperative indicates obligation). The command 'sallū' therefore makes sending blessings wajib. Additionally, the fact that Allah and the angels do this act shows its great merit.
The dalala (meaning indication) is zanni (speculative) because the imperative could be interpreted for recommendation if there is a qarina, but here no contrary indicator is present.
4
Examine the secondary proof from hadith
The hadith of Abu Humaid al-Sa'idi provides the specific formula taught by the Prophet. When asked how to send blessings, he instructed: 'Say: O Allah, send blessings upon Muhammad and his wives and his descendants…' This confirms the practice and reinforces the obligation.
The hadith is from Sahih Muslim and Sunan Abi Dawud, making it authentic.
5
Note the lexical expansions
The author defines salat differently depending on the subject: from Allah it means mercy, from angels it means seeking forgiveness, from humans it means supplication. Salam means safety. The terms Rasul and Nabi are distinguished, and Muhammad is derived from hamd (praise). The family (Al) and companions (Ashab) are also defined.
These definitions deepen understanding of the formula's meaning and the status of the Prophet.

(3) And blessings and peace upon His Messenger Muhammad and his family and all his companions. Al-salat: its meaning is supplication, but when attributed to Allah, it means mercy; to the angels, it means seeking forgiveness; to humans, it means supplication. Salam: all types of safety. The proof for sending blessings and peace is in this verse: 'Indeed, Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who believe, send blessings upon him and greet him with a greeting of peace' (Surah al-Ahzab 33:56). Translation: Allah and the angels send blessings upon the Prophet, so O believers, send blessings and peace upon him. This verse commands both salat and salam. Moreover, the excellence is that Allah Himself does this deed; so what more can be said! Scholars say that the honor of sending blessings upon the Prophet is greater than when the angels were commanded to prostrate to Adam, because blessings and peace are sent by Allah Himself. A hadith: Abu Humaid al-Sa'idi reported that they asked: 'O Messenger of Allah, how should we send blessings upon you?' He said: 'Say: O Allah, send blessings upon Muhammad and his wives and his descendants...' (Muslim, chapter on sending blessings upon the Prophet, p.175, no.407; Abu Dawud, chapter on sending blessings upon the Prophet, p.147, no.979). 'Rasul' refers to a prophet who was given a new Shariah and a book; 'nabi' is one who was not given a new Shariah. Therefore a Rasul is more excellent than a nabi. Hence the author used 'Rasul' to show greater respect. 'Muhammad' is derived from hamd (praise), meaning the praised one, in whom all praiseworthy virtues are collected. He has many descriptive names, but Muhammad and Ahmad are the most famous; his grandfather gave him this name. He became ism bi ism (a name matching his essence) because all good qualities are gathered in him. 'Al' (family) is derived from 'ahl' (family). There is disagreement on who they are: one meaning is his household and children; another meaning is those of his family for whom accepting charity was forbidden, such as the family of Ali and the family of Ja'far. 'Ashab' is the plural of sahib (companion). Those who saw the Prophet with faith and died in faith are all his companions. Upon all of them be blessings and peace.

Ruling with proofs and response to opposing schools

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school prohibits the follower from reciting behind the imam in audible prayers and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
The imam's recitation is the follower's recitation; silence is obligatory.

The question of whether a follower recites behind the imam is one of the most debated issues between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. This passage presents the Hanafi position with seven proofs, including Quran 7:204 and multiple hadiths, and responds to the Shafi'i proof from the hadith 'No prayer without Fatihah.' Understanding this dispute requires careful analysis of textual evidence and the principle of daman (imam's sufficiency).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: the follower does not recite any Quran (including Fatihah) when the imam recites aloud in prayer. The imam's recitation suffices for the follower.
2
Identify the opponent's view
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik require the follower to recite Fatihah in all prayers, whether the imam recites aloud or silently.
3

Work through the seven proofs cited: (1) Quran 7:204, (2) hadith commanding silence, (3) hadith of the Prophet's displeasure, (4) 'imam's recitation is the follower's recitation', (5) hadith that imam suffices, (6) practice of Ibn 'Umar, (7) logical argument that even Shafi'is accept sufficiency in a scenario.

4

Undoing the Knot of the Shroud in the Grave
Ruling with proof from Ibn Abi Shaybah's narration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on undoing the shroud knot after burial and explain the evidence from the practice of the Salaf.

Core Idea
After burial, all knots of the shroud should be untied as a recommended practice.

During shrouding, a knot may be tied to prevent the shroud from opening. However, after the deceased is placed in the grave, the Hanafi school recommends untying all knots. This practice, rooted in the action of the early Muslims, symbolises the deceased's release from worldly bonds. In this lesson, we examine the ruling and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is undoing the knot(s) of the shroud after the deceased is placed in the grave. The ruling in the Hanafi school is that it is recommended (mustahabb/sunnah), not obligatory.
The matn states 'ويحل العقدة' (and he should untie the knot), indicating a desirable action.
2
Understand the context
The knot was tied during shrouding for practical reasons (fear of opening). The ruling addresses what to do after burial.
3
Examine the dalil
The proof is an athar (traceable report) from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: 'When the deceased is entered into the grave, all knots are untied from him.' This is recorded in Ibn Abi Shaybah's Musannaf.
This is a qawl sahabi (saying of a Companion-level figure), which has zanni thubut and dalala.
4
Evaluate the authority
The athar is from the nawadir (rare transmissions) but is accepted as a basis for a recommended practice. The Hanafi school follows it as mustahabb.
Cross-madhhab: some scholars permit leaving the knots tied, but the preferred Hanafi view is to untie.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, after the deceased is laid in the grave, the person responsible should untie all knots of the shroud before the burial is complete.
This is a sunnah act; omitting it does not invalidate the burial.

Prohibited Acts for the Mu'takif
States three prohibited acts for the person in i'tikaf with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three prohibited acts for the mu'takif and identify the Quranic and hadith evidence for each prohibition.

Core Idea
The mu'takif must avoid all sexual contact—intercourse, touching, or kissing with desire—based on explicit Quran and hadith.

The act of i'tikaf is a spiritual seclusion in the mosque, requiring complete devotion to worship. This lesson clarifies three specific prohibitions that safeguard the sanctity of this practice. Understanding these rulings helps the mu'takif preserve the validity and reward of their i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text explicitly prohibits three acts: al-wat' (intercourse), al-lams (touching with desire), and al-qablah (kissing). Each is ruled haram for the mu'takif.
The source text uses 'yuhram' (it is forbidden).
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Two proofs are provided: a hadith from 'A'ishah (ra) and the Quranic verse 2:187. The hadith states 'لا يمس امرأة ولا يباشرها' (He should not touch a woman nor have relations with her). The verse says 'ولا تباشروهن وأنتم عاكفون في المساجد' (And do not have relations with them as long as you are staying for worship in the mosques).
The hadith is from Abu Dawood and Daraqutni.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The explicit prohibition in both the hadith and the verse directly indicates that these acts are forbidden. The hadith's wording includes general touching and relations, while the verse specifically prohibits during i'tikaf.
The prohibition applies day and night.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
There is no significant difference among the Hanafi school or between schools on this ruling; it is mutually agreed upon (muttafaq alayh).
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preponderant view)
The mufta bihi (operative opinion) is that all three acts are strictly prohibited. If any of these acts are committed with desire and result in inzal (emission), the i'tikaf is invalidated.
The authority weight is zahir riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It declares three acts haram (prohibited) for the mu'takif: intercourse (wat'), touching with desire (lams), and kissing (qablah). Invalidation of I'tikaf due to Inzal from Kissing or Touching
Ruling on i'tikaf invalidation due to inzal from kissing/touching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that i'tikaf becomes invalid (batil) if inzal occurs from kissing or touching with desire, identify the athar from Ibn 'Abbas as evidence, and explain that qada (makeup) is wajib.

Core Idea
Kissing or touching with desire during i'tikaf that leads to inzal invalidates the i'tikaf and requires qada.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a mu'takif who experiences inzal (ejaculation) as a result of kissing or touching his wife with desire. The text rules that this invalidates the i'tikaf and necessitates qada. We will examine the reasoning behind this ruling and the proof from the action of the Companion Ibn 'Abbas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a mu'takif kissing or touching his wife with desire that leads to inzal. The ruling is that the i'tikaf becomes invalid (fasid) and must be made up (qada).
Distinguish this from mere touching without desire or without inzal.
2
Understand the reasoning linking inzal to invalidation
Inzal breaks the fast, and according to the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), a valid i'tikaf requires fasting. Therefore, breaking the fast invalidates the i'tikaf.
Imam Muhammad holds that voluntary i'tikaf of less than a day does not require fasting, but for this scenario, the same consequence applies if fasting was a condition.
3
Locate the dalil
The evidence is an athar (narration) from Ibn 'Abbas: 'When the mu'takif has intercourse with his wife, he should start his i'tikaf anew.' This is recorded in Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
The athar is zanni in transmission but qati in meaning (explicit command to restart).
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The athar commands starting i'tikaf anew (ista'naf i'tikafahu), which implies that the previous i'tikaf is no longer valid. By extension, any sexual act leading to inzal (such as kissing or touching with desire) similarly invalidates.
The ruling applies analogically (qiyas) from intercourse to other sexual acts that cause inzal.
5
Identify the consequence: qada
Since the i'tikaf became invalid, the mu'takif must perform qada (makeup) of the invalidated i'tikaf. If the i'tikaf was voluntary, it becomes akin to a vow, so at least one day and night of i'tikaf become obligatory to fulfill.
Qada is wajib, not just recommended.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It states that if a mu'takif experiences inzal (ejaculation) due to kissing or touching with desire, his i'tikaf becomes invalid and qada (making it up) is required. The commentary explains the reasoning: inzal breaks the fast, and i'tikaf is not valid without fasting (according to the majority view). Furthermore, if the i'tikaf was voluntarily begun, it becomes akin to a vowed i'tikaf, obligating at least one day and night of makeup. The athar from Ibn 'Abbas is cited: if the mu'takif has intercourse, he must start his i'tikaf over. This supports the ruling that invalidation requires a fresh start. The footnote clarifies that the hadith about mosque conditions is also relevant.

Key Terms
انزال (inzal): Emission of semen, whether by kissing, touching, or other means; causes invalidation of fast and i'tikaf.
قضاء (qada): Making up an act of worship after it has been invalidated or missed; here, the mu'takif must perform i'tikaf again.
