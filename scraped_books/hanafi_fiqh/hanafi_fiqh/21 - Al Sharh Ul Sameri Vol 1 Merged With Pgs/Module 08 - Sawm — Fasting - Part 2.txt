Dying with Unpaid Fasts: Feeding by Wali
Ruling on deceased with unpaid fasts and will: feeding obligation of the wali

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on feeding for a deceased's missed fasts when a will exists, and explain the evidence and the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam Ahmad.

Core Idea
For a deceased who made a will for missed fasts, the wali feeds a poor person per day—he does not fast.

When a person dies with missed Ramadan fasts and leaves a will to fulfill them, what must the guardian or executor (wali) do? The Hanafi school rules that the wali must feed one poor person for each missed fast. This is based on a hadith from Ibn Umar. However, Imam Ahmad (Hanbali) holds that the wali should fast on behalf of the deceased, citing a hadith from Aisha. In this lesson, we will examine the Hanafi proof, the opposing view, and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the death of a person who owed qada fasts from Ramadan and has made a wasiyyah (will). The ruling is that the wali must feed a poor person for each missed day.
Feeding is wajib only if a will exists; without a will, there is no obligation on the wali.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites a hadith from Ibn Umar: 'Whoever dies and upon him is a month of fasting, then let his wali feed a poor person for every day.' This is the primary proof for the Hanafi position.
This hadith is recorded in Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'falyut'im' (let him feed) indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul (al-amr yufidu al-wujub). The feeding is explicitly per day, so the quantum is fixed.
The Hanafi school understands this as a direct obligation on the wali to feed, not to fast.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Ahmad (Hanbali) argues that the wali should fast, citing a hadith from Aisha: 'Whoever dies and upon him is fasting, his wali should fast for him.'
This hadith is in Abu Dawud.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes: the hadith of Aisha is understood as permissive (rukhsah), not obligatory. The will (wasiyyah) specifically demands feeding, not fasting. Hence, the wali's duty is to feed, consistent with the stronger textual evidence.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): Hanafi feeds; Hanbali fasts.

Breaking a Voluntary Fast: Qada is Wajib
Ruling on breaking a voluntary fast after commencement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking a voluntary fast and explain the proof from the hadith of Aisha.

Core Idea
Once you start a voluntary fast, it becomes wajib to complete; breaking it requires qada.

Many assume voluntary acts are entirely optional, but in Hanafi fiqh, once you begin a voluntary act of worship, it acquires a binding force. This lesson examines the ruling for voluntary fasting: if you break it after starting, you must make it up. We'll explore the legal reasoning and the prophetic evidence that establishes this obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its initial ruling
Determine what the ruling is for a person who enters a voluntary fast (nafl) before commencement and after commencement. Before starting it is purely optional (nafl). After starting it becomes akin to a voluntary vow (nadhr nafli).
The key is the transformation upon commencement.
2
State the ruling for breaking it
The text says: 'whoever enters into a voluntary fast then breaks it, must make it up (qada).' So qada is required.
No other penalty, just one day make-up.
3
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Aisha and Hafsah, who broke their voluntary fast because of a gift. The Prophet told them: 'Fast another day in its place.'
This is a direct command indicating obligation.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's instruction 'صوما مكانه يوما آخر' (fast another day instead) shows that a missed voluntary fast must be compensated. The imperative (صوما) indicates wujub in Hanafi usul.
The command is explicit and unqualified.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The reason qada becomes wajib is that starting the fast turns it into a voluntary vow (nadhr). Fulfilling a vow is wajib. Thus, breaking it creates an obligation to make up.
This principle applies to all voluntary acts of worship in the Hanafi school.

Feeding for Missed Fasts upon Death: Hanafi Ruling and Proof
Ruling on feeding fidya if deceased bequeaths missed fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fidya for missed fasts when the deceased bequeaths and distinguish it from the view of Imam Ahmad.

Core Idea
Feeding is obligatory only if the deceased bequeathed; the guardian feeds half a sa' of wheat per day.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person dies with missed Ramadan fasts and leaves a bequest for fidya. The Hanafi school, drawing on a hadith from Ibn Umar, rules that the guardian must feed a poor person for each missed day with a specified amount. The lesson unpacks the proof, the condition of bequest, and contrasts it with the Hanbali view that the guardian should fast on behalf of the deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
Read the matn: the ruling applies when the deceased has missed fasts and bequeaths fidya. The guardian must feed. Note that the obligation is conditional upon the bequest.
The final line of the passage emphasizes: if no bequest, no obligation.
2
Examine the proof (dalil) from hadith
The author cites the hadith 'Whoever dies and owes fasting of a month, let food be given on his behalf for each day to a poor person.' This is the primary evidence.
Hadith is from Sunan al-Tirmidhi (no. 718) and Sunan Abi Dawud (no. 2400).
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The imperative 'falyut'am' (let him feed) indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. However, the context of bequest is derived from other evidence, here the passage's own condition.
Hanafi usul: al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
4
Compare with the opposing view of Imam Ahmad
Imam Ahmad holds that the guardian should fast on behalf of the deceased, based on the hadith 'Whoever dies and owes fasting, his guardian should fast for him' (from Aisha).
Both ahadith are ahad; the Hanafi school prefers the feeding interpretation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical implication
The Hanafi mufta bihi is that feeding is wajib only with a bequest. The guardian must feed half a sa' of wheat (or equivalent) per missed day.
Without bequest, no action is required by the guardian.

Partial Fasting Obligation for New Muslims and Pubescent Children During Ramadan
Ruling on partial fasting obligation for new Muslims and pubescent children during Ramadan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three obligations of a child who reaches puberty or a disbeliever who accepts Islam during Ramadan and apply the rule that only the day of obligation requires qada.

Core Idea
When fasting becomes obligatory mid-day, only the remainder of that day must be kept and that day must be made up later.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a child reaching puberty or a disbeliever embracing Islam during Ramadan. It clarifies that obligation begins at the moment of capacity, not from the start of the month. Understanding this rule ensures that one does not overburden the newly obligated with past missed days.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Event and the Ruling
Recognize that the ruling applies to two specific cases: a boy reaching puberty (bulugh) or a disbeliever accepting Islam (islam) during the month of Ramadan.
The ruling is the same for both.
2
Understand the Three-Part Obligation
From the moment they become obligated, they must: (1) abstain from food, drink, and fast-breakers for the rest of that day out of respect; (2) fast all remaining days of Ramadan; (3) make up (qada) that specific day after Ramadan because the fast was not complete from dawn.
The imsak is not a full valid fast (since intention before dawn was missing), but it is an obligation of respect.
3
Recognize Why Past Days Are Not Required
Fasting was not obligatory before the moment of bulugh or islam, so no qada is needed for days prior. Obligation begins with capacity.
This is based on the principle that obligations apply only when the person is legally capable (mukallaf).
4
Note the Wisdom of the Ruling
The requirement to abstain for the rest of the day maintains the sanctity of Ramadan. The requirement to make up only that day ensures that the fasting obligation is fulfilled without undue hardship.

Ruling with proofs on obligation to fast remainder and make up after becoming mukallaf mid-day

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a child reaching puberty or a disbeliever embracing Islam during Ramadan, explain the evidence from hadith, and distinguish between past and future days regarding qada.

Core Idea
The obligation of fasting begins at the moment of responsibility; only the remaining days require qada, not the past.

A common question in Ramadan is what a child who becomes of age or a new Muslim should do regarding fasting. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on prophetic practice: they must respect the current day by refraining from food and drink for the rest of it, then fast all remaining days of Ramadan. The days before their obligation began are not required to be made up. This lesson examines the evidence and the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The passage rules on two persons: a child reaching puberty and a disbeliever embracing Islam, both during Ramadan. Their obligation: refrain for the remainder of the current day and fast all days after that day. No qada for past days.
The ruling is the same for both due to becoming mukallaf mid-month.
2
Locate the Dalil
The author cites three main hadiths: (1) Salamah ibn Akwa' regarding Ashura – command to fast the remainder of the day if one ate earlier; (2) 'Atiyyah al-Thaqafi – the delegation of Thaqif only commanded to fast the remaining days; (3) Hadith of Umm Hani about voluntary fasts, used to discuss the qada of voluntary acts.
The hadiths are ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for the wajib level.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
From Salamah hadith: the command to fast the remainder of the day shows that the obligation begins at the moment of entry into Islam, and reverence demands abstention for the rest. From 'Atiyyah: the Prophet only ordered fasting for the remaining days, explicitly not making up the past.
The analogy (qiyas) is that what is past was not obligatory, so no qada.
4

Although not the main focus, the passage contrasts the Hanafi view that breaking a voluntary fast requires qada (based on hadith of 'Aisha and Hafsa) with the Shafi'i view that it does not (based on Umm Hani hadith). The main ruling on obligatory fasts is not affected.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi says qada is wajib for broken voluntary fast; Shafi'i says mustahabb.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is as stated: the child/new Muslim must refrain for the rest of the day and fast the remaining days, not the past. This is firmly established in Zahir al-Riwayah.
The Shafi'i position on voluntary qada does not challenge this core ruling.

Ruling on Recovery from Insanity during Ramadan: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada for an insane person who recovers during Ramadan and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
An insane person who recovers during Ramadan must make up missed fasts; the obligation attaches to the month, not the state of sanity.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute: what happens when a person was insane for part of Ramadan and then recovers? The Hanafi school requires qada for the missed days, while the Shafi'i school does not. Understanding the reasoning behind each position is essential for grasping how the two schools define legal obligation (taklif) and the role of time-bound acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
From the matn: 'If the insane person recovers during some part of Ramadan, he makes up what has passed and fasts what remains.' Identify the two components: qada for past days and fasting remaining days.
The ruling applies only if recovery occurs within Ramadan itself.
2
Understand the Hanafi rationale
The commentator explains that the obligation of fasting applied to the entire month of Ramadan (the cause was present). Insanity only suspended performance, so upon recovery, the past days become wajib to make up. Full-month insanity exempts because the cause was never realized.
Contrast with the case of full-month insanity: no qada at all.
3
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no qada is required for days of insanity because the insane person is not mukallaf (legally responsible). He cites the hadith 'the pen is lifted from three' and the athar about the madwoman released from stoning.
The athar is about criminal liability (hadd), not worship.
4

The Hanafi school distinguishes between obligations that require intention (like prayer) and time-bound obligations (like fasting). For fasting, the time (Ramadan) is the cause. If the person becomes sane within that time, the past days become due because the entire month was addressed to him as a mukallaf once he regains sanity.
The hadith 'raf' al-qalam' applies to the moment of commission, not to the obligation that remains attached to the time.
5

Hanafis: qada wajib for days of insanity if recovery during Ramadan. Shafi'is: no qada. This affects the number of fasts a person must observe after recovery and in later days.
The mufta bihi in Hanafi school is the position of wajib qada.

Ruling on Eating Sehri or Iftar with Mistaken Belief about Dawn or Sunset
Ruling on mistaken sehri/iftar with qada and no kaffara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a person who eats sehri after dawn or breaks fast before sunset by mistake, and distinguish between qada and kaffara.

Core Idea
Eating during Ramadan by mistake requires qada but not kaffara.

Mistakes about the time of dawn or sunset can happen due to clouds or poor visibility. In such cases, the fast is invalid because eating occurred during the day, but the mistake removes the obligation of kaffara. This lesson explains the ruling and its proof from the hadith of Asma bint Abi Bakr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The text presents two cases: (1) eating sehri thinking dawn has not risen, but it had; (2) breaking fast thinking the sun has set, but it had not. Both involve a mistaken belief about the time.
2
Determine the ruling on the fast
The fast is invalid because eating occurred during the daylight hours of Ramadan. The person must make up the day (qada).
3
Understand why kaffara is not required
Kaffara (expiation) is only due for intentionally breaking the fast without a valid excuse. The mistake here is akin to forgetfulness (nisyan), which drops the obligation of kaffara. Only qada is wajib.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith from Asma (Bukhari no. 1959) narrates that people broke fast on a cloudy day thinking the sun set, but then the sun reappeared. The response was that qada is unavoidable. This proves the obligation of qada, and by implication, no kaffara because it was a mistake.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Respect for Ramadan requires refraining even when the fast is not valid from the start.

This lesson covers a specific scenario in Ramadan: a traveler returns home or a menstruating woman becomes pure during the day. While they cannot complete a valid fast for that day, they are not free to eat. Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is refraining (imsak) from food and drink for the rest of the day. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) out of respect for Ramadan, even though the fast itself is not valid because the day did not begin with a valid intention or eligibility.
2

The reason is to honor the sanctity of Ramadan. Even though the person cannot complete a full fast, they must still abstain from eating and drinking from the moment they become eligible until sunset.

The author cites the hadith of Salama ibn Akwa' from Sahih al-Bukhari where the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded those who had eaten on 'Ashura to fast the remainder of the day. This serves as an analogy for anyone who becomes eligible mid-day.

The command 'فليصم بقية يومه' (let him fast the remainder of his day) is an explicit imperative, which in Hanafi usul indicates obligation (wujub) unless there is a contrary indication. No contrary indication exists here, so imsak is wajib.

This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school. Some other schools may not require refraining because the fast is not valid. However, the Hanafi position is that imsak is wajib out of respect, and it is the mufta bihi.

The author defines this large group ('ru'ya 'amma') based on quotes from Abu Yusuf and al-Durr al-Mukhtar: either fifty men like qasama, or one or two from each mosque/neighborhood. An athar from 'Ata' supports that individual sighting before others is not followed.

Key Terms
علة (‘illa): An obstruction in the sky such as clouds, smoke, fog, dust that hinders clear vision of the crescent.
شهادة (shahada): Testimony of witnesses regarding the sighting of the crescent moon for Ramadan or Eid.
روية عامة (ru’ya ‘amma): General sighting witnessed by a large number of people (e.g., one or two from each neighbourhood) producing certain knowledge.

Imam Muhammad's Ruling on Short Nafl I'tikaf Without Fasting
Imam Muhammad's fatwa on short nafl i'tikaf without fasting, with evidence

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Nafl i'tikaf requires neither fasting nor a minimum duration — a few minutes suffice.

This passage introduces a lenient view within the Hanafi school regarding supererogatory i'tikaf. While the general principle requires fasting and a full day for any i'tikaf, Imam Muhammad presents evidence allowing a very short period, even minutes, without fasting. Understanding this difference is crucial for knowing the flexibility of nafl acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the opening statement: Imam Muhammad says nafl i'tikaf can be for a few minutes and fasting is not a condition. Note that this applies only to nafl (voluntary) i'tikaf, not to wajib or masnun.
The ruling is specific to supererogatory i'tikaf.
2
Examine the proofs
Look at the two evidences: the athar of Ya'la ibn Umayyah mentioning i'tikaf for an hour, and the hadith of Hudhayfah stating that any mosque with a mu'adhdhin and imam is suitable for i'tikaf. Consider how these support the ruling.
The hadith implies permissibility without specifying duration or fasting.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Recognize that Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf require fasting and a minimum of one day for any i'tikaf. Understand that this is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school, and Imam Muhammad's view is mufta bihi for nafl.
The difference affects practical fatwa for those wanting short i'tikaf.

Prohibition of Sexual Acts and Invalidation of I'tikaf
Ruling with proof on prohibitions during i'tikaf and invalidation upon ejaculation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prohibited acts during i'tikaf and explain when i'tikaf becomes invalid requiring qada'.

Core Idea
Sexual acts during i'tikaf are haram; if ejaculation occurs, i'tikaf is void and must be made up.

In this lesson, we examine two connected rulings from the Hanafi school on i'tikaf. First, the prohibition of intercourse, touching with desire, and kissing is established with clear Quranic and hadith evidence. Second, the consequence of violating these prohibitions through ejaculation is that i'tikaf is invalidated and requires qada'. Understanding these rulings helps maintain the sanctity of i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts and their ruling
From the matn: intercourse, touching with desire, and kissing are explicitly forbidden. Determine that the hukm is haram.
The prohibition applies day and night during i'tikaf.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The commentary cites the Quranic verse (2:187) and the hadith of Aisha. Recognize both as primary sources.
The Quran is qat'i al-thubut; the hadith is zanni al-thubut but still binding.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Quranic prohibition "la tubbashiru" is general and indicates haram. The hadith explicitly forbids touching and intimate contact.
The imperative in the hadith also indicates prohibition.
4
Identify the condition for invalidation
The second ruling states that i'tikaf is invalidated only if ejaculation (inzal) occurs from kissing or touching. Without ejaculation, the act is haram but i'tikaf remains valid.
This distinction is crucial: sin vs. invalidation.
5
Understand the qada' obligation
When i'tikaf is invalidated, qada' becomes wajib, even if the i'tikaf was nafl. The athar of Ibn Abbas and the hadith of Aisha making up i'tikaf support this.
Once started, nafl i'tikaf becomes binding like a vow.

Speech During I'tikaf: Speaking Good and Dislike of Silence
Ruling that mu'takif should speak only good and that complete silence is disliked

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on speech during i'tikaf and explain why observing complete silence as an act of worship is disliked.

Core Idea
In i'tikaf, speak only good; silence as worship is disliked, not required.

A common misconception about i'tikaf is that it requires complete silence. The Hanafi school clarifies that speech is permitted, but should be limited to beneficial words. Observing permanent silence as a devotional act is disliked because it resembles non-Islamic asceticism and has no basis in the Sunnah. This lesson explains the ruling and the evidence from the hadith of Safiyya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The mu'takif is instructed not to speak except good (khair). Observing silence (samt) as a form of worship is disliked (makruh). Identify that speaking good is mustahab, while silence as worship is makruh tanzih.
The ruling applies only to intentional silence as a devotional act, not to occasional silence.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Safiyya (Abu Dawud) where she visited the Prophet during i'tikaf and conversed with him. This shows that speech is permissible.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), so it provides zanni thubut and dalala.
3
Analyze wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's engagement in conversation indicates that speech is not prohibited. The instruction to speak only good is derived from other general teachings, not from this hadith alone.
The emphasis is that any speech should be virtuous.
4
Apply the Hanafi usul principle
In Hanafi usul, silence is not an act of worship unless prescribed. Since it is not established as sunnah, observing it as worship is makruh because it may lead to bid'ah.
The principle is that all acts of worship require explicit evidence.

Sexual Intercourse Invalidates I'tikaf Regardless of Intent
Ruling with proof: sexual intercourse invalidates i'tikaf absolutely.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sexual intercourse during i'tikaf and identify the proofs that make the ruling absolute.

Core Idea
Sexual intercourse invalidates i'tikaf whether intentional or forgetful — the prohibition is absolute because the act contradicts the state of seclusion.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling that sexual intercourse during i'tikaf voids the i'tikaf regardless of intent. The author cites both the Quranic verse (2:187) and an athar from Ibn Abbas to establish that the prohibition is unconditional. Understanding this ruling requires appreciating the inviolability of the i'tikaf state and why forgetfulness does not excuse the action.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is sexual intercourse (jima') during the state of i'tikaf. The ruling from the text is that it invalidates (batal/فسد) the i'tikaf, whether done by night or day, intentionally or forgetfully.
The ruling is absolute; no distinction is made for time or intent.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author first cites the Quranic verse (2:187): 'ولا تباشروهن وأنتم عاكفون في المساجد' (And do not have relations with them while you are secluded in the mosques). This is the foundational proof.
The verse uses a direct prohibition, which in Hanafi usul indicates obligation to abstain.
3
Locate the additional dalil
The author then cites an athar from Ibn Abbas in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba: 'إذا جامع المعتكف أبطل اعتكافه واستأنف' (If the mu'takif has intercourse, he invalidates his i'tikaf and starts over). This athar makes no mention of intent, so it supports the absolute ruling.
Even though the athar has zanni thubut, it confirms the Quranic prohibition.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
The Hanafi principle is that the prohibition is 'amm (general) and unconditiona. Since both the Quran and the athar do not except forgetfulness, the ruling applies to both intentional and forgetful acts. The author explains that i'tikaf is a devotion that reminds one of Allah, so forgetfulness in this state is not excused.
This contrasts with some schools that might distinguish based on intent.
5
Note the subsidiary ruling
The passage also includes a second ruling (8) about leaving the mosque intentionally without excuse: according to Abu Hanifa, even a short absence invalidates i'tikaf, while the two Sahibayn require absence of more than half a day. This highlights that different actions may have different thresholds.
This subsidiary ruling shows intra-Hanafi disagreement but does not affect the absolute nature of the jima' ruling.
