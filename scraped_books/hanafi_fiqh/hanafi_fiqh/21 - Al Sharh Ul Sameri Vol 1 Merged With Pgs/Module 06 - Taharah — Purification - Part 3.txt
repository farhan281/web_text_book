Ruling for Doubtful Water: Combining Wudu and Tayammum
Ruling for doubtful water purification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for using doubtful (mashkuk) water and explain why both wudu and tayammum are required.

Core Idea
When water is doubtful, perform both wudu and tayammum to ensure validity.

This passage addresses a specific situation where only water from the leftover (su'ur) of a donkey or mule is available. Such water is considered doubtful (mashkuk) in the Hanafi school — not definitively pure nor impure. To resolve the uncertainty, the school requires both wudu with that water and tayammum, ensuring the prayer is valid regardless of the water's true ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the situation and the act
This ruling applies when a person has no water except the leftover (su'ur) of a donkey or mule. The act is purification for prayer.
2
State the ruling (hukm)
The Hanafi ruling is that one must perform both wudu (with that water) and tayammum. This is an obligatory combination.
Not merely recommended; both are compulsory.
3
Explain the underlying reason
The water is mashkuk (doubtful) because there is no definitive evidence of its purity or impurity. Therefore, both acts are needed as precaution: wudu to cover the possibility of purity, tayammum to cover the possibility of impurity.
4
Note the permissibility of order
The order between wudu and tayammum does not matter; either can be done first. The phrase 'wa bi ayyihima bada'a jaaza' indicates flexibility.
5
Distinguish from other schools
The Shafi'i school considers su'ur of donkey pure, so they would only require wudu. The Hanafi ruling is unique due to the concept of mashkuk.
This legal difference stems from each school's criteria for purity.

Commentary
This passage gives the practical ruling for the doubtful water of donkey and mule. Since their su'ur is mashkuk, it is not definitively pure enough for wudu alone, nor definitively impure to render wudu invalid. Therefore, as a precaution, one must do both: perform wudu with that water (since it may be pure) and also perform tayammum (since it may be impure). The order is not important; one can do wudu first or tayammum first. This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school as a result of the doubt. The prayer performed after both acts will be valid.

Su'ur of Predatory Birds and Domestic Animals
Ruling statement with brief reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the su'ur of predatory birds and domestic animals and identify the basis for the ruling of makruh.

Core Idea
The su'ur of animals that eat impurities is makruh due to the presumption of impurity in their mouths.

This lesson focuses on the first part of the passage, which covers the su'ur (leftover water) of predatory birds and animals that live in houses, such as snakes and mice. The Hanafi school rules this as makruh (disliked) based on the likelihood that these animals have impurity in their mouths. However, if the beak or mouth is known to be pure, the su'ur becomes pure. Understanding this ruling helps you apply the principle of 'presumption of impurity' in taharah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the animals and their su'ur ruling
The text mentions predatory birds (siba' al-tuyur) and animals that live in houses (e.g., snakes, mice, cats) – their su'ur is ruled makruh. First, list these animals and note the general ruling.
Makruh here means makruh tanzihi (disliked but not sinful).
2
Understand the basis for makruh
The reason is 'presumption of impurity' (ghalabat al-najis). These animals often come into contact with impurities (e.g., a free-ranging chicken puts its beak into waste). Therefore, their mouth is presumed impure unless known otherwise.
This is a Hanafi application of istihsan based on common practice.
3
Identify the exception
If the animal's beak or mouth is known to be pure (e.g., a chicken with a clean beak), then its su'ur becomes pure because the meat of such animals is halal and their saliva follows the meat's ruling.
The exception is based on the hadith of cat su'ur, where combining two hadiths led to makruh tanzihi rather than impurity.
4
Apply the ruling to new cases
For any animal that normally eats impurities but is not known to be impure in a specific instance, the su'ur is makruh. If you are certain the mouth is clean, it is pure.
This is a practical distinction: the default ruling changes with knowledge.

The su'ur of predatory birds and those that live in houses, such as the snake and mouse, is makruh. And the su'ur of the donkey and mule is mashkuk (doubtful). If a person finds no water other than these two, he should perform wudu with them and also perform tayammum; whichever he does first is permissible. From this hadith it is known that the su'ur of a cat is pure. Therefore, by combining the two hadiths, they say that the su'ur of a cat is makruh tanzihi. The same applies to all animals that live in houses. A free-ranging chicken keeps putting its mouth into impurities, so due to the presumption of impurity in its mouth, its su'ur is makruh. If its beak is completely pure, then its su'ur is pure because its meat is eaten. (Footnote: al-dajajah: chicken; al-mukhallat: that which roams freely; siba' al-tuyur: birds that hunt and eat; al-hayyah: snake; al-fa'rah: mouse). (62) The su'ur of the donkey and the mule is mashkuk. The reason it is mashkuk is that there are two types of evidence regarding the donkey's meat and sweat. The Prophet forbade eating donkey meat. And when the meat is not halal, then its saliva should also be najis. According to this, the su'ur of the donkey should be impure. But the Prophet rode a donkey, so his clothes would have gotten donkey sweat on them, and sweat comes from flesh, and the ruling of sweat is the same as that of saliva. So if the garment was not washed due to sweat and sweat is pure, then from this perspective saliva should also be pure. So there are both types of evidence concerning donkey saliva, therefore its su'ur is mashkuk. The proof for impurity is from Jabir ibn Abdullah: 'The Messenger of Allah forbade the meat of donkeys on the day of Khaybar and permitted horses.' (Bukhari, Bab Ghazwa Khaybar, vol. 2, p. 606, no. 4219) When meat is not halal, then saliva will also not be pure. And the proof for purity is from Mu'adh: 'I was riding behind the Prophet on a donkey called 'Ufayr.' (Bukhari, Bab Ism al-Faras wa al-Himar, p. 400, no. 2856) The Prophet rode a donkey, so his clothes would have gotten sweat, and sweat is pure, so saliva should also be pure. Due to these two types of evidence, the su'ur of the donkey is mashkuk. According to Imam Shafi'i, due to the latter evidence, the su'ur of the donkey is pure. The mule: since it is born from a she-donkey, the ruling that applies to the su'ur of the she-donkey also applies to the mule, i.e., its su'ur is mashkuk. (63) So if a person finds no water other than the su'ur of the donkey and the mule, he should perform wudu with that water and also perform tayammum. Whichever he does first is permissible. Footnote (a): The Prophet forbade eating donkey meat on the day of Khaybar and permitted horse meat. (b) Mu'adh said: I was riding behind the Prophet on a donkey called 'Ufayr. When the su'ur of the donkey and mule is mashkuk, it is both pure and impure. So if there is no other water and only the su'ur of the donkey or mule is available, because it is water, one should perform wudu, but because it might be impure, one should also perform tayammum. Now, whether one performs wudu first and then tayammum, or tayammum first and then wudu, both are correct.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi legal commentary (sharh) that expands on a matn ruling regarding the water leftover (su'ur) of certain animals, providing proofs and reasoning. The author first states that the su'ur of predatory birds and domestic animals like snakes and mice is makruh (disliked, not haram). Then he addresses the su'ur of the donkey and mule, which is classified as mashkuk (doubtful) due to conflicting evidence. The author explains the opposing proofs: one hadith forbids donkey meat, implying impurity; another hadith shows the Prophet riding a donkey, implying purity of its sweat and by extension its saliva. This conflict leads to the ruling of mashkuk. Imam Shafi'i considers it pure based on the latter hadith. The ruling for the mule follows that of the she-donkey because it is born from her. Finally, the passage gives the practical ruling when no other water is available: one must perform both wudu (using that water) and tayammum, in any order, because the water may be pure or impure. The commentary also clarifies that the su'ur of a free-ranging chicken is makruh due to presumption of impurity in its beak, but if the beak is known to be pure, its su'ur is pure since its meat is halal.

مخلات (mukhālat): Birds that roam freely and eat from impurities.

Su'ur of Donkey and Mule
Ruling with proof from hadith and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the su'ur of donkey and mule is mashkuk and apply the ruling of performing both wudu and tayammum.

Core Idea
Conflicting hadith evidence on donkey purity leads to mashkuk, requiring precautionary dual purification.

This lesson covers the second major ruling of the passage: the su'ur of donkey and mule is classified as mashkuk (doubtful) due to conflicting hadith. The Hanafi school resolves the contradiction by requiring both wudu and tayammum when no other water is available. Understanding this category is essential because it illustrates how Hanafi jurists handle textual conflict and the concept of precaution (ihtiyat).

Guided Path
1
Identify the animals and the ruling of mashkuk
The text states that the su'ur of donkey and mule is mashkuk. First, note that this is a unique Hanafi category, not found in other schools.
Mashkuk means doubtful – the water is treated as possibly pure and possibly impure.
2
Examine the conflicting evidence
The author presents two hadith: the prohibition of donkey meat (Bukhari) implies impurity of its saliva, while the report of the Prophet riding a donkey implies purity of its sweat/saliva. These are the two proofs.
Both hadith are ahad (single-transmitter), so they are zanni al-thubut.
3
Understand the Hanafi reasoning for mashkuk
Because both proofs are equally strong in Hanafi methodology, neither can override the other. The conflict leads to doubt, so the water is not declared pure nor impure, but mashkuk.
Shafi'i favors the purity hadith and rules it pure.
4
Apply the practical ruling
If no other water is available, one must perform both wudu (because it may be pure) and tayammum (because it may be impure). The order is flexible.
This ensures that the obligation of purification is fulfilled regardless of the actual state of the water.
5
Note the mule's ruling
The mule follows the ruling of its mother (she-donkey), so its su'ur is also mashkuk.
This is based on the principle that the offspring follows the mother in legal rulings related to purity.

مشکوک ہونے کی وجہ یہ ہے کہ گدھے کے گوشت اور پسینے کے سلسلے میں دونوں قسم کے دلائل ہیں ۔ آپ نے گدھے کا گوشت کھانے سے منع فرمایا۔ اور جب گوشت حلال نہیں ہوگا تو اس کا نکلا ہوا تھوک بھی نجس ہوگا۔ اس اعتبار سے گدھے کا جوٹھا نا پاک ہونا چاہئے ۔ لیکن آپ گدھے پر سوار ہوئے ہیں جس کی وجہ سے آپ کے کپڑے پر گدھے کا پسینہ لگا ہوگا اور پسینہ گوشت سے نکلتا ہے اور کسی پسینے کا حکم بھی وہی ہے جو تھوک کا حکم ہے۔ اس لئے اگر پسینہ لگنے سے کپڑا نہیں دھویا اور پسینہ پاک ہے تو اس اعتبار سے تھوک بھی پاک ہونا چاہئے ۔ تو گویا کہ گدھے کے تھوک کے سلسلے میں دونوں قسم کے دلائل ہیں اس لئے گدھے کا جوٹھا مشکوک ہے۔ نجس ہونے کی دلیل یہ ہے عـن جـابـر بـن عبد الله قال نهى رسول الله الله يوم خيبر عن لحوم الحمر ورخص في الخيل (الف) ( بخاری شریف، باب غزوہ خیبرج ثانی ص ۶۰۶ نمبر ۴۲۱۹) جب گوشت حلال نہیں تو تھوک بھی پاک نہیں ہوگا۔ اور تھوک پاک ہونے کی دلیل یہ ہے عن معاذ قال كنت ردف النبى الله على حمار يقال له عفير (ب) ( بخاری شریف ، باب اسم الفرس و الحمارص ۴۰۰ نمبر ۲۸۵۶) آپ گدھے پر سوار ہوئے تو کپڑے پر پسینہ لگا ہوگا اور پسینہ پاک ہے تو تھوک بھی پاک ہونا چاہئے ۔ ان دونوں قسم کے دلائل کی وجہ سے گدھے کا جوٹھا مشکوک ہے نان امام شافعی کے نزدیک پچھلے دلائل کی وجہ سے گدھے کا جوٹھا پاک ہے۔ البغل : خچر چونکہ گدھی سے پیدا ہوتا ہے اس لئے جو حکم گدھی کے جو ٹھے کا ہوا وہی حکم خچر کا بھی ہوا۔ یعنی اس کا جوٹھا مشکوک ہے۔

جب گدھا اور خچر کا جوٹھا مشکوک ہوا تو تو وہ پاک بھی ہے اور نا پاک بھی ہے۔ اس لئے اگر کوئی اور پانی نہیں ہے صرف گدھے کا جوٹھایا خچر کا جوٹھا پانی اس کے پاس ہو تو چونکہ پانی ہے اس لئے اس کو وضو کرنا چاہئے لیکن یہ پانی ناپاک بھی ہو سکتا ہے اس لئے اس کو تیم کرنا چاہئے ۔اب پہلے وضو کرے اور بعد میں تیمم کرے وہ بھی ٹھیک ہے اور پہلے تمیم کرے بعد میں وضو کرے وہ بھی ٹھیک ہے۔
The su'ur of predatory birds and those that live in houses, such as the snake and mouse, is makruh. And the su'ur of the donkey and mule is mashkuk (doubtful). If a person finds no water other than these two, he should perform wudu with them and also perform tayammum; whichever he does first is permissible. From this hadith it is known that the su'ur of a cat is pure. Therefore, by combining the two hadiths, they say that the su'ur of a cat is makruh tanzihi. The same applies to all animals that live in houses. A free-ranging chicken keeps putting its mouth into impurities, so due to the presumption of impurity in its mouth, its su'ur is makruh. If its beak is completely pure, then its su'ur is pure because its meat is eaten. (Footnote: al-dajajah: chicken; al-mukhallat: that which roams freely; siba' al-tuyur: birds that hunt and eat; al-hayyah: snake; al-fa'rah: mouse). (62) The su'ur of the donkey and the mule is mashkuk. The reason it is mashkuk is that there are two types of evidence regarding the donkey's meat and sweat. The Prophet forbade eating donkey meat. And when the meat is not halal, then its saliva should also be najis. According to this, the su'ur of the donkey should be impure. But the Prophet rode a donkey, so his clothes would have gotten donkey sweat on them, and sweat comes from flesh, and the ruling of sweat is the same as that of saliva. So if the garment was not washed due to sweat and sweat is pure, then from this perspective saliva should also be pure. So there are both types of evidence concerning donkey saliva, therefore its su'ur is mashkuk. The proof for impurity is from Jabir ibn Abdullah: 'The Messenger of Allah forbade the meat of donkeys on the day of Khaybar and permitted horses.' (Bukhari, Bab Ghazwa Khaybar, vol. 2, p. 606, no. 4219) When meat is not halal, then saliva will also not be pure. And the proof for purity is from Mu'adh: 'I was riding behind the Prophet on a donkey called 'Ufayr.' (Bukhari, Bab Ism al-Faras wa al-Himar, p. 400, no. 2856) The Prophet rode a donkey, so his clothes would have gotten sweat, and sweat is pure, so saliva should also be pure. Due to these two types of evidence, the su'ur of the donkey is mashkuk. According to Imam Shafi'i, due to the latter evidence, the su'ur of the donkey is pure. The mule: since it is born from a she-donkey, the ruling that applies to the su'ur of the she-donkey also applies to the mule, i.e., its su'ur is mashkuk. (63) So if a person finds no water other than the su'ur of the donkey and the mule, he should perform wudu with that water and also perform tayammum. Whichever he does first is permissible. Footnote (a): The Prophet forbade eating donkey meat on the day of Khaybar and permitted horse meat. (b) Mu'adh said: I was riding behind the Prophet on a donkey called 'Ufayr. When the su'ur of the donkey and mule is mashkuk, it is both pure and impure. So if there is no other water and only the su'ur of the donkey or mule is available, because it is water, one should perform wudu, but because it might be impure, one should also perform tayammum. Now, whether one performs wudu first and then tayammum, or tayammum first and then wudu, both are correct.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi legal commentary (sharh) that expands on a matn ruling regarding the water leftover (su'ur) of certain animals, providing proofs and reasoning. The author first states that the su'ur of predatory birds and domestic animals like snakes and mice is makruh (disliked, not haram). Then he addresses the su'ur of the donkey and mule, which is classified as mashkuk (doubtful) due to conflicting evidence. The author explains the opposing proofs: one hadith forbids donkey meat, implying impurity; another hadith shows the Prophet riding a donkey, implying purity of its sweat and by extension its saliva. This conflict leads to the ruling of mashkuk. Imam Shafi'i considers it pure based on the latter hadith. The ruling for the mule follows that of the she-donkey because it is born from her. Finally, the passage gives the practical ruling when no other water is available: one must perform both wudu (using that water) and tayammum, in any order, because the water may be pure or impure. The commentary also clarifies that the su'ur of a free-ranging chicken is makruh due to presumption of impurity in its beak, but if the beak is known to be pure, its su'ur is pure since its meat is halal.

Ruling on Donkey's and Mule's Leftover Water
Ruling on doubtful water from donkeys/mules and order of wudu and tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using the leftover water (jutha) of a donkey or mule and explain why both wudu and tayammum are required, including the permissible order.

Core Idea
The leftover water of a donkey or mule is doubtful; when no other water exists, both wudu and tayammum are obligatory.

This lesson addresses a unique case in Hanafi taharah: the water left by a donkey or mule after drinking. Its purity status is considered mashkuk (doubtful). The Hanafi school therefore prescribes a precautionary approach: one must perform both wudu (using the water) and tayammum (because it may be impure). The order of these acts is flexible, reflecting the principle of ihtiyat (precaution) in cases of doubt.

Tayammum: Definition, Proofs, and the One-Mile Distance Condition
Introduces tayammum with definition, Quranic and hadith proofs, and first condition (one mile from water).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of tayammum, identify its Quranic and hadith proofs, and apply the condition of one-mile distance from water for its permissibility.

Core Idea
Tayammum is a valid purification when water is absent; a one-mile distance from water constitutes legitimate absence.

Tayammum, or dry ablution, is a Divinely granted concession when water is unavailable. This lesson unpacks its definition, the primary proofs from the Quran and Sunnah, and the first condition articulated by the Hanafi school: being a traveler or outside the city at least one mile from water. Understanding this threshold is key to correctly applying the ruling in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tayammum
Read the lexical and Shari'ah definition given in the passage. Tayammum linguistically means 'intention' and technically means intending pure earth to purify from ritual impurity.
The definition is essential for understanding its substitute nature.
2

The passage cites Surah al-Nisa 4:43 and Surah al-Ma'idah 5:6. Identify the imperative phrase 'fatayammamu' and note its command form. In Hanafi usul, a command indicates obligation unless overridden.
Thubut: qati; Dalala: zanni. The command establishes permissibility and, in some contexts, obligation.
3
Examine the supplementary hadith evidence
The hadith from Abu Dhar states that pure earth suffices for purification even if water is not found for ten years. This reinforces the ruling's breadth.
This hadith strengthens the Quranic proof by showing tayammum's sufficiency over time.
4

The passage explains that if one is a traveler or outside the city and water is at least one mile away, tayammum is permissible. The reasoning is based on hardship (haraj) in fetching water. The evidence is the practice of Ibn Umar at distances of one, two, or three miles from Medina.
The threshold is one mile; less than that implies ease of fetching water, so tayammum is not permitted.

Chapter on Tayammum
[64] (1) And whoever does not find water while he is a traveler or outside the city, between him and the city about a mile or more.
Chapter on Tayammum: Necessary note: The meaning of tayammum is to intend. And in Shari'ah, tayammum is to intend pure earth to purify from hadath (ritual impurity). Its proof is this verse: '...and you find no water, then perform tayammum with pure earth and wipe your faces and your hands.' (a) (Surah al-Nisa 4:43) Tayammum is permissible when one does not have access to water. (24) (1) Whoever does not find water, in the condition that he is a traveler or outside the city, and between him and the city is approximately a mile or more, then he will perform tayammum.)
Reasoning:
(1) The ruling of performing tayammum when water is not found is in this verse: 'If you are ill or on a journey or one of you comes from the toilet or you have touched women and you find no water, then perform tayammum with pure earth and wipe your faces and your hands with it.' (b) (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:6) (2) There is a hadith from Abu Dhar... the Messenger of Allah (s.a.w.) said: 'Pure earth is the wudu of a Muslim, even if he does not find water for ten years.' (Sunan Abi Dawud, chapter on the junub performing tayammum, p. 53, no. 332) The verse states that if one does not find water, he can perform tayammum. Core Idea
Tayammum is permitted with predominant fear of harm, not only fear of death — the Sharia removes hardship.

This broader permission contrasts with the Shafi'i restriction to fear of death or limb loss. Permissible Substances for Tayammum: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains which types of earth are valid for tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on permissible substances for tayammum and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, citing the evidence for each view.

Core Idea
Tayammum is valid on any earth-genus substance that does not burn or melt, according to the majority of the Hanafi school.

This passage examines a disagreement within the Hanafi school itself regarding the range of substances that can be used for tayammum. While the general rule is that tayammum substitutes for wudu with earth, scholars differed on how broadly 'earth' should be interpreted. The lesson contrasts the wider view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad with the narrower view of Abu Yusuf, and analyzes the evidence for each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Recognize that the dispute is about the definition of 'sa'id' (صعيد) in the Quranic verse for tayammum. Does it include only soil and sand, or all earth-genus substances?
The issue is linguistic and legal: the scope of the term 'earth' in the primary sources.
2
State the wide view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They permit tayammum with anything from the genus of the earth (jins al-ard) such as soil, sand, stone, plaster, lime, kohl, orpiment. Their criterion is any natural earth substance that is not burned by fire or melted.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position of the school.
3
State the narrow view of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf restricts tayammum to soil (turāb) and sand (raml) specifically, excluding other substances like stone and lime.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
4
Examine the evidence for each position
For Abu Hanifa: the hadith 'the earth was made for me a masjid and a purifier', the Quranic word 'sa'id', and the athar of Hammad. For Abu Yusuf: the athar of Ibn Abbas that 'the best sa'id is cultivated land'.
Compare how each side interprets the same texts.
5
Determine the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the operative ruling for fatwa because the general proofs (hadith and Quran) override the specific interpretation of Abu Yusuf.
This is the standard Hanafi position taught in later works.

Intention and Nullifiers in Tayammum
Ruling with proof on intention in tayammum and list of nullifiers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intention in tayammum, explain the rationale, and identify what nullifies tayammum.

Core Idea
In tayammum, intention is fard because earth is not inherently purifying; water is, so intention is mustahabb in wudu.

You already know that tayammum is a substitute for wudu (or ghusl) when water is unavailable. But does it require the same intention as wudu? And once you have performed tayammum, what breaks it? This passage from the matn answers both questions, drawing a clear distinction between the role of intention in tayammum versus wudu, and establishing that tayammum shares the same nullifiers as the act it replaces.

Guided Path
1
State the two rulings
Identify the two distinct rulings presented: (a) the status of intention (niyyah) in tayammum vs. wudu, and (b) what nullifies tayammum.
The first is a rule about intention; the second is about the nullifiers of the state of purity achieved by tayammum.
2
Understand the rationale for intention
Analyze why intention is fard in tayammum but only mustahabb in wudu. The key is the inherent property of the purifying agent: water is purifying (tahur) by itself, while earth is not; intention makes earth a purifier.
This is a rational (istihsani?) argument based on the nature of the substances.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
The verse 'fa-tayammamū ṣaʿīdan ṭayyiban' is interpreted as 'intend pure earth,' using the linguistic meaning of tayammum (to intend). This provides the textual evidence that intention is required.
The Hanafi school uses this to argue that intention is an essential part of the act.
4
Apply the substitution principle for nullifiers
Tayammum stands in place of wudu (or ghusl). Therefore, whatever nullifies the original act (hadath for wudu, janabah/menstruation for ghusl) also nullifies the substitute tayammum.
This is a qiyas: the ruling of the original is transferred to the substitute.
5
Distinguish between tayammum for wudu and ghusl
Note that the nullifiers differ depending on what the tayammum is substituting: if for wudu, minor hadath; if for ghusl, major impurity. The passage explicitly mentions this distinction.
This is important for practical application.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits tayammum on any earth-like substance that does not burn or melt; Abu Yusuf restricts it to soil and sand.

When water is unavailable, tayammum is a divine concession. But what exactly counts as 'earth'? This lesson unpacks a classical disagreement within the Hanafi school itself. Abu Hanifa, based on the general meaning of 'sa'eed', allows a wide range of surfaces, while his student Abu Yusuf, citing a different interpretation, limits it. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply the correct ruling in diverse circumstances.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify the substances Abu Hanifa considers valid: any earth-type material that does not burn or melt, including stones, gypsum, lime, kohl, and orpiment.
2
Identify the opposing view (Abu Yusuf)
Abu Yusuf holds that only soil (turab) and sand (raml) are valid. Note that Imam al-Shafi'i agrees with him.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's dalil
The Quranic phrase 'sa'eedan tayyiban' is general; an athar from Hammad supports using various earth types. The principle: any substance from the genus of earth that does not burn or melt.
Dalil: Quran 4:43, athar from Hammad (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah).
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's dalil
Abu Yusuf relies on an athar from Ibn Abbas that interprets 'sa'eed' as cultivated land, implying soil with growth potential. He restricts the general meaning.
Athar from Ibn Abbas: 'The best sa'eed is cultivated land.'
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, tayammum is valid on a wider range of surfaces; under Abu Yusuf, only on soil/sand. The school adopts Abu Hanifa's view as mufta bihi.
Tarjih: The later Hanafi masters give preference to Abu Hanifa due to stronger proof.

Ruling on what nullifies tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the nullifiers of tayammum and explain why finding water nullifies it with reference to the Quranic condition.

Core Idea
Tayammum is a substitute; its nullifiers mirror the original, plus gaining ability to use water.

Tayammum is a purification method for when water is absent. But how long does this purification last? This passage from the Hanafi text clarifies that tayammum is fragile: it ends not only by the same events that break wudu or ghusl, but also the moment water becomes available. Understanding this rule is essential for maintaining proper purity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of nullifiers
First, list the nullifiers common with wudu and ghusl. The text says 'everything that nullifies wudu' also nullifies tayammum. For ghusl substitution, the nullifiers are the causes of major impurity (janabah, hayd, nifas).
The text does not list wudu's nullifiers individually, but refers to the general rule.
2
Understand the substitution principle
The passage explicitly states that tayammum 'stands in place' of wudu or ghusl. This is key: the substitute carries the same rulings regarding nullification.
This is a khalifah (deputy) relationship.
3
Analyze the unique nullifier: finding water
The text adds that tayammum is nullified by 'seeing water when able to use it'. Why? Because the verse (4:43) conditions tayammum on not finding water. Once water is found, the condition is no longer met.
The amount of water required is sufficient for wudu or ghusl respectively.
4
Examine the Quranic proof
The author cites the verse 'if you do not find water, then perform tayammum with clean earth'. The logical inference is that finding water removes the permission.
The dalal is qati' (definite) because it directly conditions the ruling.
5
Apply to practical scenarios
Practice: If someone has tayammum and then sees enough water, even if they don't use it, the tayammum is broken. They must repeat tayammum or use water before praying.
This is immediate, not dependent on intention.

Tayammum Requires Pure Earth
Condition: tayammum valid only with pure (tahir) earth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of purity for earth in tayammum and identify the proofs from Quran and Sunnah that establish this condition.

Core Idea
Tayammum is valid only with pure, clean earth.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental condition for tayammum: the earth used must be pure (tahir). The Quran explicitly commands using 'sa'eedan tayyiban' – clean earth. The Prophet ﷺ confirmed this in the hadith of Abu Dharr. We will explore the textual proofs and the logical reasoning that underpins this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
Read the Arabic passage and note the explicit statement: 'لا يجوز التيمم إلا بصعيد ظاهر' – tayammum is not permissible except with pure earth. The condition is that the earth must be pure (tahir).
The word 'ظاهر' here means 'pure' (tahir), not 'apparent' – it is a synonym for 'طيب'.
2
Find the primary proof from Quran
The passage quotes the verse: 'فَتَيَمَّمُوا صَعِيدًا طَيِّبًا' (al-Ma'idah 5:6). Identify the key word 'طَيِّبًا' and understand how it imposes the condition of purity.
In Hanafi usul, the adjective 'tayyib' is a qayd (restriction) on the noun 'sa'eed'.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'tayammamu' indicates the act, and the description 'tayyiban' sets a condition. Since 'tayyib' means pure, the earth used must be free from ritual impurity (najasah).
The word 'tayyib' appears in other contexts meaning 'lawful' or 'good', but here it is understood as 'pure' by consensus.
4
Examine the secondary proof from hadith
The hadith of Abu Dharr states: 'إن الصعيد الطيب طهور' – clean earth is purifying. This reinforces that earth must be pure to be valid for tayammum.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud and is considered authentic (sahih) by Hanafi scholars.
5
Appreciate the logical inference
The passage adds a logical point: if earth is used to purify others, it must itself be pure. This is a principle of necessity – something impure cannot impart purity.
This is an istidlal (rational inference) that complements the textual proofs.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the scope of a single tayammum. They support this with athar from several Companions who said 'he performs tayammum for every prayer.' The Hanafi response is implicit in the text: the athar are interpreted as recommending rather than obligating, and the stronger evidence (Quran and hadith) supports the permissibility of multiple prayers.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tayammum replaces wudu fully; the non-relative's fear of missing the funeral creates genuine necessity.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri addresses a specific scenario: a healthy resident who is not the wali (guardian) of the deceased fears that performing wudu will cause him to miss the funeral prayer. The Hanafi school permits tayammum in this case, while Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees. Understanding the underlying principles—tayammum as a complete substitute and the irreplaceability of the funeral for a non-relative—is essential to grasp the ruling.

The hadith of Ibn 'Umar is interpreted as referring to wudu when possible, but tayammum is a valid purification in necessity.
The athar (report) from Ibn 'Abbas directly supports the Hanafi view.

The practical difference: Hanafi allows one tayammum for multiple prayers, including the funeral; Shafi'i requires a separate tayammum for each fard prayer and does not allow tayammum for the funeral if water is available but time is short.
The core issue is whether tayammum is a full substitute and whether the funeral prayer is irreplaceable for the non-relative.

Whoever begins wiping while he is a traveler then becomes a resident: if he wiped for a day and a night or more, it is necessary for him to remove his khuffs; if less than that, he completes wiping for a day and a night.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement from the matn. The passage explains the scenario where a person begins wiping over leather socks (khuff) while in a state of travel, which gives him the extended wipe duration of three days and three nights. If he then becomes a resident (muqim) before completing a full day and night of wiping, he must complete the resident's wipe duration of one day and one night from the start. However, if he had already wiped for a full day and night or more while traveling, the resident status triggers an obligation to remove the khuffs and wash the feet, because the traveler's wipe period has effectively concluded. The key Hanafi principle is that the legal effect (sariyan) of the traveler's duration can be altered only before the complete day and night passes; after that, the obligation is fixed. This ruling is consistent across the Hanafi school, with no significant ikhtilaf mentioned here.

Key Terms
مسح (mash): Wiping the feet over leather socks (khuff) with wet hands as a substitute for washing in wudu.
خف (khuff): Leather socks that cover the feet up to the ankles, commonly worn over the bare foot during wudu.
مسافر (musafir): A traveler who has set out on a journey of at least three days (approx. 48 miles or 77 km) and intends to stay less than 15 days at the destination.

Wiping over Jurmooq Worn over Khuff
Ruling on wiping over an outer sock (jurmooq) on top of khuff

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wiping over the jurmooq, identify the proof (hadith), and explain the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i objection.

Core Idea
Wiping over an outer sock (jurmooq) is valid because the two layers function as a single double-layered khuff, as proven by the Prophet's practice.

This lesson examines a practical ruling for wudu: when you wear an extra protective sock (jurmooq) over your leather khuff, can you wipe over the outer sock? The Hanafi school answers yes, based on a direct hadith and a reasoning that the two socks constitute one unit. We will explore the proof, the condition, and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wiping over the jurmooq when worn over the khuff. The hukm is permissible (ja'iz) and sunnah muakkadah.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Anas ibn Malik: the Prophet wiped over the muqayn (jurmooq) and khimar.
This hadith is from Bayhaqi and Abu Dawood.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'yamsahu ala al-muqayn' directly indicates the action, establishing permissibility.
4
Note the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i rejects it via qiyas: the jurmooq is a replacement of a replacement.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The two socks together form a double-layered sock, and the hadith overrides the analogy.

Whoever wears a jurmooq over the khuff wipes over it. [92] (10) He who wears a jurmooq over the sock will wipe over it. Jurmooq or mooq is a sock worn over the good sock for its protection. So both are socks. Therefore he can wipe over the jurmooq. The conditions for wiping over jurmooq are the same as for wiping over khuff. (2) There is a hadith: from Anas ibn Malik that the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) used to wipe over the muqayn (jurmooq) and the khimar (turban). (Sunan Bayhaqi, Bab al-Mash 'ala al-Muqayn, vol 1 p 432 no 1368; Abu Dawood, Bab al-Mash 'ala al-Khuffayn, p 23 no 153) This hadith proves that he wiped over jurmooq. Imam Shafi'i considers wiping over jurmooq impermissible. He says: the replacement for the foot is the khuff, and making the jurmooq a replacement for the khuff is a replacement of a replacement, which is not valid. We reply that both socks together become a single double-layered sock, so it is permissible. Jurmooq and mooq: a sock worn over another sock for protection is called jurmooq or mooq.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with its proof. The Arabic matn states that if someone wears a jurmooq (an outer protective sock) over the leather khuff, he may wipe over the jurmooq during wudu. The Urdu commentary explains that the jurmooq is essentially an additional layer, so the combination functions like a single double-layered khuff, thus wiping over it is valid under the same conditions as wiping over a standard khuff. The hadith of Anas ibn Malik (and the citation from Bayhaqi and Abu Dawood) is presented as evidence that the Prophet (ﷺ) wiped over the muqayn (another term for jurmooq) and over his turban (khimar), establishing the practice. The commentary then addresses an inter-madhhab dispute: Imam Shafi'i rejects the validity because he considers the jurmooq a 'replacement of a replacement,' arguing that only the khuff (the direct replacement for the foot) can be wiped over. The Hanafi response is that the two socks together form one unit, effectively a thicker sock, so wiping over the outer one is permissible. This ruling is part of the Hanafi school's position, and the proof from hadith overrides the analogy-based objection.

Key Terms
جرموق (jurmooq): An outer sock worn over the khuff for protection; also called muq or muqayn.
موق (muq / mooq): Synonym for jurmooq; an outer sock worn over the khuff.
موقين (muqayn): Dual form of muq, referring to a pair of outer socks.
