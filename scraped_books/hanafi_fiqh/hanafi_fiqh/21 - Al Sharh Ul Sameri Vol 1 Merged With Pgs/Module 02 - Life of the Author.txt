Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key details of Maulana Mir al-Din Qasimi's scholarly journey and explain why his biography matters for studying his commentary on al-Quduri.

Core Idea
A scholar's training and lineage establish the authority of his work.

This biography reveals that Maulana Mir al-Din Qasimi, despite humble beginnings, received advanced training at Dar al-Ulum Deoband under renowned hadith masters. Understanding his scholarly journey helps us appreciate the depth of his sharh on al-Quduri and the tradition he represents.

The birth date is approximate because recording was not customary; this is common in rural South Asian settings.
3

Follow the author's journey from local maktab to Dar al-Ulum Deoband. Note the institutions and the progression in complexity of subjects.
The author studied in multiple madrasas across Bihar, Gujarat, and finally Deoband, indicating a broad training.
4
Identify the hadith teachers and their significance
Recognize the names of the teachers for each major hadith collection. These teachers were senior scholars of the Deobandi tradition, lending authority to the author's transmission.
Teachers like Fakhr al-Din, Fakhr al-Hasan, Abd al-Ahad, and Husayn Ahmad Bahari were prominent Hanafi scholars.
5
Reflect on how background lends authority
Consider how the author's training under these 'mountains of knowledge' makes his commentary a reliable source for Hanafi fiqh.
The commentary inherits the chain of knowledge (sanad) from these masters to the author.

The commentary al-Thamari, part one, 9. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Conditions of the commentator. From Hadhrat Maulana Mir al-Din Qasimi sahib, may his blessings endure, by (Hadhrat Maulana) Abd al-Aziz Qasimi (sahib), rector of Jami'ah Rawdat al-Ulum, Banka district, Godda, Jharkhand, India. We praise Him and send blessings upon His noble Messenger. Year of birth. Conditions of the commentator: Hadhrat Maulana Mir al-Din sahib was born on 6 November 1950, corresponding to 25 Muharram 1370 AH. This date is not verified because there was no custom of recording dates at home. However, this is approximately the date. It has been recorded on the certificate and passport. Birthplace: He was born in Khti village, police station Meh Gaon, district Godda, province Jharkhand. This province was formerly part of Bihar; now it has been separated and made Jharkhand. This village is far from the cities of Bhagalpur and Godda in the countryside, where even now there are no facilities of electricity, water, and road. Lineage: Name: Thamir al-Din. Father's name: Jamal al-Din. Grandfather's name: Muhammad Bakhsh, known as Ladni. Great-grandfather's name: Chulahai. Community: Sheikh Siddiqi. Much later, their lineage connects to Hadhrat Abu Bakr Siddiq, may Allah be pleased with him. Therefore, this family is called Sheikh Siddiqi. There is no formal genealogy, but this is well-known in their family. Education: He received primary education in the maktab of Khti village from Mawlawi Abd al-Ra'uf, known as Goni, of Murghia Chak, district Bhagalpur. In the same maktab, he studied Urdu, Hindi, arithmetic, and Persian. At the age of twelve, in 1947, he went to Madrasah Imdad al-Ulum, Banka, for education. In 1964, he enrolled in Madrasah I'zaziyyah, Nathnagar, Bhagalpur. In 1966, he went to Dar al-Ulum Chhaphi, Gujarat. And in 1978, he enrolled for higher education at the center of knowledge and gnosis, Dar al-Ulum Deoband. In Sha'ban 1390 AH, corresponding to October 1970, he completed the Daurah Hadith (course of hadith). He studied Sahih al-Bukhari from Hadhrat Allamah Fakhr al-Din sahib, may Allah have mercy on him; Jami' al-Tirmidhi from Hadhrat Maulana Fakhr al-Hasan sahib Gangohi; Sunan Abi Dawud from Hadhrat Maulana Abd al-Ahad sahib; Sahih Muslim from Hadhrat Maulana Sharif sahib; and Sharh Ma'ani al-Athar (Tahawi) from Hadhrat Maulana Husayn Ahmad Bahari sahib. These personalities were the mountains of knowledge of that time, from whom he benefited and completed his studies.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical sketch of the author, Maulana Mir al-Din Qasimi, written by Maulana Abd al-Aziz Qasimi. It details his birth on November 6, 1950 (25 Muharram 1370 AH) in a remote village in Jharkhand, India. His lineage traces back to the first Caliph Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, hence the family is known as Sheikh Siddiqi. The biography then describes his educational journey: from local maktab studies in Urdu, Hindi, arithmetic, and Persian, to attending various madrasas in Bihar and Gujarat, and finally enrolling at Dar al-Ulum Deoband in 1978. He completed the Daurah Hadith in October 1970, studying major hadith collections (Bukhari, Muslim, Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, and Tahawi) under prominent scholars of the time, described as 'mountains of knowledge.' This biography establishes the author's credentials as a scholar trained in the Deobandi tradition, which gives authority to his sharh of al-Quduri. Notably, the birth date is approximate, and the village lacked modern amenities even at the time of writing, highlighting the author's humble beginnings.

Key Terms
دورہ حدیث (daurah hadith): The final year course in a traditional Islamic seminary specializing in the study of the six canonical hadith collections (al-Kutub al-Sittah).
مکتب (maktab): A traditional elementary school attached to a mosque or home, teaching basic literacy and religious subjects.
شجرہ (shajarah): A genealogical tree or lineage document, often oral tradition in this context.

Core Idea
Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar is a core Hanafi text, and his scholarly chain ensures its reliability.

Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar al-Quduri is one of the most widely studied Hanafi fiqh manuals. Understanding the biography of its author helps us appreciate the authority of the text. This lesson covers his name, lineage, birth, the scholarly debate over his kunya, the meaning of his nisbah, and his distinguished chain of teachers reaching back to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

The Life and Legacy of Imam al-Quduri (Mukhtasar al-Quduri)
Biographical introduction to Imam al-Quduri

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to summarize the life, scholarly chain, juristic rank, and major works of Imam al-Quduri.

Core Idea
Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar became the backbone of Hanafi fiqh, bridging the foundational era to later codification.

Imam al-Quduri’s concise manual, Mukhtasar al-Quduri, is one of the most widely studied texts in the Hanafi madrasa tradition. But who was the author? This lesson explores his name, lineage, education, scholarly endorsements, and the debate over his juristic stratum. Understanding his background is key to appreciating the authority of his text.

Guided Path
1
Identify his full name and nisbah
Locate the Arabic name sequence and understand the meaning and possible origins of the nisbah 'al-Quduri' (pot-making or a village name).
The nisbah is explained with two possibilities.
2
Trace his scholarly chain
Follow the five-intermediary chain from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani to al-Quduri. Note each link and their significance.
This chain establishes the authenticity of his fiqh transmission.
3
Note the endorsements from leading scholars
Read the statements of al-Khatib al-Baghdadi and al-Sam'ani praising his trustworthiness, fiqh leadership, and constant Qur'an recitation.
These endorsements confirm his reliability as a narrator and jurist.
4
Understand the debate on his juristic stratum
Identify the two competing classifications by Ibn Kamal Pasha (fifth stratum) and most scholars (third stratum), and grasp the reasoning behind each.
The prevalent view places him in the third stratum.
5
List his major works
Mention al-Tajrid, Masa'il al-Khilaf, and al-Taqrib, and briefly note the focus of each work.
These works show his engagement with both inter-madhhab and intra-madhhab issues.

Al-Sharh al-Thamiri, Part One. Page 18. Conditions of Imam al-Quduri. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Conditions of the author of al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri. Name and lineage: His name is Ahmad, his kunya is Abu al-Hasan, al-Quduri, attributed to a village or his profession. His father's name is Muhammad. The lineage tree is: Abu al-Hasan Ahmad bin Abi Bakr Muhammad bin Ahmad bin Ja'far bin Hamdan al-Baghdadi al-Quduri. Birth year and birthplace: He was born in 362 AH and his birthplace is the city of Baghdad. Investigation of the kunya: In [most?] copies of al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri, his kunya is written as Abu al-Sinn. But the correct view is that his kunya is Abu al-Husayn. This is mentioned in Tarikh Ibn Khallikan, Madinat al-Ulum, and Ansab al-Sam'ani. Investigation of the nisbah al-Quduri: The historian Ibn Khallikan wrote in his Tarikh Wafayat al-A'yan that al-Quduri, with qaf and dal with damma and waw with sukun, is the plural of qidr, which means pot. But the reason for the attribution to al-Quduri is not known. The author of Madinat al-Ulum says that al-Quduri means pot-making. They attribute al-Quduri to this because his family used to make pots or buy and sell them. Therefore, they attributed him to qidr and called him al-Quduri. Or they were inhabitants of a village named Qidr, and were attributed to that village and called al-Quduri. Education: Imam al-Quduri acquired knowledge of fiqh and hadith from Rukn al-Islam Abu Abdillah Hamid bin Yahya bin Mahdi al-Jurjani (d. 398 AH), who was a student of Imam Abu Bakr Ahmad al-Jassas. And Abu Bakr al-Jassas was the distinguished disciple of Abu al-Hasan Ubaydullah al-Karkhi. And Imam al-Karkhi was a student of Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i. And Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i was a beneficiary of Allamah Musa al-Razi. And Musa al-Razi was the esteemed son and scholarly protégé of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani al-Hanafi. Thus, Imam al-Quduri acquired fiqh from Imam Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) through five intermediaries. He narrated hadith from Muhammad bin Ali bin Suwayd and Abdullah bin Muhammad al-Jawshani. He had the honor of being a student of Abu Bakr Ahmad bin Ali bin Thabit al-Khatib al-Baghdadi (author of Tarikh), Qadi al-Qudat Abu Abdillah Muhammad bin Ali bin Muhammad al-Damghani, and Qadi Mufaddal bin Mas'ud bin Muhammad bin Yahya bin Abi al-Faraj al-Tanukhi (d. 432? AH). He acquired hadith from these mountains of knowledge. (new page) Al-Sharh al-Thamiri, Part One, page 19. Conditions of Imam al-Quduri. Endorsement of Imam al-Quduri: Al-Khatib al-Baghdadi says: I wrote hadith from him. He was truthful (saduq) and narrated few hadith. Imam al-Sam'ani says about him: [He was] a jurist, truthful; the leadership of the followers of Abu Hanifa in Iraq ended with him. His worth was esteemed among them and his rank elevated. He was eloquent in speech in argumentation. He was constantly reciting the Qur'an. Translation in Urdu: He was a jurist and truthful. Because of him, the Hanafi school reached perfection in Iraq. He had great status and honor. His speech and writing were very charming. He always recited the Qur'an. Qadi Abu Muhammad praised him emphatically in Tabaqat al-Fuqaha. Juristic rank: Ibn Kamal Pasha counted him and the author of al-Hidayah among the fifth stratum, i.e., the people of tarjih. But most scholars objected to this, saying that Imam al-Quduri is greater than Qadi Khan and others, and if not greater, then at least equal. They count him in the third stratum of jurists. Therefore, Imam al-Quduri should also be counted in the third rank. Thus, the prevalent view is that Imam al-Quduri is among the third stratum of jurists. The humble one (the author) investigated every issue of al-Quduri and found that most of his issues are derived from a verse, hadith, statement of a Companion, or fatwa of a Tabi'i. This shows his high scholarly standing and deep understanding of fiqh. Demise and death: Imam al-Quduri died on the 5th of Rajab 428 AH in the city of Baghdad at the age of 66. On the same day, he was buried in the area of Darb Abi Khalaf in Baghdad. Later, his body was moved to the direction of Shari' Mansur. At that time, he rests beside Abu Bakr al-Khwarizmi. The chronogram of his death: 'Lami' al-Nur' [= 428] – may Allah's vast mercy be upon him. Works: He left behind many books as legacy, among which are: (1) Al-Tajrid – in seven volumes, in which he conducted rigorous research on the disputed issues between the Hanafis and Shafi'is. He began its dictation in 205 AH? (2) Masa'il al-Khilaf – mentions the subsidiary differences between the Imam (Abu Hanifa) and his companions, but does not include their proofs. (3) Al-Taqrib – in which he mentions issues along with their proofs.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Imam al-Quduri, the author of Mukhtasar al-Quduri, a foundational text in the Hanafi madhhab. The text is in Urdu and covers his name, lineage, birth in 362 AH in Baghdad, a clarification of his correct kunya (Abu al-Husayn, not Abu al-Sinn), and various theories about his nisbah (attribution to pot-making or a village). It then details his scholarly chain: he received fiqh through five intermediaries from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, and hadith from several major scholars including al-Khatib al-Baghdadi. The passage also presents endorsements from al-Khatib al-Baghdadi and al-Sam'ani, praising him as a truthful jurist and the leader of the Hanafis in Iraq. A significant section discusses his juristic rank: while Ibn Kamal Pasha placed him in the fifth stratum (people of tarjih), most scholars argue he belongs to the third stratum, equal to or higher than Qadi Khan. The compiler remarks that his rulings are rooted in primary sources. Finally, it records his death in 428 AH and lists three major works: al-Tajrid (a comparative work on Hanafi-Shafi'i disputes), Masa'il al-Khilaf (on internal Hanafi differences), and al-Taqrib (a concise work with proofs).

Key Terms
قدوری (al-Qudūrī): A nisbah indicating either a family profession of pot-making (qidr) or a village named Qidr, used to identify Imam Ahmad al-Quduri.
مختصر القدوری (Mukhtaṣar al-Qudūrī): The concise legal manual by Imam al-Quduri, covering the most relied-upon Hanafi rulings.
طبقہ خامسہ (Ṭabaqah Khāmisah): The fifth stratum of Hanafi jurists according to Ibn Kamal Pasha, consisting of those capable of tarjih (preferring one view over another).
صدوق (Ṣadūq): A term of hadith criticism meaning 'truthful' or 'trustworthy', used to describe Imam al-Quduri as a reliable narrator.

Imam al-Quduri: Biography and Scholarly Rank
Biography of Imam al-Quduri and his scholarly contributions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical facts of Imam al-Quduri, identify his juristic rank according to the majority of scholars, and explain why his Mukhtasar is foundational for Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar became the base text for al-Hidayah, making it the most studied Hanafi matn.

Imam al-Quduri is a towering figure in the Hanafi school. His concise manual, al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri, has been taught in Hanafi madrasas for centuries and was selected by al-Marghinani as the foundation for his magnum opus, al-Hidayah. Understanding al-Quduri's biographical background and his juristic rank helps us appreciate the authority of his text and the structure of Hanafi curriculum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sources of authentication
Note the scholars who praised al-Quduri: al-Khatib al-Baghdadi (saduq), al-Sam'ani (faqih saduq), and Qadi Abu Muhammad. Understand the meaning of 'saduq' as a term of hadith criticism indicating trustworthiness.
This establishes al-Quduri's reliability as a narrator and jurist.
2
Determine his juristic rank
Ibn Kamal Pasha placed him in the fifth class (ashab al-tarjih), but the majority of scholars consider him in the third class (mujtahid of mas'alah), equal or superior to Qadi Khan. Learn the Hanafi tabaqat classification system.
The majority view is that he belongs to the third class, meaning he could derive rulings within the madhhab.
3
Memorize his key works
List the works mentioned: Tajrid (7 vols., comparative with Shafi'is), Masa'il al-Khilaf (differences with Abu Hanifa's companions), Taqrib (with proofs), Sharh Mukhtasar al-Karkhi, Sharh Adab al-Qadi, and most importantly al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri.
Al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri is the most famous and widely used.
4
Understand the significance of al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri
Note that it was chosen by the author of al-Hidayah as the base text, resulting in about 32 commentaries. Most rulings are derived from Quran, hadith, Companion sayings, or Tabi'i fatwas, not qiyas. It is comprehensive yet easy.
This text is the cornerstone of Hanafi fiqh education.
5
Note his death date and burial
He died on 5 Rajab 428 AH in Baghdad at age 66, initially buried in Darb Abi Khalaf, later moved to Mansur quarter next to Abu Bakr al-Khwarizmi.
The chronogram 'Lami' al-Nur' encodes the year.

Definition of Hanafiyat: The Collective School of Three Imams
Defining Hanafiyat and defending its methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Hanafiyat as the collective school of three imams and explain why following any of them is considered acting within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Hanafiyat is the collective school of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, not just Abu Hanifa alone.

Many assume the Hanafi school refers only to the rulings of Imam Abu Hanifa. However, this passage clarifies that Hanafiyat encompasses the positions of three foundational imams: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this breadth is crucial for correctly navigating Hanafi fiqh and fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of Hanafiyat
Locate where the author defines Hanafiyat. Note that it is explicitly stated as the collective (مجموعہ) of the schools of three imams, not just one.
The author emphasizes that acting on any of the three positions is considered Hanafi.
2
Understand the role of the Sahibayn
Recognize that Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the sahibayn) are integral to the school. Their positions are recorded in major texts like al-Quduri and al-Hidayah, and fatwa may be given according to them.
The sahibayn sometimes differed from Abu Hanifa based on hadith.
3
Analyze Imam Abu Hanifa's cautious methodology
Note the reasons given: his piety, the fact that he was the first to codify fiqh, and his reliance on cautious positions (often from Companions and Successors) to avoid criticism.
This caution explains why his positions sometimes appear weaker in hadith compared to the sahibayn.
4
Address the objection about Hanafiyat contradicting hadith
The author argues that critics focus only on Abu Hanifa's positions, ignoring that the sahibayn's differing positions are firmly rooted in authentic hadith. Thus, Hanafiyat as a whole is not contradictory.
The author notes that in such cases, the sahibayn may agree with Shafi'i and Maliki schools.
5
Synthesize the structure of Hanafiyat
Hanafiyat is a composite school where multiple valid positions coexist. The Deoband school later favored the cautious (Abu Hanifa) position, but the sahibayn's views remain part of the school.
This flexibility is a key feature of the Hanafi tradition.

ہے۔
تفصیل حالت مصنف میں ملاحظہ فرمائیں۔
Known as al-Quduri, may Allah have mercy on him. 29. The Book of Purity. From 'al-Shaykh' to the end, the expression is from one of his students. Otherwise, the author, out of humility, would only write his own name. Al-Quduri: 'Qidr' means a pot. He was called al-Quduri either because he dealt in pots or because of a village name to which the author is attributed. See details in the biography of the author.
Source pages p. 34

Commentary
This passage provides biographical and textual information about the author of the matn, Imam al-Quduri. The commentator first notes that the phrase 'al-Shaykh' (the Sheikh) and subsequent text up to the end of the introduction are actually from the author's student, not from al-Quduri himself. Out of humility, the author would only write his own name without honorifics. Then the commentator explains the name 'al-Quduri': it may derive from 'qidr' (a pot), indicating the author was either a pot seller or came from a village named Qudur. This is a common practice in nisbas (attributions). The passage serves to clarify the transmission of the text and the author's background, which is important for understanding the chain of narration in the Hanafi tradition.

Key Terms
الشيخ (al-shaykh): An older, respected person; in this context, it refers to the author al-Quduri himself, but the text notes that the phrase is inserted by a student.
القدوری (al-Quduri): The nisba of Imam Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri, either referring to a profession (pot seller) or a village.
كتاب الطهارة (kitab al-taharah): The Book of Purity, the first substantive section of the Mukhtasar al-Quduri.

Key Takeaway
The nisba 'al-Quduri' has two possible origins, and the honorifics before his name were likely added by a student, reflecting the author's humility and the layered transmission of Hanafi texts.
