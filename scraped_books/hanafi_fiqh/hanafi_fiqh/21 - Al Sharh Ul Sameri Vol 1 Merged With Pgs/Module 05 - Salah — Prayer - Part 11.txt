Praying on Top of the Kaaba: Validity, Dislike, and the Shafi'i Disagreement
Ruling on praying on top of the Kaaba, its proof, and the Shafi'i disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying on top of the Kaaba, explain the proof, and contrast the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Praying on the Kaaba is valid but disliked; prohibition does not automatically invalidate.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text tackles a specific question: is prayer on top of the Kaaba valid? The author states the Hanafi ruling, provides a hadith proof, and addresses the Shafi'i disagreement. By working through this lesson, you will understand the Hanafi approach to interpreting prohibitions and the concept of muhadhat in defining the qibla.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: prayer on top of the Kaaba is valid (sahih) but disliked (makruh). The valid status is due to the qibla being the space (muhadhat) in front of the Kaaba.
The dislike is tanzihi, not tahrimi.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the prayer is invalid (batil) because the Prophet's prohibition implies nullity.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i argument relies on the same hadith: the Prophet forbade praying in seven places, including on top of the Kaaba. They interpret 'forbid' as rendering the prayer void.
Hadith from Ibn Umar in Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafis argue that a prohibition (nahy) does not necessarily indicate invalidity; it may indicate dislike (karoohah tanzihiyyah). Additionally, the qibla condition is satisfied because the space (muhadhat) serves as the direction.
The airplane analogy clarifies that the wall is not required.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Hanafis consider the prayer valid (though disliked, so reward may be less). Shafi'is require the prayer to be repeated if one had prayed on top.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on praying on top of the Kaaba, along with the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i. The Hanafi school holds that such prayer is valid but makruh (disliked). The reasoning is that the qibla is defined by the space (muhadhat) opposite the Kaaba, not necessarily the physical structure. Thus, on the roof, the space in front is the qibla, so the prayer is valid. The proof is the hadith forbidding prayer on the roof of the Kaaba among other places; the Hanafis interpret the prohibition as indicating dislike (tanzih) rather than invalidity. They compare it to praying in an airplane, where the wall is not in front but the space suffices. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the prohibition implies invalidity. The Hanafi response is that a prohibition (nahy) does not automatically make the prayer void; it may be for respect. The footnote lists the seven forbidden places from the hadith.

Key Terms
مكروه (makruh): Disliked; an act that is not sinful but against the preferred practice.
محاذات (muhadhat): The space directly in front of or above a structure; used to define the qibla when the structure is not directly facing.
فائده (fa'idah): A beneficial note or additional point.

Ruling on Praying on Top of the Kaaba
Determines the ruling of praying on Kaaba roof and notes Shafi'i disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying on the Kaaba roof and explain why the prayer is valid despite the hadith prohibition.

Core Idea
Praying on the Kaaba roof is valid but makruh — the hadith's 'prohibition' indicates mere dislike, not nullification.

This passage addresses a specific issue: the validity of prayer performed on top of the Kaaba. The author cites a hadith listing seven disliked places for prayer, including the Kaaba roof. From this, he derives the Hanafi ruling that such prayer is disliked (makruh) but still valid. He then notes the Shafi'i position that the prayer is invalid. Understanding this ruling clarifies how the Hanafi school approaches hadith prohibitions and distinguishes between actions that nullify prayer and those that merely reduce reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its list
The passage references a hadith from Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah where the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbids prayer in seven places: rubbish dump, camel slaughtering place, cemetery, road, bathroom, camel resting place, and roof of the Kaaba. First, locate the hadith and note each place.
The hadith is classified as ahad (single narration), so its proof value is zanni.
2
Analyze the word 'prohibition' (نھی)
In Hanafi usul, the imperative or prohibition (nahy) does not automatically imply invalidity. The word 'نهى' is interpreted as karaah (dislike) unless external evidence raises it to tahrim. Apply this principle to the hadith.
Compare with Shafi'i usul, where nahy often indicates prohibition that nullifies the act.
3
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi position: prayer on the Kaaba roof is makruh tanzih (lightly disliked). If performed, the prayer is valid and does not require repetition. The dislike is due to the sacredness of the place combined with the hadith's warning.
Makruh tanzih reduces reward but does not incur sin or invalidate the act.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam Shafi'i holds that the same hadith renders the prayer invalid. His school interprets the prohibition as absolute, meaning the prayer is not accepted. Understand the basis of this disagreement.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that a Hanafi may pray there, while a Shafi'i would not.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
The author explicitly states the Hanafi position and does not adopt the Shafi'i view. Therefore, the relied-upon ruling for fatwa is that the prayer is valid but makruh.
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and remains the operative position.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The author begins by citing a hadith that lists the roof of the Kaaba among the places where prayer is disliked. From this hadith, he extracts the Hanafi ruling: praying on the Kaaba roof is makruh (disliked), but if one does so, the prayer is valid because the dislike does not reach the level of prohibitive (tahrimi). The 'Benefit' (Faaidah) notes that Imam Shafi'i considers such prayer invalid entirely, based on the same hadith; they interpret the dislike as indicating prohibition and hence invalidity. The footnote then lists seven prohibited prayer places as narrated from the Prophet (peace be upon him): rubbish dumps, camel slaughtering places, graveyards, roads, bathrooms, camel resting places, and the roof of the Kaaba. The Hanafi position on these places differs: prayer in graveyards and roads is also valid but disliked, while in bathrooms and rubbish dumps it is makruh tahrimi closer to prohibition. However, the key point here is that the Kaaba roof is not a place where prayer is completely invalid; it is merely disliked.

Key Terms
مكروه (makruh): An action that is disliked but not forbidden; performing it does not invalidate the act (prayer) but reduces reward.

Rulings on Actions That Do Not Break the Fast and Ejaculation from Kissing
Lists actions that do not break fast and rules on ejaculation from kissing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which actions do not break the fast according to the Hanafi school, explain the ruling on ejaculation from kissing or touching, and cite the supporting proofs.

Core Idea
The fast breaks only by entry or intercourse; ejaculation from kissing requires qada but no kaffarah.

In this passage, the author lists several actions that a fasting person may perform without breaking the fast, including ihtilam, cupping, kohl, kissing, and looking. Then he addresses the specific case of ejaculation from kissing or touching, ruling that qada is obligatory but no kaffarah. The commentary provides the general principle and the textual evidence. Let's examine each ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
Read the list of actions: ihtilam, looking with ejaculation, applying oil, cupping, applying kohl, kissing without ejaculation. Note that these are all acts where nothing enters the body and no intercourse occurs.
The Urdu commentary explains that the fast breaks only by entry or intercourse.
2
Identify the Ruling for Each Act
For each listed action, the ruling is that the fast remains valid (sahih). The author says 'لم يفطر' (does not break fast).
This is the Hanafi position; some schools differ on cupping and kohl.
3
Identify the Proofs (Dalil)
The commentary cites several hadith and athar: (1) Ibn Abbas and Ikrimah: 'الصوم مما دخل وليس مما خرج' (fasting is about what enters, not what exits). (2) Hadith: 'لا يفطر من قاء ولا من احتلم ولا من احتجم'. (3) Aisha on kohl and kissing. Understand how each proof supports the ruling.
These are all ahad hadith, so zanni al-thubut, but accepted in the school.
4
Analyze the Exception: Ejaculation from Kissing
Now focus on the second part: if ejaculation occurs from kissing or touching, the fast is broken. The author says 'فعليه القضاء ولا كفارة عليه' (qada is upon him, no kaffarah). Understand why no kaffarah: because complete intercourse did not occur, and kaffarah drops with doubts.
The comparable case is full intercourse which requires both qada and kaffarah.
5
Synthesize the Principle
Conclude that the fast is only broken by intentional entry of something into the body or by intercourse (complete or with ejaculation from direct contact). Actions that do not involve entry or complete intercourse do not break the fast, even if they cause ejaculation (like looking) – but kissing/touching with ejaculation is an exception because it resembles intercourse.
This distinction is based on the hadith of Maimuna and the athar of Qatada.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kissing while fasting is not inherently prohibited; the ruling depends on your ability to control desire.

Fasting is a spiritual and physical discipline, and acts of intimacy during fasting are naturally governed by caution. The Hanafi school, relying on a specific prophetic differentiation, teaches that kissing one's spouse while fasting is neither unconditionally permissible nor unconditionally prohibited. The decisive factor is the person's self-control. This lesson unpacks that nuanced ruling and its evidence.

The condition is not about age per se, but about one's assessment of their own control.

The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in fiqh.

The commentary warns that if kissing leads to intercourse, qada and kaffarah become necessary. This shows that the ruling of makruh is preventive: it becomes haram if it actually causes nullification.
The ruling of makruh is not because kissing itself is bad, but because the risk is high.

Involuntary vomiting does not break the fast; intentional vomiting requires qada only if mouthful.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: any exit without excuse invalidates i'tikaf. Sahibayn: only if over half a day.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two eminent disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The issue is whether a person performing i'tikaf invalidates it by leaving the mosque briefly without a valid excuse. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to see how different usul principles—strict qiyas versus consideration of hardship (haraj)—can lead to different rulings within the same school.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Identify the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: any departure from the mosque without a valid excuse, even for a short time, invalidates i'tikaf.
He considers staying in the mosque (luzum al-masjid) a necessary condition.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's position
State the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani: i'tikaf is not invalidated unless the absence exceeds half a day.
They set a specific time threshold.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Explain their reasoning: invalidating i'tikaf for a short absence would cause hardship (haraj), as minor exits are inevitable.
They consider the practical difficulty of strict adherence.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa argues that i'tikaf is by definition staying in the mosque; leaving breaks it. The hadith permits only unavoidable exits, not voluntary ones.
His proof is based on qiyas and the literal requirement of seclusion.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, even a 5-minute leave without excuse voids i'tikaf; under Sahibayn, a short leave is excused.
Later Hanafi fatwa often follows Sahibayn for ease, but both views are valid.

Vowed I'tikaf: Days Include Nights and Must Be Consecutive
Ruling on vowed i'tikaf: nights included, consecutiveness mandatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a vowed i'tikaf for days requires inclusion of nights and consecutiveness, and explain the proof from the athar of 'Ata and the distinction from fasting.

Core Idea
When you vow i'tikaf for days, the nights are automatically included and must be consecutive—whether you said so or not.

This lesson examines a concise but significant ruling from the Hanafi school on vowed (nadhr) i'tikaf. The matn declares that if someone obligates i'tikaf for a number of days, those days include their nights and must be performed consecutively, even if consecutiveness was not stipulated. The commentary unpacks the reasoning by contrasting i'tikaf with fasting, and provides a textual proof from the practice of the early Muslims. Understanding this ruling clarifies the nature of i'tikaf as a continuous act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is a vowed i'tikaf for a specific number of days. The ruling: it becomes obligatory to perform i'tikaf for those days together with their nights, and to do so consecutively (mutatabi'ah) even without an explicit condition.
2
Understand the Reasoning: I'tikaf vs. Fasting
Fasting occurs only during daylight, so it can be performed non-consecutively. I'tikaf, however, requires staying in the mosque both day and night, so continuity is inherent.
This analogy explains why consecutiveness is required without stipulation.
3
Analyze the Dalil: Athar of 'Ata
The commentary cites an athar (report) from 'Ata ibn Abi Rabah: a mu'takif who stipulates i'tikaf by day only and returns to his family at night is told, 'This is not i'tikaf.' This proves that i'tikaf must include both day and night, making consecutiveness necessary.
The athar is from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, classified as mursal (zanni al-thubut) but relied upon in the madhhab.
4
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Some other schools allow non-consecutive i'tikaf for vowed days. The Hanafi position requires consecutiveness, as established by the nature of the act and the athar.
This is a practical difference: if a Hanafi misses a day, the i'tikaf is invalid and must be restarted.
5
Apply the Ruling to a Scenario
If someone vows 'I will perform i'tikaf for 10 days,' he must perform 10 consecutive days and nights, starting from the first day's night. He cannot break the consecutiveness even if he intended only daytime.
Example from the commentary: 6 days includes 6 nights, all consecutive.

Core Idea
The two rak'at after tawaf are wajib due to the Prophet's constant practice.

After completing the seven circuits of tawaf, the pilgrim prays two rak'at. This lesson examines the legal status of these two rak'at in the Hanafi school. While many Muslims consider them sunnah, the Hanafi school holds them as wajib based on clear evidence from the Quran and the Prophet's practice. Understanding this ruling clarifies the difference between wajib and sunnah in acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is praying two rak'at after tawaf. The Hanafi ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory).
Wajib is slightly less than fard but still required; omission without excuse is sinful.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the Quranic verse (2:125): 'And take the Maqam Ibrahim as a place of prayer.' Also the Prophet's continuous practice (muwazabah).

Hanafis interpret the imperative 'ittakhidhu' as indicating obligation (wajib). The Prophet's constant performance without omission confirms that the obligation is actual, not merely recommended.
The principle: al-amr yufidu al-wujub (command implies obligation) unless there is evidence to the contrary.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds it as sunnah (recommended), arguing that the imperative can be for recommendation and the Prophet's practice does not reach the level of wajib.
This disagreement stems from different usul principles on interpreting commands and the weight of continuous practice.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi because the Prophet never omitted these rak'at, and his constant practice (muwazabah) is strong evidence for wujub. The Quranic command reinforces this.
Tarjih is based on the strength of the proof and the school's established methodology.

Ruling on Cutting Nails of One Hand or Foot in Ihram
States the ruling and proof for cutting nails of one hand or foot.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for cutting nails of one hand or foot in ihram and identify the proof from the athar of al-Hasan and Ata.

Core Idea
Cutting a quarter of the nails (one hand or foot) in ihram requires a dam because a quarter is treated as the whole.

The ruling on trimming nails in ihram is nuanced. While breaking a nail allows trimming without penalty, intentionally cutting nails (even partially) incurs expiation. This passage clarifies that cutting the nails of one hand or one foot — a quarter of all nails — necessitates a dam, based on the principle that a quarter of a body part is equivalent to the full part for penalty purposes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act: a person in ihram cuts the nails of one hand or one foot. The ruling: a dam (blood sacrifice) is wajib.
The matn explicitly states: 'And if he cuts nails of a hand or a foot, then on him is a dam.'
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar from al-Hasan and Ata recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah. They distinguish between trimming a broken nail (no penalty) and cutting it before breakage (dam). This implies that any intentional nail-cutting in ihram requires dam.
The athar is a hadith mawquf (statement of a Companion or follower). It is zanni in both transmission and meaning.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase is 'if he trims it before it broke, then on him is a dam.' This shows that cutting an intact nail in ihram is a violation requiring expiation. The athar does not specify quantity, but the Hanafi school applies the principle that a quarter of a body part is equivalent to the whole.
The commentary explains: 'The principle in this issue is that a quarter is considered a substitute for the whole.'
4
Identify the Hanafi principle
The principle is that cutting a quarter of the nails (e.g., nails of one hand or one foot) is treated like cutting all nails. This is analogous to shaving a quarter of the head in ihram, which also requires dam.
This principle is derived from urf (custom) and istihsan (juristic preference).
5
Apply the ruling
If a pilgrim cuts the nails of one entire hand or one entire foot (i.e., five nails on a hand or five toes on a foot), a dam is wajib. If they cut less than a quarter (e.g., only a few nails), the ruling may require sadaqah or nothing, but the text focuses on the quarter threshold.
The passage does not address less than a quarter, but by implication, the penalty scales with the amount cut.

Muhrim Does Not Own Game Through Gift or Purchase
Ruling derived from hadith of Prophet returning wild donkey gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muhrim's ownership of game and derive it from the hadith of the wild donkey.

Core Idea
A Muhrim cannot own game, whether by gift or purchase, because the Prophet's refusal in ihram sets a binding precedent.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling concerning the state of ihram: the prohibition on owning game. The source is a hadith in which the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) returned a gift of a wild donkey solely because he was in ihram. This action provides a clear legal indication that a Muhrim does not acquire ownership of game, whether through gift or purchase. Let's walk through the evidence and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is a Muhrim accepting a gift of game or purchasing it. The Hanafi ruling is that this is prohibited (haram) and does not transfer ownership.
The ruling applies to both gift and purchase because both are means of acquiring ownership.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the hadith of al-Sa'b ibn Jaththamah, in which the Prophet returned a gifted wild donkey, explaining his action by saying, 'We did not return it to you except that we are in ihram.'
This hadith is found in Sahih al-Bukhari.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's refusal indicates that a Muhrim cannot accept ownership of game, since acceptance would normally transfer ownership. The explicit reason — ihram — makes this a legislative prohibition.
The reasoning extends to purchase by analogy: if gift does not transfer ownership, purchase also cannot.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may permit a Muhrim to accept game as a gift, but the Hanafi school holds it invalid.
The difference stems from how each school interprets the Prophet's action — whether it was a general prohibition or specific to the Prophet.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon because the Prophet's statement 'except that we are in ihram' is general in wording and applicable to all Muhrims.
This ruling is part of Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi position.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Umrah is either wajib or sunnah depending on the Hanafi authority, but all agree its pillars are ihram, tawaf, and sa‘i.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Thamiri addresses two essential questions about Umrah: Is it obligatory or simply recommended? And what are its essential components? The author presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, then clearly defines the three pillars (arkan) of Umrah, supporting them with authentic hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari. Understanding this lesson will clarify both the legal status and the structural requirements of Umrah according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act under discussion
Note that the passage discusses ‘Umrah (the lesser pilgrimage) as a distinct act of worship, separate from Hajj. Recognize that the first part addresses its ruling (hukm), while the second part defines its constituent elements.
Umrah can be performed at any time of the year.
2
State the intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling
Identify that Abu Hanifa holds Umrah to be wajib (obligatory) once in a lifetime, based on a hadith from Ibn ‘Abbas. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) consider it sunnah mu’akkadah (confirmed sunnah), based on other hadith. The text presents both views without tarjih.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
3
List the three pillars of Umrah
Extract from the text that Umrah consists of three pillars (arkan): (1) Ihram (entering the state of consecration), (2) Tawaf (seven circuits around the Kaaba), and (3) Sa‘i (seven trips between Safa and Marwah). These are the essential components without which Umrah is invalid.
The author emphasizes that Umrah is the combination of these three.
4
Analyze the hadith proofs for the pillars
The text cites two hadith from Bukhari: one from ‘A’ishah describing the actions of those in Umrah during the Farewell Pilgrimage, and another from Abu Shihab instructing the companions to become halal through tawaf and sa‘i. Both establish that tawaf and sa‘i are integral parts of Umrah.
The hadith also imply that ihram is the starting point.
5
Understand the practical consequence of the dispute
The difference in ruling (wajib vs sunnah) affects the intention: if one follows Abu Hanifa, performing Umrah fulfills an obligation; if one follows the Sahibayn, it is a highly rewarded sunnah. However, in both cases the pillars must be performed correctly. The mufta bihi (operative view) in later Hanafi fatwa often follows the Sahibayn, but the wajib view is also considered.
The text does not explicitly choose a tarjih.
