Ruling on Washing Visible and Non-Visible Impurities
Expands rules for removing visible and non-visible impurities with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for purifying visible and non-visible impurities, and apply the criterion of dominant assumption (ghālib al-ẓann) for non-visible impurities.

Core Idea
For visible impurities, remove the substance; for non-visible, wash until dominant assumption – three times is recommended, not required.

In this passage, the author explains the Hanafi method for purifying two types of impurities: those you can see and those you cannot. The key is that for visible impurities, the substance must be removed, but a trace of color is excused. For non-visible impurities, you must wash until you have a strong belief that the impurity is gone, and three washes are recommended based on hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of washing impurities from garments or body. The hukm is wajib (obligatory).
2
Distinguish visible vs non-visible impurities
Determine which category the impurity falls into: visible (e.g., blood, cow dung) or non-visible (e.g., dried urine, wine).
3
Locate the dalil for visible impurities
The hadith of Aisha (Abu Dawud) shows that if the trace remains after washing, it is excused. So for visible impurities, only the substance must be removed.
4
Locate the dalil for non-visible impurities
The hadith about washing hands after sleep (Muslim) commands three washes for a possible non-visible impurity. The Hanafi use this as proof that three washes are recommended, but the criterion is dominant assumption (ghālib al-ẓann).
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require a fixed number of three washes for all impurities. The Hanafi rely on dominant assumption and consider three as recommended, not obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the method of washing impurities from garments or body. The author begins by stating the ruling for visible impurities: they must be washed until the impurity itself is gone. However, if a stain or color remains that is difficult to remove, it is excused – the cloth becomes pure even if the trace remains. This is supported by the hadith where Aisha instructs to wash the blood and if the trace remains, to change it with yellow (saffron) – indicating that complete removal of color is not required. Then the passage addresses non-visible impurities (like dried urine or wine): one must wash until the washer has a dominant assumption (ghalabat al-zann) that the impurity has been removed. Scholars recommend washing three times, drawing an analogy from the hadith about washing hands after sleep, where the impurity is non-visible. The Hanafi position is that for non-visible impurities, there is no fixed number; the criterion is the dominant assumption. Three times is recommended because it usually suffices, but it is not obligatory. The footnote reinforces the same points with additional hadith details.

Key Terms
نجاست مرئیہ (najāsat marʾiyya): Impurity that is visible to the eye, such as blood or solid filth.
غالب گمان (ghālib al-ẓann): Dominant assumption or strong probability that the impurity has been removed, based on the washer's estimation.
نجاست غیر مرئیہ (najāsat ghayr marʾiyya): Impurity that is not visible, such as dried urine or wine on a garment.

Istinja: Sunnah, No Fixed Number of Stones – Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on istinja ruling with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istinja, explain why three stones are not obligatory, and compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the number of stones required.

Core Idea
In the Hanafi school, istinja is sunnah; the goal is cleanliness, not a set number of stones.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thumayri clarifies the Hanafi ruling on istinja (cleansing after using the toilet). It asserts that istinja is a confirmed sunnah, not an obligation, and that there is no fixed number of stones required – three stones are recommended but not prescribed. The author then presents the Shafi'i view that three stones are obligatory and responds to their proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its basis
Read the opening sentences: istinja is sunnah, stones suffice, no prescribed number. Understand that the ruling is not wajib but sunnah. Note the materials allowed: stones, clods, and anything similar.
The key phrase 'ليس فيه عدد مسنون' – there is no prescribed number.
2
Examine the Hanafi proofs
Look at the hadith from Aisha: the Prophet said to take three stones and they suffice (تجزئ). The word 'تجزئ' indicates sufficiency, not obligation. Also the hadith from Abu Hurairah: 'whoever uses odd numbers has done well, and whoever does not, there is no harm.' This shows optionality.
The Hanafi school uses the principle that commands of sufficiency are not obligatory.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that three stones are obligatory based on the hadith of Salman: the Prophet forbade using less than three stones. A prohibition indicates obligation in Shafi'i usul.
The dispute hinges on how to interpret the prohibition – as obligatory or recommended.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response interprets the hadith as a recommendation (istihbab) because the underlying reason is that three normally suffice, not because the number itself is required. The school emphasizes ease and the goal of cleanliness.
The Hanafi principle: when a command can be interpreted as either obligation or recommendation, the lighter interpretation is preferred.
5
Summarize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
In practice, a Hanafi can use two stones if the area is clean, while a Shafi'i must use at least three odd-numbered stones. This difference affects daily practice for Muslims following these schools.
Understanding this dispute helps avoid rigid application and respects madhhab diversity.

(130)(12) Istinja is sunnah; stone and clod and what stands in their place suffice; one wipes the area until it is clean, and there is no prescribed number. Explanation: Istinja is sunnah. Stone, clod, wood, and any item that cleans feces are all permissible. One should wipe the area enough times until it is clean; that will suffice. For urine, an absorbent item like clod is needed; wood does not suffice because it does not absorb urine. Using stone or clod will remove most but not complete purity; but if the impurity is within a dirham's width from the outlet, Shari'ah grants leniency due to human difficulty. However, it is better to use water for complete cleanliness. Here also the main objective is to remove impurity, no matter how many clods it takes; three is not obligatory. The proof for all this is the hadith from Aisha: 'When one of you goes to relieve himself, let him take with him three stones and clean himself with them, for they suffice him.' (Abu Dawud) This shows that stone and clod are sufficient for istinja. It also shows that three stones should be used because they usually bring about cleanliness; hence it said 'they suffice him.' (2) Also from Abu Hurairah: 'Whoever uses stones for istinja, let him use an odd number; whoever does well has done well, and whoever does not, there is no harm.' (Abu Dawud) Imam Shafi'i says that three stones are obligatory, and if three do not clean, then more stones should be used, but in odd numbers. His proof is the hadith from Salman: 'He forbade us from facing the qiblah while relieving, from using the right hand for istinja, from using less than three stones, and from using dung or bone.' (Muslim) We say: This is because three usually suffice or it is recommended (istihbab), not obligatory. al-madr: clod; yunqīhi: cleans it. Footnote: (a) The Prophet said: 'When one of you goes to relieve himself, let him take three stones and purify himself with them, for they suffice him.' (b) Salman was told: 'Your Prophet taught you everything, even how to relieve yourself.' He said: 'Yes; he forbade us from facing the qiblah while relieving, from using the right hand for istinja, from using less than three stones, and from using dung or bone.'

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute on the ruling of istinja (cleansing after using the toilet). The author first states the Hanafi ruling: istinja is sunnah, not wajib. It can be performed with stones, clods, or any similar solid absorbent material. One should wipe the area until it is clean; there is no fixed number of stones required. The passage then addresses the specific case of urine: absorbent materials like clod are needed; wood does not absorb. Also, if the impurity is within a dirham's width, leniency is given. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on purification before prayer, identify the two types of hadath, and cite the Quranic proofs for each.

Core Idea
Purification from both hadath and najas is wajib before prayer; the Quran commands it.

This lesson introduces the first condition of salah: taharah. The author explains that purification from ritual impurity (hadath) and physical filth (najas) is obligatory before prayer. The proofs are from the Quran and hadith, establishing the wajib nature of this condition. We will also distinguish between minor and major ritual impurities and their required purifications.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling

The term 'يجب' indicates wujub in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Distinguish between hadath asghar and akbar
Hadath asghar (minor) requires wudu; hadath akbar (major) requires ghusl. The text clarifies that both must be removed before prayer.

3
Cite the Quranic proofs for each type
For hadath asghar: verse 'When you rise for prayer, wash your faces...' (5:6). For hadath akbar: 'If you are in a state of janabah, then purify yourselves' (5:6). For najas: 'And your garments purify' (74:4) and the hadith of Ammar listing five impurities.
The hadith is from Darqutni; it is a supporting evidence.
4
Understand that these are conditions (shurut) not pillars (arkan)
The text enumerates six conditions (shurut) that must be fulfilled before prayer. The first is purification. Conditions precede the prayer; pillars occur during it.
The other conditions include purity of place, covering awrah, intention, and facing qibla.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
If a person has urine on his clothes (najas) or is in a state of janabah, he must purify before prayer; otherwise the prayer is invalid.
Both ritual and physical purity are required.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Turning, prompting, and closing eyes are sunnah muakkadah, not fard.

This lesson opens the chapter on funerals with the initial rulings for the final moments of life and immediately after death. These actions are based on prophetic practice and are recommended, not obligatory. Understanding their hukm level prevents overburdening the dying and their families.

2

Impurity from the Deceased After Ghusl
Ruling on impurity from deceased after ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when impurity emerges from the deceased after ghusl and apply the distinction between emergence after completion and during washing.

Core Idea
After ghusl of the deceased, wash only the soiled spot – repeating the whole washing is unnecessary hardship.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation, but what happens if impurity emerges after the washing is done? This passage from a Hanafi sharh provides a practical ruling based on ease (taysir). You will learn that the entire ghusl is not repeated; only the affected area is cleaned. An athar from Imam Hammad supports this. The ruling changes if impurity occurs during the washing process — then repeating the whole ghusl is better.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Separate the ruling for impurity emerging after completion of ghusl versus during ghusl. The text gives different guidance for each.
After = don't repeat; during = better to repeat.
2
State the Hanafi position
For after completion: do not repeat; only wash the soiled area. For during: it is better to repeat the entire ghusl.
The mufta bihi is: after completion, spot-clean; during, repeat.
3
Understand the evidence (dalil)
The proof is an athar from Hammad, who was asked about this exact situation and replied, 'Wash that place.' This is a qawl sahabi (statement of a Companion-equivalent scholar) and is accepted in Hanafi fiqh as proof.
The dalil is zanni al-thubut but sufficient due to istihsan.
4
Apply the reasoning (illah)
The reason for not repeating is to avoid hardship (mashaqqah) for the washer and risk of the body decaying. This reflects the Hanafi principle of taysir (ease).
Istihsan overrides strict qiyas that might require full repetition.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools require a complete re-wash if any impurity emerges, regardless of timing. The Hanafi position is an ease justified by the athar.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf built on different weighings of evidence.

Core Idea
Drying and perfuming the deceased are recommended, not obligatory, and follow the practice of the early Muslims.

After washing the deceased, certain acts are performed to honor the body. Drying the body with a cloth prevents the shroud from getting wet. Applying hanut (a fragrant mixture) to the head and beard, and camphor to the prostration places, are recommended based on the prophetic practice. This lesson explains these rulings and their evidence.

3
Examine the evidence
The text cites athar from Ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him) that camphor is placed on prostration places, and a hadith in Bukhari. This shows the proof level is from the Companions and the Prophet (peace be upon him).
These are zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalalah, hence sunnah not wajib.

Locate the prostration places

The Sunnah Shroud for a Man: Three Garments
Ruling on the sunnah shroud for a man

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah shroud for a man, describe the three garments and their characteristics, and cite the primary hadith proofs.

Core Idea
The Prophet's shroud was three white cotton garments: izaar, unsewn qamees, and lifafah.

Shrouding the deceased is a communal obligation, but the manner follows the Prophet's example. This lesson explains the sunnah shroud for a man, consisting of three specific garments, and the evidence from authentic hadith. You will learn what each garment is, how it is made, and why three garments are recommended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah ruling
Determine the recommended number of garments for shrouding a man according to the Hanafi school.
The ruling is sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah) for three garments; two are permissible but less virtuous.
2
Describe the three garments
List the three pieces: izaar (lower wrapper), qamees (a sleeveless, collarless cloth with a slit for the head, unsewn), and lifafah (outer sheet). Note that the qamees has no sleeves, hem, or collar.
The qamees is not a regular sewn shirt; it is a rectangular cloth with a slit.
3
Examine the primary proof
Analyze the hadith of 'A'isha (may Allah be pleased with her) that the Prophet was shrouded in three white Yemeni cotton garments without a qamees or turban. This establishes the basic pattern.
The absence of qamees in the hadith is reconciled by the distinct meaning of qamees in the shroud (unsewn piece), not a sewn shirt.
4
Consider the secondary proof
Study the hadith of 'Abdullah ibn Ubayy where the Prophet gave his own qamees for shrouding. This shows the virtue and permissibility of including a qamees.
This hadith confirms that the qamees is a separate piece, not a turban.

Woman's Shroud: Recommended Garments and Placement
Rulings on woman's shroud including garment count, veil and hair placement, and avoiding combing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended number of garments for a woman's shroud according to the Hanafi school and explain the placement of the veil and hair based on prophetic practice.

Core Idea
A woman's shroud consists of five garments; the veil over the shirt; hair on the chest—all based on the Sunnah.

When preparing a deceased woman for burial, the shroud (kafan) follows specific guidelines from the Sunnah. This lesson examines the Hanafi position on the number of garments, the placement of the veil and hair, and the evidence from hadith and athar. You will learn the recommended practice and the permissible alternatives, ensuring the deceased is shrouded in a manner consistent with the Prophet's teachings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling category
The act is shrouding a deceased woman. Determine the legal ruling: sunnah muakkadah (recommended) for five garments, ja'iz (permissible) for three.
Hanafi position is five is superior; three is acceptable if needed.
2
Locate the evidence from hadith and athar
The passage cites three proofs: a hadith from a man of Banu Urwa listing five garments, an athar from Umar, and a narration from Muhammad permitting three. Identify each and its thubut/dalala.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut but supported by the athar.
3
Understand the placement of the veil
The khimar is placed over the shirt (dir') and under the outer wrap (milhafa). This mirrors the living practice. Evidence from the practice of Hafsah.
The veil is thus positioned to cover the head and neck appropriately.
4
Learn the rule about hair and avoidance of combing
The deceased woman's hair is placed on her chest, not braided nor combed excessively. Combing is disliked because it is beautification for the dead. Based on Umm Atiyyah's hadith of braiding into three and placing behind.
Light combing only if hair is severely disheveled.
5
Note the permissibility of fewer garments
Although five is recommended, it is permissible to use only three garments if necessary. This is based on the narration from Muhammad (Ibn Sirin).
The preferred practice remains five.

Cutting Nails and Hair of the Deceased
Ruling on not trimming nails or hair of the deceased.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting the nails and hair of a deceased person and identify the exceptional condition that permits trimming.

Core Idea
Nails and hair of the deceased are left untouched unless unsightly.

This passage addresses a nuanced aspect of janazah rites: whether to trim the deceased's nails and hair. The Hanafi school takes a conservative stance, considering these acts as beautification unnecessary for the deceased. However, an exception is made to prevent the body from appearing disheveled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The text explicitly states that the deceased's nails and hair are not to be cut. This is the starting point of the ruling.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given is that nails and hair are part of adornment, which the deceased no longer needs. Therefore, beautification acts are avoided.
3
Spot the exception
The passage adds an exception: if the nails or hair are excessively long and unsightly, they may be cut. This prevents the body from appearing neglected.
4
Analyze the evidence
The evidence is an athar (report) from Hammad and al-Hasan. Hammad uses an analogy: just as circumcision (a beautification act) is not performed on the deceased, nails are not trimmed. Al-Hasan allows trimming if excessive, confirming the exception.
The analogy is based on the principle that beautification acts are unnecessary after death.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, the default is not to cut, but if the nails or hair are very long and appear unseemly, trimming is permitted. The key is the intention: not for beautification but to avoid an unsightly appearance.

Not to Cut Nails or Hair of the Deceased
Ruling with proof from athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting the nails and hair of a deceased person and apply the exception based on the athar.

Core Idea
The deceased is not to be beautified; cutting nails/hair is avoided except when excessively long.

This lesson explores a specific ruling in the fiqh of janazah: the prohibition of cutting a deceased person's nails or hair. We will examine the primary textual evidence (athar) from the Companions and early scholars, and how the Hanafi school derives a recommended ruling (mandub) with a practical exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent ruling from the matn
Read the matn: 'Do not cut his nail nor shorten his hair.' This gives an initial impression of prohibition. Note that the ruling is general.
2
Analyze the first athar (Aisha's rebuke)
Aisha (RA) saw a woman combing a deceased person's hair and said: 'Why do you beautify your dead?' This establishes that beautification is inappropriate for the deceased. The underlying principle: the deceased no longer needs adornment.
The principle is general and covers cutting nails/hair as beautification.
3
Analyze the second athar (Hammad's analogy)
Hammad was asked about cutting nails of the deceased. He analogized: 'Do you think that if he were uncircumcised you would circumcise him?' Just as circumcision is not performed after death, cutting nails is also not required. This qiyas shows that such acts are not obligations.
The illa (effective cause) is that these are not necessary for the deceased's state after death.
4
Identify the exception from Hasan
Hasan al-Basri states: 'If they are excessively long, cut them.' This introduces an exception to the general prohibition: when nails or hair are unsightly or problematic, trimming is allowed.
The exception is based on necessity (being fahish, excessively long).
5
Derive the overall Hanafi position
Combining the athar: the base ruling is that cutting is disliked (makruh tanzihan) because it is a form of beautification not needed for the deceased. However, if nails or hair are excessively long, cutting becomes permissible. The recommended act (mandub) is to leave them uncut. This is the mufta bihi position.
The ruling is not absolutely forbidden; necessity can override the dislike.
