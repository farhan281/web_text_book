Permissibility of Nabidh from Honey and Figs
Explaining permissibility of non-grape/date infusions and condition of cooking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on nabidh from non-grape/date sources, identify the dalil and its wajh al-dalalah, and explain the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the condition of cooking.

Core Idea
Nabidh from honey and figs is permissible even uncooked, as only grape and date infusions are khamr by prophetic designation.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on nabidh (infusions) made from honey, figs, and other ingredients besides grapes and dates. The school's ruling hinges on the prophetic hadith that limits khamr to two specific sources, and a subsequent difference on whether cooking is required for permissibility. We will trace the proof and the preferred view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon (nabidh from honey/figs) and the hukm level (mubah/permissible according to Shaykhayn). Note the conditions: consumed without amusement (lahw) and for bodily strength.
The ruling applies specifically to infusions that are not made from grapes or dates.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited: the hadith 'al-khamr min hataayn' (khamr is from these two) - referring to grapes and dates. This is an ahad hadith with zanni thubut.
The hadith is used to argue that only beverages from these two sources are inherently khamr.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the hadith proves the ruling: the Prophet explicitly restricts khamr to two sources, so other infusions (honey, figs, etc.) are not khamr and thus not prohibited by that name. The prohibition is specifically tied to the source, not to the intoxicating property.
This is a key distinction from the Shafi'i school, which considers any intoxicating drink as khamr.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Identify the two sayings within the Hanafi school: (1) Cooking (tabkh) is a condition for permissibility. (2) Cooking is not a condition. The second view is attributed to al-Quduri and is preferred.
The reasoning: a small quantity of such nabidh does not incite desire to drink large quantities, thus it remains permissible without cooking.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Understand why the preferred view (mufta bihi) is that cooking is not required. The tarjih authority is al-Quduri, and the basis is that the small amount does not lead to intoxication, even if raw.
The mufta bihi position is that nabidh from non-grape/date sources is halal if consumed without amusement and for strength, uncooked.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the permissibility of nabidh (infusions) made from honey, figs, and other ingredients besides grapes and dates. The author begins by referencing the Prophet's hadith 'al-khamr min hataayn' (khamr is from these two), meaning grapes and dates. This is used as proof that only beverages made from these two are inherently khamr (prohibited). Therefore, nabidh from other sources like honey and figs is not khamr. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Shaykhayn), such nabidh is halal even if uncooked, provided it is drunk without amusement (lahw) and for the purpose of gaining physical strength. However, there is a difference of opinion regarding the necessity of cooking for permissibility: some say it is a condition, while others (as mentioned in al-Quduri) say it is not. The latter view is considered stronger because the small quantity of such nabidh does not lead to intoxication in large quantities. The Hanafi school's relied-upon position is that cooking is not required, and these nabidh are permissible under the stated conditions.

شیخین (Shaykhayn): The two leading Hanafi scholars after Abu Hanifa: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Permissibility of Cooked Grape Juice with Two-Thirds Evaporated
Dispute on whether concentrated grape juice is halal or haram when two-thirds gone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on concentrated grape juice (cooked down to one-third), identify the role of intention (strength vs pleasure), and compare it with the Shafi'i/Maliki positions.

Core Idea
Grape juice cooked down to one-third is halal for strength but haram for pleasure – intention determines the ruling.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute regarding concentrated grape juice. The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) permits it under a specific condition, while Imam Muhammad, Malik, and al-Shafi'i prohibit it. Importantly, the intention of the drinker – strength or pleasure – shifts the ruling even within the Hanafi fold. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi fiqh to similar modern beverages.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the cooked juice is halal even if it becomes intoxicating, provided the intention is to gain strength (taqwiyya).
This is the mufta bihi position within the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opposing schools
Note that Imam Muhammad (within Hanafi), Imam Malik, and Imam al-Shafi'i consider it haram unconditionally. They view any intoxicating grape derivative as khamr.
3
Analyze the condition of intention
Observe that the dispute is restricted to cases where the drinker intends strength (taqwiyya). If the intention is pleasure (taladhdhudh), all agree it is haram.
The Arabic text emphasizes 'idha qasada bihi al-taqwa' – intention to strengthen oneself.
4
Examine intra-Hanafi variance
Imam Muhammad has three narrations: one agreeing with Shaykhayn, one of karaha (dislike), and one of tawaqquf (abstention). The relied-upon view is that of Shaykhayn.
This shows the richness of Hanafi scholarship – multiple valid opinions exist, but the school settles on a clear mufta bihi.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis following Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf may use such juice for medicinal or strength purposes; Shafi'is and Malikis must avoid it entirely. Within Hanafi, the fatwa is given on the view of Shaykhayn.

The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute on Muthallath
Intra-Hanafi disagreement and fatwa determination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad on muthallath 'anabi and identify which is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Within the Hanafi school, the fatwa on muthallath is prohibition, despite the Shaykhayn's permissive view.

This lesson examines a famous intra-Hanafi disagreement: whether grape juice cooked down to one-third (muthallath) is permissible when it becomes intoxicating. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs) allowed it for strength, while Imam Muhammad declared it haram. The later fatwa follows Imam Muhammad, but understanding the dispute reveals the richness of Hanafi ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and contested ruling
The act is muthallath 'anabi—grape juice cooked until two-thirds evaporates, leaving one-third. The contested ruling is whether it is halal or haram when it becomes intoxicating.
2
List the Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (Shaykhayn) say halal (with intention condition); Imam Muhammad says haram absolutely. Note that Imam Muhammad also has variant narrations (halal, karahah, tawaqquf).
The passage records three narrations from Imam Muhammad: like Shaykhayn, disliked, and suspended.
3
Understand the condition of intention
The Shaykhayn's permissibility is conditional on the intention (qasd). If intended for strength (taqawwat) in worship, it is halal; if for frivolity (lahw), it is haram by consensus.
This condition is not mentioned for the opposing view.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The text explicitly states that the fatwa is on Imam Muhammad's saying (haram). Despite the Shaykhayn's authority, the operative ruling has settled on prohibition.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin.

The Proofs and Counter-Proofs on Muthallath
Comparison of proofs and usul analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the proofs of the Shaykhayn and the opposing schools on muthallath and evaluate the strength of each argument using usul al-fiqh principles.

Core Idea
The Shaykhayn interpret 'kullu muskir khamr' as non-literal; the opponents take it literally—the difference turns on a grammatical point of conjunction.

This lesson dives into the evidence cited by both sides of the muthallath debate. The opponents cite three hadiths equating all intoxicants to khamr; the Shaykhayn counter with a hadith limiting prohibition to khamr itself and a rational argument about the measure of intoxication. Understanding these proofs requires us to apply usul al-fiqh principles of literal vs. figurative interpretation and the concept of 'atf al-mughayarah.

Guided Path
1
List the opponents' proofs
The opponents (Imam Muhammad, Malik, Shafi'i) rely on three hadiths: (1) 'Kullu muskir khamr', (2) 'Ma askara kathiruhu fa qaliluhu haram', (3) 'Ma askara jarratuhu fa jar'atuhu haram'. They also use rational qiyas: intoxication corrupts the intellect like khamr, so it is haram.
These hadith are found in Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, etc.
2
Analyze the opponents' dalala
They take 'kullu muskir khamr' literally: every intoxicant is legally khamr, thus all intoxicants are prohibited absolutely. The second and third hadith reinforce that even a small amount is haram if the large amount intoxicates. Their qiyas: intoxication is the illa (cause) for prohibition in khamr, so it applies to all intoxicants.
3
List the Shaykhayn's proofs
The Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf) cite a hadith: 'Hurmimat al-khamru li 'aynihi' (Khamr is prohibited in its essence). They also offer a rational argument: the corrupting element is the intoxicating cupful (qadah), not the drink itself.
The hadith is narrated in various forms, e.g., 'Hurmimat al-khamru bi 'aynihi'.
4
Analyze the Shaykhayn's dalala
They argue that 'khamr' refers specifically to grape wine; only that substance is prohibited intrinsically. Other intoxicants are not called khamr; the hadith 'kullu muskir khamr' is either figurative or a warning. The conjunction in 'al-sukru min kulli sharab' (intoxication from every drink) indicates difference ('atf al-mughayarah), so the ruling on khamr does not automatically transfer to other drinks. The rational proof: the prohibition attaches to the measure that causes intoxication, not the entire drink.
This is a classic usul dispute over the meaning of 'kullu muskir khamr'.
5
Identify the usul principle
The key principle is the interpretation of a general text ('amm) and whether it is literal or figurative. Also, the role of conjunction ('atf) in delineating categories. The Shaykhayn employ istihsan to limit the application of the hadith, while the opponents adhere to zahir al-lafz.

Numerical example of foam loss adjustment

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Foam loss reduces the effective juice quantity; boil until one-third of the adjusted amount remains.

This passage provides a concrete numerical example of the Hanafi rule for purifying grape juice by boiling. It shows how to account for juice lost as foam before or during the boiling process, clarifying that the foam is considered part of the original juice for calculation purposes. Understanding this adjustment is essential for correctly applying the one-third rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is boiling grape juice to remove its intoxicating property. The ruling is that the remaining liquid becomes permissible (halal) when only one-third of the original (adjusted) quantity remains.

Determine the effective starting quantity
Account for any juice lost as foam before or during boiling. This foam counts as part of the original juice, so subtract it from the initial total to get the effective starting quantity. In the example, 10 measures start, 1 is lost as foam, so effective start is 9 measures.
The foam is considered part of the juice, not a separate loss.
3
Apply the one-third rule
Boil the remaining juice until only one-third of the effective starting quantity remains. For 9 measures, one-third is 3 measures. Once 3 measures remain, the liquid becomes halal.
The target is one-third of the adjusted amount, not the original amount.
4
Confirm the resulting hukm
The resulting liquid is permissible (halal) because the intoxicating content has been removed by sufficient boiling. The calculation ensures that the one-third rule is satisfied despite foam loss.

Second Rule: Boiling Grape Juice Mixed with Water
Ruling on boiling grape juice with added water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios for boiling grape juice mixed with water and calculate the remaining volume required for permissibility.

Core Idea
The permissibility of boiled grape juice depends on whether water evaporates separately or together with the juice.

The Hanafi school provides a precise rule for when grape juice becomes permissible after boiling with added water. The key factor is not just the total reduction but how the water behaves during cooking. This lesson unpacks the two possible scenarios and the exact proportions required, so you can apply the ruling to any mixture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mixture composition
First, note the initial volumes of grape juice and water, and whether water was added before cooking. This is the starting point for all calculations.
2
Determine the evaporation behavior
Classify the mixture into one of two cases: (1) water evaporates faster due to its thinness and lightness, or (2) both liquids evaporate together at the same rate. This classification is based on observable physical properties.
The Hanafi rule relies on empirical observation; if uncertain, default to the second case.
3
Apply the corresponding ruling
If water evaporates faster, first boil until all added water is gone, then continue boiling the juice until two-thirds of the juice itself evaporates, leaving one-third of the original juice. If both evaporate together, boil the entire mixture until two-thirds of the total volume is gone, leaving one-third of the total.
The rationale ensures that at least one-third of the original juice remains, either pure or as part of the mixture.
4
Verify with the numerical example
Consider 10 measures of juice and 20 measures of water (total 30). In the first scenario, after water evaporates, boil juice from 10 to 3.33 (one-third). In the second, boil total from 30 to 10 (one-third). Confirm the calculations.
The result is 10 measures remaining in the second case, which equals one-third of the total, and 3.33 in the first, which equals one-third of the original juice.
5
Apply the ruling to other mixtures
Generalize the rule for any juice-to-water ratio. First case: reduce juice to 1/3. Second case: reduce total to 1/3. Compute the final volume accordingly.
Always verify which scenario applies based on actual evaporation behavior.

Boiling Multiple Times and Condition of Before Becoming Khamr
Ruling on boiling grape juice with condition and nuance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on boiling grape juice and identify the condition that determines its permissibility.

Core Idea
Boiling prevents intoxication only before khamr forms; boiling after is ineffective.

This passage clarifies that boiling grape juice once or multiple times is equally effective in making it permissible, provided the boiling occurs before the juice becomes intoxicating (khamr). The key principle is that boiling is a preventive measure (mani'), not a curative (rafi'). Understanding this timing condition is crucial for correctly applying the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is boiling grape juice (or any potentially intoxicating fruit juice). The ruling: the resulting liquid is halal (permissible) if boiling is completed before the juice becomes khamr.
The ruling applies whether boiling is done once or multiple times.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is based on the effect of fire (athar al-nar). The text states that continued boiling after the fire is removed, due to residual heat, is still considered an effect of fire and thus valid.
The dalil is not a specific text but a general principle of physical causation.
3
Understand the Condition: Before Becoming Khamr
The critical condition is that boiling must occur before the juice becomes intoxicating. If intoxication has already set in, boiling does not remove the prohibition.
The text uses the term shamar (shamr) to refer to the intoxicating state.
4
Analyze the Nuance: Boiling After Fire Removed
If the fire is removed but the juice continues boiling due to the heat of the fire (residual effect), it is permissible. However, if the juice stops boiling and then spontaneously resumes boiling due to its own fermentation, it is not permissible because this indicates it has become khamr.
The distinction is between boiling by fire's effect and boiling by fermentation.
5
Summarize the Legal Principle
The general rule: Boiling is a preventive measure (mani') against intoxication, not a remover of prohibition after intoxication. Therefore, timing is everything.
This principle is consistent with the Hanafi view that khamr cannot be purified by physical means after it becomes najis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method to calculate how much more grape juice must evaporate after spillage to reach the halal threshold.

Core Idea
When juice evaporates and spills, the halal threshold is recalculated by a proportional formula: (1/3 original) × (remaining after spill) ÷ (remaining after first evaporation).

In the Hanafi school, grape juice becomes halal when two-thirds of it has evaporated. But what if some juice is spilled after initial evaporation? This lesson introduces a third principle: a precise calculation that accounts for both evaporation and spillage, ensuring that the final juice has undergone the required two-thirds reduction from the original volume.

Identify volumes
Determine the original juice volume, the amount that evaporated by cooking, and the amount spilled after that.
The original volume is the baseline for the two-thirds reduction.

Ruling on Animals Killed by Falls, Gore, Beating, or Wolves: The Requirement of Tadhkiyah
General ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutaraddiyah, natihah, mawqudhah, and wolf-torn animals, and apply the condition of life to determine whether tadhkiyah is required.

Core Idea
Any sign of life in a fatally injured animal requires slaughter to make it halal; hidden life counts.

Animals that fall from heights, are gored, beaten, or torn by wolves are commonly encountered scenarios. This lesson explains their legal status according to the Hanafi school. The key is whether any life remains at the time of slaughter – even a faint sign suffices according to the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cases covered
The text lists four categories: mutaraddiyah (fallen), natihah (gored), mawqudhah (beaten), and ma baqara al-dhi'b (wolf-torn). Recognize each as a type of animal that has suffered a fatal injury but may still be alive.
2
State the conditional ruling
For each category, if the animal is found alive – whether with apparent life (hayat zahirah) or hidden life (hayat khafiyyah) – and is properly slaughtered, it is halal. Otherwise it is haram (carrion).
The ruling is conditional on the presence of life at the time of slaughter.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that any life, hidden or apparent, necessitates tadhkiyah. The Sahibayn require tadhkiyah only if life is apparent. Within Sahibayn, Abu Yusuf defines apparent life as the ability to survive.
The fatwa is given on Abu Hanifa's view, making hidden life sufficient.
4
Examine the dalil
The proof is Quran 5:3: 'illa ma dhakkaytum' (except what you slaughter). Abu Hanifa interprets this exception as absolute, covering any life. The Sahibayn restrict it to cases where life is apparent, arguing that hidden life means the animal is effectively dead.
The textual operation is expansion (ta'mim) vs restriction (takhsis).
5
Apply the rule
Given a scenario: you find a sheep that has fallen from a cliff and is lying motionless but breathing faintly. According to the fatwa, this is hidden life. Slaughter it immediately to make it halal.
Emergency slaughter (dharura) does not apply because the animal is accessible.

Core Idea
One act of sending requires one tasmiyah, even if multiple prey are killed.

Core Idea
A hunting animal's pause that is a tactic does not break the sending; only rest breaks it.

The key is the animal's intention and habitual behavior, as clarified by the analogy of a dog that has adopted the habit of ambushing.

The pause is a hunting tactic (hilah), not rest (istirahat). Because it is a tactic, the sending is not cut off.

A dog that has adopted the habit of ambushing is treated the same as a cheetah. If the pause is a tactic, the prey is halal.

Hunted Animal Falling on Rock: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on cause of death in hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions on a hunted animal whose belly splits after falling on a rock, and apply the relied-upon distinction between sharp edge and blunt impact.

Core Idea
A hunted animal's belly split is halal if caused by blunt force, haram if cut by a sharp rock edge.

In the chapter of hunting (kitab al-sayd), a key question arises: if a hunter's arrow hits an animal but the animal then falls onto a rock and its belly splits, is the meat halal? The earlier Hanafi texts give an absolute ruling, but later scholars refined it based on the cause of death. This lesson traces the dispute and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling of Imam Muhammad
Imam Muhammad in al-Mabsut states: if the animal falls on a rock and stops, it is halal regardless of belly split. This is an absolute statement.

Al-Hakim al-Shahid in al-Muntaqa rules: if the belly splits, it is haram because death may be from the rock, not the arrow. He interprets the absolute statement as applying only when the belly does not split.

Imam Sarakhsi distinguishes: if the rock's sharp edge cuts the belly, death is from the rock (haram). If the belly splits from blunt force similar to falling on flat ground, death is attributed to the arrow (halal).

Sahib al-Hidayah endorses Sarakhsi's interpretation as more correct (asahh). Thus, the operative ruling follows the sharp/blunt distinction.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the ruling on a hunted animal that falls onto a rock and dies with its belly split. The original ruling from Imam Muhammad in al-Mabsut (the base text) states that if the animal falls onto a rock and stops there, it is halal regardless of whether the belly splits. However, al-Hakim al-Shahid in al-Muntaqa restricts this: if the belly splits, it is haram due to the possibility that death occurred from the impact with the rock rather than from the shot. He interprets Imam Muhammad's absolute statement as applying only when the belly does not split. Then Imam Sarakhsi reconciles both: if the animal is cut by the sharp edge of the rock, causing the split, it is haram (as per al-Muntaqa); but if the impact is similar to what would occur from falling on flat ground (i.e., the belly splits from the force of the fall, not from a sharp edge), then it is halal (as per al-Mabsut). The author of Hidayah declares Imam Sarakhsi's interpretation as more correct (asahh). This ruling hinges on the cause of death: if the wound from the arrow is the direct cause, the animal is halal; if another factor (like a rock cut) intervenes, it is haram. The final fatwa follows Sarakhsi's distinction.

Hunting with Mi'rad: When is the Game Halal?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game struck by a mi'rad and explain the distinction between hit by side versus edge, citing the hadith proof.

Core Idea
Game hit by the side of a mi'rad is haram unless it cuts; cutting is required for dhabh.

This lesson explains the Hanafi position on hunting with a mi'rad, a headless arrow. The key distinction is whether the animal is struck by the sharp edge (halal) or the blunt side (haram unless it wounds). We will examine the proof from the hadith and the underlying requirement of cutting (dhabh).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The source discusses hunting with a mi'rad. Determine the ruling for two scenarios: hitting with the edge vs. hitting with the side. The text states that if hit by the side, it is not eaten unless it wounds; if hit by the edge, it is eaten.
The ruling is conditional: side + no wound = haram; side + wound = halal; edge = halal.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim: 'What is hit by its edge, eat; what is hit by its side, do not eat.' Identify the source and its transmission status.
This is a hadith ahad, so it is zanni al-thubut.
3
Analyze the hadith's implication
The command 'eat' for the edge indicates permissibility; the prohibition 'do not eat' for the side indicates prohibition. However, the exception for wounding (if the side cuts) is derived from the general principle that dhabh requires cutting.
The hadith is explicit for the edge, but the exception for wounding with the side is based on the broader requirement of dhabh.
4

If the mi'rad hits with its side but still cuts and causes blood flow, it falls under the ruling of 'wounding' and is halal. This aligns with the condition that dhabh (cutting) must be realized.
Wounding (جرح) means cutting, not just crushing or breaking.
5
Apply the principle to similar weapons
The same logic applies to any hunting weapon without a head: if the part that strikes is sharp and cuts, it is halal; if it is blunt and kills by force alone, it is haram.
Modern examples include crossbow bolts with blunt tips vs. broadheads.

Aquatic Animal Hit by an Arrow: The Submersion Criterion
Explaining the rule for an aquatic animal hit by an arrow regarding wound submersion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi criterion of wound submersion to determine the permissibility of eating an aquatic animal hit by an arrow.

Core Idea
An aquatic animal hit by an arrow is halal only if the wound remained above water; submersion creates a presumption of death by water, not the arrow.

The Hanafi school derives a nuanced ruling for aquatic animals hunted with projectiles like arrows. While the general rule for hunting is that an arrow must wound the animal, water introduces an additional factor: the possibility that the animal drowned. This lesson examines the criterion set forth in Al-Hidayah and the rational analogy (qiyas) that supports it.

Guided Path

State the ruling for an aquatic animal hit by an arrow

The ruling depends on the condition of wound submersion, not the animal's aquatic nature.

The condition is whether the wound was submerged in water before death. If submerged, the cause of death is uncertain.
The Hanafi school uses the apparent cause (zahir) to determine the hukm.

The rationale is that if the wound is submerged, water may have entered and caused death, making it haram. If not submerged, the arrow wound is the apparent cause.
This is based on the principle that the cause of death must be the hunting implement, not an external factor.

The ruling for an aquatic animal with a submerged wound is analogized to a land animal that falls into water after being hit by an arrow: in both cases, death is likely from water, so it is haram.
The asl (original case) is the land animal; the far (new case) is the aquatic animal with submerged wound.
5
Apply to examples
Consider a duck shot over water: if the wound stays above water, it's halal; if the duck falls and wound submerges, it's haram.
The same rule applies to all aquatic animals, including fish-like creatures.

Hunting with a Mi'rad: Cutting vs. Crushing
Explaining the rule for hunting with a blunt arrow (mi'rad).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for hunting with a mi'rad and explain the distinction between hitting with the edge (had) and the side (ard) based on the hadith and the requirement of jarh.

Core Idea
A mi'rad kills by cutting (halal) or crushing (haram) — only cutting fulfills the dhabh requirement.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a nuanced ruling in hunting with a specific type of arrow. The key is not the weapon itself but the nature of the injury it inflicts. The Prophet ﷺ explicitly distinguished between the sharp edge and the side, and the Hanafi school follows this distinction precisely. Understanding this ruling helps avoid consuming meat that is not properly slaughtered or hunted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the weapon and its parts
A mi'rad is an arrow without a metal head but with sharp edges on its sides. It has two distinct parts: the sharp edge (had) and the side or thickness (ard). Determine which part strikes the animal.
The side is the non-cutting part; it crushes rather than cuts.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The Prophet ﷺ said regarding the mi'rad: 'What it hits with its edge, eat; and what it hits with its side, do not eat.' This hadith (from 'Adi ibn Hatim) is the direct proof.
The imperative 'كل' (eat) indicates permissibility for edge hits; 'لا تاكل' (do not eat) indicates prohibition for side hits.
3
Understand the distinction: cutting vs. crushing
Hitting with the edge causes a cutting wound (jarh), which allows blood to flow and fulfills the condition of dhabh. Hitting with the side crushes and breaks without cutting, which does not constitute jarh.
The Hanafi school requires jarh for valid hunting; crushing alone is insufficient.
4
Connect to the principle of dhabh
The general principle is that for meat to be halal from hunting, the animal must be wounded in a way that cuts the throat or causes blood to flow. The mi'rad's side does not achieve this.
This ruling is consistent with the earlier explanation that wounding (jarh) is necessary.
5
Apply to a scenario
If a hunter uses a mi'rad and the arrow's side hits the animal, causing death by crushing, the meat is haram. If the sharp edge cuts the animal and causes death, the meat is halal.
The ruling depends on the actual point of impact, not the intention.

Ruling on Hunting with Pellet (Bundaqah) and Stone
Explaining the rules for hunting with pellets, stones, and sharp stones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for hunting with pellets and stones, and apply the principle of cause of death to determine halal or haram.

Core Idea
A hunting implement that crushes without cutting renders the animal haram; only a cutting wound ensures halal.

In the previous lesson, we learned that the mi'rad (a blunt arrow) kills by crushing, not cutting, making the animal haram if it hits with its side. Now, we extend that principle to modern hunting implements like pellets, bullets, and stones. The key question is always: did the weapon cut (jarh) or crush? This lesson will walk you through the detailed Hanafi rulings for these cases, based on qiyas to the mi'rad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central principle
The ruling depends on the cause of death. If the implement cuts and death is certain from the wound, the animal is halal. If it crushes or kills by weight, it is haram.
This principle is derived from the hadith on mi'rad.
2
Apply to pellet (bundaqah)
Pellets from slingshots or guns crush and tear but do not cut. By qiyas to the mi'rad hitting with its side, the animal is haram.
This includes all pellets (bundaqah, chharra, bullets).
3
Apply to stones
Stones are treated differently based on weight and sharpness. A heavy sharp stone may kill by weight, so it is haram. A light sharp stone kills by cutting, so it is halal. A light sharp stone shaped like an arrow also kills by cutting, so halal.
The possibility of death by weight (ihtimal) makes it haram; certainty from cutting (ta'ayyun) makes it halal.
4
Note the practical outcome
Most modern firearms fire bullets that crush, so the animal is haram. Only sharp arrows or light sharp stones are halal. Always check the nature of the wound.
Some scholars differentiate based on the type of bullet, but the Hanafi position is as above.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the Hanafi position on a limb severed from a hunted animal before death. It begins by stating the ruling: if a hunter shoots an arrow and cuts off a limb, the whole animal is halal because the wound is a condition for permissibility, but the severed limb itself is haram. This is the Hanafi position. Imam Shafi'i disagrees, holding that if the animal dies from the cut, both are halal because the cut counts as emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari). The Hanafi response, based on the hadith 'Whatever is cut from a living animal is dead,' argues that the limb is from a living being and thus dead (and haram). The Hanafis emphasize the distinction between actual life (hayat haqiqiyyah) and legal life (hayat hukmiyyah). If the animal can survive after the cut, it is considered legally alive, so the limb is haram. If survival is impossible (e.g., the head), the limb becomes halal. The commentary also notes a nuance: if the limb is still attached by skin and might reattach, it is halal. This demonstrates the Hanafi principle of cautiousness in matters of ritual slaughter.

Key Terms
مبان (muban): The severed part, i.e., the limb cut off from the hunted animal.
ذكاة اضطراري (dhakāt idtirārī): Emergency slaughter, i.e., killing by wounding in hunting, distinct from optional slaughter.
حى (ḥayy): Living; used in the hadith to refer to an animal with life in reality and legally.

Slaughter by Severing the Head: Front vs Back Strike
Ruling on slaughter by severing head

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slaughtering a goat when the head is severed, distinguish between striking from the front and from the back, and identify the conditions and disliked aspects.

Core Idea
Cutting the jugular veins while alive makes it halal; damaging the spinal cord is makruh.

This passage from Kitab al-Dhabaih addresses a practical scenario: what if a goat's head is completely severed by the strike? The Hanafi school clarifies that the meat is halal if the jugular veins (awdaj) are cut, even if the head comes off. However, the method is disliked (makruh) if it damages the spinal cord. The direction of the strike also matters—striking from the back may lead to death before proper slaughter.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from Kitab al-Sayd (or Kitab al-Dhabaih) regarding slaughter by striking the neck. It presents two scenarios: (1) Striking the front of the neck so that the head is severed: the meat is halal because the jugular veins are cut, but the method is makruh because it damages the spinal cord (which can cause premature death before proper blood drainage). If the veins are cut while the animal is still alive, it is halal. This reflects the Hanafi principle that the essential element of slaughter (dhabh) is cutting the jugular veins (awdaj) while the animal is alive. The karahah (dislike) is for unnecessarily causing harm or affecting the spinal cord.

Ruling on an Animal Shot by Two Hunters: Ownership and Permissibility (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Ruling on ownership and permissibility when two hunters shoot the same animal, with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on ownership and permissibility when two hunters shoot the same animal, identifying the key condition of the animal's state and the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad over the amount of remaining life.

Core Idea
The first hunter owns the game after severe wounding, but it becomes haram if killed by a second arrow, with an intra-Hanafi dispute over the threshold of remaining life.

In hunting, it can happen that two hunters shoot the same animal. Who owns it and is it permissible to eat? The Hanafi school provides detailed rules based on the state of the animal after the first shot. This passage explains the core scenario, an exception, and an internal disagreement between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on the significance of the life remaining in the animal after the first wound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario and legal question
Understand the situation: two hunters shoot the same animal. The first arrow wounds it severely; the second kills it. Determine ownership and permissibility.
The key is the condition of the animal after the first shot – whether it remains 'game' (sayd) or has been removed from that state.
2
Ownership ruling and its basis
The first hunter owns the animal because he removed it from its state of resistance (hayz imtina'). The second hunter is liable for its value.
Ownership does not depend on who kills, but on who first secures control by severely wounding.
3
Permissibility condition and exception
The animal is not permissible to eat because the second arrow cannot serve as valid slaughter (dhabh) – the first hunter had the ability to perform proper slaughter. Exception: if the first wound was so severe that only life equivalent to a slaughtered animal remained (e.g., head cut off), the second arrow is irrelevant and the animal is halal.
The exception turns on whether death is attributed to the first or second arrow.
4
Introduce the intra-Hanafi dispute
When the first arrow leaves the animal unable to survive but with more life than a slaughtered animal (e.g., can live a day), there is disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Compare the two positions and identify the mufta bihi
Abu Yusuf says it is halal – the remaining life is insignificant. Muhammad says it is haram – that life is significant. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is Muhammad's, making it haram.
Practical consequence: according to Abu Yusuf, eat; according to Muhammad, do not eat.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on hunting (Kitab al-Sayd) addresses a complex scenario where two hunters shoot the same animal. The author first reviews a previous ruling: if the first shot only startles the animal without severe injury, and a second kills it, the animal is halal (since the animal was still game) and belongs to the second hunter. Then he introduces the main ruling: if the first arrow severely wounds the animal (removing it from the state of game), and a second arrow kills it, then (1) the animal belongs to the first hunter (since he removed it from its protected state), (2) it is not permissible to eat, and (3) the second hunter must pay its value to the first. The reason it is not halal is that after the first wound, the animal is no longer 'game'; it must be slaughtered properly (dhabh ikhtiyari). The second arrow is not a valid slaughter because the first had the ability to slaughter, so the second's action is like killing a captured animal, which is not permissible. This contrasts with the first scenario where the animal was still game, so the second's arrow acted as emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari). However, there is an exception: if the first arrow was so severe that the animal had only as much life as a slaughtered animal (like its head cut off), then the second arrow is irrelevant, and the animal is halal (because death is attributed to the first arrow). A further nuance arises when the first arrow leaves the animal unable to survive but with more life than a slaughtered animal (e.g., can live a day or two). Here, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Yusuf considers this life insignificant, so the second arrow does not render it haram; the animal is halal. Imam Muhammad considers this life significant, so the second arrow makes it haram. The text concludes that the ruling in this case is the same as when the animal could survive: it is not halal (i.e., Muhammad's view prevails in the commentary).

Key Terms
صيد (sayd): Wild animal that is lawful to hunt; the animal in its natural state before capture or being removed from the state of being game.
ذكاة (dhabh / dhakah): The method of slaughtering an animal to make it halal; can be ikhtiyari (optional/standard) or idtirari (emergency when standard slaughter is not possible).
حيز امتناع (hayz imtina'): The state or place where the animal can resist capture; the condition of being able to escape or defend itself.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling on two hunters revolves around the animal's state after the first shot: if it remains game, the second hunter owns it and it is halal; if it is removed from game, it belongs to the first and is haram unless the first wound is effectively fatal.
