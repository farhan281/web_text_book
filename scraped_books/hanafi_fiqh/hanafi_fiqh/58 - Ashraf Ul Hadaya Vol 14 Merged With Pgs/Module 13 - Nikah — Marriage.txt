Shurb Invalid as Mahr, Khul' Badal, and Sulh Badal
Extends invalidity of shurb to marriage, khul', and sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when shurb is specified as dower, khul' compensation, or sulh settlement, and explain the legal consequence for each contract.

Core Idea
Shurb cannot be owned through any contract, so it is invalid as consideration in marriage, khul', and sulh.

In this passage, the author continues the discussion on shurb (right to drink water) as a consideration. Having earlier established that shurb cannot be sold, he now extends this principle to three common family and settlement contracts: marriage (nikah), divorce initiated by the wife (khul'), and settlement of claims (sulh). The ruling is consistent: shurb's inherent uncertainty and non-ownability render it invalid as a specified dower or compensation, triggering default rules such as the proper dower or return of actual dower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three contracts discussed
The passage mentions nikah (marriage), khul' (divorce with compensation), and sulh (settlement of a claim). Note each context where shurb is specified as consideration.
These are all bilateral contracts where consideration is normally required.
2
State the ruling on each specification
For each contract, the text rules that the specification of shurb is invalid. The marriage and khul' themselves remain valid, but the proper alternatives take effect.
The invalidity is on the specification, not the entire contract.
3
Identify the reasons for invalidity
Two reasons are given: gross ignorance (jahl fahish) because the amount of water is unknown, and the fact that shurb cannot be owned through any contract.
The second reason is unique to shurb; it is not a transferable commodity.
4
Apply the consequences
For nikah: mahr al-mithl becomes obligatory. For khul': the wife must return the actual dower she received. For sulh: the settlement is invalid and the original claim remains.
The consequence preserves the validity of the underlying relationship while correcting the consideration.

Executor Pledging Orphan's Property for Necessity Loan
Ruling on executor borrowing for orphan’s needs and pledging orphan’s goods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor pledging orphan's property for a necessity loan and identify the conditions that make it permissible.

Core Idea
An executor may pledge orphan property for a necessity loan because necessity permits borrowing and the executor acts for the orphan's welfare.

This lesson addresses a practical question: can an executor (wasi) borrow money for an orphan's essential needs and secure that loan with the orphan's own property? The Hanafi school answers with a clear ruling of permissibility, provided the loan is for genuine necessity. Understanding this ruling helps guardians and executors manage an orphan's finances within the bounds of sharia.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an executor borrowing for the orphan's necessities and pledging the orphan's goods as security. The ruling is 'jā'iz' (permissible).
The ruling is stated directly in the text.
2
Identify the conditions for permissibility
The text specifies that the loan must be for 'clothing and food' — i.e., essential needs. This implies a condition of necessity.
The condition is derived from the phrase 'fī kuswatihi wa ṭa‘āmihi' (for his clothing and food).
3
Understand the underlying reasoning
The text gives two reasons: borrowing is permissible for necessity, and pledging is a means to fulfill the creditor's right. The executor has authority to discharge obligations from the orphan's estate.
The reasoning is based on the executor's role as a guardian and the principle of necessity (ḍarūrah).
4
Consider cross-madhhab differences
Some schools may restrict the executor's ability to pledge without explicit permission, but the Hanafi view permits it as a general rule.
This ruling is from the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah texts.

Executor Pledging for Trade on Behalf of Orphan
Ruling on executor taking or giving pledge in trade for orphan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor taking or giving a pledge when trading for an orphan and explain why both actions are permissible.

Core Idea
An executor may take and give pledges in trade for an orphan because trade is encouraged and pledges are necessary for credit.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a practical concern for guardians (executors) managing orphan property. When engaging in trade to grow the orphan's capital, credit transactions are inevitable, which require pledging. The text clarifies that both taking and giving pledges are fully permissible for the executor, grounded in the principle of necessity (darurah) for the recommended act of trading.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an executor (wasi) engaging in trade for an orphan (yatīm) and taking a pledge (irtihān) or giving a pledge (rahn). The ruling is ‘jā'iz’ (permissible).
The text uses 'jā'iz'—permissible, not obligatory.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The reason given: trading for the orphan is ‘better’ (awla) because it increases wealth (tathmīr al-māl). The Hanafi school encourages guardians to invest orphan funds beneficially.
This is mustahabb (preferred), not farḍ.
3
Recognize practical necessity
In trade, credit sales and purchases often involve pledges for security. The text states that the executor has no alternative (lā budd) but to take and give pledges—they are incidental to trade.
The necessity (hājah) here is practical, not a legal exigency.
4
Note the bilateral scope
The ruling covers both sides: taking a pledge (when the orphan is creditor) and giving a pledge (when the orphan is debtor). Both are treated equally as permissible.
The Arabic ‘irtahana wa rahana’ covers both directions.
5
Identify potential restrictions
Some other schools may restrict giving pledges due to risk, but the Hanafi position, based on necessity and benefit, allows both.
This is a mukhtalaf fīh (disputed) point.

Child Cannot Reclaim Pledge Made by Father Without Paying Debt
Ruling on child's inability to reclaim father's pledge without debt payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father's pledge of his minor child's property and explain why the pledge becomes binding (lāzim) on the child after maturity, requiring debt payment before reclaiming.

Core Idea
A father's pledge of his minor child's property is binding on the child even after maturity; the debt must be paid before reclaiming.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Hidayah regarding the father's authority over his minor child's property in pledge (rahn) transactions. The father, as guardian, can pledge the child's goods for a debt. Even after the child reaches adulthood, the pledge remains enforceable. The child cannot simply take back the pledged property without settling the underlying debt. This demonstrates the principle that the father's actions stand in the place of the child's own actions after puberty, making the contract binding (lāzim) on the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction and parties
Recognize that a father (the guardian) pledges the property of his minor child (ṣaghīr) as collateral (rahn) for a debt. The debt may be the father's or the child's.
The property belongs to the child, but the father has legal authority over it during minority.
2
Determine the child's status after maturity
The child reaches puberty (adraka) and the father dies. The child now has full legal capacity and wants to reclaim the pledged property.
The father's death is incidental; the same rule applies if the father is alive.
3
Apply the ruling: pledge remains binding
The Hanafi school rules that the child cannot reclaim the property until the debt is paid. The father's disposal is treated as if the child himself had done it after puberty, making the pledge irrevocable (lāzim) from the child's side.
This is because the father stands in the place (qāma maqāmahu) of the child as guardian, so his actions have binding effect.

Child's Right of Recourse After Redeeming Father's Pledge
Ruling on child's recourse after redeeming father's pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a child's right of recourse after redeeming a father's pledge and apply the analogy of mu'ir al-rahn to similar cases.

Core Idea
A child forced to redeem father's pledged property can recover the amount from the father's estate — necessity creates a right of recourse.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: a father pledges his child's property to secure his own debt. The child redeems it but was not expressly ordered to do so. Can he claim reimbursement? The Hanafi school, through careful analogy, affirms his right. Let us walk through the legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Recognize that the father pledges the child's property for the father's own debt. The child pays off the debt to free his property. Determine the legal status of his payment.
The child's payment is not voluntary; it is compelled by necessity.
2
Analyze the element of constraint
The text states the child is 'maztur' (constrained) because his ownership is at risk. Without payment, he would lose his property. This constraint (idhtirar) is the basis for the right of recourse.
Contrast with a voluntary payment: no recourse.
3
Understand the analogy to mu'ir al-rahn
The child is compared to one who lends a pledge (mu'ir al-rahn) to a debtor. When the lender redeems the pledge, he can recover the amount from the debtor. Here, the child is the mu'ir, the father is the debtor.
The analogy justifies the right of recourse even without express authorization.
4
Apply the analogy to the scenario
Just as a mu'ir al-rahn can recourse, the child can reclaim the paid amount from the father's estate. The father's debt is discharged by the child's payment, but the child has a claim against the father.
The father benefits: his debt is paid.
5
Note the extension to destruction of pledge
If the pledge perishes before redemption, the debt is discharged because the pledge serves as security. The child still has recourse because the father's debt was extinguished using the child's property.
This shows the ruling covers both scenarios.

Commentary
This passage addresses the situation where a father pledges his child's property to secure his own (the father's) debt. If the child pays off the debt to redeem his property, he can recover that amount from the father's estate (or from the father if alive). The reasoning: the child is compelled to pay to free his property, analogous to someone lending a pledge (mu'īr al-rahn). Similarly, if the pledged property is destroyed before redemption, the father's debt is discharged (because the pledge compensates), and the child can still seek reimbursement from the father. The commentary further gives a question-answer: even though the father did not order the payment, the child's need to protect his property justifies the recourse. An analogy is provided: Zaid asks Bakr for a loan, Bakr gives his watch as pledge; Bakr later redeems it; he can then recover from Zaid. This illustrates the child's right to recourse.

Key Terms
رجوع (ruju'): Recourse; the right to claim reimbursement from another party.
معير الرهن (mu'īr ar-rahn): One who gives something as a pledge on behalf of another; a lender of a pledge.
قرض (qard): Loan; a debt given or taken.

Father Pledges for Both Debts and Guarantees Share if Pledge Perishes
Ruling on combined pledge and guarantee upon loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guardian pledging a minor's property for combined debts and explain the guarantee obligation upon loss.

Core Idea
When a father pledges his child’s property for both debts, the pledge is valid; if it perishes, he must compensate the child for his debt’s share.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in pledge (rahn) law: a guardian pledging a minor’s property to secure both the guardian’s own debt and a debt owed by the minor. The Hanafi school permits this combined pledge but imposes a guarantee on the guardian if the pledged property is destroyed. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the balance between guardianship authority and protection of the minor’s assets.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the father (or guardian) pledging the minor's property for both his own debt and the minor's debt. Ruling: jā'iz (permissible).
Focus on the combination; each component individually is permissible.
2
Understand the condition for validity
The act is permissible because it encompasses two permissible matters. The key condition is that both underlying debts are valid.
No separate condition beyond the permissibility of each pledge.
3
Analyze the consequence upon loss
If the pledged property perishes, the father guarantees his share (hissah) to the child. This is because he used the child's property to pay his own debt.
The share is the proportion of the father's debt to the total debts secured.
4
Extend the ruling to other guardians
The same rule applies to an executor (wasi) and the paternal grandfather (jad) when there is no father or executor.
This consistency ensures minor's rights are protected regardless of guardian.

Commentary
This passage rules on a combined pledge: a father may pledge his child's property to secure both his own debt and a debt owed by the child. This is permissible because each individual pledge is permissible. However, if the pledged property is destroyed, the father must compensate the child for the portion corresponding to his own debt, because he effectively used the child's property to pay his own debt. The same rule applies to an executor (wasi) and to the paternal grandfather (jad) when there is no father or executor. This ensures the child's property is not unfairly depleted to cover the father's obligation.

Key Terms

حصہ (hissah): Share; the proportion of the value corresponding to the father's debt.
جد (jad): Paternal grandfather; in inheritance and guardianship contexts.
