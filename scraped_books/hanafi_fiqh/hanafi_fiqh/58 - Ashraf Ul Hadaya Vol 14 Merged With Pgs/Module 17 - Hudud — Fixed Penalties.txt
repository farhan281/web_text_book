Applying Hadd to Grain-Based Intoxicants
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether hadd applies to grain-based intoxicants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hadd for grain-based intoxicants and explain the evidence from Imam Muhammad's narration.

Core Idea
Hadd for intoxication applies to all intoxicants, not just grape wine, based on a general narration from Imam Muhammad.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: does the fixed punishment (hadd) for intoxication apply when the intoxicating drink is made from grains like wheat and barley? While some early scholars exempted such drinks, the later accepted position (mufta bihi) holds that hadd is obligatory for any intoxicant, based on a general statement from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is consumption of grain-based drinks leading to intoxication. The ruling under dispute is whether hadd is applied.
2
State the two Hanafi opinions
Faqih Abu Ja'far holds that hadd is not enforced, while the majority of later scholars (mashayikh) hold that it is enforced.
Abu Ja'far's view is based on analogy with bhang and mare's milk.
3
Locate the dalil
The preferred view relies on a narration from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that he imposed hadd on anyone becoming intoxicated from any drink, without specifying the source.
The text says: 'روى عن محمد فيمن سكر من الاشربة انه يحد'
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Imam Muhammad's statement is general (amm), applying to all intoxicating drinks, not limited to grape wine. Therefore, grain-based drinks are included.
The generality is explicit: 'من غير تفصيل' (without specification).
5
Understand the tarjih
The later scholars (mashayikh) preferred the hadd view because it is more effective in deterring public corruption (fasād) given that people gather on these drinks just like on khamr.
This is an example of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil).

This passage addresses whether the hadd punishment for intoxication applies to drinks made from grains like wheat and barley, as opposed to the classic khamr from grapes and dates. There are two opinions within the Hanafi school. Some scholars, like Faqih Abu Ja'far, argue that hadd does not apply because such drinks are akin to bhang (cannabis) or mare's milk, which are not subject to hadd. The Hanafi mufta bihi position is that any intoxicating drink, regardless of its source, incurs the hadd punishment for intoxication.

Extending hadd to milk-based intoxicants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the Hanafi school applies hadd punishment for consuming any intoxicating liquid, including milk-based intoxicants, and explain the proof.

Core Idea
Any intoxicating drink, regardless of base, triggers the hadd of intoxication.

In this passage, the author extends the discussion on hadd to drinks made from milk. While there is a difference of opinion among scholars, the Hanafi school's relied-upon position is that any intoxicant, including milk-based ones, incurs the hadd. This lesson focuses on that general principle and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the substance in question
The passage discusses intoxicating drinks made from milk (alban). It asks whether the hadd ruling applies to them.
Milk can be fermented into an intoxicating beverage.
2
Recall the general Hanafi position on intoxication
The Hanafi school holds that any intoxicating drink, not just Khamr (grape wine), imposes the hadd for intoxication. This is based on the hadith 'Kullu muskirin khamrun wa kullu muskirin haramun' (Every intoxicant is wine, and every intoxicant is forbidden).
This is the ruling from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Extend the ruling to milk drinks
The text states that milk drinks are 'upon this same difference.' This means the same ikhtilaf exists: some say hadd applies, others say no. But the Hanafi position is that hadd applies to any intoxicant.
The 'difference' likely refers to the earlier dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is on whether hadd is for Khamr only.
4
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that consuming an intoxicating milk drink incurs the hadd, identical to other intoxicants.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that 'kullu muskirin haramun wa al-hadd fi kulli muskirin'.

The Permissibility of Mare's Milk
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mare's milk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Abu Hanifa's qiyas argument against the permissibility of mare's milk and the mashayikh's critique, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Qiyas fails when the illa is absent: mare's milk is halal despite the disapproval of horse meat.

This passage delves into a specific intra-Hanafi dispute concerning mare's milk (laban al-rumak). Imam Abu Hanifa argued that mare's milk is not halal by analogy to horse meat, which is legally disliked. However, later scholars (mashayikh) overruled this, holding that the milk is halal because the reason for the meat's dislike does not extend to the milk.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specific case and the positions
The passage discusses whether mare's milk is halal. Abu Hanifa says no (by qiyas to meat); the mashayikh say yes.
Mare's milk is also called laban al-rumak.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's qiyas
Abu Hanifa draws an analogy (qiyas) between milk and meat: since milk is produced from the meat, and horse meat is disliked (makruh), the milk should also be disliked or not halal.
The legal principle is qiyas al-ashbah (analogy of resemblance).
3
Identify the illa (effective cause) of the meat's ruling
The passage gives two possible reasons for the dislike of horse meat: (1) preserving the material means of jihad (maddat al-jihad) and (2) respect for the horse. These are the 'illa' that justify the meat's ruling.
The mashayikh argue that these causes are specific to the meat itself.
4
Apply the mashayikh's refutation
The mashayikh argue that these causes do not apply to milk: drinking milk does not deplete horses used for jihad, nor is it a matter of disrespect. Therefore, the qiyas fails because the illa is not present in the far' (milk).
This is tahqiq al-manat: the illa is not verified in the new case.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
The mashayikh's view is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position: mare's milk is halal.
This is an example of later scholars (fatawa mutakhkhirin) correcting an earlier analogical reasoning.

Hadd for Non-Khamr Intoxicants: Two Hanafi Opinions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of hadd for intoxicants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on whether hadd (prescribed punishment) is imposed for intoxication from beverages other than khamr and identify which opinion is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Hadd is imposed for any intoxicating drink, not just grape wine, according to the relied-upon Hanafi position.

The Hanafi school debated whether the hadd punishment for drinking applies only to khamr (grape wine) or to any intoxicating beverage. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents two qawls (opinions) and declares the second—which imposes hadd broadly—as more authentic (asahh). Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi approach to new intoxicants and the principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two qawls
The first qawl holds that hadd is NOT imposed for non-khamr intoxicants. The second qawl imposes hadd for any intoxicating drink. State each position clearly.
The first qawl is attributed to Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf; the second to Imam Muhammad.

Understand the evidence

Imam Muhammad's practice is cited without detail, indicating a broad application.

The text declares the second qawl as asahh (more correct) and mufta bihi. Note that the first qawl is still a valid Hanafi opinion but not operative for fatwa.
The preference is based on Imam Muhammad's authority and the underlying rationale.

Milk-based wine that becomes intoxicating falls under the same dispute. According to the mufta bihi view, hadd is imposed if intoxication occurs.
This shows the ruling extends to any intoxicating drink, regardless of its base ingredient.

This highlights the importance of knowing the mufta bihi for judges and muftis.

Mare's Milk Wine: Permissibility According to the Mashayikh
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the permissibility of mare's milk wine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifah's prohibition of mare's milk wine and contrast it with the mashayikh's preferred view of permissibility, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Mare's milk wine is halal because the reason for horse meat's prohibition does not extend to its milk.

Within the discussion of intoxicants, the author addresses a special case: wine made from the milk of a mare (لبن الرماك). Abu Hanifah declared it impermissible by analogy to the meat of the horse, which is considered makruh or haram in some contexts. However, the later mashayikh (scholars) corrected this, arguing that the reason for the meat's ruling is specific—either respect for the horse or preserving its use for jihad—and does not apply to milk. This lesson dissects their qiyas and the principle of 'illah (effective cause) evaluation.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifah's position and its basis
Abu Hanifah holds that wine from mare's milk is impermissible (haram). His evidence is qiyas (analogy) to horse meat: since milk originates from meat (blood), the ruling on meat transfers to milk.
The text says 'اعتبارا بلحمها' — by considering the meat.
2
Identify the mashayikh's challenge
The mashayikh (later scholars) argue that the reason (illah) for the meat's prohibition does not apply to milk. They propose two possible reasons for meat's ruling: (1) to preserve the material of jihad, (2) out of respect for the horse. Neither applies to milk.
They declare the more correct (asahh) view that mare's milk wine is halal (permissible).
3
Analyze the critique of qiyas
The mashayikh's critique is that Abu Hanifah's qiyas is based on a superficial analogy (milk from flesh), but the effective cause (illah) of the meat's ruling is specific and non-transmissible to milk. This is a classic case of tahqiq al-manat (verifying the effective cause).
This demonstrates the importance of correctly identifying the illah in qiyas.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text adopts the mashayikh's view as asahh and mufta bihi. Thus, mare's milk is considered halal, and only hadd would apply if it becomes intoxicating (per the second qawl from the previous lesson).
This ruling is specific to mare's milk; other milks follow the general hadd rule.
5
Relate to the broader context
This ruling shows how later Hanafi scholarship (mashayikh) can refine and correct the positions of earlier imams based on deeper analysis of the 'illah. It also highlights the difference between literal analogy (qiyas jali) and analogy based on actual effective cause.
Students should appreciate the dynamic nature of ijtihad within the school.

Penalty for Drinking Wine Dregs: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on hadd for wine dregs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi position on the hadd penalty for drinking wine dregs and EXPLAIN the Hanafi and Shafi'i proofs, identifying the key illa in the dispute.

Core Idea
Hadd for wine dregs applies only if intoxication occurs, because the substance is defective and not consumed for pleasure.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is on the application of the fixed penalty (hadd) for drinking wine dregs. The dispute turns on whether dregs are considered wine or a separate defective substance. The Hanafi ruling, attributed to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, limits hadd to cases of actual intoxication, while Imam al-Shafi'i applies the penalty for any consumption due to the presence of wine components. We will dissect the arguments and the underlying usul principles.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school holds regarding hadd for drinking wine dregs. The text states that no hadd is applied unless the drinker becomes intoxicated.
This is the mufta bihi position: no hadd without sukr.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that hadd is applied for any amount because dregs contain a part (juz') of wine.
The opposing dalil: the presence of wine components invokes the same ruling as pure wine.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i reasoning: consuming any part of wine is like consuming wine itself, hence hadd is obligatory.
This is a literal approach focusing on the substance's origin.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response argues that dregs are defective (naaqis) because natures recoil from them, so a small amount does not lead to large consumption. They are analogous to other intoxicating drinks where hadd is only for intoxication. Additionally, heavy sediment dominates, making it like wine mixed with water.
The illa: the substance's defectiveness and the lack of inviting to excess.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, a person who drinks a tiny amount of dregs without intoxication is not flogged; under Shafi'i law, he is. This highlights the importance of intent and actual harm.
This difference also applies to modern products with trace alcohol.

He (Muhammad) said: The one who drinks it—i.e., the drinker of dregs—is not to be flogged if he does not become intoxicated. Al-Shafi'i said: He is flogged because he drank a part of wine. And for us: A small amount of it does not invite to a large amount because natures recoil from it, so it is defective wine, resembling other drinks besides wine, and there is no hadd in them except for intoxication. And because heavy sediment dominates it, so it becomes like wine that has been mixed with water if water dominates. Translation: Muhammad said: The drinker of dregs will not be flogged if it is not intoxicating. Al-Shafi'i said: Flogging is applied because he drank a part of wine. Our proof: A small amount of it does not lead to a large amount because natures are averse to it, so it is defective wine, resembling other drinks besides wine, and there is no hadd in them except for intoxication. Also because heavy sediment dominates it, so it becomes like wine when water dominates through mixing. Explanation: According to us, the drinker of wine dregs will not receive hadd unless intoxication occurs; when intoxication occurs, he will be flogged. Because it cannot be given the rulings of wine, since natures are repelled by it, so it is defective wine, falling under the ruling of other drinks, which only carry hadd if intoxication results. Then in dregs, there is sediment that dominates, so it is like wine over which water has become dominant; its drinker has no hadd. Imam al-Shafi'i says: Hadd applies because when he drank dregs, it contains components of wine, so the ruling of wine is applied.

Commentary
This is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the hadd penalty (fixed punishment for drinking wine) for someone who drinks wine dregs (durdiy). The Hanafi position, stated by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, is that no hadd (flogging) is to be applied unless the drinker becomes intoxicated (sukr). The Hanafi reasoning is that dregs are not pure wine; they are a defective form (naaqis) from which natures recoil, so a small amount does not lead to a large amount of consumption. They are analogous to other non-wine intoxicating drinks, for which hadd is only for intoxication, not for mere consumption. Additionally, the heavy sediment (thaqal) dominates, like wine mixed with water where water is dominant; in such cases, hadd is not applied unless intoxication occurs. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that hadd is applied regardless because dregs contain some part of wine (juz'), and any part of wine triggers the same ruling as wine itself. The Hanafi view is more contextual, considering the actual intoxicating potential and the nature of the substance.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): Fixed corporal punishment for drinking wine, typically flogging.
سكر (sukr): Intoxication, loss of mental control.
ناقص (naaqis): Defective, incomplete; here referring to wine dregs as defective wine.

Hadd for Wine Dregs: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Dispute on hadd for wine dregs between Hanafi and Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on hadd for drinking wine dregs and explain the thamarah al-khilaf with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Hadd for drinking wine dregs requires intoxication in Hanafi, but not in Shafi'i.

This passage addresses a specific dispute: does a person who drinks the dregs (dirdi) of wine incur the hadd punishment? The Hanafi school, as represented by Imam Muhammad, holds that no hadd is applied unless the drinker becomes intoxicated. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that any part of wine triggers hadd. Understanding this difference illustrates how each school defines the 'substance' of wine and the role of intention versus effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is drinking dirdi (wine dregs). The dispute is whether it incurs hadd (fixed punishment). Hanafi: no hadd unless sukr. Shafi'i: hadd applies regardless of intoxication.
Note the difference in focus: substance (Shafi'i) vs. effect (Hanafi).
2
Follow the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof has multiple strands: (a) natural aversion to dregs makes it deficient wine, similar to other intoxicating drinks where hadd is only for sukr; (b) analogy to water-diluted wine where hadd does not apply without intoxication; (c) the predominance of sediment over wine components.
The qiyas (analogy) to water-diluted wine is key.
3
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi, if a person drinks dregs and does not become intoxicated, no hadd. Under Shafi'i, they would receive 80 lashes. This affects the mufti's ruling in real cases.
Practical consequence of the ikhtilaf.

Commentary
Passage type: Inter-madhhab dispute. The issue is whether a person who drinks the dregs (dirdi) of wine is subject to the hadd punishment (the fixed penalty for drinking wine). According to Imam Muhammad (in the matn), the hadd is not applied unless the person becomes intoxicated. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the hadd is applied simply because the dregs contain parts of wine. The Hanafi proof is threefold: (1) A small amount of dregs does not lead to a large amount (i.e., it lacks the inviting quality of wine due to natural aversion), making it deficient wine, which is treated like other non-wine intoxicants where hadd is only for intoxication. (2) By analogy, if water dominates wine, the drinker is not hadd unless intoxicated; here, the sediment (thiqal) dominates the wine. (3) The nature of dregs is such that it is repulsive, so it is not considered true khamr. Therefore, the Hanafi position is the relied-upon view. The practical result (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Hanafi law, a person drinking dregs without intoxication incurs no hadd, whereas under Shafi'i law, he would.

حد (hadd): The fixed corporal punishment for drinking wine (khamr), typically 80 lashes.
دردی (dirdī): The sediment or dregs of wine, still containing wine components.
سکر (sukr): Intoxication, the condition of losing one's senses due to alcohol.
