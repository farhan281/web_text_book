Conditions for Halal Hunting with Dogs
Rulings on conditions for halal hunting with dogs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three essential conditions for hunting with dogs to be halal and identify which omissions render the game haram.

Core Idea
A game animal is halal only if the dog is trained, the hunter recites tasmiyah, and the dog is released by a Muslim.

This lesson examines the Hanafi conditions for valid hunting with dogs. The ruling is not automatic upon catching; specific requirements must be met at the moment of release and during the kill.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling category
Hunting with a dog is an act of ibaha (permissible) but with conditions. Identify the hukm level: halal when conditions met, haram when conditions violated.
2
List the required conditions
The first condition: the dog must be mu'allam (trained). The second condition: the hunter must recite tasmiyah at the time of release. The third condition: the release must be by a Muslim.
The condition of training can be waived if a Muslim calls an untrained dog and recites tasmiyah, according to the passage.
3
Identify what invalidates the hunt
If the dog is untrained (unless called by Muslim with tasmiyah), if tasmiyah is omitted intentionally, or if the dog belongs to an ignorant or Magian person (since they are not qualified to release), the game becomes haram.
Hanafi scholars differentiate between intentional and forgetful omission of tasmiyah; intentional makes haram, forgetful may be makruh but the relied-upon position requires tasmiyah.
4
Apply to given scenarios
Apply the conditions to each scenario: Muslim with trained dog -> halal; dog of Magian/ignorant -> haram; omitting tasmiyah intentionally -> haram; calling a non-sent dog with tasmiyah -> halal.

Partnership and Ownership in Dog Hunting
Rulings on partnership and ownership in dog hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine ownership of game when multiple dogs are involved and state the ruling on partnership in hunting.

Core Idea
When two dogs from different owners contribute to the kill, the game is halal and ownership belongs to the one whose dog killed, unless they are partners.

This lesson covers scenarios where multiple dogs are used in hunting. The Hanafi school provides clear rules for determining ownership and permissibility when dogs act together.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
There are two dogs from two different Muslim owners. One dog wounds the game, the other kills it. Determine if the game is halal.
2
Apply the ruling on partnership
The passage states that if one dog wounds and the other kills, the game is halal. This indicates that the killing action validates the hunt, regardless of the wound.
3
Determine ownership
The passage asks who owns the game. The Hanafi principle is that ownership goes to the one who released the dog that effectively caught and killed the game. If both are partners, they share.
If the dogs are released by different owners and both contribute, the game is owned by the one whose dog made the final kill, unless previous agreement.
4
Consider the scenario where the hunter himself kills after the dog wounds
The passage also says if a Muslim releases a dog, it wounds the game, and then he kills it again, the game is halal. This reinforces that the act of killing by a human is also valid.

Shooting Based on Movement or Sound: Rulings and Conditions
Ruling on shooting based on movement or sound, and the principle of Zahir al-Riwayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game caught by shooting after sensing movement or sound, and apply the conditions that the game must be fit for hunting and tasmiyah must be recited.

Core Idea
Game is halal only if the hunter intended the act of hunting and the animal is fit for consumption.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario from the chapter on shooting: a hunter shoots or releases an animal based on sensing movement or sound. The Hanafi school provides clear rulings on the halal status of the game, relying on the principle that the hunter's intention and the animal's fitness determine the outcome. This lesson will guide you through the legal reasoning and conditions involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and the Act
Read the passage and understand the situation: the hunter perceives movement or sound, then shoots or releases a dog. The game is caught dead or alive. Identify the key elements.
The act can be ramy (shooting) or irsal (releasing a dog).
2
State the Ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah
The proof from Zahir al-Riwayah states that when the act of hunting is given, the target (musab) that is ready and capable (bi qadr ra's ati'dad wa salahiyya) is halal. Explain what this means.
The phrase 'فعل اصطیاد' indicates the action must be specifically directed towards hunting.
3
Apply the Condition of Readiness and Capability
Determine whether the animal hit is a legitimate game animal (halal to eat and capable of being hunted). If it is not (e.g., pig, prohibited animal), it is not halal even if intended.
The animal must be fit (salahiyya) for hunting in the sense of being permissible to consume.
4
Distinguish Cases Where the Hit Animal is Not the Intended Target
If the hunter aims at one animal but hits another, the ruling depends on the status of the original target. If the original target was not game, the hit animal is not halal. If the original target was game, then the hit animal is halal because the hunter intended to hunt that game (the one he sensed).
The intention is key: the hunter must have intended the object of his perception.
5
State the Rulings on Tasmiyah and Dhabh
Tasmiyah (saying Bismillah) is wajib for both shooting and releasing. If the game is found alive, proper slaughter (dhabh) is necessary; otherwise, it remains haram.
This is a standing condition across hunting methods.

Ruling on game wounded by arrow that escapes then found dead

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for game hit by an arrow that dies immediately versus that which escapes and is found with another wound, and apply the condition that the cause of death must be solely the arrow.

Core Idea
Game is halal only if the arrow is the sole cause of death; any other wound makes it haram.

This passage addresses a common hunting scenario: what if the game does not die instantly? The Hanafi school requires certainty that the arrow caused the death. If the animal falls dead directly after being hit, it is halal. But if it escapes and is later found with another wound, the death is attributed to that other cause, making the meat carrion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct cases: (1) the game is hit, escapes with difficulty, and is found dead later; (2) the game is hit and falls directly to the ground dead. Recognize which scenario leads to which ruling.
The first scenario includes an intermediate question about other wounds.
2
Understand the condition for the escape scenario
In the escape scenario, the ruling depends on whether there is any wound besides the arrow. If there is another wound (e.g., from a fall or animal attack), the game is haram because the cause of death is uncertain.
The uncertain cause makes it carrion (murdar).
3
Identify the ruling for direct fall
If the arrow hits and the game falls directly to the ground and dies, the meat is halal because the arrow is clearly the cause of death.
4
Apply the principle of certainty
The key principle is that the cause of death must be certain and solely attributed to the hunter's shot. Any doubt or intervention nullifies the permissibility.
This follows the general Hanafi rule that uncertainty in the cause of death makes the animal carrion.

General Principle of Halal and Haram in Hunting
A general principle distinguishing avoidable and unavoidable causes in hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle distinguishing avoidable and unavoidable causes in hunting and apply it to determine the permissibility of game.

Core Idea
If the cause of death is unavoidable and directly from the hunt, the game is halal.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi maxim in hunting jurisprudence: the distinction between causes that can be avoided and those that cannot. Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly determining whether game killed during hunting is permissible to eat. The passage provides a specific example to illustrate the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The passage states a general rule that distinguishes between causes from which avoidance is possible and those from which it is not.
This is a qa'ida kulliyya.
2
Understand the two categories
Categories: (1) Causes that can be avoided, (2) Causes that cannot be avoided. The principle is applied based on whether the cause is unavoidable.
3
Analyze the example
The example: The game falls on a stone and stops. Since the fall on the stone is an unavoidable consequence of being shot, the game is halal.
The stone is not an independent cause.
4
Apply the rule
For other cases, determine if the cause of death is a direct and unavoidable result of the hunting action. If yes, halal; if an independent avoidable cause intervenes, it is haram.
5
Note the implication
Any independent avoidable cause (e.g., falling into water, being struck by another object) would make the game haram.

Core Idea
If an arrow severs a part of game, the Hanafi school deems the entire animal halal because the arrow acts as a slaughtering tool — the Shafi'i school disagrees.

In this lesson, we explore a classical fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerning game that is struck by an arrow which cuts off a piece of its body before the animal dies. The central question: is the meat of such an animal halal (lawful) or haram (unlawful)? The Hanafi school, relying on the principle that the arrow serves as a tool of dhabh (ritual slaughter), considers the entire animal halal. The Shafi'i school, in contrast, holds that the animal becomes carrion (maytah) because proper dhabh was not performed on the throat. This dispute illustrates how differing methodologies in usul al-fiqh lead to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling on the scenario: the entire game is halal and permissible to eat. Note that the severed part is also halal (though that nuance may be addressed separately).

2
Identify the opposing school and their view
The Shafi'i school holds that the game is haram (carrion) because the animal did not receive a proper throat cut (dhabh) after being wounded.
Imam al-Shafi'i requires the dhabh to be performed on the throat specifically.

The Shafi'i dalil: the animal was not slaughtered in the prescribed manner; the arrow wound is not a valid dhabh because it cuts elsewhere, so the animal dies without proper Islamic slaughter, thus it is maytah.

The Hanafis rely on hadith and analogy (qiyas) with killing by a trained dog.

Identify the Legal Category

2
Examine the Primary Proof
The hadith 'People are partners in three things: water, grass, and fire' provides the basis for partnership. However, the Hanafis restrict this sharing to drinking only, based on consensus (ijma). Explain why the general word 'water' is limited to shurb.

3
Follow the Rational Proofs
The text gives two rational proofs: (a) water in a well is mubah (ownerless) until actually possessed (ihraz), so the owner cannot prevent others from drinking; (b) necessity: people need water for themselves and their animals, so preventing drinking causes great hardship (haraj).
The analogy to a deer in someone's land illustrates the principle of ihraz.

If someone wants to use the assigned water to irrigate a revived land, the people (ahl al-nahr) can prevent him, whether or not it harms them. Reason: irrigation is not a necessity like drinking, and allowing it would cut off the benefit of shurb.
This shows the practical thamarah al-khilaf (consequence of the distinction).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the third type of water, which is water that has been assigned turns (like in a well or river with a fixed rotation). The ruling is that the right to drink (shurb) remains public and cannot be prevented, but the right to irrigate (saqi) can be prohibited. The text provides three proofs: (1) The hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) that people are partners in water, grass, and fire. However, this hadith was interpreted to apply only to drinking, not irrigation, by consensus. (2) A rational analogy: water in a well without appropriation is mubah (ownerless), like a wild deer on one's land; ownership only comes through actual possession (ihraz). Since the water is not yet owned, the owner cannot prevent others from drinking. (3) Necessity: people need water for themselves and their animals wherever they go, so preventing drinking would cause severe hardship. The text then adds a sub-ruling: if someone wants to use that same water to irrigate land he has revived, the people (as hab al-nahr) have the right to stop him, because irrigation is not a necessity and would deprive them of drinking water.

شرب (shurb): The right to drink water, as opposed to irrigation (saqi).
احراز (ihraz): Act of taking possession or appropriating something that is originally mubah.
مباح (mubah): Property that is originally ownerless and available for public use.

Owner's Right to Prevent Drinking from His Well
Ruling on a water owner's duty to allow drinking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an owner's right to prevent a thirsty person from entering his property to drink from his well, and identify the condition that changes the ruling.

Core Idea
Private water ownership gives control, but necessity compels sharing when no alternative exists.

This lesson examines a specific Hanafi ruling on water rights from private property. The passage, narrated from Imam al-Tahawi, balances the owner's property rights with the necessity of thirst. You will learn the two scenarios: when the owner may prevent entry and when he must provide water, including the condition to avoid damage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the owner preventing a thirsty person from entering his property to drink from a well, spring, reservoir, or river. The ruling is that the owner may prevent entry only if there is other water available nearby.
The ruling is conditional, not absolute.
2
Locate the condition for permission
The condition for the owner to prevent entry is that the thirsty person can find other water elsewhere, specifically on public land (not owned by anyone). If such water exists, the owner can refuse access.
The water must be 'near this water' and in 'no one's property'.
3
Determine the obligation when no alternative exists
If no other water is available, the owner cannot prevent entry. Instead, he must either give the person water to drink or allow him to take it himself. This becomes an obligation (wajib) to avoid harm.
The obligation is to provide water, but with a condition to protect the bank.
4
Note the additional condition to avoid damage
Even when allowing access, the thirsty person must not break the bank (ضفة) of the water source. This protects the owner's property from damage.
This condition applies in both scenarios when access is granted.

Ruling on Muthallath: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute and Its Resolution
Dispute over permissibility of grape syrup boiled to one-third

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of the Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad on muthallath, identify their proofs, and state the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Muthallath is haram in the operative Hanafi position, despite a permissible view from the Shaykhayn.

The ruling on muthallath (concentrated grape syrup) is a classic intra-Hanafi dispute with significant practical implications. While the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) permitted it under restricted conditions, Imam Muhammad declared it absolutely haram, and his view became the relied-upon fatwa. This lesson unpacks the proofs on both sides and the reasoning that led to the final position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the disagreement
Muthallath is grape syrup boiled to one-third. The core question: is it permissible or forbidden when it can intoxicate?
Note that muthallath is not khamr (wine); it is a different substance.
2
State the Shaykhayn's position and their proof
The Shaykhayn permit muthallath when consumed for obedience (ta'at), but forbid it for revelry. Their proof: hadith 'Khamr is forbidden for its essence' and 'intoxication from every drink,' with the conjunction indicating that only the intoxicating cup is forbidden.
They also argue that khamr's small quantity is forbidden because it calls to more (da'i), but muthallath is thick and does not call.
3
State Imam Muhammad's position and his proof
Imam Muhammad, along with Malik and Shafi'i, declares muthallath absolutely haram. Their proofs: hadith 'Every intoxicant is khamr,' 'What intoxicates in large quantity, its small is forbidden,' and 'A gulp from an intoxicating jar is forbidden.' Plus rational: intoxicant corrupts the intellect.
Imam Muhammad has three additional narrations: permissible, makruh, and suspension (tawaqquf).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opposing proof
The text responds that the opponent's first hadith is not established (ghayr thabit); even if accepted, it applies only to the last cup that actually intoxicates. For khamr, small is a da'i (call to) large; muthallath is not.
This shows the Hanafi method of evaluating hadith authenticity and legal interpretation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the thamarah al-khilaf
The fatwa is on Imam Muhammad's view, making muthallath haram in all cases. Practical difference: Shaykhayn allow it for lawful use; Imam Muhammad forbids it absolutely.
The later Hanafi scholars preferred the stricter view as a precaution against intoxication.

Commentary
This passage presents the inter-madhhab dispute concerning muthallath (grape syrup boiled to one-third). The author first states the ruling: the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) permit it when consumed for strength in obedience, but forbid it if for revelry (by consensus). Imam Muhammad, Imam Malik, and Imam al-Shafi'i forbid it absolutely, and the fatwa relies on Imam Muhammad's view. Three additional narrations from Imam Muhammad are mentioned: one allowing it (like the Shaykhayn), one declaring it makruh, and one suspending judgment. Then the author presents the proofs. Imam Muhammad's dalil relies on three hadith: (1) 'Every intoxicant is khamr' – equating any intoxicant with wine; (2) 'What intoxicates in large quantity, its small quantity is forbidden' – applying the principle that if the large causes intoxication, the small is also forbidden; (3) 'What intoxicates from a jar, a single gulp is forbidden' – emphasizing even minimal consumption. Additionally, a rational argument states that since intoxicants corrupt the intellect, both small and large amounts are alike. The Shaykhayn's dalil uses the hadith 'Khamr is forbidden for its own essence (li 'aynihi), its small and its large,' and the phrase 'intoxication from every drink' to differentiate khamr from other intoxicants via the conjunction (al-'atf lil-mughayarah). Their rational proof is that the actual corrupting agent is the intoxicating cup, not the entire drink; hence, only the intoxicating portion is forbidden. The author then addresses an objection to the Shaykhayn: why is a small amount of khamr forbidden when it does not intoxicate? The response explains that khamr's subtlety and fluidity lead one to consume more, so the small takes the ruling of the large by way of da'i (cause). Muthallath, by contrast, is thick and nourishing, so it does not lead to intoxication and remains permissible. Moreover, the first hadith (used by the opponent) is deemed unauthentic (ghayr thabit) and, if accepted, is interpreted to apply only to the last cup that actually causes intoxication. Thus the Shaykhayn maintain that muthallath is permissible except when it specifically intoxicates.

Conditions for Hunting with Trained Jawarih
States conditions and proof for hunting with trained jawarih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for lawful hunting with trained jawarih and apply the Quranic proof from Quran 5:4 to justify the permissibility.

Core Idea
Hunting with any trained fanged beast or clawed bird is permissible; the Quranic 'jawarih' is not limited to dogs.

Hunting with trained animals is a practical necessity in many environments. The Hanafi school, relying on a broad interpretation of Quran 5:4, permits the use of any trained predatory beast (dhi nab) or bird (dhi mikhlab) for hunting, provided the animal is properly trained and the conditions of slaughter are met. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proof from the Quran and Hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting using trained predatory animals (jawarih). According to Al-Quduri and Al-Jami' al-Saghir, it is permissible (jā'iz) with trained dogs, cheetahs, falcons, and all other trained jawarih.
The permissibility is conditional on the animal being trained and meeting the criteria of dhi nab or dhi mikhlab.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 5:4: '…and what you have taught of the jawarih, training them… so eat of what they catch for you and mention the name of Allah upon it.' The text also cites the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim and the linguistic argument that 'kalb' includes all beasts of prey.
The Quranic verse is decisive in transmission (qat'i al-thubut) but open to interpretation (zanni al-dalala) because of the words 'jawarih' and 'mukallibin'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'jawarih' is interpreted as 'kawasib' (earners), meaning animals that earn by hunting. 'Mukallibin' is interpreted as 'musallitin' (those who set on), not specifically dogs. Thus the verse's generality includes all trained beasts and birds that hunt with fangs or claws.
The imperative 'kulu' (eat) indicates permissibility (ibaha). The condition 'mention the name of Allah' is a recommendation (sunnah), not an obligation for validity according to the Hanafi school.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) restrict the permissibility to dogs only, based on a more literal reading of 'mukallibin' as dog trainers. The Hanafi school holds that the linguistic usage of 'kalb' in Arabic includes all beasts of prey, as evidenced by the Prophet's supplication against 'Utbah bin Abi Lahab where a lion was sent.
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) in the school, though the difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi tarjih relies on the generality of the Quranic verse and the linguistic scope of 'kalb'. The hadith about the lion supports that 'kalb' can mean any predatory beast. No specific hadith restricts it to dogs, so the general permissibility remains.
The text cites Al-Quduri and Al-Jami' al-Saghir as authoritative, indicating the position is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hunting (Kitab al-Sayd) presents a ruling by al-Quduri, expanded by the commentator of al-Hidayah, on the liability of a second hunter who kills game already wounded by a first hunter. The passage type is a ruling with proof, specifically using qiyas (analogy) to a sick slave. The ruling: When the first hunter shoots a game animal with a debilitating arrow that makes it impossible to escape, the game becomes owned by the first hunter. If a second hunter then shoots and kills the game, the second hunter is liable to the first for the game's value, but minus the depreciation caused by the first wound. The value is assessed on the day of destruction, not the day of payment. The author then provides a condition (takwil) from al-Hidayah: this ruling applies only when it is known that the second arrow was the cause of death and the first arrow was survivable. The commentator further explains that if the first wound did not cause halwah (incapacitation), the game remains halal for the first, and the second has no liability. The analogy to a sick slave illustrates that the second compensates for a defective animal, not a healthy one. This ruling is based on the principle that ownership was established by the first debilitating shot, and the second is a trespasser who destroys owned property.

Key Terms
ضمان (ḍamān): Liability to pay compensation for destruction of property.
مثخن (mutkhin): A debilitating shot that prevents the game from escaping.
حلوہ (ḥalwah): The state of being incapacitated such that the game cannot escape.

Second Hunter's Liability When Both Wounds Contribute to Death
Ruling from al-Ziyadat on second hunter's liability when both wounds cause death or cause unknown.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three components of the second hunter's liability and apply them to a numerical example.

Core Idea
When death results from both wounds, the second hunter pays damage plus half the current value plus half the meat value.

This lesson examines a ruling from Imam Muhammad's al-Ziyadat about a joint-hunting scenario. You will learn the precise compensation formula and its rationale, enabling you to calculate liability correctly.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the factual scenario
Determine whether it is known that both wounds caused death, or if it is unknown which wound killed. The ruling applies only when death can be attributed to both wounds.
If the first wound is instantly fatal, a different ruling applies.
2
Identify the three components of liability
The second hunter must pay: (a) the reduction in value caused by his wound, (b) half the current value of the game wounded by both wounds, and (c) half the value of the meat.
These three are explicitly stated in al-Ziyadat.
3
Apply the formula to a numerical example
Using the commentary's example (starting value 20, after first wound 16, after second wound 12, meat value 6), calculate each component: damage = 4, half current value = 6, half meat = 3, total = 13.
Check that the first hunter's damage is already accounted for separately.
4
Understand the rationale
Since both wounds contributed to death, the second hunter is treated as destroying half the animal. He does not pay for half the meat again because that is already included in the live animal compensation.
This avoids double compensation (takrir al-daman).
5
Note the authority
This ruling is from Imam Muhammad's al-Ziyadat, a text of Zahir al-Riwayah, and thus authoritative within the Hanafi school.
Zahir al-Riwayah texts are the highest authority in the school.
