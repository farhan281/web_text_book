Ushr and Kharaj on Revived Land
Ruling on ushr and kharaj for Muslim reviving dead land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on ushr and kharaj for a Muslim who revives dead land, and identify the exception when kharaj becomes due.

Core Idea
A Muslim reviving dead land pays ushr; kharaj applies only when irrigated with kharaj water.

This passage addresses the tax liability on land revived by a Muslim. The key distinction is between ushr (tithe on produce) and kharaj (land tax). The Hanafi position is that ushr is wajib because kharaj cannot be imposed initially on a Muslim. However, if the land is watered with kharaj water, kharaj becomes due. Understanding this distinction is important for proper application of zakat and land taxation.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling on the tax liability for a Muslim who revives dead land. The Arabic matn establishes that the reviver owns only the usufruct (istighlal) of the land, not the corpus (raqabah). The Urdu commentary explains that consequently, the land is subject to ushr (tithe) rather than kharaj (land tax), because it is not permissible to impose kharaj on a Muslim initially. However, if the land is irrigated with water from kharaj land (e.g., water from a kharaji canal), then kharaj becomes applicable. This exception is based on the principle that kharaj is tied to the water source. The hadith referred to ('man ahya ardan mayyitan fahiya lahu') is the basis for ownership through revival, and the tax follows from the nature of the land.

Key Terms
عشر (ushr): A tithe (10% or 5%) on the produce of land owned by a Muslim, payable as zakat or tax.
خراج (kharaj): A land tax imposed on conquered lands, typically on non-Muslim owners or on land irrigated by kharaj water.
استغلال (istighlal): Usufruct – the right to use and derive benefit from land, as opposed to ownership of the corpus (raqabah).
