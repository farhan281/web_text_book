The Ruling on Hunting After a Mistaken Sound: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Dispute and proof for hitting game when hunter mistakes sound

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the permissibility of game caught when the hunter mistook the sound, and explain the reasoning behind the differences between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Zufar.

Core Idea
The act of hunting is valid regardless of the source of the sound; the permissibility of the struck game depends on its own nature.

Hunting involves intention and action. But what happens when a hunter acts based on a mistaken sound? The Hanafi school presents a nuanced ruling with internal differences. In this passage from the Hidayah, we explore a scenario where a hunter hears a sound, thinks it is game, shoots, and hits different game. The key issue is whether the act qualifies as hunting (istiyad). We will analyze the three positions — the relied-upon view of Zahir al-Riwayah (Abu Hanifa) and the dissents of Abu Yusuf and Zufar — and the proof that supports the majority position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Legal Question
The scenario: a hunter hears a sound, assumes it is game, shoots or releases a hunting animal, and hits game that may be different from the one whose sound was heard. The legal question: is the caught game (musab) halal?
Focus on the key variables: the source of the sound (game vs. human/domestic) and the nature of the struck animal (edible, predatory, pig).
2
State the Zahir al-Riwayah (Abu Hanifa) Position
According to the authoritative transmitted position, the game is halal regardless of the type of animal whose sound was heard (edible, non-edible, pig). The act of hunting (istiyad) is the criterion, and it applies to all game.
This is the mufta bihi view.
3
Identify the Opposing Views of Abu Yusuf and Zufar
Abu Yusuf excludes the pig (khanzir) because it is najis al-ayn; if the sound was from a pig, the struck game is not halal. Zufar excludes all non-edible animals (ghayr ma'kul al-lahm) because releasing a hunting animal for them is not for permissibility.
Note the different rationales: extreme impurity vs. purpose of the act.
4
Analyze the Proof (Dalil) for the Zahir al-Riwayah Position
The proof is based on qiyas: the term 'istiyad' is not restricted to edible meat; it applies to any game. The act of hunting is permissible in itself. The permissibility of consumption depends on the object's capacity (meat, skin, etc.). This is analogous to shooting at one game and hitting another, which is permissible.
The key phrase: 'ism al-istiyad la yakhtassu bi al-ma'kul'.
5
Apply the Distinction: Sound from Human or Domestic Animal
If the sound turns out to be from a human or a domesticated animal (e.g., a pet bird, a tied deer), the struck game is not halal because the act is not considered hunting (istiyad). The intention alone is insufficient; the act must objectively be hunting.
This reinforces that the act's classification, not the hunter's assumption, is decisive.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hunting (Kitab al-Sayd) deals with a scenario where a hunter hears a sound, assumes it is the sound of game, and shoots or releases a hunting animal. The arrow or animal then hits and kills some game, which may be different from the animal whose sound was heard. The central question: is the resulting game halal? The passage presents the position of Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmitted position of the Hanafi school, from the six core works of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) and two dissenting views from Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Zufar. The Zahir al-Riwayah holds that the game is halal regardless of what type of animal the sound came from, as long as the sound was indeed from game. The key principle is that the act of hunting (istiyad) is the criterion, not the edibility of the meat. Imam Abu Yusuf excludes the pig specifically because of its extreme impurity (najis al-ayn), arguing that no part of a pig can be made permissible. Imam Zufar excludes all non-edible animals (ghayr ma'kul al-lahm) because releasing a hunting animal for them is not for permissibility (i.e., not for consumption). The passage then provides the proof (dalil) for the Zahir al-Riwayah position: the term 'istiyad' (hunting) is not restricted to edible animals; hunting is a permissible act in itself. The permissibility of consumption then depends on the object that is hit: if it is an edible animal, its meat and skin are permissible; if it is a predatory animal, only its skin may be used; if it is a pig, nothing is permissible. The analogy is made to hitting unintended game: if one shoots at one game and accidentally hits another, the second is also halal because the act is still considered hunting. Finally, the passage addresses the case where the sound turns out to be from a human or a domesticated animal: in that case, the game struck is not halal because the act is not one of hunting. Domestic birds that return to houses and tied deer are considered domesticated. The commentary explains that the key is whether the act is classified as istiyad; if yes, the resulting animal's permissibility depends on its own type (edible, predatory, or pig). If not, the animal is not halal. This passage illustrates an intra-Hanafi scholarly difference (ikhtilaf) and the authority weight: the Zahir al-Riwayah position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.

Key Terms
اصطياد (istiyad): The act of hunting, i.e., pursuing and capturing game. In this passage, it refers to the intentional act of a hunter aiming at what he believes is game.
مصاب (musab): The game that is hit and caught by the arrow or hunting animal. It is the direct object of the hunt.
آہٹ (aahat): Urdu word for sound or noise. In this context, it refers to the sound made by an animal (or human) that the hunter hears.
نجس العین (najis al-'ayn): Intrinsically impure; the pig's entire body is considered inherently impure, so no part can be made pure or permissible.

Refuting Imam Zufar's Interpretation of the Hadith on Pledge Increase
Refuting Imam Zufar's interpretation of a hadith on pledge increase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Imam Zufar's interpretation and the mainstream Hanafi interpretation of the hadith on pledge increase, and state the evidence from Ali that supports the mainstream view.

Core Idea
The increase on a pledged property belongs to the mortgagor, not the mortgagee; the mortgagee is a trustee.

In this passage, the commentator responds to Imam Zufar's argument that a hadith about increase and decrease in pledge implies the mortgagee is liable for decrease and entitled to increase. The clarification is that the hadith actually refers to the sale of the pledged property, not its destruction. The proof is a narration from Ali that the mortgagee is a trustee regarding increase, which would be contradicted if the hadith were about destruction.

Guided Path
1
State the disagreement

This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
2

The key question is whether the hadith's context is destruction or sale. The commentator argues that Imam Zufar mistook the context.
3
Analyze the evidence from Ali
The commentator cites a narration from Ali that 'the mortgagee is a trustee over increase.' This implies that if the property increases, the mortgagee does not own it—it belongs to the mortgagor.
This is a Qawl Sahabi, used as evidence.
4

If the hadith were about destruction, the mortgagee would be liable for decrease and entitled to increase, contradicting Ali's statement. Therefore, the hadith must be about sale to avoid contradiction.
This is a method of tawfiq (reconciliation) between proofs.
5
Conclude the ruling
The mainstream position is that the mortgagee is a trustee for increase; any increase in the pledged property belongs to the mortgagor. The mortgagee is only entitled to repayment of the debt.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Therefore, some scholars said that 'jabran an al-rahin' is a scribal or reader's error, and some scholars interpreted it as 'without his consent'. For details, see (Nata'ij al-Afkar).

Commentary
This passage highlights a textual issue in the original manuscript. The phrase 'جبراً عن الراهن' (coercively upon the pawner) is considered problematic. Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani) is reported to have used the phrase 'حسین اعسر الراهن' (Husayn is more distressed than the pawner) instead. Early Hanafi authorities like al-Samarqandi and al-Bazdawi confirm this. Consequently, some scholars argue that 'جبراً عن الراهن' is a mistake by the scribe or reader, while others interpret it as meaning 'without the pawner's consent'. The commentator directs the reader to Nata'ij al-Afkar for further detail.

Key Terms
جبرا عن الراهن (jabran 'an al-rahin): A problematic phrase in the text meaning 'coercively upon the pawner'.
حسین اعسر الراهن (husayn a'sar al-rahin): The correct phrase according to Imam Muhammad: 'Husayn is more distressed than the pawner'.
