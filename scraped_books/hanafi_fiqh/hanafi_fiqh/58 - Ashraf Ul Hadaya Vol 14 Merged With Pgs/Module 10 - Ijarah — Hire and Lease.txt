Cost Sharing for a Common Canal: Abu Hanifa vs the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on cost sharing for digging a common canal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Abu Hanifa's position on cost sharing for a common canal and explain his analogy-based proof.

Core Idea
Benefit obtained means cost obligation ends: Abu Hanifa exempts upper landowners once their land is irrigated.

When multiple landowners share a canal, who should pay for digging the portion that serves only downstream lands? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differed on this question. This lesson unpacks Abu Hanifa's argument that the upper landowner, having already received the benefit, is not obligated to finance the benefit of others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
State the practical question: Do owners of upper land along a common canal have to contribute to the cost of digging the section that lies beyond their own property?
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds that once the canal passes a person's land and that person has obtained irrigation benefit, he is exempt from further costs.
3
Analyze His Proof
Examine the two analogies he uses: (1) The purpose of digging is benefit; once benefit is secured, no further obligation. (2) The owner of a channel on another's roof is not required to maintain that roof.
4
Contrast with the Sahibayn's View
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani argue that all owners must share costs to the end, because the lower owners also need water and surplus water must go somewhere.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, upper owners stop paying after their own land; under the Sahibayn, all continue sharing. Later Hanafi texts consider Abu Hanifa's view the more correct one (asahh).
The tarjih is mentioned in later Hidayah: 'والاول اصح'.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute about cost sharing (sarfa) for digging a common canal. Imam Abu Hanifa argues that the cost should not be imposed on those whose land is already irrigated once the canal passes their property. His proof: the purpose of digging is to obtain irrigation benefit; once that benefit is secured for the upper landowner, he is not required to finance the benefit of others. Moreover, the owner of the lower land (the channel) is not obliged to maintain the channel—analogous to a case where someone has a right to pass water over another's roof; the roof owner maintains it, not the water user. Additionally, the upper landowner can block the water at his own boundary, so he is not forced to let water flow downstream.

Key Terms
صارفة (sarfah): The cost or expense of digging a canal, shared proportionally among beneficiaries

D) Never

Key Takeaway
Imam Abu Hanifa exempts upper landowners from canal digging costs beyond their own benefit, based on the principle that obligation follows direct benefit.

When Is a Landowner Released from Digging Costs?
Intra-Hanafi ruling on when sarfa is lifted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions within the Hanafi school regarding when a landowner is relieved from contributing to canal digging costs, and apply the relied-upon view based on the rational proof (dalil aqli).

Core Idea
Cost ends when digging passes beyond his land, not his channel mouth.

In Hanafi fiqh, when partners share a canal, the cost of digging is distributed proportionally. This lesson examines the precise point at which a particular landowner stops bearing that cost. The source presents two positions: one from Imam Muhammad and the relied-upon view from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Understanding the reasoning sharpens your ability to apply similar rules of shared expenses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed point
The issue: At what stage does a landowner's obligation to share digging costs (sarfa) end? Two answers: (1) when digging passes beyond his land, or (2) when it passes the mouth of his private channel.
Both views are within the Hanafi school.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
They hold that the cost ends once the digging has gone beyond the land of that owner. This is the first opinion mentioned.
This is also the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
3
State the position of Imam Muhammad
Imam Muhammad reported that the cost ends when digging passes the mouth (fūha) of the owner's channel.
This view is narrated from Muhammad, but it is not preferred.
4
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The first view is preferred because the owner has the right to open his channel intake at any point along the canal — above or below. Therefore, the mouth is not a fixed boundary; the land itself is the relevant unit.
This is a rational argument based on the owner's discretion.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, when excavating a shared canal, each landowner's share ceases as soon as the digging line passes their land boundary. The channel mouth location does not affect the cost distribution.
This prevents disputes over who pays for which segment.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the point at which a landowner's share of the digging cost (sarfa) comes to an end. The relied-upon position (al-awwal asahh) is that the cost ceases when the excavation has passed beyond the land of that owner, not merely when it reaches the mouth of his own channel. The alternative view, attributed to Imam Muhammad, is that the cost continues until the digging passes the opening of his private channel. The reason the first view is preferred: the owner has discretion to open the intake of his channel at any point along the canal, so the mouth is not a fixed boundary. Therefore, the cost follows the land, not the channel entrance.

Key Terms
فوهة النهر (fūhat al-nahr): The mouth or opening of a private channel leading from the main canal
سرفة (sarfa): The cost of digging a shared canal, allocated proportionally among landowners

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school relies on the land boundary, not the channel mouth, to determine when a landowner stops paying canal digging costs.

Opening Water After Excavation Passes One's Land
Ruling on right to open water after cost ceases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on when a landowner may open water after the canal has been dug past his land, and identify which is the relied-upon view.

Core Idea
After excavation passes his land, the landowner may open water immediately; delaying until partners finish is a caution.

This passage addresses a practical issue in irrigation law: once a canal has been dug past a landowner's property and he no longer bears maintenance costs, may he open water to his land immediately? The Hanafi school has two narrations, and the passage also clarifies that drinking-water users are exempt from digging costs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the ruling concerns the permissibility (ibaha) of opening water after the canal has passed the landowner's property and his share of cost has ceased.
2
Extract the two narrations
Note the first view: immediate permission because the digging obligation is complete for that landowner. Note the second view: must wait until all partners finish, to avoid exclusivity.
The second view is precautionary (ihtiyat).
3
Understand the reasoning for each
First view: evidence is the ending of obligation (lā intahā al-kurā fī haqqihi). Second view: evidence is negation of exclusive benefit (nafy al-ikhtiṣāṣ).
Both are rational arguments (dalīl 'aqlī).
4
Note the status of ahl al-shiffah
Drinking water users (those without land) are not required to contribute to digging costs because they are uncountable (lā yuḥṣawn) and are followers (atbā') of the landowners.
They follow the ruling of the principals.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The first view (immediate permission) is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position, though the second is acknowledged as a cautious alternative.
Implicit tarjih from the commentary.

Source Text

When the digging passes beyond his land, so that his maintenance (cost) is dropped, it is said to him: he may open the water to irrigate his land because the digging has ended in his right. And it was said: he does not have that until his partners finish, to negate exclusivity. And there is nothing upon the people of drinking (ahl al-shiffah) from the digging, because they are countless, and because they are followers.
Source pages p. 71

Commentary
This passage discusses the rights of a landowner after the canal has been dug past his land and he is no longer required to contribute to the cost. There are two narrations: (1) He may immediately open water to irrigate his land because the digging obligation for him is complete. (2) He may not open water until all his partners have finished their digging, to avoid the appearance that he is monopolizing the canal for his exclusive benefit. The second view is precautionary. Additionally, those who only drink water (ahl al-shiffah, i.e., people without land who simply take water for drinking) are not required to contribute to the digging cost because they are uncountable (hence cannot be assessed) and because they are followers (tabi'un) of the landowners; the ruling follows the principals, not the followers.

Key Terms
أهل الشفة (ahl al-shiffah): People who only drink water from the canal, not for irrigation; they have no land

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
After a landowner's obligation for canal digging ceases, the relied-upon Hanafi view permits him to open water immediately; drinking water users (ahl al-shiffah) are not liable for digging costs.
