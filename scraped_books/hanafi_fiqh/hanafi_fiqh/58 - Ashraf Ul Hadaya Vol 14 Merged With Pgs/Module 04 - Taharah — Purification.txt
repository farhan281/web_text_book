Principles of Cooking Grape Juice
Three principles on cooking grape juice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi principles for cooking grape juice, apply the two-thirds evaporation rule, and calculate the permissible amount when additions occur.

Core Idea
Grape juice becomes halal only when two-thirds of the total mixture evaporates, under specific conditions of burning and handling.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents three fundamental principles regarding the cooking of grape juice. These principles address the necessary evaporation amount, the sign of proper cooking (burning before turning to vinegar), and the calculation when part of the juice is lost and then replenished. Mastery of these rules is crucial for determining the permissibility of grape juice in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three qawa'id
Read the Arabic text and isolate each principle. The first concerns the measure of evaporation, the second the condition of burning, and the third the calculation after addition.
The text numbers the principles as qa'idah ula, thaniyah, and thalithah.
2
Analyze the first principle
Understand that two-thirds of the total liquid (including any added water) must evaporate. This is the key measure for permissibility.
The example given: water and syrup both evaporate together; total reduction by two-thirds renders it halal.
3
Analyze the second principle
Note the condition: the juice must burn upon two boilings, and this must occur before it turns into vinegar. Burning is a sign that the intoxicating property is removed.
If it becomes vinegar first, it is not considered properly cooked under this rule.
4
Analyze the third principle
Learn the calculation when part of the juice is lost and then more liquid is added. The final one-third is taken from the entire mixture (original plus added).
Example: 10 ratls, 1 lost, 3 added; take one-third of the total (12 ratls) = 4 ratls remaining for halal.
5
Apply the principles
Given a scenario with specific quantities, determine if the juice is halal by checking each condition: two-thirds evaporation, burning before vinegar, and correct calculation of the final one-third.

Commentary
This passage presents three fundamental principles (qawa'id) regarding the cooking of grape juice in Hanafi fiqh. The first principle determines that the measure for permissibility is the reduction of the total volume (juice and water combined) by two-thirds; once two-thirds evaporates, the remaining liquid is considered halal. The second principle adds a condition: if the grape juice is boiled twice (two boilings) and burns (caramelizes) before it turns into vinegar, that burning is a sign of proper cooking. The third principle handles a scenario where part of the juice is lost and then more is added: from an original ten ratls, after one ratl evaporates, three ratls are added; the remaining one-third must be calculated from the complete mixture (i.e., the original amount plus the added liquid). These rules ensure that the intoxicating property is removed.

Ruling on Prey Attacked by Dog and Found Alive
Ruling with proof by consensus; intra-Hanafi dispute noted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prey found alive after a predator attack and apply the condition of slaughter to determine halal or haram.

Core Idea
Prey found alive must be slaughtered to be halal, even if belly is torn.

In this lesson, we examine a specific hunting scenario: a dog tears the belly of prey, spilling its contents, but the prey is still alive when the hunter arrives. What is the ruling? The Hanafi school holds that if the prey is alive and the hunter slaughters it, it is halal, regardless of the injury. Imam Abu Yusuf adds a nuance about the hunter's ability to slaughter. We will unpack the consensus and the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting prey attacked by a dog. The ruling is conditional on slaughter: if the prey is alive and slaughtered, it is halal; otherwise it is haram.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is consensus (ijma) of the scholars, as indicated by the phrase 'by consensus'.
Consensus is a binding proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The consensus establishes that life at the time of slaughter is the criterion for halal. The torn belly does not affect the ruling as long as the animal is still alive.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf adds a condition: if the hunter had time and ability to slaughter but refrained, the prey becomes haram even if later slaughtered.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the consensus: slaughter while alive renders the prey halal. Imam Abu Yusuf's view is considered an additional restraint but does not override the general ruling.
The majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is followed.

Dhimmi Ownership of Dead Land by Revival: The Condition of Imam's Permission
Ruling on dhimmi's ownership of dead land via ihya with Imam's permission condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dhimmi's ownership of dead land through ihya and explain the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the Imam's permission.

Core Idea
A dhimmi owns revived dead land like a Muslim, but Abu Hanifa requires the Imam's permission as a condition.

This passage from Al-Quduri establishes that a dhimmi (non-Muslim protected citizen) can own dead land by reviving it (ihya) just as a Muslim can. The underlying principle is that ihya is a legal cause of ownership. However, Imam Abu Hanifa imposed one additional condition: the permission of the Muslim ruler (Imam). Let us examine the ruling, the reasoning, and the internal Hanafi difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The matn states that a dhimmi owns dead land by reviving it just as a Muslim does. Who is the dhimmi? What is the act? Understand that ownership is acquired through revival.
2
Recognize the stated cause (sabab)
The text says 'because reviving is a cause of ownership'. This is the talil: ihya al-mawat is a sabab for ownership.
3
Note the exception introduced by Abu Hanifa
'Except that according to Abu Hanifa, the Imam's permission is a condition (shart).' This modifies the ruling by adding a requirement.
4
See the analogy to other causes
The text mentions equality in all causes of ownership, including seizure (istila). This shows consistency in the madhhab's principles.
5
Distinguish the intra-Hanafi positions
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad do not require permission; the mufta bihi is that ihya alone suffices for ownership.
The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn.

Commentary

It states that a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state) acquires ownership of dead land by reviving it (ihya) just as a Muslim does, because the act of reviving is a legal cause (sabab) for ownership. However, according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the permission of the Imam (ruler) is a condition for this ownership to take effect. The passage then clarifies that the condition of Imam's permission is agreed upon in the case of a dhimmi, so according to Abu Hanifa's school, there is no difference between Muslim and dhimmi in needing permission. The discussion also notes that mere marking (tahjir) is not sufficient for ownership; actual revival (building or cultivation) is necessary.

Methods of Tahjir (Marking Dead Land)
Describing permissible methods of marking dead land without stones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible methods of tahjir (marking dead land) according to the Hanafi school and distinguish tahjir from actual ihya (revival).

Core Idea
Tahjir marks a priority claim but does not grant ownership until actual revival (ihya) is completed.

This passage from the Hidayah explains that marking dead land (tahjir) can be done in several ways without using stones. The Hanafi school recognizes various acts as valid tahjir, each establishing a right of priority but not ownership. Understanding these methods is crucial for anyone who wishes to claim and later revive barren land.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage lists permissible acts of tahjir. These are mubah (permissible) and establish a priority right (haqq al-awlawiyyah), not ownership.
Tahjir is distinct from ihya.
2
List the methods
The author mentions four methods: (1) sticking dry branches around the land, (2) cleaning the land and burning its thorns, (3) cutting grass/thorns and placing them around with soil without completing a dam, and (4) digging a well of one or two cubits.
All four are valid tahjir.
3
Recognize the distinction
These acts are only tahjir, not ihya. Ihya requires more substantial development (e.g., building a complete dam, cultivating, or constructing a building).
The condition 'without completing the dam' ensures only marking.
4
Note the dalil
The fourth method (digging a well) is supported by a hadith: 'Whoever digs a well of a cubit's measure is a muhajjir.' The hadith is weak according to Imam Zaylai but still used to establish the ruling.
The term 'muhajjir' confirms it is tahjir, not ihya.
5
Understand the condition
The author specifies 'without completing the dam' (min ghayr an yatimma al-musannah). This means the marker does not build a full protective dam, which would constitute ihya.
A complete dam around the land is considered ihya, not tahjir.

Liability for Deaths in a Well: The First vs Second Well
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for well deaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on liability for deaths in a well and distinguish the legal reasoning between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a well dug without the imam's permission.

Core Idea
A well dug without permission is not transgressive — Abu Hanifa's concept of tahjir shields the digger from liability.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi tort case: two wells, one dug legally, the other illegally. The ruling is clear — no liability for the first, full liability for the second. But behind this simplicity lies a subtle intra-madhhab debate: what if the first well was dug without the imam's permission? The Two Companions agree on no liability, but Abu Hanifa provides a unique justification: tahjir (enclosure) is a non-transgressive act even without ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios and their rulings
First well: no liability. Second well: full liability. Note the decisive factor: transgression (taʿaddī).
Liability hinges on whether the digger exceeded his rights.
2
Understand the role of transgression (taʿaddī)
In Hanafi tort law, liability for harm caused by a pit or well requires that the digger be transgressive. If he digs in his own property or with permission, he is not transgressive.
Taʿaddī is defined as acting beyond one's legal rights.
3
Examine the issue: digging without the imam's permission
The problem: when a well is dug on mawat (dead) land without the imam's permission, the digger does not own the land. Does that make him transgressive?
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) also say no liability, but they do not rely on tahjir.
4
Compare Abu Hanifa's tahjir reasoning with the Sahibayn's approach
Abu Hanifa considers the digging itself as tahjir (enclosure), which is a permissible act even without ownership. Thus, it is not transgressive. The Sahibayn agree on the outcome but do not explicitly use tahjir.
This is a dispute over taʿlīl (legal reasoning), not the ruling itself.
5

Harim of a Qanat: Hanafi Dispute on the Protected Area of an Underground Canal
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qanat harim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the harim of a qanat and apply the mufta bihi view to a practical scenario.

Core Idea
A qanat's harim is disputed: Abu Hanifa says none until water surfaces, Sahibayn say like a well, and later scholars compromise based on need.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses the harim (protected area) of an underground canal (qanat). Unlike an open canal, a qanat is covered, raising questions about water rights. The Hanafi school has internal disagreement among the founding imams and later scholars. This lesson examines the arguments and the final relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of harim
Harim is the protected area around a water source to prevent encroachment. Its measurement varies by type: well (40 cubits), spring (500 cubits), tree (5 cubits).
The qanat is an underground channel; its classification determines the harim.
2
Identify the three positions
Abu Hanifa: no harim until water appears on the surface (qiyas to open canal). Sahibayn: harim like a well (40 cubits, qiyas to well). Later masha'ikh: harim as needed, but if water appears, 500 cubits (like a spring).
Imam Muhammad alone reportedly held the well analogy.
3
Analyze the qiyas reasoning
For Abu Hanifa, the qanat is a canal, so it follows the open canal ruling (no harim). For Sahibayn, it is an excavation like a well, so 40 cubits. The masha'ikh distinguish between the covered state and when water surfaces.
The illa for each analogy is the physical nature and function.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical implication: if someone digs a well or another qanat near a qanat, the permissibility depends on which harim measurement is applied.
This affects property rights and dispute resolution.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that of the masha'ikh: harim according to need, but if water appears on the surface, it becomes like a spring and gets 500 cubits.
This combines flexibility with a default measurement.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on reviving abandoned riverbeds by distinguishing between cases where the river may return and where it cannot.

Core Idea
If the river may return, the land remains a river; if not, it becomes dead land—but only if it is not harim.

The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between land that was once a riverbed but may be reclaimed by the river, and land that is permanently abandoned. In this passage from al-Hidayah, the ruling hinges on the possibility of the water's return and the public need for the river as a natural watercourse. Understanding this ruling protects both public rights and private ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios

If the river may return, revival (ihya) is not permissible because the land is still considered part of the river for public benefit. If the river cannot return, the land is treated as dead land (mawat), and revival is permissible subject to conditions.
The prohibition in the first case is based on public need (hajat al-‘ammah) for the river.

Even if the river cannot return, the land must not be the harim (protected area) of an inhabited settlement. If it is harim, it cannot be revived as dead land.

The dominance of water (qahr al-ma’) over the land negates any private ownership. Thus the abandoned bed belongs to the Imam (ruler) and is subject to public interest.
This principle is general: whatever is overcome by water cannot be privately owned until the water permanently recedes.

The Right to Drink (Shiffa): Owner Cannot Prevent
States the basic ruling that the owner cannot prevent drinking from water.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shiffa and identify who has the right to drink and the owner's obligation.

Core Idea
The right to drink water overrides ownership—no owner may prevent another from drinking.

This lesson introduces a fundamental principle in Hanafi water law: the owner of a water source cannot prevent people or animals from drinking directly from it. This right, called shiffa, ensures access to water for basic survival. We will examine the ruling, its implications, and the rationale behind it.

Guided Path

Identify the water sources
List the water sources mentioned: river (nahr), well (bi'r), and qanat (qanat). These are natural or man-made water courses.

State the ruling
The owner has no right to prevent anyone from drinking (shiffa). The ruling is wajib: allowing is obligatory, preventing is haram.
3
Identify the beneficiaries
Shiffa is for humans (bani Adam) and animals (baha'im). Both have the right to drink directly from the source.

The commentary explains that in terms of drinking, all share (ishtirak). Water is a common resource for basic needs.
5

Shiffa (الشفة) literally means 'lip' and refers to drinking by placing the lips to the water. The term emphasizes direct drinking without taking water away.

Core Idea
Using a canal owner's water without permission is prohibited because the water and its passage are the owner's right, just as water in a vessel belongs to the one who collected it.

This passage provides the second proof for why a person may not irrigate their fields from another's canal without the owner's permission. Understanding this ruling is essential for property and irrigation rights in Hanafi law.

5

Without the owner's permission, using another's canal water or its passage is prohibited. With permission, it is permissible. The proof is based on ownership rights and analogy with water in a vessel.

A public canal benefits all, so its expense is from the public treasury — the Imam must arrange it.

This passage clarifies the Hanafi position on public infrastructure. The ruling is clear: the Imam (ruler) is responsible for digging canals that serve the community. Ruling on purification of wine vessels for nabidh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the purification methods for old and new vessels according to the Hanafi school, and explain the disagreement between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding new vessels.

Core Idea
Old vessels require three washes; new vessels with absorbed wine require more thorough purification – a Hanafi internal dispute.

This lesson addresses a practical fiqh issue: vessels that previously held wine and are to be used for making nabidh. The Hanafi school agrees on the method for old vessels but splits on new vessels. Understanding the basis of this dispute reveals the school's methodology regarding absorbed impurities (mā lā yan'asir).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and prerequisite
The act is making nabidh in a vessel that previously contained wine. The prerequisite is purification of the vessel before use.
Nabidh is a permissible date drink in the Hanafi school if the vessel is pure.
2
Distinguish old vs. new vessels
The text defines 'atiq' (old) as vessels used repeatedly and saturated with wine residue; 'jadid' (new) as vessels where wine has been absorbed more recently.
Old vessels are more porous but have reached saturation.

State the agreed ruling for old vessels
Wash the old vessel three times; it becomes pure and suitable for nabidh. This is undisputed.
Three washes are a standard purification method in Hanafi fiqh for such cases.

Imam Muhammad holds that washing does not purify new vessels because the wine has deeply permeated the material, making removal impossible by simple washing.
This is based on the principle of 'ma la yan'asir' – substances that do not release liquid when pressed.
5

Abu Yusuf says washing and drying each time, or filling the vessel with water repeatedly until the water runs clear, does purify the vessel.
His method is more practical and is often adopted in later Hanafi practice.

The dispute centers on 'ma la yan'asir' – whether absorbed impurities can be removed by washing when they cannot be pressed out.
This is a key distinction in Hanafi purification rulings.

New earthen vessels that have held wine are absorbed with impurity; old ones are not.

The Hadd Penalty: Why Drinking is the Cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the hadd penalty for khamr is contingent on the act of drinking (shurb) and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
Hadd is tied to drinking, not mere use or intoxication.

While the prohibition of benefiting from khamr is broad, the application of the hadd penalty is narrow. This lesson focuses on the legal cause (sabab) for hadd and why non-drinking uses do not trigger it, contrasting the Hanafi view with that of Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal cause of hadd
The passage explicitly states 'sharb hi hadd ka sabab hai' (drinking is the cause of hadd). This is the foundational principle.

2
Contrast with non-drinking uses

The presence of intoxication alone is insufficient; the mode must be drinking.
3
Examine the case of cooked khamr in broth
Even if khamr is added to broth and consumed, hadd is not imposed unless intoxication occurs. Cooking changes the substance, but more importantly, it is still not the act of drinking raw khamr.
The text says 'cooking has reached it'—cooking may remove the intoxicating potential, but the ruling is based on the act.
4
Evaluate the cross-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that any consumption of khamr, including in food, is subject to hadd. The Hanafi response is that the cause must be literally drinking (shurb), not just consumption.
This is an usul al-fiqh difference in the interpretation of the hadith and the cause.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In Hanafi, a person who eats a dish cooked with wine does not receive hadd (though they commit a sin); in Shafi'i, they would be liable for hadd.
This difference significantly affects judicial application.

The First Principle: Calculating Two-Thirds Evaporation After Froth Deduction
Principle on calculating two-thirds evaporation after froth deduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle for treating froth during boiling of grape juice and apply it to calculate the required two-thirds evaporation.

Core Idea
Froth lost during boiling is treated as nonexistent; only the remaining juice is used for the two-thirds calculation.

In previous lessons, we learned that grape juice must lose two-thirds of its volume through cooking to become halal. But what about the froth that bubbles and is thrown off during boiling? Does it count toward the two-thirds? This lesson clarifies the first principle set by Imam al-Marghinani: the froth is considered as if it never existed, and only the remaining juice after skimming is used to calculate the required evaporation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the Arabic statement: 'الاصل ان ماذهب بغليا نه بالنار وقذفه بالزبد يجعل كان لم يكن'. This establishes that the amount lost through boiling and froth is treated as nonexistent.
This is a fundamental Hanafi rule for taharah of grape juice.
2
Understand the Existence Ruling
The froth is disregarded; we do not count it as part of the original quantity. Only the remaining liquid after removing froth is considered the juice to be cooked.
Example: 10 vessels initial, 1 vessel froth → effective quantity = 9 vessels.
3
Calculate Two-Thirds of the Remainder
Take the effective quantity (after deducting froth) and compute two-thirds of that. That amount must evaporate.
For 9 vessels effective, two-thirds is 6 vessels. So 6 must evaporate, leaving 3 halal.
4
Apply to a Given Case
Given a starting quantity and froth loss, first subtract froth, then calculate two-thirds of the remainder to find what must evaporate.
Practice with the example: 10 total, 1 froth → 9 effective → 6 evaporate → 3 remain halal.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Other schools may require complete transformation (istihalah) of the juice, not simply reduction by two-thirds. This principle is specific to the Hanafi school.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.

2

When a Trained Dog's Prey Becomes Haram Due to Another Dog
Stating the ruling from al-Quduri with dalil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prey caught by a trained dog when an untrained or Magian's dog joins, and explain the proof from hadith and the principle of ghalabat al-hurma.

Core Idea
Participation of a non-trained dog renders the prey haram, even if the owner's dog is trained.

When hunting with a trained dog, the prey is halal only if the dog is properly trained and dispatched with tasmiyah, and no other dog participates. This lesson examines the case where an untrained dog, a Magian's dog, or a dog without tasmiyah joins the hunt. The Hanafi school rules the prey haram, citing a hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim and the legal principle that when halal and haram combine, prohibition prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting with a trained dog. The ruling is that if any other dog (untrained, Magian's, or without tasmiyah) joins, the prey is haram (not to be eaten).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from 'Adi ibn Hatim (mawquf on Ibn Mas'ud) that explicitly states the prohibition when another dog shares in the catch.
The hadith is ahad and zanni in transmission and meaning.
3
Understand the legal principle
The second proof is the principle of ghalabat al-hurma: when a permissive factor (trained dog, tasmiyah) and a prohibitive factor (untrained dog) combine, the prohibitive side prevails, either by textual indication or by precaution.
Precaution is taken because haram must be avoided, while halal may be left without sin.
4
Apply the ruling
In practice, if a Muslim dispatches a trained dog with tasmiyah, but an untrained dog joins and helps catch the prey, the prey is not lawful. This applies even if the trained dog did most of the work.
The participation of any non-mu'allam dog triggers the prohibition.
5
Consider the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow the prey if the trained dog is the primary catcher. The Hanafi position is based on the literal hadith and the maxim of ghalabat al-hurma.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the passage presents the Hanafi view as authoritative.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It begins by citing a hadith from 'Adi ibn Hatim (may Allah be pleased with him) regarding hunting. The author then presents the ruling from al-Quduri: if a Muslim's trained dog (kalb mu'allam) is accompanied by another dog that is either untrained (ghayr mu'allam), a Magian's dog, or a dog on which the name of Allah was not recited (i.e., without tasmiyah), then the prey is not to be eaten. Two proofs are given: first, the explicit hadith of 'Adi, which likely states that if a dog other than the hunter's own participates, the prey is forbidden. Second, a general legal principle: when a permissive factor (mubih) and a prohibitive factor (muharram) combine, the prohibitive side prevails, either by a specific textual indication (nass) or by precaution (ihtiyat). The sharh elaborates that the hadith of 'Adi is recorded in the Musnad of 'Abd al-Razzaq as a mawquf saying of Ibn Mas'ud, or the precautionary approach is taken because haram must be avoided while halal may be left without sin, so the safer choice is to refrain from eating. This reflects the Hanafi methodological priority of ihtiyat when evidence is conflicting.
