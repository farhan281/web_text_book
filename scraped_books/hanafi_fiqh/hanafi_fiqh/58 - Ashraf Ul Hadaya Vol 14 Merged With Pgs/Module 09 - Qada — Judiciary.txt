Two Types of Judicial Rulings: Qada Tark and Qada Milk
Defines two types of judicial rulings and applies them to the harim dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qada tark and qada milk, and explain why the harim dispute is treated as qada tark.

Core Idea
Qada tark is reversible; qada milk is final. The harim dispute is qada tark, not ownership determination.

Before examining the Hanafi disagreement over the river precinct (harim), the author clarifies a fundamental classification of judicial rulings. A judge's decision can either be a 'judgment of abandonment' (qada tark) or a 'judgment of ownership' (qada milk). The former allows the judge to change his mind later; the latter is final. This distinction is crucial for understanding why the embankment dispute is not a final ownership verdict but a provisional ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of qada
The text states there are two: qada tark (judgment of leaving) and qada milk (judgment of ownership).
Qada tark is also called qada bi al-tark.
2
Distinguish their legal effect
Qada tark can be changed later; qada milk is binding and cannot be reversed for the same case.
The judge retains authority to revise in qada tark.
3
Apply to the harim dispute
The author specifies that the dispute over the embankment is qada tark, not qada milk.
This means the ruling is provisional.
4
Note the consequence
Because it is qada tark, the river owner can later bring evidence to change the ruling; otherwise, if it were qada milk, the losing party would be permanently bound.
This preserves the possibility of justice with new evidence.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya is an exposition of the Hanafi dispute regarding the harim (precinct) of a river when an embankment (musanna) lies between the river owner (sahib al-nahr) and the land owner (sahib al-ard). The author first introduces a fundamental classification of judicial rulings (qada) into two types: (1) qada tark (judgment of leaving things as they are) and (2) qada milk (judgment of ownership and entitlement). In qada tark, the judge can later change his decision and issue a contrary ruling; in qada milk, once a decision is given, it cannot be reversed for the same incident. The application here is that the dispute over the harim is treated as qada tark, not qada milk. Consequently, according to Abu Yusuf, the embankment is left in the possession of the land owner, while according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), it is left with the river owner. The passage then presents Imam Abu Hanifa's response to the Sahibayn's proof. The Sahibayn argued that the river owner uses the embankment to hold water for his river, and the land owner uses it to repel water from his land, so the river owner's entitlement is not established. Imam Abu Hanifa counters that there is no dispute about the portion that actually holds water; the dispute is about the portion beyond that which is suitable for planting. He analogizes the situation to a wall belonging to one person on which another's beams rest: the owner of the wall cannot demolish it because the other's right is attached, even though the wall is his property. Similarly, the land owner cannot demolish the embankment because the river owner's right is attached, but this does not give the river owner ownership. Then the author clarifies from al-Jami' al-Saghir that the dispute specifically arises when neither party has possession (i.e., no tree or mud on the embankment). If one has possession, that person is more entitled. Finally, the passage lists sub-issues: the ruling on trees (trees go with the land owner according to Abu Hanifa, with the river owner according to the Sahibayn), dumping mud (some say it follows the dispute, others say the river owner can do so if not excessive), and passage (Abu Hanifa prohibits the river owner from passing, but necessity may allow). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad also differ on the extent of the harim (half the river's width vs. full width), with the latter considered more convenient.

Key Terms
قضاء ترك (qada tark): A judicial ruling that leaves an object in the current state of possession without determining ownership; reversible in a new suit.
قضاء ملك (qada milk): A judicial ruling that determines ownership and entitlement; final and not reversible in the same incident.

The Hanafi Dispute: Who Owns the Embanmment in the River Harim?
Presents the intra-Hanafi dispute over ownership of the embankment, including proofs, response, and sub-issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the ownership of the musanna, and explain the proof and counter-proof using the analogy of the wall and beams.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: the embankment belongs to the land owner; the Sahibayn: it belongs to the river owner — the dispute hinges on whether attachment creates ownership or just a right.

Having established that the dispute over the river precinct falls under qada tark, the text now delves into the substantive disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. At issue is the ownership of the embankment (musanna) that lies between the river owner's land and the neighboring land owner's property. Abu Hanifa argues that the embankment is part of the land owner's property, while the Sahibayn argue it belongs to the river owner as part of the harim. The debate revolves around a powerful analogy and the nature of attached rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute — What is the specific disagreement?
The embankment is not in anyone's possession. Abu Hanifa says it belongs to the land owner; the Sahibayn say it belongs to the river owner.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, not inter-madhhab.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof — The analogy of the wall and beams
Abu Hanifa argues that the river owner's right to use the embankment is like another's beams resting on a wall: the wall owner retains ownership, but cannot demolish it due to the attached right.
This analogy establishes that attachment does not transfer ownership.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof — Both parties have a use
The river owner uses the embankment to hold water; the land owner uses it to repel water. Neither's use grounds ownership; but the river owner has a stronger need for maintenance (dumping mud, etc.).
The Sahibayn emphasize utility and the river owner's ongoing need.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's response — The portion holding water is not disputed
The dispute is about the part suitable for planting, not the water-holding part. The river owner's right is only an attachment, not ownership.
Abu Hanifa narrows the scope of the disagreement.
5
Note the sub-issues and partial reconciliation by al-Faqih Abu Ja'far
On trees: Abu Hanifa says the tree belongs to the land owner; Sahibayn say to the river owner. On mud dumping: some say it follows the dispute, others say the river owner can dump unless excessive. Al-Faqih Abu Ja'far adopts Abu Hanifa's view on trees and the Sahibayn's view on mud dumping.
This shows a practical compromise.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya is an exposition of the Hanafi dispute regarding the harim (precinct) of a river when an embankment (musanna) lies between the river owner (sahib al-nahr) and the land owner (sahib al-ard). The author first introduces a fundamental classification of judicial rulings (qada) into two types: (1) qada tark (judgment of leaving things as they are) and (2) qada milk (judgment of ownership and entitlement). In qada tark, the judge can later change his decision and issue a contrary ruling; in qada milk, once a decision is given, it cannot be reversed for the same incident. The application here is that the dispute over the harim is treated as qada tark, not qada milk. Consequently, according to Abu Yusuf, the embankment is left in the possession of the land owner, while according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), it is left with the river owner. The passage then presents Imam Abu Hanifa's response to the Sahibayn's proof. The Sahibayn argued that the river owner uses the embankment to hold water for his river, and the land owner uses it to repel water from his land, so the river owner's entitlement is not established. Imam Abu Hanifa counters that there is no dispute about the portion that actually holds water; the dispute is about the portion beyond that which is suitable for planting. He analogizes the situation to a wall belonging to one person on which another's beams rest: the owner of the wall cannot demolish it because the other's right is attached, even though the wall is his property. Similarly, the land owner cannot demolish the embankment because the river owner's right is attached, but this does not give the river owner ownership. Then the author clarifies from al-Jami' al-Saghir that the dispute specifically arises when neither party has possession (i.e., no tree or mud on the embankment). If one has possession, that person is more entitled. Finally, the passage lists sub-issues: the ruling on trees (trees go with the land owner according to Abu Hanifa, with the river owner according to the Sahibayn), dumping mud (some say it follows the dispute, others say the river owner can do so if not excessive), and passage (Abu Hanifa prohibits the river owner from passing, but necessity may allow). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad also differ on the extent of the harim (half the river's width vs. full width), with the latter considered more convenient.

Key Terms
حريم (harim): The precinct or curtilage around a river or well, used for maintenance (e.g., dumping mud, passing) and belonging to the owner of the water source.
مسناة (musanna): An embankment or levee built to hold water or direct its flow.
شغل (shughl): Occupation or possession; having a tree or dumped mud on the land gives priority.
غرس (ghars): Planting or trees on the land, considered a sign of possession.

Key Takeaway
The embankment dispute reveals a fundamental Hanafi principle: attachment of a right does not transfer ownership; ownership remains with the party whose property is attached, but the right-holder cannot be deprived of the necessary use.
