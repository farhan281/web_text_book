Water Rights Among Partners: Proportional Shares and Upstream Damming
Ruling on distributing water shares and conditions for upstream damming

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for dividing water shares among partners by land size and explain the conditions under which an upstream partner may build a dam.

Core Idea
Water shares follow land size; upstream damming requires consent and minimal harm.

This passage addresses two common disputes in shared irrigation: how to divide water among partners proportionally, and whether an upstream partner may build a dam to irrigate his land. The Hanafi school provides clear rulings based on the purpose (maqsad) of the right and the principle of avoiding harm to others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and ruling for water shares
The passage describes partners arguing about their water share (sharb) from a shared river. The ruling is that the water is distributed according to the size of each partner's land.
This is the default rule unless they agree otherwise.
2
Understand the reasoning (dalil)
The proof is a logical analogy: water is for irrigating land, so the share matches the land's need. The passage contrasts this with a path (tariq), which is shared equally because the purpose (passage) does not vary with property size.
This is a qiyas based on the purpose (maqsad) of the right.
3
Analyze the upstream damming scenario
If an upstream partner cannot reach water without damming, he does not have the unilateral right to dam because it would nullify the rights of downstream partners. He must irrigate only with his share.
Unilateral damming is prohibited due to harm (ḍarar) to others.
4
Explore consented damming
If all partners consent to the upstream partner damming to irrigate his share, or they agree on a rotation where each dams in his turn, it becomes permissible because they own the right.
Consent transforms the ruling from prohibited to permissible.
5
Apply the principle of least harm
Even with consent, if the upstream partner can dam using a plank (a non-harmful method), he must not use a method that clogs the river (like earth), because that causes additional damage to others.
This reflects the Hanafi maxim: 'Harm must be removed' and 'Choose the lesser harm.'

Distribution of Water in a Shared Stream
Ruling on water distribution with a distinction from pathways.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for water distribution in shared streams and explain the rationale based on purpose compared to shared pathways.

Core Idea
Water shares in a shared stream are proportional to land area, not equal, because the purpose is irrigation.

When partners share a water source and dispute over shares, Hanafi fiqh provides a principle based on the purpose of the water. This lesson examines a short ruling with a clear distinction between water rights and pathways, showing how legal reasoning (talil) derives a just distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is being ruled upon? The water share (sharb) in a shared stream. The ruling: water is distributed according to the extent of land each partner owns.
The ruling is wajib (obligatory) to implement fairness.
2
Understand the proof (talil)
The proof is the purpose (maqsad): irrigation benefit. Since irrigation need varies with land size, the share must vary proportionally.
This is a rational proof based on the goal of the right.
3
Distinguish from the opposite case
Contrast with a shared pathway (tariq): passage is uniform for all regardless of house size. So the ruling differs.
The illa (effective cause) is the nature of the benefit.
4
Note the illa
The illa is the purpose: irrigation requires variable amounts; passage does not.
This illa is jaliyyah (clear).

Ruling on Upstream Partner Damming a Shared Stream
Ruling on upstream partner damming shared stream

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an upstream partner damming a shared stream, identify the conditions for permissibility, and explain the underlying principle of harm.

Core Idea
One partner cannot harm others: damming a shared stream requires mutual consent.

In a shared irrigation channel, upstream partners may think they can dam the stream to water their fields. But Hanafi fiqh places limits: harming downstream partners is not allowed. This passage from al-Hidayah explains the default prohibition and the exceptional permissibility when consent is given. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is damming (yuskir) a shared stream by an upstream partner. The default ruling is that he does not have the right to do so unilaterally.
The Arabic text says 'lam yakun lahu dhalik' – he does not have that right.
2
Understand the reason: harm to others
The reason for prohibition is that it nullifies the rights of downstream partners (ibṭāl ḥaqq al-bāqīn). Harm to others is a key principle in Hanafi fiqh.
The principle 'la ḍarara wa la ḍirār' applies here.
3
Note the exception: mutual consent
If all partners agree – either allowing the upstream partner to dam alone or taking turns – then damming becomes permissible. Consent transforms the ruling.
The text says 'fa in tarāḍaw ... jāz' – if they mutually agree, it is permissible.
4
Apply the condition: minimal harm method
Even with consent, if a less harmful method (e.g., a plank) suffices, one must avoid causing additional harm by blocking the stream with earth or stones.
The Arabic 'bi-lawḥ' indicates a plank that can be removed easily.
5
Recognize the principle: rights are balanced
The ruling balances the upstream partner's need for water against the collective rights of all partners. No one has an absolute right to harm others.
This is a practical application of the maxim 'al-umur bi-maqāṣidihā' and 'la ḍarara'.

Rights of Partners in a Shared Stream: Canals and Mills
Ruling on using shared stream for new canals or mills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on digging canals or installing mills on a shared stream, identify the condition for permissibility without consent, and apply the analogy to similar devices.

Core Idea
A partner may not unilaterally alter shared property if it causes harm; harmless use of one's own land is free.

Partnership in a shared stream (nahr mushtarak) gives each partner a right to use the water, but not to unilaterally alter the stream's structure. This lesson examines when a partner may dig a new canal or install a water mill. The key principle is that harming the common property or the water flow is prohibited, but if the installation is on one's own land and causes no harm, it is permissible even without consent. The same ruling applies to water wheels and bucket pulleys.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general ruling
The text states that no partner may dig a canal from the shared stream or install a water mill on it without the consent of all partners. This is the default ruling: it is haram (prohibited) without consent.
The reason given is breaking the bank and occupying common space.
2
Locate the exception and its conditions
An exception is provided: if the mill does not harm the stream or the water flow, and its location is on the partner's own land (not on the common bank), then it is permissible without consent. The reasoning is that this is a disposition in one's own property with no harm to others.
Harm to the stream means breaking its bank; harm to the water means altering its natural course.
3
Apply the analogy (qiyas) to other devices
The text extends the same ruling to water wheels (daliyah) and bucket pulleys (saniyah) by explicit analogy. These are like the mill in terms of causing harm, so the same consent requirement and exception apply.
This shows how Hanafi fiqh uses qiyas to derive rulings for similar cases.
4
Derive the practical application
In practice, any partner who wishes to add a structure that affects the shared stream must either obtain consent or ensure the structure is entirely on his own land and causes no harm to the stream or water flow. If harm occurs, the act is prohibited.
The burden of proof that there is no harm lies on the partner making the change.

Prohibition of Unilateral Widening in Water Division by Holes
States the ruling against unilateral widening of holes in water distribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a partner may not unilaterally widen his water outlet in a shared canal, and explain the qiyas (analogy) used to extend the ruling from waterwheel to holes.

Core Idea
A partner may not unilaterally enlarge his water intake hole; it takes water beyond his share without consent.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah extends a previously established ruling: just as a partner may not widen the opening of a shared waterwheel to take extra water, he also may not widen his individual hole when distribution is through holes. The text uses an implicit analogy (qiyas) to ensure consistency. In this lesson, we break down the legal reasoning and its practical implications for irrigation partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is a partner widening his own hole in a shared canal. The ruling is that it is not allowed (haram).
The source explicitly states 'no one has the authority to widen his own hole'.
2
Locate the original ruling (asl)
The original ruling (from earlier in the text) prohibits widening the opening of a shared waterwheel, as that would increase one partner's share without consent.
This asl is not quoted in this passage but is referenced implicitly.
3
Identify the effective cause (illa) linking asl and far'
The illa is the unilateral alteration of a fixed distribution method, leading to taking more water than the allocated share.
Both the waterwheel opening and the hole are means of distribution; altering either upsets the agreed division.
4
Verify the transfer of hukm via qiyas
Since the illa is present in both cases, the same prohibition (haram) applies to widening the hole.
This is qiyas jali (clear analogy), as the common cause is evident.
5
Note the practical consequence
No partner may unilaterally enlarge his intake, even if he claims it remains within his share; change must be by mutual consent.
The ruling protects partnership integrity and prevents disputes.

Core Idea
Diverting water to a land without a right is prohibited because it may lead to a false claim of ownership over time.

This passage addresses a common issue in shared irrigation canals: can a shareholder use his water share to irrigate another plot that has no water rights in that canal? The harm here is not immediate but potential—the creation of a false presumption.

The 'tanbih' (warning) adds that if all partners consent, the diversion becomes permissible. This shows that the prohibition is based on protecting the rights of others, not on the act itself being inherently harmful.

One might think that as long as the total water taken from the canal is unchanged, diverting to another land should be allowed. The text rejects this because the issue is not about water quantity but about the legal rights attached to specific land.
Water rights (shurb) are tied to specific land parcels.

This reinforces the principle that any change that increases one's benefit from shared property without consent is prohibited.

Key Terms
نظير (nazir): An analogous case used to illustrate or support a ruling.
دہانہ (dihanah): The mouth or outlet of a canal; closing it stops further water flow.
