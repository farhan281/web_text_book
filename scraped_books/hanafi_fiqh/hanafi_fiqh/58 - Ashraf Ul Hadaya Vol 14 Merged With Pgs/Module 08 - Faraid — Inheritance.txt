Dispute on the Harim of Irrigation and Watering Wells
Presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on the harim of wells for different purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the harim of bir al-nadih, and evaluate the proofs each side uses.

Core Idea
The harim of a well depends on its use: Sahibayn distinguish between watering and irrigation wells, while Imam Abu Hanifa treats all wells equally at 40 cubits.

In this lesson, we explore a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about the protective boundary (harim) of wells. The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn centers on whether the harim should vary based on the well's purpose. By understanding their proofs and reasoning, you will see how Hanafi scholars apply general and specific texts, analogy (qiyas), and juristic preference (istihsan).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of wells
Distinguish bir al-atan (watering well, hand-drawn) from bir al-nadih (irrigation well, animal-powered). Note their different purposes and how this affects the need for space.
2
State the positions clearly
Imam Abu Hanifa: harim of 40 cubits on all sides for every well. Sahibayn: 40 cubits for bir al-atan, 60 cubits for bir al-nadih.
The difference applies only to bir al-nadih.
3
Analyze the proofs of the Sahibayn
Examine their specific hadith (mursal from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab) and their rational argument: irrigation wells need more space for animals and longer ropes.
The hadith is considered weak (mursal) by some, but Sahibayn act upon it.
4
Analyze the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa
Examine his general hadith ("Whoever digs a well gets 40 cubits") and his use of qiyas/istihsan: since both well types can be used interchangeably, need is equal.
Imam prioritises the general, agreed-upon hadith over the specific, disputed one.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
See how later authorities differ: Fatawa Tatarkhaniyya and Fatawa Kubra prefer Sahibayn; Durr Mukhtar and al-Hidayah's style prefer Imam. No single position is universally mufta bihi.
This reflects the richness of Hanafi ijtihad.

Chapter of Reviving Dead Lands: Translation of Quduri: He said: If the well is for watering animals, its harim is forty cubits due to the saying of the Prophet, peace be upon him: 'Whoever digs a well gets forty cubits around it to let his cattle rest.' Then it is said that it is forty from every side (i.e., total forty) and the correct view is that it is forty from every side because the earth is soft and water will return to the well if another is dug within that distance. If the well is for irrigation, its harim is sixty cubits according to the Sahibayn, whereas according to Abu Hanifa it is forty cubits. Commentary: In the previous section it became known that a well has a harim. Now the question arises how much the harim is. He said: If the well is for drawing water by hand to give to animals and the animals rest near it, then its harim is forty cubits, i.e., forty cubits on each side. If the well is for irrigating fields, then there is a dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa says its harim is also forty cubits, while the Sahibayn say its harim is sixty cubits. The proofs of both sides will follow. The harim of bir al-atan is forty cubits as mentioned in the hadith. This hadith is in Ibn Majah and Musnad Ahmad: the first narrated by Abdullah ibn Mughaffal, the second by Abu Huraira. Note: 'atan' is the place around the well where cattle are made to rest. Bir al-atan means a well whose purpose is only to water animals, not to irrigate fields. 'Nadih' originally refers to the camel used for watering, then came to be used for any camel even if not carrying water. Since oxen or camels used to be hitched to waterwheels and chains until recently in our region, now electricity and tube wells have replaced them. Thus a well used for irrigation is called bir al-nadih. Note: The forty cubits on each side is considered correct. Note 3: The dispute between Imam and the Sahibayn regarding waterwheels is mentioned. In Fatawa Tatarkhaniyya and Fatawa Kubra, the opinion of the Sahibayn is given mufta bihi. In Durr Mukhtar, the opinion of Imam is given mufta bihi. Allama Qasim ibn Qutlubugha preferred Imam's opinion, and the style of Sahib al-Hidaya also supports preference of Imam's opinion. The proof of the Sahibayn: For them: His saying, peace be upon him: 'The harim of a spring is five hundred cubits, the harim of bir al-atan is forty cubits, and the harim of bir al-nadih is sixty cubits.' And because there is sometimes a need to move the animal for drawing water, and the rope may become long, whereas bir al-atan is used by hand so the need is less, so a distinction is necessary. Commentary: The venerable Sahibayn presented this proof: first, a hadith which is mursal from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab, but even as mursal it is not with these exact words. Second, a rational proof: in bir al-nadih, camels etc. are hitched and sometimes the rope (pulley) becomes long, so more harim is needed, hence sixty cubits. In bir al-atan, this need is not present because water is drawn by hand, so it is necessary to differentiate between the two: forty for the first, sixty for the second. The proof of Imam Abu Hanifa: For him is what we have narrated without distinction, and the general hadith that is agreed upon and acted upon is preferable, in his view, to the specific hadith whose acceptance and practice are disputed. And because analogy (qiyas) prevents entitlement to harim, since the person's action is in the digging spot and entitlement arises from action. So in the amount upon which both hadiths agree, we abandon qiyas; and in the amount where they conflict, we retain qiyas. And because sometimes water is drawn from bir al-atan by camel, and from bir al-nadih by hand, so the need in both is equal. And he can make the camel go around the well, so there is no need for extra distance. Translation: And the proof of Abu Hanifa is the hadith we narrated without distinction, and the general [hadith] which is agreed upon in acceptance and practice is preferable, in his view, to the specific [hadith] in whose acceptance and practice there is dispute. And because qiyas denies entitlement to harim, since the action is in the digging spot and entitlement is by action. So in the amount upon which the two hadiths agree, we have left qiyas; and in the amount where they conflict, we have preserved qiyas. And because sometimes water is drawn from bir al-atan by camel, and from bir al-nadih by hand, so the need in both is equal. And it is possible for him to turn the camel around the well, so he does not need an increased distance. Commentary: This is the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa whose summary is: The first hadith we narrated is general for every well, whether bir al-atan or bir al-nadih, to the same extent, and there is agreement on that extent and practice upon it. The hadith mentioned by the Sahibayn is specific, because it mentions separate rulings for bir al-atan and bir al-nadih. Moreover, there is dispute in its acceptance and practice. So the first hadith is general and agreed upon and practiced, the second is specific and disputed. According to Imam's principles, the former is preferable. Second proof: Qiyas would demand that no harim be given at all because entitlement is by action and the action is in the digging spot. But they agreed on some harim from the general hadith, so qiyas is abandoned there. Where they disagree, qiyas is retained. And the need is equal in both types of wells, etc.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the harim (exclusive precinct) of wells. It begins by stating the ruling from Quduri's matn: the harim of a well used for watering animals is forty cubits based on a hadith. For irrigation wells, there is a difference: Abu Hanifa says forty cubits, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) say sixty cubits. The commentator explains that the harim is the land around the well that must be left vacant to protect the well's water and access. The key distinction is between bir al-atan (well for watering animals by hand) and bir al-nadih (well for irrigation using a camel or waterwheel). The Sahibayn argue from a specific hadith that specifies different harims for springs (500 cubits), bir al-atan (40), and bir al-nadih (60). They also reason that irrigation wells need more space for animals to move and for longer ropes. Imam Abu Hanifa relies on a general hadith ("Whoever digs a well gets forty cubits around it") which he considers agreed upon and acted upon, whereas the specific hadith of the Sahibayn is mursal and disputed. He also uses qiyas: since the harim is based on actual need, and both types of wells can sometimes be used interchangeably (hand or animal), the need is equal, so the harim should be the same. The commentator notes that later authorities differ on which view is mufta bihi: some prefer the Sahibayn, others prefer Imam Abu Hanifa, but the style of the Hidayah favors Imam's view. This is a classic example of the Hanafi methodology of preferring a general, agreed-upon proof over a specific, disputed one, and of using istihsan (juristic preference) based on need.

Key Terms
حریم (harim): The precinct or protective boundary of a well (or other property) within which others may not dig or construct without permission.
بیرالعطن (bir al-atan): A well dug solely for watering animals, where water is drawn by hand and animals rest around it.
بیر الناضح (bir al-nadih): A well used for irrigation, where water is drawn using a camel or animal-powered device.
ذراع (dhira'): A cubit, a unit of length approximately the length of a forearm, varying between 18-24 inches.

The Pawned Slave Killed by Another Slave: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on substitute pawn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions (Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar) on the treatment of a substitute slave in a pawn contract and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When a pawned slave is destroyed and replaced by a substitute, the substitute inherits the pawn's legal status—full debt remains due.

This passage from al-Hidayah examines a nuanced scenario: a pawned slave is killed by another slave. The killer slave is handed over as compensation. The central question: does the substitute stand completely in place of the original pawn, or does it only partially replace it? Three leading Hanafi authorities offer differing answers, each grounded in a distinct conception of the mortgagee's possession (yad istifa') and the nature of substitution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and core question
A pawned slave (marhun) is killed by another slave. The killer slave is given as diyat (blood money) to the mortgagee. The key issue: how does this substitute affect the pawn contract and the debt?
The original pawn is destroyed, but a substitute (badal) is provided.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
They hold that the substitute stands in place of the original in flesh and blood. The mortgagor must pay the full debt to redeem the substitute; the mortgagee takes the substitute as full satisfaction.
This is the relied-upon (zahir al-riwayah) position.
3
State Muhammad al-Shaybani's alternative
Muhammad gives the mortgagor an option: either pay the full debt and take the substitute, or deliver the substitute to the mortgagee in lieu of the debt.
This view treats the event as similar to a sale where the subject matter is destroyed before delivery.
4
State Zufar's position
Zufar argues that the substitute becomes a new pawn only for its value (which is a tenth of the original). The debt reduces proportionally.
His reasoning: the mortgagee's hand is one of recovery (yad istifa'), and the original pawn's destruction crystallizes the debt, but the substitute only replaces part of the value.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response to Zufar
The companions (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) counter: the substitute is not a mere replacement of value but a physical substitute. If the original remained but its price decreased, the full debt would still be owed. So here, the substitute fully replaces the original, and the full debt remains.
This is based on the continuity of the pawn contract; the substitute enters the same status as the original.

قال وإن قتله عبد قيمته مائة فدفع مكانه افتكه بجميع الدين وهذا عند أبي حنيفة وأبي يوسف وقال محمد هو بالخيار إن شاء افتكه بجميع الدين وإن شاء سلم العبد المدفوع إلى المرتهن بماله وقال زفر يصير رهنا بمائة له أن يد الرهن يد استيفاء وقد تقرر بالهلاك إلا أنه أخلف بدلاً بقدر العشر فيبقى الدين بقدره ولأصحابنا على زفر أن العبد الثاني قائم مقام الأول لحماً ودماً ولو كان الأول قائماً وانتقص السعر لا يسقط شيء من الدين عندنا لما ذكرنا فكذلك إذا قام المدفوع مكانه.
He said: And if another slave worth a hundred kills him, and he (the killer) is handed over in his place, then he (the mortgagor) ransoms him with the entire debt. This is according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Muhammad said: He has an option: if he wishes, he ransoms him with the entire debt; if he wishes, he delivers the handed-over slave to the mortgagee in lieu of his property (debt). Zufar said: It becomes a pawn for a hundred, because the mortgagee's hand is a hand of recovery, and it has been established by the destruction (of the original), except that he has left a substitute to the extent of a tenth, so the debt remains to its extent. And our companions' argument against Zufar: The second slave stands in place of the first in flesh and blood. And if the first had remained and its price decreased, nothing of the debt would be dropped according to us for what we mentioned. So likewise when the handed-over stands in its place.

Commentary
This passage deals with a scenario where a pawned slave (marhun) is killed by another slave. The killer slave is given as compensation (diyat) to the mortgagee. The scholars differ: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the mortgagor must redeem the substitute slave by paying the full debt (i.e., the killer slave becomes the mortgagee's property in full satisfaction). Muhammad gives the mortgagor an option: either pay the full debt and take the substitute, or leave the substitute with the mortgagee in lieu of the debt. Zufar holds that the substitute becomes a new pawn for only the value of the killer (which is a tenth of the original pawn's value), and the debt reduces accordingly. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is based on the idea that the substitute stands in exactly the same place as the original pawn, so it is as if the original pawn is still there but its value has decreased; thus the full debt remains. Zufar's analogy is rejected because the substitute is not a mere replacement of value but a physical substitute.

Key Terms
بدل (badal): The substitute (the killer slave) given in place of the pawned slave.
يد استيفاء (yad istifa'): Possession for recovery; the mortgagee's possession is aimed at recovering his debt.
عشر (ushr): A tenth; referring to the killer slave's value being one-tenth of the original pawn.
