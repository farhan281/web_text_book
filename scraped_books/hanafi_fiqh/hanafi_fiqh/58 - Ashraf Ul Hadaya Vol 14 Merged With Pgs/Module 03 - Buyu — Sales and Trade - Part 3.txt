The 'Adl's Two Options After Buyer Recovers Price
Explaining the 'adl's recourse options if the buyer recovers the price from him

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to the 'adl (custodian) when the buyer recovers the price from him and explain the legal reasoning (dalil) behind each option.

Core Idea
The 'adl has two routes: recover from the rahin (who caused the liability) or recover from the murtahin (whose possession becomes void upon annulment).

The 'adl, who paid the price to the murtahin, may find himself liable when the buyer recovers the price due to eviction (istihqaq). This passage from al-Hidayah explains the 'adl's right to recover his loss from either the rahin (pledgor) or the murtahin (pledgee), providing a rational basis for each option. Understanding this ruling ensures the 'adl is not left helpless and clarifies the ultimate liability of the rahin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
The buyer has recovered the price from the 'adl. The 'adl is out-of-pocket. Determine the legal question: from whom can the 'adl recover his loss?
2
Recognize the two options
The 'adl has a choice (khiyar): (a) resort to the rahin for the price, or (b) resort to the murtahin for the price.
Both options are valid; the 'adl may choose.
3
Extract the reasoning for each option
For option (a): The rahin caused the 'adl's liability ('adl khassa), so he must indemnify. For option (b): The sale being annulled voids the price; the murtahin's possession must be undone as it was received as price.
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): causation and necessity.
4
Trace the consequences for the murtahin's possession
If the 'adl recovers from the rahin, the murtahin's possession becomes valid and secure. If the 'adl recovers from the murtahin, the murtahin's possession is nullified, but his debt right against the rahin revives.
The ultimate loss falls on the rahin in both cases.

When Buyer Pays Price Directly to Murtahin
Stating the rule when buyer pays murtahin directly

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of recourse when the buyer pays the murtahin directly and explain why the 'adl is not liable.

Core Idea
The buyer can only sue the party who actually received the price; the 'adl as agent is not liable if he never took possession.

In a pawn sale (rahn), the 'adl acts as an agent for the rahin. If the buyer mistakenly pays the price directly to the murtahin instead of the 'adl, a question of liability arises: can the buyer later recover the price from the 'adl? This lesson clarifies that the 'adl cannot be held liable because he never possessed the price—the liability stays with the murtahin who received it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
List the four parties: rahin (pledgor), murtahin (pledgee), 'adl (agent), and buyer (mushtari). Note the role of each in the sale.
The 'adl is appointed by the rahin to sell the pawned property.
2
Determine who received the price
In the given scenario, the buyer paid the price to the murtahin, not to the 'adl. This is the key fact.
3
Apply the rule of agency liability
According to Hanafi law, an agent is only liable for what he personally takes possession of. Since the 'adl never received the price, he has no obligation to return it.
The principle: 'al-wakil la yadman illa ma qabadahu' (the agent is not liable except for what he takes possession of).
4
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, the buyer cannot sue the 'adl. Instead, he must recover the price from the murtahin, who is the principal (muwakkil) in this context.
The term 'muwakkil' here refers to the murtahin because the sale was for his benefit.

Ruling on the 'adl's recourse if his agency was not a condition of the pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of recourse for an 'adl who incurs liability after selling pledged property when his agency was not a condition of the pledge contract and apply it to analogous scenarios.

Core Idea
An agent who suffers loss from executing his agency recovers from his principal, not from the third-party creditor.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in pledge (rahn) transactions: the 'adl (trustee) is authorized by the rahin (pledgor) to sell the pledged property after the contract of pledge, and this authorization was not part of the original pledge terms. If the 'adl sells and pays the murtahin (creditor) but later faces an eviction claim (istihqaq), from whom does he recover? The answer shapes our understanding of agency liability in Hanafi law.

The 'adl recovers the compensation from the rahin (his principal), not from the murtahin. The reason: the 'adl acted as the rahin's agent, so any loss from the agency falls on the principal.

The ruling illustrates the broader Hanafi principle that an agent's liability from executing the agency is borne by the principal, unless the agency was a condition of the contract in which case the creditor might be liable.

Destruction of Pledge: Murtahin Recovers Full Value Without Riba
Explaining that murtahin recovers value of pledge upon destruction without riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a pledge is destroyed and explain how the murtahin recovers full value without riba, using the concept of yad al-istifa'.

Core Idea
Destruction of the pledge confirms the murtahin's recovery; he recovers full value without riba via yad al-istifa'.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a pledged item (marhun) is destroyed, the murtahin does not lose his right; rather, he is deemed to have recovered his debt from its value. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling, focusing on the unique Hanafi concept of yad al-istifa' (possession for recovery) and how it avoids riba.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
The murtahin cannot claim anything from the rahin after destruction; the rahin is discharged.
This is the core hukm: la yarji'u 'ala al-rahin bi shay'.
2
Understand yad al-istifa'
The murtahin's possession from the start is for recovery; destruction confirms that recovery is complete.
Yad al-rahn is yad al-istifa' min al-ibtida'.
3
Trace the riba avoidance mechanism
The second proof explains that the murtahin first recovers the exact debt, then upon destruction recovers the remainder, avoiding riba.
First: mustawi al-mi'a; then: mustawi tis'a mi'a bi al-halak.
4
Contrast with death without killing
If a slave dies naturally (not killed), the murtahin recovers the entire value via the slave without riba, since no monetary excess is created.
Bikhilaf ma idha mata min ghayr qatl ahad.

Commentary
This passage addresses a ruling about the destruction of a pledged item (marhun) and its effect on the debt. The author confirms that the murtahin (creditor-pledgee) does not claim any additional amount from the rahin (debtor-pledgor) after the pledge is destroyed. Two proofs are given. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on a mortgagee's sale of pawned property at the mortgagor's order and determine the disposition of proceeds.

Core Idea
Sale by mortgagor's order voids the pawn and leaves the debt except what is recovered.

When a mortgagor instructs the mortgagee to sell the pawned item, the mortgagee acts as the mortgagor's agent. Hanafi fiqh treats this as if the mortgagor retrieved the pawn and sold it himself. This has two key consequences: the pawn contract ends, and the debt remains except for the amount satisfied from the sale. Understanding this rule protects both parties' rights in a common transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the mortgagee selling the pawned item upon the mortgagor's order. State the ruling: the sale is valid; pawn terminates; debt remains except recovered amount; surplus belongs to mortgagor.
The hukm is sahih (valid) and effective.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (talil)
The author analogizes: by granting permission, the mortgagor is deemed to have taken back the pawn and sold it himself. This is a qiyas that changes the nature of the possession from a pawn-holder's custodianship to an agent's sale.
Qiyas: asl = mortgagor selling own property; far' = mortgagee selling with permission; illa = the permission gives constructive repossession.
3
Apply the effect on the pawn contract
Since the sale is as if by the mortgagor, the pawn (rahn) becomes void. The mortgagee's right of retention ends. The property is no longer security.
The pawn contract is nullified because the pawned item leaves the mortgagee's hands as a trust.
4
Determine the impact on the debt
The debt remains except to the extent of what the mortgagee recovered from the sale. If the sale amount is less than the debt, the mortgagor still owes the balance. If more, the surplus is returned to the mortgagor.
The example: debt of 1000, sale for 100 → 900 returned to mortgagor.
5
Note the mufta bihi and cross-madhhab note
This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school (Zahir al-Riwayah). Some other schools may treat the permission differently, but Hanafi ruling is settled.
Authority: Al-Hidayah; weight: zahir_riwayah; status: mukhtalaf fih.

Dispute Between Rahin and Murtahin on Ransom vs Handover
Ruling on disagreement between rahin and murtahin regarding paying ransom or handing over the slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a disagreement between rahin and murtahin regarding paying ransom or handing over a pledged slave, and identify the rationale (dalil) for preferring the party who opts to pay ransom.

Core Idea
When rahin and murtahin disagree, the one willing to pay ransom prevails because it causes no harm to the other.

In a pledge (rahn) transaction, if the pledged slave commits a crime, both the pledgor (rahin) and the pledgee (murtahin) are obligated to fulfill the guarantee. A dispute arises when one wants to pay ransom and the other wants to hand over the slave. This lesson examines the Hanafi resolution of this dispute and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path

Identify the parties and their obligations
Recognize that both rahin and murtahin share the daman (guarantee) for the slave's crime, each covering a portion—daman for the murtahin and amanah for the rahin.
2
Understand the two options: ransom or handover
Both options are permissible if agreed. Handover voids the murtahin's debt.
3
Analyze the query about murtahin's capacity
The murtahin cannot directly hand over since he is not the owner, but his consent is necessary; the text uses musamahah (accommodation) to express this.
4
Examine the disagreement scenario
One party wants ransom, the other handover. Determine which claim is accepted.
5
Apply the rationale (dalil aqli)
The one offering ransom is preferred because it causes no harm to the other party and does not nullify the murtahin's debt.

The Child of a Pledged Slave: Murtahin Not Liable for Its Crime
Ruling that murtahin is not liable for the child's crime

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the child of a pledged slave (walad ar-rahn) and explain why the murtahin is not liable for its crime, including the concept of mutabarri'.

Core Idea
The child of a pledged slave is not guaranteed, so the murtahin is not liable for its crime; voluntary payment is a donation.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Islamic law of pledge (rahn): when a female pledged slave gives birth, her child is not part of the pledge in terms of guarantee. If that child commits a crime, the murtahin (creditor holding the pledge) is not liable for the compensation. However, the murtahin may voluntarily pay the ransom, but he cannot recover it from the rahin (pledgor). This ruling is based on a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa and highlights the distinction between guaranteed and non-guaranteed items in the pledge contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the crime committed by the child of a pledged slave (walad ar-rahn). The ruling: the murtahin is not liable for the guarantee; he may voluntarily pay ransom but cannot recover from the rahin.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a narration from Abu Hanifa that the murtahin does not recover when the rahin is present.
The narration is a qawl sahabi (statement of a Companion) in the Hanafi framework, but here it's from Abu Hanifa, who is a tabi'i.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The murtahin's payment is voluntary because he could have chosen not to pay, leaving the liability on the rahin. Thus, he is a mutabarri' (volunteer) and cannot reclaim.
The key reasoning: the child is not maddmun (guaranteed), so the murtahin has no inherent duty to pay.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is another opinion that the murtahin can recover if the rahin is absent or refuses to pay.
This ikhtilaf is intra-Hanafi and will be discussed later in the text.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Al-Marghinani prefers the narration that the murtahin cannot recover when the rahin is present, making him a volunteer.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Rahin's Payment After Murtahin Refuses: Setoff Against Debt
Ruling on deduction from murtahin's debt when rahin pays ransom after murtahin refuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that half the ransom paid by the rahin is set off against the murtahin's debt, determining the new debt and pledge status.

Core Idea
The rahin's payment of the murtahin's obligation is not voluntary; half the ransom offsets the debt.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the contract of pledge (rahn): what happens when the pledgee (murtahin) refuses to pay a required ransom for the pledged property, and the pledgor (rahin) pays it instead. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on the principle that the murtahin's obligation is necessary and the rahin is not a volunteer. Understanding this ruling helps you grasp how setoff (muqasah) operates in contractual obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their obligations
In a rahn contract, the rahin pledges a slave to secure a debt. The murtahin is the pledgee. If the slave commits a crime, ransom may be required. The murtahin is obligated to pay half the ransom (due to daman) unless he refuses.
This obligation arises from the murtahin's possession of the slave.
2
Determine the consequences of the murtahin's refusal and the rahin's payment
If the murtahin refuses, the rahin may pay the entire ransom to save the slave. The key ruling is that the rahin is not a volunteer (mutatabbi') because the murtahin's obligation is binding. Therefore, the rahin can claim recourse.
Volunteers cannot claim reimbursement; non-volunteers can.
3
Apply the setoff (muqasah) rule
Half the ransom paid by the rahin is credited against the murtahin's debt. Compare the amounts: if half the ransom equals or exceeds the debt, the entire debt is void. If half is less, the debt is reduced by that amount, and the slave remains pledged for the remaining debt.
The setoff occurs automatically, not requiring the murtahin's consent.
4
Conclude the new debt status and pledge
After the setoff, the slave remains a pledge for the remaining debt, if any. The murtahin's debt is partially or fully satisfied.
If the debt is fully voided, the pledge is released.
5
Understand the underlying principle: no volunteerism
The rahin's payment is treated as discharging the murtahin's wajib obligation. This prevents unjust enrichment and ensures the rahin can recover from the murtahin.
The principle of luzum (necessity) makes the payment non-voluntary.
