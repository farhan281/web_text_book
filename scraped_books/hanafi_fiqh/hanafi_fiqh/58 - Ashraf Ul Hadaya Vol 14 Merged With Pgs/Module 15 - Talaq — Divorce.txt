Ruling on Permissible Intoxicating Drinks: Hadd and Divorce Consequences
Imam Muhammad's ruling on drinks beyond the four intoxicants in al-Jami' al-Saghir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on drinks made from wheat, barley, honey, and corn, and explain why no hadd applies and why divorce is invalid.

Core Idea
Not all intoxicating drinks are khamr; Abu Hanifa rules drinks from four grains halal, exempt from hadd and divorce.

Imam Muhammad's text in al-Jami' al-Saghir offers a clear and unique statement: drinks beyond the four known intoxicants are permissible. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its implications for hadd and divorce, and the legal reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of drinks
Locate the phrase 'ما سوى ذلك من الأشربة' – drinks other than the four intoxicants. These include wheat, barley, honey, and corn drinks.
The four intoxicants refer to grape wine (khamr), date wine, raisin wine, and a specific other.
2
Note the ruling: 'لا بأس به'
Imam Muhammad declares these drinks permissible (halal/mubah). This is a direct ruling from the matn.
The commentators stress this generality is unique to this work.
3
Understand the consequence for hadd
Even if a person becomes intoxicated from such a drink, he is not subject to the hadd penalty for drinking alcohol.
Hadd is only for khamr; these drinks are not khamr in Hanafi law.
4
Understand the consequence for divorce
A divorce pronounced by a person intoxicated from these drinks does not take effect; it is like the divorce of a sleeper.
The intoxication does not render the person legally responsible (mukallaf) for talaq.
5
Compare with parallel cases
The text analogizes to intoxication from bang (hashish) and mare's milk – both non-khamr intoxicants with similar rulings.
This shows the principle applies to any substance not classified as khamr.

And he (Imam Muhammad) said in al-Jami' al-Saghir: 'And besides that from the drinks, there is no harm in it.' They (the scholars) said: This answer, in this generality and clarity, is not found in any other work. And it is a text (nass) that what is made from wheat, barley, honey, and corn is halal according to Abu Hanifa, and its drinker will not be subject to hadd according to him even if he becomes intoxicated from it, and the divorce of an intoxicated person from it does not occur, being like a sleeper, and like one whose mind has left due to bang (hashish) and mare's milk (laban al-ramak).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' al-Saghir, stating that any drink other than the four intoxicating drinks (ashribah arba'ah) is halal (no prohibition). This is considered a unique text (nass) that generalizes to drinks made from wheat, barley, honey, and corn. According to Abu Hanifa, such drinks are not subject to hadd even if they cause intoxication, because their intoxication does not constitute the legal intoxication (sukr) that triggers hadd. Furthermore, the divorce pronounced by a person intoxicated from such drinks is invalid, like that of a sleeper or an insane person from bang or mare's milk. This ruling is specific to Abu Hanifa's position; the commentaries note that this answer is not found in other works with such clarity.

Key Terms
لا بأس به (la ba'sa bihi): There is no harm; it is permissible (mubah).
نص (nass): A clear textual statement that serves as a primary evidence for a ruling.
السكران (al-sakran): One who is intoxicated; here from permissible drinks, his divorce is invalid.
