Maintenance Expenses on the Pledgor: Specific Examples
Lists specific expenses of islah wa ibqa' on the pledgor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that expenses for preservation and improvement (islah wa ibqa') of pledged property are wajib on the pledgor, and list the specific examples given in the text.

Core Idea
Expenses that preserve or improve pledged property fall on the owner (pledgor), not the pledgee.

In a pledge contract, the pledged property remains the owner's responsibility. Therefore, any cost needed to maintain or enhance its value is borne by the pledgor. This lesson examines the specific examples cited by Imam al-Quduri in the matn: clothing of slaves, wet nurse for the child of a pledged slave, irrigation, canal digging, pollination, harvesting, and similar interests.

Guided Path
1
State the General Principle
Any expenditure that is for islah (improvement) or ibqa' (preservation) of the pledged property is wajib on the pledgor (rahin). This is because ownership and benefit remain with the pledgor.
This principle is derived from the broader rule that expenses of maintenance follow ownership.
2
Identify the Listed Examples
From the text, list: (1) clothing of slaves (kiswat al-raqiq), (2) hiring a wet nurse for the child of a pledged slave woman, (3) irrigating an orchard, (4) digging a canal, (5) pollinating date palms, (6) harvesting fruit, and (7) any other similar interests.
These are not exhaustive; they illustrate the scope of islah wa ibqa'.
3
Understand the Rationale
Each of these expenses is essential to preserve the property's utility or enhance its value. For example, clothing protects a slave's health; irrigation and pollination ensure crop production. Without them, the property would deteriorate.
The child of a pledged slave is considered part of the pledge if born during the pledge, so its upkeep is on the pledgor.
4
Distinguish from Guarding Expenses
Expenses for guarding (hifz) – e.g., renting a watchman or storage – are on the pledgee (murtahin) because they protect the pledgee's right of security. This is not islah wa ibqa' but protection from loss.
This distinction is crucial to avoid confusion.

Recovery of Expenses by a Co-Owner: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains when a co-owner can recover expenses from another, with an intra-Hanafi dispute from Nawadir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on recovery of co-owner expenses and explain how the dispute is rooted in the authority of the qadi to impose interdiction (hajr).

Core Idea
A co-owner's right to recover expenses hinges on the qadi's authority to interdict a present adult — a point of intra-Hanafi disagreement.

In al-Hidayah, the default ruling (Zahir al-Riwayah) is that a co-owner who spends on joint property with the qadi's order can recover the amount from the other co-owner. Without the qadi's order, the spender is considered a volunteer. However, the Nawadir records a significant disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on whether the qadi's order suffices when the other co-owner is present. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its foundation in the law of interdiction (hajr).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Zahir al-Riwayah ruling
The passage first states the relied-upon position: recovery is allowed only if the qadi ordered the expense. Understand the rationale: the qadi's order transforms the spender from a volunteer into an agent.
2
Recognize the Nawadir reports
The passage then cites two reports from Nawadir: Abu Hanifa says no recovery if the other co-owner is present, even with a qadi's order; Abu Yusuf says recovery is allowed in all cases.
3
Link to the issue of interdiction (hajr)
The commentary explains that this dispute is a branch of a deeper question: Can a qadi place a present, capable adult under interdiction? Abu Hanifa says no, so the qadi's order is ineffective; Abu Yusuf says yes, making the spender a guardian.
4
Compare the rationales
For Abu Hanifa, the present co-owner retains full capacity, so any order is futile; the spender remains a volunteer. For Abu Yusuf, the qadi's order creates valid guardianship, entitling the spender to reclaim expenses.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa's view, a co-owner who spends with a qadi's order on a present partner cannot recover, potentially discouraging needed repairs. Under Abu Yusuf's view, recovery is always possible, encouraging maintenance.

Mortgaging Undivided Property: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
States the ruling on mortgaging undivided property, with two Hanafi proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mortgaging an undivided share (مشاع) and explain the two proofs from the purpose of mortgage (ید استیفاء) and the requirement of permanent detention (حبس دائم).

Core Idea
Mortgaging an undivided share is invalid because exclusive possession for recovery is impossible and permanence is lost.

This passage introduces a key inter-madhhab dispute: can you mortgage an undivided share of jointly-owned property? The Hanafi school, following al-Quduri, says no; Imam al-Shafi'i says yes. The difference stems from each school's definition of the purpose of a mortgage. We will examine the two Hanafi proofs and see how they rely on the purposes of the contract.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling from the text: Mortgaging an undivided share (مشاع) is not permissible. The property must be divided before it can be mortgaged.
This is the default position in al-Hidayah and the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.
2
Identify the opposing school's view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that mortgaging an undivided share is permissible because the purpose of a mortgage, in his view, is that the property can be sold to recover the debt. Since selling an undivided share is valid, mortgaging it is also valid.
The Shafi'i view focuses on the potential to sell, not on immediate possession.
3
Analyse the first Hanafi proof (purpose: yad al-istifa')
The Hanafi school defines the ruling/effect of mortgage as 'establishing the hand of recovery' (ید استیفاء) – the mortgagee must have exclusive possessory control to recover the debt. In an undivided share, such exclusive control is impossible because the co-owner also has rights. Therefore, the mortgage cannot achieve its purpose and is invalid.
This is a teleological argument based on the maqsad (purpose) of the contract.
4
Analyse the second Hanafi proof (permanent detention vs. muhayah)
Mortgage requires permanent detention (حبس دائم) of the property. With an undivided share, co-owners must alternate use (مهاياة) – e.g., each uses it for a month. This breaks the permanence required, making the mortgage invalid, analogous to saying 'I mortgage to you for a day.'
The analogy shows that even temporary permission to possess defeats the purpose of mortgage.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In practice, Hanafis require the property to be physically divided (partitioned) before it can be mortgaged. Shafi'is allow the contract on the undivided share itself, without prior partition.
This affects real estate transactions in jurisdictions where Hanafi or Shafi'i law is applied.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new chapter on what can and cannot be mortgaged. It presents the Hanafi ruling from al-Quduri that mortgaging an undivided share (مشاع) is invalid, and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position that it is valid. The author then provides two rational proofs for the Hanafi position. First, the purpose of mortgage in the Hanafi school is to establish a 'hand of recovery' (ید استیفاء), meaning the mortgagee must have exclusive possessory control to recover his debt. In an undivided share, such exclusive control is impossible, so the mortgage cannot be valid. Second, mortgage inherently requires permanent detention (حبس دائم) of the property; it is not a temporary arrangement. With an undivided share, the co-owners must alternate use (مهاياة), which destroys the permanence required. The analogy is made to saying 'I mortgage to you for a day,' which is invalid. Therefore, the Hanafi position prohibits mortgaging any undivided property, whether it is capable of division or not. This illustrates the Hanafi use of legal purpose (maqsad) and systematic reasoning in deriving rulings.

مهاياة (muhayah): Alternate use or division of benefits among co-owners by time or space.

Hibah and the Ruling on Supervening Division (Shuyu' Tari)
Question and answer providing analogical support

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of supervening division (shuyu' tari) on a gift (hibah) and distinguish it from its effect on a mortgage (rahn).

Core Idea
A gift remains valid despite a partial retraction because supervening division does not harm it, unlike a mortgage which is invalidated.

In classical Hanafi fiqh, the validity of a contract can be affected by changes occurring after its conclusion. This lesson examines a specific scenario: what happens when a donor takes back an unspecified part of a gifted property? The text clarifies that the gift remains valid due to the principle of supervening division (shuyu' tari), while the same situation invalidates a mortgage contract. Understanding this distinction helps you apply the correct ruling based on the contract type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling and the objection
The text presents the ruling that a gift is not invalidated by taking back an unspecified part. An objection is raised: why then is an initial gift of a divisible part not permissible?
2
Analyze the distinction between initial and supervening division
In an initial gift of a part, the donor would incur an unwanted loss due to division. In a supervening division (where the gift was already fully possessed), possession was already complete, so no such loss is imposed.
3
Examine the supporting examples
The text gives two cases: (1) Gift: taking back an unspecified part does not invalidate. (2) Mortgage: excluding an unspecified part invalidates the mortgage.
4
Determine the legal principle (illa)
In a gift, the contract is already complete with possession, so later division does not affect its validity. In a mortgage, the contract requires specificity; supervening division corrupts it.

Invalidity of Rahn for Amanah Property
Ruling with proof for invalidity of rahn for amanah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that rahn is invalid when given in exchange for amanah property and explain the rational proof (qiyas) behind it.

Core Idea
Rahn requires a guaranteed counter-value; amanah is not guaranteed, so rahn is batil.

In this lesson, we examine a key condition for the validity of rahn (pawn/pledge) in Hanafi fiqh: the counter-value (badal) must be a guaranteed debt or property. The text presents four scenarios where the property held by the other party is an amanah (trust) – wadi'ah, ariyah, mudarabah, and shirkah – and explains why rahn taken in exchange for such property is invalid. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish valid security arrangements from void ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that in all four cases (wadi'ah, ariyah, mudarabah, shirkah), the rahn contract is batil (invalid). Confirm that the hukm level is batil.
Batil means void ab initio, producing no legal effects.
2
Understand the nature of amanah
Recognise that in each scenario, the property in the other party's hands is an amanah (trust). The holder is not liable for loss unless negligent.
Amanah includes intentional deposits (wadi'ah) and incidental possession (e.g., cloth blown by wind).
3
Apply the rational proof (qiyas)
The text implies a qiyas: rahn is valid only when the counter-value is guaranteed (mudman). Amanah is not guaranteed. Therefore, rahn for amanah is invalid. Identify the asl (valid rahn requires mudman), far' (rahn for amanah), illa (absence of guarantee).
The illa is the lack of daman (liability) on the part of the person holding the amanah.
4
Note the legal consequence
If the pawned property is destroyed in the mortgagee's possession, it does not become a liability; it remains amanah. No compensation is due.
This distinguishes from a valid rahn where loss would be compensated.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
The Hanafi ruling is mukhtalaf fih; the Shafi'i school allows rahn for any financial right, including amanah. Understand the difference in principle.
This ikhtilaf highlights different views on the nature of rahn.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof (hukm ma'a al-dalil) from the commentary on Al-Hidayah. It explains when a pawn (rahn) is invalid because the counter-value (badal) is not a guaranteed debt (mudman). The text gives four examples where the property given as collateral is actually a trust (amanah) or similar: wadi'ah (trust deposit), ariyah (loan for use), mudarabah (profit-sharing partnership), and shirkah (partnership). In all these, the property in the hands of the other party is not guaranteed—if it is destroyed, no compensation is due. Therefore, if someone tries to secure such a property by taking a pawn in exchange, the pawn contract is invalid (batil). The property remains as amanah, so if it perishes in the mortgagee's possession, no liability (daman) arises. The passage then distinguishes between 'mudman bi-ghayrihi' (guaranteed by something else), such as the sold item (mabi') in the seller's possession before delivery. The seller does not guarantee the sold item itself; if it is destroyed, the sale is void and the price is waived, but no compensation is paid. Thus, taking a pawn in exchange for such an item is also invalid. A note (tanbih) clarifies that if the pawned property itself is destroyed but not the original counter-value, the sale remains valid but the pawn still invalid.

Key Terms
رہن (rahn): A contract where property is given as security for a debt or obligation.
امانت (amanah): Property held in trust, where the holder is not liable for loss unless negligent.
مضمون (mudman): Guaranteed; property that obligates compensation if destroyed.

Invalidity of Rahn for Mudman bi-Ghayrihi
Explanation of invalidity of rahn for mudman bi-ghayrihi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mudman bi-ghayrihi and explain why rahn is invalid when the counter-value is such property, using the example of mabi' in the seller's hand.

Core Idea
Mudman bi-ghayrihi is not guaranteed; rahn for it is batil like rahn for amanah.

This lesson builds on the previous one by introducing another category of non-guaranteed property: mudman bi-ghayrihi, meaning property that is guaranteed by something else rather than in itself. The classic example is the sold item (mabi') while still in the seller's possession before delivery. If the item is destroyed, the seller does not compensate the buyer; instead, the price is forfeited. Thus, taking a pawn in exchange for such a property is invalid. Understanding this concept is crucial for distinguishing between property that can serve as a valid counter-value for rahn and property that cannot.

Guided Path
1
Define mudman bi-ghayrihi
The passage defines mudman bi-ghayrihi as an item whose destruction does not obligate compensation (like or value), but instead the right in exchange for it falls. Identify the sold item in the seller's hand as the example.
Mudman bi-ghayrihi is contrasted with mudman bi'aynihi (guaranteed in itself).
2
Understand the legal consequence
If the sold item perishes in the seller's possession, the sale becomes void and the price is cancelled. The seller is not liable for the item's value.
This is because the risk of loss before delivery is on the buyer? Actually in Hanafi, before delivery, the risk is on the seller, but the item is not guaranteed in the sense of compensation; rather the sale is void.
3
Apply the ruling on rahn
Since the counter-value (the sold item in this context) is not guaranteed, rahn taken in exchange for it is invalid. The text explicitly states this.
The ruling is the same as for amanah: both are ghayr mudman.
4
Distinguish from mudman bi'aynihi
Contrast with mudman bi'aynihi (e.g., debt) where rahn is valid. Know the difference in terms of guarantee.
This distinction is important for identifying which properties can be counter-values.
5
Note the tanbih (note)
The passage adds a note: if the pawned property (marhun) is destroyed but the counter-value (the sold item) remains, the sale is valid but the pawn was invalid. No compensation is due.
This clarifies that invalidity of rahn does not affect the underlying contract.

Validity of Rahn for Mudman bi-Aynihi (Guaranteed Items)
States and explains the ruling on rahn validity for mudman bi-'aynihi items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that a pawn (rahn) is valid only when the counter-value is mudman bi-'aynihi, and identify valid and invalid rahn scenarios based on this condition.

Core Idea
A pawn is valid only if the counter-value is guaranteed in itself — deliverable or compensated by like/value.

The validity of a pawn (rahn) hinges on the nature of the obligation it secures. This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that rahn is valid when the counter-value is a 'guaranteed item' (mudman bi-'aynihi). Understanding this category is essential for distinguishing valid from invalid rahn transactions. The text provides a rational proof and four concrete examples to illustrate the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the overall hukm for rahn when the counter-value is mudman bi-'aynihi? The ruling is sahih (valid).
2
Define the condition
What does 'mudman bi-'aynihi' mean? It means the item is guaranteed in itself: if it exists, it must be delivered as is; if destroyed, its like (for fungibles) or value (for non-fungibles) must be paid.
3
Understand the proof
The proof (dalil) is that the obligation is 'daman mustaqirr' — an established guarantee. Since the right is definite, securing it with a pawn is valid.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) from the nature of guarantee.
4
Apply the condition to examples
The text gives four examples: usurped property, consideration for khul', dower, and compromise for intentional homicide. Confirm that each is mudman bi-'aynihi.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school holds that rahn is valid for any known financial right, not only mudman bi-'aynihi. Understand the practical difference.
Hanafi position is more restrictive.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof (hukm ma'a al-dalil) that clarifies when a pawn (rahn) is valid. The counter-value (badal) must be 'mudman bi-'aynihi', meaning it is guaranteed in itself. This means that if the item exists, it must be delivered as is; if it is destroyed, either its like (if fungible) or its value (if non-fungible) is owed. The text gives four examples: usurped property (maghsub), consideration for khul' (badal khul'), dower (mahr), and the consideration for a compromise in intentional homicide (badal sulh 'an dam 'amd). In each case, the person obliged to deliver the item (e.g., the usurper, the husband, the killer) can give a pawn to secure that obligation. The proof is that these obligations are guaranteed (daman mustaqirr), so securing them with a pawn is valid. The commentary (tashrih) further explains that if the counter-value is of a type that has likes (e.g., measurable or weighable items), the like must be delivered; if it has no like (e.g., horses or unique items), the value is due. Thus, rahn is valid in such cases.

Key Terms
مضمون بعينه (mudman bi-'aynihi): An item that is guaranteed in itself; upon its destruction, its like or value is obligatory.
مغصوب (maghsub): Property taken by force (usurped); the usurper is liable for its delivery or value.
بدل ضلع (badal khul'): The consideration paid by a wife to her husband for khul' (divorce by mutual consent).
بدل صلح عن دم العمد (badal sulh 'an dam al-'amd): The consideration paid in a compromise to avoid retribution (qisas) for intentional homicide.

Wasi Pledging Orphan's Property to Himself or Relatives
Ruling on wasi's pledge contracts with himself or relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding a wasi's pledge contract involving orphan property and distinguish between valid and invalid scenarios based on guardianship and the father's exception.

Core Idea
A wasi cannot handle both sides of a pledge; only a father's abundant compassion allows that exception.

This passage from al-Hidayah examines four scenarios where a wasi (guardian of an orphan) engages in a pledge (rahn) contract with the orphan or his own relatives. The central ruling is that such pledges are invalid because one person cannot act as both offeror and acceptor, and the wasi, being deficient in compassion, cannot be analogized to a father. The text also distinguishes between those under the wasi's guardianship and those outside it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the contract type
The wasi is the guardian of an orphan. The contract is rahn (pledge), where property is given as security for a debt. The scenarios involve the wasi pledging orphan property to himself, to his own minor child or slave without debt, or pledging his own property to the orphan.
2
Recognize the principle: one person cannot handle both sides
Just as in a sale contract, one person cannot be both seller and buyer. In pledge, the same applies: offer and acceptance must come from two distinct parties. The wasi is a pure agent, so he cannot represent both sides.
This is the primary reason for invalidity.
3
Understand the father's exception
A father can pledge his minor child's property to himself due to his abundant compassion (wufur al-shafaqah). This exception is specific to the father and cannot be extended to the wasi, who is deficient in compassion (qasir al-shafaqah).
The exception is based on istihsan (juristic preference).
4
Distinguish based on guardianship
Pledging to a minor child or a slave without debt is like pledging to himself because the wasi has guardianship over them. Pledging to an adult son, father, or slave with debt is valid because the wasi has no guardianship over them.
Guardianship (wilayah) is key.

Permissibility of Wasi Borrowing and Pledging Orphan's Property
Ruling with expansion: wasi borrowing and pledging for orphan's needs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a wasi borrows for an orphan's necessities and pledges the orphan's property, and identify the conditions that make it permissible.

Core Idea
A wasi may borrow and pledge orphan's property for essential needs; necessity justifies the transaction.

This passage addresses a practical guardianship scenario: the wasi (guardian) needs funds for the orphan's food and clothing, so he borrows money and pledges the orphan's property as collateral. The Hidayah rules this permissible, and we will explore the reasoning behind it. Understanding this ruling illustrates how necessity (darurah) interacts with property rights in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties involved
Recognize that the wasi is acting on behalf of the orphan. The act consists of two parts: borrowing money (istidānah) and pledging the orphan's property (rahn). The purpose is to provide for the orphan's clothing and food.
The orphan's property is being used as security, not sold or wasted.
2
Determine the ruling (hukm) for each part
The text states both borrowing and pledging are permissible (ja'iz). Note that the ruling is not obligatory but permissible, contingent on necessity.
The hukm level is 'ja'iz' (permissible), not 'mustahabb' or 'wajib'.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The text gives two reasons: (1) borrowing is permitted out of necessity (li al-hajah), and (2) pledging fulfills a right (iifa' al-haqq). The wasi has the authority to pay the orphan's obligatory expenses from the orphan's wealth.
The necessity here is specific: the orphan's basic needs (clothing, food).
4
Identify the implicit conditions
From the reasoning, deduce conditions: the borrowing must be for genuine necessity (e.g., clothing, food); the pledge must be for securing repayment; the wasi must act in the orphan's best interest.
The absence of necessity would render the transaction impermissible.
5
Apply the ruling to a similar scenario
Consider if the wasi borrows for the orphan's education or medical treatment—does the same ruling apply? Yes, by extension, because these are also necessities.
Analogous reasoning (qiyas) extends the ruling beyond the explicit examples.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on a Broken Pledged Pot: Imam Muhammad vs. the Two Shaykhs
Intra-Hanafi dispute on disposal of a broken pledged pot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Muhammad and the two Shaykhs regarding a broken pledged pot, explain their proofs, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Breakage of a pledge is not destruction; the creditor must compensate for the loss in value, which becomes a new pledge.

When a pledged item breaks but is not destroyed, what is the legal consequence? This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute where Imam Muhammad analogizes breakage to destruction, allowing the debt to be cancelled, while the two Shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) insist on compensating the loss in value to avoid pre-Islamic forfeiture. The relied-upon position is that of the two Shaykhs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
The pledged pot is broken – its value is reduced but the substance remains. Determine whether this is equivalent to destruction (halāk) or a distinct category requiring compensation for the loss.
The distinction between 'inksār' (breakage) and 'halāk' (destruction) is key.
2
State Imam Muhammad's position and his proof
Imam Muhammad gives the pledgor two options: redeem the broken pot bearing the loss, or treat it as satisfaction of the debt. He analogizes breakage to destruction because free redemption is impossible, and by consensus destruction cancels the debt.
His argument is based on qiyās: breakage is like destruction when the utility is lost.
3
State the position of the two Shaykhs and their proof
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the pledgor must compensate the loss in value, and the broken pot remains a pledge. They argue that in true destruction, recovery is through value, not the debt itself. Treating the pot as satisfaction would be 'ighlāq al-rahn' (pre-Islamic forfeiture).
They emphasize the prohibition of ighlāq al-rahn and the necessity to avoid ribā.
4
Examine the rebuttal to Imam Muhammad
The commentary refutes Imam Muhammad by pointing out that his analogy fails because the pledge still exists, and that making it against the debt would be a jāhilī custom. The correct method is to assess the value and make the creditor liable for it, which then becomes a new pledge.
The commentators add that liability for value is not just preferred but obligatory.
5
Analyze the third scenario where value < weight
When the pot's value (8 dirhams) is less than its weight (10 dirhams), all three imams agree that the creditor must be liable for the value. Imam Muhammad agrees because setting off the pot by weight would cause loss to the pledgor, and by value would involve ribā.
This shows that the dispute is resolved in cases where value is less than the debt.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on the Hidayah, presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a broken pledged item (a pot). The central issue is the treatment of a pledge that has become defective (broken) but not destroyed. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani analogizes breakage to complete destruction, giving the pledgor (rahin) two options: either bear the loss and redeem the broken pot while still owing the full debt, or treat the broken pot as satisfaction of the debt, thereby extinguishing the debt. He argues that since free redemption is impossible due to the defect, the breakage effectively equates to destruction, and in true destruction the debt is cancelled by consensus. The two Shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) disagree and hold that when the pot is broken, the debtor must compensate for the loss in value, and the pot remains as a pledge. The author of the commentary refutes Imam Muhammad by pointing out that in destruction, the pledge is replaced by its value (qimah), not by the debt itself. Setting off the broken pot against the debt would be 'ighlaq al-rahn' (closing the pledge), a pre-Islamic practice of forfeiture prohibited in Islam. Thus, the correct ruling is that the broken pot is valued, and that value becomes the new pledge; the creditor (murtahin) is liable to the debtor for that value, and a set-off occurs between the value and the debt. The passage then discusses a third scenario where the pot's value is less than its weight (e.g., value 8 dirhams vs weight 10 dirhams). Here all agree that the creditor becomes liable for the value (8 dirhams) — either its good value from a different type or inferior value from the same type — and this value is held as a pledge. Imam Muhammad agrees in this case because when the value is less than the weight, setting off the pot by weight would overcompensate the creditor (causing loss to the debtor) and setting it off by value would involve riba (since value is less than debt). Therefore, the only valid path is liability for value. The commentary concludes by noting that the author of Hidayah preferred mentioning the third scenario before the second due to its more detailed nature.

Key Terms
انکسار (inksār): Breakage or defect in a pledged item that reduces its value but does not destroy it entirely.
ہلاکت (halākat): Complete destruction of the pledged item, where its substance is gone.
تضمین بالقیمہ (tamdīn bi'l-qīmah): Making the debtor liable for the value of the pledged item (i.e., compensating the pledgor for the loss in value).
اغلاق الرهن (ighlāq al-rahn): The pre-Islamic practice where the creditor automatically becomes owner of the pledged property if the debt is unpaid, forfeiting the pledgor's rights.

Dispute Over Pledge Amount Between Mu'ir and Musta'ir
Ruling on whose statement is accepted in a dispute over the pledge amount.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of whose statement is accepted when the mu'ir and musta'ir dispute the amount for which the loaned item was to be pledged, and evaluate the analogy used.

Core Idea
When mu'ir and musta'ir disagree on the pledge amount, the mu'ir's word stands—just as he could deny the entire loan.

You lend a valuable item to someone and ask them to pledge something as security for its return. Later, you disagree on the value of that pledge. Whose word should the judge accept? This lesson explains the Hanafi position on this procedural matter, which uses qiyas (analogy) from a simpler case: if the lender denies giving permission to pledge entirely, his statement is taken as true. Since the amount is just a detail (description) of that permission, the same rule applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Dispute
First, recognize the disagreement: the mu'ir (lender) claims he ordered a pledge of a certain amount, and the musta'ir (borrower) claims a different amount. For example, mu'ir says 500, musta'ir says 300.
This is a dispute over a description of the contract, not over the contract's existence.
2
Recall the Rule for Denying the Origin
In Hanafi law, if the mu'ir completely denies having given permission to pledge, his statement is accepted (with an oath). This is because he is denying the entire contract.
The principle: the party denying a claim is given credence, especially when they could have made a stronger denial.
3
Apply Qiyas (Analogy)
The amount is a description of the permission. Just as the mu'ir's statement is accepted in denying the origin (the permission itself), it is also accepted in denying a description of it. Thus, his statement on the amount is taken as correct.
This is a clear qiyas jaliy (evident analogy).
4
Note the Textual Correction
Some copies of al-Hidayah incorrectly read 'kama' (as), which would compare mu'ir and musta'ir directly and lead to a wrong conclusion. The correct reading is 'lawla' (if not for), which makes the analogy about the act of denial, not the parties.
Always check critical editions; a single letter can change the meaning.
5
Conclude the Ruling
Therefore, in a dispute over the pledge amount, the mu'ir's statement is accepted. The judge should decide based on his assertion, after requiring an oath if necessary.
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi.

Liability for Pledge Destroyed Before Loan Disbursement
Ruling with proof on pledgee liability for destroyed pledge before loan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a pledged item is destroyed before the promised loan is given, and explain the principle that a promised debt is like an actual debt.

Core Idea
A promised debt is treated as existing for liability purposes.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the contract of rahn (pawn) where a borrowed item is pledged as security for a loan that has been promised but not yet disbursed. If the pledged item is destroyed before the loan is given, who bears the loss? The Hanafi school holds the pledgee liable, based on the legal maxim that the promised is like the existing. We will walk through the reasoning and the practical consequences for all parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the transaction
Three parties: the mu'ir (owner who lends an item for pledging), the musta'ir/rahin (borrower who pledges the item), and the pledgee who promises a loan. The pledge is made for a future loan, not a current one.
The item is borrowed specifically to be pledged.
2
Recognize the event: destruction before loan
The pledged item perishes in the hands of the pledgee before the promised loan is given. This triggers the question of liability.
The named amount (the promised loan) and the value of the item are equal in the case presented.
3
Apply the principle 'maw'ud mithl mawjud'
The Hanafi maxim that a promised thing is considered as existing for legal purposes. Here, the promised debt is treated as if it were actually given, so the pledge is considered as security for an existing debt.
This is the key proof (dalil aqli).
4
Determine the pledgee's liability
Since the debt is treated as existing, the pledgee is liable for the amount of the promised loan (equal to the value of the item). He must compensate the rahin.
The rahin recovers the value from the pledgee.
5
Determine the mu'ir's right against the rahin
The mu'ir (original owner) can recover the same amount from the rahin because the safety of the monetary value of the pledge is achieved either by the rahin receiving compensation from the pledgee (here) or by discharge of the rahin's debt (if loan had been given).
The rahin remains liable to the mu'ir.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on a pledge made for a promised debt (dain maw'ud) where the pledged item is destroyed before the loan is actually given. The ruling: the pledgee (mortgagee) is liable for the promised amount (equal to the value of the item) to the rahin (pledgor). The proof is the principle that a promised debt is treated like an actual debt ('the promised is like the existing'). Then the mu'ir (the original owner who lent the item for the purpose of pledging) may recover the same amount from the rahin, because the safety of the monetary value of the pledge is achieved either by the rahin receiving the value from the pledgee or by his debt being discharged. In this case, the rahin's liability to the mu'ir remains; therefore he must pay the mu'ir the amount he receives from the pledgee. The commentary provides a concrete example: Khalid borrows Zaid's blanket, pledges it to Bakr for a promised loan of 1000 rupees. The blanket is destroyed before Bakr gives the loan. Bakr must pay 1000 rupees to Khalid (since the promise is like actual). Zaid then recovers that 1000 rupees from Khalid. This is consistent with the Hanafi position that the mu'ir's right is in the monetary value of the item.

مستعیر (musta'ir): One who borrows an item for use, here specifically to pledge it.

Manumission of a Borrowed Slave Pledged as Collateral
Ruling on owner freeing a borrowed pledged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a mu'ir freeing a borrowed slave that is pledged, and explain the pledgee's two legal options.

Core Idea
Ownership of the slave's body permits manumission even when pledged; pledgee can claim debt from rahin or value from mu'ir.

This lesson explores a scenario where a borrowed slave has been pledged by the borrower to a third party. The original owner (mu'ir) then frees the slave. Does the manumission hold? What rights does the pledgee have? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer that balances the owner's dominion with the pledgee's security interest, granting the pledgee a choice between two remedies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Recognize the mu'ir (owner/lender), the rahin (borrower/pledgor), and the murtahin (pledgee). The borrowed slave is the pledged asset.
2
Determine the validity of the manumission
The manumission is valid because the mu'ir retains ownership of the slave's corpus (milk al-raqaba). The pledge does not transfer ownership.
Ownership of the usufruct (manfa'a) is separate; the pledge grants only a security right, not ownership.
3
Analyze the pledgee's first option
The pledgee may demand the debt directly from the rahin, since the pledged asset is no longer available to satisfy the debt.
This option treats the pledge as if it had never been created, reverting the debt obligation to the rahin.
4
Analyze the pledgee's second option
The pledgee may claim the slave's market value from the mu'ir, because the pledgee's right was attached to the slave with the mu'ir's consent, and the mu'ir destroyed that right by manumitting.
The mu'ir pays the value, which then serves as a substitute pledge until the debt is paid.
5
Understand the substitute pledge mechanism
The value paid by the mu'ir remains with the pledgee as a pledge. When the rahin pays the debt, the pledgee returns the value to the mu'ir, because recovering the value is like recovering the original chattel.
This ensures the pledgee does not lose his security while the mu'ir ultimately bears no permanent loss.

Commentary
This passage deals with the situation where a borrowed slave (from a loan of usage, 'ariyah) has been pledged by the borrower to a third party as security for a debt. The original owner (mu'ir) then manumits (frees) the slave. The ruling: the manumission is valid because the mu'ir retains ownership of the slave's corpus (raqaba). However, the pledgee (mortgagee) has two options: (1) He may demand the debt from the rahin (the borrower/pledgor), because the slave is no longer in his possession to recover the debt, so his right to the debt remains. (2) He may hold the mu'ir liable for the slave's value, because the pledgee's right (to hold the slave as security) was attached to the slave's neck with the mu'ir's consent, and the mu'ir destroyed that right by manumitting the slave. In this case, the mu'ir must pay the slave's value to the pledgee, and this value will remain with the pledgee as a substitute pledge until the rahin pays his debt. Once the debt is paid, the pledgee returns the value to the mu'ir, because the value stands in place of the original pledged item. The commentary clarifies with an example: the borrowed slave is with Bakr (pledgee). Zaid (owner, mu'ir) frees him. Bakr can either get his debt from Khalid (the rahin) or take the slave's value from Zaid, keep it as pledge, and return it to Zaid when Khalid pays.

خیار (khiyār): An option or choice given to a party in a contract. Here, the pledgee has an option between two remedies.
قیمت (qīmat): Market value of the slave at the time of manumission, which the mu'ir must pay as compensation.

Effect of Price Decrease on Pledged Property
Ruling that market price drop does not reduce debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on market price drop in rahn, explain the majority proof, and evaluate Imam Zufar's counter-argument.

Core Idea
Market price drop does not reduce debt; only physical loss does.

This passage from the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn) addresses whether a drop in market value affects the debt secured by a pledge. The Hanafi school distinguishes between market fluctuation (nuqsan al-sa'r) and physical loss (nuqsan al-'ayn). Imam Zufar dissents, but the majority holds that the debt remains intact. The ruling is based on the concept of mortgagee's possession as a realization of debt (qabd al-istifa').

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling
Determine the exact legal question: Does a decrease in the market price of the pledged property reduce the debt proportionally? The passage explicitly contrasts physical loss with price drop.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad vs Zufar).
2
State the Hanafi majority position and proof
The majority holds that price drop does not reduce the debt. Their proof: In sale, a price drop does not give an option to rescind; in usurpation, it does not create liability. Only physical loss (nuqsan al-'ayn) reduces the debt because the mortgagee's possession is one of realization (qabd al-istifa').
The key concept is qabd al-istifa' – the mortgagee is considered to have recovered the debt to the extent of the property's value.
3
Understand Imam Zufar's opposing view and proof
Imam Zufar analogizes price drop to physical loss: both reduce value, so the debt should be proportionally reduced. He applies qiyas (analogy) on the illah of value decrease.
Zufar's position is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and refutation
The Hanafi response rebuts the analogy: Price is external and does not affect the legal attachment of the right. The mortgagee's possession is a possession of realization, which only applies to the 'ayn (physical object) and its loss, not to market fluctuations. The ruling is consistent across sale and ghasb.
The refutation highlights that the illah is not value per se, but physical loss.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Zufar's view, if pledge value drops by half, half the debt is cancelled. Under the majority view, the full debt remains due. This affects the mortgagee's right to claim and the pledgor's obligation.
The mufta bihi (relied upon) position is the majority view.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the effect of a decrease in market price (nusqan al-saman) on the debt secured by a pledge (rahn). The ruling states that a mere drop in market price does not reduce the debt; the pledged property remains collateral for the full amount. This is contrasted with physical loss (nusqan al-'ayn), where loss of a part of the property proportionally reduces the debt because the mortgagee's possession is one of realization (istifa'). Imam Zufar dissents, analogizing price decrease to physical loss since both reduce value. The Hanafi proof (proof is based on analogy (qiyas) and consideration of legal effects in analogous areas): (1) In sale, a price drop does not give the buyer an option to rescind (khiyar); (2) In usurpation (ghasb), a price drop does not create liability for the usurper. Only physical loss of the ‘ayn creates liability. The reason is that mortgagee’s possession is a possession of realization (qabd al-istifa'), meaning the mortgagee is considered to have recovered the debt to the extent of the value of the property. Hence, physical loss gives proportional recovery, but price fluctuation does not. In the subsequent discussion, the text addresses the scenario where a free person kills the pledged slave whose value has dropped to 100. The killer must pay 100 (value at time of destruction). This 100 becomes substitute property (badal) and takes the place of the original pledge. The mortgagee holds it; if the debt is later paid, the substitute is released. This ruling is based on the principle that the mortgagee's right is attached to the monetary value (maliyah), not the physical substance of the property. Therefore, even though the compensation is technically blood money (diya), in the mortgagee’s hands it is treated as a substitute for the value and remains subject to the pledge rights.

Key Terms
نقصان السعر (nuqsān al-sa'r): Decrease in market price of the pledged property due to fluctuation in demand.
نقصان العين (nuqsān al-'ayn): Physical loss or damage to the pledged property itself.
قبض الاستيفاء (qabḍ al-istīfā'): Possession that serves as realization of a right; in rahn, the mortgagee's possession of the pledge is considered a recovery of the debt to the extent of its value.

Price Drop and Substitute in Mortgage: No Reclaim for Deficiency
Explains effect of price drop and substitute for a mortgaged slave killed by a free man

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a decrease in the price of mortgaged property does not reduce the debt and why the mortgagee cannot reclaim any deficiency after taking the substitute from a killer.

Core Idea
A price drop does not reduce the mortgage debt; the mortgagee's possession is a hand of collection, so the substitute fulfills the entire debt.

This passage from the sharh of Al-Hidayah addresses a subtle situation: a mortgaged slave's value drops from 1000 to 100 dirhams, then a free man kills the slave. The killer pays 100 dirhams as compensation. Does this 100 dirhams cover the mortgage debt? Can the mortgagee claim the remaining 900 from the mortgagor? The Hanafi school answers with two core principles: the mortgagee's possession is a 'hand of collection' from the start, and the compensator matches the loss. These principles lead to the surprising ruling that the mortgagee is deemed to have collected the full debt already and cannot reclaim anything.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
First ruling: a decrease in market value does not cancel any part of the debt; the property remains mortgaged for the full original amount. Second ruling: when a killer pays the substitute value, the mortgagee takes it and cannot reclaim any remaining debt from the mortgagor.
These are the key legal consequences derived from the passage.
2
Locate the dalil (proof) for each ruling
The passage cites two principles: (1) 'al-jābir bi-qadr al-fā'it' (compensation equals the lost) – the killer pays value at time of destruction. (2) 'yad al-istīfā'' (hand of collection) – the mortgagee's possession is for recovering the debt, so the destruction confirms collection of the full original value.
The proofs are internal Hanafi usul principles, not direct Quran or hadith.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the riba objection
A critic might say: receiving 1000 dirhams of debt satisfaction for a 100 dirham substitute is riba. The Hanafi response has two parts: (1) The mortgagee is deemed to have collected 1000 from the start, so there is no exchange of 100 for 1000. (2) Alternatively, the mortgagee first collects 100 (the cash substitute), and the remaining 900 are collected when the slave perishes – this sequential approach avoids riba.
This shows how Hanafi usul uses legal fictions (taqdīr) to prevent riba while preserving the purpose of the contract.
4
Contrast with natural death case
If the slave dies naturally, no substitute is paid, and the mortgagee collects the full debt by the slave itself. There is no riba concern because the slave is a commodity, not money, and the collection is immediate.
This contrast highlights that the riba objection only arises when cash is received as a substitute.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the mortgagee cannot reclaim anything. This is supported by the majority of the Hanafi school and is the basis for fatwa.
Imam Zafar's dissenting view is considered incorrect because the substitute inherits the ruling of the original monetary value.

Ruling When Mortgagor Pays Ransom After Mortgagee Refuses
States ruling and calculation when rahin pays fidyah after murtahin refuses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on set-off when the mortgagor pays the ransom after the mortgagee refuses, and calculate the effect on the debt.

Core Idea
Half the ransom is set off against the debt because the mortgagor is not a volunteer.

In a mortgage, if the pledged slave commits a crime requiring ransom, the mortgagee (creditor) has the right to ransom to preserve the security. If he refuses and the mortgagor (debtor) pays, what happens to the debt? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: the mortgagor is not acting voluntarily, and half the ransom is automatically deducted from the debt, reflecting the mortgagee's obligation to pay that half.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the mortgagee (murtahin) refuses to pay the ransom for a crime by the pledged slave, and the mortgagor (rahin) pays it instead.
This is a sub-case of the broader rules of mortgage (rahn).
2
Understand the Principle
The debt's cancellation is a necessary outcome (lazim) whether the mortgagee, mortgagor, or slave pays. Thus the mortgagor is not considered a volunteer (mutabarri') — he has a right of recourse.
This distinguishes it from a gratuitous payment, which would not create a set-off.
3
Apply the Calculation
Compare half the ransom to the total debt. If half the ransom equals or exceeds the debt, the entire debt is void. If less, deduct that amount from the debt. The slave remains pledged for the remaining balance.
The mortgagee was obligated to pay half the ransom, so set-off occurs naturally.

If the mortgagee ransoms while the mortgagor is present, he is a volunteer; and if he is absent, he is not a volunteer. This is the statement of Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Hasan, and Zufar said: The mortgagee is a volunteer in both cases because he ransomed the property of another without his command, so he resembles a stranger. And for him (Abu Hanifa), the proof is that when the mortgagor is present, it is possible to address him, so when the mortgagee ransoms, he has volunteered like a stranger. But when the mortgagor is absent, addressing him is impossible, and the mortgagee needs to rectify the guaranteed property, and he cannot do that except by rectifying the trust, so he is not a volunteer. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether the mortgagee (murtahin) is considered a volunteer (mutabarri') when he pays the ransom (fidyah) for a crime committed by the pledged slave, without the mortgagor's (rahin) permission. The issue is whether the mortgagee can later recover the ransom from the mortgagor. Imam Abu Hanifa distinguishes based on the mortgagor's presence: if present, the mortgagee could have asked permission, so paying without it makes him a volunteer with no recourse; if absent, the mortgagee is forced to pay to preserve the security, so he is not a volunteer and can recover. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), along with Hasan ibn Ziyad and Zufar, hold that regardless of presence, the mortgagee is a volunteer because he paid for another's property without authorization, akin to a stranger. They argue that necessity does not change the legal nature. The Hanafi school typically follows Abu Hanifa's distinction, as it balances the mortgagee's duty to protect the security with the mortgagor's right to decide. The practical difference: only under Abu Hanifa's view can the mortgagee recover if the mortgagor is absent.

Key Terms
متطوع / متبرع (mutatawwi' / mutabarri'): One who performs an act voluntarily, without legal obligation, and thus has no right to compensation.
مضمون (madmun): Property that is guaranteed or under liability; here refers to the portion of the slave that is a security for the debt.
امانت (amanah): Property held in trust; here refers to the portion of the slave beyond the debt value.
