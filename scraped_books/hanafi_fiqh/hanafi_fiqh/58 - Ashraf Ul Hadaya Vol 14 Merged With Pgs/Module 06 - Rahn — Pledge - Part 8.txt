Release of Debt by Mortgagee and Destruction of Pledge
Ruling that mortgagee's release or gift of debt removes liability on pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the liability of the mortgagee when the debt is extinguished before the pledge is destroyed, and identify the exception when the mortgagee becomes a usurper.

Core Idea
If the debt is extinguished, the pledge is no longer guaranteed — the mortgagee is not liable unless he becomes a usurper.

This passage addresses a key scenario in pledges (rahn): what happens if the mortgagee releases the debt or gifts it, and then the pledged property is destroyed. The Hanafi school, by istihsan, holds that the mortgagee is not liable because the debt – the basis of the guarantee – is gone. An exception occurs if the mortgagee wrongfully withholds the pledge after demand, becoming a usurper. The same principle applies when a woman holds a pledge for her dower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The mortgagee releases or gifts the debt; then the pledge is destroyed in his hand. The ruling is that he is not liable – it is destroyed without compensation.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is istihsan, based on the principle that the pledge is guaranteed by the debt or its cause.
Istihsan is a departure from strict analogy for a more equitable outcome.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Extinguishing the debt removes the cause of guarantee; thus, liability falls away.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar holds that the mortgagee is still liable. The Hanafi proof is presented as the preferred view.
This is an intra-Hanafi disagreement.
5
Evaluate the exception
If the mortgagor demands the return of the pledge and the mortgagee refuses, the mortgagee becomes a usurper (ghasib) and is liable for its value.
This exception preserves the rights of the owner when the mortgagee wrongfully withholds.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on the liability of the mortgagee when the debt is extinguished before the pledged property is destroyed. The author begins by stating the ruling: if the mortgagee releases the mortgagor from the debt or gifts the debt, and then the pledge is destroyed in the mortgagee's hand, it is destroyed without compensation (the mortgagee is not liable) by istihsan, contrary to Imam Zufar. The Hanafi proof is that the pledge is guaranteed either by the debt itself or by its cause (such as a promised debt). When the debt is extinguished by release or gift, neither the debt nor its cause remains, so the basis for liability is gone. An exception occurs if the mortgagor demands the return of the pledge and the mortgagee refuses; then the mortgagee becomes a usurper (ghasib) and is liable for the pledge. The passage then extends the same ruling to four cases involving a woman mortgagee for dower: (1) she releases the husband from the dower debt, (2) she gifts the dower debt, (3) she apostatizes before consummation (which automatically cancels the dower), and (4) she divorces by khul' in exchange for the dower. In all these cases, the dower debt is extinguished before the pledge is destroyed, so the woman is not liable. This is a clear application of the principle that liability on a pledge depends on the existence of the debt or its cause. The passage is an expansion (sharh) explaining the ruling with examples and the internal Hanafi disagreement with Zufar.

Key Terms
ابراء (ibra'): Legal release from a debt, extinguishing the obligation.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference, a departure from strict analogy (qiyas) for a more equitable ruling.
غاصب (ghasib): Usurper, one who wrongfully takes possession of another's property.

Liability for Pawn Destroyed After Debt Discharge
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for pawn destroyed after debt discharge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on liability for a pawn destroyed after debt discharge and distinguish the exception for wrongful refusal.

Core Idea
No liability on the creditor after discharge unless he wrongfully refused return, making him a usurper.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the liability of a creditor when a pawned item is destroyed after the debt has been fully discharged but before the item is returned. The Hanafi position is that the creditor is not liable, as the debt and its basis are completely extinguished. However, Imam Zufar holds that liability is required in all cases. The author then provides an important exception: if the creditor wrongfully refused to return the pawn when asked, he becomes a usurper and is liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what legal question is being addressed: Is the creditor (murtahin) liable when a pawned item is destroyed after the debt is fully discharged but before its return? The Hanafi ruling: no liability, except when the creditor wrongfully refused to return the item upon request.
The act is the destruction of the pawn while in the creditor's possession post-discharge.
2
Locate the dalil
The Hanafi proof is rational (dalil aqli): the debt is completely extinguished (sāqiṭ), and the basis (jahat al-dayn, i.e., the promise of loan) is also gone. Therefore, there is nothing for the pawn to secure, so no liability arises from its destruction.
The author does not quote a textual source; the reasoning is purely analogical.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) holds that the creditor is always liable after discharge, without the exception for refusal. The passage does not state his proof.
This ikhtilaf shows diversity within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyse the exception
If the debtor (Khalid) explicitly asks for the return of the pawn (e.g., a mare) and the creditor (Zayd) refuses without valid reason, the creditor becomes a usurper (ghasib). As a usurper, he is liable for the destruction of the item because he held it without authority.
The exception arises because refusal to return after demand removes the creditor's right to retain the pawn.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam Zufar's view, the creditor must always pay compensation if the pawn is destroyed after discharge. Under the Hanafi view, compensation is only due if the creditor wrongfully refused return. In practice, this affects whether the creditor is financially responsible.
The author sides with the Hanafi position, which is the mufta bihi.

Commentary

Liability on Creditor When Pawn Destroyed After Payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the legal effect of istifa (collection) from ibra (release) in the context of pawn, and state the resulting liability when the pawn is destroyed after payment.

Core Idea
Collection does not extinguish the debt's cause; a destroyed pawn after payment makes the creditor liable to return the proceeds.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario in pawn (rahn) law: what happens if the creditor receives payment of the debt but the pawn is later destroyed? The Hanafi ruling imposes liability on the creditor, obliging him to return the payment. The key lies in the difference between payment (istifa) and release (ibra). Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The creditor (mortgagee) has received payment of the debt either from the debtor or from a third-party volunteer. After this collection, the pawn (the collateral) is destroyed while still in the creditor's possession.
The payment is made before the pawn is returned, which is the crucial timing.
2
State the ruling
The pawn is considered to have perished against the debt (i.e., the debt is deemed paid), and the creditor must return the payment he collected to whoever paid it (the debtor or the volunteer).
The creditor cannot keep the payment; he is liable to return it.
3
Contrast with ibra (release)
If the creditor had released the debtor from the debt (ibra) instead of receiving payment, the creditor would not be liable if the pawn were destroyed. The ruling differs because ibra completely extinguishes the debt.
Ibra is an act of forgiveness; it severs the debt entirely.
4
Analyze the wajh al-farq (reason for distinction)
In ibra, the debt falls completely (saqit aslan), so there is no remaining legal basis (mujib) for the pawn. In istifa, the underlying cause of the debt (mujib) remains; only the act of collection has occurred. Since the cause persists, the pawn is still held in lieu of the debt. When the pawn is destroyed, the first payment is confirmed, and the second (if any) becomes void.
The mujib is the obligating cause, which continues to exist after payment.
5
Apply the same reasoning to analogous cases
The same rule applies if the creditor uses the debt to buy a thing or makes a settlement (sulh) for a specific item, as these are also forms of collection (istifa).
Any transaction that substitutes the debt with a specific asset is treated as istifa.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the mortgagee's liability when the pledge perishes after payment versus after discharge, and explain the legal rationale based on the survival of the original debt obligation.

Core Idea
Ibra' extinguishes the debt; istifa' leaves the obligation intact, so the pledge remains security.

A subtle but critical distinction in the law of pledge (rahn) determines whether the mortgagee is liable if the pledged property is destroyed after the debtor pays. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains why payment (istifa') does not cancel the underlying debt, while discharge (ibra') does. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying liability rules in collateral arrangements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, recognize that the text contrasts two situations: (1) the mortgagee receives the debt via payment (istifa') from the pledgor or a volunteer, and (2) the debt is discharged (ibra'). In both, the pledge perishes after the event.
The outcome differs: liability in istifa', no liability in ibra'.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
In istifa': the mortgagee must return what he received (or its value). In ibra': no liability; the debt is simply forgiven and the pledge perishes without consequence.

The author explains: ibra' completely extinguishes the debt (اسقط الدين اصلا). In istifa', the debt's cause (موجب) remains, but collecting again is impossible because the debtor would demand his payment back. Thus the original obligation is intact, so the pledge still secures it. When the pledge perishes, it discharges that remaining obligation, and the mortgagee must give back the payment.
Key point: istifa' does not remove the debt; it merely satisfies it provisionally.

The rule applies equally if the mortgagee purchases a commodity with the debt or settles (sulh) with a commodity. These are also forms of istifa' and trigger the same liability if the pledge perishes.
This shows the principle is broad: any satisfaction (istifa') leaves the debt intact.
5
Draw the practical consequence
The mortgagee must be careful: after receiving payment, he should return the pledge promptly. If the pledge perishes before return, he owes the payment back. Only ibra' (explicit forgiveness) fully releases the pledge's security function.
In modern finance, this affects collateral management in Islamic transactions.

Commentary
This passage discusses a scenario in Kitab al-Rahn where both the debtor (rahin) and creditor (murtahin) agree that there is no outstanding debt. However, the pledged item (marhun) is in the possession of the creditor and is destroyed before being returned. The ruling is that the marhun is considered destroyed 'against the debt' (yahlik bi al-dayn), meaning it is liable for the debt and the creditor is not responsible for its value. The legal reasoning is that by their agreement that there is no debt, they have not actually discharged the debt (as in ibra'), but rather they have merely acknowledged that there is no debt at present. However, the possibility (jihah) of the debt remains because they could later agree that the debt existed. Since the possibility of the debt remains, the marhun remains liable. In contrast, if the creditor had explicitly released the debtor from the debt (ibra'), the debt and its possibility would be completely terminated, and the marhun would not be liable. The commentary illustrates that this ruling is consistent with the general principle: as long as the debt or its direction remains, the marhun is liable (mudmun). This is an expansion of the Hidayah matn, providing a clarifying example.

Key Terms
تصادق (taṣāduq): Mutual agreement or confirmation between parties regarding a fact, here the nonexistence of debt.
جهة (jihah): Direction or possibility of a legal obligation; here, the potential for the debt to be considered existing.
ابراء (ibrāʾ): Legal release or discharge from a debt, permanently extinguishing the debt and its possibility.
مضمن بالدين (muḍman bi al-dayn): Liable for the debt; the marhun is considered to have been destroyed as compensation for the debt, so the debtor must still pay.

Mortgage Liability Persists Despite Agreement of No Debt
Expands a principle with an illustrative example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a mortgage remains liable when parties agree there is no debt, and distinguish between the effect of mere agreement (which leaves jihah al-dayn intact) and ibra' (which extinguishes both debt and its direction).

Core Idea
Mortgage liability continues as long as the possibility of debt exists — only an explicit discharge extinguishes both debt and its direction.

This passage from the commentary of al-Hidayah sharpens a key Hanafi principle: a mortgaged property remains liable (madhmun) for a debt even when both parties verbally agree that no debt currently exists. The reasoning hinges on the concept of 'jihah al-dayn' — the legal direction or potential for debt to be revived. Understanding this ruling clarifies when a mortgage truly ends and when it merely appears to be dormant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
The passage reiterates that a mortgaged property is liable as long as either the debt itself (dayn) or its direction (jihah) remains. Note the two distinct bases for liability.
Direction (jihah) means the possibility of debt being recognized or revived.
2
Analyze the given example
The example: mortgagor and mortgagee agree there is no loan, then the property is destroyed in the mortgagee's custody. According to Hanafi fiqh, the property is still liable (madhmun bil-dayn). Understand why — because the parties might later agree to revive the debt.
The text mentions 'sham ko' (evening) to illustrate possibility of later agreement.
3
Trace the concept of 'jihah al-dayn'
This is the linchpin: the 'direction of debt' remains as long as there is any legal possibility that a debt could be acknowledged. Mere agreement of no debt does not eliminate this direction; it only denies the current existence of debt.
Other schools may not accept jihah as sufficient; they require an actual debt.
4
Distinguish ibra' from mere agreement
Ibra' (discharge) is explicitly mentioned as completely extinguishing both the debt and its direction. Understand the difference: ibra' is a definitive legal act, while a mutual statement of 'no debt' is ambiguous and leaves room for revival.
Ibra' is a formal release; mere agreement may be a temporary denial.
5
Note the cross-madhhab implication
The Hanafi position is unique in recognizing jihah al-dayn. Other schools may rule that if both parties agree there is no debt, the mortgage becomes void immediately. Recognize that this ikhtilaf has practical consequences for property liability.
This dispute is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both views have scholarly backing.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof (ta'lil) from the commentary of al-Hidayah. It reiterates a previously established principle: a mortgaged property remains liable (madhmun) as long as either the debt itself or the 'direction' (jihah) of the debt persists. The author provides an illustrative example: if both the mortgagor and mortgagee agree that no debt currently exists, but the property is destroyed in the mortgagee's custody, the property is still liable for the original debt. The reasoning is that the parties might later agree to revive the debt—for instance, later in the same day (sham ko = evening). This possibility means the 'direction of debt' (jihah al-dayn) remains intact, and thus the mortgage continues to secure a potential debt. However, if there is an explicit discharge (ibra'), both the debt and its direction are completely extinguished, and the property is no longer liable. This distinction underscores the Hanafi view that a mortgage can secure not just an existing debt but also a potential future debt as long as there remains a legal connection (jihah) to a debt.

Key Terms
مرہون مضمون بالدین (marhūn madhmūn bil-dayn): The mortgaged property is held as a pledge and is liable for the debt; its destruction in the mortgagee's possession does not extinguish the debt obligation.
جہت دین (jihah al-dayn): The legal connection or direction of debt; the potential for a debt to exist or be revived, which is sufficient to maintain the mortgage's liability.
ابراء (ibra'): A discharge or release from debt; it extinguishes both the debt and its direction, terminating the mortgage's liability.
