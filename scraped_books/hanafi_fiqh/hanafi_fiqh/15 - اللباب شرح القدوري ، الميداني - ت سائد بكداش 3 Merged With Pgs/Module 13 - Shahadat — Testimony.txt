Exception in Confession: Rulings on Amount and Genus
Ruling on exception of the whole and different genus with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on exception of the whole, majority, and of a different genus in confession, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on these issues.

Core Idea
Exception of the whole is void; exception of different genus is valid by value via istihsan.

This lesson examines the rules of exception (istithna) within a confession (iqrar) according to the Hanafi school. The passage from al-Lubab presents the general rule that exception of a lesser or greater amount is valid, but exception of the whole is treated as a retraction. It then explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding exception of the majority and exception of a different genus, highlighting the roles of qiyas and istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is iqrar (confession) with an exception (istithna). The default ruling is that exception of a lesser or greater amount is valid and the confession binds for the balance.
This is the clear position of the matn (al-Quduri).
2
Recognize the three exception scenarios
The passage covers three exceptions: (a) less or more (valid), (b) the whole (void, treated as retraction), (c) different genus (valid by value via istihsan). Identify these and their rulings.
Exception of the whole is never valid because it contradicts the confession.
3
Pinpoint the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf opposes the equating of less and more for the majority (more than half). Muhammad and Zufar oppose the different genus exception, holding it invalid by qiyas.
Dispute is within the school; the mufta bihi is the matn position and istihsan.
4
Understand role of qiyas vs istihsan
For different genus, qiyas (analogy to same-genus exception) leads to invalidity, but istihsan (juristic preference based on custom and ease) makes it valid by subtracting value. For majority exception, Abu Yusuf's qiyas on the whole is rejected by the majority.
Istihsan is a distinctive Hanafi source that overrides qiyas when necessary.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position: exception of less or more is always valid (including majority), and exception of a different genus is valid by value. This is the position of the matn, Abu Hanifa, and the later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.
Tarjih authority: al-Quduri's Mukhtasar is Zahir al-Riwayah; istihsan is preferred by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, addresses the rules of exception (istithna) in a confession (iqrar). It begins with a general ruling: whether the exception is of a lesser or greater portion, the ruling is the same—the exception is valid. However, if the exception covers the entire confessed amount (istithna al-jami'), it is treated as a retraction (ruju') and is not accepted; the confession stands with the full amount. An intra-Hanafi dispute is then reported: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, exception of less or more is permissible, while Abu Yusuf held that if the exception is of the majority (more than half), the exception is void and the full confessed amount is due. The text notes that the relied-upon (mu'tamad) position is that of the matn (al-Quduri), which equates less and more. Next, if a person confesses to owing dirhams but excepts something of a different genus (e.g., a dinar or wheat), the ruling is that he owes the dirhams minus the value of the excepted item, not the item itself. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) adopted by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. In contrast, the qiyas (analogy) would hold the exception invalid, which is the view of Muhammad and Zufar. The commentary by al-Isbijabi confirms that the correct and relied-upon position is istihsan, as affirmed by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): A legal confession or acknowledgment of a right owed to another.
استثناء (istithna): An exception made by the confessor to exclude part of the confessed amount.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a more suitable ruling based on custom or necessity.

Key Takeaway

Ambiguous Confession: 'One Hundred and a Dirham' Interpreted by Custom
Ruling on interpreting ambiguous confession by custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of customary interpretation (urf) to resolve ambiguous confessions involving numbers and units.

Core Idea
Custom clarifies what 'one hundred and a dirham' means – the hundred are all dirhams.

When someone confesses a debt with an ambiguous phrasing like 'one hundred and a dirham', a question arises: does the word 'dirham' explain the hundred, or is it an additional item? The Hanafi school relies on customary usage ('urf) to resolve this. By custom, people omit repeating the unit word, so the single mention explains the number. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguity
Recognize that the phrase 'one hundred and a dirham' is ambiguous: it could mean 100 dirhams, or 100 unspecified items plus one extra dirham.
The ambiguity arises because the conjunction 'and' can be read as explanatory or additive.
2
Apply the custom principle
According to the passage, people find it burdensome to repeat the unit word, so they suffice with mentioning it once. Thus, 'dirham' explains the number, making the hundred all dirhams.
This principle is not a grammatical rule but a recognized 'urf that overrides literal conjunction.
3
Scope the ruling
The ruling applies only to items frequently transacted as standard units (e.g., dirhams, dinars, makilat, mawzunat). For rare items, the ambiguity remains unresolved.
The passage explicitly mentions 'makilat' (measured items) and 'mawzunat' (weighed items) as falling under the same principle.
4
Determine the consequence
The valid interpretation is that the debtor owes 100 dirhams (or the equivalent unit). This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
This outcome is sahih (valid) because it is based on accepted custom.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab's commentary on al-Quduri addresses an ambiguous phrasing in a confession. If someone says 'I owe you one hundred and a dirham', the question is whether the 'dirham' qualifies the hundred (making it all dirhams) or is an additional item (100 unspecified items plus a separate dirham). The ruling is that by customary usage ('urf), the hundred are all dirhams because people normally omit repeating the unit of currency and the word 'dirham' explains the number. This applies only to items frequently transacted, like dirhams and dinars. For measured or weighed items (makilat and mawzunat) like grains, the same principle applies because such items are commonly owed as debts in salam (advance payment), qard (loan), or as a price (thaman).

Key Terms
عُرف (urf): Customary practice that is accepted as a basis for legal rulings when no explicit text exists.
ذمة (dhimmah): The legal personality or capacity that bears obligations and rights; a debt is established in the dhimmah.
