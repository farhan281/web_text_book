Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Owner's Choice When Value Equals or Is Less
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on owner's option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Zahir al-Riwayah position on the owner's option when the reappeared property's value equals or is less than the guarantee, and evaluate al-Karkhi's contrary view.

Core Idea
The owner retains the option even if the property's value is equal or lower; al-Karkhi's dissent is rejected.

In the previous lesson we saw the basic scenarios. The passage then closes with a subtle but important case: what happens when the reappeared property's value is exactly the same as or even less than the compensation? Here an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf arises. The dominant view (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds that the owner still has the option. Imam al-Karkhi disagreed, arguing the option is lost. This lesson explores the reasoning behind each view and why the majority prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specific scenario in the dispute
The dispute occurs in the last section (al-faṣl al-akhīr), i.e., when the guarantee was based on the usurper's statement with oath, and the reappeared property's value equals or is less than the guarantee amount.
The earlier part already covered the case where value is more.
2
State the Zahir al-Riwayah position
According to the apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah), which is deemed the most correct (al-aṣaḥḥ), the owner still has the option to take back the property and return the compensation, even though the value is equal or lower.
Why? Because the owner's consent was not perfected.
3
Understand al-Karkhi's opposing view
Imam al-Karkhi held that once the value is equal or less, the owner's option is lost. He apparently reasoned that the owner is not harmed, so the option should no longer exist.
Al-Karkhi was a major Hanafi jurist, but his view is considered weak here.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and tarjih
The text rejects al-Karkhi's view. The reason given is that the owner's consent was not perfected because he claimed more and was not given it. The owner's dissatisfaction remains, so the option persists. This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah, which is stronger than a single scholar's opinion.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the dispute over the value of usurped property and the options after the property reappears. It begins with the general rule: the value is determined by the usurper's statement with his oath, unless the owner has proof of a higher value. Then it discusses two scenarios: (1) If the property was guaranteed based on the owner's claim or evidence or the usurper's refusal to swear, and the property later appears with higher value, the owner has no choice—the usurper keeps the property because the owner's consent to that amount was achieved. (2) If the guarantee was based on the usurper's statement with oath, the owner has the choice to either confirm the guarantee and take the compensation, or take back the property and return the compensation. The passage then addresses the case where the property's value equals or is less than the guaranteed amount, especially in the last scenario (owner's choice). According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the answer is the same: the owner still has the choice, contrary to al-Karkhi who held that the choice is lost. The reason is that the owner's consent was not perfected because he was not given what he claimed. This is the relied-upon position (al-ashah).

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah): The apparent transmission, referring to the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that contain the majority and authoritative positions of the Hanafi school.
الأصح (al-aṣaḥḥ): The most correct opinion, used by later Hanafi jurists to indicate the preferred ruling among multiple views.
الكرخي (al-Karkhī): Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi (d. 340 AH), a prominent Hanafi jurist known for his usul work and occasional disagreements with the early masters.

Offspring and Increase of Usurped Property: A Trust
Ruling that offspring and increase are a trust

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that offspring and increase of usurped property are a trust and identify the conditions under which the usurper becomes liable for their loss.

Core Idea
Offspring and increase of usurped property are a trust; liability only arises from transgression or refusal to return.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a person usurps property, the original item must be returned, but what about the offspring, milk, fatness, or fruit that comes from it? Are these also guaranteed by the usurper? This lesson clarifies that such increases are considered a trust in the usurper's hands—meaning he is not liable for their accidental perishing unless he wrongfully interferes or refuses to hand them over when the owner demands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Recognize that the passage declares the offspring and increase of a usurped property as a trust (amānah) in the usurper's hand. This is the core declaratory ruling.
The ruling applies to both connected (muttaṣil) and disconnected (munfaṣil) increase.
2
Understand the classification of increase
The commentary divides increase into connected (like fatness and beauty) and disconnected (like milk). Fruit before appearance is also trust. Understand this classification because it may affect how the increase is valued or returned.
Connected increase is inseparable; disconnected can be separately possessed.
3
Note the default: no liability
If the increase perishes without any fault of the usurper, there is no liability. The usurper is like a trustee for the increase.
The rationale: the owner never had hand over the increase, so the usurper's hand is like that of an amīn.
4
Recognize the exceptions: transgression or refusal
Liability attaches if the usurper transgresses (e.g., destroys, eats, sells the increase) or if the owner demands the increase and the usurper refuses to return it.
Transgression makes the usurper a ḍāmin (guarantor) for the increase.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Given a scenario where a usurped cow gives birth and the calf dies naturally, determine whether the usurper must compensate. Apply the ruling: the calf was a trust, no transgression, so no liability.
Contrast with a scenario where the usurper sells the calf—then he is liable.

The Ruling: Increase of Usurped Property is a Trust
Ruling on amanah status of offspring and increase of usurped property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the offspring and increase of a usurped item are a trust in the usurper's hands and identify the two exceptions that make the usurper liable.

Core Idea
Increase of usurped property is a trust; no liability unless transgression or refusal to return.

In this lesson, we examine a key principle from Kitab al-Ghasb: the offspring, fruit, and other increases of a usurped item are not considered part of the original usurpation. Instead, they are a trust (amanah) in the usurper's hands. This has practical implications: accidental loss does not create liability. However, two specific actions can trigger liability. We will explore these rules and their underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Status
The act is the usurper holding offspring/increase. The status is amanah (trust), not liability.
The increase is not usurped itself; it is merely in the usurper's hands as a trust.
2
Understand the Default Rule
Default: no liability for accidental perishing of the increase.
The increase is not part of the original usurpation, so it inherits the ruling of an amanah.
3
Learn the First Exception: Transgression
If the usurper commits ta'addi (destroys, eats, sells) on the increase, liability arises.
Transgression on an amanah triggers liability.
4
Learn the Second Exception: Owner's Demand and Refusal
If the owner demands return and the usurper refuses, liability arises.
The refusal changes the amanah to a guarantee.

The Al-Karkhi Dispute: Khiyar When the Original Property Reappears
Discussion of intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on khiyar when original property reappears

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the lack of option (khiyar) when the original usurped property reappears after the usurper has paid compensation, and contrast it with al-Karkhi's view.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah: no option for owner when original property reappears with equal or lesser value. Al-Karkhi: option due to incomplete consent.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: the usurper has paid compensation for the usurped item (e.g., because he destroyed it), but later the original item reappears undamaged. What is the ruling? The text states that according to the authoritative Zahir al-Riwayah, the owner has no option to take the original property and return the compensation, contrary to the opinion of al-Karkhi. We analyze the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The original property (ain) appears after the usurper has paid compensation for it.
The compensation paid is typically equal to or less than the value of the original property.
2
State the Majority Hanafi Position
According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the owner has no option (khiyar) to reclaim the original; the compensation stands.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
State al-Karkhi's Position
Al-Karkhi held that the owner has an option to take the original and return the compensation.
Al-Karkhi was a later Hanafi scholar (d. 340 AH).
4
Examine the Arguments
Majority: the compensation was a valid substitute, consent is considered complete. Al-Karkhi: the owner's consent was not complete because he wanted the specific item, not value.
The difference hinges on the completeness of consent (rida).
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah declares the majority view as the most correct (asahh) and it is the relied-upon position.
This is an intra-Hanafi tarjih.

Book of Deposit. The deposit is a trust in the hand of the depositee; if it perishes in his hand, he is not liable for it. And the depositor may keep it himself or with those in his household. Its connection to usurpation: it turns into it upon violation or transgression. Lexically, it means leaving; legally, it is appointing another to guard one's property. It is also a name for what the depositee guards, as the author expressed: 'al-wadi'ah' (pattern fa'ilah, meaning maf'ulah, with ta' to make it a noun, as in Ibn al-Athir's Nihayah) – a trust in the hand of the depositee. If it perishes in his hand without transgression, he is not liable, because people have need for deposits; if we made him liable, people would refrain from accepting deposits, and their interests would be disrupted. (Hidayah). [Custody of the Deposit:] The depositee may keep it – i.e., the deposit – himself or with those in his household, because the apparent is that he undertakes to guard another's property in the manner he guards his own; and because he has no alternative but to delegate to his household, as he cannot constantly stay home nor carry the deposit with him when going out. Those in his household are those who reside with him and whose expenses he bears, such as his wife, child, hired worker, and slave. In al-Fatawa: it is whoever lives with him, whether he bears his expenses or not. (Jawharah). If he keeps it with someone else – i.e., other than his household – or deposits it with someone else, he is liable, because the owner consented to his hand, not to another's, and hands are trusts, and because a thing does not guarantee its like, just as an agent cannot appoint another. Except if a fire occurs in his house, then he may hand it over to his neighbor; or if the depositee is on a ship and the wind rages such that he fears drowning, then he may throw it to another ship, because the means of preservation becomes determined in this situation, and the owner would consent to it. He is not believed regarding that except with evidence, because he claims an exigency that drops liability.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri opens the Book of Deposit (Kitab al-Wadi'ah). It begins by defining the deposit and its basic ruling. The author states that a deposit is an amanah (trust) in the hands of the depositee; if it perishes without his fault, he is not liable. The rationale is public need: people often need to leave goods with others, and imposing liability would discourage people from accepting deposits, harming public interest. The passage then explains who may keep the deposit. The depositee may keep it himself or delegate to his household ('iyal) – those living with him and dependent on him, such as wife, children, hired help, and slaves. A later fatwa also includes anyone living with him even if not financially dependent. However, if he delegates to someone outside his household, he becomes liable if the deposit is lost, because the owner only consented to his trustworthiness. Exceptions are made for emergencies: if his house catches fire, he may hand the deposit to a neighbor; if he is on a sinking ship, he may throw it to another vessel. In such cases, necessity dictates the action, and the owner is deemed to consent. The depositee must prove the emergency with evidence; otherwise, his claim of necessity is not accepted.

Key Terms
الوديعة (al-wadi'ah): A deposit; legally, appointing another to guard one's property, also the property itself.
المودَع (al-mudi'): The depositor (the one giving the deposit).
المودَع (al-mustawda' (or al-mudi' in some readings)): The depositee (the one receiving the deposit).

Custody of the Deposit: Who May Keep It and Exceptions
Explains rules of custody for deposits and emergency exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whom the depositee may delegate to for custody of the deposit, and state the emergency exceptions and their burden of proof.

Core Idea
The depositee may keep the deposit with his household, but delegation to others makes him liable unless necessity intervenes.

This lesson continues from the definition of wadi'ah to discuss the practical rules of custody. The depositee has the right to keep the deposit himself or delegate to his household ('iyal). However, if he gives it to someone outside his household, he becomes liable for any loss, because the owner only consented to his trustworthiness. Two exceptions are provided: fire and sea storm, where necessity allows delegation to a third party. The depositee must prove the emergency with evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule for custody
The text says: 'وللمودع أن يحفظها بنفسه وبمن في عياله' — the depositee may keep it himself or with his household.
Household ('iyal) includes wife, children, hired help, slaves, and even non-dependents living with him per al-Fatawa.
2
State the consequence of delegating to outsiders
If he keeps it with anyone outside his household, or deposits it with them, he becomes liable (ضَمِن).
Rationale: owner consented only to the depositee's hand, not another's.
3
Learn the emergency exceptions
Two cases are exempted: (1) fire in the house, so he may give to a neighbor; (2) fear of drowning on a ship, so he may throw it to another ship.
In these cases, necessity determines the best preservation method, and the owner is deemed to consent.
4
Understand the burden of proof
The depositee must prove the emergency with evidence (بينة) because he claims a necessity that drops liability.
Without proof, he is not believed and remains liable.

Ruling on Depositary Delegating Custody of Deposit
States the ruling and exceptions for delegating deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a depositary gives a deposit to someone else, and identify the exceptions and evidentiary requirements.

Core Idea
A depositary becomes liable if he entrusts the deposit to anyone outside his household, unless a well-known emergency forces him.

This lesson addresses a key ruling in the chapter of al-Wadi'ah: the depositary's obligation to personally safeguard the deposit. The source text from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains that handing over the deposit to someone else triggers liability, with important exceptions for household members and emergency situations. We will examine the conditions, exceptions, and the role of evidence in such claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
The depositary is liable if he keeps the deposit with someone else or re-deposits it. This is the default rule.
The liability is called daman.
2
Define the exception for household
Who counts as 'iyal (household)? Those living with him and under his maintenance: wife, child, hired servant, slave. A fatwa expands to anyone living with him regardless of maintenance.
This exception removes liability.
3
Understand emergency exceptions
If a fire breaks out, he may give it to a neighbor; if on a sinking ship, he may transfer it to another ship. Necessity overrides the default rule.
The owner's implicit consent is presumed in emergencies.
4
Analyze evidence requirement
The depositary's claim of necessity is only accepted with bayyinah (legal evidence), unless the emergency is widely known (e.g., a public fire).
This is because he is claiming a cause that removes liability after it has been established.
5
Connect to underlying principle
The prohibition stems from the owner's consent being specific to the depositary's custody; delegation without permission is a breach of trust.
The same principle applies to agents and trustees.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains a ruling from the chapter of al-Wadi'ah (deposit). The passage type is a ruling statement with its conditions and exceptions. The basic ruling: if the depositary (the person entrusted with the deposit) gives the deposit to someone outside his household for safekeeping, or re-deposits it with another person, he becomes liable for it (daman). The reason is that the owner agreed to trust only the depositary's hand, not another's, and because an agent cannot delegate his task to another without permission. The commentary defines 'ahl' (household) as those who reside with the depositary and whose maintenance he provides: his wife, children, hired servant, and slave. A fatwa extends it to anyone living with him, regardless of maintenance. Exceptions are emergencies: if a fire breaks out in his house, he may hand the deposit to a neighbor; or if he is on a ship in danger of sinking, he may cast the deposit into another ship. In such cases, necessity dictates that he must save the deposit, so the owner would implicitly consent. However, he is not believed in claiming necessity without evidence (bayyinah), because he is asserting a cause that removes liability after liability has already been established. If the emergency is widely known (e.g., a public fire), evidence is not required. This ruling guards against the misunderstanding that a depositary can freely delegate custody without consequences.

بينة (bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically two male witnesses or one male and two females.

Mixing a Deposit with Depositary's Property: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mixing deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa holds that the depositor only gets daman and contrast it with the Sahibayn's view, identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Mixing a deposit to indistinguishability gives the depositor only compensation, not a share.

This passage addresses a common transaction: a depositary mixes the deposit with his own property. Is the depositor entitled to a share in the mixture? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. Understanding this dispute illuminates the school's approach to property rights and liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? The depositary mixes the deposit with his property until indistinguishable. The ruling: he is liable (daman).
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa says the depositor has no claim to the mixed property, only liability. Reason: complete consumption (istihlak) because access to the specific object is impossible.
This is the mufta bihi.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say if mixed with the same type, the depositor may choose partnership or liability. Reason: partial consumption; value can be accessed via division.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the school.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, depositor never becomes a co-owner; under Sahibayn, he can opt for partnership.
5
Identify the relied-upon position
Later scholars (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, etc.) chose Abu Hanifa's view.
This is the mufta bihi for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage discusses a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding mixing a deposit with the depositary's own property. The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute. The ruling: If the depositary mixes the deposit with his own property such that it becomes indistinguishable, he is liable for it (daman). The point of disagreement is the depositor's option after mixing. According to Abu Hanifa (the Imam), the depositor has no claim to the mixed property itself; his only recourse is to claim the value (daman) from the depositary. The reason is that the specific object of his right (ayn) is completely lost (consumed) since it cannot be distinguished. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), if the mixing is with property of the same type (e.g., both are wheat of similar quality), the depositor may choose: either take the liability (daman) or become a partner in the mixed property (shirkah) by mutual division. They argue that although the physical object is not accessible, the meaning (value) is accessible through division, so it is only partial consumption. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to al-Tashih is that of Abu Hanifa, chosen by later authorities like al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah. This ruling guards against the misunderstanding that mixing always results in shared ownership; Abu Hanifa's view emphasizes strict liability to protect the owner's rights.

Key Terms
خلط (khalat): Mixing the deposit with the depositary's own property in a way that makes them indistinguishable.
استهلاك (istihlak): Consumption or destruction of the specific object, making it impossible to return.
شركة (shirkah): Partnership in the mixed property by mutual agreement.

Explaining liability when depositary uses deposit and then replaces it

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Returning the equivalent does not absolve liability if mixing occurs — mixing is a transgression that makes him liable for the whole.

This lesson addresses a specific situation in the law of deposit (wadi‘ah). When a depositary (the person entrusted with property) uses part of the deposit, his liability depends on whether he returns the equivalent and mixes it with the remainder. The Hanafi school holds that such mixing constitutes a transgression that transfers full liability to the depositary. We will examine two cases from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage carefully: first scenario – depositary spends part, the rest perishes naturally (no replacement). Second scenario – depositary spends part, then returns an equivalent and mixes it with the rest, then the whole perishes.
The key distinction is whether the depositary returned and mixed an equivalent.
2
Determine liability in each scenario
For the first, liability is only for the amount spent (دلك القدر). For the second, liability is for the entire deposit (الجميع) because the mixing is a transgression.
The difference arises from the act of mixing, which is considered istihlāk (consumption).
3
Understand the underlying principle
Mixing the depositary’s own property with the trust creates a situation where the depositary is considered to have consumed (istihlāk) the deposit. The trust ceases to be a pure amānah and becomes a guarantee (ḍamān).
This is based on the Hanafi principle that any transgression (ta‘addī) that alters the nature of the amānah shifts liability.

Permanent Liability After Denial of Deposit
Ruling statement with talil (causative reasoning) on denial after demand.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, in the Hanafi school, a depositary who denies a deposit after demand remains liable even if he later confesses, and distinguish this from ordinary violation.

Core Idea
Denial after demand permanently dissolves the trust; confession cannot revive it without a new contract.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in deposit law: what happens when the owner demands his property back and the depositary denies having it. The Hanafi ruling is clear—the depositary becomes liable immediately, and that liability persists even if he later admits the truth. Understanding the legal mechanism behind this ruling is crucial for grasping how trust-based contracts interact with transgression.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its immediate consequence
The depositary denies the deposit after the owner demands it. The ruling is that he becomes liable (daman) for the deposit, meaning he must compensate even if the deposit perishes.
The key event is the combination of demand and denial.
2
Understand the legal mechanism of dissolution
The owner's demand for return effectively revokes the depositary's authority to preserve the deposit. The depositary's denial acts as a rescission (faskh) from his side. Together, the contract of deposit is completely dissolved.
Both parties have contributed to terminating the contract.
3
Recognize the implication: no revival without renewal
Once the contract is dissolved, it cannot come back to life except by a new contract. Therefore, even if the depositary later confesses, the original trust relationship is gone, and he remains liable.
Confession does not equate to a new contract.
4
Contrast with simple violation (mukhalafah)
In a mere violation (e.g., using the deposit without permission), the contract remains intact because the preservation command still exists. Returning to compliance restores the trust and absolves liability. Here, denial with demand dissolves the contract entirely, so no return is possible.
The distinction hinges on whether the contract is dissolved or merely violated.

Explaining effects of removing transgression and denial on liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a depositary removes transgression or denies the deposit after demand, and explain the underlying rationale (removal of cause vs termination of contract).

Core Idea
Removing transgression restores trust and lifts liability; denying after demand permanently attaches liability.

In Hanafi fiqh, a depositary (mustawda') holds property as an amanah (trust). If he commits ta'addi (transgression) by using the deposit, he becomes liable. However, if he stops and returns it to proper custody, the liability ceases because the cause is removed. In contrast, if the owner demands the deposit and the depositary denies having it (juhud), the contract is terminated and liability becomes permanent, even if he later confesses. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind these two distinct outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two separate situations: (1) the depositary removes transgression and returns the deposit to its place; (2) the owner demands the deposit and the depositary denies it. For each, note the ruling on liability.
Scenario 1: liability ceases. Scenario 2: liability attaches and persists.
2
Analyze scenario 1: removal of transgression
For the first scenario, the cause of liability (transgression) is removed. The command to preserve remains, so the depositary's hand is treated like the owner's hand legally. Thus, the deposit returns to being a trust (amanah) and liability ends.
Key principle: zawa al-sabab (removal of cause) lifts the effect.
3
Analyze scenario 2: denial after demand
For the second scenario, the owner's demand for return terminates the depositary's authority to hold (faskh from owner). The depositary's denial is a rescission from his side. Together, they terminate the deposit contract completely. Hence, the depositary becomes a usurper (ghasib) by retention, and liability attaches permanently.
Even if he later confesses, the contract is not revived without a new agreement.
4
Contrast with mere violation (mukhalafah)
The passage distinguishes this from a mere violation of instructions (e.g., moving the deposit without using it). In that case, returning to conformity restores the trust because the command to preserve persists. Denial, however, severs the contract.
Reference to Al-Hidayah: '…unlike [the case of] violation where return to conformity [restores the contract] due to the persistence of the command.'

The Joint Deposit Dispute: Giving a Share to One Depositor
Intra-Hanafi dispute on joint deposit share payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the depositary's duty when one joint depositor claims his share of a fungible deposit, and identify the mufta bihi position with its reasoning.

Core Idea
A depositary cannot partition joint property; for fungibles, Abu Hanifa forbids giving a share to one depositor without the other.

When two people deposit fungible goods (like money) jointly with a depositary, and one depositor comes to claim his share, a critical question arises: must the depositary wait for the other depositor, or can he hand over the claimed share immediately? This lesson explores the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), along with the important distinction between fungible and non-fungible goods.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Understand the factual scenario: two people jointly deposit fungible goods (ذوات الأمثال) with a depositary. One depositor appears alone and demands his share.
The deposit is joint (مشاع), meaning each has an undivided share.
2
Identify the Dispute
Abu Hanifa rules that the depositary must not give anything until both are present. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that he may give the share to the present depositor.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both positions are within the school.
3
Analyze Each Side's Reasoning (Talil)
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the depositary is being asked to give a physically separated portion (مُفْرَز), but the depositor's right is in the undivided share (مشاع). Separation requires formal division (قسمة), which the depositary has no authority to perform. The Sahibayn argue: the depositary is merely returning what was entrusted to him; the share is already determined by the original deposit.
The key difference lies in whether the depositary's act is considered a 'return' or a 'partition'.
4
Note the Restriction to Fungibles
The text expressly limits the dispute to fungible goods (ذوات الأمثال). For non-fungible goods (القيميات), it is agreed (اتفاقاً) that the depositary cannot hand over a share without the other depositor's presence.
This restriction, cited from Al-Hidayah and Al-Fayd, avoids any misunderstanding.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (Al-Mahbubi, Al-Nasafi, Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah) rely on Abu Hanifa's view. Thus, the operative position is that the depositary must wait for both depositors.
This tarjih is recorded in Al-Tashih, which the commentator cites.

Deposit with Two Depositees: Division of Divisible vs Indivisible Property
Ruling on deposit with two depositees for divisible/indivisible property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi ruling for a deposit with two depositees, distinguishing between divisible and indivisible property.

Core Idea
For divisible deposits, depositees must split the property; for indivisible, one may hold with permission.

This lesson addresses a specific deposit scenario: when a person entrusts property to two individuals. The key factor is whether the property can be physically divided. The Hanafi school derives the ruling from the owner's implicit consent based on practical realities of joint custody.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and scenario
Understand the basic act: a man deposits property with two depositees. The property may be divisible (e.g., grain) or indivisible (e.g., a horse).

Classify the property as divisible (mā yūqsam) or indivisible (mā lā yūqsam). Divisible property can be split without significant loss of value.
Examples of indivisible: a single animal, a sword.
3
Apply the ruling for divisible property
For divisible property, it is impermissible for one depositee to hand over the whole to the other. They must divide the property equally and each guard his half.

For indivisible property, it is permissible for one depositee to guard the whole, but only with the permission of the other depositee.
The owner is deemed to have consented to this arrangement implicitly.
5

The ruling is based on rational inference from the owner's knowledge that the two depositees cannot be together at all times. For divisible property, the owner expects division; for indivisible, the owner expects one to hold it with the other's consent.
Implicit consent (dalālah) is treated as explicit text (nas) in Hanafi law.

Deposit Location: Same Courtyard vs Different Courtyard
Ruling on liability for storing deposit elsewhere

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a depositary stores a deposit in a different room or house, and apply the concepts of beneficial condition (shart mufid) and security equivalence (hirz).

Core Idea
A condition to store in a specific place binds only if it changes the security level; otherwise it is ignored.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on conditions about where to keep a trust (wadi'a). The key principle is that a condition must be beneficial—i.e., it must affect the degree of security (hirz)—to be enforceable. Moving a deposit within the same courtyard does not change security, so no liability; moving to a different courtyard does. The commentaries of al-Isbijabi and al-Hidayah add further nuance regarding practical feasibility and apparent defects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine whether the depositary stored the deposit in a different house within the same courtyard or in a different courtyard. The ruling depends on this distinction: same courtyard = no liability; different courtyard = liability.
2
Understand the basis: beneficial condition
In Hanafi law, a condition is binding only if it is beneficial (shart mufid). Here, the condition specifies a location. If the alternative location provides equal security (hirz), the condition is not beneficial and thus not binding. Houses in the same courtyard share the same hirz; different courtyards have different hirz.
The text explicitly states that the condition is not beneficial because the two houses in one courtyard do not differ in hirz.
3
Apply the nuance from Sharh al-Isbijabi
The ruling that moving within the same courtyard incurs no liability applies only when the depositary cannot practically avoid moving. If he can avoid moving (i.e., he has the ability to keep it in the specified house), then he must adhere to the condition; otherwise he is liable.
This adds a practical dimension: impossibility excuses, but possibility binds.
4
Apply the nuance from al-Hidayah
If the two houses in the same courtyard have a noticeable difference in security—for example, one has an obvious defect—then the condition becomes beneficial and violating it incurs liability. The condition is then considered beneficial because it aims to secure the deposit.
Thus the equality of hirz is key; if it is unequal, the condition is enforceable.
5
Synthesize the underlying principle
The overarching rule: conditions in a deposit contract are evaluated based on whether they provide a real benefit (change in security) and whether compliance is practically possible. If a condition is both beneficial and feasible, violating it makes the depositary liable for any loss.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on substance over form.

Four Types of ʿĀriyah (Loan)
Classifies loans into four categories based on restrictions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a loan (ʿāriyah) into its four types based on restrictions in time and use, and apply the ruling on exceeding specified terms.

Core Idea
A loan is either unrestricted or restricted; the borrower must abide by limitations unless using something similar or better.

Al-Hidayah presents a clear classification of loans (ʿāriyah) into four categories. Understanding this classification is essential because it determines the borrower's permissible scope of use. The core distinction is between unrestricted (muṭlaqah) and restricted (muqayyadah) loans, with further subdivisions in time and benefit. An important exception allows the borrower to deviate from restrictions if the substitute is similar or better, reflecting the principle of istiḥsān.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types
List the four types as given by al-Hidayah: (1) unrestricted in time and benefit, (2) restricted in both, (3) restricted in time only, (4) restricted in benefit only.
The fourth is the opposite of the third.
2
Understand the muṭlaq vs muqayyad distinction
Muṭlaq (unrestricted) grants full freedom; muqayyad (restricted) imposes limits on time, benefit, or both. The borrower must adhere to these limits unless an exception applies.
The exception is only for the second type (both restricted).
3
Apply the exception rule
When the loan is restricted in both time and benefit, the borrower may use something similar to the specified item or better, but not worse. This exception is based on the lender's presumed consent.
Example: borrowing to carry wheat, you may carry similar wheat or something better like rice, but not barley if it is inferior.
4
Differentiate the third and fourth types
In type three, time is fixed but you may choose how to use the item. In type four, use is fixed but you may choose when. In both, you cannot exceed the specified limitation.
The exception does not apply to these types because only one aspect is restricted.

The first type is absolute (muṭlaq), giving the borrower full freedom in time and manner of use. The second is completely restricted (muqayyad), where the borrower must adhere exactly to the specified terms, except that he may use something similar or better if the lender would likely consent (as in the example of carrying a different but equivalent grain). The third type restricts the time period but leaves the manner of use open. The fourth restricts the use but not the time. In all restricted cases, the borrower must not exceed the specified terms; the exception for similar or better applies only in the second type where both are restricted.

Key Terms
إعارة (iʿārah): Another term for ʿāriyah (loan).
مطلقة (muṭlaqah): Unrestricted; the borrower has full freedom in time and use.
مقيدة (muqayyadah): Restricted; the borrower must follow specified limitations.

Condition for Sub-Lending in ʿĀriyah
Ruling on condition for sub-lending in ʿāriyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a borrower may sub-lend a borrowed item and identify when sub-lending is prohibited.

Core Idea
A borrower may sub-lend only if the item's utility does not change with the user—otherwise it's prohibited.

You have borrowed a horse or a book, and you want to let your friend use it. Is that allowed? The Hanafi school sets a key condition: the borrowed item must not vary in its functionality depending on who uses it. This lesson explains that simple rule, based on the lender's consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is sub-lending, i.e., the borrower giving the borrowed item to another person. The default is that the borrower may use the item as if he owns it, so sub-lending is allowed in principle.
This is based on the phrase 'على حسب ما مَلَك' – as he owns it.
2
State the condition
Sub-lending is only permissible if the item's utility does not differ with the user. If the item's value or functionality changes depending on who uses it, sub-lending is prohibited.
The condition is expressed by 'ولذا شرط أن لا يختلف باختلاف المستعمل'.
3
Explain the rationale
The lender consented to this specific borrower's use, not to the use of others. If the item differs with the user, allowing someone else would exceed the consent.
The phrase 'لأنه رضي باستعماله، لا باستعمال غيره' gives the reason.
4
Apply with an example
Example: A borrowed horse that only the borrower can ride effectively – sub-lending is not allowed. A borrowed book that anyone can read – sub-lending is allowed.
The difference is based on the item's dependency on the user's skill or attributes.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement regarding the permissibility of a borrower sub-lending the borrowed item. The Hanafi position is that the borrower may use the item as if he owns it, so he may allow others to use it as well, but only if the item's utility does not change based on the user. For example, a horse that only a specific rider can handle cannot be sub-lent. The lender consented to this particular borrower's use, not to someone else's. If the item differs, the borrower cannot lend it to another.

Key Terms
عارية (ʿāriyah): A contract where a person gives property to another to benefit from its usufruct without compensation, with the obligation to return the item itself.
مستعمل (mustaʿmal): The user; the person who uses the borrowed item. Here it refers to the borrower or anyone the borrower allows to use the item.

The Four Types of Borrowing (I'arah)
Classification of four types of i'arah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any i'arah contract into one of the four types based on time and use restrictions, and determine the borrower's permissible actions.

Core Idea
An absolute borrowing gives the borrower free rein; a restricted borrowing binds him to the specified terms.

In this lesson, we examine Al-Hidayah's classification of borrowing (i'arah) into four categories based on two variables: whether the time period and the type of use are absolute (mutlaq) or restricted (muqayyad). Understanding these categories is essential for determining the borrower's rights and limits when no specific terms are mentioned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dimensions
Recognise that i'arah contracts are classified by time (al-waqt) and benefit (al-intifa'). Both can be absolute (mutlaq) or restricted (muqayyad).
The passage uses 'time' and 'benefit' as the two independent variables.
2
Map the four combinations
List the four logical combinations: (1) absolute in both, (2) restricted in both, (3) restricted in time + absolute in benefit, (4) absolute in time + restricted in benefit.
This is the precise classification given in the text.
3
Apply the rule of 'amal bil-itlaq or bil-taqyid
For absolute terms, act on the absolute (wide discretion). For restricted terms, act on the restriction (adhere to specified terms).
The borrower must operate within the boundaries set by the lender's permission.
4
Identify the exception in type 2
In type 2 (restricted both), the borrower cannot exceed the specified use, but if he uses something similar (e.g., carrying one type of grain instead of another of equal value) or better (e.g., using a camel for riding instead of carrying), it is permissible.
This exception is based on the principle that the borrower is an agent (amin) and should not be disadvantaged.

أوجه الإعارة : 
قال في «الهداية»: وهذا إذا كانت الإعارة أربعة أوجه :
أحدها : أن تكون مطلقة في الوقت والانتفاع، وللمستعير فيه أن ينتفع به في أي نوع شاء، وفي أي وقت شاء، عملاً بالإطلاق.

والثالث: أن تكون مقيَّدة في حق الوقت، مطلقة في حق الانتفاع.
والرابع عكسه وليس له أن يتعدى ما سماه. اهـ
The aspects of borrowing: He said in Al-Hidayah: This is when borrowing has four aspects: First: that it is absolute in time and benefit, and the borrower may benefit from it in any type he wishes and at any time he wishes, acting upon the absolute nature. Second: that it is restricted in both, and he may not exceed what he specified, acting upon the restriction, unless it is similar to that or better. Third: that it is restricted with respect to time and absolute with respect to benefit. Fourth: the opposite, and he may not exceed what he specified. End.

Commentary
This is a passage from Al-Hidayah classifying the four types of borrowing (i'arah) based on how the loan is worded regarding time and use. The first type is absolute in both, giving the borrower full freedom. The second is restricted in both, so the borrower must adhere to the specified time and use, unless he uses something similar or better (e.g., borrowing a beast for 10 qafiz of wheat but carrying 10 qafiz of another grain). The third is restricted in time but absolute in use, and the fourth is restricted in use but absolute in time. In both mixed cases, the borrower cannot exceed the specified restriction.

Key Terms
إعارة (iʿārah): A contract whereby the owner gives another permission to benefit from the usufruct of property without charge, with the obligation to return the property.
مطلق (muṭlaq): Absolute; unrestricted by time or type of use, giving the borrower full discretion.
مقيد (muqayyad): Restricted; limited to a specific time or specific use as specified in the contract.
