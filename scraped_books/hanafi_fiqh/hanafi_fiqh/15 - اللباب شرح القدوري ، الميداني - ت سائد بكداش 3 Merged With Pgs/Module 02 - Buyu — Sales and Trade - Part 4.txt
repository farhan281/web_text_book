Separating Mahram Slave Relatives: Ruling and Proof
Explains the prohibition of separating mahram slave relatives in sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on separating mahram slave relatives in a sale, identify the proof from hadith, and apply the scope conditions (blood mahram, common ownership).

Core Idea
Separating mahram slaves is prohibited but the sale remains valid.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific but important ruling in Islamic commercial law: the prohibition of separating two slaves who are mahram relatives by sale. The text explains that while the sale itself is valid (sahih), the act of separation is disliked (makruh tahrimi) due to prophetic hadith. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between prohibitions that affect the contract's validity and those that affect the parties' actions. Let's examine the ruling, its proof, and its precise scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
First, identify what act is being ruled upon: separating two minor slaves who are blood mahram relatives (e.g., siblings) by sale. The ruling is that such separation is prohibited (makruh tahrimi), but the sale contract itself is valid (sahih).
Note the Hanafi distinction: the prohibition does not invalidate the sale.
2
Locate the Dalil
Next, find the evidence (dalil) cited. Two hadith are quoted: one threatening separation from Allah for separating a mother and child, and another where the Prophet (ﷺ) commanded 'Ali to return a slave brother he had sold. Both are ahad, hence the ruling level is makruh tahrimi, not haram.
The dalil is from Sunnah ahad (zanni al-thubut) and the meaning is also zanni (zanni al-dalalah).
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Analyze how the hadith proves the ruling. The imperative 'Return him' (ردّه) indicates a command, which in Hanafi usul denotes prohibition (tahrim) unless there is evidence to the contrary. The threat in the first hadith reinforces this. The prohibition is for a meaning external to the contract, so it does not affect validity.
The reasoning (تعليل) is that the prohibition is for a meaning adjacent to the contract, not in its core (صلب العقد).
4
Determine the Scope and Conditions
The text specifies that the prohibition applies only to blood mahram relatives (nasab), not to rada' (milk) mahram, non-mahram relatives, or spouses. Both slaves must be owned by the same person. If owned separately, selling one is allowed. Also, separation due to a rightful claim (e.g., debt, crime) is permitted.
Scope is narrow: the texts are contrary to qiyas, so they are restricted to their precise scope.
5
Evaluate the Mufta Bihi Position
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that separating mahram slaves is makruh tahrimi and the sale is valid. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah. The ruling is mukhtalaf fih (other schools differ on the validity or scope).
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf mentioned.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri discusses the ruling on selling two slaves who are mahram relatives. The matn states that if a person owns two minor slaves who are blood mahram (i.e., related to each other so that marriage is forbidden, such as brother and sister), they cannot be separated by sale. The sharh explains that the prohibition is not due to a defect in the contract itself but to an external consideration, so the sale is valid (hence the price is owed and ownership transfers) but it is makruh (disliked). The proof is from two hadith: one about separating a mother and child, and another where the Prophet commanded 'Ali to return a slave brother he had sold. The ruling extends to cases where one is adult and one minor, as the same mercy and comfort are at stake. The prohibition is specifically for blood mahram relatives (sibling, parent-child), not for rada' (milk) relatives, nor for non-mahram relatives (like cousins), nor for spouses; thus separating those is allowed. Both slaves must be under the same owner; if they have different owners, selling one is fine. Also, if separation is due to a legitimate claim (e.g., one slave is taken for a crime, or sold to pay a debt), it is permissible because the aim is to remove harm from others.

البيع الفاسد (al-bay' al-fasid): An invalid sale; here the text clarifies that the sale is not fasid but valid (sahih) with dislike (karahah).
الكراهة (al-karahah): Dislike; here it is makruh tahrimi (prohibited dislike) because the proof is zanni.

Separating Mahram Relatives in Sale: Ruling and Conditions
Explaining the prohibition of separating mahram relatives in sale, its conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on separating mahram relatives in a sale, identify the conditions for the prohibition, apply the exceptions, and explain the difference between validity and dislike.

Core Idea
Separating mahram relatives in sale is makruh tahrimi but the sale remains valid.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific prohibition in sales: the separation of close relatives who are mahram (permanently forbidden to marry). The ruling is based on a prophetic practice and is an exception to general permissibility. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between the validity of contracts (sahih/fasid) and the character of the action (halal/makruh/haram).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling Level
Identify the specific act being ruled upon: selling two mahram relatives separately (e.g., a parent and child) such that they are separated. Determine the hukm level: the text states it is 'makruh' but the sale is 'ja'iz' (valid). This is classified as makruh tahrimi (prohibited-close-to-haram) because the prohibition is based on a text, though the contract remains valid.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is the hadith: 'He is not of us who does not show mercy to our young and respect our elders' (Tirmidhi) and the practice of the Prophet ﷺ separating between the two slave sisters Mariya and Sirin (adults), which indicates that the prohibition is not absolute but is restricted to cases where a minor or dependency is involved.
The Hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), so the ruling is at the level of makruh tahrimi, not haram.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah (How the Dalil Proves the Ruling)
The hadith commands mercy and care for the young; separating a minor from a caretaker (the mahram relative) violates this mercy. However, the text came contrary to analogy (qiyas), so it is restricted to its specific scenario: mahram relatives in a sale. The prohibition does not extend to all separations or to non-mahram relatives.
The illa (cause) is the existence of mahram relationship and the need for care.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf (Scholarly Disagreement)
Some scholars (e.g., from the Shafi'i school) consider such sales invalid (batil) because they regard the prohibition as affecting the validity of the contract. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the act (makruh) and the contract (valid), because the pillars of the sale are sound, and the defect is external.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the sale is valid and what remedies apply.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih (Preponderance) and Mufta Bihi
The Hanafi position, as confirmed by al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah, is that the sale is valid and the separation is makruh tahrimi. This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) for fatwa. The authority is Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the ruling on separating close relatives in a sale. The passage type is an expansion probe, as the commentator elaborates on the original text of Al-Quduri. The core issue: It is prohibited to separate a parent from a child in a sale, as this severs natural bonds of affection and care. The prohibition extends to cases where one party is an adult and the other a minor, because the minor derives comfort from the adult's presence. The underlying cause (ʿilla) for this prohibition is mahram kinship — those who are permanently forbidden to marry due to blood or marriage relationship. Therefore, the prohibition does not apply to: (1) a mahram through breastfeeding who is not a blood relative, (2) a blood relative who is not a mahram (e.g., a cousin), (3) spouses, as their separation is permitted. Additionally, both individuals must be under the same ownership; if one belongs to the seller and the other to another person, selling the one is permissible. If the separation is justified by a right, such as handing over a slave for a crime committed, selling to repay a debt, or returning a defective item, then it is allowed — because the aim is to remove harm from others, not to harm the slave. If someone does separate them without such a right, the sale is valid (since its pillars are sound) but it is makruh (disliked). If both are adults, there is no prohibition or dislike in separating them, as the reason for mercy and comfort does not apply. The proof for this is the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ separating between the two sisters Mariya and Sirin, who were adult slave women. The commentator also references the hadith 'He is not of us who does not show mercy to our young and respect our elders' to emphasize the importance of compassion.

استيام (isti'yām): Interfering in a sale that is already being negotiated between two parties
بيع فاسد (bayʿ fāsid): A sale that is invalid due to a defect in a condition or description but not in the contract pillars

Two rulings on separating slave sisters: minors vs. adults

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on separating purchased slave sisters and apply the distinction between minor and adult siblings.

Core Idea

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition, ruling, and conditions of iqalah, and apply the rule that invalid conditions do not nullify the rescission.

Core Idea
Iqalah is a rescission (faskh) of a sale, not a new sale; it is recommended and must return the exact first price.

This lesson introduces iqalah (rescission of sale) as a recommended act in Hanafi fiqh. You will learn its definition, the proof from hadith, the essential condition of returning the first price, and the effect of invalid conditions. Understanding the distinction between iqalah as faskh and a new sale is critical for applying the correct rulings in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1

The ruling is mandub (recommended). The proof is the hadith: 'Whoever rescinds a sale for a Muslim, Allah will rescind his stumble.' Note the chain and the indication of recommendation.
The hadith is ahad but accepted by Hanafi scholars.

If the parties stipulate a different price or deferment, the condition is void but the iqalah remains valid, and the first price must be returned.
This reinforces the nature of iqalah as faskh; as a new sale, such conditions would nullify it.

Unlike a sale, iqalah does not involve bargaining or a new price. It is a cancellation of the original contract. The five issues mentioned in the Jawharah illustrate practical differences.
This distinction has practical consequences in rulings on defects, third-party rights, and timing.

Rescission (Iqalah): Conditions and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Its Legal Nature
Defines rescission, its conditions, and the intra-Hanafi dispute on its nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of rescission (Iqalah), explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on whether it is a revocation (faskh) or a new sale (bay') for third parties, and apply this to the pre-emption example.

Core Idea
Rescission is a revocation for the parties; for third parties, it is a revocation if possible, otherwise a new sale.

This lesson examines the chapter on rescission (Iqalah) from the Hanafi fiqh tradition. You will learn the core ruling that rescission must be with the like of the first price, the exception when a defect occurs, and the important intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf regarding whether rescission is a revocation (faskh) or a new sale (bay') in relation to third parties such as pre-emptors.

Guided Path
1
State the general ruling on conditions
Identify the explicit condition: rescission must be with the equivalent of the first price in type and amount. Note that if a deviant condition (less/more price, delay, etc.) is stipulated, the condition is void but the rescission remains valid.
Exception: if a defect arises with the buyer after the sale, rescission with a lower price is valid.
2
Understand the legal nature for contracting parties
For the two parties, rescission is a revocation (faskh), meaning the original sale is annulled retroactively. No specific wording is required; any expression of cancellation suffices.
Even actions like cutting a shirt immediately after saying 'I rescind you' can indicate acceptance.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi dispute on third parties
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that rescission is a revocation for third parties as well, but if revocation is impossible (e.g., sold item changed), it becomes a new sale. Imam Abu Yusuf holds the opposite: it is a sale unless impossible, then a revocation; if neither possible, the rescission is void.
This dispute is not about the validity of rescission but its effect on outsiders.
4
Apply the practical consequence: pre-emption
If a pre-emptor waived his right in the original sale and then the parties rescind, under Abu Hanifa’s view rescission is a revocation so the pre-emptor cannot claim; but if it became a new sale (because revocation impossible), he can. Under Abu Yusuf, it is a new sale so he can claim unless impossible, then revocation (no claim).
The matn and commentary favor Abu Hanifa's position as the primary view.

The Nature of Iqalah: New Sale or Rescission?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on legal nature of iqalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the legal nature of iqalah and identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) view with its supporting authorities.

Core Idea
Iqalah is a new sale for third parties but a rescission between the parties — and that is the relied-upon Hanafi view.

In the Hanafi school, the mutual rescission of a sale (iqalah) is not a simple matter. The three founding imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad — differed on whether iqalah is a new sale (bay') or a rescission (faskh). This disagreement has practical consequences, especially for the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). In this lesson, you will work through the three positions and learn which one became the operative ruling (mufta bihi) for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three imams and their default views

The phrase 'بيع جديد في حق غيرهما' refers to Abu Hanifa's view.
2
Note the backup positions
Each imam provides a secondary classification if the primary one is impossible. Identify these fallback positions: Abu Hanifa makes it a rescission; Abu Yusuf makes it a rescission if sale impossible; Muhammad makes it a sale if rescission impossible.
If neither classification works, the iqalah becomes void.
3
Apply the perspective distinction
Understand that Abu Hanifa's view distinguishes between the contracting parties and third parties. For the parties, iqalah is a rescission; for third parties (e.g., a pre-emptor), it is a new sale.
This is key to understanding the practical consequence.

Locate the tarjih
The author reports that al-Isbijabi in al-Tashih declared Abu Hanifa's view as correct, and other later scholars (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah) concurred.
This establishes the mufta bihi position.
5
Derive the practical consequence
The consequence is that when iqalah occurs on real estate, the shafi' (pre-emptor) has the right of pre-emption according to Abu Hanifa because iqalah is treated as a new sale for him. Under the other views, this right may not exist.
This thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the dispute) is what affects actual fiqh practice.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his student Abu Yusuf regarding the buyer's remedy when fraud is discovered in murabahah (cost-plus sale) and tawliyah (sale at cost). Deferring a Spot Debt and the Exception for Loans
Ruling that a spot debt may be deferred and explanation of the exception for loans

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on deferring a spot debt, identify the conditions for valid deferment, and explain why loans are an exception based on Hanafi reasoning.

Core Idea
A creditor may defer any spot debt except a loan, because loan deferment becomes riba.

In Hanafi sales law, a debt that is due immediately (thaman ḥāl) may be postponed by mutual agreement, subject to conditions. However, this general rule does not apply to loans (qarḍ), where deferment is prohibited due to the risk of riba and the charitable nature of the loan. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its conditions, and the reasoning behind the exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the deferment (ta'jīl) of a spot debt by the creditor with the debtor's acceptance. The ruling is: sahih (valid) for most spot debts, but batil (invalid) for loans.
Spot debts include sale prices and compensation for consumed goods.
2
Distinguish between permissible and impermissible deferment
The deferment is valid if the new term is known (e.g., a specific date) or if it has slight ambiguity (e.g., harvest time). It is invalid if the term is grossly ambiguous (e.g., when the wind blows) or if the debtor's ability to pay is uncertain (e.g., until he becomes affluent).
Gross ambiguity makes the deferment void; the debt remains spot.
3
Analyze the proof for permissible deferment
The Hanafi proof is twofold: (1) The creditor has the right to forgive the entire debt, so he may also grant a temporary delay (a fortiori reasoning). (2) These types of debts (e.g., sale price) may be established as deferred from the start, so it is permissible for deferment to occur after the contract (qiyas 'ala al-mubtada').
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) using qiyas.
4
Examine the exception for loans
Loans are excluded because they are charitable contracts (like 'ariyah) in inception and become an exchange in conclusion. Deferring a loan would either impose a binding condition on a charity (invalid) or create a sale of money for money on credit, which is riba.
Thus, loan deferment is invalid from both perspectives.
5
Apply the ruling to practical scenarios
After understanding the legal reasoning, apply the rule: a seller can agree to defer the price with the buyer; a lender cannot defer repayment of a loan. If a loan is deferred, the transaction becomes riba and is prohibited.
This distinction is crucial for avoiding prohibited transactions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of riba, identify its prohibition, and apply the Hanafi effective cause to determine whether a transaction involves riba.

Core Idea
Riba is any excess in exchange of same-genus measurable or weighable goods.

This lesson unpacks the foundational definition of riba from the Hanafi text al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri. Understanding what riba means linguistically and legally is essential before studying its specific forms. The passage defines the effective cause that triggers prohibition: measurement (kayl) or weight (wazn) combined with same genus (jins). This definition forms the basis for all rulings on riba in Hanafi fiqh.

The linguistic meaning is 'increase'; legal definition adds conditions.

Riba is prohibited (haram) in every measurable (makil) or weighable (mawzun) commodity when sold with its genus with an excess.

The cause is measurement with genus (al-kayl ma'a al-jins) or weight with genus (al-wazn ma'a al-jins). Note the alternative formulation 'quantity with genus' (al-qadr ma'a al-jins) as more comprehensive.
The cause combines the method of measurement and the unity of genus.

Ribawi goods are not limited to foodstuffs; any commodity that is measured by volume or weight is subject to riba when exchanged with its genus with excess.
This distinguishes Hanafi from Shafi'i, who limit to foodstuffs.

Test transactions by checking: same genus? excess in quantity? measurable or weighable? If all three, riba is present.
Example: 1 kg wheat for 1.1 kg wheat is riba; 1 kg wheat for 1 kg wheat is permissible if hand-to-hand.

Definition and General Ruling of Riba
Defines riba and states its prohibition with the cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of riba and apply the Hanafi criterion (kail/wazn + jins) to identify prohibited surplus transactions.

Core Idea
Riba is any interest or surplus in exchange that is not offset by equivalent compensation, and its prohibition attaches to all measurable or weighable goods traded for like kind.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri opens the chapter on riba. It provides both a linguistic and a Shariah definition of riba and then establishes the general ruling: riba is prohibited in all measurable (makil) and weighable (mawzun) items when sold with their own kind (jins) with disparity (tafadul). The cause ('illa) is the combination of measurement or weight with genus identity. This is the foundational principle for riba al-fadl in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification of riba
Distinguish between riba al-fadl (surplus in exchange) and riba al-nasi'ah (delayed payment). This passage focuses on riba al-fadl: the prohibited surplus when selling like items.
Hanafis derive riba al-fadl from the hadith of the six commodities, while riba al-nasi'ah is derived from the Quranic prohibition on riba.
2
Identify the legal definition of riba
Riba is defined legally as 'an excess devoid of compensation by a legal standard, stipulated for one of the two contracting parties in a commutative transaction'. Parse each component: fadl (excess), khali 'an 'iwad (no corresponding countervalue), bi-mi'yar shar'i (according to a Shariah measure), and the condition that it is for one party.
The definition excludes cases where the excess is offset by something else (like quality) or where the standard is not legal (customary standards not recognized).
3
Apply the Hanafi condition: all makil and mawzun
According to the Hanafi school, riba al-fadl applies to every item that was measured (kayl) or weighed (wazn) by custom at the time of the Prophet ﷺ. This includes non-food items like iron and lead, as long as they were measured/weighed.
The Shafi'i school differs, limiting riba to food items and currency (gold/silver). The practical difference is significant in modern trade.
4
Determine when surplus is prohibited
Riba al-fadl is prohibited only when two conditions are met: (1) the items are of the same genus (jins), and (2) the exchange involves surplus (tafadul). If genus differs, surplus is allowed (e.g., sell wheat for barley in unequal amounts).
If both items are same genus and same measure/weight, no riba (e.g., 1 kg wheat for 1 kg wheat). The surplus must exist for prohibition.
5
Note the cause ('illa) from al-Hidayah
The cause of prohibition is kail (measurement) or wazn (weight) combined with genus (jins). Some Hanafi texts use qadr (capacity) as a more comprehensive term that includes both kail and wazn.
The 'illa is the reason the law attaches. Hanafis emphasize that the 'illa is not food or currency, but the property of being measured/weighed by custom.

Conditions of Riba: Genus and Measure
Explains when riba is prohibited based on genus and measure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios for riba based on genus and measure and apply each to determine the permissibility of disparity and delay in a sale.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, riba is prohibited when both genus and measure (volume/weight) coincide; absence of either permits disparity but may still forbid delay.

The prohibition of riba is a cornerstone of Islamic finance, and its precise conditions are essential for valid transactions. This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri unpacks the interplay of genus (jins) and measure (kayl/wazn) that triggers riba in sales. By mastering these rules, you will be able to analyze any exchange of ribawi goods and determine its ruling with clarity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two attributes
Recognize that riba concerns two attributes: the genus (jins) of the commodity and its standard measure (kayl for volume, wazn for weight). Items that are not normally measured by volume or weight (e.g., handfuls, apples, fals coins, tiny amounts of gold/silver) are exempt from the measure condition.
Hanafi school defines the 'illa as the combination of genus and measure; other schools include edibility or currency.
2
Recognize the three possible combinations
There are three scenarios: (1) both genus and measure are present, (2) both are absent, (3) one is present and the other absent. Each yields a different ruling for disparity (tafadul) and delay (nasa').
3
Apply the ruling for each combination
When both are present: both disparity and delay are haram. When both are absent: both are halal. When only one is present: disparity is halal but delay is haram. Note that quality (jouda) has no effect—even good-for-bad must be equal in measure.
4
Note the exceptions
Items that are not subject to standard measure (e.g., a handful for two handfuls) allow disparity, but delay is still haram if genus is same. Also, the salam contract is an exception for weighed items: currency can be paid in advance for weighed commodities like cotton or iron, even though both are mawzun.
The salam exception prevents blocking most salam transactions.
5
Understand the 'illa
The legal cause (illa) for the riba prohibition is the combination of genus and measure. This is a product of Hanafi usul and differs from other madhahib.

Commentary

The core principle: when exchanging two items of the same genus (jins), both must be equal in measure (kayl or wazn) and the transaction must be hand-to-hand (without delay). This means that if an item is customarily sold by measure, it is considered a measured item for riba purposes; if by weight, it is a weighed item. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Selling Meat for Live Animal
Records disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on selling meat for an animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on selling meat for a live animal, explain the reasoning behind each, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
Selling meat for a live animal is permissible according to the majority Hanafis; Imam Muhammad requires a surplus to avoid gharar.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of exchanging butchered meat for a live animal of the same species. Understanding this difference sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi imams apply principles of weighed vs. non-weighed goods and the prohibition of gharar. We will follow the arguments of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is selling meat (e.g., mutton) for a live animal (e.g., a sheep). Determine the hukm according to each imam.
The dispute is about permissibility, not validity conditions.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
They permit the sale unconditionally, provided the specific items are identified and the genus is the same.
Their dalil: it is a sale of a weighed item (mawzun) for a non-weighed item (ghayr mawzun), so no equality required.
3
State the position of Imam Muhammad
He requires that the meat sold be more than the meat contained in the animal; otherwise it is not permissible.
His reasoning: breaking meat increases volume, causing inequality and resembling sale by guess (jizaf).
4
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf focus on the inherent difference in type (weighed vs. non-weighed). Muhammad focuses on the practical uncertainty after butchery.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different interpretations of gharar.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi view
The practical difference: under the majority, one can sell 1 kg of meat for a live goat without checking its meat content; under Muhammad, one must ensure the meat sold exceeds the animal's meat. The majority view is relied upon for fatwa.
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the position of the two imams.
