Zakat from the Interdicted Person's Wealth
Ruling that zakat is obligatory on the safih's wealth and its collection method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that zakat is fard on the safih's wealth, explain its proof, and describe the procedure the judge must follow to ensure valid payment with intention.

Core Idea
Zakat is fard on the safih's wealth; the judge hands him the amount for intention but sends a trustee to prevent misuse.

This lesson examines a critical fiqh rule: despite financial interdiction (hajr), zakat remains obligatory on the safih's property. The proof is that zakat is a divine command like prayer and fasting, which are not waived by legal incapacity. However, since intention (niyyah) is a condition for the validity of worship, the judge cannot pay on his behalf; instead, he gives the zakat amount to the safih so he can perform the payment himself with proper intention. To safeguard the funds from being misused, a trustee accompanies the safih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what legal act is being discussed (zakat from the safih's wealth) and what the ruling is (fard).
2
Locate the dalil
Identify the proof: the obligation is from Allah, similar to prayer and fasting. This indicates that divine obligations are not affected by interdiction.
The proof is a qiyas (analogy) to prayer and fasting, which are obligatory on the safih without dispute.
3
Understand the implementation procedure
Since niyyah is required for worship, the judge gives the zakat amount directly to the safih so he can pay with his own intention. However, to prevent misuse, a trustee (amin) is sent with him.
This balances the obligation of zakat with the need to protect the safih's wealth.
4
Note the epistemological weight
This ruling is from Al-Hidayah and is the mufta bihi (operative) position. It is agreed upon within the Hanafi school.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwa literature).

Paying Zakat for a Partner Without Permission
Ruling and liability in partner zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying zakat for a partner without permission and apply the rule of liability when both partners pay.

Core Idea
Paying another's zakat without permission is prohibited; if both pay, the second is liable.

This lesson examines a scenario where two partners authorize each other to pay their respective zakat. The issue arises when both actually pay: does the second payment count? The Hanafi school holds that the second payer is liable to compensate his partner because the obligation was already fulfilled, regardless of his knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Recognize that paying zakat for a partner without explicit permission is prohibited (haram). Zakat is a personal obligation and not part of the partnership business.
The act is not valid unless authorized.
2
Understand the scenario of mutual authorization
Both partners give each other permission to pay their respective zakat. This sets the stage for a potential conflict.
Authorization can be explicit or implicit?
3
Determine the consequence when both pay
If both actually pay, the first payment fulfills the obligation. The second payment is not valid as zakat, so the second payer becomes liable (damin) to reimburse the partner.
Liability is regardless of whether the second payer knew of the first payment.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
According to Abu Hanifa, knowledge does not affect liability. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) are reported to hold that if the second payer was ignorant, he is not liable.
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view.

Liability of the Second Payer in Partnership Zakat
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for second zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a partner paying zakat on behalf of another without permission, and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the liability of the second payer when both pay in succession.

Core Idea
Once zakat is paid, the obligation is discharged — a second payment is invalid and the payer is liable.

In partnership (shirkah), partners often handle zakat payments collectively. But what happens if one partner pays the other's zakat without authorization, or if both are authorized and pay in succession? This lesson explores the general prohibition and the specific intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the second payer is liable if unaware of the first payment. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi legal reasoning (qiyas) and recognize how later authorities settled the matter.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling
Identify the act (a partner paying zakat of another without permission) and its ruling (haram). Recognize the exception when permission is given.
2
Describe the scenario
Both partners authorize each other to pay, and they pay in succession. The first payment fulfills the obligation.
Payments must be in succession, not simultaneous.
3
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the second payer is liable (damir) regardless of knowledge. His proof is qiyas to an agent selling a manumitted slave.
The subject matter (zakat obligation) no longer exists.
4
Identify the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the second payer is not liable if unaware. They consider ignorance as excusing.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (al-Asrar, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi) preferred Abu Hanifa's view, making it the operative position.
The practical consequence: the second payer must bear the loss.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Book of Partnership concerning zakat payment by partners. The matn states that one partner may not pay zakat on behalf of the other without permission, because zakat is not part of trade operations. When both partners authorize each other and then pay in succession, Abu Hanifa holds the second payer liable regardless of knowledge, because the first payment has already fulfilled the obligation, making the second payer's payment non-compliant, similar to an agent who sells a slave after the owner has freed him. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ, saying the second is not liable if unaware. Later authorities in 'al-Tashih' and 'al-Asrar' support Abu Hanifa's view. The commentator further notes that this ruling applies only to successive payment; if both pay simultaneously, each is liable for the portion of the other, and they either set off debts or reclaim the surplus.

وكالة (wakalah): Agency; the contract by which one person appoints another to act on his behalf.
عَزْلٌ حُكْمي (azl hukmi): Constructive dismissal; automatic termination of agency without notification, due to death, apostasy, etc.

Keeping Expenses from Nazr Wealth
Explains dispensation to keep expenses before charity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi dispensation allowing a person to keep necessary expenses from wealth due for charity (nazr) and identify the factors that determine the duration of withholding.

Core Idea
Need takes precedence over charity: keep living expenses from nazr wealth to prevent hardship.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a critical question: if a person has only the wealth he vowed to give as charity, is he forced to give it all and become destitute? Through istihsan, Hanafi law rules that he may keep enough for his dependents' expenses until he earns again. This ruling balances the obligation of a nazr with the imperative to avoid harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation and the problem
Recognize that the person has obligated charity (nazr) on his wealth but possesses no other assets. The apparent ruling would require immediate payment, leading to hardship.
2
State the dispensation
He may keep an amount sufficient for himself and his dependents until he earns other wealth. Once he earns, he must give charity equal to what he kept.
3
Explain the rationale
Istihsan is applied because his need takes precedence to prevent harm (darar). The obligation is not lifted but postponed.
The principle 'الضرر يُزال' supports this.
4
Note the variation in time periods
The amount kept is not fixed; it depends on the person's earning cycle: daily earner (craftsman) keeps one day, monthly earner (fruit income) keeps one month, annual earner (estates) keeps one year, businessman keeps until his capital returns.
This reflects the diversity of livelihoods.

Commentary

For a businessman, he keeps until his capital returns to him. This is a dispensation based on istihsan (juristic preference) to prevent harm.

Obligation of 'Ushr on Revived Land
Ruling on 'ushr and ownership of revived land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on 'ushr for revived land, explain the exception for kharaj water, and distinguish between the two Hanafi views on reclaiming abandoned revived land.

Core Idea
A Muslim reviving dead land pays 'ushr, not kharaj, unless irrigated with kharaj water; the reviver fully owns the land and can reclaim it if abandoned.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies the tax obligation on land revived by a Muslim. It establishes the default rule of 'ushr, the exceptional case of kharaj water, and a subsidiary dispute about ownership rights. Understanding this helps distinguish land categories in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is reviving dead land and cultivating it. The ruling is that 'ushr (tithe) is wajib on the produce. Note the reason: kharaj cannot be initially imposed on a Muslim.
2
Recognize the exception
If the land is irrigated with kharaj water (water from a canal system built on kharaj land), then kharaj becomes due instead of 'ushr. The ruling follows the water source.
This is a unique Hanafi principle: water determines land tax category.
3
Analyze the subsidiary dispute
The text presents two views on what happens if the first reviver abandons the land and a second person cultivates it. Some say the second is more entitled because the first only owned the usufruct; the most correct view (al-ashahh) says the first can reclaim the land because he owns the corpus by revival.
The hadith 'Man ahya ardan maytatan fahiya lahu' is the evidence for full ownership.
4
Apply the dalil
The hadith 'Whoever revives dead land, it is his' (Sunan al-Tirmidhi) supports the preferred view. The phrase 'fahiya lahu' (it is his) indicates ownership of the corpus, including the right to reclaim.
The dalil has zanni thubut but qati dalala in the Hanafi view.

Source Text

Commentary
This passage addresses the tax obligation on land revived by a Muslim. The ruling is that the reviver must pay 'ushr (one-tenth of the produce) — not kharaj (land tax). The reason is that kharaj is a tax originally imposed on non-Muslim lands conquered by force or treaty; it cannot be initially imposed on a Muslim. However, if the land is irrigated with water from a kharaj canal (e.g., a river dug by the Imam from kharaj land), then kharaj applies because the land takes the ruling of the water source. The text then discusses a scenario where the first reviver abandons the land and a second person cultivates it. There are two views: some say the second is more entitled because the first only owned the usufruct (manfa'ah) and not the corpus (raqabah). But the most correct view (al-ashahh) is that the first owner can reclaim the land from the second because he owns the land outright by revival, as per the hadith 'Whoever revives dead land, it is his.'

Key Terms
العشر (al-'ushr): The tithe paid on agricultural produce from 'ushri land (land owned by Muslims and not subject to kharaj). One-tenth if naturally watered, one-twentieth if artificially watered.
الخراج (al-kharaj): Land tax imposed on conquered lands that remain in the hands of non-Muslims or are granted to Muslims with kharaj liability.
