Handling Incomplete Textual Evidence in Fiqh
Truncated ruling on divorce and acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a fiqh passage is truncated and explain why a ruling cannot be derived from incomplete text.

Core Idea
A truncated passage cannot yield a reliable ruling — textual completeness is a prerequisite for istinbat.

In your study of Hanafi fiqh, you will encounter passages that are incomplete due to manuscript damage, OCR errors, or editing gaps. This lesson examines a truncated ruling on divorce and acknowledgment during death illness. The goal is not to derive a ruling but to recognize the limits of incomplete evidence and the discipline of refraining from speculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
The passage begins by stating that a man divorces his wife during his death illness. Note the specific details: three divorces or less at her request.
The act is talaq (divorce) in marad al-mawt (death illness).
2
Identify the subsequent action
After the divorce, the man makes an acknowledgment (إقرار) – likely a debt or financial obligation. The text cuts off at 'ثم أقر', so the content of the acknowledgment is unspecified.
The acknowledgment is the focus of the missing ruling.
3
Recognize the truncation
The text ends abruptly with 'ثم أقر' and does not complete the ruling. The square bracket notation '(ثلاثاً)' indicates an alternative reading, but the main text is incomplete.
This truncation may be due to manuscript damage or OCR error.
4
Consider the missing elements
To derive a ruling, we would need the completion: does the acknowledgment take effect? Is it valid despite the divorce? The missing portion likely contains the hukm (ruling).
Without the full text, any speculation is unreliable.
5
Apply the principle of textual completeness
Conclude that no ruling can be deduced from this fragment. A responsible faqih or student must seek the complete source before deriving any legal conclusion.
This principle protects against erroneous fatwa.

Confession of Debt to Divorced Wife During Terminal Illness
Ruling on confession of debt to divorced wife in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a terminally ill husband divorces his wife at her request, confesses a debt to her, and dies during her iddah, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
She receives the lesser of the debt or inheritance to prevent collusion.

This passage from Al-Lubab addresses a scenario where a husband in terminal illness divorces his wife at her request, then confesses a debt to her, and dies while she is still in her waiting period. The law presumes possible collusion to prefer the confession over inheritance. Therefore, she gets the smaller of the two amounts. The ruling also depends on whether the divorce was at her request and whether her iddah ends before his death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base scenario and its ruling
The base scenario is: husband divorces his wife at her request during terminal illness, confesses a debt to her, and dies while she is in her iddah. The ruling is that she receives the lesser of the debt confessed or her inheritance share.
2
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The reason is potential collusion (they are both accused). The rule prevents abuse by limiting her to the lesser amount, as it is impossible to determine which claim is genuine.
This is a form of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking the means to evil).
3
Recognize the condition: divorce at her request
If the divorce is not at her request, the husband is considered a 'fugitive' (farr) from inheritance law. In that case, the confession is invalidated, and she receives full inheritance.
The term 'farr' applies when he divorces to deprive her of inheritance.
4
Recognize the condition: iddah ongoing at death
If her iddah ends before his death, she is no longer his wife. Then the confession stands, and she receives the debt, but she gets no inheritance.
This is because the marriage tie has ended, and the debt is independent.
5
Summarize the three possible outcomes
1) Divorce at her request, death in iddah: lesser of debt/inheritance. 2) Divorce not at her request: full inheritance, confession void. 3) Divorce at her request but iddah expired before death: debt stands, no inheritance.

Commentary
This ruling covers a scenario where a terminally ill man divorces his wife at her request, then confesses a debt to her, and dies while she is still in her 'iddah. Because both could be colluding to give her more through confession (which is paid from the estate before inheritance) than her inheritance share, the law gives her the lesser of the two: either the confessed debt or her inheritance. If the divorce was not at her request, he is considered a 'fugitive' (farr) from inheritance law, and she gets her full inheritance and the confession is void. If her 'iddah ends before his death, she is no longer his wife, so the confession stands but she gets no inheritance.

Key Terms
عدة (iddah): Waiting period after divorce.
فار (farr): One who flees from inheritance laws by divorcing to deprive wife of inheritance.

Key Takeaway
To prevent collusion, a divorced wife gets the lesser of the confessed debt or her inheritance when the husband dies during her iddah after a divorce at her request.
