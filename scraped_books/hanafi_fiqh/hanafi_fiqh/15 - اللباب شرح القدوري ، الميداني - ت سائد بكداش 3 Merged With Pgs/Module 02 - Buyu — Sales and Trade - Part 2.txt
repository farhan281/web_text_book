Duration of Khiyar al-Shart: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on option duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maximum duration of khiyar al-shart according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, explain the basis for each view, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is limited to three days—Abu Hanifa's text-bound view prevails over qiyas.

The option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) allows either party to annul a sale within an agreed period. But how long can that period be? The Hanafi school is divided: Abu Hanifa holds it to a strict three-day maximum, while his two chief disciples allow longer if specified. This lesson unpacks the textual and analogical reasoning behind each position and shows which view became the operative ruling in the school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position as per Abu Hanifa
Identify the core ruling: the option is permissible for three days or less; exceeding this makes the sale fasid (void). Note that this is the position recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
The text says 'والخيار ثلاثة أيام، فما دونها' (the option is three days or less).
2
Identify the Sahibayn's contrary view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that a longer period is valid if the duration is specified (muddah ma'lumah). They analogize to deferment of payment (ta'jil fi al-thaman).
3
Analyze the proof for Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa argues that khiyar al-shart is established 'ala khilaf al-qiyas (contrary to analogy) by a specific textual evidence (nass). Therefore its scope is restricted to what the text specifies, i.e., three days. Extending it would revert to the original qiyas which prohibits such options.
The phrase 'على خلاف القياس بالنص' is key.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof and the Hanafi response
The Sahibayn argue by need (hajah) and analogy with deferment. The Hanafi response (from Abu Hanifa's side) is that the clear textual limit overrides qiyas; the need is met within three days, and any extension invalidates the contract.
The tarjih authorities (al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'a, etc.) prefer Abu Hanifa's evidence.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
Practical consequence: if a period longer than three days is stipulated, the sale is fasid according to Abu Hanifa, but valid per the Sahibayn. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is Abu Hanifa's view.
The text cites 'al-Tuhfa' as stating 'الصحيح قول أبي حنيفة'.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the chapter on Khiyar al-Shart (Option of Stipulation). The commentator begins by stating that the option of stipulation is permissible in sales for any party: the seller, the buyer, or both together. He notes that this option is placed before other options in the text because it prevents the inception of ownership, while the option of sight follows because it prevents completion, and the option of defect comes last because it prevents bindingness. The option can be stipulated within the contract or even after it by a few days, but not before the contract.

The main focus of this lesson is the duration of the option. The ruling is that the option period is three days or less. Abu Hanifa holds that it cannot exceed three days, because the option was established by textual evidence (the Prophet's hadith) contrary to analogy, so it must be strictly limited. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani argue that a longer period is permissible if a specific time is named, based on the need for deliberation and the analogy with deferment of payment. Later Hanafi scholars like al-Mahbubi and Sadr al-Shari'a preferred Abu Hanifa's view, making it the relied-upon position (al-mu'tamad). An additional note clarifies that if a sale is conditioned on the approval of a third party, the time for that approval must be specified for the contract to be valid.

Duration of Khiyar al-Shart: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
States the ruling on maximum duration of khiyar al-shart with intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the maximum duration of khiyar al-shart and apply the mufta bihi position in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is limited to three days by default; extending it is khilaf al-qiyas and thus impermissible according to Abu Hanifah.

This lesson examines the dispute between Abu Hanifah and his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the maximum duration of the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) in sales. Understanding this dispute requires grasping the concept of khilaf al-qiyas and how textual evidence limits the scope of legal options. We will follow the reasoning of each side and see why later Hanafi authorities upheld Abu Hanifah's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Basis
The matn states that the option period in a sale is three days or less. Abu Hanifah rules that stipulating more than three days is not permissible, because khiyar was established contrary to analogy (khilaf al-qiyas) by textual evidence, so it must be restricted to the amount specified in the text.
The ruling is based on the principle that exceptions to qiyas cannot be extended beyond their textual scope.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's Contrary View
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that stipulating a longer period is permissible if the parties specify a known duration, even perpetuity. They argue that the option is legislated for the need of deliberation to prevent loss, and that need may require a longer period, analogous to deferring the payment of the price.
Their view uses qiyas (analogy) to extend the ruling, which Abu Hanifah rejects because the original ruling is khilaf al-qiyas.
3
Analyze the Proofs
Abu Hanifah's proof: the khiyar ruling is an exception, so it must be confined. The Sahibayn's proof: the illa (effective cause) is the need for deliberation, which can be present for longer periods, so qiyas allows extension. The commentary notes that later scholars responded to the Sahibayn's evidence.
The core dispute is whether khiyar can be extended by qiyas when it was originally established as khilaf al-qiyas.
4
Evaluate the Tarjih
Al-Marghinani in al-Tuhfah, followed by al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, al-Nasafi, and Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili, preferred Abu Hanifah's proof. They established that the mufta bihi (reliant position) is Abu Hanifah's view.
The tarjih is based on the strength of Abu Hanifah's usul principle regarding khilaf al-qiyas.
5
Apply the Mufta Bihi
In a scenario where parties stipulate a 7-day option, the contract is fasid (defective) according to the mufta bihi. The buyer must either complete the sale within three days or rescind, or the contract must be restructured.
The practical consequence: a longer option period cannot be enforced; if stipulated, it invalidates the sale.

Ownership and Liability During Khiyar al-Shart
States the effect of khiyar al-shart on ownership and liability for loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal effect of khiyar al-shart on ownership during the option period and apply the liability rule when the buyer takes possession during the seller's option.

Core Idea
During khiyar al-shart, ownership never leaves the seller; buyer possession is liability, not ownership.

Khiyar al-shart (stipulated option) creates a suspended sale. This passage clarifies a critical point: regardless of which party holds the option, ownership of the sold item remains with the seller until the option expires or is exercised. The ruling on liability for loss depends on whether the buyer has taken possession and who holds the option. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion about risk and ownership during the option period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage treats two cases: (1) the seller has the option, (2) the buyer has the option. For each, note the ruling on ownership and on liability if the item perishes.
These are the two basic types of khiyar al-shart.
2
State the ownership rule for seller's option
When the seller has the option, the sold item remains in the seller's ownership, even if the buyer has taken physical possession.
This is agreed upon (ittifaqan).
3
State the liability rule for seller's option with buyer possession
If the buyer takes possession during the seller's option and the item perishes, the buyer must pay the value (for unique goods) or its equivalent (for fungibles).
The sale is rescinded by destruction because it was suspended; the buyer's possession is treated as possession under a purchase offer (sawm al-shira').
4
State the ownership rule for buyer's option
When the buyer has the option, ownership still remains with the seller; the buyer does not become owner and the item does not enter his ownership by consensus.
This is by consensus (ijma').
5
Trace the dalil for buyer's liability
The reasoning: the sale is suspended (mawquf) and cannot be executed without the subject matter. Upon destruction, the sale is automatically rescinded. The buyer's possession is not as owner but as a prospective buyer, which carries liability like an offer to purchase.
The proof is rational (dalil aqli) based on the nature of a suspended contract.

Buyer's Ownership During Khiyar al-Shart: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Rulings on khiyar al-shart with intra-Hanafi dispute on buyer's ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on buyer's ownership during khiyar al-shart and explain the differing views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, identifying the practical consequence (thamarah) in liability for perishing.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: buyer does not own during option; Sahibayn: he does.

In khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation), the buyer may have the right to rescind the sale within a specified period. But does the buyer actually own the item during that period? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and the practical implications for liability if the item is destroyed in the buyer's hands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
Determine the exact point of disagreement: Does the buyer acquire ownership of the purchased item during his khiyar al-shart?
The dispute is only about the buyer's option; the seller's option is agreed upon.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa says the buyer does not own. His proof: the price remains in the buyer's ownership; if the item also entered his ownership, both countervalues would be in one ownership, contradicting the nature of exchange (mu'awadah).
He argues that the item is mawquf (suspended) until the option ends.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the buyer owns. Their proof: the item left the seller's ownership; if it does not enter the buyer's, it would be ownerless, which has no precedent in Shari'ah.
They reason that the sale is binding on the seller, so ownership must transfer.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The difference appears when the item perishes in the buyer's hand during the option. According to Abu Hanifa, the buyer pays the market value (qimah) or its like (mithl); according to the Sahibayn, he pays the named price.
This is because ownership affects liability: if buyer owns, he owes the price; if not, he compensates as for a trust (amanah) that perished.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (al-Tahawi, Burhan al-Shari'ah, etc.) adopted Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position.
Thus, in fatwa, the buyer does not own during his option.

Commentary
This passage from Lubab al-Quduri deals with khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation), a condition in a sale contract allowing one or both parties to rescind within a specified period. The text first states the general ruling: the seller's option prevents the sold item from leaving his ownership, even if the buyer takes possession. If the buyer takes possession and the item perishes in his hand during the option period, he must compensate according to its value (for value-goods) or like item (for fungibles), because the sale is rescinded upon perishing, and the item is considered held on approval (sawm al-shira'). If it perishes in the seller's hand, the sale is rescinded and the buyer owes nothing, analogous to an absolute sale without option.

For the buyer's option, the text clarifies that it does not prevent the sold item from leaving the seller's ownership by consensus, but the buyer does not own it according to Abu Hanifa. This is followed by an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the buyer does own the item during his option. Their reasoning is that if the item left the seller's ownership without entering the buyer's, it would become ownerless, which is not recognized in Shari'ah. Abu Hanifa rebuts that the price remains in the buyer's ownership, so if the item also enters his ownership, both countervalues would be in one person's ownership, contradicting the very nature of exchange (mu'awadah), which requires separation of countervalues. The text then notes that the relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's, supported by later authorities. The practical consequence (thamarah) appears in the case of perishing: according to Abu Hanifa, the buyer owes the market value or like item; according to the Sahibayn, he owes the named price. However, the mufta bihi position is that the buyer owes the named price if he perishes the item after being unable to return it, as stated at the end.

Permanent vs. Non-Permanent Defects During Khiyar al-Shart
Ruling on defects during khiyar al-shart with two categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a permanent defect ('ayb lazim) and a non-permanent defect ('ayb ghayr lazim) and state the Hanafi ruling regarding the buyer's option in each case.

Core Idea
During khiyar al-shart, a permanent defect is deemed accepted; a non-permanent defect only ends the option if it persists until expiry.

In the chapter on khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation), we learn that defects can arise in the sold item during the option period. The Hanafi school distinguishes by the nature of the defect: permanent or temporary. This distinction determines whether the buyer retains the right to cancel or is bound to the contract. Understanding this ruling helps avoid disputes in sales transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of defects
Read the passage and note that it divides defects into permanent ('ayb lazim) and non-permanent ('ayb ghayr lazim).
Permanent defects are those that do not disappear, such as loss of a limb. Non-permanent are temporary, like illness.
2
Rule for permanent defects
Whatever the source of the permanent defect (buyer, third party, act of God, or the sold item itself), the buyer is considered to have accepted it. The option continues, but after confirmation, the buyer cannot return the item for that defect.
The buyer's option is not extinguished, but the defect is not a ground for return after the sale becomes binding.
3
Rule for non-permanent defects
If the defect ceases within the option period, the buyer retains full option. If it persists until the end of the period, the contract becomes binding because returning the item without the defect is impossible.
The rationale is ta'adhdhur al-radd (impossibility of return in a sound condition).
4
Apply the distinction
Given a scenario, first classify the defect as permanent or non-permanent. Then determine if the defect ceased by option expiry for non-permanent ones.
This step is crucial for practical application.

Valid Stipulation vs Void Excess in Sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a valid stipulation of a known desirable quality and a void stipulation of an unknown excess in a sale, and state the Hanafi ruling for each.

Core Idea
Stipulating a known quality gives an option; stipulating an unknown increase voids the sale.

In Hanafi sales law, a buyer may stipulate a desired quality in the purchased item. If that quality is missing, the buyer has the right to cancel. But what if the stipulation involves an unknown quantity, like a specific amount of milk? This lesson explains the critical distinction between a known quality and an unknown excess, and why the latter voids the sale entirely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: a sale with a stipulation regarding the item's quality. Note the two scenarios: (1) stipulation of a known desirable quality vs (2) stipulation of an unknown excess.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle on jahl
Recall that jahl (ignorance) regarding the price or subject matter can invalidate a sale. Here, an unknown excess in a stipulation creates jahl about the item's value, making the sale void (fasid).
Contrast with a known quality, which is not jahl.
3
State the ruling for known quality
If the buyer stipulates a known, desirable quality (e.g., the animal is milk-giving) and it is absent, the sale is valid but the buyer has an option (khiyar) to cancel due to the missing attribute.
The attribute must be recognizable and not purely quantitative.
4
State the ruling for unknown excess
If the buyer stipulates an unknown increase (e.g., exact milk quantity, specific baking amount), the sale is void (fasid) because it introduces jahl in a term that affects the price.
The unknown quantity is considered an excessive stipulation that cannot be ascertained.
5
Note the thamarah (practical consequence)
The practical difference: in the first case, the buyer can cancel or keep the item; in the second, the sale is automatically invalid and must be rescinded.
This affects whether the buyer can claim compensation or simply return the item.

Core Idea
Seeing the indicative part of an item before sale removes the buyer's option of inspection.

In the chapter on the Option of Inspection (Khiyar al-Ru'ya), the Hanafi school balances the buyer's right to inspect with practicality. This passage outlines specific scenarios where seeing a part of the item suffices to remove the option, based on the principle that the part indicates the whole. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for determining when a sale becomes binding after sight-unseen purchases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and exceptions
The default rule is that a buyer who purchases an item sight-unseen has the option to cancel upon seeing it. The passage lists exceptions where seeing a specific part removes that option. The exceptions depend on the part being indicative of the whole.

For the folded cloth, the outer surface suffices only if the inner part is uniform and implied by the outer. If there is a distinct feature like embroidery on the inner side, seeing the outer alone does not remove the option. Understand the nuance: what is 'intended' matters.

The passage concludes with the principle: seeing all of the sold item is not required due to difficulty (ta'azzur). Therefore, the law suffices with seeing what indicates knowledge of the intended (al-muqsud). This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference) based on custom and necessity.
The principle is that the option is removed when the buyer has seen enough to form a reliable judgment.

Consider a scenario: A buyer sees only the label of a sealed box of dates. Does this remove the option? According to the principle, the face of the pile (wajh al-sabrah) suffices because the pile's face indicates the quality of the whole. The label does not indicate the contents similarly, so the option remains. Practice by reasoning from the given examples.
The key is whether the part seen is 'inducive of knowledge of the intended'.
5

The Hanafi position is pragmatic. Other schools, like the Shafi'i, may require that the buyer had no prior sight at all for the option to exist; partial sight before contract does not remove the option. Understanding this ikhtilaf helps in comparative fiqh.
The Hanafi view is considered 'mukhtalaf fih' (disputed) across schools.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For uniform goods, seeing a sample suffices, but inferiority gives the option of defect.

When you buy a large quantity, do you need to inspect every single item? Hanafi fiqh provides a nuanced answer based on the nature of the goods. In this lesson, we examine the classical ruling and a practical refinement by a later scholar, which shows how custom (urf) fills the gaps in legal reasoning.

Identify the type of goods

Apply the default rule
If the goods are uniform, seeing one sample suffices to remove the option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'ya). The buyer is deemed to have approved the rest based on the sample.

The option of defect is based on hidden defect, not on lack of inspection.

This follows commercial custom and is a practical extension of the rule.

Core Idea
Seeing one container suffices to drop the option of inspection, even if multiple containers exist.

Tarjih is based on custom and practicality.

Under the Balkh view, the buyer retains the option until all containers are seen; under the Iraqi view, the option is lost upon seeing one container.

Core Idea
Courtyard suffices for Abu Hanifa but Zufar requires interior inspection; the later fatwa follows Zufar.

The option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'yah) protects a buyer who has not seen the purchased property. A key dispute within the Hanafi school is whether seeing only the courtyard and exterior of a house is enough to extinguish this option. This lesson examines the views of Abu Hanifa and Zufar, and explains why later authorities preferred Zufar's stricter position.

Guided Path

Zufar (a leading Hanafi scholar) disagrees: the buyer must see the interior of the rooms to have a valid inspection.

3

Understand Zufar's basis: practical caution and the possibility of differences between rooms that are not apparent from outside.

The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the buyer loses the option after seeing the courtyard; under Zufar, the buyer retains the option until the interior is seen.

A blind person's sale is valid but he retains the option to cancel because he has not seen the item; the sight of his agent binds him, but a messenger's sight does not.

The text affirms that his transactions are valid, but uniquely grants him an ongoing option to rescind upon seeing the item. We will also explore an important intra-Hanafi dispute: does the sight of a messenger (rasul) bind the blind principal, or only that of an agent (wakil)? Understanding this distinction reveals deeper principles of agency and reliance in Hanafi contract law.

1

The text states that a blind person's sale and purchase are valid (ja'iz) and he has khiyar al-ru'yah. The basis is that he cannot see, so he buys unseen.
The option is a right to cancel upon first sight of the item.
2

In earlier times, exterior view sufficed; today interior inspection is required due to variation. This illustrates that the option adapts to custom ('urf).

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider messenger and agent equal; Abu Hanifa distinguishes. Al-Hidayah presents Abu Hanifa's proof and indicates preference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the methods by which a blind buyer's option of sight is dropped according to the Hanafi school, and distinguish between the ruling for movable goods and real estate.

Core Idea
For a blind buyer, option of sight drops by touch, smell, or taste; for real estate, only a description suffices.

We now turn to the specific case of a blind buyer who purchases an unseen item. The option of sight exists to protect against unknown defects, but how can a blind person exercise it? The Hanafi school provides clear guidance: if the item can be identified by other senses, those senses substitute for sight; otherwise, a detailed description is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a blind person purchasing an unseen item. The ruling is that the option of sight drops when the buyer gains knowledge through alternative senses or description.
The option is a right that can be waived; knowledge achieves its purpose.
2
Locate the dalil
The reasoning is qiyas: other senses provide knowledge like sight; real estate requires description analogized to salam contracts.
The basis is achieving the objective (tahsil al-maqsud).
3
Note the ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf holds that standing in the place and expressing satisfaction also drops the option. Ibn al-Humam critiques this as weak and notes another narration from Abu Yusuf.
Simulation (tashabbuh) is not equivalent to actual sight for real estate.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that real estate requires description; the alternative view is weak and not acted upon.
Al-Tuhfah and Abu Nasr al-Aqta' confirm this as the correct view.

The Suspended Sale (Bayʿ al-Fuḍūlī): Owner's Option and Conditions for Ratification
Ruling on sale of another's property without permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of a sale by an unauthorized person and identify the conditions that must exist for the owner's ratification to be effective in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A sale of another's property without permission is suspended — valid only if the owner ratifies while the object, owner, and parties remain.

This lesson examines a distinctive feature of Hanafi contract law: the possibility of a third party selling someone else's property without prior authorization. The Hanafi school does not consider such a sale void ab initio but rather suspended (mawquf), giving the owner a choice to ratify or rescind. We will explore the conditions for valid ratification and the legal effects once ratified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its initial status
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of a non-owner selling property without permission. Its initial legal status is suspended (mawquf) — not valid but not void.
Contrast with Shafi'i view that such a sale is void even with ratification.
2
Determine the owner's options
The owner has two options: to ratify (ijazah) the sale, making it valid, or to rescind (faskh) it, rendering it void.
3
List the conditions for valid ratification
For ratification to be effective, four elements must still exist: the object of sale, the owner, the unauthorized seller, and the buyer. If any has changed or perished, ratification cannot revive the sale.
These are often summarized as 'بقاء الأربعة' (the four remaining).
4
Examine the effect of ratification
Ratification retroactively makes the sale as if the unauthorized seller was an agent. The price belongs to the ratifying owner if still in the seller's hands; if lost, the seller is not liable if he acted as a trustworthy holder.
The buyer and unauthorized seller may also rescind before ratification.
5
Consider the intra-Hanafi nuance on sight
Al-Hasan reported a view that the owner should appoint an agent to see and take delivery of the item, aligning with Abu Hanifah's principle that the agent's sight substitutes for the owner's. This affects the option of sight (khiyar al-ru'yah).
This nuance is not the main ruling but shows the depth of Hanafi reasoning.

These rulings are from al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage is from the sharh (commentary) of al-Lubab on the matn of al-Quduri, discussing the Chapter of Option of Defect (khiyar al-‘ayb). The author first states the general rule: a defect is anything that reduces the market value according to merchant custom. This is a matter of custom, not a fixed definition, and is determined by experts of that trade. Then the text addresses three specific behaviors—running away, bed-wetting, and theft—in the context of a slave. These are considered defects when they occur in a minor (a discerning child who would be reprimanded for such acts) while still in the seller's possession. However, if the slave reaches puberty and then exhibits these behaviors after coming into the buyer's possession, they are NOT considered defects entitling a return, unless the same behavior recurs after puberty at the buyer's side. The reasoning, borrowed from al-Hidayah and al-Fath, is that the cause differs between childhood and adulthood. In a child, these acts stem from physical weakness, love of play, or lack of caution; in an adult, they stem from internal malice or disease. Thus, they are legally different defects. The buyer may return the slave only if the exact same defect (i.e., occurring at the same stage of life) existed at the seller and persists or appears at the buyer. The passage ends by clarifying the precise wording of al-Quduri's matn: 'until it recurs after puberty' means the act must occur after puberty in the seller's possession and then recur after puberty in the buyer's possession for the return to be valid.

Defect Recurrence After Puberty: Condition for Return
Explains ruling on returning a slave if a defect recurs after puberty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a pre-puberty defect allows return after puberty and explain the reasoning (taʿlīl) based on differing causes.

Core Idea
A defect before puberty is not a defect after puberty unless it recurs — the cause changes with age.

This passage from Lubāb al-Qudūrī clarifies a subtle point in sales law regarding defects in slaves. The buyer's right to return (khiyār al-ʿayb) depends on whether the defect existed at the time of sale. But what if the defect only showed before puberty? The Hanafi school provides a detailed criterion based on the cause of the behavior. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the return option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the rule: a defect that appeared only before puberty does not allow return unless the same behavior recurs after puberty in the buyer's possession and was also present after puberty in the seller's possession.
2
Understand the Taʿlīl (Reasoning)
The cause of the behavior differs with age: childhood actions stem from immaturity or physical weakness, while the same actions after puberty stem from internal disease or evil nature. Hence they are legally different defects.
3
Apply the Distinction
If the defect recurs after puberty in the buyer's hand and was also present after puberty in the seller's hand, the defect is one and return is allowed. If it only appeared before puberty in the seller's hand, it is not a valid defect for return.
4
Contrast with Consistent Occurrences
If the defect was present in both hands during childhood or consistently after puberty, return is allowed. The key is the identity of the cause.
5
Examine the Commentary
The passage from al-Fatḥ and al-Jawhara al-Nayyira reinforces that the recurrence must be of the same defect type and cause, not a mere similarity in behavior.

In Hanafi sales law, not every undesirable condition in a purchased slave is a legal defect. This lesson explores a key distinction: conditions like bad breath, armpit odor, and even adultery are considered defects in a female slave but not in a male slave, unless special circumstances arise. The reasoning is grounded in the different intended purposes (مقصود) for each gender, as explained by the classical Hanafi commentators.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Defect Option After Partial Consumption or Sale
Sharh expanding on the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding remedy after partial consumption or sale of defective goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi intra-school positions on the buyer's remedy after partially consuming or selling defective goods and explain the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When the buyer consumes part of defective goods, the mufta bihi allows returning the remainder and recovering the deficiency of the consumed part.

This passage from Al-Lubab reveals an important intra-Hanafi dispute: what remedy does a buyer have if he discovers a defect after having partially consumed or sold the goods? Abu Hanifa denies any recourse, while the Sahibayn offer different degrees of compensation. To understand the operative ruling, we must trace the evolution from qiyas to istihsan and the later tarjih by the mutakhkhirin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the asl
Recognize that Abu Hanifa analogizes consumption to selling or killing a defective slave, where the buyer's act of guarantee (damān) precludes any return or compensation. This is the qiyas position.
The asl is the case of selling or killing the slave before defect discovery.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's istihsan objection
The Sahibayn override the qiyas with istihsan: the buyer should not be left without any remedy. They propose recovery of the defect deficiency (naqsān al-‘ayb). Note the two narrations for eating: Abu Yusuf recovers deficiency on the whole item; Muhammad returns the remainder and recovers only on the eaten part.
The dispute only applies to consumption (akl), not killing (qatl).
3
Analyze the selling sub-case
For selling part, the initial view from both Sahibayn denies any remedy. A later narration from Muhammad allows returning the remainder with a proportional refund of the price, but no recovery for the sold portion.
This is based on the principle that the buyer's disposal by sale is voluntary and considered acceptance.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Later scholars (al-Sadr al-Shahid, Abu Ja‘far, Abu al-Layth) preferred Muhammad’s view for its leniency. The mufta bihi is: if the buyer ate part, he returns the remainder and recovers the deficiency of the eaten portion; if he sold part, he returns the remainder with its share of the price and does not recover the deficiency of the sold part.
This balances the buyer's right to compensation with the inability to return the consumed part.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Given a buyer who consumes a glass of milk from a carton and then discovers the milk is sour, state the remedy: he returns the remaining carton and recovers the defect deficiency on the consumed glass (the difference between the value of good milk and sour milk per glass).
This illustrates the practical application of the mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab, a commentary on Al-Quduri's matn, addresses the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the buyer's remedy when a defect is discovered after the buyer has consumed or sold part of the goods. The chapter is 'Option of Defect' (khayar al-ayb). The author first states the positions: Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer recovers nothing because he has performed an act of guarantee (damān) on the sold item, making return impossible; he analogizes this to the cases of selling or killing the slave. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the buyer recovers the deficiency in value caused by the defect (naqsān al-ayb) by way of istihsan (juristic preference). The text notes that the fatwa is on the Sahibayn's view, as stated in Al-Bahr and Al-Nihayah. However, a refinement from Al-Jawharah clarifies that the dispute only applies to consumption (akl), not killing (qatl), where there is near-consensus that no recovery is made.

The passage then discusses two sub-scenarios: eating and selling. For eating, Abu Hanifa maintains no recovery. From the Sahibayn, there are two narrations: one from Abu Yusuf that the buyer recovers the deficiency on the entire item (both consumed and unconsumed), and another from Muhammad that the buyer returns the remaining unconsumed portion and recovers the deficiency only on what was eaten. The fatwa is upon Muhammad's view, as favored by later scholars like al-Sadr al-Shahid for the benefit of people.

For selling part, the Sahibayn initially hold that the buyer neither returns the remainder nor recovers anything. Muhammad has a narration that he returns the remainder without recovering the deficiency of what he sold. Another narration from Muhammad (in Al-Khaniyya) says he returns the remainder with a proportional refund of its share of the price, and this is the relied-upon fatwa.

The final summary (al-hasil) states the mufta bihi: if the buyer sold or ate part, he returns the remainder and recovers the deficiency of what he ate, but not of what he sold. This balances the buyer's right to compensation for the consumed part with the inability to return it, while the sold part is treated as a separate transaction where the buyer has already disposed of it.

استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; departing from qiyas due to a stronger consideration (e.g., equity).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mufta bihi ruling for returning defective food when part is sold or eaten, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
If part of defective food is sold, return remainder without compensation; if eaten, return remainder with compensation.

When a buyer discovers a defect in food after purchase, the general rule is to return all or keep all. But what if the buyer has already sold or eaten part? The Hanafi school has a dispute within itself. This lesson unpacks the positions of Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and the eventual relied-upon fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and default rule
Imagine a buyer finds a defect in a measured food item after part has been sold or consumed. The default rule from general matns is: if any part is defective, the buyer may return the entire item or keep it. But this applies only when the whole item remains in his possession.
Default rule from matns: 'له رده كله أو أخذه'.
2
Identify the disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf holds: no return of the remaining portion and no compensation. Muhammad holds: return the remaining portion but no compensation for the sold part. Later versions from Muhammad allow proportional return.
Source: al-Dhakhira; the dispute is recorded in al-Asl.
3
Trace the tarjih (preference) process
Later scholars like al-Sadr al-Shahid, Abu Ja'far, and Abu al-Layth preferred Muhammad's view for ease (rifqan bi al-nas). This became the mufta bihi.
Tarjih authority: al-Sadr al-Shahid; see al-Tashih.
4

The mufta bihi distinguishes sale vs. consumption: if sold, return remainder without compensation for sold part; if eaten, return remainder and claim compensation for the eaten part.
From Jami' al-Fusulayn and others.

The objection is raised that general texts say 'return all or keep all'. The reply: those texts assume the entire commodity is still owned by the buyer; or they reflect a non-Muhammadan view. In our scenario, part is disposed of, so the specific ruling applies.
