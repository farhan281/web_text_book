Liability When the Pledgee Lends the Pledge Back to the Pledgor
Ruling that the pledgee's liability ceases when the pledge is lent back to the pledgor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when the pledgee lends the pledge to the pledgor and explain why liability ceases upon the pledgor's taking possession.

Core Idea
Lending the pledge back to the owner nullifies the pledgee's liability because possession, the cause of liability, returns to the owner.

In this passage, we analyze a key scenario in the law of pledge (rahn): what happens when the pledgee voluntarily returns the pledged item to the pledgor as a loan (i'arah)? The ruling is clear: upon the pledgor's taking possession, the pledgee's liability (daman) ends. This is because liability is tied to possession; once the owner repossesses the item, the risk reverts to him. The passage also addresses the consequences of the pledgor's death while holding the pledge as a loan, affirming the pledgee's priority over other creditors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the pledgee lending the pledged item to the pledgor. The ruling is that upon the pledgor's taking possession, the pledge leaves the liability of the pledgee.
2
Locate the cause (illah) for liability
In Hanafi law, the pledgee's liability for loss of the pledge stems from his possession (qabd). The text states: 'because by his loan and his taking possession, the possession that necessitates liability is annulled.'
3
Explain how returning possession annuls the cause
When the pledgor retakes possession, the cause of liability (the pledgee's possession) is nullified. Therefore, if the pledge perishes in the pledgor's hand, no one owes anything, as it is destroyed in the hand of its owner.
4
Apply to the case of the pledgor's death
If the pledgor dies while the pledge is in his hand as a loan, the pledgee retains priority over other creditors. This shows the pledgee's right of detention is not lost by the temporary return.
The right to reclaim the pledge remains.

Sale of the Pledge Upon the Death of the Pledgor
Ruling on sale of pledge after pledgor's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who sells the pledge after the pledgor's death and explain the role of the executor and the judge.

Core Idea
Upon the pledgor's death, the executor sells the pledge to repay the debt; if none, the judge appoints one.

When a pledgor dies, the pledged item does not become ordinary estate to be inherited; it is specifically designated to satisfy the debt. This lesson presents the clear Hanafi ruling: the executor (wasi) is responsible for selling the pledge, and if no executor exists, the judge (qadi) appoints one to ensure the debt is paid. The ruling ensures the pledgee's right is protected and the obligation is fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary agent
Recognize that the executor (wasi) of the deceased pledgor is the one who sells the pledge. The executor stands in the place of the deceased to manage his affairs.
Wasi is typically appointed by the deceased in a will or by the judge if needed.
2
Understand the purpose of the sale
The sale is specifically to repay the debt owed to the pledgee. The proceeds are used to settle the obligation, not distributed to heirs until after debt payment.
This prioritizes the right of the creditor over inheritance.
3
Recognize the backup procedure
If the deceased left no executor, the judge (qadi) appoints one to carry out the sale. The judge has authority to protect the rights of Muslims who cannot act for themselves.
The appointed executor acts under the judge's order.
4
Grasp the legal rationale
The judge's appointment is justified by his general role as overseer of Muslims' rights. The sale is obligatory to fulfill the debt, and the judge ensures it happens.
This ruling is from Hidayah and is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Pledge Liability and Reclamation in Hanafi Fiqh
Rulings on pledge perishing, reclamation, and pledgor's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for a pledge when it perishes in the pledgor's hand, explain the mortgagee's right to reclaim, and describe the procedure upon the pledgor's death.

Core Idea
Liability for the pledge follows possession: no liability in pledgor's hand, liability reverts to mortgagee upon reclamation.

In this lesson, we examine three key rulings from the Book of Pledge: the fate of the pledge if it perishes in the pledgor's possession, the mortgagee's right to reclaim it, and what happens when the pledgor dies. The underlying principle is that liability (daman) is tied to possession. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone dealing with collateral security, as they define who bears the risk of loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage covers: (1) pledge perishes in pledgor's hand, (2) mortgagee reclaims the pledge, (3) pledgor dies and the pledge must be sold.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1: the pledge perishes without any liability. For scenario 2: if the mortgagee takes possession, liability for loss reverts to him. For scenario 3: upon pledgor's death, his executor (or a judge-appointed executor) sells the pledge to pay the debt.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The principle is that liability follows possession. The pledgor is the owner and possesses the pledge as owner, so loss is his own. The mortgagee, when he takes possession, becomes liable because he holds the pledge as security (like an owner in terms of retention).
This is derived from the phrase 'يد مالكه' (hand of its owner) and 'عيد سببه' (the cause returns).
4
Apply to a hypothetical
Consider: A gives a watch as pledge to B for a debt. If the watch is stolen while in A's house, A is not liable to B. If B takes the watch and it is stolen from B, B is liable to A for its value. If A dies, his executor must sell the watch to repay B; if no executor, the judge appoints one.

Witnessing and Mortgage Rules for Fugitive Slave Return
Rulings on witnessing and mortgage for fugitive slave reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi requirement of witnessing when taking a fugitive slave and identify who bears the reward when the slave is mortgaged.

Core Idea
Without witnesses at the time of taking, the returner of a fugitive slave forfeits the reward.

This passage from al-Lubāb fī Sharḥ al-Qudūrī addresses two key points for those returning fugitive slaves: the necessity of having witnesses to prove the intent to return, and the special case where the slave is mortgaged. The Hanafi school, particularly Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, emphasize that witnessing is not merely recommended but essential for claiming the reward. For a mortgaged slave, the reward falls on the mortgagee, not the owner, reflecting the mortgagee's beneficial interest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
First, understand that the passage gives two distinct rulings: (1) the requirement of witnesses for the returner to claim reward, and (2) the liability for reward when the slave is mortgaged falls on the mortgagee.
2
Analyze the witnessing condition
The text states that according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, witnessing (ishhād) at the time of taking is obligatory (ḥatm). Without it, the returner gets no reward because the lack of witnessing indicates he may have taken the slave for himself.
This is an intra-Hanafi position; Abu Yusuf's view is not mentioned here.
3
Examine the consequence of omission
If the returner omits witnessing, the reward is completely forfeited. The passage draws an analogy: like a seller who detains the sold item to secure payment—if it perishes, the seller loses the price. Similarly, if the slave perishes in the returner's custody, the reward drops.
The analogy reinforces that the returner's possession is considered as securing the reward.
4
Apply the mortgage rule
When the fugitive slave is mortgaged (rahn), the reward is due from the mortgagee, not the owner. This is because the mortgagee holds the beneficial rights and the slave is in his custody by virtue of the debt.
The ruling reflects the principle that liability follows possession and benefit.
5
Integrate the two rulings
Note that the witnessing requirement applies regardless of whether the slave is mortgaged. For a mortgaged slave, the returner must still have witnesses to claim the reward from the mortgagee.
The passage does not explicitly say this, but it follows logically.
