Hiring a Tailor with Multiple Options: The Limit of Three
Ruling on validity of khiyar in hiring (ijara) with up to three options.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a tailor with alternative wages and apply the qiyas to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Four or more alternatives in hiring invalidate the contract due to excessive uncertainty.

When hiring a tailor, an employer may offer different stitching styles with different wages. The Hanafi school permits this flexibility, but with a limit: up to three alternatives the contract is valid; four or more renders it invalid. This ruling is derived by analogy (qiyas) to the well-known rule in sale of garments where choosing among up to three garments is valid. Understanding this limit helps avoid contracts tainted by gharar (excessive uncertainty).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Case and Ruling
The case: an employer says to a tailor, 'If you stitch this garment in Persian style, you get one dirham; if in Roman style, two dirhams.' The ruling: the contract is valid (jaza), and the tailor earns the agreed wage for whichever style he chooses. The same applies for three options.
Two or three options are explicitly valid.
2
Identify the Limit
If the choice is among four options, the contract is not valid (lam yajuz). The passage explicitly states this is the limit.
Four options constitute excessive gharar.
3
Trace the Proof: Qiyas to Sale
The ruling is based on analogy to sale. If a buyer purchases two or three garments with the option to choose one, the sale is valid. But if the option is among four, it is invalid. The same principle applies in ijarah (hiring).
The asl (original case) is sale of garments with khiyar; the far' (new case) is hiring a tailor.
4
Extract the Illa (Effective Cause)
The illa is the number of alternatives causing uncertainty (gharar). In both sale and ijarah, when the choice exceeds three, the uncertainty becomes excessive (gharar fahish), which invalidates the contract.
The illa is jaliyyah (evident) because it directly causes contractual ambiguity.
5
Apply the Ruling to Similar Contracts
The same rule applies to any hiring contract where the worker has a choice among different tasks with different wages, provided the number of alternatives does not exceed three.
This is an example of tatbiq (application) of the qiyas.

Conditional Wages in Ijarah: Ta'jil vs Ta'liq
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditional wages in ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on conditional wages in ijarah and distinguish between a valid expediting condition (ta'jil) and an invalid suspension (ta'liq).

Core Idea
A condition to expedite is valid; a condition to suspend is not — the difference determines the wage.

This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a hirer can offer different wages for different completion times. The core issue is whether such conditions are valid incentives or prohibited suspensions (ta'liq) that introduce uncertainty into the contract. We will analyze the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and the conditional wages
The contract is ijarah (hiring for sewing). The hirer offers two wages: one dirham for today, half a dirham for tomorrow. Determine which condition is at issue.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's distinction between ta'jil and ta'liq
Abu Hanifa sees 'today' as an expediting condition (ta'jil) that incentivizes early work without suspending the contract. 'Tomorrow' is a true suspension (ta'liq) because it makes the wage contingent on a future event, introducing gharar.
The key is that ta'jil is valid, ta'liq is invalid in contracts of exchange.
3
Note the Sahibayn's opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold both conditions valid. They argue that both are definite and mutually exclusive; whichever time is chosen, the corresponding wage is clear, so no uncertainty exists.
4
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, if the tailor sews tomorrow, he gets ajr al-mithl capped at half a dirham. Under Sahibayn, he gets the full half dirham. The dispute affects the actual wage due.
The text does not give a tarjih (preferred view); both are transmitted in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Choice between two valid contracts is valid; wage uncertainty resolves upon performance.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Occupying beyond the lease term does not automatically require extra rent; the later fatwa distinguishes based on property's profit nature.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a tenant rents a property for one month but stays for two. The Hanafi school provides a layered ruling — first by strict analogy (qiyas), then by the manifest transmission with an option (khiyar), and finally a refined fatwa that distinguishes between residential and commercial properties. Understanding this evolution sharpens your ability to see how Hanafi fiqh balances textual principles with practical realities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the basic ruling
Read the opening: the tenant rents for one month at one dirham, occupies for two. The immediate ruling is that he owes rent for the first month only. Note that this is based on qiyas (analogy) — the contract ended, no new obligation arises.
This is the minimalist position; it protects the tenant but may seem unfair to the landlord.
2
Recognize the manifest transmission (zahir al-riwaya) and the option (khiyar)
The source then states that the manifest transmission is not the strict qiyas but rather that each party has a khiyar during the first night and day of the second month. This means the landlord can demand rent or eviction, and the tenant can leave or pay. This option prevents hardship.

Later authorities introduce a crucial distinction: if the property is prepared for exploitation (i.e., used for rental income), the tenant must pay for the second month; if not, he owes nothing. This reconciles conflicting narrations.

4
Apply the distinction to different property types
Identify properties that are 'prepared for exploitation' — e.g., rental houses, shops, bathhouses — versus personal residences not income-generating. The ruling changes accordingly.
The illa (effective cause) is commercial use; the custom ('urf) of profit-oriented leases triggers the obligation.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The difference is real: in a residential lease, the extra month incurs no extra rent and the landlord may only ask the tenant to leave; in a commercial lease, the tenant must pay the market rate for the extra period.
This demonstrates how Hanafi fiqh adapts to economic realities without abandoning principle.

Workers with No Effect Cannot Retain the Object
Ruling on retention and guarantee for workers like porters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling for workers whose work leaves no trace on the object and distinguish between the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A worker whose work leaves no trace cannot retain the object; if retained and lost, no liability but no wage.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a specific scenario in hire (ijara): what happens when the worker's work does not physically affect the object? Such workers, like porters, have no right to retain the object for their wage. But what if they do retain and the object is lost? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of worker
Determine whether the worker's labour leaves a physical effect on the object. Examples from the text: porter, boatman, washer of garments (purification, not improvement).
Tailoring leaves a trace; portage does not.
2
State the basic rule
Such a worker has no right to retain the object to secure payment of the wage. Retention is not permitted as a means to enforce payment.
3
Analyze the scenario of retention and loss
If the worker retains the object and it perishes, what is the ruling? The answer differs between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
4
Present Imam Abu Hanifa's position
Imam holds that the worker is not liable because retention is not a transgression (ta'addi); the object remains a trust (amanah). However, the worker forfeits his wage because the object perished before delivery.
No liability, no wage.
5
Present Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn hold the worker liable, giving the hirer a choice: either claim the value of the object unworked (with no wage) or the value after work (with wage).
Liability with hirer's option.

Commentary

Subcontracting in Ijarah with General Authorization
Ruling on the hirer delegating work to others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on subcontracting in ijarah and identify the condition (general vs restricted authorization) that determines its permissibility.

Core Idea
Unrestricted authorization allows delegation; the work is a debt-like liability, not tied to the artisan's person.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from al-Quduri's Matn on ijarah: when may an artisan hire someone else to do the work he was contracted to perform? The answer hinges on whether the hirer gave unrestricted authorization (itlaq) or required personal performance. The commentary analogizes the obligation to a debt (dayn) that can be fulfilled by the debtor or his agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is the artisan subcontracting the work to another person. The ruling is permissibility (ja'iz/mubah) under the condition of general authorization.
If the contract restricts personal performance, subcontracting is not allowed.
2
Analyze the Condition: General vs Restricted Authorization
If the hirer says 'do this work' without specifying that the artisan must do it personally, that is itlak (unrestricted). If the hirer says 'you do it yourself,' then subcontracting is prohibited because the hirer trusted the artisan's skill.
3
Understand the Proof (Dalil)
The work becomes an obligation in the artisan's dhimma (liability), similar to a debt. A debt can be repaid by the debtor or by someone he deputizes. Qiyas to debt repayment supports permissibility.
This qiyas is reinforced by 'urf (custom), as artisans commonly employ assistants.
4
Apply to a Scenario
Given a concrete case – e.g., a plumber hired without restriction – determine whether subcontracting is valid based on the condition of authorization.
If the plumber was told 'fix the pipes,' he may send his apprentice; if told 'fix it yourself,' he may not.

Disputes Between Hirer and Hired: Description, Wage, and Free vs. Paid Work
Hanafi rules for ijara disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi rule for disputes over work description or wage, and EXPLAIN the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on free vs. paid work.

Core Idea
In ijara disputes, the customer's word with oath prevails; the artisan becomes liable for unauthorized disposal.

This lesson covers two common disputes in hire contracts (ijara): disagreement over the description or wage of the work, and disagreement over whether the work was done for free or for a wage. The Hanafi school applies the principle 'al-qawl qawl al-munkir' (the denier's word is taken) but with nuances: the customer must swear an oath, and the artisan may become liable for the property. A key intra-Hanafi difference arises in the second scenario, where Abu Yusuf conditions the ruling on the customer being a regular (harif).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenarios
First, locate the two disputes: (1) disagreement over description (e.g., qaba vs. shirt) or wage amount, and (2) disagreement over whether work was free or paid. Understand the parties involved and the conflicting claims.
The second scenario is cut off in this passage; Abu Yusuf's full position may require additional sources.
2
Apply the principle al-qawl qawl al-munkir
For each dispute, determine who is the denier and who is the claimant. In scenario 1, the customer denies ordering that description or wage. In scenario 2, the customer denies liability for wage. The denier's word is taken with oath.
The customer must swear because admitting the claim would incur liability.
3
Determine the consequence after the customer's oath
If the customer swears, the artisan becomes damin (liable) because he disposed of the cloth without proper permission. The customer has a choice: either claim compensation for the cloth or take the garment and pay the equivalent wage.
This choice balances the artisan's labor against the customer's property rights.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf in scenario 2
Abu Hanifa holds that the customer's word is taken unconditionally. Abu Yusuf adds a condition: the customer must be a harif (regular customer). The passage cuts off, but the implication is that Abu Yusuf may give weight to customary dealing.
The mufta bihi is likely Abu Hanifa's view, as it aligns with the general principle.
5
Summarize the thamarah (practical outcome)
In practice, under the Hanafi school, the customer's sworn statement determines the ruling in both types of disputes, unless the artisan can prove otherwise. The customer's choice after oath provides a flexible remedy.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Hire Disputes: Whose Word Prevails?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimonial weight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on hire disputes (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and identify the mufta bihi position based on later tarjih.

Core Idea
In hire disputes, Abu Hanifa favors the owner as denier; the Sahibayn introduced exceptions based on custom and public reputation; later fatwa follows Muhammad's view.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a practical dispute between a cloth owner and a tailor over the existence and amount of hire. The Hanafi school presents three different views on whose statement is accepted with an oath. Understanding this ikhtilaf requires grasping the principles of qawlu al-munkir and shahadat al-zahir, and the role of istihsan in overriding strict analogy. The lesson will walk through the rulings and the eventual tarjih that settled the fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenarios
The passage covers two disputes: (1) the amount of hire (owner says 10, tailor says 20) and (2) whether work was for hire or gratis. Both fall under the same legal analysis.
2
State Abu Hanifa's default rule
Abu Hanifa holds that in both scenarios, the owner's statement is taken with his oath because he is the denier (munkir) and permission originates from him.
This is based on the principle 'al-qawl li al-munkir'.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's exception
Abu Yusuf introduces a condition: if the owner is a regular customer (harif) of the tailor, the tailor's statement is accepted because prior dealing indicates a hire agreement.
This is an istihsan based on custom.
4
Analyze Muhammad's exception
Muhammad rules that if the tailor is publicly known to work for hire (a professional), his statement is accepted with his oath, relying on the evidence of apparent circumstances (shahadat al-zahir).
This istihsan considers the public reputation of the craftsman.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later authorities like Khwahar Zadah gave fatwa on Muhammad's view, making it the operative position despite Abu Hanifa's qiyas being supported by earlier scholars.
See al-Durr and al-Jawhara for confirmation.

Craftsman's Wage Claim: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on burden of proof for craftsman's wage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a craftsman's wage claim, and identify the mufta bihi position(s) according to later Hanafi scholarship.

Core Idea
A craftsman's entitlement to wages depends on either prior dealings or public reputation for hired work.

This passage from the Book of Ijara addresses a common legal problem: a craftsman claims he worked for hire, but the owner denies. Within the Hanafi school, there is a dispute on how to resolve the burden of proof. Imam Abu Hanifa relies on custom and prior dealings, while Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani considers the craftsman's public reputation as apparent evidence (shahadat al-zahir). Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi principles of evidence and custom in commercial disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
The scenario: a craftsman (e.g., tailor) claims he performed work for an owner and demands wages, but the owner denies commissioning the work or denies it was for hire. There is no direct evidence.
The craftsman is the plaintiff, the owner is the defendant.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds: if the craftsman is a regular customer (harif) of the owner – meaning they have prior dealings – he gets his wage because their custom establishes that he works for hire. If not, he gets nothing. The proof is custom ('urf) and the principle that prior dealings specify the direction of hire.
This is the default rule stated first in the text.
3
State Muhammad al-Shaybani's position and its basis
Muhammad holds: if the craftsman is publicly known in his trade as working for hire, and his circumstances indicate that, then his word is accepted with an oath, acting on shahadat al-zahir (apparent evidence). The craftsman's reputation serves as circumstantial evidence that the work was for hire.
Abu Yusuf likely agrees with Muhammad on this point (Sahibayn).
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, a craftsman without prior dealings gets no wage; he cannot even sue. Under Muhammad's view, a craftsman with a public reputation for hired work can claim wages upon oath. The practical outcome depends on how 'prior dealings' or 'public reputation' is assessed by the qadi.
The difference is in the burden of proof and the weight given to custom versus public reputation.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preponderance) in later scholarship
The author of al-Tashih preferred Abu Hanifa's proof and answered the Sahibayn's proofs. Many later scholars (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah) relied on this tarjih. However, Khwahar Zadeh gave fatwa on Muhammad's view. Thus, there is divergence in the mufta bihi position.
You should be aware of both views and which authority each follows.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In a void lease, fair wage is due, capped at the named wage if known, uncapped if unknown or illegal.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies the mandatory compensation in a void lease (ijara fasida). The Hanafi school differentiates between defects that preserve a known named wage and those that eliminate any reference. Understanding this distinction ensures correct payment for benefit received.

Identify the cause of invalidity
First, determine what makes the lease void. Is it a corrupt condition, ambiguity, ignorance of the wage, no wage, or an illegal subject matter?

Commentary
This passage discusses when an ijarah (lease/hire) contract can be terminated due to valid excuses that cause undue hardship. The text provides two examples: (1) A person rents a shop to conduct business, then loses all his money, making it impossible to continue; (2) A person leases out property, then becomes insolvent and cannot pay debts except by selling the leased property. In such cases, the judge (qadi) can rescind the contract so the property can be sold to satisfy debts. The commentary explains that the harm must be extra, not anticipated in the original contract. The passage then highlights an internal Hanafi dispute: One narration from al-Ziyadat says the termination requires a qadi's decree, while al-Jami' al-Saghir states that the ijarah is automatically rescinded upon the excuse without need for a judge. A reconciliation is offered: if the excuse is obvious and undeniable, no qadi is needed; if not, a qadi must decide. The text cuts off before completing the reconciliation, but the differing positions are clear.

Excuses Allowing Termination of Ijarah Contracts
Excuses for rescinding ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the excuses that permit rescission of an ijarah contract according to the Hanafi school and state whether a qadi's ruling is required based on the nature of the excuse.

Core Idea
An ijarah contract can be rescinded if an unforeseen harm makes continuation oppressive; apparent excuses need no judge, hidden ones do.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi rules on when a lease or hire contract (ijarah) may be cancelled due to supervening excuses. The source text from al-Lubab gives concrete examples and highlights an important intra-madhhab debate: does rescission always require a qadi's ruling, or does it occur automatically? The harmonized answer depends on whether the excuse is apparent or hidden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two examples of excuses
List the two excuses explicitly mentioned in the text: loss of capital by the lessee and insolvency of the lessor with debts that can only be paid from the leased property.
These are not exhaustive; they illustrate the principle of unforeseen harm.
2
Understand the rationale
The excuse must cause a harm that was not stipulated in the contract and makes continuation excessively burdensome.
The harm must be unanticipated; mere change of mind does not suffice.
3
Note the textual dispute
Al-Ziyadat states 'the qadi rescinds' indicating need for qadi; al-Jami' al-Saghir says the contract is 'annulled' implying automatic rescission.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf that requires reconciliation.
4
Apply the harmonization
If the excuse is apparent (like lost capital), no qadi needed; if hidden (like debt), qadi's ruling is required.
Apparent means easily verifiable by any observer; hidden means it requires investigation or admission.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The harmonized view is relied upon as presented in al-Hidayah, which balances the two riwayat.
This is the operative position for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the excuses that allow a party to rescind an ijarah (leasing) contract.
