Definition of Mudarabah (Qirad) – Linguistic and Legal Meaning
Definition and linguistic origin of mudarabah contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mudarabah, identify its linguistic origin, and distinguish the roles of the capital provider and the worker.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is a profit-sharing partnership with capital from one and labor from the other.

This lesson introduces the mudarabah contract, a foundational partnership form in Hanafi commercial law. The passage from al-Quduri defines it both linguistically and legally, placing it after the chapter on partnership (shirkah) due to their close connection. Mastering this definition is essential for understanding all later rulings on mudarabah, including its conditions and permissible variations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract's placement
Observe that the author places mudarabah immediately after shirkah (partnership) because mudarabah is a specific type of partnership. This placement signals that many rulings of shirkah apply to mudarabah unless specified otherwise.
In Hanafi texts, chapter ordering often reflects conceptual relationships.
2
Analyze the linguistic derivation
The term 'mudarabah' is derived from 'al-darb fi al-ard' (striking/traveling in the land). This emphasizes that the worker (mudarib) typically travels for trade, and through his effort he becomes entitled to a share of the profit.
The linguistic connection highlights the active role of the worker.
3
Extract the legal definition
The legal definition is: 'a contract (with offer and acceptance) for partnership in profit, with capital from one of the two partners and labor from the other.' Note that the partnership is specifically in profit, not in loss – loss is borne solely by the capital owner.
The definition specifies 'partnership in profit' to clarify that loss is not shared.
4
Identify the essential elements
From the definition, the core elements are: (1) offer and acceptance, (2) one party providing capital (rabb al-mal), (3) the other providing labor (mudarib), (4) mutual agreement on profit shares.
These elements must be present for a valid mudarabah contract.

Definition and Legal Basis of Mudarabah
Defines mudarabah and its legal basis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of mudarabah, identify its essential elements, and distinguish it from bida'ah and qard.

Core Idea
Mudarabah requires capital from one, work from the other, and shared profit; otherwise the contract changes.

Mudarabah is a foundational contract in Islamic finance. This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri defines it precisely and explains why it is legislated. Understanding its essential elements and how it differs from similar arrangements like bida'ah and qard is crucial for correct application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definition
Read the definition carefully: mudarabah is derived from 'darb fi al-ard' (travel for trade). Legally, it is a contract of partnership with capital from one and work from the other, with profit shared. Note the requirement of offer and acceptance.
The phrase 'and work from the other' is textually confirmed in most editions.
2
Distinguish from bida'ah and qard
The passage explains that if profit is all for the capital owner, it becomes bida'ah (voluntary agency). If all profit goes to the worker, it becomes qard (loan). Recognize that mudarabah requires profit sharing.
If both contribute capital, it is a regular shirkah (partnership).
3
Understand the rationale for legislation
Mudarabah is legislated because of societal need: some have capital but lack skill, others have skill but lack capital. It benefits both parties and the wider economy.
This rationale is based on public interest (maslahah).
4
Analyze the primary evidence (dalil)
The evidence cited is the Prophet's tacit approval (taqrir). He found people practicing mudarabah and confirmed it. In Hanafi usul, taqrir is a form of Sunnah that indicates permissibility (ibahah).
The dalil is ahad in transmission (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is decisive (qat'i al-dalalah).
5
Apply the ruling to new scenarios
Use the definition and distinctions to classify any given arrangement as mudarabah, bida'ah, qard, or shirkah. Ensure the profit-sharing condition is explicitly stated.
This step builds practical fiqh literacy.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri opens the Book of Mudarabah (Qirad) with a definition and legal basis. The author first defines mudarabah as a contract of partnership where one party provides capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides work (mudarib), with profits shared between them according to agreement. He notes that the book's arrangement places mudarabah after shirkah (partnership) because it includes some elements of partnership. Linguistically, 'mudarabah' is derived from 'darb fi al-ard' (striking/traveling in the land for trade), as the worker typically travels for business. Legally, it is a contract formed by offer and acceptance. The author clarifies a textual variant in al-Quduri's matn: the phrase 'and work from the other' is included in most editions and commentaries, and its inclusion completes the definition. A crucial point follows: if the profit is entirely assigned to the capital owner, the arrangement becomes bida'ah (ibda'), where the worker is a volunteer agent with no share. Conversely, if all profit goes to the worker, it becomes a loan (qard). True mudarabah requires profit sharing between both parties. If both contribute capital, it becomes a regular partnership (shirkah). The passage then provides the rationale for mudarabah's legislation: it serves a societal need, connecting those with capital but lacking business acumen with skilled workers who lack capital. This is supported by a hadith: the Prophet (peace be upon him) found people practicing mudarabah and confirmed them in it, making it a Sunnah taqrir (tacit approval) evidence. Thus, mudarabah is a valid, legislated contract in the Hanafi school.

المضاربة (al-mudarabah): A contract of partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides work, with profit shared between them.
البضاعة (al-bida'ah): An arrangement where capital is given to a worker to trade, but all profit goes to the capital owner; the worker is a volunteer agent (wakeel mutabarri').
القرض (al-qard): A loan where the worker (mudarib) receives all profit; this is not mudarabah but a loan contract.

Commentary
This passage introduces the book of Mudarabah (silent partnership). It first states that mudarabah is valid only when there is capital from one party and work from the other; otherwise it becomes something else. If the entire profit is stipulated for the capital owner, it is called bida'a (commodity placement) and is not a mudarabah; the worker is merely a volunteer agent. If the entire profit is for the worker, it is considered a loan (qard) of the capital, not a partnership. If both parties provide capital, it becomes a partnership (shirkah). Then the passage explains the wisdom behind the legislation: to benefit both the wealthy who lack business skills and the skilled poor who lack capital.

قرض (qard): A loan of capital where the worker gets all profit; the worker is a borrower and must return the capital.
شركة (shirkah): A partnership where both parties contribute capital; distinct from mudarabah where only one contributes capital.

Evidence for Mudarabah: Prophetic Practice and Hadith Analysis
Evidence for mudarabah from Prophetic practice and response to hadith criticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school uses the Prophet's practice (taqrir) and partnership hadith as evidence for the permissibility of mudarabah, and evaluate the response to criticisms of the hadith's authenticity.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is permissible because the Prophet confirmed the practice and participated in partnership, even if the specific hadith chain is disputed.

This lesson delves into the primary evidence that the Hanafi school presents for the validity of mudarabah (profit-sharing partnership). You will examine a hadith about the Prophet's partnership with al-Sa'ib, along with scholarly critiques of its chain and Ibn al-Humam's robust defense. By the end, you will appreciate how Hanafis rely on multiple strands of evidence—including the Prophet's tacit approval (taqrir)—to establish a ruling even when individual narrations are questioned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evidence
The author first mentions that the Prophet was sent while people practiced mudarabah and he confirmed them (taqrir). Then he cites a specific hadith about the Prophet's partnership with al-Sa'ib. These are two distinct proofs.
Taqrir is a form of sunnah (sunnah taqririyyah) accepted by all schools.
2
Examine the hadith
Read the hadith: the Prophet says 'Welcome to my brother and partner; he was neither difficult nor contentious.' This directly shows the Prophet engaged in partnership.
Multiple narrations exist (Ahmad, Abu Dawud, Ibn Majah).
3
Understand the criticism
Al-Mundhiri, al-Suhayli, and Ibn Abd al-Barr note that the hadith has significant irregularity (idhtirab) in its chain and text, making it weak as a proof.
The criticism focuses on the chain's reliability, not the meaning.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Ibn al-Humam argues that the weakness only affects identifying the specific partner, not establishing the fact of the Prophet's participation. The fact is also supported by the practice of people and the Prophet's confirmation.
This shows the Hanafi method of cumulative evidence.
5
Confirm the ruling
Based on the combination of taqrir, the hadith (even with its chain issues), and consensus, the Hanafi school rules mudarabah as permissible (mubah).
The ruling is established in the zahir al-riwayah.

All conditions aim to maintain the fundamental character of mudarabah as a profit-sharing contract where risk and reward are shared proportionally.
Any deviation that introduces a fixed return or owner control invalidates the contract.

Commentary
This passage details the conditions for a valid mudarabah contract, expanding on the matn of al-Quduri. The author lists several conditions: First, the capital must be of the type that is valid for partnership, i.e., existing, known, and fungible currency (gold, silver, or their equivalents). Second, the profit share must be a common fraction (musha' – e.g., half, third), not a fixed sum of dirhams, because a fixed sum would destroy the partnership element and make it a loan. Third, the capital must be physically handed over to the mudarib so he can trade with it. Fourth, the capital owner must not have a hand in the work, meaning he cannot stipulate that he will also work, as that would interfere with the mudarib's exclusive control. The text also discusses special cases: if the owner gives merchandise and asks the mudarib to sell it first and then use the proceeds as mudarabah capital, it is valid because the contract admits addition (idafah) – it combines agency for selling and mudarabah for trading. Similarly, instructing the mudarib to collect a debt and then use it is valid. However, if the mudarib already owes the owner a debt, instructing him to work with that debt is invalid because the debt is not tangible capital in hand. The phrase 'it is agency and hire' (tawkīl wa ijāra) in some manuscripts of al-Hidayah indicates the dual nature of the contract, though the copy of al-Lubab omits 'wa ijāra'. The fourth condition is emphasized: the owner's involvement prevents the mudarib's complete control (khulūṣ yad) over the capital.

The Hanafi school defines specific prohibitions: buying certain close relatives of the capital owner, or trading in unspecified goods or locations. The same applies if he returns only part of the capital, as the part is treated like the whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a mudarib purchases a relative and the value increases, and explain why the mudarib is not liable for the increase.

Core Idea
When a mudarib's purchase of a relative increases in value, his share is emancipated; he is not liable—the emancipated slave must compensate the investor.

This lesson addresses a specific ruling in mudaraba: what happens when the investment agent (mudarib) uses the capital to buy a slave who is his relative, and the slave's value later increases. The Hanafi school holds that the mudarib's ownership of a relative triggers automatic emancipation of his share, and he bears no liability for the value increase because it occurred without his action. However, the investor's share remains encumbered, and the emancipated slave must work to pay it off. We will examine the reasoning (ta'lil) based on analogy to inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The mudarib uses mudaraba capital to purchase a slave who is his relative (e.g., father, brother). The purchase is valid and creates partial ownership for the mudarib and the investor.

Understand the two consequences
First, the mudarib's share in the slave is automatically emancipated because he owns part of his relative. Second, the mudarib is not liable for any increase in the slave's value.
Emancipation (itq) is a direct legal effect of owning a relative.

Examine the reasoning for non-liability
The increase occurred without any action (la sun') from the mudarib—it is a market rise, not a profit from his effort. Therefore, it is not subject to guarantee.

The investor still owns his share of the slave's value. The emancipated slave must work (yas'a) to earn the value of that share and pay it to the investor.
This is similar to mukataba (contract of manumission) where a slave buys his freedom.
5
Note the analogy to inheritance
The text explicitly compares the increase to inherited property: just as an heir is not liable for an increase in inherited property because it comes from outside his effort, so the mudarib is not liable here.
This analogy (qiyas) is the proof (dalil) for the ruling.
