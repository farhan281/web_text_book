Disputed Coin Quality in Acknowledgment: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Ruling on disputed coin quality in acknowledgment of debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute regarding the acknowledgment of counterfeit coins and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
An absolute acknowledgment of debt implies sound coins; claiming a defect is a retraction, but timing matters for Sahibayn.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a debtor's claim that the coins are counterfeit is credible. Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn differ based on the timing of the claim. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school values certainty in contractual obligations and how later scholars resolved the ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Identify the acknowledgment: debtor says 'I owe you 1000 dirhams from the price of goods' and adds 'but they are counterfeit'. The creditor claims they are good coins.
This is the core fact pattern for the dispute.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's ruling and reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules the debtor must pay good coins. Reason: the absolute contract (mutlaq al-'aqd) implies freedom from defect; claiming defect is a retraction of part of the acknowledgment. He analogizes to a seller who claims the sold item is defective after the buyer says it was sound.
The analogy (qiyas) to a sale dispute is key.
3
Contrast with Sahibayn's view and their criterion
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani distinguish based on timing: if the claim of counterfeit is made in the same utterance (mawsulan), the debtor is believed; if later (mufsalan), he is not. They give weight to the connectedness of the statement.
Sahibayn do not reject the analogy entirely; they qualify it.
4
Identify the mufta bihi and the authorities who endorsed it
Later scholars (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili) relied on Abu Hanifa's view. The mufta bihi is that the debtor must pay good coins regardless of timing.
Al-Tashih confirms this tarjih.
5
Apply the distinction to a practical example
Example: A says 'I owe you 1000 dirhams from the price of goods, and they are counterfeit' (connected). Under Sahibayn, A is believed and owes only counterfeit value; under Abu Hanifa, A owes good coins. If the claim is made later (disconnected), both agree A is not believed.
The practical difference is clearest in the connected scenario.

Surplus After Paying Health Debts Goes to Illness Acknowledgment
Explains the priority of health debts over illness acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a sick person's acknowledgment is valid but subordinate to prior health debts, and apply the condition that surplus after paying health debts goes to fulfill the illness acknowledgments.

Core Idea
A sick person's acknowledgment is valid but health debts take priority; only the surplus goes to illness declarations.

This passage from Sharḥ al-Qudūrī addresses a common concern: what happens when a person in death illness acknowledges debts to some people but also has pre-existing debts from his health? The text clarifies that the health debts are paid first; any remaining estate is then used to fulfill the illness acknowledgments. If there are no health debts, the acknowledgment is immediately effective, and the acknowledged creditor takes precedence over the heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) a sick person has both health debts and illness acknowledgments, and (2) a sick person has only illness acknowledgments with no health debts.
The ruling differs based on the presence of health debts.
2
Analyze the first scenario (health debts exist)
Start by noting that health debts must be paid in full. If any surplus remains after paying all health debts, that surplus is used to satisfy the acknowledgments made during illness. The acknowledgment is valid in itself, but its effect was suspended due to the prior rights of health creditors.
The creditor from illness is second in line.
3
Analyze the second scenario (no health debts)
When there are no pre-existing debts from health, the sick person's acknowledgment is valid outright, and the acknowledged creditor has priority over the heirs in receiving that share of the estate.
This is because paying debts is a primary need, and heirs only inherit after debts are cleared.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
The text explains that an acknowledgment is valid in its essence. However, it was rejected in favor of health creditors because their rights are prior. Once health creditors are satisfied, the acknowledgment's validity reappears. Similarly, if no health debts exist, there is no conflict, so the acknowledgment takes effect immediately.
This reasoning aligns with the Hanafi principle that debts take precedence over inheritance.

Sick Person's Acknowledgment to Heir: Invalid Unless Confirmed
States rule that a sick person's acknowledgment to a heir is invalid unless other heirs confirm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sick person's acknowledgment to an heir and explain the role of other heirs' confirmation.

Core Idea
A sick person's acknowledgment to an heir is invalid unless other heirs confirm — their attached rights are the impediment.

When a person is in their death illness, their property becomes tied to the rights of all heirs. An acknowledgment to one heir would unfairly favor that heir. This lesson explains the invalidity and the exception when other heirs confirm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a sick person acknowledging (iqrar) a debt or item to an heir. The ruling is that it is invalid (batil).
This applies to acknowledgment during marad al-mawt (death illness).
2
Understand the reason (illah)
The reason is the attachment (ta'alluq) of the rights of all heirs to the sick person's property during death illness. Singling out one heir nullifies the rights of others.
This is similar to the prohibition of favoring some creditors over others.
3
Recognize the exception
If the remaining heirs confirm (tasdiq) that the acknowledgment is true, it becomes valid. Confirmation lifts the impediment.
Confirmation implies the heirs waive their right to object.
4
Connect to practical application
The ruling protects the equitable distribution of the estate. Confirmation is the only way to validate such an acknowledgment.
The confirmation must be from all other heirs who have a share.

Preferential Payment of Creditors in Death Illness
States rule that a sick person cannot preferentially pay some creditors except for debts incurred during illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sick person preferentially paying some creditors over others and identify the exceptions for debts incurred during illness.

Core Idea
A sick person cannot pay one creditor over another; but debts incurred during illness are treated differently.

During death illness, the estate is meant to be distributed equitably among all creditors and heirs. Paying one creditor preferentially wrongs others. However, debts related to the illness itself are an exception because they benefit the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a sick person paying the debt of some creditors but not others. The ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuz); it is prohibited.
This applies to death illness (marad al-mawt).
2
Understand the reason (illah)
Favoring some creditors nullifies the right of other creditors to equal treatment. The estate should be used to satisfy all debts proportionally.
This is similar to the rule about acknowledgment to heirs.
3
Recognize the exceptions
It is permissible to pay a debt that was borrowed during the illness, or to pay the price of an item purchased during the illness. These are new obligations incurred for the sick person's needs.
These exceptions show that debts arising during the illness are considered separate from pre-existing debts.
4
Apply the rule
If a sick person pays a pre-existing debt to one creditor exclusively, the other creditors can challenge the payment after death. The payment may be adjusted.
The ruling protects the principle of equal treatment among creditors of the same rank.

Order of Pre-emptors in Shuf'ah
Defines shuf'ah and establishes priority order with cascade

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of pre-emptors in shuf'ah and apply the cascade when a prior pre-emptor waives.

Core Idea
Strongest connection gives strongest right: partner in property first, then partner in rights, then neighbor.

This lesson explains the legal order of pre-emptors (shuf'ah) in Hanafi fiqh. The passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri defines shuf'ah and then establishes a cascade based on the strength of connection to the property. Understanding this order is crucial for any real estate transaction involving joint ownership or adjacent rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is shuf'ah and what is its hukm? The passage defines shuf'ah as taking ownership of real estate compulsorily from a buyer, and it is wajib (obligatory) for the most entitled pre-emptor, but it is a right that can be waived.
Wajib here means the pre-emptor has the right to claim; it is not sinful to not claim.
2
Locate the evidence
The author provides a rational proof (dalil aqli) for the order: because the connection through partnership (ittisaal bil-shirkah) is stronger. The partner in the property has connection in every part, so he is strongest.
The proof is not scripture but a rational legal argument (ta'lil).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause ('illa) for prioritization is the strength of connection. The partner in the property is connected to every atom of the property, so his right is strongest. The partner in rights (water/path) is connected only to that benefit, so he is next. The neighbor has the weakest connection (mere adjacency), hence last.
This is a qiyas-like reasoning on the order.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i does not grant shuf'ah to the neighbor; only to partners. Additionally, within the Hanafi school, the condition that water/path must be private is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad; Abu Yusuf may have differed.
The text explicitly states the condition is from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, implying Abu Yusuf's view is not relied upon here.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) Hanafi position is that shuf'ah follows this order with the condition that water and path must be private. The tarjih authority is al-Hidayah (and al-Maydani follows it).
This is the ruling for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri opens the Book of Pre-emption (Kitab al-Shuf'ah). It first defines shuf'ah linguistically as 'joining' and legally as a right to compulsorily take ownership of real estate from the buyer, compensating him for his purchase price. The author then presents the order of pre-emptors: the first right belongs to the khaleet (partner) in the very property sold. This condition is specifically attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani (Abu Yusuf may have a different opinion, but it is not elaborated here). The overall ruling is that shuf'ah is wajib (obligatory) but it is a right that can be waived.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: pre-emption right never lapses; Muhammad: lapses after one month.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does the pre-emptor's right to reclaim a sold share expire if he delays? Practical consequence: if a pre-emptor delays over a month, his claim is valid per Abu Hanifa but invalid per Muhammad. The later fatwa (mufta bihi) is on Muhammad's view, as cited by many later jurists (Qadikhan, al-Dhakhira, etc.).

The existence of this cause is sufficient, regardless of divisibility.

Shuf'ah protects co-owners from the imposition of an unwanted partner by allowing them to purchase the sold share.
This ruling applies even if the property is small or indivisible.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is wajib in real estate to prevent neighbor harm, but its scope may shift with time—the operative fatwa can restrict it.

Pre-emption is a unique right in Islamic law that allows a co-owner or neighbor to step into a sale and acquire a property share to prevent future harm. Hanafi jurists have carefully delineated when this right applies. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies that shuf'ah is obligatory in real estate—even indivisible property—but not in movable goods, ships, or buildings sold without land. It also reveals an important internal debate: while the classical position is broad, later fatwa restricts shuf'ah to cases where the buyer intends harm, reflecting the principle that rulings can change with time.

The upper floor partakes of the land's permanence.

The text states: 'al-hikmatu daf'u darari su'i al-jiwar' (the wisdom is removing the harm of bad neighbors). This wisdom applies to both co-owners and neighbors. It explains why shuf'ah is only in real estate: permanence of residence creates ongoing neighbor relations, while movables do not.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Shuf'ah in Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri presents the foundational rulings on what types of property incur the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The author begins by stating the general principle: shuf'ah is wajib (obligatory) in all real estate (al-'iqar), even if the property is indivisible, such as a bathhouse, mill, well, or small houses. The reason ('illa) for shuf'ah is the connection in ownership (al-ittisal fi al-milk) and the wisdom is to prevent the harm that may arise from a new neighbor. The text then notes an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the operative fatwa. While the apparent riwaya (Zahir al-Riwayah) makes shuf'ah broadly applicable, later scholars—citing the work Al-Jami' al-Khani—stated that the fatwa today is on the saying of Imam Muhammad, which restricts shuf'ah to cases where the buyer intends to cause harm. This is due to changing social conditions. However, al-Husam al-Shahid had said the fatwa was on the saying of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). The commentator reconciles by suggesting that the fatwa has reverted to Imam Muhammad's view. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that fatwa can change with time (taghayyur al-zaman). The text then clarifies that shuf'ah applies to real estate and what is in its ruling, such as an upper floor (al-'ulu) which, even if it does not have a path in the lower floor, is considered real estate due to its right of stability (haqq al-qarar). Conversely, no shuf'ah applies to movable property (al-manqul), such as goods ('urud) and ships, because movable property lacks the permanence that real estate enjoys. The wisdom (hikmah) of removing neighbor's harm requires continuity, which moveable property does not have. Additionally, no shuf'ah applies to buildings or palm trees when sold separately from the land (al-'arsa), because these lack their own stability and are considered movable. This contrasts with an upper floor, which, if sold separately, still entitles the buyer to shuf'ah in the lower floor if there is no separate path, because the upper floor partakes of the stability of the land. The text concludes that this ruling is found in Al-Asl (of Imam Muhammad).

General Rulings on Shuf'a: Equality and Condition of Monetary Consideration
Ruling and reasoning for equality and condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two main rulings on shuf'a from Al-Quduri: (1) equality of Muslim and dhimmi in entitlement, and (2) the condition that the property must be acquired with monetary consideration (iwad mal), and explain the legal reasoning (talil) behind each.

Core Idea
Shuf'a is based on cause, not status: Muslim and dhimmi are equal; it only applies when purchase is for monetary wealth.

The Book of Shuf'a opens with two foundational rules that define its scope. First, the right of pre-emption applies equally to Muslims and dhimmis—a clear statement that the ruling follows the cause, not the religious identity. Second, shuf'a is only established when the property is acquired through a monetary consideration. This lesson unpacks these rules and the reasoning behind them, drawing from the matn of Al-Quduri and its commentary in Al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the first two sentences of the passage. The first ruling states that Muslim and dhimmi are equal in shuf'a. The second ruling states that when real estate is owned by a consideration that is wealth (mal), shuf'a is established.
The passage also includes an exception regarding right-of-way, which modifies the second ruling.
2
Analyze the reasoning for equality
The author provides a talil: they are equal in the cause (sabab) and wisdom (hikmah). The sabab for shuf'a is shared ownership or adjacency, and the hikmah is preventing harm caused by new construction. Since both causes are present in Muslims and dhimmis, the ruling is the same.
This is a classic Hanafi use of causal reasoning (talil) to justify a ruling.
3
Examine the condition of monetary consideration
The second ruling requires that the acquisition of the property be through an iwad that is mal (wealth). This condition is necessary so that the pre-emptor can match the price exactly, either in the same currency (form) or in equivalent value.
If the iwad is not mal (e.g., a marriage dowry), shuf'a does not apply.
4
Expand the scope using 'ownership'
The author uses 'ownership' (malik) instead of 'sale' (bay') to include transactions like a gift with a condition of compensation (hiba bi shart al-iwad), which is effectively an exchange of wealth.
This shows the Hanafi method of expanding rulings by focusing on the underlying meaning ('illa') rather than the literal wording.
5
Note the exception
The passage restricts the second ruling by excluding cases where there is a right-of-way. If the property includes a path owned by another, the neighbor is already a partner in the path, and the application of shuf'a may differ.
This exception is a reminder that general rules often have specific exceptions in fiqh.

Exceptions to Shuf'a: Non-Monetary Consideration
Lists exceptions to shuf'a due to non-monetary consideration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six transactions where shuf'a does not apply and explain why the nature of consideration (mal vs. non-mal) determines the right of pre-emption.

Core Idea
Shuf'a only applies when the consideration is wealth (mal); non-monetary consideration negates the right.

The right of shuf'a (pre-emption) arises from a sale, but not every transfer of real estate triggers it. In this passage, the author of Al-Lubab enumerates specific transactions where, despite a house changing hands, shuf'a is not established. The common thread is that the consideration given in exchange is not considered 'wealth' (mal) in the Hanafi legal sense. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying the rules of pre-emption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six excluded transactions
List each of the six cases mentioned: (1) house as dowry in marriage, (2) house as compensation in khul', (3) house as rent payment, (4) house as settlement for intentional killing, (5) house as price for freeing a slave, (6) house as settlement by denial or silence.
Note that all involve a house as the thing transferred, but the countervalue is not money or a standard sale item.
2
Determine the underlying principle
For each transaction, ask: is the consideration (iwad) considered 'mal' (wealth) in Hanafi fiqh? In these cases, it is not: marriage dowry, khul' compensation, and settlement for intentional killing are not financial exchanges; rent is a service; freeing a slave is an act of piety; settlement by denial is to avoid litigation.
The core rule: shuf'a requires a sale where both the subject matter and the price are mal.
3
Analyze the textual correction from Al-Hidayah
The passage notes that in some copies of Al-Mukhtasar, the phrase 'alayha' (upon it) was used, but the correct word is 'anha' (for it). This changes the meaning: the defendant does not admit the house was ever taken; he simply pays to end the dispute.
This correction emphasizes that no transfer of ownership is recognized, so no shuf'a.
4
Distinguish intentional vs. accidental killing
The passage explicitly restricts the exclusion to intentional killing (qatl al-'amd). For accidental killing (qatl al-khata'), the compensation is financial (diya), which is mal, so shuf'a would apply.
This distinction shows that the nature of the consideration (penal vs. compensatory) is decisive.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A settlement with acknowledgment, silence, or denial makes pre-emption wajib; the buyer's own claim binds him.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced scenario: when the pre-emptor and buyer make a settlement (sulh) about the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The ruling is that pre-emption becomes obligatory (wajib) even if the buyer denies the right, because the buyer's conduct in making a settlement effectively admits a right in his own perception. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and clarifies the scope of settlement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key elements

State the central ruling
The matn states: if the buyer settles with acknowledgment, pre-emption becomes wajib. The sharh expands: the same applies if the buyer remains silent or denies the right. The settlement itself triggers the right.
3

The buyer, by entering a settlement, is acting as if the pre-emptor has a right (in his own opinion). He is treated according to his own claim (zann), so the right is established even if he verbally denies it.
This is a unique Hanafi reasoning: a person's actions can create legal consequences based on their own presumption.
4
Note the practical scope
The ruling covers all three scenarios: acknowledgment, silence, and denial. The pre-emptor's right is confirmed by the settlement itself, not by an external proof.
This avoids the need for the pre-emptor to prove his right in court; the settlement acts as a conclusive admission.

Oath of Buyer When Pre-emptor Fails to Bring Price
Ruling on swearing the buyer when the pre-emptor cannot bring the price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the buyer's oath in a shuf'a case when the pre-emptor cannot pay, identify the form of the oath (on certainty), and apply the consequences of the buyer's response.

Core Idea
The buyer's oath in shuf'a is on certainty because it concerns his own act and possession.

In the Hanafi school, when a pre-emptor (shafiʿ) claims a property but cannot produce the sale price, the procedure does not stall. Instead, the buyer (mushtari) is required to swear an oath denying the pre-emptor's claim. This lesson examines the ruling, the form of the oath, and the possible outcomes based on the buyer's response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger condition
The pre-emptor is unable to bring the price. This is the condition that activates the buyer's oath.
Inability can mean lack of funds or failure to produce the amount at the hearing.
2
State the required response
The buyer is sworn by Allah, either denying the purchase or denying that the pre-emptor has any right of shuf'a in the property.
The oath is administered by the judge.
3
Determine the oath form and its reason
The oath is on certainty (ʿala al-banaat), meaning it is absolute and without exception. The reason is that the oath concerns the buyer's own act and what is in his possession originally.
This distinguishes it from oaths about others' actions, which may be on knowledge (ʿala al-ʿilm).
4
List the possible outcomes
If the buyer swears, the claim is dismissed. If he refuses to swear, confesses, or the pre-emptor brings proof, judgment is given for the pre-emptor—provided the buyer does not deny the demand. If the buyer denies the demand (i.e., that the pre-emptor ever claimed), his statement is accepted with his oath.
The 'denial of demand' is a separate defense.

Effect of Price Reduction or Increase on Pre-Emptor
Rulings on price modifications affecting the pre-emptor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for each scenario of price change (partial reduction, full reduction, increase) before the pre-emptor exercises his right and explain the reasoning (talil) behind each ruling.

Core Idea
A partial price reduction benefits the pre-emptor; a full reduction does not; an increase never binds him.

After a sale, the seller and buyer may adjust the price before the pre-emptor takes the property. The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes these adjustments: a partial reduction is treated as part of the original contract, so the pre-emptor benefits; a full reduction is a separate act (ibrāʾ) and does not reduce the pre-emptor's liability; an increase would harm the pre-emptor, so it is not binding. This lesson unpacks the reasoning from Al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the passage and list the three possibilities: (1) seller reduces part of the price, (2) seller reduces the entire price, (3) buyer increases the price.
2
Understand the principle of 'connection to the contract'
The key term is يلتحق بأصل العقد (connected to the original contract). A partial reduction is considered a modification of the contract itself; a full reduction is not.
3
Apply to partial reduction
Since the partial reduction connects to the contract, it appears in favor of the pre-emptor. The pre-emptor pays the reduced price. Even if reduction occurs after taking, he recovers from the buyer.
4
Apply to full reduction
Full reduction (hatt al-kull) is not connected; no price remains. Hence, the pre-emptor must still pay the original price. This is analogous to the seller's forgiveness in sales.
5
Apply to increase and note the rationale
Increase is not binding because it would harm the pre-emptor (ḍarar). The pre-emptor is entitled to take at the lower amount. Analogy: renewing the contract at a higher price does not bind him.
Compare with partial reduction which benefits him.

Core Idea

The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of the apparent narration: no shuf'a. This means that if a shafee waives after hearing the full purchase, he cannot later claim a part. The practical consequence: buyers have certainty after a waiver, but the shafee must be cautious before waiving.

Pre-emption Rights When Surrender is Based on Incomplete Information
Rulings on when pre-emptor surrenders based on incomplete information and the intra-Hanafi dispute in the opposite case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules regarding the effect of surrender on shuf'a when the pre-emptor was misinformed about the extent of the sale, and distinguish between the primary (zahir al-riwaya) position and Abu Yusuf's alternative view.

Core Idea
Surrender of the whole is surrender of its parts — unless the pre-emptor's need and ability differ.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'a) can be lost by explicit surrender or by inaction. But what happens if the pre-emptor surrenders based on incorrect information about the sale? In this lesson, we explore two scenarios from al-Hidayah that test the boundaries of surrender, and examine an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a mistaken surrender can be undone when the pre-emptor's actual need is different.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) Pre-emptor is told half was sold, surrenders, then learns the whole was sold. (2) Opposite: told whole was sold, surrenders, then learns only half was sold. Identify the difference in information received.
The first scenario grants shuf'a; the second denies it in the primary view.
2
Analyze the Reasoning in the First Scenario
In the first scenario, surrender was based on the assumption of partnership (harm from co-ownership). When the full purchase is revealed, no partnership exists, so the precondition for surrender (harm of partnership) is absent. Thus, shuf'a revives.
The rationale is that surrender is conditional on the perceived harm.
3
Analyze the Reasoning in the Second Scenario (Primary View)
In the second scenario, the pre-emptor surrendered the whole. The primary view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) holds that surrendering the whole necessarily includes surrendering its parts. Thus, even when only half was sold, the surrender of that half is binding.
The maxim: 'Surrender of the whole is surrender of its parts.'
4
Examine Abu Yusuf's Contrary View
Abu Yusuf disagrees. He argues that the pre-emptor's surrender was based on the assumption of full purchase; if he had known only half was sold, he might have chosen to take it. Since his need may be only for half and he can afford it, the surrender should not be binding.
This view considers the pre-emptor's actual circumstances.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Practical Difference
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the primary view (no shuf'a). The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the pre-emptor can reclaim the right to half. Under the primary view, he cannot; under Abu Yusuf's view, he can, provided he proves need and ability.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school; both positions are valid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the hila of buying a small share at a high price to defeat shuf'a and identify its conditions and cross-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
A high-priced tiny share can legally block a pre-emptor's right without violating shuf'a rules.

Hanafi fiqh permits lawful stratagems (hiyal) to avoid harm from a harmful or tyrannical neighbour. This passage presents a clever device: the buyer purchases a very small share of the property at an inflated price, so the pre-emptor's right of shuf'a attaches only to that tiny share at an exorbitant cost, effectively discouraging him from claiming. Meanwhile, the buyer acquires the rest of the property as a co-owner, gaining priority over the neighbour. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its conditions, and the scholarly disagreement surrounding such measures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its purpose
The act is buying a small share of a property at a very high price. The purpose is to discourage the pre-emptor (shafi') from exercising his right of shuf'a by making the price prohibitively high.
