Definition and Conditions of Ijarah (Leasing)
Definition of ijarah as a contract for benefits and its conditions of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah and identify the two conditions for its validity: knowledge of benefit and wage.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract for known benefits with a known wage; ignorance invalidates it.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri opens the Book of Ijarah. It gives the technical definition of ijarah as a contract for benefits (manafi‘) in exchange for a wage (ajr). The author then lays down the essential conditions for a valid ijarah: the benefit and the wage must be known. The reasoning is analogized to sale, where ignorance of price or commodity leads to dispute. Understanding these foundational elements is crucial for mastering the fiqh of leasing and hire.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Identify that ijarah is a contract on benefits (manafi‘), not on the physical asset itself. Note that it is concluded moment by moment as benefits arise.
The object (ayn) is mentioned only to connect offer and acceptance; the actual effect is on the benefit.
2
Extract the conditions
From the phrase 'ولا تصح حتى تكون المنافع معلومة، والأجرة معلومة', pick out that both the benefit and the wage must be known (ma‘lum).
Knowledge here means specification in a way that eliminates dispute.
3
Understand the analogy to sale
The text states that whatever can be a price in sale can be a wage in ijarah because the wage is the price of the benefit.
This is a qiyas: ijarah is like sale in requiring certainty of the countervalue.
4
Apply the illa (reason)
The reason for the condition is avoidance of dispute (jahalah leads to dispute). This illa is shared with sale.
If either benefit or wage is unknown, the contract is fasid (defective).
5
Test with examples
Consider a case where the benefit is not precisely specified (e.g., 'rent my car for a trip' without distance) — the contract is fasid. Or a wage that is unknown (e.g., 'I will pay you what is fair') — also fasid.
Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously.

It first provides the lexical and technical definition: ijarah is a contract for benefits (manafi') in exchange for a wage (ajr). The contract is formed moment by moment as benefits arise. The passage establishes the foundational principles of the contract of ijarah according to the Hanafi school.

منفعة (manfa'ah): Benefit or utility derived from an asset, which is the object of ijarah.

Definition and Conditions of Ijarah
Opening definition and conditions of ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah and identify the two essential conditions for its validity: known benefits and known wage.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on benefits; validity requires both benefit and wage to be known.

The chapter on ijarah (hire/lease) opens with a precise definition: it is a contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for a consideration ('iwad). The Hanafi school emphasizes that like any commutative contract, ignorance (jahalah) in the subject matter (benefit) or its substitute (wage) invalidates the contract. This lesson unpacks the definition, the conditions of knowledge, and the analogy with sale.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of ijarah
Ijarah is defined as a contract on benefits, not on the object itself. The object remains with the lessor; only the usufruct is transferred. This distinguishes it from sale.
The contract is concluded hour by hour as benefits accrue.
2
Identify the two essential conditions
The text states: 'It is not valid until the benefits are known and the wage is known.' These two conditions are necessary for validity; ignorance leads to dispute.
Both the benefit (manafi') and the wage (ujrah) must be ma'luma (known).
3
Understand how knowledge is achieved
Benefits are made known either by specifying the duration (e.g., hiring a house for a month) or by specifying the work (e.g., hiring someone to dye a garment).
Duration applies to leases of property; work applies to service contracts.
4
Analyze the analogy with sale
The maxim: 'Whatever is valid as a price in sale is valid as a wage in ijarah.' This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) because wage is the price of benefit.
The reverse is not always true: leasing a benefit for a benefit is allowed in ijarah but not in sale.
5
Note the exceptions: awqaf and istihsan
For awqaf (endowments), long-term ijarah is restricted to prevent ownership claims. Also, non-fungible wages (like animals) are allowed by istihsan, though not as price in sale.
The chosen view limits ijarah of waqf land to 3 years and other waqf to 1 year.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the chapter on Ijarah (hire/lease) from the commentary on al-Quduri. It begins with a definition: Ijarah is a contract on benefits (manafi') for a consideration ('iwad). The author then states two essential conditions for validity: the benefit and the wage must both be known (ma'luma). The reason given is that ignorance (jahalah) in the subject matter or its substitute leads to dispute, analogous to ignorance of price or commodity in sale. Next, a general principle is established: anything that is valid as a price (thaman) in a sale contract is valid as a wage (ujrah) in an ijarah contract, since the wage is the price of the benefit. However, the reverse is not necessarily true; for example, one can lease a benefit for a benefit, which is not allowed in sale. The commentary then explains how benefits can be made known: either by specifying the duration (muddah) of the lease, such as hiring a house for a known period for dwelling, or by specifying the work (amal) to be done, such as hiring someone to dye a garment. The duration can be any length, but for awqaf (endowments) there is a restriction: the chosen view is that long-term ijarah (more than three years for land, more than one year for other properties) is not permitted to prevent the lessee from claiming ownership. The passage also mentions that non-fungible items (like animals) can be given as wage by istihsan, even though they are not valid as price in sale, according to Abu Hanifa. This commentary clarifies the foundational rules of ijarah in the Hanafi school.

المنافع (al-manafi'): The benefits or usufructs that are the subject matter of the ijarah contract.
الأجرة (al-ujrah): The wage or consideration paid in exchange for the benefits, which can be any fungible or non-fungible property permissible as price in sale or by istihsan.

Three Methods of Specifying Benefit in Ijarah
Explains the three methods to specify benefit in ijarah contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three methods of specifying benefit in Hanafi ijarah and apply them to determine the validity of a contract.

Core Idea
Benefit in ijarah must be known; knowledge is achieved by duration, work specification, or identification.

In Hanafi fiqh, the subject matter of an ijarah contract is the benefit (manfa'ah) derived from an asset or service. To ensure the contract is valid (sahih) and free from gharar (uncertainty), the benefit must be precisely known. This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains three distinct methods of specification: by setting a duration, by describing the work and specifying details, or by physically identifying the object and destination. Understanding these methods is essential for drafting sound ijarah contracts in everyday life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three methods
Read the passage and list the three ways a benefit becomes known: by duration (al-muddah), by work and specification (al-'amal wa al-tasmiyah), and by identification and pointing (al-ta'yin wa al-isharah).
These correspond to common scenarios: renting a house for a month, hiring a tailor to sew a specific garment, or hiring a porter to move a specific box.
2
Understand each method with examples
For each method, note the example given: duration (houses for dwelling, lands for cultivation), work (dyeing a garment with specified color, sewing, carrying a known quantity), identification (transporting 'this food' to a known place).
The examples show how detail removes ambiguity.
3
Recognize the exception for waqf and orphan's land
The passage notes a key exception: for waqf (endowment) properties, long-term leases are restricted (more than three years for agricultural land, more than one year for other properties) to prevent the tenant from claiming ownership.
This is a mufta bihi position from the later Hanafi scholars.
4
Identify the invalidating omission
The passage states that if both time and place are omitted when hiring an animal for riding, the contract is fasid (invalid). This shows that complete failure to specify leaves the benefit unknown.
The condition is that at least one method must be used; partial specification suffices.
5
Apply the rule
Given a modern scenario (e.g., renting a car for a weekend trip), determine which method is used and whether the benefit is known. Alternatively, identify a flawed contract and suggest the missing specification.
Use the three methods as a checklist.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the requirement that the benefit (manfa'ah) in an ijarah contract must be known (ma'lumah) to avoid gharar (uncertainty). The author presents three methods of specification: (1) by duration – e.g., renting a house for a set period; the contract is valid regardless of length, except for waqf properties where long-term leases exceeding three years (for land) or one year (for other properties) are disallowed. (3) by identification and pointing – e.g., hiring someone to transport a specific pile of food to a specific location. The passage also notes that if both time and place are omitted when hiring an animal for riding, the contract is fasid (invalid). Additionally, a footnote clarifies that things not valid as a price in sale (e.g., animals, dissimilar items) can be valid as wages in ijarah, citing Abu Hanifah's istihsan for hiring a wet nurse with food and clothing.

Renting Houses and Shops for Dwelling Without Specifying Activity
States the ruling that renting houses and shops is valid for dwelling even without specifying activity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on renting houses and shops for dwelling without specifying the activity and explain that the contract is valid because the benefit is known by custom.

Core Idea
Renting a house for dwelling is valid even without specifying activity because custom makes the benefit known.

The Hanafi school relies on customary practice (urf) to determine the intended benefit in rental contracts. When a property like a house or shop is rented for dwelling, the benefit is considered sufficiently known even if the tenant does not detail his exact activity. This lesson explores the ruling and its reliance on urf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is renting a house or shop for dwelling. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
The contract is not void or fasid.
2
Understand the basis: customary use (urf)
The benefit is known because the normal custom (urf) of such properties is dwelling. Therefore, explicit specification is not required.
Urt fills the gap when the benefit is obvious.
3
Apply the principle
This ruling applies when the customary use of the property is dwelling. If the custom is different, the ruling may change.
Always consider local custom.

Specifying the Benefit in Ijarah by Description or Indication
Defining two methods to specify the leased benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two methods of specifying the benefit in an ijarah contract and apply them to determine contract validity.

Core Idea
Benefit must be known: describe in detail or point to the physical thing.

In an ijarah (lease/hire) contract, the object is the benefit (manfa'ah), which must be clearly known to both parties. This short passage from Al-Lubab explains two valid ways to achieve that knowledge: through detailed description or through physical indication. Understanding these methods is essential to forming valid contracts and avoiding disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods
The passage states that the benefit becomes known either by description (wasf) or by specification and indication (ta'yin wa isharah).
2
Analyze the description method
Examples include specifying tailoring details (fabric, thread, quality) or hiring a mount to carry a known quantity or ride a specified distance.
The description must remove major uncertainty.
3
Analyze the indication method
Pointing to a physical object (e.g., 'this food') and a known destination suffices to make the benefit known.
This method applies when the object is present and identifiable.

The lessee may use the premises for any purpose except those that damage the building — blacksmithing, laundering, and milling.

This passage from Al-Quduri sets out the basic rule for hiring a house or shop without specifying the use. Because the customary use is residence, the contract is valid without detailed specification. However, the lessee is barred from activities that inherently harm the structure, as these are presumed damaging unless explicitly permitted.

Default validity relies on custom.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is custom (urf): the phrase 'because the customary use is residence' indicates that custom determines the default scope of the contract.
Custom is a recognized secondary source in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Custom defines the implicit understanding of the contract; since people usually rent for living, that becomes the default purpose.
This prevents the contract from being void for ambiguity (gharar).
4
Note the exceptions
The author explicitly prohibits blacksmithing (hidadah), laundering (qisarah), and milling (tahanah) because they cause obvious damage.
These are examples, not an exhaustive list.
5
Evaluate the underlying reason
The ‘illa (effective cause) is harm to the building: heavy pounding, excessive water, and vibration weaken the structure. Any activity with similar harm is prohibited unless the landlord permits it.
The prohibition protects the property and prevents disputes.

Condition for Valid Hire of Agricultural Land
Valid ijarah of agricultural land requires stating the crop or leaving it to the lessee's choice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a valid ijarah of agricultural land in the Hanafi school and explain why the crop must be specified or discretion given.

Core Idea
A hire contract for farmland requires either naming the crop or granting free choice — otherwise uncertainty invalidates it.

In Hanafi fiqh, the validity of hiring land for agriculture hinges on avoiding uncertainty (gharar). The text from al-Lubab explains that without specifying the crop or giving the lessee freedom to choose, the contract is void due to potential dispute. This lesson unpacks the condition and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hiring agricultural land. The ruling is that it is permissible in principle but conditional for validity.
2
State the condition
The contract is not valid until either the crop to be planted is named, or the lessor says 'on condition that he plants whatever he wishes'.
These are the only two ways to satisfy the condition.
3
Understand the underlying cause ('illa)
The requirement exists to eliminate gharar (uncertainty) that could lead to disputes. Different crops have different durations and soil impacts; not knowing which creates ambiguity.
Removing uncertainty is a core principle in Hanafi mu'amalat.
4
Note the consequence of omission
If neither crop is specified nor discretion granted, the contract is invalid (batil).
The text explicitly says 'la yasihh al-aqd' — the contract is not valid.
5
Apply to a scenario
Consider a lease for 'agricultural purposes' without naming a crop. Without discretion, this is invalid. If the lessor adds 'you may plant anything', it becomes valid.

Ruling with proof on prohibited trades for hired builder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three prohibited trades for a hired builder according to the Hanafi school and explain the legal reasoning based on apparent harm.

Core Idea
Harm to the building prohibits blacksmithing, laundering, and milling for a hired builder unless specified.

The Hanafi school sets clear boundaries for what a hired builder may do. While generally free to perform any work that does not damage the building, three trades are explicitly excluded due to their inherent harm. This lesson examines the ruling and its basis in the principle of preventing harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general permission
The text begins with a general rule: the hired worker may do everything. Identify the condition implied: 'everything that does not harm the building.'
The general permission is conditional on lack of harm.
2
List the three exceptions
Note the three specific trades excluded: blacksmithing, laundering (fulling), and milling. These are named explicitly.
These are not exhaustive but represent clear examples of harmful activities.
3
Understand the legal cause (illah)
The text states the reason: these trades cause apparent damage (darar zahir) by weakening the building. This is the basis for prohibition.
The illah is harm, which is a recognized principle in Hanafi usul.

Leasing Land: Specification, Removal, and Compensation
Ruling on land lease, specification, and end-of-term removal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid land lease and apply the rules for removing buildings and trees at the end of the lease term.

Core Idea
Lessee must remove permanent improvements unless owner pays their value as removed.

Leasing land for farming or building is a common transaction. The Hanafi school sets clear conditions to avoid disputes: the crop must be specified or delegated, and at lease end, the lessee must clear the land of structures and trees. However, the owner has an option to retain improvements by paying their salvage value. This lesson unpacks these rules and the exception for crops with a definite end.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its essential conditions
Determine what makes a lease of land valid: specification of the intended crop or delegation to the lessee. Understand why this is required to avoid uncertainty (gharar).
The text says: 'the contract is not valid unless he specifies what he will plant, or says: on condition that he plants whatever he wishes.'
2
Understand the specification requirement for crops
Analyze why crops must be specified: because different crops affect the land differently and some cause harm. Specification prevents future disputes.
Alternatively, delegation removes ambiguity by giving the lessee full discretion.
3
Distinguish between crops and permanent improvements
Note the difference between seasonal crops (zar' baql) and permanent improvements like buildings and trees. Crops have a known end, so they can be left with rent until harvest. Buildings and trees have no fixed end, so they must be removed.
This distinction is crucial for end-of-lease obligations.
4
Analyze the end-of-lease obligations
State the default rule: lessee must remove all buildings and trees and deliver the land empty. This prevents harm (darar) to the landowner from indefinite occupation.
The text says: 'it is incumbent upon him to remove the building... and deliver it empty.'
5
Evaluate the exception and its condition
Examine the landowner's option to pay the value of improvements 'as removed' (maqlūʿan) and take ownership. Note the extra exception when removal would diminish the land: then the owner may take possession without the lessee's consent.
This balances rights and prevents damage to the land.

Leasing a Vacant Plot for Building or Planting (Ijārat al-Sāḥah)
Ruling on leasing land for building/planting with end-of-term obligations

Learning Objective

Restricting the User in a Hire Contract: The Principle of Variation (Tafāwut)
Explains conditions for validity of restricting the user in a hire contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a user specification in a hire contract is valid only when the item's condition or value varies with the user, and determine liability for breach.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a user stipulation in hire binds only if users cause meaningful variation in the item's harm or wear.

When you hire an item, you may want to restrict who uses it. But does such a restriction always hold? The Hanafi school says no—it depends on whether the item's benefit changes with the user. This passage from Al-Lubab explains the principle of tafāwut (variation) and its consequences for liability. Let's walk through the rule and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic scenarios
The text opens with examples: hiring an animal for riding or a garment for wearing without specifying the user. In these cases, the user is effectively restricted to the hirer or the first person he allows, because people differ in how they ride or wear—so the user matters.
Note: The initial action of using it oneself or letting one person use it makes that user the intended one.
2
Recognize the key condition: tafāwut (variation)
The author states: 'وكذلك كل ما يختلف باختلاف المستعمل' (Likewise everything that varies by variation of the user). The validity of a user specification hinges on whether the item's usage causes meaningful differences in risk or wear. If yes, the stipulation is binding.
Tafāwut is the 'illa (effective cause) for binding the condition.
3
Determine liability for breach
If a valid specification is violated (e.g., the hirer lets someone else ride when a specific rider was named), and the animal perishes or garment is ruined, the hirer is liable for the full value. Reason: hire and liability do not coexist; since he violated the condition, the hire (upright) is not paired with liability.
The phrase 'ولا يجتمع الأجر والضمان' means hire (wage) is not due together with liability for destruction.
4
Apply the exception: items without variation
Real estate (عقار) and items that do not vary by user are excepted. Even if a specific resident is stipulated, the hirer may substitute another without liability because the restriction is ineffective due to lack of tafāwut.
The text says: 'فلا يعتبر تقييده' – its restriction is not considered.
5
Handle load specification on animals
If the hirer specifies a type and quantity of load (e.g., five qafizes of wheat), he may load similar (e.g., lentils) or less harmful (e.g., barley) items because the harm to the animal is the same or less. This is not a violation of a valid restriction.
The key is comparison of harm (darar); anything equal or less in harm is permissible.

Defines permissible load when hiring a transport animal and liability for causing harm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi criterion of harm (ḍarar) to determine whether a substitute load is permissible in an animal hire contract, and state the liability for unauthorized use that causes harm.

Core Idea
In Hanafi ijara, the lessee may use the animal with loads equal or less harmful than specified; more harmful loads require consent.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rules on permissible and impermissible changes to the load in an animal hire contract. The key is not merely weight but the nature of the load and its potential harm to the animal. We will see how the school derives liability when unauthorized use causes damage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specified load and its harm level
Determine the load type and amount agreed in the contract. Assess the harm it causes to the animal (e.g., wheat is moderate).
The contract defines the baseline harm.
2
Compare the substitute load's harm
Evaluate the substitute load relative to the specified one: similar, less, or more harmful. Hanafi law considers distribution and toxicity, not just weight.
Iron is more harmful than cotton of equal weight due to concentration.
3
Apply the principle: equal or less harm permissible; more harm impermissible
If the substitute is equal or less harmful, it falls under the contract permission. If more harmful, it is not permitted and incurs liability for any damage.
The principle is based on the implied consent to similar or lesser uses.
4
Consider special cases: cotton vs iron
Note that even if weight is identical, the nature of the load matters. Cotton spreads, iron concentrates, causing greater harm.
This illustrates the harm criterion over mere weight.
5
Determine liability for unauthorized use causing harm
If unauthorized use (e.g., extra rider, excessive load) causes the animal's death, the lessee is liable. In the case of one authorized and one unauthorized rider, liability is half the animal's value.
Liability is based on the proportion of unauthorized use contributing to the harm.

Explains restrictions on loading and pillion rider liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the restrictions on loading a hired animal and apply the rule of liability (half value) when a pillion rider causes the animal's death.

Core Idea
Using a hired animal beyond the agreed scope incurs liability; extra riders trigger half-value guarantee.

When you hire an animal for transport, the contract specifies the permissible use. If you exceed that—by loading heavier or more harmful goods, or by taking an extra rider—you violate the owner's consent. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rulings on such violations, focusing on the liability that arises when the animal perishes due to unauthorized use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Find the sentence that sets the overarching rule: whoever is entitled to a specified benefit may use it, its like, or less; using more is not permissible because it falls outside the permission.

Analyze the first case: loading harmful goods
The author gives an example: if hired to carry wheat, cannot carry salt or iron (more harmful). Then, if hired to carry a specified quantity of cotton, cannot carry iron of the same weight because iron concentrates and harms the animal.

The owner can choose to hold either the hirer or the pillion rider liable. If the pillion rider pays, he can recover from the hirer only if the hirer was the contracting party. The full hire is still due if the animal reached its destination.
This shows interplay between contract, guarantee, and hire.

The ruling applies only if the animal dies (perishes) and if the extra person is a pillion rider (irdāf), not a usurper sitting in the saddle. In usurpation, no hire is due.
These conditions prevent misapplication.

First, if the animal is hired to carry a specified quantity of cotton, the hirer cannot load iron of the same weight because iron concentrates on the back while cotton spreads out, causing more harm. Second, if the animal is hired for riding and the hirer allows another man to ride behind him, causing the animal's death, the hirer is liable for half its value because both rode, but only the hirer had permission. The text clarifies that liability is based on the number of riders, not weight, because a light rider might be ignorant and cause harm while a heavy rider might be gentle. The owner can choose to hold either the hirer or the pillion rider liable; if the pillion rider is held liable, he can seek recourse from the hirer only if the hirer was the one who hired the animal. It also mentions that according to al-Nihaya and al-Muhit, if the animal reaches its destination and then perishes, the full hire is due along with half the value, as the guarantee is for the unauthorized rider and the hire for the permitted ride. The passage ends by noting that these rulings are specific to the case of death and to the pillion rider being a follower; if the extra person sat in the saddle, he would be a usurper and no hire would be due, as guarantee and hire cannot combine.

Core Idea
The owner may choose to hold either the rider or the radif liable, but the radif can recover from the rider only if the rider was the hirer.

In this passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, we examine a specific scenario in hire contracts: when a hired riding animal is destroyed while carrying two persons—the primary rider (raakib) and a second rider (radif). Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying Hanafi liability principles in hire contracts.

Guided Path

Identify the parties in the hire contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a hirer loads extra weight on a hired animal and identify the conditions that trigger proportional vs. full value liability.

Core Idea
The hirer is liable for the proportion of the excess load, but full value if the animal cannot bear it or if he rides on top.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Ijarah: when a hirer loads a hired animal with more than the agreed weight, causing its destruction. The Hanafi school distinguishes between proportional liability for excess weight and full liability in cases of overburdening or riding on the load. Understanding these conditions is crucial for determining the correct compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
Recognize that the hirer loads more than the agreed amount, and the animal dies as a result. The key conditions for proportional liability are: the animal can bear the load, the rider does not ride on top, and any additional rider (radif) must hold himself.
The extra load must be of the same kind (e.g., same grain type).
2
Determine the default ruling
The default is proportional liability: the hirer pays the fraction of the animal's value equal to the fraction of excess weight. For example, if he loaded 6 qafiz instead of 5, he pays 1/6 of the value.
The footnote illustrates: 1 qafiz extra out of 6 total = 1/6 liability.
3
Identify exceptions that trigger full liability
Full value becomes due in two scenarios: (1) if the animal cannot bear the load (i.e., the total load is beyond its capacity), and (2) if the hirer rides on top of the load.
Riding on top is a clear violation; here the hirer's weight is added to the load.
4

If the radif (second rider) cannot hold himself and is like goods, liability is according to his weight. Also, if the animal can bear both loads normally but the extra weight is still added, the proportional rule applies.
The condition 'if he carries it on his shoulder' indicates concentrated weight, which still triggers proportional liability.
5

Take the example: hired for 5 qafiz, loaded 6 qafiz, animal dies. Calculate the extra fraction: 1/6. The hirer pays 1/6 of the value. Now modify: if the animal could barely carry 5 qafiz, then full value is due.

Liability for Customary Reining or Striking of a Hired Animal: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for customary actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on liability for customary reining or striking of a hired animal, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: liable for any harmful extra action; Sahibayn: no liability for customary actions; fatwa on Abu Hanifa.

In a contract of ijarah (hire) of an animal, what happens if the hirer, during use, reins in the animal or strikes it in a customary manner and the animal dies? This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Jawharah presents the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, and concludes with the tarjih. Understanding this dispute clarifies the concept of permission (idhn) and its conditions in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the basis of liability
First, note the actions: reining in the animal (kabbaha) or striking it (daraba) in a customary manner. The dispute is about whether these actions, when causing death, make the hirer liable (daman).
Two types: customary (muta'arif) and non-customary. The case here is customary.
2
Understand the reasoning of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa argues that permission to use the animal is conditioned (muqayyad) on safety. Since driving can be achieved without reining or striking, these are considered unnecessary intensifications. Any harm removes the protection of permission, so the hirer is liable.
Key principle: idhn muqayyad bi shart al-salamah.
3
Understand the reasoning of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that customary actions are implicitly permitted in the contract. The animal perished due to a combination of permitted (transport) and unpermitted (rein/strike) causes, but since the primary cause is permitted, liability is divided or not imposed for the unpermitted part.
They apply the concept of 'the cause being transport' (al-sabab al-naql) and division of the act.
4
Identify the mufta bihi and thamarah al-khilaf
The text states that the fatwa is according to Abu Hanifa, as per al-Jawharah. The practical difference: if a hirer reins in a horse customarily and it dies, he must compensate (Abu Hanifa); if the Sahibayn's view were followed, he would not.
Tarjih: 'wa alayhi al-fatwa' indicates the relied-upon position.

Customary actions in a contract are either permitted or not; Abu Hanifa holds liability even for customary acts, while the Sahibayn do not, and the fatwa follows Abu Hanifa.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the liability of a common worker, identify the thamarah al-khilaf, and identify the mufta bihi position according to later Hanafi authorities.

Core Idea
A common worker is a trustee, not a guarantor, unless an overwhelming calamity occurs.

This passage addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: when a common worker (ajir mushtarak) such as a dyer or tailor holds goods, if those goods perish accidentally, must he compensate the owner? Imam Abu Hanifa views the worker as a trustee (amin) and thus not liable, while his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold him liable except for overwhelming disasters. The passage further notes which position became the relied-upon madhhab and a later compromise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of worker
Understand the definition of ajir mushtarak: a worker who works for multiple people and is only entitled to wages upon completion. Examples: dyer (sabbagh), fuller (qassar).
Distinguish from ajir khas (private worker) who works exclusively for one person and gets wages periodically.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa: the goods are a trust (amana) in the worker's hand; if they perish without negligence, he does not guarantee. The basis is that the worker's possession is with the owner's permission, making him a trustee.
Trustees (amin) are not liable for accidental loss.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the worker does guarantee the goods, except for overwhelming calamities (e.g., widespread fire, overpowering enemy). Their basis is that the common worker has a measure of control and should be accountable.
The exception for overwhelming events shows they do not hold him strictly liable.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the worker pays nothing for accidental loss. Under Sahibayn, he pays full value unless it was an overwhelming calamity.
Later practice introduced a compromise: half the value.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the later compromise
According to al-Tashih, the fatwa is based on Abu Hanifa's view (no guarantee), and this is relied upon by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi. However, later scholars (as in al-Durr) gave fatwa for a compromise of half the value to settle disputes.
This shows evolution of fatwa within the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri deals with the common hired worker (al-ajīr al-mushtarak). A hired worker guarantees property damage from his work, but not harm to humans.

Liability of the Cupper and Veterinarian for Harm to Patient
Ruling on liability of cupper and vet for harm caused within or beyond usual limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a cupper or veterinarian is liable for harm to the patient and apply the ruling to scenarios of usual vs. excessive treatment.

Core Idea
A cupper or vet is not liable for harm within the usual bounds, but becomes liable if he exceeds the customary site.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the liability of a cupper (al-fassad) and a veterinarian (al-bazzagh) for complications arising from their work. The key distinction is whether they acted within the usual limits (al-mu'tad) or exceeded them. The Hanafi ruling balances the permission granted by the patient with the unpredictability of individual constitutions (sarayah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The passage states that if the cupper or veterinarian does not exceed the usual site and has permission, there is no liability for resulting harm.
The term 'fala daman' means no guarantee or compensation.
2
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The reason is that complications (sarayah) are beyond human control because they depend on the patient's constitution, which cannot be known. Therefore, the practitioner cannot be held to guarantee safety.
This is a form of istihsan based on custom and necessity.
3
Identify the exception
If the practitioner exceeds the usual site (e.g., cuts an unusual vein), then liability arises. If the harm is limited to excess without death, he guarantees the entire excess. If death results, he guarantees half the diya.
The half diya is apportioned because death was caused partly by permitted and partly by non-permitted action.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a cupper who bleeds from the usual site, but the patient dies from infection. No liability. If he cuts an unusual vein and patient dies, half diya.
This ruling applies to both human and animal treatment.

The first ruling is for workers like washermen and tailors: they cannot demand any wage, even partial, until they complete the entire contracted work. The reason given is that partial work does not provide any separate benefit to the hirer; the benefit is only realized upon full completion. However, if they stipulate in the contract that the wage is payable in advance or in installments, then that stipulation is binding and they can demand it earlier. The second ruling concerns a baker hired to bake a specific amount of flour in the hirer's home. The baker is not entitled to his wage until he removes the bread from the oven, because that is the completion of the work. The passage then explores a scenario: if the bread burns or falls before removal, the baker gets no wage because it was destroyed before delivery (qabl al-taslim). But if he removes it and then it burns without his fault, he still gets his wage and is not liable. The passage includes a discussion from al-Hidayah about the underlying reasoning: analogy (qiyas) would suggest wage entitlement hour by hour to ensure fairness, but this would cause hardship because the worker would be constantly demanding payment and not free for other work. Therefore, istihsan (juristic preference) set the default rule that wages are due only at completion. This illustrates the Hanafi methodology of preferring istihsan over qiyas when there is potential harm.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning that would require wage entitlement proportional to time worked.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference that sets the rule at completion to avoid harm to the worker.
التسليم (al-taslim): Delivery of the completed work to the hirer, after which wage is due.

Cook's Custom and Brickmaker's Completion: Custom and Intra-Hanafi Dispute in Ijara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply custom ('urf) to determine the scope of work in an ijara contract and state the intra-Hanafi dispute on when a brickmaker is entitled to wages, distinguishing between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In hire contracts, custom defines the work scope; for brickmaking, the Sahibayn require arranging bricks for wage entitlement.

This lesson presents two distinct rulings from the Hanafi school regarding hired workers. The first establishes that a cook's duty includes ladling (gharf) based on custom. The second reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute: at what stage does a brickmaker earn his wage? Abu Hanifa holds that forming the bricks is enough, while the Two Companions require arranging them. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the school relies on custom and the nature of contractual completion.

Guided Path

The analogy with transportation shows that benefit exists even without the subsequent task.
4

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the wage is not due until the bricks are arranged (tashrij). Their implied reasoning: customarily, bricks are delivered in arranged stacks; forming alone does not deliver the promised benefit.
The text does not elaborate on their reasoning, but it stems from the idea that the contracted work includes customary completion.

If the bricks are destroyed after forming but before arranging, Abu Hanifa would require payment, the Sahibayn would not. This determines who bears the risk of loss.
In later Hanafi fatwa, the Sahibayn's view is often relied upon (mufta bihi).

When Does a Brickmaker Earn His Wage?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wage entitlement in brickmaking contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state both Hanafi positions on when a hired brickmaker earns his wage, derive the thamarah al-khilaf, and apply the role of custom in resolving the dispute.

Core Idea
Wage entitlement turns on what constitutes 'work'; custom may override qiyas in practice.

When you hire a brickmaker, does he earn his wage the moment he forms the bricks, or only after he stacks them to dry? The Hanafi school has two views: Abu Hanifa says the wage is earned upon forming the bricks (iqamah), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require arranging them (tashrij). This seemingly narrow dispute has real consequences: if the bricks are destroyed after forming but before arranging, Abu Hanifa gives the worker his wage, the Sahibayn do not. In this lesson, we examine the proofs, the practical difference, and how later scholars relied on custom to decide between these positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two rulings
Read the Arabic passage carefully. The case is hiring a man 'to beat bricks' (li-yadriba labinan). Identify the two rulings: (1) when does he become entitled to the wage? (2) if he removes the bricks and they burn without his fault, what is the ruling on his wage and liability?
2
Map the intra-Hanafi dispute
The key ikhtilaf is between Abu Hanifa (al-Imam) and the Sahibayn. State each position precisely: wage earned upon 'iqamah' (forming) vs 'tashrij' (arranging). Note the wording: 'استحق الأجرة إذا أقامه' vs 'لا يستحقها حتى يشرجه'.
Remember: 'قالا' refers to both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
3
Analyse the proof for each view
Abu Hanifa's dalil is that 'tashrij' is extra work akin to transport; one can benefit from the bricks before arranging (e.g., transport them to the site). The Sahibayn's dalil is that arranging is the final necessary step; without it the work is incomplete. Who uses a better analogy?
The passage includes a commentary: 'قيل: والتشريج عمل زائد كالنقل...' supporting Abu Hanifa.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if bricks are destroyed after forming but before arranging, Abu Hanifa says the worker still gets his wage (he completed the essential work). The Sahibayn say no wage (work not fully complete). This is the crucial test case.
Note: this is a scenario where the liability ruling (later in the passage) also applies; the passage gives a separate case with fire.
5
Evaluate the tarjih and role of custom
Later authorities differed: al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi relied on Abu Hanifa; al-'Uyun gave fatwa on the Sahibayn. The commentator (Ibn Qutlubugha) suggests that if custom is uniform, custom should be followed. Why does custom override qiyas here?
