The Legal Effect of a Slave's Statements
Explains the legal effect of a slave's statements and confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the validity of a slave's statements and distinguish between confessions of debt and confessions of hadd/qisas in terms of immediate binding effect.

Core Idea
A slave's statements are valid for himself but not for his master; financial confessions bind after freedom, but criminal confessions bind immediately.

This passage from al-Lubab explains the legal capacity (ahliyyah) of a slave in making statements such as confessions and divorce. The Hanafi school balances the slave's inherent capacity with the master's property rights, leading to nuanced rules that depend on the type of confession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Recognize that a slave's statements (e.g., contracts, confessions) are legally valid concerning his own person because he has legal capacity (ahliyyah).
This is based on the slave's humanity and capacity (ahliyyah al-wujub wa al-ada).
2
Note the restriction
Understand that these statements are not valid concerning the master's rights, because their enforcement could harm the master's property by attaching debts to the slave's person or earnings.
This protects the master's ownership (milk).
3
Distinguish between types of confessions
Financial confessions (debt) do not bind the slave immediately because of an impediment (lack of ownership), but become binding after freedom. Confessions of hadd or qisas bind immediately because in matters of life and limb, the slave retains the original basis of freedom.
The reason (illah) for immediate binding in blood matters is that the slave is considered free regarding his own blood; the master cannot confess on his behalf.
4
Apply to divorce
A slave's divorce is valid because he is competent for it and it does not affect the master's ownership or benefits.
Divorce only concerns the marital relationship, not property.

Acknowledging a Stranger then Claiming Paternity
Ruling on the interplay between acknowledgment and paternity claim in illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a sick person acknowledges a stranger and later claims paternity, explain the legal reasoning (illa) of retroactive lineage, and apply it to similar cases.

Core Idea
Paternity relates back to conception, revealing the acknowledged person was an heir — nullifying the earlier acknowledgment.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi inheritance law: a terminally ill person first makes an acknowledgment (iqrar) to a stranger, then later claims that person is his son. The ruling hinges on the principle of istinad al-nasab ila al-uluq — that lineage relates back to the time of conception. Understanding this ruling is crucial for distinguishing between valid gifts to non-heirs and invalid ones to heirs during illness (marad al-mawt).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) made by a sick person (during marad al-mawt) to a stranger. Later, the sick person claims the stranger is his son. The acknowledgment and claim are both credible.
Stranger means a non-heir at the time of acknowledgment.
2
State the ruling
The lineage (nasab) of the claimed son is established (yathbut). The original acknowledgment is nullified (yabtul). The acknowledged person becomes an heir, so the acknowledgment to an heir during illness is invalid.
3
Locate the legal basis (dalil)
The ruling is based on the principle that a paternity claim relates back to the time of conception (istinad al-nasab ila al-uluq). This makes the person a son from the very beginning, retroactively.
This is derived from the maxim: 'The claim of lineage is based on the possibility of birth and relates back to the time of conception.'
4
Apply the qiyas (analogical reasoning)
The illa (effective cause) is that the acknowledgment was to someone who turns out to be an heir. Since an acknowledgment to an heir during illness is invalid, the same applies here. The far' (new case) is any later claim that retroactively makes the acknowledged person an heir.
The qiyas is jaliyy (clear), as the illa is directly present.
5
Note conditions and exceptions
The paternity claim must be plausible (the person could have been born from him, considering age etc.). The acknowledged person must confirm the claim. If not, or if birth is impossible, the original acknowledgment stands.

The Spending Rule: Who is Entitled Without a Court Order?
Establishes the rule for nafaqah from missing person's wealth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule from al-Hidayah to distinguish dependents entitled to nafaqah from the missing person's wealth with or without a court order.

Core Idea
Spending from a missing person's wealth follows the same entitlement as in his presence: those who need a court order in presence cannot get it in absence.

The passage presents a crucial principle from al-Hidayah: entitlement to spending from the missing person's wealth depends on whether the dependent would need a court order for nafaqah in the husband's/father's presence. This distinction prevents injustice and respects the impossibility of a court order against an absent person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
The rule: anyone entitled to nafaqah without qada in presence gets it in absence; anyone needing qada in presence does not.
The qada is merely assistance for the first group; for the second, it is the basis of obligation.
2
Apply to first category
List examples: young children, adult females, chronically ill adult males. Why? Because in Hanafi law, these are entitled without court order.
Adult females include daughters and wives? The passage says النساء من الكبار.
3
Apply to second category
List examples: brothers, sisters, uncles/aunts. They need qada even in presence, so cannot get nafaqah from missing person's wealth.
This is because qada against an absent person is not possible.
4
Consider conditions for spending
Spending is only from liquid assets (dirhams/dinars/bullion) held by qadi, depositary, or confessing debtor, with acknowledgment of relationship.
If a depositary pays without qadi order, he is liable; debtor not discharged.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab commences the chapter on the missing person (al-mafqud). It first defines the missing person linguistically and legally: one who is absent, whose whereabouts are unknown, and whose state (alive or dead) is not known. The ruling then explains the role of the qadi: he appoints a guardian to protect the missing person's wealth—preserving real estate, collecting produce, selling perishable goods—and to collect debts that debtors acknowledge. The reasoning given is that the qadi is the supervisor for everyone unable to manage his own affairs, and the missing person is such. The guardian is also authorized to spend on the missing person's dependents: wife, young children (including lower lineage), and parents (including higher lineage), from his wealth. Importantly, the marriage is not dissolved due to absence alone.

The passage then provides a crucial rule from al-Hidayah distinguishing between dependents who are entitled to spending without a court order in the missing person's presence (e.g., young children, adult females, chronically ill adult males) and those who require a court order even in his presence (e.g., siblings, maternal uncles/aunts). For the first category, spending is made from his wealth during absence because the qadi's order is merely confirmatory. For the second, no spending is given because it would require a court order against an absentee, which is impermissible.

Finally, a condition is added: spending is only from the missing person's wealth if his assets are dirhams, dinars, or bullion, and are in the possession of the qadi, a depositary, or a debtor who acknowledges both the property and the relationship (marriage or kinship), unless these are obvious to the qadi. If the depositary or debtor pays without the qadi's order, they incur liability or are not discharged. The passage ends by reiterating that the marriage is not terminated by absence.
