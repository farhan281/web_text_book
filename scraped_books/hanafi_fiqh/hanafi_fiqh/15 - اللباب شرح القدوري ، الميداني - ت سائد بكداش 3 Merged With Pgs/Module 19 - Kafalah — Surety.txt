Defines kafalah and states the ruling on surety of the person with a hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define kafalah in Hanafi law and state the ruling on surety of the person with its proof.

Core Idea
Surety of the person is permissible; the guarantor is liable if the principal fails — Prophet's hadith proves.

In this lesson, we begin the Book of Suretyship (Kitab al-Kafalah). The author first connects it to the previous book on Agency, noting that both involve seeking assistance from another. Then he provides the linguistic and legal definitions of kafalah and divides it into two types. The focus here is on surety of the person (kafalah bi al-nafs), and the ruling that it is permissible, supported by a clear hadith. We will analyze this ruling and its evidence.

The text divides kafalah into kafalah bi al-nafs and kafalah bi al-mal. Note that both can be combined.
3
State the ruling on surety of the person.
The text says it is 'جائزة' (permissible). In Hanafi fiqh, this means it is valid and binding once concluded.
4
Locate the dalil (proof).
The Prophet's hadith 'الزعيم غارم' is cited. Find its sources (Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi) and understand its classification as hasan.
5
Explain the wajh al-dalalah.
The word 'غارم' indicates liability. Therefore, the guarantor (زعيم) becomes liable to fulfill the obligation if the principal fails, which proves the validity of the contract.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Suretyship (Kitab al-Kafalah). It first explains the connection to the previous book (Agency): both involve seeking assistance from another. Then it gives the linguistic and legal definitions of kafalah: linguistically 'joining', and legally 'joining a liability to a liability for the purpose of claim'. This means that the surety's liability is added to the principal's liability, so the creditor can claim from either. It then divides kafalah into two types: surety of the person (kafalah bi al-nafs) and surety of property (kafalah bi al-mal). The text proceeds to state the ruling on surety of the person: it is permissible (ja'izah). The proof is the general nature of the Prophet's statement: 'al-za'im gharim' meaning 'the guarantor is liable'. The commentator provides the full hadith and sources. The meaning is that the surety becomes a guarantor (dammin) if the principal (makful) fails to fulfill his obligation. The note also explains the regional linguistic variations for the term 'surety'. In Hanafi fiqh, kafalah bi al-nafs is a contract where a person guarantees the presence of another person (for a claim or debt). It is binding and the surety must produce the principal or pay if he fails.

Key Terms
الكفالة (al-kafalah): Linguistically: joining. Legally: joining a liability to a liability in claim.
الزعيم (al-za'im): The surety or guarantor, as per the hadith.
غارم (gharim): Someone who is liable to pay a debt or obligation.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri introduces the Book of Kafalah (suretyship). The author first explains the connection with the previous book (wakalah/agency): both involve seeking assistance from another person. He defines kafalah linguistically as 'joining' and legally as 'joining a liability to a liability in the demand', meaning the guarantor's liability is added to that of the principal debtor. There are two types: suretyship of the person (kafalah bi al-nafs) and suretyship of property (kafalah bi al-mal), and they can be combined. The ruling that kafalah bi al-nafs is permissible is supported by the hadith 'al-za'im gharim' (the guarantor is liable), which is a general proof for the validity of guaranty. The commentary then details the wording required to form a valid kafalah bi al-nafs contract. Expressions like 'I guarantee his person', 'neck', 'soul', etc., are valid because they refer to the entire person. Mentioning an undivided fraction (half, third) is also valid because a person is indivisible for the purpose of suretyship; mentioning part is like mentioning the whole. Additionally, phrases like 'I am surety for him' or 'he is my responsibility' suffice. The guaranteed obligation is to produce the person of the principal if required, analogous to producing property in financial guaranty.

Kafalah al-Nafs: Obligation, Valid Expressions, and Delivery Condition
Defines the obligation, valid formulas, and time condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation of the guarantor, identify valid verbal expressions, and explain the condition of time stipulation in kafalah al-nafs according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Kafalah al-nafs is binding like a debt, requiring delivery of the person; valid expressions cover any phrase denoting the whole.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on guarantee of the person (kafalah al-nafs). The passage from al-Lubab clarifies the nature of the obligation, the verbal formulas that constitute a valid contract, and the special condition regarding delivery time. Understanding these elements is essential for correctly applying this contract in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation and its basis
The text states that the guarantee obligates production of the person, analogizing it to property guarantee. Locate the qiyas reasoning: 'كما في المال' (as in property).
The analogy shows that any obligation on the principal can be guaranteed.
2
Enumerate valid verbal expressions
List the phrases given: تكفلت بنفسه، برقبته، بروحه، بجسده، برأسه، بنصفه، بثلثه، ضمِنتُه، هو عليّ، أنا به زعيم, etc. Note that mentioning a common part is valid because the self is indivisible.
The principle: any expression denoting the whole in language or custom suffices.
3
Distinguish invalid expressions
The text explicitly excludes 'أنا ضامن بمعرفته' (I guarantee his acquaintance) because it only guarantees knowledge, not production.
This highlights that the guarantee must be for the person's presence, not merely knowing him.
4
Analyze the condition of time
If a delivery time is stipulated, the obligation becomes due at that time, analogous to a deferred debt. The guarantor must produce then.
This is a condition that modifies the obligation's timing.
5
Apply consequences of failure
Failure to produce leads to imprisonment by the ruler after due process: first ascertain reason, then if the person is absent, grant a grace period for travel time.
The ruler's involvement ensures fairness; immediate imprisonment is avoided.

Commentary
This passage is from Kitab al-Kafalah in al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri, dealing with kafalah bi al-nafs (guarantee of the person). The author begins by stating the essence of the obligation: the guarantor (kafil) must produce the guaranteed person (makful bihi) when demanded by the principal (asil, the one who originally owes attendance). This is analogous to kafalah bi al-mal (guarantee of property), where the guarantor must pay the debt. The proof is qiyas: just as attendance is obligatory on the principal, it can be undertaken by a guarantor. 

The text then enumerates the valid verbal expressions for concluding the contract. These include explicit terms like 'takaffaltu bi nafs fulan' (I guarantee the person of so-and-so) or words referring to the whole in reality or custom, such as 'raqabah' (neck), 'ruh' (spirit), 'jasad' (body), 'ra's' (head), 'badan' (body), 'wajh' (face), etc. Even mentioning a common part like 'half' or 'third' is valid because the self is indivisible in the context of guarantee; mentioning a part is like mentioning the whole. Also valid are phrases like 'dhamintuhu' (I guarantee him), 'huwa alayya' (he is upon me), 'ila' (to me), 'ana bihi za'im' (I am his guarantor), 'qabil bihi' (I am surety for him). 

An important exception: if one says 'ana damin bi ma'rifatihi' (I guarantee his acquaintance), this is not a valid kafalah bi al-nafs because the guarantor only committed to knowing him, not to producing him. 

The text then discusses a condition regarding time. If the principal stipulates that the guaranteed person must be delivered at a specific time, the guarantor must produce him when demanded at that time, similar to a deferred debt becoming due. If he fails to produce him, the judge (hakim) may imprison him, but not immediately upon first failure—he should first ascertain the reason. If the guaranteed person himself is absent (i.e., has fled or is not present), the judge grants the guarantor a period equal to the time needed for the person to go and return. If that period expires without production, the judge may imprison the guarantor for refusal to fulfill the right. 

Throughout, the authority is from al-Hidayah, which itself draws from Zahir al-Riwayah. The Hanafi position is clearly that kafalah bi al-nafs is a binding contract with specific requirements and consequences.

Death of the principal extinguishes the surety's obligation to produce him.

This lesson examines a clear and uncontested ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when the principal in a suretyship for the person (kafalah bi al-nafs) dies, the surety is automatically discharged. Confirm consensus or disagreement
All Hanafi authorities agree on this ruling (intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is none). The ruling is muttafaq alayh within the school.
This reflects the principle that ancillary obligations follow the primary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi evidentiary rule in a guarantor-creditor dispute to determine whose statement is accepted and explain why the principal debtor's admission does not bind the guarantor.

Core Idea
When no evidence exists, the guarantor's word with oath prevails over creditor and debtor admissions against him.

This lesson examines a specific procedural rule in Hanafi suretyship (kafalah) regarding disputes over the amount owed. When the guarantor and creditor disagree and no evidence is presented, whose statement is taken? The Hanafi school applies the universal maxim 'al-qawl qawl al-munkir' (the word of the denier with oath). This passage also clarifies that the principal debtor's admission of a larger amount does not affect the guarantor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The ruling is that the guarantor's statement with his oath is accepted regarding the amount he acknowledges when no evidence (bayyinah) is presented. This is the default evidentiary rule in the absence of proof.
The guarantor is the denier (munkir) of any excess claimed by the creditor.
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof is the prophetic hadith: 'al-bayyina 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' (The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier). This hadith is the foundation of the maxim.
The hadith is narrated by Bukhari and Muslim; its transmission is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is definitive (qati al-dalala) in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
In the dispute, the guarantor denies any amount beyond what he acknowledges. Thus he is the denier (munkir). The hadith assigns the oath to the denier, so the guarantor's statement with his oath is accepted.
The creditor is the claimant (mudda'i) and must bring evidence to prove the extra amount.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
This ruling is not disputed within the Hanafi school or across schools; it is an application of a universally accepted legal maxim.
The ruling is classified as muttafaq alayh.
5
Evaluate the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
The practical result is that the guarantor is only liable for the amount he admits unless the creditor provides evidence for a larger amount. The principal debtor's admission of a larger amount does not affect the guarantor's liability.
The principal debtor's admission is binding only on himself; he has no authority over the guarantor.

Core Idea
A guarantor recovers from the principal only if he guaranteed by his order; without order he is a volunteer.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi rules of kafalah (suretyship) concerning the guarantor's right of recourse (ruju'). The key distinction is whether the guarantor acted with the principal debtor's authorization or without it. This distinction determines whether the guarantor can demand reimbursement after paying the creditor. We also explore conditions on the amount recoverable and the timing of recourse.

Guided Path

Identify the two types of kafalah
Read the text and note that kafalah is valid both with the principal's order (kafalah bi-amrihi) and without it (kafalah bi-ghayri amrihi). The permissibility is the same, but the legal consequences differ.
The text explicitly states both are permissible.
2
Understand the ruling for kafalah bi-amrihi
When the guarantor pays with the principal's order, he recovers from the principal what he paid, provided he pays exactly what he guaranteed in quantity and quality. If he pays something different, he recovers what he guaranteed, not what he paid.
The reason: by paying, he owns the debt (like inheritance).
3
Understand the ruling for kafalah bi-ghayri amrihi
Without the principal's order, the guarantor has no right of recourse. He is considered a volunteer (mutabarri'). Therefore, he cannot recover anything from the principal.
The text states: 'And if he gave surety without his order: he does not recover what he pays; because he is a volunteer.'
4
Note the timing of recourse
The guarantor cannot demand payment from the principal before he himself pays the creditor. He does not own the debt until he pays. This is contrasted with an agent for purchase, who can demand reimbursement before payment because he acts as an agent.
The text explicitly mentions this exception.

The passage then details the rules for recourse: If the guarantor pays with the principal's order, he recovers from the principal what he paid, provided he pays exactly what he guaranteed in quantity and quality. If he pays something different (e.g., broken coins instead of sound, or a different currency), he recovers the value of what he guaranteed, not what he actually paid. The reason is that by paying, he owns the debt (like inheriting it) and thus recovers what he guaranteed. This contrasts with a person ordered to pay a debt (like a mere agent), who recovers what he pays because he had no personal obligation. If the guarantor gave surety without the principal's order, he cannot recover anything because he is a volunteer (mutabarri'). Additionally, the guarantor cannot demand the money from the principal before he himself pays the creditor; he does not own the debt until payment. This is distinguished from an agent for purchase (wakil bi al-shira'), who can demand reimbursement before paying because he is acting as an agent and the purchase price is owed from the moment of purchase. The rulings here are foundational to Hanafi contract law and are all from Zahir al-Riwayah.

رجوع (ruju'): The right of the guarantor to demand reimbursement from the principal debtor after paying the creditor.
متبرع (mutabarri'): One who acts voluntarily or charitably without expectation of return; here, a guarantor without order is considered a mutabarri'.

Two Co‑Debtors as Mutual Sureties: Recourse Rules
Explains the ruling on recourse when two co-debtors are mutual sureties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a co‑debtor who pays more than half of the joint debt may recover only the excess payment from the other.

Core Idea
A co‑debtor paying up to half pays as principal; only the excess is recoverable as surety.

When two persons are jointly indebted and each is also a surety for the other, paying off the debt raises a question of who bears the cost. Hanafi law resolves this by priority: what you pay up to your share is your own responsibility; only what you pay beyond that can you claim back. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and shows you how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and the Debt
Two persons, A and B, both owe a debt to a creditor. Each is also a surety (kafeel dhamin) for the other. The debt is joint.
The suretyship is with the order (bi-amrihi) of the other debtor.
2
Determine the Amount Paid
Suppose one debtor pays a certain amount. We need to split that amount into two parts: up to half the total debt, and any excess.
Half of the debt is the individual share of each debtor.
3
Apply the Half Rule
If the payment does not exceed half, the paying debtor cannot recover anything. If it exceeds half, he can recover only the excess.
The rule: لا يرجع حتى يزيد على النصف (he does not have recourse until he exceeds half).

Up to half, the roles of principal debtor and surety conflict. The principal role is given priority because it extinguishes both debt and claim. The surety role does not involve any personal debt.

5
Determine the Recourse Amount
The recourse is only for the amount paid beyond half. For example, total debt = 100, payer pays 60. Half = 50. Excess = 10. So payer recovers 10 from the other.
The payer cannot recover the first 50 because that was his own obligation.
