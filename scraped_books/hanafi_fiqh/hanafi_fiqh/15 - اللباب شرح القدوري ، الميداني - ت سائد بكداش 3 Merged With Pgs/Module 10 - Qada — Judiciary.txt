Guardianship of a Bankrupt Debtor: Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute with proof on bankruptcy guardianship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hajr of a bankrupt debtor, identify the dalil for each, and state the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa forbids guardianship over a bankrupt debtor to preserve legal capacity; the Sahibayn allow it, and this is the fatwa.

This lesson explores the Hanafi internal disagreement on whether a judge can place a bankrupt debtor under legal guardianship (hajr). The source presents Abu Hanifa's reasoning, the Sahibayn's opposing view, and the eventual tarjih. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to identify the operative ruling in a madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What is the specific question being debated? It is whether the judge can restrict a bankrupt debtor from transacting (sell/buy) to protect creditors.
The term used is 'hajr' (legal guardianship/interdiction).
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that hajr is impermissible because it wastes the debtor's legal capacity (ahliyyah). He supports this with Quran 4:29, which invalidates trade without mutual consent.
The ruler selling the debtor's property without consent would be such trade.
3
Understand the objection and response
An objector raises the case of a slave: the master can restrict the slave's transactions. The Hanafi response: the slave's capacity is disregarded due to disbelief (origin of slavery), which does not apply to a free believer.
This clarifies that hajr is not a general tool but is specific to certain incapacities.
4
Recognize the Sahibayn's view and the tarjih mechanism
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) permit hajr on the bankrupt debtor. The text notes that later authorities (Burhani, Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, al-Baalbaki) give fatwa on their view, which is also a narration from Abu Hanifa.
This tarjih is based on maslahah (protection of creditors) and is the relied-upon position.
5
Apply the mufta bihi to a practical scenario
If a debtor has assets but refuses to sell, the judge may impose hajr and sell the property directly to satisfy debts. Imprisonment alone (Abu Hanifa's view) is not the operative practice.
The practical difference is whether the judge acts as seller or merely compels the debtor to sell.

Commentary
This passage addresses the issue of placing a bankrupt debtor under legal guardianship (hajr). The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute with a proof. It begins by stating Abu Hanifa's view: he does not impose guardianship on the bankrupt person. Then it mentions that the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) have a different view, and that the fatwa is given according to their view, which is also reportedly a narration from Abu Hanifa. The text explains that if a person is in debt and creditors demand his imprisonment and guardianship, Abu Hanifa refuses guardianship because it would nullify his legal capacity (ahliyyah), which cannot be done for a specific harm like the creditor's loss. An objection is raised: why is guardianship allowed for a slave due to his master? The response is that the slave's humanity is disregarded because of his disbelief (i.e., slavery originated from disbelief, otherwise a believer cannot be enslaved originally). Then the text continues: if the debtor has property, the ruler does not dispose of it (sell it) because that would be a form of guardianship or non-consensual trade, which is invalid according to the Quranic verse prohibiting consuming wealth wrongfully (4:29). Instead, the ruler imprisons the debtor indefinitely until he sells his property himself to repay his debts. The imprisonment is a means to compel him to fulfill his obligation, since repaying debts is obligatory and procrastination is oppression. Thus, the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is to imprison the bankrupt debtor to force repayment, but not to place him under guardianship to sell his property. The fatwa is on the view of the two companions (which is also a narration from Abu Hanifa).

المفلس (al-muflis): A bankrupt person whose debts exceed their assets.

Imprisonment of the Debtor in Hanafi Fiqh: Contractual vs. Non-Contractual Debts and Procedure
Hanafi ruling on detention of debtor for contractual and non-contractual debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on judicial detention of debtors, distinguishing between contractual and non-contractual debts, and explain the procedure for investigation, release, and post-release surveillance.

Core Idea
A debtor who incurred debt by contract is imprisoned automatically; for other debts, imprisonment requires proof of wealth; if proven insolvent, he must be released.

This lesson from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents the Hanafi position on when a debtor may be imprisoned by the court. The ruling hinges on the nature of the debt and the default assumptions about the debtor's solvency. You will learn the key distinctions and the procedural steps a judge follows to balance the rights of creditors with the Quranic command to grant respite to the insolvent.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between contractual and non-contractual debts
Identify which debts arise from a contract (e.g., mahr, kafalah) and which arise from other causes (e.g., compensation for usurped property, blood money). This is the starting point for the ruling.
Contractual debts imply the debtor was solvent at the time of undertaking the obligation.
2
State the rule for each category
For contractual debts, the debtor is imprisoned without further proof. For non-contractual debts, the debtor is not imprisoned unless the creditor provides evidence (bayyinah) that the debtor has wealth.
The text says 'he does not detain him' (لم يحبسه) in non-contractual cases.
3
Explain the rationale
The contract itself is evidence of solvency when the debt was incurred. For non-contractual debts, the default assumption is insolvency (al-asl huwa al-i'sar), so detention is not justified until proven otherwise.
This rational proof (qiyas) is the dalil for the distinction.
4
Describe the process of investigation and release
The judge detains the debtor for a period (discretionary, typically 2-3 months), investigates his condition via neighbors, and if convinced of insolvency, releases him. The Quranic command (al-nazirah) obligates release.
Evidence of poverty after detention is accepted; before detention, the majority do not accept it.
5
Apply post-release rights of creditors
After release, creditors may keep the debtor under surveillance but cannot restrict his daily activities or enter his home without permission. The creditor may choose surveillance over continued detention.
This balances the debtor's freedom with the creditor's right to recover the debt.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri deals with the Hanafi ruling on detaining a debtor (المفلس). The author begins by distinguishing between two types of debts. For debts incurred by a contract (such as mahr in marriage and kafalah in suretyship), the debtor is imprisoned because the very act of entering such a contract indicates that he was solvent and capable of fulfilling the obligation at the time of the contract. For other debts, such as compensation for usurped property (عوض المغصوب) or blood money (أرش الجنايات), the default assumption is that the debtor is insolvent (الأصل هو الإعسار). Therefore, he is not imprisoned unless the creditor proves with evidence that the debtor has concealed wealth. The judge is to believe his claim of poverty in the latter case.

Next, the author addresses the duration of detention. This is not fixed; the judge has discretion based on the circumstances, and the correct madhhab position as stated in multiple principal works is that the precise duration is left to the judge's ijtihad.

The process for release is then explained. If, after investigation (e.g., asking neighbors), the judge becomes convinced that the debtor has no wealth, he must release him, based on the Quranic command (النظرة إلى ميسرة, Surah al-Baqarah 2:280) to grant respite until ease. Similarly, if the debtor produces evidence of his poverty after detention, his evidence is accepted in one reliable narration (رواية واحدة) and he is freed. However, if he brings such evidence before detention, the majority of scholars do not accept it, because this would preempt the judge's process.

Finally, the passage discusses the rights of creditors after the debtor's release. They may keep him under surveillance to prevent his disappearance, but they cannot prevent him from conducting his daily business (buying, selling, traveling). They cannot force entry into his home; they must wait outside. If the debtor prefers to be detained rather than watched, the creditor has the choice (الخيار للطالب) to choose surveillance or detention.

Key Terms
الحجر (al-hajr): Legal interdiction or detention of a debtor by a judge to compel payment or investigate insolvency.
المفلس (al-muflis): The insolvent debtor who is unable to pay his debts and is subject to judicial detention.
النظرة (al-nazirah): The Quranic command to grant respite to a debtor in difficulty until he becomes able to pay.
البينة (al-bayyinah): Clear evidence, typically testimony of two male witnesses or one male and two females, used to prove the debtor's wealth or poverty.

Exception of Value in Confession: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on exception in confession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on exception of value in confession, identify the scholars on each side, and explain the basis of istihsan overriding qiyas.

Core Idea
Istihsan permits exception of a known value in confession, overriding qiyas that would render it invalid.

In the Book of Confession, al-Quduri presents a ruling that if a person says, 'I owe him one hundred dirhams except the value of a dinar,' he is liable for the net amount. This ruling, as the commentator explains, is based on istihsan and is adopted by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. However, qiyas would require the exception to be invalid, a view held by Muhammad and Zufar. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and identifies the relied-upon ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a confession (iqrar) in which the person states a debt amount and then excepts a known value. The ruling in the matn is that the confession is valid and binding for the net amount (i.e., the hundred minus the excepted value).
The excepted item must be a known value like a dinar or a qafiz.
2
Locate the two positions
Al-Isbijabi identifies the foundational reasoning: the rule is based on istihsan, which is the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. The alternative qiyas position, held by Muhammad and Zufar, holds that the exception is invalid, making the full amount binding.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: the three senior imams split 2 vs 2.
3
Understand the qiyas reasoning
The qiyas analogizes this confession to a normal confession without exception. In a normal confession, the affirmation is unconditional. Adding an exception introduces a condition that contradicts the affirmation, creating uncertainty (jahalah). Therefore, the exception should be invalid, and the entire amount is due.
The illa (effective cause) is the contradiction/uncertainty.
4
Understand the istihsan reasoning
Istihsan overrides qiyas because of custom and necessity. In transactions, people commonly except a known value, and the value is determinable. The exception does not create total uncertainty because the value can be appraised. Hence, the confession is valid for the net amount.
Istihsan is a preferred legal reasoning over strict analogy.
5
Note the mufta bihi
Later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi confirm that the correct position is istihsan. This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) ruling for fatwa.
The text states 'wa al-sahih jawab al-istihsan' and attributes it to those scholars.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of an exception (istithna') in a confession (iqrar). The matn states that if a person says 'I owe him one hundred dirhams except the value of a dinar,' then he is liable for the hundred dirhams minus that value. The commentator explains that this ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference), followed by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position, however, is the istihsan, as confirmed by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi. The reasoning is that istihsan allows flexibility in transactions where the object of debt is determined by value rather than specific items.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): A statement that acknowledges a right or debt against oneself.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that departs from qiyas for a stronger reason (e.g., custom or necessity).
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning that extends a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause.

Interpretation of Conjunctive 'Waw' in Numerical Confessions
Ruling on 'waw' in numerical confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that the conjunctive 'waw' in 'one hundred and a dirham' is interpreted as explanatory due to custom ('urf), and apply this principle to similar cases involving measured and weighed items.

Core Idea
When custom dictates, the 'waw' in 'one hundred and a dirham' acts as an explanation, not a conjunction — all hundred are dirhams.

The Hanafi school often uses custom ('urf) to resolve ambiguities in speech. In this lesson, we examine a classic example: a confession of 'one hundred and a dirham' (مائة ودرهم). The apparent conjunctive 'waw' is not adding a separate item but explaining that the hundred are dirhams. This interpretation is based on common usage, as people find it burdensome to repeat the word 'dirham'. The same principle extends to other goods frequently traded by measure or weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confession phrase
The source text gives the phrase 'له علي مائة ودرهم' (I owe him one hundred and a dirham). Note the grammatical 'waw'.
2
Analyze the grammatical role of 'waw'
Normally 'waw' is conjunctive, but here custom gives it an explanatory meaning. How do we know? Because people typically mean the hundred are dirhams.
3
Apply the custom-based interpretation
Understand that the ruling is based on 'urf (custom). The 'waw' is considered 'atf bayani (explanatory conjunction). This prevents the need to repeat the word.
4
Extend to other commodities
The text mentions that the same applies to measured and weighed items (like foodstuffs, precious metals) because they are commonly established as debts in salam and loans. Recognize this as a qiyas from the original case.
5
Evaluate the underlying reasoning (talil)
The reason ('illa) is frequency of use and the burdensome nature of repetition. This also applies to dinars and other fungible goods.

This passage clarifies a critical nuance: the dispute is not about the permissibility (jawaz) of agency in litigation—both sides agree it is permissible—but about whether it is binding (lazim) on the opponent. Abu Hanifa requires the opponent's consent for the agency to be binding; without consent, the opponent can refuse and demand the principal appear personally. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider the agency binding regardless of opponent's consent. The text notes confusion about Muhammad's position, but the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is the view of the two (Sahibayn) that it is binding. An exception is made if the principal is sick and cannot attend, in which case even Abu Hanifa's requirement of consent may be waived (the agent can proceed). The rule applies equally to all persons regardless of status, gender, or marital history.

Appointing an Agent Without Opponent's Consent: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Explaining the intra-Hanafi dispute over agency without opponent consent and the preferred view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on appointing an agent without the opponent's consent, distinguish the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn permit agency without the opponent's consent in all cases; their view is the operative ruling today.

Litigation often requires personal attendance, but what if the plaintiff is absent, traveling, or a secluded woman? Abu Hanifa restricted the power to appoint an agent without the opponent's consent to these specific cases, while the Sahibayn opened it more broadly. This lesson explores the intra-Hanafi dispute and the final adopted view that now governs fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Recognize that the question is whether the opposing party's consent is required for a valid agency appointment in litigation. The passage focuses on the exceptions outlined by Abu Hanifa and the general permission of the Sahibayn.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa held that agency without the opponent's consent is only valid if (1) the opponent is absent for a journey of three days or more, (2) the principal intends to travel, or (3) the principal is a secluded woman (mukhadarah) unaccustomed to appearing in court.
These are the exceptions where consent is waived.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) held that agency without the opponent's consent is permissible in all cases, without restriction. Their view is based on a broader principle of facilitating litigation.
They do not condition permissibility on any particular circumstance.
4
Analyze the later tarjih
Later authorities (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'a, Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili, Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar, Abu al-Layth, and the author of Mukhtarat al-Nawazil) preferred the Sahibayn's view. Their reasoning is considered stronger in every book.
The text cites multiple scholars and works endorsing the Sahibayn.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The operative ruling for fatwa is that the opponent's consent is not required to appoint an agent in any case. This simplifies the process and protects the rights of litigants who may be unable to attend court.
This is the position now relied upon in Hanafi fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the conditions under which a person may appoint an agent without the consent of the opposing party in litigation. The text presents the view of Abu Hanifa (implied by the initial statement) and the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The matn (likely from al-Quduri) states that if the opponent is absent for a journey of three days or more, or if the principal is a person intending travel, or a secluded woman who does not normally appear in public, then the appointment of an agent is valid without the opponent's consent. Abu Hanifa apparently required the opponent's consent in other cases, while the Sahibayn allowed it generally. The commentary then cites 'al-Tashih' that a group of later scholars preferred the view of the Sahibayn, including al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'a, and Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili, and that his proof is preferred in every book. The preferred view (mufta bihi) for fatwa is the position of the Sahibayn, allowing agency without the opponent's consent in these circumstances.
