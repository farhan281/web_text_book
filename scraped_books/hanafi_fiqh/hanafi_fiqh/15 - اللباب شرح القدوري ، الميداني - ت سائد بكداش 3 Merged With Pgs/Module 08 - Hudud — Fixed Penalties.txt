Slave's Acknowledgment of Hadd or Qisas and Validity of His Divorce
Ruling on slave's criminal acknowledgment and divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's acknowledgment of hadd or qisas and explain why his divorce is valid, using the reasoning from the text.

Core Idea
A slave's acknowledgment of personal crimes and his divorce are valid because he retains freedom in blood matters and marriage is his personal contract.

This lesson clarifies two independent rulings about a slave's legal capacity in Hanafi fiqh. First, when a slave admits to a hadd crime or qisas, his confession is binding immediately, without needing the master's consent. Second, his divorce is valid and takes effect. The reasoning hinges on the distinction between actions that affect the master's property rights and those that are purely personal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two separate rulings
The passage gives two distinct rulings: acknowledgment of hadd/qisas is immediately binding, and divorce is valid. Note that each has its own rationale.
These are independent; do not confuse them.
2
Understand the rationale for criminal acknowledgment
The text says the slave is 'left upon the original freedom regarding blood.' This means that for bodily and criminal matters, the slave is treated as free. Hence his acknowledgment is binding.
This is a key principle: in blood-related matters, the slave's original freedom is preserved.
3
Understand the rationale for divorce
Divorce is a personal act of the husband. It does not affect the master's ownership or benefits, so the slave's capacity is unrestricted.
The master has no ownership over the wife, so divorce does not diminish the master's property.
4
Apply the rulings to a hypothetical
Imagine a slave who acknowledges a hadd crime. The judge proceeds immediately without the master's consent. Similarly, if he divorces his wife, it is effective.
This shows the practical independence of the slave in these domains.
5
Distinguish from property matters
In contrast, a slave's acknowledgment of property debts may not be binding because it affects the master's ownership. This distinction is key.
The ruling only applies to personal criminal and marital actions, not to financial transactions involving the master's property.

General Rule of Agency and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Litigation in Hudud
States general rule of agency and intra-Hanafi dispute on litigation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule of agency in Hanafi fiqh, apply it to litigation, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding delegation of proof for hadd and qisas.

Core Idea
Delegation is permissible for all rights a person can claim, but proving hudud remains contested.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar establishes the foundational rule for agency (wakalah): any action that a person may legally perform personally may be delegated to another. The author then applies this rule to litigation and discusses whether it extends to proving fixed punishments (hudud) and retaliation (qisas). An intra-Hanafi dispute emerges between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on this specific application. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school balances general principles with the special nature of certain rights.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule of agency
Identify the maxim 'Every contract that a person may conclude himself, it is permissible for him to authorize another to do so.' Recognize its rationale: to fulfill needs when a person cannot act personally.
This is a kulli (universal) rule in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Apply the rule to litigation
The passage states that delegation is allowed for litigation (filing suits and defending) and for proving rights. Note the exception: execution of penalties is postponed.
Litigation includes both claims and defenses.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Al-Isbijabi reports that Abu Hanifa permits delegation of proof for all rights, including hadd and qisas. Abu Yusuf opposes this specifically for proving hadd and qisas.
Muhammad al-Shaybani often agrees with Abu Yusuf on this point.
4
Analyze the proofs of each side
Abu Hanifa relies on the general rule and the need for facilitation. Abu Yusuf argues that hudud are rights of Allah requiring personal testimony and cannot be delegated.
The dispute is about proof, not execution.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, a principal can hire an agent to prove a hadd claim in court. Under Abu Yusuf, the principal must personally testify for the hadd portion. For financial rights, both agree delegation is permissible.
The dispute specifically concerns proving the crime, not claiming compensation.

Agency in Execution of Hudud and Qisas: The Condition of Presence
States the ruling on agency for execution of rights with exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency in execution of hudud and qisas, identify the condition of the principal's presence, and explain the underlying rationale (shubha al-afw) based on the principle that hudud are repelled by doubts.

Core Idea
Agency for hudud execution is invalid if the principal is absent because the doubt of pardon repels the hadd.

Agency (wakalah) is a broad contract that permits one to act on behalf of another in various matters, including the execution (istifa') of established rights. However, not all rights can be executed by an agent without restriction. This lesson examines an important exception: hudud (fixed penalities) and qisas (retribution). Why would Islamic law restrict agency in such serious matters? The answer lies in the delicate nature of these penalties and the principle that doubts avert their application. We will explore the precise condition that makes agency valid or invalid.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and the exception
Read the first part of the passage: 'ويجوز التوكيل بالاستيفاء إلا في الحدود والقصاص'. Identify that agency for execution is generally permissible, but hudud and qisas are excepted. Note that the exception is not absolute; it is qualified by a condition.
The exception does not say 'never' but is tied to a specific scenario.
2
Identify the condition that invalidates agency
Find the phrase 'مع غيبة الموكل عن المجلس'. The text explicitly states that agency is invalid when the principal (muwakkil) is absent from the court (majlis). This absence triggers the invalidity. Conversely, presence validates the agency.
The condition is spatial and temporal: absence from the court session at the time of execution.
3
Extract the causal reasoning (ta'lil)
The passage provides the reason: 'لأنها تندرى بالشبهات، وشبهة العفو ثابتة حال غيبته'. Understand that hudud are repelled by doubts, and when the principal is absent, there is a doubt that he may have pardoned the offender. This doubt prevents the application of the hadd or qisas.
For qisas, pardon (afw) is a clear right of the victim or heirs. For hudud, although pardon is not generally accepted, in some cases (e.g., theft before reporting to authorities) forgiveness can affect the penalty. The Hanafi school considers the mere possibility of pardon as a doubt.
4
Contrast with the state of presence
The passage adds 'بخلاف حالة الحضرة؛ لانتفاء الشبهة'. When the principal is present, the doubt of pardon is removed – he would have explicitly declared his intent. Therefore, agency is valid.
This shows the ruling is not a blanket prohibition but a conditional one.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Consider a case: A thief is convicted of hadd theft. The victim appoints an agent to request execution of the hand-cutting penalty, but the victim is out of the country. According to the ruling, the agency is invalid because the doubt of pardon exists. The judge should not proceed until the victim is present or explicitly confirms no pardon.
This illustrates the practical importance of the condition.

Can a Judge Compel Surety in Hudud? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compelling surety in hadd and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on compelling kafala bil-nafs in hudud and qisas and distinguish between pure hudud and hadd al-qadhf based on the presence of private right.

Core Idea
The judge cannot compel surety in pure hudud; compulsion is allowed only when a private right exists, as in hadd al-qadhf.

This passage from Lubab al-Muharrar presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the judge's authority to compel an accused to provide a surety (kafala bil-nafs) in hadd and qisas cases. Abu Hanifa held that compulsion is impermissible in pure hudud, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allowed it in certain cases. Later scholars like al-Nasafi and al-Mahbubi preferred Abu Hanifa's view, establishing the mufta bihi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Recognize that the dispute is about the judge's power to compel an accused to produce a surety (kafala bil-nafs) in hudud and qisas cases, not about the permissibility of voluntary surety.
The matn says 'Abu Hanifa, and they (the two) said: It is permissible.' The dispute is on compulsion.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa: judge cannot compel. The reason is that pure hudud (like theft, zina) involve only God's right, and averting hudud is preferred. Compulsion would contradict this principle.
Hadd al-qadhf is an exception because it includes a private right.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: judge can compel in hadd al-qadhf, and initially they held the judge can demand surety in all hudud and qisas. Their reasoning: the private right in qadhf justifies compulsion.
Their initial view is broader, but later settled on restricting compulsion to cases with private right.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In a theft case (pure hadd), the judge cannot force the accused to provide a surety. In a slander case (qadhf), the judge can compel.
This difference affects how cases are processed.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later scholars (al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi) selected Abu Hanifa's view as the operative position: no compulsion except where private right exists.
The authority_weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin.

The Permissibility of Sulh Over Claims and Its Prohibition for Hudud
Ruling on permissible and impermissible sulh claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which types of claims can be settled by sulh and which cannot, and explain the Hanafi reasoning based on the nature of rights (right of men vs. right of Allah).

Core Idea
Sulh is valid for property and injury claims but not for hadd, because hadd is a right of Allah that cannot be compromised.

Sulh (amicable settlement) is a flexible contract in Hanafi fiqh, but its scope is limited by the nature of the right involved. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies where sulh is permissible—property, benefits, and injury claims—and where it is absolutely forbidden: claims of hadd (fixed punishments). The reasoning rests on whether the right belongs to individuals or to Allah, and whether the claim is for a fixed monetary amount or not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissible claims
List the categories for which sulh is allowed: property (amwal), benefits (manafi'), intentional injury (jinayat al-amd), and accidental injury (jinayat al-khata').
Benefits are analogous to lease (ijara); intentional injury includes qisas.
2
Understand the reasoning for intentional injury
For intentional injury, the claim is for qisas, which is a right of the victim. Since qisas is not a fixed monetary right, the parties can agree on any amount, even exceeding the diyah.
The text says: 'القصاص ليس بمال' (qisas is not wealth).
3
Understand the limitation for accidental injury
For accidental injury, the claim is for diyah, which is fixed by shari'ah. Settling for more than the prescribed amount is invalid, and the excess must be returned.
Diyah is a fixed monetary right; increasing it would annul the shari'ah determination.
4
Grasp the prohibition for hudud
Hadd punishments are rights of Allah (huquq Allah). Since they are not owned by any individual, sulh is absolutely prohibited—no compensation can be taken to drop a hadd claim.
This is a key distinction between rights of Allah and rights of men.

And sulh is not permissible from a claim of hadd because it is the right of Allah, and it is not permissible to take compensation for the right of another.)
Source pages p. 410

Commentary
This passage summarizes the scope of sulh. Sulh is valid for claims involving property (amwal) and benefits (manafi'), because these are similar to sale and lease. It is also valid for claims arising from intentional (amd) and accidental (khata') injury, whether involving life (nafs) or body parts less than life (ma duna). For intentional injury (qisas), the claimant can settle for a sum, and even exceed the diyah amount since qisas is not a fixed monetary right. For accidental injury, the claim is essentially for diyah (blood money), which is fixed; thus settling for more than the prescribed diyah is not valid, and any excess must be returned. However, sulh is absolutely not allowed for claims of hadd (fixed punishments like for theft or fornication) because these are rights of Allah (haqq Allah), and it is not permissible to accept compensation to drop such a claim. This is a major distinction between rights of Allah and rights of men in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
جناية العمد (jinayat al-amd): Intentional injury, such as murder or battery.
جناية الخطأ (jinayat al-khata'): Accidental injury, where the penalty is diyah (blood money) fixed by law.
حد (hadd): Fixed punishment prescribed by shari'ah for crimes like theft, fornication, etc.
دية (diyah): Blood money, a fixed compensation for killing or injury.
