Definition of Waqf and the Intra-Hanafi Ownership Dispute
Defines waqf and presents intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of waqf according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, explain the consequences on binding nature, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Waqf is detention of property; Abu Hanifa retains ownership, Sahibayn transfer it to Allah – changing binding effect.

This passage introduces the chapter on Waqf (endowment) and links it to gift. Both are voluntary transfers, but gift conveys both corpus and benefit, while waqf conveys only benefit. The key legal question is: who owns the corpus after waqf? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the waqif retains ownership, so the waqf is revocable unless a judge rules it binding. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that ownership transfers to Allah, making the waqf immediately binding. Understanding this distinction is crucial for the legal validity and irrevocability of endowments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions
Locate the linguistic meaning 'detention' and then the two legal definitions. Note that Abu Hanifa defines waqf as detention of the corpus on the waqif's ownership with charity of benefit; the Sahibayn define it as detention of both corpus and benefit under Allah's ownership.
The legal definition is the source of the dispute.
2
Contrast the positions on ownership
Abu Hanifa: waqif retains milk (ownership), therefore waqf is not binding (lazim) – he can retract and sell. Sahibayn: ownership is transferred to Allah, so waqf is immediately binding and irrevocable.
This is a clear intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Examine the exception for judge's ruling
The text notes that even according to Abu Hanifa, if a judge rules the waqf binding after a dispute, it becomes binding. This is because the judge's ijtihad can make it binding.
The judge's ruling transforms a non-binding waqf into a binding one under Abu Hanifa's view.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn – waqf is binding. However, the text also acknowledges the mechanism of judge's ruling to achieve binding under Abu Hanifa's view.
Most later Hanafi works adopt the Sahibayn's view as mufta bihi.

Definition of Waqf and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Bindingness
Defines waqf and presents the three Hanafi imams' positions on its binding nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on the bindingness of waqf and identify the practical consequence of each.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, waqf is not automatically binding—it depends on the imam's view.

Waqf (endowment) is a significant act of charity, but its legal effect varies among the Hanafi imams. This passage from Al-Lubab defines waqf and presents the differing views on when the endower's ownership is transferred—if at all. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for correctly applying waqf rulings and avoiding errors in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions of waqf
Note the linguistic meaning (habs) and the legal definitions according to Abu Hanifa (detention of corpus in endower's ownership) and the two companions (detention of both corpus and benefit under Allah's ownership).
The definitional difference underlies the dispute on bindingness.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that ownership does not leave the endower unless either a judge rules it binding or the waqf is tied to the endower's death (ta'liq bi al-mawt).
Without these, the waqf is revocable and the property can be sold.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that ownership leaves by mere utterance of the declaration, making the waqf immediately binding.
This view is preferred in later fatwa practice.
4
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad holds that ownership leaves only after four conditions are met: appointment of a trustee with delivery, specific property, no retained benefit for the endower, and perpetuity.
These conditions ensure the waqf is fully effected.
5
Extract the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a scenario: the endower declares a waqf but later wants to sell the property. Under Abu Hanifa, sale is allowed before a judge's ruling; under Abu Yusuf, it is not allowed; under Muhammad, it depends on whether the conditions are fulfilled.
This demonstrates the real-world impact of each position.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab begins the chapter on Waqf (endowment), explaining its definition and then detailing the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding when the waqf becomes binding and ownership is transferred. The author first connects waqf to hibah (gift) by noting both are donations, but hibah is preferred because it donates both substance and benefit. The definition: linguistically, waqf means 'detention' (habs); legally, according to Abu Hanifa, it is the detention of the substance (the corpus) in the ownership of the endower while the benefit is given as charity (i.e., usufruct goes to beneficiaries). According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani), it is the detention of both substance and benefit under the ownership of Allah. The core issue: does the endower retain ownership? Abu Hanifa says ownership does not leave the endower (i.e., the waqf is non-binding, revocable) unless either (1) a judge (the ruler) rules it binding, or (2) the endower ties it to his own death (ta'liq bi al-mawt), making it like a will that takes effect from one-third after death. The text then gives the reasons: the judge is a mujtahid, and the ruling resolves the dispute. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership transfers immediately upon utterance (the declaration of waqf) regardless of conditions. Muhammad holds that four conditions must be met: (1) appointing a trustee (wali) and handing over the property; (2) the waqf property must be specifically identified (mufrazan); (3) the endower must not retain any benefit for himself; (4) the waqf must be perpetual (mu'abbad), usually by ending with the poor. The passage then clarifies that when the waqf is tied to death, it is treated like a will, binding from one-third and only after death. The correct position (mufta bihi) appears to be that waqf is binding only with one of these two exceptions according to Abu Hanifa, but this is later reconciled with the other positions. The teacher should note that this difference affects practical questions: under Abu Hanifa's view, the endower can sell the property before the judge's ruling or his death; under Abu Yusuf, it is immediately binding; under Muhammad, it requires delivery and conditions. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school today is closer to Abu Yusuf's view with some conditions, but this text presents all three positions.

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Linguistically: detention (habs). Legally according to Abu Hanifa: detention of the substance (corpus) in the ownership of the endower, with the benefit given as charity. According to the two companions: detention of both substance and benefit under the rule of Allah's ownership.
الحبس (al-habs): Linguistically: detention or confinement. It is the root meaning of waqf.

Mu'allaq Bi-Mawt Waqf and Ownership Transfer
Explains mu'allaq bi-mawt ruling and ownership dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waqf suspended upon death and compare the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on when ownership leaves the waqif.

Core Idea
A waqf suspended upon death is like a will – binding only after death and limited to one-third; ownership transfer is disputed among the three imams.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains the ruling on a waqf that is conditioned on the waqif's death (mu'allaq bi-mawt). It clarifies that such a waqf is treated like a will (wasiyyah), becoming effective only after death and limited to one-third of the estate. However, the three founding imams of the Hanafi school disagreed on when the waqif's ownership ceases, leading to significant practical differences. Understanding this dispute is essential for correctly applying waqf rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on mu'allaq bi-mawt waqf
The text states the correct view (al-sahih) that a waqf suspended upon death is like a will: it becomes binding only after death and is limited to one-third of the estate. This is the position attributed to Abu Hanifa.
This contrasts with immediate waqf, which according to some views is binding immediately.
2
Locate the three positions on ownership transfer
Abu Hanifa (through the correct view): ownership does not cease before death. Abu Yusuf: ownership leaves immediately upon utterance. Muhammad: ownership leaves only after four conditions are fulfilled (trustee, segregation, no reserved benefit, perpetuity).
The text does not quote a direct dalil for each, but the positions are clearly stated.
3
Compare the conditions for validity
Abu Yusuf requires no conditions beyond utterance. Muhammad requires four conditions. The text notes that the third condition (no benefit for the waqif) has no clear transmission from Muhammad and will be discussed later.
This illustrates the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on the threshold for waqf validity.
4
Determine the fatwa preference
The text reports that the fatwa is on the saying of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding the permissibility of the waqf leaving the waqif's ownership. This means the operative position is that the waqf can remove ownership, contrary to Abu Hanifa's view that the waqif retains ownership.
The fatwa does not specify which of the two is preferred on all subsidiary issues.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri covers two interconnected issues: the ruling on waqf suspended upon death (mu'allaq bi-mawt) and the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding when the waqif's ownership ceases. The author first states the madhhab's correct position: a waqf conditioned on death is treated like a will – it becomes binding only after death and is limited to one-third of the estate. This is the view of Abu Hanifa. He then presents the disagreement of the two main disciples: Abu Yusuf holds that ownership leaves the waqif immediately upon uttering the waqf statement, whether or not the property is handed over to a trustee or the destination is specified perpetually. Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage from Lubab al-Quduri addresses the validity of endowing an undivided share (musha') in jointly owned property. The Condition of Perpetuity in Waqf: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on perpetuity in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding explicit mention of perpetuity in waqf and identify the practical consequence of each position.

Core Idea
Perpetuity is a condition for waqf by consensus; the dispute is whether it must be explicitly stated.

Waqf is a perpetual dedication. But what happens if the donor specifies a temporary destination like 'for my children only'? When a person endows exactly half of a property, the waqf and the owner become co-owners. Any partition would require the waqf to be both a party and the one being partitioned, which is legally impossible. This lesson explains the two methods the judge may use to resolve this.

In Hanafi waqf, the preferred view is that ownership is removed only after actual delivery (taslim) to the beneficiaries, not by mere utterance.

Delivery of Waqf Properties: The Role of Custom in Appointing a Mutawalli
Discussion on delivery of mosque and graveyard waqf to custodian

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi views on delivery of a graveyard waqf and explain how custom determines the ruling.

Core Idea
For a graveyard waqf, delivery to a mutawalli is valid when custom supports appointment, even if not customary.

This passage from al-Hidayah discusses whether a graveyard waqf requires formal delivery to a caretaker (mutawalli) like a mosque, or whether an appointed caretaker can receive it. The text presents two views and concludes that custom ('urf) determines the validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqf property types
Note that the text discusses a mosque and a graveyard as types of waqf property. For mosques, it is assumed that no active mutawalli is needed.
2
Understand the first view
One view treats the graveyard like a mosque: no delivery to a mutawalli is required because the mosque has no caretaker by default.
This view implies that the graveyard is self-maintaining or that its upkeep is a communal responsibility.
3
Understand the second view
Another view treats the graveyard like public facilities (water station, caravanserai) where a mutawalli can be appointed and delivery is valid.
This view acknowledges practical needs like sweeping and closing doors.
4
Analyze the role of custom
The text states that if a mutawalli is appointed, delivery is valid even if contrary to normal custom. Custom ('urf) is the deciding factor.
Custom can override the default presumption that no mutawalli is needed.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
The preferred position (mufta bihi) is that delivery to a mutawalli is valid when supported by custom, regardless of the property type.
This reflects the Hanafi principle of giving weight to 'urf.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): A charitable endowment, where property is held in perpetuity for religious or philanthropic purposes.
متولي (mutawalli): The custodian or manager of a waqf property, responsible for its upkeep and distribution of benefits.
تسليم (tasleem): Physical delivery or handing over of the waqf property to the mutawalli.
عرف (urf): Customary practice that is considered a source of law in the Hanafi school when not conflicting with primary texts.
