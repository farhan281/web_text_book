Estimation of Nafaqah During Iddah Based on Husband's Income Cycle
Ruling on estimation of nafaqah during iddah per husband's profession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the amount of sustenance during iddah for different types of earners and apply the principle of variability based on income cycle.

Core Idea
The nafaqah of a woman in iddah varies with the husband's income cycle, not a fixed amount.

How much maintenance should a woman receive during her iddah? The answer is not a single number; it depends on how the husband earns his living. This passage from the Hanafi tradition explains the principle: the amount is estimated based on the period it takes for the husband to have access to his wealth. Let's examine the categories laid out by the scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the payment of nafaqah (maintenance) to a woman during her iddah period. The ruling is that it is wajib, but the amount is not fixed globally.
Nafaqah includes food, clothing, and lodging.
2
Classify the husband's profession into the four categories
The text mentions four types: muhtarif (craftsman/laborer), sahib al-ghallah (rental income earner), sahib al-diya' (landowner), and sahib al-tijarah (businessman). Each has a different income cycle.
These are exemplary categories; the principle applies analogically.
3
Determine the income cycle for each category
The muhtarif earns daily, the sahib al-ghallah receives rent monthly, the sahib al-diya' harvests yearly, and the sahib al-tijarah's capital cycles periodically (e.g., quarterly, semi-annually).
The cycle is the period after which the person typically has disposable income.
4
State the nafaqah period derived from the cycle
The muhtarif provides for one day at a time, the sahib al-ghallah for one month, the sahib al-diya' for one year, and the sahib al-tijarah for the duration until his capital returns.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
5
Apply the principle to a hypothetical scenario
If a woman's husband is a carpenter (muhtarif), she should receive enough for one day each day. If he is a shopkeeper with daily sales, similar ruling. The key is the frequency of income receipt.
This prevents hardship by not demanding a lump sum for an entire period.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on estimating the amount of maintenance (nafaqah) for a woman during her iddah period. The heading 'Kitab al-Hibah' (Book of Gift) appears but the content actually belongs to the discussion of nafaqah post-divorce. The ruling states that the amount is not fixed globally because people's circumstances differ. Instead, it depends on the husband's profession and income cycle. A craftsman (muhtarif) earning daily should provide for one day at a time. A property owner (sahib al-ghallah) receiving monthly rent should provide for a month. A landowner (sahib al-diya') harvesting yearly should provide for a year. A businessman (sahib al-tijarah) should provide based on his capital turnover period. This is the relied-upon position from Al-Hidayah, based on the principle that maintenance is paid as means become available, not far in advance.

Key Terms
المحترف (al-muhtarif): A craftsman or laborer who earns daily wages.
صاحب الغلة (sahib al-ghallah): A person who owns property generating monthly rental income.
صاحب الضياع (sahib al-diya'): A landowner whose harvest is annual.
صاحب التجارة (sahib al-tijarah): A businessman whose capital cycles periodically.

Maintenance for Dependents of the Missing Person
Establishes the rule for spending on dependents of a missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle for maintenance of a missing person's dependents and identify which dependents are entitled without a court order.

Core Idea
Those entitled to maintenance without a court order when present are also entitled in absence; others are not.

When a person goes missing, their dependents need financial support. The Hanafi school provides a clear principle (dabit) to determine who can receive maintenance from the missing person's wealth without a court order. This lesson unpacks that principle and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Distinguish dependents entitled to maintenance without a court order in the missing person's presence from those who need a court order. The first category includes minor children, adult females, and chronically ill adult males. The second includes siblings, uncles, and aunts.
The level of dependency (e.g., earning ability) determines the category.
2
Understand the implication for absence
The dabit states that those in the first category are entitled to maintenance from the missing person's wealth without a court order in his absence. For the second category, a court order is impossible because it cannot be given against an absent person, so no maintenance is due.
The court order in the first category is merely 'assistance' (i'ānah), not the basis of entitlement.
3
Examine the conditions for payment
Payment is only from specific types of wealth (cash or bullion) that is in the custody of the judge, a depositary, or a debtor who acknowledges both the wealth and the relationship (if not already known to the judge).
If the depositary or debtor pays without a judge's order, they bear liability.
4
Note the marriage preservation
The passage emphasizes that the missing person's wife remains married; absence does not necessitate separation.
This is a separate ruling but related to the overall treatment of the missing person.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Missing Person (Kitab al-Mafqud) provides a key principle (ضابط) regarding maintenance (nafaqah) for dependents of a missing person. The text quotes al-Hidayah to establish the rule: whoever is entitled to maintenance from the missing person's wealth without a court order when he is present is also entitled to it from his wealth in his absence; the court order then merely assists. Conversely, those who need a court order to get maintenance in his presence cannot get it from his wealth in his absence, since a court order against an absent person is not possible. The first category includes minor children, adult females (daughters, wives), and chronically ill adult males (who are unable to earn). The second category includes relatives like brothers, sisters, maternal uncles, and maternal aunts, who must normally sue for maintenance. Additional conditions are given: the wealth must be in specific forms (cash or bullion) and in the custody of the judge, a depositary, or a debtor who acknowledges both the wealth and the marriage/kinship (if those are not already known to the judge). If the depositary or debtor pays without a court order, the depositary is liable and the debtor is not discharged. Finally, the text states that the missing person's wife is not separated from him due to his absence, as absence alone does not dissolve the marriage.

Key Terms
المفقود (al-mafqud): A person whose whereabouts are unknown, not confirmed dead or alive.
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Financial maintenance covering food, clothing, and shelter for dependents.
ضابط (dabit): A legal principle or controlling rule that encompasses multiple cases.
