Resale of Defective Slave and Return to Original Seller
Ruling on the effect of qadi's judgment on rescission of a defective sale through multiple transactions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qadi's judgment and private agreement regarding the effect of rescission of a defective sale and apply the ruling to determine whether a buyer can return the item to the original seller.

Core Idea
A qadi's judgment rescinds a sale ab initio; private agreement creates a new sale, affecting the right to return to prior sellers.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a multi-party sale chain where a defective slave is returned. The determinative factor is whether the return was effected by a qadi's judgment or by mutual consent. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining the rights of prior sellers and the retrospective effect of rescission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
Visualize the chain: original seller → first buyer → second buyer. The second buyer returns the slave to the first buyer due to a defect. The question is whether the first buyer can then return it to the original seller.
The slave is an example; the ruling applies to any commodity.
2
Recognize the two modes of acceptance
The first buyer's acceptance of the return can occur in two ways: (1) by a qadi's judgment based on evidence, refusal of oath, or confession; (2) without a qadi's judgment, i.e., by private mutual agreement.
3
Understand the legal effect: ab initio rescission vs. new sale
A qadi's judgment effects a rescission ab initio (faskh min al-asl), treating the original sale as if it never existed. A private agreement is considered a new sale (bai' jadid) with respect to third parties, even though it rescinds the contract between the immediate parties.
4
Derive the ruling for each case
If acceptance was by qadi's judgment, the first buyer may return the slave to the original seller (because the rescission retroactively invalidates the original sale). If without qadi, the first buyer may NOT return to the original seller (because the return is a new sale; the original sale remains valid).
The ruling is based on the legal maxim that qadi's judgment has binding authority to undo contracts retroactively.
5
Apply to the second buyer's right
The second buyer's return is valid in both cases. The difference is only in whether the first buyer can pass the defect back to the original seller. This protects third-party transactions from retroactive disturbance without judicial authority.

Returning a Defective Item After Re-Sale: The Role of Qadi Acceptance
Ruling on second buyer's right to return defect to original seller

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi rule for when a first buyer can return a defective item to the original seller after the second buyer has returned it, and EXPLAIN why qadi acceptance changes the legal nature of the return.

Core Idea
Without qadi acceptance, the return is a new sale, not a rescission—original seller is a third party.

In the chapter on the Option of Defect, Imam al-Quduri presents a nuanced ruling: the right of a buyer to pass back a defective item to the original seller depends on whether the intermediate return was court-approved. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its practical consequences, including a special rule for measured and weighed goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Consider the chain: original seller → first buyer → second buyer. The second buyer finds a defect and returns the item to the first buyer. Now the first buyer wants to return it to the original seller.
The ruling applies whether the item is a slave or any other commodity.
2
Distinguish the two cases based on qadi acceptance
Case 1: The second buyer's return was accepted by a qadi's judgment (with evidence, refusal of oath, or confession). Case 2: The return was accepted without qadi involvement (mutual agreement).
Qadi acceptance includes formal rulings and recognitions.
3
Understand the legal basis for each case
With qadi: the return is treated as a rescission (faskh) from the origin, as if the sale never occurred. Without qadi: the return is considered a new sale between the first and second buyer, making the original seller a third party.
Faskh annuls the contract ab initio; a new sale creates fresh obligations.
4
Note the third-party effect
Since the original seller is a third party to the new sale, the first buyer cannot unilaterally return the item to him. The original seller's rights are protected.
This is a unique Hanafi protection against chain defaults.
5
Recognize the additional rule for measured/weighed items
For a measured or weighed item that is defective, the buyer may return the entire lot or keep it, but cannot return only the defective part. This applies when the whole lot remains in his possession, or it reflects the view of non-Muhammad.
This sub-rule prevents partial returns that could be inequitable.

Stipulation of Exemption from All Defects: Validity and Effect
Ruling on seller's condition exempting from all defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a seller's blanket exemption from all defects and apply it to determine whether a buyer can return an item for a hidden defect.

Core Idea
A seller's condition exempting from all defects is valid even for unknown defects—buyer forfeits the right to return.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hanafi chapter on the Option of Defect (Khayar al-'Ayb). When a seller explicitly stipulates exemption from all defects, the buyer's right to return the item for any defect is completely waived. This applies even if the defects are not listed individually. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its context
Identify the act: a sale where the seller stipulates exemption from all defects. The ruling: the buyer may not return the item due to any defect.
The ruling applies to defects existing at contract or arising before delivery.
2
Specify the condition's scope
The exemption covers every defect, even those unknown to both parties at the time of sale. The seller does not need to enumerate them.
Contrast with other schools that may require enumeration or knowledge.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The Hanafi justification: exemption from unknown rights is valid because it does not lead to dispute—the buyer has agreed to accept the item as is.
This is based on the principle that a clear stipulation prevents future conflict.
4
Recognize the Hanafi position's authority
This ruling is from Al-Quduri, confirmed by Al-Hidayah. It is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school.
No intra-Hanafi dispute; the ruling is consistent across the major texts.
5
Compare with other schools
Some schools (e.g., Maliki) may require the seller to explicitly list defects to enforce exemption. Others (e.g., Shafi'i) generally allow it if the buyer is informed.
This difference affects the practical enforceability of such conditions.

The Void Sale (Bāṭil)
Definition of void sale (bāṭil) within a three-category framework.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a bāṭil sale according to Hanafi fiqh, identify its criteria (invalid in essence and description), and state the legal consequence that the contract is void and the item must be returned.

Core Idea
A void sale is invalid in both essence and description; it produces no legal effects.

In Hanafi law, not all invalid sales are the same. The most severe category is bāṭil, a sale that is entirely void from its foundation. This lesson unpacks the definition of bāṭil as given in al-Lubab, distinguishing it from other non-valid sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its category
The passage states that bāṭil is a sale that is 'not legislated in its essence and its description.' Essence refers to the pillars (offer and acceptance) and the subject matter (the item sold). Description refers to conditions and attributes of the transaction.
2
Recognize the criteria for bāṭil
A sale is bāṭil if either the essence is defective (e.g., contract from a madman, selling carrion) or the description is fundamentally flawed (e.g., price is wine or entirely unknown). The defect must be inherent, not rectifiable.
3
Apply to examples
Examples from the passage: selling a dead animal (carrion) because the subject matter is invalid; a contract made by a minor without understanding because the pillar is missing. Both are bāṭil.
4
Distinguish from other categories
Unlike fāsid (valid essence, defective description) or makrūh (valid essence and description, external prohibition), bāṭil has no possibility of correction and produces no transfer of ownership.

Core Idea
Slaves with a binding right to freedom cannot be sold; their sale is batil because they are effectively not owned outright.

This lesson explores the Hanafi rulings on selling slaves who have ties to freedom: the umm walad (mother of the master's child), the mudabbar (declared free after master's death), and the mukatab (under a kitabah contract). List the three special slaves

Three Prohibited Sales: The Hanafi Ruling on Karahah Without Invalidation
Listing three prohibited sales with their ruling of karahah without invalidation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the three prohibited sales (talqi al-jalb, bay' al-hadir li'l-badi, sale at Friday adhan) and explain why they are makruh tahrimi but not invalid.

Core Idea
A prohibited sale is not necessarily invalid; the Hanafi school distinguishes between prohibitions on the cause and on the contract itself.

In this passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, we encounter three sales that the Prophet ﷺ explicitly prohibited. However, the Hanafi school teaches that prohibition does not always nullify the contract. Understanding when a sale is valid but sinful is essential for distinguishing between permissibility (hukm taklifi) and contractual validity (hukm wad'i). This lesson breaks down the three cases and the legal principle behind the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three prohibited sales
List the three transactions mentioned: meeting caravans before market (talqi al-jalb), a townsman selling for a Bedouin at inflated prices (bay' al-hadir li'l-badi), and selling during the Friday adhan. Note the common element: each involves harm or confusion.
The passage also briefly mentions exceptions (e.g., when no harm occurs, or those not obligated to Friday prayer).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The passage cites an explicit prohibition from the Prophet ﷺ (صريح النهي) without quoting the exact hadith. The known proofs include the hadith 'لا يبع حاضر لباد' (a townsman should not sell for a Bedouin) and the Quranic verse 'وإذا نودي للصلاة من يوم الجمعة فاسعوا إلى ذكر الله وذروا البيع' (Surah al-Jumu'ah:9). The Hanafi usul principle: an explicit command or prohibition that is zanni in transmission results in makruh tahrimi, not haram.
In Hanafi usul, an explicit prohibition (صريح النهي) indicates tahrim (prohibition), but if the dalil is ahad (zanni al-thubut), the ruling is makruh tahrimi, not haram.
3
Distinguish between hukm taklifi and hukm wad'i
The prohibition applies to the act of the sale (making it sinful), but the contract itself (the exchange of ownership) remains valid. The Hanafi school holds that the invalidity of a contract requires the prohibition to be related to the essence of the contract (e.g., riba, gharar). Here, the prohibition relates to external factors (timing, place, or agency) that do not render the contract fasid.
This is a key Hanafi principle: 'النهي لا يقتضي الفساد إلا إذا عاد إلى الذات' (prohibition does not necessitate invalidity unless it pertains to the essence).
4
Apply the ruling to each case
For each of the three sales, confirm that the contract is valid (sahih) but the act is makruh tahrimi. Also note the exceptions: meeting caravans without harm is not disliked; selling at Friday adhan is only prohibited for those obligated to pray.
The passage emphasizes that the sale remains valid even if the parties are sinful.
5
Evaluate cross-madhhab differences
Some other schools (e.g., some Shafi'i views) consider such sales invalid (fasid) because they view the prohibition as affecting the contract itself. The Hanafi school maintains validity based on the principle of distinguishing between types of prohibitions.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses three types of prohibited sales that fall under the category of 'corrupt sale' (al-bay' al-fasid) in the sense that they are makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) but do not render the contract invalid. The three types are: 1) Meeting caravans before they reach the market (talqi al-jalb), 2) A townsman selling on behalf of a Bedouin (bay' al-hadir li'l-badi), and 3) Selling during the Friday adhan. The author first states the prohibition generally, then explains the reasoning. For meeting caravans, the harm is that the townsman buys goods outside the market, depriving local sellers of profit and potentially confusing prices. However, if there is no harm, it is not disliked. For the townsman selling for a Bedouin, the harm is that the townsman, acting as an agent, can inflate prices to the detriment of the townspeople. Two interpretations are given: one from Al-Hidayah that this occurs when the townspeople are in need, and another from Al-Halwani that the townsman acts as a broker to raise prices. The latter is considered more correct. For selling during the Friday adhan, the prohibition applies to the first adhan (after the time of zuhr enters) for those obligated to attend Friday prayer. Those not obligated (e.g., women, travelers) are excluded. The final ruling summarizes that all these transactions are makruh tahrimi due to explicit prohibition, but they are not invalid; the contract is valid but sinful. This distinction is characteristic of the Hanafi school: acts prohibited for reasons other than the essence of the contract do not render the contract null.

Key Terms
تلقي الجلب (talqi al-jalb): The practice of going out to meet incoming traders (caravans) before they reach the market to buy their goods at a low price, thereby depriving the townspeople of a fair chance to buy.
بيع الحاضر للبادي (bay' al-hadir li'l-badi): A townsman (resident of city) selling goods on behalf of a Bedouin (resident of desert) in a way that harms the townspeople by inflating prices.
مكروه تحريماً (makruh tahrimi): An act that is prohibited by a doubtful proof (zanni) and is sinful; but unlike haram, it does not necessitate punishment in the afterlife if avoided with regret.
