Excuse of Changing Mind About Travel in Ijarah of Animal
Ruling on rescinding ijarah of animal when hirer or owner changes travel plans

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a hirer or owner of a rented animal can rescind the ijarah due to change of travel plans, and identify the mufta bihi position regarding the owner's illness.

Core Idea
The hirer may cancel for change of travel; the owner may not, even if ill—asymmetry rooted in avoidable harm.

This passage from al-Lubab tackles an important nuance in ijarah contracts: who has the right to rescind when travel plans change. The rule is not symmetric—the hirer has an excuse, but the owner does not, because the owner can delegate the animal. The text also presents a difference of opinion about the owner's illness, concluding with the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the contract
Recognise the two parties: hirer (musta'jir) and owner/lessor (mukari). The contract is ijarah of a specific riding animal for travel.
The owner is called mukari specifically for transport animals.
2
State the rule for the hirer
If the hirer changes his mind about travel, he may rescind the ijarah; this change of intention is a valid excuse.
The text affirms this without dispute.
3
State the rule for the owner
If the owner changes his mind about travel (e.g., decides not to make the trip), that is not a valid excuse. The owner can send the animal with an agent.
The owner is not obligated to travel personally with the animal.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on owner's illness
According to al-Asl (Imam Muhammad's original narration), if the owner falls ill and cannot travel, it is still not an excuse. Al-Karkhi narrates that illness is an excuse due to unavoidable harm.
This ikhtilaf appears under the heading of the owner's change of mind.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
Al-Durr al-Mukhtar states that fatwa is given by the first opinion (owner's illness is not an excuse). The owner's harm is avoidable by delegation, unlike the hirer's harm.
The tarjih is based on the rationale of darar (harm) and the ability to avoid it.

Who Bears the Cost of Returning Items: The Hanafi Rule on Four Categories
Four rulings on return costs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which party bears the cost of returning each type of item (borrowed, rented, usurped, deposited) and explain the rationale based on the obligation to return.

Core Idea
The cost of return follows the obligation to return — who must return pays.

This lesson expands on a concise passage from al-Quduri's Matn, clarified by later Hanafi scholars. The passage lists four distinct scenarios — borrowed, rented, usurped, and deposited items — and assigns the cost of returning each to a specific party. Understanding this ruling sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi fiqh links financial liability to legal obligations (wujub al-radd).

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
The passage covers borrowed items (al-ariyah), rented items (al-ijarah), usurped items (al-ghasb), and deposited items (al-wadiah). Recognize each context.
2
For each, determine who must return the item
The party obligated to return differs: borrower (musta'ir) returns borrowed item; lessor (mu'jir) takes back rented item; usurper (ghasib) returns wrongfully taken item; depositor (mudi') retrieves deposited item.
For rent, the lessee only enables return, not physically returns.
3
Link obligation to cost
Whoever is obligated to return bears the expense of doing so. The cost is an ancillary obligation (tābiʿ) of the main duty.
4
Understand the rationale for each
Borrower benefits so pays; lessor owns and collects; usurper wrongfully possesses; depositor benefits from safekeeping.
5
Recognize the authority
The footnote attributes this rule to the later scholars (muta'akhkhirūn) via al-Hidayah and al-Bināyah — it is the relied-upon (muftā bihī) position.
This is fatawa mutakhkhirin weight.

The book of Muzara'ah. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: It is permissible. The author's restriction to one-third and one-fourth is based on custom in that. (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: It (the contract) is permissible because of what is narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) contracted with the people of Khaybar for half of what comes out of fruit or crop, and because it is a contract of partnership between capital and work, so it is permissible by analogy to mudarabah. The fatwa is according to their view, as in Qadi Khan, al-Khulasa, Mukhtarat al-Nawazil, al-Haqa'iq, al-Sughra, al-Tatimma, al-Kubra, al-Hidaya, al-Mahbubi, and al-Nasafi relied on it, as in al-Tashih. In al-Hidaya: The fatwa is according to their view due to the people's need for it and because of the manifest practice of the ummah with it, and analogy is abandoned due to practice, as in istisna'. End. The forms of muzara'ah and the explanation of what is permissible and invalid from it: Since the practice and fatwa are according to their view, the author branched from it, and it (muzara'ah) according to them is of four forms: If the land and seed belong to one person, and the work and oxen to another: the muzara'ah is valid. And if the land belongs to one, and the work, oxen, and seed to another: the muzara'ah is valid. And if the land, oxen, and seed belong to one, and the work to another: it is valid. He said: (It according to them is of four forms), it is valid in three of them and invalid in one: 1. Because if the land and seed belong to one, and the work and oxen to another, the muzara'ah is valid, and the owner of the land and seed becomes the hirer of the worker, and the oxen are subsidiary to him because the oxen are the tool of work. (And) likewise if the land belongs to one, and the work, oxen, and seed to another: the muzara'ah is also valid, and the worker becomes the hirer of the land for a portion of the produce. (And) likewise if the land, oxen, and seed belong to one, and the work to another: it is also valid, and the landowner becomes the hirer of the worker for a portion of the produce.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of muzara'ah (sharecropping). It begins by stating the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) that muzara'ah is permissible, contrasting with Abu Hanifa who considered it invalid. The Sahibayn support their view with two proofs: first, the hadith that the Prophet (peace be upon him) contracted with the people of Khaybar for half of the produce; second, analogy (qiyas) to mudarabah, since both are partnerships between capital and labor. The author notes that although Abu Hanifa’s view might be based on qiyas to hire (ijara) with uncertainty, the fatwa is on the view of the Sahibayn due to public necessity and widespread practice, as stated in al-Hidaya. Then the passage explains the forms of muzara'ah according to the Sahibayn: it has four forms, three valid and one invalid. The text enumerates only the three valid forms: (1) land and seed from one party, work and oxen from another, (2) land from one, work, oxen, and seed from another, (3) land, oxen, and seed from one, work from another. In each, the arrangement is analyzed as either the landowner hiring the worker or the worker hiring the land, with the other inputs being subsidiary. The fourth (invalid) form is implied to be where no party provides sufficient inputs to make the contract resemble a valid partnership or hire, but it is not detailed here.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzāra‘ah): A contract where one party provides land and/or seed and another provides work and/or oxen in exchange for a share of the produce.
البذر (al-badhr): Seeds used for planting in agriculture.
البقر (al-baqar): Oxen or plough animals used as tools for cultivation.
