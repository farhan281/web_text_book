Pre-emptor's Options When Buyer Demolishes Building
Ruling on pre-emptor's choice after demolition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pre-emptor's options when the buyer demolishes the building and explain the legal reasoning (talil) that prevents him from taking the demolished materials.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor may only take the land at its share of the price, not the demolished materials.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies a specific scenario in the law of pre-emption (shuf'ah). When a buyer demolishes a building, the pre-emptor faces a choice: accept the land with a reduced price proportional to the materials, or abandon pre-emption. The reasoning hinges on the distinction between intentional demolition and natural destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the buyer's demolition of the building. The ruling gives the pre-emptor a choice: take the land with its share of the price or leave it; he cannot take the demolished materials.
2
Locate the talil (reasoning)
The commentary explains: because the demolition is an intentional act of destruction, the materials gain a portion of the price. This contrasts with a natural calamity, where no portion is assigned.
The talil is based on the buyer's deliberate action.
3
Contrast with natural calamity
If the building collapsed naturally, the pre-emptor would take the land at the full price, because no intentional destruction occurred.
4
Determine the consequence
Since the materials are separated from the land and have a corresponding price, the pre-emptor cannot claim them because they are no longer a follower (tabi') of the land.
5
State the final rule
The pre-emptor may either take the land (at its share of the price) or decline pre-emption entirely. He has no right to the demolished materials.

Pre-emptor Takes Fruit on Land by Istihsan
Dispute between qiyas and istihsan on fruit inclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a pre-emptor takes fruit along with land, and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor takes fruit if it was mentioned in the sale, based on istihsan; strict qiyas would exclude it.

In pre-emption (shuf'ah), the pre-emptor steps into the buyer's shoes. But what about fruit growing on the land? Does it automatically transfer? This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies a dispute within the Hanafi school: between strict analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan). The answer turns on whether the fruit was mentioned in the sale, and the concept of 'followership' (tab'iyyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling from the matn
The matn says the pre-emptor takes the land with its fruit. This is the starting point: the default is inclusion.
2
Understand the condition from al-Hidayah
Al-Hidayah clarifies that this only applies if the fruit was mentioned in the sale contract. Without mention, fruit does not enter the sale.
This restriction is crucial.
3
Analyze the qiyas argument
By strict qiyas, fruit is not a follower (tab') because it does not enter the sale without mention. It resembles movable goods in a house, which are separate.
Qiyas would exclude fruit from shuf'ah.
4
Analyze the istihsan argument
Istihsan overrides qiyas due to physical attachment: the fruit is connected to the land like a building, making it a follower. Hence the pre-emptor takes it.
The 'illah of attachment is key.
5
Identify the practical consequence
If the buyer takes the fruit before the pre-emptor, the pre-emptor's price is reduced proportionally because the fruit was part of the intended sale.
This follow-up rule shows consistency.

The Pre-emptor's Options of Sight and Defect
Ruling with qiyas proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why the pre-emptor retains the options of sight and defect despite the buyer's waiver, and apply the qiyas to similar transfer cases.

Core Idea
Taking by shuf'ah is a new sale, so the pre-emptor enjoys all buyer's options, unaffected by the buyer's waiver.

In this passage, al-Quduri states that when a pre-emptor takes property via shuf'ah, he inherits the same options a buyer would have, including the right to rescind upon sighting or upon discovering a hidden defect. The commentary elaborates on the rationale: the taking is analogized to a sale, so the options follow. Even if the original buyer had explicitly waived defect liability (shart al-bara'ah), the pre-emptor is not bound by that waiver because his transaction is independent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the ruling clearly: the pre-emptor (shafi') has khiyar al-ru'yah (option of sight) and khiyar al-'ayb (option of defect) when taking property by shuf'ah.
This applies even if the buyer had previously seen the property or waived defects.
2
Identify the Proof
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to a sale. The text says 'taking by shuf'ah is in the position of a sale' (al-akhdh bi al-shuf'ah bi manzilat al-shira').
This is a jaliy (clear) qiyas because the illa is transparent.
3
Break Down the Qiyas
Asl (original case): a regular sale contract. Hukm: the buyer has options of sight and defect. Far' (new case): taking by shuf'ah. Illa: transfer of ownership in exchange for price. Therefore, the same options apply.
The illa is tahqiq al-manat: the effective cause is present in both.
4
Note the Expansion
The commentary expands that these options are not removed by the buyer's condition of immunity (shart al-bara'ah) because the pre-emptor's acquisition is a new, independent sale.
This distinguishes shuf'ah from a simple assignment of the original contract.
5
Consider the Practical Effect
The pre-emptor can rescind if he finds a defect he was unaware of, regardless of the original buyer's waiver. This protects the pre-emptor from hidden defects he did not agree to.
The thamarah (practical outcome) is that the pre-emptor's rights are as robust as a direct buyer's.

The pre-emptor must pay cash now or wait; taking on credit is invalid as it binds him to a condition he never agreed to.

When a property is sold on credit, the pre-emptor's right of shuf'ah remains, but the payment method is not tied to the original contract. This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies the two permissible options and the reasoning behind the prohibition of taking immediately with deferred payment.

Both options are valid; the pre-emptor chooses.

The credit term was a condition between seller and buyer only. The pre-emptor's right arises by law, not by contract. His consent to a deferred payment cannot be assumed from the buyer's acceptance, as individuals differ in their preferences.
The principle: al-umur bi-maqasidiha – the pre-emptor's intention is to take the property, not to assume the buyer's contractual terms.

Effect of Buyer's Return on Pre-emptor's Right
Ruling on pre-emption after buyer's return of property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a pre-emptor's right revives when the buyer returns the property due to option, defect, or mutual rescission, and apply the distinction between court-ordered and non-court returns.

Core Idea
Court-ordered return voids pre-emption; non-court return revives it.

Pre-emption gives a neighbor or partner the right to buy property sold to an outsider. But what happens if the buyer returns the property after the pre-emptor has forfeited? Does the pre-emptor get a second chance? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rule: it depends on whether the return is by court order or not. A court-ordered return is a full rescission; a private return or mutual rescission is treated as a new sale vis-à-vis third parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenario
Start with the baseline: the pre-emptor has already forfeited his right. Then the buyer returns the property. Determine the method of return: by court order (due to option or defect) or without court order (by mutual agreement or independent choice).
The pre-emptor's forfeiture is assumed.
2
Apply the court-ordered return rule
If the return is by a judge's ruling due to an option of inspection, option of stipulation, or a defect, then pre-emption does not revive. This is because the court's order makes the return a rescission from every perspective—the contract is undone completely, and ownership reverts to the original seller as if the sale never happened.
The ruling applies regardless of whether delivery had occurred.
3
Apply the non-court return rule
If the buyer returns the property without a court order (e.g., by exercising a stipulated option directly, or by mutual rescission), then the pre-emptor's right revives. In the eyes of third parties, this is a new sale: it meets the definition of sale (exchange of property by mutual consent), and the pre-emptor is a third party.
The commentary notes a contrary view in al-Durar, but the relied-upon position is that pre-emption revives absolutely.
4
Understand the legal reasoning (talil)
The key is the nature of the return: court order makes it a rescission (faskh) in all aspects; private return is a rescission only between the two parties but a new sale for others. Pre-emption attaches to new sales, not to rescissions.
Also note: return for defect before delivery is a rescission from the origin even without court order; after delivery, it follows the rule above.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six causes that automatically invalidate a wakalah contract and explain the intra-Hanafi difference on the definition of continuous insanity.

Core Idea
Agency automatically ends when the principal's legal capacity or command ceases.

This passage from al-Lubab enumerates the events that immediately nullify a contract of agency (wakalah). Understanding these causes is crucial because they operate automatically, whether the agent knows or not. The text also contains an important intra-Hanafi dispute about the duration of continuous insanity and the condition for accepting news of dismissal.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the six causes
Identify and internalize the six events listed: death, continuous insanity, apostasy with joining dar al-harb, mukatib's incapacity, interdiction of ma'dhun, and dissolution of partnership. These are the core of the lesson.
These causes are arranged in the matn as what invalidates agency.
2
Understand the underlying rationale
Each cause terminates agency because the principal's legal capacity or command ceases. For example, death ends legal personality; continuous insanity removes legal capacity; apostasy with joining dar al-harb severs the relationship; the mukatib's failure returns him to slavery; interdiction removes permission; partnership dissolution ends joint command.
This is a constructive dismissal ('azl hukmi) — agency ends without express revocation.
3
Apply the constructive dismissal principle
These events invalidate agency whether the agent knows or not. The agent's subsequent actions are void because the basis of agency (the principal's command) no longer exists.
This distinguishes it from express dismissal, which requires knowledge to take effect in some cases.
4
Differentiate temporary vs continuous insanity
The text specifies 'continuous insanity' (junun mutbiq). Temporary bouts or unconsciousness do not invalidate agency. Know the Hanafi definition: Abu Yusuf says one month, Muhammad says one year. The relied-upon view is one month.

This passage addresses a common situation in sales: can an agent who sells goods be held personally liable for the payment? The Hanafi school rules that such a guarantee is invalid because the agent's role is that of a trustee (amin), not a debtor. Conditions for Valid Settlement with Gold, Silver, and Debts
Explains conditions for valid settlement when estate includes varied assets and debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid settlement when an estate contains gold, silver, and other assets, and identify when a settlement involving debt is invalid, and apply the Hanafi legal stratagem to make it valid.

Core Idea
Settlement must avoid riba: gold/silver given must exceed claimant's share of that type, and debt transfer requires debtor's consent.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanded by al-Maydani, presents two critical rulings on sulh in inheritance. The first ensures the settlement avoids riba when the estate contains ribawi items. The second illustrates a common pitfall when debts are included, and offers a stratagem (hila) to achieve a valid outcome. Understanding these rulings requires careful attention to the classification of the transaction as sale or currency exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two distinct cases: (1) estate includes gold, silver, and other assets; (2) estate includes a debt owed to the deceased.
Each has different rules.
2
Analyze the first scenario
When heirs settle by giving gold or silver, the amount given must exceed the claimant's share of that type. The excess compensates for the claimant's share of other assets. Hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) is required for the part equal to his share, as it is considered sarf (currency exchange).
Avoids riba al-fadl.
3
Analyze the second scenario
If the estate includes a debt and the heirs include it in the settlement (by discharging the claimant from his share and taking the debt), the settlement is invalid. This is because it transfers a debt to someone other than the debtor without the debtor's consent, which is invalid.
Invalidity of the debt portion voids the whole contract.
4
Understand the legal stratagem (hila)
To validate such a settlement, the parties can stipulate that the heir (or third party) exempts the debtors from the claimant's share of the debt (isqat), effectively forgiving it. This makes the settlement valid because it is a relinquishment, not a transfer. Alternatively, a third party can lend the claimant his share, and then the heirs settle over the rest.
The hila converts the invalid debt transfer into a valid forgiveness.

Gift with Condition of Consideration: Hybrid of Gift and Sale
States the hybrid nature and sale effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid gift with condition of compensation and identify which sale effects apply after exchange.

Core Idea
A gift conditioned on compensation is a gift initially but a sale in its effects.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift that requires the donee to give something in return is a unique hybrid. It starts as a gift by name, but once the compensations are exchanged, it is governed by the rules of sale. This lesson explains the conditions for its validity and the practical consequences, such as the right to return the item for defects and the application of pre-emption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its dual nature
The act is a gift (hiba) made conditional upon the donee providing a specified compensation. The Hanafi school classifies it as a gift initially but as a sale in its ultimate effects.
The key is the phrase 'bi-shart al-iwad' (with condition of compensation).
2
List the conditions for validity
The contract must satisfy the conditions of a valid gift: mutual exchange of compensations (taqabud), specification of the gift (tamyiz), and the gift must not be joint property (adam al-shuyu').
These are the shurut from the conditions_pillars block.
3
Understand the transformation into sale
Once both compensations are exchanged, the contract takes on the rulings of a sale. The donee can return the item if it has a defect (radd bil-'ayb), the option of inspection (khiyar ar-ru'yah) applies, and pre-emption (shuf'ah) becomes obligatory.
These sale effects apply only after exchange.
4
Distinguish specified vs unspecified compensation
If the compensation is specified and the donor says 'on condition that you compensate me with X', the hybrid applies. If the compensation is unknown, the condition is void and it remains a pure gift. Also, if the donor uses the preposition 'ba' (e.g., 'I give you this for X'), it is a sale from the start.
This distinction is crucial for proper application.
5
Apply the sale effects to a scenario
Given a scenario, determine whether the hybrid applies and then list which sale effects are present (defect return, inspection option, pre-emption).
Practice identifying the conditions and effects in real cases.

Owner's Option in Usurpation of Altered Property
Ruling on owner's option when usurped property is enhanced

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to the owner of a usurped item that has been enhanced, apply the distinction between qimi and mithli, and explain the reason for the rule.

Core Idea
The owner of a usurped enhanced item may choose between claiming original value or taking the item and paying for the increase.

Usurpation (ghasb) is a prohibited act, but what happens when the usurper adds value to the property? The Hanafi school gives the owner a choice to either claim the value of the original property or take the enhanced item while compensating the usurper for the increase. This ruling balances the rights of both parties and is grounded in the nature of the property. In this lesson, we examine the reasoning behind this option and its application to a dyed garment and mixed flour.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ghasb of a garment that is then dyed, or flour mixed with ghee. The ruling is that the owner has an option (khiyar) between two remedies.
The option is given to the owner, not the usurper.
2
Distinguish between qimi and mithli
The garment is qimi (non-fungible, valued by appraisal) and the flour is mithli (fungible, has a like substitute). This distinction affects the valuation in the first option.
Qimi: value is assessed by experts; mithli: value is based on market weight or volume.
3
Explain the two options
Option 1: The owner claims the value of the item in its original state (e.g., white cloth, unmixed flour) and leaves the enhanced item with the usurper. Option 2: The owner takes the enhanced item and pays the usurper for the increase in value caused by the dye or mixing.
The increase is determined by the actual market value difference, not arbitrary.
4
Contrast with the beam case
If a beam is built into a wall, the owner can demand its return by demolition because the beam is separable. In contrast, dye penetrates and perishes upon removal, so the only just solution is the option.
The key distinction is separability of the enhancement.
5
Understand the reasoning
The option is given because the owner is the owner of the original (asl), and both sides must be considered: the owner's right to the property and the usurper's entitlement for the value added.
This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference) over strict qiyas.

While all agree that permission leads to ownership, the controversy arises when permission is absent. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi position on land ownership and the role of the Imam.

Three-Year Limit for Reviving Dead Land and Prohibition Near Inhabited Areas
Ruling and commentary on ihya al-mawat limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the three-year limit for reviving dead land and explain why land near inhabited areas cannot be revived.

Core Idea
Marking land without cultivation does not grant ownership; after three years the Imam reallocates it, and inhabited areas' appurtenances are reserved for public use.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, explained by al-Maydani, addresses two key issues in the law of reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). First, it sets a time limit for actual cultivation after receiving permission. Second, it protects the common areas around villages from being privatized. Understanding these rules ensures that land development serves the public interest and prevents hoarding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage gives two distinct rulings: (1) If a person who marked dead land does not revive it within three years, the Imam takes it back and gives it to another. (2) It is not permissible to revive land that is close to an inhabited area.
These rulings are linked by the principle of public benefit.
2
Understand the condition and effect of tahjir
Tahjir (marking) only gives a priority right, not ownership. Actual revival (ihya) is required. The three-year limit is a deadline to complete the revival; otherwise the priority is lost.
The imam's grant is conditional upon timely cultivation.
3
Analyze the rationale for the three-year limit
The reason given is that the imam's purpose in granting the land is to benefit the Muslims through agricultural yield (and thus tax). If the land lies idle, the imam reallocates it to someone who will cultivate it.
Maslahah (public interest) justifies reallocation.
4
Apply the prohibition of reviving near inhabited areas
Land near a settlement is considered part of the village's appurtenances (marafiq) needed for grazing, threshing, etc. Such land is not mawat and cannot be privately owned.
Like roads and rivers, these areas are reserved for common use.
