Acknowledgment for a Fetus: Conditions and Outcomes
Rulings on fetus acknowledgment with conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid acknowledgment for a fetus and determine the distribution of the money in various birth scenarios.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment for a fetus is valid only if a valid reason (bequest or inheritance) is stated; the child must be born alive within a viable period.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays out the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging a debt owed to an unborn child (haml). The key is that the fetus is considered a potential owner, but the acknowledgment must be supported by a valid reason, such as a bequest or inheritance. The outcome depends on whether the child is born alive, stillborn, or twins, and whether the stated reason is legally possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt for a fetus. Determine when it is valid and when it is void.
2
List the conditions for validity

Consider the three scenarios: stillborn, live twins, and the case of an impossible reason. For each, determine who gets the money.
4
Trace the legal reasoning
Understand why a stillborn child results in the money returning to the original bequeather/heir: because the fetus never acquired ownership.
The acknowledgment is effectively for the bequeather/heir, contingent on live birth.

Core Idea
A paternity confession during terminal illness is valid and the child inherits, provided the child confirms and it is biologically possible.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains that a person can legally establish paternity over a boy even while terminally ill. The ruling is not invalidated by the illness because the confession is a form of establishing lineage, which is given strong legal effect. However, several conditions must be met, including the boy's confirmation and biological plausibility. Once paternity is proven, the boy becomes a full heir and shares in the estate.

Acknowledgment of Lineage for Non-Immediate Relatives
Ruling on acknowledgment of lineage for non-immediate relatives and inheritance consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging lineage of non-immediate relatives and identify the conditions under which such acknowledgment affects inheritance.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a brother or uncle does not establish legal lineage, but can give them inheritance if no known heir exists.

In Hanafi fiqh, a person has limited authority (wilaya) to establish lineage. You can only acknowledge the lineage of your parents, children, spouse, and master. For others—like brothers, uncles, or grandfathers—your acknowledgment is not accepted to prove lineage. However, it may still affect inheritance under certain conditions. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its reasoning based on the text of al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of the ruling
Determine which relatives are excluded from being acknowledged for lineage: everyone except parents, children, spouse, and master. The key is that the acknowledger lacks wilaya over these persons.
Wilaya (authority) is central: you cannot impose lineage on someone else.
2
Understand the two inheritance scenarios
If the acknowledger has a known heir (even distant), that heir takes priority. If there is no known heir, the acknowledged person inherits all wealth, even though lineage is not established.
The acknowledgment affects property but not lineage.
3
Apply the specific case of a brother after father's death
If a man's father dies and he acknowledges another brother, the brother's lineage from the father is not established, but he shares the inheritance. This is because the acknowledgment has two elements: lineage (not under authority) and property (under authority).
The ruling distinguishes between establishing lineage and sharing wealth.
4
Analyze the grandson scenario
A grandson's acknowledgment of lineage is not accepted even if confirmed, because it would attribute lineage to the grandson's father (the middle person), over whom the grandson has no authority.
This reinforces the principle of wilaya.
5
Trace the reasoning (ta'lil)
The underlying rationale is that lineage is a matter of established fact and cannot be created by someone without direct authority. The law protects the certainty of lineage and prevents manipulation of inheritance.
This ta'lil explains why the ruling is consistent across similar cases.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Acknowledgment (Kitab al-Iqrar) deals with the ruling on acknowledging the lineage (nasab) of persons other than the immediate family chain—parents, children, spouse, and master (mawla). The Hanafi position is that such an acknowledgment is not accepted to establish legal lineage, because a person does not have authority (wilaya) to establish lineage for others beyond his own direct ascendants, descendants, spouse, and master. However, the acknowledgment can have an effect on inheritance. If the acknowledger has a known heir (by blood or other recognized tie), that heir takes priority over the acknowledged person. But if there is no known heir, the acknowledged person receives the entire inheritance, even though the lineage is not legally established. The passage also addresses a specific scenario: if a man dies and his son acknowledges a brother (i.e., another son of the deceased), the brother's lineage is not established, but he shares in the inheritance because the acknowledgment involves two elements—lineage (which cannot be forced) and property (over which the son has authority). Similarly, a grandson's acknowledgment of lineage is not accepted because it would attribute lineage to someone else (his father). The underlying principle is that acknowledgment of lineage is only accepted for those over whom the acknowledger has authority (wilaya) by blood or relationship. The commentary explains that the ruling is based on the limitation of authority; a person can only establish lineage for himself and his immediate dependents, not for others.

Key Terms
الإقرار (al-iqrār): A legal acknowledgment or admission of a right or obligation, here specifically acknowledgment of lineage (nasab) and its inheritance consequences.
النسب (al-nasab): Lineage or parentage; the legal relationship of descent that determines inheritance and other family rights.
الولاية (al-wilāyah): Legal authority or capacity to act on behalf of another; here, the authority to establish lineage for someone else, which is limited to direct relatives and the master over the slave.

Inheritance Claim: Depositary Must Hand Over to Sole Heir
Ruling on heritage claim of deposit by sole heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the depositary is commanded to hand over to the sole heir who claims the deposit and explain the reason based on ownership transfer.

Core Idea
Upon the owner's death, the deposit becomes the heir's property; the depositary must hand over if both agree.

This passage addresses a specific inheritance scenario: a man claims that his father died, leaving a deposit, and that he is the sole heir. If the depositary (e.g., a banker or trustee) believes the claim, Islamic law commands him to hand over the deposit. The reason is clear: upon the owner's death, ownership of the deposit immediately transfers to his heirs. Both parties agree on this transfer, so the depositary is not giving away someone else's property but returning property that now belongs to the claimant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A claimant declares that his father died, leaving a deposit, and he is the only heir. The depositary believes this statement.
2
State the ruling
The depositary is commanded (yumarru) to hand over the deposit to the claimant.
The imperative 'amr' indicates obligation (wajib).
3
Explain the reason
Upon death, the deposit ceases to be the owner's property and becomes the heir's property. The depositary is no longer holding another's property.
Passage: 'because his property does not remain after his death; as both have agreed that it is the property of the heir.'
4
Note the condition
The ruling applies only when both the claimant and depositary agree that the deposit now belongs to the heir.
This mutual acknowledgment is key; the depositary is not forced to accept a bare claim without belief.

Scholastic Dispute: Is the Claimant an Agent or the Heir?
Intra-Hanafi dispute over the identity of the claimant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two interpretations of the phrase 'he claims that his father died' and explain why the relied-upon view takes the claimant as the heir himself.

Core Idea
The depositary is never ordered to hand over to an agent claiming agency; only the direct heir may receive without proof.

The passage includes a subtle scholastic dispute about the pronoun in the phrase 'if he claims that his father died'. Some early commentators, like al-ʿAynī, interpreted the claimant as an agent representing the heir. Qāḍī Zādah strongly objects, stating that the depositary is never commanded to hand over to a mere claimant of agency. The correct understanding is that the claimant is the heir himself. This intra-Hanafi disagreement highlights the precision required in legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed phrase
The Arabic phrase 'ولو ادعى أنه مات أبوه' (if he claims that his father died) is ambiguous: who is the 'he'?
The pronoun could refer to the claimant himself or to someone else.
2
Present the two interpretations
Al-ʿAynī interpreted the claimant as an agent of the heir (i.e., the claimant says 'the father of the heir died'). Qāḍī Zādah interprets the claimant as the heir himself (i.e., the claimant's father died).
These are both within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze Qāḍī Zādah's argument
He argues that the depositary is never commanded to hand over to a self-proclaimed agent, because that would open the door to fraud. The text says 'أمر بالدفع إليه', but if he were an agent, the depositary would not be ordered to hand over without proof of agency.
Qāḍī Zādah says: لا مجال لأن يكون الضمير راجعاً إلى من قال إني وكيل.
4
Determine the relied-upon view
The majority of Hanafi scholars, including the later authoritative commentators, adopt Qāḍī Zādah's interpretation. The passage itself (footnote) presents this as the correct understanding.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
State the practical consequence
The ruling about handover applies only to the direct heir, not to an agent. An agent would need separate authorization and the depositary would not be commanded to hand over merely on his claim.
This prevents misuse of the ruling by unauthorized persons.

Presumption of Death for the Missing Person: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on the missing person's death presumption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the presumption of death for a missing person, identify their proofs, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
The mufta bihi for the missing person's death is 90 years, but the classical positions range from 120 years to the death of peers.

This passage from the Book of the Missing Person presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on when a missing person is legally presumed dead. The rulings differ based on whether one adopts a fixed age limit (120 or 90 years) or the death of all peers. Understanding this ikhtilaf and its resolution is crucial for inheritance and marital matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
The passage opens with the 120-year rule: when a missing person completes 120 years from birth, we rule his death. This is based on the apparent observation that no one lives beyond that age.
This is a narration from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa, considered a nawadir transmission.
2
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text then cites three positions: (a) 120 years from Abu Hanifa via al-Hasan, (b) death of peers (موت الأقران) from Imam Muhammad (zahir al-madhhab), and (c) 90 years from later scholars. The death-of-peers criterion requires that all of the missing person's contemporaries have died; if even one peer remains, death is not ruled.
There is also a sub-dispute: whether peers from all lands or only from the missing person's own town are considered. Khiwahrizadah preferred the latter.
3
Assess the tarjih (preference)
Shaykh Muhammad ibn Hamid fixed the age at ninety years, and this became the mufta bihi. Later authorities like al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah followed this view.
The 90-year rule is a practical compromise, easier to apply than the death-of-peers criterion.
4
Apply the ruling
In practice, a missing person is presumed dead after 90 years from birth unless evidence suggests otherwise. This ruling affects inheritance, the wife's iddah and remarriage, and division of wealth.
The death-of-peers criterion, though still known, is not the operative position for fatwa.
