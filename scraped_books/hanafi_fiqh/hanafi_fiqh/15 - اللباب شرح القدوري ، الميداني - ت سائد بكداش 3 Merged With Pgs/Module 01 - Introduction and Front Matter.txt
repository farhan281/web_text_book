Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri is the essential commentary on the Hanafi matn for centuries.

This lesson examines the title page of the third volume of Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri, a standard commentary on Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. We will identify the main figures associated with this work: the original matn author (al-Quduri), the commentator (al-Maydani), and the modern editor (Bakdash). Understanding this bibliographic information is the first step in properly situating the fiqh content that follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (original text)
Look for the name of the original work being commented upon. In this case, it is 'Mukhtasar al-Quduri', a renowned Hanafi fiqh primer by Imam Ahmad al-Quduri (d. 428 AH).
The phrase 'شرح المختصر القُدُورِيّ في الفقه الحنفي' indicates the matn is al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
2
Identify the commentator and his era
Find the name of the scholar who wrote the commentary. Here it is Sheikh Abdul Ghani al-Ghunaymi al-Maydani al-Dimashqi (1222-1298 AH). Note his nisbah and dates.
Al-Maydani is a later Hanafi scholar; his commentary is widely used in traditional curricula.
3
Identify the editor and publisher
Check for the name of the critical editor (محقق) and the publishing house. This edition is edited by Professor Dr. Sa'id Bakdash and published by Dar al-Nashr al-Islamiyya.
Critical editions (tahqiq) verify the text against manuscripts, adding scholarly value.
4
Synthesize the chain of transmission
Understand how these elements relate: al-Quduri wrote the matn, al-Maydani wrote the sharh, and Bakdash produced the modern critical edition. This chain shows the living tradition of Hanafi scholarship.
Knowledge of this chain is important for proper citation and contextual understanding.

Introduction to Kitab al-Buyu': Placement and Definition of Sale
Opening of Kitab al-Buyu' with definition and placement rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the chapter on sales is placed after worship and before marriage in the Hanafi fiqh manual, and state the linguistic definition of bay'.

Core Idea
The need for sales is universal and sustains life, so it follows worship and precedes marriage.

This lesson introduces the Book of Sales from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri. The author explains the arrangement of chapters in the fiqh manual and defines the term 'bay'. Understanding this placement helps you see the structure of Hanafi fiqh and the integration of worldly transactions with worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the placement rationale
Read the first part of the passage. The author states that the chapter on sales (al-buyu') is placed after worship (al-ibadat) and before marriage (al-nikah). Identify the reason given: the need for sales is more general (a'amm) because it includes both young and old and is the foundation of livelihood.
The rationale is based on necessity and hierarchy of needs.
2
Analyze the linguistic definition of bay'
The passage defines 'bay' linguistically as the exchange of one thing for another, whether property or not. Note that this includes barter and any exchange, not just monetary sales. The text cites a proof: 'Indeed, Allah...' (though the full proof is not quoted).
The legal definition in Hanafi fiqh adds specific conditions like offer and acceptance.
3
Understand the pluralization of 'bay'
The passage explains why the verbal noun 'bay' is pluralized as 'buyu'. It can refer to the object sold (passive participle) or to different types of sales. Ibn Abidin's commentary elaborates on this.
This grammatical point affects how we categorize sales in fiqh.
4
Connect to the structure of fiqh manuals
Reflect on how the placement reflects the priorities of the Hanafi school: worship first, then worldly transactions essential for life (sales), then family matters (marriage). This ordering is pedagogical and practical.
Other schools may order chapters differently.

Commentary
This is the opening passage of the Book of Sales (Kitab al-Buyu') from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, a Hanafi fiqh manual. The author, al-Maydani, begins by explaining the placement of this chapter within the overall structure of the book. He states that the sales chapter follows the chapter on worship (particularly prayer) and precedes the chapter on marriage. The reason given is that the need for sales is more general (a'amm), as it encompasses both young and old, and it is the foundation of livelihood which sustains the body. Some other authors, however, placed marriage first because marriage is considered an act of worship. The passage then defines the word 'bay' (sale). It explains that linguistically, 'bay' is a verbal noun (masdar) meaning the exchange of one thing for another, whether property or not. It can also be used to mean the object sold (al-mabi'), and when pluralized, it can be pluralized either as if it were the object (hence 'buyu') or with consideration of the different types of sales. The commentator quotes a note from Ibn Abidin explaining why a verbal noun is pluralized: either because it is used in the sense of the object (the sold items) which are many, or because it is pluralized by type, such as effective sale, suspended sale, irregular sale, void sale, etc. This sets the stage for the detailed discussions of sales that follow in the chapter.

البيع (al-bay'): Linguistically: exchanging one thing for another, whether property or not. Legally: a contract of exchange of property for property with mutual consent, but here the definition is purely linguistic.
مبادلة (mubadalah): Exchange; the act of giving one thing for another.

Wikalah is both a legal delegation and a spiritual call to rely on Allah as the ultimate Agent.

This passage opens the Book of Agency by linking it to Mudarabah and then presenting the legal and linguistic definitions. It also includes a profound spiritual insight from Ibn al-Humam, reminding us that true agency lies in making Allah our Wakil. Definition, Pillars, Conditions and Types of Sulh (Compromise)
Defines sulh, its elements, and classifies its types with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sulh, state its pillar and conditions, distinguish the three types of sulh, and apply the Hanafi ruling that all three types are permissible.

Core Idea
Sulh is a contract to remove dispute; its pillar is offer and acceptance, and all three types (acknowledgment, silence, denial) are permissible.

The chapter on sulh (compromise) follows agency, guarantee, and transfer of debt because all involve assisting in fulfilling needs. Sulh linguistically means peace after conflict. Legally, it is a contract that removes dispute and ends litigation. Understanding its definition, essential elements, and types is crucial for applying this important means of dispute resolution in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Read the definition: 'a contract that removes dispute and cuts off litigation.' Note the linguistic origin (peace after conflict) and the key legal effect (ending the dispute).
The definition emphasizes the purpose: ending conflict, not just any agreement.
2
Determine the pillar (rukn) and conditions
Identify the pillar: offer and acceptance (ijab wa qabul). Then list the conditions: intellect, puberty, freedom (with exception for permitted minors/slaves), and that the subject matter must be known if it requires possession.
Freedom is a condition except when a minor or slave is permitted and there is benefit; see footnote in text.
3
Recognize the three types of sulh
Based on the defendant's response: with acknowledgment (iqrar), with silence (sukut - neither admit nor deny), and with denial (inkar). All are permissible (ja'iz).
Sulh with denial is distinctive to Hanafi; some schools require acknowledgment for validity.
4
Understand the proof (dalil)
The Quranic verse 'And do not quarrel' (8:46) implies that avoiding discord is legislated, so sulh to prevent quarreling is permissible. This is the evidence for the permissibility of all types.
The dalalah is zanni: the prohibition of quarreling implies permission of its opposite (sulh).
5
Note the legal effect
Upon valid sulh, ownership of the consideration transfers to the plaintiff, and the defendant loses the right of recovery. The dispute is conclusively resolved.
This effect occurs even in sulh with denial, as the contract is causa for removing discord.
