Borrowing Fungibles in Absolute Terms Is a Loan (Qard)
Ruling with rational proof and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on absolute borrowing of fungibles, explain the rational proof (istihsan) based on consumption necessity, and identify the exception when a specific non-consumptive use is designated.

Core Idea
Absolute borrowing of consumable fungibles is qard, not i'arah.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a critical distinction in Hanafi fiqh: when does borrowing a fungible item become a loan? The answer depends on whether the borrower intends to consume the item or simply use it without consumption. Understanding this ruling helps avoid confusion between two similar but legally distinct contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The passage states that borrowing dirhams, dinars, measured items, weighed items, and roughly counted similar items 'when absolute' is a loan (qard). Identify what 'absolute' means: no specific non-consumptive use is mentioned.
2
Understand the rational proof (istihsan)
The proof: borrowing (i'arah) grants usufruct, but these items cannot be used without consuming their substance. Necessity requires transfer of ownership, making it qard. This is istihsan over strict qiyas (which would keep it i'arah).
The Hanafi school uses istihsan to prioritize practical necessity.
3
Note the exception
If the borrower specifies a non-consumptive use (e.g., calibrating a scale with dirhams or decorating a shop), it remains i'arah, not qard. The borrower only has the right to that specific benefit.
4
Compare with other schools
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider all borrowing of fungibles as i'arah, but with liability for value. The Hanafi position is qard for absolute borrowing.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Example: Borrowing 100 dirhams to spend on food is qard; borrowing the same coins to use as a paperweight is i'arah.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof from Al-Hidayah. The Hanafi position is that borrowing fungible items like dirhams, dinars, measured, weighed, and roughly counted items in absolute terms (without specifying a particular use) is actually a loan (qard), not a borrowing. The reason is that borrowing (i'arah) grants the right to use the usufruct while returning the very item, but these items cannot be used without consuming them. For example, using money for spending requires its consumption. Therefore, by necessity, ownership of the items passes to the borrower, and the contract becomes a loan where he must return an equivalent in quantity and quality. However, if the borrower specifies a non-consumptive use, such as using dirhams to calibrate a scale or decorate a shop, then it remains a true borrowing (i'arah) and not a loan, because the items are returned intact.

Ruling on borrowing land for building or planting

Core Idea
Borrowing land for construction is permissible, but the lender can revoke anytime – the contract is non-binding.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on borrowing land (i'arah) for building or planting. The passage from al-Lubab clarifies that such a loan is permissible because it transfers a usufruct, analogous to renting. However, unlike a rental contract, the lender retains the right to revoke at any time, and the borrower must remove any structures or plants upon revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is borrowing land for building or planting. The ruling is permissible (ja'iz).
2
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The permissibility is based on qiyas: it is a type of benefit (manfa'ah) like dwelling, which can be owned through ijarah, so likewise through i'arah.
3
Recognize the non-binding nature
The lender may revoke whenever he wishes because the contract is ghayr lazim (non-binding).
4
Distinguish from other contracts
Compare with ijarah (rental) which is binding for the agreed term; i'arah is not binding even after the borrower has taken possession and invested in construction.

Borrowing Fungibles Is a Loan Unless Specified
Ruling on borrowing fungibles as loan with qualification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on borrowing fungible items and distinguish between cases where it is considered qard or 'ariyah based on the specified purpose.

Core Idea
Borrowing coins or food is a loan unless you borrow for non-consumptive use.

When you borrow something that gets used up, like money or rice, is it a loan or just a borrow? The Hanafi school has a clear rule: if the thing is consumed in normal use, the contract becomes a loan (qard). But there’s an important exception: if you specify that you won’t consume it—like using coins to weigh—then it stays a true ‘ariyah. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Default Ruling
The passage states that borrowing dirhams, dinars, measured items, and weighed items is considered a loan (qard). Recognize that this is the default ruling when no specific purpose is mentioned.
2
Understand the Legal Reasoning
The reasoning: ‘Ariyah grants only usufruct (manafi‘), but these items cannot be used without consuming their substance. Therefore, ownership of the substance must transfer, which happens through gift or loan. Loan (qard) is chosen because it is the lesser (requires repayment). Thus, the borrower must return an equivalent (mithl).
3
Note the Qualification – When Purpose Is Specified
The passage qualifies: this ruling applies only when the purpose is unspecified. If the borrower specifies a non-consumptive use (e.g., to calibrate a scale or decorate a shop), it remains a valid ‘ariyah, not a loan. The borrower is entitled only to that benefit and must return the exact item.
This shows the importance of niyyah (intent) in contracts.
4
Recognize Cross-Madhhab Difference
Imam al-Shafi‘i holds that borrowing money is always ‘ariyah (non-binding) with a guarantee, not qard. The Hanafi position relies on the nature of consumption necessitating ownership transfer.
The practical difference: Hanafi makes the contract binding (lazim); Shafi‘i keeps it non-binding but with liability.

The reason is that 'ariyah is defined as the transfer of usufruct (manafi'), but these items cannot be used without consuming their substance (istihlak al-'ayn). Thus, to make the contract valid, ownership of the substance itself must be transferred. This can happen either through a gift (hiba) or a loan (qard). The author notes that qard is the lesser of the two (because it requires repayment), so the ruling is that the borrower must return a similar item (mithl). If the borrower specifies a purpose that does not consume the item—such as using dirhams to calibrate a scale or using them as decoration—then the contract remains a proper 'ariyah, not a loan, and the borrower is only entitled to the specified benefit without consuming the item. The reasoning is that the default ruling applies only when the usage is not specified; when the purpose is explicitly non-consumptive, the ruling is different.

Borrowing Land for Building or Planting and Revocation Rules
Ruling on borrowing land for building or planting, revocation, and compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on borrowing land for building or planting, explain the lender's right to revoke, and determine when compensation is due based on the presence or absence of a fixed term.

Core Idea
Borrowing land for building or planting is permissible but revocable; compensation depends on whether a fixed term was promised.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses the Hanafi position on borrowing land for construction or cultivation. It establishes the permissibility of such borrowing, the lender's right to revoke at any time, and the important distinction in compensation when a fixed term has been set. Understanding these rules is crucial for both lenders and borrowers in property transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is borrowing land for building or planting date palms. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz). This is because it is a type of benefit, and benefits can be contracted via ijarah (lease), so by analogy via 'ariyah (borrowing).
Note: The analogy is to ijarah, where the benefit is owned by the lessee.
2
Analyze the nature of the contract
The 'ariyah contract is non-binding (ghayr lazim). The lender can revoke at any time because the contract does not transfer ownership but only temporary use, and the lender retains the right to reclaim the property.
This is different from ijarah, which is binding for the agreed term.
3
Determine the effect of revocation
Upon revocation, the lender may force the borrower to remove any structures or planting (qal') to vacate the land. However, if removal would harm the land (e.g., cause damage), the structures are left in place and the lender pays their value as removed (i.e., market price of materials).
The harm exception prevents waste and damage to the land.
4
Evaluate the compensation rule
If no fixed term was specified, the borrower bears the removal cost with no compensation from the lender. If a fixed term was set and the lender revokes before its expiration, the lender must compensate the borrower for the decrease in value of the structures due to removal (ma naqasa bi al-qal').
The key factor is whether the borrower relied on a promise of duration.
5
Understand the underlying reasoning
The borrower without a fixed term is considered mugh'tarr (self-deluded but not deceived), so he bears the risk. With a fixed term, the borrower is maghrur (deceived by the lender's promise), so the lender compensates to prevent harm (daf'an li al-darar).
This is based on the principle of ghurur (deception) and the rule that harm should be removed.

Liability for Removal of Improvements on Borrowed Land
Ruling with proof from reasoning and Hidāyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a lender must compensate the borrower for removing improvements from borrowed land and distinguish between a fixed-term and indefinite loan.

Core Idea
Compensation for removal depends on whether a time was set: no set time – borrower bears cost; time set and revoked early – lender pays.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a borrower who builds or plants on land that he has borrowed for use (al-‘āriyah). When the lender demands the land back, he can require the borrower to remove his improvements. But who bears the cost of removal and any loss in value? The answer turns on whether a time limit was agreed. The passage also addresses a special narration by al-Ḥākim al-Shahīd and the separate case of sowing crops.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The borrower builds or plants on borrowed land. The ruling: the lender may require removal. Determine the hukm level (obligation) and the conditions for compensation.
2
Locate the condition: time set or not
The key distinction is whether a time limit was specified for the loan. If no time, no compensation (borrower is mughatir). If time set and lender revokes early, compensation is due (borrower is maghrūr).
The terms mughatir and maghrūr are central to the reasoning.
3
Trace the reasoning (ta‘līl)
Why does the ruling differ? The borrower who trusts an absolute contract (mughatir) assumes the risk. The borrower who relies on a promise of time (maghrūr) is deceived by the lender’s early revocation, so the lender must compensate for the decrease.
This is a ta‘līl based on prevention of harm and fairness.
4
Note the intra-Hanafī ikhtilāf and exception
Al-Ḥākim al-Shahīd narrates that the lender may alternatively keep the improvements for their value. Also, if removal harms the land, the lender chooses. For planting crops, the borrower may stay until harvest with rent, regardless of time.
These are additional rulings from the Hidāyah and other sources.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In 'aariyah the borrower pays return costs; in ijarah the owner does.

This lesson examines a practical question: when you borrow or rent an item, who pays to return it? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on the nature of the contract. In a gratuitous loan ('aariyah), the borrower benefits and must bear the cost of returning. In a rental (ijarah), the owner retains ownership and receives rent, so returning is the owner's duty. Understanding this difference prevents disputes and ensures fair allocation of expenses.

Guided Path

For ijarah, the lessee's obligation is only tamkīn (enabling the owner to take possession) and takhliyah (vacating). The actual return (radd) is not required of him; hence the owner bears the cost.
The owner still owns the item and receives rent, so return is part of his ownership responsibility.

This lesson examines the first scenario from the source: borrowing an animal and returning it to the owner’s stable. Rulings on the Foundling (Laqīṭ)
Five Hanafi rulings on foundling status and claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five Hanafi rulings on foundling precedence, religion, and claims of lineage/freedom, and apply the principle of istiḥsān to distinguish accepted benefits from rejected harms.

Core Idea
The foundling's apparent freedom and Islam are protected; claims that benefit are accepted but those that harm are rejected.

Foundlings are children of unknown parentage, and Islamic law establishes special rules to protect their rights and status. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents five key rulings: how to resolve competing claims, how place determines the child's religion, how a dhimmi's paternity claim affects lineage and religion, why slavery claims are rejected, and why a slave's paternity claim is accepted while the child remains free. These rulings balance apparent evidence (ẓāhir) with juristic preference (istiḥsān) to benefit the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and subject
The passage covers claims regarding a foundling (laqīṭ). Identify which ruling applies to each type of claim: two claimants, a dhimmi paternity claim, a slavery claim, and a slave's paternity claim. Also note the ruling on religion based on place.
2
Apply the mark-based precedence rule
When two claimants each claim the foundling, check if one describes a physical mark. The one who describes a mark is more entitled because the mark corroborates his claim. If neither describes a mark, the child is equally shared.
The mark is not a pillar but a evidence supporting one claim over another.
3
Determine religion based on place of discovery
If the foundling is found in Muslim territory (city or village), the child is Muslim. If found in a dhimmi village, synagogue, or church, the child is dhimmi. This is the apparent ruling (ẓāhir al-riwāyah).
There are multiple narrations; ẓāhir al-riwāyah gives primacy to place.

If a dhimmi claims the foundling is his son and the child was found in a Muslim territory, the lineage is established (paternity recognized) but the child remains Muslim. This uses istiḥsān: the benefit of lineage is accepted, but the harm of changing religion is rejected.
The child inherits from the dhimmi father but retains Islamic status.

Evaluate claims of slavery and paternity by a slave
A claim that the foundling is a slave is not accepted unless proven; the child remains free (apparent freedom is preserved). A slave's claim of paternity is accepted because it benefits the child (gives him a father), but the child remains free.
The Ṣāḥibayn note that a free man's claim takes precedence over a slave's, and a Muslim's over a dhimmi's.

Defining Mawat and the Abu Yusuf–Muhammad Dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on the classification of previously-owned abandoned land as mawat, and state the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) for reviving such land.

Core Idea
Land owned in Islam but abandoned: Abu Yusuf says it's mawat; Muhammad says it belongs to the Muslim community.

Reviving dead land is a key means of acquiring ownership in Hanafi law, but what counts as 'dead'? In this passage from al-Lubab, the author defines mawat and then presents a sharp intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad over land that was once owned but is now abandoned and ownerless. Understanding this disagreement is essential for knowing when revival leads to ownership and when the land is held for the community.

Identify the two types of mawat
The passage distinguishes between land that is ancient ruins with no known owner (type one) and land that was owned in Islam but whose owner is now unknown (type two). Both must be far from inhabited areas.

Apply the shouting test
Land is far if a person standing at the farthest edge of the cultivated area cannot hear his own shout when calling from the land in question.

The dispute centers on the second type: land that was owned in Islam but abandoned so long that the owner is unknown.

Under Abu Yusuf, any person can revive the land and acquire ownership. Under Muhammad, the land cannot be revived individually; it belongs to the community and requires the imam's permission.
Later Hanafi fatwa often follows Abu Yusuf for practicality.

The Hanafi school sets specific conditions for land to be considered mawat, including distance from civilization and absence of ownership. An important intra-madhhab difference exists between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding land that was once owned but whose owner is unknown. Understanding this distinction is crucial for knowing how the Imam may dispose of such land.

1
Extract the conditions of mawat
Identify the two categories from the matn: (1) land never owned in Islam, and (2) land owned in Islam but owner unknown. Also note the distance condition: a shout from the edge of cultivated land must not be heard.
The shout test is a practical, not precise, measure.
2

Compare Abu Yusuf (considers land owned in Islam with unknown owner as mawat) with Muhammad al-Shaybani (says it is not mawat but belongs to the Muslim community). Understand al-Zayla'i's reconciliation: Quduri included it based on similarity in ruling, not essence.

This applies only if the land is also far from settlement.
4

Consider a barren plot two miles from the nearest village where a shout is not heard. Apply the conditions: no known owner, far. It is mawat. If it was once owned but owner unknown, it is treated as mawat in ruling.

Revival of Dead Land and the Imam's Permission
States ruling on reviving mawat with/without Imam's permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reviving dead land with and without the Imam's permission, and explain the qiyas to ghanimah used by Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Dead land revived without Imam's permission is not owned because it is analogous to communal spoils.

This lesson addresses a fundamental rule of land ownership in Hanafi fiqh: the revival of dead (unowned) land. While the act of making barren land productive is encouraged, the juridical effect—ownership—depends on the Imam's authorization. We will explore the consensus view when permission is given, Abu Hanifa's reasoning for denying ownership without it, and the underlying analogy to spoils of war.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its two scenarios
Recognize that the ruling applies to reviving dead land (ihyā' al-mawāt) and distinguishes between having the Imam's permission or not.
The act itself is reviving barren land; the difference is the juridical consequence.
2
Understand the consensus (ittifāq) with permission
When the Imam grants permission, the reviver owns the land—this is agreed upon among Hanafi imams.
This shows the Imam's authority to allocate public resources.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's view without permission
According to Abu Hanifa, without permission the reviver does not own it. The reason is that dead land in Islamic territory is treated as spoils (ghanīmah) for the Muslim community, acquired through conquest (ījāf al-khayl wa al-rikāb).
This is an explicit qiyas jaliy: the illa is that both are communal property obtained by force.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that revival without permission can still grant ownership if the land is truly unowned, as they view the analogy to spoils as weak.
The text does not elaborate their proof, but the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: no ownership without the Imam's permission. This aligns with the public interest and the Imam's role in distributing communal lands.
Later Hanafi scholars have confirmed this as the standard fatwa.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership of Revived Land Without Imam's Permission
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership through revival of dead land without imam's permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on ownership of revived land without Imam's permission, identify the analogical reasoning used, and apply the mufta bihi ruling to a practical scenario.

Core Idea
The reviver of dead land owns it without permission according to the Sahibayn; Abu Hanifa requires the Imam's grant.

Land is a special category of property. While the Hanafi school agrees that reviving dead land can give ownership, the famous dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students centers on whether the Imam's permission is required. The Sahibayn analogize to firewood and hunting; Abu Hanifa sees land as fundamentally communal. This lesson dissects the arguments and clarifies the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions

They use qiyas on mubah items like firewood and hunting: once one takes possession of a mubah thing, ownership is established.

Core Idea
A canal in another's land does not automatically grant a harim; Abu Hanifa demands proof, while the Sahibayn allow a narrow musannat.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about the rights of a canal owner whose waterway passes through someone else's property. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze property rights and evidentiary burdens in Hanafi fiqh.

Abu Hanifa relies on the principle that the default (zahir) is for the landowner; any exception needs evidence. The Sahibayn rely on necessity: the canal cannot be maintained without some access, so a limited right is implied.
The dispute is over the evidentiary threshold and the scope of implied rights.

Under Abu Hanifa, a canal owner who cannot prove an agreement or ancient use gets no protected land. Under the Sahibayn, he automatically gets a narrow embankment. In later Hanafi practice, the Sahibayn's view is often preferred for ease of maintenance.
The difference is whether the canal owner can claim any land without explicit proof.

Core Idea

In Hanafi law, a master may permit his slave to engage in trade. However, the master can revoke this permission by imposing a restriction (hajr). The Muzara'ah (Sharecropping) Dispute: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with fatwa resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on muzara'ah, state the evidence for each side, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Muzara'ah is invalid by qiyas but upheld by istihsan due to need — the Sahibayn's view is the fatwa.

Sharecropping is a common agricultural partnership, but its legal status sparked a significant intra-Hanafi debate. In this lesson, we explore why Imam Abu Hanifa deemed it invalid, while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, upheld its validity. We will trace the proofs, understand the tarjih (preference), and see how the school eventually adopted the Sahibayn's view as the operative fatwa due to public necessity and ongoing practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Dispute
Recognise that muzara'ah is a contract where one party provides land and seeds, and the other works for a share of the crop. The core dispute is whether this contract is valid (sahih) or invalid (batil).
The term 'muzara'ah' is often used interchangeably with 'mukhabarah' in some narrations.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Proofs
Imam Abu Hanifa holds muzara'ah invalid. His proofs include: a hadith forbidding al-mukhabarah; an analogy (qiyas) to the prohibited 'qafiz al-tahan' (hiring for a fixed measure of flour); and the presence of unknown or non-existent wages, which invalidate the contract.
He interprets the Prophet's Khaybar transaction as a land tax (kharaj muqasamah), not as muzara'ah.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and Proofs
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold muzara'ah permissible. Their proofs include: the same Khaybar hadith, which they interpret as a legitimate muzara'ah contract; and an analogy to mudarabah (profit-sharing), as both involve a partnership between capital and labor.
They consider that the Prophet's approval of the Khaybar deal demonstrates permissibility.
4

Despite Abu Hanifa's stronger analogy (qiyas), the later Hanafi authorities prefer the Sahibayn's view. The reason is public need (hajat al-nas) and the widespread practice of the Ummah (ta'amul). Analogy is set aside by istihsan (juristic preference), similar to the case of manufacturing contracts (istisna').
Numerous sources (Qadykhan, al-Hidayah, etc.) confirm the fatwa is on the Sahibayn's statement.
5
Apply the Mufta Bihi to Real Contracts
In practical terms, a muzara'ah contract where the worker gets a known share (e.g., one-third or one-half) is valid according to the mufta bihi. Conditions such as uncertainty about the share or exploitative terms (mukhabarah) would still render it invalid.

Valid and Invalid Forms of Muzara'ah According to Sahibayn
Classification of sharecropping forms with qiyas justification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the four forms of muzara'ah as valid or void based on the allocation of land, seed, oxen, and labor, and explain the qiyas reasoning used by the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Only three allocations of land, seed, oxen, and labor yield a valid sharecropping contract; the fourth is void because it cannot be reinterpreted as a lease.

The Hanafi school, through Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), established that sharecropping (muzara'ah) can be valid under specific conditions by reinterpreting it as an underlying lease (ijara). This lesson breaks down the four possible allocations of the four essential elements—land, seed, oxen, and labor—and shows why three are valid and one is void. Understanding this classification is key to applying Hanafi fiqh in agricultural contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four components
List the four resources in a sharecropping contract: land (al-ard), seed (al-badr), oxen (al-baqar), and labor (al-amal). These are the elements whose allocation determines validity.
Oxen represent draft animals; labor is the work of the farmer.
2
List the four possible forms
From the text, enumerate the four forms studied by the Sahibayn: (1) land+seed vs labor+oxen; (2) land vs labor+oxen+seed; (3) land+oxen+seed vs labor; (4) land+oxen vs seed+labor. Forms 1-3 are valid; form 4 is void.
Additional invalid forms exist but are not part of this core four.
3
Reinterpret each form as a lease (ijara)
For each form, attempt to construe it as a lease where one party rents something from the other in exchange for a share of the produce. In valid forms, one party becomes the hirer and the other the hired, with the remaining resource treated as subsidiary (tabi').
For form 1: landowner hires the worker; oxen are subsidiary to labor. For form 2: worker hires the land. For form 3: landowner hires the worker. For form 4: neither ascription works.
4
Determine validity using qiyas
If the reinterpretation yields a valid lease (known consideration, determinable benefit), the muzara'ah is sahih; if not, it is batil. The Sahibayn used istihsan to override analogical objection (gharar) because of customary practice.
Abu Hanifa rejected all forms due to gharar; the Sahibayn's view is the mufta bihi.

Stating a condition and citing an internal Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In muzara'a, the crop must be shared proportionally; a fixed quantity invalidates, but some Hanafis allow omitting the period based on custom.
