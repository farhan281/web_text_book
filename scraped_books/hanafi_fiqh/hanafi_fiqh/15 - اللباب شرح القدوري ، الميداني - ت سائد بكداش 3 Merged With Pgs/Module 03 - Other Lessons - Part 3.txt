Testifying (Ishhad) After Initial Demand and Its Effect on Lapsing of Pre-Emption
Explains the procedure of ishhad and the intra-Hanafi dispute on lapse by delay.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure of ishhad in pre-emption and explain the Hanafi dispute on whether the right lapses by delay, identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
After ishhad, the pre-emption right does not lapse by delay — Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position.

In this lesson, we examine the step after the initial demand (talab al-muwathaba) in pre-emption: the testification (ishhad). The classical Hanafi texts explain that ishhad stabilizes the right, but there is an internal dispute about whether indefinite delay can cause the right to lapse. We will follow the reasoning of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and see which view prevails in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Understand the procedure of ishhad
Identify whom to testify against: the seller if the property is still in his possession, the buyer (even if not in possession), or at the property itself. The condition for testifying against the seller is that he still has possession.
Ishhad is a verbal declaration before witnesses.
2
Recognize the effect of ishhad
Once ishhad is performed, the pre-emption right is established (stabilized) and does not lapse by mere delay.
This is the immediate consequence according to the texts.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa holds the right does not lapse by delay at all. Imam Muhammad says it lapses after one month without excuse. Abu Yusuf has a narration that it lapses after one or two sessions.
The dispute is on the effect of delay after ishhad.
4
Analyze the proofs (ta'lil)
Abu Hanifa argues that a once-established right only lapses by explicit forfeiture (isqat), analogous to other rights. Muhammad argues that indefinite delay harms the buyer who cannot freely dispose of the property, so a one-month limit is set.
The harm argument is a secondary consideration in Hanafi methodology.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: the right does not lapse by delay. This is the apparent madhhab (zahir al-madhhab) and the basis for fatwa, as stated in al-Hidayah and relied upon by al-Nasafi.
This is the operative ruling for contemporary practice.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the procedure of establishing pre-emption (shuf'a) after the initial demand (talab al-muwathaba). It explains that the pre-emptor must rise from his session and have witnesses bear witness (ishhad) against either the seller (if the property is still in his possession), the buyer (even if not yet in possession, since he is the owner), or at the property itself (since the right is attached to it). The condition for witnessing against the seller is that he still has possession; otherwise, he is not a litigant. The form of this demand is a verbal statement before witnesses. Once this is done, the pre-emptor's right is established and does not lapse due to delay alone, according to Abu Hanifa. This is the apparent position of the Hanafi school and the basis for fatwa, as stated in al-Hidayah and relied upon by al-Nasafi. Imam Muhammad holds that if the pre-emptor delays for a month after witnessing without excuse, his right lapses, reasoning that indefinite delay harms the buyer. Abu Yusuf has a narration that it lapses after one or two sessions. However, the relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa: once the right is established, it only lapses by explicit forfeiture (isqat).

Key Terms
الشُفْعَة (al-shuf'a): The right of a co-owner or neighbor to pre-empt (buy) a property that has been sold to an outsider, by paying the price to the buyer.
طَلَبُ المُوَاثَبَة (talab al-muwathaba): The immediate demand made by the pre-emptor upon learning of the sale, without delay.
الإِشْهَاد (al-ishhad): Having witnesses bear witness to one's claim of pre-emption, to establish the right securely and prevent it from lapsing by denial.

No Pre-emption in Buildings and Palm Trees Sold Without Land
Textual variant confirming no shuf'ah for movable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shuf'ah for buildings and palm trees sold without land, and explain why an upper story is treated differently based on the criterion of qarar (permanence).

Core Idea
A building or tree sold without land is movable – no pre-emption; an upper story is immovable due to its permanence, so pre-emption applies.

This passage addresses a textual variant in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, confirming that pre-emption (shuf'ah) does not apply to buildings and palm trees if sold without the land (al-arsa). The reasoning is that these items lack permanence (qarar) and are thus considered movable property. However, an upper story (al-'ulu) has a right of permanence because it is attached to the lower story, making it immovable and subject to shuf'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling buildings or palm trees without the land. The ruling is that no pre-emption (shuf'ah) attaches to this sale; the buyer does not acquire a right of shuf'ah over the property.
The ruling is explicit: 'لا شفعة في البناء والنخل إذا بيع دون العرصة'
2
Locate the illah (reason)
The text says 'لأنه لا قرار له' (because it has no permanence). This lack of qarar makes the building or tree movable (naqli) property.
Movable property is not subject to shuf'ah in Hanafi law.
3
Contrast with the upper story
The upper story (al-'ulu) is distinguished because it has a right of permanence (haqq al-qarar) due to its attachment to the lower story. Therefore, it is considered immovable and shuf'ah applies to it.
The upper story also grants its owner the right to claim shuf'ah in the lower floor if no separate pathway exists.
4
Recognize the criterion
The key legal criterion for shuf'ah is qarar (permanence). Property with qarar is immovable and subject to shuf'ah; property without it is movable and exempt.
This distinction prevents unjust claims on transient or unattached assets.

Equality of Muslim and Dhimmi in Pre-emption
States the ruling that Muslims and non-Muslim subjects are equal in the right of pre-emption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the equality of Muslim and dhimmi in pre-emption and explain the legal cause (sabab) and wisdom (hikmah) behind it.

Core Idea
Muslim and dhimmi share the same cause and wisdom for pre-emption, so their right is equal.

This passage establishes a fundamental principle in Hanafi property law: the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) applies equally to Muslims and non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis). The rule is derived from the shared legal cause (sabab) — joint ownership — and the preventive wisdom (hikmah) — avoiding harm. Understanding this helps us see how Hanafi fiqh grounds legal equality on common practical grounds rather than religious identity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text clearly states that Muslims and dhimmis are equal in shuf'ah. This is the explicit ruling.
Shuf'ah is the right of pre-emption when a co-owner sells his share.
2
Understand the reasoning structure
The equality is justified by equality in the cause (sabab) and the wisdom (hikmah). These two elements are the basis for the ruling.
The sabab is the legal cause that triggers the right; the hikmah is the underlying rationale.
3
Analyze the cause (sabab)
The cause is 'ittisal al-milk' (continuity of ownership) – both Muslim and dhimmi have the same type of shared ownership in the property.
Shared ownership is the objective condition that gives rise to the right of pre-emption.
4
Analyze the wisdom (hikmah)
The wisdom is 'daf' al-darar' (repelling harm) – both face the same potential harm from a new co-owner, so the right protects both equally.
Harm here is often related to the inconvenience or damage from a new partner.
5
Conclude the derivation
Since both the cause and wisdom are common to Muslim and dhimmi, the ruling (entitlement to shuf'ah) must be common. Therefore, they are equal.
This is a classic example of qiyas: equal in 'illah leads to equal in hukm.

Condition for Shuf'ah: Ownership for a Property Consideration
Explains that shuf'ah is established only when real estate is acquired for a property consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that triggers shuf'ah and explain why the Hanafi school uses the term 'ownership' rather than 'sale' to include gifts with compensation.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is established only when real estate is acquired for a material consideration — even a gift with compensation counts.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah, we learn a crucial condition for the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The text clarifies that shuf'ah does not apply just because someone owns real estate; it applies only when that ownership was acquired for a consideration that is property (mal). This is because the pre-emptor (shafee') must be able to step into the buyer's shoes and pay the same value. The author's careful choice of words expands the scope to include transactions like gifts with a condition of compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is owning real estate for a consideration that is mal. The ruling is that shuf'ah becomes wajib (established).
The hukm level is 'wajib' according to the Hanafi position.
2
Locate the reason (talil)
The reason given is 'because it is possible to observe the condition of the law, which is to own for the same as the buyer owned, in form or value.' This connects the condition to the purpose of shuf'ah: enabling the pre-emptor to match the consideration.
This is a teleological reasoning (talil bi al-ghayah).
3
Note the textual expansion via word choice
The author uses 'owned' (malak) instead of 'sold' (bay') to include hibah bi shart al-'iwad (gift with compensation), which is essentially an exchange of property for property.
This ensures the right of shuf'ah is not circumvented by using a different contract form.

Cases Where Shuf'ah Does Not Apply
Lists six exceptions to shuf'ah with rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify six transactions that do not trigger shuf'ah and explain the reasoning based on the nature of the consideration.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only applies when property is exchanged for property; non-property considerations do not trigger pre-emption.

Shuf'ah is a right to pre-empt a sale, but not every transfer of property activates it. This passage from al-Lubab lists six specific transactions where shuf'ah does not arise, along with the underlying principle: only genuine exchanges of property for property are subject to shuf'ah. Understanding these exceptions helps delineate the boundaries of this right.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the six transactions
Read the passage carefully and list the six cases where shuf'ah is denied: dower (mahr), khul' compensation, rental, composition for intentional homicide, emancipation of a slave, and settlement by denial or silence.
The text also notes a variant: 'anha (from it) replaces 'alayha (upon it) for the last case.
2
Identify the common element
In each case, ask: is the consideration given for the house a property (māl)? The passage states shuf'ah is only for exchanges of property for property.
Examples: dower is a marital right, rental is for usufruct, blood money for intentional homicide is not due as property, emancipation is an act of worship.
3
Distinguish between intentional and accidental homicide
The text restricts the exception to intentional homicide ('amd). For accidental homicide (khaṭa'), the compensation is property (diyah), so shuf'ah would apply. Note this nuance.
Intentional homicide: compensation is not property (it's either qisas or forgiveness). Accidental: diyah is property.
4
Analyze settlement by denial vs. acknowledgment
If the owner settles by denying the claimant's right (denial), he pays to avoid an oath; this is not a property exchange. If he acknowledges the claimant's ownership and then settles, it becomes an exchange of property, triggering shuf'ah.
The textual correction from al-Hidayah emphasizes that denial implies the property never left his possession.
5
Apply the principle to new cases
Given the rationale, test whether shuf'ah would apply in cases like a gift (hibah) or a waqf. Gifts are not sales, so shuf'ah does not apply; waqf is not a transfer of ownership for consideration.

Core Idea
A partial price reduction benefits the pre-emptor; a full reduction does not because it is not integral to the sale.

When the seller reduces the price after the sale, does the pre-emptor automatically benefit? The Hanafi school distinguishes between a partial reduction, which attaches to the original contract, and a full reduction, which is treated as a separate gift. Similarly, if the buyer voluntarily increases the price, the pre-emptor is not obliged to pay more. This lesson unpacks these distinctions through clear rulings and their underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios

2

A partial reduction is seen as part of the original sale contract because some price remains. Therefore, the pre-emptor steps into the buyer's shoes and takes at the reduced price.

A full reduction leaves no price, so it cannot be part of the sale. It is a separate gift, so the pre-emptor still owes the original price.
Contrast with partial reduction: the existence of remaining price makes it integral.

The buyer's voluntary increase would harm the pre-emptor if it were forced on him. Since pre-emption is a right that should not cause additional burden, the increase is ignored.

Core Idea
Equal cause of entitlement yields equal shares in pre-emption, regardless of ownership proportions.

When multiple co-owners all qualify for pre-emption, a question arises: does the right divide according to their ownership stakes or equally? The Hanafi school, relying on the unity of the cause (all are co-owners), rules that each person gets an equal share. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical applications including forfeiture, absence, and judicial intervention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Multiple pre-emptors who are equal in the cause of entitlement, typically co-owners in the property. Confirm that they all share the same basis for pre-emption (shirkat al-milk).
2
State the rule
Pre-emption is divided among them according to the number of heads (عدد الرؤوس), not according to their ownership shares in the property. Each pre-emptor gets an equal portion of the sold share.
Example: if two co-owners own different fractions (e.g., 1/2 and 1/3), they each get half of the pre-empted portion.
3
Understand the proof
The cause (sabab) of entitlement is the same for all – co-ownership. Equality in cause necessitates equality in the right. The text argues: if one pre-emptor were alone, he would take all; so when multiple share the same cause, they divide equally.
This is a talil (causal reasoning) – the illah (effective cause) is the equal status of being a co-owner.
4
Apply corollaries
Analyze the additional rules: (a) if one pre-emptor forfeits his right, even if to another specific person, the right goes to the remaining pre-emptors, not to the designated one. (b) If some are absent, the judge gives the property to the present ones, but the absent one can reclaim his share if he returns. (c) After a judge's ruling, if the present pre-emptor sells the property, the absent one can only take half of his share (the other half was severed by the ruling).
These rules protect the rights of all pre-emptors while allowing practical settlement.
5
Contrast with alternative views
Note that some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) distribute pre-emption according to ownership shares. The Hanafi divergence is based on considering the cause of entitlement (co-ownership) as the sole determinant, not the quantum of ownership.
This highlights the Hanafi commitment to the primacy of the cause (sabab) over the quantum (miqdar).

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab deals with the distribution of pre-emption rights when multiple pre-emptors are entitled. The core ruling is that if the pre-emptors are equal in the cause of entitlement (e.g., all are co-owners in the property), then the pre-emption is divided equally among them according to the number of persons, not according to their ownership shares. The reason is that they all share the same cause of entitlement (shirkat al-milk), so they are equal in the right. This is confirmed by the fact that if only one pre-emptor existed, he would get the whole property. The text explicitly states that differences in the amount of ownership (e.g., one owns half, another a third) are not considered; all pre-emptors share equally per head. Additional rules: if one pre-emptor forfeits his right, even if he forfeits in favor of another specific pre-emptor, the right remains for the remaining pre-emptors (it does not pass to the designated one). If some pre-emptors are absent, the judge decides among those present, because the absent one might not claim. However, if the judge gives the entire property to a present pre-emptor and then the absent one returns, the absent one can claim his share back. But if the present pre-emptor has already taken possession and sold the property, the absent one can only claim half (since the judge's decision severed his right to half). This passage is from Al-Hidayah and is part of the standard Hanafi doctrine on shuf'a.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor can either take the property with compensation for improvements at removal value, or force the buyer to remove them.

This lesson examines a ruling from Mukhtasar al-Quduri concerning the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) when the buyer has made improvements (building or planting) before the pre-emptor's claim is adjudicated. Core Idea
When buildings or trees on pre-empted property are destroyed by nature, the pre-emptor still pays full price — improvements are subsidiary to land.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi pre-emption law: what happens if the property's buildings or trees are destroyed by natural causes before the pre-emptor takes possession? The General Principle of Agency in Hanafi Fiqh
States the general rule for valid delegation of contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for valid delegation of contracts and apply it to determine which contracts can be undertaken by an agent.

Core Idea
If you can do it yourself, you can delegate it — agency is coextensive with personal legal capacity.

This lesson introduces the foundational principle of wikalah (agency) in the Hanafi school. The matn from Al-Quduri — as explained by al-Maydani — establishes a clear rule: any contract that a person is legally permitted to execute personally can also be delegated to an agent. The rationale is practical: a principal may face hardship in acting directly, and the law accommodates this need. Understanding this rule is essential for structuring valid commercial and family transactions through representatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
Recognize that the rule applies to contracts (عقود) and other legal dispositions. Confirm that the act in question is one that a competent adult may lawfully perform personally.
The rule does not apply to acts that are prohibited or that require personal qualifications, e.g., testifying in court or performing a personal oath.
2
Apply the permissibility test
Ask: is the principal himself permitted to conclude this contract? If yes, then delegation is valid. If the act itself is not permissible (e.g., a usurious sale), it cannot be delegated.
The permissibility of the act is primary: only ḥalāl acts can be validly delegated.
3
Determine the scope of delegation
The rule is general: it covers all types of contracts — sales, leases, marriage, divorce (if the husband can divorce himself), etc. The agent steps into the shoes of the principal for that specific act.
But agency is not valid for acts that require personal performance, like swearing an oath of divorce on behalf of someone else, or for acts that are purely physical (e.g., praying on behalf of another – that is not permitted).
4
Understand the underlying rationale (dalīl)
The author gives the reason: 'because he may be unable to do it himself due to certain circumstances, so he needs to delegate another, and thus he is excused.' This rational (ʿaqlī) proof shows that the rule is based on removing hardship (rafʿ al-ḥaraj).
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle of istiḥsān and the maxim 'al-ḥaraj yudfaʿ' (hardship attracts facility).
5
Consider cross-madhhab agreement
The general rule is agreed upon by the other schools of fiqh, though there may be differences in some specific exceptions. The Hanafi position is the broadest in terms of what can be delegated.
For example, the Shāfiʿī school restricts agency in matters that require direct witnessing, but this does not contradict the basic principle.

Agency for Execution: Exception for Hudud and Qisas

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Agency for execution is valid for all rights except hudud and qisas when the principal is absent – the doubt of pardon wards off the punishment.

In this passage, al-Quduri presents a nuanced ruling on agency in the context of executing legal rights. While agency is generally allowed for collecting debts or delivering property, an exception applies to fixed punishments (hudud) and retaliation (qisas). Understanding this exception requires grasping the rationale: these punishments are 'warded off by doubts,' and the principal's absence introduces a doubt that he may have pardoned. Let's unpack the ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The text begins by stating that agency is permissible for execution and discharge of all rights. This establishes the default permissibility.

In presence, there is no doubt because the principal can explicitly state his intent. Thus, agency is valid. Absence introduces uncertainty, making the agency invalid.
This distinction shows the reasoning is based on the absence of doubt.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Agency is valid only if the principal has capacity to authorize the act and can be bound by its legal results.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn establishes the foundational prerequisites for an agency (wakalah) contract. These two conditions—capacity to dispose and subjection to rulings—ensure that the principal is legally competent to delegate and to bear the consequences. Understanding these conditions prevents invalid agency arrangements that waste time and property.

Guided Path

Identify the first condition
Look for the phrase 'أن يكون الموكل ممن يملك التصرف'. This means the principal must have the legal capacity to perform the act delegated (e.g., sell, buy, marry).

Lack of either condition makes the agency invalid (batil).

Conditions (shurut) are requirements for validity. Pillars (arkan) are essential components that constitute the contract. Here we focus on two shurut; pillars are the principal, agent, and offer/acceptance.
If a condition is missing, the contract is invalid. If a pillar is missing, there is no contract at all.

Conditions for Valid Agency (Wakalah) in Hanafi Fiqh
Conditions for valid agency and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four Hanafi conditions for valid agency and explain the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the principal's capacity and subjection to rulings.

Core Idea
A valid agency requires the principal to be capable and bound, and the agent to understand and intend the contract.

Agency (wakalah) is a contract where one person authorizes another to act on their behalf. But not every person can be a principal or an agent. Hanafi fiqh lays down specific conditions to ensure the contract is valid and the legal consequences properly attach. In this lesson, we explore those conditions as set out in the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its classification
Recognize that the passage defines the conditions (shurut) that must exist for agency to be sahih (valid); if any is missing, the agency is batil (void).
2
List the two conditions for the principal (muwakkil)
From the text, the principal must (1) have the legal capacity to perform the delegated transaction (yamliku al-tasarruf), and (2) be subject to the rulings of that transaction (talzamuhu al-ahkam), meaning he bears ownership or liability.
The text discusses whether these are one condition or two.
3
List the two conditions for the agent (wakil)
The agent must (3) understand the contract—grasp its legal effect of transferring ownership and counter-value, and (4) intend that effect (ya'qil al-aqd wa yaqsiduhu).
A minor or insane person lacks these, so their agency is invalid.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the principal's conditions
The text presents two interpretations: (a) capacity and subjection are two separate conditions; (b) they are one condition encapsulating both. The second (one condition) is deemed more correct (al-asahh). Understand why: an agent permitted to sub-delegate has capacity but is not subject to the sub-agency's rulings.
This dispute affects whether sub-delegation without permission is valid.
5
Apply the judicial ruling related to obstinate litigants
A judge may accept the proxy of a litigant who refuses to appear (knowing obstinacy) without his consent, but not if the principal intends to harm the opponent. This is the mufta bihi position of later scholars.
This shows the practical operation of agency conditions in court.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the conditions for the validity of agency (wakalah). The author begins with a brief judicial ruling: a judge may accept the proxy of a reluctant litigant if he knows the litigant is being obstinate, but not if the principal is trying to harm his opponent. This is the relied-upon position of the later Hanafi scholars. Then, the main text (from al-Tashih) enumerates two conditions for the principal: (1) he must be capable of performing the transaction he delegates, because the agent derives his authority from the principal; (2) he must be subject to the legal rulings of that transaction, meaning he owns the property in question. Regarding the agent, two conditions are stated: (a) he must understand the contract—i.e., grasp its legal effect of transferring ownership—and (b) he must intend its purpose. If two conditions for the principal are considered separate, the first excludes cases where the agent sub-delegates without permission; the more correct view, however, treats capacity and subjection as one condition, since an agent permitted to delegate is not subject to the rulings of the sub-agency. Similarly, an agent who is a minor or insane cannot validly act because he lacks understanding and intent.

Key Terms
الوكالة (al-wakālah): A contract whereby one person (al-muwakkil) authorizes another (al-wakīl) to perform a legal act on his behalf.
مِن شَرْطِ الوكالة (min sharṭ al-wakālah): Part of the conditions for the validity of agency; the text presents two conditions for the principal and two for the agent.
يَمْلِكُ التَّصَرُّفَ (yamliku al-taṣarruf): The principal must have legal capacity to perform the act he is delegating (e.g., he must own the property he authorizes selling).
تَلْزَمُهُ الأحْكَامُ (talzamuhu al-aḥkām): The principal must be legally responsible for the transaction's consequences, meaning he is the one who acquires ownership or liability.

For valid agency in Hanafi fiqh, the principal must be capable and bound by the transaction, and the agent must understand and intend the contract's legal effects.

Conditions for a Valid Agent in Wakalah
Explanation of conditions for agent validity and their application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for a valid agent in wakalah and apply them to cases involving minors and slaves.

Core Idea
The agent must understand and intend the contract; legal effects bind the principal, not the agent.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri clarifies the fundamental conditions for an agent's validity in the contract of agency (wakalah). It addresses two interpretations of the matn and resolves an intra-Hanafi disagreement, then applies the ruling to cases of interdicted minors and slaves. Understanding this will help you correctly determine when an agency is valid and who bears the contractual obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations of the phrase 'wa talzamuhu al-ahkam'
Examine how the author explains two possible readings of the matn: one treats 'binding of rulings' as a separate condition for the agent; the other merges it with the ability to act, making it a single condition that automatically binds the principal.
The second interpretation is deemed more correct (asahh) and is the operative view.
2
Understand the concepts of 'understanding the contract' and 'intending it'
Define 'ya'qilu al-aqd' as comprehension of the legal effect (dispossession and acquisition) and 'yaqsiduhu' as acting deliberately for the benefit of the transaction. Both are required for the agent's validity.
A minor with discernment (tamyiz) meets the 'understanding' condition even if not yet legally responsible.
3
Apply the conditions to the case of an interdicted minor or slave agent
Note that the text rules: appointing an interdicted minor who understands buying and selling, or an interdicted slave, is valid as agency. The agency is sahih, but rights and liabilities do not attach to the agent; they attach to the principal.
This illustrates that the agent's capacity does not need to include liability; the principal bears the legal effects.
4
Determine the effect of the agent's limited capacity on future liability
Consider the commentary: after the slave is emancipated, rights become binding on him because the impediment (master's right) is gone. For the minor, even after reaching majority, rights do not bind him because his own right (limited capacity) is not nullified by maturity.
This distinction shows that the impediment type matters: others' right vs. own right.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri discusses the conditions for the validity of an agent in the contract of agency (wakalah). The author first addresses the qualification that the agent must be 'one who understands the contract and intends it.' There are two interpretations of the phrase in the matn. The first interpretation treats 'understanding' and 'intention' as separate conditions, making the ability to act (ownership of disposal) and the binding of rulings (legal effects) two distinct conditions. According to this view, if the principal authorizes the agent to appoint a sub-agent, the agent has the right to act but not to delegate unless specifically permitted; the rulings (e.g., ownership) bind the principal, not the agent. The second interpretation, which the commentator deems more correct (asahh), merges the ability to act and the binding of rulings into a single condition: the agent must be capable of understanding and intending, but the binding of rulings is not a condition for the agent per se; it automatically attaches to the principal. Evidence is that if the principal permits the agent to appoint a sub-agent, the sub-agent's actions are valid and the rulings bind the principal, not the original agent. The commentator explains that 'understanding the contract' means comprehending its legal effect: the agent dispossesses the seller of the sold item and acquires the price for him, and the opposite for the buyer. 'Intending' means acting for the benefit of dispossession and acquisition; if a minor who does not understand or an insane person is appointed, the agency is void because they lack the necessary comprehension. The passage then branches into specific rulings: if a free adult or an authorized minor/slave (who has been given permission to trade) appoints a similar person (i.e., someone with full capacity), the agency is valid because the principal owns the disposal right and the agent is capable of valid speech. If a free adult or authorized person appoints an interdicted minor who understands buying and selling, or an interdicted slave, the agency is also valid but with a caveat: rights and liabilities (such as warranty or payment) do not attach to the minor or slave because of their deficient legal capacity. Instead, the rights attach to the principals (the free adult or authorized person who appointed them). However, if the slave is later emancipated, the rights become binding on him (since the master's right ceased). For the minor, even after reaching maturity, the rights do not become binding because the impediment was his own right (i.e., his limited capacity), and that right is not nullified by reaching majority. This ruling highlights the Hanafi principle that legal capacity (ahliyyah) affects the binding of contractual obligations.

Key Terms
وكيل (wakīl): The person appointed to act on behalf of another in a contract or transaction (agent).
يعقل العقد (ya'qilu al-'aqd): Understands the meaning and legal implications of the contract, i.e., comprehension of dispossession and acquisition.
يقصده (yaqṣiduhu): Intends the contract for its purpose, i.e., acting deliberately and not in jest or carelessness.
محجور (maḥjūr): A person under legal interdiction due to minority or slavery, meaning they cannot independently enter contracts or undertake liabilities.

Validity of Agency with Minor or Slave Agents
Ruling on appointing a minor or slave who understands business as agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on appointing a restricted minor or slave as agent, explain why rights attach to the principal, and distinguish the consequences after emancipation or majority.

Core Idea
An agent needs only capacity to express, not full liability; the principal bears all rights and obligations.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar on agency (wakalah). The question is whether a free adult may appoint a minor under guardianship (mahjur) or a slave as his agent. The Hanafi school answers yes, provided the minor or slave understands the transaction. However, the liability for the contract stays with the principal. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the appointment (tawkīl) of a restricted minor or slave as an agent for buying and selling. The ruling is 'jāz' (valid). Note that the passage also covers agency by a free adult or a permitted slave/minor as principal, but the focus is on the agent's status.
The ruling is permissive, not obligatory.
2
Locate the proof (dalīl)
The author says: 'lianna al-muwakkil mālik al-tasarruf, wa al-wakīl min ahl al-ibārah'. The principal owns the right to dispose (ownership of the act), and the agent is among those capable of expression (i.e., understanding and verbally executing the contract).
The proof is qiyās based on the separation between capacity to perform and capacity to bear liability.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalālah
How does this prove validity? Full legal personality (ahliyyat al-wujūb) is not required for agency; what matters is the ability to understand and articulate the contract (ahliyyat al-adā'). The principal's ownership of disposal justifies delegating the execution.
This distinguishes agency from contracts that transfer ownership directly, where full capacity is needed.
4
Note the limitation on liability
The passage states: 'lā tata‘allaqu bihimā al-ḥuqūq'. Rights and obligations do not attach to the minor or slave agent because they cannot validly undertake liability (dhimma). Instead, they attach to the principal, who is the closest person to the transaction.
There is an exception: after the slave is freed, obligations bind him; after the minor reaches majority, obligations do not retroactively bind him.
5
Identify the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih: other schools may not allow a restricted minor or slave to act as agent at all, because they lack full legal personality.
The Hanafi tarjīh is based on the practicality of facilitating commerce and the principle of capacity to perform (ahliyyat al-adā').

Classification of contracts in agency with talil

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When an agent attributes a contract to himself, he becomes the principal party (asil) in rights and obligations.

In Hanafi agency law, not all contracts made by an agent automatically bind the principal. The key distinction is whether the agent attributes the contract to himself or to the principal. This lesson focuses on the first type: contracts like sale, purchase, and lease, where the agent acts in his own name. The passage from Al-Lubab explicates the reasoning: the agent is the actual contracting party, so rights attach to him, not the principal.

Guided Path
1

Contrast with the Shafi'i view where the principal is always the true party.

Core Idea
A contract attributed to the agent binds the agent; one attributed to the principal binds the principal.

In Hanafi law, an agent (wakil) can conclude contracts in two ways: either as a principal in the transaction or as a mere messenger (safir). Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri, addresses the scope of general agency and the types of ambiguity (jahalah) that affect its validity. The matn states that a principal may give a general agency by saying 'Buy for me whatever you see.' The commentary explains that this is effective because the principal has delegated the decision to the agent's judgment. The key principle is that slight ambiguity is tolerated in agency by way of istihsan (juristic preference), because agency is built on flexibility and is a form of assistance. The text then categorizes ambiguity into three types: (1) excessive (fahishah): ambiguity of genus (e.g., cloth, animal, slave) which invalidates the agency even if the price is given, because the agent cannot determine which specific item is intended; (2) slight (yasirah): ambiguity of type (e.g., donkey vs. horse, or a specific type of cloth) which does not invalidate the agency; and (3) intermediate (mutawassitah): between genus and type (e.g., slave or house) — if the price or type is specified, it is valid and follows the ruling of slight ambiguity; if neither is specified, it is invalid and follows the ruling of excessive ambiguity. A footnote clarifies that the phrase 'slight ambiguity is tolerated' is an addition found in some manuscripts of al-Lubab but not in al-Hidaya.

جهالة فاحشة (jahālah fāḥishah): Excessive ambiguity at the genus level (e.g., cloth, animal, slave) that prevents the validity of the agency even if the price is stated.

The Agent's Detention and Guarantee: Three Hanafi Positions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on guarantee type for detained item perishing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on the guarantee when an agent detains a purchased item that perishes, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
When a detained item perishes, the agent's guarantee changes: pledge, sale, or transgression.

This passage presents a classical intra-Hanafi dispute on the legal consequences when an agent detains a purchased item to secure the price and the item subsequently perishes. Three prominent Hanafi imams—Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani (with Abu Hanifa), and Zufar—offer different analogies that lead to distinct outcomes. Understanding this dispute deepens your grasp of how qiyas (analogy) operates in Hanafi fiqh and how ikhtilaf within the school is resolved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize the factual situation: an agent buys an item for a principal, detains it because the principal hasn't paid the price, and then the item perishes while in the agent's possession. The key question: what guarantee applies?
The agent has a right to detain the item for the price, but the perishing triggers the dispute.
2
State the Three Positions
List each imam's view: Abu Yusuf (pledge guarantee, ḍamān al-rahn), Muhammad and Abu Hanifa (sale guarantee, ḍamān al-mabī'), Zufar (transgression guarantee, ḍamān al-ta'addī).
3
Analyze the Analogies
For each position, identify the asl (original case) and the illa (effective cause) that justifies the analogy. Consider why the same situation can be analogized to a pledge, a sale, or a transgression.
Focus on the key element: the agent's right to detain.
4
Compute Practical Outcomes
Use the example from the passage to calculate the agent's recovery under each view: price 15, value 10. Abu Yusuf: agent recovers 5; Muhammad: agent recovers 0; Zufar: agent recovers the difference (price - value = 5). Note when the outcomes diverge (e.g., if value > price).
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is the difference in who bears the loss.

Identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position: the view of Muhammad al-Shaybani and Abu Hanifa, which holds that the agent is like a seller, so the price is waived upon the item's destruction.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on what guarantee applies when the agent detains the purchased item to secure the price and it subsequently perishes. Abu Yusuf analogizes the detention to a pledge (rahn): the agent's right to detain is like a pledgee's right, so the guarantee is limited to the lesser of the item's value and the price he paid. For example, if the price was 15 and value 10, the agent recovers only 5 from the principal. Zufar goes further: since he holds that the agent has no right to detain, the detention is an act of transgression (ta'addi), so the agent must guarantee the full value. Muhammad (and Abu Hanifa) analogize to a sale: the agent is like the seller, so the detention is to secure the price; if the item perishes, the debt (price) is waived entirely, and the agent bears the loss. The relied-upon view in the madhhab is Muhammad's view (also Abu Hanifa's).
