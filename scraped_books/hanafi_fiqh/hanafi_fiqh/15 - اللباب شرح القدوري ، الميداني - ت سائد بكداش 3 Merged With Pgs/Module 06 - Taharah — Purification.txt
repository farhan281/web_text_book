Definition and Causes of Hajr (Legal Restriction)
Definition of hajr and listing of its causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of hajr and list its three causes with their rationales as given in the text.

Core Idea
Hajr prevents the effectiveness of speech acts, not all actions, due to intellectual deficiency or conflicting rights.

This passage introduces the chapter on legal restriction (hajr). The author first distinguishes the linguistic and legal meanings of hajr, narrowing it to the prevention of verbal dispositions. He then enumerates three causes that trigger this restriction, each rooted in either a deficiency in intellect or a conflict of rights with a master. Understanding these causes is essential for determining who may validly transact.

Guided Path
1
Define hajr linguistically and legally
Identify the two meanings: in language, it is prevention (man'); in law, it is prevention of the effectiveness of a verbal disposition (tasarruf qawli). Note that it only affects speech acts.
The legal definition is narrower than the linguistic one.
2
List the three causes
Extract the three causes: minority (sighar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun).
These are the only causes mentioned in this passage.
3
Analyze the rationale for minority
For a non-discriminating child (ghayr mumayyiz), he has no intellect; for a discriminating child (mumayyiz), his intellect is deficient. Hence restriction applies.
Discrimination (tamyiz) is the ability to distinguish beneficial from harmful.
4
Analyze the rationale for slavery
A slave has legal capacity (ahliyyah) but is restricted to protect the master's right to benefit from the slave's services; otherwise the slave could lease himself out.
The restriction is based on protecting another's right, not intellectual deficiency.
5
Analyze the rationale for insanity
Continuous insanity means no intellect (like a non-discriminating child); intermittent insanity means deficient intellect (like a discriminating child).
The status depends on the presence or absence of lucid intervals.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Kitab al-Hajr. The author first defines hajr: linguistically it means prevention or prohibition; legally (shar'an) it is the prevention of the legal effectiveness of any verbal disposition (tasarruf qawli) by the person under restriction. This definition focuses on the effect of hajr on actions that require speech (e.g., sales, marriages, divorces). He then lists the three causes: (1) Minority (sighar): a child who is non-discriminating (ghayr mumayyiz) has no intellect, and even a discriminating (mumayyiz) child has deficient intellect. (3) Insanity (junun): if completely continuous (no lucid intervals), the person lacks intellect like a non-discriminating child; if intermittent, his intellect is deficient. The Hanafi school treats these as restrictions on disposition, not on capacity itself.

Transactions of Minors: Non-Discriminating and Discriminating
Ruling on minor's transaction validity with rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings for non-discriminating and discriminating minors' transactions and state when guardian permission is effective.

Core Idea
A discriminating minor's transaction can be cured by guardian permission; a non-discriminating minor's cannot.

This passage from al-Quduri establishes the fundamental Hanafi rule on the capacity of minors to transact. The key distinction is the presence of discernment (tamyiz) and the requirement of guardian authorization. Understanding this graded capacity is essential for correctly applying the rulings in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of minors
The passage distinguishes between ghayr mumayyiz (non‑discriminating minor) and mumayyiz (discriminating minor). A mumayyiz is one who can understand the basics of transactions.
Typical age for tamyiz is 7 years and above.
2
Note the ruling for each category
For the ghayr mumayyiz, the transaction is absolutely invalid (lā yajūz muṭlaqan) – no permission can cure it. For the mumayyiz, the transaction is invalid unless the guardian grants permission.
3
Understand the rationale
The guardian’s permission is a sign (āya) of the minor’s capacity (ahliyyah). If the minor had no capacity at all, the guardian would not permit.
This reveals the Hanafi principle that capacity is graded, not all‑or‑nothing.
4
Apply to a scenario
Example: A 14‑year‑old discriminating minor buys a smartphone without permission. The transaction is suspended (mawqūf) – it becomes valid only if the guardian ratifies it. A 6‑year‑old non‑discriminating minor buying the same phone: the transaction is void (bāṭil) even if the guardian later approves.

Commentary
This passage is a series of rulings from the matn of al-Quduri with commentary by al-Maydani. The matn states: (1) The transaction of a minor who is non-discriminating (ghayr mumayyiz) is absolutely invalid (la yajuz), with no exception. (2) The transaction of a discriminating minor (mumayyiz) is invalid unless the guardian grants permission. If permission is given, the transaction becomes valid because the guardian's permission serves as evidence of the minor's capacity (ahliyyah). (3) The transaction of a slave is invalid unless the master (sayyid) permits it. The restriction exists to protect the master's rights; if the master permits, he waives his right, and the slave transacts based on his capacity. If the slave is also a minor, he is treated like a free minor (i.e., separate permission from guardian may be needed). (4) The transaction of a continuously insane person (muglab 'ala aqlihi, one who never has lucid intervals) is absolutely invalid, even with guardian permission. The commentator clarifies that 'muglab' means one who never regains sanity. For the intermittent insane (yujannu wa yufiqu), his ruling is like that of a discriminating minor—his transactions during lucid periods are valid with permission, and during insanity invalid. The passage ends with a note on the effects: when the matn says 'whoever sells something from these,' the reference includes the minor, slave, and the intermittent insane, as indicated by the phrase 'while he is rational.'

الغير المميز (al-ghayr al-mumayyiz): A minor who lacks discernment, generally below age 7.
الأهلية (al-ahliyyah): Legal capacity to have rights and perform acts.

Transactions of Slaves and the Insane
Rulings on transaction capacity of slaves and the insane

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for slave transactions (with/without master's permission) and for the continuously insane versus the intermittent insane, and explain the rationale based on rights and capacity.

Core Idea
Slave transactions are suspended on master's permission; continuously insane transactions are void absolutely; intermittent insane are treated like discriminating minors.

This portion of al‑Quduri extends the principles of capacity to slaves and the insane. For slaves, the prohibition is to protect the master's right – permission waives that right. For the insane, the ruling depends on the continuity of the condition: those who never have lucid intervals cannot transact at all, while those who sometimes regain sanity have partial capacity similar to a discriminating minor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties: slave and two types of insane
The passage covers the transaction of a slave ('abd) and the insane (majnūn). The insane are divided into ‘mughlab ‘alā ‘aqlih’ (continuously insane) and ‘yujannu wa yufīqu’ (intermittent insane).
The commentator clarifies the terms.
2
State the ruling for each
Slave: transaction not permissible without master's permission; with permission it is valid. Continuously insane: absolutely not permissible in any circumstance. Intermittent insane: ruling like a discriminating minor (permission needed during lucid periods).
3
Understand the rationale
For the slave, the restriction is because of the master's right. When the master permits, he waives his right, and the slave transacts with his own capacity (if adult sane) or as a minor. For the continuously insane, the flaw is in the person's mind, so no permission can cure it. For the intermittent insane, during lucidity they have partial capacity.
4
Apply to scenarios
Example: A slave sells goods without master's permission – suspended. Master later permits – valid. If the slave is also a minor, he is like a free minor (needs guardian and master permission?). A continuously insane person signs a contract – void. An intermittent insane person signs a contract during a lucid moment – suspended on guardian permission.

Rulings on Minors and Insane Persons: Speech Acts vs. Property Damage
States two rulings: invalidity of speech acts and liability for damages

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the invalidity of speech acts and the liability for property damage for minors and insane persons, applying the Hanafi principle that intent is not required for liability in property destruction.

Core Idea
Minors and insane persons cannot make valid speech acts, but they are liable for damages they cause, regardless of intent.

This lesson examines a foundational ruling in the Hanafi school regarding legal incapacity. The text from al-Hajr distinguishes between statements and physical actions: while contracts and other verbal dispositions of minors and insane persons are entirely void, they are obligated to compensate for any property they destroy. This distinction hinges on the role of intent and the nature of the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and separate the rulings for speech acts (contracts, confessions, divorce, manumission) and physical acts (itlaf). Note the different outcomes.
Speech acts are all invalid; physical destruction incurs liability.
2
Understand the basis for speech acts
The passage says 'because their statements are not considered.' Legal capacity (ahliyyah) requires both understanding and intention, which minors and insane persons lack for valid speech acts.
This applies regardless of discernment (tamyiz).
3
Analyze the ruling for property damage
The text states that if they destroy something, compensation is wajib. The reason given: 'due to the actual existence of destruction and its lack of need for intent.'
Liability is based on the act itself, not the mental state.
4
Examine the supporting analogy
The author draws a qiyas (analogy) with a sleeping person who rolls over and destroys property. Just as the sleeping person is liable despite no intent, so are minors and insane persons.
This shows the ruling is not unique to minors—it's a general principle for involuntary damage.

Handing Over Property to a Minor at Age 25
Ruling on property handover at 25 despite lack of discernment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for handing over a minor's property at age 25 and explain the rationale behind it.

Core Idea
At age 25, a minor's property must be handed over even without discernment, because further interdiction is futile.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a specific age threshold for a minor's financial independence. While guardianship typically continues until the ward shows prudent judgment (rushd), the Hanafi school sets an outer limit at 25 years. The reasoning is practical: if a person has not developed discernment by that age, continued withholding of their property is unlikely to be corrective. The commentary adds a vivid illustration of how 25 is the theoretical minimum for becoming a grandfather, underscoring the point that by this age further delay is pointless.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings in the text
The first ruling: if the minor transacts in his property before the specified period, his transaction is valid because he possesses legal capacity (ahliyyah) at puberty. The second: when he reaches 25, his property is handed over even without discernment.
The specified period is the age of 25.
2
Understand the reasoning for the age limit
The prevention (hajr) is by way of discipline (ta'dib). After 25, discipline is generally ineffective, so withholding property serves no purpose. The commentator gives a hypothetical scenario: a boy reaching puberty at 12 could become a grandfather at 25, emphasizing the length of time.
The rational proof is based on futility (i'tibar al-'adam al-fa'idah).
3
Define key terms: rushd and ahliyyah
Rushd is legal discretion or ability to manage property prudently. Ahliyyah is legal capacity. The minor has ahliyyah at puberty, but rushd may be absent. After 25, the requirement for rushd is waived for the purpose of handover.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a 26-year-old who has never shown financial prudence. According to this text, his guardian must hand over his property. No further delay is permitted.
This applies even if the minor is not discerning.

No Pre-emption in Movables and Ships
States ruling that shuf'ah does not apply to movable goods and ships with wisdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that shuf'ah does not apply to movables and ships and explain the wisdom behind it.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is only for permanent property — movables and ships lack permanence, so no pre-emption.

This lesson examines the boundaries of the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) in Hanafi fiqh. The source passage from Al-Quduri and Al-Hidayah establishes that shuf'ah applies only to immovable property (like land and buildings) and not to movable goods or ships, based on the wisdom of preventing permanent neighborly harm.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
The passage clearly states: 'لا شفعة في العروض، والسفن' — no shuf'ah in movables and ships. Memorize this foundational ruling.
2
Identify the wisdom (hikmah)
The text gives the reason: shuf'ah was established to repel the harm of bad neighborliness, which is permanent only in immovable property.
The term 'على الدوام' emphasizes permanence.
3
Understand the negative analogy
Movable property does not have permanence like real estate, so the harm is not comparable. The analogy (qiyas) is rejected—movables cannot be equated with immovables for shuf'ah.
This is a talil (reasoning by wisdom) rather than strict qiyas.
4
Note the authority and reliance
This ruling is from the matn of al-Quduri and supported by al-Hidayah. It is part of the zahir al-riwayah and is the mufta bihi position.
No known intra-Hanafi disagreement on this point.

Foundling in Church: Religion Presumed by Place of Discovery
Ruling on foundling in church with fourfold classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a foundling found in a place of worship and apply the fourfold classification to determine the child's religion.

Core Idea
The foundling's religion follows the place of finding, not the finder's religion, in the Zahir al-Riwayah.

When an abandoned infant is discovered in a church or synagogue, how do we determine their religion? The Hanafi school uses a legal presumption based on the location of discovery. This passage presents four scenarios depending on who finds the child and where, and explains that the dominant narration gives priority to the place.

The passage from al-Durr presents four scenarios based on the finder's religion and location: (1) Muslim in Muslim place → Muslim; (2) Kafir in kafir place → Kafir; (3) Kafir in Muslim place; (4) Muslim in kafir place.
The 'opposite' refers to the fourth scenario.

Apply the principle of place priority
Zahir al-Riwayah considers the place as the determining factor because the place has priority (sabaq). Thus, in scenarios 3 and 4, the child's religion follows the place, not the finder.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi variation
There is a narration that considers the finder's religion, but the relied-upon position is place. The 'qīla' indicates a weaker opinion.
