Reversion in Hawala: Insolvency vs. Death
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the third condition for reversion in hawala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the muhal 'alayh's right to revert upon the muhal's insolvency, and explain the evidence from Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Reversion requires destruction of liability, not mere difficulty of collection.

In hawala, the muhal 'alayh pays the debt to the muhal on behalf of the muhil. Under certain conditions, he may revert to the muhil to recover his payment. This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a judge's declaration of the muhal's insolvency is sufficient for reversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed condition
Read the passage to find what third condition the Sahibayn add for the muhal 'alayh's reversion.
They add judicial declaration of insolvency.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's objection
Understand why Abu Hanifa rejects this condition based on the nature of liability.
He argues the debt remains in the muhal's liability.
3
Compare with the case of death
Note how Abu Hanifa distinguishes death from insolvency: death destroys liability, insolvency does not.
4
Identify the tarjih
Find which view is considered preponderant according to later Hanafi authorities.
Al-Nasafi and the author's shaykh confirm Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani add a third condition for the muhal 'alayh to revert to the muhil even while the muhal 'alayh is alive: when a judge declares the muhal 'alayh insolvent (muflis). This is because the muhal 'alayh can neither collect from him nor pursue him effectively. Abu Hanifa opposes this, arguing that the debt remains in the muhal's liability; mere difficulty of collection does not give the muhal 'alayh the right to revert, similar to the case of the muhal being absent. However, if the muhal dies, reversion is allowed because the liability is destroyed. The commentator in al-Tashih notes that al-Nasafi adopted Abu Hanifa's opinion and found his evidence stronger. The author's shaykh confirms that the relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): Transfer of debt; a contract where a creditor (muhil) transfers his claim to a second creditor (muhal) against the debtor (muhal 'alayh).
المحال عليه (al-muḥāl ʿalayhi): The person on whom the debt is transferred, i.e., the original debtor who now owes the new creditor.
المحيل (al-muḥīl): The original creditor who transfers the debt to another.

Key Takeaway
The right of reversion in hawala is limited to the death of the muhal; judicial insolvency does not trigger it according to the relied-upon Hanafi view.

Disputes in Hawalah: Claim of Prior Debt and Agency
Two rulings on hawalah disputes with reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a muhil claims prior debt after hawalah and explain why his statement is not accepted.

Core Idea
A muhil's claim of prior debt after hawalah is rejected because hawalah is not an acknowledgment.

After a hawalah transaction is completed and the muhal 'alayh pays the muhtal, disputes may arise about the nature of the transfer. The text presents two scenarios: in the first, the muhil claims the hawalah was for a prior debt owed to him; in the second, he claims it was actually an agency. The rulings depend on who is the plaintiff and who is the denier, and on the linguistic usage of 'hawalah'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims in each scenario
First scenario: muhal 'alayh demands reimbursement from muhil; muhil claims prior debt. Second scenario: muhil demands payment from muhtal, claiming agency; muhtal claims it was a hawalah for a debt.
The muhal 'alayh has already paid the muhtal.
2
Apply the principle that hawalah is not an acknowledgment (iqrar)
In the first scenario, the muhil's claim of a prior debt is a counterclaim. Since hawalah is valid without acknowledging any debt by the muhil, the muhal 'alayh remains a denier, and his statement is accepted.
The cause of reversion (qada dayn bi-amrih) is established.
3
Distinguish the second scenario based on linguistic usage
In the second scenario, the word 'hawalah' can also mean agency. The muhtal claims a debt, which the muhil denies. Therefore, the muhil's statement is accepted with his oath.
Burden of proof lies on the claimant (muhtal).

And if the muhal 'alayh demands from the muhil the like of the hawalah money with which he made the hawalah and paid it to the muhtal, and the muhil says: I only made the hawalah for a debt that was due to me from you, his statement is not accepted, and he must pay the like of the debt with which he made the hawalah; because the cause for reversion has been realized, which is the payment of his debt by his command, and hawalah is not an acknowledgment of debt, because it is valid without it; however the muhil claims a debt against him while he denies it, and the statement is that of the denier. Commentary
This passage contains two rulings: First, after the muhal 'alayh pays the muhtal, if he demands reimbursement from the muhil, and the muhil claims that the hawalah was for a prior debt owed by the muhal 'alayh to him (i.e., he already owed him), the muhil's claim is not accepted. The muhal 'alayh's right of reversion has been established because he paid the muhil's debt by his command (qada' dayn bi-amrih). The hawalah itself is not an acknowledgment of debt by the muhil (i.e., it does not prove that the muhil actually owed the muhal 'alayh). Therefore, the muhil's claim of a prior debt is a counterclaim, and the denier's (muhal 'alayh) word is taken. Second, if the muhil demands the money from the muhtal (the new creditor) claiming that the hawalah was actually a wakalah (agency) to collect, and the muhtal says it was a hawalah for a debt, then the muhil's statement is accepted with his oath, because the muhtal claims a debt against the muhil, and the muhil denies, and the word 'hawalah' can be used for agency. The muhtal bears the burden of proof.

Key Terms
الإقرار (al-iqrār): Acknowledgment; the hawalah is not an acknowledgment of debt by the muhil.
الوكالة (al-wakālah): Agency; used in contrast to hawalah in the second dispute.

The Muheel's Statement is Accepted in Hawala Disputes
Ruling on whose statement is accepted in a hawala dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that in a dispute over the nature of a hawala (debt transfer vs agency), the muheel's statement with his oath is accepted because he denies the debt.

Core Idea
The denier's statement with oath prevails – the muheel's denial shifts the burden.

This passage from al-Hidayah deals with a procedural conflict in hawala. When the muheel (transferor) claims he only appointed the muhtal (transferee) as an agent to collect the debt, and the muhtal claims the transfer was for a debt owed to him, the muheel's statement is accepted with his oath. This is based on the principle that the denier's statement is taken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting claims
The muheel claims the hawala was an agency (wakala) to collect a debt. The muhtal claims it was a transfer of a debt owed to him by the muheel.
These are mutually exclusive claims about the nature of the transaction.
2
Determine who is claiming and who is denying
The muhtal is claiming a debt from the muheel. The muheel denies that any debt is owed. Thus the muheel is the denier (munkir), and the muhtal is the claimant (mudda'i).
The legal maxim assigns the oath to the denier.
3
Apply the legal principle
The general rule is: 'al-bayyina 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' (the burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier). Since the muhtal has no proof, the muheel's statement with his oath is accepted.
This principle is derived from a hadith and is universally accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Consider the linguistic nuance
The word 'hawala' can also mean 'wakala' (agency) in some usages. This ambiguity supports the muheel's interpretation, but the ruling is already decided by the denial principle.
This strengthens the muheel's claim but is not the primary basis.
5
State the final ruling
The muheel's statement with his oath is accepted, and the muhtal must bring proof if he insists on his claim.
This is a procedural ruling, not a substantive one.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on conflicting claims in hawala (debt transfer). The muheel claims he transferred the debt only as an agent (wakala) to collect payment, while the muhtal claims the transfer was for a debt owed by the muheel to him. The ruling is that the muheel's statement is accepted with his oath. The reasoning is that the muhtal is claiming a debt from the muheel, and the muheel denies it; the principle is that the denier's statement (with oath) is accepted. Furthermore, the word hawala is sometimes used for agency, so ambiguity favors the muheel. This is a procedural ruling based on established legal maxims.

The Ruling on Suftaja (Loan to Secure Safe Passage)
Definition and ruling of suftaja as a disliked loan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suftaja and explain why it is disliked based on the hadith 'qard jarra naf'an'.

Core Idea
A loan that gives the lender a benefit is disliked because it resembles riba.

In this lesson, we examine suftaja—a loan arrangement where the lender benefits from safe passage of money. The Hanafi school rules it as makruh due to the prohibition on loans that bring benefit to the lender. We will explore the definition, the evidence from the hadith, and the practical implications for transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that suftaja is disliked. Confirm the act is a loan with a specific benefit to the lender.
The word 'safatij' is plural of suftaja.
2
Understand the definition and form
The passage defines suftaja as a loan where the lender avoids travel risk. The form: giving money to a merchant to deliver to a friend in another city.
The benefit is stipulated, making it problematic.
3
Locate the dalil
The author quotes al-Hidayah: the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade a loan that brings a benefit. This hadith is the primary evidence.
The full hadith is 'Every loan that brings a benefit is riba'.
4
Analyze wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'qard jarra naf'an' is general. Suftaja falls under it because the lender receives the benefit of safe passage.
The benefit is the avoidance of risk, which is a clear advantage.
5
Note the intra-school and cross-school positions
The Hanafi position is that suftaja is makruh tahrimi when the benefit is stipulated. Other schools may differ, e.g., Shafi'i permits it without stipulation.
The mufta bihi is that it is disliked.

وتكره السَّفَاتِجُ ، وهي قَرْضُ استفاد به المقرِضُ أَمْنَ خَطَر الطريق . وصورته كما في الدرر: أن يدفع إلى تاجر مبلغاً قرضاً، ليدفعه إلى صديقه في بلد آخر ؛ ليستفيد به سقوط خطر الطريق. قال في الهداية: وهذا نوع نَفْع استفيد به، وقد نهى رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم عن قرض جرَّ نفعاً.
And suftaja is disliked, and it is a loan by which the lender benefits from safety from the danger of the road. Its form as in al-Durar: that he gives to a merchant a sum as a loan to deliver it to his friend in another town, so that he benefits from the avoidance of the danger of the road. He said in al-Hidayah: This is a type of benefit derived, and the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) forbade a loan that brings a benefit.
Source pages p. 403

Commentary
This passage defines suftaja (plural: safatij) and gives its ruling. Suftaja is a type of loan where the lender gives money to a trader traveling to another city, with the understanding that the trader will deliver the money to the lender's contact there. The lender avoids the risk of carrying cash on the road. This is disliked because it involves a loan (qard) that brings a benefit to the lender (safe passage), which falls under the prohibition of 'every loan that brings a benefit is riba' as narrated from the Prophet. The hadith, though weak in some chains, is supported by the practice of the Companions. The disliked ruling applies when the benefit is stipulated; if not stipulated, it is not disliked.

Key Terms
السفاتج (al-safatij): The plural of suftaja; a loan where the lender benefits from safe passage of money to another location.
القرض (al-qard): A loan; a contract where one party gives money to another with the understanding that it will be returned, without any additional benefit.
