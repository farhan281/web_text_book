Definition and Formation of Bay' in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition and formation of the sale contract in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of bay' and identify the conditions necessary for its formation.

Core Idea
A valid sale in Hanafi fiqh is formed solely by offer and acceptance in past tense verbs.

This lesson begins the Book of Sales (Kitab al-Buyu') from al-Quduri's matn. The author first explains why sales are placed after acts of worship, then defines bay' both linguistically and legally. The key ruling for formation is stated: a sale is concluded (yan'aqid) by offer and acceptance when both are expressed in past tense verbs. Understanding this foundation is critical for distinguishing valid contracts from incomplete ones in Hanafi mu'amalat.

Guided Path
1
Define bay' linguistically and legally
Read the linguistic definition: 'exchange of one thing for another' and the legal definition: 'exchange of property for property with mutual consent'. Note that the legal definition narrows the scope to property and consent.
The linguistic definition includes barter of services, but the legal definition restricts to property.
2
Identify the formation condition
Focus on the statement: 'It is concluded by offer and acceptance if both are in past tense verbs.' This is the core condition for a contract to be mun'aqid (formed).
Past tense verbs like ba'tu (I sold) and ishtaraytu (I bought) are required. Present tense or imperatives do not form the contract.
3
Analyze the components: offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul)
Offer is the first statement from either party; acceptance is the second. In Hanafi fiqh, both must be in the past tense and must correspond in subject matter and session.
The session (majlis al-'aqd) requirement is not explicitly in this text but is a standard Hanafi condition.
4
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The text reflects the Hanafi position. Compare with the Shafi'i school, which allows present tense or other forms as long as intention is clear. Understand the practical difference.
This ikhtilaf affects whether a sale is valid in different madhhabs.
5
Apply the ruling
Given a scenario, determine if a sale is formed according to Hanafi fiqh. For example, if a seller says 'I sell you this for $10' (present tense), the contract is not formed. Only 'I sold you this for $10' works.
Always check the tense of both offer and acceptance.

Valid Offer and Acceptance in Sale Contracts
Explanation of valid offer and acceptance phrases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid phrases for offer and acceptance in a sale contract and apply the Hanafi principle that meaning (not literal wording) is the basis of validity.

Core Idea
Any expression that conveys consent creates a valid sale, including mutual exchange without words.

In Hanafi fiqh, the formation of a sale contract (bay') rests on mutual consent (muradat). The classical texts discuss the verbal formulas used for offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). This lesson examines the valid phrases and introduces the key principle that meaning, not literal wording, is what the Shari'ah considers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What contract is being discussed? What is the general ruling on its formation?
The act is sale (bay'). The ruling: valid (sahih) if offer and acceptance indicate consent.
2
Recognize the primary verbal form
The text states that past-tense verbs like 'biktu' (I sold) and 'ishtaraytu' (I bought) are valid. Why? Because sale is a disposition (insha'), and the Shari'ah has used the past tense for creating it.
Note the shift: past tense is normally for informing, but here it creates a new legal state.
3
Apply the principle of meaning
The text expands to other phrases: 'raditu' (I am pleased), 'a'taytuka bi kadha' (I give you for such), etc. These are valid because they convey the same meaning as the past tense forms.
Meaning is the criterion, not the exact wording.
4
Understand the case of ta'ati
Mutual exchange without words (ta'ati) is also valid for all items, valuable or cheap. This is based on the realization of mutual consent, which is the underlying requirement.
This is the correct position (as-sahih) according to the Hidayah.

Khiyar al-Qabul: The Option of Acceptance in Hanafi Sales Law
Ruling on options in sale with proof and rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-qabul, identify the limits of the offeree's acceptance, and explain why the session is considered one unit.

Core Idea
The offeree must accept exactly the offer; partial acceptance is invalid unless prices are separate.

When one party makes an offer in a sale, the other party does not become bound immediately. Instead, the offeree has an option: accept the offer exactly as made, or reject it. This period, known as khiyar al-qabul, is limited to the session (majlis) in which the offer was made. The Hanafi school views this as necessary to ensure mutual consent, and the offeror may retract before acceptance. The source passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains these principles and their rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the rights of the parties
State the Hanafi position: after an offer, the offeree has khiyar al-qabul (option to accept or reject) in the session, and the offeror can retract before acceptance.
The offeree's option is a built-in feature, not a separate stipulation.
2
Understand the rationale behind the session
The session is treated as a single continuous unit ('saa'ah waahidah') to remove hardship and bring ease. It applies whether parties are face-to-face, or the offer is via writing or messenger.
The session ends when the parties physically separate or an act indicating disinterest occurs.
3
Note the limits on acceptance
Acceptance must be for the entire item for the entire price. Partial acceptance or acceptance with a different price is invalid unless the offeror specified separate prices for each item.
This protects the offeror from being bound to a transaction they did not consent to.
4
Observe the extension to indirect communication
If the offer is made via writing or a messenger, the offeree's option begins upon receiving the message and lasts until the end of that session.
The session of receipt counts as the majlis.
5
Recognize the inter-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) do not recognize the offeror's right to retract; they consider the offer binding once made. The Hanafi position emphasizes mutual consent (taradi).
The practical consequence: a Hanafi mufti allows retraction; a Shafi'i mufti would not.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri elaborates on the Khiyar al-Qabul (option of acceptance) in sales. After an offer is made, the offeree (the other party) has an option: he may accept the offer exactly as made (full item for full price) during the session (majlis), or he may reject it. This option is necessary because without it, the offeree would be bound involuntarily. Additionally, the offeror (mujib) may retract his offer at any time before acceptance, as no right of the offeree is violated. The option period is limited to the duration of the session; the session is considered one continuous time unit to facilitate ease and avoid hardship. If the offer is made via writing or a messenger, the session is considered at the time the writing reaches the offeree or the message is delivered. Finally, the acceptance must be exactly for the entire item and entire price; partial acceptance is invalid because the offeror did not consent to less or to splitting the contract, unless each item's price was separately specified (making them independent contracts).

Khiyar al-Qabul: Options Before Acceptance
Explains the offeree's option to accept in the sitting and the offeror's right to revoke before acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules governing khiyar al-qabul, including the offeree's option and the offeror's revocation, and explain how the 'sitting' (majlis) is defined in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The offeree may accept or reject in the same sitting; the offeror may revoke before acceptance; the sitting lasts as long as no turning away occurs.

This lesson explains the initial phase of a sale contract: the period between offer and acceptance. You will learn the rights of both parties: the offeree's option to accept or reject, and the offeror's ability to revoke before acceptance. Additionally, the Hanafi definition of 'majlis' is clarified, emphasizing that it is a temporal and contextual unit, not merely a physical location.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their options
The offeree (the one who receives the offer) has the right to accept or reject. The offeror (the one who makes the offer) has the right to retract the offer before acceptance.
These rights exist only in the pre-acceptance phase.
2
Understand the temporal scope
The offeree's option to accept is limited to the same sitting (majlis). Acceptance after the sitting ends is invalid without a renewed offer.
The sitting is defined by presence and engagement, not just location.
3
Define the sitting (majlis)
The sitting continues as long as the parties remain engaged in the transaction. Any action indicating turning away—such as talking about unrelated matters—ends the sitting, even if they remain in the same place.
Ibn Abidin clarifies: the sitting is valid until there is evidence of disinterest.
4
Extend the ruling to written and mediated offers
If the offer is made via writing or a messenger, the sitting for acceptance is the time when the offeree receives and reads the letter or hears the message. The acceptance must occur in that same sitting.
This maintains the principle that acceptance must happen without undue delay.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, if someone offers to sell an item in person, the buyer must accept or reject while still in the negotiation context. If the buyer walks away or engages in other conversation, the option is lost.
The offeror can revoke anytime before acceptance, even if the offeree is still thinking.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Pointing to an item removes the need to know its exact quantity, except in ribawi exchanges where equality must be known.

In a sale, the price and commodity must be known to avoid dispute. However, the Hanafi school provides a nuanced rule: if the item is physically present and pointed to, its exact quantity need not be known — unless it involves ribawi goods exchanged for the same type. For prices that are not physically specified, both quantity and quality must be clearly stated. This lesson unpacks these distinctions and their underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of consideration
The passage divides considerations into 'specified' (al-mushar ilayhi) — physically pointed to — and 'absolute' (al-thaman al-mutlaq) — not physically specified. Understand this binary distinction.
Specified considerations remove ambiguity by sight; absolute prices rely on description.
2
State the ruling for each category
For specified considerations, the sale is valid without knowing the exact quantity, unless the item is ribawi and exchanged for its own type. For absolute prices, the sale is invalid unless both quantity and quality are known.
Ribawi exception: if the sale involves two ribawi items of the same type (e.g., gold for gold), equality in quantity must be known even if pointed to.
3
Analyze the legal rationale (dalil aqli)
The reason given is that delivery and taking delivery are obligatory by the contract. Any ambiguity (jahalah) that leads to potential dispute prevents the fulfillment of this obligation, thus invalidating the sale.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the principle of preventing conflict (man' al-niza').
4
Apply the rule to concrete scenarios
Consider buying a car by pointing (specified) vs. ordering a car with a vague price (absolute). Apply the conditions to determine validity.
For ribawi goods like wheat, if you point to a heap and say 'I sell you this wheat for that wheat', equality must be known (measured).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In a unit price sale, each unit is treated as a separate item, giving the buyer an option to take a proportionate share or reject the whole.

This passage from al-Lubab explores the khiyar (option) of the buyer when the actual measure of goods sold by unit price (e.g., per cubit) differs from the stated measure. It covers two scenarios: deficiency and excess, each with distinct rulings based on the principle that mentioning the unit price makes the description an independent element. Understanding this ruling is crucial for contracts involving measured goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that this is a unit price sale: the seller states a total measure and a total price, but also specifies the price per unit (e.g., each cubit for a dirham).
The key is that the price per unit is explicitly mentioned.
2
Distinguish deficiency vs. excess
The ruling changes depending on whether the actual measure is less (deficiency) or more (excess) than the stated measure. The source treats them separately.
Both cases give the buyer an option, but the reasoning differs slightly.
3
Apply the ruling for deficiency
If the measure is deficient, the buyer may either take the goods at a pro-rata price (e.g., 90 dirhams for 90 cubits) or reject the sale entirely. He cannot be forced to pay the full price.
This is because each cubit is like a separate item; taking less than stated at full price would violate the unit price.
4
Apply the ruling for excess
If the measure exceeds the stated amount, the buyer may either take all at the unit price (paying more) or reject the sale. The seller cannot force the buyer to take the excess.
This avoids forcing a transaction on the buyer without consent (tafarruq al-safqah).

The rational basis: mentioning the price per unit elevates the description (measure) to an independent element. Each unit is analogous to a separate piece of cloth. This reasoning is consistent with Hanafi usul: the description becomes an integral part of the contract.

Order of Payment and Delivery in Sales
Rulings on order of payment and delivery with talil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the order of payment and delivery in a sale of present goods with prompt price, identify the exceptions, and apply the rule to barter and simultaneous delivery scenarios.

Core Idea
Buyer pays first when goods are present and price prompt, because price is only specified by possession.

In a sale, both parties have rights and obligations. The Hanafi school prescribes a specific order for payment and delivery based on the nature of the goods and price. This passage from al-Lubab explains when the buyer must pay first, when the seller must deliver first, and how delivery is validly accomplished. Understanding this order prevents disputes and ensures fairness in transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling and its conditions
The text states that for present, unencumbered goods sold for a prompt price, the buyer is ordered to pay first. Note the three conditions: goods must be present (not absent), unencumbered (not occupied), and price must be immediate (not deferred).
If any condition is missing, the order changes.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (talil)
The reason given is that the buyer's right becomes specified in the sold item by the contract, but the seller's right in the price is not specified until possession. Therefore, to give the seller a specified right, the buyer must pay first.
This reasoning is based on the Hanafi principle that fungibles (mithliyyat) are not specified by mere contract.
3
Identify the exceptions
If the goods are absent or encumbered (e.g., leased), the buyer is not ordered to pay until the goods are brought or vacated. If the price is deferred, the seller cannot demand payment before delivery because the credit term starts from delivery of the goods.
These exceptions modify the general rule.
4

Once the buyer pays, the seller is ordered to deliver the goods. If the seller delivers before payment, the sale is final and the seller cannot reclaim the goods.
The seller's right to reclaim is lost.
5
Apply to barter and delivery method
In barter (goods for goods) or currency exchange (price for price), both parties must deliver simultaneously because both items are equally unspecified. Delivery is validly accomplished by clearing (takhliyah) so the buyer can take possession without obstacles.
Clearing constitutes constructive possession.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sales in al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the rules of delivery in a sale contract. It distinguishes between a cash sale (where the price is monetary) and a barter sale (commodity for commodity or price for price). In a cash sale, once the buyer pays the price, the seller is obligated to deliver the sold item, as the seller has taken possession of the price. However, if the seller voluntarily delivers the item before receiving payment, he cannot later reclaim it. In a barter sale, since both items are specified and equal in status, both parties must deliver simultaneously. The passage then defines delivery (taslim) as takhliya — vacating the item so that the buyer can take control without any physical or legal obstruction. This is considered constructive possession (qabd hukman) in Hanafi law.

تسليم (taslim): Delivery of the sold item to the buyer by vacating (takhliya) enabling possession.
تخلیہ (takhliya): Vacating the item so the buyer can take possession without barrier.

Option of Stipulation (Khiyar al-Shart): Definition and Scope
Defines khiyar al-shart and its scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of khiyar al-shart, identify when it can be stipulated, and distinguish the Hanafi position on its duration from other schools.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is permissible in the contract or after, even days later, for any party.

This lesson introduces the key concept of khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation) in Hanafi sales law. Unlike other options that arise by default, khiyar al-shart is created by an explicit condition agreed by the parties. The passage explains its scope: it can be given to either party or a third party, and it can be stipulated at the time of contract or later. Understanding this option is fundamental to structuring flexible sales agreements in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and ruling
Read the passage to find that khiyar al-shart is ja'iz (permissible). It is not obligatory, but if stipulated, it is valid and binding.
Ja'iz means legally permissible, not obligatory.
2
Determine the parties entitled
The option can be for the seller alone, buyer alone, both together, or even a third party. This is stated explicitly.
The text says 'for the seller, the buyer, and both' and also mentions 'for others'.
3
Understand the timing of stipulation
The option can be inserted in the body of the contract or after it, even after days. But it cannot be stipulated before the contract exists.
This is a key Hanafi flexibility: stipulation after the contract is valid.
4
Relate it to the hierarchy of options
The author placed khiyar al-shart before khiyar al-ru'yah and khiyar al-'ayb because it prevents the initial effect of the contract, while the others prevent completion or binding.
Understanding this hierarchy helps in classifying different options.

Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation). It states that this option is permissible (ja'iz) in a sales contract — it can be stipulated by the seller alone, the buyer alone, both together, or even a third party. The author notes that this option is placed before other options in the text because it prevents the initial effect of the contract (which is the transfer of ownership). It is followed by khiyar al-ru'yah (option of inspection) which prevents the completion of the effect, and finally khiyar al-'ayb (option of defect) which prevents the binding nature of the contract. The option of stipulation can be inserted in the very contract or added later, even days after, but it cannot be stipulated before the contract exists. This aligns with Hanafi principles where a condition must be attached to a valid contract.

Key Terms
خيار الشرط (khiyar al-shart): An option stipulated in a sale contract allowing a party to cancel or confirm the sale within an agreed period.
