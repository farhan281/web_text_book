Tarjih on Age of Majority: Sahibayn's View Preferred
Preference among later Hanafi authorities on age of majority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mufta bihi position on the age of majority in the Hanafi school and explain why later authorities preferred the Sahibayn's view.

Core Idea
Later Hanafi authorities prefer Sahibayn's 15-year rule for both genders as the operative fatwa.

In this passage, we learn how leading later Hanafi scholars resolved a classical dispute on the age of majority. While Abu Hanifa held that majority is at 18 for boys and 17 for girls, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) set 15 for both. This passage records that later authorities like Burhani, Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, and Ba'labakki formally adopted the Sahibayn's view for fatwa. Understanding this tarjih is crucial for applying the correct ruling in matters of legal capacity, such as marriage and financial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
Abu Hanifa held that age of majority is 18 for boys and 17 for girls. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) held 15 for both genders. This is the core intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
Both views are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Recognize the tarjih authorities
Imam Burhan al-A'imma al-Burhani, Imam al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, and al-Ba'labakki are later authoritative Hanafi jurists. Their statement carries weight in the school's hierarchy.
These scholars belong to the fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwa) layer.
3
Understand the basis of tarjih
They explicitly state that 'by it (the Sahibayn's view) fatwa is given'. Al-Ba'labakki adds that the Sahibayn's view is a transmission (riwayah) from Abu Hanifa himself, implying it is not a rejection but a preference of one transmission.
The tarjih does not reject Abu Hanifa; it selects the stronger riwayah for practice.
4
Note the practical implication
For a boy or girl without signs of puberty, majority is set at 15 years (Islamic lunar). This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) for fatwa and qada.
If physical signs appear earlier, majority is established earlier regardless of age.

Commentary
This short passage records the preference of later Hanafi authorities regarding the earlier dispute on the age of majority. The commentators include Imam Burhan al-A'imma al-Burhani, Imam al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah – all towering figures of the later Hanafi school. They explicitly state that the fatwa (the operative ruling for practice) is given according to the Sahibayn's view (15 years for both genders). Al-Ba'labakki adds that this view is actually a transmission (riwayah) from Abu Hanifa himself, implying that the earlier master also held this opinion in some transmissions. Therefore, the relied-upon position is that boys and girls without signs of puberty become adults at fifteen. The text is essentially a tarjih (preference) that resolves the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Key Terms
فتوى (fatwā): The operative legal ruling given for practical application, distinguished from theoretical legal opinion.
رواية (riwāyah): A transmission of a legal opinion attributed to an imam, in this case Abu Hanifa.

Judge's Sale of the Bankrupt's Property
Ruling on judicial sale of bankrupt's assets and distribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the judge to sell a bankrupt's property, the order of asset liquidation, and the minimum clothing exempt from sale.

Core Idea
A bankrupt's essential clothing is exempt; the rest is sold in a fixed order to repay creditors.

When a debtor refuses to sell his property to repay debts, the judge steps in to sell it and distribute the proceeds. This lesson explains the judge's role, the priority of assets to sell, and the important exemption of basic clothing that must be left for the bankrupt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering condition
The judge's sale only occurs if the bankrupt refuses to sell his own property. Identify the conditional clause that activates judicial intervention.
The text says 'إن امتنع المفلس من بيعه'.
2
Determine the order of sale
The text prescribes a sequence: cash first, then trade goods, then real estate. Understand the reasoning: ease and speed of debt repayment.
Why is cash prioritized? Because it is most liquid.
3
Apply the exemption for clothing
The bankrupt is allowed to keep one set (dast) of clothing. A second view says two sets are needed to allow for washing. Recognize the difference and the underlying rationale.
The exemption ensures the debtor can attend to daily needs and court appearances.
4
Outline the distribution method
After sale, the proceeds are divided among creditors proportionally according to their debts.
Proportional distribution is the default; priority among creditors is not addressed here.

Commentary

The sharh explains that the judge does the selling and distribution. It then details the order of sale: first cash, then trade goods, then real estate; the easiest to sell is prioritized to expedite debt repayment. The word 'dast' is defined as a garment sufficient for daily needs. The source referenced is Hidayah, a standard Hanafi text.

Returning Deposit to Owner's House: Liability or Not?
Reconciles conflicting narrations about guarantee when deposit is returned to owner's house.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on returning a deposit to the owner's house and apply the distinction between ordinary household utensils and precious items to determine liability.

Core Idea
Returning a deposit to the owner's house suffices only for ordinary household items, not precious ones.

The base text states that returning a deposit to the owner's house without delivery creates liability, but a conflicting narration says otherwise. The Hanafi school reconciles this by considering the nature of the item. This lesson unpacks the reconciliation and its rational proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the apparent conflict
The act is returning a deposit (or usurped property) to the owner's house without personally handing it over. The base matn says 'he is liable' (ḍamin), but another narration says 'he is not liable' (lā yaḍman). Note the contradiction.
2
Locate the reconciliation
The commentator explains that the non-liability applies only to ordinary household utensils (ālāt al-manzil) because custom (ʿurf) considers their return to the house as sufficient delivery. Precious items (al-aʿyān al-nafīsah) must be returned directly to the owner.
The reconciliation is found in al-Hidayah and relied upon in the school.
3
Understand the rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī)
The proof for liability when returning precious items is that the depositee must undo his wrongful act or fulfill the trust by returning to the owner himself. The owner’s act of depositing the item shows he would not be satisfied with it being left in the house; otherwise he would have left it there himself.
For ordinary items, custom (ʿurf) implies consent, so the reasoning does not apply.
4
Apply the distinction
Given a scenario, classify the item as ordinary (e.g., cooking pot) or precious (e.g., diamond ring). If ordinary, returning to the house without delivery discharges liability; if precious, liability remains until the owner receives it personally.
This distinction is the muftā bihī position.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and a reconciliation of textual variations. The base matn states that if someone returns a deposit to the owner's house without delivering it personally, he is liable. However, a different narration from al-Jawharah says 'he is not liable'. The commentator reconciles by specifying that the non-liability applies to ordinary household utensils (ālāt al-manzil), because it is customary to return them to the house. For precious items (al-a‘yān al-nafīsa), they must be returned directly to the owner, and if returned only to the house, the person remains liable. The proof is rational: the usurper or depositee must undo his wrong or fulfill the trust by returning to the owner himself, as the owner would not have deposited the item if he were satisfied with it being left in the house. This distinction is relied upon in the Hanafi school as per al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
الوديعة (al-wadī‘ah): Property given to someone for safekeeping without compensation.
آلات المنزل (ālāt al-manzil): Ordinary household utensils and tools.
الأعيان النفيسة (al-a‘yān al-nafīsah): Precious items such as valuables and jewelry.

Defining al-Sail: The Water Barrier in Reviving Dead Lands
Lexical definition of a technical term

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the precise lexical meaning of 'al-sail' as used in Hanafi fiqh of reviving dead lands and distinguish it from other terms.

Core Idea
Al-sail is a man-made dam, not a natural stream — a crucial distinction for ownership of water structures.

In the Hanafi fiqh of reviving dead lands, the term 'السيل' (al-sail) appears in rulings about irrigation and barriers. This lesson clarifies its precise lexical meaning, distinguishing it from a flowing stream. Understanding this term is essential for correctly applying the rules of land ownership and water rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary lexical function of 'السيل'
Read the phrase 'السيل لحبس الماء' — it explicitly states that al-sail is for holding water. This is the core function.
The term is defined by its purpose, not its form.
2
Note the synonym provided
The text says 'ويسمى: السَّدُّ' — it is also called a dam. This confirms it is a barrier, not a natural watercourse.
Lexical cross-reference: see al-Mughrib and al-Misbah al-Munir.
3
Understand the physical description
The passage adds 'والمراد ما يكون كالجسر' — what is meant is something like a bridge. This implies a structure you can walk on, reinforcing that it is man-made.
The author's statement 'يمشي عليها' (walk on it) illustrates this.
4
Contrast with a misconception
Al-sail does not mean a stream or flow of water. It is a barrier built to control water, which affects legal rulings on ownership and irrigation rights.
This distinction prevents misapplication in furu' (branches) of fiqh.

The intended meaning is a structure that resembles a bridge, allowing people to walk on it. This definition is crucial for understanding the rulings regarding irrigation and ownership in the context of land revival.

السَّدُّ (al-sadd): A dam; a synonym for السيل in this context.
