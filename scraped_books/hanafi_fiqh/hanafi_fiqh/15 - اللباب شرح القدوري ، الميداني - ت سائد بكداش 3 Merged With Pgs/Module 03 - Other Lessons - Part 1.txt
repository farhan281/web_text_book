Selling Fruit with Exception of Known Weight
Intra-Hanafi dispute on istithna' in fruit sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling fruit with an exception of a known weight and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute and tarjih.

Core Idea
Selling fruit while excepting some leaves remainder unknown, invalidating the sale due to gharar.

In this lesson, we examine a specific sale scenario: a seller wants to sell all the fruit on a tree but keep a certain number of ratls (pounds) for himself. Is this permissible? The Hanafi school has an internal debate, and understanding it clarifies the school's method of weighing textual transmissions against analogy. We will follow the author's layered discussion: the dominant view, the minority view, and the final relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and default ruling
Start by stating the scenario: selling fruit (e.g., dates on a palm) with an exception of a known weight. The text opens by saying this is not permissible. Identify why: the remainder after exception is unknown (gharar).
The text contrasts with excepting a specific tree, where the remainder is known by sight.
2
Explore the minority view and its proof
Some Hanafis (al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, al-Tahawi) hold it permissible. Their proof: a principle that anything that can be sold independently can be excepted, with analogy to selling a qafiz (measure) from a known heap of grain.
Al-Nasafi argues that the Zahir al-Riwaya (authoritative transmission) supports this permissibility.
3
Analyse the response to the minority view
The dominant position responds that the analogy is weak because in the heap case the total is known, but with fruit on a tree the total is unknown until ripening. Thus the remainder remains uncertain.
The author cites al-Fath that non-permissibility is 'more analogous' to the Imam's methodology.
4
Determine the tarjih and mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is non-permissibility. This is stated in al-Hidaya, al-Mukhtar, al-Ikhtiyar, and confirmed by later authorities. The minority view is based on a weaker transmission (riwayat al-Hasan).
This shows how later Hanafi jurists prefer the more cautious ruling to avoid gharar.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of selling fruit with an exception of a known weight. The author states the dominant position in the school: it is not permissible. The reasoning is that after excluding a known quantity (e.g., 10 ratls of dates), the remainder in the tree or bunch is unknown, creating gharar (uncertainty), which invalidates the sale. This is contrasted with the case where one sells a fruit orchard but excepts a specific palm tree, because the remaining fruit is ascertainable by sight. The relied-upon works al-Hidaya, al-Mukhtar, and others affirm this. However, a minority view, attributed to al-Hasan ibn Ziyad (a narration from Abu Hanifa via al-Hasan) and al-Tahawi, allows it based on a principle: anything that can be sold independently can be excepted. They analogize to selling a specific measure (qafiz) from a heap, which is valid because the heap is known. Al-Nasafi, following al-Hidaya's suggestion, argued that the Zahir al-Riwaya (established transmission from Abu Hanifa and the two companions) should permit it by this qiyas. But the author reports from al-Fath that the position of non-permissibility is more analogous (aqyas) to the Imam's (Abu Hanifa's) methodology. Thus the mufta bihi (operative position) is non-permissibility, as it avoids uncertainty.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithnā’): Exception: excluding something from the object of sale while selling the rest.
ثمرة (thamara): Fruit, especially dates on the palm tree.
ظاهر الرواية (ẓāhir al-riwāya): The apparent narration: the six core books of Imam Muhammad (the Hākim group) which represent the authoritative transmission of the school.
رواية الحسن (riwāyat al-Ḥasan): The narration of al-Hasan ibn Ziyād, a transmission from Abu Hanifa that is not part of ẓāhir al-riwāya.
قفيز من صبرة (qafīz min ṣubra): A specific measure (qafīz) from a heap (ṣubra) of grain; a classic example of a sale that is valid because the heap is known.

Selling Wheat in Ears and Beans in Pods: Conditions and Exceptions
Ruling on selling grain in natural containers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling grain in its natural covering and identify the condition that distinguishes valid from void sales.

Core Idea
Selling grain in its ear is valid if sold with a different genus; otherwise risk of ribā voids the contract.

This passage addresses a common agricultural transaction: selling wheat still in the ear, or beans still in the pod. The Hanafi school permits this sale but with important conditions to avoid uncertainty (gharar) and ribā. The buyer is given a right of inspection (khiyār) because the exact quality inside cannot be known at the time of contract. However, not all hidden items can be sold—those considered non-existent by custom, such as milk in the udder, are void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissible sale
The passage first states that it is permissible to sell wheat in its ears, beans in pods, and similar items like rice and sesame.
The permissibility is the default ruling for these items.
2
Note the seller's obligation and buyer's option
The seller is required to extract the grain (or deliver the ears) and the buyer has khiyār (option to annul if unsatisfactory).
The khiyār is because the exact quality inside is not fully known.
3
Identify the condition for validity
The sale is valid only when the wheat/beans are sold with an item of a different genus (e.g., cash). If sold for the same genus (e.g., wheat for wheat), it is invalid due to ribā.
Ribā al-fadl is the concern when exchanging foodstuffs of the same type in unequal or uncertain quantities.
4
Distinguish void sales
Sales of items that are considered non-existent by custom, such as milk in the udder, pit in a date, seeds in cotton, or chaff on wheat, are void.
These items are not considered actual property because they are embedded and require processing to extract.

Conditions of Pledge: Mufarrag, Mumayyaz, and Effect of Qabd
Commentary on conditions for validity of rahn and effect of qabd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for a valid pledge (mufarrag and mumayyaz) and explain why delivery (qabd) is necessary for the contract to become binding.

Core Idea
A pledge is not binding until delivered, and the property must be free of the pledgor's rights and specifically identified.

In Hanafi fiqh, a pledge (rahn) is a security contract that requires specific conditions for validity. This passage from al-Lubab explains that the pledged property must be mufarrag (free from any encumbrance of the pledgor) and mumayyaz (identified and not an undivided share). Additionally, the contract only becomes binding upon delivery (qabd) by the mortgagee. Let's unpack these conditions and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
The text states that the pledged property must be 'mufarrag'an' (free from the pledgor's rights) and 'mumayyazan' (specifically identified). Note that these are conditions for validity, not pillars.
Mufarrag excludes property like land with crops owned by the pledgor.
2
Understand the effect of delivery
The passage explains that before delivery, the pledgor has the option to withdraw from the pledge. Once the mortgagee takes possession, the pledge becomes binding and enters into the mortgagee's liability (daman).
This is similar to the rule in gift (hiba).
3
Apply to a scenario
Consider a person pledging a car that is free of liens (mufarrag) and specifically identified by VIN (mumayyaz). If the car is not handed over, the pledgor can still withdraw. Once the mortgagee receives the keys and takes possession, the pledge is binding.
The mortgagee becomes responsible for safe custody.

Condition for Valid Rahn: Guaranteed Debt
States a condition with rational proof and an exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that a valid rahn must secure a guaranteed debt and apply it to distinguish between guaranteed debts and non-guaranteed obligations like darak.

Core Idea
A pledge is only valid for a guaranteed debt; securing a non-guaranteed obligation is invalid.

In Hanafi fiqh, the contract of rahn (pawn/pledge) is a security device. Its very purpose is to secure a debt. Therefore, if the debt is not guaranteed—meaning the debtor is not legally liable to pay—then the contract defeats its purpose and is invalid. This lesson unpacks the key condition and a special exception (darak guarantee).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and condition
Read the matn: 'A pledge is not valid except for a guaranteed debt.' The ruling is that the contract is batil unless the debt is guaranteed. The condition is 'dhan maminun'.
All debts in Hanafi are inherently guaranteed unless specified otherwise.
2
Understand the proof (dalil)
The commentator gives a rational proof: rahn is legislated for securing a debt; securing something that is not guaranteed is meaningless (laghw). This is an istihsan argument.
Istihsan here is a juristic preference based on purpose.
3
Analyze the exception: darak guarantee
The phrase 'maminun' is emphatic but also excludes the darak guarantee. Darak is a promise to compensate if a sold item is evicted; it is not an existing debt. A pledge for darak is invalid.
Darak is contingent; rahn requires an actual or definitely future debt.
4
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Now test: Is a pledge valid for a loan of 100 dirhams? Yes, because it is a guaranteed debt. Is a pledge valid for a promise to pay if something happens? No, because it is not guaranteed at the time of the contract.
The debt must be maminun at the time of pledging.

The Pawn: Guarantee Limited to the Lesser of Value or Debt
Explains how the pawn is guaranteed by the lesser and rulings upon destruction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school limits the guarantee of a pawn to the lesser of its value or the debt, and determine the consequences when the pawn is destroyed.

Core Idea
A pawn guarantees only the lesser of its value or the debt – the surplus is a trust.

When a pawn is held by a mortgagee, it is not automatically equivalent to the entire debt. The Hanafi school applies a precise rule: the pawn is guaranteed only by the lesser of its appraised value or the debt amount. Any surplus value remains a trust, and any shortfall reduces the debt proportionally. This lesson unpacks the three scenarios upon destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two variables
Determine the value of the pawn at the time of the contract and the amount of the debt secured.
Value is assessed at the day of pledge.
2
Compare the variables
The guarantee is limited to the lesser of the two amounts. If value equals debt, the guarantee covers the full debt; if value is greater, the surplus is a trust; if value is less, only the value is guaranteed.
The particle 'min' in the text indicates the lesser.
3
Apply to the three scenarios
Work through the cases: 1) value = debt: deemed full collection; 2) value > debt: surplus is trust; 3) value < debt: debt reduced by the value.
The mortgagee is considered to have collected the guaranteed amount.
4
Determine the legal effect
For each scenario, state the resulting liability: full satisfaction, no liability for surplus (unless negligence), or remaining debt still owed.
The mortgagee's liability is limited to the guaranteed amount; any excess loss falls on the mortgagor.

(2) If value exceeds debt, the surplus is considered a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's hands, not guaranteed. (3) If value is less than debt, that amount reduces the debt, and the mortgagee may recover the remainder from the mortgagor. The reasoning is that recovery is only to the extent of financial value.

Core Idea
A pledge held for a whole debt binds every part of the collateral until the debt is fully paid.

In the Hanafi school, when a debtor pledges multiple items as collateral for a single debt, the creditor holds all items until the entire debt is cleared. Interdiction (Hajr): Rulings on the Transactions of the Insane, Minor, and Slave
States and explains rulings on interdicted persons' transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the transactions of the continuously insane, the discerning minor/slave, and explain the guardian's option to ratify or rescind suspended contracts.

Core Idea
Transactions of the totally insane are void; those of the minor, slave, and intermittently insane are suspended pending guardian approval.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expounded in Al-Lubab, establishes the fundamental principles of legal interdiction (hajr). It distinguishes between those lacking all legal capacity (continuously insane) and those with partial capacity (minors, slaves, intermittent insane). Understanding these categories is essential for determining the validity of contracts in Islamic finance and daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of interdicted persons
The text mentions the insane (majnoon), the minor (sabi), and the slave (abd). Note that the 'insane' is divided into two types: the continuously insane and the one with lucid intervals.
The continuously insane is called 'al-maghloob ala aqlihi' – one whose mind is completely overpowered.
2
Distinguish continuous insanity from intermittent insanity
The author explains that the continuously insane person's transactions are absolutely void (la yajuzu) in all circumstances, even with guardian permission. In contrast, the intermittent insane person (yujann wa yufiq) is treated like a discerning minor (mumayyiz).
The key is the presence of understanding (ya'qil al-bay'a) – the intermittent insane has lucid intervals where he understands.
3
Understand the suspended contract and guardian's option
If a minor, slave, or intermittent insane person sells or buys while understanding and intending the transaction, the contract is concluded suspended (mawquf). The guardian (wali) has the option (khiyar) to ratify if there is benefit (maslahah) or to rescind (faskh).
The contract is suspended because of the potential for harm (darar) to the minor/slave. Ratification by the guardian confirms the benefit and makes the contract effective.
4
Grasp the distinction between statements (aqwal) and actions (af'al)
The passage concludes that the three causes of interdiction (insanity, minority, slavery) necessitate restriction only in statements, not in actions. Actions are evident by observation, so their effects are immediate (e.g., liability for damage). Statements require intention and sound reason, which these persons lack, so their contracts are either void or suspended.
For example, if a minor destroys property, liability is immediate; if he makes a sale, it is suspended.

Interdiction Affects Utterances, Not Actions
States the principle that causes of interdiction restrict utterances, not actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that legal interdiction affects utterances only, and explain the rationale based on the need for intent in utterances.

Core Idea
Interdiction blocks the validity of utterances, not liability for actions.

In Hanafi fiqh, individuals under interdiction (due to minority, insanity, or profligacy) are restricted in legal capacity. You might assume that all their acts are invalid, but the rule is more nuanced: it is their utterances—contracts, divorces, acknowledgments—that are void, while their physical actions remain legally effective. This lesson unpacks the reasoning: actions are undeniable sensory facts, whereas utterances derive legal force from the Law, which requires a valid intent that the interdicted person lacks.

Guided Path

The exact three causes are dealt with elsewhere in the book; here we work with the general principle.
2
Distinguish Utterances from Actions
Classify examples: utterances are spoken acts like contracts, divorces, and acknowledgments; actions are physical deeds like causing damage. The rule separates legal treatment of these two categories.
Utterances require the actor to have intent (qasd) to be legally effective.
3
Analyze the Rationale
Actions are effective because they exist by sensation and observation (ḥissan wa mushāhadatan) and cannot be ignored. Utterances depend on law (sharʿ) and require intent; an interdicted person lacks valid intent, so utterances are ineffective.
The exception: an action with a 'subtle ruling' (ḥukm yandurru) may require intent, which is not covered here.

Given a hypothetical: a 10-year-old sells a bicycle (utterance) and later breaks a window (action). The sale is void; the window break incurs liability. Verify using the principle.
This shows the practical difference: utterances are null, actions are binding.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for sale, manumission, marriage, and dower of a person under hijr, and apply the principle of hazl (jest) to identify which dispositions are affected by hijr.

Core Idea
Dispositions not affected by jest remain valid under hijr; sale and excess dower are restricted.

Legal interdiction (hijr) protects a spendthrift's wealth, but not all their actions are invalid. This lesson examines four key dispositions — sale, manumission, marriage, and dower — and the principle that determines their validity: whether the act is affected by hazl (jest). The text follows the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the four dispositions
The passage explicitly mentions sale, manumission, marriage, and dower. List them and note that each has a distinct ruling.
Sale is the only disposition that is not executed unless the judge permits; the rest are either valid or partially valid.
2
Apply the hazl principle
According to the Sahibayn, every disposition affected by jest (hazl) is affected by hijr. Determine which of the four are affected by jest. Sale can be done in jest, so hijr prevents it. The underlying rationale is that hijr is meant to protect the mahjur's property, but it does not override personal acts that are essential and not tainted by frivolity. The principle is: 'كل تصرف يؤثر فيه الهزل يؤثر فيه الحجر وما لا فلا'.

Core Idea
The interdicted person's manumission and marriage are valid; only the proper dower is binding.

The Hanafi school, through the principle of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), distinguishes between transactions affected by jest (hazl) and those that are not. Fixed Minimums for Vague Descriptions in Iqrar
Explains minimum amounts for vague terms in confession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fixed minimum amounts for 'mal 'azim' and 'darahim kathirah' in Hanafi iqrar, explain the linguistic and analogical proofs, and identify the mufta bihi position in the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
In iqrar, vague descriptors are given fixed minimums: 200 dirhams for 'great wealth', and 10 dirhams for 'many dirhams' per Abu Hanifa.

When a person makes a confession (iqrar) using vague terms like 'great wealth' or 'many dirhams', Islamic law cannot leave the amount undefined. The Hanafi school sets objective minimums to give effect to the descriptor. This lesson examines how the nisab of zakat and linguistic patterns determine these thresholds, and why the later authorities preferred Abu Hanifa's view over that of his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two vague descriptors and their fixed minimums
From the text, locate the rulings for 'mal 'azim' (200 dirhams) and 'darahim kathirah' (10 dirhams according to Abu Hanifa). Note that these are the minimums that the confessor cannot reduce below.
The amounts are not arbitrary but derive from established principles (nisab and linguistic usage).
2
Understand the proof for 'mal 'azim'
The text states that 'mal 'azim' refers to property that makes its owner rich (ghani). The nisab of zakat (200 dirhams) is the standard for wealth, so any lesser amount cannot be described as 'great'. The descriptor cannot be ignored.
This is a straightforward application of the nisab concept.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's linguistic proof for 'darahim kathirah'
Abu Hanifa argues that the plural name 'kathirah' reaches its farthest extent at ten because the counting pattern changes after ten (e.g., 'ten dirhams' vs. 'eleven dirhams'). Therefore, ten is the maximum number that the word 'many' can signify without further specification.
This is a linguistic (lughawi) approach based on how Arabic usage distinguishes plural forms.
4
Compare the Sahibayn's analogical proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani analogize 'darahim kathirah' to 'mal 'azim', arguing that both should be set at 200 dirhams, the nisab of wealth. They see no reason to differentiate between the two descriptions.
Their proof is qiyas (analogy) on the nisab, while Abu Hanifa relies on direct linguistic analysis.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position and tarjih authority
The commentary from al-Tashih states that the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's, followed by later authorities such as al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi, and Sadr al-Shari'a. Thus, in practice, the minimum for 'darahim kathirah' is 10 dirhams.
Even though the Sahibayn's view is also authoritative, the later Hanafi jurists preferred the Imam's view.

The conjunction 'wa' in 'kadha' triples the minimum dirham count compared to its absence.
