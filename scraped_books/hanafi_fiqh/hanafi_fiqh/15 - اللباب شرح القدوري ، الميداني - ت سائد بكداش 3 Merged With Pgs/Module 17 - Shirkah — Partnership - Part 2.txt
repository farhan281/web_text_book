Mudarib Marrying Slaves: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mudarib marrying slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on a mudarib marrying a slave from mudarabah capital and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Marriage is not trade – the mudarib cannot use capital for it, except as Abu Yusuf analogized to leasing.

The mudarabah contract grants the mudarib authority to trade with the capital, but does that extend to marrying a slave? The Hanafi school splits: all three imams agree the mudarib cannot marry a male slave, but Abu Yusuf differs on female slaves using qiyas. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the basis of authority
The mudarib is authorized to trade and do what is necessary for trade. Marriage is not trade, so the default is that it is outside authority.
2
State the point of agreement (ittifaq)
All three – Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf – agree that the mudarib cannot marry a male slave from mudarabah capital.
The text explicitly says 'bi al-ittifaq' for the male slave.
3
State the dispute on the female slave
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold it is not permitted; Abu Yusuf analogizes to leasing (ijarah).
4
Analyze the dalil of each side
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: marriage is not trade nor necessary; the contract only covers trade. Abu Yusuf: qiyas – like leasing a female slave brings earnings (mahr, saved maintenance).
The Hanafi response distinguishes leasing as trade-related; marriage is not primarily for earning.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
If a mudarib marries a female slave from capital, according to the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) the marriage is invalid; Abu Yusuf says valid. The mufta bihi is the majority view.
The text implies the majority view is the relied-upon position.

Commentary

The sharh clarifies that there is agreement (ittifaq) regarding the male slave – all three (Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, Abu Yusuf) agree it is not allowed. For the female slave, however, there is ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold it is not permitted, because marriage is not a trade nor necessary for trade, and the mudarib's authorization is limited to trade and its necessities. Abu Yusuf analogizes it to leasing (ijarah) the female slave, arguing that marriage is a form of earning (iktisab) because it brings the mahr and saves maintenance expenses. The commentary notes the disagreement without explicitly stating which view is relied upon, but by mentioning the agreement on the male slave and the difference on the female, it implies that the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the default position.

Straw Ownership in Valid Muzara'a: Default and Custom
Intra-Hanafi dispute on straw ownership and conditions of validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default Hanafi position on straw ownership in muzara'a, explain the Mashayikh of Balkh's counter-argument based on custom, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Straw belongs to the seed owner by default, but custom overrides when not stipulated.

In a valid sharecropping contract (muzara'a), the division of produce is as agreed. But what about the straw — the dried stems left after harvest? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute. The original text assigns straw to the seed owner because it is a growth of his property. However, later scholars from Balkh argued that custom should govern in the absence of stipulation, making the straw shared. This lesson unpacks that dispute and explains the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage addresses who owns the straw in a valid muzara'a contract when the parties did not explicitly agree on it.
Straw is a byproduct of the crop; its ownership can affect the economic balance of the partnership.
2
State the default position from the matn
The text says: 'ثم التبن يكون لصاحب البذر' — the straw belongs to the owner of the seed. The reason given is that straw is the growth (nama') of the seed.
This is the explicit ruling in the original Quduri text and is considered the zahir al-riwayah.
3
Present the opposing view of the Mashayikh of Balkh
The Mashayikh of Balkh held that the straw is shared between the landowner and the worker because (1) custom (urf) governs what is not stipulated, and (2) straw is a dependent (tb') of the grain, and the dependent follows the original in sharing.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: both sides are within the school.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
If a dispute arises, the default position gives all straw to the seed owner, while the Balkh position divides it. The practical consequence is significant in farming communities where straw has economic value.
The passage also warns that stipulating straw for a third party invalidates the contract, as it could lead to only straw being produced, cutting the partnership.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The later relied-upon position is that of the Mashayikh of Balkh, considering custom. The text presents their reasoning in detail and highlights the problem with the alternative, indicating it is the preferred view for fatwa.
This is an example of how later Hanafi jurists (mutakhkhirun) sometimes adopt a position different from the zahir al-riwayah based on practical need.

However, the Mashayikh of Balkh held a different view: the straw is shared between both parties. Their reasoning is based on custom (urf) where no explicit stipulation exists, and because straw is a dependent (تبعة) of the grain, and the dependent follows the principle of the original. They warn that if the parties stipulate that the straw goes to someone other than the seed owner, the contract becomes invalid, as it would lead to a situation where only straw is produced, cutting off the partnership. The passage then lists conditions for validity: the land must be suitable for cultivation, and the land must be handed over to the worker. The remaining conditions are referenced from al-Hidayah: both parties must be legally competent, free, and adult. Compensation in Invalid Muzara'a: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings when land yields nothing and when contract is fasid, with internal Hanafi difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on compensation in a fasid muzara'a contract and evaluate the intra-Hanafi difference between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
In a fasid muzara'a, the worker's compensation is capped by the agreed share per the two shaykhs, but Muhammad awards full market wage.

This lesson addresses two scenarios: when the land yields no crop, and when the muzara'a contract is fasid. The key legal question is what compensation the worker receives when the contract is invalid. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: while the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) impose a cap based on the agreed share, Imam Muhammad argues for full market value. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how fasid contracts are treated differently from valid ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize that the passage first rules on no produce (worker gets nothing) and then on an invalid contract (fasid) with two sub-cases depending on seed ownership.
The first ruling is straightforward; the second involves the ikhtilaf.
2
State the ruling for no produce
When the land yields nothing, the worker gets nothing because he was hired for a share of the produce, which did not materialize.
Rooted in qiyas: no subject matter, no entitlement.
3
Analyze the fasid contract ruling
When the contract is fasid, the produce belongs to the seed owner. Then determine compensation for the other party based on who provided the seed.
Seed ownership determines who gets the produce as growth of property.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
If the seed is from the landowner, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the worker gets ajr mithl but not exceeding the agreed share; Imam Muhammad says full ajr mithl without cap. If the seed is from the worker, the landowner gets ajr mithl for land.
The dispute is only when the seed is from the landowner.
5
Understand the reasoning for the cap
The two shaykhs argue that the worker consented to forgo any excess beyond the agreed share (رضائہ بسقوط الزیادة). Muhammad counters that a fasid contract requires full compensation for services rendered.
The tarjih in later Hanafi works follows the view of the two.

Commentary
This passage covers two scenarios: when the land yields no crop, and when the muzara'a contract is invalid (fasid). In the first case, even if the contract was valid, if no produce comes out, the worker gets nothing. The reason is that the worker is essentially hired for a share of the produce; if there is no produce, he has no entitlement. In the second case, when the contract is invalid, the produce goes to the owner of the seed, because it is the growth of his property. Then the question is what the other party gets. If the seed was from the landowner, the worker is entitled to the wage equivalent (ajr mithl) for his labor, but with a cap: it cannot exceed the share that was agreed upon in the contract. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Imam Muhammad disagrees; he says the worker gets the full wage equivalent without any cap, because the landowner benefited from the worker's services through a fasid contract, and thus must pay the full market value. The text then notes that the later scholars al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi followed the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two). If the seed was from the worker, then the landowner gets the wage equivalent for his land (since the worker benefited from the land through a fasid contract).

Key Terms

فسدت (fasadat): The contract is invalid (fasid), meaning it does not meet the legal conditions but may still have some effects requiring compensation.
البذر (al-badhr): The seed that is sown; it can be provided by either the landowner or the worker.
