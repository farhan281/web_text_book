The Preventer of Connected Increase in the Gifted Item
Explains the second preventer: connected increase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify what constitutes a connected increase that prevents revocation and distinguish it from disconnected increase or mere value appreciation.

Core Idea
A connected physical increase that adds value makes the gift irrevocable because separation is impossible.

When a gifted item grows through the donee's action, the donor cannot revoke because the increase is inseparable. This lesson unpacks the second preventer: connected increase. We will examine the categories of increase, the conditions, and the rationale behind this rule.

Guided Path
1
Define connected increase
The text defines 'ziyadah muttasilah' as a physical increase in the gifted item itself that is attached and adds value, such as building on land, planting trees, or fattening an animal.
The increase must be in the item's substance, not just its market value.
2
Explain the rationale
Two reasons: (a) It is impossible to revoke the original without the increase because they are inseparable. (b) Revoking with the increase would mean taking something not included in the original contract, which is unfair.
This is based on istihsan and practicality.
3
Distinguish disconnected increase
Disconnected increase (munfasilah), like offspring (walad) or compensation for injury (arsh), does not prevent revocation. The donor may revoke the original item but leaves the disconnected increase with the donee.
Offspring are considered separate property belonging to the donee.
4
Identify limitations
The text imposes four restrictions: (a) Must be an increase, not a decrease. (b) Must be connected. (c) Must be in the item itself, not just in value (e.g., market appreciation does not count). (d) Must cause an increase in value; trivial additions that do not add value do not prevent revocation.
Each restriction excludes a common misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the preventers (mawani') that make a gift irrevocable once it has been validly given. The discussion opens with the ruling that revoking a gift is makruh (disliked) but legally binding—meaning the revocation itself is valid, albeit disliked. This is supported by the famous hadith comparing the revoker to a dog returning to its vomit, emphasizing the moral reprehension of the act. The focus then shifts to the preventers of revocation. The author mentions that there are seven preventers, symbolically represented by the letters 'دمع حزقه' (dam' hizqih). Although the text enumerates all seven, it only details the first two at this stage: compensation ('iwad) and connected increase (ziyadah muttasilah).

Regarding compensation: If the donee gives the donor a compensation ('iwad) for the gift and the donor takes possession of that compensation, the gift becomes irrevocable. The key condition is that the donee must explicitly state that the compensation is in return for the entire gift; a general gift without such specification does not bar revocation. The reason is that the purpose of the gift—to benefit the donee—is fulfilled by the exchange, making revocation unjust.

Regarding connected increase: If the gifted item itself gains a connected increase that adds to its value—such as constructing a building on land, planting trees, or fattening an animal—the donor cannot revoke the gift. The rationale is that it is impossible to separate the increase from the original item, and since the increase was not part of the original contract, revoking with the increase would involve taking something not given. The author notes several restrictions: (a) Only increase (ziyadah) prevents; decrease (nuqsan) does not. (b) The increase must be connected (muttasilah); disconnected increases like offspring or compensation (arsh) do not prevent revocation—the donor may revoke the original item but leave the disconnected increase with the donee. (c) The increase must be in the item itself (bi-nafsiha); an increase in value alone (e.g., market appreciation) does not prevent revocation, as the physical item remains unchanged. (d) The increase must cause a rise in value; otherwise it is negligible. The passage thus clarifies that these two preventers are based on practical impossibility or contractual fairness, reflecting Hanafi principles of equity (istihsan).

Key Terms
الهبة (al-hibah): A gift; a contract of transfer of ownership without compensation.
العوض (al-'iwad): Compensation given by the donee to the donor in exchange for the gift, which prevents revocation.
الزيادة المتصلة (al-ziyadah al-muttasilah): A physical increase in the gifted item that is inseparable and adds to its value, such as building, planting, or fattening.
المعوض (al-mu'awwid): The donee who gives compensation ('iwad) to the donor for the gift.

Preventers of Revoking a Gift (Mawani' al-Ruju')
Lists and explains four preventers of gift revocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four preventers of gift revocation in the Hanafi school and apply them to factual scenarios to determine whether revocation is possible.

Core Idea
Four conditions permanently block the donor's right to revoke a gift: compensation, connected increase, death, or transfer of ownership.

The Hanafi school generally permits a donor to revoke a gift after it has been given, but this right is not absolute. The classical texts identify four preventers (mawani') that permanently eliminate the donor's right to revoke. Understanding these preventers is crucial for anyone dealing with gift transactions, as they affect the finality of the transfer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default position on gift revocation
In Hanafi fiqh, the donor may revoke a gift unless prevented. The default is permissibility but this right is eliminated by specific preventers.
The default rule is that the donor can revoke as long as the gift is still in the donee's possession and has not changed.
2
List the four preventers
The passage enumerates four mawani': compensation ('iwad), connected increase (ziyada muttasila), death of either party, and the gift leaving the donee's possession.
Memorize these four categories as they are exhaustive.
3
Examine each preventer's conditions
For compensation: the donee must give something and the donor must take possession with clear wording that it is for the entire gift. For increase: it must be connected and increase value; disconnected increases or value-only increases do not prevent.
The text specifies that an increase in value alone (without physical change) does not prevent revocation.
4
Apply to sub-rulings
The text provides applications: if the donee sells half the gift, the donor can revoke the remaining half; if the buyer returns the gift due to a defect, the donor cannot revoke; if the donee gifts it to another and revokes, the first donor can revoke.
These cases illustrate how the fourth preventer (khuruj min al-milk) operates in nuanced scenarios.
5
Distinguish connected vs. disconnected increases
Connected increases like building, planting, fattening prevent revocation. Disconnected increases like offspring or compensation for defect (arsh) do not; the donor may revoke the original but not the increase.
The key is whether the increase can be separated without damaging the original.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the preventers (mawani') that permanently eliminate the donor's right to revoke a gift (ruju' fi al-hiba). The author lists four, each with conditions and exceptions. First: the donee compensates the donor ('iwad) and the donor takes possession, provided clear wording indicating compensation for the entire gift. Second: the gifted item increases by a connected, intrinsic increase that enhances value (e.g., building, planting, fattening). Disconnected increases (e.g., offspring, arsh for defect) do not prevent revocation of the original. Increase in value alone (without physical change) also does not prevent. Third: the death of either party — death of donee transfers ownership irrevocably; death of donor makes his heir a stranger to the contract. Fourth: the gift leaves the donee's possession through a legal transaction (e.g., sale or gift), as the donor's authorization is exhausted. The text further refines the fourth preventer with sub-rulings from al-Muhit and al-Fayd regarding specific scenarios.

خروج من الملك (khuruj min al-milk): The gifted item leaving the donee's ownership (e.g., by sale, gift to another), preventing the original donor from revoking.

Preventers of Gift Revocation: Kinship and Spousal Relationship
Ruling with proof via istihsan and commentary expansion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two categories of donees that prohibit gift revocation in Hanafi fiqh, identify the condition that mahram must be by lineage (nasab), and apply the rule that the spousal relationship must exist at the time of the gift.

Core Idea
Revoking a gift to a mahram relative or spouse is prohibited because the gift's purpose is to strengthen family bonds.

In Hanafi fiqh, a donor generally has the right to revoke a gift after delivery (al-ruju' fi al-hibah), provided no legal impediment exists. This passage from Al-Lubab identifies two key exceptions where revocation is permanently prohibited: gifts given to a mahram relative by lineage and gifts between spouses. The ruling is based on istihsan, prioritizing the social objectives of maintaining kinship ties (silat al-rahim) and spousal affection. Understanding these exceptions is vital for correctly applying gift laws in family and marital contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and the exceptions
Start by recognizing that Hanafi law allows gift revocation generally. This passage introduces two exceptions where revocation is forbidden. Pinpoint the exact phrasing: 'fala ruju' fiha' (there is no revocation in it).
The exceptions are stated as absolute prohibitions.
2
Analyze the first exception: mahram relative by lineage
Read the text: 'وإن وهب هبة لذي رَحِمٍ مَحْرَم منه نسبًا' (And if he gives a gift to a blood-related mahram relative by lineage). Note the restriction 'nassaban' (by lineage) – this excludes mahram relationships through suckling (ridaa') or marriage (musaahara). Identify the rationale given: 'li-anna al-maqsuda fiha silat al-rahim' (because the objective in it is maintaining kinship ties).
The commentator explicitly clarifies that mahram must be by lineage.
3
Analyze the second exception: gifts between spouses
Read: 'وكذلك ما وهب أحد الزوجين للآخر' (And similarly, what one spouse gives to the other). Note that the rationale is analogous: 'li-anna al-maqsuda fiha al-silah kama fi al-qarabah' (because the objective in it is connection, like in kinship).
Spousal bond is treated like family tie for this ruling.
4
Examine the timing condition for spousal gifts
The commentator adds an important condition: 'وإنما ينظر إلى هذا وقت العقد' (Consideration is only given to this at the time of the contract). If the donee marries the donor after the gift, revocation is allowed. If the donee divorces after receiving the gift, revocation remains prohibited. Trace this in the text.
This nuance shows the ruling depends on the relationship status at the moment of the gift.
5
Synthesize and apply to a scenario
Combine the two exceptions. Test with a scenario: A man gives a car to his cousin (ibn 'amm). Can he revoke? Cousin is not a mahram relative, so revocation is allowed. Next, he gives a necklace to his fiancée before marriage, then marries her later. At the time of the gift, she was not his wife, so revocation is allowed (per the timing rule). If he gave it after marriage, revocation is prohibited.
The rule is relational and time-sensitive.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab, the commentary on Al-Quduri, discusses two preventers (mawani') to the donor's right to revoke (رجوع) a gift after delivery. The first is when the donee is a mahram relative by lineage (نسب), such as brother, sister, uncle, aunt, etc. The rationale (ta'lil) is that the purpose of such a gift is to strengthen family ties (صلة الرحم), and once given, that purpose is fulfilled. Revocation would contradict that objective. The commentator clarifies that 'mahram' here means only those related by blood lineage; if the relationship is via fosterage (رضاع) or marriage (مصاهرة), the donor retains the right to revoke. The second preventer is when the gift is between spouses. The ruling is the same: no revocation, because the intent is to foster love and connection between them, analogous to the kinship case. However, the commentator adds an important condition: the spousal relationship must exist at the time of the gift. Key Rulings on Charity (Sadaqa) in Hanafi Fiqh
Stating rules for sadaqa: requires delivery, not valid in undivided share, multiple recipients, irrevocability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four key rulings on sadaqa according to the Hanafi school and explain the rationale behind each.

Core Idea
Charity is like a gift: it requires delivery, cannot be in an undivided share, and once delivered, it is irrevocable — even to the undeserving.

This passage from the chapter on gifts in al-Lubab sets out the essential rules for sadaqa (charity). Like gifts, charity depends on actual delivery to be valid. However, unlike gifts, charity is not revocable because the donor has already secured the intended reward from Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal nature of sadaqa
The passage begins by equating sadaqa to a gift (hiba). What does this mean for its validity conditions? Note that both are gratuitous transfers requiring delivery (qabd).
2
Examine the condition of delivery (qabd)
Sadaqa is not valid except with delivery. Recall that in Hanafi law, gifts are not complete without possession. The same principle applies to charity.
3
Understand the prohibition on undivided shares (musha')
Why is sadaqa of a divisible undivided share invalid? Because delivery of an undivided share without partition is impossible. The recipient cannot take exclusive possession.
An exception is given for multiple recipients acting as deputies.
4
Analyse the rule of irrevocability (la yasihhu al-ruju')
Once delivered, sadaqa cannot be revoked, even if given to a rich person. This is by istihsan because the purpose (reward from Allah) has been achieved.
The Hidayah is cited as authority for this ruling.

Vows to Give Sadaqah: Scope Depends on Wording (Wealth vs. Property)
Ruling on vow to give sadaqah with wealth vs property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on vows to give sadaqah using 'wealth' versus 'property', and apply the principle of istihsan to determine the obligated items.

Core Idea
A vow's scope is determined by wording: 'my wealth' limits to zakatable items; 'my property' covers everything.

How far does a vow to give charity reach? The Hanafi school distinguishes between 'wealth' (māl) and 'property' (milk) in a subtle but crucial way. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains the ruling, using istihsan to restrict the scope for 'wealth' while leaving 'property' unrestricted. Mastering this distinction will sharpen your understanding of how intent and wording shape legal obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct vow formulations: 'with my wealth' (bi-mālī) and 'with my property' (bi-milkī). Note the different rulings for each.
The Arabic terms māl and milk are the key.
2
Analyze the qiyas on 'wealth'
Under strict analogy (qiyas), 'wealth' would include all possessions, because 'māl' is a general term. The passage states the qiyas position: the person must give all his wealth.
Qiyas here is the starting point, not the final ruling.
3
Understand the istihsan override
Istihsan (juridical preference) overrides the qiyas for 'wealth'. The reason: the servant's obligation is measured by Allah's obligation (zakat), so the vow is interpreted as referring to surplus zakatable wealth, which is the common understanding of charity.
The basis is that people usually give charity from their surplus, not from every asset.
4
Contrast 'property' (milk)
The word 'property' is broader and not restricted by the Lawgiver's categories. Therefore, the vow with 'my property' obligates all possessions, and no istihsan exception applies.
The generality of 'milk' is preserved because there is no evidence to limit it.
5
Apply to concrete assets
From the footnote: for 'wealth', only gold, silver, trade goods, grazing animals, and tithed produce are due. For 'property', all owned items – including furniture, clothes, etc. – must be given.
This shows the practical impact of the two wordings.

Vow to Give Charity: The Distinction Between Māl and Milk
Hanafi ruling on vow to give charity with distinction between wealth and property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a vow using 'maal' versus 'milk' and state the Hanafi ruling for each, applying the principle of istihsan.

Core Idea
The word used in a vow determines the extent of charity: 'maal' restricts to zakatable assets via istihsan, while 'milk' obligates all property.

When a person vows to give charity, the precise wording matters. In Hanafi fiqh, the term 'maal' (wealth) and 'milk' (property) carry different legal implications. By strict analogy, both would require all possessions, but the Hanafi school applies istihsan to limit 'maal' to only zakatable assets. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms
Locate the two key terms in the passage: 'maal' (مال) and 'milk' (ملك). Understand their definitions: 'maal' refers to wealth that is subject to zakat, while 'milk' is a broader term encompassing all owned property.
This distinction is central to the ruling.
2
State the qiyas (analogy)
The text presents the qiyas: if someone vows to give charity with his 'maal', the literal meaning includes all property, so the analogy would require giving everything. Recognize that this is the strict logical outcome.
Qiyas is the default method, but it may be overridden.
3
Apply istihsan to 'maal'
The Hanafi school overrides the qiyas with istihsan. The reasoning: a human obligation is measured by Allah's obligation. Since Allah made charity obligatory only on zakatable wealth, the vow is interpreted to refer to that same category. Hence, only zakatable assets are required.
Istihsan is a principle of juristic preference.
4
Contrast with 'milk'
The word 'milk' is more general and is not restricted by divine obligation. Therefore, no istihsan applies, and the vow obligates all property without exception.
The generality of 'milk' remains untouched.
5
Consider the exception for need
The passage allows the person to keep enough for essential expenses of self and dependents until they can earn again, then give the equivalent. This applies to both wordings, as the final view considers both to refer to surplus wealth.
Hardship is avoided.

Definition of Ghasb and Ruling on Daman Mithl with Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Valuation Date
Legal definition of ghasb and ruling on daman mithl, then intra-Hanafi dispute on valuation date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ghasb, explain the ruling on daman mithl, and distinguish the three Hanafi positions on valuation date when the mithl is unavailable.

Core Idea
Usurpation: taking property wrongfully removes owner's hand; liability for like (mithl) is wajib; when mithl is lost, valuation date splits the Hanafi school.

This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces the book of ghasb, linking it to waqf. It defines ghasb linguistically and legally, then states the default rule: one who usurps something with a standard must compensate with its like. The reasoning given is that this balances the form (jins) and value (maliyah). When the standard becomes unavailable, a three-way dispute emerges among the Hanafi masters on when to value the property. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi fiqh uses calibrated principles to achieve justice in changing circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the definition of ghasb
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions. Note the key elements: valuable (mutaqawwim), protected (muhtaram), without permission, removes owner's hand. Distinguish actions that constitute ghasb (using a slave, riding an animal) from those that do not (sitting on a carpet).
The definition from al-Hidayah is authoritative.
2
Understand the ruling on daman mithl
When usurped property has a standard (mithl) and perishes, the usurper must provide its like. Grasp the wisdom: this combines form (kind) and value, most effectively preventing harm.
Mithl applies to fungibles like grains, weights, and measures.
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute on valuation date
When the like becomes unavailable (e.g., not found in market), valuation differs: Abu Hanifa: day of litigation; Abu Yusuf: day of usurpation; Muhammad: day of unavailability. Consider the thamarah al-khilaf: compensation amount may vary with price fluctuations.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view (litigation day).
4
Identify the mufta bihi and practical application
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya) adopt Abu Hanifa's position. In practice, a judge will determine the value at the time of litigation.
This ensures finality and aligns with the time of judgment.

Ruling for Qimi Property and Mixed Mithli
Ruling statement with rational argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for qimi property (value on day of usurpation) and identify when a mithli becomes qimi when mixed with a different genus.

Core Idea
Qimi property requires value payment; mixing mithli with a different genus makes it qimi.

In Hanafi fiqh, property is classified as mithli (fungible) or qimi (non-fungible) based on whether it has a standard like. This passage provides the core ruling for qimi property and clarifies that a mithli mixed with a different genus loses its fungible status, becoming qimi. Understanding this distinction determines the liability of a usurper or destroyer.

Guided Path
1
Define qimi and mithli
Qimi is property with no standard like due to individual variance; mithli has a standard like (e.g., grains, oils). Memorize the definitions.
2
State the ruling for qimi
For qimi property, the usurper must pay its market value on the day of usurpation by Hanafi consensus.
The valuation date is fixed at the time of usurpation, not later.
3
Understand the reasoning
Because individuals vary, consideration of form (like) is impossible, so only monetary value (meaning) is considered to prevent harm.
This is a rational legal reasoning (ta'lil).
4
Apply to mixed mithli
When a mithli is mixed with a different genus (e.g., wheat with barley), it loses its standard like and becomes qimi, so value must be paid.
Mixing with the same genus would keep it mithli; mixing with a different genus changes its nature.

Procedure When a Usurper Claims the Object Perished
Describes the legal procedure when a usurper claims the object has perished

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure when a usurper claims the object has perished and explain the rationale for detaining the usurper.

Core Idea
The usurper's mere claim of destruction is not accepted; the judge detains him until the truth becomes clear.

This lesson explains the legal procedure when a usurper (ghāṣib) alleges that the usurped object has perished. According to the Hanafi school, the court does not automatically accept the usurper's statement. To protect the owner's right and to encourage the return of the object if it still exists, the judge detains the usurper until his claim is verified or until the judge's dominant presumption is that the object is truly gone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The usurper claims the usurped object has perished (hālik). The question is how the court should handle this claim.
2
State the default rule
The usurper is not believed by his mere statement (lā yusaddaq bi-mujarrad qawlihi). His bare assertion is insufficient.
The usurper is considered a wrongdoer (kha'in), so his claim is suspect.
3
Describe the court's action
The judge detains the usurper (ḥabasahu al-ḥākim) until he knows of his truthfulness and it becomes the dominant presumption that if the object were still present, the usurper would have produced it.
Detention is used as a means to compel the truth and protect the owner's right.
4
Explain the rationale
The rationale is 'mubālagha fī al-iḥtiyāl ilā īṣāl' – an exaggeration in striving to deliver the right to its owner. The court exerts maximum effort to secure the owner's property.
5
Note the legal effect
The detention continues until the judge is convinced of the destruction or it becomes the overwhelming presumption. Once that occurs, the usurper is released and must pay compensation (the value of the object).

Core Idea
Real estate cannot be physically moved; ghasb requires removal of hand by transport.

The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on whether ghasb (unlawful appropriation) applies to real estate (ʿiqār). The general rule is that ghasb only applies to movable property that can be transported. When someone usurps land or a building and it perishes due to a natural disaster like a flood, should the usurper be liable? Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say no, while Muhammad says yes. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and demonstrates how a single qiyas (analogy) can lead to divergent rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule of Ghasb
The text begins by stating that ghasb applies only to what can be moved and transported. Understand that this is the default principle: ghasb is about physically taking and moving property.
Movement/transport (naql) is key to the definition of ghasb.
2
Locate the Disputed Case
The text presents the specific scenario: a person usurps real estate, and then the property perishes in his hands due to a heavenly affliction (e.g., flood). The dispute is whether the usurper is liable (ḍamān).
The destruction is by an act of God, not the usurper's action.
3
Analyse Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's Argument
They argue that ghasb does not occur for real estate because the property remains in its place; only the owner is distanced. This is analogous to a situation where the owner is separated from his cattle while the cattle remain—that is not considered ghasb.
Key analogy: 'like when the owner becomes distant from cattle'.
4
Analyse Muhammad's Argument
Muhammad argues that the usurper has established hand (yad) over the property, and by necessity the owner's possession is removed. This removal of possession itself triggers liability, similar to movable property.
The text is cut off, but the core reasoning is that establishing hand implies liability.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi Position
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: no liability. Understand the practical consequence: a usurper of real estate does not pay compensation if it is destroyed by natural causes.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) generally hold the usurper liable.

The Hanafi Definition of Ghasp: Why It Only Applies to Movable Property
Defining ghasp and its scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ghasp and explain why it is restricted to movable property, distinguishing it from mere transgression (ta'addi) in immovable property.

Core Idea
Ghasp requires the physical removal of the owner's hand by transport; immovable property cannot be relocated, so it is not subject to ghasp.

This lesson unpacks the opening line of the ghasp chapter in al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri: 'والغصب فيما ينقل ويحول'. The Hanafi school defines ghasp narrowly, requiring the usurper to physically move the property. This definition has direct legal consequences: if someone seizes land or a building, they are not considered a ghāṣib in the strict sense, and the rules of guarantee differ accordingly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitional element
The text states that ghasp is only in what is movable and transportable. Recognize that this is a restrictive condition.
2
Analyze the rationale
The author explains: 'because removal of the hand is by transport' (إزالة اليد بالنقل). The essence of ghasp is physically removing the owner's control over the object.
3
Apply to immovable property
For immovable property like land, transport is impossible. Therefore, the act of occupying land does not constitute ghasp; it is a transgression (ta'addi) but not ghasp.
4
Note the legal effect
Because ghasp is not established, the strict liability rules for ghasp (e.g., guarantee even if property perishes by natural calamity) do not automatically apply to immovable property. This sets the stage for the intra-Hanafi dispute.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Does a Usurper of Immovable Property Guarantee It If It Perishes?
Presenting the intra-Hanafi dispute on guarantee of immovable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the guarantee of immovable property perishing by natural calamity, and identify the mufta bihi positions for waqf and non-waqf property.

Core Idea
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, no guarantee for immovable property destroyed by natural causes; Muhammad says guarantee is wajib—the dispute is resolved differently for waqf and non-waqf.

This lesson examines the core of the ghasp chapter: the intra-Hanafi disagreement about whether someone who unlawfully occupies immovable property must compensate the owner if the property is destroyed by a natural calamity, like a flood. The text presents the reasoning of each side and then cites later authorities who settled the matter: for non-waqf property, the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf prevails; for waqf property, Muhammad's view is followed.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: no guarantee. Muhammad: guarantee. Understand the practical difference: one party pays, the other does not.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's proof
They argue that ghasp is not realized because removal of the hand requires transport; immovable property remains in place. The usurper's act only distances the owner, not the property. They compare it to the owner being distanced from his livestock without the livestock moving.
3
Analyze Muhammad's proof
He argues that establishing the usurper's hand on the property necessarily removes the owner's hand, because two hands cannot coexist on one property. Thus, ghasp is established and guarantee follows.
4
Identify the consensus point
All three imams agree that if the usurper directly causes damage (e.g., demolishing, causing wear through dwelling), he must compensate. This is destruction (itlaf), which is independent of the ghasp definition.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later authorities distinguished: for ordinary (non-waqf) property, the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is relied upon. For waqf property, Muhammad's view is the basis for fatwa.

Core Idea
Real estate liability for indirect acts splits: no guarantee for non-waqf, guarantee for waqf.

When a usurper causes damage to real estate indirectly—like weakening a wall by residing—the Hanafi school has an internal dispute. The primary matn of al-Quduri suggests liability, but later scholars reconcile the positions by distinguishing between private and endowed property. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the operative fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Read the matn ruling
Identify the base ruling: the usurper guarantees any diminution caused by his act, including demolition and residence. Note that this is the statement attributed to all three Imams.
The matn presents the ruling as unanimous, but the commentary reveals the dispute.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
The commentary cites that Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf do NOT guarantee diminution from mere residence, while Muhammad does. Understand the basis: they view residence as indirect, not direct destruction.
The dispute hinges on what constitutes 'destruction' (itlaf) in real estate.
3
Trace the later tarjih and reconciliation
Later scholars like al-Quhistani and al-Ayni reconcile by distinguishing between non-waqf and waqf. For non-waqf, Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf's view (no liability) is correct; for waqf, Muhammad's view (liability) is relied upon for fatwa.
This protects endowment property from exploitation.

If the building's value exceeds the log's value, the usurper becomes owner and pays compensation — demolition would cause harm without benefit.

والقولُ في القيمة قولُ الغاصب مع يمينه، إلا أن يقيم المالك البينة بأكثر من ذلك. فإن ظهرت العين، وقيمتها أكثر مما ضَمِن، وقد كان ضَمِنَها بقول المالك، أو ببينة أقامها أو بنكول الغاصب عن اليمين : فلا خيار للمالك. وإن كان ضَمِنَها بقول الغاصب مع يمينه : فالمالك بالخيار : إن شاء أمضى الضمان، وإن شاء أخَذَ العين، وردَّ العوض. والقولُ فى القيمة) إذا اختلفا فيها : (قولُ الغاصب) ؛ لإنكاره الزيادة، والقول قولُ المنكر، (مع يمينه)، كما مر. (إلا أن يقيم المالك البينة بأكثر من ذلك)؛ لإثباته بالحجة. (فإن ظهرت العين) بعد ذلك ( وقيمتها أكثر مما ) كان (ضمن، وقد كان ضمنها بقول المالك، أو ببينة (أقامها المالك، (أو بنكول الغاصب عن اليمين: فلا خيار للمالك)، وهي للغاصب؛ لأنه تم له الملك بسبب اتصل به رضا المالك، حيث ادعى هذا المقدار. (وإن كان ضمنها بقول الغاصب مع يمينه : فالمالك بالخيار: إن شاء أمضى الضمان ولا خيار للغاصب ولو قيمته أقل ؛ للزومه بإقراره، وإن شاء أخذ العين وردَّ العوض) ؛ لأنه لم يتم رضاه بهذا المقدار، حيث يدعي الزيادة، وأخذه دونها؛ لعدم الحجة. ولو ظهرت العين وقيمتها مثل ما ضمنه، أو دونه في هذا الفصل الأخير : فكذا الجواب في ظاهر الرواية، وهو الأصح، خلافاً لما قاله الكرخي ؛ لأنه لم يتم رضاه، حيث لم يُعْطَ ما يدعيه، والخيار لفوت الرضا. «هداية».
And the saying about the value is the saying of the usurper with his oath, unless the owner brings evidence of more than that. If the [original] property appears and its value is more than what he had guaranteed, and he had guaranteed it by the saying of the owner, or by evidence he brought, or by the refusal of the usurper to take the oath, then there is no choice for the owner. But if he had guaranteed it by the saying of the usurper with his oath, then the owner has the choice: if he wishes, he may affirm the guarantee, and if he wishes, he may take the [original] property and return the compensation. The saying about the value, if they differ, is the saying of the usurper because he denies the increase, and the saying is the saying of the denier with his oath, as before. Unless the owner brings evidence of more than that; to establish it by proof. If the [original] property appears after that and its value is more than what he had guaranteed, and he had guaranteed it by the saying of the owner, or by evidence that the owner brought, or by the usurper's refusal to take the oath, then there is no choice for the owner, and it belongs to the usurper; because ownership is perfected for him due to a cause connected to the owner's consent, since he claimed this amount. But if he had guaranteed it by the saying of the usurper with his oath, then the owner has the choice: if he wishes, he may affirm the guarantee, and there is no choice for the usurper even if its value is less, because it is binding by his acknowledgment, and if he wishes, he may take the [original] property and return the compensation; because his consent to this amount was not perfected, since he claims the increase, and he takes it without it for lack of proof. And if the [original] property appears and its value is equal to what he had guaranteed, or less, in this last section, then the answer is the same in the apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah), and it is the most correct, contrary to what al-Karkhi said; because his consent was not perfected, since he was not given what he claims, and the choice is due to the loss of consent. [From al-Hidayah]

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the dispute over the value of usurped property and the options after the property reappears. It begins with the general rule: the value is determined by the usurper's statement with his oath, unless the owner has proof of a higher value. Then it discusses two scenarios: (1) If the property was guaranteed based on the owner's claim or evidence or the usurper's refusal to swear, and the property later appears with higher value, the owner has no choice—the usurper keeps the property because the owner's consent to that amount was achieved. (2) If the guarantee was based on the usurper's statement with oath, the owner has the choice to either confirm the guarantee and take the compensation, or take back the property and return the compensation. The passage then addresses the case where the property's value equals or is less than the guaranteed amount, especially in the last scenario (owner's choice). According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the answer is the same: the owner still has the choice, contrary to al-Karkhi who held that the choice is lost. The reason is that the owner's consent was not perfected because he was not given what he claimed. This is the relied-upon position (al-ashah).

Key Terms
الغاصب (al-ghāṣib): The person who takes another's property without right, with the effect of removing the owner's hand.
الضمان (al-ḍamān): The obligation to pay the value of the usurped property as a guarantee.
الخيار (al-khiyār): The option or choice given to a party (here, the owner) to either uphold the compensation or reclaim the property.
