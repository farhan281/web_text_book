Two Agents Must Act Jointly in Discretionary Acts
States the rule of joint agency requiring collective action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding two jointly appointed agents and identify which acts require their joint action.

Core Idea
Two agents appointed together must act jointly; the principal trusted their combined judgment, not one alone.

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi agency law: when a principal appoints two agents jointly, each cannot act alone. The rule applies to transactions that require personal judgment (ra'y), such as sale or divorce. Even if the price is predetermined, some discretion remains, so joint action is still required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the appointing condition
The principal says 'I appoint you both'—this creates a joint agency. The wording indicates that the principal relies on both agents together.
If the principal says 'I appoint each of you separately', the rule may differ.
2
Recognize the ruling
Neither agent may act alone in the matter for which they were appointed. Doing so is prohibited (haram) and the act is invalid.
The ruling is based on the principal's satisfaction with both opinions, not one.
3
Determine the scope
The rule applies to acts that require judgment (ra'y), such as sale, khul', lease, etc. Mechanical acts may be exempt.
Acts like delivery of a specific item do not require discretion.
4
Consider the objection regarding fixed price
Even if the principal specifies the price, the agents still need to exercise judgment on terms and conditions. The estimation does not eliminate all need for opinion.
The passage cuts off here; the full argument would elaborate on remaining discretion.
5
Apply the practical consequence
If one agent acts alone, the act is invalid (batil) because it violates the principal's condition. The principal or the other agent may void the transaction.
This protects the principal's intent and ensures collective decision-making.

Joint Agency: The Principle of Collective Action in Transactions Requiring Opinion
States the rule on joint agency and lists exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joint agency, explain the rationale (talil), and apply the exceptions where individual action is permitted.

Core Idea
Joint agents must act together in matters of judgment; only purely expressive acts can be done alone.

When a principal appoints two agents jointly, he has consented to the combined wisdom of both. This lesson explores the boundary between matters requiring collective deliberation (ra'y) and those where individual action suffices because they are mere expression (ta'bir mahd). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly structuring agency contracts and avoiding invalid transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The matn states that when two agents are appointed jointly (e.g., 'I appoint you both'), neither may act alone in the matter for which they are appointed. The rule applies whether the price is specified or not. Recognize that the ruling is not absolute but qualified by what follows.
The literal text says 'ليس لأحدهما أن يتصرف' – it is not permissible for one to act.
2
Understand the talil (legal reasoning)
Al-Hidayah provides the reasoning: the principal consented to the opinions of both, not just one. Even when the consideration is determined, deliberation on increase and selection of buyer remains necessary. This is the proof for the ruling.
The operative phrase: 'لأن الموكل رضي برأيهما، لا برأي أحدهما' – because the principal consented to both their opinions, not to one alone.
3
Distinguish matters of ra'y from ta'bir mahd
Matters requiring opinion (ra'y) include sale, khul', and similar transactions where judgment is needed. Pure expression (ta'bir mahd) includes talaq without compensation, freeing a slave, returning a trust, or paying a debt – these require no deliberation; one agent alone can perform them.
The text says 'هذه الأشياء لا يُحتاج فيها إلى الرأي، بل هو تعبير محض' – these things do not require opinion, but are mere expression.
4
List the exceptions and their rationale
Exceptions: litigation (due to impossibility of joint action in court, though opinion is needed beforehand), and the four purely expressive acts. The text explains that for litigation, meeting together is impossible and would cause conflict; thus individual action is allowed.
The exception for litigation is qualified: opinion is still needed beforehand to evaluate the case.
5
Apply the rule to successive vs. joint appointments
If the agents were appointed successively (one after another), each may act alone because the principal consented to each individually at the time of appointment. This contrasts with joint agency where consent is to both together.
The text qualifies the ruling with 'قيَّدنا بالمعيَّة' – we restricted it by being together.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wakalah) in al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri presents the ruling on joint agency. The passage type is 'ruling with proof' as the commentary in al-Hidayah provides the legal reasoning. The matn states that when a principal appoints two agents jointly (e.g., 'I appoint you both'), neither agent may act alone in transactions requiring opinion, such as sale or khul', even if the price is specified. The reasoning is that the principal consented to both opinions, not one alone. Determination of the consideration does not eliminate the need for joint opinion, as they may still deliberate on increasing the price or selecting a buyer. The passage then lists exceptions: litigation (because joint action is impossible in court, and opinion is needed beforehand), as well as unilateral acts like talaq without compensation, itq without compensation, returning a trust, or paying a debt. These require no deliberation—they are pure expression, so one agent suffices. Additionally, if the agents were appointed successively (not jointly), each may act alone individually. The passage concludes with a lengthy footnote (marked (1)) detailing the thamara (practical outcome) of an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability when an agent sells below market value—a scenario not directly part of the main rule but related to the earlier discussion of price reduction. The footnote contrasts Abu Yusuf (agent recovers excess from principal if sale below value, opposite if above), Zufar (principal recovers from agent the like or value), and Muhammad (no liability as the object perishes). This is a secondary ikhtilaf reinforcing the complexity of agency law.

Key Terms
وكالة (wakālah): A contract by which one person appoints another to act on his behalf in a permissible matter.
رأي (ra’y): Deliberation and opinion, especially in transactions requiring judgment such as sale, khul', and selecting a buyer.
تعبير محض (ta‘bīr maḥḍ): Pure expression or uttering; acts that require no discretion, such as talaq, itq, returning a trust, or paying a debt.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Agent Liability When Selling Below the Stated Price
Footnote detailing intra-Hanafi dispute on agent liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions (Abu Yusuf, Zufar, Muhammad) on an agent's liability when selling below the fixed price and calculate the practical outcome in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf: agent recovers excess from principal if sale below value; Zufar: principal recovers deficiency; Muhammad: no liability.

The general rule that joint agents must act together is settled, but what happens when an agent does act alone and sells below the market price? The Hanafi school has three distinct views, each based on different principles of guarantee (daman). This lesson unpacks that ikhtilaf and its practical thamarah.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
An agent sells an item for a fixed price of 15, but the market value is either 10 (sold below value) or 20 (sold above value). The question is: does the agent owe anything or recover anything?
The scenario is from the footnote: 'إذا كان الثمن خمسة عشر مثلاً، وقيمة المبيع عشرة'
2
Abu Yusuf's position
According to Abu Yusuf, if the price is less than value, the agent recovers the surplus from the principal (because the principal benefited from the agent's sale). If the price is more than value, the principal recovers the surplus from the agent (as guarantee for the excess).
The text: 'فعند أبي يوسف يرجع الوكيل على الموكل بالفضل' – agent recovers surplus from principal when sale is below value.
3
Zufar's position
Zufar holds that the principal recovers from the agent the like (if fungible) or value of the deficiency in all cases. So if sold below value, principal recovers loss from agent.
The text: 'وعند زفر : يرجع الموكل على الوكيل بمثله إن كان مثلياً' – principal recovers from agent the like if fungible.
4
Muhammad's position
Muhammad says no liability either way. The sold item perishes with the sale, so nothing is due. The sale is valid; the principal's recourse is only against the agent for violation of agency, not for the price difference.
The text: 'وعند محمد : لا يتفاوت الحال ... لأنه يسقط بهلاك المبيع' – the case does not differ; it falls with destruction of the sold item.
5
Calculate thamarah
Take a concrete example: fixed price = 15, market value = 10. Under Abu Yusuf: agent recovers 5 from principal. Under Zufar: principal recovers 5 from agent. Under Muhammad: no money changes hands. Evaluate the practical consequence for each.
The thamarah is the difference in who owes whom.

Termination of Agency When Principal Acts Personally
Ruling on the automatic termination of agency when the principal performs the delegated act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that agency terminates automatically when the principal acts in the delegated matter, and explain the legal rationale.

Core Idea
The principal's personal action in the delegated matter automatically voids the agency.

Agency (wakala) is a contract where one person appoints another to act on their behalf. A common question is whether the agent's authority continues if the principal does the job himself. The Hanafi school clarifies that the agency ends immediately once the principal acts, whether personally or through another agent. This lesson explains the ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the situation where a principal (muwakkil) personally performs the act for which he appointed an agent (wakil). The ruling is that the agency becomes void (batalat al-wikala).
The ruling applies to any delegated act: sale, purchase, divorce, manumission, etc.
2
Determine the cause of termination
The cause is the principal's action itself (either personally or through another agent) in the same matter. The text states: 'then the principal acts in that matter himself, or through another agent'. This triggers the voidance.
It does not require the principal to explicitly revoke the agency; the action itself suffices.
3
Understand the legal effect
The effect is immediate and automatic voidance of the agency contract. The agent no longer has authority to act because the subject matter no longer exists for the agent to act upon.
This is called 'legal dismissal' (عزل حكمي) - the principal's act effectively dismisses the agent, even without verbal revocation.
4
Recognize the scope of the rule
The ruling is unconditional: whether the principal acts personally or appoints another agent, the first agency ends. It applies to all types of delegated acts (buying, selling, divorce, freeing slaves).
The rule is general and does not depend on the agent's knowledge of the principal's action.
5
Apply the rationale
The reasoning given is that after the principal's action, 'the agent is unable to act' because the object of the agency is gone. The agency becomes pointless.
This is a practical application: if the principal has already bought the item, the agent cannot buy it again for him.

Agent Cannot Buy from Close Relatives: The Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on agent's prohibition from transacting with close relatives and the disagreement with the two companions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi disagreement on an agent's sale to relatives and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) and the later tarjih within the school.

Core Idea
An agent's sale to close relatives is invalid under Abu Hanifa due to suspicion, but the two companions allow it at fair price.

In this passage, the author presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scope of an agent's authority when dealing with close relatives. Imam Abu Hanifa takes a strict position, prohibiting such transactions entirely due to the suspicion of partiality (shuhma) and the interconnectedness of interests. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), adopt a more flexible view, allowing the transaction as long as the price is fair, with a clear exception for the agent's own slave and mukatab. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school balances textual commands with rational analysis (istihsan) and how later authorities resolved the conflict.

Guided Path
1
Understand Abu Hanifa's ruling and its rationale
State that according to Abu Hanifa, an agent for sale or purchase cannot contract with his close relatives (father, mother, grandparents, children, grandchildren, spouse, slave, mukatab). The reason is shuhma (suspicion of partiality) and because the benefits are interconnected, making it akin to self-dealing.
Note: The rejection of testimony for these relatives is cited as a parallel indicator.
2
Identify the two companions' opposing view and reasoning
State that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow such transactions at the fair price (bimithl al-qima). Their reasoning: the agency is absolute, and the properties are separate; thus, no conflict of interest arises. They make an exception for the agent's own slave and mukatab because the slave's property belongs to the master, and the mukatab's earnings are partially owned.
Note: The exception shows that where actual ownership overlap exists, even the two companions agree on prohibition.
3
Examine the later tarjih (preponderance)
The author notes that al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi (later Hanafi authorities) preferred the evidence of the two companions. This means the operative ruling (mufta bihi) in many Hanafi circles is the permissibility at fair price, except for slave and mukatab.
Note: The phrase 'رجّحوا دليله' indicates they preferred the Sahibayn's proof, not necessarily Abu Hanifa's.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the contract is invalid (batil) and must be rescinded. Under the Sahibayn, the contract is valid if the price is fair, and the principal (client) cannot object solely because of the relationship.
Note: If the price is unfair, the contract becomes invalid even for the Sahibayn because it would be a sale below value.

This lesson examines an exception to the general rule that a depositee should not deliver a deposit to a claimant without proof. If the claimant asserts that the depositor has died and that he is the sole heir, and the depositee believes him, the depositee is ordered to deliver. The key principle is that upon death, the property no longer belongs to the deceased; it passes to the heirs. Therefore, the claimant is claiming his own property, not another's.

Core Idea
Believing a claimant does not oblige the depositary to hand over; admission in another's property is invalid.

The Hanafi school is precise about the legal effect of admissions (iqrār). Simply believing someone who claims to be an agent does not create a duty to deliver the deposited item. This lesson examines the ruling and its rationale, highlighting the critical distinction between a deposit (a specific asset belonging to another) and a debt (an obligation in the depositary's own liability).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: a person claims to be an agent of the absent owner to receive a deposit, and the depositary believes the claimant.
The depositary is the person holding the deposit (wadīʿah).
2
Identify the ruling
The text states: 'he is not commanded to hand over to him' (لم يُوْمَر بالتسليم إليه). This means it is prohibited (haram) to deliver the deposit to the claimant.
The ruling is categorical: no obligation to hand over.
3
Understand the reason
The depositary's admission (believing the claimant) would effectively transfer ownership of the deposit to the claimant, but the deposit belongs to the original owner. An admission affecting another's property is legally ineffective.
The key principle: iqrār is binding only regarding one's own rights, not another's property.
4
Note the exception for debt
The text contrasts with debt: if the claimant claims to be an agent to collect a debt owed by the depositary, the depositary is commanded to hand over because the debt is his own liability.
Debt is an obligation in the depositary's liability, not a specific asset of another.
5
Apply the principle
In any scenario where an admission concerns property of another, the act of handing over is prohibited regardless of belief.
This protects the rights of the absent owner.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A depositary's confirmation of a sale is not binding because it admits another's property not owned by the confessor.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario from al-Hidayah regarding a deposit (wadi'ah). What happens when someone claims to have purchased the deposited item from its owner, and the depositary (the person holding the deposit) confirms this claim? The ruling is surprising: the depositary is not ordered to hand it over. Let us understand why.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
Who is the claimant, the depositary, and the owner? What does each party assert? The claimant says he bought the deposit from the owner; the depositary confirms this claim.
The owner is not present in the dispute; he is the original owner of the deposit.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that the depositary is not ordered to hand over the deposit to the claimant. The judge does not compel delivery.
The depositary remains under the obligation to return the deposit only to the owner.
3
Analyze the legal reason (ta'lil)
Why is this the ruling? Because the depositary's confirmation is an admission of ownership of another (the claimant) while the owner is alive. Such an admission is not valid against the actual owner.
In Hanafi law, a person's admission (iqrar) is only binding against oneself, not against a third party without that party's confirmation.
4
Apply the principle of iqrar
The depositary's statement is treated as a claim, not a valid admission. The owner has not confirmed the sale, so the deposit remains the owner's property.
If the owner were to confirm the sale, the ruling would change; the depositary would then be ordered to hand over.
5
Note the condition of the owner being alive
The text specifies 'as long as he is alive.' If the owner is deceased, different inheritance rules apply, and the depositary's confirmation might be considered differently.
This lesson focuses on the living owner scenario.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kafala bil-mal is valid even for an unknown debt, as long as the debt itself is valid and cannot be dropped except by payment or discharge.

This lesson examines the foundational ruling on kafala bil-mal (suretyship for a debt). The Hanafi school declares it permissible whether the debt amount is known or unknown, based on the principle of expansion (tawassu'). The key condition is that the debt must be a valid, enforceable debt. We will unpack the ruling, its conditions, and the creditor's options.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
State that kafala bil-mal is the act of standing surety for someone else's debt. The ruling is permissible (jaa'iz), and it becomes binding upon conclusion of the contract.
2
Identify the condition of a valid debt
The debt must be 'dayn sahih' – a valid, enforceable debt that can only be extinguished by payment or discharge. This excludes debts like the substitute for kitabah (manumission contract).
3

Kafala is based on tawassu' (expansion/facilitation), so uncertainty about the exact amount does not invalidate the contract as long as the underlying debt is valid.
4

The creditor may sue either the original debtor (asil) or the surety (kafil) because surety is an additional liability, not a substitution.

In contrast, kafalah is for addition (damm), meaning the surety's liability is added to the principal's. Shariah rulings follow linguistic meanings. Therefore, in hawalah the muhil is discharged, except in cases of tawa as defined. The commentary also clarifies that tawa literally means destruction, and the muhtal's right is only considered lost in these two ways. The footnote adds that 'securing' (tawthiq) is done by choosing the most solvent and best able to pay.

Key Terms
التوى (al-tawa): The loss of the muhtal's right to recover the debt from the muhal alayhi, defined specifically as two causes: denial with oath or death insolvent.
النقل (al-naql): Transfer; the linguistic meaning of hawalah, indicating that the debt moves entirely from one liability to another.
الضم (al-damm): Addition; the linguistic meaning of kafalah, indicating that the surety's liability is added to the original, not substituted.

Key Takeaway
In hawalah, the muhil is discharged; the muhtal can only revert if tawa occurs, which is strictly two causes: denial with oath or death insolvent, because hawalah transfers the debt entirely unlike kafalah.
