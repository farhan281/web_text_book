Ruling on Phrasing of Acknowledgment: Debt vs. Trust
Distinguishing between debt and trust acknowledgment based on wording.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between debt and trust acknowledgments based on specific Arabic phrases and apply the Hanafi rule regarding connected vs. disconnected qualification.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, 'له علي' means debt; 'له عندي' means trust—the wording alone determines liability.

This lesson examines a key rule from Kitab al-Iqrar. In Hanafi fiqh, the exact wording of an acknowledgment determines whether the obligation is a debt (dayn) or a trust (amanah). A debt is guaranteed and must be repaid, while a trust is not guaranteed unless negligence occurs. The text also addresses when a person may later change the classification by adding a qualification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Key Phrases
Note that 'له علي' and 'قبلي' indicate debt, while 'له عندي', 'معي', 'في بيتي', etc. indicate trust. The linguistic difference is crucial.
2
Understand the Linguistic Basis
'علي' implies obligation (sighah ijab), 'قبلي' implies guarantee (daman). 'عندي' and similar phrases imply mere possession without guarantee.
3
Determine the Effect
For debt phrases, the acknowledgment creates a guaranteed liability. For trust phrases, it creates a non-guaranteed custody.
4
Analyze Connected vs. Disconnected Qualification
If the speaker immediately adds 'وديعة' (trust) after a debt phrase, he is believed because the phrase can bear that meaning. If separated, the acknowledgment stands as initially phrased.
The separation is judged by silence or a pause.
5
Apply to Similar Phrases
Phrases like 'في بيتي', 'في كيسي' are treated like 'عندي'—they indicate trust because they denote possession without obligation.

Resolving dispute between debtor and creditor over deferment of a debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a debtor acknowledges a deferred debt but the creditor denies the deferment, and explain the rationale based on the principle 'al-yamin 'ala al-munkir'.

Core Idea
Debtor's claim of deferment is unproven; debt is due immediately and creditor is sworn.

When a debtor admits a debt but claims it is deferred, and the creditor agrees on the debt but denies the deferment, a conflict arises. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: the debt becomes due immediately, and the creditor must take an oath that there was no deferment. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The debtor acknowledges a debt but adds a condition: it is deferred. The creditor admits the debt but rejects the deferment. This creates a dispute over an additional term.
The debtor is the claimant regarding the deferment; the creditor is the denier.
2
Apply the principle 'al-yamin 'ala al-munkir'
Since the creditor denies the deferment, he is the denier of that claim. The burden of proof is on the debtor to prove deferment; if he cannot, the creditor's denial stands, and the debt is due immediately.
The oath is administered to the creditor to confirm his denial.
3
Determine the legal consequence
The debt becomes immediately binding (halan) because the deferment is not proven. The creditor is sworn to swear that the debt was not deferred.
This protects against false claims of deferment and preserves the creditor's right.

Validity condition for exceptions in acknowledgments.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An exception in acknowledging a debt must be immediately connected; otherwise, the full debt stands.

When a person acknowledges a debt but wishes to exclude a portion, Hanafi law demands that the exception be uttered without delay. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its rationale, emphasizing why connection is critical to prevent ambiguity in legal declarations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making an exception (istithna) in an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt. The ruling: the exception is valid (sahih) and only the remainder is due, provided the exception is connected.
2
Identify the condition
The condition is ittisal (connection) – the exception must be stated immediately after the acknowledgment without any interruption. If disconnected, the exception is void.
The text explicitly states 'لا بد من الاتصال' (connection is necessary).
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The exception is considered a redefinition of the acknowledgment: by saying 'except X', the speaker effectively acknowledges only the remainder. But because the exception is a separate utterance, it must be directly linked to avoid confusion.
The Arabic says: 'لأن الاستثناء تكلم بالباقي' – the exception is speaking of the remainder.
4
Note the consequence of disconnection
If the exception is disconnected (e.g., after a pause or unrelated speech), the original acknowledgment stands in full, and the exception is ignored.
This follows from the principle that the iqrar is binding unless contradicted immediately.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi ruling for a debtor who claims the acknowledged currency was defective and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa versus the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An absolute acknowledgment implies soundness; claiming defect is a retraction.

When a debtor acknowledges a debt but then claims the currency was defective, a dispute arises over whether his claim is accepted. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ based on whether the claim is connected to the acknowledgment. This lesson analyzes the legal reasoning and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The debtor acknowledges a debt from the price of goods or a loan, then claims the dirhams were defective (zyuf). The creditor asserts they were good (jiyad).
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
According to Abu Hanifa, the debtor must pay good dirhams; his claim of defect is not accepted because it is a retraction from the implied obligation of soundness.
The ruling is based on qiyas to a sale where the seller claims defect and the buyer denies; the buyer's word is taken.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad distinguish based on connection: if the debtor mentions the defect immediately (mawsul), he is believed and pays defective; if disconnected (mafsul), he is not believed.
They treat a connected statement as part of the original acknowledgment.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
Abu Hanifa's response: even if connected, the claim of defect contradicts the absolute implication of soundness and is therefore a retraction. The default in contracts is freedom from defect.
The analogy to sale is strong because both contracts imply soundness.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
Later authorities (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili) affirmed Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position.
The Sahibayn's view is considered weak; the debtor must pay good dirhams in all cases.

Thus the prior confession is voided directly by the establishment of paternity.

Core Idea

Definition and Order of Preemptors
Defines preemption and lists priority of preemptors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define preemption (al-shuf'ah) and state the Hanafi order of preemptors with the conditions for each level.

Core Idea
Preemption is an obligatory right for partners and neighbors in a fixed order.

This passage from al-Lubab defines the legal right of preemption and establishes a clear priority among claimants. You will learn who can claim preemption and in what sequence, a crucial rule in Hanafi property law.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition of preemption
Read the linguistic and legal definition. Preemption (shuf'ah) means 'joining' linguistically. Legally, it is the right to take ownership of sold real estate compulsorily from the buyer by paying the price he paid. Note that it is described as 'obligatory' (wajib), meaning an enforceable right.
The word 'compulsorily' (جبراً) indicates the right can be enforced even if the buyer objects.
2
Identify the first priority preemptor
The text states: 'الشفعة واجبة للخليط في نفس المبيع' (Preemption is obligatory for the partner in the very sold property). This is the highest priority – the co-owner of the sold property itself.
If this partner exists and demands, no one else can preempt.
3
Identify the second priority preemptor
The text continues: 'ثم للخليط في حق المبيع' (Then for the partner in a right of the sold property). This includes those who share a right attached to the property, such as a water share (شرب) or a private path (طريق).
This right must be appurtenant to the property, not a separate right.
4
Identify the third priority preemptor
Finally: 'ثم للجار الملاصق' (Then for the adjacent neighbor). The neighbor must be immediately adjacent (ملاصق); a neighbor across a public road is not included. Even if his door faces a different alley, he still qualifies.
The condition 'ملاصق' excludes neighbors separated by a public thoroughfare.
5
Note when lower priorities activate
The lower priority only applies if the higher preemptor is absent or waives their right ('إذا لم يكن، أو كان وسلم'). 'سلم' means he accepted the sale and did not demand preemption.
Thus the order is absolute; you cannot skip a level if a higher preemptor exists and asserts their right.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two stages of shuf'a (establishment by sale and settlement by notification) and explain why immediate demand (talab al-muwathaba) is necessary.

Core Idea
Shuf'a is a weak right: it arises from sale but must be immediately demanded and officially notified to be enforceable.

This lesson introduces the fundamental rulings of pre-emption (shuf'a) as expounded in the Hanafi school. You will learn when the right arises, what makes it legally binding, and the critical first step the shafi' must take upon learning of a sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and the right
Recognize that shuf'a becomes established (tajibu) by the contract of sale (bay'). The sale is the cause (sabab) that triggers the right of pre-emption.
2
Understand the condition for enforceability
The right only becomes settled and enforceable (tastaqirr) through official notification (ishhad) with witnesses. Without ishhad, the right remains vulnerable.
Ishhad is a separate step after learning of the sale.
3
Appreciate the nature of the right
Shuf'a is a weak right (haqq da'if) that can be nullified by abandonment (i'rad). Hence immediate demand (talab al-muwathaba) is necessary to show the shafi' is not disinterested.
The weakness stems from it being a right that conflicts with the purchaser's ownership.
4
Trace the transfer of ownership
Ownership only transfers to the shafi' upon voluntary handover by the purchaser or a judge's order. Mere claim is insufficient; actual taking (akhdh) is needed.
5
Summarize the first type of demand
Talab al-muwathaba must be done in the same sitting (majlis) where the news of the sale reaches the shafi'. It is the first of three types of demand, and it establishes his intention.
The text focuses on this first demand; the other two (talb al-taqrir and talb al-khusuma) are mentioned in al-Hidayah but not detailed here.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab explains the rules of demand (talab) in the context of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The two narrations are in al-Nawadir, and the second is taken by al-Karkhi because since the option of ownership is established for him, there must be a time for deliberation, as in the case of the mukhayyarah (woman given choice). End. He said in al-Haqa'iq: The demand is immediate; thus it is narrated from Abu Hanifa, and it is the apparent madhhab, and it is the correct. However, the apparent of the matn and Kafi al-Hakim is that he has the time of the gathering, and therefore he said in al-Idah: It is the most correct, so be aware. Then he rises from it (his place) and makes witnesses upon the seller if the sold property is in his hand, or upon the buyer, or at the property. So if he does that, his pre-emption is confirmed and does not lapse. (Then he rises from it): meaning from his gathering after the immediate demand, (and makes witnesses upon the seller if the sold property is in his hand): meaning it has not been delivered to the buyer, (or) makes witnesses upon the buyer: meaning the purchaser even if he does not have possession because he is the owner, or at the property because the right is attached to it. The condition of witnessing upon the seller is when the property is in his hand because if he does not have possession, witnessing upon him is not valid since he is no longer a party, as he has neither hand nor ownership, thus he becomes like a stranger. The form of this demand is that he says: So-and-so bought this house, and I am its pre-emptor, and I have demanded pre-emption and I now demand it, so bear witness to that. (So if he does that mentioned), his pre-emption is confirmed and does not lapse thereafter.

Commentary
This lesson from Kitab al-Shuf'ah explains the timing and procedure for demanding pre-emption. The passage begins by stating the core rule: if the pre-emptor (shafi') does not demand immediately, his right is void. It gives an example: if he is told about the sale while reading a book, and the pre-emption mention is in the beginning or middle, but he continues reading to the end without demanding, his right is lost. This is the position of the majority of scholars and is a narration from Imam Muhammad. However, another narration from Muhammad grants him the time of the entire gathering (majlis). The two narrations are recorded in al-Nawadir. Al-Karkhi adopted the second narration, reasoning that since the pre-emptor has an option to take ownership, he needs time to deliberate, analogizing to the case of a woman given the choice in divorce (mukhayyarah) who has the whole gathering to decide. The text then clarifies that according to al-Haqa'iq, the demand must be immediate (fawr), which is reported from Abu Hanifa, apparent in the madhhab, and considered correct. Nevertheless, many texts and Kafi al-Hakim indicate the gathering time, and al-Idah considers this the most correct, so one should be attentive to this difference.

After establishing the immediate demand, the passage describes the next step: after the immediate demand (al-muwathabah), the pre-emptor must rise from his place and make a formal witness. The witness can be made upon the seller if the property is still in his possession (i.e., not yet delivered to the buyer), or upon the buyer, or at the property itself. The condition for witnessing upon the seller is that he still has possession; otherwise, he is not a valid party. The form of the witness is stated: the pre-emptor declares the sale, his right, and that he has demanded and now demands, asking others to bear witness. If he completes this, his pre-emption is firmly established and cannot be lost afterward.

Thus, the lesson covers both the timing (immediacy vs. gathering) and the procedural step of witness for confirming the right.

Key Terms
الإشهاد (al-ishhad): The act of taking witnesses to formally record the pre-emption demand, thereby confirming the right.
