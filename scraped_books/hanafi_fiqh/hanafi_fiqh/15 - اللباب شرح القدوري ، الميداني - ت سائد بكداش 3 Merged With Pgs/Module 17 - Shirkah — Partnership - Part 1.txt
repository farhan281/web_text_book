Definition and Types of Partnership (Shirkah)
Definition and broad classification of partnership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shirkah and distinguish its two main categories: shirkat al-amlak and shirkat al-'uqud.

Core Idea
Partnership in Hanafi law is either by ownership (no contract) or by contract, with distinct legal effects.

In this lesson, we begin the Book of Partnership. The author defines shirkah linguistically and legally, then divides it into two fundamental types. Understanding this classification is essential because the rules governing each type differ significantly—especially regarding agency, liability, and profit-sharing. Let's parse the definition and the two categories carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate the linguistic and legal definitions of shirkah. The legal definition is quoted from al-Qahistani's al-Mudmarat.
The key term is 'ikhtisas' (exclusive right) – it implies a shared entitlement that excludes others.
2
Distinguish the two main types
Note that shirkah is divided into two primary types: shirkat al-amlak (partnership in ownership) and shirkat al-'uqud (contractual partnership).
This distinction is foundational in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the causes of shirkat al-amlak
List the causes mentioned: inheritance, purchase, gift, appropriation (istila'), accidental mixing, or deliberate mixing that makes separation impossible.
These causes can be compulsory (jabri) like inheritance, or voluntary (ikhtiyari) like purchase.
4
Understand the scope of 'mixing'
The text specifies that mixing either happens without action or by deliberate mixing that prevents complete separation. The key is that the property becomes indistinguishable.
This has implications for proof of ownership and disposal rights.
5
Contrast with shirkat al-'uqud
Although not detailed here, recognize that the other type is based on a contract (aqd) and will be covered later.
For now, remember that shirkat al-‘uqud requires mutual agreement and creates agency.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab begins the Book of Partnership (Kitab al-Shirkah) by providing a definition of shirkah. Linguistically, shirkah means mixing (al-khultah). Legally, it is defined as the joint entitlement of two or more persons to a single property or entity. This definition is quoted from al-Qahistani's work al-Mudmarat. The author then divides partnership into two major types: 1) Shirkat al-Amwal (partnership in ownership) – which is joint ownership of property, arising from inheritance, purchase, or any other means, whether compulsory (like inheritance) or voluntary (like buying together, accepting a gift together, or acquiring property by force or appropriation). It also includes cases where their property mixes unintentionally or by deliberate mixing that makes separation impossible except with hardship. 2) Shirkat al-'Uqud (contractual partnership) – which will be detailed later. This foundational distinction is crucial in Hanafi fiqh because the rules governing each type differ significantly, particularly regarding agency, liability, and profit-sharing.

Key Terms
الشركة (al-shirkah): Linguistically: mixing; legally: the joint entitlement of two or more persons to a single property or entity.
شركة الأملاك (shirkat al-amlak): Partnership in ownership: joint ownership of property without any contract, arising from inheritance, purchase, gift, appropriation, or accidental mixing.
شركة العقود (shirkat al-'uqud): Contractual partnership: a partnership formed by mutual agreement to engage in business or trade, with sharing of capital and profits.

Disposition in Ownership Partnership Requires Permission
States the ruling for ownership partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for disposition in shirkat al-amlak and identify when permission is required.

Core Idea
In ownership partnership, each partner is a stranger to the other's share — no transaction without permission.

This lesson opens the Book of Partnership with a foundational rule: in a partnership that arises from shared ownership (shirkat al-amlak), partners have no inherent authority over each other's shares. Unlike contractual partnerships, no agency exists until permission is granted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the partnership type
Recognize that this ruling applies to shirkat al-amlak (ownership partnership), not shirkat al-aqd (contractual partnership).
Shirkat al-amlak arises from inheritance, purchase, etc., not from a mutual contract.
2
State the default ruling
The default is that a partner may not dispose of the other partner's share. The text says: 'فلا يجوز لأحدهما أن يتصرف في نصيب الآخر' (it is not permissible for either to dispose of the other's share).
Dispose includes selling, leasing, gifting, etc.
3
Identify the exception
The exception is explicit permission (إذن) from the other partner. The text continues: 'إلا بإذنه' (except with his permission).
Permission can be explicit or implicit, but must be clearly established.
4
Understand the analogy
Each partner is like a stranger (أجنبي) regarding the other's share. This means they have no special rights and must follow the same rules as any non-owner.
This reinforces the need for permission.

This is because this partnership does not imply any agency (wakalah) between them.

The Four Types of Contractual Partnership and Definition of Mufawadah
Introduces the four types of contractual partnership and defines the first

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four types of contractual partnership in Hanafi fiqh and state the three conditions of equality required for a valid mufawadah partnership.

Core Idea
Mufawadah requires full equality in capital, actions, and debts — a general partnership with mutual delegation.

This passage from al-Lubab introduces the second type of partnership, shirkat al-'uqud (contractual partnership), listing its four subtypes. The first subtype, mufawadah, is defined in detail, emphasizing the conditions of equality. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for distinguishing partnerships in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types
From the passage, list the four types of shirkat al-'uqud: mufawadah, 'inan, shirkat al-sana'i' (crafts), and shirkat al-wujuh (credit). Notice that the passage only defines mufawadah in detail.
Memorize these four names; they are the standard Hanafi classification.
2
Extract the definition of mufawadah
The passage defines mufawadah as a partnership where two men are equal in three matters: their capital (مال), their actions (تصرف), and their debts (دين). Write down these three conditions.
Equality in capital means equal contribution; in actions means equal authority to transact; in debts means equal liability.
3
Understand the reason for equality
The passage states that mufawadah is a general partnership in all trades (شركة عامة في جميع التجارات) and each fully delegates authority to the other. Because of this unlimited delegation, equality is necessary to prevent injustice.
The term 'mufawadah' is derived from 'tafwid' (delegation) and also from 'musawah' (equality).
4
Note the poetic quote
The passage ends with a poetic line: 'لا يصلح الناس فوضى لا سراة لهم' (People are not good when they are equal without leaders). This reinforces that mufawadah requires equality but also leadership (delegation) to function.
The quote is used to illustrate that absolute equality (fawda) without structure is problematic; in mufawadah, the partners delegate to each other, creating order.

شركة الوجوه (shirkat al-wujuh): Partnership based on creditworthiness, where partners buy on credit and sell, sharing profits.

Definition and Conditions of شركة المفاوضة
Defines شركة المفاوضة and states its conditions and valid parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of شركة المفاوضة, list its conditions of equality and party qualifications, and identify which parties are eligible for this contract.

Core Idea
In شركة المفاوضة, partners must be equal in capital, management, and debt — any inequality invalidates the contract.

This lesson explores the Hanafi definition of شركة المفاوضة (partnership of equal agency) and its strict conditions. Unlike other partnership forms that allow disparity, this type requires complete equality between partners in three key areas: capital, management, and debt. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly classifying partnerships and ensuring valid contracts according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is forming a شركة المفاوضة. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) only when all conditions are met; otherwise it is invalid (ghayr ja'iz).
2
List the conditions of equality
Equality is required in three areas: capital (mal), management (tasarruf), and debt (dayn). This equality must be present from the beginning to the end of the partnership.
Only property in which partnership is valid is considered for capital equality.
3
Identify the valid parties
The partners must be free (not slaves, even mukatab or ma'dhun), Muslim or dhimmi, adult, and sane. Both must possess equal legal capacity.
4
Recognize factors that invalidate
Slavery, minority, or any inequality in the three areas at any point renders the partnership invalid.

Conditions for Partnership: The Equality Requirement
States conditions for partnership validity with equality requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions of equality required for a valid Hanafi partnership and identify which categories of persons are excluded.

Core Idea
Partnership requires full equality in property, disposition, debt, and suretyship; any disparity invalidates it.

In this passage, al-Maydani outlines the foundational conditions for a valid partnership (shirkah) in Hanafi law. The key requirement is musawah (equality) in four specific capacities. Understanding these conditions is essential for determining when a partnership contract is valid or invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general condition
The text begins: 'It is permissible between two free, Muslim, adult, sane persons.' Note the four attributes: free, Muslim, adult, sane. These are the baseline requirements for each partner.
These are not the only conditions; equality between partners is also required.
2
List the four areas of equality (musawah)
The author specifies equality in: (1) property type, (2) disposition capacity, (3) debt capacity, (4) suretyship capacity. Why? Because partnership is a contract of mutual agency and guarantee.
Equality must exist both at the start and throughout the partnership.
3
Examine invalid pairings
Partnership is invalid between free & slave, adult & minor, Muslim & kafir (per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). The common thread: inequality in one or more of the four areas.
Even a mukatab or ma'dhun slave lacks full equality.
4
Understand the rationale for each
A slave lacks independent disposition and suretyship. A minor lacks suretyship entirely. A dhimmi possesses capacities (e.g., in slaughter) that a Muslim does not, causing inequality in disposition.
The dispute on Muslim-kafir is detailed in the next lesson.

Partnership requires currency capital because profit is tied to liability for what is guaranteed in the dhimma.

Partnership is a contract based on joint investment and profit-sharing. The Hanafi Legal Trick for Partnership in Goods
Explains the procedure to form a valid partnership in goods via a sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi legal trick (hilah) to form a valid partnership in goods and distinguish the two stages of co-ownership (sharīkay milkin vs. sharīkay 'aqd).

Core Idea
A partnership in goods is made valid by first converting the goods into co-owned cash through a reciprocal sale.

The Hanafi school permits a clever legal stratagem to overcome the prohibition of direct partnership in goods. This lesson unpacks the two-step process: a mutual sale creates joint ownership, and then a contract-based partnership is formed. Understanding this hilah reveals how the school balances textual limits with practical needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Why can't partnership in goods (shirkat al-'urud) be formed directly? Because each good is specific ('ayn), not fungible (mithli) like cash. The capital must be known and divisible, which is difficult with specific goods.
The Hanafi school requires the capital in a partnership to be fungible or at least known and capable of division.
2
Understand the Two-Step Hilah
The solution is a reciprocal sale: each partner sells half of his goods to the other at a price equal to half of the other's goods. This creates joint ownership (sharīkay milkin) of the total goods, but each owns a specific share.
The sale must be valid and the price must be determined, but since both parties are exchanging similar goods, it is allowed.
3
Distinguish the Two Types of Co-ownership
After the sale, the partners become co-owners by property (sharīkay milkin), meaning they each own an undivided half of the entire stock. This does not grant mutual agency.
Sharkay milkin does not allow one partner to transact in the other's share without permission.
4
Form the Contract Partnership
Then, they contract a partnership (shirkat 'aqd). At this point, the capital for the partnership is the cash from the sale (i.e., the price received). Since cash is fungible, the partnership becomes valid.
The second step converts them into partners by contract (sharīkay 'aqd), which includes mutual agency.
5

Partnership in Hanafi fiqh is built upon the contributed capitals. When those capitals are lost, the partnership may dissolve. This lesson examines two key scenarios: when capital is destroyed before any purchase is made, and when one partner buys before the loss occurs. Understanding these rules prevents disputes over ownership when misfortune strikes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct situations: (1) capital destroyed before any purchase, and (2) one partner purchases before the other's capital is destroyed.
These scenarios hinge on the timing of the purchase relative to the loss.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1, the partnership becomes void entirely (if all capital is lost) or partially (if only one partner's capital is lost). For scenario 2, the purchased goods remain joint property according to their profit-sharing agreement.
The reason for voidance is that the partnership is 'specified' (ta'ayyun) by the capitals.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The partnership is defined by the two capitals; their loss removes the subject matter. The owner of the surviving capital did not consent to share profit from his wealth alone.
This is based on the nature of 'inan partnership (shirkat al-'inan).
4
Recognize the exception when a purchase occurs before loss
If a purchase is made while the partnership is still valid, ownership becomes joint at that moment. Subsequent loss of another partner's capital does not retroactively affect that ownership.
Vested rights are protected once the transaction is completed.
5
Note the contractual nature per Imam Muhammad
Imam Muhammad emphasizes that the partnership is a 'contract of partnership' (shirkat 'aqd), meaning each partner has full authority to transact (e.g., sell) partnership property.
This reinforces that the partnership is a legal entity for transactions.

Core Idea
Fixing a partner's profit invalidates the partnership; profit must be a proportion of actual profit, not a fixed sum.

In a partnership contract, profits must be shared according to an agreed proportion, not a fixed amount. This lesson examines what happens when partners stipulate a fixed sum for one partner, a condition that undermines the very nature of partnership. The Hanafi school addresses this with a clear ruling and an interesting intra-Hanafi dispute about the legal nature of such an arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited condition
The partners stipulate a fixed sum of dirhams (e.g., 100 dirhams) for one partner from the profit, regardless of the actual profit earned.
This is known as 'ربح مسمى' (named profit) and is invalid.
2
Understand why it invalidates the partnership
Such a condition severs the partnership (انقطاع الشركة) because it guarantees one partner a fixed return, potentially leaving nothing or less for the other partner, thus defeating the purpose of shared risk and reward.
The proof (dalil) is that the condition causes the partnership to be disconnected from actual profit.
3
Compare the two Hanafi views on the nature of such an arrangement
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds it as a contract-based partnership (shirkat 'aqd) that is invalid due to the condition. Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad considers it a partnership of ownership (shirkat amlak).
The relied-upon (mu'tamad) position is that of Muhammad.
4

In Muhammad's view, a partner may sell the entire partnership property (including the other's share) because of agency (wakalah). In Hasan's view, each partner cannot dispose of the other's share without permission.
This difference arises from classifying the arrangement as a contract or co-ownership.
5
Apply the ruling when the partnership is invalid due to such a condition
The condition is void, and profit is distributed according to capital ownership. For example, if capital is half and half but they stipulated profit in thirds, the profit is divided equally.
Profit follows ownership of capital (الربح بقدر الملك).

The Hanafi Ruling on Credit Partnership (Shirkat al-Wujūh)
Explaining the validity and profit rules of credit partnership

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Credit partnership (shirkat al-wujūh) is a unique form of partnership where partners contribute no capital but leverage their reputations to buy on credit. The Hanafi school validates this form, but strictly ties profit distribution to ownership share. Why? Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partnership ends when a partner dies or apostatizes with joining dar al-harb because agency, its foundation, is voided by death.

This lesson examines a fundamental rule of Hanafi partnership law: the automatic voiding of the contract upon the death of a partner or upon apostasy combined with joining the abode of war (dar al-harb). The ruling is not arbitrary; it derives from the nature of partnership itself, which necessarily includes mutual agency (wakalah). Since agency is universally voided by death, any event equivalent to death—such as an apostate joining enemy territory—triggers the same consequence. Understanding this principle helps you grasp the logical structure behind many Hanafi contract rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering events
The text specifies two events that void a partnership: (1) the death of one partner, and (2) apostasy combined with joining dar al-harb. The latter is treated analogously to death.

2
State the legal consequence
The consequence is that the partnership becomes void (batil). This means the contract ceases to exist from that moment; any further transactions are not valid under the partnership.
Void (batil) is stronger than fasid; it is as if the contract never existed.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The reason given is that partnership includes agency (wakalah). Each partner acts as an agent for the other, and agency is a necessary component for the partnership to function.
Agency is a distinct contract in Hanafi fiqh that terminates upon death.
4
Apply the principle to the events
Since agency is voided by death, and apostasy with joining dar al-harb is legally analogous to death (the person is cut off from the Muslim community), the partnership is voided in both cases.
The analogy (qiyas) is explicit: 'because it is analogous to death' (لأنه بمنزلة الموت).
5
Recognize the practical outcome
The surviving partner cannot continue operating under the partnership. The assets must be liquidated and shares distributed to the heirs of the deceased or the estate of the apostate.
If the partners wish to continue, they must form a new partnership contract.

Return of Profit When Mudarabah Capital Is Lost
Ruling on reversing profit after capital loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on reversing profit distribution in mudarabah when capital is lost, and explain why pre-capital profit distribution is invalid.

Core Idea
Profit divided before capital recovery is invalid and must be returned to restore capital.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a critical scenario in mudarabah: what happens if profit has already been distributed but the capital is later lost? The ruling is clear: the profit must be returned to the capital owner until the capital is fully restored. This lesson unpacks the rationale—that profit cannot be lawfully taken until the capital is secure—and explores the consequences for both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The mudarabah contract is still active, the parties have already divided the profit, and then all or part of the capital is lost.
The contract remains valid; the key is that loss occurs after profit distribution.
2
State the ruling
The parties must undo the profit division: the mudarib returns the profit to the rabb al-mal until the original capital is fully recovered.
This applies whether the loss is total or partial.
3
Explain the legal reasoning
Profit division before capital recovery is invalid because the capital is the principal. Any 'profit' taken before capital is secure is actually part of the capital. When capital is lost, it becomes clear that what was distributed was from capital, so it must be returned.
The capital is the root (asl); profit is the branch (far'). The branch cannot be separated before the root is intact.
4
Determine the outcomes
After returning the profit to restore capital, if anything remains, it is genuine profit to be shared as per the contract. If the returned profit is insufficient to cover the loss, the mudarib is not liable for the shortfall.
The mudarib is a trustee (amin) and does not guarantee capital against losses beyond his control.
5
Extract the underlying principle
The ruling rests on two principles: (1) profit cannot be lawfully taken until capital is secure; (2) the mudarib is a trustee, not a guarantor.
These principles govern profit distribution in mudarabah.

Profit Retained After Contract Rescission and Renewal
Ruling on profit retention after contract termination and renewal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether previously distributed profit must be returned after contract rescission and renewal, and explain the legal basis for the ruling.

Core Idea
A rescinded contract is independent of a new contract; each stands on its own.

You have learned in previous lessons that if capital is lost before profit distribution, profit is not owed. But what if profit was already distributed and the contract was ended and then restarted? This passage clarifies that the first profit is untouchable because the first contract is closed. The second contract bears its own losses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sequence of events
List each event in order: profit division, rescission of the first contract, formation of a new contract, loss of capital under the new contract.
The order is critical to the ruling.
2
Determine which contract the loss relates to
The loss occurs after the new contract begins. Therefore, it attaches to the second contract, not the first.
The first contract had already ended by rescission.
3
State the ruling and the reason
The ruling is that the first profit is not returned to offset the loss. The reason: the first contract was terminated by rescission; the second is a new contract with no legal connection to the first.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle of contract independence.
