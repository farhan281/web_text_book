Definition and Ruling of the Foundling (Laqīṭ)
Defines laqīṭ linguistically and legally, states its status and maintenance source.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the foundling linguistically and legally, state its ruling (free, Muslim), and identify the source of its maintenance.

Core Idea
The foundling is free and Muslim by default; the state treasury must support him.

This lesson introduces a new chapter on the foundling (laqīṭ). We begin with the core ruling: the foundling is free and Muslim, and his maintenance is an obligation upon the public treasury. This concise statement carries important legal implications about the status of abandoned infants in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Recall the linguistic meaning
Identify the root and derived meaning of 'laqīṭ': something picked up from the ground.
The word is a passive participle (فعيل بمعنى مفعول).
2
State the legal definition
Define the foundling as an infant abandoned by his family due to poverty or fear of accusation.
The definition is precise: a newborn thrown out, not an older child.
3
Identify the ruling on freedom and religion
The foundling is presumed free and Muslim, following the locale where found.
If found in a Muslim area, he is Muslim; if in non-Muslim area, non-Muslim.
4
Determine the source of maintenance
Maintenance is wajib upon the public treasury (bayt al-māl).
Reason: the child is Muslim, unable to earn, has no wealth or relatives; also, his inheritance goes to the treasury.
5
Connect to the principle of al-kharāj bi al-ḍamān
Understand the rationale: profit is paired with liability; since the treasury inherits from him, it must support him.
This is a legal maxim that justifies the ruling.

The Finder's Right and Claim of Lineage for Foundlings
States the finder's priority and the claimant's word with oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi rulings on foundling custody and lineage claims, apply them to scenarios, and explain the reasoning based on prior possession and istihsan.

Core Idea
The finder's prior possession gives exclusive custody; lineage claims require oath and are accepted unless the finder claims first.

In this passage from the Book of Foundling (Kitab al-Luqit), the Hanafi school establishes two key principles. First, the finder's act of taking a foundling grants him a right of custody that no one else can override. Second, if someone later claims the child as his son, his word is accepted with an oath, by way of istihsan, because establishing lineage benefits the child. However, the finder has priority if he himself claims the child's lineage, even if he is a non-Muslim and the outsider is Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal outcomes
Read the passage and extract the two distinct rulings: (1) no one may take the foundling from the finder, (2) a claimant's word with oath is accepted.
2
Trace the legal basis for each ruling
For the first ruling, note the basis: 'li thubut haqq al-hifz lahu bi sabq yadihi' (due to establishment of right of custody by prior possession). For the second, note 'istihsanan' because the claim benefits the child by granting lineage, and lineage is honorable.
Istihsan overrides strict analogy (qiyas) because of the public interest.
3
Apply the exception when the finder also claims
If the finder himself claims the child's lineage before the outsider, he is more entitled ('awla'), even if he is a dhimmi and the outsider is Muslim. This shows the priority of the finder's prior possession.
The finder's prior possession and established care give him a stronger claim.

Foundling Disputes: Preference by Mark and Evidence
Ruling statement on foundling disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rulings on foundling claims when two claimants dispute, including preference by a mark, shared lineage, and the effect of prior claim and proof.

Core Idea
A mark described by one claimant gives priority; without a mark, they share lineage; prior claim holds unless proof rebuts.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a common judicial scenario: two individuals each claim a foundling as their son. The jurists provide clear rules based on evidence—a physical mark, prior claim, or formal proof (bayyinah). Understanding these rules is essential for applying the principles of evidence (zahir) and strength of proof in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two individuals claim a foundling. Determine if one describes a distinctive mark, if a claim preceded, or if proof (bayyinah) is brought.
The text presents three sub-scenarios: (1) one describes a mark, (2) neither describes a mark, (3) one claimant precedes, then proof appears.
2
Apply the rule for a mark
If one claimant describes a mark that matches the child, he is given priority because the mark is apparent evidence (zahir) supporting his claim.
The mark authenticates the claim more than a bare assertion.
3
Apply the rule when no mark
If neither describes a mark, the child is considered the son of both—shared lineage due to equal claim strength.
This defaults to partnership when evidence is balanced.
4
Apply the rules for prior claim and proof
If one claimant made his claim first without opposition, the child is his. However, if the other later brings clear proof (bayyinah), that proof overrides the prior claim because proof is stronger.
Bayyinah (usually two witnesses) is the strongest form of evidence in Hanafi law.

The Foundling Claim: Abode Presumption and Istihsan
States the ruling when a dhimmi claims a foundling found in Muslim or dhimmi areas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a dhimmi claims a foundling in Muslim land and in dhimmi land, and explain the istihsan reasoning that balances benefit and harm.

Core Idea
A foundling's Islam follows the abode; a dhimmi's claim can only benefit the child, not harm him.

This lesson examines a specific fiqh ruling about a foundling (laqeet) found in Muslim territory when a dhimmi claims the child. The Hanafi school holds that the child remains Muslim despite the claim because the abode confers Islam. However, the dhimmi's claim is accepted for establishing lineage, which benefits the child. This ruling is based on istihsan, a rational preference for the child's welfare. The companion scenario—foundling in dhimmi territory—is introduced but not fully stated; we infer the ruling by the same principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage addresses two locations: foundling in Muslim territory (cities or villages of Muslims) and foundling in dhimmi territory (villages of dhimmis or a church). The first scenario is ruled completely; the second is introduced but the ruling is implied.
The ruling for dhimmi territory can be inferred from the first scenario and the general principle.
2
Apply the Abode Principle
In Hanafi fiqh, the child's religion is determined by the abode (dar). Found in dar al-Islam → Muslim; found in dar al-dhimma → dhimmi. This is a strong presumption not lightly overridden.
The text explicitly says 'taba'an lil-dar' – following the abode.
3
Analyze the Dhimmi Claim
The claim has two effects: establishing lineage (benefit) and negating the child's Islam (harm). Istihsan (juristic preference) dictates accepting the beneficial part while rejecting the harmful part.
The reasoning is: the claim endorses benefit and harm; shari'ah prefers benefit over harm.
4
Infer the Missing Ruling
Applying the same reasoning to dhimmi territory: the child's default status is dhimmi. If a dhimmi claims the child, both lineage and religion align, so the claim is fully accepted—no conflict requiring istihsan.
If the claimant were a Muslim in dhimmi land, istihsan would again apply: lineage established and Islam affirmed, both benefits, so claim accepted fully.

Ruling that a claim of ownership over a foundling is not accepted without proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on claiming ownership of a foundling and explain why evidence is required.

Core Idea
A foundling is presumed free; a claim of ownership must be proven by evidence.

In this lesson, we examine a brief but important ruling from the Hanafi school regarding foundlings (laqīṭ). The text states that if someone claims a foundling is his slave, the claim is not accepted without legal evidence (bayyinah). This ruling protects the child's freedom and upholds the default presumption of human liberty. Let's break down the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The claimant asserts that the foundling is his slave (male or female). The ruling applies to both.
The text explicitly covers both 'abd (slave) and amah (slave woman).

Identify the condition
The text says 'except with evidence (bayyinah)'. So legal proof is necessary for the claim to be accepted.
Bayyinah in Hanafi law typically means two male witnesses or one male and two females.
3
Determine the default status
Without evidence, the foundling remains free (hurr). The default is freedom.
This applies even if the claimant is the person who found and raised the child.
4
Understand the rationale
Because the foundling's parentage is unknown, freedom is the original state of humans (al-ādamah).
The text says 'because he is apparently free'.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a slave's claim of paternity over a foundling establishes lineage and frees the child, and explain the reasoning based on benefit and preference.

Core Idea
A slave's claim of fatherhood over a foundling is accepted if it benefits the child, preserving the child's freedom.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario: a slave claims that a foundling (a child of unknown parentage) is his son. Normally, a slave cannot own property or have full legal capacity, but here the claim is accepted. Why? Because it benefits the child by establishing his lineage and confirming his freedom. The Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to prioritize the child's welfare over formal obstacles.

Understand the benefit rationale
The text says 'because it benefits him' (لأنه ينفعه). The benefit is stating the lineage and preserving freedom. The claim is accepted because it is advantageous to the child.
Benefit to the child is the key criterion.

Even though a child born to a slave mother would be a slave, the foundling is presumed free because he was found in a state of apparent freedom. Thus, the child's freedom remains intact.
The principle: certainty (apparent freedom) is not overridden by doubt.
4
Understand the preference hierarchy
If multiple claimants come forward, preference is given to the one who provides more benefit: a free claimant over a slave, and a Muslim over a dhimmi. This ensures the child's welfare is maximized.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A perishable lost item needs only brief announcement; the finder may sell or use it to prevent loss.

In this lesson, we examine an exception to the general rule that a lost item must be announced for a full year. When the item is perishable (like fruit or milk), Islamic law recognizes the risk of spoilage and provides a practical solution that balances the owner's rights with the need to avoid waste.

2
State the modified duty
The finder must announce the item, but only until the point when he fears it will spoil. After that, he may act to preserve value.
General announcement is one year; this exception shortens that period.

The ruling prevents total loss of the item's value. The finder acts as a trustee who either sells the item (keeping the price for the owner) or uses it himself, intending to compensate if the owner appears.
This is based on the principle of 'necessity' (ḍarūrah) and the concept of preserving wealth (ḥifẓ al-māl).
4

After permissible use or sale, the finder is still accountable: if the owner appears later, the finder must return the item (if still in hand) or its value/price.
The finder becomes a borrower (ʿāriyya) or agent, not an owner.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri deals with the rulings of spending on found property (luqatah) and the judge's role. The passage is divided into two main sections: first, the ruling of the finder's spending without the judge's permission versus with his permission; second, how the judge handles an animal that is brought to him. Regarding the first: if the finder spends on the lost item without the judge's permission, he is a volunteer (mutabarri‘) and cannot claim reimbursement. This is because his authority (wilayah) over the item is deficient—he is only entrusted to preserve it, not to incur expenses on behalf of the owner. If, however, the judge authorizes the spending, the expense becomes a debt (dayn) on the owner, recoverable by the finder. The reason is that the judge has authority (wilayah) over the property of an absent person, acting in his interest. The text then addresses an objection regarding the hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade taking stray camels. The response is that the prohibition applies when there is no fear for the animal's safety. If there is fear (e.g., predation or harm), taking it for preservation is not only permissible but superior. The indication is in the Prophet's words 'until its owner finds it,' which implies that the owner is expected to seek it, but if the animal is in danger, the finder should intervene. The second part explains the judge's options when the finder brings a found animal. The judge examines the situation: (1) If the animal can be used for benefit (e.g., milking, riding), he hires it out and uses the hire to cover its expenses. This preserves the animal intact without creating a debt for the owner. (2) If the animal has no such benefit and the judge fears that feeding it will consume its entire value, he sells it and keeps the price safe for the owner. This is to preserve the 'meaning' (value) when preserving the 'form' (actual animal) is impossible. (3) If the most beneficial course is to spend on it (e.g., because it will be returned soon or is valuable), the judge authorizes spending, making it a debt on the owner. The commentary notes that according to Al-Hidayah, the finder only recovers from the owner if the judge explicitly stipulates recourse; otherwise, the expense is considered a donation. The same rulings apply to a runaway slave (abq).

When someone claims a found item, the finder must decide whether to return it. The Hanafi text clarifies that a mere claim is insufficient—evidence is required for compulsion. However, if the claimant provides an accurate description, the finder may voluntarily return it. This distinction balances protecting the true owner and avoiding wrongful possession.

Finder's Use of Found Item and Charity to Relatives
Stating rules on who may use the found item and to whom charity may be given.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a rich finder versus a poor finder regarding using the found item, and identify to whom charity may be given.

Core Idea
A poor finder may use the found item; a rich finder may not, but may give it to his poor relatives.

The finder of a lost item (luqatah) is commanded to announce it for one year. If the owner does not appear, the finder must give it in charity on behalf of the owner. This lesson examines the subsequent question: may the finder himself use the item or give it to his relatives? The Hanafi school distinguishes between a rich and poor finder and between rich and poor recipients.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
The passage covers four distinct situations: (1) giving charity from the found item to a rich person, (2) a rich finder using it for himself, (3) a poor finder using it for himself, and (4) a rich finder giving it to his poor relatives. List them clearly.
2
Determine the ruling for each
For each scenario, state whether it is permissible (mubah) or not permissible (haram). The text uses 'la bā's' (no harm) for permissible and 'lam yajuz' (not permissible) for forbidden.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The found item becomes like charity from the owner. The finder acts as an agent. Charity can only be given to those deserving (poor). A rich person is not deserving; a poor person is. Hence, a rich finder is not deserving, but a poor finder is.
4
Analyze the exception for relatives
Even a rich finder may give the item to his father, son, mother, or wife if they are poor. Why? Because they are deserving of charity, and there is consideration (nazar li al-jānibayn) – the relatives benefit and the owner may also be rewarded.
