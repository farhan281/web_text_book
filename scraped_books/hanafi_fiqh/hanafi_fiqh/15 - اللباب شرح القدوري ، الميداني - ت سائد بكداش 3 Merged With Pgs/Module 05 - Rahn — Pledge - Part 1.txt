Definition and Binding of Rahn (Pledge)
Defining rahn and stating its conclusion and completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of rahn, explain that possession is a condition of binding (luzum) rather than validity, and identify the required form of possession for movable property according to the relied-upon view.

Core Idea
Rahn is valid by offer and acceptance, but binding only upon possession — like a gift in Hanafi law.

We now begin the Book of Rahn (Pledge). This lesson lays down the essential definition and structure of the pledge contract. Understanding that possession is a condition of binding (luzum) and not validity (jawaz) is crucial, as it affects the rights of the parties before delivery. The passage also clarifies an intra-Hanafi dispute on how possession is achieved for movable property.

The definition sets the foundation for understanding its role as security.

This is a key point: the contract exists but is revocable before possession.

This affects how possession is legally established in practice.
4
Note the condition that the pledged item must be 'collected' (mahwazan)
The pledged item must be in a gathered state, not scattered like fruit on trees. This condition prevents uncertainty about the quantity.
The text cuts off; this condition is part of the requirements for valid possession.

Definition and Binding Nature of the Pledge Contract (Rahn)
Opening of Kitab al-Rahn with definition, formation, and conditions of the pledge contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define rahn in Hanafi fiqh, explain that it becomes binding only upon possession, and identify the three conditions for valid possession.

Core Idea
A pledge is incomplete until the pledgee takes possession — possession makes it binding.

This lesson begins the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn) from al-Lubab. The pledge is a common security transaction in Islamic law, usually following a sale or loan. In the Hanafi school, the contract is not fully effective simply by agreement; possession by the pledgee is essential for it to become binding. We will explore the legal definition, the role of possession, and the specific conditions that must be met for valid possession.

Guided Path
1

The term rahn means detention. Legally, it is detaining property for a right that can be satisfied from it.
2

The contract is formed by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul), like other contracts.
3

The contract is not binding until the pledgee takes possession; possession is a condition of bindingness (luzum), not validity (jawaz).
This differs from the view in al-Mujtaba that possession is a condition of validity.
4

In the apparent narration, takhliyah (vacating) suffices for immovables; Abu Yusuf required actual transfer for movables; the first view is more correct.
Al-Hidayah declares takhliyah sufficient as the correct view.
5

The pledged item must be: (1) mahuzan (gathered/collected), (2) mufarraghan (empty of the pledgor's right), (3) mumayyazan (distinct/not undivided).
These conditions ensure the pledgee has clear control.

Once possession is taken under these conditions, the contract becomes binding and the item enters the pledgee's liability (daman).

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Rahn (Pledge/Mortgage) in al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri. It begins by defining the pledge: a secure transaction where property is held as collateral for a debt. The author first notes the connection to sale, as pledges usually follow sales. Linguistically, rahn means 'detention'; legally, it is the detention of something for a claim that can be satisfied from it. The contract of pledge is formed by offer (ījāb) and acceptance (qabūl), like other contracts. However, it is not fully effective by mere agreement; it is completed and becomes binding (yatimmu wa yalzamu) only by possession (qabḍ). This indicates that possession is a condition of bindingness (sharṭ luzūm), not validity (sharṭ jawāz), similar to the gift contract. This differs from what is reported in al-Mujtabā, where possession is considered a condition of validity. The author then quotes al-Hidāyah, stating that in the apparent narration (ẓāhir al-riwāyah), vacating (takhliyah) suffices for possession for immovables. Abu Yusuf held that for movable property, actual physical transfer is required; the first view (takhliyah sufficing) is considered more correct. The passage then lists conditions for the pledge to be effective upon possession: the pledged item must be: (1) maḥūzan (collected/in hand), i.e., gathered and not scattered like fruit on trees or crops in fields; (2) mufarghan (empty), i.e., not occupied by the pledgor's right such as fruit on the trees or crops on the land—the pledge applies to the land without the produce; and (3) mumayyazan (distinct), i.e., not undivided (ghayr musha‘). These conditions ensure the pledge is specified and under the pledgee's control. Once the pledgee takes possession under these conditions, the contract becomes binding and the pledge enters the pledgee's liability (ḍamān). Before possession, the pledgor retains the option to deliver or revoke the pledge.

Key Terms
الرَّهْنُ (ar-rahn): Linguistically: detention; legally: detention of a property for a right that can be exacted from it.
القَبْض (al-qabd): Possession or taking control; for the pledge contract, it includes both physical taking and takhliyah (vacating) for immovables.
مَحُوزاً (mahūzan): Collected and gathered; the pledged item must be in a state of being together, not scattered.

Pledge Valid Only for Guaranteed Debt and Liability of the Pledge
Explanation of the validity condition and liability rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a pledge is valid only for a guaranteed debt and determine the mortgagee's liability as the lesser of the pledge's value or the debt.

Core Idea
A pledge secures only a guaranteed debt; the mortgagee's liability is the lesser of the debt or the pledge's value.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies two essential rules of Hanafi pledge law. First, a pledge is only valid if it secures a debt that is guaranteed (mazmun). Second, if the pledge is lost, the mortgagee compensates with the lesser amount. Understanding these rules prevents common errors in structuring real estate or chattel pledges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The validity of a pledge (rahn). What is the ruling? It is not valid except for a guaranteed debt.
2
Locate the dalil
The text provides a rational proof: rahn was legislated for securing a debt; securing something non-guaranteed is futile (laghw).
This is a teleological argument (maqsad-based).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the proof lead to the ruling? If the debt is not guaranteed, there is nothing to secure, so the contract becomes pointless. Therefore, a pledge for a non-guaranteed debt is invalid.
The key terms: istithqa' (security) and laghw (futility).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school allows pledging for non-guaranteed debts under certain conditions. The Hanafi school disagrees based on the purpose of rahn.
This is a valid ikhtilaf; both positions have internal logic.
5
Analyse the liability of the pledge
If the pledge is lost in the mortgagee's hands, he is liable for the lesser of the debt or the pledge's value. This is a second ruling derived from the nature of the contract.
The particle 'min' in the text indicates the lesser of the two amounts.

Commentary
This passage explains the fundamental rule that a pledge (rahn) is only valid for a debt that is guaranteed (mazmun) by the debtor. The rationale is that the purpose of rahn is to secure payment; securing a non‑guaranteed item is meaningless. The commentary then addresses the liability of the pledge (daman) after it has been delivered: the mortgagee is liable for the lesser of the pledge's value and the debt amount. If the debt is smaller, the pledge covers only the debt; if the pledge's value is smaller, it covers only that value. The footnote clarifies that 'guaranteed' (mazmun) is an emphasis, as all debts are inherently guaranteed; but it excludes a specific invalid practice: rahn for 'daman al‑darak', which is a guarantee against eviction of a sold item. For example, if someone says to another 'I have not sold to so‑and‑so, but I am responsible for the price if it is evicted,' and takes a pledge for that promise, it is invalid because the debt is not yet owed. This ruling is confirmed in the Hanafi sources al‑Jawhara and al‑Binaya.

Key Terms
دين مضمون (dayn mazmun): A debt that is guaranteed, meaning the debtor is liable to repay it; it exists and is due.
ضمان الرهن (daman al-rahn): The liability of the pledge property in the hands of the mortgagee; he is responsible for its loss or damage and must compensate with the lesser of its value or the debt.
ضمان الدرك (daman al-darak): A guarantee of the purchase price in case the sold item is evicted from the buyer; pledging for this is invalid in Hanafi.

Three Scenarios of Pledge Value Relative to Debt
Ruling on pledge value relative to debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings for pledge value relative to debt and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind each.

Core Idea
A pledge offsets debt to the extent of its value; surplus is trust, deficit remains owed.

A pledge (rahn) is a security for a debt. When the debt becomes due, the value of the pledged property determines how much of the debt is satisfied. This lesson presents three scenarios from the Hanafi school: value equals debt, exceeds debt, or falls short. The rulings follow naturally from the principle that the pledge serves as satisfaction to the extent of its value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the Arabic and translation to find the three cases: 1) value equals debt, 2) value exceeds debt, 3) value is less than debt.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For each case, extract the legal consequence: (1) debt considered paid, (2) surplus is trust, (3) debt reduced and balance claimed.
3
Understand the legal effect (hukman)
When value equals debt, the pledgee is 'considered as having received his debt' — a legal fiction (hukman) of satisfaction, not actual ownership.
4
Grasp the underlying reasoning (ta'lil)
The text provides reasons: offsetting of mutual liabilities (taqasu), trust status for surplus (amanah), and satisfaction to the extent of value for deficiency.

Core Idea
If the pledge perishes during the contract meeting, the pledgee is deemed satisfied; if it perishes after separation, the contract is void.

Pledges secure debts, but what happens when the pledged item is destroyed before the parties leave the contract meeting? This lesson examines the Hanafi position on the destruction of a pledge in sarf (currency exchange) and salam (forward sale) contracts. The ruling hinges on constructive possession during the meeting, with a qiyas proof linking the pledge's monetary homogeneity to satisfaction of the debt.

If the parties separate before the pledge perishes, the contracts become void due to loss of actual possession. This highlights the critical timing of possession.
Separation ends the contract meeting, so constructive possession is no longer effective.

After mutal rescission, the pledge becomes security for the capital. If the pledge perishes in the meeting, the pledgee is satisfied up to its value; excess is trust, deficiency is claimable.
These sub-cases refine the rule when the pledge's value does not equal the debt.

In Hanafi fiqh, the pledge (rahn) is a security contract that often includes an agency to sell the pledged property when the debt matures. Liability of the Pledged Slave: Circularity in Daman
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the pledged slave's injuries and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A pledged slave's injury to the mortgagee is null due to circular liability, except where qisas applies.

This lesson examines the liability of a pledged slave for injuries it causes to the pledgor, mortgagee, or their property. The general rule is that such injuries are null (hadar) for monetary compensation, but there is an important intra-Hanafi disagreement. Understanding the underlying reasoning—especially the concept of circular liability (daman)—is key to grasping the positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is the injury (jinaayah) caused by the pledged slave (rahn). The affected parties are the pledgor (raahin), the mortgagee (murtahin), and their property. Note that the slave is pledged as collateral.
The slave is in the possession (daman) of the mortgagee.
2
Understand the nullity rule (hadar) and its reasoning for the pledgor
The injury to the pledgor is null because the pledgor is the owner of the slave. Drawing an analogy: a slave's injury to his owner that requires monetary compensation is always null since the owner is the one entitled to the slave's earnings. This establishes the baseline rule.
This reasoning is straightforward and not disputed.
3
Analyze the circularity argument for the mortgagee
If the injury to the mortgagee were compensable, the mortgagee would also be liable for an equivalent amount because the slave is in his possession (daman). Imposing liability on the mortgagee would create a circular situation where he pays himself or engages in a useless exchange. Hence the nullity.
This is the core of Abu Hanifa's position.
4
Note the exception for qisas
The nullity rule applies only to monetary claims. Injuries that require qisas (retaliation) are valid by consensus. Qisas is a personal right, not a financial one, and thus not subject to the circularity problem.
Qisas is not considered 'hadar'.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the injury to the mortgagee is valid and becomes a claim against the slave's value (raqabah), separate from the mortgagee's own potential liability. Abu Hanifa rejects this due to circularity. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa, the mortgagee gets nothing; under the Sahibayn, he can claim from the slave's price.

Division of Debt Between Pledge and Its Growth
Explanation of debt division between original pledge and its growth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the division of debt between the original pledge (valued at seizure) and its growth (valued at redemption) using the Hanafi method.

Core Idea
Debt is apportioned proportionally between the original pledge and its growth, valued at different times.

When a pledged item generates growth (like fruit or offspring) between seizure and redemption, the debt is not simply covered by the entire item. Instead, the Hanafi school divides the debt proportionally between the original pledge (valued at seizure) and the growth (valued at redemption). The share of debt corresponding to the original is discharged, and the share corresponding to the growth must be paid by the pledgor to redeem that growth. This lesson walks through the calculation method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debt and components
Note the total debt owed and identify the two parts: the original pledge (as it was at seizure) and the growth (any increase that has appeared).
Growth can be natural, like fruit, or legal, like profit.
2
Determine the relevant values
Find the value of the original pledge at the time of seizure (qabd) and the value of the growth at the time of redemption (fikak). These are the base figures.
The original is valued at seizure because that is when it becomes a security; the growth is valued at redemption because it is only then that it is intended to be redeemed.
3
Calculate each share proportion
Add the two values to get total value at redemption. Then share of original = (original value) / (total value); share of growth = (growth value) / (total value).
These proportions represent the fraction of debt attributed to each part.
4
Apply proportions to the debt
Multiply the debt by the share of the original to find the amount discharged. Multiply the debt by the share of the growth to find the amount the pledgor must pay to redeem the growth.
The sum of discharged and paid equals the total debt.

Even within the Hanafi school, there is a valid disagreement (ikhtilaf).
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view)
The later Hanafi authority Ibn 'Abidin and others have preferred the absolute prohibition, aligning with the principle that any benefit from the pledge beyond security constitutes riba.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) today is that use is not permitted even with permission; if stipulated, it invalidates the contract.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling concerning the pledgee's (murtahin) use of the pledged item. The matn states that such use constitutes ghasb (usurpation), making the pledgee liable for the full value. The commentator explains that the pledgee's right is limited to detention (habs) for the purpose of securing the debt; he has no right to benefit from the pledge. The commentary also references Al-Hidayah and Al-Durr al-Mukhtar, noting differing views on whether the pledgee may use the pledge with the pledgor's permission. The preferred view (as per Ibn 'Abidin) is that it is not permissible even with permission, as it may involve riba. The footnote distinguishes between contractual liability (damān al-'aqd), where compensation is based on the agreed price, and usurpation liability (damān al-ghasb), where compensation is based on equivalence. This ruling is foundational in the Hanafi madhhab, emphasizing that the pledge is a security only, not a source of benefit.
