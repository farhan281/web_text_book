Definition of Tawa and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Recourse
Defines tawa and presents the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the assignee's recourse and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on what constitutes tawa.

Core Idea
The assignee may only revert to the assignor in two specific cases: denial with oath or death insolvent – bankruptcy is not sufficient.

In hawalah, the assignor is normally discharged from liability. But what happens if the assignee cannot collect from the assignee? The passage defines when the assignee can revert to the assignor, and the Hanafi masters disagree on the scope of tawa. Abu Hanifa limits it to two cases, while the Sahibayn add a third: a judge's ruling of bankruptcy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule of non-recourse
The matn states that the assignee does not revert unless tawa occurs. Understand that this is the default rule: the assignor is discharged.
The rule is that hawalah transfers the debt and frees the assignor.
2
Define tawa according to Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa specifically defines tawa as either the assignee's denial with oath and no evidence, or the assignee's death insolvent. Memorize these two.
Both must be proven; denial alone is not enough without an oath.
3
Note the addition by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
The Sahibayn add a third case: a judge's ruling of bankruptcy during the assignee's lifetime. They argue this causes inability to collect.
This is the point of dispute: does judicial bankruptcy constitute tawa?
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: If the assignee is declared bankrupt but does not die, Abu Hanifa does not allow recourse; the Sahibayn do. This affects whether the assignor remains liable.
The difference matters in real cases of insolvency.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later scholars, including al-Nasafi and the author of al-Tashih, preferred Abu Hanifa's view. The commentator confirms that all texts and commentaries correct Abu Hanifa's position.
The operative position for fatwa is Abu Hanifa's narrower definition.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the definition of توى (loss) that allows the assignee (المحتال) in a hawalah contract to revert to the assignor (المحيل) for reimbursement. The matn states the general principle: the assignee cannot revert unless his right is lost (يتوى). The sharh then explains that according to Abu Hanifa, توى is limited to two specific cases: (1) the assignee (المحال عليه) denies the hawalah and takes an oath, and there is no evidence to prove the hawalah, or (2) the assignee dies insolvent. Zufar objects, arguing by analogy to kafalah (guarantee) that the assignor should not be discharged because both hawalah and kafalah are contracts of securing (توثق). The Hanafi response is that hawalah is linguistically a transfer (نقل) so once the debt is transferred, the assignor's liability is cleared; kafalah is an annexation (ضم), so the analogy fails because legal rulings conform to linguistic meanings. The sharh then elaborates on Abu Hanifa's two cases. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) add a third case: a judge's declaration of insolvency during the assignee's lifetime. Abu Hanifa's proof: the debt remains in the liability during life; difficulty of collection does not justify recourse, just as absence does not, while death destroys the liability entirely. The text concludes that the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's, as favored by al-Nasafi and the author of al-Tashih, and the commentator's shaykh confirms that all texts and commentaries correct Abu Hanifa's view.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawalah): Transfer of debt from one person to another; the assignor transfers his debt to the assignee, who becomes liable to the creditor.
المحيل (al-muhil): The assignor, the original debtor who transfers his debt to another.
المحتال (al-muhtal): The creditor who receives the assignment; the assignee (original creditor).
المحال عليه (al-muhal 'alayh): The person on whom the debt is assigned; the one who becomes liable to pay the creditor.
التوى (al-tawa): Loss or destruction of the assignee's right that entitles the assignee to revert to the assignor.

Zufar's Analogy of Hawalah to Kafalah and Its Refutation
Discusses Zufar's analogy to kafalah and the Hanafi refutation based on linguistic meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Zufar's qiyas linking hawalah to kafalah, identify the Hanafi refutation based on linguistic meaning, and evaluate the strength of the analogy.

Core Idea
Hawalah is a transfer (naql), not a guarantee – the linguistic meaning governs the legal ruling, breaking the analogy to kafalah.

Zufar argued that hawalah is like kafalah, so the assignor should never be discharged. The Hanafi school responds by distinguishing the linguistic nature of the two contracts, affirming that legal rulings follow linguistic meanings. This lesson unpacks the qiyas and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
State Zufar's position
Zufar says the assignor is not freed (لا يبرأ) because hawalah is a contract of securing like kafalah.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the qiyas
Zufar analogizes hawalah to kafalah, claiming both are contracts of securing (توثق). The asl is kafalah, the far is hawalah. The illa is 'securing the debt'.
This is an illa that both contracts share.
3
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response rejects the analogy because hawalah is linguistically a transfer (نقل), not a guarantee (ضم). The debt moves from one liability to another, so the assignor's liability is cleared.
The response uses istihsan based on linguistic meaning.
4
Apply the principle that legal rulings follow linguistic meanings
The Hanafi usul principle: الأحكام الشرعية وفاق المعاني اللغوية. The linguistic meaning of hawalah dictates its legal effect.
This principle is important in Hanafi usul and distinguishes it from other schools.
5
Note the rejection and tarjih
The qiyas is rejected (fasad al-i'tibar). The majority of Hanafis follow the view that hawalah is a transfer. Zufar's view is weak and not relied upon.
The mufta bihi is that the assignor is discharged.

Assignor's Claim of Prior Debt in Hawalah
Ruling on the assignor's claim that the hawalah was for a prior debt, and its rejection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when an assignor claims a prior debt in hawalah and explain the legal reasoning (qiyas) behind it.

Core Idea
A hawalah is not an acknowledgment; the assignor's claim of a prior debt requires proof, not automatic discharge.

In hawalah, when the assignee pays the debt and then seeks reimbursement from the assignor, a dispute may arise: the assignor claims the hawalah was for a prior debt. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that such a claim is not accepted without evidence, based on the principle that hawalah is a transfer, not an acknowledgment. Understanding this ruling is crucial for practitioners dealing with debt transfers in Islamic finance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
The assignor (muhil) assigns his debt to the assignee (muhal 'alayh), who then pays the creditor. The assignee sues the assignor for reimbursement.
2
Recognize the assignor's defense
The assignor claims the hawalah was for a prior debt he already had against the assignee, i.e., the assignor was already a creditor.
3
Understand the legal cause of the suit
The assignee's right to sue arises from the fact that he paid the assignor's debt by the assignor's order (qada' daynihi bi-amrihi). This is the sabab of the suit.
4
Apply the principle that hawalah is not iqrar
Hawalah is a contract of transfer, not an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a prior debt. Therefore, the assignor's claim is a separate allegation requiring proof.
5
Conclude the ruling
The assignor's statement is not accepted; he must pay the equivalent of the hawalah amount unless he can prove his claim of a prior debt.

Commentary

Definition and Conditions of Sulh (Reconciliation)
Defines sulh linguistically and legally, states rukn and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of sulh, identify its rukn, and list its conditions of validity.

Core Idea
Sulh is a contract that ends disputes; its validity depends on capacity, known subject, and a compensable right.

This lesson opens Kitab al-Sulh from al-Lubab. We explore why sulh follows agency, guaranty, and transfer of debt—all assist in fulfilling needs. We then define sulh, distinguish its pillar (offer and acceptance) from its conditions, and examine exceptions for minors or slaves acting with permission.

Guided Path
1
Understand placement
Note the author's connection: sulh, like agency, guaranty, and transfer of debt, helps fulfill needs, so it is placed after these chapters.
This shows the logical flow of the Hanafi fiqh corpus.
2
Define sulh linguistically and legally
Linguistically: musalaha (reconciliation after disagreement). Legally: a contract that removes dispute and cuts off litigation.
The legal definition emphasizes the purpose: ending conflict.
3
Identify the rukn (pillar)
The rukn is offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). Without these, no contract forms.
In Hanafi usul, all contracts require ijab and qabul.
4
List the shurut (conditions)
Conditions: sanity, adulthood, freedom (except with permission for minor/slave), subject matter known if possession is needed, and the right must be compensable (e.g., property or debts, not pre-emption).
Each condition ensures the contract is sound and just.
5
Note exceptions
A minor or slave can make sulh if permitted and it benefits them. The subject may be unknown if no possession is needed. Non-compensable rights (like pre-emption) cannot be subject.
These exceptions reflect Hanafi flexibility within defined limits.

The gift contract (hibah) is a common transaction in daily life, but its legal structure differs from sale. In Hanafi fiqh, the donor's offer is the only pillar for validity, while acceptance is required only for the recipient's ownership. This lesson unpacks the definition and the nuanced ruling, along with an intra-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
Define hibah linguistically and legally
Identify both definitions from the text. The linguistic definition is general (donating, bestowing), while the legal definition is specific: transfer of ownership of a specific item without compensation.
The legal definition excludes sale (which involves compensation) and other transfers.
2
Identify the ruling: gift is valid by offer and acceptance
The author states that gift is valid by offer and acceptance, but immediately qualifies that offer is a pillar and acceptance is not. Understand that acceptance is needed for the donee's ownership, not for the contract's validity from the donor's side.
The expression 'valid' (tashih) is deliberately used instead of 'concluded' (in'aqad) as in sale.
3
Analyze the pillar (rukn) of gift
The text says offer is a rukn for the giver; acceptance is not a rukn, by istihsan. The reasoning: gift is a donation, so it is completed from the donor's side alone.
Compare with sale where both offer and acceptance are pillars.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute with Imam Zufar
Imam Zufar holds that acceptance is a pillar, analogizing to other contracts. The dominant Hanafi position (mufta bihi) follows Abu Hanifa's view that acceptance is not a pillar.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Distinguish between validity from donor's side and completion for donee
The gift is valid upon offer alone, but the donee's ownership requires acceptance. The text illustrates with an oath: if someone swears not to give a gift and then makes an offer without acceptance, the oath is not broken because the gift is not complete.
This practical example highlights the distinction.

Completion of a Gift: The Role of Possession
Ruling with proof: gift completion and possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for gift completion and distinguish between valid and invalid possession scenarios, including the role of istihsan.

Core Idea
A gift is completed only by possession; possession in the session is valid by istihsan, but after separation requires explicit permission.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains a key Hanafi rule: a gift (hiba) is not binding until the donee takes possession. But not all possession is equal—timing and permission matter. We will explore what makes possession valid, why the offer itself implies permission, and what happens when the donor forbids. By the end, you will be able to apply these rules to common gift scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage opens with 'وتتم بالقبض' — the gift is completed by possession. This is the central Hanafi principle. Note: possession is a condition for the transfer of ownership.
2
Distinguish two possession scenarios
The text contrasts possession 'in the session' (في المجلس) without command, and possession 'after separation' (بعد الافتراق). The first is valid by istihsan; the second is invalid unless the donor explicitly permits.
Session means the same gathering where offer was made.
3
Understand the istihsan dalil
Why is possession in the session valid without command? Because the offer itself is an implied permission (إذن دلالة). This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference). The text states: 'لأن الإيجاب إذن له بالقبض دلالة'.
Implicit permission is weaker than explicit command, but sufficient unless overridden.
4
Analyze the exception: explicit forbiddance
If the donor explicitly forbids possession, then even in the session, possession is invalid. The explicit word overrides the implied permission.
This applies whether in session or after.
5

The passage briefly describes complete possession: for movables, customary possession; for real estate, taking key or vacating; for divisible items, division; for indivisible, by following the whole. This is practical detail from the matn.

Verbal Formulas for a Valid Gift Contract
Listing of verbal expressions for a valid gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven valid gift expressions and apply the rule that any word indicating tamlik suffices, with intention resolving ambiguity.

Core Idea
A gift contract is valid with any expression that clearly transfers ownership, even without the word 'hiba'.

In Hanafi law, a gift (hiba) is a transfer of ownership without compensation. While the term 'wahaba' is explicit, many other expressions also conclude a valid gift. This lesson identifies seven such formulas and explains how context and intention determine the transaction's nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit (sarih) expressions
Three words are explicitly used for gift: 'wahaba' (I gifted), 'nahala' (I bestowed), and 'a'taytu' (I gave). They clearly indicate tamlik (transfer of ownership).
2
Recognize expressions that depend on context
Words like 'at'amtu' (I fed) and 'ja'altu' (I made) can indicate a gift when used appropriately: 'at'amtu' with edible items means gift of the item itself; 'ja'altu' with the preposition 'li' (for you) implies ownership.
3
Understand the special case of 'at'amtu' with non-food
If 'at'amtu' is used for land or other non-edible items, it does not transfer ownership but creates a loan (ariya) because the item cannot be 'eaten'.
4
Note the role of intention for ambiguous expressions
The word 'hamaltu' (I mounted you) is ambiguous: by default it is a loan (ariya) for riding, but if the donor intends a gift of the animal, the intention makes it a valid gift.

Stating the ruling on gifting undivided shares in divisible versus indivisible property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between divisible and indivisible property for the purpose of gifting an undivided share, and state the Hanafi ruling for each case.

Core Idea
The gift of an undivided share is valid only in indivisible property; in divisible property, the share must be physically separated.

In Hanafi fiqh, gifting a share in joint property (musha') is not automatically invalid. The ruling depends on whether the property can be divided without losing its benefit. This lesson unpacks that distinction by examining the conditions for validity and the underlying reasoning (talil).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of property
The text divides property into two types: what can be divided (يُقسم) while still being beneficially used after division, and what cannot be divided without losing its intended benefit.

The underlying cause is the possibility of complete possession. Where it is possible, it is required; where not, incomplete possession is accepted.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Gifting an undivided share is valid only in indivisible property; in divisible property it is invalid unless physically separated.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays down a key distinction in Hanafi gift law. The validity of a gift of an undivided share (musha') depends entirely on whether the property can be divided without losing its utility. Division enables complete possession (qabd kamil); where division is impossible, deficient possession (qabd qasir) is accepted. Understanding this rule prevents disputes over ownership and delivery.

Examples of divisible: land, grain, cloth. Examples of indivisible: a slave, a small bath, a handmill.

For divisible property, a gift of an undivided share (مشاع) is invalid unless the gifted portion is physically separated (مَحُوزة) from the donor's ownership. For indivisible property, such a gift is valid even without separation.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the preventers (mawani') that make a gift irrevocable once it has been validly given. The discussion opens with the ruling that revoking a gift is makruh (disliked) but legally binding—meaning the revocation itself is valid, albeit disliked. This is supported by the famous hadith comparing the revoker to a dog returning to its vomit, emphasizing the moral reprehension of the act. The focus then shifts to the preventers of revocation. The author mentions that there are seven preventers, symbolically represented by the letters 'دمع حزقه' (dam' hizqih). Although the text enumerates all seven, it only details the first two at this stage: compensation ('iwad) and connected increase (ziyadah muttasilah).

Regarding compensation: If the donee gives the donor a compensation ('iwad) for the gift and the donor takes possession of that compensation, the gift becomes irrevocable. The key condition is that the donee must explicitly state that the compensation is in return for the entire gift; a general gift without such specification does not bar revocation. The reason is that the purpose of the gift—to benefit the donee—is fulfilled by the exchange, making revocation unjust.

Regarding connected increase: If the gifted item itself gains a connected increase that adds to its value—such as constructing a building on land, planting trees, or fattening an animal—the donor cannot revoke the gift. The rationale is that it is impossible to separate the increase from the original item, and since the increase was not part of the original contract, revoking with the increase would involve taking something not given. The author notes several restrictions: (a) Only increase (ziyadah) prevents; decrease (nuqsan) does not. (b) The increase must be connected (muttasilah); disconnected increases like offspring or compensation (arsh) do not prevent revocation—the donor may revoke the original item but leave the disconnected increase with the donee. (c) The increase must be in the item itself (bi-nafsiha); an increase in value alone (e.g., market appreciation) does not prevent revocation, as the physical item remains unchanged. (d) The increase must cause a rise in value; otherwise it is negligible. The passage thus clarifies that these two preventers are based on practical impossibility or contractual fairness, reflecting Hanafi principles of equity (istihsan).

Key Takeaway
Compensation prevents revocation of a gift only if the donee explicitly declares it as compensation for the entire gift.
