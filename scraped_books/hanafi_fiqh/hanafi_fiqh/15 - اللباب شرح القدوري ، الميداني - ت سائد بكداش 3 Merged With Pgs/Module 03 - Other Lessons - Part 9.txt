Conditions of Sharecropping: Produce Must Be Shared Proportionally
Explains the condition that produce must be shared proportionally and lists invalid stipulations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that the produce in sharecropping must be shared proportionally (musha'an) and identify which stipulations render the contract invalid.

Core Idea
In sharecropping, the produce must be shared as an undivided proportion — fixed amounts destroy the partnership and invalidate the contract.

Sharecropping (muzara'ah) is a partnership where the landowner and farmer share the harvest. The Hanafi school requires that the produce be shared proportionally (musha'an) to ensure genuine risk-sharing. This passage from al-Lubab explains this essential condition and gives concrete examples of stipulations that break the partnership, such as fixed amounts or allocating specific parts of the yield to one party. Understanding these rules helps avoid invalid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core condition
Read the passage and locate the statement that the produce must be musha'an (shared proportionally). Understand that this is a fundamental requirement for a valid sharecropping contract.
The term musha'an means the produce is undivided and shared according to an agreed fraction.
2
Understand the rational basis
The condition is imposed to realize true partnership (sharika). If one party is guaranteed a fixed amount regardless of the harvest, the element of risk-sharing is lost, and the contract becomes more like a guaranteed return.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) rooted in the nature of partnership.
3
Apply the condition to the examples
Work through each example in the passage: fixed measures (qifzan), specific canals (madiyanat), straw and grain allocation. For each, determine why it violates the proportional sharing requirement.
The key is that any stipulation that could result in one party getting nothing while the other gets all the produce destroys the partnership.
4
Recognize invalidity (batil)
When the condition is violated, the contract becomes batil (invalid). The passage explicitly states this for each prohibited stipulation.
Batil means the contract has no legal effect and the parties must return to the status quo.
5
Note the exception
If the parties share the grain proportionally and do not mention the straw, the contract is valid. This shows that incidental byproducts follow the main produce.
The straw is considered incidental; silence about it does not invalidate the contract.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on sharecropping (muzara'ah) in al-Lubab. It states a key condition: the produce must be shared proportionally (musha'an) between the parties to ensure true partnership. The author first notes a difference of opinion regarding another condition—specifying the duration. In some lands, the fatwa accepts a sharecropping contract without a set period, based on the first planting. However, there is a conflict in the fatwa references. Then the main discussion begins: if the parties stipulate fixed measures of grain for one of them, the contract is invalid (batil) because it may lead to no surplus after paying that fixed amount, thus destroying the partnership. The same applies if they stipulate the produce from specific canals or streams for one party, as the yield might be limited to that source. Similarly, if they allocate the straw to one and the grain to the other, a blight could prevent grain formation, making the share unequal. Likewise, if they split the straw equally but give all grain to one, the partnership is undermined. However, if they split the grain equally and remain silent about the straw, the contract is valid because the main objective (grain) is shared, and the straw is incidental. Consequence of No Produce in Muzara'ah
Ruling with rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a worker in a valid muzara'ah if no produce occurs, and explain the Hanafi legal reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
In muzara'ah, the worker is paid from the produce; no produce means no wage.

This lesson examines a short but important ruling from al-Quduri's matn on sharecropping. When a valid muzara'ah contract yields no produce, what is the worker's entitlement? The answer might seem harsh, but it follows logically from the contract's structure. Let's explore the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario and ruling
Read the matn: 'If the land does not produce anything, then the worker gets nothing.' Note the condition (no produce) and the consequence (no wage).
The ruling is clear and unconditional.
2
Understand the worker's legal status
The commentary explains the reason: the worker is like a hirer (musta'jir) who is paid from the produce. His wage is the agreed share of the crop.
This status is crucial for the analogy.
3
Apply the rule when no produce exists
Since the worker's ownership of the wage depends on the existence of the produce, if no produce exists, his ownership never materializes.
This is a rational (aqli) proof based on the contract's nature.
4
Recognize the consensus within the Hanafi school
The text does not mention any intra-Hanafi dispute; this is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
No differing opinions are recorded here.

Rulings When Muzara'ah Is Invalid and Dispute on Worker's Wage
Details distribution of produce and wages in invalid muzara'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling for an invalid muzara'ah contract and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the worker's wage.

Core Idea
In an invalid muzara'ah, the worker gets market wage capped at the stipulated share—consent to the contract implies waiver of excess.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with the consequences of an invalid muzara'ah (sharecropping) contract. When the contract is fasid, the produce belongs to the owner of the seed. The key dispute within the Hanafi school is whether the worker's compensation is limited by the agreed share or set at the full market rate. Understanding this ikhtilaf illuminates the school's approach to balancing contractual consent with fair compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling for invalid muzara'ah
The produce (growth) belongs to the owner of the seed regardless of who provided labor or land. This is the foundational principle from Zahir al-Riwayah.
The rationale: produce is the growth of the seed, so it follows ownership of the seed.
2
Distinguish two scenarios based on seed provider
If seed is from landowner, the worker gets ajr mithlihi (market wage) for his labor. If seed is from worker, the landowner gets ajr mithliha (market rent) for his land. Compensation is due because the benefit was received via an invalid contract.
The party not providing seed receives compensation.
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf when seed is from landowner
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the worker's ajr mithl is capped at the share stipulated in the contract. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the worker gets full ajr mithl without any cap. The proof for the cap is the worker's consent to the contract implies waiver of excess. Muhammad argues the contract is void, so compensation must reflect true value.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school; the dispute is about the effect of consent in a fasid contract.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
Ali al-Qari in al-Tashih reports that al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi followed the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, making it the relied-upon position for fatwa. Thus, the worker's wage is market wage capped at the stipulated share.
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the majority view.

Ruling When One Party Refuses to Work in Muzara'ah
Specifies when parties can be forced to work in a valid muzara'ah contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of compulsion for each party in a muzara'ah contract and identify when a valid excuse dissolves the contract.

Core Idea
The seed owner cannot be forced before casting his seed; the other party is always forced unless a valid excuse exists.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in muzara'ah: what happens when one party refuses to work? The text reveals an asymmetry based on seed ownership and the timing of refusal, drawing an analogy to ijara contracts. Understanding this distinction prevents injustice and ensures the binding nature of the contract is respected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties in the muzara'ah contract
There are two parties: the seed owner (صاحب البذر) and the one who is not the seed owner (الذي ليس من قبله البذر). The seed owner provides the seeds; the other party (typically the landowner or worker) provides land or labour.
In some contracts, the seed may be provided by the landowner or the worker; the key is who owns the seeds.
2
Determine the ruling for the seed owner's refusal
If the seed owner refuses before casting his seeds, he is not forced. The reason is that forcing him would harm him by consuming his seeds. After casting, he is forced because the harm has already occurred.
This ruling is specific to before casting; the text 'قيدنا بكونه قبل إلقاء البذر' shows the condition.
3
Determine the ruling for the non-seed owner's refusal
The non-seed owner is forced to work by the judge. The contract is binding (lazim) like an ijara (hire) contract, and no harm comes to him by performing the work.
Exception: if he has a valid excuse that would dissolve an ijara contract, the muzara'ah is also dissolved.
4
Understand the analogy to ijara
The seed owner's case is compared to hiring a laborer to demolish a house and then the hirer changes his mind. Just as the hirer is not forced, the seed owner is not forced before casting. The non-seed owner's case is directly akin to an ijara worker who must work unless excused.
The analogy highlights that harm (ضرر) is the key factor for non-compulsion.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a scenario with a seed owner who refuses after casting, or a non-seed owner who refuses without excuse, state whether the judge can compel them.
This step solidifies the distinction.

Rulings on refusal to work in sharecropping

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings when each party refuses to work in a sharecropping contract and explain the reasoning based on the analogy to ijarah.

A seed-owner is not forced before sowing; the worker is always forced to work.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri explains the consequences when one party in a sharecropping contract refuses to work. The ruling distinguishes between the seed-owner and the worker, and it changes based on whether the seed has been sown. The key principle is preventing harm and the binding nature of the contract, akin to ijarah.

1

Recognize that the contract involves a seed-owner (sahib al-badhr) and a worker (who does not own seed).
The seed-owner provides land and seed; the worker provides labor.
2

If the seed-owner refuses before sowing, he is not forced; if after sowing, he is forced.
The reason is harm (wasting seed) before sowing, which disappears after sowing.
3

Ruling on post-harvest expenses in muzara'ah and effect of stipulating them on the worker

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Stipulating harvest expenses on the worker alone invalidates the contract in Zahir al-Riwayah, but custom can validate it in later fatwa.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a practical issue in sharecropping (muzara'ah): who bears the costs of harvesting and related tasks after the crop is ready? The default rule is that both parties share according to their shares. However, a common stipulation places all these expenses on the worker alone. The text presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of this condition, with the primary narration ruling the contract invalid (fasid) and a permissive view gaining traction in later regions based on custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
First, note the default ruling: expenses of harvesting, lifting, threshing, winnowing, and guarding are shared between the landowner and worker according to their shares. This applies whether or not the contract period has expired, because the crop becomes joint property once ready.
The contract ends when the crop is ready, so post-harvest work is on both parties.
2
Understand the stipulation and its effect per Zahir al-Riwayah
If the parties stipulate that all these expenses are solely on the worker, the muzara'ah contract becomes invalid (fasid). The condition is not required by the contract and benefits only one party (the landowner), thus it corrupts the contract.
This is the position of the apparent narration (Zahir al-Riwayah).

Despite the default ruling, there is a different transmission from Abu Hanifa (via al-Hasan ibn Ziyad) and a view from Abu Yusuf that this condition is permissible. This creates a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
The permissive view is considered a rare transmission (nawadir).
4

The permissive view relies on customary practice (ta'amul) and analogy with istisna' (commissioned manufacture) contracts, where custom governs terms. Scholars argue that if such a condition is customary in a region, it should be allowed.
This follows the principle that custom (urf) can determine contractual terms.
5
Note the regional preference and mufta bihi
Later scholars from Balkh, Khorasan, Iraq, and Bukhara preferred the permissive view and gave fatwa accordingly. In regions where the condition is customary, the contract is valid; elsewhere the default ruling remains.
The permissive view is the mufta bihi in those regions due to established custom.

The Hanafi school permits musaqah for a wide range of plants, from date palms to vegetables, provided the worker's share is a known common fraction. We will also examine three important rulings: when a musaqah is valid on a tree with existing fruit, the compensation due when the contract is invalid, and the effect of a party's death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and essential condition
The passage begins by stating that the parties specify a part of the fruit as a common share. Understand that the share must be musha' (common and undivided) to maintain partnership.
A fixed quantity would cut off partnership – not allowed.
2
Determine the scope of permissible plants
The text lists: date palms, trees, grapevines, al-ratab (fresh shoots, interpreted as all vegetables), and eggplant roots. The permissibility is based on general need.
Al-ratab includes all vegetables, as explained in the commentary referencing al-Durr.
3
Analyze the ruling on existing fruit
If the tree already has fruit at the time of contract, the contract is valid only if the fruit can still increase with the worker's labor. If fully ripe, it is invalid.
The worker must have something to do – no work means no contract.
4
Apply the rule for an invalid musaqah
If the contract is fasid (invalid), the worker receives an equivalent wage (ajr al-mithl) because it is analogous to an invalid hire.
This protects the worker's labor even if the contract is defective.
5
Examine the effect of death
Musaqah is voided by death of either party, like hire. However, if the landowner dies, the worker may continue even if the heirs object.
The continuation right protects the worker's investment and effort.

Grounds for Rescinding Musaqah and Muzara'ah
Listing excuses for rescission of sharecropping contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the excuses that allow rescission of Musaqah and Muzara'ah contracts according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A sharecropping contract can be rescinded when unforeseen harm arises that neither party agreed to bear.

This passage expands the rules of Ijarah (hire) to the special agricultural contracts of Musaqah and Muzara'ah. It provides two concrete examples from Al-Hidayah where the contract may be dissolved due to unconsented hardship. Understanding these excuses clarifies the Hanafi principle of preventing harm (darar) in contractual relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
The passage states that the excuses for rescinding Ijarah apply equally to Musaqah and Muzara'ah. First recall what those excuses are in Ijarah (e.g., mutual consent, judicial termination for hardship).
This analogy is based on the shared contractual structure of ongoing work for a share of produce.
2
Analyze the first example: theft
If the worker is a thief and there is fear he will steal fruit before ripening, the landowner suffers a harm he did not consent to. This justifies rescission to protect the landowner's property.
The theft must be actual and feared; mere suspicion may not suffice.
3
Analyze the second example: illness
If the worker becomes too ill to work, requiring him to hire substitutes imposes extra financial burden not originally agreed upon. Rescission prevents this unconsented hardship.
The illness must be severe enough to prevent the worker from performing the work himself.
4
Extract the underlying principle
Both excuses share a common rationale: the contract imposes a harm (darar) that was not originally stipulated or anticipated. Hanafi law allows dissolution to remove such harm.
This principle is derived from the broader maqasid of preventing hardship and injustice.
5
Apply to new scenarios
Consider whether other unforeseen harms (e.g., the worker becomes a habitual drunkard and neglects trees) would justify rescission under the same principle.
The key test is whether the harm is unconsented and beyond what was implicitly accepted in the contract.

Invalid Condition in Plantation Partnership
Ruling on invalid plantation sharing contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a landowner gives land to a planter with a condition to share land and trees equally, and explain why the contract is invalid.

Core Idea
A partnership cannot give a share of pre-existing property without work or payment — such a condition invalidates the contract.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common contractual scenario: a landowner and a planter agree to share both land and trees. At first glance, it seems like a fair arrangement, but the Hanafi school rules it invalid. Why? Because the planter would be receiving a share of the land without contributing to its acquisition or improvement. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this ruling and the proper compensation due to the planter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract terms
What did the landowner give? For how long? What condition did they set? The text says: 'gives a white land to a man for a known number of years, for him to plant trees, on condition that the land and the trees be between the owner and the planter in halves.'
White land means barren land with no trees.
2
Determine the ruling
Is it valid or invalid? The text states: 'that is not permissible' (lam yajuz). So the contract is invalid (fasid).
3
Understand the reason
Why is it invalid? Because the condition stipulates partnership in what was already present before the partnership (the land) without any contribution from the planter's work. The planter is getting a share of land for free.
The core legal principle: you cannot acquire a share of pre-existing property without work or payment.
4
Know the consequences
What happens to the trees and fruit? They belong to the landowner. What does the planter get? He receives the value of his planting (qimat gharṣihi) and a fair wage for his work (ujrat mithlihi).
This prevents unjust enrichment.
5
Apply the reasoning
Can you think of a similar condition that would be invalid? For example, if a worker is given a shop to run and they agree to share ownership of the shop itself, that would be invalid for the same reason.
The key is whether the partner contributes to acquiring the asset before sharing.

Compensation in Invalid Musaqah
States compensation for invalid musaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation in a fasid musaqah contract and identify what the worker receives.

Core Idea
In a fasid musaqah, landowner keeps produce; worker gets value of planting and fair wages.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from Al-Quduri on the consequences of an invalid musaqah (sharing of trees) contract. Unlike valid musaqah where parties share the produce, a defective contract shifts entitlements: the landowner retains all fruit and trees, but the worker is compensated for both materials (value of planting) and labor (equivalent wage). This ensures no unjust enrichment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the landowner (rabb al-ard) and the worker/planter (gharis) in a musaqah contract.
The contract is a form of partnership in produce.
2
Understand the invalid condition
A musaqah becomes fasid (invalid) if essential terms like share ratio are missing or uncertain.
The phrase 'qabla al-shirkah' indicates the partnership was not properly formed.
3
Memorize the worker's compensation
The worker receives: (a) value of his planting (qimat gharasihi) and (b) customarily equivalent wage (ujrat mithlihi) for his work.
These are two separate entitlements.
4
Memorize the landowner's retention
All fruit (thamar) and trees (ghars) belong to the landowner.
The worker does not share in the produce.
