Selling Fresh Dates for Dried Dates: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Genus Unity
Ruling with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on exchanging fresh and dried fruits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding exchanging fresh dates for dried dates and identify the mufta bihi opinion.

Core Idea
Fresh and dried dates share the same genus; moisture is not a separate genus, so equal exchange is permissible per Abu Hanifa.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether fresh dates (rutab) can be exchanged for dried dates (tamr) in equal measure. Abu Hanifa permits it because the genus is identical, while the Sahibayn object, arguing that moisture alters the substance. Understanding this dispute clarifies how Hanafi jurists weigh physical change against legal genus in riba contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Abu Hanifa rules that selling fresh dates for dried dates in equal volume is permissible. His reasoning: fresh dates are still dates; the only difference is water, which is not a separate ribawi item.
This view applies to all fruits that dry, e.g., grapes for raisins, figs for dried figs.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that such an exchange is not permissible. Their implied proof: moisture changes the nature, so equality cannot be guaranteed – the fresh fruit is a different type requiring weight-based measurement or drying first.
The Sahibayn's objection is not quoted verbatim in the text but is inferred from the dispute.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa's response: moisture is not an independent genus (jins); the fruit itself is the same substance. Thus, the exchange is governed by the rule: same genus, same volume, permitted.
This follows the principle that deficiency or addition of a non-substantial element does not change the legal genus.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a merchant can directly exchange fresh dates for dried dates in equal volume. Under the Sahibayn, the fresh dates must be dried first, or the exchange is invalid.
The same applies to grapes vs. raisins and similar fruits.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative opinion)
Later Hanafi authorities (al-Isbijabi, al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'ah) upheld Abu Hanifa's view as correct and relied upon. Thus, the mufta bihi is the permissibility of equal-volume exchange.
This tarjih is based on the strength of the dalil and the majority of later scholars.

Fresh fruit may be exchanged for its dried form by weight equality, provided they are of the same genus and the fruit dries naturally.

The Condition for Exchanging Fruit for Oil
Ruling on exchanging fruit for oil with condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for the validity of selling olives for olive oil and explain how this condition prevents riba.

Core Idea
Exchanging fruit for oil requires that the oil given exceeds the fruit's oil content — the surplus compensates for the waste (dregs).

The sale of an oil-yielding fruit (like olives or sesame) for its extracted oil is not a simple exchange. Since the fruit contains both oil and solid waste (dregs), an equal exchange could involve riba (usury). This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling: the sale is valid only if the oil given is more than the oil content of the fruit, so that the excess oil counterbalances the waste.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is selling olives for olive oil (or sesame for sesame oil). The ruling: 'لا يجوز' (not permissible) unless a condition is met.
The prohibition is due to potential riba.
2
State the condition for validity
The oil being sold must be greater than the amount of oil in the fruit. Why? Because the fruit contains both oil and dregs (waste). To avoid riba, the oil given must compensate for the dregs.
Example: If olives yield 0.2 L oil and 0.8 kg dregs, the oil sold must exceed 0.2 L.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The exchange must be equivalent in value. The fruit's oil value is matched by an equal amount of oil, and the extra oil covers the dregs. This ensures no riba.
This is analogous to selling meat for a live animal, where extra meat compensates for non-meat parts.
4
Recognize the analogy
Just as meat sold for a live animal must exceed the animal's meat content, oil sold for fruit must exceed the fruit's oil content.
The underlying principle is avoiding unequal exchange of the same genus.
5
Apply the condition
When buying olive oil from a farmer who has olives, you must ensure the oil you receive is more than the oil that could be pressed from the olives given. If exactly equal, the sale is invalid.
This prevents riba in sale of homogenous goods.

Natural vs. Manufactured Differences in Ribawi Exchanges
Maxim distinguishing natural vs. manufactured variations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that natural variations are disregarded while manufactured variations invalidate equality in ribawi exchanges.

Core Idea
Natural differences do not break riba equality; only human-caused changes do.

In ribawi exchanges (like-for-like sales of the same genus), equality of measure is required. But what if the items differ naturally, like fresh vs. dried dates, or due to processing, like wheat vs. flour? The Hanafi school has a clear maxim to resolve this—a key tool for any student of fiqh al-mu'amalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The maxim distinguishes al-tafawut al-khalqi (natural variation) and al-tafawut bi sunʿ al-ʿibād (manufactured variation). Note the examples provided: fresh/dried dates and good/bad quality for natural; wheat/flour and fried wheat for manufactured.
Natural variation includes inherent differences like ripeness, quality, or drying.
2
Understand the legal effect
Natural differences are 'sāqiṭ al-iʿtibār' (disregarded), meaning they do not affect the riba ruling. Manufactured differences 'yufsid' (invalidate) the exchange if equality is attempted; such items become different genera for riba purposes.
Invalidation means the sale is fasid (void) if done as like-for-like without proper barter rules.
3
Apply to new cases
Given a new scenario, classify the variation as natural or manufactured. If natural, the exchange can proceed with equality of measure; if manufactured, the exchange must follow barter rules (different genera, free from riba).
Examples: yogurt from milk? Milk to yogurt is manufactured (fermentation) so different genera. But ripe vs. unripe fruit is natural.
4
Compare cross-madhhab views
The Shafi‘i school treats grinding (e.g., wheat to flour) as a change that potentially creates a new commodity, but their criteria differ. In Hanafi, any human action that alters the substance or form makes the item a different genus for riba.
Shafi‘is may allow equality in some cases if the change is minimal, but Hanafis stick to the maxim strictly.

Commentary
This maxim from al-'Inayah (a commentary on al-Hidayah) establishes a key principle for ribawi exchanges: when two items are of the same genus but differ due to a natural process (like drying or natural quality variation), the difference is ignored for the purpose of riba. Thus, fresh dates can be exchanged for dried dates equally if they are of the same genus. However, when the difference is caused by human action (such as grinding wheat into flour or frying it), that difference invalidates the exchange of like for like. The reason is that human manufacturing changes the intrinsic nature of the item, creating a new commodity. Therefore, such exchanges must be governed by the rules of barter (different genera exchange), not riba equality. This maxim helps determine which transformations are legally significant.

التفاوت بصنع العباد (al-tafawut bi ṣunʿ al-ʿibād): Difference caused by human manufacturing (e.g., grinding, cooking)

Exchange of a solid for its extract is valid only if the extract exceeds the amount present in the solid, compensating for the pulp.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Quduri's matn, expanded by al-Maydani, concerning exchanges of foodstuffs that share a common genus but differ in form. The key principle is to prevent riba by ensuring that the element subject to ribawi rules (the fat/oil) is exchanged in equal quantity, while any excess is attributed to the valuable byproduct (the pulp). Identify the forbidden transaction

2
Extract the condition for permissibility
Look for the word 'until' (حتى). The transaction becomes permissible if the oil/tahini given is more than the amount naturally present in the solid. This ensures the fat is exchanged equally.
The excess is for the pulp (al-tufl), which has value.
3
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The commentator explains that the ruling is based on riba: the fat is the same genus, so equal exchange is required. The excess payment is actually for the pulp, which is a separate commodity.
This is an application of the maxim 'al-birr bi'l-birr' for ribawi items.
4
Generalize the ruling
The final sentence extends the ruling to anything whose pulp has value, such as walnuts for oil or milk for ghee. The same condition applies.
This is a qiyas (analogy) based on the shared illa of valuable pulp.

Different animal species produce different genera of meat, allowing unequal exchange; same species meat requires equal weight.

This passage clarifies a common fiqh question about buying and selling meat. The Hanafi school distinguishes between meats from different animal species, treating them as different commodities that can be traded with disparity. Salam (advance payment sale) is a special concession in Islamic law for those who need capital but do not have the goods immediately. However, to prevent disputes and uncertainty, the Hanafi school imposes strict conditions on its validity. In this lesson, we examine the key conditions from the matn of al-Quduri and the sharh of al-Lubab, focusing on why deferment, known term, known measure, and generic description are essential. Understanding these conditions is crucial for distinguishing a valid salam contract from a fasid one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Salam is a deferred sale; its basic validity requires deferment and a known term. If delivery is immediate or the term is unknown, the contract is invalid.
The ruling is from Quran and Sunnah, but the reasoning is based on the purpose of salam as a concession.
2
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
Salam was legislated as a concession for those who cannot deliver immediately (e.g., the poor). If the seller could deliver now, the concession would not apply, so deferment is necessary.
This is a key principle: a concession is valid only when the need exists.
3
List the conditions for validity
From the text, identify the seven conditions mentioned by Abu Hanifa: known genus, known type, known measure (non-compressible), known term, known place of delivery, quality description, and price paid at contract session. Also note secondary conditions like non-compressible measure.
The text explicitly mentions the first condition and implies others via invalidations.
4
Identify invalidating factors (mufsidat)
The text lists: immediate delivery, interrupted delivery, unknown measure, specifying to a particular village or tree (unless for description), and compressible measure (except by custom).
These all lead to uncertainty (gharar) and potential dispute.
5
Note exceptions and differing views
The minimum term is one month (preferred view), but some say three days or half a day. Also, Abu Yusuf allows compressible containers if customary, like water skins.
This shows the use of istihsan (juristic preference) in Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on salam (salam sale) outlines the essential conditions for the validity of a salam contract according to the Hanafi school. The author begins by stating that salam must be deferred (not immediate delivery) with a known term—this is because salam was legislated as a concession to help those in need by allowing them to sell goods they will produce later. If the seller could deliver immediately, the concession would not apply. The minimum term is one month, though there are differing views. The term must be known to avoid dispute. The measure (like a specific person's container or arm) must not be used if its capacity is unknown, as that leads to uncertainty. Delivery at the place of contract or its opposite, or interrupted delivery, is invalid. If interruption occurs after the right to delivery is established, the buyer chooses between waiting or canceling and taking back capital. Salam is also invalid if the object is specified to food from a particular village or fruit from a specific tree due to risk of destruction. However, if such specification is merely to describe quality, it is allowed. The measure must be non-compressible; compressible containers are invalid unless customarily used, like water skins per Abu Yusuf. The commentary draws from al-Hidayah and other sources, explaining the rationale (ta'lil) behind each ruling, focusing on prevention of dispute (manāzi'a).

The Seven Conditions for a Valid Salam Contract (Abu Hanifa)
Lists the seven conditions for a valid salam contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven conditions for a valid salam contract according to Abu Hanifa and identify which omission renders the contract fasid.

Core Idea
A valid salam contract requires seven specified conditions; omitting any invalidates the contract.

In this lesson, we examine the strict requirements set by Imam Abu Hanifa for the validity of a salam contract (forward sale). The passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar enumerates seven conditions that must be clearly stated in the contract to avoid uncertainty (gharar). Understanding these conditions is essential for ensuring that such deferred transactions are legally sound in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the preliminary prohibition
The passage first states that salam is invalid for food from a specific village or fruit from a specific tree, due to the risk of defect affecting delivery. Note this as an exception to the general permissibility of salam.
This is a separate ruling not part of the seven conditions.
2
List the first five conditions
The first five conditions are: known genus (e.g., wheat/barley), known type (e.g., Khurasani/baladi), known quality (e.g., good/bad), known quantity (by measure/weight), and known delivery term (minimum one month). These ensure the subject matter is fully specified.
These are prerequisites for a valid contract; any missing renders it fasid.
3
Analyze the sixth condition: knowledge of capital
The sixth condition requires knowledge of the capital amount when the capital is of a type where the contract depends on knowing its quantity (e.g., items measured by volume, weight, or count). For specific items like a garment or animal, indication suffices.
This condition prevents uncertainty about the price paid in advance.
4
Examine the seventh condition: delivery place
The seventh condition is specification of the delivery place if the seller has a specific location. If no place is mentioned, the default rule applies (likely the buyer's location).
This condition is required only if relevant; otherwise, it is waived.
5
Conclude the validity consequence
Omitting any of these conditions makes the salam contract fasid (invalid) according to Abu Hanifa. This underscores the school's emphasis on clarity and certainty in forward sales.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Conditions of Salam and the Dispute on Capital Specification and Delivery Place
Listing conditions and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven conditions of a valid Salam contract and distinguish the position of Abu Hanifa from that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding specification of capital and delivery place.

Core Idea
A Salam contract requires certainty in type, description, measure, term, capital, and delivery place — but the later Hanafi position eases two conditions.

Salam (advance payment sale) is a valid exception to the general prohibition of selling what is not in possession, but the Hanafi school imposes strict conditions to prevent dispute. The classical matn of al-Quduri lists seven conditions for a valid Salam. This passage from al-Lubab explains each condition and then presents an important intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad relaxed two of the conditions — specification of the capital amount when the capital is identified by pointing, and naming the delivery place even when the object requires freight. The later relied-upon position follows the two students.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seven conditions from the matn
List out each condition as stated: known type, known description, known measure, known term, knowledge of capital amount if its measure matters, and naming the delivery place if freight is involved. The seventh condition is implicitly the 'known capital amount' and 'delivery place'.
The matn uses 'six' but the sharh adds a seventh; the total conditions are seven.
2
Understand the rationale for each condition
Each condition ensures that the contract is free from uncertainty (gharar). Type, description, and measure define the object. Term fixes the delivery time. Capital amount must be known to avoid dispute over the price.
The minimum term is one month according to the Hanafis.
3
Recognize the exception for capital identified by pointing
The passage states that for cloth and animals, pointing to them suffices for identification of the capital, unlike commodities like wheat that require measurement. This is agreed upon.
The dispute arises when the capital is similarly identifiable.
4
Analyze the dispute of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that if the capital is identified by pointing (e.g., a specific animal), no further specification of its amount is needed. They also argue that even if the object has freight, the delivery place need not be named; delivery occurs at the contract place.
Their proof: analogy to price in a sale and the default rule that delivery is at the place of contract.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later authorities (al-Nasafi, Burhan al-Shari'ah, etc.) have adopted the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad as the relied-upon position for fatwa.
This is recorded in al-Tashih.

Specifying Capital and Place of Delivery in Salam: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's View
Narration of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's opinion on capital and place specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the necessity of naming capital and specifying place of delivery in a salam contract, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
In salam, pointing to specific capital suffices; default place is the contract location.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi difference over two conditions of the salam (advance payment sale) contract. While Imam Abu Hanifa required explicit naming of the capital and explicit designation of the delivery place, his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani held that pointing to an identifiable capital is sufficient and the place defaults to the contract location unless otherwise stipulated. Later Hanafi authorities relied on the Sahibayn's view, making it the operative position for fatwa.

Guided Path

Core Idea
Salam requires three hidden conditions to avoid riba, ensure specifiability, and remove uncertainty.

The Hanafi classical text on Salam (Quduri's Mukhtasar) lists primary conditions, but later scholars like al-Isbijabi added three more that are essential for contracts to remain valid (sahih). Guided Path
1
Condition 1: Avoid both attributes of riba

Ensure the contract is binding (batt) without any option period for either party. Options introduce uncertainty (jahl) about delivery, conflicting with Salam's immediate payment and deferred delivery.
Some other madhhabs allow options in Salam; Hanafi view is strict.

Definition and Ruling of Currency Exchange (Sarf)
Defines sarf and states conditions: equality in weight and hand-to-hand delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of sarf and explain the two conditions for its validity: equality in weight and hand-to-hand delivery.

Core Idea
Sarf is a sale of gold and silver: equal weight and immediate delivery are mandatory.

This passage introduces the chapter on Sarf, the specific rules for exchanging gold and silver. Because these items are ribawi, any exchange of the same type must be exactly equal in weight and both parties must take possession before leaving the transaction session. Understanding these conditions is essential to avoid riba in currency dealings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that Sarf is a type of sale (bay') where the items exchanged are from al-athman (gold and silver). This places it under the rules of riba.
The commentator relates it to the four types of sales: object for object, object for debt, debt for object, and debt for debt. Sarf is the fourth type.
2
State the definition of Sarf
Memorize the definition: 'Sarf is a sale when each of the two exchange items is from the genus of prices (gold and silver).' This makes it distinct from other sales.
3
Explain the condition of equality in weight
When exchanging gold for gold or silver for silver, they must be equal in weight (mithlan bi-mithl), regardless of differences in quality or craftsmanship. Quality does not justify an excess because in riba, quality is not considered when the same genus is exchanged.
This prevents riba al-fadl (excess in quantity).
4
Explain the condition of hand-to-hand delivery
Both parties must take physical possession (qabd) of their respective items before they separate from the transaction session (majlis). If they leave together, it is valid as long as possession occurred before.
This prevents riba al-nasi'ah (delay).
5
Summarize the consequence of violation
If either condition is violated (inequality or lack of immediate delivery), the transaction is invalid (batil) due to riba. The contract is not sahih without both conditions.
The Hanafi ruling is that both conditions are necessary for validity.

Book of Sarf (Currency Exchange)
Sarf is: a sale when each of its two compensations is from the class of mediums of exchange (gold and silver).
So if one sells silver for silver, or gold for gold: it is not permissible except like for like, even if they differ in quality and workmanship, and it is necessary that both compensations be taken possession of before separation.
Book of Sarf
Since sales with respect to the sold item are of four types: sale of a tangible for a tangible, tangible for a debt, debt for a tangible, and debt for a debt, and the first three were explained, he began to explain the fourth, and said:
(Sarf is: a sale when each of its two compensations is from the class of mediums of exchange): gold and silver. So if one sells silver for silver, or gold for gold: it is not permissible except like for like, meaning equal in weight, (even if they differ in quality and workmanship) due to what has passed in riba that quality has no value when it meets its same genus in what riba is established in.
And for its validity, it is necessary to take possession of both compensations before separation, physically, so that even if they leave the session walking together in one direction, or sleep in the session, or lose consciousness, the sarf is not void. [Hidayah]
(And if one sells gold for silver: disparity is permissible) due to difference of genus, (and immediate possession is obligatory) due to prohibition of delay.
(And if they separate in sarf before taking possession of both compensations, or one of them, the contract is void) due to loss of the condition of validity, which is possession before separation.
And for this reason, stipulating an option is not valid in it, because possession does not remain obligatory, nor is credit valid, due to loss of possession.
If the one who has the option or credit rescinds it before separation, it returns to being permissible, because it is removed before the corruption is established, unlike after separation, because it is established. Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Al-Quduri's matn, explaining the chapter on Sarf (currency exchange). The commentator, Al-Maydani, begins by defining sarf as a sale where both considerations are from the class of mediums of exchange, i.e., gold and silver. This distinguishes it from other sales. The core rulings are: (1) When exchanging gold for gold or silver for silver, equality in weight is required regardless of quality or workmanship. This is based on the riba principle (as previously discussed) that within a ribawi genus, quality has no effect; only like-for-like is permitted. (2) Immediate possession (qabd) of both items is mandatory before the parties separate from the session (majlis). If they separate without possession, the contract becomes void. The commentator addresses scenarios: if they walk together, sleep, or faint, it is not considered separation, so the contract remains valid. (3) When exchanging gold for silver (different genera), disparity in quantity is allowed, but immediate possession is still obligatory to avoid riba al-nasi'ah. (4) Stipulating an option (khiyar) or credit (ajal) is invalid in sarf because it prevents the required immediate possession. However, if the party rescinds the option or credit before separation, the contract becomes valid again. (5) The price (thaman) in a sarf contract cannot be used (by the recipient) before possession. The passage concludes with marginal notes confirming that sleeping does not break the session and that credit is invalid.

Key Terms
الصرف (al-ṣarf): A sale where both considerations are from the class of mediums of exchange (gold and silver).
الربا (al-ribā): Usury or unlawful increase; the ruling that quality has no value when exchanging same genus in ribawi items.
