Conditions of Validity: Immediate Possession and Prohibition of Options/Credit
Conditions of validity: possession, separation, and prohibited stipulations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the requirement of immediate possession in sarf, identify what constitutes proper separation, and apply the rules regarding exchange of different genera and stipulation of options or credit.

Core Idea
Immediate physical possession before separation is essential; options and credit invalidate the contract.

This lesson continues with the remaining conditions for a valid sarf contract. You will learn that immediate possession (qabd) of both items is required before the parties separate from the session, and what actions do and do not count as separation. We will also cover the exchange of gold for silver, where disparity is allowed but immediate possession remains obligatory. Finally, the prohibition of stipulating options (khiyar) or credit (ajal) will be examined, along with the cure of rescinding them before separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the requirement of qabd
The text states: 'it is necessary to take possession of both compensations before separation.' This is a condition for validity.
Qabd means physical possession, such as taking hold of the gold/silver.
2
Define separation (iftiraq)
Separation is physical departure from the session. Walking together, sleeping, or fainting does not constitute iftiraq.
The text explicitly says that if they walk together in one direction, sleep, or faint, the contract is not void.
3
Apply to gold-for-silver exchange
When exchanging gold for silver (different genera), disparity in weight is allowed, but immediate possession is wajib (obligatory) to avoid riba al-nasi'ah (delay riba).
The text says: 'وإذا باع الذهب بالفضة : جاز التفاضل، ووجب التقابض'.
4
Analyze the effect of options and credit
Stipulating an option (khiyar) or a term (ajal) is invalid because they prevent the required immediate possession.
The text explains that such stipulations make possession not obligatory, and the contract becomes void.
5
Examine the cure
If the party who has the option or credit rescinds it before separation, the contract becomes valid again. After separation, it is void and cannot be revived.
This shows the importance of the timing of the cure.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Al-Quduri's matn, explaining the chapter on Sarf (currency exchange). The commentator, Al-Maydani, begins by defining sarf as a sale where both considerations are from the class of mediums of exchange, i.e., gold and silver. This distinguishes it from other sales. The core rulings are: (1) When exchanging gold for gold or silver for silver, equality in weight is required regardless of quality or workmanship. This is based on the riba principle (as previously discussed) that within a ribawi genus, quality has no effect; only like-for-like is permitted. (2) Immediate possession (qabd) of both items is mandatory before the parties separate from the session (majlis). If they separate without possession, the contract becomes void. The commentator addresses scenarios: if they walk together, sleep, or faint, it is not considered separation, so the contract remains valid. (3) When exchanging gold for silver (different genera), disparity in quantity is allowed, but immediate possession is still obligatory to avoid riba al-nasi'ah. (4) Stipulating an option (khiyar) or credit (ajal) is invalid in sarf because it prevents the required immediate possession. However, if the party rescinds the option or credit before separation, the contract becomes valid again. (5) The price (thaman) in a sarf contract cannot be used (by the recipient) before possession. The passage concludes with marginal notes confirming that sleeping does not break the session and that credit is invalid.

Key Terms
القبض (al-qabḍ): Taking physical possession of the item; in sarf, required before the parties separate.
الافتراق (al-iftirāq): Separation of the parties from the contract session (majlis), whether by bodies or by walking away.
الخيار (al-khiyār): Option to rescind a contract; invalid in sarf because it prevents immediate possession.
الأجل (al-ajal): Credit term; stipulating a deferred payment or delivery is invalid in sarf.

The Core Ruling of Sarf: Disparity Allowed, Receipt Obligatory
States the core ruling of sarf with conditions and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that disparity is permissible and receipt is wajib in sarf, and explain the proof from the prohibition of riba al-nasi'a and difference of genus.

Core Idea
In sarf, quantity disparity is allowed due to different genus, but hand-to-hand receipt is wajib to avoid riba al-nasi'a.

This lesson introduces the foundational ruling for currency exchange (sarf) in the Hanafi school. The passage from Al-Hidayah establishes two key principles: disparity in quantity is permissible between gold and silver, but immediate receipt in the same session is obligatory. Understanding this ruling is essential for anyone dealing with currency transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon and what the hukm is for each part: disparity permissible (ja'iz) and receipt wajib.
The act is selling gold for silver (sarf).
2
Locate the Dalil for Each Ruling
For disparity: the reason given is 'li-ikhtilaf al-jins' (difference of genus). For receipt: 'li-hurmat al-nasi'a' (prohibition of riba al-nasi'a). Identify the source text for these proofs.
The dalil is derived from the general prohibition of riba in the Quran and Sunnah, specifically the hadith on gold and silver exchange.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Explain how the dalil proves the ruling. Difference of genus allows disparity because the prohibition of riba al-fadl applies only to same genus. Prohibition of riba al-nasi'a requires immediate receipt.
This is a clear talil (reasoning by cause).
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that disparity is not allowed in gold for silver because they consider gold and silver the same genus for riba. The Hanafi position is based on the difference in genus.
This ikhtilaf is mentioned in the analytical toolbox.

Invalidation of Sarf: Conditions and Exceptions
Expands on conditions of sarf, invalid factors, and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition that separation before receipt invalidates sarf, identify why option and term are invalid, and recognize when sleep or unconsciousness does not affect the contract.

Core Idea
Separation before full receipt invalidates sarf; option and term nullify the obligatory receipt, but sleep and fainting in the session are not considered separation.

Building on the core ruling, this lesson examines what breaks a sarf contract. The passage details that if the parties separate before receiving both countervalues, the contract is batil. It also explains why options and terms are invalid in sarf, and clarifies that involuntary interruptions like sleep or fainting do not count as separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Condition for Validity
Receipt before separation is a condition of validity (shart al-sihha) in sarf. If this condition is lost, the contract becomes invalid.
This condition is specific to sarf and not required in ordinary sales.
2
Determine What Constitutes Separation
Separation occurs when the parties physically leave the session (majlis) or when one party leaves without the other. The passage provides exceptions: sleeping or fainting in the same session does not count as separation.
The definition of separation is based on the ability to continue the negotiation in the same place.
3
Analyze Why Option and Term are Invalid
Stipulating an option (khiyar) or a term (ajal) is invalid because these delay the obligatory immediate receipt. The receipt must occur in the session before separation; any condition that prevents immediate receipt defeats the purpose.
However, if the option or term is rescinded before separation, the contract becomes valid because the obstacle is removed before the contract becomes corrupt.
4
Apply the Exceptions
If both parties sleep or faint in the same session, the contract remains valid because they are not considered separated. The session continues until they physically leave.
This is based on the principle that separation requires a deliberate or physical departure from the place of contract.

Selling Gold for Silver by Guess (Mujazafah)
Ruling on guess sale of gold for silver

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling gold for silver by guess and identify the condition of hand-to-hand receipt in the session.

Core Idea
In sarf, equality of weight is waived when species differ, but hand-to-hand receipt remains mandatory.

This lesson examines a key exception in Hanafi sarf law: selling gold for silver by estimate (mujazafah). While ribawi rules normally require exact equality for same species, when two different ribawi items are exchanged, equality is not a condition. However, the requirement of immediate exchange (taqabud) in the session is still wajib. Understanding this helps clarify the boundaries of riba prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is selling gold for silver without exact measurement (mujazafah). The ruling: it is permissible (ja'iz).
2
Locate the dalil (reasoning)
The proof is: 'because equality in it is not a condition' (li-anna al-musawata fihi ghayr mashrutah). This is because gold and silver are different species, so the condition of equality (mumathalah) is not required.
3
Identify the remaining condition
Though equality is waived, the condition of hand-to-hand receipt (taqabud) in the session (majlis) still applies. Note: Sarf requires immediate exchange regardless of equality.
If it were gold for gold or silver for silver, both equality and receipt would be required.
4
Distinguish from same-species exchange
In same-species sarf, both equality and immediate receipt are required. Here, due to different species, only receipt is required.
5
Practical implication
This ruling allows estimate transactions in currency exchange where exact weighing is impractical, as long as both parties exchange the items in the same sitting.

Understanding this ruling sharpens your ability to apply the rules of sarf (currency exchange) to composite transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two components
Determine the two items being sold: the sword (a non-currency commodity) and the silver ornament (treated as currency for the exchange). Then note the overall nature of the contract.
The sale involves both a commodity (sword) and a currency-like item (silver ornament), which invokes sarf rules.
2
Apply the separability condition
Check whether the silver ornament can be removed from the sword without causing damage. The ruling differs based on separability: if only with damage, the ornament cannot be delivered intact; if without damage, delivery is possible. Both cases lead to the same basic ruling but for different reasons.
Inability to deliver without damage makes the ornament sale impossible due to inability to fulfill delivery.
3
Incorporate the sarf rule of immediate delivery
Recognize that exchanging silver (the ornament portion) for silver (price) requires hand-to-hand delivery (taqabud) in the same session. If the ornament is sold separately, this condition cannot be met because the ornament is still attached.
This applies regardless of separability; the ornament sale is always void because immediate delivery of the ornament as a separate item is not possible in the context of the sale.
4
Apply the quantity condition for the price silver
If the separated silver (the price paid) is equal to or less than the silver in the ornament, or unknown, the entire sale is invalid. This is to prevent riba (usury) in the exchange of same-type currency.
The condition ensures that the sale does not become an unequal exchange of silver without immediate delivery.

The Principle of Selling Currency with Other Items
States the rule for sarf when currency is sold with another item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for a mixed sale involving currency and distinguish when each condition applies based on the type of currency.

Core Idea
When selling currency with a mixed-goods item, two conditions apply only if the price and the currency content are the same type.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle (dabit) governing mixed sales where currency (gold or silver) is sold together with an item that contains the same type of currency, such as a silver-plated vessel or gold-embroidered cloth. Understanding this principle is essential to avoid riba (usury) in such transactions. The rule distinguishes between same-type and different-type currency, each with different conditions for validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the item
Determine what is being sold: the price (currency) and the merchandise (item containing some currency). For example, silver coins for a silver-plated jug.
The item is described as mufaddad (silver-plated) or muzarkash (gold-embroidered).
2
Determine the type of the price currency
Note the kind of currency offered as payment (e.g., silver coins, gold dinars).
3
Compare the price currency with the currency content in the item
Check if the price is of the same genus as the metal in the item. For example, silver coins for a silver-plated item → same type; gold coins for a silver-plated item → different types.
4
Apply the conditions based on the comparison
If same type: require both (a) the price to exceed the value of the currency content in the item (ziyadat al-thaman) and (b) immediate hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud). If different types: only taqabud is required.
The increase condition prevents riba al-fadl; the qabd condition prevents riba al-nasi'ah.

Partial Payment and Partial Claim in Silver Sale
Details effects of partial qabd and partial istihqaq on silver vessel and nugget

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rulings on partial payment and partial third-party claim in a silver sale, and distinguish the buyer's rights when the item is a vessel versus a nugget.

Core Idea
Partial payment creates shared ownership; a third-party claim gives an option only if splitting harms value.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses two distinct scenarios in a silver sale (sarf): first, when the buyer pays only part of the price before parting; second, when a third party later proves ownership of part of the silver item. The rulings differ based on whether the defect (sharing) is caused by the buyer's own action or is inherent, and whether splitting the item affects its utility. Understanding these nuances is essential for applying sarf rules correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two situations: (1) the buyer paid only part of the price before parting, and (2) a third party claims ownership of part of the item after the sale.
2
Analyze the partial payment scenario
For scenario 1, the contract is valid for the paid portion but void for the unpaid portion because sarf requires full hand-to-hand exchange before parting. The item becomes jointly owned proportionally. Note that the defect (sharing) is supervening (tari') and caused by the buyer's failure to pay.
The buyer has no option here because the sharing stems from his own action.
3
Analyze the third-party claim scenario
For scenario 2, if a third party proves ownership of part of a silver vessel, the buyer has an option (khiyar) to either take the remainder at a proportional price or return the entire item and get a refund. This is because the forced sharing is a defect that the buyer did not consent to.
The ruling applies to a shaped vessel where splitting would harm its value.
4
Understand the 'illah for khiyar
The buyer's option in scenario 2 is granted to protect him from a defect he did not cause. The underlying reason is that a shaped vessel loses value when split, so the buyer would be harmed if forced to accept the remainder.
If the item is a silver nugget (nuqrah), splitting does not diminish its value, so no option is given.
5
Apply the distinction between vessel and nugget
When the sold item is a silver nugget (uncoined silver) and part is claimed, the buyer must take the remainder for its share without option. The key is that a nugget's value is uniform; splitting does not create a defect.
This shows that the 'illah for khiyar is the harm from splitting, not the fact of partial ownership alone.

Partial Payment in Silver Vessel Sale: Validity and Supervenient Invalidity
Ruling on partial validity in ṣarf when payment and parting are incomplete

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partial payment in a silver vessel sale and explain why the contract is partially valid and partially void using the principle of supervenient invalidity.

Core Idea
In a silver vessel sale, partial payment and parting splits the contract: valid for paid portion, void for unpaid.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in currency exchange (ṣarf) where the parties part after only part of the price has been paid. Understanding the Hanafi ruling is crucial for correctly determining ownership and avoiding disputes when transactions are incomplete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Transaction as Ṣarf
Recognize that selling a silver vessel for silver dirhams is a currency exchange subject to the rules of ṣarf, requiring hand-to-hand receipt (qabd) before parting.
Gold/silver for gold/silver is ṣarf.
2
Recall the Condition of Hand-to-Hand Receipt
In ṣarf, both countervalues must be exchanged immediately in the same sitting. Parting before full receipt invalidates the missing portion.
3
Apply the Condition to Partial Payment
When part of the price is received before parting, the condition is fulfilled for that part. The contract thus remains valid for the paid portion and becomes void for the unpaid portion.
4
Understand Supervenient Invalidity (al-Fasād al-Ṭāriʾ)
The contract initially becomes valid, but the subsequent act of parting before full receipt introduces invalidity only for the unpaid part. This invalidity does not spread to the already valid portion.
The vessel becomes jointly owned in proportion to payment.

Buyer's Option When Part of Vessel Is Claimed
Ruling on buyer's option when part of sold item is claimed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when part of a sold item is claimed by a third party and explain the reasoning based on the defect of unintended partnership.

Core Idea
When part of the sold item is validly claimed by another, the buyer may keep the remainder at a reduced price or cancel the sale — partnership is a defect.

The passage from Al-Lubab explains a specific application of khiyar al-'ayb (option of defect). When a third party proves ownership of part of the purchased item, the sale is not void but a partnership arises. Since this defect is not caused by the buyer, he has the option to either accept the remaining share at a proportional price or rescind the contract. This ruling illustrates how Hanafi fiqh balances the rights of the buyer with the claims of a true owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering event
The event is istihqāq: a third party proves ownership of part of the sold item through clear evidence (burhān).
This is not a mere claim but established proof.
2
State the buyer's two options
The buyer may either take the remaining part at a price proportional to its share of the original total price, or return the entire item to the seller.
The buyer is not forced to accept a partial ownership.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'līl)
The partnership (shirkah) that results from the claim is considered a defect ('ayb). Since this defect occurred without any action by the buyer, he has the right to choose.
The maxim: 'Harm is removed' (al-ḍarar yuzāl) underlies this.
4
Distinguish from the previous case
In the earlier ruling (not shown here), the partnership arose because the buyer only paid part of the price. There, the buyer caused the defect, so no option was granted. Here, the partnership existed from the start due to the claim.
The key variable is the origin of the partnership: buyer's action vs. external claim.
5
Apply to similar scenarios
Any situation where a third party proves ownership of part of the sold item triggers this option, as long as the buyer did not cause the defect.
For example, if a creditor proves a lien on a portion of goods.

Selling Uncoined Silver with Partial Claim
Ruling on sale of uncoined silver when part is claimed; no option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when part of sold uncoined silver is claimed and explain the reason for no option.

Core Idea
Division does not harm homogeneous silver, so no option for the buyer.

This lesson examines a specific case in sales: when a third party claims ownership of part of a sold piece of uncoined silver (nuqrah). Unlike manufactured items, uncoined silver is uniform; splitting it does not cause a defect. As a result, the buyer must accept the remaining portion at a reduced price and cannot rescind the sale. Understanding this ruling highlights how the nature of the object affects contractual rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Recognize that the sale involves uncoined silver (nuqrah), not a manufactured object. Then identify the event: a third party successfully claims a portion of that silver after the sale.
The ruling differs if the object is a vessel or other non-homogeneous item.
2
State the ruling
The buyer takes the remaining silver at its proportionate share of the price. He has no option (khiyar) to return it or reject the transaction.
The sale remains valid for the unclaimed portion.
3
Identify the underlying reason (illa)
The reason is that division (tab‘id) does not harm the uncoined silver. Because it is homogeneous, splitting it does not reduce its value or create a defect.
This principle is expressed as 'la yadurruha al-tab‘id'.
4
Contrast with the previous case
Compare with the case of a silver vessel: if part is claimed, the buyer has an option because a vessel loses value when divided. The distinction lies in the object's nature.
This shows how the Hanafi school applies the same rule based on the illa of harm.

Splitting a Dirham: Unity of Contract and Riba
Intra-Hanafi dispute on contract unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a split transaction involving fulus and a fractional dirham, distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions, and apply the rule of ṣafqa wāḥida (single contract).

Core Idea
If one contract combines a valid exchange with riba, the whole is invalid per Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn allow partial validity.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about a transaction where a person gives a dirham to a money-changer and requests both fulus and a fractional dirham (deficient in weight). Is the entire sale invalid because riba taints part of it? Or can the valid part stand alone? Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi rule on the unity of the contract (ṣafqa wāḥida) and its exceptions when the contract is effectively separated.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A person gives a money-changer a dirham and says: 'Give me with half of it fulus, and with half of it a half dirham except a grain (i.e., slightly less than half a dirham).'
The half dirham deficiency creates riba (unequal exchange of silver).
2
Identify the riba issue
The exchange of a half dirham (full weight) for a half dirham except a grain is ribawi because it is an unequal exchange of the same genus (silver for silver). This renders that part of the transaction invalid.
Fulus (copper coins) are not of the same genus as silver, so exchanging fulus for part of the dirham is valid.
3
Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa rules that the entire transaction is invalid (fāsid) because it is a single contract (ṣafqa wāḥida). The invalidity from the riba part spreads to the whole—'the invalid part corrupts the entire contract' (yashīʿ al-fasād).
His reasoning: the contract is one unbroken transaction; you cannot separate it.
4
Sahibayn's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the fulus part is valid, and only the remainder is invalid. They consider the transaction as effectively two separate exchanges—the fulus exchange is independent and not corrupted by the riba in the other part.
They argue that the invalidity does not spread because the parts are separable in effect.
5
Resolution with repeated wording
If the person repeats the words (e.g., 'Give me fulus' then separately 'Give me the half dirham except a grain'), Abu Hanifa agrees with the Sahibayn because the unity of the contract is broken. The fatwa follows the Sahibayn's view in practice, especially when the transaction is implicitly separate.
The commentator notes that if the wording is repeated, Abu Hanifa's answer is the same as theirs.

Ruling on a Sale with Mixed Currency: The Principle of Splitting Transactions
Explaining a ruling on a sale where payment includes fulus and a dirham minus a grain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sale combining fulus and a fractional dirham payment, and explain the legal reasoning that splits the transaction into two independent sales.

Core Idea
A sale combining fulus and a fractional dirham is valid because the repetition of 'give' splits it into two independent exchanges.

The passage from Kitab al-Sarf in al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a nuanced transaction: a buyer requests half a dirham's worth of fulus (copper coins) and a half dirham minus a grain (a slightly smaller amount). At first glance, this complex pricing might seem invalid due to uncertainty (gharar) or riba. However, the Hanafi school validates it by analyzing the language used: the repetition of the word 'give' (أعطني) indicates two separate offers, turning one transaction into two distinct sales. This ruling exemplifies the Hanafi approach of scrutinizing wording to preserve contractual validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction components
Note the two distinct requests: (1) fulus worth half a dirham, and (2) a half dirham minus a grain. The total consideration is one full dirham.
The half dirham minus a grain is a fractional amount, creating a pricing discrepancy.
2
Recognize the legal issue
The non-standard pricing could raise concerns about uncertainty (gharar) or riba al-fadl. The Hanafi ruling must address these potential defects.
In sarf, immediate exchange (taqabud) is required; the ruling also assumes this condition is met for each split part.
3
Apply the principle of multi-sale splitting
The repetition of the word 'give' in the buyer's statement splits the single utterance into two separate offers. Each offer constitutes an independent sale contract.
This is a linguistic analysis: the wording indicates two distinct transactions.
4
Determine the countervalue for each sale
In the first sale, fulus are exchanged for half a dirham. In the second sale, the half dirham minus a grain is exchanged for its equivalent (i.e., half dirham minus a grain). The remaining grain from the full dirham is effectively the price for the fulus.
The total payment of one dirham is allocated accordingly.
5
Conclude the ruling
Since the sales are independent, each is evaluated on its own merit. If one sale turns out to be invalid (e.g., due to a defect), the other remains valid.
This is the key practical consequence: partial invalidity does not void the entire contract.

Ruling on joint pledge when two creditors share one collateral

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joint pledge when two creditors share one collateral and apply it to divisible and indivisible property.

Core Idea
When two creditors share one pledge, each holds the entire item — not a share — until all debts are paid.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with a practical scenario: a debtor pledges a single item to two creditors. The Hanafi ruling clarifies that the pledge is not split; instead, each creditor's claim attaches to the whole item. The ruling distinguishes between divisible and indivisible property and touches on an internal Hanafi disagreement about liability when one creditor takes possession of the entire collateral.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two creditors (murtahinan) share one pledged item (rahn) from a single debtor (rahin).
The debtor owes two separate debts secured by the same collateral.
2

Each creditor's claim covers the entire item, not just a proportional share. The liability (daman) is for the share of his debt from the item.
The entire pledged item is security for both debts simultaneously.
3

If the item is indivisible (e.g., a car), and one debt is paid, the entire item remains with the other creditor until his debt is satisfied. If divisible (e.g., grain), each detains half.
The key factor is the nature of the pledged asset.
4

The marginal note mentions that according to Abu Hanifa, if one creditor takes possession of the entire item, he is liable (dhamin) for its value, contrary to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
The mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa.

Sale Conditional on Pledge: Ruling and Seller's Options
Ruling on validity of sale conditional on pledge or guarantor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sale conditional on a pledge or guarantor, and apply the seller's options when the buyer refuses.

Core Idea
A sale with pledge condition is valid but non-binding; the seller may rescind if the buyer refuses.

In Hanafi fiqh, a seller may stipulate that the buyer provide a pledge or guarantor for the price. Such a condition is considered valid because it supports the contract's purpose of securing payment. However, since pledge and guarantee are gratuitous acts, the buyer cannot be forced to comply. This lesson explains the ruling and the seller's recourse when the buyer refuses.

Guided Path

Identify the act and ruling
Recognize that this is a sale (bay') with an additional condition (shart) requiring the buyer to pledge a specific item or provide a guarantor. The Hanafi ruling is that the sale is valid (ja'iz) and the condition is permissible.
The condition is 'suited to the contract' (mulaim li al-'aqd) because it secures the obligation.
2
Understand the legal nature of the condition
The condition is valid but not enforceable (ghayr lazim). The buyer cannot be compelled to deliver the pledge because rahn is a gratuitous contract (tabarru') on the part of the pledgor.
The commentary cites al-Jawhara: 'no compulsion in gratuitous acts'.
3
Identify the seller's khiyar (option)
If the buyer refuses to deliver the pledged item, the seller has the option to either accept the sale without the pledge or rescind the entire contract. This khiyar arises due to the loss of the desired condition.
The seller's option is not unlimited; it is exercised upon the buyer's refusal.
4
Note exceptions preventing rescission
The buyer can avoid rescission by either paying the price immediately (halan) or paying the value of the pledged item, which then substitutes as the pledge.
Paying the price achieves the purpose (tahsil al-maqsud) of securing payment.

Ruling on Acknowledging a House While Excluding the Building

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The building is included in meaning – exclusion of a subsidiary part is void.

When you acknowledge a house, does it include the building? The Hanafi school says yes. Even if you explicitly say 'except the building,' the exclusion fails. Why? Because the building is implicitly part of the house in meaning, even if not spelled out. This lesson unpacks that logic through a clear analogy with a ring and its gem, and examines when exclusions are valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What does the Hanafi school rule when a person acknowledges a house but excludes the building? The text says both house and building go to the recipient. The exclusion is void (batil).
The acknowledgment itself is valid, but the exclusion clause is ineffective.
2
Understand the reasoning
Why is the exclusion ineffective? Because the building is included in the house in meaning (ma‘nan), not in wording (laẓan). Istithnā' only works for what the speech literally covers. Acknowledgment is a verbal action (tasarruf lafẓī).
The word 'house' (dār) does not explicitly mention 'building' (binā').
3

The text analogizes to the gem (fass) in a ring and the palm tree (nakhla) in an orchard – they are included as subsidiaries (taba‘an). Apply the same analogy to building in house. The illa is: the building is part of the house in meaning.
This is qiyas jaliy – the illa is clear.
4
Distinguish valid exclusions
If he says 'except a third of the house' or 'except a room', that is valid because those are explicitly covered by the word 'house' in wording.
The distinction turns on whether the excluded part is lexically encompassed.
5
Apply to new cases
Given the principle, determine whether other components (e.g., foundation, roof, walls) are included. They are subsidiaries, so they are included. Only if the exclusion specifies a part that is explicitly within the linguistic scope of 'house' is it valid.
Extension of the rule to similar scenarios.

Acknowledging Building and Land: The Distinction Between ‘Arṣa and ‘Arḍ
Distinguishes between acknowledging building and land separately

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between acknowledging a building along with ‘arṣa (plot) versus ‘arḍ (land) and state the legal consequence for each case.

Core Idea
The term ‘arṣa excludes the building; acknowledging ‘arḍ includes the building as subsidiary.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with a specific scenario in acknowledgments: when someone says 'the building is mine and the land is for someone else.' The ruling depends entirely on which word is used for land—‘arṣa or ‘arḍ. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether the building goes to the recipient or stays with the declarant. We will analyze the reasoning behind this, based on the principle that what is subsidiary follows the principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) concerning a building and land. The ruling is that the acknowledgment is valid and effective: the building belongs to the declarant and the land to the recipient, but only if the correct term is used.
The hukm is 'sahih' (valid) – meaning the statement has legal effect.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a qiyas (analogy) to the case of acknowledging a house. Just as acknowledging a house includes the building because it is part of the house, acknowledging ‘arḍ (land) includes the building because the building is subsidiary to the land.
This is a 'dalil aqli' – rational analogy based on the relationship between principal and subsidiary.

The word ‘arṣa specifically denotes the bare plot without any building. So saying 'the building is mine and the ‘arṣa is for so-and-so' is like saying: the empty land (without building) is for him, and the building remains mine. In contrast, ‘arḍ (land) is a term that, by custom, includes whatever is on it (like buildings). So acknowledging ‘arḍ includes the building.
The distinction hinges on the linguistic and customary meaning of the terms.

If the declarant uses ‘arḍ instead of ‘arṣa, the building goes to the recipient. The principle is: 'al-bina’ taba‘ li al-arḍ' (the building is subsidiary to the land). This is derived from the general rule that acknowledgment of the principal includes the subsidiary.
This shows that in Hanafi fiqh, legal effects are sensitive to the precise wording used.

Dispute on Confession 'Thawb fi 'Asharat Athwab'
Intra-Hanafi dispute on interpretation of ambiguous confession phrase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the ambiguous confession 'thawb fi 'asharat athwab' and explain the role of custom (adah) in resolving the intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
When custom renders an interpretation impossible, the law treats it as impossible — only one cloth is owed.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over the meaning of a confession: 'I owe him a cloth in ten cloths.' The key is whether 'fi' means literal containment or an inversion. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rely on custom to rule that ten cloths cannot be a container for one, so only one cloth is due. Muhammad al-Shaybani allows alternative readings, obligating eleven. The relied-upon position follows the two elders.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed phrase and its ambiguity
The phrase 'thawb fi 'asharat athwab' can be read as 'a cloth inside ten cloths' or as an inversion meaning 'ten cloths inside a cloth'. The ambiguity triggers the dispute.
The word 'fi' (in) is the source of ambiguity.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf and their proof
They hold that only one cloth is owed. Their proof is custom (adah): it is not customary for ten cloths to be a container for one, and what is impossible by custom is like impossible in reality.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on adah.
3
State Muhammad al-Shaybani's alternative interpretation and proof
Muhammad argues that eleven cloths are owed. He offers two possibilities: the phrase could mean precious cloths wrapped in ten, making the container interpretation possible, or it could be an inversion (taqdim wa ta'khir) meaning 'ten cloths in a cloth', where the one cloth is the container.
Muhammad's interpretation relies on linguistic possibility rather than custom.
4
Analyze the tarjih (preference) by later scholars
Later Hanafi authorities like al-Nasafi and al-Mahbubi upheld the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. They considered Muhammad's reading to be a strained departure from the apparent meaning and from established custom.
The tarjih is based on consistency with customary usage and avoiding unnecessary complication.
5
Determine the mufta bihi ruling and practical consequence
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that only one cloth is due. The practical difference between the two views is significant: one cloth versus eleven cloths.
Students should remember that the mufta bihi is the position of the two elders.

Confession of Defective Item: Believed with Oath
Ruling on confession of a defective item with rational proof and intra-Hanafi difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the confession of a defective item, explain the rational proof, and distinguish the position of Abu Yusuf from the majority.

Core Idea
A confessor's claim of defect is accepted with oath; ghasb does not require soundness.

Imagine someone confesses to usurping a cloth but then produces a damaged one. Is he believed? The Hanafi school answers that he is, with an oath, because usurpation is not limited to sound items. This lesson explores the ruling, its proof, and a disagreement within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
We have a person who confesses to usurping a cloth (ghasb) and then produces a defective cloth, claiming it is the same item. The question is whether his claim about the defect is accepted.
The item is defective, not sound.
2
State the Ruling
The ruling is that his statement is accepted with his oath. The judge or claimant cannot require him to bring a sound item.
Oath is required to confirm his honesty.
3
Understand the Proof
The proof is rational: 'ghasb does not require soundness' (لأن الغصب لا يختص بالسليم). The act of usurpation is defined by taking property, not by its quality. So a defective item can be the subject of ghasb.
This is a general principle: ghasb applies to any property.
4
Apply to Dirhams and Wadiah
The same ruling applies if he confesses to dirhams (usurpation or trust) and says they are counterfeit, whether he says this immediately or later. The principle is consistent: the nature of the transaction does not require high quality.
The statement is considered a clarification of the type, not a contradiction.
5
Note the Intra-Hanafi Difference
Abu Yusuf distinguishes: if the claim of defect is made separately (munfasilan) after the confession, he is not believed, by analogy with the price in sales. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) accept it in both cases.
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view.

Commentary
This passage provides a ruling and its rational proof. The scenario: a person confesses to having usurped (ghasb) a cloth, but when he produces it, it is defective. He claims that this is the cloth he usurped. The ruling: his statement is accepted with an oath. The proof is that ghasb (usurpation) is not confined to sound items; one can usurp any item regardless of quality. Therefore, the defective condition does not contradict the confession. The same applies if he confesses to dirhams and then says they are counterfeit: he is believed, whether he says it immediately or later, because ghasb and wadi'ah (trust) do not require the items to be of high quality. The statement is seen as a clarification of the type, not a contradiction. However, Abu Yusuf has a different opinion: if the claim of defectiveness is made separately (not at the time of confession), he is not believed, by analogy with the price (thaman) in a sales contract. The relied-upon position is not explicitly given here, but the text states 'qauluhu qauluhu' (his statement is accepted) without qualification, suggesting the general acceptance. The mufta bihi is likely the acceptance in both cases, as the commentary of al-Lubab usually follows the majority.

Interpretation of 'Khamsa fi Khamsa' in Confession
Ruling on ambiguous phrase in confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for the ambiguous confession 'five in five' and apply the principle that the confessor's plausible intention determines the amount owed.

Core Idea
The phrase 'five in five' binds either five (multiplication) or ten (addition), based on the confessor's plausible intention.

Confessions in Islamic law are taken seriously, but when the wording is ambiguous, the confessor's intent becomes decisive. This short passage addresses the phrase 'five in five' – does it mean 5×5=25, 5+5=10, or something else? The Hanafi answer protects the confessor from over-liability by allowing his plausible linguistic intention, provided the language can bear it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous phrase
The confession is 'I owe him five in five' (khamsa fi khamsa). The word 'fi' (in) is ambiguous: it can mean multiplication or addition.
2
Recognize the two possible linguistic interpretations
Multiplication: 'five in five' as five times five, but the text says multiplication does not increase money, only parts. Addition: 'five with five' meaning ten.
The word 'fi' can mean 'with' (ma'a) in Arabic.
3
Apply the confessor's intention
If the confessor explains he meant multiplication, he owes five. If he meant addition, he owes ten. The wording can bear both, so his stated intention is accepted.
This follows the general Hanafi principle of interpreting ambiguous confessions according to the confessor's plausible intent.
4
State the ruling
The ruling is straightforward: multiplication → 5, addition → 10. The obligation is determined by the confessor's explanation.

Commentary
This passage defines the ruling for an ambiguous confession using the phrase 'five in five'. The word 'fi' (in) can be interpreted as multiplication or addition. If the confessor explains that he meant multiplication (darb), the binding amount is five (one five), not twenty-five. The reasoning is that multiplication does not create new money; it only multiplies the units. In Hanafi jurisprudence, the focus is on the intent of the confessor. If he intended addition (khamsa ma'a khamsa), the amount is ten because 'fi' can also mean 'with'. The ruling is straightforward: the confessor's intended meaning, if plausible linguistically, determines the obligation. This follows the general principle that ambiguous phrases in confessions are interpreted according to the confirmer's intention, but only if the phrase can bear that meaning.
