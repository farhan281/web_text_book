Ruling on prohibition of granting communal resources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the Imam is prohibited from granting resources essential to all Muslims, and explain the rationale based on communal necessity.

Core Idea
Essential communal resources like salt mines cannot be privatized by the Imam.

You already know that the Imam can grant 'dead land' to individuals. But can he grant a salt mine or a public well? This lesson clarifies a critical limitation: what is needed by the entire Muslim community must remain public.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the Imam granting (iqta') a resource; the ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuz).
This is a prohibition (haram).
2
Identify the object of the grant
The object is 'what Muslims are in need of' (ma la ghina lil-muslimin 'anhu), exemplified by salt mines and public wells.
These are resources of general necessity.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason is the general need (ghina al-Muslimin) which prevents monopolization and privatization.
The text says 'due to what we have mentioned,' referring to the previous principle.
4
Note the implication
This ruling limits the Imam's discretionary authority: he cannot grant any resource; communal necessities are exempt.

Ruling on path rights in successive land revivals

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When land is revived in succession around a central plot, the first reviver's path lies in the fourth reviver's land.

Imagine four people each revive a piece of land around a single central plot. The first reviver is left without a path to his land. Where does his path go? This lesson from al-Durar provides an elegant solution based on implied consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Four people sequentially revive (ihya) land surrounding a central plot. Each revival is adjacent to the previous one.
The revival is of mawat (dead land).
2
Recognize the problem
The first reviver's land becomes surrounded by the second, third, and fourth revivals. He loses his original access path.
The first reviver did not reserve a path initially.
3
Understand the implied consent
By his silence (sukut) as the others revived the surrounding plots, the first reviver implicitly agreed to the new arrangement.
His silence is interpreted as consent.
4
Apply the ruling
His right of way is now in the fourth reviver's land, because the fourth revival completed the enclosure and effectively created a new path.
The text says 'his path is in the fourth land'.

Harim of a Well: General Principle and Camel Well Measurement
Ruling on protected area around a well

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the harim of a well and apply the 40-cubit measurement for camel wells.

Core Idea
The digger of a well owns its harim: for camel wells, 40 cubits from each side.

When a well is dug in unowned wilderness, the digger acquires a protected area (harim) essential for its use. For camel watering wells, this harim is fixed at forty cubits from each side. This lesson clarifies the general principle and the specific measurement, including a difference of opinion about the Imam's permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its ruling
The act is digging a well in the wilderness (bariyyah). The ruling: the digger owns its harim (protected area) from all sides.
Harim is defined as the area necessary for full benefit of the well.
2
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on permission
Abu Hanifa requires the Imam's permission for digging; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider digging itself a form of ihya (revival) and do not require permission.
This difference is inherited from earlier discussions on reviving dead land.
3
Note the specific ruling for camel wells (al-'atan)
If the well is for watering and resting camels, the harim is specifically forty cubits from each side, not just a customary amount.
The term 'atan refers to the place where camels kneel and are watered.
4
Understand the rationale for the measurement
The measurement accounts for soft ground where water may seep beyond the immediate well; it ensures the well's full function.
Some said forty from all sides (total), but the correct view is from each side.

This measure ensures the well's benefit without encroachment.

The Protected Area (Hareem) of Wells: Rulings and Internal Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hareem of nadih well

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hareem dimensions for different well types and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the nadih well.

Core Idea
The hareem of a well depends on its type and need; for camel-driven wells, Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on the extent.

In the context of reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat), the Hanafi school grants the digger of a well a protected area (hareem) necessary for its use. This lesson examines the varying dimensions for different well types and focuses on an internal dispute: for a well irrigated by a camel-driven waterwheel, does the hareem extend 40 or 60 cubits? The resolution of this dispute illustrates how later Hanafi authorities weigh evidence and prefer certain views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Note that whoever digs a well in dead wilderness owns its hareem. This is a right attached to the revival of land.
Hareem is defined by functional need.

Distinguish well types

3
Note the agreed dimension for ‘itan
For bi'r al-‘itan, the hareem is 40 cubits from each side. This is agreed upon.
Some said total, but correct is per side.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute for nadih
Abu Hanifa: 40 cubits (analogy with ‘itan). Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): 60 cubits (greater need due to camel space).
This is the core dispute of the lesson.
5
Evaluate the preferred view
Later scholars like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi preferred Abu Hanifa's view (40 cubits). Also note the need-based view from Mukhtarat al-Nawazil as a sound alternative.
The mufta bihi is 40 cubits, but need can override.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the rulings on the hareem (protected area) around wells and springs in the context of reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). The passage type is an expansion of the matn by al-Quduri, presenting the ruling and then clarifying details with internal Hanafi disputes.

Yet he also cites from 'Mukhtarat al-Nawazil' that the hareem is determined by need, which is considered the soundest view.

Third, for a flowing spring, the hareem is three hundred cubits from each side. This large area is needed for irrigation channels and reservoirs. The author refutes a report from al-Tahawi claiming five hundred cubits, calling it baseless.

The passage illustrates how the Hanafi school uses custom and need to determine property rights in dead land, and shows the internal methodology of weighing evidence.

عطن (‘iṭan): A well where camels are made to kneel and water is drawn by hand.

General Permission for a Slave to Trade
Ruling on validity of slave's transactions with general permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a slave's transactions when given general trade permission and identify which transactions are included.

Core Idea
General permission to trade makes a slave's commercial transactions valid by agreement.

The following passage from al-Lubab (Sharh al-Quduri) introduces the concept of the permitted slave (al-ma'dhun). It clarifies that when a master gives his slave open-ended authorization to engage in trade, the slave's dealings are valid across all commercial activities. This ruling is agreed upon in the Hanafi school and stems from the general scope of the word 'trade'. Let's examine the legal definitions and the specific transactions that become permissible.

1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon: a master giving his slave general permission to trade. The ruling is that the slave's transactions are valid (sahih) by agreement.
2
Understand the definition of idhn
The passage defines 'idhn' linguistically as informing and technically as removal of restriction (hajr) and dropping of the master's right. This establishes the legal capacity granted.
The definition is from al-Hidayah
3
List the permitted transactions
The text enumerates specific transactions: buying, selling, pawning (both giving and taking), and renting/hiring. These are considered practices of merchants.
The list is illustrative, not exhaustive
4
Note the basis for generality
The ruling applies because the term 'trade' (تجارة) is general and covers all types. The permission is not restricted to a specific type.
5
Confirm the authority level
The ruling is 'by agreement' (اتفاقاً), indicating it is the established position in Zahir al-Riwayah and not subject to intra-Hanafi dispute.

The Legal Definition of Idhn and Types of Permission for Trade
Opening of the Book of the Permitted Slave: defining permission and its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general permission, permission for a type, and permission for a specific thing, and state whether the slave becomes a ma'dhun in each case.

Core Idea
General permission or permission for a type makes a slave a ma'dhun in all trades; permission for a specific thing does not.

This passage opens the Book of the Permitted Slave (Kitab al-Ma'dhun) in Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri. It defines the legal concept of idhn (permission) and immediately presents the three scenarios a master may use when permitting his slave to trade. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for determining the slave's legal capacity in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the legal definition of idhn
Read the definition: idhn linguistically means 'informing' and legally means 'lifting the restriction (hajr) and dropping the right of the master.' This sets the foundation: permission removes the master's control over the slave's trade.
The definition is from Al-Hidayah, a core Hanafi text.
2
Identify the three types of permission
The passage presents three cases: (1) general permission ('I permit you to trade' without specification), (2) permission for a type (e.g., 'only in wheat'), and (3) permission for a specific thing (e.g., 'buy this garment').
The second and third are easily confused; note the difference between a type (jins) and a specific item (mu'ayyan).
3
Determine the legal outcome for each type
For general permission: the slave is a ma'dhun and his transactions are valid in all trades. For permission for a type: surprisingly, the slave also becomes a ma'dhun in all trades. For permission for a specific thing: the slave is NOT a ma'dhun; it is mere utilization (istikhdam).
The key is that permission for a type still lifts restriction generally, while a specific task is not considered permission to trade.
4
Understand the consequence for acknowledgment (iqrar)
The ma'dhun slave's acknowledgment of debts, usurpations, and deposits is valid because it is a necessary requisite of trade. Without this, people would avoid dealing with him.
This applies whether or not the slave already has debts, as long as the acknowledgment relates to trade.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of the Permitted Slave (al-Ma'dhun) in Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri. The author first explains the suitability of this topic after the chapter on reviving dead land: giving permission to a slave or a minor is a form of 'revival' of their capacity. The definition of 'idhn' (permission) is then given: linguistically it means informing, and legally it means lifting the restriction (hajr) and dropping the right (of the master to prevent the slave's transactions), as in Al-Hidayah. The main ruling is that if a master gives his slave general permission to trade, without specifying a particular type, then the slave's transactions in all types of trade are valid (ja'iz) by agreement (ittifaqan) because the word 'trade' is general. Consequently, the slave may buy, sell, give in pledge, take in pledge, hire, and rent, as these are common practices of traders. Furthermore, even if the master restricts permission to a specific type, e.g., only wheat trading, the slave becomes a permitted slave (ma'dhun) in all types of trade, because the permission is considered a lifting of the restriction and a manifestation of the slave's ownership, which cannot be limited. However, if the master permits the slave for a specific thing, like buying a specific garment or food, then the slave is not considered a 'ma'dhun' at all; it is merely a utilization (istikhdam) of the slave's service, and if it were to grant the status of a permitted slave, the door of utilization would be closed. Finally, the acknowledgment (iqrar) of the permitted slave regarding debts, usurpations (ghusub), and deposits is valid because it is a necessary corollary of trade; otherwise, people would avoid dealings. This ruling applies whether or not the slave himself had debts at the time of acknowledgment, as long as the acknowledgment pertains to trade matters. A variant from the Al-Quduri manuscript adds that in case of specific permission, the slave is not a permitted slave except for that specific item.

Key Terms
إذن (idhn): Linguistically: informing; legally: lifting the restriction (hajr) and dropping the right of the master over the slave's transactions.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave who has been given permission by his master to engage in trade, entitling him to valid commercial transactions.
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment by the slave of a debt, usurpation, or deposit, which is valid because it is a requisite of trade.

The Legal Capacity of the Permitted Slave (Ma'dhun)
Defines scope of permission and prohibitions for a ma'dhun slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the scope of a ma'dhun's permission and identify which transactions are valid or invalid, applying the Hanafi principle that permission removes hajr entirely.

Core Idea
Permission for trade removes all restrictions and grants full trading capacity; gratuitous acts remain prohibited.

This lesson examines the legal capacity of a slave whom the master permits to trade (ma'dhun). The Hanafi school treats such permission as a removal of the master's guardianship (hajr), thereby revealing the slave's own ownership. We will explore how general permission cannot be limited to specific trade items, why the slave's acknowledgment of debts is valid, and which transactions he cannot perform, including marriage and gifts.

Guided Path
1
Define the ma'dhun and the nature of permission
Understand that permission for trade is considered a removal of hajr (restriction), not a narrow license. This is the foundational principle.
2
Distinguish general permission from specific instruction
If the master says 'I permit you to trade in wheat only', the slave becomes fully permitted for all trade. But if he says 'buy this specific garment', the slave is not a ma'dhun because it is a mere istikhdam (use).
The key is whether the master intended a general removal of hajr or a specific task.
3
Analyze the validity of iqrar (acknowledgment)
The ma'dhun's acknowledgment of debts and usurpations is valid because it is an accessory of trade. If it were invalid, no one would deal with him.
There is a distinction between acknowledgment in health and in terminal illness.
4
Identify prohibited acts
Marriage, kitaba, emancipation, and gifts (except small food/hospitality) are prohibited because they are gratuitous acts outside the scope of trade.
The prohibition on marriage applies to both the ma'dhun's own marriage and marrying off his slaves.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on marrying off female slaves
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad forbid it; Abu Yusuf permits it. The relied-upon position is the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
The practical consequence: if a ma'dhun marries off his female slave, the marriage is invalid according to the mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from the book of al-Ma'dhun (the permitted slave) in the Hanafi school sets out the legal capacity of a slave whom the master has permitted to engage in trade. The commentator (al-Maydani) expands on the matn of al-Quduri. The key principle is that permission for trade effectively removes the master's guardianship (hajr) and reveals the slave's own ownership capacity. Therefore, if the master says, 'I permit you to trade in wheat only,' the slave becomes fully permitted for all types of trade, because permission is a blanket removal of restriction and cannot be limited by the master's specification. However, if the master says, 'Buy this specific garment for me' or 'buy this food to eat,' that is not a general permission but merely a specific instruction (istikhdam), and the slave does not become a legally permitted trader. As for the slave's acknowledgment of debts and usurpations (ghasb), it is valid because it is a necessary corollary of trade; otherwise no one would deal with him. This applies as long as the acknowledgment is made during his health; if made during his terminal illness, the debts of health take precedence, just as with a free person. The slave is prohibited from marrying himself or marrying off his own slaves (male or female) because marriage is not trade. On marrying off his female slave, there is a difference: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad forbid it absolutely, while Abu Yusuf permits it. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to later authorities (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, etc.) is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. The slave also cannot make a kitaba contract (contract of gradual emancipation), cannot emancipate a slave for money (or without money), and cannot give gifts (with or without compensation), because all these are gratuitous acts not included in the permission for trade. The only exception is giving a small amount of food or offering hospitality to someone one feeds—this is allowed as a minor customary gift that does not harm the creditors' rights.

Key Terms
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave whom the master has permitted to engage in trade, thereby removing his restriction (hajr) and granting him legal capacity for commercial transactions.
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or confession of a debt or liability. For a ma'dhun, it is valid in matters related to trade.
غصوب (ghusub): Usurpations; wrongful appropriation of property. The ma'dhun's acknowledgment of such is valid.

The Limits of a Ma'dhun's Permission: Prohibited Acts and an Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Lists prohibited actions for a ma'dhun slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibited acts for a ma'dhun slave and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding marrying off his female slave.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun cannot engage in tabarru', only trade; scholars differ on whether marrying off his female slave is a donation.

A ma'dhun is a slave permitted by his master to trade, but this permission is restricted to trade-related acts. Acts of donation (tabarru') like marriage, manumission, and gifting are generally prohibited. However, there is a well-known difference within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that he cannot even marry off his female slave, while Abu Yusuf permits it. Later authorities favor the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding this dispute illuminates how Hanafi jurists reason about the scope of permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ma'dhun's capacity
Recall that a ma'dhun is a slave given permission to trade, limiting his acts to trade and its necessities. Non-trade acts require separate permission.
The permission (idhn) is the key determinant.
2
List the prohibited acts
From the text: marriage (for himself or his slaves), kitabah (contract of manumission), manumission for consideration, gifts with or without compensation. These are all considered tabarru'.
The exception: small food gifts and hospitality are allowed as trade necessities.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The prohibition is because these acts are donations (tabarru') from beginning to end, or at least at inception, and thus fall outside the permission for trade.
Even gifts with compensation are considered donations because the compensation is not the primary purpose.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the majority in Zahir al-Riwayah) prohibit the ma'dhun from marrying off his female slave. Abu Yusuf permits it, considering it a trade-related act. Later authorities (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, etc.) preferred the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, making the mufta bihi the permissive position.
The dispute shows ta'lil at work: Abu Yusuf may view marriage of a slave as a potential trade benefit.
5
Apply the ruling
For fatwa today, the ma'dhun may marry off his female slave (following the preferred view), but still cannot marry himself, manumit, or give gifts beyond small food offers.
The exception for hospitality remains.

How Debts of a Ma'dhun Slave Are Collected
Explains how debts of a ma'dhun are collected from his person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mechanism for collecting debts from a ma'dhun and identify the conditions and exceptions.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun's debts are attached to his person; he can be sold once if the master is present, otherwise he must earn to pay.

This passage explains the Hanafi ruling on the settlement of debts incurred by a ma'dhun slave in trade. Since the slave is authorized to trade (ma'dhun), his debts are binding on his person (raqabah). However, the remedy for creditors is limited — the slave is sold only once, and only under specific conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debt attachment
The text states that debts of the ma'dhun are attached to his person (raqabah). This means his person can be sold to satisfy the debts.
The attachment is like an encumbrance on the slave.
2
Note the sale mechanism
The judge sells the ma'dhun at the request of the creditors. This is the primary remedy for the creditors.
The creditors do not sell him themselves; it is done by the judge.
3
Recognize condition of master's presence
The sale only occurs if the master is present. If the master is absent, the slave cannot be sold.
The master is the defendant in the slave's person.

The Runaway Slave's Acknowledgment: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of iqrar after hajr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the runaway permitted slave's acknowledgment, distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
A runaway slave's acknowledgment remains valid because his actual possession persists despite interdiction.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: when a permitted slave runs away, does his acknowledgment (iqrar) of debts or trusts remain valid? The disagreement hinges on whether interdiction (hajr) removes legal capacity entirely, or whether the slave's actual possession (yad) preserves that capacity. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to reason from legal cause (illah) and identify the operative view in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and legal question
What is the scenario? A slave who was given permission to trade (al-madhun) runs away (ibaq). Does his subsequent acknowledgment of a debt, trust, or other property right in his hand remain valid?
The running away itself triggers interdiction by implication.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa says the acknowledgment is valid regarding property in his hand. His proof: the slave's hand (yad) remains in reality, and the condition for its nullification by interdiction is that the hand be free of need, which is not the case; the acknowledgment itself proves the hand exists.
Focus on the concept of 'yad haqiqiyyah' (actual possession).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the acknowledgment is not valid. Their proof: the basis for valid acknowledgment is either permission (idhn) or the hand (yad). Permission is removed by interdiction, and the hand is nullified because the hand of an interdicted person is not legally considered.
Contrast with Abu Hanifa's view: the Sahibayn treat the hand as entirely nullified.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
What changes in practice? If the slave acknowledges a debt, according to Abu Hanifa the master must pay from the slave's hand; according to the Sahibayn, the acknowledgment is void and the debt is not binding.
This affects the rights of third parties dealing with the slave.
5
Note the tarjih (preference) in the school
According to Al-Hidayah and later scholars, Abu Hanifa's view is mufta bihi (the operative position for fatwa). This is the relied-upon view.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the reasoning that actual possession endures.

Sales Between the Ma'dhun and His Master: The Condition of Market Price
Rulings on sales between an indebted ma'dhun and his master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a sale between a ma'dhun and his master is valid and apply the principle of tuhma to determine validity in new scenarios.

Core Idea
A sale between a ma'dhun and his master is valid only when the price eliminates suspicion of collusion.

This lesson examines the specific rulings for transactions between an indebted ma'dhun (a slave permitted to trade) and his master. The key factor is not the relationship itself but the presence or absence of suspicion (tuhma) that the ma'dhun might favor his master at the expense of his creditors. Understanding how the price level determines validity is essential for anyone studying Hanafi commercial law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and status
Note that the ma'dhun is specifically described as indebted (مَدْيُون). This is crucial because the suspicion of collusion matters only when the ma'dhun owes debts to third parties. The master is also known to be the owner of the slave.
If the ma'dhun were not indebted, the ruling might differ – but the text explicitly conditions the ruling on debt.
2
Parse the first scenario: ma'dhun sells to master
The text says: إذا باع العبد المأذون المديون من المولى شيئاً بمثل قيمته، أو أكثر : جاز البيع؛ لعدم التهمة. So the sale is valid if the price equals or exceeds the market value. The reason given is 'absence of suspicion' – there is no fear that the ma'dhun is favoring his master because the master is paying a fair or premium price.
The Arabic phrase 'بمثل قيمته' means 'at like value' i.e., market price.
3
Parse the first scenario: invalid condition
The text continues: فإن باعه بنقصان ولو يسيراً: لم يجز البيع؛ لتمكن التهمة. If the price is lower than market value, even slightly, the sale is invalid due to the 'presence of suspicion'. The suspicion is that the ma'dhun is unfairly benefitting his master at the expense of creditors.
The word 'يسيراً' emphasizes that any loss, no matter how small, triggers invalidity.
4
Parse the second scenario: master sells to ma'dhun
The text says: وإن باعه المولى شيئاً بمثل القيمة، أو أقل: جاز البيع. Here the sale is valid if the master charges a price equal to or lower than market value. The reason is 'absence of suspicion and appearance of benefit' – the master is not exploiting a position of power; rather, the ma'dhun (and indirectly his creditors) benefit.
The text does not explicitly state the ruling for a price above market value, but by implication and consistent logic, if the master sells at a premium, the sale would be invalid due to suspicion of exploitation.
5
Extract the underlying principle
The common thread is the avoidance of tuhma (suspicion) that could harm the ma'dhun's creditors. When the price favors the master (ma'dhun sells cheap, or master sells dear), the transaction is presumed suspect and invalid. When the price is neutral or favors the ma'dhun, it is valid.
This principle is a rational (dalil aqli) inference, not based on a specific source text, but derived from the objectives of Sharia to protect creditors.

Core Idea
A condition in sharecropping that risks excluding one party from the primary produce invalidates the contract.

The sharecropping contract (muzara'ah) requires both parties to share in the primary crop. If a stipulation creates a scenario where one party could end up with nothing from that primary produce, the contract becomes invalid (batil). Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings for a valid sharecropping contract (muzara'ah). It first states that when the contract is valid, the harvested output is shared between the parties exactly as they agreed. The default rule for straw (tibn) is that it belongs to the owner of the seed (rabb al-badhr) because it is considered the growth (nama') of his seed. However, there is a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school: the scholars of Balkh (Mashayikh Balkh) hold that the straw should also be shared between the two parties. Their reasoning is based on custom (urf) in matters not explicitly stipulated, and on the principle that the straw is subsidiary (taba') to the grain; since the subsidiary follows the original, and the original (the grain) is shared, the straw should also be shared. The standard Hanafi position rejects this and insists the straw belongs to the seed owner. Musaqah: When It Is Valid and How It Ends
Rulings on musaqah validity and termination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a valid musaqah contract, identify when it becomes invalid, and explain the effect of death on the contract.

Core Idea
Musaqah is only valid if the crop still needs work; otherwise it is an invalid contract entitling the worker to equivalent wages, and it ends upon death.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, explained by al-Maydani, lays out the foundational rules of musaqah (sharecropping of trees). It clarifies the critical condition for a valid contract—the presence of ongoing work—and the consequences of invalidity. Additionally, it details how death of either party terminates the contract but protects the worker's right to complete the work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling musaqah validity
Determine that musaqah is permissible (ja'iz) only if the fruit or crop still requires work to increase (e.g., watering, tending). If it has ended (fully ripe or harvested), it is not permissible.
The condition is 'yazīdu bil-'amal' (increases with work).
2
Recognize the invalid contract consequence
If musaqah is invalid (fasid) because the condition is not met, the worker is entitled to the equivalent wage (ujr al-mithl) for the work done, by analogy to an invalid ijarah.
This protects the worker from uncompensated labor.
3
Analyze termination by death
Musaqah is annulled by the death of either party, as it is analogous to ijarah. However, if the landowner dies, the worker may continue; if the worker dies, his heirs may continue; if both die, the worker's heirs have the choice.
The right to continue belongs to the worker or his heirs, not the landowner's heirs.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Musaqah (sharecropping of trees) with key rulings from the matn of al-Quduri, explained by al-Maydani. The first ruling distinguishes permissible from impermissible musaqah based on whether the trees or crops still require work to increase. If the fruit is still developing or the crop is green (needs watering, tending), the contract is valid because the worker's labor has effect. If the fruit is fully ripe or the crop already harvested (ended), the contract is invalid because no work remains—the worker would receive wages for nothing. The commentator clarifies that this mirrors the logic of ijarah (hire): the worker earns only for actual labor. The second ruling: if a musaqah contract is invalid (fasid), the worker still gets the equivalent market wage (ujr al-mithl), treating it like an invalid ijarah—the analogy (qiyas) is based on the common element of compensation for work. The third ruling: musaqah is annulled by the death of either party, again by analogy to ijarah, which is a non-binding contract terminated by death. The commentator then details the positions: if the landowner dies, the worker may continue the work on the land even if the heirs object; if the worker dies, his heirs may continue even if the landowner objects; if both die, the worker's heirs have the choice to continue or not because they take the worker's right. This shows the Hanafi position that the right to continue is transferable to heirs, preventing loss of labor already invested. The reference to 'al-Durar' (by al-Mawsili) adds further detail.

Key Terms
مساقاة (musaqah): A contract where a worker tends to fruit-bearing trees or crops in exchange for a share of the produce; specifically for trees (not seasonal crops like wheat) in this context.
أجر مثله (ujr al-mithl): The market-equivalent wage paid to the worker if the musaqah contract is invalid, compensating for labor already performed.
وَصْلِيَّة (wasliyyah): A grammatical term indicating that the word 'in' (إن) here is a conjunctive particle meaning 'even if', not a conditional.
