Acceptance of a Minor's Claim of Puberty
Ruling on accepting a minor's claim of puberty with qiyas to menstruation claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a minor's claim of puberty and apply the qiyas reasoning that underpins it.

Core Idea
A minor's claim of puberty is accepted on their word because it is a private matter, just like a woman's claim of menstruation.

In Hanafi law, the onset of puberty (bulugh) triggers full religious obligations. But how do we know if a child has reached puberty? This passage addresses the situation when a boy or girl on the verge of puberty claims they have already reached it. The ruling is clear: their word is accepted, provided no external evidence contradicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
Determine the scenario: a boy or girl nearing puberty (rāhaqa) whose status is ambiguous claims they have reached puberty.
2
State the ruling
The ruling: their statement is accepted (al-qawlu qawlahuma) and they are treated as adults for all legal purposes.
3
Find the proof (dalil)
The reason: this is a matter not known except from themselves. Since the external appearance does not contradict, their word is taken.
4
Note the qiyas
The ruling is analogous to a woman's claim of menstruation, which is also accepted on her word alone.
The 'illa (effective cause) is that both matters are private and only known to the individual.
5
Apply the minimum age conditions
Abu al-Fadl al-Mawsili specifies minimum ages: 12 years for a boy, 9 years for a girl. Below these ages, the claim is not accepted without external evidence.
This is the chosen (mukhtar) position in the Hanafi school.

Rulings for Ambiguous Hermaphrodite: Prayer and Circumcision
Specific rulings for ambiguous hermaphrodite's prayer position and circumcision funding.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on where an ambiguous hermaphrodite must stand in congregational prayer and explain how his circumcision expenses are to be covered.

Core Idea
The ambiguous hermaphrodite stands between rows to avoid gender doubt, and his circumcision is funded from his wealth or the public treasury.

This lesson addresses two practical rulings for the ambiguous hermaphrodite (khuntha mushkil). First, during congregational prayer, he must stand between the rows of men and women to avoid any misplacement. Second, if he reaches the age of desire, his circumcision must be arranged, with costs borne by his own wealth or by the public treasury if he is indigent. These rulings highlight the Hanafi concern for clarity and communal support.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues
The passage provides two distinct rulings: one about prayer position and one about circumcision expenses. Recognize that these are separate legal matters.
2
Analyze the prayer ruling
The hermaphrodite stands between the rows of men and women when praying behind the imam. Consider that this is a precautionary measure to avoid any uncertainty regarding gender.
This applies only in congregational prayer where the imam is leading.
3
Analyze the circumcision ruling
If he has wealth, a female slave is bought from his own money to circumcise him. If he is poor, the imam uses public treasury funds to purchase a slave, who is then sold after the procedure and the money returned to the treasury.
The sale of the slave after circumcision ensures the public funds are not permanently expended.
4
Identify the underlying principles
The permission to view a slave's private parts for necessity allows the circumcision. The use of bayt al-mal is justified because it is established for the necessities of Muslims.
These principles illustrate the flexibility of Hanafi fiqh in accommodating exceptional cases.

Rulings on the Ambiguous Hermaphrodite: Prayer and Circumcision
Ruling statement with commentary from Al-Hidayah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings for the khuntha mushkil in congregational prayer and circumcision, and explain the Hanafi principle of al-ahwat wa al-awthaq.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil stands between rows and is circumcised at public expense if needed.

In this lesson, we examine the unique rulings for the ambiguous hermaphrodite (khuntha mushkil) as given in Al-Quduri and Al-Hidayah. The Hanafis apply the principle of extreme caution (al-ahwat wa al-awthaq) to resolve doubts about gender in religious obligations. We will see how this principle manifests in the specific cases of congregational prayer and the obligation of circumcision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
Recognize that the khuntha mushkil is a person whose gender is indeterminate. The Hanafi school assigns special rulings based on caution.
2
Understand the principle
Note the statement from Al-Hidayah: 'the most cautious and secure course is taken, and a ruling is not established when doubt exists about its establishment.' This is the guiding principle.
This is the usul behind all khuntha rulings.
3
Apply to prayer
For congregational prayer, the khuntha stands between the rows of men and women to avoid definitively being in the wrong row.
This avoids potential nullification of prayer.
4
Apply to circumcision
Circumcision is obligatory. A slave-girl is purchased from the khuntha's wealth, or from the treasury if he is poor, to perform the circumcision. Afterward, the slave is sold and the money returned.
The need for circumcision takes precedence over gender ambiguity.

The Obligation of Bearing Witness for the Reward of a Runaway Slave
Ruling on the necessity of bearing witness for the reward

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that bearing witness (ishhad) is wajib for entitlement to the reward (ja'l) when taking a runaway slave, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Bearing witness is obligatory to prove intent; without it, no reward.

In this passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, Al-Maydani explains that the taker of a runaway slave must bear witness at the time of taking. This is to show that he takes it for the owner, not for himself. The ruling is based on the principle that intention is evidenced by action, and the omission of witnessing indicates that the taker intended to keep the slave. The passage also notes a variant view that denies the reward entirely, which is also considered valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is taking a runaway slave. The condition is bearing witness (ishhad) at the time of taking, declaring the intention to return it to the owner.
2
State the ruling on ishhad
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, bearing witness is wajib (hatm) for entitlement to the reward (ja'l). Without it, no reward is given.
This is the primary Hanafi position.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The text from Al-Hidayah: 'The omission of ishhad is an indication (amara) that he took it for himself.'
The proof is based on circumstantial evidence.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some copies of Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar state that no reward is due at all if ishhad is omitted, and the author (Al-Maydani) calls this view 'saheeh' (valid).
This alternative is based on qiyas with the seller who detains the sold item.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the majority view, the taker forfeits the reward; under the variant view, he also forfeits it. Both agree no reward, but the reasoning differs.
The difference is more in the legal reasoning than the outcome.

Allocation of Reward for a Mortgaged Runaway Slave
Ruling on reward allocation for a mortgaged runaway slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the division of reward (ja'l) between the mortgagee (murtahin) and the pledgor (rahin) when a mortgaged runaway slave is returned.

Core Idea
The mortgagee pays the reward proportionally to the debt he is owed.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a runaway slave is mortgaged. The reward for returning the slave is not simply due from the owner; the mortgagee, who has possessory rights, bears the reward up to the value of the debt. If the slave is worth more, the excess is paid by the pledgor. This ruling reflects the principle that liability follows right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
The slave is mortgaged: the pledgor (rahin) is the debtor/owner, the mortgagee (murtahin) is the creditor holding the slave as collateral. The reward (ja'l) is paid to the returner.
2
Determine who has possession (yad)
The mortgagee has the yad (possession) of the slave. Therefore, the primary responsibility for the reward falls on him.
This is based on the principle that the one who benefits from the possession bears the cost.
3
State the general rule
If the value of the slave equals or is less than the debt, the mortgagee pays the full reward. If the value exceeds the debt, the mortgagee pays only the portion equal to the debt, and the pledgor pays the remainder.
This is because the mortgagee's right is limited to the guaranteed amount (al-madmun).
4
Apply the rule with a numerical example
Suppose the slave is worth 100, debt is 80, reward is 10. Mortgagee pays 80/100 * 10 = 8, pledgor pays 2. If slave worth 80 or less, mortgagee pays all 10.
The reward is divided proportionally based on the value of the slave relative to the debt.
5
Understand the basis from the text
The text cites Al-Fayd: the mortgagee's right is to the extent of the guaranteed amount. This is the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind the proportional division.
This is a clear example of how fiqh rulings are derived from underlying principles.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri covers two rulings related to runaway slaves ('ibaq). First, the taker of a runaway slave should bear witness (ishhad) at the time of taking, declaring that he is taking it to return to its owner. This is based on a statement in Al-Hidayah: bearing witness is obligatory (hatm) according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani; without it, no reward (ja'l) is due because the omission indicates the taker intended it for himself. The author notes a variant reading in some copies of Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar where no reward is given at all—this is also considered valid, drawing an analogy to a seller who detains the sold item for the price: if it perishes, the price is forfeited. Second, if the slave is mortgaged (rahn), the reward is on the mortgagee (murtahin) because he has possession (yad). The reward is divided proportionally: if the slave's value equals or is less than the debt, the mortgagee pays the full reward; if greater, the mortgagee pays only the portion corresponding to the debt, and the pledgor (rahin) pays the rest, since the mortgagee's right is limited to the guaranteed amount. This follows the principle in Al-Fayd. The passage thus highlights the importance of intention and evidence in fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
المرتهن (al-murtahin): The creditor who holds the slave as collateral for a debt.
الراهن (al-rahin): The debtor who pledges the slave as collateral.
الجعل (al-ja'l): The reward given to the person who returns a runaway slave to its owner.
