Thirteen Rulings Where Silence Indicates Legal Consent
Compilation of thirteen specific applications where silence is a legal indicator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the thirteen cases where silence is treated as a legal indicator in Hanafi fiqh and classify each as consent, acceptance, denial, endorsement, or acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Silence speaks in law: thirteen rulings treat it as consent, acceptance, or denial, but each has context.

In Hanafi fiqh, silence (sukut) can carry significant legal weight. The commentator of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq has compiled thirteen distinct rulings where silence is treated as an explicit legal indicator. These span sales, pledges, agency, oaths, family matters, and judicial proceedings. Understanding when silence counts and when it does not is essential for accurate legal analysis. This lesson presents the full list and highlights the key principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context of each ruling
For each of the thirteen rulings, note which area of fiqh it belongs to: transactions (buyu, ijara, rahn, shirkah), oaths (ayman), family (nikah, nafaqah), or judicial (qada).
This helps later application.
2
Determine the legal effect of silence
In each case, what does the law treat the silence as? Categories: consent (rida), acceptance (qabul), denial (inkar), endorsement (ta'dil), acknowledgment (iqrar).
For example, silence of the seller on extra goods is consent to transfer ownership.
3
Note conditions or exceptions
Some rulings have qualifiers: the informant's character (adil vs fasiq), presence of an excuse, or the role of the silence (e.g., recipient of sadaqah vs gift).
The defect case distinguishes between upright and corrupt informants according to Abu Hanifa.
4
Recognize intra-Hanafi disagreements
Two rulings have explicit disputes: the sale of real estate by a wife/relative (Samarqand vs Bukhara) and the defect case (Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn). Mark these for deeper analysis.
The mufta bihi for the defect case is the Sahibayn's view: silence is consent regardless.
5
Use the poem as a mnemonic
The passage includes a versified summary. Try to match each line to the numbered rulings; this aids memorization.
The poem condenses each case into a short phrase.

Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf on Silence in Defect Disclosure and Property Sales
Detailed treatment of two intra-Hanafi disputes on silence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two key intra-Hanafi disagreements related to silence—defect disclosure and property sales—and identify the mufta bihi position in each.

Core Idea
Silence in defect sales and property deals splits Hanafi authorities: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn, Samarqand vs. Bukhara.

Among the thirteen rulings on silence, two cases generate notable intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: (1) whether silence after being informed of a defect before sale constitutes consent, and (2) whether a husband's silence when his wife or relative sells real estate is an acknowledgment that he has no claim. These disagreements reflect different underlying principles about trust and the significance of silence. This lesson dissects both disputes, their evidence, and the operative positions for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the first ikhtilaf: defect disclosure
Restate the scenario and the two positions: Abu Hanifa says silence is consent only if the informant is upright (adil); Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say silence is consent even if the informant is corrupt (fasiq).
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn view.
2
Analyze the basis of each position
Abu Hanifa emphasizes the need for reliable information; a corrupt informant may be lying. Sahibayn focus on the buyer's opportunity to investigate and his silent acceptance of the risk.
This is a disagreement over the strength of the default assumption when information is questionable.
3
State the second ikhtilaf: property sale by wife/relative
Samarqand scholars treat the husband's silence as acknowledgment that he has no claim; Bukhara scholars require explicit objection. The mufti must consider the context.
No single mufta bihi is given; it depends on the mufti's judgment.
4
Compare the two ikhtilaf
Both disputes revolve around whether silence can be considered a positive act (consent or acknowledgment) absent explicit speech. The difference lies in the legal presumption: in defect disclosure, the default is that the buyer seeks a sound item; in property sales, the husband's silence may be ambiguous.
The mufti must weigh the circumstances.
5
Apply the reasoning to a new scenario
Given a novel situation (e.g., silence on an error in a contract), use the principles from these ikhtilaf to determine whether the silence is binding.
This trains usul al-ifta skills.

The Ten Types of Fasid (Defective) Transactions
Lists ten defective transactions with their legal consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten defective transactions and state the specific ruling for each.

Core Idea
Each fasid transaction has distinct legal effects — knowing them prevents misapplication of rulings.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between fasid (defective) and batil (void) contracts. This passage from al-Khulasa enumerates ten specific defective transactions across various chapters of fiqh (sale, lease, marriage, etc.) and provides the precise legal consequence for each. Understanding these rulings is essential for correctly handling invalid contracts in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ten types
Read the passage and list each of the ten defective transactions mentioned. They cover nikah, bay', ijara, rahn, sulh, qard, hiba, mudaraba, kitaba, and muzara'a.
The list starts with 'النكاح الفاسد' and ends with 'المزارعة الفاسدة'.
2
For each type, note the ruling
For each transaction, the passage states a specific legal effect. For example, in a fasid sale the item is guaranteed; in a fasid lease, the lessee must pay fair rent and the asset is a trust; in a fasid pledge, the pledgor may revoke, etc.
Some rulings involve trust (amanah), others involve guarantee (daman), and some allow revocation.
3
Distinguish fasid from batil
Recognize that fasid contracts are not completely void; they have limited legal effects (e.g., obligation to return, payment of compensation). Batil contracts have no legal effects at all.
For example, a fasid sale requires the buyer to return the item (or its value), while a batil sale would not transfer any right.
4
Group similar rulings
Observe patterns: some transactions place the subject matter as a trust (amanah) in the hands of the counterparty (e.g., lease, pledge, mudaraba), while others involve guarantee (madmin) (e.g., sale, gift).
Mudaraba and pledge: capital/asset is a trust. Sale and gift: item is guaranteed.

Commentary
This passage from al-Khulasa provides a concise list of ten types of defective (fasid) transactions in Hanafi fiqh and their legal consequences. The rulings are: 1) Invalid marriage (nikah fasid) – the ruling was covered earlier. 2) Invalid sale – the item is guaranteed (i.e., the buyer must return it or its value). 3) Invalid lease – the lessee must pay the hire of the like, and the asset is a trust. 4) Invalid pledge – if it is of an undivided share, the pledgor may revoke; if it perishes, it is a trust. 5) Invalid settlement – either party can revoke. 6) Invalid loan – if the object is of a type that is disparate (like animals), the loan is invalid, but any subsequent sale is valid. 7) Invalid gift – it is guaranteed by value on the day of taking possession, and does not transfer ownership. 8) Invalid mudaraba – the capital is a trust. 9) Invalid kitaba – the slave must pay the greater of the named amount or his value. 10) Invalid muzara'a – produce goes to the seed owner; the worker gets the equivalent of his wage if the land is from the seed owner, otherwise the worker gets the produce but must pay rent.

Twenty-One Invalid Transactions in Hanafi Fiqh
Lists invalid transactions from two sources with poems.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the twenty-one invalid transactions in Hanafi fiqh and state the basic ruling for each type as given in the source.

Core Idea
There are twenty-one fasid transactions with distinct legal effects; knowing them is essential for a Hanafi jurist.

This lesson compiles the twenty-one invalid transactions (tasarrufat fasidah) from two major Hanafi references: al-Khulasah lists ten, and al-Nahr adds eleven more. The passage also includes two poems to aid memorization. Understanding these transactions is crucial because in Hanafi fiqh, fasid is distinct from batil: fasid transactions may produce some legal consequences (e.g., guarantee, return of property) while batil transactions have no effect at all. Grasping this nuance will sharpen your ability to classify and rule on contracts, sales, and other legal acts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of tasarruf fasid
Start by recognizing that fasid transactions have a defect in their conditions (shurut) or pillars (arkan) but are not completely void. They often create obligations like guarantee or return of the object.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes fasid from batil; fasid can sometimes be rectified or has intermediate effects.
2
Memorize the first ten from al-Khulasah
Learn the ten transactions: invalid marriage, sale, lease, pledge, settlement, loan, gift, mudarabah, kitabah, and muzara'ah. Each has a special ruling, e.g., sold item is guaranteed in fasid sale.
The source gives brief rulings for each; focus on the key legal effect.
3
Add the eleven from al-Nahr
The additional eleven are: invalid charity, khul', partnership, salam, kafalah, agency, waqf, iqalah, sarf, wasiyyah, and division. Note that some of these do not distinguish between fasid and batil in the texts.
For many of these, the ruling is that they are treated like their batil counterparts.
4
Use the poems as a mnemonic
The two poems list all twenty-one in rhyme. Recite them to reinforce memory. The first poem enumerates them in a single line; the second gives a more detailed breakdown.
Poems are traditional memorization aids in Hanafi learning.
5
Apply the knowledge to classify transactions
When encountering a new transaction, check whether any condition is missing or defective. If so, identify which fasid type it falls under and recall the corresponding ruling from this lesson.
Practice with examples: a sale with unknown price is fasid bay'; a lease without fixed term is fasid ijara.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of fasid (invalid) transactions in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by citing al-Khulasah's list of ten: invalid marriage, sale, lease, pledge, settlement, loan, gift, mudarabah, kitabah, and muzara'ah. Then the commentator adds that al-Nahr added eleven more: charity, khul', partnership, salam, kafalah, agency, waqf, iqalah, sarf, wasiyyah, and division, making twenty-one. He explains each briefly: for invalid charity, it is like an invalid gift and returns by guarantee; invalid khul' makes divorce irrevocable; invalid partnership with profit based on capital is valid but without liability for loss; invalid salam treats capital as usurped; invalid kafalah with unknown principal creates no obligation; invalid agency, waqf, iqalah, etc., have no distinction between fasid and batil. He then presents two poems summarizing the twenty-one. These poems serve as a memorization aid for students to recall the types of invalid contracts and their specific rulings.

Key Terms
تصرف فاسد (tasarruf fasid): A transaction that is invalid due to a defect in its conditions or pillars, but may have certain legal effects (e.g., guarantee, return of property).
نكاح فاسد (nikah fasid): An invalid marriage that produces some legal consequences like lineage and iddah, but requires separation.
بيع فاسد (bay' fasid): An invalid sale that does not transfer ownership but gives possession rights and ensures the sold item is guaranteed.
