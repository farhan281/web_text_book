The Epistemological Status of Fiqh: A Zanni Science
Methodological note on the structure and epistemology of fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qat'i and zanni sciences and explain why fiqh is classified as zanni according to Hanafi methodology.

Core Idea
Fiqh is a probabilistic (zanni) science because its evidence is not definitively certain — unlike kalam, arithmetic, and geometry.

Before diving into the detailed rulings of divorce, the commentator pauses to remind us of the nature of fiqh itself. This short but crucial passage explains the hierarchical structure of fiqh texts and the epistemological status of fiqh as a zanni (probabilistic) science. Understanding this will shape how you approach fiqh rulings: they are derived from preponderant evidence, not absolute certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchical structure of fiqh works
Recognize that compiled sciences are organized into categories: book (kitab), chapter (bab), and section (fasl). Each is a subcategory of the higher level.
In Hanafi fiqh, the largest division is a kitab (e.g., Kitab al-Tahara), then abwab (chapters), then fusul (sections).
2
Classify sciences as qat'i or zanni
Distinguish between certain sciences (qat'i) like kalam, arithmetic, and geometry, and probabilistic sciences (zanni) like fiqh.
Qat'i sciences have definitive proofs; zanni sciences rely on evidence that requires interpretation (e.g., ahad hadith, qiyas).
3
Explain why fiqh is zanni
Fiqh is zanni because its evidence is often from sources that are not absolutely certain in transmission or meaning, such as ahad hadith and analogical reasoning (qiyas).
This does not mean fiqh is uncertain; rather, its rulings are based on preponderant opinion (ghalabat al-zann).
4
Apply the classification to a fiqh example
Consider a ruling derived from a mutawatir hadith (obligation of five prayers) versus a ruling from qiyas (e.g., riba in modern finance). The latter is more clearly zanni.
Even rulings from clear texts can become zanni when subject to different interpretations (e.g., differing usul principles).
5
Reflect on the implication for studying fiqh
Because fiqh is zanni, there can be legitimate ikhtilaf (differences of opinion) among scholars. The Hanafi school follows the position deemed most preponderant.
This epistemological humility encourages respect for other valid opinions within the framework of ijtihad.

Commentary
This short passage is a methodological note ('فصل') by the commentator on the structure of fiqh literature. He explains that within the chapter ('باب') on pronouncing divorce, there are two sub-sections based on the type of pronouncement: added (to time), described, similar, etc., related to whether the wife is entered upon or not. He then makes a broader point about the classification of sciences: every compiled discipline has categories (books, chapters, sections). He distinguishes between certain sciences (like kalam, arithmetic, geometry) and probabilistic sciences (like fiqh). This is important because it reminds the student that fiqh rulings are zanni (probabilistic) in many cases, derived from evidence that is not definitively certain. The passage ends with a quote from al-Ramli from al-Walwalijiyya starting a new topic on time-limited divorce, but the text cuts off there.

Key Terms
علم مدون (ilm mudawwan): A codified or compiled science, which has a systematic structure of books, chapters, etc.
ظني (zanni): Probabilistic; based on evidence that is not definitively certain, leading to preponderant opinion.
باب (bab): Chapter; a subdivision of a 'kitab' in fiqh works.
فصل (fasl): Section; a further subdivision within a chapter.
كتاب (kitab): Book; the highest division of a compiled fiqh work, e.g., Kitab al-Tahara.
