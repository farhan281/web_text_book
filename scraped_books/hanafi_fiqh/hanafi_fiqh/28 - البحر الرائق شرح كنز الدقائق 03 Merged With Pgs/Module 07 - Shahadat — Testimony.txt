Furu' on Christian Testimony: Three Cases
Subsidiary rulings on accepting Christian testimony in various cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three subsidiary rulings on accepting testimony from Christian witnesses and identify the key factor (potential capital consequence) that determines acceptance or rejection in each case.

Core Idea
Christian testimony is accepted or rejected based on the parties' faith and the consequence of the case.

The general rule that testimony of dhimmi witnesses is accepted in certain transactions has specific exceptions. These furu' illustrate how the ruling changes depending on who the testimony is for or against, and whether accepting it could lead to capital punishment. The third case involves a disagreement within the Hanafi school between the author and Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases
Read the source passage and list the three distinct scenarios: (1) testimony on Islam of a Christian, (2) testimony for wages, (3) testimony on an entitlement after a sale.
Each case has two sub-variants (e.g., man vs. woman, Muslim vs. non-Muslim entitlement).
2
For each case, determine the parties and subject
Note who the witnesses are (two Christians), who is being testified about, and what the testimony concerns (Islam, wages, ownership). Pay attention to the direction: who benefits?
The second ruling has an 'cakse' (opposite) which reverses the ruling.
3
State the ruling: accepted or rejected
Based on the text, record whether the testimony is accepted (tuqbal) or not (la tuqbal).
The first ruling has two sub-rulings: man denies → rejected; woman → accepted. The second: for Muslim's wages → accepted; opposite → rejected. The third: rejected, but Abu Yusuf accepts.
4
Identify the underlying rationale
For the first ruling, the reason is potential capital punishment for apostasy if the man's Islam is confirmed. For a woman, there is no execution, so testimony is accepted.
For the second and third, the rationale is based on the status of the party owed or entitled: testimony for a Muslim is accepted, for a non-Muslim it is not.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
In the third case, Abu Yusuf disagrees and would accept the testimony. Recognize that this is a valid difference within the school.
The author's position is the mufta bihi according to the text.

Commentary
These are subsidiary rulings (furu') derived from the principle of accepting testimony of dhimmis. The first ruling: two Christians testify that a Christian man has embraced Islam; if he denies, their testimony is not accepted because it could lead to his execution for apostasy if he refuses to return to Islam (as the commentary explains). However, if the person is a Christian woman, her testimony for Islam is accepted because a woman apostate is not executed. Second ruling: Christians testifying against a non-Muslim (e.g., a dhimmi) for wages owed to a Muslim: accepted, but not the reverse (Muslim testifying for wages of a Christian? Actually 'لا في عكسه' means the opposite is not accepted: if Christians testify against a Muslim for wages of a non-Muslim, it is not accepted). Third ruling: Christians testifying that a Christian buyer is entitled to an item bought from a Muslim—this is rejected according to the author, contrary to Abu Yusuf who would accept it.

Key Terms
فروع (furū‘): Subsidiary rulings derived from a general principle; specific cases.
أجرة (ujrah): Wages or hire payment.

Effect of Witness Retraction on Marriage: The Condition of Compensation
Ruling on validity of marriage when retracting witness marries freed slave girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a retracting witness's marriage to a freed slave girl and identify the condition that determines its validity.

Core Idea
A witness who retracts cannot marry the freed slave until compensation is paid; timing determines validity.

This passage from al-Zahiriyyah presents a scenario where witnesses retract their testimony about a slave's emancipation. The judge had already ruled for freedom, but retraction creates liability. When one witness marries the now-free woman, Abu Yusuf distinguishes between marriage before and after the compensation judgment. This lesson unpacks that condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the marriage of a retracting witness to the freed slave girl. The ruling is that the marriage is valid only after compensation is paid.
2
Spot the two scenarios
The passage presents two timelines: marriage before the judgment regarding compensation, and marriage after that judgment. The ruling differs.
3
Determine the hukm level
Before compensation: the marriage is fasid (invalid) and the couple is separated. After compensation: the marriage is sahih (valid).
In Hanafi fiqh, fasid marriage requires separation.
4
Note the condition
The condition for validity is the payment of compensation (or a judicial ruling on liability). This resolves the uncertainty of ownership caused by the retraction.
5
Understand the rationale
The retraction creates doubt: the witnesses claimed the slave was free but then contradicted themselves. Until compensation is paid, the woman's status is ambiguous, making the witness's marriage invalid.

Commentary
This passage from al-Zahiriyyah presents a case where two witnesses testify that a man freed his slave girl, but the man denies it. The judge rules for freedom. Then the witnesses retract their testimony. One of them marries the now‑free woman. Abu Yusuf's ruling distinguishes between two scenarios: If the marriage occurs before the witnesses have paid the compensation (the value of the slave girl to the original owner), the marriage is invalid and the couple must be separated. If the marriage occurs after the compensation has been paid, the marriage is valid. This is because, before compensation, the witnesses' retraction leaves the ownership status uncertain, effectively treating the woman as still a slave in terms of the witness's ability to marry her (since a witness cannot marry a slave girl he claims to have been freed without due process). After compensation, the matter is settled, and the marriage is valid.

Evidence Required to Prove Fosterage (Rada')
Stating the evidentiary standard for proving fosterage and discussing the weight of a single witness's report before and after marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the evidentiary standard for proving fosterage in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish when a single witness's report is considered for precaution versus required for court.

Core Idea
Fosterage is proven like financial claims: two men or one man and two women; a single report is only for precaution, not court.

This lesson examines the evidentiary standard for establishing fosterage (rada') that renders marriage invalid. The Hanafi school analogizes fosterage to financial transactions, requiring the same level of proof. However, a nuance arises regarding the role of a single trustworthy witness, especially before marriage. This lesson will unpack the textual proof, the intra-school dispute, and the practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling and its proof
Find the explicit statement that fosterage is proven by what proves financial claims. Note the dalil: the analogy to invalidation of ownership in marriage.
The principle is that prohibition (hurma) in marriage is tied to ownership, so same evidence as mal.
2
Distinguish fosterage from purely religious prohibitions
Compare with the meat case: a single report suffices for religious matters because it does not involve ownership. Understand the difference in evidentiary standards.
This contrast is crucial for the school's reasoning.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on a single woman's testimony before marriage
Identify the two narrations: one accepts, one does not. See how the author reconciles them by considering the justice of the reporter.
The dispute is about precautionary fatwa vs. court evidence.
4
Apply the distinction between pre-contract and post-contract testimony
Understand why testimony to fosterage before marriage is less accepted: it challenges the original validity. Post-marriage testimony suggests a nullifying event, so precaution is easier.
This informs the mufti's approach: for fatwa, act on precaution; for court, full evidence.
5
State the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon view is that court requires two men or one man and two women. A single trustworthy report may be used for personal precaution but is not enforceable.
This is the operative rule for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the evidentiary standard for proving fosterage (rada'). The author begins by quoting the matn: fosterage is proven by what proves financial claims (mal), namely the testimony of two just men or one man and two just women. The justification is that the prohibition (hurma) established by fosterage cannot be separated from the removal of ownership (in the sense of marriage right) in the case of marriage; the invalidation of ownership (i.e., marriage) requires the same evidence as financial claims. The text contrasts this with the case of buying meat: if a single person tells you it is from an animal slaughtered by a Zoroastrian (Majusi), you must abstain because it is a purely religious matter where consumption is prohibited regardless of ownership. The principle is that financial matters are proven by two men or one man and two women; fosterage, since it invalidates marriage, is treated similarly. The author then engages with a dispute within the Hanafi school: whether a single woman's testimony before marriage is accepted. He cites two narrations: one accepts it, one does not. The text then reports from al-Khaniyya that before marriage, if a woman claims she breastfed the parties, the man is free to disbelieve her. However, if a single trustworthy person (adl thiqah) reports fosterage after marriage, it is recommended (ahwat) to separate, based on a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him). The author tries to reconcile by suggesting that the first narration (rejecting the single witness) applies when the justice of the reporter is unknown, and the second (accepting for precaution) when known. Finally, the key distinction: testimony to fosterage that predates the contract is treated differently from testimony to fosterage that occurs after the contract. Pre-contract testimony challenges the original validity of the marriage, so stronger evidence is needed; post-contract testimony acknowledges the marriage was valid but claims a subsequent corrupting event, so precautionary acceptance is easier.

Key Terms

خبر الواحد (khabar al-wāḥid): Report from a single individual (male or female); in legal evidence, it does not suffice for establishing fosterage that invalidates marriage, but may be considered for precaution.

Testimony of Agents in Divorce Delegation: Valid vs Invalid Scenarios
Ruling on validity of agents' testimony about divorce delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agents' testimony about divorce delegation and distinguish between valid and invalid testimony based on the nature of the act reported.

Core Idea
Agents' testimony is valid only when they report a physical act (informing), not a verbal delegation.

In Hanafi law, testimony must be based on direct observation. When two agents claim to have carried out a delegation of divorce, the form of their testimony matters. This passage from Jami' al-Fusulayn clarifies that agents can validly testify that they informed the wife of a delegation, but not that they themselves performed the verbal delegation. This distinction hinges on whether the reported act is directly observable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two cases: (1) agents testify that they were ordered to inform the wife, did so, and she divorced herself – testimony valid. (2) agents testify that they were told to delegate and they did so – testimony invalid.
The key is the nature of the act: informing vs. delegating.
2
Determine the operative distinction
In the valid scenario, the agents report their own act of informing (a physical, observable event). In the invalid scenario, they report a verbal act of delegation (less directly observable). Hanafi rules require testimony to be based on direct sensory experience (mushahadah).
Verbal acts are considered less reliable for testimony unless witnessed by others.
3
Apply the analogy to sales agency
The passage draws an analogy to a case in sales: agents testifying that they informed someone of an agency appointment is valid, similar to the divorce case. This reinforces that the principle is general: any testimony about an act of informing is acceptable.
This analogy shows consistency across different areas of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage addresses the validity of testimony from two agents regarding the delegation of divorce. In the first scenario: the agents state that the husband ordered them to inform the wife that he delegated authority to her; they informed her, and she later divorced herself. Their testimony is valid because they are reporting their own action (informing) and the husband's order. In the second scenario: the agents claim that the husband said to them 'Delegate to her' and they did so (i.e., they verbally delegated to her). This testimony is not valid because they are testifying about a verbal act of delegation that they performed, which is not directly witnessed by others. The analogy given is with agency in sale: if agents testify that they were ordered to inform someone of agency and they did so, their testimony is valid because it reports an observable act. But if they testify that they were told to delegate and they did, it is less verifiable.

Key Terms
