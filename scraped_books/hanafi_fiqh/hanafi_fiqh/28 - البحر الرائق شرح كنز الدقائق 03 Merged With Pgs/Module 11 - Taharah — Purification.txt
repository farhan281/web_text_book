The Authority of a Father to Marry His Son’s Female Slave to His Son’s Slave
Explaining rulings on who may authorize a slave's marriage and the permissibility of concubinage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Zufar regarding the validity of a father marrying his son's female slave to his son's slave, and identify the mufta bihi position with its basis.

Core Idea
The father’s authority over his son’s property does not extend to marrying his son’s male slave to his son’s female slave in the Hanafi relied-upon view.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute about the scope of a father's authority over his son's slaves. While the general rule is that a father cannot marry off his son's male slave, Abu Yusuf allows an exception when the marriage is to his son's female slave. Zufar disagrees, and the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) aligns with the majority of the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act in question is a father marrying his son's female slave (jariya) to his son's male slave (abduh). The dispute is whether this marriage is valid.
This is a narrow issue within the broader topic of who has authority to marry slaves.
2
State the positions
Abu Yusuf holds it is permissible; Zufar holds it is not permissible. The majority (zahir al-riwayah) agrees with Zufar.
Abu Yusuf's view is an exception to the general rule that a father cannot marry his son's male slave.
3
Examine the proofs
Abu Yusuf relies on the father's general authority over his son's property and the benefit (offspring). Zufar argues that a father has no authority over a male slave due to lack of benefit (unlike a female slave where offspring are a benefit).
The texts cited include al-Mabsut and al-Zahiriyyah.
4
Determine the tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that it is not permissible, based on the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) as recorded in al-Mabsut.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah prefers the prohibition.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
If the father performs such a marriage, according to Abu Yusuf it is valid; according to Zufar it is invalid (and the couple would be committing zina). Practically, the mufta bihi is that it is invalid.
This affects the legality of the marriage and the status of any children.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explaining the chapter on marriage of slaves from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author begins by citing a branch from Fath al-Qadir regarding a merchant who gives a slave-girl to his slave for concubinage (tasarrī). The ruling is that a slave is absolutely prohibited from having a concubine, whether the master permits it or not. This is because lawful sexual intercourse is only established through either right-hand possession (milk al-yamin) or a valid marriage contract. A slave does not possess right-hand possession, so his only lawful avenue is marriage. This is a clear ruling established by the primary texts.

The author then discusses the case of a slave owned by multiple partners (mushārak). Such a slave cannot be married without the unanimous permission of all co-owners. If one co-owner marries the female slave without the other's permission and the husband consummates, the other co-owner has the right to annul the marriage. Upon annulment, the co-owner who did not consent receives half the proper dower (mahr al-mithl), and the husband receives the lesser of half the proper dower or half the specified dower. This ruling is taken from al-Zahiriyyah.

Next, the passage addresses the marriage of a mukātab (slave contracted to earn his freedom). The permission of the master's heirs is sufficient for the mukātab's marriage because they do not own his person—he is like a free man—but the wala (clientage) belongs to them.

The commentary then clarifies who has the authority to marry off a slave. The term 'master' in this context includes not only the owner but also anyone with the authority to arrange a slave's marriage, even if not the owner. Thus, the father, grandfather, judge, and executor (waṣī) may marry off an orphan's female slave, but they cannot marry off a male slave due to lack of benefit. The mukātab and the partner in a muwāfaḍa (equal partnership) can marry off a female slave but not a male slave. The authorized slave (ma'dhūn), the muḍārib (sleeping partner), and the partner in an 'inān (limited partnership) cannot marry off a female slave either, contrary to the view of Abu Yusuf.

A significant intra-Hanafi dispute is mentioned: the father marrying his son's female slave to his own son's slave. According to Abu Yusuf, this is permissible, while Zufar holds it is not. The author notes that this is an exception to the general rule that the father cannot marry off his son's male slave. However, in al-Mabsut, the ruling according to the apparent narration (zahir al-riwāya) is that it is not permissible, so the exception is not accepted.

The passage then discusses the legal nature of a slave's marriage when it is suspended (tawaqquf), waiting for ratification (ijāza). The author explains that the marriage is a cause for the present, but its ruling is delayed until ratification. Upon ratification, the permissibility is retroactive to the time of the contract, similar to a suspended sale. This is contrasted with a suspended delegation of divorce (tafwīḍ al-ṭalāq) which is not retroactive because it admits of suspension and its effect is only from the time of ratification.

Finally, the author reiterates that only those who can manumit a male slave can marry him off, but for a female slave the authority is broader. He quotes al-Durar and al-Bazzaziyyah to support this, and notes that the executor can marry off a female slave based on explicit texts.

Key Terms
تسرّى (tasarrī): Taking a female slave as a concubine for sexual intercourse without marriage.
متعة (mut'ah): Temporary marriage (mut'ah) is not mentioned here; but the text implies it is not an option for slaves since only marriage or milk al-yamin are lawful.
ولاية (wilayah): Legal authority to contract marriage on behalf of another.
توقف (tawaqquf): Suspension: a contract whose validity is pending ratification (ijazah).
إستناد (istinad): Retrospective effect: when ratification makes the contract effective from the time of the original action.

Selling a Slave for Mahr and Nafaqah: One-Time vs Repeated Sale
Ruling on sale for mahr vs. nafaqah with proof from al-Mabsut

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling on selling a slave for mahr (one-time) versus nafaqah (repeated sales) and apply the condition of the master's permission.

Core Idea
Mahr is recovered once via sale; nafaqah requires repeated sales as it accrues.

When a slave marries with the master's permission, debts like mahr and nafaqah become enforceable against him. The Hanafi school draws a critical distinction: a slave is sold only once for mahr, but can be sold repeatedly for nafaqah because it is a recurring obligation. This lesson unpacks the rationale and the conditions that trigger sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal basis
The passage opens with the rule: if a slave marries with permission, he is sold for the mahr. Understand that the sale is a means to satisfy a debt, and it is permitted because the debt arose with the master's consent, similar to a trade debt (dīn al-tijāra).
The debt attaches to the slave's person (raqabah) because the master's permission makes it binding.
2
Distinguish mahr from nafaqah
The text specifies that for mahr, the slave is sold only once; if the sale price does not cover the full mahr, the remainder is deferred until he becomes free. For nafaqah, however, he is sold repeatedly as new installments arise, because nafaqah renews with time (mutajaddid).
This distinction is based on the nature of the debt: lump sum vs. periodic.
3
Examine the exception for deferred mahr
If the slave was sold for the immediate (mu'ajjal) portion of the mahr, and later the deferred (mu'ajjal) term comes due, he can be sold again because the first sale covered only part of the total mahr. This is derived from the principle that he is sold for the entire mahr once.
This shows that the one-time sale rule applies to the total mahr, not its installments.
4
Confirm the reliance on al-Mabsut
The commentary cites al-Mabsut to support the repeated sale for nafaqah. This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position from Zahir al-Riwayah. Note that the passage also hints at a marginal view that questions repeated sales, but the majority upholds it.
Al-Mabsut is a core text of the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Without permission, no mahr is due until freedom; with ratification, istihsan imposes only the specified mahr, not two mahr by analogy.

When a slave marries without the master's permission, the legal consequences hinge on whether consummation occurred and whether the master ratifies. The Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) to avoid an unjust doubling of mahr that would result from strict qiyas. This lesson unpacks these nuances.

If the slave marries without permission and does not consummate, the marriage has no legal effect (lā ḥukma lah). If consummation occurs, the master has two options: separate (tafrīq) or ratify (ijāza).
The debt does not appear in the master's right (lam yaẓhar fī ḥaqq al-mawlā).

If the master separates the couple, the wife has no right to mahr until the slave becomes free. The debt is treated as one the slave incurred himself, not binding on the master.
This is because the debt did not become evident with the master's permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the grammatical attachment of 'من الرضاع' affects the Hanafi ruling on marrying the mother of a foster uncle and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The permissibility of marrying the mother of a foster uncle depends on whether 'من الرضاع' attaches to the mother or the uncle.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines a dispute within the Hanafi school about a specific marriage prohibition. The ruling hinges on the grammatical structure of a phrase, highlighting the interplay between Arabic syntax and legal reasoning. Understanding this case sharpens your ability to parse how late Hanafi commentators reconciled conflicting statements through nuanced interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the ruling being disputed? The dispute is about marrying the mother of a foster uncle (أم العم من الرضاع) or the mother of a foster maternal uncle (أم الخال من الرضاع). Is she prohibited or permissible?
2
Locate the grammatical pivot
The phrase 'من الرضاع' (from fosterage) in the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: 'إلا أم أخيه من الرضاع'. The dispute centers on whether this phrase attaches to the word 'أم' (mother) or to 'أخيه' (his brother) or to both.
The attachment is a grammatical issue of whether it is an adjective (صفة) or a circumstance (حال).
3
Understand the two positions
The commentator of Kanz (al-Sharih) holds that the mother of a foster uncle is prohibited (haram). The author of al-Ghayah holds that she is not prohibited (mubah). Each argues based on different assumptions about the attachment.
Al-Sharih assumes the phrase attaches to the uncle; al-Ghayah assumes it attaches to the mother or both.
4
Trace the rebuttals and responses
Fatḥ al-Qadīr critiques al-Ghayah's exclusive enumeration of reasons for prohibition. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq then presents three possible attachments and a fourth special case, showing that the permissibility depends on the scenario.
The author argues that al-Sharih neglected the last two faces that make the mother permissible.
5
Apply the author's conclusion
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that the mother of a foster uncle is permissible when the phrase attaches to the mother only or to both, or when the uncle is a foster relative only through the father/mother. In other cases, she may be prohibited.
This reconciliation respects both earlier viewpoints.

I say: It is not necessarily so, because its attachment to the annexed only is not correct in the fourth face, not in the first face; so for its attachment to the annexed only, there are two forms: in one form the mother is not lawful, and in another she is lawful. Commentary
This passage is an intra-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the ruling on marrying the mother of one's foster paternal uncle or foster maternal uncle. The issue revolves around the grammatical attachment of the phrase 'من الرضاع' (from fosterage) in the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: 'إلا أم أخيه من الرضاع' (except the mother of his brother from fosterage). The commentator (al-Sharih) of Kanz initially argues that the mother of a foster uncle is not prohibited, but the author of al-Ghayah objects that this is incorrect because fosterage creates the same prohibitions as lineage. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq then clarifies that there are multiple possible interpretations depending on whether 'من الرضاع' attaches to the uncle (the annexed) or to the mother (the annexer), leading to different rulings. He presents three main faces: (1) if 'من الرضاع' attaches only to the uncle, and the uncle is a foster relative while his mother is a blood relative, then she is forbidden because she becomes the grandmother or the wife of the grandfather; (2) if it attaches only to the mother (i.e., the mother is a foster relative to the uncle, but the uncle is a blood relative), then she is not forbidden; (3) if it attaches to both, she is not forbidden. A fourth face is also mentioned where the terms 'عم' and 'خال' themselves are interpreted restrictively to mean those nursed with one's father or mother. The passage concludes with the author's own resolution that the attachment to the annexed only has two scenarios, and the statements of scholars should be interpreted in the scenario where the mother is lawful, to reconcile the different views. This analysis is claimed as a unique contribution of this book.

The Principle of Predominance in Fosterage: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Mixed Milk
Commentary on ruling that when milk is mixed, the predominant component determines legal effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mixed milk in fosterage and explain the difference between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
When milk is mixed with another substance, the predominant component decides the fosterage; but when two women's milk mixes, does prohibition attach to both or only the dominant?

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi fiqh of fosterage: the role of predominance (ghalaba) when milk is mixed with other substances. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains that if milk is mixed with water, medicine, or sheep's milk, the predominant substance determines the ruling. The most important discussion concerns the mixing of milk from two women, where there is a known dispute between the eponymous Imams. Understanding this dispute is essential for correctly applying the ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
The passage begins by stating that milk derivatives (buttermilk, cheese) do not establish fosterage. Then it introduces the predominance principle: when milk is mixed with another substance, the predominant component is legally considered.
The term 'rada'' (fosterage) applies only to the ingestion of human milk directly, not to processed products.
2
Understand the predominance rule
If milk is mixed with water or medicine, the mixture is judged by the predominant element. If water is dominant, no fosterage; if equal, fosterage is established. The same applies to sheep's milk, which itself has no effect.
Abu Yusuf adds a condition: if the additive changes both taste and color, the mixture is no longer considered milk; if only one, it still is.
3
Explore the intra-Hanafi dispute on two women's milk
When milk of two women is mixed, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that only the woman whose milk is predominant becomes a foster mother. Imam Muhammad holds that both become foster mothers, because the genus (human milk) does not overwhelm another instance of the same genus.
This ikhtilaf is recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah, with Muhammad's view also attributed to Abu Hanifa in another narration.
4
Apply the tarjih (preference)
The commentators (al-Ghaya, Sharh al-Majma') consider Muhammad's view as 'azhar wa ahwat' (more apparent and precautionary). It is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) today.
In the case of equality between two women's milk, there is consensus that both establish prohibition, as there is no predominance.

Core Idea
The word for finger (asba') is predominantly feminine, though both genders are attested; it has ten known variant pronunciations.

In Hanafi fiqh texts, the word for finger (asba') appears frequently in discussions of wudu (wiping between fingers) and tayammum.
