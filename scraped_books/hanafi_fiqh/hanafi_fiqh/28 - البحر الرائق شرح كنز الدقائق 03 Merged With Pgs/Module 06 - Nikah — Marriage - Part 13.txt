Fosterage and Marital Prohibitions: The Role of Consummation
Ruling statement on fosterage with senior-junior wives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on fosterage when a senior wife nurses a junior wife, and explain how consummation (dukhul) affects the permanence of prohibition.

Core Idea
If the senior wife has been consummated with, both she and the junior become permanently prohibited; if not, only the senior and first nursed junior are prohibited.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains how fosterage creates marital prohibitions similar to blood lineage, with a special focus on polygamous situations. The key variable is whether the nursing wife has been consummated with—a distinction that changes the ruling completely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? A senior wife nurses a junior wife from the same husband. The ruling depends on whether the senior wife has been consummated with.
2
Distinguish by consummation
If the senior wife has been consummated with, both she and the junior become permanently prohibited (they become mother and daughter by fosterage). If not consummated, only the senior and the first nursed junior become prohibited; the second nursed junior remains permissible.
The order of nursing matters when consummation is absent.
3
Apply the principle to the witness case
The initial example about witnesses confirms that the same prohibition applies to blood relatives, showing that the nursing scenario is not exclusive.
The nursing case is analogous to blood lineage.
4
Note the exception
Nursing by a wife's aunt does not create prohibition because it does not establish a direct mother-daughter relationship.
Only direct ascendants/descendants through fosterage are prohibited.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a detailed commentary on the rulings of fosterage (رضاع) as they relate to marriage prohibitions, particularly in polygamous households. The commentary opens by establishing the general principle that fosterage creates the same marital prohibitions as blood lineage. The first case involves a man whose wife is testified to be his sister by two just witnesses—the marriage is void completely, and if intercourse occurred, the hadd penalty applies. The author then clarifies that the mention of a co-wife nursing is not exclusive; the same prohibitions apply to the senior wife's sister, mother, and daughter by fosterage, depending on whether the senior wife has been consummated with. The commentator then cites al-Bada'i' to explain that nursing by a wife's aunt does not create prohibition because it does not establish a direct mother-daughter relationship. The core of the passage deals with multiple scenarios of a wife nursing her co-wives. The key distinction is whether the nursing wife (الكبيرة) has been consummated with (دخل بها) or not. If not consummated, only the nursing wife and the first nursed co-wife become prohibited (due to becoming mother and daughter), while the second nursed co-wife remains permissible. If consummated, all become prohibited permanently. The passage then discusses cases of a stranger woman nursing multiple junior wives, with rules depending on the order and simultaneity of nursing. The principle is that sisterhood through fosterage prohibits marriage, but the prohibition is limited to the number that would create a forbidden combination. Further scenarios involve multiple pairs of senior and junior wives, leading to complex outcomes. The commentary from al-Muhit adds cases involving the father and son's wives. The passage concludes with rulings on fosterage during the waiting period (عدة) after divorce, emphasizing that the prohibition of combination during iddah is like that during marriage. The final part clarifies that the junior wife's marriage need not be current at the time of nursing; past marriage is sufficient to trigger the prohibition. The commentator also critiques the phrasing of the matn, suggesting a more precise formulation. Throughout, the commentary follows the Hanafi school's detailed casuistry, demonstrating how the general rules of fosterage are applied to specific factual scenarios.

Key Terms
رضاع (radāʿ): Fosterage; the act of a woman nursing a child not her own, establishing a legal relationship that creates marital prohibitions similar to blood.
دخل بها (dakhala bihā): Consummated the marriage by sexual intercourse.
عدة (ʿidda): Waiting period after divorce or death of husband, during which a woman cannot remarry.

Fosterage During Iddah: The Analogy to Marriage
Expands ruling on fosterage during iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that fosterage during iddah creates the same prohibitions as fosterage during marriage, using qiyas jaliy.

Core Idea
Iddah is like marriage for the prohibition of combining fosterage mothers and daughters.

This lesson explores a specific application of fosterage (radāʻ) prohibitions: when does breastfeeding during the waiting period (iddah) affect a man's other wives? The passage from al-Bahr al-Rāʼiq cites al-Badāʼiʻ and al-Muhīt to establish that the iddah period retains the legal character of marriage for the purpose of prohibiting the combination of women related by fosterage. Understanding this ruling requires tracing the qiyas (analogy) from marriage to iddah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) a divorced woman in iddah suckles another wife of her ex-husband; (2) the sister of a woman in iddah suckles another wife. In both, the suckled wife becomes forbidden (tantaqīʻ).
The key factor is the iddah, not the current marriage.
2
Extract the legal principle (asl)
The original ruling (asl) is: if a woman suckles her husband's other wife during marriage, that wife becomes a permanent mahram and the combination of the two women is prohibited.
This is established in the section on radāʻ.
3
Identify the new case (farʻ)
The new case is: the same suckling occurs during the iddah (after divorce) instead of during an ongoing marriage.
The iddah is a period after divorce/death where the woman cannot remarry.
4
Determine the effective cause (ʻilla)
The ʻilla is the existence of a marital tie (ḥurmat al-jamʻ) that prohibits combining two women who are mother-daughter by radāʻ. This tie persists during iddah because the woman is still legally bound to her husband in terms of prohibitions.
The ʻilla is jaliy (evident) and extracted by tahrīr al-manāṭ.
5
Derive the ruling for the new case
Since the ʻilla exists in the iddah as well, the same prohibition applies: the suckled wife becomes forbidden, and the combination of the two women is prohibited.
The passage explicitly states: 'الجمع في حال العدة كالجمع في حال قيام النكاح'.

Commentary
This passage expands on the ruling that fosterage during the iddah period has the same effect as fosterage during an existing marriage. The author cites two sources: al-Badāʼiʻ and al-Muhīt. They argue that the prohibition of combining two women (one being a mother by radāʻ and the other her daughter) applies during iddah because the iddah retains the legal bond of marriage in terms of prohibitions. The proof is by qiyas (analogy): just as combining such women is forbidden during marriage, it is also forbidden during iddah. Additionally, it is not required that the small wife be currently married to the man at the time of suckling; it suffices that such a marriage existed in the past.

Mahr Obligations When Fosterage Ends Marriage
Mahr rules for both women and the apostasy analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mahr obligations for the big woman and small girl when fosterage causes separation, and distinguish when the big woman receives full mahr, half mahr, or no mahr.

Core Idea
The big woman's mahr depends on fault and consummation; the small girl always gets half.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq explains how mahr is determined when fosterage (rida') occurs after marriage, leading to separation. The big woman may lose her mahr or get half, while the small girl always receives half. Understanding who is at fault is key to applying the rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two women
The 'big woman' is the foster mother, the 'small girl' is the foster daughter. Recognize their legal relationship to the husband.
The big woman is the wife who nurses; the small girl is the other wife who is nursed.
2
General rule for the big woman
If the husband has not had intercourse with her and the separation is attributed to her (she caused the fosterage), she gets no mahr. This is analogized to apostasy.
The phrase 'like her apostasy' indicates a qiyas: just as an apostate wife forfeits mahr, so does the big woman who causes the separation.
3
Exceptions for the big woman
If she was coerced, asleep, insane, or if someone else used her milk, the separation is not attributed to her, so she gets half mahr.
In these cases, she is not at fault, so she receives half the specified mahr.
4
Effect of consummation
If the husband had intercourse with the big woman, she gets full mahr regardless of fault. However, she loses maintenance during iddah if she caused the separation.
Consummation entitles her to full mahr, but iddah maintenance depends on fault.
5
Rule for the small girl
The small girl always gets half mahr because the separation is not attributed to her. An objection regarding apostasy of her parents is answered: apostasy is prohibited, unlike fosterage, so the rule stands.
Her dower is always half, even if she joins dar al-harb with apostate parents.

Husband's Recourse Against the Big Woman: Conditions and Principles
Recourse conditions and mutasabbib principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the five conditions for the husband to recover the small girl's mahr from the big woman, and explain the principle of mutasabbib liability.

Core Idea
Recourse requires intentional corruption by the big woman; the mutasabbib is liable only with transgression.

This passage continues with the husband's right to recoup from the big woman the half-mahr he must pay to the small girl. The ruling is not automatic; it depends on the big woman's intent. The text establishes the principle that an indirect causer (mutasabbib) is liable only if he transgresses (ta'addi), similar to a well-digger. Five precise conditions define intentional corruption.

Guided Path
1
Basic rule of recourse
The husband recourses against the big woman for the half-mahr he owes the small girl, but only if the big woman intentionally caused the corruption (ta'ammud al-fasad).
Without intent, no recourse.
2
Principle of mutasabbib liability
A causer (mutasabbib) is liable only when there is transgression (ta'addi). The example: a well-digger is liable if he digs on public property (transgression) but not on his own property.
This principle governs many Hanafi damage cases.
3
Distinct cases
The killer of a wife before consummation does not owe the husband anything because the husband gains something from the killing. Two unrelated women each nursing one of two small girls married to the same man are not liable individually because the corruption requires both acts, unlike the mother-daughter case where the big woman alone causes the prohibition.
These contrasts clarify when liability attaches.
4
Five conditions for intentional corruption
1) The big woman must be sane. 2) She must know of the marriage. 3) She must know that fosterage corrupts. 4) She must not be in need (e.g., not hungry). 5) She must be awake. All five must be present.
If any condition is missing, there is no ta'ammud and no recourse.
5
Evidentiary rule
The big woman's statement with an oath is accepted regarding her intent, unless outward signs (like feeding despite being full) indicate otherwise. This is because intent is internal.
The burden of proof is on the husband to show her intent, but her word is taken if no evidence contradicts.

Return of Half Mahr When Older Wife Intentionally Breastfeeds Younger Wife
Sharh explaining conditions and qiyas for mahr recovery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the husband's recovery of half-mahr from the older wife and explain the qiyas used to derive the liability.

Core Idea
The husband recovers half-mahr from the older wife only if she intentionally corrupts the marriage; without intent, there is no liability.

This lesson examines a specific ruling within Hanafi family law: the recovery of half the mahr from an older wife who intentionally breastfeeds a younger co-wife, causing the marriage to be corrupted. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the conditions for liability and the analogical reasoning (qiyas) that supports it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the older wife breastfeeding the younger wife, causing the marriage to become invalid due to fosterage. The ruling is that the husband can recover half the mahr he owes the younger wife from the older wife.
Half-mahr is due because the marriage was valid but corrupted before consummation.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas: comparing the older wife's act to digging a well on another's property. In that case, the digger is liable only if he acted transgressively. Similarly, the older wife is liable only if she acted intentionally (ta'addi).
The well-digger analogy is a standard Hanafi example for indirect causation (tasabbub).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The common effective cause ('illa) is the transgressive act (fi'l muta'addi) that leads to financial loss. The older wife's intentional breastfeeding is transgressive because she knows it will corrupt the marriage, causing the husband to lose half the mahr.
If she breastfeeds without intent (e.g., sleeping, forced), there is no transgression.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The passage contrasts with the killer of the wife before consummation, who is not liable for the mahr. The distinction is that the husband gains something from the killing (e.g., blood money), so imposing mahr liability would be double compensation.
This shows that liability for indirect damage requires that the victim not receive compensation from another source.
5
Apply the conditions for intent
Ta'amudd requires five conditions: sanity, knowledge of the marriage, knowledge that fosterage corrupts, no genuine need (full stomach), and being awake. If any condition is missing, the husband cannot recover.
The older wife's word is accepted with an oath unless evidence shows she intended.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a ruling found in Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the return of the half-mahr when a marriage is corrupted by fosterage. The author begins by quoting the matn: the husband can recover half the mahr from the older wife if she intentionally caused the corruption (ta'amudd al-fasad); otherwise, he cannot. The commentary clarifies that the husband's recourse is conditional on the older wife's deliberate act, likening the situation to the principle of daman bi al-tasabbub (liability through causation) with ta'addi (transgression). The classic example of the well digger is cited: if one digs a well on his own property, he is not liable; if on public property, he is liable. Similarly, the older wife is liable only if she acted with transgression (ta'addi) by intentionally breastfeeding the small wife knowing it would corrupt the marriage. The text then contrasts this with the case of a person who kills the wife before consummation: the killer is not liable for the mahr because the husband gains some benefit (i.e., the blood money or inheritance?) and to avoid double liability. Next, the author addresses a scenario where two stranger women (ajnabiyatan) each breastfeed one of two small girls who are married to one man. Even if both act deliberately, neither is held individually liable because the corruption arises from the combination of the two acts (joining the two sisters as co-wives), and each act alone would not have caused the corruption. This is distinguished from the main case where the corruption is due to joining mother and daughter, which is attributed solely to the older woman. The text then enumerates five conditions for ta'amudd: (1) sanity, (2) knowledge of the marriage, (3) knowledge that fosterage is a corruptor, (4) no genuine need (e.g., not breastfeeding out of hunger), (5) being awake. The author notes that if the older woman claims lack of intent, her word with an oath is accepted as long as no evidence of intent appears. Finally, the passage extends the liability to a stranger who physically put the breast in the mouth of the small child, making that stranger liable for the half-mahr as well.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores the evidence required to establish fosterage (rada'), which creates a marriage prohibition. Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the evidence required to prove fosterage (rada'). The author begins by stating that fosterage is established by the same evidence as property: the testimony of two just men or one man and two just women. This is because the prohibition of marriage due to fosterage is inseparable from the termination of marital rights, and the termination of rights requires two male witnesses. The author then notes the distinction with cases like purchasing meat where a single informant suffices for prohibition, because in those cases the prohibition of consumption is separate from ownership rights. The passage then delves into the disagreement regarding the testimony of a single woman, particularly before marriage. Some narrations accept it, others do not. The relied-upon position is that a single woman's testimony is not accepted either before or after marriage, based on the principle that a single witness cannot establish a legal claim that contradicts the apparent validity of the marriage. However, if a trustworthy person informs after marriage, it is recommended to separate out of caution. The passage also discusses the distinction between rada' occurring before and after the marriage contract. The author concludes with the mu'tamad (relied-upon) position and answers an objection regarding the husband's ownership over a minor wife.

Preferred Actions and Four Cases of Fosterage Claims
Preferred actions and four-case analysis for fosterage claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred actions (afdal) for husband and wife when a woman reports fosterage, and apply the four-case analysis to determine the effect on marriage and mahr.

Core Idea
Fosterage claims affect marriage only if believed; preferred actions guide but are not obligatory.

The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents practical guidance for scenarios where a third party claims that a married couple were foster-siblings. It distinguishes between preferred actions (afdal) and the actual legal effect based on the parties' belief. Understanding this nuance is crucial for applying precaution while respecting established marriage rights.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish preferred from mandatory
Identify which actions are marked as afdal (better) and which are binding legal consequences.
Afdal implies sunnah, not fard.
2
Analyze the four cases
Work through the four scenarios from Khazanat al-Fiqh: both believe, both deny, husband denies/wife believes, husband believes/wife denies.
Belief affects marriage validity and mahr.
3
Determine mahr consequences
Note the mahr rules for each case: full, half, mahr al-mithl, or nothing.
Consummation changes the obligation.
4
Understand the role of predominant opinion
When both deny, the husband's predominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) guides whether to separate as a precaution.
This is based on precaution, not evidence.
5
Apply the rule of oath and refusal
In case 3, the wife can demand an oath; refusal leads to separation.
Yamin and nukul are procedural tools.

Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on remedy
If the slave enters an invalid marriage with a free woman or mukatiba and consummates, what happens? Abu Hanifa says the slave is sold immediately to pay the lesser of his value or the dower. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say he works after emancipation to pay the dower. Their reasoning differs: Abu Hanifa sees the permission as covering the act but the prohibition exists; the Sahibayn see the permission as invalid for such a marriage.
The thamarah al-khilaf: immediate sale vs. post-emancipation work.
4
Extend to the mudabbar and mukatib cases
The passage also covers a mudabbar slave: his marriage is valid with the dower being his value, and he works to pay it. For a mukatib, the same rule applies. An objection arises: does the mukatib's giving his person as dower entail terminating the kitaba? The answer is no, because that would invalidate the marriage itself, which is not allowed.
The discussion reinforces the principle that the marriage is valid if the preventer is absent and permission is given.

Commentary
This passage begins with a reference to a previous issue about acknowledgment, then moves to the core topic: a master telling his slave to marry on his own account. The ruling is that if the slave marries a slave woman, mudabbara, or umm walad, it is valid because the preventer (the wife owning his person) is absent since she belongs to her master. However, if he marries a free woman, it is invalid because of the conflict of ownership: the free woman would own him by the marriage contract. Similarly, marrying a mukatib is invalid because she would have a right to his person. The author then discusses the consequence of entering into such an invalid marriage: according to Abu Hanifa, the slave is sold to pay the lesser of his value or the dower; according to the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), he works to pay after emancipation. The passage also covers the case of a mudabbar slave: the marriage is valid with the value in his person, and he works to pay. The author addresses an objection regarding the mukatib: does his undertaking to give his person as dower entail termination of the kitaba? The answer is that it does not, because that would invalidate the marriage. Finally, the passage ends with a note that all these cases are valid if proper permission and conditions are met.

Key Terms
على رقبتك (ala raqabatika): Meaning 'on your own account' or 'on your own person', i.e., the slave is responsible for the dower.
مكاتب (mukatib): A slave who has a contract of kitaba to earn his freedom by paying installments.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave who is promised freedom upon the master's death (tadbir).
