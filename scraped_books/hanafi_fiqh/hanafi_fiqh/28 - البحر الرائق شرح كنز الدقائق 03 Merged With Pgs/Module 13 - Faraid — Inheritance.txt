Inheritance Between Spouses After Apostasy: Istihsan Overrides Qiyas
Ruling on inheritance between apostate spouses with istihsan override

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance between spouses when one apostatizes and explain the distinction based on health versus illness using the concept of istihsan.

Core Idea
Apostasy does not automatically cut inheritance; timing and execution risk matter.

Inheritance rules are generally clear, but what happens when one spouse leaves Islam? This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq tackles that very question. The Hanafi school, using istihsan (juridical preference), creates a nuanced ruling that balances fairness with apostasy law. You will learn the specific conditions under which a Muslim spouse may inherit from an apostate spouse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cases
The passage discusses several scenarios: husband apostatizes, wife apostatizes, both apostatize. For each, note the ruling on inheritance.
2
Spot the distinction
The key factor is whether the apostasy occurred during health or illness. For the husband, no such distinction because his apostasy always puts his life at risk (execution if he refuses to return). For the wife, only if she apostatizes during illness does the husband inherit.
The concept of 'farr' (fugitive) is used: one who apostatizes to deprive heirs.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The ruling is based on istihsan. The analogy of being a 'fugitive' (farr) is used: the husband who apostatizes is like one trying to deprive his wife of inheritance, so she inherits. The wife's apostasy during illness also suggests intent to deprive, so he inherits. In health, she is not considered a fugitive.
Istihsan overrides qiyas which would deny inheritance.
4
Compare with the opposing view
Zufar follows strict qiyas: no inheritance at all because apostasy nullifies marriage retroactively. The text refutes this by invoking istihsan and the farr concept.
Zufar's view is a valid intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf but the mufta bihi is istihsan.
5
Apply to scenarios
Practice: If a husband apostatizes in health and is killed, does wife inherit? Yes. If a wife apostatizes in health and dies, does husband inherit? No. If she apostatizes during illness, yes.

His saying: 'He inherits from his apostate wife' etc. This is if her apostasy was during her illness. He said in al-Khaniyah from the chapter of the woman observing 'iddah who inherits: If a man apostatizes (Allah forbid) and is killed or joins dar al-harb or dies in dar al-Islam upon apostasy, his wife inherits from him. And if a woman apostatizes then dies or joins dar al-harb, if the apostasy was during health, the husband does not inherit from her, and if it was during illness, the husband inherits from her by istihsan. And if both apostatize together then one becomes Muslim, if the Muslim dies, the apostate does not inherit from him, and if the apostate dies and he is the husband, the Muslim wife inherits from him, and if the apostate wife dies, if her apostasy was during illness, the Muslim husband inherits from her, and if it was during health, he does not inherit. I say: The difference is that his apostasy is like a death sickness because he is killed if he refuses to return to Islam, so there is no difference between his apostasy in illness or health, so he is considered a fugitive, so she inherits from him when he dies, and she is in the 'iddah, unlike her apostasy during health because she is not killed, so she is not considered a fugitive.

Commentary
This passage details the inheritance rules between spouses when one apostatizes. The key distinction is whether the apostasy occurred during health or illness. For a husband who apostatizes, his wife inherits from him regardless of whether his apostasy was in health or illness, because his life is at risk (he may be executed), so it is treated like the sickness of death. For a wife who apostatizes, the husband inherits only if her apostasy occurred during illness; if in health, he does not inherit. The reasoning is that the husband's apostasy puts him in a position similar to a fugitive who attempts to deprive heirs, while the wife's apostasy in health does not carry the same implication. The text also covers cases where both apostatize and one returns to Islam, and cases of joining dar al-harb. The relied-upon position is istihsan, which allows inheritance in these scenarios, contrary to qiyas (the opinion of Zufar) which would deny inheritance.

Key Terms
إستحسان (istihsan): Juridical preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a more equitable ruling.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning that applies an existing ruling to a new case based on a common cause.
فار (farr): A fugitive, one who tries to deprive rightful heirs by changing circumstances; relevant in inheritance law.
