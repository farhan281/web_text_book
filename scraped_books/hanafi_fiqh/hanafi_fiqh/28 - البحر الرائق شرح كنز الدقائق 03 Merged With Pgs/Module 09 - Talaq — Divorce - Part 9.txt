Immediate Effect of Conditional Divorce on a Past Event
Ruling that conditional divorce on a past event takes immediate effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional divorce based on a past event and explain the principle of 'suspending on an existing thing gives immediate effect'.

Core Idea
A conditional divorce based on a past event takes immediate effect because the condition is already fulfilled.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a key Hanafi principle: when a conditional divorce refers to a known past event, the divorce is effective immediately. The commentary also addresses a related objection concerning statements of disbelief. Understanding this ruling clarifies the distinction between genuine conditions and mere acknowledgments of existing facts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the opening line: 'وإن كان لشيء مضى طلقت' — if the condition refers to something that has passed, she is divorced. State the ruling in simple terms.
The ruling is that divorce is immediate and valid.
2
Extract the legal maxim
The text gives the reason: 'لأن التعليق بالكائن تنجيز' — suspending on an existing thing is immediate effect. Understand this maxim and its application.
This maxim is the proof for the ruling.
3
Examine the examples
The text lists scenarios: 'if my father is in the house' while he is there, 'if it is night' while it is night, etc. Apply the maxim to each.
All these cases result in immediate divorce.
4
Analyze the objection about disbelief
The text raises an objection: if the principle applied to disbelief, saying 'he is a disbeliever if you did X' while X occurred would make one a disbeliever. Follow the response: disbelief requires a change of belief, which does not occur here.
The distinction is that the utterance is not a real pronouncement of disbelief; only the ruling of the wording occurs.
5
Conclude with the practical outcome
The divorce ruling is clear: conditional on a past event is like an immediate divorce. For disbelief, the ruling differs due to the nature of the act.
This reinforces that the maxim applies to transactions and family law, not to matters of belief.

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling when a conditional divorce refers to a past event that is known to have occurred. The principle is: 'suspending on an existing thing gives immediate effect' (التعليق بالكائن تنجيز). Therefore, if a wife says 'I will if so-and-so came' and he indeed came, the divorce takes effect immediately, as if it were unconditional. This applies equally to present events (e.g., 'if my father is in the house' while he is there) and identifications (e.g., 'if this is my father' and it is). The text then addresses an objection: if someone says 'he is a disbeliever if you did such and such' while knowing the action occurred, would that make him a disbeliever? The chosen position is no, because disbelief requires a change of belief, which does not occur merely by uttering a conditional statement. However, the wording that necessitates disbelief (e.g., 'he is a disbeliever in Allah') does not require a change of belief when uttered unconditionally. The key distinction is that when the condition is known to exist, the utterance is considered to have occurred at the time of the condition, not after, so it is not a real pronouncement of disbelief. Thus, for divorce, the ruling is clear: conditional on a past event is like an immediate divorce.

Key Terms
تنجيز (tanjiz): Immediate effect; a conditional statement on an existing fact is considered immediate execution.
التعليق بالكائن تنجيز (al-ta'liq bi al-ka'in tanjiz): A legal maxim meaning that suspending a ruling on something already existing gives immediate effect.

Key Takeaway

Timeless Condition: 'Whenever You Wish'
Ruling on divorce conditioned on wife's wish using timeless words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for divorce phrases using timeless words and explain the difference between delegation and suspension.

Core Idea
A husband's phrase 'you are divorced whenever you wish' is treated as delegation, giving the wife power to divorce once at any time.

When a husband uses phrases like 'متى شئت' or 'إذا شئت', the Hanafi school applies a nuanced analysis: are these pure conditions that expire, or delegations that continue? The answer affects the wife's ability to divorce after the immediate gathering. This lesson unpacks the linguistic reasoning and the key dispute within the school, ensuring you understand the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its legal classification
Recognize phrases like 'متى شئت' and 'إذا شئت' as conditional divorce statements. Determine whether they are treated as delegation (tamlik) or suspension (ta'liq).
In Hanafi fiqh, the default is suspension, but here the jurists treat them as delegation in meaning.
2
Understand the linguistic basis for 'mata'
The word 'متى' is purely temporal and general for all times. Thus, the wife may divorce herself at any time, but only once because it generalizes over times, not actions.
This is the ruling in al-Hidayah and supported by Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze the dispute over 'idha'
The Sahibayn treat 'إذا' like 'متى' (time), while Abu Hanifa also considers it delegation because the matter is placed in her hand. Fath al-Qadir objects, viewing it as a pure condition that expires after the gathering.
The mufta bihi is the position of al-Hidayah (treating it like 'متى').
4
Recognize the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If treated as delegation, the wife can divorce at any later time; if as a condition, she must decide in the gathering. This affects real-world cases like delayed decisions.
The difference hinges on whether the husband intended to give ongoing power or a one-time condition.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
According to the relied-upon fatwa, for both 'متى' and 'إذا', the wife may divorce at any time with one divorce, and her refusal before willing does not revoke the delegated right.
This ruling is from al-Hidayah and al-Muhit.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling on expressions such as 'you are divorced whenever you wish' (mata shi'ti) and similar timeless conditions. The central issue is whether such a statement is a delegation (tamlik) or a pure suspension (ta'liq). According to the author of al-Hidayah, with 'mata' and 'mata ma', the word is for time and is general, so the wife can divorce herself at any time with one divorce only. Her refusal is not effective because she only becomes owner of the divorce at the moment she wills. She cannot give herself more than one divorce because the word generalizes over times, not actions. This is the position of the Hanafi school. However, Fath al-Qadir objects, arguing that it is not delegation at all but a suspension. The response is that the jurists have treated it as delegation in all practical respects, so it is restricted to the gathering and can be voided by indication of aversion. The commentary from al-Muhit clarifies that it combines meanings of suspension and delegation. For the word 'idha' (if/when), the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) treat it like 'mata', while Abu Hanifa considers it possibly a condition but because the matter is in her hand, it does not go out by doubt. Fath al-Qadir again objects, saying 'idha' is pure condition and thus the wife loses the right after the gathering. The text also covers 'hin' (time) and combinations of 'in' (if) and 'idha' (when), where multiple conditions apply.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Word Order in Delegated Divorce: All Formulations Equal?
Resolving the effect of word order in conditional divorce delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of word order in a conditional divorce delegation and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Word order in delegation does not narrow the wife's right: Abu Hanifa sees both 'idha' and 'in' as equal.

In this passage from Fath al-Qadir, a subtle but important intra-Hanafi dispute is resolved. The husband delegates divorce using two conditional particles, 'idha' and 'in'. The passage clarifies that according to Abu Hanifa, the order does not matter—the wife can act at any time. Abu Yusuf's distinction is noted but not adopted as the operative fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the husband's statement and the two particles
The husband says 'You are divorced if you wish, if you will' or the reverse. The particles in question are 'idha' (when) and 'in' (if).
The exact wording is crucial for the dispute.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that both formulations are equivalent; the wife may divorce herself at any time she wishes (متى شاءت).
This is the relied-upon position.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position and his distinction
Abu Yusuf agrees if 'in' is delayed, but if 'in' is advanced, the wife must will in the assembly; otherwise the delegation is void.
His reasoning: 'in' implies immediacy.
4
Identify the practical consequence
If the wife leaves the assembly without willing, Abu Hanifa allows her to will later, while Abu Yusuf's advanced case voids the delegation.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its rationale
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as it grants broad authority to the wife consistent with the husband's delegation.
The tarjih is based on the principle of honoring the delegation.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the interpretation of a husband's statement delegating divorce to his wife using two conditional particles: 'إذا' (when) and 'إن' (if). This delegation is a form of ta'liq (suspension) of divorce on a future condition.

In Arabic, 'كلما' is composed of 'كل' (every) and 'ما' (what), and it indicates repetition: every time a condition occurs, a result follows. Here, it implies that each separate wish triggers a distinct divorce.
The Hanafi usul distinguishes 'umum al-infirād' (generality of individuality) from 'umum al-ijtimā'' (generality of combination). 'كلما' operates on the former.

The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view, as it aligns with the literal meaning of 'كلما'.
5

If the wife claims she wished for a divorce yesterday but the husband denies it, her word is not accepted because she cannot initiate the condition after the fact. Each wish must be present and executable.
This is because the condition (the wish) must be contemporaneous with the statement; past wishes are not within her power to create now.

The passage cites Quran 46:25 ('تدمر كل شيء') to illustrate that 'كل' can mean 'much' rather than 'every single thing', supporting the idea that 'كل' with 'ما' indicates repetition and not universal simultaneity.

Interpretation of 'حيث شئت' and 'أين شئت' in Delegation of Divorce
Expansion on delegation rulings for place-phrases and second husband effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for 'حيث شئت' and 'أين شئت' in delegation of divorce and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of a second husband.

Core Idea
Words of place in divorce delegation are conditions requiring the wife's wish in the council; a second husband demolishes all prior divorces per the relied-upon opinion.

In this lesson, we examine how the Hanafi school interprets place-based phrases in delegation of divorce. The key insight is that 'حيث شئت' and 'أين شئت' are not literal but conditional, requiring the wife's immediate wish in the council. We also explore a subtle disagreement between Imam Muhammad and the Sahibayn regarding the effect of a subsequent marriage on the delegation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its literal meaning
Recognize that 'حيث شئت' and 'أين شئت' are adverbs of place (ẓarf makān) meaning 'where you wish' and 'wherever you wish'. Literally, they refer to location.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle that divorce has no connection to place
Since divorce (ṭalāq) is a legal act unrelated to physical location, the literal meaning cannot be intended. The text must be interpreted metaphorically (majāz).
This is an application of istiḥsān: giving precedence to the meaning over the literal wording when the literal is impossible.
3
Interpret metaphorically as a condition (sharṭ)
The words are treated like the conditional particle 'in' (if), implying that the divorce is suspended on the wife's wish expressed in the council. The council becomes the locus for the condition.
The author argues that 'حيث' and 'أين' convey a sense of delay (taʼkhīr), so they function like 'in' rather than 'matā' (when).
4
Determine the consequences of leaving the council
If she stands up from the council without expressing her wish, the condition is broken and the divorce does not occur. She loses her right to delegate.
The phrase 'فيه يبطل بالقيام' means 'in it (the condition) it becomes invalid by standing up'.
5
Analyze the effect of a second husband on the delegation
After a second marriage, the first marriage is completely replaced. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, all previous divorces (including less than three) are demolished, so the delegation is void. Muhammad holds that only three are demolished; less than three remain and can be used in a new marriage with the first husband.
This intra-Hanafi dispute hinges on the extent of 'hadm' (demolition) by the second husband.

Ruling on 'كيف شئت' in Delegation of Divorce
Intra-Hanafi dispute on delegation with 'كيف شئت'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'كيف شئت' in divorce delegation, identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
The divorce occurs immediately as revocable; only the manner is delegated.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: when a husband says to his wife 'You are divorced how you wish,' does the divorce take effect immediately or only upon her wish? Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on the interpretation of the word 'كيف' (how). Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly applying the rules of delegating divorce (tafwīḍ al-ṭalāq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed phrase
The phrase 'كيف شئت' (how you wish) in the context of divorce delegation. The dispute centers on whether this phrase makes the divorce itself conditional or only delegates the manner.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the divorce occurs immediately as revocable (raj'ī) for a consummated wife, and the manner (e.g., irrevocable bā'in or triple) is delegated to her. If she does not wish, it remains revocable.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, the divorce is conditional on the wife's wish because the manner (the state) is inseparable from the act. If she stands up before wishing, no divorce occurs.
4
Analyze the linguistic basis
Abu Hanifa argues that 'كيف' is originally an adverb for state (ḥāl) and does not imply conditionality. The Sahibayn argue that since the state is attached, the original action is also attached.
5
Apply the practical consequence
If the wife leaves the council without choosing, Abu Hanifa rules a revocable divorce occurs; the Sahibayn rule nothing occurs. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's position.

Commentary
This passage discusses a dispute regarding the expression 'divorce yourself from three as you wish' (طَلِّقِي من ثلاث ما شِئتِ). Imam Abu Hanifa rules that she cannot divorce all three; she can only divorce less than three (i.e., one or two). The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that she may divorce all three because 'ma' indicates generality and 'min' is for clarification (i.e., 'from that which is three'). The text from al-Tahrir prefers the view that 'min' indicates partiality (tab'id), so the phrase means 'from the set of three, choose what you wish' — implying she cannot take the whole set. The passage also notes an application: if a woman returns after another husband (i.e., after being divorced thrice and remarried) and the husband says this, then according to the two she could divorce thrice again? But the response is that the place (mahall) remains after two divorces because the quality of permissibility (hilla) still exists; the oath covers the type, so it extends even if not intended. This is a complex technical discussion about the scope of delegation when limited to three.

The Partitive 'Min' in Delegated Divorce
Imam Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn disagree on wife's ability to issue all three divorces.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the wife's capacity to issue three divorces when the husband uses the phrase 'طلاقي من ثلاث ما شئت', explain the linguistic basis (partitive vs clarifying 'min'), and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The partitive 'min' restricts the wife to less than three divorces in conditional delegation.

When a husband delegates divorce authority to his wife with the wording 'my divorces from three as you wish', does she have the power to end the marriage with all three? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples disagree. The resolution hinges on the precise meaning of the Arabic preposition 'min' (from). This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks the linguistic reasoning and settles on the preferred view.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the wording
Identify the husband's statement: 'طلاقي من ثلاث ما شئت' (my divorces from three as you wish). This is a conditional delegation (tafwid al-talaq) where the wife is empowered to divorce herself.
The wording includes 'min thalath' (from three) which is the key phrase.
2
Identify the two positions
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the wife can divorce less than three but not three. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that she can divorce all three.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze the linguistic argument
Imam interprets 'min' as partitive (للتبعيض), meaning 'some of the three'. The Sahibayn interpret 'min' as clarifying (للبيان), meaning 'as you wish from the three' – which does not exclude the whole.
The ruling turns on whether 'min' indicates a portion or merely specification.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam's view, if the wife attempts a third divorce, it is ineffective (batil). Under the Sahibayn, it is effective and the marriage is irrevocably terminated.
This is the practical difference in outcome.
5
Note the tarjih and extension
The commentary in Al-Tahrir prefers Imam's view. An objection from Al-Muhit about the third divorce after two is answered by saying the 'mahal' (subject) persists because the attribute of being lawful remains.
The later tradition relies on Imam's position as mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is an expansion probe (sharh) on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, addressing the fiqh of conditional divorce. The commentator begins by stating the ruling: if a husband says to his wife 'you divorce as you wish' (تطلق ما شاءت), she may divorce as she wishes, and if she apostatizes (rejects Islam) she returns to disbelief (i.e., her divorce is effective? Actually the text says 'وإن ردت ارتد' meaning if she refuses? Possibly a separate ruling). The core issue is the wording 'طلاقي من ثلاث ما شئت' (my divorces from three as you wish). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, she can divorce less than three (i.e., one or two) but not all three. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that she can divorce all three because they interpret 'ما' (ma) as general (للمعموم) and 'من' (min) as clarifying (للبيان), making the phrase mean 'as you wish from the three' i.e., any number including three. Abu Hanifa argues that 'من' is partitive (للتبعيض), so it means 'some of the three', which excludes the whole. The commentary in Al-Tahrir prefers Abu Hanifa's view, noting that if it were clarifying, the phrase would be 'as you wish from that which is the three', but the addition of 'من' before the number emphasizes partitivity. Then the commentator cites Al-Muhit addressing an objection: if she divorces two, can she later divorce the third? The objection says that after two divorces, she is no longer lawful to him without an intervening marriage (زوج آخر), so the remaining divorce cannot be issued. The answer is that the 'subject' (محل) of the oath remains after two divorces because the attribute of being lawful (صفة الحل) remains potentially; the oath covers the type, so the third divorce can still occur as a consequence of the original oath. Finally, the commentator notes that 'the will' (المشيئة) is restricted to the created being, i.e., the wife, not to Allah. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of linguistic analysis (talil) to derive rulings from ambiguous contractual language.

Key Terms
المشيئة (al-mashi'ah): The will or option given to the wife to divorce herself at her discretion.
من (min): A preposition interpreted as partitive (للتبعيض) by Abu Hanifa, indicating a portion of the whole.
المحل (al-mahal): The subject of the oath, i.e., the wife's capacity for divorce remaining after two divorces.
