Rulings on Quantity and Recipients in Jaza' al-Sayd Feeding
Expanding the feeding quantity and recipient restrictions in expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required quantity per poor person and list the prohibited recipients in jaza' al-sayd feeding according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Jaza' al-sayd feeding uses fitrah quantities but excludes close family from receiving.

In jaza' al-sayd, the penitent may feed the poor as an expiation. The Hanafi school draws an analogy to zakat al-fitr for the quantity: half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates/barley per poor person. However, certain recipients are prohibited: one's own ascendants, descendants, and spouse. Additionally, giving to a dhimmi is permissible though a Muslim is preferred. This lesson unpacks these rulings and the scope of the analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quantity per poor person
The text explicitly states the quantities: half a sa' of wheat or a full sa' of dates or barley. This matches the standard for zakat al-fitr, establishing the analogy in amount.
The sa' is a volumetric measure, approximately 2.4 kg for wheat.
2
List the prohibited categories of recipients
The text prohibits giving to one's father (or any ascendant), one's child (or any descendant), one's spouse (husband/wife), and those whose testimony is not accepted for the giver (e.g., a partner in a lawsuit). These restrictions mirror those for other obligatory charities.
The prohibition is based on the general rule for wajib sadaqa; giving to such relatives would be like giving to oneself.
3
Understand the scope of the analogy
The analogy to fitrah is specifically in the quantity and the overall obligation, not in all details. For example, the mode of giving (ibaha vs tamlik) differs, as will be seen in a later lesson.
This is crucial: the commentator notes that the author's objection about ibaha is answered by limiting the analogy to quantity.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the rulings related to the feeding option in the expiation for killing game while in ihram (jaza' al-sayd). The commentator explains that the author (the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq) draws an analogy between this feeding and zakat al-fitr (fitrah) in terms of the quantity per poor person: half a sa' of wheat or a sa' of dates or barley. However, the commentator notes that unlike fitrah, in jaza' al-sayd the number of poor people is specified (the text indicates it should be six, though not explicitly stated here), so one cannot give all the food to a single poor person in one go. He also clarifies that it is permissible to give the food to a dhimmi, though a Muslim is preferable. Additionally, one cannot give to one's own ascendants or descendants, nor to one's spouse, as this is the rule for all obligatory charities. The commentator addresses an objection: in jaza' al-sayd, merely allowing the poor person to consume (ibaha) suffices, unlike fitrah where ownership (tamlik) is required. This is based on the ruling of Imam al-Aspijabi. He also discusses the permissibility of paying the monetary value instead of the actual food, stating it is allowed. Finally, he addresses the question of feeding multiple poor people over several days or in one day, concluding that the relied-upon position is that feeding six poor people in one day to one person does not count for the whole; it only counts for one.

Key Terms
جزاء الصيد (jaza' al-sayd): The expiation required from a muhrim who kills game; it consists of an equivalent sacrifice, feeding the poor, or fasting.
الفطرة (al-fitrah): Zakat al-fitr, the obligatory charity at the end of Ramadan.
إباحة (ibaha): Permission; allowing the poor person to consume the food without transferring ownership.
تمليك (tamlik): Making someone an owner; transferring ownership of the food to the poor person.

The Dispute on Mode of Giving and Scheduling in Jaza' al-Sayd Feeding
Analyzing two disputes: mode of giving and feeding schedule in expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that ibaha suffices in jaza' al-sayd feeding and identify the mufta bihi position on feeding multiple poor to one person in one day.

Core Idea
In jaza' al-sayd, permission to eat suffices; tamlik is not required, and feeding one person for multiple days counts only for one.

This lesson addresses two points of dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the feeding expiation for killing game. First, does the penitent need to give ownership (tamlik) of the food, or does merely allowing the poor to consume (ibaha) suffice? Second, if the penitent gives the total amount for six poor people to a single person in one day, does that count for all six? The Hanafi school, based on a limited analogy to fitrah, holds that ibaha is sufficient and that feeding six at once only counts for one.

Guided Path
1
State the objection on mode of giving
The author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq drew a full analogy to fitrah, which requires tamlik. Some objected that ibaha should not suffice in jaza' al-sayd.
This objection is based on a strict analogy.
2
Present the Hanafi response on mode
The commentator clarifies that the analogy is only in quantity, as stated in al-Nahr: 'التشبيه إنما هو في المقدار'. Thus, the mode is not bound by fitrah. Imam al-Aspijabi explicitly stated that ibaha suffices.
The text is absolute and does not require tamlik.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on feeding schedule
If the penitent gives the food of six poor people to one person over six days (e.g., half a sa' each day), it is valid. But if given in one day, there is a difference: some scholars say it counts for all six, but the majority say it only counts for one, and the fatwa is on that.
The majority opinion is relied upon.
4
Determine the mufta bihi on schedule
The relied-upon position is that giving the entire amount to one person in one day only counts for one recipient. The penitent must feed the remaining five poor separately.
This is from the fatwa mentioned in the text.
5
Note the permissibility of paying value
The text also states that it is permissible to pay the monetary value of the food instead of the actual grains, following the analogy to fitrah where value is accepted.
This is an additional flexibility.
