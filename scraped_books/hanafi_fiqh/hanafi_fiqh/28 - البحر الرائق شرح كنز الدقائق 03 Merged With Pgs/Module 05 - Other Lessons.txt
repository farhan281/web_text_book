Hiring for Acts of Worship: Hanafi Ruling and Exceptions
Critical refinement of a fatwa on hiring for Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring for Hajj and other acts of worship, identify the exceptions (Quran, fiqh, imamah, adhan), and explain the refutation of the opposing view from Qadikhan.

Core Idea
Hiring for acts of worship is generally invalid in the Hanafi school, except for teaching Quran, fiqh, leading prayer, and adhan.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a potential contradiction: Qadikhan's Fatawa appears to permit hiring for Hajj. The author clarifies that the relied-upon Hanafi position prohibits hiring for worship, and the apparent permission is based on a different legal mechanism (nafaqah mithli, not ajr). This lesson unpacks the dispute, the refutation, and the subsidiary consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the general Hanafi position
Identify the rule that hiring for worship (e.g., Hajj) is not permissible. Note the source of authority: matn, al-Mukhtar, al-Majma', etc.
The rule is established in major Hanafi texts.
2
Identify the exceptions
List the four acts exempted: teaching Quran, teaching fiqh, leading prayer (imamah), and giving adhan.
These are mentioned as exceptions in various texts; collected in Tanwir al-Absar.
3
Formulate the objection
State Qadikhan's apparent ruling: if a prisoner hires someone to perform Hajj for him, the Hajj is valid and the agent deserves 'like wage'.
This seems to contradict the general prohibition.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The author explains that Qadikhan used 'ajr al-mithl' figuratively; the actual entitlement is 'nafaqah al-mithl' (maintenance), not contractual wage. The hiring is void, but the agent is compensated by way of sufficiency.
This preserves the principle while addressing the edge case.
5
Evaluate subsidiary consequences
If hiring were allowed, many subsidiary rules would break: obligation to return surplus, condition of spending from deceased's money, etc. This shows the internal consistency of the school.
The refutation relies on consistency with established furu'.

Three Cases Where Abu Yusuf Retracted from Istihsan to Qiyas
Lists three specific cases where Abu Yusuf changed his ruling from istihsan to qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three cases where Abu Yusuf changed his position from istihsan to qiyas and explain why qiyas prevailed in each.

Core Idea
Even senior Hanafi jurists like Abu Yusuf can retract from istihsan when qiyas proves stronger.

This lesson examines three famous cases (masail) from the Hanafi school where Imam Abu Yusuf initially adopted an istihsan-based ruling, then later retracted to a qiyas-based ruling. These cases illustrate that istihsan does not always override qiyas; the mujtahid must weigh the evidence. Understanding these retractions deepens your appreciation of how Hanafi fiqh evolves through rigorous reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common pattern
Notice that in all three cases, Abu Yusuf first held the same view as Muhammad al-Shaybani (istihsan), then later adopted a different view based on qiyas. This pattern shows internal development within the school.
The three cases involve: (1) guarantee/pledge for mut'ah, (2) sajdah tilawah on repeating a verse, (3) master's choice after slave's injury victim dies.
2
Analyze each case's istihsan vs qiyas
For case 1, istihsan extends guarantee and pledge to mut'ah; qiyas restricts them to what was specified. For case 2, istihsan requires a new sajdah for each recitation; qiyas suffices with one. For case 3, istihsan denies a second choice for the master; qiyas grants it.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is real: liability, recommended acts, and contractual options change.
3
Understand why qiyas prevailed
The passage states that Abu Yusuf found 'the strength of the qiyas' (quwwat al-qiyas) to be compelling. This means the analogical reasoning in these cases was more consistent with general principles and less prone to arbitrariness.
In Hanafi usul, qiyas is a primary source; istihsan is a departure, so it must be justified. Here, the justification was insufficient.
4
Note the riwayah status
The passage attributes the istihsan view to both Muhammad and Abu Yusuf's first view, and the qiyas view to Abu Yusuf's final view. This is a classic example of intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf where the later authority (Abu Yusuf) changed his position.
In fatwa practice, the later view of a mujtahid is often given more weight (al-akhdhu bil-akhar).

Commentary
This passage lists three famous cases (masail) where Imam Abu Yusuf changed his position from istihsan (juristic preference) to qiyas (analogy) in his later view. In the first case, a person guarantees the mahr al-mithl, but the wife is entitled to mut'ah; by istihsan the guarantee and pledge extend to mut'ah, but by qiyas they do not. Abu Yusuf later adopted qiyas. The second case: if a person recites a sajdah verse in one rak'ah and repeats the same verse in the next rak'ah, by istihsan each recitation requires a separate prostration, but by qiyas one prostration suffices. His final view is qiyas. The third case: a slave injures someone below the degree of murder; the master may hand over the slave or pay ransom; if he pays ransom and then the victim dies, by istihsan the master is not given a second choice, but by qiyas he is given a second choice (since the injury escalated). Abu Yusuf's final view is qiyas. In all three, Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan held the istihsan view, and Abu Yusuf initially agreed with him but later retracted. The passage highlights the dynamic nature of Hanafi fiqh and the role of reasoning in refining rulings.

Key Terms
كفيل (kafīl): A guarantor who undertakes responsibility for a debt or obligation.
رهن (rahn): A pledge or collateral given as security for a debt.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; departing from qiyas for a stronger reason (custom, necessity, or equity).
قياس (qiyās): Analogical deduction; extending a ruling from an original case to a new case due to shared effective cause ('illah).

The Definition and Legal Effect of Rada'
Defining rada' and its legal effect of marriage prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of rada' and explain why even a small amount of suckling establishes a permanent marriage prohibition.

Core Idea
Rada' (fosterage) is defined as suckling from a human breast within a specified time, and it creates a permanent mahram bond even if the milk is little.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi definition of rada' (fosterage) and its central legal effect: the establishment of a permanent marriage prohibition. The passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq explains both the linguistic origin of the term and the technical shar'i definition, emphasizing that the prohibition applies even if the amount of milk consumed is small. Understanding this definition is crucial for distinguishing fosterage from other forms of feeding and for recognizing the resulting mahram relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its definitional components
The passage defines rada' as 'suckling (maṣṣ) of an infant (raḍīʿ) from the breast (thady) of a human woman in a specified time (waqt makhṣūṣ).' Break down each component: the act of sucking, the source (human breast), the recipient (infant), and the temporal condition.
The Hanafi definition requires the act of sucking; pouring milk directly into the mouth does not count.
2
Note the legal effect: prohibition (ḥurmah)
The author states 'wa ḥurrima bihi wa in qalla fī dhālik' – prohibition is established by it even if little in that (specified time). This is a core Hanafi ruling: even one suckling creates a mahram relationship.
This distinguishes the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i requirement of five separate feedings.
3
Distinguish the technical definition from linguistic usage
The passage gives extensive linguistic background: rada' in language simply means suckling the breast absolutely. In legal terminology, it is restricted to human milk, within a specific time (before weaning), and from a human woman.
The legal definition narrows the general linguistic meaning.
4
Recognize the placement of this book in the fiqh structure
The author explains why the Book of Rada' appears after the Book of Nikah: offspring is the purpose of marriage, and infants need breastfeeding. The general rule was mentioned earlier; here the details are given.
This shows the logical arrangement of fiqh chapters.
5
Address the historical note about authorship
The passage mentions a debate: some say this book is not from Imam Muhammad but from his students, though the majority consider it among his early works. This affects the authority weight of the rulings.
The majority opinion (ʿāmmatuhum) accepts it as part of the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah.

The Grammar of Talaq: Does 'Anti al-Thalath' Effect One or Three Divorces?
Dispute over the word 'al-Thalath' in the talaq phrase – does it make three or one?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute over the phrase 'Anti al-Thalath' and identify the grammatical basis for each position.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, 'Anti al-Thalath' is ambiguous: grammar determines whether it means three talaqs or one.

This passage dives into an intra-Hanafi dispute over the grammatical interpretation of the word 'al-Thalath' in the divorce phrase 'Anti al-Thalath'. Ibn al-Humam critiques Muhammad al-Shaybani's answer, and later scholars like Ibn al-Sa'igh, al-Damamini, and al-Shamni contribute their analyses. Understanding this dispute requires close attention to Arabic grammar and how it shapes legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its ambiguity
The phrase 'Anti al-Thalath' can be parsed grammatically in two ways: 'al-Thalath' in the accusative (nasb) as an absolute object, or in the nominative (raf') as a predicate. Each reading leads to a different legal result.
Nasb implies 'I divorce you with three talaqs'; raf' could mean 'you are thrice-divorced' (one talaq via metaphorical genus) or 'you are the thrice-divorced woman I remember' (three talaqs via remembered covenant).
2
Trace the dispute among Hanafi scholars
Muhammad al-Shaybani answered based on the apparent meaning, leading to three talaqs. Ibn al-Humam objected, arguing that the more probable reading is the remembered covenant, which also gives three, but by different reasoning. Ibn al-Sa'igh, al-Damamini, and al-Shamni then debated specific grammatical points.
Ibn al-Humam's objection is that Muhammad's answer relied on a less probable grammatical analysis.
3
Note the reliance on Arabic grammar
The dispute revolves around the meaning of the definite article 'al-' (lam) in 'al-Thalath'. Is it for the real genus (impossible), metaphorical genus (one talaq), collective whole (three as a set), or remembered covenant (three)? Al-Shamni systematically refutes each alternative.
Key terms: al-ahd al-dhikri (remembered covenant), al-maf'ul al-mutlaq (absolute object).
4
Distinguish between intention and apparent meaning
The passage clarifies that the husband's intention matters but is limited by the wording. If he says 'Anti al-Thalath' and claims no talaq, his claim is not accepted because the wording does not bear it. If he says 'Anti minni bi-thalath' and claims one, that is possible as a kinayah.
The ruling depends on whether the phrase is a sarīh (direct) or kināyah (allusive).
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In a talaq context, the default is three talaqs. The husband's denial of intention is not credited. The muftā bihi is that three occur.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Istinad and Compensation for Injury to a Partially Freed Slave
Commentary on injury compensation for a partially freed slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istinad to determine that compensation for an injury to a partially freed slave is calculated based on slave value, not free person value.

Core Idea
Compensation refers back to the original ownership, not the expected freedom.

When a slave is partially freed through a bequest and then suffers an injury before full freedom, the compensation is not based on the slave's future status as a free person. Instead, it is determined by the ownership that existed at the time of injury. This ruling hinges on the key Hanafi concept of istinad, which anchors legal effects to their original causes. In this lesson, we will unpack the logic behind this ruling and see how it applies through analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
Read the passage to find the main ruling: if a slave bequeathed (partially freed) is injured during the month of the bequest, the compensation (arsh) is that of a slave, not a free person. Note the phrase 'لا استناد في الفائت والخلف'.
The ruling applies even though the slave becomes free at the end of the month.
2
Understand the Principle of Istinad
The text states that there is no istinad (legal reference back) for the lost benefit (future freedom) and the substitute (compensation). The substitute follows the original in what it accepts, namely ownership (al-milk), not freedom. This means the compensation is linked to the status at the time of injury.
Istinad is a Hanafi device that can retroactively apply rulings, but here it is explicitly excluded for the lost freedom.
3
Analyze the First Analogy: Injury to the Sa'i
The passage gives an analogy: if someone acting as an agent (sa'i) in his father's kitabah is injured, the compensation is for him as a slave, not as a free person, even if he later becomes free. This parallels the case of the partially freed slave.
The key is that the injury occurred while the person was still in a state of ownership (partial slavery).
4
Apply the Khalaf Principle
The substitute (khalaf) – here, the compensation – inherits the legal characteristics of the original (the hand) in matters of ownership, not freedom. Since the original right (the hand) was owned, its substitute is also subject to ownership rules.
This principle ensures consistency: the substitute does not change the legal nature of the original.
5
Distinguish What Istinad Accepts
The text clarifies that istinad applies to 'what it accepts' (مالا يقبله). It accepts ownership-based effects (e.g., establishing a debt) but does not accept freedom-based effects (e.g., nullifying manumission). Thus, the compensation is calculated as for a slave.
This distinction prevents retroactive reclassification of events based on future changes in status.
