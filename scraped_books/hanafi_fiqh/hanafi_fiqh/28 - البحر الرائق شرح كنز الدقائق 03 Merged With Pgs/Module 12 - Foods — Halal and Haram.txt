Ruling on Accepting Food Invitation from Magians and Christians vs. Jews
Ruling on accepting food invitation from non-Muslims with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for eating food invited by a Magian, Christian, and Jew, and explain the underlying reason for the distinction.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the food of Magians and Christians is disliked; Jewish food is permissible due to valid slaughter.

This lesson examines the Hanafi classification of food from non-Muslim hosts. Understanding the distinction between Magians, Christians, and Jews is essential for a Muslim who receives an invitation, as the ruling differs based on the host's religious affiliation and their slaughter practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of non-Muslim hosts
The passage specifies Magians (Zoroastrians), Christians (Nasara), and Jews (Yahud). Note that each is treated differently in terms of the permissibility of their food.
Magians are akin to polytheists; Christians and Jews are People of the Book but their slaughter methods differ.
2
State the ruling for each category
For Magians and Christians, eating their food is makruh (disliked). For Jews, it is permissible (la ba'sa – no harm). This is the explicit ruling in the text.
'Makruh' here is interpreted as makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) in the Hanasi school.
3
Explain the reason for dislike of Magian and Christian food
Magians cook animals that died by unnatural causes (strangled, beaten, fallen). Christians do not have a valid slaughter; they rely on Muslim slaughter or strangle animals. Thus their food is suspect.
The impurity of the cooking vessels is also a concern, as indicated by the later discussion on buying meat from the market.
4
Explain the permissibility of Jewish food
Jews follow a lawful method of slaughter (dhabh) and only consume meat from their own slaughter or that of Muslims. Hence their food is considered pure.
This is consistent with Quranic allowance for food of the People of the Book, but Hanafis restrict it to Jews due to their known slaughter practices.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on accepting an invitation to eat from a non-Muslim. The author distinguishes between three types: Magian, Christian, and Jew. For a Magian or Christian, it is disliked (makruh) because their food is likely impure: Magians cook animals that died by strangulation or beating, and Christians do not have their own valid slaughter; they rely on Muslim slaughter or strangle animals. For a Jew, there is no harm because Jews only eat from their own slaughter or that of Muslims, which is considered valid. The commentator then expands on the scenario where the inviter claims to have bought meat from the market. The ruling is that his word is accepted: if he says he bought from a scriptuary (Jew or Christian), it is permissible; if from a Magian, it is forbidden. However, the permissibility might only mean it is not carrion, so it could still be disliked due to the pots having been used for impure meats. The commentator also notes a previous opinion that it is better to avoid the slaughter of People of the Book except when necessary. This passage highlights the Hanafi concern for purity in food and the distinctions among non-Muslim groups.

Key Terms
مجوسي (majūsī): A Magian (Zoroastrian), whose slaughter is invalid and whose food is impure.
نصراني (naṣrānī): A Christian; their slaughter is also invalid because they do not follow proper method.
يهودي (yahūdī): A Jew; their slaughter is valid as they follow Mosaic law.
ذبيحة (dhabīḥah): An animal slaughtered by the proper method; the text discusses the validity of non-Muslim slaughter.
كراهة (karāhah): Dislike; here it is likely makruh tahrimi due to impurity.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the food of Jews is permissible, but the food of Magians and Christians is disliked due to problematic slaughter and cooking practices.
