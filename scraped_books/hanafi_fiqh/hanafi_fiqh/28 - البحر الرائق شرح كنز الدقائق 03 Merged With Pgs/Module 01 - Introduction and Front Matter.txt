Understanding the Layers of Hanafi Legal Commentaries: Matn, Sharh, Hashya
Introducing the title, authors, and publication details of the work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three layers (matn, sharh, hashya) in a classical Hanafi fiqh text and identify the authors of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, and Minhat al-Khaliq.

Core Idea
The matn is the spine, the sharh is the muscle, the hashya is the fine-tuning — three layers of one legal tradition.

When you open a Hanafi fiqh book like al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, you are not reading a single work but a layered conversation spanning centuries. This lesson introduces the three textual strata present in this volume: the core matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq), its commentary (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq), and the supercommentary (Minhat al-Khaliq). Understanding this architecture is essential for navigating the text and appreciating the depth of Hanafi scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three textual layers
Look at the title page and editorial note: there is the matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq), the sharh (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq), and the hashya (Minhat al-Khaliq). Recognize that each layer is a distinct work by a different author.
2
Recognize the authors and their eras
Note the authors: al-Nasafi (d. 710/1310) wrote the matn, Ibn Nujaym (d. 970/1563) wrote the sharh, and Ibn 'Abidin (d. 1252/1836) wrote the hashya. Observe the chronological progression.
3
Understand the page layout
From the editor's note, visualize the page: matn at the top, sharh immediately below, and hashya at the bottom. This physical arrangement mirrors the intellectual hierarchy.
4
Appreciate the scholarly chain
Reflect that the hashya comments on the sharh, which comments on the matn. This chain represents the accumulation of Hanafi legal thought over 500 years.

Commentary
This passage is the title page and editorial note for the third volume of al-Bahr al-Ra’iq, a foundational commentary in the Hanafi school. It identifies the three layers of text: (1) the matn, Kanz al-Daqa’iq by Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi (d. 710/1310), a concise legal manual summarizing the relied-upon positions of the Hanafi school; (2) the sharh, al-Bahr al-Ra’iq by Ibn Nujaym al-Misri (d. 970/1563), a detailed commentary elucidating the matn; and (3) the hashya, Minhat al-Khaliq by Ibn Abidin (d. 1252/1836), a supercommentary refining and expanding the sharh. This work is central to Hanafi fiqh study, providing a comprehensive treatment of legal rulings with thorough scholarly apparatus.

Key Terms
متن (matn): The core text, here Kanz al-Daqa'iq, which is a concise compendium of Hanafi rulings.
شرح (sharh): Commentary, here al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, which expands and explains the matn with proofs and details.
حاشية (hashyah): Marginal notes or supercommentary, here Minhat al-Khaliq, which further clarifies and critiques the sharh.

Navigating a Classical Hanafi Fiqh Work via its Table of Contents
Opening heading of the table of contents for volume three

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the table of contents in a Hanafi fiqh text and explain its role in navigating the work.

Core Idea
The fihris (table of contents) is the roadmap to a classical Hanafi fiqh text.

This brief opening heading marks the beginning of the table of contents for the third volume of one of the most important Hanafi commentaries, Al-Bahr al-Raiq. While it contains no legal ruling, it is an essential tool for navigating the dense material that follows. Understanding how to use a fihris is a practical skill for any student of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the key terms
Identify the words 'فهرس محتويات' (fihris al-muhtawayat) meaning 'table of contents' and 'الجزء الثالث' (al-juz' al-thalith) meaning 'the third volume'.
These phrases orient you to the part of the book you are in.
2
Understand the structure
Realise that this heading separates the table of contents from the main body. The fihris will list chapters (abwab) and sometimes subchapters (fusul) with page numbers.
Classical indexes often follow the same order as the main text.
3
Apply to navigation
When researching a specific topic, first consult the fihris to locate the relevant chapter, then turn to the corresponding page in the main text.
This saves time and shows the scope of topics covered in the volume.
4
Relate to the broader work
Al-Bahr al-Raiq is a sharh (commentary) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The fihris helps you see how the commentator organised his exposition across multiple volumes.
Volume three likely covers a specific range of fiqh chapters (e.g., transactions, marriage, divorce).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major chapters and sections within Kitab al-Hajj, Kitab al-Nikah, and Kitab al-Talaq as listed in the table of contents of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Core Idea
The table of contents maps the fiqh journey from Hajj obligations to marital contracts to divorce rulings.

This passage is a table of contents from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq, a major Hanafi fiqh commentary. It lists the chapters (abwab) and sections (fusul) for three key books: Hajj, Nikah, and Talaq. Understanding this structure helps you locate specific rulings and see the logical flow of the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main books
Look for the distinct 'Kitab' headings: Kitab al-Hajj, Kitab al-Nikah, and Kitab al-Talaq. Note that Kitab al-Radaa also appears but is likely a smaller section.
The table of contents may repeat some entries due to formatting.
2
List the chapters under Kitab al-Hajj
From the index, extract all 'Bab' and 'Fasl' entries that appear before Kitab al-Nikah. This includes topics like killing game while in ihram, passing the miqat, and Hajj on behalf of others.
Some chapters may span multiple sections.
3

Observe that 'Kitab al-Radaa' appears twice, possibly due to the index being compiled from multiple volume sections. Also, 'Fasl fi al-Kafa'ah' appears twice, once as a fasl under Bab al-Awliya' and once separately.
Such repetitions may indicate overlapping topics or editorial quirks.
