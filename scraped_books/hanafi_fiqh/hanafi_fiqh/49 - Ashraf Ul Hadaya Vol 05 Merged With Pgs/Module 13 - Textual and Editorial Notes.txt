Refuting the Qiyas: Emancipation vs. Divorce Divisibility
Refuting analogies that emancipation is splittable like divorce and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects the analogy between emancipation and divorce regarding divisibility, and identify the intermediate state criterion that distinguishes them.

Core Idea
Without an intermediate state, halal/haram conflict forces wholesomeness; emancipation's kitabah allows division.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam al-Shafi'i analogized emancipation to divorce and pardon of qisas, arguing that just as those are indivisible, so is emancipation. The Hanafi response, presented in this passage, refutes the analogy by highlighting the existence of an intermediate state (kitabah) between slavery and freedom, which has no parallel in marriage/divorce or qisas/pardon. The usul principle that when halal and haram combine, precedence is given to haram, forces the entire act to be treated as one whole in those cases, whereas emancipation allows partial freedom because of the transitional state.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponents and their analogy
The Sahibayn and Imam al-Shafi'i compared emancipation (itq) to divorce and pardon of qisas, arguing that just as those cannot be divided, neither can emancipation. Notice the claim: they used qiyas to assert that freeing a share of a jointly-owned slave results in complete freedom, analogizing to divorce and qisas.
The opponents relied on surface similarity: all involve a person releasing a right or obligation partially.
2
Extract the key distinction: presence of intermediate state
The commentator points out that between slavery and freedom there exists the state of kitabah (manumission contract), where the slave is partly slave (in essence) and partly free (in earnings). No such intermediate state exists between marriage and divorce or between qisas and pardon. This difference breaks the analogy.
Kitabah is a unique Hanafi institution; other schools may not recognize it as a distinct intermediate state.
3
Apply the usul rule of halal/haram conflict
When halal and haram combine in one matter, the principle of ihtiyat (caution) gives precedence to haram. In divorce, if half a wife is divorced, the halal (permissible marriage) and haram (divorce) conflict, so the whole becomes haram. Similarly in qisas, pardoning half makes the entire qisas forbidden. Thus divorce and qisas are technically divisible but the rule forces them to be treated as whole. Emancipation, however, has no such conflict because the intermediate kitabah state resolves the tension, allowing the freed share to be free and the other to remain slave.
This rule is found in usul al-fiqh: 'idha ijtama'a al-halal wa al-haram yughallabu al-haram'.
4
Distinguish istilad (umm walad) from divorce/qisas
The opponents also analogized emancipation to making a slave an umm walad. The commentator responds that istilad is actually divisible: if a jointly-owned slave-girl gives birth and one partner claims the child, only that partner's share becomes umm walad (partial). Upon compensating the other partner, it becomes complete. This shows istilad is truly splittable, unlike divorce. So the analogy to istilad does not support the opponents' claim, because istilad is not analogous to divorce either.
Istilad is different from emancipation in that it can be partial initially and later become complete by compensation.

And it is not in divorce... etc. This is a response to the Sahibayn and Imam Shafi'i's analogy regarding divorce, pardon of qisas, and istilad. Thus he said: Comparing emancipation to divorce or pardon of qisas is not correct, because between marriage and divorce or between qisas and pardon there is no intermediate state. Unlike between freedom and slavery, where there is the state of kitabah (contract of manumission), which is slavery in essence but freedom in earnings and dealings. Since there is no such state between marriage and divorce, if one divorces half a woman, that half becomes forbidden due to divorce, and the other half remains permissible due to marriage. In usul al-fiqh, the rule is that if halal and haram are combined, precedence is given to haram over halal, making the entire woman forbidden to be cautious. Similarly with pardon: when half of qisas is pardoned, that half becomes forbidden due to the pardon, and the other half is permissible. So by giving precedence to haram, it is said that the entire qisas is pardoned, and now taking qisas is forbidden. As for comparing it to istilad, that is also not correct, because istilad (making a slave an umm walad) according to Imam Abu Hanifa is divisible, just as emancipation is divisible. Even if one makes his share of a mudabbar slave-girl an umm walad, it remains limited to that share, and similarly with a purely owned slave-girl. When he compensates his partner for corrupting ownership, he becomes full owner of the slave-girl after paying compensation, so the istilad becomes complete. Its explanation: If a slave-girl is jointly owned between Zaid and Khalid, and she gives birth, and Zaid claims the child is from his seed, then half of the slave-girl becomes his umm walad. But he has corrupted Khalid's share, because an umm walad is like a free person such that she cannot be sold. Therefore Zaid must compensate Khalid for his share. When he pays compensation and becomes full owner, the entire slave-girl becomes his umm walad, just as if one makes his own exclusive slave-girl an umm walad, she becomes fully umm walad. If the jointly-owned slave-girl is mudabbar (both said she is free after their death), then in this case, the one who claims the child retains his share as umm walad, while the remaining share remains mudabbar. Thus it is established that istilad can be split.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute where the commentator refutes the analogy of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam al-Shafi'i that emancipation can be split like divorce, pardon of qisas, or istilad (making a slave an umm walad). The commentator explains that emancipation has an intermediate state (kitabah) between slavery and freedom, whereas marriage and divorce, or qisas and pardon, do not. When halal and haram conflict, the rule (qa'idah) is to give precedence to haram for caution, so half divorce makes the whole woman haram, and half pardon invalidates the entire qisas. This shows that divorce and qisas are not divisible. Emancipation, however, is divisible because of the intermediate kitabah, but the Sahibayn and Shafi'i incorrectly analogized it to those. The passage also distinguishes istilad, which at the time of claim is partial (only the claimant's share becomes umm walad), and upon compensation it becomes complete. Thus istilad is splittable, unlike divorce. The author follows the order: first states the opponents' qiyas, then refutes each analogy with the intermediate state rule and the istilad example.

Key Terms
اعتناق (i'taq): Emancipation or freeing of a slave; the act of 'itq.
متجري (mutajazzi): Divisible; capable of being split into parts. Here said of 'itq and istilad.
کتابت (kitabah): A contract of manumission where a slave purchases his freedom by deferred payments; an intermediate state between slavery and freedom.
استیلاد (istilad): Making a female slave an umm walad (mother of the master's child), giving her partial freedom rights.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Sa'yah When Partners Testify Against Each Other
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sa'yah and wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on sa'yah when partners testify against each other and explain the Sahibayn's alternative based on wealth conditions.

Core Idea
A partner's claim that the other freed the slave creates sa'yah; wealth can block sa'yah only in Sahibayn's view.

Two partners in a slave each claim that the other freed the slave. What happens to the slave? Must he work to compensate both, or is he free from earning depending on the partners' wealth? This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on the obligation of sa'yah and the fate of wala'. Understanding this difference illuminates deeper principles about the effect of self-incrimination and the role of wealth in manumission compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the core legal question
Read the passage to identify the exact situation: two partners, a slave, each testifies that the other freed his share. What is the ruling on sa'yah (slave's earning to compensate)?
The slave's status becomes uncertain; each partner's claim creates a legal effect for himself.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Summarize Abu Hanifa's ruling: slave performs sa'yah for both partners regardless of wealth. Identify his reasoning: each partner's belief makes the slave a mukatab in his view, so enslavement is prohibited; compensation via daman is impossible due to denial, so sa'yah is the only option. Wealth does not prevent sa'yah.
Key principle: a person's own claim creates binding legal effects for himself.
3
Explain the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Summarize Sahibayn's ruling: sa'yah depends on wealth. If both wealthy, no sa'yah (each claims daman from the other). If both poor, sa'yah for both. If one wealthy one poor, sa'yah only for the wealthy partner. Their reasoning: wealth of the manumitter prevents sa'yah; the partner's claim of daman absolves the slave.
Contrast: for Sahibayn, the possibility of daman affects sa'yah; for Abu Hanifa, daman is not possible due to denial.
4
Analyze the dispute on wala' (clientage)
According to Abu Hanifa, wala' belongs to both partners. According to Sahibayn, wala' is suspended until they agree on who freed. Understand why: each partner attributes wala' to the other, and the other disclaims it.
Wala' determines inheritance rights from the freed slave.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Contrast a concrete scenario: both partners wealthy. Under Abu Hanifa, slave pays both shares; under Sahibayn, slave pays nothing. This shows the real-world impact of the ikhtilaf.
The difference affects the slave's financial burden and the partners' compensation.

And compensation is impossible due to the partner's denial, so the other (option) is determined, and that is sa'yah. And the wala' belongs to both of them because each of them says: 'My partner's share became free by his freeing, and his wala' is for him, and my share (became free) by sa'yah, and its wala' is for me.' If both are poor, he performs sa'yah for both, because each of them claims sa'yah upon him, whether truthful or false, as we explained, because the manumitter is poor. If one is wealthy and the other poor, he performs sa'yah for the wealthy one because he does not claim guarantee against his partner due to his poverty, rather he claims sa'yah upon him, and he is not absolved from it. And he does not perform sa'yah for the poor one because he claims guarantee against his partner due to his wealth, thus he becomes an absolver of the slave from sa'yah. And wala' is suspended in all that according to them, because each of them declares his partner (unworthy) and he absolves himself, so it remains suspended until they agree on the freeing of one of them.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on a complex scenario in the Book of 'Itaq (Emancipation). The issue: two partners in a slave each testify that the other partner freed the slave. The ruling is about what happens to the slave's status and the requirement of sa'yah (earning to compensate his value).

Imam Abu Hanifa's position: The slave must perform sa'yah for each partner to compensate their share, regardless of whether the partners are wealthy or poor. The reasoning is that each partner, by his own testimony, believes that the other freed his share, so in his own estimation the slave becomes a mukatab (a slave under contract for freedom). Consequently, he cannot enslave the slave; he must seek sa'yah. The right to sa'yah is certain whether the partner is truthful or false, because the slave is either his mukatab or his slave. The wealth of the manumitter does not prevent sa'yah according to Abu Hanifa, and since compensation (daman) from the partner is impossible due to denial, the only option is sa'yah. The wala' (clientage) goes to both partners because each claims the share of the other was freed by the other's action and his own share was freed by sa'yah.

Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) differ: If both partners are wealthy, no sa'yah is required. This is because each partner claims compensation (daman) from the partner, thus absolving the slave. If both are poor, the slave performs sa'yah for both, since each claims sa'yah upon him. If one is wealthy and the other poor, the slave performs sa'yah only for the wealthy partner (who cannot claim compensation due to the other's poverty) and not for the poor partner (who claims compensation from the wealthy partner, thereby absolving the slave). Regarding wala', it is suspended according to the Sahibayn because each partner attributes it to the other and the other disclaims it; it remains pending until they agree on one of them freeing the slave.

The passage also includes a definition of wala' (الولاء) as the inheritance obtained from a freed slave or through a contract of clientage. This clarifies the legal term used throughout the discussion.

This ruling illustrates the Hanafi principle that a person's own claim can create legal effects for himself, even if it is not true against others. The slave's obligation to work (sa'yah) is a mechanism to protect the partners' rights when the slave's freedom status is uncertain.

Key Terms
الولاء (al-wala'): The inheritance that comes from a freed slave or through a contract of clientage (mawalat).
السعاية (al-sa'yah): The slave's obligation to work and earn money to compensate the master's share when he is partially freed.
المكاتب (al-mukatab): A slave who has entered a contract (kitabah) with his master to earn his freedom by paying a sum.

Tadbir by a Co-Owner: Immediate Effect Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on immediate effect of tadbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether tadbir by a co-owner is immediate (munajjiz), and explain the practical difference in liability for compensation and ownership of wala'.

Core Idea
Tadbir by a co-owner is immediate only on his share per Abu Hanifa, but covers the whole slave per the Sahibayn — affecting compensation and wala'.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute about the nature of tadbir (making a slave free upon the master's death) when performed by a co-owner. The central question: is tadbir immediate (munajjiz) like emancipation, or conditional and therefore covering the entire slave? This difference leads to contrasting conclusions on who owes compensation to silent partners and how the clientage bond (wala') is distributed. By analyzing the proofs and practical consequences, you will appreciate how a seemingly technical classification significantly impacts real-world liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue: Is tadbir munajjiz?
Recognize that the dispute hinges on whether tadbir has immediate effect or is deferred. Imam Abu Hanifa holds it is immediate, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold it is not.
This is the foundational point from which all other differences flow.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, tadbir is like emancipation: immediate. Therefore, it only affects the share of the one who made tadbir. Other co-owners retain full rights over their shares, including the option to free, make tadbir, or seek compensation.
The proof is analogy (qiyas) to emancipation, as tadbir is a branch of it.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn hold that tadbir is not immediate; it is a conditional declaration of future freedom. As soon as a co-owner declares tadbir, the entire slave becomes mudabbar, making the declarant a guarantor (damin) for his partners' shares.
They reject the qiyas because tadbir is a deferral, not an immediate act.
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Apply the two positions to a scenario with three co-owners: A makes tadbir, B frees the slave, C is silent. Under Abu Hanifa, C takes compensation only from A (the mudabbar). Under the Sahibayn, A compensates both B and C, and A receives all wala'.
This shows how the classification directly determines financial liability and wala' rights.
5
Examine the proof: qiyas and its limits
See that Abu Hanifa uses qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) from emancipation to tadbir. The Sahibayn respond that the difference (deferment) prevents the analogy. This highlights how subtle differences in the ‘illa (effective cause) can lead to divergent rulings.
The dispute also echoes a similar disagreement on whether emancipation itself is immediate.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal nature of tadbir (making a slave free upon the master's death) when the slave is jointly owned. The text compares the views of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The core issue is whether tadbir is munajjiz (immediate/executory) like emancipation, or mu'allaq (conditional) similar to a will. Imam Abu Hanifa considers tadbir to be immediate and therefore only effective on the share of the one who makes it, leaving the other co-owners' shares unaffected. In contrast, the Sahibayn hold tadbir as non-immediate (not executory), meaning it applies to the entire slave from the moment of declaration, making the mudabbar a guarantor for his partners' shares. The text applies this principle to a scenario with three co-owners: one makes tadbir, then a second frees the slave, while a third remains silent. The detailed discussion explains the rights to compensation (tawan) for the silent partner and the liability of the mudabbar and the emancipator. Key concepts include daman mu'awadah (guarantee of exchange) vs daman jinayah (guarantee of crime), and the valuation of the slave's freedom (wala') distribution. The passage also introduces a qiyas (analogy) to a case of istilad (claim of umm walad) to justify the ruling. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) view is not explicitly stated, but the text presents both positions.

Claim of Umm Walad by One Co-Owner
Intra-Hanafi dispute on claim of umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the claim of umm walad by one co-owner and apply the principle of certainty (yaqin) and the effect of acknowledged lineage.

Core Idea
When umm walad is claimed by one co-owner, certainty gives half service; the Sahibayn allow sa'yah for freedom.

This lesson examines a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding a jointly owned slave-girl. One co-owner claims she is the umm walad of the other, but the alleged master denies. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on the proper ruling. Understanding their reasoning—rooted in principles of certainty and the legal effect of acknowledgment—reveals the practical difference in outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
Understand the basic facts: two co-owners of a slave-girl; one claims she is umm walad (mother of child) of the other co-owner; the latter denies. The legal question is: what happens to the slave-girl's service and status?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his argument from certainty
Abu Hanifa holds that the slave-girl serves the denying co-owner on alternate days (half the time). His reasoning uses certainty (yaqin): if the claim were true, all service goes to denier; if false, denier gets half. Since we cannot be certain, we take the certain half. Also, the claimant's acknowledgment of umm walad implies lineage, which is irrevocable and prevents treating him as having made istilad.
The key principle: what is certain remains; doubtful is not added.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their qiyas
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the denier may require the slave-girl to work (sa'yah) to earn half her value, and then she becomes free. They analogize to a case where a buyer acknowledges pre-sale emancipation (the acknowledgment is binding) and to a Christian's umm walad who converts to Islam (she works for freedom).
Their qiyas treats the claimant's acknowledgment as if he had made her umm walad, preventing his share from service and requiring her to earn freedom for the denier's share.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave-girl remains in joint ownership and serves the denier half the time. Under the Sahibayn, she can earn her freedom and becomes fully free after paying half her value by work.
The difference turns on whether the claimant's acknowledgment is treated as an admission against him (Sahibayn) or not (Abu Hanifa).
5
Evaluate the tarjih and operative position
The excerpt does not state which view is mufta bihi. Later Hanafi works typically follow Abu Hanifa's position unless specified. Students should be aware that this is an intra-school ikhtilaf with practical consequences.
In usul al-iftaa, the Zahir al-Riwayah usually relies on Abu Hanifa's view, but the Sahibayn's view may be given weight in specific circumstances.
