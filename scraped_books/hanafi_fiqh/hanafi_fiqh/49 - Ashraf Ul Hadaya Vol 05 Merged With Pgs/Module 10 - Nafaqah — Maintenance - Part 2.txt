Father's Obligation to Maintain Minor Child Despite Religious Difference
Ruling with proof on father's maintenance obligation despite religious difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father's obligation to maintain his minor child regardless of religious difference and identify the two primary proofs (Quranic dalalat al-nass and juz' analogy).

Core Idea
A father must support his minor child even if their religions differ; the child is a part of him.

When a minor child follows a different religion than the father—whether the child converted to Islam or apostatized—does the father's duty of maintenance change? The Hanafi school holds that religious difference has no effect. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind that ruling, drawing on Quranic implication and the concept that the child is a 'part' (juz') of the father, analogous to his own self.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its scope
Identify the exact ruling: the father must provide maintenance (nafaqah) for his minor child, even if the child follows a different religion. Note that this applies only when the child has no wealth of his own.
The father's obligation is unconditional regarding belief.
2
Recognize the first proof: Quranic dalalat al-nass
The author cites verse 2:233, which says 'And upon the father is their provision.' Although the verse explicitly speaks of nursing mothers, the Hanafi interpretation uses dalalat al-nass (implication) to extend the obligation to the child's maintenance, since the nursing benefits the child.
The verse is qat'i in transmission but zanni in indication for this specific ruling.
3
Recognize the second proof: child as juz' (part) of father
The child is considered a part (juz') of the father. Since a person must maintain himself, by analogy (in the meaning of himself) he must maintain that which is part of him, i.e., his child.
This is a form of qiyas jali, based on the nature of the child as an extension of the father.
4
Compare with the wife's maintenance analogy
The text also analogizes to the wife's maintenance: a husband must maintain his wife even if she differs in religion, because the cause is a valid marriage contract that establishes ihtibas (restraint). Similarly, the father's obligation is based on paternity, not religion.
This reinforces that the ruling is not based on shared faith but on the legal relationship.
5
Identify the condition: the child's lack of wealth
The obligation applies only if the minor has no personal wealth. If the child has property, maintenance comes from that, because the principle is that a person's maintenance is from his own wealth.
This is the default rule in nafaqah: the wealthy must support themselves.

And the maintenance of the minor is obligatory upon his father even if he differs from him in religion, just as the maintenance of the wife is obligatory upon the husband even if she differs from him in religion. As for the child, it is due to the absoluteness of what we recited: 'And upon the father is their provision' [Quran 2:233] and because the child is a part of him, so it is in the meaning of himself. As for the wife, it is because the cause is the valid marriage contract, for maintenance is in exchange for the restraint (ihtibas) established by it. And the marriage contract is valid between a Muslim man and a disbelieving woman, and restraint is entailed by it, so maintenance becomes obligatory. And in all that we have mentioned, maintenance is obligatory upon the father only if the minor has no wealth of his own. If he does, the principle is that a person's maintenance is from his own wealth, whether he is young or old. Explanation: He said that the maintenance of another is obligatory upon the father even if he differs from him in religion. For example, the minor has accepted Islam while the father is a disbeliever, or the father is Muslim and the child becomes apostate. Note that the Islam and apostasy of a discerning child are valid. Similarly, the wife's maintenance is obligatory upon the husband even if she differs in religion, e.g., she is Jewish or Christian. The proof for the obligation of the child's maintenance is the verse we recited earlier: 'And upon the father is their provision' – in the matter of wives, it is 'ibarat al-nass (explicit text), and in the matter of children, it is dalalat al-nass (implication of the text). And because the child is a part of his father, so it is in the ruling of his own self. And since the maintenance of one's own self is obligatory, so the maintenance of one's part, i.e., the child, is also obligatory. As for the wife's maintenance, its proof is that the cause is a valid marriage contract. Because by this marriage, the woman binds herself to the husband; in exchange, maintenance is obligatory. And it is evident that a valid marriage contract takes place between a Muslim man and a Kitabi disbelieving woman, as Allah says: 'And [lawful in marriage are] chaste women from those who were given the Scripture' [Quran 5:5]. So when their marriage is valid, then restraint (ihtibas) is entailed by it, and when restraint is entailed, maintenance becomes obligatory upon the husband. Then it is clear that in all the cases we have mentioned, the father's obligation for the child's maintenance applies only when the minor has no wealth of his own. And if the minor has wealth of his own, his maintenance is from his own wealth, for the principle is that a person's maintenance is from his own wealth, whether he is young or old. Benefits: The case of the minor having wealth is that he inherited from someone or received a gift. And if the minor has land, clothes, animals, etc., and they are needed for his maintenance, the father becomes a trustee to sell them and spend on his maintenance. (Mu'ini Sharh Hidayah)

Commentary
This passage establishes the ruling that a father is obligated to provide maintenance (nafaqah) for his minor child, even if the child follows a different religion than the father. The author provides two proofs for this: first, the Quranic verse 'Wa 'ala al-mawludi lahu rizquhunna' (2:233), which is interpreted through dalalat al-nass (implication) to include children, and second, because the child is considered a part (juz') of the father, so maintaining the child is analogous to maintaining oneself. The passage then compares this to the obligation of a husband to maintain his wife, which is based on the valid marriage contract and the wife's restraint (ihtibas). The author clarifies that this obligation applies only if the minor child has no personal wealth; if the child has wealth, his maintenance is from his own assets. The commentary adds examples: a child who accepts Islam while his father is a disbeliever, or a child who becomes an apostate, still has the right to maintenance from the father. The father's obligation is not affected by religious differences. The passage also notes that a discerning child's Islam or apostasy is legally valid.

Key Terms
جزء (juz'): A part or component; the child is considered a part of the father's self.
احتباس (ihtibas): The wife's restraint or confinement to the marriage, making herself available exclusively to the husband.
دلالۃ النص (dalalat al-nass): Implication of the text; a meaning inferred from the text rather than explicitly stated.

The Father's Obligation to Support His Minor Child
Ruling and proof for father's obligation to support minor child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a father's obligation to support his minor child, identify the Quranic and rational proofs, and apply the condition that the child must have no personal wealth.

Core Idea
A father must support his minor child even if their religions differ—the child is considered a part of himself.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah establishes a key principle in Hanafi family law: the father's duty to maintain his minor child. The ruling is unconditional regarding religion but conditional on the child's lack of personal wealth. We will examine the two proofs provided: the Quranic verse and the legal analogy of the child being a part of the father.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
State that the father's obligation to support his minor child is wajib (obligatory). Note that it applies even if father and child differ in religion.
2
Understand the condition for obligation
The obligation only applies when the minor child has no personal wealth. If the child has wealth, maintenance comes from the child's own property.
3
Analyze the first proof: Quranic verse
The verse (2:233) 'wa 'ala al-mawludi lahu rizquhunna' explicitly orders provision for nursing mothers, but by implication (dalalat al-nass) obligates the father to support the child, as the child's sustenance is part of the mother's maintenance.
4
Analyze the second proof: Rational analogy
The child is considered a part (juz') of the father. Since maintaining oneself is obligatory, maintaining one's part (the child) is also obligatory.
5
Note the comparison with spousal nafaqah
The obligation for a wife's maintenance is based on the marriage contract and the wife's restriction (ihtibas) for the husband. This analogy is used to show that religious difference does not affect the obligation.
The wife's obligation is also unconditional on religion.

Core Idea
Parents' need is met by the child alone; sons and daughters share equally in Zahir al-Riwayah.

This passage establishes a key principle of Hanafi nafaqah law: only the child is obligated to support his or her parents. No other relative shares this duty. Parents' maintenance is wajib exclusively on the child; in Zahir al-Riwayah, sons and daughters share equally.

This lesson explores a foundational rule in Hanafi fiqh: that the maintenance of needy parents falls solely on their children. Given a needy father with a wealthy son and daughter, each child must pay an equal share of the father's maintenance under the relied-upon position.
For example, if total maintenance is $600, each pays $300 regardless of income.

Explanation: The issue is that if the parents are needy and their child is wealthy, then their maintenance is specifically obligatory upon him. No one else can share with him in giving maintenance. Because parents can make a concession (ta'wil) in the wealth of their own child due to the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' (Anta wa maluka li-abik), and they cannot make a concession in the wealth of others. Thus, both (parents) become wealthy by virtue of their child's wealth, and the maintenance of a wealthy person is not obligatory upon others. Hence, no one other than the child will share in their maintenance. The second proof is that maintenance is a gift that becomes obligatory due to relationship; therefore, whoever is closer to the parents is more entitled to give them maintenance. And since the child is closer to the parents than other people, the parents' maintenance is only obligatory upon him. And in parents' maintenance, son and daughter are equal. This is the ruling of Zahir al-Riwayah, and it is correct. So if a father is needy and he has a daughter and a son who are wealthy, then it equally includes both of them. In contrast, if a needy person has a brother and a sister who are wealthy, then their maintenance upon them is obligatory in thirds: two-thirds on the brother and one-third on the sister, just as in inheritance the brother gets two-thirds and the sister one-third. Because in this case, the cause of maintenance being obligatory is inheritance. Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi said that the father's maintenance is also obligatory upon the son and daughter in thirds: two-thirds on the son and one-third on the daughter. Imam Sarakhsi analogized maintenance to inheritance, meaning in both places the rule 'for the male the share of two females' applies.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an intra-Hanafi dispute. It begins by establishing that the maintenance (nafaqah) of needy parents is exclusively obligatory upon their own child, not shared with other relatives. The reason is a special concession (ta'wil) based on the hadith 'Anta wa maluka li-abik' (You and your wealth belong to your father), which gives parents a right to their child's wealth. This concession does not extend to the wealth of others. Also, the child is the closest relative, so he is most entitled to spend. The ruling in Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad) states that this maintenance is equally obligatory on sons and daughters—that is, each child bears an equal share. The commentary confirms this as the correct position. However, Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi analogizes nafaqah to inheritance, arguing that since the cause of obligation for siblings is inheritance (as per the verse 'wa ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' mentioned later), and for siblings the share follows the inheritance ratio (2:1 male:female), the same should apply to parents—sons bear two-thirds and daughters one-third. This is a notable intra-madhab ikhtilaf. The author of Ashraf al-Hidayah sides with Zahir al-Riwayah (equal shares) as the sounder view.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance, including food, clothing, and shelter, obligatory on certain relatives for those in need.
تاویل (ta'wil): A concession or interpretation that gives parents a right in their child's wealth based on the hadith 'Anta wa maluka li-abik'.
ظاہر الروایۃ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that form the authoritative basis of the Hanafi school.

Maintenance for Dhu Rahim Mahram: Conditions and Proof
Defines conditions for nafaqah on dhu rahim mahram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which nafaqah is wajib for a dhu rahim mahram, identify the Quranic proof, and apply the ruling to determine whether a specific relative is entitled to maintenance.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib on a dhu rahim mahram only if needy and unable to earn due to minority, womanhood, chronic illness, or blindness.

Maintenance (nafaqah) for relatives is not a blanket obligation; the Hanafi school sets precise conditions. In this lesson, we explore when a dhu rahim mahram (a marriage-prohibited blood relative) is entitled to nafaqah from you. The ruling hinges on both need and the inability to earn, with specific indicators that clarify the obligation. We will also examine the unique case of parents, where the obligation applies even if they can earn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being ruled upon? It is the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) from one relative to another. The ruling is that it is wajib under certain conditions. Note the hukm level: wajib, not fard.
In Hanafi fiqh, nafaqah on relatives is classified as wajib, meaning it is established by evidence that is zanni in transmission or meaning.
2
List the conditions for nafaqah
The text specifies that the recipient must be a dhu rahim mahram and must be needy. The need must be established by one of four indicators: minority (saghir), being an adult woman, chronic illness (zamana), or blindness. These indicate incapacity to earn.
For an adult male, need alone is not enough; he must also be chronically ill or blind. A healthy adult male who can work is not entitled, even if poor.
3
Locate the dalil
The proof is from the Quran: 'wa ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:233). The passage also cites a reading (qira'ah) of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud: 'wa ala al-warithi dhi al-rahmi al-mahrami mithlu dhalik', which explicitly mentions 'dhu rahim mahram'.
The standard verse is qat'i al-thubut, but its dalala for this ruling is zanni. The reading of Ibn Mas'ud is shadhdhah (anomalous) but used as a supportive proof.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse prove this ruling? The verse obligates something on the heir, similar to the mother's maintenance. The reading specifies that the heir must be a dhu rahim mahram. Combined with the principle that kindness (silah al-rahim) is obligatory in close kinship, the obligation of nafaqah on dhu rahim mahram relatives is established.
The wajh al-dalalah relies on the addition 'dhi al-rahmi al-mahrami' to limit the general term 'al-warithi'.
5
Note the exception for parents
The passage contrasts the general rule with parents: even if parents are able to earn, their nafaqah on children is wajib. This is because children are commanded to remove hardship from parents, and earning causes hardship for them.
This exception is based on the hadith and general duty to honor parents, overriding the usual condition of incapacity.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning maintenance (nafaqah) for a category of relatives called dhu rahim mahram (a relative with whom marriage is permanently prohibited). The author defines the conditions under which such a relative is entitled to nafaqah from another relative: the recipient must be needy and must belong to one of four categories indicating incapacity to earn: (1) a minor child (male or female), (2) an adult woman (since earning is not typically expected of her), (3) an adult male who is chronically ill (zamana), or (4) an adult male who is blind. These conditions serve as indicators of need ('umarat al-haja) because such a person cannot earn a living. The proof is from the Quranic verse 'wa ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (And upon the heir is like that) and a variant reading from Abdullah ibn Mas'ud that specifies 'dhu rahim mahram'. The principle is that kindness (silat al-rahim) is obligatory in close kinship, and dhu rahim mahram defines that closeness. The passage also contrasts this with parents: even if parents are able to earn, their nafaqah is still obligatory upon children because earning causes them hardship, and children are commanded to remove harm from them. This is based on the hadith and general command to honor parents.

Key Terms
ذی رحم محرم (dhu rahim mahram): A relative with whom marriage is permanently prohibited due to blood relationship (e.g., mother, father, sister, brother, grandfather, grandmother, etc.) – but not including those who are mahram by marriage or fosterage only.
لنجا (zamana): Chronic illness or disability that prevents earning a livelihood.
صغیر (saghir): A minor child who has not reached puberty, unable to earn independently.
وارث (warith): Inheritor/heir. The verse 'wa ala al-warithi' indicates that the obligation of nafaqah applies to a person who is an heir of the recipient relative.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two foundational principles of nafaqah liability—proportionality to inheritance share and enforceability by court—and explain their proofs from the Quran and the maxim al-ghurm bi al-ghunm.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib on heirs proportional to inheritance and can be enforced by the court.

This lesson explores a key ruling from the Hanafi school: nafaqah (maintenance) owed to a needy relative who is also an inheritor is not a fixed amount but is directly tied to the share of inheritance the payer receives. Furthermore, the court can compel payment. We will examine the Quranic proof and the legal maxim that justify this proportional obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the two aspects: nafaqah is wajib according to the amount of inheritance, and the heir can be compelled to pay it.
The ruling is from Al-Quduri's matn.
2
Locate the Primary Dalil
The Quranic verse 'wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:233) is cited. Identify that it mentions 'al-warith' (the heir).
The verse is qat'i in thubut (transmission) but zanni in dalalah (interpretation).
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Explain how the verse implies proportionality: specifying 'the heir' indicates that the obligation is linked to the inheritance, and 'mithlu dhalik' suggests the amount is similar to the mother's maintenance but scaled according to the inheritance share.
This is an istinbat (derivation) by the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the Secondary Dalil (al-ghurm bi al-ghunm)
The maxim 'liability is according to benefit' provides a rational proof: one who benefits from inheritance must bear the corresponding burden of supporting the decedent's dependents.
This maxim is used in many areas of Hanafi fiqh.
5
Note the Enforcement Mechanism
The text states the heir can be compelled (jabr) by the judge to fulfill this wajib obligation. Understand that this is a means to ensure the right is not neglected.
Compulsion is only for an established wajib right.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the matn of Al-Quduri (the base text commented upon). It establishes two principles for nafaqah among relatives who are inheritors: (1) The obligation of nafaqah is proportional to the amount of inheritance each heir receives from the person in need. For example, if a father dies and leaves two sons, each son inherits half; if the mother is needy, each son must pay half of her nafaqah. (2) The heir may be compelled by the judge to pay this nafaqah. The proof is twofold: (a) The Quranic verse 'wa ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:233) explicitly mentions 'the heir', indicating that the obligation is on those who inherit, and the amount is tied to the inheritance share. (b) The legal maxim 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' (liability is according to benefit): one who enjoys the benefit of inheritance must bear the corresponding liability of supporting the decedent's dependents. Compulsion is necessary to enforce the payment of an established right.

Nafaqah for Needy Dhi Rahim Mahram: Proportional to Inheritance
Defines dhi rahim mahram and rules for nafaqah obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for nafaqah being wajib on an inheritor for a needy dhi rahim mahram and identify the proof from Quran and qira'ah.

Core Idea
Nafaqah for needy relatives is wajib on inheritors proportional to their inheritance share — a burden matched with benefit.

This passage from Al-Hidayah explains the Hanafi ruling on nafaqah for a specific category of relatives: those who are both blood relatives and permanently prohibited in marriage (dhi rahim mahram). The ruling establishes that such relatives, if needy, are entitled to nafaqah from the inheritor, and that the amount is determined by the inheritor's share in the inheritance. We will examine the conditions of need, the proof from the Quran and the qira'ah of Ibn Mas'ud, and the legal maxim that supports the proportional obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category and need condition
Determine who qualifies as dhi rahim mahram (blood relative permanently forbidden in marriage). Then check if the relative is needy: minor, adult woman, or adult male blind/paralyzed (unable to earn).
Being able to earn removes need, except for parents (see exception).
2
State the ruling: nafaqah is wajib on the inheritor
If the conditions are met, the inheritor must provide nafaqah. The obligation is wajib, not just recommended.
The ruling is established in the zahir al-riwayah.
3
Understand the primary proof
The Quranic verse (2:233) says 'wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' and the qira'ah of Ibn Mas'ud adds 'dhi al-rahim al-mahram'. This indicates the inheritor's obligation towards such relatives.
The qira'ah is zanni al-thubut but supports the Hanafi position.
4
Apply the amount principle
The nafaqah is wajib according to the measure of inheritance (e.g., if one inherits 1/2, the nafaqah is in that proportion). The maxim 'al-ghurm bil-ghunm' (gain accompanies liability) is cited as proof.
This ensures justice: the burden matches the benefit.
5
Note the exception for parents
Even if parents can earn, nafaqah is still wajib on children due to the command to remove hardship from them.
This is based on the general duty to honor parents.

Nafaqah of Adult Children: The Father's Sole Obligation vs. Proportional Sharing
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nafaqah proportion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Zahir al-Riwayah position on nafaqah of adult children and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute with its evidence.

Core Idea
For adult children, the relied-upon Hanafi view is that all nafaqah is on the father alone.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: when an adult daughter or disabled adult son requires maintenance, does the father bear the entire burden, or do both parents share proportionally? The matn of Quduri suggests a division based on inheritance shares, but the commentator clarifies that the stronger transmitted position (Zahir al-Riwayah) places all nafaqah on the father. We will examine the proofs and the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Hanafi positions
There are two narrations within the Hanafi school: (1) the Zahir al-Riwayah (the core transmitted texts) holds that all nafaqah is on the father; (2) the narration of Khassaf and Hasan holds that it is divided into thirds: two-thirds on father, one-third on mother.
Note that the second narration is also attributed to Imam Shafi'i.
2
Analyze the proof for Zahir al-Riwayah
The primary proof is Quran 2:233: 'wa 'ala al-mawlud lahu rizquhunna wa kiswatuhunna.' The lam in 'al-mawlud lahu' indicates specification (ikhtisas) of the father, making him solely responsible. Additionally, the adult child is analogized (qiyas) to a minor child, whose nafaqah is entirely on the father.
The qiyas relies on the common element of needing maintenance, but the other position distinguishes based on guardianship.
3
Analyze the proof for the proportional view
The opposing view (Khassaf/Hasan/Shafi'i) reasons that nafaqah should be proportional to inheritance shares. Since parents inherit from the child in a ratio of 2:1 (father:mother), the nafaqah is likewise divided.
This principle is applied for non-parent relatives as well, as shown later in the passage.
4
Evaluate the distinction regarding guardianship
The Zahir al-Riwayah responds by distinguishing a minor from an adult: for a minor, the father has both wilayah (guardianship) and mu'nah (financial responsibility), including paying fitrah, so he alone is responsible. For an adult, wilayah is absent, but the Quranic specification still applies, so the father remains solely responsible.
The other view considers the absence of wilayah as a reason for the mother to share.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text indicates that the Zahir al-Riwayah position is the stronger and relied-upon view. This is because it is from the core transmitted books (Kutub al-Zahir al-Riwayah) and the Quranic proof is direct and clear.
Thus, in fatwa, the father bears all nafaqah for adult children.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah is a ruling with proof regarding the obligation of nafaqah for close relatives (dhawi arham mahram). It begins by establishing the general principle: nafaqah for such relatives is wajib only upon a person who is wealthy (ghani), not upon a needy person. The reasoning is that nafaqah is a form of sila rahmi (maintaining family ties); a needy person is himself deserving, so he cannot be obligated to support others. This is contrasted with the nafaqah of a wife and minor children, which are obligatory even on a poor husband/father because the obligation arises from explicit commitment (marriage) or dependency (minor child standing in place of wife). The proof includes the hadith 'خذي من مال أبي سفيان...' which indicates the husband's obligation. The passage then addresses the intra-Hanafi dispute on the measure of 'wealth' (ghina) that triggers nafaqah for relatives. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that one is considered wealthy if one owns nisab (the minimum threshold for zakat, i.e., 200 dirhams of surplus wealth). Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that wealth is measured by having surplus income beyond one's household expenses for a month (or daily surplus from regular earnings). The text states that the fatwa is on Imam Abu Yusuf’s opinion (nisab standard), but it clarifies that the nisab referred to is the same nisab that makes zakat prohibited (i.e., wealth exceeding basic needs). Thus, if a person has 200 dirhams worth of surplus assets, he must provide nafaqah to his needy close relatives.

نصاب (nisab): The minimum amount of surplus wealth (200 dirhams or equivalent) that triggers zakat obligation; here used as the measure of wealth for nafaqah.

Father's Right to Sell an Absent Son's Movable Property for His Own Maintenance
Ruling on father selling absent son's movable property for his nafaqah: Abu Hanifa's istihsan vs Sahibayn's qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father selling his absent adult son's movable property for his own nafaqah and explain the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani).

Core Idea
Father may sell absent son's movable goods for his own nafaqah by istihsan, but not immovable.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the father's authority to sell his absent adult son's property for his own maintenance. The central question is whether the father retains any guardianship over his son's property after the son reaches majority. Imam Abu Hanifa, using istihsan (juristic preference), permits the sale of movable property based on the father's wilayat al-hifz (guardianship of preservation), while the Sahibayn apply strict qiyas and prohibit all sales. Understanding this dispute illuminates how Hanafi jurists balance textual principles with equitable considerations.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and two positions
Identify the exact legal question: Is it permissible for a father to sell his absent adult son's movable property to cover his own nafaqah? State Abu Hanifa's position (jaiz for movable only) and the Sahibayn's position (ghair jaiz for all).
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, so both views are within the school.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's qiyas reasoning
Trace the Sahibayn's argument: guardianship (wilaya) ends at majority; therefore the father has no authority to sell any property. They apply qiyas to the case where the son is present — the father cannot sell then, so similarly when absent.
They also note that the father cannot sell for any debt except nafaqah, but they still deem the sale invalid.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's istihsan reasoning
Imam Abu Hanifa introduces the concept of wilayat al-hifz (guardianship of preservation), analogous to an executor (wasi) who can sell an absent heir's perishable property. The father's compassion (shafqat) is greater, so he is more entitled.
This istihsan overrides the strict qiyas that would forbid the sale.
4
Note the distinction between movable and immovable
Abu Hanifa limits the permission to movable property (manqul) because selling movables is an act of preservation—they can spoil or be lost. Immovable property (aqar) is self-preserving and does not require sale.
This distinction is key to limiting the scope of the exceptional ruling.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a father can sell his absent son's movable goods for his own maintenance; under Sahibayn, he cannot and must seek a remedy through the qadi. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
The text presents Abu Hanifa's view more fully, indicating its preponderance.

Core Idea

Core Idea
Relative's maintenance is need-based and lapses when need is deemed fulfilled; wife's maintenance is a debt that persists.

In this passage, al-Quduri addresses a practical scenario: a judge has decreed maintenance for a relative (child, parent, or kin) and for a wife. Time passes without payment. Identify the two types of maintenance decrees

Chapter of Maintenance. They can work and earn for themselves. Because in this there is consideration for both sides: consideration for the slave is that when he works he will live, and consideration for the master is that his ownership remains, and if he wishes he can sell. And if both are not capable of earning, e.g., a lame slave, or a slave girl whom people do not hire, then in that case the master will be compelled to sell them. Because both are entitled to maintenance and possess the capacity of entitlement, and by selling them both their rights are fulfilled and the master's right is substituted, i.e., the price of the slave will be obtained. In contrast, if a husband becomes unable to give maintenance to his wife, he will not be compelled to divorce. Because the wife's maintenance becomes a debt upon the husband. Therefore it will be deferred. As we explained in the previous issue: unlike the maintenance of the wife when the judge decrees it, because it is obligatory while she is wealthy and it does not lapse. And the maintenance of a slave does not become a debt on the master. For this reason, canceling the slave's maintenance would be necessary, and canceling someone's right is not permissible. Therefore the master will be compelled to sell. The conclusion is that if the master becomes unable to give maintenance to his slave, the master will be compelled to sell him; and if the husband becomes unable to give maintenance to his wife, he will not be compelled to divorce. The reason for the difference between the two is that if the master is compelled to sell his slave, the master's ownership is removed but with a substitute, i.e., he obtains the price. And if not compelled, the slave's right would be lost without any substitute because the slave's maintenance is not a debt on the master. And if the husband is compelled to divorce his wife, the husband's ownership would be lost without any substitute, and in not divorcing, the woman's right is temporarily lost but a substitute remains, because when the judge decrees, the woman's maintenance becomes a debt on the husband. So her right is deferred but not void. And if an owner does not give fodder to his animals, he will not be compelled to give fodder or sell them. Because compelling the owner is a type of judicial decree, and for a judicial decree the presence of a person in whose favor the decree is given is necessary, and that person must have the capacity for entitlement. Since animals do not have the capacity for entitlement, they cannot be the subject of a judicial decree, and when they cannot be the subject, the judge cannot give any verdict for them. Therefore we said that the owner of animals is not compelled to give fodder or sell. But he will be advised religiously between him and Allah to give fodder or sell, and if he does not do so, he will be sinful. There is a hadith that a woman entered Hell because of a cat which she imprisoned until it died; she neither released it to eat insects of the earth nor gave it food. And the author mentioned that the Prophet (peace be upon him) prohibited causing pain to animals, and indeed in not giving fodder there is causing pain. Similarly, the Prophet (peace be upon him) prohibited wasting wealth. So from the hadith of Mughira: The Messenger of Allah said: "Verily Allah has prohibited for you wasting wealth." And it is obvious that in not giving fodder to animals, there is destruction of one's wealth. And from Imam Abu Yusuf there is a narration that the owner should be compelled to give fodder to animals, and this is the saying of Imam Shafi'i, Imam Malik, and Imam Ahmad. And the correct is what we stated first. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) from 'Ashraf al-Hidayah' on the chapter of nafaqah from al-Hidayah. It discusses three cases: a master unable to maintain his slave, a husband unable to maintain his wife, and an owner unable to feed his animals. The author first explains the ruling for a slave who can earn: the master is not compelled to sell because the slave can provide for himself, thus preserving both the slave's life and the master's ownership. If the slave cannot earn (e.g., lame or crippled), the master is compelled to sell the slave because the slave is entitled to maintenance and has the capacity to deserve it. Selling fulfills the slave's right (the price goes to the master, who uses it for maintenance) and the master gains the price, so neither right is lost without substitute. In contrast, if a husband cannot provide for his wife, he is not compelled to divorce because the wife's maintenance becomes a debt on the husband when a judge decrees it. Thus, her right is not lost but deferred. The key difference is that the slave's maintenance does not become a debt on the master, so if the master is not compelled to sell, the slave's right would be lost completely. For the husband, the wife's right is preserved as a debt. For animals, the ruling is that the owner is not compelled to feed or sell them because animals lack the legal capacity to deserve (istihqaq). A judge can only issue a decree in favor of someone who can be the subject of a decree (munqadi lahu), i.e., a person with legal personality. Since animals are not legal persons, the judge cannot compel the owner. However, the owner is religiously obligated to feed them and will be sinful if he does not. The author supports this with evidence: the hadith of the woman who imprisoned a cat and entered Hell, and the hadith prohibiting causing pain to animals and wasting wealth. Imam Abu Yusuf transmitted a view that the owner is compelled to feed animals, and this is also the position of Imam Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad. But the author declares the correct view (sahih) to be the earlier one (that the owner is not compelled), which is the Hanafi position according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the relied-upon position).

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance or sustenance including food, clothing, and shelter that one is obligated to provide for dependents or property.
استحقاق (istihqaq): Legal entitlement or capacity to deserve something; used here to refer to the ability to be the beneficiary of a judicial decree.
قضاء (qada): A judicial decree or order issued by a judge; involves compulsion.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, judicial compulsion requires the beneficiary to have legal capacity; thus, animals cannot be subjects of a decree, while wives and slaves can.
