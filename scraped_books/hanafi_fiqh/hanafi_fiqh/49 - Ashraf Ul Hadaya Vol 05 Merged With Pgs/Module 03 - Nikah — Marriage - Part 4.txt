Child of Slave Woman from Husband is Owned by Her Master
Ruling with proof for child of slave woman from husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the ownership of the child of a slave woman married to a man, explain the application of tarjīḥ (preponderance) as the method of resolving the conflict between the mother's and father's claims, and identify the exception for the deceived (maghrūr) husband.

Core Idea
The child of a slave woman belongs to her master, not the husband, because the mother's side preponderates and the husband consented.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a slave woman gives birth to a child from her husband, a conflict arises: the child's lineage and ownership could trace to the mother (making it the master's property) or to the father (making it the husband's). The school resolves this by giving preponderance (tarjīḥ) to the mother's side, based on custody rights and the physical fact that the husband's sperm is consumed. An exception is made for a deceived husband (maghrūr) who married the woman thinking she was free — in that case, the child belongs to the father, preserving justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Conflict
The text states: 'And her child from her husband is owned by her master.' This is the fundamental ruling. The conflict (tanāfu‘) is that the child can be traced to either parent; ownership cannot simultaneously belong to the master (through the mother) and the husband (through the father).
The conflict is inherent: the master claims via the mother, the husband via his seed.
2
Understand the Method of Preponderance (Tarjīḥ)
The text gives two reasons for preferring the mother's side: (1) custody (ḥaḍāna) is with the mother, and (2) the husband's water (sperm) is consumed by the wife's water. These make the mother's connection stronger. This is a rational preference, not based on a revealed text – hence it is istiḥsān (juristic preference).
Tarjīḥ is a standard method for resolving conflicting evidence when both sides have some basis.
3
Note the Role of Consent
The text adds: 'and the husband has consented to it.' The husband, by freely marrying a slave woman, implicitly accepts that any offspring will belong to her master. This consent reinforces the preponderance of the mother's side.
Consent is a key factor; its absence changes the ruling.
4
Recognize the Exception (Maghrūr)
The text contrasts: 'contrary to the child of the deceived man, because the parent did not consent.' If a free man marries a woman believing she is free but she is actually a slave, he is maghrūr (deceived). His child is not owned by the master; instead, the father must pay the child's value. The ruling changes because consent is absent.
This exception shows that the underlying rationale is not just lineage, but also fairness and lack of deception.
5
Evaluate the Proof (Istidlāl)
The proof is istiḥsān: a rational preference derived from the stronger claim of the mother's side. It is not based on a explicit text, but on a combination of custody, physical consumption, and consent. The Hanafi school relies on such reasoned preference when apparent analogy (qiyās) would lead to hardship or injustice.
Istiḥsān is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school, though disputed by others.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement with proof. The ruling: If a slave woman (bāndī) gives birth to a child from her husband (who is also a slave or free, but it doesn't matter), the child belongs to the master of the slave woman, not to the husband. The proof is that there is a conflict (tanāfu‘): the child comes from both parents’ sperm, so if we follow the mother, the child belongs to her master; if we follow the father, it belongs to the husband. Since both cannot be true, we give preponderance (tarjīḥ) to the mother's side. The text gives two reasons: (1) custody (ḥaḍāna) which belongs to the mother, and (2) the husband's sperm is consumed by the wife's fluid (istiḥlāk). Also, the husband consented to this outcome by marrying a slave woman, knowing the consequences. An exception is the case of a deceived man (maghrūr) who married a woman thinking she was free but she was a slave; his child is not owned by the master because he did not consent.

Key Terms
مملوك (mamlūk): Owned as property, subject to the master's ownership rights.
ترجح (tarajjaḥa): To be preponderant; giving the mother's side more weight over the father's.
مغرور (maghrūr): A person deceived into thinking a slave woman is free when marrying her.

Child of a Free Woman is Free in All Cases
Ruling with proof that child of free woman is free

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the status of a child born to a free woman and explain the qiyas proof that the child follows the mother's freedom.

Core Idea
A child's freedom is determined by the mother, not the father.

This short but powerful passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a fundamental rule in Hanafi family law: the child of a free woman is free regardless of the father's status. The author provides a clear qiyas (analogy) based on the established principle that the child follows the mother in matters of enslavement. We will unpack this ruling and its proof to understand why the mother's side is considered preponderant (rājiḥ).

Guided Path
1
State the Ruling
Read the first part of the passage: 'And the child of a free woman is free in every case.' This is the hukm – a clear, unconditional ruling.
The ruling applies whether the father is free, a slave, or of any status.
2
Identify the Proof (Dalil)
The proof is qiyas (analogy). The passage says 'because her side is preponderant, so he follows her in the quality of freedom, just as he follows her in being owned.' The basis is the mother's preponderance (rujḥān).
This analogy is built on an already established rule: the child follows the mother in slavery.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah (Legal Link)
The point of comparison (ʿilla) is the mother's preponderant side. Since the child follows the mother in being a slave (mamlūka), by parity of reasoning the child follows her in freedom when she is free.
The principle of 'following the mother' is consistent across all categories.
4
List the Exemplified Categories
The passage lists five categories where the child follows the mother: being owned (mamlūkiyya), being enslaved (raqīqa), being a mudabbara, being an umm walad, and being a mukātaba. This reinforces that the rule is general.
Each category is a specific case of the mother's status determining the child's status.
5
Conclude the Principle
The ruling is absolute: a free mother always gives birth to a free child, regardless of the father's condition. The proof is strong because it is based on a consistent analogical reasoning accepted by all major schools.
This principle is 'muttafaq ʿalayh' (agreed upon) among the four schools, though the reasoning may differ slightly.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof. The ruling: A child born to a free woman is free regardless of whether the father is free or a slave. The proof is that the mother's side is preponderant (rājiḥ) as established previously. Therefore, just as the child follows the mother in being owned (mamlūka) when the mother is a slave (i.e., the child is also owned), similarly the child follows the mother in freedom when the mother is free. The text lists several categories where the child follows the mother's status: being owned (mamlūkiyya), being enslaved (raqīqa), being a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death), being an umm walad (a slave mother of the master's child), and being a mukātaba (a slave under a contract of manumission). This shows the general principle that the child's status in terms of freedom or enslavement is determined by the mother.

Key Terms
حرة (ḥurra): Free woman, not a slave.
راجح (rājiḥ): Preponderant; the mother's side is given greater weight.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A female slave who has been promised freedom upon her master's death.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has given birth to her master's child and becomes free upon his death.
مكاتبة (mukātaba): A female slave under a contract of manumission (kitāba).

Partial Validity of Tadbir Among Co-Owners (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tadbir divisibility and subsequent remedies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on tadbir divisibility, identify the options available to co-owners under his view, and explain the chain of guarantees that results.

Core Idea
Tadbir is divisible like emancipation, giving each co-owner options over his share.

This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about whether tadbir (manumission by will) can be partial when a slave is co-owned. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that tadbir is divisible, analogizing it to emancipation. The Sahibayn disagree, arguing tadbir is a single indivisible disposition. The text follows Abu Hanifa's view and then details the legal options for co-owners and the resulting guarantees and distribution of wala'. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply qiyas and navigate conflicting rulings within a single madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Locate the statement that tadbir is divisible according to Abu Hanifa, contrary to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Note that this is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The disagreement is on tajazzi (divisibility).
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
The passage states tadbir is like emancipation (itq) because it is a branch (shu'ba) of itq. This is a qiyas jaliy. Understand that emancipation is divisible, so by analogy, tadbir is divisible.
The illa is being a branch of itq.
3
Map the options for co-owners under Abu Hanifa's view
When one co-owner makes tadbir, the other(s) retain ownership of their share. They have several options: make tadbir, emancipate, make kitabah, take guarantee from the mudabbir, employ for compensation, or leave. If one chooses emancipation, the other options fall.
The silent partner has two causes for guarantee: tadbir and emancipation.
4
Trace the guarantee and wala' distribution
The silent partner can take daman mu'awada from the mudabbir. The mudabbir can then claim one-third of the value from the emancipator (since the mudabbir had two-thirds). Wala' is divided: two-thirds to mudabbir, one-third to emancipator. Under Sahibayn's view, the entire slave becomes mudabbar, and the mudabbir must compensate both partners fully; wala' goes entirely to the mudabbir.
Contrast daman mu'awada with daman jinaya.

Since in his view it is divisible, it is limited to his share. And he has spoiled the shares of the others by making tadbir, so each of the two [other co-owners] has the option to make tadbir of his share, or to emancipate, or to make a kitabah contract, or to take a guarantee from the mudabbir, or to employ the slave for compensation, or to leave him as he is, because his share remains in his ownership, though defective due to the partner's corruption, since [the mudabbir] blocked all ways of benefiting from him by sale or gift as mentioned. However, he has the right to take a guarantee from the mudabbir so that the guarantee becomes a guarantee of compensation, for that is the principle, even such that usurpation is made a guarantee of compensation according to our principle. And this is possible regarding tadbir because it is capable of transfer from one ownership to another at the time of tadbir, but not possible in emancipation because at that point the slave is either a mukatab or free, according to the disagreement over the two principles. And the mukatab's consent to rescission is necessary for him to accept transfer. For this reason, he takes guarantee from the mudabbir. Then the mudabbir has the right to take guarantee from the emancipator for one-third of the value of his mudabbir, because he spoilt his share as a mudabbir. And the guarantee is estimated by the value of the spoil. The value of a mudabbir is two-thirds of his value as a qinn, as they stated. And he does not guarantee the value that he acquired through the guarantee from the silent partner, because his ownership is established retroactively, and this is established in one way but not another, so it does not appear with respect to guarantee. And the wala' between the emancipator and the mudabbir is in thirds: two-thirds for the mudabbir and one-third for the emancipator, because the slave was emancipated from their joint ownership in that proportion. And since tadbir is not divisible in their view, the entire slave becomes mudabbir for the mudabbir, and he has spoiled the shares of his two partners as we explained, so he guarantees them. And this does not differ based on prosperity or poverty because it is a guarantee of ownership, so it resembles istilad, unlike emancipation because it is a guarantee of tort. And the wala' entirely belongs to the mudabbir. This is clear.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether tadbir (manumission by will) can be partial (tajazzi) when a slave is owned by multiple co-owners. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that tadbir is divisible, analogizing it to emancipation (itq), since tadbir is a branch of itq. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) disagree, maintaining tadbir is indivisible. The text follows Abu Hanifa's view. It then details the legal consequences when one co-owner makes the slave mudabbar: the other co-owners have several options—they can also make tadbir of their share, emancipate, make kitabah, seek compensation from the mudabbir, employ the slave, or leave him. If one of them chooses emancipation, that right is fixed and other options lapse. The silent (non-participating) partner can claim compensation from the mudabbir (but not from the emancipator) because this compensation is a compensation of exchange (daman mu'awada), which is the original form of guarantee. This is possible in tadbir because the slave is capable of transfer at that time, but not in emancipation where the slave becomes a mukatab or free. The mudabbir can then claim one-third of the value from the emancipator, as the mudabbir's value is two-thirds of the slave's value as a qinn (unencumbered slave). Wala' (clientage) is divided: two-thirds to the mudabbir, one-third to the emancipator. Under the Sahibayn's view, tadbir is indivisible, so the entire slave becomes mudabbar for the mudabbir, and he must compensate the other co-owners for their shares. This compensation does not vary with the mudabbir's wealth because it is a compensation for acquiring ownership, similar to istilad (bearing a child to the master). The passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of reasoning by analogy (qiyas) and the importance of the distinction between daman mu'awada and daman jinaya.

Key Terms
تدبير (tadbir): A contract where a master declares that his slave will be free upon the master's death.
تجزى (tajazza): Being divisible; in this context, that a single tadbir can apply to only part of the slave's value (i.e., the mudabbir's share).
ضمان معاوضة (daman mu'awada): A guarantee that results in the guarantor acquiring ownership of the guaranteed item in exchange for compensation; an exchange-based guarantee.
