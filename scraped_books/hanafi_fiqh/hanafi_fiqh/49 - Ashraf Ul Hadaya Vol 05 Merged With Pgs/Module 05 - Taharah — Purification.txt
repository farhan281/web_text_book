Effect of Tayammum on the Husband's Right of Return
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether tayammum nullifies the right of return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether tayammum nullifies the husband's right of return (ruj'ah) and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Tayammum nullifies the right of return only if prayer is performed, per the two shaykhs.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced disagreement within the Hanafi school between Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) about the legal effect of tayammum when performed by a woman in her waiting period after a revocable divorce. The dispute revolves around whether tayammum, as a purification of necessity, carries the same consequences as wudu, particularly the nullification of the husband's right to take back his wife. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply the Hanafi principles of necessity (darurah) and concomitant effects (lawazim).

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
State the precise question: does a woman's performance of tayammum during her iddah nullify the husband's right of return, and does prayer matter?
The context: a revocable divorce during menstruation, after which the wife performs tayammum.
2
State the two positions
Imam Muhammad holds that tayammum, like wudu, is absolute purification, so it nullifies the right immediately. The two shaykhs hold that tayammum only purifies for the act intended (prayer), so nullification occurs only if prayer is performed.
Know the key difference: with or without prayer.
3
Analyze the proofs
Imam Muhammad argues tayammum is absolute purification, citing that all rulings of wudu apply (mosque entry, Quran recitation, etc.). The two shaykhs argue tayammum is only purifying by necessity, effective only at prayer time.
Trace how each proof relies on a different understanding of tayammum's nature.
4
Examine the objection and response
Objection: The principle 'what is established by necessity does not go beyond its place' would block nullification even after prayer. Response: Another principle states 'when something is established by necessity, it is established with all its necessary consequences' – prayer establishes purification, which implies cessation of menstruation, which implies nullification of return.
Note the interplay of two legal maxims.
5
Determine the tarjih and thamarah
The preferred view is that nullification occurs only after prayer is completed, not at the start. The practical difference: if a woman does tayammum but does not pray, the right of return remains.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Imam Muhammad's proof is that when one does not have power over water, tayammum is absolute purification, so all the rulings that are proven from wudu are proven from it, such as entering the mosque, reciting the Quran, touching the Quran, performing prayer, and performing prostration of recitation. Thus tayammum reached the level of wudu. Therefore, whatever was the ruling of wudu, the same ruling applies to tayammum. And the proof of the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is that the reality of tayammum is impure and not purifying, but the Shari'ah out of necessity considered it as purifying. The necessity is that if a person does not have power over water for several days and the Shari'ah did not permit tayammum, then many obligations and duties would be incumbent upon him, making it difficult for him to perform them. Therefore, tayammum was legislated due to this necessity, and the necessity is realized at the time of performing prayer, not before. So it became known that if prayer is performed after tayammum, then purification is obtained due to tayammum, otherwise not. For this reason we said: if he performed tayammum and prayed, then due to the attainment of purification, the ruling of return (رجعة) is nullified; and if he did not pray, then due to the non-attainment of purification, the ruling of return is not nullified. And 'and all the established rulings' is a response to Imam Muhammad's principle (that the rulings proven from wudu are also proven from tayammum). The summary of the response is that the proof of these rulings from tayammum is due to the necessity of performing prayer. For example, reciting the Quran is necessary for the validity of prayer because it is a pillar of prayer; the mosque because it is the place of prayer; the prostration of recitation because it is among the consequences of recitation; and touching the Quran because if someone forgets what suffices for prayer before reciting, he needs to open and look at the Quran, which cannot be done without touching the Quran. Some scholars have raised an objection to the proof of the two shaykhs. The objection is that tayammum is a purification of necessity, and necessity is realized at the time of performing prayer, not before, and it is an established principle that 'that which is established by necessity does not go beyond its place'. So the requirement of this principle would be that the ruling of return is not nullified by tayammum, even if the woman prays after tayammum, until she performs wudu or the time of prayer passes. The answer: Without doubt, 'that which is established by necessity does not go beyond its place' is a principle, but there is also the principle that 'when something is established by necessity, it is established with all its necessary consequences'. And at the time of performing prayer, from the consequences of the establishment of purification are the cessation of menstruation and from the consequences of the period of waiting (iddah) is the nullification of the right of return. And the rule is that the necessary consequence of a necessary consequence is itself a necessary consequence. Therefore, at the time of performing prayer, the ruling of the nullification of return is established from the establishment of purification. (From 'Inayah and other commentaries, know this). Then, according to the two shaykhs, some scholars say that the ruling of return is nullified as soon as prayer is started, and some are of the opinion that it is nullified after finishing prayer, so that the ruling of the validity of prayer is established. This is the correct opinion, because the state after starting prayer is like the state before starting. For if during prayer he sees water and has the power to use it, the effect of tayammum does not remain. Unlike after finishing prayer.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs (Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf) regarding the effect of tayammum on the husband's right of return (رجعة) when the wife is in her waiting period after a revocable divorce. The context: a husband divorces his wife during her menstruation (which is prohibited but occurs), and the wife then performs tayammum and prays. Does this nullify the husband's right to take her back? Imam Muhammad argues that tayammum is a complete purification (طہارت مطلقہ) like wudu, so it brings about all the same effects, including the cessation of menstruation (which ends the period of prohibition for return). The two shaykhs argue that tayammum is only a purification of necessity, effective only for the specific act for which it was intended (prayer), and does not extend beyond that. Therefore, the right of return is nullified only if the wife actually performs the prayer, because the necessity for purification is specifically for prayer. The passage then addresses an objection that the principle 'that which is established by necessity does not go beyond its place' would imply that even after prayer, the right of return is not nullified until wudu is performed. The response is that another principle applies: 'When something is established by necessity, it is established with all its necessary consequences.' Since the purification from tayammum (established for prayer) necessarily implies the cessation of menstruation, and that in turn implies the nullification of the right of return, therefore the right of return is nullified after the prayer is performed. The passage concludes with a discussion among the two shaykhs' followers about whether nullification occurs at the start of prayer or after completion, and the preferred view is after completion, because if water becomes available during prayer, tayammum's effect is lost.

Key Terms
تیم (tayammum): Dry purification using earth or dust when water is unavailable, considered a substitute for wudu or ghusl.
رجعت (ruj'ah): The husband's right to take back his wife during the waiting period (iddah) of a revocable divorce without a new marriage contract.
طہارت مطلقہ (taharat mutlaqa): Absolute purification that removes all states of impurity and renders one legally pure for any act of worship.
ضرورت (darurah): Necessity that justifies a departure from normal rules, such as allowing tayammum when water is absent.

Incomplete Ghusl and the Right of Return: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Ruling on incomplete ghusl and its effect on the husband's right of return with istihsan and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on incomplete ghusl regarding the right of return, explain why istihsan overrides qiyas, and distinguish between missing a full limb and less than a limb.

Core Idea
Missing a full limb in ghusl leaves the right of return intact; missing less than a limb nullifies it—istihsan overrides qiyas.

In this lesson, we examine a nuance of purification after menstruation: if a woman forgets to wash a part of her body during ghusl, does her ghusl count? The Hanafi school makes a surprising distinction based on the size of the missed area, applying istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas to reach a practical conclusion based on certainty and precaution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The woman performs ghusl after menstruation but forgets to wash a part of her body. The question is whether her ghusl is considered valid for the purpose of ending the waiting period and nullifying the husband's right of return (ruj'ah).
This only applies if she forgets, not intentionally skips.
2
Distinguish the Two Cases
The forgotten part is either a full limb (عضو کامل) or less than a limb (مادون العضو). The ruling differs based on this distinction.
A full limb includes arm, leg, head, etc. Less than a limb is a small area like a fingertip or a patch of skin.
3
Apply the Ruling
If a full limb or more is missed, the right of return is not nullified (remains). If less than a limb is missed, the right of return is nullified (lost).
This is the mufta bihi ruling based on istihsan.
4
Contrast with Qiyas
By qiyas: if a full limb is missed, since most of the body is washed, the ghusl should be considered valid and the right nullified. If less than a limb is missed, because impurity is indivisible, the ghusl should be invalid and the right remains. Istihsan reverses this.
The qiyas arguments are explained in the passage.
5
Understand the Istihsan Reasoning
For less than a limb: the area dries quickly, so one cannot be certain water did not reach it. Therefore, precaution (احتیاط) dictates that the ghusl is considered valid, nullifying the right. For a full limb: the area is large, dries slowly, and people are not usually negligent, so certainty of missing is high, and the ghusl is invalid, thus the right remains.
Istihsan here is based on practical certainty and ease.
6
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Regarding rinsing the mouth and nose (مضمضة واستنشاق), there are two narrations: one equates them to a full limb, the other to less than a limb. The relied-upon view follows the latter.
This is because their obligation is disputed (sunnah vs fard), unlike other limbs.

Commentary
This passage deals with a specific ruling: a woman who has completed her menstruation and performs ghusl, but forgets to wash a part of her body. The question is whether her ghusl is considered valid enough to nullify the husband's right of return (رجعة) during the waiting period. The ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) as opposed to qiyas (analogy). According to the relied-upon position, if the forgotten part is a full limb (عضو کامل) or more, the right of return is not nullified because the ghusl is incomplete and the woman remains in a state of major impurity (جنابت). If the forgotten part is less than a limb (مادون العضو), the right of return is nullified because the small area is likely to have been washed or dries quickly, so precaution dictates that the ghusl is considered valid. The text then explains the qiyas arguments: for a full limb, qiyas would suggest that since she washed most of the body, the ghusl should be valid and the right of return nullified; for less than a limb, qiyas would suggest that since impurity is indivisible, the ghusl is invalid and the right remains. But istihsan overrides qiyas based on the practical consideration of certainty. The passage also discusses a sub-issue: regarding rinsing the mouth (مضمضة) and nose (استنشاق), there is a difference of opinion among the Hanafi imams. Abu Yusuf in one narration equates them to a full limb, while in another narration (which is also the view of Imam Muhammad) they are like less than a limb, because their obligation is disputed. The explanation at the end clarifies that the subject of qiyas is not explicitly stated by Imam Muhammad, and the discussion is about which part is the subject of the analogy.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that deviates from qiyas in favor of a more suitable ruling based on considerations of necessity, custom, or ease.
عضو کامل (uzw kamil): A complete limb of the body, such as an arm, leg, or head. In this context, any limb that is typically considered a distinct part.
رجعت (ruj'ah): The husband's right to take back his wife during the waiting period of a revocable divorce.
مضمضة واستنشاق (madmadah wa istinshaq): Rinsing the mouth and sniffing water into the nose, which are parts of wudu and ghusl.

Definition and Practices of Hidad (Mourning) During 'Iddah
Defines hidad and lists prohibited acts with exceptions for necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the actions prohibited during hidad and identify which exceptions are allowed according to necessity.

Core Idea
Mourning in iddah requires abandoning all adornments including kohl and oil, even unscented, except for genuine medical necessity.

This lesson defines the practice of hidad (mourning) during a woman's iddah. You will learn the specific prohibitions of perfume, adornment, kohl, and oil, and understand the exceptions allowed for necessity. The source text from al-Hidayah provides the detailed rules with evidence from hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Hidad is wajib for a woman in iddah. It is defined as abstaining from specific items to express grief and avoid temptation.
The text mentions two linguistic forms: al-hidad and al-ihdad.
2
List the prohibited items
The prohibited items are perfume (tib), adornment (zinah), kohl (kuhl), and oil (duhn), both scented and unscented.
These items are prohibited because they incite desire and contradict the state of mourning.
3
Understand the rationale
Two reasons: first, to express regret and grief; second, to prevent falling into the forbidden (sadd al-dhara'i).
The hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) is cited: he did not permit the woman in iddah to apply kohl.
4
Apply the exception for necessity
Necessity (udhr) like pain or medical need allows the use of these items for medicinal purposes, not adornment. The text from al-Jami' al-Saghir specifies 'except for pain'.
If a woman is accustomed to oil and fears pain if she stops, and the need is apparent, it becomes permissible.
