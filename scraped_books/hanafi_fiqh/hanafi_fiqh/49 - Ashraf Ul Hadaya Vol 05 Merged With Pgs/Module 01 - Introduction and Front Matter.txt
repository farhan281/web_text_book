Rulings on Divorce During Death Illness
Table of contents with ruling summaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a wife's inheritance when the husband recovers after giving divorce during death illness, and identify the key factor that changes the ruling.

Core Idea
Recovery from deathbed illness cancels the special inheritance protection for the wife after divorce.

This passage from the table of contents of Ashraf al-Hidaya volume five provides a preview of key rulings on divorce during death illness (marad al-mawt). These summaries show how the Hanafi school distinguishes between divorces given during terminal illness versus health, especially regarding the wife's inheritance rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Read each table of contents entry and note the key factors: state of health at time of divorce, recovery, cause of death.
For example, 'If he gave three divorces during illness, then recovered, then died: the wife does not inherit.'
2
Extract the ruling
State the specific inheritance ruling (whether the wife inherits or not) for each scenario.
3
Note the underlying principle
Recovery indicates the illness was not the cause of death, so the protective rules for deathbed divorce cease.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that deathbed divorces are treated as if made after death for inheritance purposes, but recovery revokes that status.
4
Contrast with related entries
Compare with the entry where the husband does not recover: she inherits. Also note intra-Hanafi differences (Imam Zufar, Imam Muhammad).

If he gives three divorces at the woman's request, or says 'I give you the choice' and she says 'I choose myself', or the woman initiates khul', then the husband dies and the woman is in 'iddah, will she inherit? The husband told the woman during death illness: 'I gave you three divorces while healthy and your 'iddah has passed,' the woman confirmed; then the woman made a confession of debt or will; what will the woman get? Statements of jurists. A person who is besieged in a fort or in a battle line and gives his wife three divorces: the woman does not inherit. But if that person came out to fight a man or presented himself for retaliation or to be killed in the body, and he died or was killed for that reason, then the woman inherits. A man telling his wife while healthy: 'When the beginning of the month comes, or you enter the house, or if you meet so-and-so, or if so-and-so enters the house, then you are divorced' — all these conditions are fulfilled but the husband is ill: the woman does not inherit. But if the husband's statement was during illness, she will inherit. He gave three divorces during illness, then recovered, then died: the woman does not inherit. Imam Zufar's viewpoint: He gave divorce during death illness, the woman (God forbid) apostatized, then became Muslim, then the husband died during that illness and she was in 'iddah: she does not inherit. But if she did not apostatize, rather she gave the husband's son permission to have intercourse with her, she inherits. Reason for difference: He accused his wife of adultery while healthy, and during death illness he performed li'an: the woman inherits. Imam Muhammad's viewpoint: He performed ila' with his wife while healthy, then due to ila' the woman became irrevocably divorced, and the man is ill: the woman does not inherit. But if the ila' was during death illness, she inherits. Every divorce in which the husband has the right to revoke, in all such cases the woman inherits. Chapter of Revocation: A man gave his wife one or two revocable divorces; the husband can revoke his wife whether she is willing or not. Verbal and actual revocation. It is recommended to have two witnesses for revocation, and revocation without witnesses is valid. Commentary
This passage is the front matter of Volume 5 of Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah by Maulana Jameel Ahmad Sukruzi. It opens with copyright and publication information, stating that all rights belong to the publisher in Pakistan and warning against unauthorized reproduction. It includes a note to readers requesting feedback on any errors. Then follows a table of contents (fihrist) listing the topics covered in the volume, focusing on the chapter of divorce during death illness (Bāb Ṭalāq al-Marīḍ) and the chapter of revocation (Bāb al-Rajʿah). The headings summarize various rulings: inheritance rights of a woman divorced during terminal illness depending on circumstances, choice of divorce, khul', apostasy, li'an, ila', and the effect of recovery. The table of contents serves as a structural overview for the student, not as a ruling itself. The final section briefly summarizes the chapter on revocation, stating the basic rule that a husband may revoke a revocable divorce whether the wife consents or not, and that witnesses are recommended but not required.

Key Terms
باب الرجعة (Bāb al-Rajʿah): Chapter on revocation of divorce — the section of fiqh dealing with a husband revoking a revocable divorce during the waiting period.
مرض الموت (Maraḍ al-Mawt): Deathbed illness — a condition in which the person is likely to die from that illness, creating special rules regarding divorces and bequests to protect the wife's inheritance.

Key Takeaway
Recovery from deathbed illness after divorce cancels the wife's inheritance right; the key factor is whether the husband remained terminally ill until death.

Overview of Hidanah and Nafaqah in Hanafi Fiqh
Table of contents listing chapters on custody and maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key Hanafi rulings on child custody (hidānah) and maintenance (nafaqah) as outlined in the table of contents.

Core Idea
Child custody prioritizes maternal relatives; maintenance is obligatory on the husband and father.

This lesson introduces the major topics covered in the sections on child custody (hidānah) and maintenance (nafaqah) from a classical Hanafi text. The table of contents lists specific questions and rulings, which will be explored in subsequent lessons. Understanding this overview will help you navigate the detailed discussions that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main sections
The passage is divided into two main sections: custody (hidānah) and maintenance (nafaqah). Note the headings and subheadings listed.
The custody section covers entitlements, durations, and interschool differences.
2
Note the key parties
Identify the parties involved: mother, maternal grandmother, father, child, slave women, dhimmi women, etc.
Each party's entitlement depends on conditions like relationship, religion, and capacity.
3
Highlight interschool differences
The TOC explicitly mentions Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint on child's choice and maintenance during iddah. Recognize where Hanafi and Shafi'i positions diverge.
These conflicts will be detailed in later lessons.
4
Recognize conditions and exceptions
Many entries list conditions for maintenance (e.g., nashizah, imprisoned, minor wife) and exceptions for custody (e.g., dhimmi mother, removal from city).
Note the recurring theme of conditional entitlement.

Calling your own slave 'my master' frees him because only the meaning 'freed slave' fits.

This passage addresses a specific expression that triggers automatic emancipation in Hanafi law. When a master addresses his slave as 'my master' (hādhā mawlay), the word 'mawla' must be understood in the sense of 'freed slave' because all other meanings are impossible in this context. This leads to the ruling that the slave is freed immediately, without any need for the master to intend emancipation. The passage also clarifies the distinction between what is accepted in conscience (diyanatan) and what is enforceable in court (qada'an).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is a master saying 'hādhā mawlay' (this is my master) to his own slave. The ruling is that the slave is freed immediately and completely.
2
Analyze the Possible Meanings of 'Mawla'
The word 'mawla' has several meanings: lord, master, emancipator, freed slave, client, etc. Eliminate each meaning that is impossible in this context (e.g., the slave cannot be the master's lord or emancipator).
Only 'freed slave' remains as a real, non-metaphorical meaning.
3
Classify the Expression as Sarih
Because only one plausible meaning remains, the word 'mawla' is considered sarih (clear) for emancipation, like the words 'anta hurr' (you are free). Sarih expressions do not require intent.
Hanafi usul distinguishes sarih (effective without niyyah) from kinayah (requires niyyah).
4
Apply the Ruling to Male and Female Slaves
The same ruling applies if the master says 'hādhihi mawlātī' (this is my mistress) to his female slave; she is freed without intent.
The passage explicitly extends the ruling to female slaves.
5
Distinguish Diyanah and Qada'
If the master later claims he meant religious clientage or that he lied, this claim may be accepted in conscience (diyanatan) but not in court (qada'an), because it contradicts the apparent meaning of his words.
This protects the slave's freedom in practice.

The one from the east and the other from the west; there is no closeness between them, neither real closeness nor by lineage nor by place. So religious closeness is determined. The conclusion is that religious clientage is metaphorical. Whereas here the discussion is in the real meaning. Therefore this meaning also is not intended. And since this person called the slave 'master', therefore 'master' cannot be in the meaning of the emancipator, because attribution to the slave is contrary to his being the emancipator. So when none of these meanings are intended, then the meaning of 'freed slave' is determined. And the word 'master' in the meaning of freed slave is like a clear (sarih) word, and a clear word does not require intention. Therefore, by the statement 'this is my master', the slave becomes free without intention. Similarly, if one says to his female slave 'this is my mistress', she also becomes free without intention. The proof is what we have stated under 'this is my master'. And if the owner claims that he intended religious clientage or that he lied, then in conscience he may be believed, but the judge will not believe him, because this intention is contrary to the apparent meaning, and intention contrary to the apparent is valid in conscience but not in judgment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding emancipation by the word 'mawla'. The author explains that when a master says to his slave 'hādhā mawlay' (this is my master), the slave becomes free without any intention. The reasoning is that the word 'mawla' has multiple meanings: lord, master, emancipator, freed slave, client, etc. Here, the master is addressing his own slave as 'mawla', so the meanings of 'lord' or 'master' are excluded because the slave is not a master over his owner. 'Emancipator' is also excluded because the slave did not free the master. 'Religious clientage' (muwalāt) is metaphorical, but the context requires a real meaning. Thus, only 'freed slave' remains. The word 'mawla' in this sense is considered like a clear (sarih) expression for emancipation, which does not require intent. The same applies to a female slave. If the master claims he meant something else, it is accepted in conscience but not in court because it contradicts the apparent meaning.
