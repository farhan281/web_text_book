Sheikhayn vs Muhammad on Sa'aya for Co-Owned Slave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the amount of sa'aya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sheikhayn and Imam Muhammad positions on sa'aya for a co-owned slave with conflicting oaths, and explain the proof structure for each view.

Core Idea
When only half freedom is certain, full sa'aya cannot be wajib; the Sheikhayn divide the obligation, Muhammad requires full.

This passage from Ashraf ul Hadaya presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the obligation of sa'aya when two co-owners of a slave make contradictory conditional oaths. The legal challenge is that the identity of the master whose oath is broken is unknown, leading to uncertainty about how much the slave must earn. The Sheikhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) resolve this by distributing the half freedom over both masters, requiring half sa'aya. Imam Muhammad insists on full sa'aya because judgment cannot rest on an unknown.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
Two co-owners make contradictory oaths: one says 'if Zayd does not enter the house, the slave is free,' the other says 'if Zayd enters, the slave is free.' The day passes without knowledge of Zayd's entrance.
2
State the Sheikhayn position
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, half the slave is certainly free (since one condition must have occurred), and the slave must earn half his value for both owners.
This is the mufta bihi view.
3
Identify Sheikhayn's proof
We are certain that half the sa'aya has dropped. Certainty of half prevents full obligation. The ignorance of which master's sa'aya drops is removed by distributing the freed half over both masters.
Analogy: freeing one of two unspecified slaves makes each half-free and each earns half.
4
State Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad says the slave must earn his full value (if both masters are poor). If both are wealthy, no sa'aya; if one wealthy and one poor, earn half for the wealthy only.
He conditions the ruling on the masters' financial status.
5
Explain Muhammad's proof
The person against whom sa'aya is to be dropped is unknown, and judgment cannot be passed on an unknown entity. Analogy: 'One of us owes you a thousand dirhams' – no judgment can be made due to ignorance.
He treats the ignorance as insurmountable without distribution.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a jointly-owned slave when two co-owners make contradictory conditional oaths. The case: one co-owner says, 'If Zayd does not enter the house tomorrow, this slave is free.' The other says, 'If Zayd enters, this slave is free.' The day passes without knowledge of whether Zayd entered. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (Sheikhayn), half the slave is certainly free because one condition must have occurred, and the slave must earn half his value for both masters. Imam Muhammad holds that the slave must earn his full value because the identity of the master whose sa'aya is dropped is unknown. The Sheikhayn respond that ignorance is removed by distributing the freed half over both masters, making both subject to the earning obligation. The commentary then explains that this dispute also branches on whether wealth (yasar) prevents the obligation of sa'aya. Abu Hanifa holds wealth does not prevent, while the Sheikhayn hold it does. The preferred (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sheikhayn: half freedom and half earnings.

Key Terms
سعاية (sa'aya): The slave's earning of his value or part thereof to gain freedom, typically when partially free.
حانث (hanith): One who breaks his oath, i.e., the condition he swore by is fulfilled.
جهالة (jahala): Ignorance or uncertainty about a relevant fact, here the identity of the master whose sa'aya drops.
شيوع (shuyu'): Distribution or apportionment of a right or obligation among multiple parties.

Contradictory Oaths on Separate Slaves: No Freedom Due to Excessive Ignorance
Ruling on contradictory conditional oaths on separate slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two masters make contradictory conditional oaths on separate slaves and explain why excessive ignorance (jahala fahisha) prevents judgment.

Core Idea
When both the master and slave are unknown, excessive ignorance blocks any ruling — no slave is freed.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in qada (judicial rulings) where two individuals each own a slave and make contradictory conditional oaths. The key legal principle at work is that a judge cannot issue a ruling when the parties—the one against whom the ruling is made (maqdi 'alayhi) and the one in whose favor it is made (maqdi lahu)—are both unknown, as this constitutes jahala fahisha (excessive ignorance). This ruling is unanimously held within the Hanafi school and is considered agreed upon (muttafaq alayh).

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their oaths
We have two masters, each owning a separate slave. One master swears: 'If Zayd enters the house tomorrow, my slave is free.' The other swears: 'If Zayd does not enter, my slave is free.' Both conditions are contradictory, and it remains unknown whether Zayd entered or not.
The oaths are conditional upon the same event but in opposite ways.
2
Determine the unknown elements
Two things are unknown: (1) which master's condition was actually fulfilled (the maqdi 'alayhi), and (2) which specific slave is to be freed (the maqdi lahu). Both are essential for a valid judgment.
The judge must know exactly upon whom and in whose favor to rule.
3
Apply the principle of excessive ignorance
Since both the subject and object of the ruling are unknown, the ignorance is deemed excessive (jahala fahisha). According to Hanafi usul, such ignorance makes any judicial decree impossible (mumtani').
This contrasts with jahala yasira (minor ignorance), which may be overlooked.
4
Contrast with the single-slave case
If the two masters jointly owned a single slave, the slave is known even if the specific master is not. In that case, the known (the slave) prevails over the unknown (which master's condition was met), and half the slave is freed.
This contrast highlights that only when both parties are unknown does judgment become impossible.
5
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, in the case of two separate slaves, no slave is freed. The judge cannot issue any decree due to excessive ignorance. This ruling is unanimously accepted within the Hanafi school.
The proof is rational: judgment requires certainty, which is absent here.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling agreed upon by all Hanafi scholars (mujma' alayh). The case involves two separate slaves owned by two different masters, each making a conditional oath about the entry of Zayd. Because both the identity of the master whose oath is fulfilled and the identity of the slave to be freed are unknown, the ignorance (jahala) is excessive (fahisha), preventing any judgment. This is contrasted with the case of a single slave owned jointly by two partners, where the slave is known and only the master is unknown, so the judge can rule that half the slave is free. The proof is based on the principle that judgment cannot be passed on an unknown person. This ruling is unanimous within the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
مقضى عليه (maqdi 'alayhi): The person against whom the ruling is made; here, the master whose slave is freed.
مقضى له (maqdi lahu): The person in whose favor the ruling is made; here, the slave who is freed.
جہالت فاحشہ (jahala fahisha): Excessive or gross ignorance about the essential parties of a legal transaction, preventing judgment.

When both the party against whom the ruling is made and the beneficiary are unknown, excessive ignorance prevents any judgment — no slave becomes free.
