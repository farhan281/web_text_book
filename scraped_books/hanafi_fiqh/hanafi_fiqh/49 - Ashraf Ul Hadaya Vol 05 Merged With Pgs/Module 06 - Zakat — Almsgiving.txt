Freeing Blind or Double-Amputee Slave in Zihar Kaffarah
Ruling on physical defects preventing valid slave emancipation for zihar kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which physical defects in a slave invalidate zihar kaffarah according to the Hanafi school and explain the principle of jins al-manfa'ah.

Core Idea
A slave is valid for kaffarah only if the entire category of benefit remains; mere impairment does not invalidate.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a critical nuance in the validity of slave emancipation for zihar kaffarah. The Hanafi school distinguishes between loss of the entire category of benefit (e.g., sight, walking) and mere impairment (e.g., one eye, one limb). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the kaffarah rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act under discussion is freeing a slave for zihar kaffarah. The ruling determines whether the emancipation fulfills the obligation.
The act is valid (sahih) or invalid (ghayr sahih) depending on the slave's condition.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the principle of jins al-manfa'ah (entire category of benefit). The author states that if the entire category of benefit (sight, grip, walking) is lost, the freeing is invalid.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the purpose of kaffarah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: the purpose of freeing a slave is to enable worship; if the slave lacks a basic benefit category, the purpose is defeated, so the act is invalid.
Blindness removes sight, double amputation removes grip/walking.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Shafi'i argues that any irreversible defect (like one-eyed) invalidates. The Hanafi school responds that impairment does not equal total loss.
The difference hinges on whether the category of benefit remains functionally present.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position (one-eyed valid) is relied upon (mufta bihi) because it preserves flexibility and aligns with the principle of jins al-manfa'ah.
The deaf slave is also valid by istihsan, despite qiyas suggesting otherwise.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah is a commentary on rulings about physical defects in slaves for kaffarah. It opens with a response to Imam Shafi'i's argument that the intention of freeing is to seek closeness to Allah. The response clarifies that the actual purpose is to free a slave to enable worship, not merely emancipation. Then the author discusses the definition of raqabah and a grammatical note. The core ruling states that a blind slave or one with both hands or feet amputated is not valid for kaffarah because the entire category of benefit (sight, grip, walking) is lost. A one-eyed slave or one with a hand and foot on opposite sides amputated is valid because the category is only impaired, not lost. However, if the hand and foot are on the same side, walking becomes impossible, so the category is lost. The commentary explains Sahib Inayah's principle that validity requires the slave to be perfectly owned, the intention connected, the category of benefit remaining, and the freeing without compensation. It also notes Imam Shafi'i's opposing view on one-eyed slaves: he considers any irreversible defect as impairing validity, equating it to blindness. The Hanafi position is that impairment does not bar validity as long as the category of benefit is not entirely lost.

Key Terms
جنس المنفعة (jins al-manfa'ah): The entire category of a particular benefit, e.g., sight, grip, walking.
الرق (al-riqq): The state of being a slave; here, perfect serfdom (kamil al-riqq) is required.

Freeing a Mukatab for Kaffarah Zihar is Invalid
Ruling with proof on freeing a mukatab for kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that freeing a mukatab for kaffarah zihar is invalid and explain the proof that kitabah does not negate slavery.

Core Idea
Freeing a mukatab for kaffarah is batil because it involves compensation, turning worship into trade.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, the commentator explains why freeing a mukatab (a slave under a kitabah contract) for the kaffarah of zihar is invalid. The ruling hinges on the requirement that kaffarah be a pure act of worship, free from any element of trade. The text provides two proofs: first, that freeing a mukatab involves forfeiting the kitabah payment, making it a transaction; second, that kitabah does not negate the slave's status as a slave, so emancipation is not complete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Freeing a mukatab for kaffarah zihar. Ruling: batil (invalid). The text states: 'this act of worship is no longer purely for the sake of Allah but becomes trade.'
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
Two proofs are given. First, the direct statement that it becomes trade. Second, the argument that 'kitabah does not negate it (slavery)', so the mukatab remains a slave.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof works)
The second proof: kitabah removes an obstacle (the restriction on the slave's ability to trade) but does not remove the underlying state of being a slave (riqq). A thing is only removed by its opposite; kitabah is not the opposite of riqq. Therefore, the mukatab is still a slave, and freeing him is not unconditional emancipation.
The text compares kitabah to idhn fil-tijarah (permission to trade), which also does not remove slavery.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
The text does not mention differences, but this is a mukhtalaf fih issue; the Hanafi position is that freeing a mukatab for kaffarah is invalid, while other schools may allow it.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that it is invalid. This is based on the requirement that kaffarah be pure worship, and the involvement of compensation negates that purity.

Chapter of Zihar. Ashraf al-Hidaya Urdu commentary on Hidaya Volume 5. [If the slave] paid the kitabah, then this act of worship is no longer purely for the sake of Allah but becomes trade. For this reason, freeing such a mukatab will not fulfill the kaffarah. 'And kitabah does not negate it' is the second proof. The summary is that a mukatab is a slave before kitabah, and due to kitabah, the state of being a slave is not removed, because a thing is only removed by its opposite, and kitabah is not the opposite of slavery. This is because the contract of kitabah is the name for removing the obstacle that existed due to his being a slave and a slave-girl, and removing a prohibition and obstacle is not contradictory to slavery. Just as if a master permitted one of his slaves to trade, that slave does not cease to be a slave because permission to trade is not contradictory to slavery. 'Except that it is with compensation' is an answer to a question. The question is: If kitabah is like permission to trade in removing an obstacle, then the master should have the right to rescind the contract of kitabah independently, just as the master is independent in revoking permission for trade. However, the master cannot rescind the contract of kitabah alone without the consent of the mukatab. The answer to this question is that the contract of kitabah is the name for removing an obstacle and restriction with compensation. Thus, kitabah is binding on the master, unlike permission to trade, which is the name for removing an obstacle without compensation. Therefore, it is not binding on the master. So, when this difference exists, analogizing one to the other is not correct. The second answer is that even if we concede that the contract of kitabah prevents freeing for kaffarah, when he frees this mukatab for kaffarah, due to the requirement of freeing, the contract of kitabah will be rescinded before the freeing itself. Because the contract of kitabah is susceptible to rescission. So when the contract of kitabah was rescinded before the master's freeing, then this is freeing a non-mukatab, not a mukatab. Hence no problem remains. 'Except that it is conceded to him, but regarding earnings...' [this] is also an answer to a question. The question is: If freeing a mukatab for kaffarah is valid and due to the requirement of freeing the contract of kitabah was rescinded, then the mukatab's children and his earnings should all belong to the master, just as in kaffarah if someone frees his slave who was permitted to trade, and the slave has some goods from his earnings, all goes to the master. The first answer is that for the mukatab, freedom is due to his being a mukatab. So when that is the case, his children and earnings will not leave his ownership. The second answer is that here the rescission of kitabah is established due to the validity of freeing out of necessity, and what is established out of necessity remains within the scope of necessity. Therefore, for the permissibility of freeing in kaffarah, the rescission of kitabah will be apparent, but not for his children and earnings. So when for the children and earnings of the mukatab, kitabah is not rescinded, then his children and earnings will be for him, not for the master.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah deals with the ruling of freeing a mukatab (a slave under a contract of kitabah, i.e., working to purchase freedom) for the kaffarah of zihar. The author establishes that such a freeing is invalid (the kaffarah is not fulfilled) because the kafarah requires an act of pure worship solely for Allah's sake, but freeing a mukatab involves the master giving up the kitabah payment (the consideration for freedom), thus it resembles a trade. The author then provides two proofs: first, the direct statement that it becomes trade; second, the argument that kitabah does not negate slavery (riqq), so the mukatab remains a slave and freeing a slave who owes money is not a perfect emancipation. Two objections are raised and answered. The first objection analogizes kitabah to permission to trade (idhn fil-tijarah), arguing that if idhn does not remove slavery, why can't the master revoke kitabah unilaterally just as he can revoke idhn? The answer distinguishes kitabah as a contract with consideration ('iwad), making it binding on the master, whereas idhn is gratuitous and revocable. The second objection concerns the children and earnings of the mukatab after the forced rescission of kitabah: if the contract is rescinded, shouldn't they revert to the master? The answer distinguishes between the rescission being necessary for the validity of freeing (limited to the person) and not extending to property/offspring. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute. A person who owes kaffarah frees half of a co-owned slave and guarantees the value of the other half. All three imams agree: if the person is poor (mu'sir), the freeing is invalid. Because then the slave must work to earn the value of the other half (sa‘āyah), making the freeing a compensated transaction, which is not allowed for kaffarah.
The presence of compensation ('iwad) is universally opposed to kaffarah.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya deals with a specific scenario in kaffarah zihar: a person who owes kaffarah frees half of a slave that he co-owns with someone else, and then guarantees the value of the other half (promises to pay the co-owner the value of his share). The ruling depends on the wealth of the person freeing the slave. If the person is wealthy (mūsir), then Abu Hanifa says the kaffarah is not valid, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two companions, al-Sāhibayn) say it is valid. The reasoning behind each: The companions argue that by guaranteeing the value, the person becomes the owner of the co-owner's share (because the guarantee is a form of purchase), so he owns the whole slave at the moment of emancipation, making it a valid emancipation from his own property. Abu Hanifa argues that the co-owner's share first leaves the ownership of the co-owner and then enters the ownership of the emancipator via the guarantee; this interim deficiency (the share being out of his ownership at the time of the act of freeing half) prevents the kaffarah from being valid. If the person is destitute (mu‘sir), then all agree the kaffarah is not valid, because the slave would have to earn (sa‘y) to compensate the co-owner, making the emancipation 'with compensation' ('iwad), which is not acceptable for kaffarah. So the intra-Hanafi dispute exists only in the case where the emancipator is wealthy and guarantees the value.

Zihar Kaffarah: Feeding Sixty Poor When Unable to Fast
Ruling on feeding when unable to fast for zihar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for zihar kaffarah when unable to fast, specify the required quantities per poor person, and explain the Hanafi position on giving value versus specified food items.

Core Idea
Feeding sixty poor is wajib; each receives half sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates/barley, but specified items cannot substitute each other.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the expiation for zihar when the person cannot fast. It clarifies the obligatory amount per poor person and introduces a key fiqh principle: while giving the monetary value is permissible for unspecified items, the specified food items must be given in the exact prescribed quantity. Understanding this ruling helps avoid common mistakes in fulfilling the kaffarah obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Proof
Recognize that feeding sixty poor is wajib when the person is unable to fast. The primary proof is the Quranic verse (58:4) and the hadith of Aus bin al-Samit and Sahl bin Sa'd.
The obligation is sequential: freeing a slave, then fasting two months, then feeding sixty poor.
2
Determine the Quantity per Poor Person
Each poor person must receive half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates or barley. This is derived from the hadith and qiyas on sadaqat al-fitr, where the same measure applies so far fulfilling the daily need of a poor person.
Half sa' of wheat is approximately 1.5 kg; one sa' of dates/barley is about 3 kg.
3
Understand the Permissibility of Giving Value
The author states that the value of the prescribed food items can be given instead, which is the Hanafi position. However, value can only be given for items not explicitly mentioned (ghayr manṣūṣ ʿalayh).
This is based on istihsan and the objective of feeding the poor, as detailed in the Book of Zakat.
4
Apply the Rule on Substitution
Specified items (wheat, dates, barley) cannot be exchanged for each other even if the monetary values match. For example, half a sa' of dates is not valid because the nass requires one sa' of dates. Only non-specified items (e.g., rice) can be given as value.
The rule prevents mixing specified items: each has its own prescribed quantity that must be adhered to.

Zihar feeding quantity is analogized to sadaqat al-fitr, but the number of poor is fixed unlike sadaqat.

This passage from the commentary on Hidayah first corrects an error in the name of a hadith narrator. The analogy is made on sadaqat al-fitr, but a crucial difference is noted: in zihar, the number of recipients is fixed at sixty, while in sadaqat al-fitr, only the quantity per person matters.

Commentary
This passage first corrects an error in Hidayah regarding the narrator's name: it should be Sahl bin Matar or Salamah bin Sahar, not Sahl bin Sahar. The commentator then reaffirms the ruling derived from the hadiths. Next, he presents a rational proof (qiyas) for the quantity of food: by analogy with sadaqat al-fitr, the amount per poor person should be half a sa' of wheat or its equivalent. However, he notes a key difference: in zihar, both the number of recipients (sixty) and the quantity per person are fixed, while in sadaqat al-fitr only the quantity matters, not the number of recipients. Therefore, in sadaqat al-fitr, one fitra can be divided among multiple poor, and multiple fitras can be given to one poor, but in zihar each poor must receive exactly the prescribed amount. Finally, the author of Hidayah reiterates that the Hanafi position allows giving value, as stated by al-Quduri.

Giving Mixed Grains in Zihar Kaffarah
Ruling on combining different grains for kaffarah based on genus unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on combining different grains for zihar kaffarah and explain the proof based on genus unity and purpose.

Core Idea
Mixing different foods of the same genus in kaffarah is valid because the purpose of feeding is achieved, not the specific grain.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the chapter on Zihar: whether one can combine different types of grains, such as wheat and dates, to fulfill the kaffarah. The Hanafi school permits this based on the principle that the genus (jins) of food items is the same and the goal of feeding is met. We will explore the reasoning and evidence behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? Here, it is the validity of giving combined grains (e.g., wheat and dates) for zihar kaffarah. The ruling is that it is valid (ja'iz).
The act is a form of feeding (it'am) for kaffarah.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author's proof is that the purpose (maqsud) of feeding is achieved and the genus (jins) is unified. The Quranic obligation is to feed, and any food of the same genus fulfills that.
No explicit text is quoted; the proof is a reasoned argument (talil).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the proof lead to the ruling? Because the goal of kaffarah is to feed and alleviate hunger, and wheat, dates, and barley all serve that same purpose. They are considered one genus in terms of nutritional value.
This is based on 'urf (custom) and maqasid (objectives).
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i may require tamlik (transfer of ownership) of the specific grain, not merely feeding. The Hanafi school focuses on the purpose rather than the form.
The difference leads to practical consequences in how kaffarah is administered.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi. It is considered more practical and in line with the broader objective of the law.
The school prioritizes ease (taysir) while maintaining validity.

Explanation: In one sa' there are four maunds, so one maund is a quarter of a sa'. Because the purpose, i.e., giving food, is found, because both are of one genus, in the sense that the purpose of both things is to give food and remove hunger, therefore completing one with the other is permissible.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the permissibility of combining different grains when giving kaffarah for zihar. The author explains that the Quranic requirement is to feed poor people, and the goal is to satisfy hunger. Since wheat, dates, and barley are all food items of the same genus (jins), the purpose of feeding is achieved even if the amounts are different. The Hanafi position is that one maund of wheat (quarter sa') plus two maunds of dates or barley (half sa') together fulfill the obligation because the total quantity meets the requirement. The proof is that the goal (hصول المقصود) is what matters, and the genus is unified (الجنس متحد).

Key Terms
جنس (jins): The type or category of food items; here, wheat, dates, and barley are considered one genus because they all serve the same purpose of feeding.
