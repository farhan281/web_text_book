Lineage of a Widow's Child: The Two-Year Rule vs. Imam Zufar
Ruling on establishing lineage for a widow's child with Imam Zufar's objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the maximum period for establishing lineage of a widow's child and evaluate Imam Zufar's objection.

Core Idea
A widow's child is attributed to the deceased husband within two years: the Shariah recognizes a possible maximum pregnancy.

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi dispute: the maximum period after the husband's death within which a child's lineage can be established to him. The majority position holds a two-year window, while Imam Zufar restricts it to the 'iddah period plus six months. Understanding the reasoning behind both views is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi family law.

Guided Path
1
State the general Hanafi ruling
Identify the act: establishing the lineage of a child born to a widow. State the general Hanafi position: the child's lineage is established if born within two years of the husband's death.
2
Describe Imam Zufar's dissenting view
Imam Zufar holds that lineage is only established if the child is born within ten months and ten days from death (iddah of four months ten days plus six months). Explain his reasoning: the Shariah fixed the iddah; after its expiry without apparent pregnancy, the iddah is complete, and conception after that is uncertain.
3
Analyze the Hanafi majority response
The majority argues that there is another method to end the iddah: delivery (Quran 65:4). Thus, the possibility of pregnancy continuing up to two years is recognized. The widow is not like a minor girl, because an adult woman can be pregnant.
4
Identify the mufta bihi and its implications
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the two-year rule. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah. The practical implication is that a widow's child born up to two years after death is considered the deceased's child for inheritance and lineage.

Commentary
This passage addresses the establishment of lineage for a child born to a widow (a woman whose husband has died). The general Hanafi ruling is that the child's lineage is attributed to the deceased husband if born within two years of his death. However, Imam Zufar presents a dissenting opinion. He argues that if the child is born after the completion of the widow's 'iddah (four months and ten days) plus six months (i.e., after a total of ten months and ten days from death), the lineage should not be established. His reasoning is that the Shariah has fixed the 'iddah period for a widow as four months and ten days (based on Quran 2:234). Once that period expires without evidence of pregnancy (since the pregnancy was not apparent), the Shariah's ruling is that the 'iddah is completed, just as if the woman had acknowledged it. Therefore, any child born after that point cannot be attributed to the deceased husband. If the child is born within six months of the expiry of the 'iddah, then it can be established because conception must have occurred before the end of the 'iddah. But if born after six months, there is a possibility of conception after the 'iddah, so lineage is not established. The passage then responds to Imam Zufar by noting that for a widow, there is an alternative method for the expiration of 'iddah: delivery of the child (Quran 65:4). If she delivers a child, then the 'iddah ends with delivery, even if less than four months ten days have passed (though this is not the case here since we are considering after full 'iddah). The text explains that the widow's case is different from the minor girl's case because for a minor, pregnancy is not originally possible (she is not a vessel for pregnancy before puberty), so her 'iddah is purely determined by months. For an adult widow, pregnancy is possible, so the 'iddah can end either by months or by delivery. The passage also clarifies that if the widow is a minor herself (below puberty but cohabited), different rules apply: then the lineage is established only if the child is born within ten months and ten days from death, according to the two parties (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), while Abu Yusuf extends it to two years. This lesson focuses on the widow who is not a minor.

Key Terms
متوفی عنہا زوجہا (mutawaffa 'anha zawjuha): A woman whose husband has died, i.e., a widow.
وضع حمل (wad' al-haml): Delivery of the child; it is one way the 'iddah can end for a pregnant woman.
عدت وفات (iddat al-wafat): The waiting period of a widow after her husband's death, normally four months and ten days.
صغیرہ (saghirah): A minor girl, who has not reached puberty.

Istihsan over Qiyas in Inheritance: The Boy's Mother as Wife
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheritance via istihsan vs qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of istihsan to override qiyas in inheritance cases and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a son does not imply marriage by qiyas, but custom makes it so by istihsan.

A man declares a boy to be his son, then dies. The boy's mother appears, claiming to be the man's wife. Does she inherit? Strict analogical reasoning (qiyas) says no, because lineage can be established without marriage. But the Hanafi school, applying juristic preference (istihsan), says yes, based on custom. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
A man publicly acknowledges a boy as his son, then dies. The boy's mother, a free woman known as the mother, claims she was the man's wife.
No marriage witnesses or documents exist.
2
State the qiyas ruling and its basis
Qiyas argues: lineage can be established by valid marriage, invalid marriage, doubtful intercourse, or ownership. Since the acknowledgment does not specify the means, it does not imply marriage. Thus the mother does not inherit.
This is a strict analogy from the principle that lineage is independent of marriage.
3
State the istihsan ruling and its basis
Istihsan (preferred by Imam Muhammad in al-Nawadir) holds: in this context, custom dictates that the only proper way for a free, known mother to have a son is through a valid marriage. Hence the acknowledgment implies marriage, and she inherits.
Custom (urf) overrides the general qiyas here.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under qiyas, the woman gets nothing; under istihsan, she gets her prescribed share as wife. Both the mother and son inherit.
The son's inheritance is uncontested.
5
Note the authority and reliance
Imam Muhammad's view in al-Nawadir (a nawadir text) is the mufta bihi. Later Hanafi masters prefer istihsan over qiyas in this case.
This shows the weight of istihsan in Hanafi methodology.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a case where a man declares a boy to be his son, then dies. The boy's mother comes and claims to be the man's wife. The ruling according to istihsan (preferred by Imam Muhammad in al-Nawadir) is that the woman is considered his wife and the boy his son, and both inherit. The qiyas (analogical reasoning) would deny inheritance to the woman because lineage can be established through various means (valid marriage, invalid marriage, doubtful intercourse, ownership), so acknowledging the boy as a son does not necessarily imply a valid marriage with the mother. The istihsan basis is that in this scenario, the woman is known to be free and the boy's mother, and customarily, a valid marriage is the only proper means for establishing lineage. Therefore, her claim is accepted, and she is treated as a wife. The passage then raises an objection: even under istihsan, the marriage should be established only by implication (iqtida) and only to the extent needed—which is to establish lineage—so the woman's inheritance should not follow. The text is cut off, but the response likely argues that the need extends to inheritance because the woman would be left without support if denied. This is a classic example of istihsan overriding qiyas in Hanafi methodology.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): A juristic preference over qiyas based on custom, necessity, or public good.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning from a known case to a new case based on a common effective cause (illah).
