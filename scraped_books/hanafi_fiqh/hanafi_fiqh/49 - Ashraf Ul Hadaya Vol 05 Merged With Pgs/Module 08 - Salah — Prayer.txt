Rulings of Mourning (Hadad) for a Widow
Expanding on the definition, proof, and exceptions of mourning for a widow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of hadad, apply the Hanafi proof of its obligation, and identify valid exceptions for necessity.

Core Idea
A widow in 'iddah must abstain from all adornment and perfume — mourning is wajib, not optional.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah clarifies the compulsory nature of mourning (hadad) for a woman whose husband has died. The author defines hadad, provides two legal reasons for its obligation, cites the primary hadith evidence, and explains the exceptions allowed due to genuine necessity. Understanding these rules helps you correctly apply the obligations and permissions during the 'iddah period.

Guided Path
1
Define hadad and identify its ruling
What is the legal definition of hadad/ihadad? What level of obligation does it carry (fard, wajib, etc.) and why? According to the Hanafi school, it is wajib because the evidence is ahad hadith.
The author defines it as abandoning perfume, adornment, kohl, and oil except for an excuse.
2
Analyze the two legal reasons (illah)
The author gives two reasons: expressing grief over the loss of the marriage blessing, and preventing temptation since these items incite desire while she is prohibited from remarriage.
The second reason shows the ruling is preventative.
3
Examine the primary hadith evidence
The hadith of Umm Salamah where the Prophet (ﷺ) forbade the widow from using kohl. How does this prohibition indicate obligation? In Hanafi usul, a prohibition (nahi) indicates that the act is prohibited, and here it is understood as a command to abandon, making avoidance wajib.
The explicit prohibition (نھی) is key.
4
Identify the exceptions for necessity
When is kohl or oil permitted? The text mentions 'udhr (excuse) such as eye pain or fear of illness. What distinguishes medicinal use from adornment? The exception is only for genuine necessity (darurah) or predominant likelihood of harm.
The purpose must be treatment, not beautification.
5
Apply the ruling to other items
The prohibition extends to henna, dyed clothes, and silk. Are there any other exceptions? How does the ruling apply to modern equivalents like makeup? The principle is: avoid anything that constitutes adornment or fragrance.
Silk is allowed if needed due to skin condition, etc.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion_probe on the ruling of mourning (hadad) for a widow in 'iddah. The author defines hadad/ihadad as abstaining from perfume, adornment, kohl, and oil (whether scented or not) except for a valid excuse, specifying in al-Jami' al-Saghir 'except due to pain'. Two legal reasons ('illah) are given: (1) expressing grief (ta'assuf) over the loss of the marriage blessing, and (2) preventing temptation since these things incite desire while she is prohibited from remarriage until the 'iddah ends. The primary proof is the hadith where the Prophet (ﷺ) refused permission for a widow to use kohl. The prohibition extends to oil because it contains fragrance and adorns the hair, similar to the prohibition on a pilgrim in ihram. The exception (al-'udhr) is for necessity, such as medicinal use, not adornment. If a woman habitually used oil and genuinely fears illness from abandoning it, the predominant likelihood is considered as actual, and she may use it. Similarly, silk is allowed if needed. The prohibition also includes henna dye and clothes dyed with safflower (usfur) or saffron due to fragrance. The author cites a hadith from Umm Salamah to reinforce these restrictions. The overall ruling is that mourning is wajib (obligatory) for a woman whose husband dies, and these prohibitions are part of fulfilling that obligation.

Key Terms
حداد (hadad): Mourning by a woman in 'iddah upon her husband's death, involving abstention from perfume, adornment, kohl, and oil.
إحداد (ihdad): Alternate term for hadad (mourning), same meaning.
معتدة (mu'taddah): A woman who is observing the waiting period ('iddah) after her husband's death or divorce.
عذر ('udhr): A valid excuse (such as illness or necessity) that allows a woman in mourning to use otherwise prohibited items like kohl or oil for medicinal purposes.

Who Is Obligated to Observe Mourning (Hadad)?
Stating the ruling that a disbeliever, minor, and slave woman are not obligated to mourn except with exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the obligation of mourning (hadad) for a disbeliever, a minor, and a slave woman, and explain the reasoning (ta'lil) for each case.

Core Idea
Mourning is wajib only on a mukallafah; disbeliever and minor are exempt; slave woman mourns but may exit if master's right trumps.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn clarifies three categories of women regarding the obligation of mourning (hadad). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the rules of 'iddah in Hanafi fiqh, especially the nuanced treatment of the slave woman whose rights and obligations intersect with those of her master.

Commentary
This is a ruling_statement from al-Quduri's text, explained by the commentator. The passage addresses the category of women upon whom mourning (hadad) is not obligatory. Two categories are exempt: (1) a disbelieving woman (kafirah) because she is not obligated to follow Shari'ah injunctions; (2) a minor girl (saghirah) because she is not legally responsible (mukallafah). The third category is a slave woman (amah): she is obligated to observe mourning because she is addressed by the Shari'ah in matters that do not conflict with her master's rights. However, the commentator distinguishes between prohibitions that do not harm the master's right (like avoiding perfume) and those that do (like being forbidden to leave the house). The latter is not required because it would nullify the master's right, and the right of a slave (or the master's interest) takes precedence due to need. In summary, mourning is wajib on the slave woman as well, but her mourning does not include the prohibition of leaving the house if the master needs her services.

Key Terms
كافرة (kafirah): A woman who does not believe in Islam; she is not obligated to perform Shari'ah acts of worship including 'iddah mourning.
صغيرة (saghirah): A minor girl who has not reached puberty; the divine address (taklif) is removed from her, so no obligation.
أمة (amah): A female slave; she is obligated to observe mourning because she is mukallafah, but her mourning is limited to avoid conflicting with her master's rights.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah provides the ruling on hiring one's wife or a woman in her iddah to breastfeed her own child for wages. Initially, the woman may be excused due to possible inability to breastfeed. However, when she demands wages and attempts to breastfeed, her ability becomes evident, and the obligation becomes binding, so she cannot take wages. The text then distinguishes between revocable divorce (talaq raj'i) and irrevocable divorce (talaq bain). For a revocable divorced woman in her iddah, there is consensus (bi riwayatin wahidah) that hiring is impermissible because the marriage is still intact. For an irrevocably divorced woman, there are two narrations: one prohibits hiring because some effects of marriage remain (e.g., iddah, nafaqah, and housing), and the other permits it because the marriage is fully dissolved. The commentary emphasizes the underlying reasoning (ta'lil) for each position, showing how the partial survival of marriage affects the ruling. The Hanafi school relies on the first narration (prohibition) for the revocable case, while for the bain case, the mufta bihi is the permissibility according to the second narration, as the text indicates 'jai'za hai' (permissible).

Maintenance Despite Religious Difference: Exceptions for Close Relatives
States the general rule of nafaqah with religious difference and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule on maintenance with religious difference, identify the exceptions (wife, parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren), and explain the proofs for each exception, including the harbi limitation.

Core Idea
Maintenance for close relatives is wajib despite religious difference, but not for harbis.

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses a critical question: does disbelief negate the obligation of financial maintenance (nafaqah)? The general principle is that maintenance is not owed to non-Muslims, but exceptions are carved out for those who are part of the individual's being through contract or partiality. We will explore these exceptions and their proofs, including the limitation for enemies at war.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Identify the opening statement: 'Maintenance is not obligatory with difference of religion except for...' This sets the baseline that religious difference normally negates the obligation.
The rule is not absolute; it has specific exceptions.
2
List the excepted relatives
Note the categories: wife, parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren. These are the only ones for whom maintenance remains wajib despite difference in faith.
The wife is listed separately due to a different basis (contract) vs. partiality for blood relatives.
3
Analyze the proof for the wife
The text argues that the wife's maintenance is due to the marriage contract, which obligates her confinement for the husband's benefit. This obligation does not depend on shared religion.
The wife's nafaqah is a contractual right, not a charitable one.
4
Analyze the proof for ascendants and descendants
For blood relatives, the concept of 'partiality' (juz'iyyah) is invoked: they are considered parts of the individual. Just as one must maintain oneself regardless of one's own disbelief, so too must one maintain one's parts.
This is a qiyas argument: asl (self) → far' (parts) → illa (oneness of being).
5
Identify the harbi exception and its proof
The obligation is lifted if the non-Muslim relative is a harbi (enemy combatant). The proof is Quran 60:8-9, which forbids kindness to those who fight Muslims in religion.
Even if the harbi enters Dar al-Islam with aman, maintenance is not wajib.

Sibling Maintenance and Emancipation Across Religions
Compares sibling maintenance and emancipation across religions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that sibling maintenance is not obligatory across religions and explain the distinction from emancipation using the concepts of original cause (al-illah al-asliyyah) and confirming cause (al-illah al-mu'akkidah).

Core Idea
Maintenance requires shared religion; emancipation requires only kinship.

This passage tackles a fine-grained issue: why does Islamic law obligate emancipation when a Muslim owns a non-Muslim relative, but not obligate maintenance between siblings of different religions? The Hanafi school resolves this by analyzing the severity of the harm and the nature of the cause. Understanding this distinction sharpens one's grasp of Hanafi usul and the role of confirming causes (al-illah al-mu'akkidah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the opening line: it states that maintenance is not obligatory between a Muslim and a Christian sibling. Then note the exception: if a Muslim purchases a Christian sibling, the slave is freed immediately. Hold these two rulings in mind.
2
Locate the textual proof for maintenance
The passage cites the Quranic verse 'wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (2:233) to link maintenance to inheritance. Since inheritance requires shared religion in Hanafi law, maintenance is negated when religions differ.
This is a dalil naqli with zanni dalala.
3
Locate the hadith proof for emancipation
The hadith 'Man malaka dha rahim mahram minhu 'ataqa 'alayhi' establishes emancipation based solely on kinship and mahramiyyah, without any condition of shared religion.
The hadith is a sunnah ahad but accepted in this issue.
4
Analyze the qiyas comparison
The author draws a qiyas: the original case (asl) is emancipation, where the cause (illah) is mere kinship. The new case (far') is maintenance, where the cause is kinship plus shared religion (a confirming cause). The absence of shared religion in the far' prevents the ruling.
The qiyas is khafi (hidden analogy) and uses tahqiq al-manat.
5
Understand the difference in causes
Permanent ownership of a relative is a graver harm (greater severance) than deprivation of maintenance. Therefore, the stronger cause (kinship alone) suffices for emancipation. For maintenance, a lesser harm, a confirming cause (shared religion) is needed.
This is the rational basis (talil) for the distinction.

This passage clarifies two related rulings. Since inheritance does not occur between a Muslim and a non-Muslim (dhimmi or harbi), maintenance is not due between them. This is because the hadith 'Whoever owns a mahram relative' establishes freedom based on kinship and mahramiyyah alone, without requiring shared religion. The author explains the underlying rationale: severing ties by perpetual ownership is a greater harm than merely depriving of maintenance, so the stronger cause (kinship alone) suffices for the graver harm, while the lesser harm requires a confirming cause (kinship plus shared religion). Second, the passage states that no one else shares with the child (son or daughter) in the obligation to maintain parents. The parents have a direct claim on the child's wealth (as seen in inheritance and maintenance texts), and the child is the closest relative to them. This obligation applies equally to sons and daughters according to the apparent narration, which is preferred.

Parents' Maintenance: The Child's Exclusive Obligation
States the exclusive obligation of children for parents' maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that only the child is responsible for parents' maintenance, and that this obligation applies equally to sons and daughters.

Core Idea
Parents' maintenance is solely the child's duty, shared equally among children.

This passage concludes with a distinct ruling: when it comes to parents, no one else shares the obligation with the child. This includes both sons and daughters, and the child's priority over others is grounded in the parents' special connection to the child's wealth by textual proof. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi hierarchy of maintenance obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states: 'No one will share with the child in the maintenance of his parents.' This means the full burden falls on the child, not on other relatives like siblings or uncles.
2
Understand the reasoning
Two reasons are given: (1) Parents have a special connection (ta'wil) to the child's wealth by textual proof (nass), e.g., inheritance and maintenance verses. (2) The child is the closest person to the parents, so he is most entitled to be liable.
The term 'ta'wil' here means a legal claim or interpretation that gives them access.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi position on equality
The text says this obligation is upon males and females equally in the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah), and that this is correct because the meaning (al-ma'na) includes both.
This is the mufta bihi position; some earlier scholars might have differed.

Maintenance of Slaves and Animals in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on maintenance of slaves and animals with proofs and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on nafaqah for slaves and animals, identify the proof from hadith, distinguish the legal capacity of slaves vs. animals, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding forced sale.

Core Idea
A slave's maintenance does not become a debt; if the master cannot provide, the slave must be sold to preserve both rights.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes the obligation of a master to provide maintenance (nafaqah) to his slaves, based on the Prophet's command. It differentiates between slaves and wives (whose nafaqah becomes a debt) and between slaves and animals (which lack legal entitlement). It also records an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf held the master should be forced to provide directly, but the relied-upon view is to force sale.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove obligation? The imperative verbs 'اطعموهم' and 'البسوهم' indicate wujub per Hanafi usul (al-amr yufidu al-wujub). The context shows it refers to the genus, not exact quality.
The master is not required to match his own consumption level exactly.
4
Note the exceptions and distinctions
The passage contrasts slaves with wives (whose nafaqah becomes a debt) and animals (who lack istihqaq). Understand why the remedy differs: sale vs. deferment vs. moral duty.
Wife's nafaqah can be delayed; animal nafaqah is not judicially enforceable.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf argues the master should be forced to provide directly, but the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) say force sale. The text prefers the majority: 'والاصح ماقلنا'.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Yusuf, master bears direct expense; under the majority, he can sell to avoid hardship.

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah commentary deals with the rulings of maintenance (nafaqah) for slaves and animals. It opens with an Arabic matn stating the obligation on the master to provide for his slaves, supported by the hadith commanding kind treatment. The master must feed and clothe his slaves, but the level is not required to be equal to his own. If the master withholds maintenance, the slave may earn for himself if capable; if not (e.g., due to chronic illness or inability to find work), the master is forced to sell the slave so that the proceeds can sustain the slave while the master recoups his value. This contrasts with the wife's maintenance, which becomes a debt on the husband and can be delayed, and with animals, which lack legal entitlement (istihqaq) and therefore no judicial compulsion is applied. However, owners are still sinning if they neglect animals, as evidenced by the hadith of the woman who imprisoned a cat. The passage also notes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Yusuf holds that the master should be forced to provide directly, but the relied-upon view (the correct one per the text) is to force sale, preserving both rights through substitution.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance or financial support including food, clothing, and shelter; legally required for certain dependents.
مملوک (mamluk): A slave owned by another person; both male (ghulam) and female (jariyah) are included.
استحقاق (istihqaq): Legal entitlement to a right; having the capacity to claim something.
خلیفہ (khalifah): Substitute or replacement; here refers to the price received for selling the slave, which substitutes the ownership.
قرضہ (qardah): Debt; a financial obligation that remains on the liable person and can be deferred.

Locate the dalil
Find the hadith quoted: 'Any Muslim who emancipates a believing person...' Recognize it as a hadith ahad (individually transmitted).
The hadith appears in various collections; the commentator cites al-Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Examine how the hadith indicates recommendation. The promise of reward encourages the act, but there is no command or threat for omission, so it falls under mandub in Hanafi usul (al-amr yufidu al-nadb).
The Hanafi principle: a command that does not include an explicit obligation yields recommendation.
4
Note the practical recommendation
Scholars recommend that a man free a male slave and a woman free a female slave to maximize the matching of limbs in the promised reward.
This is a furu' (branch) derived from the main ruling, enhancing the reward.
5
Distinguish from other schools
While most schools agree on recommendation, some may consider emancipation as mustahabb or even wajib in specific contexts. The Hanafi school consistently holds it as mandub.
This ikhtilaf is a mercy; each school has its proofs.

Four Conditions for Valid Emancipation
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for valid emancipation (itq) according to the Hanafi school, explain the rationale for each, and distinguish between the Hanafi and Maliki positions on emancipating another's slave.

Core Idea
Emancipation requires full ownership: a slave cannot free another.

Emancipation (itq) is a significant legal act that completely changes a slave's status. The Hanafi school specifies four conditions that must be met for the emancipation to be valid. This lesson unpacks each condition, the textual evidence from the Prophetic hadith, and a key nuance: that emancipating another's slave is not void but suspended (mawquf). We will also address the minority view of Imam Malik.

Guided Path
1
List the four conditions
From the matn of al-Quduri, identify the four prerequisites: the emancipator must be free, adult, sane, and own the slave.
These are cumulative – all must be present for a valid emancipation.
2
Understand the rationale for each condition
Explain why a slave cannot free another (lack of ownership capacity), why a minor is excluded (emancipation is harmful to the minor's estate), why an insane person is excluded (no capacity for legal disposition), and why ownership is essential (emancipation transfers full freedom, which requires full dominium).
The harm (darar) principle is central for the minor condition.
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The hadith 'There is no emancipation for what a person does not own' is the primary evidence. Analyze how the negation indicates general invalidity and how the phrase 'what a person does not own' covers both no ownership and ownership by another.
This hadith is ahad, so it is zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala in the Hanafi view.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
Compare the Hanafi position (emancipation of another's slave is sahih mawquf) with Imam Malik's view (a father can free his minor child's slave). Identify the basis of each and the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).
The Hanafi response rests on the hadith and the distinction between guardianship (wilayah) and ownership (milk).
5
Note the Hanafi nuance: sahih vs. nafidh
Understand that an emancipation of a non-owned slave is valid (sahih) in itself but does not take effect (nafidh) without the owner's permission. This distinguishes it from a batil (void) act.
This nuance is from the author of Hidayah and reflects Hanafi istihsan.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and includes a minor inter-madhhab dispute. It states the four conditions for a valid emancipation (itaq) according to Imam Quduri's matn: the emancipator must be (1) free (not a slave), (2) adult (baligh), (3) sane (aqil), and (4) must own the slave (fi milkihi). The commentator (Ashraf al-Hidayah) expands on each condition. 

1. Freedom: A slave cannot emancipate another because a slave has no legal ownership capacity. This is based on the principle that emancipation is effective only in property, and a slave has no property.

2. Adulthood: A minor (sabi) is not qualified because emancipation is inherently a harmful act (the guardian loses the slave's value), and minors are not permitted to cause such harm. Therefore, even a guardian (wali) cannot emancipate a slave on behalf of a minor.

3. Sanity: An insane person lacks legal capacity (ahliyyat al-ada). Hence, if an adult claims he was insane at the time of emancipation, his statement is accepted because it attributes the act to a state incompatible with valid disposition.

4. Ownership: The slave must belong to the emancipator. This is proven by the hadith: 'There is no emancipation for what a person does not own.' 

The passage discusses subsidiary cases. If an adult says he emancipated while he was a child, his statement is accepted and the emancipation is void, because he attributes it to a time when he lacked capacity. Similarly for insanity. If a child declares 'every slave I own is free when I become adult', it is invalid because the child's statement is not binding (laysa bi-ahl li-qawl mulzim). 

The commentator also discusses a dispute with Imam Malik. The majority (jumhur), including Hanafis, hold that one cannot emancipate another's slave. Imam Malik held that a father can emancipate the slave of his minor child, but not the slave of his adult child. The Hanafi proof is the hadith. However, the author of Hidayah makes a nuanced point: if someone emancipates another's slave, the emancipation is 'sahih' (valid in itself) but 'la yanfudh' (does not take effect) without the owner's permission. This means the emancipation is legally sound (the words are effective), but its consequence (the slave becoming free) is suspended upon the owner's approval. If the owner permits, the emancipation becomes effective. This subtlety is important: the Hanafi position is not that the act is void, but that it is suspended. This aligns with the Hanafi principle of istihsan and the possibility of subsequent ratification.

مَوْقُوف (mawqūf): Suspended; a legal act whose effect is contingent upon the permission of the rightful owner.
مُلْك (milk): Ownership; the right of full legal dominium over property.
