The Divisibility of Tadbir: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tadbir divisibility and its consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on tadbir divisibility and apply the legal consequences for co-owners including options, liability, and wala' distribution.

Core Idea
Tadbir is divisible according to Abu Hanifa, giving co-owners options; the Sahibayn see it as indivisible, leading to full compensation.

When one co-owner declares a slave mudabbar (freedom upon death), does it affect only his share or the entire slave? This passage from the Hidayah commentary unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on the divisibility of tadbir. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi law balances property rights and contractual intent, providing a rich example of legal reasoning through analogy (qiyas) and the assignment of options to protect co-owners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The core question: when two people co-own a slave and one declares tadbir (freedom upon the master's death), does the declaration affect only the declarant's share or the entire slave? This determines the legal status of the other co-owner's share and their available remedies.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that tadbir is divisible (mutajazzi). His proof: tadbir is a branch (shu'ba) of itq (manumission), and itq is divisible. Therefore, the declaration only applies to the declarant's own share; the other co-owner's share remains in his ownership, though 'corrupted' (because the mudabbar blocks sale or gift).
The key analogy: tadbir is to itq as a branch is to a tree – the same ruling on divisibility applies.
3
State Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold tadbir to be indivisible. The entire slave becomes mudabbar for the first declarant, and subsequent acts by other co-owners are void. Their proof: tadbir, like full manumission, is a single act that cannot be split.
This intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf has practical consequences for compensation and wala'.
4
Analyze the options for co-owners under Abu Hanifa
Under Abu Hanifa, the non-declaring co-owner has six options: (1) make his share mudabbar, (2) free it (itq), (3) make it mukatab, (4) hold the mudabbar liable (daman mu'awada), (5) seek earnings from the slave (istis'a'), or (6) leave it as is. If he frees his share, his right becomes fixed and the other options fall away. The silent partner then has a claim against either the mudabbar or the manumitter, but can only enforce daman mu'awada against the mudabbar.
The choice of option is critical – the student should see how Abu Hanifa's view empowers the co-owner.
5
Understand liability and wala' distribution
Under Abu Hanifa, if the silent partner claims from the mudabbar, the mudabbar can in turn claim one-third of the mudabbar's value from the manumitter (because the manumitter corrupted the mudabbar's share). The wala' is divided proportionally: two-thirds to the mudabbar and one-third to the manumitter. Under the Sahibayn, the first mudabbar compensates his partners for two-thirds of the slave's value (daman tamalluk, independent of wealth), and wala' goes entirely to him.
The difference in liability type (daman mu'awada vs daman tamalluk) and wala' distribution is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage provides the juristic principle (asl) underlying the dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding the divisibility of tadbir (declaration of a slave's freedom upon the master's death). The author of the commentary, expanding on the Hidayah, explains Abu Hanifa's view: tadbir is divisible because it is a branch of itq (manumission), and itq is divisible. Therefore, when one co-owner makes the entire slave mudabbar, he only affects his own share; his act is an ifsad (corruption) of the others' shares, not a valid transfer of their ownership. Consequently, the other co-owners retain ownership of their shares, albeit in a corrupted state—they cannot sell or gift the slave because the mudabbar has blocked those avenues. They have several options: make their own share mudabbar, free it, make it mukatab (contract of manumission for payment), hold the mudabbar liable for the value, seek earnings from the slave (istis'a'), or leave the situation as is. If one of them chooses to free his share, his right becomes fixed, and the other options fall away for that share. The silent partner now has two causes for potential liability: the mudabbar's tadbir and the manumitter's itq. However, he can only claim from the mudabbar, because that claim can be structured as daman mu'awada (compensation for exchange), which is the standard form of liability for acts like ghasp (usurpation). Tadbir allows for transfer from one ownership to another, so daman mu'awada is possible. Manumission, on the other hand, results in the slave becoming free (or mukatab) immediately, making retraction impossible without the slave's consent, so daman mu'awada is not feasible. Therefore, liability falls on the mudabbar. The mudabbar then has the right to claim from the manumitter one-third of the mudabbar's value, because the manumitter corrupted the mudabbar's share. The value of the mudabbar is two-thirds of the qin (pure slave) value. Importantly, the mudabbar does not owe compensation to the silent partner for the value he received from him (through the silent partner's claim), because his ownership of that portion is retrospective and incomplete. The wala' (inheritance clientage) is distributed proportionally to the extent of ownership at the time of manumission: two-thirds to the mudabbar and one-third to the manumitter. According to the two companions, tadbir is indivisible, so the entire slave becomes mudabbar for the first declarant, and he must compensate his partners for their shares with two-thirds of the slave's value. This is daman tamalluk (liability for possession), not daman jinnaya (liability for injury), so it does not depend on the mudabbar's wealth. The wala' goes entirely to the mudabbar. The passage concludes by affirming this as the apparent ruling.

Key Terms
ضمان معاوضة (daman mu'awada): Liability arising from a compensatory exchange, such as compensation for usurpation or destruction of property.
استسعاء (istis'a'): Seeking earnings from a slave; the master allows the slave to work and pay for his freedom.
ولاء (wala'): Clientage; the relationship between a freed slave and his manumitter, entitling the latter to inherit from the former in certain cases.
تدبير (tadbir): A declaration by a master that a slave will be free upon the master's death.
افساد (ifsad): Corruption; in this context, the act of rendering another's share unusable for sale or gift.

The Denied Claim of Umm Walad in Joint Ownership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of a denied umm walad claim in joint ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a jointly-owned female slave claimed as umm walad, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf using the principles of certainty and binding acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Certainty fixes half-service; a binding lineage claim prevents treating the claimant as the actual contriver.

When two co-owners disagree over a female slave—one claiming she is the other's umm walad, the other denying—the Hanafi school splits on the consequence. Imam Abu Hanifa prioritizes certainty of half-service and the irreversibility of lineage acknowledgment, while the Sahibayn apply a qiyas that turns the acknowledgment back on the claimant. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each view and the practical difference in outcome.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi (Abu Hanifa's) position
Identify the exact ruling given by Imam Abu Hanifa regarding the slave's service and any istisʿāʾ.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's opposing view
State what Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold, and note the key difference: istisʿāʾ for half value leads to freedom.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's dalil (qiyas)
Work through the analogy: claimant's acknowledgment turns back on him like a buyer's acknowledgment of prior freeing. Then apply the parallel to umm walad of a Christian who becomes Muslim.
The qiyas treats the acknowledgment as if the claimant himself made her umm walad.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response
Understand why certainty of half-service is decisive, and why the binding nature of lineage prevents treating the claimant as an actual contriver. No istisʿāʾ.
The acknowledgment of nasab (lineage) is lazim (binding) and cannot be undone by rejection.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Compare the practical outcomes: under Abu Hanifa, slave serves denier half-time and is idle half-time; under Sahibayn, denier can force istisʿāʾ leading to freedom. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's.
The mufta bihi is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa as presented in al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal status of a jointly-owned female slave when one co-owner (the 'claimant') asserts that she is the umm walad (mother of a child) of the other co-owner (the 'denier'), but the denier rejects this claim. The core issue is the effect of the denial on the claimant's acknowledgment and the consequent rights of service and potential emancipation. The text first states the position of Imam Abu Hanifa: the slave is to be stopped for one day (serving no one) and serves the denier for one day. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold instead that the denier may, if he wishes, compel the slave to earn half her value (through istisʿāʾ, i.e., working to pay a sum), after which she becomes free and the claimant has no further claim. The Sahibayn's reasoning is analogical: since the claimant's partner did not confirm the acknowledgment, the acknowledgment reverts to the claimant himself, as if he had declared her his own umm walad. They draw an analogy to a case in sales where a buyer's acknowledgment that the seller freed the item pre-sale is treated as the buyer's own freeing when the seller denies. Therefore, the claimant cannot take service from her, and the denier's share remains in his ownership, requiring istisʿāʾ for emancipation, similar to a Christian's umm walad who becomes Muslim and must earn her freedom. Abu Hanifa's counter-argument is based on certainty: if the claimant were truthful, the entire service would go to the denier (since she would be his umm walad); if false, the denier gets half service (as co-owner). Half is certain, so only that is established. The claimant's share produces no service because his own acknowledgment of umm walad (implying lineage) is a binding statement that cannot be undone by the partner's rejection; hence he cannot be treated as a true contriver of umm walad. Thus no istisʿāʾ is imposed on the slave from him.

Key Terms
ام ولد (umm walad): A female slave who bears a child to her master, thereby becoming entitled to freedom upon the master's death.
استسعاء (istisʿāʾ): A procedure where a slave is required to work to earn a specified sum to pay for their freedom.
اقرار (iqrār): Acknowledgment or confession of a right or status, here specifically the claimant's declaration that the slave is the denier's umm walad.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on a Jointly-Owned Slave-Girl Claimed as Umm Walad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on rights over a jointly-owned slave-girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on this dispute, explain their proofs, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Core Idea
A claimant who asserts umm walad status for a shared slave-girl loses all rights; the denier gets half service or may enforce earning.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute where two co-owners hold a slave-girl jointly. One co-owner claims she is the umm walad (mother of the master's child) of the other co-owner. The other denies. How should the slave-girl be treated? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples offer different solutions based on their analysis of certainty, property rights, and the binding nature of acknowledging lineage (nasab).

Guided Path
1
Identify the core factual scenario
Two people jointly own a slave-girl. One (the claimant) declares that she is the umm walad of the other (the denier). The denier denies this. Determine what rights and services are in dispute.
The claimant's declaration is not an admission against himself; it asserts a status that would free her after the denier's death.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa rules that the slave-girl serves the denier one day and rests the next (no service to the claimant). She is not compelled to earn money.
His reasoning: half service is certain for the denier (full if claimant truthful, half if false). The claimant's claim includes lineage acknowledgment, which is irrevocable, so he cannot be treated as owner.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the denier may force the slave-girl to earn half her price. When she pays it, she becomes free, and the claimant has no further right over her.
Their qiyas: the claimant's unconfirmed claim reverts to him, like a buyer who claims the seller freed the slave before sale; here the claimant is deemed to have made her his own umm walad.
4
Analyze the proofs (adilla) of each side
Abu Hanifa: The denier is entitled to half service as a certainty. The claimant's iqrar bi al-nasab is legally irreversible; he cannot simultaneously be treated as an owner. Sahibayn: The claimant's claim returns to himself; he cannot take service. The denier's share remains his property, so he can enforce earning (takhrij) to recover his half.
The key difference: Abu Hanifa focuses on certainty of half service; the Sahibayn focus on the return of the claim to the claimant.
5
Distill the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave-girl serves the denier half-time and never works for the claimant. Under the Sahibayn, the denier can compel her to earn her half price, eventually freeing her and extinguishing the claimant's interest.
This practical difference affects the slave-girl's workload, her path to freedom, and the claimant's potential rights.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (Abu Hanifa vs. the Two Companions) regarding a jointly-owned slave-girl when one co-owner (the claimant) asserts she is the umm walad (mother of a child) of the other co-owner (the denier), but the denier rejects this. Abu Hanifa's ruling: The slave-girl should pause one day (serve neither) and serve the denier the next day. She must not be made to earn; instead, the sharing of services respects the certainty of half-ownership. The Sahibayn rule: The denier may make her earn half her price through labour, and when that price is paid, she becomes free and the claimant has no claim. The Sahibayn argue by qiyas: if the claimant's statement is not confirmed by the other co-owner, his claim reverts to himself, like a buyer who claims the seller freed the slave before sale — the freedom is attributed to the buyer, not the seller. Thus here, the claimant is treated as if he made her his own umm walad, so he cannot take service; the denier's share remains his property, so she must earn to be free. Abu Hanifa counters: If the claimant were truthful, the denier would get full service; if false, half service. Half service is certain, so she serves the denier half-time. The claimant cannot take service because he simultaneously claims she is the denier's umm walad and demands compensation (tawān) from the denier. Additionally, claiming umm walad implies claiming the child's lineage (nasab), which is a binding matter not voidable by denial. Thus the claimant cannot be analogized to someone who actually makes her his umm walad. The commentary concludes by affirming Abu Hanifa's position.

Freeing Jointly-Owned Umm Walad: Guarantee Required?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on guarantee for freeing joint umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the guarantee for freeing a jointly-owned umm walad and explain the disagreement over whether the umm walad is mutaqawwim.

Core Idea
Whether a co-owner must pay for freeing a joint umm walad hinges on whether she is mutaqawwim or not.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about a jointly-owned umm walad freed by one wealthy co-owner. The core question is whether the freeing co-owner must compensate the other for his share. Imam Abu Hanifa rules no compensation is due, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require payment of half her value. The disagreement stems from differing views on whether an umm walad is considered legally valuable (mutaqawwim) for the purpose of compensation.

Commentary
This passage presents another intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a jointly-owned umm walad (a slave-girl who has borne a child to her master). Here, both co-owners have acknowledged the child, so she is the umm walad of both. Then one co-owner, who is wealthy, frees her without the consent of the other. The ruling: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the freeing co-owner does not owe any guarantee (damān) for the other's share. According to the Two Companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), he must guarantee half her value. The difference hinges on whether the umm walad's financial value (māliyya) is considered legally exchangeable (mutaqawwim) or not. Abu Hanifa holds that her value is not a basis for monetary valuation (ghayr mutaqawwim) because she is possessed for the purpose of giving birth to her master's child, not for trade. Her 'property' status serves only to facilitate the lineage of the child. Thus, when freed wrongfully, the co-owner does not owe compensation for the value of the share because the value is not established. The Two Companions argue that the umm walad is indeed a property that can be benefited from in multiple ways (intercourse, hiring, service), which indicates her value (taqawwum). The fact that she cannot be sold does not negate her value, as seen in the case of a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) where benefits like hiring and service remain; similarly here, the prohibition of sale does not eliminate value. They further support their view by citing the case of a Christian's umm walad: if she converts to Islam, she is required to earn her freedom (sa'āya), which proves her value. However, her value is reduced to one-third of a full slave-girl because the benefit of sale and post-death earning are lost. Abu Hanifa's counter-argument: The concept of value (taqawwum) arises from the slave being held as property (ihrāz li al-ta'qwīm). The umm walad, however, is held for the sake of lineage (ihrāz li al-nasab), not for value. Value is secondary. Consequently, she does not have to earn for creditors or heirs, unlike a mudabbar. The reason is that the cause (sabab) of her partial freedom (juz'iyya) due to the child is immediately realized, though its effect on ownership is suppressed for the sake of benefit; therefore, the cause actually removes value. In the mudabbar, the cause is deferred to after death, and his sale is prevented because his purpose (freedom at death) is already determined. Thus the two cases differ. As for the Christian's umm walad, the ruling of making her a mukātaba (earning via kitāba) is an exceptional measure to avoid harm to both parties (the Christian owner loses his right and the Muslim umm walad cannot be owned by a non-Muslim). The obligation of the kitāba payment does not require the umm walad to be mutaqawwim; it is a contractual obligation. The passage concludes with a brief Urdu clarification of the scenario.

Guarantee for Co-Owner Freeing Umm Walad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on umm walad property value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a co-owner freeing an umm walad, and explain the underlying difference in assessing her property value (taqawwum).

Core Idea
The umm walad's value hinges on whether she is preserved for lineage or for wealth — causing a split between Abu Hanifa and his students.

This passage delves into a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: when two men co-own an umm walad, and one frees her while wealthy, must he compensate the other? The answer depends on whether the umm walad is considered valuable property (mutaqawwim). Imam Abu Hanifa says no, the Sahibayn say yes. Their disagreement is rooted in different views of her legal purpose — lineage preservation versus beneficial use.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling: the freeing co-owner owes no guarantee. Understand why: the umm walad is not mutaqawwim because she is kept for lineage, not wealth.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
State the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the freeing co-owner must pay half her value. Their reasoning: the umm walad yields benefits (intercourse, hire, service) which are signs of property.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Examine their arguments: benefit indicates value; prohibition of sale does not negate value (analogy with mudabbar); the case of a Christian's umm walad converting and being required to earn supports value.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's rebuttal
Understand Abu Hanifa's distinctions: she is preserved for lineage (ihtiraz li al-nasab), so value is subsidiary. The cause (juz'iyyah) already exists, nullifying value. The Christian umm walad's earning is a special case to prevent harm.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: the freeing co-owner pays nothing (Abu Hanifa) or half the value (Sahibayn). Also, after one co-owner dies, the other gets no earnings from her (Abu Hanifa) or does (Sahibayn).

And the acknowledgment of being umm walad... is a response to the statement of the Sahibayn. The summary of the response is that the acknowledgment of being umm walad includes the acknowledgment of the child's lineage, and this acknowledgment of lineage is a binding matter. Therefore, it cannot be rejected. So there remains no possibility that the acknowledging co-owner is considered the one who made her umm walad, as the Sahibayn have considered. If an umm walad is shared between two men, and one of them frees her while he is wealthy, is there any guarantee upon him or not? The statements of the jurists: And if the umm walad was between them, and one of them freed her while he was wealthy, then there is no guarantee upon him according to Abu Hanifa, and they (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said he guarantees half of her value, because the property status of the umm walad is not considered valuable (ghayr mutaqawwim) according to him, and it is valuable (mutaqawwim) according to them. And upon this principle many issues are built, which we have mentioned in Kifayat al-Muntaha. The evidence for their statement is that benefit is derived from her through intercourse, hiring, and service, and this is the indication of being valuable. And the prohibition of selling her does not negate her value, as in the case of a mudabbar. Do you not see that if the umm walad of a Christian becomes Muslim, then earnings are required upon her, and this is a sign of value. However, her value is one-third of the value of a pure slave girl, as they said, because the benefit of selling and earning after death is lost. Unlike the mudabbar, because only the benefit of selling is lost, but the benefits of earnings and service remain. And Abu Hanifa's evidence is that value (taqawwum) is through preservation (ihtiraz), and she is preserved for lineage, not for value, and preservation for value is subsidiary. Hence she does not earn for a creditor or an heir, unlike the mudabbar. And this is because the cause (sabab) in her case is already present, namely the partial connection (juz'iyyah) established through the child, as known in the prohibition of affinity, except that its effect did not appear in the right of ownership due to the necessity of deriving benefit. So the cause worked in the nullification of value. In the mudabbar, the cause takes effect after death, and the prohibition of selling him is due to the realization of his purpose. So they differ. And in the case of the Christian's umm walad, we judged that she becomes a mukatabah upon him, to remove harm from both sides, and the obligation of the kitabah payment does not require value (taqawwum). The reason for the Sahibayn's statement is that the umm walad is a source of benefit. Thus, it is permissible for the master to have intercourse with his umm walad, similarly to give her on hire, and to take service from her, and these things cannot be without ownership (milk al-yamin), and ownership of the right hand cannot be without valuable property. So it is proven that the umm walad is valuable property. And by the prohibition of her sale, there is a response to the question. The question is that the sale of the umm walad is prohibited, and the prohibition of her sale is evidence of her not being valuable. So it is concluded that the umm walad is not valuable. Answer: The prohibition of her sale does not negate her being valuable property, just as the sale of a mudabbar is prohibited while he is valuable property. So consider that if the umm walad of a Christian becomes Muslim, then it is agreed upon that upon her, earning and paying the value is obligatory, and the obligation of earning upon the umm walad is a sign of her being valuable. However, it is necessary that the value of the umm walad is one-third of the value of a pure slave girl, as the elders said. The evidence is that from her, the benefit of selling and earning after death is lost. Unlike the mudabbar, from whom only the benefit of selling is lost, but the earning and service remain. Thus, after the master's death, he will earn for the master's creditors and serve the master until his death. The evidence of Imam Azam Abu Hanifa is that value is attached to a thing that is kept for wealth in one's possession, while the umm walad is kept only for lineage, not for wealth. That is, keeping her for wealth is not intended, rather lineage is intended. This is the reason that after the master's death, she does not earn for any creditor or heir. Contrary to the mudabbar, because the mudabbar is not kept for lineage, but for wealth. So there is a difference between mudabbar and umm walad. And the reason for the difference is that in the case of the umm walad, the cause (sabab) is present currently, i.e., the relationship of partial connection between the master and the umm walad is established through the child, as has passed in the section on prohibition of affinity. Yes, it is necessary that the effect of this cause (partial connection) did not appear regarding the removal of ownership, because there is still need for the master to derive benefit from the umm walad. That is, if she did not remain in ownership but became free, then enjoyment would be lost. So the cause has had no effect yet. Yes, in the nullification of value, the cause had an effect. Intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs and subsidiary cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on whether an Umm Walad is mal mutaqawwim and explain the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf) in joint ownership cases.

Core Idea
The Umm Walad is valuable property, but only for one-third the price of a regular slave, due to lost benefits of sale and post-death earnings.

The answer determines what happens when co-owners free her: must they pay compensation? This lesson unpacks the proofs on both sides and the practical rulings that follow.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the legal status of an Umm Walad (a slave woman who bears a child to her master). The Sahibayn's argument is that an Umm Walad is a source of benefit: the master can have intercourse with her, hire her out, and take service from her. All these require ownership (mulk yamin), and ownership requires the object to be valuable property. Therefore, she must be valuable property. The text then raises an objection: if she is valuable, why is her sale prohibited? The prohibition of sale (from the master after the child is born) seems to indicate she lacks value. The response is that the prohibition of sale does not negate value, as seen in the case of a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) whose sale is also prohibited yet he is valuable property. The text further supports the view that Umm Walad is valuable by citing that if a Christian's Umm Walad becomes Muslim, she must earn and pay her price by consensus. This earning is a sign of her value. However, a nuance is added: the price of an Umm Walad is only one-third that of an ordinary slave girl. The reason is that two benefits are lost from her: the benefit of sale and the benefit of earning after the master's death (since she becomes free upon his death). In contrast, a mudabbar loses only the benefit of sale; he still must earn for creditors after the master's death and serve until the master's death. Thus, his value remains full. Then Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is presented: price is attached to something kept for wealth, but an Umm Walad is kept primarily for lineage (nasab) through her child, not for wealth. Therefore, after the master's death, she does not work for creditors or heirs, unlike a mudabbar. The distinction is further clarified: in the case of Umm Walad, the cause (sabab) of her eventual freedom—her connection through her child—is already present during the master's life, but its effect on ownership is postponed because the master still needs her services. In contrast, the cause for a mudabbar (the master's death) is not yet present.
