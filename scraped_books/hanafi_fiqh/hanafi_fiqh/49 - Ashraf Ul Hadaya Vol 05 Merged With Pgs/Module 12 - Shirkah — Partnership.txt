Partial Emancipation of a Jointly Owned Slave: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Explaining the intra-Hanafi dispute on freeing a share of a jointly owned slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options of the non-freeing partner according to Abu Hanifa and the two options according to the Sahibayn, and explain the dalil of each position.

Core Idea
A partner's right to free his share or claim compensation does not depend on the emancipator's wealth according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn restrict it.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a jointly owned slave when one partner frees his share. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on the options available to the other partner and the distribution of patronage. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi method of reasoning across qiyas and hadith.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the initial ruling
Identify that one partner frees his share, and that share becomes free. This is agreed upon. Then note the disagreement about the other partner's options.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
List his three options for the partner (free, claim compensation, make slave earn) and the corresponding patronage distribution depending on the partner's choice.
The third option (istis'a') is available even if the emancipator is wealthy.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position
They limit the partner to only compensation if the emancipator is wealthy, or making the slave earn if insolvent. No third option, no recoupment for the emancipator, and patronage solely for the emancipator.
This is more restrictive.
4
Analyze the proofs
Examine the Sahibayn's hadith-based proof and Abu Hanifa's qiyas (garment-dye analogy). Understand why each side interprets the texts differently.
The hadith is seen as a division by Sahibayn; Abu Hanifa sees it as not restricting the third option.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Note the practical consequences: under Abu Hanifa, the partner has more flexibility and patronage can be shared; under the Sahibayn, the partner's options are limited and patronage remains with the emancipator.
This affects financial obligations and inheritance via wala'.

(Issue) A slave owned jointly by two partners: one of them freed his share, so his share becomes free. What happens to the other share? And when the slave is between two partners, one of them frees his share, it becomes free. Then if the emancipator is wealthy, his partner has a choice: if he wishes, he may also free (his share); if he wishes, he may claim compensation from his partner for the value of his share; and if he wishes, he may make the slave earn (to pay for his share). If he claims compensation, the emancipator may recoup from the slave, and the patronage (wala') belongs to the emancipator. If he frees or makes the slave earn, then the patronage is shared between them. If the emancipator is insolvent, the partner has a choice: if he wishes, he may free, or if he wishes, he may make the slave earn, and in both cases, the patronage is shared between them. This is according to Abu Hanifa. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: He (the partner) has no option but to claim compensation if the emancipator is wealthy, and to make the slave earn if the emancipator is insolvent, and the emancipator does not recoup from the slave, and the patronage belongs solely to the emancipator. Translation: If a slave is jointly owned by two partners, and one of them freed his share, then his share becomes free. Then if the emancipator is wealthy, his partner has the option: either he also frees his share, or he takes compensation from his partner for the value of his share, or he makes the slave earn. Then if he took compensation, the emancipator recoups from the slave, and the patronage belongs to the emancipator. If the partner freed his share or made the slave earn, then the patronage is shared between them. If the emancipator is insolvent, the partner has the same option: either free his share or make the slave earn, and in both cases patronage is shared. This is according to Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn said: The partner only has the option to claim compensation when the emancipator is wealthy, and to make the slave earn when insolvent, and the emancipator does not recoup from the slave, and the patronage belongs solely to the emancipator. Explanation: The scenario of the issue is clear, and for the proofs, see the next lines. This issue is based on two principles: first, the divisibility of emancipation or not, as we have explained; second, that the wealth of the emancipator does not prevent the slave from earning (to compensate the partner) according to him (Abu Hanifa), while according to them (Sahibayn) it does prevent it. Their proof is the saying of the Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding a man who frees his share: 'If he is wealthy, he guarantees; if he is poor, he makes (the slave) earn for the other share.' This is a division, and division negates partnership. His (Abu Hanifa's) proof is that the monetary value of the partner's share is retained by the slave, so he can guarantee it, just as if the wind sweeps away a garment of a person and throws it into another's dye so that the garment becomes dyed, then the owner of the garment owes the value of the other's dye, whether the owner is wealthy or poor. The same applies here, except that if the slave is poor, he makes him earn. Then wealth means ease (al-yusr) i.e., he owns wealth enough to pay the value of the other's share, not opulence, because by ease the consideration is balanced from both sides, realizing the emancipator's intention of piety and delivering the due of the silent party.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a jointly owned slave when one partner frees his share. The matn (from Al-Hidayah) states the ruling, and the commentary explains the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). According to Abu Hanifa, whether the emancipator is wealthy or insolvent, the partner has three options: free his own share, claim compensation from the emancipator, or make the slave earn to pay for his share. If compensation is taken, the emancipator can recoup from the slave (as the slave benefited indirectly), and the patronage (wala') goes to the emancipator. If the partner frees or earns, patronage is shared. The Sahibayn limit the partner to only two options: compensation if the emancipator is wealthy, and making the slave earn if insolvent, with no recoupment for the emancipator, and patronage solely for the emancipator. The commentator then gives the foundations: first, the divisibility of emancipation (which both agree is divisible here), and second, whether the emancipator's wealth prevents the option of making the slave earn. The Sahibayn rely on a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) which specifies 'if wealthy, guarantee; if poor, earn'—indicating division and no partnership. Abu Hanifa uses a rational analogy (qiyas): the partner's share's value is retained by the slave, so he can demand compensation from the emancipator (who can then recoup from the slave), similar to a case where wind blows a garment into someone's dye. The commentator then clarifies that 'wealth' here means ability to pay the value (yusr) not general opulence. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as the commentary is from the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
ولاء (wala'): Patronage (clientship) that binds the freed slave to the emancipator; it determines inheritance rights and other obligations.
استسعاء (istis'a'): Making the slave earn money to pay for his freedom or compensation; a method of gradual manumission or settling debts.
ضمان (daman): Guarantee or compensation; here refers to the partner claiming the value of his share from the emancipator.
تجزى الاعتاق (tajazzu al-i'taq): The divisibility of emancipation; the principle that a slave can be partially freed share by share.
