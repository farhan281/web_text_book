Father Selling Absent Son's Movable Property for Nafaqah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on father's sale of absent son's movables for maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Imam Abu Hanifa permits a father to sell his absent adult son's movable property for his own maintenance, and contrast this with the Sahibayn's position.

Core Idea
A father may sell his absent son's movables for his own maintenance by istihsan, but not immovables.

This passage addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: when an adult son is absent and not providing maintenance, may his father sell the son's property to cover his own needs? The answer differs within the school. Imam Abu Hanifa permits the sale of movables as an exceptional ruling based on istihsan, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that no sale is allowed, following strict qiyas. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how Hanafi jurists balance textual authority with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: sale of movables is permissible (ja'iz) by istihsan; sale of immovables is not.
The text states: 'according to Abu Hanifa it is permissible, and this is istihsan'.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
State the position of the Sahibayn: they prohibit the sale of both movables and immovables. This is the ruling by qiyas.
The text says: 'According to the Sahibayn, selling all of these is not permissible, and this is qiyas'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Analyse the Sahibayn's dalil: the son's majority severs the father's guardianship (wilayah), so no authority to sell. Selling for any debt other than maintenance is also prohibited.
They analogize to the case where the son is present: the father cannot sell without permission.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Examine Abu Hanifa's rebuttal: the father retains guardianship of preservation (wilayat al-hifz), analogous to an executor (wasi). Selling movables is preservative; immovables are self-preserved.
Abu Hanifa uses istihsan: the executor analogy and the father's greater compassion override qiyas.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the father sells movables, the sale is valid and he may take his maintenance from the price. According to Sahibayn, the sale is invalid and the buyer must return the property.
This difference directly affects the validity of the transaction and the rights of the parties.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a father's right to sell his absent adult son's movable property for his own maintenance. The author first states the core rule: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, such a sale is permissible (ja'iz) as an exception based on istihsan (juristic preference), but if the father sells immovable property (land or house), it is not permissible. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that selling either movable or immovable is not permissible, and this is the qiyas (analogical ruling). The reasoning of the Sahibayn is that upon the son reaching majority, the father's guardianship (wilayah) over him is severed, so he has no authority to sell his property. Furthermore, the father cannot sell for any debt other than maintenance, and the mother similarly lacks such authority. Imam Abu Hanifa's counter-argument is that the father retains guardianship of preservation (wilayat al-hifz) over the absent son's property. He argues by analogy to an executor (wasi): if an executor can sell the absent heir's property for preservation, the father is even more entitled due to his greater compassion. However, this guardianship is limited to movable property because movables are subject to waste, while immovables are self-preserved. Relatives other than the father have no such guardianship at all. The passage further notes that since the father's right (maintenance) is of the same type as the price, he may take his maintenance from the sale proceeds, just as he may sell a minor child's property for maintenance due to complete guardianship. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated, but the text gives weight to Abu Hanifa's view.

Key Terms
ولایت (wilayah): Guardianship or authority over someone or something, especially legal authority to manage property.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that deviates from qiyas due to a stronger evidence or need, used here to permit the sale of movables for father's maintenance.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Co-owner's Options After Partial Emancipation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on co-owner's options

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on a co-owner's options after partial emancipation, identify the two foundational principles underlying the dispute, and apply the Hanafi tarjih to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa gives the co-owner three options; the Sahibayn restrict to one based on the emancipator's wealth.

When a partner frees his share in a jointly-owned slave, the co-owner acquires certain rights. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the co-owner's options. This lesson dissects the dispute, its proofs, and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is partial emancipation by one co-owner in a jointly-owned slave. The disputed ruling concerns what options the other co-owner has: can he free his own share, take compensation from the emancipator, or make the slave earn?
The dispute also affects wala (patronage) rights.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the co-owner has three options regardless of the emancipator's wealth: free his share, take compensation from the emancipator, or make the slave earn. If compensation is taken, the emancipator can recover from the slave. Wala is shared if the co-owner frees or takes earnings, otherwise belongs to the emancipator.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) restrict the co-owner to only one option based on the emancipator's wealth: if wealthy, only compensation from the emancipator (who cannot recover); if poor, only make the slave earn. Wala belongs solely to the emancipator in both cases.
The Sahibayn rely on a hadith making a clear division.
4
Analyze the two foundational principles
First, tajazzi al-i'taq (divisibility of emancipation): Abu Hanifa holds it divisible, so partial emancipation is valid; Sahibayn hold it is not. Second, yasar al-mu'tiq la yamna'u sa'yata al-'abdi: Abu Hanifa says wealth does not prevent making the slave earn; Sahibayn say it does.
These principles underpin the different rulings.
5
Evaluate the proofs and tarjih
The Sahibayn's proof is a hadith: 'In kana ghaniyan damin, wa in kana faqiran us'iya fi hussat al-akhar.' Abu Hanifa uses qiyas (analogy of cloth blown into dye) and argues the hadith is conditional. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's position as it is more comprehensive and based on istihsan.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah presents both but leans to Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the options available to a co-owner when his partner (the emancipator) frees his share in a jointly-owned slave. The passage first states the ruling according to Imam Abu Hanifa: the co-owner has three options regardless of the emancipator's wealth. He may free his own share, take compensation (the value of his share) from the emancipator, or make the slave work to earn the value of his share. If the co-owner takes compensation, the emancipator can recover that amount from the slave, and the wala (patronage) belongs solely to the emancipator. If the co-owner frees his share or takes earnings, the wala is shared between them. If the emancipator is poor, the co-owner's options are the same except he cannot take compensation from the poor emancipator; he can only free his share or make the slave earn, and wala is shared.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) restrict these options: if the emancipator is wealthy, the co-owner can only take compensation, and the emancipator cannot recover from the slave; if the emancipator is poor, the co-owner can only make the slave earn, and in both cases wala belongs exclusively to the emancipator.

The commentator explains that this issue rests on two foundational principles: first, whether emancipation can be partial (tajazzi al-i'taq) – Abu Hanifa holds it is divisible, the Sahibayn do not; second, whether the emancipator's wealth prevents the slave from being made to earn – Abu Hanifa says it does not, the Sahibayn say it does.

The Sahibayn's proof is the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'If he is wealthy, he is liable; if he is poor, the slave earns for the other's share.' This hadith makes a clear division, and division negates partnership, so the co-owner has only one option depending on the emancipator's wealth. Abu Hanifa's proof is based on the analogy of a cloth blown by wind into another's dye: the owner of the cloth must pay the value of the dye, whether he is wealthy or poor, because the dye is retained with him. Similarly, the value of the co-owner's share is retained with the slave, so the co-owner can demand compensation from the slave (by making him earn). The response to the hadith is that the Prophet's division was stated as a condition (suspended on wealth/poverty), and the absence of the condition does not necessitate the absence of the ruling, i.e., when the condition (poverty) is absent, the slave earning is not necessarily prohibited.

Key Terms
تجزي الاعتاق (tajazzi al-i'taq): The divisibility of emancipation; whether freeing a share of a slave is valid as partial emancipation.
سعاية (sa'yah): The act of a slave earning money through work to pay off his value to the co-owner.
ولاء (wala'): Patronage rights that arise from emancipation, giving the emancipator inheritance rights from the freed slave.
ضمان (daman): Liability to pay compensation; here, the emancipator's obligation to pay the co-owner the value of his share.
يسار تيسير (yasar al-taysir): Sufficient wealth to pay the value of the other's share without hardship.

The Two Principles and the Wealthy Manumitter
Deriving rulings from two principles in joint emancipation (wealthy case).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two principles underlying joint emancipation and derive the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn when the manumitter is wealthy.

Core Idea
Divisibility of emancipation and the ban on earning for the wealthy govern joint emancipation.

This lesson unpacks the Hidayah's treatment of joint emancipation. The rulings hinge on two key principles: whether emancipation can be divided (tajazzi' al-'itq) and whether a wealthy owner's slave can be made to earn (sa'aya). These principles explain why Abu Hanifa and his two students reached different conclusions on compensation, earning, and patronage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two principles
The passage states that the rulings are built on two principles: (1) divisibility of emancipation (tajazzi' al-'itq) and (2) the rule that a wealthy manumitter's slave cannot be made to earn (sa'aya). Recognize that these are the foundational premises.
The Sahibayn reject divisibility; Abu Hanifa accepts it.
2
Apply Sahibayn's view: non-divisibility and no earning for wealthy
According to the Sahibayn, emancipation cannot be divided. When one co-owner frees his share, the entire slave becomes free. Since the manumitter is wealthy, the slave cannot be made to earn to repay him.
The slave's earning is prohibited in the state of wealth (la sa'aya fi hal al-yasar).
3
Derive the rulings on Sahibayn's view
The manumitter must pay compensation (daman) to the silent partner for his share. The manumitter cannot recover this from the slave because the slave is not required to earn. The wala' (patronage) goes to the manumitter because the emancipation originated from him.
The entire slave is freed; the silent partner receives his share value.
4
Apply Abu Hanifa's view: divisibility
According to Abu Hanifa, emancipation can be divided. Thus only the manumitter's share is freed; the silent partner's share remains his property.
The silent partner may choose to free his share or claim compensation.
5
Derive the rulings on Abu Hanifa's view
The silent partner may demand compensation from the manumitter for spoiling his share. The manumitter, having paid the compensation, stands in the partner's place and may make the slave earn to recover that amount. The wala' is for the manumitter because he becomes the owner of the entire slave by paying compensation.
The manumitter can also free the rest of the slave if he wishes.

ترجمہ پھر صاحبین کے قول پر تخریج (حکم نکالنا ) ظاہر ہے۔ پس آزاد کرنے والے کا غلام سے مال عمان واپس نہ لینا اسلئے ہے کہ خوشحال کی حالت میں غلام پر سعایہ واجب نہیں ہے اور ولاء آزاد کرنے والے کے واسطے ہے۔ کیونکہ پوری آزادی آزاد کرنے والے کی طرف سے ہے۔ اسلئے کہ (آزادی) متجری نہیں ہوتی اور رہا امام صاحب کے قول پر تخریج کرنا، سو (شریک کو اپنا حصہ ) آزاد کرنے کا اختیار ہے۔ کیونکہ باقی میں اس کی ملک قائم ہے۔ کیونکہ امام صاحب کے نزدیک اعتناق کے ٹکڑے ہو سکتے ہیں اور تاوان لینے کا ( حق ) اسلئے ہے کہ آزاد کرنے والا (اپنے) شریک کے حصہ کو خراب کرنے کی وجہ سے اس پر ظلم کرنے والا ہے۔ چنانچہ اس پر سوائے آزاد کرنے اور اس کے توابع اور کمائی کرا لینے کے بیچنا اور ہبہ کرنا وغیرہ ممتنع ہے۔ اصل دلیل کی وجہ سے جو ہم بیان کر چکے اور آزاد کرنے والا تاوان کی رقم غلام سے واپس لے گا۔ کیونکہ آزاد کرنے والا تاوان دے کر ساکت کے قائم مقام ہو گیا اور ساکت کو یہ اختیار تھا کہ غلام سے کمائی کرائے ۔ پس ایسے ہی آزاد کرنے والے کو بھی یہ اختیار ہوگا اور اس وجہ سے کہ آزاد کرنے والا ادائے مہمان کی وجہ سے ضمناً اس غلام کا مالک ہو گیا۔ تو ایسا ہو جائے گا۔ گویا پورا غلام اسی کا ہے۔ حال یہ کہ اس نے اس کا ایک جز آزاد کیا ہے۔ تو اس کو اختیار ہے کہ باقی کو آزاد کر دے یا اگر چاہے تو کمائی کرا کے قیمت لے لے اور اس صورت میں ولاء آزاد کرنے والے کیلئے ہوگی۔ کیونکہ پورے غلام کی آزادی

اس کی طرف سے ہوئی ۔ کیونکہ اداء ضمان کی وجہ سے وہ پورے غلام کا مالک ہو گیا۔

اور رہا امام ابو حنیفہ کے قول پر ان حکموں کا نکالنا تو وہ اس طرح ہے کہ دوسرے شریک کو بھی اپنا حصہ آزاد کرنے کا اختیار ہے۔ کیونکہ اپنے حصہ میں اس کی ملک موجود ہے۔ اسلئے امام ابو حنیفہ کے نزدیک اعتاق متجری ہو سکتا ہے اور اس کو یہ بھی اختیار ہے کہ وہ آزاد کرنے والے سے اپنے حصہ کا تاوان لے لے۔ اسلئے کہ آزاد کرنے والے نے اس کے حصہ پر ظلم کیا کہ اسکا حصہ فاسد کر دیا۔ کیونکہ سوائے آزاد کرنے اور اس کے توابع یعنی مد بریا مکاتب کرنے یا اس سے کمائی کرا لینے کے۔ اس کو یہ قدرت نہ رہی کہ اپنے حصہ کو فروخت یا ہبہ وغیرہ کر سکے۔ چنانچہ ہم اس کو بیان کر چکے۔ رہا یہ کہ آزاد کرنے والے نے جو تاوان دیا اس کو غلام سے واپس لے سکتا ہے۔ اس وجہ سے کہ جب اس نے اپنے شریک کو تاوان ادا کر دیا۔ تو وہ شریک کا قائم مقام ہو گیا اور شریک کو یہ اختیار تھا کہ غلام سے کمائی کرائے تو آزاد کرنے والے کو بھی یہ اختیار ہو گا اور دوسری دلیل یہ ہے کہ آزاد کرنے والا اداء ضمان کی وجہ سے اس غلام کا ضمنا ما لک ہو گیا۔ اگر چہ وہ صریحی ملکیت کے قابل نہیں ہے تو ایسا ہو گیا کہ گویا پورا اعلام اسی کا ہے اور حال یہ ہے کہ اس نے اس کا ایک جز آزاد کیا ہے تو اس کو اختیار ہے کہ چاہے باقی بھی آزاد کر دے اور چاہے کمائی کر کے قیمت لے لے۔ پھر تاوان ادا کرنے کی صورت میں آزاد کنندہ کو اس غلام کی پوری ولاء ملے گی کیونکہ پورا غلام آزاد کرنا۔ اس کی طرف سے ہوا۔ اسلئے کہ اداء عضمان کی وجہ سے وہ پورے غلام کا مالک ہو گیا۔

But if he does not enter, it is not necessary that he is not freed; rather he can be freed by the master saying 'You are free' without any condition. Similarly in this issue, even though the manumitter is wealthy, the slave can be made to earn. It is clear that the manumitter upon whom the compensation is obligatory, the consideration in his wealth is the ease of payment (yasar al-taysir), not abundant wealth. Yasar al-taysir is that he owns enough wealth to pay the value of the partner's share, and this amount is surplus over his debts and family expenses. This is the zahir al-riwayah, and Imam Shafi'i, Imam Malik, and Imam Ahmad hold this view. Some mashayikh considered the nisab of freedom, but the preferred madhhab is that of zahir al-riwayah. The proof is that considering ease of payment takes care of both: the manumitter will get the reward and closeness to Allah due to the emancipation, and the silent partner will get his right.

The reason for the Sahibayn's view on emancipation: 'al-'iyad'? Then the derivation on their view is apparent: the manumitter does not recover from the slave what he paid because there is no earning (sa'aya) in the state of wealth, and the wala' is for the manumitter because the entire emancipation is from his side due to non-divisibility. As for the derivation on Imam Abu Hanifa's view, the partner has the option to emancipate because his ownership remains in his share, since emancipation can be divided according to him. The compensation is due because the manumitter has wronged him by spoiling his share, since selling, gifting, etc. other than emancipation and its consequences and earning are prevented from him. The manumitter recovers from the slave what he paid because he has taken the place of the silent partner by paying the compensation, and the silent partner had that right through earning, so also for the manumitter. And because he becomes the owner of the slave impliedly by paying the compensation, so it is as if the entire slave is his, and he has emancipated a part of him, so he may emancipate the rest or make him earn if he wishes. The wala' is for the manumitter in this case because the emancipation is entirely from his side, since he owned [the slave] by paying the compensation.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and inter-madhhab comparison regarding the case where one co-owner of a slave emancipates his share. The author explains that the rulings are derived from two principles: (1) whether emancipation can be divided (tajzi' al-'itq); (2) whether a wealthy manumitter's slave can be made to earn (sa'aya) to pay compensation to the other partner. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that emancipation cannot be divided (la yatajuzza'), so when one partner emancipates his share, the entire slave becomes free because the emancipation applies to the whole. Since the manumitter is wealthy, the slave cannot be made to earn (as per the principle that a wealthy owner's slave is not subjected to earning for the partner's share). Instead, the manumitter must pay the value of the partner's share as compensation (daman). The manumitter cannot recover this from the slave because the slave is not obliged to earn in this state. The wala' (patronage) goes to the manumitter because the emancipation originated from him. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that emancipation can be divided (yatajuzza'). Therefore, only the manumitter's share is freed; the partner's share remains his property. The partner may either free his share, or claim compensation from the manumitter due to the harm caused (because the manumitter rendered the partner unable to sell or gift his share). The manumitter, having paid the compensation, stands in the place of the partner and may make the slave earn to recover that amount. Also, by paying, he becomes the owner of the entire slave impliedly, so he may free the rest or make him earn. The wala' attaches to him in this case as well, because the entire emancipation ultimately proceeds from him. The passage then discusses the case where the manumitter is insolvent (mu'sir). In that case, according to the Hanafis, the manumitter may still free his share (since his ownership remains) or make the slave earn, and the wala' is for him. The slave cannot recover from the manumitter because he is earning to free his own neck, not paying a debt for the manumitter. This differs from the case of a pledged slave emancipated by an insolvent pledgor, where the slave can recover because he is earning to free himself or pay the pledgor's debt. Imam Shafi'i agrees with the Sahibayn when the manumitter is wealthy, but for the insolvent, he holds that the silent partner's share remains his property to be sold or gifted, because there is no basis for compensating the partner (due to insolvency), nor for making the slave earn (as the slave is not a criminal and does not consent), nor for freeing the entire slave (which would harm the silent partner). The Hanafi response is that earning (sa'aya) is possible because it is based on the retention of value (ihtibas al-maliyya), not on criminality. Therefore, one should not combine conflicting strengths (ability to own by paying and inability due to insolvency) in one person.

Key Terms
تجزّي العتق (tajazzi' al-'itq): Whether emancipation can be divided or applied partially. Sahibayn say no, Abu Hanifa says yes.
السعاية (al-sa'aya): Compulsory earning by the slave to pay off a debt or compensation, typically used when a slave is partially freed.
التضمين (al-tadmin): Making the manumitter liable to pay compensation (daman) to the silent partner for the value of the partner's share.
الولاء (al-wala'): Patronage relationship between the emancipator and the freed slave, giving the former inheritance rights under certain conditions.
يسار التيسير (yasar al-taysir): Ease of payment: the manumitter is considered wealthy if he can easily pay the compensation without straining his basic needs and debts.

Joint Emancipation with an Insolvent Manumitter: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Deriving the ruling for the insolvent manumitter and the dispute with Shafi'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings when an insolvent co-owner emancipates his share, and explain the Hanafi justification for sa'aya.

Core Idea
An insolvent manumitter may make the slave earn; Shafi'i rejects this, but Hanafis base it on ihtibas al-maliyya.

This lesson examines the case where the manumitter is insolvent. The Hanafi school allows the manumitter to either free his share or make the slave earn, with the wala' going to him. Imam Shafi'i disagrees, holding that the silent partner's share remains his property. We explore the proofs and the Hanafi response based on the principle of retention of value.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling for the insolvent manumitter
When the manumitter is insolvent, he may either free his share (since his ownership remains) or make the slave earn to pay the partner's share. The wala' is for the manumitter in both cases.
The slave earns to free his own neck, not to pay the manumitter's debt.
2
Understand why the slave cannot recover from the manumitter
The slave cannot recover what he earns from the manumitter because the manumitter is insolvent (nothing to recover) and the slave is earning for his own freedom, not paying a debt for the manumitter.
This is different from the case of a pledged slave where the slave can recover.
3
State the Shafi'i position on the insolvent case
Imam Shafi'i holds that the silent partner's share remains his property; he may sell or gift it. There is no compensation because the manumitter is insolvent, no earning because the slave is not a criminal and does not consent, and no full emancipation because it would harm the silent partner.
Shafi'i agrees with Sahibayn on the wealthy case but differs on the insolvent case.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i proof
Shafi'i's proof: There is no basis for making the partner liable (insolvency), no basis for earning (slave's consent?), and full emancipation would harm the partner.
The Shafi'i position focuses on protecting the silent partner's property rights.
5
Explain the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: Earning (sa'aya) is valid even without the slave's consent because it is based on the retention of value (ihtibas al-maliyya), not on criminality. The slave is not paying a debt for the manumitter but freeing himself. Thus, there is no contradiction in the slave earning from his own labor.
This is the key point: sa'aya is a means of freeing the slave, not a punishment.

But if he does not enter, it is not necessary that he is not freed; rather he can be freed by the master saying 'You are free' without any condition. Similarly in this issue, even though the manumitter is wealthy, the slave can be made to earn. It is clear that the manumitter upon whom the compensation is obligatory, the consideration in his wealth is the ease of payment (yasar al-taysir), not abundant wealth. Yasar al-taysir is that he owns enough wealth to pay the value of the partner's share, and this amount is surplus over his debts and family expenses. This is the zahir al-riwayah, and Imam Shafi'i, Imam Malik, and Imam Ahmad hold this view. Some mashayikh considered the nisab of freedom, but the preferred madhhab is that of zahir al-riwayah. The proof is that considering ease of payment takes care of both: the manumitter will get the reward and closeness to Allah due to the emancipation, and the silent partner will get his right.

The reason for the Sahibayn's view on emancipation: 'al-'iyad'? Then the derivation on their view is apparent: the manumitter does not recover from the slave what he paid because there is no earning (sa'aya) in the state of wealth, and the wala' is for the manumitter because the entire emancipation is from his side due to non-divisibility. As for the derivation on Imam Abu Hanifa's view, the partner has the option to emancipate because his ownership remains in his share, since emancipation can be divided according to him. The compensation is due because the manumitter has wronged him by spoiling his share, since selling, gifting, etc. other than emancipation and its consequences and earning are prevented from him. The manumitter recovers from the slave what he paid because he has taken the place of the silent partner by paying the compensation, and the silent partner had that right through earning, so also for the manumitter. And because he becomes the owner of the slave impliedly by paying the compensation, so it is as if the entire slave is his, and he has emancipated a part of him, so he may emancipate the rest or make him earn if he wishes. The wala' is for the manumitter in this case because the emancipation is entirely from his side, since he owned [the slave] by paying the compensation.

Ashraf al-Hidaya Urdu commentary Volume Five: Explanation: The author of Hidaya said that this issue is based on two principles, so the ruling in this issue is derived from both principles. So the derivation on the Sahibayn's view is apparent: because according to the Sahibayn emancipation cannot be divided, so the entire slave is freed; because the manumitter's wealth prevents the slave from earning, so compensation is obligatory on him for the partner, and earning is not possible; the manumitter cannot take the compensation from the slave because when he paid it in the state of wealth, the slave was not obliged to earn; and the wala' is for the manumitter because the entire emancipation occurred from his side, as emancipation is not divisible.

As for deriving the rulings on Imam Abu Hanifa's view, it is as follows: The other partner also has the option to emancipate his share because his ownership remains in his share, since according to Imam Abu Hanifa emancipation can be divided. He also has the option to take compensation for his share from the manumitter because the manumitter wronged him by spoiling his share, as he is prevented from selling or gifting his share except by emancipation and its consequences (such as making a mudabbar or mukatab or earning from him). As for the manumitter recovering the compensation from the slave, it is because when he paid the compensation to his partner, he took the partner's place, and the partner had the right to make the slave earn, so the manumitter has that right. Another proof: by paying the compensation he becomes the slave's owner impliedly, though not explicitly, so it is as if the entire slave is his, and he has freed a part, so he may free the rest or make him earn and take the value. Then in case of paying compensation, the manumitter gets the entire wala' because the entire emancipation occurred from his side, since he became the owner of the entire slave by paying the compensation.

The issue when the manumitter is insolvent: And in the case of the manumitter's insolvency, he may either emancipate (his share) because his ownership remains, or he may make the slave earn as we explained, and the wala' is for him in both cases because the emancipation is from his side. The slave who earns does not recover from the manumitter what he paid, by consensus among us, because he earns to free his neck, and he does not pay a debt on behalf of the manumitter since the manumitter has nothing due to his insolvency. Unlike the case of a pledged slave when the insolvent pledgor emancipates him, because the slave earns for freeing his neck or pays a debt on behalf of the pledgor, so he recovers from him. Imam Shafi'i's view in the case of the wealthy manumitter is like the Sahibayn's view. He said regarding the insolvent: the silent partner's share remains his property, to be sold or gifted, because there is no way to make the partner liable due to his insolvency, nor to make the slave earn because the slave is not a criminal and does not consent, nor to free the entire slave due to harming the silent partner. So what we have specified is necessary. We say: earning is possible because it does not require criminality, but is based on the retention of value; thus one should not combine the strength that necessitates ownership and the weakness that negates it in one person.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and inter-madhhab comparison regarding the case where one co-owner of a slave emancipates his share. The author explains that the rulings are derived from two principles: (1) whether emancipation can be divided (tajzi' al-'itq); (2) whether a wealthy manumitter's slave can be made to earn (sa'aya) to pay compensation to the other partner. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that emancipation cannot be divided (la yatajuzza'), so when one partner emancipates his share, the entire slave becomes free because the emancipation applies to the whole. Since the manumitter is wealthy, the slave cannot be made to earn (as per the principle that a wealthy owner's slave is not subjected to earning for the partner's share). Instead, the manumitter must pay the value of the partner's share as compensation (daman). The manumitter cannot recover this from the slave because the slave is not obliged to earn in this state. The wala' (patronage) goes to the manumitter because the emancipation originated from him. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that emancipation can be divided (yatajuzza'). Therefore, only the manumitter's share is freed; the partner's share remains his property. The partner may either free his share, or claim compensation from the manumitter due to the harm caused (because the manumitter rendered the partner unable to sell or gift his share). The manumitter, having paid the compensation, stands in the place of the partner and may make the slave earn to recover that amount. Also, by paying, he becomes the owner of the entire slave impliedly, so he may free the rest or make him earn. The wala' attaches to him in this case as well, because the entire emancipation ultimately proceeds from him. The passage then discusses the case where the manumitter is insolvent (mu'sir). In that case, according to the Hanafis, the manumitter may still free his share (since his ownership remains) or make the slave earn, and the wala' is for him. The slave cannot recover from the manumitter because he is earning to free his own neck, not paying a debt for the manumitter. This differs from the case of a pledged slave emancipated by an insolvent pledgor, where the slave can recover because he is earning to free himself or pay the pledgor's debt. Imam Shafi'i agrees with the Sahibayn when the manumitter is wealthy, but for the insolvent, he holds that the silent partner's share remains his property to be sold or gifted, because there is no basis for compensating the partner (due to insolvency), nor for making the slave earn (as the slave is not a criminal and does not consent), nor for freeing the entire slave (which would harm the silent partner). The Hanafi response is that earning (sa'aya) is possible because it is based on the retention of value (ihtibas al-maliyya), not on criminality. Therefore, one should not combine conflicting strengths (ability to own by paying and inability due to insolvency) in one person.

Key Terms
السعاية (al-sa'aya): Compulsory earning by the slave to pay off a debt or compensation, typically used when a slave is partially freed.
احتباس المالية (ihtibas al-maliyya): Retention of value; the principle that a slave has inherent value that can be used to generate earnings to free himself.
المعسر (al-mu'sir): Insolvent person who cannot pay debts or compensation easily.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Compensation for Partner's Share When Purchasing a Relative
Intra-Hanafi dispute on emancipation by purchase of a relative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on compensation when a father and partner jointly purchase the father's son, and evaluate the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Buying a close relative is emancipation; the partner's consent in the purchase cancels compensation.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute. When a father and another person jointly purchase the father's son, the father's share becomes free automatically because purchasing a mahram relative is legally equivalent to emancipation. The question is whether the father must compensate the partner for the loss of his share. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that no compensation is due because the partner consented by entering the purchase. The two disciples (Sahibayn) analogize it to a stranger freeing his share in a jointly-owned slave, requiring compensation if the father is wealthy, or istis'a if poor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
The act is the joint purchase of a slave who is the son of one of the purchasers. The ruling concerns the emancipation of the father's share and potential compensation.
Focus on the father's portion only.
2
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
The father's share becomes free; no compensation owed to partner; partner can free his share or make the slave earn.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah from Abu Hanifa.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's objection
They require the father to guarantee half the value if wealthy, or the son earns half if the father is poor.
The same disagreement applies to gift, charity, bequest, and oath cases.
4
Analyze the proofs
Sahibayn use analogy to a unilateral emancipation by a stranger. Abu Hanifa argues that the partner consented by participating in the purchase, which is the cause of emancipation.
Consent removes liability.
5
Determine the thamarah and authority
Practical difference: liability vs no liability. The Zahir al-Riwayah supports Abu Hanifa's view that knowledge/ignorance does not change the ruling.
Later Hanafi practice may adopt Sahibayn for financial equity.

Book of Emancipation. When two men buy the son of one of them, the father's share becomes free because he owns a portion of his relative and his purchase is emancipation, as has passed. No compensation is due from him whether the other knows he is his partner's son or not. Likewise if they inherit him. The partner has the option: if he wishes he frees his share, and if he wishes he makes the slave earn. This is according to Abu Hanifa. Their proof is that the father nullified his partner's share by emancipation because buying a relative is emancipation, and it became as if the slave was between two strangers and one freed his share. His (Abu Hanifa's) proof is that the partner consented to the nullification of his share, so he does not guarantee him, just as if he expressly permitted him to free his share. The indication of that is that he participated with him in what is the cause of emancipation, namely purchase, because buying a relative is emancipation such that he is acquitted from the liability of expiation according to us. This guarantee is a guarantee of nullification in the apparent view of the two such that it varies with wealth and poverty and falls with consent. The ruling does not differ between knowledge and ignorance. This is the apparent narration from him because the ruling revolves around the cause, just as when someone tells another, 'Eat this food' while it is owned by the commander and the commander does not know he owns it.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah deals with an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling when two men purchase a slave who is the son of one of them. The passage first presents the ruling from the matn of al-Hidayah: the father's share becomes free automatically because purchasing a close relative (mahram) is considered an act of emancipation in Hanafi law. The father owes no compensation to his partner. The partner retains the option to either free his own share or require the now-free slave to earn its value through istis'a. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa. The commentary then explains the disagreement of the two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani: they hold that in the case of purchase, the father must either guarantee half the slave's value if wealthy, or if poor, the son must earn half its value for the partner. The same disagreement applies when ownership is acquired through gift, charity, or bequest, and also when one partner had sworn an oath to free the slave upon purchasing half. The analysis follows the author's order: first the ruling, then the proofs of both sides. The Sahibayn argue that by freeing his own share, the father effectively nullifies the partner's share because the purchase itself causes emancipation of the entire slave, and this is analogous to a slave jointly owned by two strangers where one frees his share—in that case the freeing party must compensate the other (if wealthy) or the slave earns (if poor). Abu Hanifa's proof is that the partner's consent to the nullification is implied by his having entered into the purchase with the father, knowing that the father's purchase of his son would cause emancipation. Since consent removes the basis for compensation, no guarantee is owed. The passage further clarifies that the ruling does not depend on whether the partner knew the slave was the father's son; the cause is the purchase itself. This is the Zahir al-Riwayah from Abu Hanifa. The commentary also addresses a potential inaccuracy: the matn calls purchase the cause of 'itq, whereas strictly it is the cause of ownership which is the cause of 'itq. But the resolution is that rulings can be attributed to the cause of the cause. The passage uses a simile: if a commander tells someone to eat food that unknowingly belongs to the commander, the eater is not liable. Similarly, the partner's action of buying with the father is like giving permission.

Key Terms
شراء قريب (shira' qarib): The act of purchasing a close relative (such as a son) which, in Hanafi law, is deemed an emancipation (itq) of that relative.
استسعاء (istis'a'): A legal mechanism where a newly freed slave is required to earn money to compensate a co-owner for the portion of his value that was lost due to partial emancipation.
ضمان (daman): Financial liability or guarantee to pay compensation for damage or loss of property; here, the father's potential guarantee to the partner for the value of the partner's share.

According to Imam Abu Hanifa, when a father and another man jointly purchase the father's son, what happens to the father's share?
A) The father's share becomes free immediately and he must compensate the partner half the value.
B) The father's share becomes free immediately and he owes no compensation to the partner.
C) The entire slave remains in partnership and no emancipation occurs.
D) The father's share becomes free only if the partner gives explicit permission.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, purchasing a mahram relative causes immediate emancipation; the partner's consent by participation removes liability for compensation, per Ibn al-Humam's preferred view.
