Liability for Collapsing a Wall While Extinguishing Fire
Ruling on liability for collateral damage during a public emergency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tort liability when acting to prevent public harm and explain the proof (qiyas) that supports it.

Core Idea
Public necessity overrides individual tort liability: no compensation for collateral damage when extinguishing a community fire.

A fire rages in a town. To reach it, you climb your neighbour's wall, and it collapses. Are you liable? The Hanafi school says no—and in this lesson, we trace the proof from a striking analogy: using another's weapon to repel an enemy attack. This is not a random exception; it is a principled application of the maxim that public harm takes precedence over individual rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the passage: a person rides (climbs) another's wall to extinguish a public fire. The wall collapses. The ruling is: no liability for the value of the wall.
Focus on the action and the consequence: the act is done for public benefit.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author provides an analogy (qiyas): repelling an enemy using another's tool. If the tool is destroyed, no liability. This is the proof text.
The exact Arabic analogy is cited: 'كما إذا حَمَلَ العدو على المسلمين...'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning)
The effective cause (illa) is general public harm (darar 'amm). Both fire and enemy attack threaten the entire community, so the same exemption from liability applies when acting to remove that harm.
The illa is the public necessity, not the identity of the actor.
4
Connect to Hanafi legal maxims
The underlying principle is that preventing public harm takes priority over individual property rights. This is expressed in the maxim 'al-darar al-amm yuzalu' (public harm must be removed) and 'daman al-mal bi al-idhn' (liability is waived when acting for the public good).
This maxim governs many emergency situations in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Evaluate the practical application
In fatwa, if someone damages property while genuinely acting to avert a public calamity (like fire, flood, enemy attack), they are not liable. The action must be proportionate and necessary.
If the fire was small or the wall was unnecessary, liability may still apply.

And whoever rides (climbs) the wall of another's house to extinguish a fire that has occurred in the town, and a wall of the house collapses due to his riding: he does not guarantee the value of the wall; because the harm of the fire is general upon the Muslims, so it is incumbent upon the general Muslims to repel that from themselves, just as if an enemy attacked the Muslims, and a man repelled that enemy from them by using another's tool until the tool was destroyed: he does not guarantee anything of its value, likewise this.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a specific scenario: a fire breaks out in a town (a public calamity), and a person rides (i.e., climbs or mounts) the wall of someone else's house to extinguish the fire. In doing so, the wall collapses due to his action. The ruling is that he is not liable for the value of the wall. The proof (ta'lil) is based on the principle of public necessity: the harm from fire is general and endangers the entire community, so each Muslim has a duty to help repel it. The act of climbing the wall is for the public benefit, and any incidental damage is excused. This is analogized (qiyas) to the case of repelling an enemy attack: if a man uses another's weapon to fight off the enemy and the weapon is destroyed, he is not liable because he was defending the community. The same logic applies here. This ruling reflects the Hanafi prioritization of preventing widespread harm (darar 'amm) over individual property rights in emergencies.

Key Terms
حريق (hariq): A fire that poses a public danger, obligating community participation in extinguishing it.
ضرر عام (darar 'amm): Harm that threatens the entire community, such as fire or enemy attack.
