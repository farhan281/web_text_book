Wadi'ah and Ijarah: Stipulating Guarantee is Void
Ruling on the legal effect of a guarantee stipulation in wadi'ah and ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that stipulating guarantee in wadi'ah and ijarah is void and explain that liability only arises through transgression (ta'addi).

Core Idea
Wadi'ah and ijarah are pure trusts; a stipulation of guarantee cannot create liability.

In Hanafi fiqh, some contracts of trust allow parties to stipulate liability, while others do not. This lesson examines the ruling for wadi'ah (deposit) and ijarah (lease), where even an explicit guarantee clause is legally ineffective. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly advising on liability in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracts
Note that the passage discusses two contracts: wadi'ah (deposit for safekeeping) and ijarah (lease of usufruct). Both are classified as trusts (amanat) in Hanafi law.
2
State the general rule
The rule: these contracts are 'never made liable' even if guarantee is stipulated. This means the bailee (in wadi'ah) or lessee (in ijarah) is not liable for loss or damage unless they commit transgression.
Transgression (ta'addi) includes misuse, negligence, or exceeding the terms of the contract.
3
Understand the exception
Liability only arises through actual transgression (ta'addi), not through a contractual stipulation. This exception preserves the trust nature of the contract.
4
Contrast with 'ariyah
The passage implies a contrast with 'ariyah (borrowing), where stipulating guarantee is valid and effective. Understanding this comparison solidifies the rule.
'Ariyah is a gratuitous loan of an item for use, and Hanafi law allows a guarantee clause to make the borrower liable.
5
Identify the authority
The passage references 'al-Karkhi', indicating that this ruling is attributed to the Hanafi scholar al-Karkhi, a key figure in the later Hanafi tradition.
Al-Karkhi's views are often relied upon in fatwa (mufta bihi) for this issue.

Commentary
This is a differentiating ruling within Hanafi fiqh. While 'ariyah allows a valid stipulation of guarantee, wadi'ah (deposit) and ijarah (hire/lease) do not. The underlying reason is that these contracts are fundamentally trusts (amanat) by their nature, and their essential character cannot be altered by a contractual condition. The only way liability attaches in wadi'ah and ijarah is through actual transgression (ta'addi), such as misuse or negligence. This ruling is attributed to the Hanafi scholar al-Karkhi, indicating its authority.

Liability for a borrowed animal depends on negligence, not merely on being asleep.

The key factor is negligence (tafrit). The ruling also analogizes to the depositary (muda') when the borrower sleeps sitting.

Liability arises from negligence (mudayyi'). In scenario 1, no negligence; scenario 2, negligence.
Negligence is determined by customary standards of care.
4

Al-Sarakhsi explicitly equates borrower and depositary in this ruling.

Correcting Errors in a Borrowed Book: The Role of Implicit Permission
Ruling on correcting errors in a borrowed book

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on correcting errors in a borrowed book and apply the concept of implicit permission (idhn dalalah) to distinguish permissible from impermissible actions.

Core Idea
Correcting a borrowed book requires either explicit or implicit permission; otherwise it is unauthorized disposal.

This lesson examines a practical issue: a borrower finds an error in a borrowed book. May he correct it? The ruling hinges on the owner's presumed willingness and the concept of implicit permission (idhn dalalah). We will explore the two scenarios and their legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The borrower finds an error in a borrowed book and considers correcting it. This is a potential disposal of another's property (tasarruf fi mulk al-ghayr).
The act is not the reading itself, but altering the book.
2
Determine the owner's known or unknown attitude
The ruling split depends on whether the borrower knows that the owner dislikes correction. If known, correction is impermissible; if unknown, it is permissible.
Knowledge of objection is key.
3
Apply the principle of disposal of another's property
Disposal without permission is generally not allowed. If the owner dislikes correction, correcting is an unauthorized disposal and thus haram (or makruh tahrimi).
The text says 'yanbaghi an la yuslihahu' (should not correct), indicating prohibition.
4
Apply implicit permission (idhn dalalah)
If the owner's dislike is unknown, custom (urf) gives implicit permission to make minor corrections, as it is a common expectation in lending books. Thus, correction is permissible (ja'iz).
Implicit permission is recognized in Hanafi fiqh as valid.
5
Note the absence of obligation
Even if correction is permissible, the borrower is not obliged to do it. No sin is incurred for leaving it uncorrected, as the act is not wajib.
The text explicitly states 'la ithma 'alayhi'.

Custom is a recognized source of law in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply to similar cases
If the lender does not consent, the borrower becomes liable because he left the animal without permission—a form of negligence (ta'addi).
This principle applies to any borrowed property left in a location without consent.

Liability is contingent on the item being in the taker's possession without permission.

The principle: In a loan (ariyah), permission must be explicit and actual delivery must occur. A promise alone does not authorize taking. Unauthorized taking is ghasb, which makes the taker a guarantor of the item.
This principle applies to all forms of property, not just bulls.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar opens the Book of Musaqat, explaining the fundamental conditions for a valid musaqat contract. Musaqat is a contract of sharecropping on fruit trees, typically date palms or vines. The core ruling: the contract is valid only if the fruit can still be improved by the worker's labour. If the fruit is already ripe and no work can increase it, then the contract is invalid because the worker would receive a share without contributing work. This is based on the principle that compensation is for work. 

If the contract is invalid (fasid) due to some defect, the worker is entitled to fair wages (ajr al-mithl) for his work, analogous to a corrupt hire contract (ijarah fasiadah) or corrupt muzara'ah. There is an intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Yusuf holds that the fair wages cannot exceed the stipulated share, while Muhammad holds that they are given whatever they amount to, even if more. The relied-upon position in later Hanafi fatwa is Muhammad's view, ensuring full compensation for the worker's labour.

The owner's death terminates the contract because the trees pass to his heirs; the worker's death because he cannot personally perform the work. This ruling underscores that musaqat is a personal contract based on the worker's direct labour.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Death does not void musaqat: the worker or his heirs may continue to preserve the fruit and earn their share.

This lesson examines what happens when the musaqat partnership is interrupted by death of a party or expiration of the contract term. The Hanafi school provides clear rules to protect the worker's labour and the owner's property, ensuring fairness when the fruit is still unripe. The key legal principle is دفع الضرر (preventing harm) — the contract does not simply collapse, but allows continuation to avoid waste.

These options balance interests.

When the contract term expires, the worker may still continue but without extra pay (بغير أجر) because trees cannot be hired.
This prevents the worker from claiming additional wages.

Commentary
This passage addresses scenarios where the musaqat contract is interrupted by death or expiry of the term, ensuring that the worker's labour is not wasted and the fruit is preserved.

When the owner dies while the fruit is unripe, the worker may continue to tend the trees until ripening, even if the heirs object. If the worker prefers to take his share immediately as unripe fruit, the heirs have three options: (1) harvest and divide, (2) give the worker the cash value of his share, or (3) spend money to ripen the fruit and recover costs from the worker's share. These options balance the interests of both parties.

When the worker dies, his heirs may step in to continue the work, as they inherit his rights. The owner cannot object, as this benefits both sides. If the heirs choose to harvest unripe fruit, the owner gets similar options.

When both parties die, the worker's heirs have priority to continue, being the worker's successors. If they decline, the owner's heirs decide.
