This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses kafa'ah (suitability) among mawali, focusing on how many of their ancestors embraced Islam. Core Idea
Kafa'ah in wealth requires both mahr and nafaqa—leniency in mahr does not waive its necessity.

The Hanafi school holds that suitability (kafa'ah) in marriage includes financial capability. But what exactly must a husband be able to provide? This lesson dissects an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa requires both mahr (dower) and nafaqa (maintenance), while his student Abu Yusuf considers only nafaqa. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the legal reasoning behind a key condition of marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What aspect of kafa'ah is being disputed? The passage focuses on wealth: what must a man be able to provide to be a suitable match?
The issue is not the existence of kafa'ah but its scope.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa: both mahr and nafaqa are necessary. Abu Yusuf: only nafaqa is necessary. Note which position is attributed to the Zahir al-Riwayah.
Zahir al-Riwayah is the relied-upon transmission of the school.

Identify the mufta bihi

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The father may reduce or increase mahr for his minor children, but the Sahibayn limit it to negligible amounts.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of the father's wilaya in marriage. While Abu Hanifa and Zufar grant the father and grandfather absolute authority to vary the mahr when marrying off minor children, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict this authority to minor fluctuations that are ordinarily overlooked. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for grasping the Hanafi school's refinement of guardianship rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on father's marriage of minors with mahr deviation and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa upholds father's contract regardless of mahr; Sahibayn invalidate if mahr exceeds tolerable limits.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra comments on al-Quduri's ruling on a father's marriage of his minor children. While the matn declares the contract valid even with mahr deviation, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani disagree, restricting the father's discretion to avoid gross overreaching. Understanding this dispute illuminates the balance between parental authority and child protection in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's ruling
The matn states that if a father marries his minor daughter with less than her fair mahr, or his minor son with more than the fair mahr, it is valid (jāza). This is the starting point of the dispute.
The ruling is specific to the father and grandfather; other guardians lack this authority.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Zufar uphold the matn's ruling. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) reject it unless the deviation is within the tolerable difference (ghaban yasir). They consider the entire contract invalid if the deviation is gross.

The Sahibayn analogize the father's marriage authority to his authority over the minor's property. In property, a guardian cannot transact with gross overreaching; similarly, in marriage, he cannot set a mahr that deviates excessively from the fair mahr.
The analogy is based on the minor's financial interest: mahr is a right of the wife (or the bride's right in her own marriage).
4

If the father contracts with a gross deficiency or excess, according to Abu Hanifa the marriage stands and is binding on the minor. According to the Sahibayn, the marriage is invalid (batil) and must be annulled; the minor may challenge it upon majority.
The practical difference affects the minor's ability to exit the marriage after puberty.

The passage defines what constitutes 'tolerable difference' (ma yataghabanu fīhi). It is reported as less than half the mahr (from Shaykh Muwaffaq al-Din) or less than one-tenth (another opinion). Any deviation beyond that is gross (ghaban fahish).

Non-Father Guardians and the Precautionary Double Contract
Ruling for non-father guardians and precautionary double contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the limitation on non-father guardians in mahr matters and apply the precautionary double contract from al-Nawadir.

Core Idea
A marriage by a non-father guardian is valid only if the mahr is within the tolerable difference; otherwise, a double contract is precaution.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah clarifies a key restriction on guardians other than the father and grandfather when marrying a minor. It explains that such a guardian cannot validly contract a marriage if the mahr deviates beyond what is normally tolerated in commercial exchange. The commentary then cites a remarkable precaution from Imam Muhammad's al-Nawadir: conducting two contracts to ensure validity. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the practical safeguard.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and restriction
The passage first states that the prior ruling allowing deviation in mahr is exclusive to the father and grandfather. For any other guardian, it is not valid (la yajuz) unless the mahr difference is within the tolerable difference (yutaghanab fihi).
2
Understand the condition
The only condition that makes it valid is when the mahr is within the range that reasonable people would overlook in a transaction. This is a narrow exception.
3
Learn the precaution from al-Nawadir
Imam Muhammad recommends that to be safe, the guardian should perform two marriage contracts: one with a specified mahr and one without specification. If the specified mahr is deficient, the first fails, but the second (with mahr al-mithl) remains valid.
4
Apply the reasoning
Recognize that this precaution combines two contracts so that the marriage is not jeopardized by an invalid mahr term. It reflects the Hanafi emphasis on preserving the validity of the nikah.

Validity of Marriage with Specified or Unspecified Dower
Ruling on validity and minimum dower

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of marriage with or without specifying the dower and identify the minimum dower amount.

Core Idea
Marriage is valid even without specifying dower; the minimum is ten dirhams.

This lesson covers two foundational rules of Hanafi marriage law: first, the marriage contract is valid whether the dower (mahr) is specified or not; second, the minimum dower is set at ten dirhams. We will also see that even if a man marries a woman with a condition that she has no dower, the marriage remains valid but the condition is void and she is entitled to the proper mahr (mahr al-mithl).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The marriage contract (nikah). What is the hukm? It is valid (sahih) in all scenarios described.
Validity means the marriage is legally effective and produces all normal effects.
2
Determine the conditions for validity
The passage implies that specification of dower is not a condition for validity. The only requirement is that if a dower is mentioned, it must be at least ten dirhams.
Hanafi school: mahr is not a pillar (rukn) of marriage, only an obligation (wajib).
3
Note the minimum dower
The minimum amount for a valid specified dower is ten dirhams. If less is specified, the marriage is still valid but she receives the proper mahr (mahr al-mithl).
This is based on the prophetic tradition that a hand (as a gift) or something of value is sufficient; Hanafis set a minimum to prevent trivial amounts.
4
Understand the effect of a void condition
If a man marries with the condition that she has no dower, the condition is void (batil) but the marriage remains valid. The wife is then entitled to the proper mahr.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that marriage is not invalidated by conditions that contradict its essential nature.

Nikah al-shighar is valid in the Hanafi school but the condition of waiving dower is void; each woman deserves proper mahr.

This lesson examines a controversial marriage known as nikah al-shighar (exchange marriage). The Hanafi school affirms its validity, arguing that the core marriage contract is sound even though the prohibited condition of waiving dower is void. In contrast, the Shafi'i school invalidates the entire transaction. We will work through the Hanafi rationale, the key distinction between types of shighar, and the practical consequences of this difference, as well as the related ruling on minimum dower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Recognize that the central issue is whether a marriage contracted on the condition that each party waives the dower (nikah al-shighar) is valid.
The opposing view is held by Imam al-Shafi'i.
2
State the Hanafi position
According to the Hanafi school, the contract is valid (mun'aqid) but the condition is void. Each woman is entitled to her proper dower (mahr al-mithl).
The text says 'عندنا يجوز النكاح' – according to us, the marriage is permissible.
3
Distinguish the prohibited type
The Prophet's prohibition applies to a different form: where a man allows his slave to marry a woman with the slave himself as the dower. That marriage is invalid because the woman would own the slave.
This distinction is key to reconciling the Hanafi position with the hadith.
4
Compare with Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the entire exchange marriage is invalid, regardless of whether dower is later paid.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that in the Hanafi school the marriage stands, while in the Shafi'i school it must be annulled.
5
Apply the minimum dower ruling
The passage concludes with a rule: the minimum dower is ten dirhams or its value on the day of the contract, not on the day of payment.
This is a separate but related rule applicable to any valid marriage.

The Dower (Mahr) and Shighar Marriage: Hanafi Rulings and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Expansion probe on dower rulings with inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on nikkah al-shighar, explain the minimum dower of 10 dirhams, and distinguish the Hanafi position from al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Marriage is valid even without a named dower; shighar is valid with mahr mithl due, contrary to al-Shafi'i.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah expands on the dower (mahr) in the marriage contract. You will learn the foundational Hanafi principle that marriage is not invalidated by the absence of a dower or by naming a dower below the minimum. The key inter-madhhab dispute concerning nikkah al-shighar (exchange marriage) is presented, along with the Hanafi interpretation of the prophetic prohibition. Sub-rulings on the minimum dower, the effect of naming less than ten dirhams, and the valuation of non-monetary dowers are also clarified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational ruling on dower
The commentator states that marriage is valid whether a dower is named or not. This is the root Hanafi position: mahr is wajib but not a condition of validity. Understand that if no dower is named, the wife is entitled to mahr mithl (the appropriate dower).
2
Determine the minimum dower and its consequences
The minimum dower is ten dirhams, or its equivalent value on the day of the contract. If a dower less than ten is named, the wife is entitled to the minimum (ten) – this is the majority Hanafi view. If divorce occurs before consummation with less than ten named, she receives half the minimum (five).
Note: Zufar holds she receives mahr mithl instead of ten, and mut'ah instead of five on pre-consummation divorce.
3
Analyze the shighar dispute: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Nikkah al-shighar is an exchange marriage where each man gives his female relative to the other without a dower. The Hanafi position: the contract is valid (mun'aqid), the condition is void, and each woman receives mahr mithl. Al-Shafi'i holds the entire contract invalid. Follow the argument: the prohibition in hadith is interpreted by Hanafis as limited to cases devoid of dower, not to the exchange structure itself.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in Hanafi fiqh the marriage stands and mahr mithl is due; in Shafi'i the marriage must be annulled.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi difference (Zufar)
The text notes that Zufar disagrees with the majority (Abu Hanifa & Sahibayn). If less than ten dirhams is named, Zufar says she gets mahr mithl (not the minimum). On pre-consummation divorce with less than ten named, Zufar says mut'ah is due, not five. Identify which position is mufta bihi (the majority view).
The commentator implicitly adopts the majority view; Zufar's is a minority within the school.
5
Apply the valuation rule for dower commodities
When the dower is a non-monetary item (e.g., a garment), the binding value is its worth on the day of the contract, not the day of possession or receipt. Work through the examples: if the value drops, she receives the item only; if it rises, she receives the item plus the difference to reach the named or minimum value.
This ensures certainty and prevents exploitation from value fluctuations.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The division of mahr increases depends on whether they are connected to or arise from the original property, and in whose possession they occur.

When the mahr is a specific item (like a slave or tree), its value may increase before the marriage is consummated and then divorce occurs. How is that increase divided? The Hanafi school offers detailed rules based on the nature of the increase—whether it is connected or separate, whether it arises from the original item or is external—and on whether the increase occurred while the husband still had possession or after the wife took possession. This lesson walks through these scenarios, including the known differences between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two axes
The ruling depends on two things: (1) the type of increase (connected vs. disconnected; originating from the original property vs. external), and (2) whether the increase occurred in the husband's hand before the wife took possession, or in the wife's hand after she took possession.
Connected increases are physically attached (e.g., fatness, speech). Disconnected increases are separate (e.g., offspring, fruit). Originating means it came from the property itself, external means from outside (e.g., earnings, gifts).
2
Increases in husband's hand: originating increases
If the increase is originating (connected or disconnected) and occurs in the husband's hand before the wife takes possession, both the original and the increase are halved by consensus. Example: cow gives birth to a calf; both cow and calf are divided equally.
There is no ikhtilaf here.
3
Increases in husband's hand: external increases
If the increase is disconnected and external (e.g., earnings, gifts) and occurs in the husband's hand, there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa says the original is halved but the increase goes entirely to the wife; the Sahibayn say both are halved. Also, for a connected external increase (e.g., dyeing a cloth), the woman is deemed to have taken possession, and she owes half the value on the day of judgment.
The dyeing case is unique because it makes the woman a possessor.
4
Increases in wife's hand: originating increases
If the increase occurs after the wife has taken possession: for connected originating increases (e.g., fatness), Abu Hanifa says halving is prevented—the wife keeps all and owes half the value of the original; the Sahibayn say halving is not prevented, so both are halved. For disconnected originating increases (e.g., offspring), halving is prevented by consensus—wife keeps all and owes half the value of the original.
Consensus applies to disconnected originating increases in wife's hand, but ikhtilaf on connected.
5
Increases in wife's hand: external increases
The passage begins to discuss this case but cuts off. The missing part likely says that for disconnected external increases (e.g., earnings, gifts) in the wife's hand, the rule follows the same pattern as in the husband's hand, perhaps with similar ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The type of increase and who holds possession determine mahr division — with consensus and ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira examines how increases in mahr are divided when divorce occurs before consummation. It distinguishes between connected and disconnected increases, and whether they arise from the original property. The key dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn revolves around disconnected increases not from the original when in the husband's hand, and connected increases arising from the original when in the wife's hand.

Guided Path
1
Classify the increase
Identify whether the increase is connected (muttasil) or disconnected (munfasil), and whether it arises from the original (haditha min al-asl) or not (ghayr haditha). These distinctions govern the ruling.
Examples: fatness = connected & from origin; child = disconnected & from origin; gift = disconnected & not from origin; dye = connected & not from origin.
2
Determine where the increase occurred
Has the wife taken possession (qabd) of the original mahr? The ruling differs if the increase occurs in the husband's hand (before possession) or in the wife's hand (after possession).
3
Apply consensus rules
For certain categories, all imams agree: e.g., connected or disconnected increases arising from the original occurring in the husband's hand are halved; disconnected non-original increases in the wife's hand belong entirely to her and the original is halved.
4
Identify the ikhtilaf with Sahibayn
In two scenarios, Abu Hanifa differs: (1) disconnected non-original increase in husband's hand – Abu Hanifa gives all increase to wife, Sahibayn halve it; (2) connected original increase in wife's hand – Abu Hanifa prevents division (wife keeps all, husband gets half value), Sahibayn say division proceeds.
The practical difference: e.g., if a car is gifted as mahr and wife receives a bonus before possession, Abu Hanifa says bonus is hers fully; Sahibayn say half belongs to husband.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Abu Hanifa, as it is more cautious for the wife and respects the effect of possession.

The reason for the difference is that 'entry into the vulva' (i.e., the right to sexual intercourse through marriage) is considered a valuable right (mutqawwim) that can only be acquired in exchange for valid compensation, so if the specified dower is invalid, she gets mahr al-mithl. However, 'exit' (i.e., the release of the wife through khul') is not considered a valuable right in the same way; therefore, the husband is not entitled to any compensation if the agreed-upon consideration is invalid. This shows the Hanafi principle of distinguishing between acquisition of rights and relinquishment of rights.

ويجوز للمولى أن يَهَبَ صداق أمته ومدبرته وأم ولده؛ لأنه ملكه. وليس له أن يهب مهر مكاتبته ، ولا يبرأ الزوج منه بدفعه إليه.
And it is permissible for the master to give away the dower of his female slave, his mudabbara, and his umm walad because it is his property. And it is not permissible for him to give away the dower of his mukataba, nor is the husband discharged from it by paying it to him.
Source pages p. 321

Commentary
This is a ruling statement from the matn of al-Quduri. It distinguishes between different categories of female slaves regarding the master's right to gift their dower. The master owns the dower of his ordinary female slave (amah), his mudabbara (slave promised freedom after master's death), and his umm walad (slave mother of his child), because they are his property and the dower is considered his right. However, for a mukatab slave (one who has a contract of manumission), the master cannot give away her dower because she is on the path to freedom and her earnings belong to herself. Additionally, if the husband pays the dower to the master, the husband is not discharged from the debt; he must pay the mukataba herself or ensure she receives it.

Key Terms
مولى (mawla): Master or owner of a slave.
مكاتبة (mukataba): A female slave who has a contract of manumission (kitaba) to earn her freedom by paying installments.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A female slave who has been promised freedom upon the master's death (tadbir).
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master.

Key Takeaway
