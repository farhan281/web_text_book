Valid and Invalid Seclusion (Khalwa) and Its Effect on Mahr
Defines valid seclusion and its financial consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define valid and invalid seclusion, list the impediments that invalidate it, and state the resulting mahr and iddah obligations.

Core Idea
Valid seclusion triggers full mahr and iddah; invalid seclusion only triggers iddah and half mahr.

In Hanafi marriage law, a private meeting between spouses after the marriage contract can have significant financial consequences. The key is whether that seclusion is 'valid' (sahiha) or 'invalid' (fasida). This lesson unpacks the precise definition of valid seclusion and the impediments that render it invalid, and then explains how the mahr and iddah obligations vary accordingly.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the core ruling
From the passage, extract the basic rule: if a husband is alone with his wife with no impediment to intercourse and then divorces her, she gets full mahr and must observe iddah.
This is the default consequence of valid seclusion.
2
2. Define valid and invalid seclusion
The text defines valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha) as the wife surrendering herself without any natural or legal impediment to intercourse. Invalid seclusion occurs when an impediment exists.
Impediments can be natural (e.g., sickness, presence of a third party) or legal (e.g., ihram, fasting an obligatory fast).
3
3. Classify the impediments
List all impediments mentioned in the passage: sickness preventing intercourse, vaginal closure (ratq), third person present, ihram (obligatory or voluntary), obligatory fast (Ramadan, make-up fasts, but not expiation/vow fasts), menstruation, postnatal bleeding.
Voluntary fasts are disputed: the sound narration considers them impediments, another does not.
4
4. Understand the consequence of invalid seclusion
If seclusion is invalid, divorce only obligates half mahr, but iddah is still required as a precaution (ihtiyat).
Iddah is wajib even without actual intercourse due to the presumption of possible intercourse.
5
5. Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The passage mentions a difference in narration (riwaya) regarding optional fasts and make-up fasts. One view equates them with obligatory fasts as impediments; another treats optional fasts as non-impediments. The sound narration (riwaya sahiha) adopted in the text includes them.
This ikhtilaf affects the validity of seclusion in specific scenarios.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It clarifies the concept of valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha) and its effect on the dower (mahr) and waiting period (iddah) upon divorce. The ruling states that if a husband is alone with his wife in a valid seclusion with no impediment to intercourse, and then divorces her, she is entitled to the full mahr and must observe iddah. Valid seclusion is defined as the wife surrendering herself without any natural or legal impediment to intercourse. Natural impediments include sickness that prevents intercourse, vaginal closure (ratq), or the presence of a third person. Legal impediments include both being in ihram, fasting an obligatory fast, or the wife being menstruating or in postnatal bleeding. Voluntary fast does not invalidate seclusion. The passage also discusses differences in narrations (riwayat) regarding fasting other than Ramadan; the sound narration equates voluntary and make-up fasts with obligatory fast in preventing valid seclusion, while another narration considers optional fast like obligatory. The effect of invalid seclusion is that only half the mahr is due, but iddah is still obligatory. The reasoning for iddah is precaution against the possibility of intercourse. The commentator further explains that illness means any condition preventing intercourse or causing harm. Prayer is analogized to fasting: obligatory prayer is like obligatory fast, voluntary like voluntary. Some scholars mention that sunnah prayers like fajr and four before dhuhr prevent valid seclusion. For ihram, both obligatory and voluntary hajj/umrah are treated equally because they require expiation (dam) if the marriage is consummated in that state.

Key Terms
خلوة صحيحة (khalwa sahiha): Private seclusion between spouses with no natural or legal impediment to intercourse.
مانع (mani'): An impediment that prevents the permissibility or possibility of intercourse, either by nature (e.g., sickness) or by law (e.g., ihram).
عدة (iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband to ascertain pregnancy.

Khalwa is invalid if illness, Ramadan fast, ihram, or menstruation exists; divorce then gives only half mahr.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the conditions that invalidate a valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha) in marriage. The author first lists situations that prevent khalwa from being considered valid: one spouse is ill (with an illness that prevents intercourse or causes harm), one is fasting in Ramadan, one is in ihram for Hajj or Umrah (whether obligatory or supererogatory), or the woman is menstruating. In such cases, if the husband divorces her before consummation, only half the dowry is obligatory, because the seclusion is not considered valid. The text then distinguishes between fasting in Ramadan (which requires expiation if broken) and voluntary fasts or expiatory fasts, where only making up the fast is required; thus, the latter do not invalidate khalwa. For Hajj and Umrah, expiation is required in both obligatory and supererogatory forms, so both invalidate khalwa. Another narration equates supererogatory fasting with obligatory fasting. The commentary clarifies that illness means any condition preventing intercourse or causing harm. Prayer is likened to fasting: obligatory prayer prevents khalwa, but supererogatory prayer does not, according to one view. However, a minority view from al-Wajiz states that the sunnah of Fajr and the four rak'ahs before Dhuhr do prevent khalwa. Next, the passage discusses the issue of the husband not knowing the woman (i.e., not recognizing her) before seclusion; such a meeting is not considered khalwa until he knows her. The presence of a third party who understands the situation invalidates khalwa. A sleeping person is considered as present because he might be pretending to sleep. Some say daytime presence of a third party invalidates khalwa while nighttime does not. For a blind person, if they can discern the situation, khalwa is invalid; otherwise valid. For a deaf person, daytime presence is a barrier but not nighttime. The man's slave girl: Abu Yusuf says her presence invalidates, Muhammad says valid. For the woman's slave girl, the fatwa is that khalwa is valid. Lastly, if a dog belonging to the woman is present, al-Halwani says it invalidates because it may bark; if it belongs to the man, it is valid.

Key Terms
خلوة صحيحة (khalwa sahiha): A valid seclusion between husband and wife that, if followed by divorce before consummation, obligates the full dower.
مرض (marad): An illness that prevents intercourse or causes harm if intercourse occurs.
محرم (muhrim): One who has entered the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah.

Distinguishing Ramadan Fasting from Other Fasts in Khalwa
The distinction between Ramadan fast and other fasts regarding khalwa validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ramadan fasting invalidates khalwa whereas expiatory and vow fasts do not, and identify the dispute regarding supererogatory fasting.

Core Idea
It is the necessity of expiation that makes a fast invalidate khalwa — where no kaffara, no invalidation.

The passage continues by clarifying that not all fasts affect khalwa equally. The key factor is whether breaking the fast requires expiation (kaffara). This distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rule in various fasting scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rule
Fasting in Ramadan invalidates khalwa because expiation is mandatory if intercourse occurs.
2
Compare with other fasts
Expiatory (kaffara) and vow (nadhr) fasts do not invalidate because no expiation is due; only qada (make-up) is required.
3
Analyze supererogatory (nafl) fasting
The text notes a dispute: one narration equates nafl to obligatory fasting, but the main position is that nafl does not invalidate.
4
Apply the rationale to a new case
The same logic applies to prayer: obligatory prayer invalidates khalwa but supererogatory does not.

Third Party Presence: When Does It Invalidate Khalwa?
Rules for third party presence during khalwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for evaluating whether the presence of a third party (awake, asleep, blind, deaf, or unaware) invalidates khalwa, considering awareness and time of day.

Core Idea
Khalwa is invalid if a third party is present who could potentially be aware of the couple's actions — awareness and time matter.

The passage extends beyond physical barriers to discuss the presence of other people or animals during seclusion. The central principle is that for khalwa to be valid, the couple must be truly alone without anyone who can perceive their situation. This lesson unpacks the nuanced rules for various third parties.

Guided Path
1
Assess the third party's awareness
If a person understands the situation (y'aqilu halahuma), khalwa is invalid. A sleeping person is considered aware because they might be pretending.
2
Consider time of day
For some categories (sleeping person, deaf person), daytime invalidates but nighttime does not.
3
Evaluate sensory ability
Blindness: if the blind person knows the situation (can infer), invalid; otherwise valid. Deaf: daytime invalid, night valid.
4
Apply to a scenario
Example: a blind person present who cannot tell what is happening (e.g., at night) does not invalidate.

The presence of the woman's own slave girl does not invalidate khalwa; the man's slave girl is disputed; a woman's dog invalidates if it barks.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Khulwah with a majbub triggers half mahr, not full, because actual intercourse is impossible.

A majbub (a man with both penis and testicles removed) performs valid khulwah (seclusion) with his wife and then divorces her. What is her mahr? This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa rules full mahr, while his two foremost students hold for half. Understanding their reasoning reveals how the Hanafi school evaluates the legal effect of khulwah when consummation is physically impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, recognize the scenario: a majbub secludes with his wife (valid khulwah), then divorces her. The ruling sought is the mahr obligation.
2
State the Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa: full mahr. Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): half mahr. Both agree iddah is wajib by consensus.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Why the difference? Abu Hanifa considers valid khulwah equivalent to actual consummation in triggering full mahr. The Sahibayn argue that since intercourse is impossible (majbub), khulwah cannot substitute—so it is like divorce before consummation, giving half mahr.
The key is whether khulwah's legal effect depends on the possibility of intercourse.
4

Despite the disagreement on mahr, both sides agree iddah is wajib. Why? As a precaution (ihtiyat) to avoid uncertainty about potential pregnancy, even if impossible. This shows that iddah can be triggered by the mere possibility of intercourse at the time of khulwah.
Iddah serves a different purpose—to establish non-pregnancy—so it is more cautious.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars preferred the Sahibayn's view (half mahr) as the operative ruling for fatwa, considering that khulwah alone cannot confer full mahr when intercourse is impossible.
This reflects the practical tendency to adopt the more cautious position regarding financial obligations.

Rulings on Seclusion with Impotent, Eunuch, and Woman with Atresia
Rulings on mahr and 'iddah after seclusion with specific physical conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on mahr and 'iddah when a man secludes with his wife and then divorces, for cases of impotence, eunuch, and female atresia.

Core Idea
Full mahr is due after seclusion and divorce even if impotent; half mahr and no 'iddah for atresia because intercourse is impossible.

This passage addresses three specific situations where consummation is impossible or incomplete. The rulings on mahr and 'iddah depend on the nature of the impediment. We will examine each case and the reasoning behind the differing outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases
Note the three distinct parties: impotent ('anin), eunuch (khasi), and a woman with atresia (ratqa'). The spouse is affected differently.
Impotent and eunuch are male; atresia is a female condition.
2
Understand the ruling for impotent and eunuch
If either secludes with his wife (without obstacles) and then divorces, she gets the full mahr by consensus. Seclusion is treated like actual consummation for mahr.
Consensus among Hanafi imams.
3
Analyze the ruling for atresia
Seclusion with a woman who has atresia (vaginal closure) gives only half mahr and no 'iddah. Understand why: the condition validates seclusion for mahr but prevents 'iddah because intercourse is impossible.
Half mahr is half of the specified mahr or mahr mithl; 'iddah is waived because the purpose (verifying pregnancy) cannot occur.
4
Explain the underlying principles
Full mahr after seclusion normally assumes possibility of consummation. For atresia, the impossibility of intercourse reduces mahr and eliminates 'iddah. For impotent/eunuch, possibility exists but may not be realized, yet seclusion still obligates full mahr.
The ruling on impotent is based on the principle that seclusion presumes ability unless proven otherwise.

Exchange Marriage: Validity and Mahr
Hanafi validation of exchange marriage with mahr mithl, contra Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on exchange marriage (giving daughter on condition of receiving daughter/sister) and distinguish it from prohibited shighar.

Core Idea
Exchange marriage is valid if each woman receives mahr mithl; it is not shighar.

This passage addresses a controversial form of marriage where one man marries his daughter to another on condition that the other marries his daughter or sister. The Hanafi school validates both contracts, unlike the Shafi'i school which considers it prohibited shighar. We will analyze the Hanafi reasoning and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two men exchange female relatives in marriage. One contract is made conditional on the other.
Typically: A marries his daughter to B, on condition B marries his daughter to A.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
Both marriages are valid (ja'iz) and each woman gets mahr mithl (the customary mahr for women of her status).
This applies even if the condition is explicit.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i considers this as shighar (exchange marriage without mahr), which is forbidden and invalid. The Hanafis disagree.
Shighar in hadith is prohibited because it denies women their mahr.
4
Understand the Hanafi distinction
According to the text, this is not shighar because the women receive mahr mithl. The condition is valid as a deal between the men, but it does not deprive the women of their right.
The text refers to a prior discussion (probably about shighar) that distinguishes the two.
5
Determine the practical outcome
In Hanafi, the marriages stand. In Shafi'i, they would be invalid and must be annulled. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
Note that the condition does not affect the mahr of the women.

The master owns the children of his female slave, so he owes their nafaqah—not the father.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents four distinct cases involving children born from marriages where one or both parents are slaves. The rulings clarify that nafaqah (maintenance) follows ownership: if the child is owned, the owner pays; if free, the mother or her relatives bear the cost. These rules reflect the Hanafi integration of property law with family obligations.
