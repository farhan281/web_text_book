The Voidness of Conditional Discharge
Ruling on conditional ibra being void with rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional ibra and explain why it is void based on the distinction between tamlik and isqat.

Core Idea
A conditional discharge of debt is void because ibra is a transfer of ownership, not a pure relinquishment.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in debt law: can a creditor forgive a debt on the condition of a future event? The Hanafi school answers with a clear 'no', and the reasoning hinges on understanding the legal nature of ibra. By unpacking the classification of ibra as tamlik (granting ownership) versus pure isqat (relinquishment), you will see why conditions are impermissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the passage and locate the key statement: conditional discharges are void (batil). Note that the text opens with concrete examples of conditional phrases.
The ruling is explicit: 'fa hadha kulluhu batil'.
2
Understand the nature of ibra
The passage states that ibra is a form of tamlik (granting ownership). Contrast this with pure isqatat (relinquishments) like talaq and itaq. In Hanafi law, tamlik transfers ownership, while isqat removes a right without transfer.
This distinction is the core of the proof.
3
Apply the general principle on conditions
The text asserts that conditional suspension (ta'liq) is specific to pure isqatat and does not extend beyond them. Since ibra is tamlik, it cannot be conditional.
The principle is a qa'ida: 'al-ta'liq bi al-shart yakhtass bi al-isqatat al-mahda'.
4
Derive the ruling for the examples
Apply the principle to each example: 'When tomorrow comes, it is yours' – conditional; 'If you pay half, you are free from the rest' – conditional; all are void. Conclude that an ibra must be absolute.
The practical outcome: the debt remains due until a valid unconditional discharge.

And someone who has a thousand dirhams due from another, and says: 'When tomorrow comes, it is yours', or 'You are free from it', or 'If you pay me half, then you are free from the remaining half': all this is void; because discharge (ibra) is a form of granting ownership (tamlik), and conditional suspension is specific to pure relinquishments (isqatat) such as divorce and emancipation, so it does not extend beyond them.

Commentary
This passage rules on making a discharge of debt (ibra) conditional upon a future event. The Hanafi position is that such conditional discharges are invalid. The reasoning given is that ibra is a form of tamlik (granting ownership of the debt to the debtor), not a pure isqat (relinquishment). Conditional clauses are only valid in pure isqatat like talaq (divorce) and itaq (emancipation), which are pure removals of a right. Since ibra involves transferring ownership, it cannot be made conditional. The examples illustrate various attempts to condition the discharge, all of which are void.

Key Terms
إبراء (ibra): A discharge of debt, considered a form of tamlik (granting ownership) rather than pure isqat.
إسقاطات محضة (isqatat mahda): Pure relinquishments of rights, like talaq and itaq, which can be conditional.

Ruling on Refusing Compensation for Destroyed Property
States procedure when the usurper offers but the owner refuses compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure when a usurper offers compensation and the owner refuses, and explain the role of the judge in compelling acceptance.

Core Idea
The judge can compel the owner to accept compensation, preventing indefinite liability.

This passage from al-Waqi'at addresses a practical situation: a person destroys another's property and offers its value, but the owner refuses both the payment and absolution. How is such a stalemate resolved? The ruling gives the judge a crucial role in ensuring justice and finality. Understanding this procedure is essential for anyone dealing with liability for destroyed property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key actions
List the sequence: usurper destroys the garment, offers its value, owner refuses to accept and refuses to absolve. Note that the property is no longer returnable in kind (istihlak).
2
Understand the legal consequence
The usurper remains liable as long as the owner refuses. The conflict is unresolved. The ruling provides a mechanism to end this stalemate.
3
Locate the judicial intervention
The case is elevated to the judge (al-hakim). The judge's authority includes compulsion (yujbiruhu) – i.e., forcing the owner to accept the offered value.
4
Determine the effect of the judge's action
Once the judge compels acceptance, the usurper's liability is discharged. The owner cannot later claim further compensation for that property.

Source Text

قال في «الواقعات»: إذا استهلك لرجل ثوباً، فجاء إليه بقيمته، فقال : لا آخذها، ولا أجعلك في حلّ يُرفع الأمر إلى الحاكم حتى يُجبره
He said in al-Waqi'at: If a person destroyed a garment belonging to a man, and then came to him with its value, and he [the owner] said: 'I will not take it, and I will not absolve you,' the matter is raised to the judge until he compels him.
Source pages p. 146

Commentary
This passage from the work al-Waqi'at (a collection of later Hanafi fatawa) addresses a situation where a usurper (or destroyer) has destroyed a garment (istahlaka thawban) and then offers its compensatory value (qimah) to the owner. The owner refuses to accept the payment and refuses to absolve the usurper (la aj'aluka fi hill). The ruling is that the matter is to be brought before the judge (al-hakim), who will compel (yujbiruhu) the owner to accept the value. This prevents the owner from unjustly refusing compensation and keeping the usurper in a state of liability. The underlying principle is that the usurper becomes entitled to discharge his liability by paying the value, and the owner cannot unreasonably withhold acceptance. The judge enforces this to avoid ongoing conflict and to uphold the right of the usurper to be free from further claim. The passage is incomplete in the OCR but conveys the main ruling.

Key Terms
الواقعات (al-Waqi'at): A later Hanafi fatwa collection compiled by Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi, containing non-Zahir al-Riwayah rulings.
استهلك (istahlaka): To destroy or consume a property (here a garment) so that it cannot be returned in kind.
قيمة (qimah): Compensatory value, market price of the destroyed item.
