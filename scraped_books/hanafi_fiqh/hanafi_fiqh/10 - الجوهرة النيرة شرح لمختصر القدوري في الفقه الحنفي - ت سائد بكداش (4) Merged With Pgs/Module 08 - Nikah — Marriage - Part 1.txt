Marriage Claim Compromise and Hadd Penalty Details
Rulings on compromise in marriage claims and hadd penalties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the validity conditions of compromise in marriage claims and in hudud penalties within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A marriage claim compromise is valid like khul' only when the woman pays the man; compromise in hudud slander before litigation drops the penalty.

In the Hanafi school, compromise (ṣulḥ) is a legitimate tool to resolve disputes, but its validity depends on the nature of the claim and the timing. This passage from Kitāb al-Ṣulḥ illustrates two scenarios: a marriage dispute and a slander (qadhf) penalty. Understanding these distinctions is crucial to applying the rules of ṣulḥ correctly, especially when the claims involve fixed penalties or personal rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two marriage claim scenarios
Read the first part: a man claims marriage, woman denies and pays to drop the claim; then a woman claims marriage, man pays to drop. Note the rulings.
The ruling differs based on who pays.
2
Analyze the condition for validity
The first case is valid because it is analogous to khul' (divorce initiated by wife with compensation). The second is invalid because the man is paying to dismiss a claim that may be legitimate, resembling a bribe.
The principle: matters of Muslims are assumed valid when possible.
3
Understand the hudud distinction
For qadhf (slander), compromise before litigation drops the penalty; after litigation, the penalty remains and no payment is due. For theft and zina, compromise never affects the hadd because it is a right of Allah.
Qadhf is considered a right of Allah in Hanafi school, but before court the claimant can waive it.
4
Apply the ruling to hypothetical cases
Consider a scenario: A man falsely accuses a woman of adultery, then pays her before court to drop the case. The hadd falls and no payment is owed. If he pays her after court, the hadd is not dropped.
The timing of the compromise is decisive.

Sulh in Marriage Claims: The Khul' Analogy
Ruling on sulh when one party claims marriage and the other denies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two cases of sulh in marriage claims and explain why the first is valid as analogous to khul' while the second is not.

Core Idea
Sulh in marriage claims is valid only when it mirrors khul'—the woman pays to end a disputed marriage.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira presents a sharp contrast: when a man claims marriage and the woman pays him to drop the claim, the settlement is valid and treated like khul'. But if the woman claims and the man pays her, the settlement is generally invalid. Understanding the reasoning behind this asymmetry will deepen your grasp of how Hanafi fiqh uses analogy (qiyas) and the principle that Muslims' affairs are presumed valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the source carefully. There are two scenarios: (1) a man claims marriage, woman denies, she pays him to drop the claim; (2) a woman claims marriage, man denies, he pays her to drop the claim. Note the different outcomes.
The gender and direction of payment are crucial.
2
Understand the first scenario as khul'
The text says the first scenario is 'in the meaning of khul''. Analyse why: the woman is paying to end a potential marriage (the claimed marriage). In khul', the wife pays to obtain separation from her husband. Here, the claim is unresolved, but the settlement effectively prevents the marriage from being established, which is similar to ending it.
The payment compensates the man for giving up his claim to marriage.
3
Analyse the invalidity of the second scenario
The man pays the woman to drop her claim. The commentator explains that this cannot be a valid exchange. If the payment is for separation (furqa), the husband should not pay for divorce (he receives, not gives). If it is not for separation, then the payment has no corresponding benefit, so the sulh is invalid.
Contrast with khul': the wife pays; here the husband pays, which reverses the normal flow.
4
Note the textual variation
Some copies of the text rule that the second scenario is valid, treating the payment as an increase in the mahr (dower). This suggests that the ruling is not completely settled and that later Hanafi scholars debated it.
This variation shows how fiqh evolves through different riwayat (transmissions).
5
Apply the principle of presumption of validity
The commentator invokes the principle that Muslims' affairs are assumed valid if possible. The first scenario can be seen as a valid khul'-like transaction, so it is upheld. The second cannot be construed as a valid exchange, so it fails.
This principle (haml al-Muslimin 'ala al-sihha) is a key tool in Hanafi fiqh.

Gift to a Minor: Father's and Stranger's Gifts
Ruling on gift to minor: father vs. stranger

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the validity conditions of a father's gift and a stranger's gift to a minor, and apply the rule of guardianship possession in gift transactions.

Core Idea
A father's gift to his minor son is owned by contract alone; a stranger's gift requires the father's possession.

Gift transactions involving minors raise questions of legal capacity. The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between a gift from the father and one from a stranger. In this lesson, you will explore the rationale behind this distinction and learn when the minor actually owns the gifted property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Note that the text presents two distinct scenarios: a father giving to his minor son, and a stranger giving to a minor. The ruling differs in each case, and the key variable is who is the donor and who is the guardian.
2
Understand the basis for the father's gift
The father's gift is complete by contract alone because the father's possession substitutes for the minor's possession. The legal principle is that the guardian's hand is like the child's hand for beneficial acquisitions.
This applies only if the object is in the father's control or his depositary's hand.
3
Recognize exceptions to the father's gift
If the object is in the possession of a third party (e.g., pledged, usurped, or sold with a void sale), the father must take new possession, because he does not have effective control.
4
Explain the stranger's gift
For a stranger's gift, the gift is not complete until the father (or the person maintaining the child) takes possession on the minor's behalf, because the stranger does not have guardianship.
If the father is dead, the person who maintains the child may take possession.
5
Apply the rulings
Practice with scenarios: determine when a gift is valid and what actions are needed to transfer ownership to the minor.

Gift to a Mahram Relative: Rules of Revocation
States ruling and conditions for irrevocable gift to mahram relative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revocation of a gift to a mahram relative and identify the conditions that permit or prevent revocation.

Core Idea
A gift to a free mahram relative becomes irrevocable after delivery.

The Hanafi school generally allows a donor to revoke a gift after delivery, but this right is restricted when the donee is a close blood relative (mahram) to encourage family ties. This lesson examines the ruling and its conditions, including the effect of non-delivery, partial alienation, re-gifting, and the case where the donee is a slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the matn: 'And if he gives a gift to a mahram relative, then there is no revocation in it.' Understand that the default rule of permissibility of revocation is excepted for mahram relatives.
Mahram means a relative with whom marriage is permanently prohibited (e.g., father, brother, son).
2
Check the condition of delivery
The commentary clarifies that revocation is prohibited only after delivery (taslim). Before delivery, the donor may revoke because the gift is not yet complete.
Delivery (qabd) is a condition for the gift to be binding in Hanafi law.
3
Examine the freedom condition
The commentary adds: the donee must be free. If the donee is a slave, even if a mahram, the gift is not considered a proper sila rahim because a slave cannot own or dispose of property. Hence revocation remains permissible.
This shows the underlying rationale: upholding kinship ties (sila rahim) is the basis for the exception.
4
Apply related rules
The text also mentions: If the donor sells or gives away part of the gifted property, he may revoke the remainder but not the alienated part. Also, if the donee re-gifts the item to a third party and then the original donor revokes, the first donee may recover the item from the third party.
These are logical extensions of the general law of hiba.

Hiba Between Spouses: No Revocation
States that gifts between spouses are irrevocable like those to mahram, with timing conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revocation of gifts between spouses and identify the time of the gift as the decisive factor.

Core Idea
Gifts between spouses are irrevocable because marriage is treated like blood kinship; timing determines revocability.

This passage extends the rule of irrevocable gifts from mahram relatives to spouses. The key principle is that marriage creates a relationship analogous to blood lineage, as evidenced by inheritance rights. However, the timing matters: if the gift occurs before marriage, revocation is still permitted even if they later marry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main rule
Read the first sentence: 'وكذلك ما وهب أحد الزوجين للآخر' meaning gifts between spouses are similarly (irrevocable) like gifts to mahram.
Note the word 'وكذلك' linking to the previous rule.
2
Understand the rationale
The author explains that marriage is treated like lineage (أجريت مجرى القرابة) and evidence is inheritance rights.
This is the core analogy (qiyas) grounding the rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two valid ways for a woman to claim a foundling and explain why her claim alone is insufficient.

Core Idea
A woman's claim of a foundling is invalid unless her husband confirms or she brings evidence – lineage cannot be attributed to a third party by her word alone.

This lesson examines a specific rule in Hanafi family law: when a woman (other than the mother) claims a foundling as her child, her claim is not automatically accepted. The ruling protects the rights of the husband, since the child's lineage would be attributed to him. The matn from Quduri gives two conditions that make such a claim valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is a woman claiming a foundling as her own child. The default ruling is that her claim is not valid (لَا يَصِحُّ) – it has no legal effect.
This default protects third parties (the husband) from unwanted lineage.
2
List the two exceptions
The claim becomes valid in two ways: (1) the husband confirms her claim (تصديق الزوج), or (2) she produces legal evidence (إقامة البينة) proving the child is hers.
Both conditions are alternatives; only one is needed.
3
Understand the underlying reason
The text gives the reason: 'because it involves attributing lineage to someone else' (لأن فيه حمل النسب على الغير). The 'other' is the husband – acknowledging the child would make it his son/daughter without his consent.
This reason is based on the principle that one person's statement cannot bind another (إقرار المرء على نفسه لا على غيره).
4
Note cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is that the husband's confirmation is needed. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow a woman's claim without husband's confirmation if she can prove the child is hers through clear evidence such as birth records or witnesses.
The practical consequence: in the Hanafi school, a woman's unilateral claim is insufficient.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on joint motherhood

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on joint motherhood and evaluate the tarjih (preferred view).

Core Idea
Lineage (nāsab) is a legal status—Abu Hanifa separates it from biological birth, allowing joint motherhood.

This passage presents a fascinating intra-Hanafi disagreement over two women each claiming the same foundling. Abu Hanifa rules the child is the son of both, while his two main disciples disagree. Understanding the reasoning behind each view reveals a key principle: lineage in Hanafi fiqh is a construct for legal benefits, not merely a biological fact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Two women each produce evidence (bayyinah) that a foundling is her son. They both claim the same child. What is the legal issue being debated here?
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules that the child is the son of both women. His key reasoning: 'إثبات النسب لا يقتضي إثبات الولادة' (establishing lineage does not require establishing birth).
Nāsab is a legal construct, not merely a biological fact.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the child is not the son of either woman. Their reasoning: it is impossible for two women to have given birth to the same child (استحالة أن تلد امرأتان ولداً واحداً).
They equate nāsab with biological motherhood.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response to the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa separates legal lineage from physical birth. Lineage carries legal consequences: prohibition of marriage-affinity (muṣāhirah), custody rights (ḥaḍānah), and inheritance (irth). These benefits function regardless of actual birth.
The dispute is about the nature of nāsab: is it purely biological or a legal status?
5
Determine the mufta bihi and why
Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) because it is more beneficial for the child and avoids leaving the child motherless. Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah prefers Abu Hanifa's view.
The school weights practical benefit and legal consequence over biological impossibility.

A dhimmi's claim to a foundling establishes lineage but cannot change the child's Islam – benefit is accepted, harm is rejected.

This lesson examines a nuanced case from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira. This ruling is an example of istihsan: preferring a ruling that achieves benefit and prevents harm over a strict analogy. Strict analogy might say the child follows the father's religion, but istihsan overrides this to preserve the child's Islam.
Istihsan is a key Hanafi source of law.

Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow the child to follow the father's faith because lineage determines religion. The Hanafi position is based on the child's presumed Islam from the location and the principle of avoiding harm.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When two slaves claim a foundling, the child’s freedom or slavery depends on which principle prevails: the claimants’ status or the foundling’s default freedom.

In this lesson, we examine a short but revealing intra-Hanafi disagreement. The matn states that if two male slaves both claim that a foundling is their son, the foundling is considered their son. However, the two imams disagree on the child’s legal status: does he become a slave, or does he remain free? The answer turns on how we weigh the effect of the claimants’ own slave status against the default principle that a foundling is presumed free.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Common Ground
The act is the id‘ā’ (claim) of two slaves (mamlūkayn) that a foundling is their son. All agree that the foundling becomes their son (nasab is established).

State Abu Hanifa’s Position
Abu Hanifa rules: the foundling becomes a slave ('abd). The implied basis: because the claimants are slaves, their status attaches to the child – the principle that a child’s status follows the lower status of the parents.

3
State Muhammad’s Position
Muhammad al-Shaybani rules: the foundling remains free (ḥurr). His basis: the foundling’s default status is freedom (al-aṣl fī al-laqīṭ al-ḥurriyya), and the claim of paternity does not override that default.
Muhammad prioritizes the foundling’s presumed freedom over the claimants’ slave status.
4

The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the child is a slave and can be bought, sold, etc. Under Muhammad, the child is free and retains full legal capacity.
This dispute affects the child’s legal capacity and the rights of custody and inheritance.
5
Note the Authority Weight
Both positions are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah. Later Hanafi works (e.g., al-Hidayah) often prefer Abu Hanifa’s view, but the text itself does not declare a muftā bihī.
The mufti should be aware of both views and the tarjīḥ in later sources.

Finder Cannot Marry the Foundling
Stating prohibition and reason for the finder marrying the foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the finder marrying a foundling, explain the underlying principle of wilaya, and identify the proper guardian for a foundling's marriage.

Core Idea
Without wilaya, the finder cannot marry the foundling; only the judge holds that authority.

A foundling (laqīṭ) is an abandoned child with no known family. The finder (multaqiṭ) often assumes responsibility for the child's care. However, when it comes to marriage, the finder has no legal standing to act as guardian (walī). This lesson explores the ruling and its logical basis in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the finder contracting marriage for the foundling. The ruling is that it is not permissible (batil).
Batil means the contract is legally void and produces no effects.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is aqli (logical reasoning): the finder has no ownership, kinship, or delegated authority over the foundling.
Dalil aqli relies on necessary logical consequences.
3
Understand the principle of wilaya
In Hanafi law, marriage of a minor (or anyone unable to consent) requires a guardian with wilaya. Only relatives (asabah) or the judge possess this.
Wilaya is the legal capacity to bind another person in a contract.
4
Note the exception
The judge (qadi) is the default guardian for those without a family guardian. He may contract marriage for a foundling if it is in the child's interest.
This is a standard rule: 'al-sultānu waliy man lā waliya lahu'.
5
Consider cross-madhhab differences
Some schools allow a non-relative to act as guardian in cases of necessity, but Hanafis strictly limit wilaya to relatives and the judge.
This ikhtilaf has practical implications for foundling marriages in non-Muslim jurisdictions.

A husband disappears without trace: should his wife be allowed to remarry after a period of time? This lesson examines the disagreement between the Hanafi and Maliki schools. Core Idea
Marriage is not trade; a ma'dhun's permission is limited to trade, so any marriage contract is void without the master's approval.

The ma'dhun (permitted slave) is authorized to trade, but marriage is outside that scope. This lesson explains why he cannot marry or arrange marriages for his slaves, and what happens if he nevertheless consummates such an unauthorized marriage. The mahr becomes a debt that is only collectible after he becomes free.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling

The permission (idhn) given to a ma'dhun is exclusively for trade (tijarah). Marriage is not a trade activity, so it falls outside the scope of his authority.

If the ma'dhun marries without permission and consummates, the marriage is not validated retroactively. However, the wife is entitled to a mahr (dower), which becomes a debt on the ma'dhun. The text says 'فالمهر عليه بعد الحرية' – the mahr is upon him after freedom.

The commentary provides examples where only one expression suffices without acceptance: when a man marries his female relative (like a cousin) as his own wife, or when a guardian marries two minors (both parties under his guardianship), or an agent acting for both sides. In such cases, the single statement 'I married...' serves as both offer and acceptance. This is the Hanafi position, but Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) disagreed, requiring two separate expressions. The commentary then justifies the use of the future tense by istihsan (juristic preference), overruling qiyas (analogy) which would reject it since the future tense is normally interrogative or conditional. The reason for istihsan is that matters of marriage do not involve bargaining or haggling, so the intent of the speaker is clear despite the grammatical form.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar enumerating women permanently prohibited for marriage. The commentary follows the order of the matn. First, it discusses the prohibition of mothers and grandmothers (f: ancestors by blood), all of whom are haram regardless of degree. The author corrects the matn's phrasing regarding 'بأمه' noting that 'أمه' without preposition is grammatically correct, citing Quran 33:37. A secondary point distinguishes between 'zawwaj' meaning marriage and joining in Paradise. Next, the commentary covers daughters, granddaughters, sisters, nieces, and aunts (paternal and maternal), including those of higher generations like great-aunts. The wisdom is to honor kinship and prevent disrespect through sexual relations. Then it addresses the wife's mother (f: the mother of a woman a man marries). This prohibition is absolute—by mere valid contract, even without consummation—as derived from Quran 4:23 which does not mention consummation. However, if the marriage to the daughter is fasid (invalid), the mother-in-law is not prohibited unless actual consummation, lustful gaze, or touch occurs. Next is the wife's daughter (stepdaughter), which requires consummation with the mother; she is prohibited only if the husband has had intercourse with her mother, whether the stepdaughter is in his custody or not. For fasid marriage, the same conditions apply: consummation or equivalent actions trigger prohibition.

Prohibition of the Stepdaughter and Father's Wife (Sabab)
Commentary on al-Quduri's matn regarding stepdaughter and father's wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the prohibition of the stepdaughter (rabibah) and the wife of one's father, distinguishing between valid and fasid marriage.

Core Idea
The stepdaughter is prohibited only after consummation with her mother; the wife of one's father is prohibited by valid contract, but conditionally in a fasid marriage.

Marriage creates two important prohibitions: the stepdaughter (daughter of one's wife) and the wife of one's father. The Hanafi school requires consummation with the mother for the stepdaughter to become prohibited, while the father's wife is prohibited by mere valid contract. This lesson examines the conditions and the Quranic proofs (4:22 and 4:23).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the stepdaughter (rabibah)
The stepdaughter (daughter of one's wife) is prohibited only if the husband has consummated the marriage with her mother. The text says: 'Nor the daughter of his wife with whom he has consummated.'
This is based on Quran 4:23: 'وَرَبَائِبُكُمُ اللَّاتِي فِي حُجُورِكُم' which includes the condition 'with whom you have consummated' (اللاتي دخلتم بهن).
2
Distinguish between valid and fasid for stepdaughter
If the marriage to the mother is valid, consummation triggers the prohibition of the stepdaughter. If the marriage is fasid, the stepdaughter is still prohibited after consummation (as the text mentions 'دخل بها').
The commentator extends the prohibition to the stepdaughter's children as well.
3
Identify the ruling on the wife of one's father
The wife of one's father (and grandfathers) is prohibited by mere valid contract, based on Quran 4:22: 'وَلَا تَنكِحُوا مَا نَكَحَ آبَاؤُكُم'.
The verb 'نَكَحَ' includes both contract and intercourse, so prohibition applies by valid contract.
4
Distinguish for fasid marriage for father's wife
If the father's marriage was fasid, the son is prohibited only if consummation or equivalent occurred.
Same principle as with the wife's mother.
5
Apply to scenarios
Example: A man marries a woman and consummates. Her daughter (from a previous marriage) becomes his mahram permanently. If he divorces the mother, he cannot marry the stepdaughter.

Prohibition of Women Due to Affinity (Musaharat)
Explains prohibition of wives' mothers, daughters, and paternal/son's wives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of women prohibited by affinity and apply the condition of consummation to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
The mother of a wife is prohibited immediately; a stepdaughter only after consummation.

In this lesson, we study the prohibitions of marriage that arise from affinity (musaharat) — relationships created by marriage. The text, based on the Hanafi school, explains the rulings from Quranic verses and clarifies the conditions under which these women become permanently forbidden. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly identifying permissible marriage partners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four types of women prohibited by affinity: wife's mother, wife's daughter (stepdaughter), father's wife, and son's wife. Note that these are permanent prohibitions.
The text also extends to grandfathers and grandsons.
2
Locate the Quranic proof
For each category, find the corresponding verse: wife's mother (Quran 4:23), wife's daughter (4:23 by implication), father's wife (4:22), son's wife (4:23).
The verse for son's wife uses "حَلَائِلُ أَبْنَائِكُم".
3
Analyze the condition of consummation
Determine which prohibitions are immediate (wife's mother upon valid contract; father's wife upon valid contract or intercourse) and which require consummation (stepdaughter; all cases when the marriage is fasid).
Lustful touching or looking is equivalent to consummation in the Hanafi school.
4
Examine the exception for a son's slave-girl
A son's slave-girl is not prohibited to the father unless the son has had intercourse with her, because she is not called "حليلة" (lawful wife).
This is based on the wording of the verse.
5
Apply to a scenario

Remember: mother is immediate; daughter requires consummation, which occurred.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from Hanafi marriage law: a man marries a free woman but has not yet consummated the marriage. Since the marriage contract establishes farash over the free wife, she is already 'under his farash'. The prohibition is on combining sisters under his right to intercourse, whether that right comes from marriage or ownership.

This lesson addresses a specific prohibited marriage combination: two sisters in a single contract. The ruling is clear from the Hanafi texts: the contract is batil (void) from the start. The marriage is batil (void) ab initio. The man must be separated from both.
