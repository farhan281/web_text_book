Learning Objective

Core Idea
The umm walad's usufruct is not compensable upon destruction; the mudabbara's corpus is.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a usurper who causes the death of an umm walad (mother of the master's child) or a mudabbara (slave promised freedom at death) must pay compensation. Abu Hanifa distinguishes sharply based on the nature of ownership: only usufruct for the umm walad, but full property for the mudabbara. The two companions argue by analogy that both have a recognized property value.

The mufta bihi follows the Sahibayn, so the usurper pays for both.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A usurper is not liable for natural death, but must pay diya through his 'āqila for external causes.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah presents a series of rulings by Abu Hanifa on the liability of a person who usurps a free minor. The key distinction is the cause of death: natural causes absolve the usurper, while external causes (beast, snake, third-party killing) trigger diya on the usurper's 'āqila. Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying Hanafi tort law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
The usurper's liability depends on the cause of death: natural vs. external. If natural, no liability. If external, liability rests on the usurper's 'āqila.
2
Categorize the Causes of Death
List the scenarios: natural illness (no liability), beast/snake attack (liability on 'āqila), accidental killing by a third party (heirs have option), intentional killing (heirs choose execution or diya from usurper's 'āqila), minor's own tort (no liability on usurper), suicide (no liability).

The 'āqila pays the diya for accidental or external causes. For intentional killing, the usurper's 'āqila pays only if the heirs choose that option, and then they recover from the killer's wealth.

Ruling on Minor's Suicide in Usurper's Hand
States no liability on usurper when minor causes own death by suicide or fall

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the liability of a usurper when a minor in his custody causes his own death, and explain the principle of direct causation (al-janayah ala al-nafs).

Core Idea
A usurper is not liable for a minor’s death if the minor kills himself, because the minor is the direct cause of his own injury.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the law of usurpation (ghasb): what happens when the minor held by the usurper takes his own life? The Hanafi ruling is clear—no liability on the usurper. The reasoning rests on the principle that the minor is the jani (perpetrator) against himself, and the usurper’s possession does not create liability for self-inflicted harm. This case sharpens your understanding of causation (al-sababiyyah) in liability.

Identify the act and the parties
The act is defined by two alternatives: the minor kills himself (qatala nafsahu) or throws himself from a riding beast (taraha nafsahu min dabbatin). The parties are the minor (the actor) and the usurper (the possessor).
Note that the minor is the active agent in both cases.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that there is no liability (la daman) upon the usurper. This is a definitive declaration of non-liability.
3
Extract the legal reason (ta'lil)
The text gives the reason directly: 'because he (the minor) is the perpetrator against himself' (li-annahu huwa al-jani 'ala nafsih). Thus, the minor's own action severs any causal link from the usurper.
This is a ta'lil bi al-'illah: the minor being the jani is the effective cause (illah) for non-liability.
4

The general principle is that liability of the hand (daman al-yad) covers only external harm caused by the possession, not self-inflicted harm. The minor's voluntary act breaks the chain of causation.
Compare with cases where the usurper directly causes harm; then he would be liable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on liability for opening cages, doors, and tethers, and evaluate the Shafi'i distinction of immediacy in the bird case.

Core Idea
Liability for escape requires direct causation; volition of the creature or independent external cause breaks the chain.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a series of cases about a person who opens a cage, a door, or unties a restraint, resulting in escape of a bird, slave, animal, or liquid. The rulings differ between Abu Hanifa, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and al-Shafi'i, revealing deep principles about causation (tasabbub) and directness (mubashara) in Hanafi law of torts. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for applying daman correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its consequence
The act is opening or untying a restraint (cage, door, tether). The consequence is the escape of a living being or liquid. Determine the directness of the causal link.
2
Distinguish between beings with and without volition
Birds and sane slaves have independent volition (ikhtiyar) to move. Insane slaves and animals lack volition and depend entirely on the act. This distinction affects liability.
Hanafis: volition severs causation unless the act overcomes it (e.g., scaring).
3
Compare the Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa holds no liability in all cases because the escape is not directly caused. Muhammad holds liability for animals and insane slaves, but not for sane slaves. Understand the basis in directness.
Muhammad views animal as lacking volition, so untying is direct cause.
4
Apply the principle to the waterskin case
If the fat is liquid, untying causes immediate flow — direct causation, so liability. If solid and melts by sun, the sun is an independent cause — no liability.
Reinforces that liability requires the act alone to produce the loss.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of cases about liability when someone opens a container or restraint from which a living creature or liquid escapes. Dispute on Mingled Dirhams: Ibn Shubruma vs Abu Hanifa
Complete dispute on ownership of remaining dirham when two dirhams and one dirham are mingled.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on proportional ownership of mingled goods and contrast it with Ibn Shubruma's reasoning based on tracing certain loss.

Core Idea
When goods are inextricably mixed, each owner becomes a co-owner of every part—loss is shared proportionally, not by tracing individual items.

This passage presents a classic dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Kufan scholar Ibn Shubruma over the division of a remaining dirham after two dirhams from a mixed pool of three are lost. The disagreement hinges on whether the loss should be traced to a specific owner or whether each owner's share is proportional to their contribution to the mixture. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi principle of co-ownership arising from mixture (shirkat al-amlak).

Guided Path
1
State the case
Identify the facts: Person A has 2 dirhams, Person B has 1 dirham. The coins are mixed inextricably, then 2 dirhams are lost, leaving 1. What share does each get?
2
Identify Ibn Shubruma's position and proof
Ibn Shubruma argues that of the 2 lost dirhams, one is certainly A's (the second of A's two). The other lost dirham could be either A's second or B's single. So the remaining dirham must be split between them in halves.
He focuses on what is certain and treats the uncertain as equally probable.

Abu Hanifa holds that once mixed, the coins become a partnership (shirkah) where each owns a fraction of every coin: A owns 2/3 of each, B owns 1/3. Any loss is proportional; so the remaining dirham is divided in the same ratio.
This is the Hanafi principle of shirkat al-amlak.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Ibn Shubruma, A gets 1/2; under Abu Hanifa, A gets 2/3. The Hanafi view gives A more because his initial contribution was larger.
The difference is one-sixth of the remaining coin.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
Abu Hanifa's ruling is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school. The narrator initially favored Ibn Shubruma's reasoning but then accepted Abu Hanifa's upon hearing the full argument.
This underscores that apparent intuitive logic (tracing) may conflict with deeper principles of co-ownership.

Definition and ruling for deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define wadi'ah, distinguish it from amanah, and state the Hanafi ruling on the depositary's liability for loss.

Core Idea
A deposit is a trust; the depositary is not liable for loss unless he transgresses.

In this lesson, we examine the opening of the Book of Deposit in Hanafi fiqh. The author defines wadi'ah, explains its linguistic root, and provides the key legal distinction between a deposit (intentional safekeeping) and a general trust (unintentional possession). The central ruling—that the depositary is not liable if the deposit perishes without his fault—is justified by the public need for accepting deposits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its linguistic origin
Note that 'wadi'ah' is derived from 'wada'a' meaning 'to leave'. The poet's verse illustrates this. Understand that the legal meaning builds on this idea of leaving property with another.
2

The definition includes: leaving tangible property (al-a'yan) with a person capable of preserving it, while ownership remains with the depositor. This distinguishes it from a transfer of ownership.

An interdicted slave's consumption without permission creates no immediate liability, only a deferred debt after emancipation.

This passage examines a specific case of liability when a slave consumes a deposit. The ruling depends on whether the slave had the master's permission to possess the deposit. While there is consensus when permission exists, a dispute arises when an interdicted slave acts without permission. Understanding this intra-Hanafi dispute reveals the school's reasoning on slave capacity and the nature of liability.

The act is 'consumption' (istihlak) of a deposit by a slave. Two scenarios: (1) with master's permission (regardless of slave being permitted or interdicted), (2) without master's permission (only interdicted slave considered).
The first scenario has consensus (ijma).

If the slave took possession with the master's permission, he is liable unanimously. The liability becomes a debt (dayn) on the slave, but it is deferred until after emancipation because the slave cannot own property.

The dispute arises when the slave is interdicted and took possession without the master's permission. Here, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold deferred liability, while Abu Yusuf holds immediate liability and sale of the slave.

The two argue that the slave has no legal capacity to incur debt without master's consent, so no immediate liability. Abu Yusuf views the consumption as a tort that creates immediate liability regardless of permission.
The Hanafi response emphasizes the slave's lack of capacity akin to an animal's act.

Emergency Transfer of Deposit: Conditions for Non-Liability
Sets conditions for depositee's liability when transferring deposit in fire or shipwreck

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Transferring a deposit during fire or shipwreck does not incur liability if the depositee proves the emergency and takes the best preservation measure.

This passage expands on the exception from the matn: normally the depositee cannot transfer the deposit without permission, but in genuine emergencies like fire or shipwreck, he may do so without liability. However, this exception is conditioned upon proof of emergency and choosing the safest course. Later Hanafi scholars added further conditions to prevent abuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exception
State the basic ruling: the depositee is not liable when he transfers the deposit to a neighbor during a fire, or to another ship when fearing drowning.
This is a dispensation from the general rule that the depositee must not move the deposit without permission.
2
Understand the rationale
Recognize that the law allows this because transferring is the only practical way to preserve the deposit, and the owner would implicitly consent to such necessary measures.
The rationale is based on necessity and implied consent.
3
Recognize burden of proof
The depositee must prove the emergency with bayyinah (evidence), as he claims a necessity that removes liability, similar to claiming permission to deposit elsewhere.
Without proof, the depositee is liable.
4
Apply Al-Halwani's condition
If the depositee has family members available to safely take the deposit, he must choose them over a stranger. If he gives it to a stranger when family is available, he is liable.
This ensures the best preservation method is used.
5
Apply Khuwahar Zadeh's condition
The fire must be overwhelming (ghalib) and actually surrounding the deposit. If the fire is not of this nature, the depositee is liable.
Mere fear of fire is insufficient; the danger must be imminent and severe.

Depositee Mixing Deposit with Own Property: Liability or Partnership?
Ruling on depositee mixing deposit with his own property indistinguishably

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions when a depositee mixes a deposit with his own property indistinguishably and explain the practical difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's views.

Core Idea
Mixing of same type: complete consumption vs. partial consumption determines owner's right to claim partnership.

When a depositee mixes the deposited item with his own property of the same type so thoroughly that they become indistinguishable, the owner's property is effectively lost. The Hanafi school is divided on the legal consequence: does the owner lose all claim to the mixed property and only hold a debt, or does he retain an option to share ownership? This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's strict view and the more flexible approach of his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
State the main Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: the owner has no recourse to the mixed property; his right becomes a debt (daman) against the depositee. This is because the mixing is considered complete consumption (istihlak min kull wajh).
The key term is 'istihlak' (consumption) – physical inaccessibility equals total loss of property identity.
2
State the opposing view (Sahibayn) and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that if the mixing is with a similar type (e.g., same grain or currency), the owner may choose between compensation or partnership (shirkah) in the mixed property. Their proof: the owner cannot access his property in form (surah) but can access it in meaning (ma'nan) through division, so consumption is partial.
The analogy used by the Sahibayn is a qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) based on the possibility of division.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Returning a like after spending does not remove liability if mixed; ceasing misuse and retaking custody restores trust.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, presents two critical rulings on depositary liability. First, if the depositary spends part of the deposit and later returns an equivalent but mixes it with the remainder, he becomes liable for the entire deposit because mixing constitutes consumption of the trust. Second, if the depositary misuses the deposit (e.g., rides an animal, wears a garment) but then stops and retakes proper custody, the liability ceases and the deposit returns to being a trust. These rulings distinguish between mere intention and actual action, and between mixing and mere return.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct situations: (1) the depositary spends part, returns a like, and mixes it; (2) the depositary transgresses by using the deposit, then ceases and retakes possession.

2
Analyze the first scenario: spending, return, mixing
If the depositary spends part, then returns a like and mixes it with the remainder, he becomes a guarantor for the entire deposit. The mixing is key: it makes identification impossible and thus constitutes consumption.
If mixing does not occur before loss, the remainder perishes as a trust.

If the depositary misuses the deposit (rides animal, wears garment, etc.) but then stops and returns it to his own custody, the liability (damān) ceases. The deposit reverts to being a trust.

Liability of the Depositary for Spending and Mixing
Explains ruling when depositary spends part, returns similar, mixes, and it perishes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that mixing the deposit with one's own property creates guarantee, and distinguish cases where only intention exists without action.

Core Idea
Mixing the deposit with one's own property makes the depositary a guarantor — intention alone does not create liability.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi fiqh of deposits (wadi'ah). The depositary (muda') is initially a trustee, but certain actions transform him into a guarantor. The key issue is when spending part of the deposit and then replacing it with one's own property and mixing it with the remainder results in full liability. Understanding the reasoning (ta'lil) behind this ruling is essential for distinguishing between transgression (ta'addi) and mere intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The depositary spends part of the deposit, then returns a similar amount from his own property, mixes it with the remaining deposit, and then the whole perishes. The ruling is that he guarantees the entire deposit.
The act of mixing is crucial; without it, the liability may differ.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The reason (ta'lil) given is that the depositary has made himself a destroyer (mutlif) by spending some of it and mixing the remainder with his own property. Mixing (khalt) is interpreted as annihilation (istihlak), because the deposit becomes indistinguishable from his property.
The similar amount he returns is his own property; mixing it with the trust property effectively destroys the trust's identity.
3
Contrast with the case of mere intention
If the depositary takes some of the deposit with the intention to spend it, but then changes his mind, returns the exact same item to its place, and it later perishes without any mixing, he does not guarantee it. Intention without a physical act of transgression does not create liability.
The deposit remains in his possession as a trust (amanah) as long as it is not physically altered or mixed.
4
Note the precaution about timing
The mention of mixing is a precaution: if the remainder perishes before mixing, it perishes as a trust and there is no guarantee. But once he mixes, he becomes a transgressor (mut'addi) and is liable for the whole.
This shows that the moment of mixing is what triggers liability, not the earlier spending.
5
Derive the underlying principle
The principle is that liability in deposit follows from actual physical transgression (ta'addi) that compromises the trust property, not from mere intention. Mixing is a form of ta'addi because it destroys the specificity of the deposit.
This principle is consistent with the Hanafi maxim that intention alone does not necessitate legal consequences.

Ending transgression restores trust; denial after demand creates guarantee because the depositary is no longer a trustee.

In deposit law, the depositary is a trustee (amin), but certain acts—transgression (ta'addi) and denial after demand (jahada)—trigger guarantee (daman). This passage clarifies that transgression is not permanent: if the depositary stops the transgression and regains possession as a trustee, the guarantee lifts. However, denial after the owner's demand immediately makes him a guarantor because the demand ends his preservative duty. The text also presents a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about where denial must occur to take effect.

Two Depositors, One Depositary: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute Over Delivery
Intra-Hanafi dispute on joint deposit delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position from the Sahibayn's on delivering a share to one joint depositor and explain the talil (reasoning) behind each view.

Core Idea
Until both depositors are present, a depositary must not deliver to one—Abu Hanifa's caution vs. the Sahibayn's analogy to joint debt.

When two people jointly deposit fungible goods with a single depositary, and one returns to claim his share, a disagreement arises among the Hanafi masters. Abu Hanifa insists on waiting for both owners, while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue for immediate delivery of the demanded share. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each view and the critical distinction between deposits and debts.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Identify the factual scenario: two depositors, one depositary, fungible goods (makil/mawzun), one depositor demands his share.
The dispute is only about divisible fungible items, not unique items.
2
Identify the Two Positions
State Abu Hanifa's position (no delivery until both present) and the Sahibayn's position (immediate delivery of the share).
The matn reports the views without tarjih.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's Qiyas
Examine their analogy to a joint debt: a creditor can demand his share of a debt immediately.
Their dalil: 'he demands his right, so give it, as in joint debt'.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Response
Understand Abu Hanifa's distinction: a deposit is a specific property (ayn) unlike a debt (dayn), and the depositary cannot partition without authority.
His dalil: the segregated portion contains both rights; partition requires both owners' consent or judicial division.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, depositary must refuse; under Sahibayn, he must deliver.
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi.

If two men deposit a trust with a man, then one of them comes and demands his share from it: nothing is given to him until the other comes, according to Abu Hanifa. And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: His share is given to him. The dispute is concerning that which is measured by volume or weight. For them (the two): He demands his share from him, so he is ordered to give it to him, as in a joint debt, and because he demands delivery of what he entrusted, which is half. For Abu Hanifa: He demands delivery of the absent one's share, because he demands the segregated portion, and his right is in the undivided share, and the segregated specific portion contains both rights, and his right is not distinguished except by division, and the depositor does not have authority to divide, unlike a joint debt, because he demands delivery of his right to him, since debts are discharged with their equivalents.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a joint deposit when one depositor returns. The matn states that according to Abu Hanifa, the depositary should not hand over the share until both depositors are present, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the depositary must give the demanding depositor his share. The dispute is limited to fungible items (makil and mawzun). The Sahibayn draw an analogy to a joint debt: a creditor can demand his share of a debt. Abu Hanifa distinguishes: in a deposit, the property is specific and undivided; delivering a segregated portion would affect the absent owner's right, as the depositary lacks authority to partition. Debts are different because they are discharged by equivalent payment, not specific property. The author here merely reports the views without giving a preferred position.

Key Terms
وديعة (wadīʿah): A trust deposit, where one person leaves property with another for safekeeping without compensation.
مكيل وموزون (makīl wa mawzūn): Items measured by volume (like grain) or by weight (like silver).
مشاع (mushāʿ): Undivided share in a joint property.
مفرز (mufraz): A segregated, specific portion of a joint property.

Key Takeaway
The intra-Hanafi dispute on joint deposits turns on whether a depositary can partition fungible goods; Abu Hanifa requires both owners' presence, while the Sahibayn allow immediate delivery by analogy to debt.
