Core Idea
A man may not marry two women who would be prohibited to each other if they were male and female.

This short but powerful rule from the Hanafi school provides a comprehensive framework for understanding which pairs of women cannot be married to the same man simultaneously. Rather than listing every possible combination, the rule uses a simple test: if the two women were a man and a woman, would their marriage be prohibited? If yes, then combining them is forbidden. This principle applies equally to relationships established by blood (nasab) and by fosterage (rada'). Mastering this rule allows you to deduce many specific marriage prohibitions from a single maxim.

Underline that the rule is about combining two women in marriage to the same man, not about the women's own marriage to each other.
The test is hypothetical: imagine the two women as a pair of a man and a woman, then check if marriage between them is prohibited.
2
Understand the two sources of prohibition: lineage and fosterage
The passage explicitly mentions two bases: al-nasab (blood lineage) and al-rada' (fosterage). In Hanafi fiqh, fosterage creates the same permanent marriage prohibitions as lineage, provided the fosterage conditions are met (e.g., the child was nursed at least one full day or five separate times). This means that the rule applies not only to blood relatives (like sisters, aunts) but also to foster relatives (like foster sisters, foster aunts).
Fosterage prohibitions are limited to those who are mahram through the foster mother or father.

(b) A woman and her paternal aunt: if the aunt were a man, she could not marry her niece (uncle-niece marriage is prohibited). (c) A woman and her foster sister: the same logic applies because fosterage mirrors lineage. Work through these examples to see the rule in action.
The rule does not require the women to be mahram to each other for the prohibition to apply? Actually, the test is precisely whether they are mahram: if they cannot marry each other when imagining one as male, then they are mahram, and combining them is haram.
4
Identify the edge case: when the rule does NOT apply
If two women are not related in a way that would prohibit marriage between them (if one were male), then combining them is permissible. For example, two unrelated women, or a woman and her cousin (since cousins can marry), can be married to the same man simultaneously. The rule only covers relationships that create eternal prohibition, not temporary ones (like a sister-in-law after divorce).
The rule is specifically about 'combining' at the same time; it does not apply to serial marriages.

Commentary

The Hanafi position is clear: it is not permissible (haram) for any guardian, including father and grandfather, to compel a mature, intelligent virgin woman to marry. Consent of Mature Virgin: Guardian's Authority and Silence as Consent
Ruling on guardian's compulsion and consent by silence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on guardian's authority over a mature virgin and identify when silence constitutes valid consent.

Core Idea
A mature virgin's silence is consent only if the suitor is identified and the asker is the proper guardian.

This lesson addresses a key dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding the extent of a guardian's authority over a mature, sane virgin in marriage. The Hanafi position holds that the guardian cannot force her; rather, he must seek her permission. Her silence, laughter, or silent crying can indicate consent, but only under specific conditions. We will examine these conditions and the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the guardian (wali) cannot force a mature, sane virgin into marriage. He must seek her permission (isti'mar).
This applies to all guardians, including father and grandfather.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Al-Shafi'i holds that the father and grandfather can force a mature virgin, and a woman's own statement is insufficient to conclude a marriage.
The Shafi'i view is based on a different interpretation of guardianship verses.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i relies on the Quranic verse 'Marry those who are single among you' (24:32) and the hadith 'No marriage without a guardian,' arguing that the guardian's authority is absolute for virgins.
He also analogizes to minors, where the father has full authority.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the mature virgin has full legal capacity; the hadith 'The virgin is asked for permission, and her permission is her silence' proves her consent is valid. The guardian's role is to facilitate, not impose.
The verse and hadith cited by al-Shafi'i are general and do not override her capacity.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Hanafi law, a mature virgin can contract her own marriage without a guardian's approval. Under Shafi'i, such a marriage would be invalid.
This affects real-life fiqh applications for women married without a wali.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the authority of a guardian over a mature virgin in marriage, with detailed conditions for valid consent. The Hanafi position: A guardian (wali) cannot force a mature, sane virgin (bikr balighah 'aqilah) into marriage; he must seek her permission (isti'mar). Her silence, laughter, or silent crying (without sound) counts as consent. If she explicitly refuses, he cannot marry her. Al-Shafi'i held that the father and grandfather can force a mature virgin, and that a woman's own statement does not conclude a marriage at all. The commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that the ruling applies to both virgin and non-virgin, though the matn specifically mentions the virgin. It cites al-Hidayah to elaborate that if the person seeking permission is not the rightful guardian, or if a more entitled guardian exists, silence is not consent because it is not a reliable indication. The husband must be identified so her inclination can be gauged; otherwise silence is meaningless. Mentioning the dower (mahr) is not required for consent when the one marrying is the father or grandfather—just naming the husband suffices. For other guardians, both husband and dower must be mentioned. If the suitor is not compatible (kuf'), silence is not consent because the guardian lacks authority to marry her to a non-compatible person. Crying with sound indicates displeasure and thus denies consent; crying without sound is interpreted as sadness at leaving family, which implies acceptance.

بكر (bikr): A virgin woman who has never been married.
سكوت (sukut): Silence of the woman when asked about marriage, which is interpreted as consent under specific conditions.
استئمار (isti'mar): The act of seeking permission or consultation from the woman regarding her marriage.
