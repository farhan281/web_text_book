Definition and Classification of Kafalah
Defines kafalah and its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definitions of kafalah, distinguish its two types, and explain why kafalah bil-nafs is valid even without the principal's permission.

Core Idea
Kafalah adds a guarantor without transferring the debt; guaranteeing a person obliges producing him, not ensuring his cooperation.

Kafalah (suretyship) is a vital contract in Islamic law, ensuring rights without shifting liability. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanded by al-Ghunaymi in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, lays the groundwork by defining kafalah both linguistically and legally, then classifying it into two distinct forms. It also addresses a key objection about guarantees made without the principal's consent, a point that often confuses students.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions
Note the linguistic meaning (al-damm, joining) and the Quranic evidence (Al 'Imran: 37). Then parse the legal definition: joining one dhimma to another for demand, without transferring the debt.
The debt remains on the principal (asil).
2
Classify the two types of kafalah
Kafalah bil-nafs (guarantee of the person) and kafalah bil-mal (guarantee of property/debt). The passage focuses on the first type.
The object of guarantee (madhmun bihi) differs by type.
3
Extract the ruling for kafalah bil-nafs
It is permissible (ja'iz) and valid, whether with or without the principal's permission. The guarantor is obliged to produce the guaranteed person.
The ruling is from the matn: 'فالكفالة بالنفس جائزة'.
4
Analyze the hypothetical objection
The objection: without permission, the guarantor cannot produce the principal (who may refuse). The reply: the guarantor can physically produce him, but the principal is not legally obliged to comply; validity depends on possibility of performance (imkan al-ada'), not entitlement.
The distinction between 'yqdir' (ability) and 'yastahiq' (entitlement) is key.
5
Determine the madhmun bihi
In kafalah bil-nafs, the madhmun bihi is the production (ihdar) of the guaranteed person. The guarantor's duty is to bring him to the required place.
The text states: 'المضمون بها : إحضار المكفول به'.

This passage is a definition and classification of kafalah (suretyship/guarantee). Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligations of a surety when a delivery time is stipulated in kafalah bi al-nafs and identify the conditions for discharge.

Core Idea
A surety must deliver the guaranteed person at the stipulated time or face judicial detention; discharge occurs upon actual delivery in a litigable place.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayirah explains the legal effect of stipulating a specific time for delivery in kafalah bi al-nafs. It clarifies the surety's duties, the consequences of failure, and the conditions for discharge. The commentary expands the ruling to cover cases where the guaranteed person is absent or surrenders voluntarily.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Read the base text: if a time is stipulated, the surety must bring the guaranteed person when demanded at that time. If he fails, the judge imprisons him.
2
Note the condition for discharge
The surety is discharged when he delivers the person in a place where the creditor can legally sue him (i.e., where a court has jurisdiction).
Delivery must be in a litigable place.
3
Examine the case of absence
If the guaranteed person is absent from the town, the judge grants a travel period. If the surety does not know the location, the demand drops until he learns it.
4
Examine voluntary surrender
If the guaranteed person voluntarily surrenders to the creditor, the creditor must accept and the surety is discharged.
This fulfills the object of the guarantee.

The Phrase 'I Guarantee Him for Three Days' in Kafalah: Indefinite Suretyship Unless Explicit Discharge
Ruling on the phrase 'I guarantee him for three days' in kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the phrase 'I guarantee him for three days' and identify the condition that limits the suretyship.

Core Idea
Without explicit discharge, a conditional time phrase in kafalah makes the surety liable indefinitely.

Kafalah contracts often hinge on precise wording. This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling: what happens when a surety says 'I guarantee him for three days'? The answer may surprise you—unless the surety adds a condition of freedom after the term, he remains liable forever. Understanding this nuance is essential for correctly structuring guarantee agreements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the phrase used
The act is kafalah (suretyship) and the specific phrase is 'I guarantee him for three days'. Recognize that the phrase includes a temporal element but no explicit discharge clause.
2
State the default ruling from the narration
According to the narration from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the surety is a 'kafil abadan'—liable indefinitely until the guaranteed person is produced, regardless of the three-day reference.
This is because the temporal phrase alone is not sufficient to limit liability.
3
Identify the exception that limits the term
If the surety explicitly adds 'when they pass I am free' or a similar discharge condition, then the suretyship ends with the stipulated time.
The exception shows that clear stipulation can override the default.
4
Note the source of the ruling
The passage cites 'al-Yanabi'' as the source. This is a later Hanafi work, confirming the transmission from Muhammad.
Note the chain of authority: from Muhammad al-Shaybani, preserved in later compilations.
5
Consider the practical implication
The ruling emphasizes the need for precise language in kafalah. A surety who intends a limited guarantee must articulate it explicitly.
This protects both the surety and the creditor from ambiguity.

Place of Delivery in Kafalah: Court, Market, Desert, Rural
Rulings on place of delivery in kafalah bi al-nafs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the place of delivery in kafalah bi al-nafs and evaluate the effect of different locations (court, marketplace, desert, rural area) on the surety's discharge.

Core Idea
Delivery discharges the surety only where the creditor can litigate; desert and rural areas are insufficient.

In kafalah bi al-nafs (surety for the person), the guarantor must produce the guaranteed person at a place where the creditor can pursue legal action. This lesson examines the rulings on valid places of delivery, including the marketplace, desert, and rural areas, and a later opinion that the marketplace may no longer suffice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Stipulated Place
The contract typically specifies delivery in the court (majlis al-qadi). Understand that the court is the default place where the creditor can litigate effectively.
2

The text states that delivery in the marketplace discharges the surety because the objective (bringing the person) is achieved. However, a later opinion (qila) holds that in the commentator's time, the marketplace does not discharge because it is a place of defiance. Note the similar ruling for delivery in the outskirts of the town.
The later opinion reflects a change in social circumstances.
3
Analyze Delivery in Desert and Rural Areas
Delivery in a desert (barriyyah) does not discharge because the creditor cannot litigate there nor bring the person to a judge. Similarly, delivery in rural areas (sawad) without a judge is ineffective.
The absence of a judge is the key factor.
4
Extract the Underlying Principle
The common thread is that delivery must occur in a place where the creditor has the ability to bring the person to court for litigation. If that is impossible, the surety is not discharged.
This is a condition for valid delivery.

Multiple Sureties: When One Performs, Are All Discharged?
Rulings on multiple sureties for one person or one debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for discharge of multiple sureties and identify the condition that determines discharge for person suretyship.

Core Idea
Multiple sureties for a person are discharged by one's action only if they are joint sureties; separate sureties remain liable.

This lesson clarifies how multiple sureties are affected when one of them fulfills the obligation. The ruling differs depending on whether the suretyship is for a person or a debt, and for person suretyship, on whether the contract was joint or separate. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying suretyship contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of kafalah
The passage discusses suretyship for a person (kafalah bi al-nafs) and suretyship for a debt (kafalah bi al-mal). Identify which scenario you are dealing with.
2
For person suretyship, classify the contract type
Determine whether the sureties entered into a single joint contract (kafalah wahidah) or separate independent contracts (kafalah mutafarriqah).
This classification is crucial for the ruling.
3
Apply the rule for person suretyship
If the contract is single, one surety bringing the person discharges all. If separate, only the performing surety is discharged; the others remain liable.
Each separate contract creates an independent obligation to bring the person.
4
Apply the rule for debt suretyship
Regardless of whether the suretyship is single or separate, if one surety pays the entire debt, all sureties are discharged.
The debt is a single obligation; payment satisfies it entirely.
5
Understand the legal reasoning
For person suretyship, each contract imposes a distinct duty; for debt suretyship, the accessory nature means performance by one extinguishes the obligation for all.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that kafalah bi al-nafs is a personal guarantee, while kafalah bi al-mal is ancillary.

Delivery of the Guaranteed Person: Market vs Wilderness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when delivering a guaranteed person releases the guarantor, and distinguish between delivery in a place of judicial authority versus a wilderness.

Core Idea
Delivery frees the guarantor only where litigation is possible; the market suffices, the wilderness does not.

Death of the guaranteed person or guarantor ends the personal guarantee; death of the creditor transfers the right to heirs.

However, if the creditor dies, the obligation persists: the guarantor must produce the guaranteed person to the heirs. If he delivers to only some, he is released only from their share. Discharge of Surety (Kafalah bi al-Nafs) upon Death of Principal, Surety, or Creditor
From a Hanafi sharh text on kafalah, explaining discharge conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on discharge of a surety (kafil bi al-nafs) upon the death of the principal, the surety himself, or the creditor, and apply the rule of partial discharge upon delivery to some heirs.

Core Idea
Death of the principal or surety discharges the surety; death of the creditor does not.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses key scenarios in kafalah bi al-nafs (suretyship for a person's presence). The rulings hinge on the ability to produce the principal. We will explore how death of the principal, the surety, or the creditor affects the obligation, as well as the effect of delivering the principal to some heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The discharge of the surety (kafil bi al-nafs) from the obligation to produce the principal. The ruling depends on who dies.
The text gives multiple sub-rulings based on different parties' deaths.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The evidence is rational: inability ('ajz) to perform. The text states 'لعجزه عن إحضاره' (due to his inability to produce him).
No explicit Quran/hadith; the ruling is based on the principle that obligations cease when impossible.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: If the principal dies, the surety cannot physically produce him, so the obligation ends. Similarly, if the surety dies, he cannot produce himself; property cannot substitute for personal presence.
Contrast with kafalah bi al-mal where property can be taken from the estate.
4
Apply the rule to the creditor's death
Death of the creditor does not discharge the surety because the right to demand production passes to the heirs.
The text says 'لم يبرأ' and elaborates on delivery to heirs.
5
Analyze partial delivery to heirs
If the surety delivers the principal to some heirs, he is discharged only for their share. The remaining heirs still have the right to demand.
This is similar to partial payment of a debt; the obligation is not extinguished for the rest.

Combined Kafalah: Guarantee of Debt if Principal Not Produced
Explains ruling when surety guarantees debt conditional on failure to produce principal

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In combined kafalah, fulfilling the monetary condition does not release the surety from personal suretyship; each obligation stands independently.

This lesson examines a specific type of suretyship (kafalah) where the surety combines two obligations: guaranteeing to produce a person by a deadline, and guaranteeing to pay a debt if he fails. The Hanafi school rules that these are separate obligations; discharging one does not discharge the other. We will explore the ruling, the reasoning, and a practical example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two obligations in the combined kafalah
Recognize that the surety undertakes both a personal suretyship (kafalah bi al-nafs) and a conditional monetary guarantee (daman al-mal).
The condition triggers the monetary guarantee only upon failure to produce the principal.
2
State the ruling upon failure to produce the principal
If the surety fails to produce the principal by the deadline, he must pay the debt but is NOT discharged from the personal suretyship.
The text uses the example of a thousand dirhams.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning
The two obligations are distinct because each is based on a different cause. Paying the debt satisfies only the monetary guarantee; the personal suretyship remains to be fulfilled.
The principle is that obligations are not automatically substituted.
4
Apply to the husband/dower example and answer the benefit question
The same ruling applies if a surety guarantees to produce a husband, failing which he pays the dower. The benefit of continuing personal suretyship is that the creditor may have another claim against the principal.
Even after debt payment, the creditor can still demand the principal's presence for other debts.

Kafalah in Hudud and Qisas: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Presents intra-Hanafi dispute on kafalah in hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's position that kafalah bi al-nafs is invalid in hudud and qisas, and distinguish the Sahibayn's counter-position.

Core Idea
The imam is commanded to avert hudud, so kafalah – which secures punishment – contradicts that policy.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of personal suretyship (kafalah bi al-nafs) in cases of hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). Abu Hanifa forbids it based on the principle of averting punishments, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) permit it, emphasizing the personal right of the claimant. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how Hanafi scholars balance public policy with individual rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Dispute
The text states: 'kafalah bi al-nafs is not permitted in hudud and qisas according to Abu Hanifa; they (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said it is permitted.' Note the clear disagreement within the Hanafi school.
The dispute is over the validity of a surety for the person of the accused.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's Dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that kafalah is for tawthiq (securing rights), but the imam is commanded to avert hudud (dar' al-hudud). Since securing the accused facilitates punishment, it contradicts the policy of averting punishments.
This is a rational (aqli) argument based on the general legal policy of the Shari'ah.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's Implicit Dalil
The Sahibayn likely argue that in qisas and some hudud, the victim or their guardians have a personal right (haqq adami). The duty to avert hudud does not overrule the claimant's right to secure the accused for retribution or compensation.
The text does not elaborate their proof; later Hanafi works often cite the personal nature of qisas.
4
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
According to Abu Hanifa, a surety cannot be taken for a person accused of a hadd crime or qisas; the claimant cannot demand a guarantor. According to the Sahibayn, such a surety is valid.
The practical consequence is whether the accused can be released on surety pending trial.
5
Consider the Tarjih
This passage does not give a preferred view, but later Hanafi authorities (especially in fatwa) often adopt the Sahibayn's position for qisas due to the personal right involved, while adhering to Abu Hanifa's view for purely hudud cases.
The mufta bihi may vary depending on the nature of the case.

Commentary
This passage deals with whether kafalah bi al-nafs (personal suretyship) is valid in cases of hudud (fixed punishments for crimes like theft, zina) and qisas (retaliatory punishments for murder or battery). Abu Hanifa holds that it is not valid. His reasoning: kafalah is a form of 'tawthiq' (securing the right of the claimant). However, in hudud and qisas, the Islamic legal policy (siyasa shar'iyya) encourages 'daf' al-hudud' (averting punishments) by any lawful means, and securing the presence of the accused for execution of the punishment contradicts this policy. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Paying the guaranteed debt does not cancel the person suretyship—both obligations stand independently.

Suretyship (kafala) can involve both guaranteeing a person's appearance and guaranteeing a debt. This lesson explains the ruling when these two are combined: if the surety fails to produce the principal and pays the debt, he remains bound by the person suretyship. The reasoning is that the principal may have other debts, so the person suretyship continues to secure them.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Suretyship in Hudud and Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kafala bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on kafala bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas, explain the reasoning behind each, and apply the rulings to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
Kafala bi al-nafs in hudud is impermissible under Abu Hanifa due to dar' al-hudud, but the Sahibayn allow it for qadhf and qisas as they involve victims' rights.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of taking a surety for the appearance of a defendant in hudud and qisas cases. While Abu Hanifa prohibits it to uphold the principle of warding off punishments, his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, permit it in cases where victims' rights are involved. Understanding their reasoning and the practical differences is essential for applying Hanafi fiqh in criminal procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Note the two positions: Abu Hanifa rules kafala bi al-nafs invalid in hudud and qisas, while the Sahibayn rule it permissible. This is the central ikhtilaf within the school.
This is not about kafala for the punishment itself, but for the defendant's presence.
2
Analyze the reasoning behind each view
Abu Hanifa relies on the principle of dar' al-hudud – the judge is commanded to avoid securing the defendant to facilitate dropping the punishment. The Sahibayn counter that in qadhf and qisas, the victim's personal right is at stake, making securing appropriate.
The Sahibayn still distinguish between pure hudud (e.g., zina) and those with victim rights.
3
Understand the practical thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the judge cannot compel a surety; the plaintiff is told to keep the defendant close until witnesses are brought. Under the Sahibayn, the judge can compel a surety in qadhf and qisas.
Voluntary suretyship is valid by consensus regardless of the dispute.
4
Examine the consensus exceptions
All agree that kafala cannot be taken for the actual punishment itself – the surety cannot undergo hudud or qisas on behalf of the defendant. Also, if the defendant voluntarily provides a surety, it is valid according to all.
This confines the dispute to compulsory suretyship for appearance.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of kafala bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas. Abu Hanifa holds it impermissible, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold it permissible. The commentator provides the reasoning: suretyship is a means of securing (tawaththuq), and in hudud and qisas, the judge is commanded to avoid securing the defendant and instead to drop the punishment if possible (dar' al-hudud). The Sahibayn's view is that it is permissible. The commentary then adds from al-Hidayah a related ruling: although kafala per se may be impermissible according to Abu Hanifa, the judge cannot compel the defendant to provide a surety at his view (Abu Hanifa); but according to the Sahibayn, the judge can compel a surety in cases of qadhf and qisas because these involve the rights of the victim. In purely God-given hudud (e.g., zina, shurb), even the Sahibayn would not compel. If the defendant voluntarily provides a surety, it is valid by consensus. The scenario illustrates the practical difference: in a qadhf case, Abu Hanifa's ruling leads to the judge not ordering a surety but rather instructing the plaintiff to keep the defendant close until the case is decided; the Sahibayn's ruling leads to the judge ordering a surety for his appearance. Finally, all agree that kafala cannot be taken for the actual punishment itself (e.g., to have the surety undergo the punishment) because the punishment cannot be exacted from the surety—only from the offender.

Creditor's Option in Kafalah: Claim from Debtor or Guarantor
States ruling and rationale from definition of kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the creditor's option in kafalah and explain its basis in the Hanafi definition of kafalah as damm al-dhimma.

Core Idea
Kafalah joins liability, it does not transfer it — the creditor may claim from either party.

This lesson examines the ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar on the creditor's option in a contract of guarantee (kafalah). The text clarifies that the original debtor remains liable, and the creditor can choose whom to demand payment from. The rationale is rooted in the Hanafi definition of kafalah as 'joining of liability' (damm al-dhimma), which implies addition, not substitution.

Guided Path
1

Locate the three parties: the creditor (al-makful lah), the original debtor (al-asil), and the guarantor (al-kafil). Understand their roles.
2
State the ruling
The creditor has the option: if he wishes, he demands from the original debtor; if he wishes, he demands from the guarantor. He may also demand from both simultaneously.
This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.
3
Understand the rationale
Kafalah is defined as 'damm al-dhimma' (joining liability to liability). This means the guarantor's liability is added to, not substituted for, the original debtor's liability. Therefore, the original debtor is not discharged.
The text says: 'because kafalah is the joining of one liability to another in terms of claim, and that requires the persistence of the first'.
4

The creditor may claim from both parties together because the essence of kafalah is joining. If the guarantor pays, he can seek reimbursement from the original debtor (this is a separate ruling not in this passage).
Contrast with a transfer of debt (hawalah) where the original debtor is released.

Effect of Discharging Principal or Surety in Kafalah
Rulings on discharge in suretyship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on how discharge of the principal debtor or surety affects the other party, and apply the condition of acceptance for discharge of the principal.

Core Idea
Discharge of the principal automatically frees the surety; discharge of the surety does not free the principal.

In suretyship (kafalah), the surety’s liability is accessory to the principal debtor’s debt. When the creditor discharges the principal, the accessory obligation ends. But what if the creditor only discharges the surety? And must the principal accept the discharge? This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar untangles these rules with clear reasoning and analogies.

Guided Path
1
State the main rule
Identify the ruling: when the creditor discharges the principal or recovers payment from him, the surety is automatically discharged.
This applies whether the surety guaranteed at the principal’s request or not.
2

The reason is that the surety only guaranteed what was in the principal’s liability. Once that liability is extinguished by payment or discharge, nothing remains for the suretyship to attach to.
This is a rational proof based on the nature of accessory obligation.

Note that discharge of the principal requires his acceptance. If he rejects it, the discharge is revoked. If he dies before acceptance or rejection, his death stands as acceptance.
This is because ibra' is considered a donation (tabarru') in Hanafi law, which requires acceptance.

If the creditor discharges only the surety, the principal is not discharged. Similarly, a delay granted to the principal benefits the surety, but a delay granted only to the surety does not benefit the principal.
The parallel between discharge and delay is justified by qiyas: delay is a temporary discharge.
5
Explore the ikhtilaf
If the principal rejects the discharge, does the debt return against the surety? Some Hanafi scholars say yes, some say no. The text does not give a preferred view.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute left unresolved in this passage.

Suspending Discharge from Suretyship on a Condition: Tamlik vs. Isqat
Ruling on conditional discharge in suretyship with explanation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional discharge of a surety, explain the tamlik vs. isqat analysis, and distinguish the treatment of the principal debtor's discharge.

Core Idea
Conditional discharge of a surety is invalid because it involves tamlik, which cannot be conditional; but a narration considers it valid as pure isqat.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah examines a key distinction in Hanafi contract law: whether discharge from suretyship (bara'ah) is a transfer of ownership (tamlik) or a pure waiver (isqat). The ruling affects whether it can be suspended on a condition. We will unpack the reasoning, the intra-Hanafi dispute, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is suspending the discharge of a surety (kafil) upon a condition. The ruling according to Zahir al-Riwayah is 'la yajuz' (not permissible).
This is the main position of the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the reason: tamlik cannot be conditional
Discharge of the surety contains the meaning of tamlik (transfer of ownership) because the surety is considered to own a right to demand payment from the principal. Tamlik, like sale, cannot be suspended on a condition.
This is based on qiyas on other tamlik contracts.
3
Note the alternative narration: pure isqat
A narration (ruwiya) considers it valid because the surety only has a right to demand repayment, not ownership of the debt itself. Hence, it is a pure waiver (isqat) akin to divorce or manumission, which can be conditional.
This narration is from the Nawadir, not Zahir al-Riwayah.
4

Acceptance in Kafalah and the Sick Person Exception
Analysis of requirement of acceptance in kafalah and its exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on acceptance in kafalah, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the exception for a sick person.

Core Idea
Kafalah is bilateral requiring creditor's acceptance in the session; necessity relaxes this for a sick person's heir.

This lesson examines the condition of acceptance (qabul) in a kafalah contract according to the Hanafi school. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) require the creditor's acceptance in the same session, drawing an analogy to sale. Abu Yusuf dissents, analogizing kafalah to a unilateral acknowledgment. An exception allows a sick person's heir to guarantee debts without immediate acceptance, illustrating the principle of necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The matn states that kafalah is not valid except with the acceptance of the creditor in the session. Note that hawala has the same rule.
This is the position of the two imams (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad).
2
Locate the Two Imams' Evidence
They analogize kafalah to sale (qiyas 'ala al-bay') because it involves transfer of the right of demand (tamlik), requiring mutual consent in the session.
The offer is a condition, so it cannot extend beyond the session.
3
Identify Abu Yusuf's Dissent
Abu Yusuf argues that acceptance is not needed in the session; later approval suffices. He analogizes to iqrar, a unilateral imposition of liability.
Two narrations exist: one requiring approval, another not requiring any approval.
4
Analyze the Practical Consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: if acceptance occurs after the session, the kafalah is invalid per the two, but valid per Abu Yusuf. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is the two imams' view.
This aligns with general contract principles.
5
Note the Exception for the Sick Person
If a sick person asks his heir to guarantee his debt, the kafalah is valid even without the creditors' acceptance in the session, due to necessity.
This exception applies only when the debtor is sick and the guarantor is his heir.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on the Quduri matn addresses the condition of acceptance (qabul) in a kafalah contract. The general rule, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (referred to as 'the two' – qawlahuma), is that the creditor's acceptance must occur in the same session as the offer. They draw an analogy with sale (bay'), where both offer and acceptance are required in the session. Abu Yusuf, however, holds that acceptance is not necessary in the session; it is sufficient if the creditor learns of it later and approves. He compares kafalah to an acknowledgment (iqrar), which is unilateral. There are two narrations from Abu Yusuf: one requiring approval, another not. The text then provides detailed scenarios illustrating the dispute. Finally, an exception is noted: if a sick person (marid) asks his heir to guarantee his debt, the kafalah is valid even if the creditors are absent, due to necessity. This exception underscores that the general rule is relaxed to facilitate the settlement of debts for those in need.

Key Terms
قبول (qabul): Acceptance by the creditor (makful lah) of the guarantee; required in the session of contract according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
مجلس العقد (majlis al-aqd): The contractual session where offer and acceptance take place; continuity is required for validity.
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership or right; here, the transfer of the right to demand payment from the guarantor.
إقرار (iqrar): A unilateral acknowledgment of a debt or liability; does not require acceptance.
حوالة (hawala): Transfer of debt from one debtor to another; similar to kafalah in requiring acceptance.
