Drunkard's Actions Effective: Reasoning and Accidental Slip
Proof and counter-opinions on drunkard's acts; accidental slip in divorce and manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a drunkard's divorce is effective and evaluate the intra-Hanafi counter-argument of al-Karakhi and al-Tahawi.

Core Idea
A drunkard's actions are binding because his loss of reason is sinful; he remains mukallaf as a deterrent.

This passage addresses a critical dispute within the Hanafi school: whether a drunkard's divorce takes effect. While some early Hanafi authorities argue that lack of reason nullifies the act, the majority holds him accountable. The author provides proofs from the Quran and legal principles, and also discusses accidental slips of the tongue in divorce and manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Recognize that the drunkard's actions are effective because his loss of reason is due to sin (ma'siyah). This is the foundational Hanafi position.
The principle is rooted in deterrence (zajr).
2
Examine the proof
The author cites Quran 4:43 and the fact that the drunkard incurs hadd and qisas, showing his legal responsibility.
The Quranic address implies the drunkard is mukallaf.
3
Analyze the counter-opinion
Al-Karakhi and al-Tahawi argue the drunkard lacks reason, so his divorce is void. Understand their rationale.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Evaluate the rebuttal
The author responds that the loss of reason is sinful, so by legal fiction (hukman) it is considered remaining for punishment.
This is a key point in Hanafi legal theory.
5
Apply to accidental slips
The passage extends the principle: even if the tongue slips accidentally, divorce and manumission take effect. Note the minority narration by Hisham from Muhammad that manumission does not take effect, but the relied-upon view is that both are effective.
The dispute is resolved in favor of effectiveness.

Divorce of the Intoxicated: Valid Despite Intoxication
Intra-Hanafi dispute on divorce by intoxicated person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority ruling on divorce by an intoxicated person, identify the premises of the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the mufta bihi view to a hypothetical scenario.

Core Idea
An intoxicated person's divorce is valid because his sin nullifies the excuse of lost intellect.

Divorce pronounced while intoxicated raises a tension between lack of awareness and legal accountability. The Hanafi school takes a firm deterrent position: because intoxication is a sinful cause of losing intellect, the law treats the person as sane to prevent exploitation. This lesson examines the majority and minority Hanafi views, the proofs each side employs, and the practical difference for a wife's marital status.

Guided Path
1
State the majority Hanafi position
Identify the ruling that all transactions of an intoxicated person, including clear (ṣarīḥ) divorce, are valid and take effect immediately without intention. This is the position of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and the generality of early Hanafis.
This is the Zāhir al-Riwāyah position.
2
Identify the minority intra-Hanafi view
Al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi hold that divorce by an intoxicated person does not occur until the person later confirms intention after becoming sober. Their reasoning is that valid intention ('aql) is lacking.
This is a later Hanafi opinion, not part of Zāhir al-Riwāyah.
3
Examine the proofs for the majority
The majority relies on Quran 4:43 (addressing the intoxicated) and the fact that the intoxicated is subject to ḥadd and qiṣāṣ, proving he is mukallaf. The key principle: loss of intellect due to sin is not a valid excuse; the person is deemed sane as a deterrent.
Quran 4:43 is used indirectly; the command 'do not approach prayer' implies addressability.
4
Analyze the minority proof and the response
The minority argues that the intoxicated lacks intellect, so his utterances are void, like the insane. The majority responds: the cause is sinful, so the law treats him as sane by legal fiction (ḥukman) to deter. The minority also rely on the need for intention in divorce, but the majority counters ṣarīḥ does not need intention.
The dispute hinges on the legal effect of a sinful cause of incapacity.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the practical consequence
The majority view (all transactions valid) is the relied-upon position. The practical difference: according to the majority, the wife is immediately divorced (possibly ba'in); according to the minority, she remains married until the husband confirms intention. If he confirms later, divorce occurs by consensus.
The text mentions that if the intoxicated later confesses intention, all agree divorce occurs at that time.

Commentary
This passage addresses the validity of divorce (and other legal acts) performed by an intoxicated person (sukran). The primary Hanafi position, held by Abu Hanifa and the majority of the early scholars (as reflected in the Zāhir al-Riwāyah), is that all transactions of an intoxicated person are valid and binding. The reasoning is twofold: (1) his loss of intellect is due to a sinful act, so as a deterrent, he is treated as legally sane — his absence of mind is not given effect; (2) he is still a legally accountable person (mukallaf), as evidenced by the fact that he incurs the fixed penalty for slander (hadd al-qadhf) and retaliation for murder (qisas). The Qur'anic verse prohibiting prayer while intoxicated (4:43) indicates that the intoxicated person is addressed by the Law, otherwise the prohibition would be meaningless. 

However, two later Hanafi authorities, al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, differed. They argued that since the intoxicated person's intellect is gone, his pronouncements are not legally effective — like an insane person. They relied on the general principle that intention (niyyah) is necessary for divorce, and an intoxicated person lacks valid intention. The text then recounts a rebuttal: the cause of the loss of intellect is sinful, so he is treated as sane by legal fiction (ḥukman) to deter him. 

The commentator then discusses the role of intention in different contexts. A clear (sarih) expression of divorce (e.g., 'You are divorced') does not require intention according to the dominant view; even if one misspeaks or does not intend divorce, it takes effect. This is established by the example of a man who intends to say 'Give me a drink' but accidentally says 'You are divorced.' According to the sound transmission (sahih), this constitutes a valid divorce. In contrast, a narration from Hisham ibn al-Hakam from Muhammad al-Shaybani indicates that for manumission ('You are free'), the same slip does not free the slave — but the sounder view is that both divorce and manumission occur. 

The text then transitions to a discussion about the phrasing of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. Some manuscripts say 'Divorce occurs if he said, “I intended divorce by it”' — this is interpreted as referring to the coerced (mukrah) and intoxicated person. According to al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, if the intoxicated person later soberly confesses that he intended divorce, then it takes effect at that moment (by consensus). The majority of Hanafis, however, hold that a clear expression of divorce from an intoxicated person takes effect immediately, without any need for intention. Therefore, the phrase might be a scribal error, or it might refer to allusive expressions (kinayat). Finally, the passage discusses divorce by writing (kitab): if one writes a clear divorce text intending divorce, it takes effect; otherwise it does not. There is also a view that a clear writing (mustabin) is like an explicit verbal expression.

Key Terms
سكران (sukrān): A person who is intoxicated due to consumption of an intoxicant (khamr, nabidh, etc.) to the point of loss of rational control.
مكره (mukrah): A person who is coerced under threat to perform an act, such as pronouncing divorce.
صريح (ṣarīḥ): A clear, explicit expression of divorce, such as 'You are divorced' (ṭāliq), which does not require intention to effect divorce.
كناية (kināyah): An allusive expression that could imply divorce but has other possible meanings, requiring intention (niyyah) to be effective.
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Coercion that compels a person to act against his will under threat of harm.

Divorce of the Intoxicated Person: The Role of Later Confirmation
Ruling on sukran affirming intention after sobriety

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the position of al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi regarding the divorce of an intoxicated person who later affirms intention, and distinguish it from the general Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Later confirmation of intention can validate the divorce of an intoxicated person, by consensus among the later Hanafi masters.

This lesson examines a specific case in Hanafi divorce law: when an intoxicated person (sukran) pronounces talaq but later, after regaining sobriety, confirms that he intended the divorce. The general Hanafi rule is that explicit talaq from a sukran is effective even without intention, making later confirmation seemingly irrelevant. However, al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi introduced a nuanced position: the confirmation itself becomes the basis for the divorce, and there is consensus that divorce occurs at that moment. We will explore the reasoning, the intra-Hanafi dispute, and the practical application.

Guided Path
1
State the General Hanafi Position on Sukran's Talaq
First, recall the established Hanafi rule: when an intoxicated person pronounces an explicit divorce (talaq sarih), it is effective regardless of intention, because the words are unambiguous and intoxication does not prevent the legal effect of explicit pronouncements.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn, recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the Variation by Al-Karkhi and Al-Tahawi
Al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, later Hanafi authorities, considered the case where the sukran later declares that he had intended the divorce. They ruled that this confirmation is accepted, and the divorce takes effect at the time of confirmation, not at the original utterance.
This is a departure from the general rule that intention is not needed.
3
Analyse the Proof Structure
Their reasoning likely rests on the principle that when a person's mental state is compromised, his subsequent clarifying statement should be given weight. They considered the confirmation as a new act of talaq or a ratification that completes the legal effect.
No direct textual dalil is cited; it is a qiyas or istihsan based on the nature of intention.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: Under the general position, the divorce was effective immediately upon utterance, and the later confirmation is irrelevant. Under al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, the divorce is only effective upon confirmation, which affects the timing for iddah, remarriage, and other consequences.
The mufta bihi view is that of al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, as adopted by later Hanafi scholars.

Explicit Talaq from an Intoxicated Person: The Hanafi Majority View
Majority holds explicit talaq from sukran occurs without intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi position on explicit talaq (sarih) from an intoxicated person and distinguish it from the position of al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi, while also correcting a common textual misunderstanding about kinayat.

Core Idea
Explicit talaq from an intoxicated person is valid without intention according to the Hanafi majority.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a key issue in Hanafi divorce law: whether an intoxicated person's explicit words of divorce take effect. The majority of Hanafi scholars rule that they do, regardless of intention. The author also clarifies a textual ambiguity in the matn, ensuring that the reader correctly distinguishes between explicit (sarih) and allusive (kinayah) divorce expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the majority Hanafi position
Note that 'عامة أصحابنا' (the majority of our companions) holds that explicit talaq from a person intoxicated by wine or nabidh is effective without intention. This is the mufta bihi position.
Explicit words like 'anti taliq' are sarih and do not require niyyah.
2
Recognize the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi disagree, requiring intention even for sarih from a sukran. This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
The passage does not detail their proof, but it is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
3
Understand the textual correction
The author notes that the phrase 'divorce occurs if he says: I intended divorce' in some manuscripts is a scribal error; it correctly applies to kinayat, not sarih. Kinayat require intention; sarih do not.
This shows the importance of precise manuscripts in fiqh.
4
Distinguish sarih from kinayah
Sarih: clear words (e.g., 'anti taliq') that effect divorce without intention. Kinayah: allusive phrases (e.g., 'you are free') that require intention to count as divorce.
This distinction is foundational in Hanafi talaq law.
5
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
The majority view is relied upon for fatwa. A drunken husband who utters sarih talaq is considered to have divorced, even if he claims no intention.
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar regarding conditional divorce. The author begins by stating the basic rule: when divorce is attached to a condition (ta'liq), the divorce occurs immediately upon the fulfillment of the condition, provided the wife is still in the husband's ownership at that time. The example given is 'If you enter the house, you are divorced.' Next, the word 'kullu' (every) is discussed: when used in conditional divorce, it repeats the effect on nouns but not verbs; for instance, if a man says 'Every woman I marry is divorced,' and then marries another woman, she is divorced, but this does not apply repeatedly to one woman. Then a ruling from Imam Zahir al-Din is quoted: if a man says 'If I marry you, you are divorced' at a time when she is already divorced three times (thus permanently forbidden), and then after an intervening husband he marries her, the conditional divorce does not occur because at the moment of conditioning she was not a lawful wife. The commentary then explains the reasoning behind the basic rule: because the husband has ownership (milk) at the time of the condition, and the condition simply suspends the divorce, making the occurrence equivalent to an immediate pronouncement. If the wife leaves his ownership before the condition occurs (e.g., through divorce or death), then upon the condition's fulfillment, no divorce falls, and the oath is dissolved because the husband no longer has the capacity to divorce her. Conversely, if an insane man issues a conditional divorce and becomes sane before the condition, the divorce does not occur because at the time of utterance he lacked capacity. The passage ends by reiterating that valid ta'liq requires the husband to be a possessor of the marriage at the time of the condition.

Conditions for Valid Suspended Divorce and Words of Condition
Explaining conditions for ta'liq al-talaq and listing words of condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for a valid suspended divorce and apply the ruling when the husband becomes insane or loses ownership.

Core Idea
A suspended divorce is valid if the husband owns at utterance; legal effect follows the condition, not later insanity.

Conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq) is a common practice where the husband attaches divorce to a future event. The Hanafi school carefully defines the conditions for its validity. This lesson unpacks the key ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: the husband must be the owner at the time of the conditional utterance, and the divorce falls upon fulfillment of the condition even if he later becomes insane. We will also learn the words that can be used as conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is suspending divorce upon a condition (ta'liq al-talaq). The ruling: it is valid (sahih) only if the husband is an owner (malik) of the wife at the time of the conditional utterance, or if he adds the condition to his ownership (i.e., she is within his ownership when the condition occurs).
'Owner' here means being the husband in a valid marriage.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The text provides two arguments: (1) an example: if a man says to his wife 'If you enter the house...' then divorces her irrevocably and her iddah ends, no divorce falls upon entry because she is no longer his property. (2) An analogy (qiyas) with the impotent man (anna): if a judge separates them after a deadline while the man is insane, it counts as a divorce, showing that legal effects can be tied to a prior valid utterance.
The proof is not a direct Quranic verse but a rational argument and analogy.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal link)
The illa (effective cause) is that the condition was validly spoken when the husband was sane and owner. The divorce is considered to occur by legal construction (hukman) at the time the condition is met, regardless of his mental state at that moment. This is why the insane husband's conditional divorce uttered while sane still takes effect, but an insane person's utterance is invalid from the start.
The analogy with the impotent man reinforces that the legal effect respects the state at utterance.
4
Note the practical example of the foreign woman
If a man says to a non-wife (ajnabiyyah) 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' and later marries her, no divorce occurs upon entry. This shows that the condition must be added to his ownership at the time of utterance or the condition itself must be linked to a scenario within his ownership.
The key is that the divorce is not effective outside a marriage.
5
Identify the words of condition
The text lists the words of condition: إن (in), إذا (idha), إذا ما (idha ma), كل (kull), كلما (kullama), متى (mata), متى ما (mata ma). It explains why they are called 'words' (alfaz) rather than 'letters' (huruf): some are nouns (like كل and متى) that accept tanween, while others are letters. The word 'إن' is mentioned first because it is purely for condition without implying time.
These words all serve to introduce a conditional clause; the ruling is the same regardless of which word is used.

Core Idea

Conditional divorces (ta'liq al-talaq) are common in Hanafi jurisprudence, but what happens when the condition occurs after the marriage has ended and the couple remarries? Learning Objective

Core Idea
Her word is law on her own state; her word is suspect on another's.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a nuanced scenario in conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq). However, this trust is not extended when her testimony affects others, such as a co-wife or a slave. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rulings in cases where personal knowledge is exclusive.

Determine whether the condition in the husband's statement is something only the wife can know (e.g., menstruation, love, hatred) or whether it could be known externally.

Check the scope of the statement
Is the wife claiming the condition for herself only, or does her claim also affect others (e.g., co-wife, slave)? The ruling depends on whether the claim involves others.
Her statement is accepted for herself but not for others due to suspected partiality.
3
Apply the principle of trustworthiness
For her own case, accept her statement because she is trustworthy (aminah) regarding her own state, as no one else can know it.
This is based on the maxim that what is only known to a person is accepted from them.
4

When her statement affects others, she is considered suspect (muttahamah) due to self-interest, so her statement is not accepted unless the husband confirms or the condition is externally known.
This prevents injustice to others based on her unilateral claim.
5
Consider timing and external knowledge
If she informs after the condition has lapsed (e.g., after menstruation and purification), her statement is not accepted. Also, if the condition becomes known externally, the ruling may change.
The condition must still be in effect at the time of her statement.

Conditional Divorce for a Pregnant Woman
Ruling on conditional divorce for a pregnant woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a conditional divorce when the condition is 'if you become pregnant' and identify when the oath is not broken.

Core Idea
A condition of future pregnancy does not trigger divorce for an existing pregnancy; the oath is not broken.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi divorce law: a husband says to his already pregnant wife, 'If you become pregnant, you are divorced.' At first glance, one might think the current pregnancy fulfills the condition. However, the Hanafi school interprets the condition as referring to a future pregnancy. Even if the husband intended the current pregnancy, the divorce does not occur because pregnancy is an indivisible state. Understanding this nuance prevents incorrect application of conditional divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the condition
The husband says to his pregnant wife: 'If you become pregnant, you are divorced.' The act is a conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq). Recognize that the wife is already pregnant at the time of utterance.
The phrase 'if you become pregnant' is a suspensive condition (shart mu'allaq).
2
Interpret the default meaning of the condition
In the Hanafi school, the default interpretation of such a condition (haml) is that it refers to a future pregnancy (haml mustaqbal), not the existing one. This is because the verb 'become pregnant' implies a new occurrence.
This aligns with the principle that conditions are understood as future events unless context indicates otherwise.
3
Analyze the exception: intention of the current pregnancy
If the husband explicitly intended the current pregnancy when making the statement, the condition is considered to refer to the existing pregnancy. However, even then, the oath is not broken (la yahanuth) and divorce does not occur.
The passage explains that pregnancy is a single meaning (ma'na wahid) with no multiple parts, so the condition cannot be 'triggered' for an already existing state.
4
Derive the legal consequence
Conclude that no divorce takes place in either scenario: the condition is either unfulfilled (future pregnancy) or, if intended for the current pregnancy, it does not cause a breach of oath because the condition is indivisible.
The ruling preserves the stability of marriage and avoids hasty divorce based on ambiguous wording.

Twins and Conditional Divorce: The Role of Uncertainty
Ruling on conditional divorce with twins and unknown order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi ruling on conditional divorce when the order of birth is unknown, distinguishing between the binding court ruling (qadā') and the recommended scrupulosity (tanazzuh).

Core Idea
When order unknown, court enforces minimum divorces; caution recommends maximum.

This lesson from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah deals with a nuanced case of conditional divorce. A husband sets different divorce numbers based on the gender of the child, and the wife delivers twins with unknown order. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that separates what is legally binding from what is recommended for piety. Understanding this distinction is crucial for both judges and individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition and the actual event
The husband said: 'If you give birth to a boy, you are divorced once; if a girl, twice.' The wife delivered both a boy and a girl, but the order of birth is unknown.
This is a conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq).
2
Analyze the two possible sequences
If the boy came first, one divorce occurs and the 'iddah ends with the girl's birth. If the girl came first, two divorces occur and the 'iddah ends with the boy's birth. In both cases, the 'iddah ends with the birth of the second child.
The 'iddah definitely ends regardless of order.
3
Apply the principle of uncertainty in court
Because the order is unknown, the court enforces the lesser number (one divorce) to avoid imposing a possibility. This is based on the legal maxim: 'Certainty is not removed by doubt.'
This is the qadā' ruling.
4
Understand the recommended scrupulosity (tanazzuh)
Out of caution (ihtiyāt) and piety, the husband is advised to consider himself as having issued two divorces (the higher number) to ensure he has fulfilled his obligation if the higher number was actually due.
This is not binding in court but is encouraged.
5
Conclude on the 'iddah
Regardless of the order, the 'iddah has definitely concluded because the birth of the second child ends it. The wife may remarry after observing the proper 'iddah, but the marriage is effectively dissolved.
The marriage is ended with certainty.
