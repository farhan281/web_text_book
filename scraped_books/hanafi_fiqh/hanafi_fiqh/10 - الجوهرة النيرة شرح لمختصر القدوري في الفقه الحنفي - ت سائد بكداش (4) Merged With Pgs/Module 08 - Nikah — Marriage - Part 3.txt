Conditions for Virgin's Consent by Silence
Details rules for virgin's silence as consent in marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a virgin's silence is considered consent in Hanafi marriage, and apply those conditions to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Silence of the virgin is consent only when the suitor is specified and she does not cry with sound.

In Hanafi marriage, a virgin's consent is essential, but her silence can speak volumes—provided the right conditions are met. This lesson walks through the precise rules that turn silence into valid consent, and the exceptions that void it. Understanding these conditions is key to ensuring a marriage is properly concluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Silence alone is not consent; the suitor must be specified. The text begins by affirming that silence only becomes consent when the guardian clarifies who is proposing.
2
Examine the condition of specifying the suitor
The guardian must name the proposed husband; otherwise silence has no effect. If no suitor is named, the 'extraction of desire' is not correct.
3
Analyze the effect of crying
Crying with sound (wailing) indicates displeasure and negates consent; silent tears from sadness at leaving family do not negate consent. The text distinguishes between sound and no sound, and even mentions sweet vs salty tears as indicators.
4
Consider the guardian's identity and mention of mahr
If the guardian is the father or grandfather, mentioning the husband suffices without specifying mahr. If the guardian is someone else, the mahr must also be mentioned for silence to be consent.
5
Apply to verbal responses and multiple suitors
If the virgin says 'someone else is better' before the marriage, it is not permission; if she says it after the marriage, it is acceptance. If multiple suitors are listed and she is silent, it is consent to any of them.

Commentary
This passage discusses the conditions under which a virgin's silence can be considered consent to marriage. The text begins by affirming that silence alone is not consent; the guardian must specify the suitor. This is because silence is ambiguous—it could indicate consent or reluctance. Once the suitor is named and she remains silent, her silence is taken as consent. However, if no suitor is specified, her silence has no legal effect. The passage then addresses the issue of specifying the mahr (dower). The stronger position (al-sahih) is that mentioning the mahr is not a condition for consent, since marriage is valid without a dower being named. However, some scholars held that consent requires mention of the mahr. In the Hanafi school, if the marrying guardian is the father or grandfather, specifying the husband is sufficient, but if the guardian is someone else (like a brother), then the mahr must also be mentioned. Next, the text rules that if the guardian marries her to a non-compatible (ghayr kuf') husband, her silence is not consent because the guardian lacks authority for such a marriage. Regarding crying: crying with sound (wailing) indicates displeasure and nullifies consent, while silent tears from sadness at leaving family are evidence of approval. Some scholars distinguish by the nature of tears—sweet or salty, cold or hot—but the primary distinction is the presence or absence of sound. The passage also covers two scenarios of verbal response: if the virgin says 'someone else is better,' this is not permission; but if she says it after the marriage has already taken place, it is retroactive acceptance (ijaza). Finally, if the guardian lists multiple potential husbands and she remains silent, it is considered consent to any of them, so the marriage is valid.

Key Terms
بكر (bikr): A virgin woman who has never been married.
رضا (ridā): Consent of the woman to the marriage, which is a condition for validity.
كفء (kuf'): Compatibility in social status, religion, lineage, etc. between spouses.
مهر (mahr): The dower or bridal gift given by the husband to the wife, a mandatory payment in marriage.

Verbal Consent Required for Previously Married Woman
Establishes that a thayyib must explicitly verbalize consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a thayyib must verbally consent to marriage, distinguishing it from the case of a virgin.

Core Idea
A thayyib must speak her consent; silence is not enough, unlike a virgin.

In Hanafi marriage law, the consent of the woman is essential. However, the method of expressing consent depends on her marital status. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra clarifies that a previously married woman (thayyib) must articulate her consent explicitly, while a virgin's silence is sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is seeking permission (isti'dhan) from a thayyib for marriage. The ruling is that her consent must be by speech (qaul).
2
State the primary proof
The hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'The virgin is asked for permission, and the thayyib expresses herself.' This hadith explicitly distinguishes the two cases.
Hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative forms 'tu'stamar' and 'tu'rib' indicate obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. The contrast shows that for the thayyib, speech is required.
Hanafi principle: al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
4
Consider the rational explanation ('illa)
Speech is not considered a flaw for a thayyib since she has been married before, so there is no impediment to her speaking. For a virgin, speaking might indicate lack of modesty.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
The Hanafi school unanimously holds that verbal consent is wajib for a thayyib; silence is insufficient.
This is the position of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Consent in Marriage: Virgin (Bikr) vs. Previously Married (Thayyib)
Explains consent requirements for virgin and thayyib, and effect of loss of virginity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the consent requirements for a virgin and a thayyib in Hanafi marriage, identify the legal effect of various causes of virginity loss, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the zina case.

Core Idea
A virgin's silence is consent; a thayyib must speak—except where virginity is lost by sin, a dispute divides the school.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between a virgin (bikr) and a previously married woman (thayyib) in matters of marital consent. This lesson explores the proofs from the Sunnah, the exceptions for non-sexual loss of virginity, and an important internal debate over a woman who lost her virginity through fornication (zina). Understanding these rules ensures that marriage contracts are valid and that guardians act in accordance with the Shari'a.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling for each type
Read the first part of the passage to determine the consent requirement for a thayyib (must speak) and for a virgin (silence suffices). Note the additional discussion on tears and the guardian's listing of suitors.
The text states: 'فلا بد من رضاها بالقول' for thayyib, and for virgin silence is consent unless she says 'someone else is better'.
2
Analyze the hadith proof and its Hanafi interpretation
The hadith 'البكرُ تُستأمر، والثيب تعرب عن نفسها' is cited. Understand that Hanafis interpret 'isti'mar' as allowing silent consent for the virgin, while 'ta'rib' requires explicit speech for the thayyib.
The reasoning: speech from a virgin indicates lack of modesty, but from a thayyib it is not a flaw.
3
Examine the effect of non-sexual loss of virginity
The passage lists cases like a leap (wathba), menstruation, wound, or aging—she remains in the ruling of a virgin (silence suffices). Identify the consensus point.
Even if physical virginity is gone, the woman retains the modesty of a virgin for consent purposes.
4
Explore the intra-Hanafi dispute on zina
Abu Hanifa holds that a woman who lost virginity by zina is still a virgin (silence enough). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) say she is a thayyib (must speak). Understand each side's proof and the thamarah al-khilaf.
Later fatwa prefers the Sahibayn's view as a precaution.
5
Note additional rulings on dower collection and ijaza
The passage mentions that the father may take the dower of a virgin without permission, but not of a thayyib. Also, if a marriage is contracted without proper consent and the woman later says 'someone else was better', it counts as ijaza (retrospective validation).
Ijaza completes the contract, but only if the woman had the capacity to consent at the time.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyirah deals with the conditions of consent in marriage for a virgin (bikr) and a previously married woman (thayyib), and the effect of loss of virginity. First, the text states that for a thayyib, consent must be verbal; silence is not enough. There is a discussion about interpreting tears as consent or dislike depending on their nature. For a virgin, if she says 'someone else is better' when the guardian mentions a suitor, that is not considered permission. However, if the guardian marries her and then informs her, her statement 'someone else was better' is considered validation (ijaza), meaning she retrospectively approves. If the guardian lists multiple men and she remains silent, marriage to any is valid because silence indicates consent to any. The hadith of the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) is cited: 'The virgin is asked for permission (musta'mar) and the thayyib declares herself.' The reasoning given is that speech from a thayyib is not unbecoming, whereas from a virgin it shows lack of modesty. If lost by fornication (zina), there is a dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds she remains in the ruling of a virgin, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) say she is like a thayyib and must speak. If lost by doubtful intercourse or invalid marriage, all agree she is a thayyib because the Law imposed iddah, dower, and lineage. Finally, the passage notes that if a man marries a woman thinking she is a virgin but finds her to be a thayyib upon consummation, she still receives full mahr. The father may take the mahr of a virgin without her permission, but not that of a thayyib without her permission.

ثيب (thayyib): A woman who has previously had lawful sexual intercourse (by valid marriage); for consent she must speak explicitly.
إجازة (ijāzah): Retrospective approval/validation of a marriage contract that was performed without prior consent.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Zina and Virginity Status
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zina and virginity status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on virginity lost by zina and apply the consensus conditions.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa treats zina-loss as bikr; the Sahibayn treat it as thayyib — unless punishment, habit, or notoriety exists, then consensus.

The previous lesson covered accidental causes. Here we enter the contested area: fornication (zina). The three founding Hanafi imams differ on whether a woman who lost virginity through zina is still considered a virgin for marriage consent. This lesson unpacks their arguments and the limits of the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that the woman remains a bikr; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold she is a thayyib.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
2
Understand the rationale
For Abu Hanifa, zina is not a manifest act (the Law does not attach 'iddah or mahr to it). For the Sahibayn, zina is analogous to shubha in being a form of intercourse that can lead to pregnancy.
The underlying principle is whether the Law treats the act as manifest.
3
Apply the condition
If the zina was punished (hadd), or habitual, or notorious, then there is consensus that she is a thayyib.
These conditions make the act public and akin to shubha.
4
Contrast with shubha/invalid marriage
For loss by shubha or invalid marriage, all Hanafis agree she is thayyib because the Law imposes 'iddah, mahr, and establishes nasab.
This is the litmus test for 'manifest act'.
5
Determine the practical consequence
In the absence of the conditions, Abu Hanifa's view allows silence as consent; the Sahibayn require explicit consent. The mufti chooses based on the circumstances.
The ikhtilaf has a direct impact on marriage procedure.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Nafaqah is tied to availability, mahr to ownership: father claims mahr but not nafaqa for minor.

The key is whether the mother had authority to receive the mahr on behalf of the daughter.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah is a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri covering several rulings on guardianship (wilayah) in marriage. It begins by addressing a woman who has no guardian from the 'asabah (agnatic relatives); in that case, her master who freed her (mawla al-'itaqah) can marry her off. The commentator explains that mawla al-'itaqah is considered the last of the 'asabah and takes precedence over dhawi al-arham (uterine relatives). Next, the passage discusses the situation when the nearest guardian is absent with an 'interrupted absence' (ghaybah munqati'ah). In such a case, the next guardian in line may perform the marriage. The order of female guardians is given from al-Mustafa: mother, daughter, son's daughter, daughter's daughter, son's son's daughter, and full sister. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the invalid grandfather (al-jadd al-fasid) being preferred over the sister according to Abu Hanifa. The passage then lists women from the father's side who have guardianship by consensus, and then cites the difference between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad regarding women from the mother's side (mother and maternal aunt). Abu Yusuf's position is clarified as being with Abu Hanifa according to the correct view. The principle that dhawi al-arham are preferred over the ruler (sultan) is stated. The commentator then addresses the definition of ghaybah munqati'ah, offering multiple opinions: al-Quduri's definition as a place reachable by caravans only once a year; the estimation of three days in al-Mustafa and al-Fatawa al-Kubra (upon which fatwa is given); a criterion based on loss of a suitable match; and Zufar's view that it is when the guardian's location is unknown. The Hanafi position that the farther guardian is preferred over the ruler is contrasted with al-Shafi'i's view that the ruler is preferred. Exceptions are noted: for a slave woman, if her master is absent, her relatives cannot marry her off; and an executor (wasi) has no authority to marry minors even if appointed by the father, because he lacks kinship. This passage provides a comprehensive treatment of priority among marriage guardians.

Key Terms
مولى العتاقة (mawla al-'itaqah): The master who freed a slave; considered the last of the 'asabah in marriage guardianship.
الغيبة المنقطعة (al-ghaybah al-munqati'ah): An absence of the guardian such that communication is cut off; defined variously as once-yearly caravan access, three days, or when seeking opinion causes loss of a suitable match.
الولي الأبعد (al-wali al-ab'ad): The more distant guardian in the hierarchy of marriage guardians, who takes precedence over the ruler when the closer guardian is absent.
ذوو الأرحام (dhawi al-arham): Uterine relatives (non-agnatic); they have guardianship after 'asabah and mawla al-'itaqah.
الوصي (al-wasi): An executor appointed by the father (or another) to manage affairs; he does not have authority to marry minors because he lacks kinship.

Defining Extended Absence and Its Legal Consequences for Guardianship
Definition of extended absence and its effect on wali priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the different Hanafi definitions of extended absence (ghaybah munqati'ah) and apply the rule that the farther guardian takes precedence over the sultan.

Core Idea
When the nearest guardian is unreachable for consultation, the farther guardian steps in—unless the woman is a slave-girl.

A guardian's absence can prevent a woman from marrying. But how long must the absence be to shift the right to the next guardian? The Hanafi school offers several benchmarks, from three days to once-a-year caravans. This lesson unpacks those definitions and the key principle: the farther wali is preferred over the sultan. It also explains an exception for slave-girls and the limited role of an executor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule on guardian hierarchy
The text states that when the nearer guardian is absent in an extended absence, the farther guardian is more entitled than the sultan. Understand this as the default Hanafi position.
This rule is from zahir al-riwayah.
2
Examine the multiple definitions of ghaybah munqati'ah
Al-Quduri defines it as once-a-year caravan access; other scholars say three days; another view ties it to losing the suitable match; Zufar links it to unknown whereabouts; al-Sughdi includes wanderers and hidden persons. Each definition determines when the farther wali may act.
The operative fatwa is on three days.
3
Note the exception for slave-girls
If the woman is a slave-girl and her master is absent, relatives cannot marry her. This is a specific exception to the general rule.
This is because the master's permission is required.
4
Understand the executor's lack of authority
An executor (wasi) appointed by the father cannot marry minors even if so instructed, because he lacks blood kinship.
This distinguishes Hanafi from other schools.
5
Recognize the inter-madhhab difference
Al-Shafi'i holds that the sultan is more entitled than the farther wali even during an extended absence. This contrasts with the Hanafi position.
The practical difference: validity of marriage contracted by the sultan.

The Grandfather vs Brothers Dispute in Marriage Guardianship
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on grandfather vs brothers priority in guardianship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the priority between the paternal grandfather and brothers in marriage guardianship, and explain the practical difference.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa gives the grandfather absolute priority over brothers; the Sahibayn allow either to act.

When a woman's father is absent, who has the right to be her guardian: her paternal grandfather or her brothers? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. Abu Hanifa holds that the grandfather is more entitled, regardless of whether the brothers are full or consanguine. His two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, maintain that either of them may perform the marriage. This lesson unpacks the dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the three imams' positions clearly
Abu Hanifa: grandfather is more entitled than brothers (full or consanguine). Abu Yusuf & Muhammad: each of them may independently give the woman in marriage.
The text says 'عند أبي حنيفة' and 'عندهما'.
2
Identify the scope of the dispute
The dispute applies whether the brothers are full (from both parents) or consanguine (same father). The grandfather is defined as paternal grandfather (ab al-ab).
The text explicitly includes both types of brothers.
3
Understand the practical thamarah al-khilaf
If the grandfather marries the woman without the brothers' consent, the marriage is valid according to Abu Hanifa but invalid according to the Sahibayn (if they consider the brothers have equal right). Similarly, if a brother marries her, Abu Hanifa would hold it invalid if the grandfather is present.
This affects the validity of past marriages.
4
Consider the broader context of wali hierarchy
The grandfather-brother issue is part of the general ranking of guardians in Hanafi fiqh: father > grandfather > brother > nephew, etc. The dispute is on the relative rank of grandfather vs brother.
The text does not provide explicit daleel; it is based on ijtihad.

Kafa'ah in Marriage: Rulings on Timing, Right-Holder, and Superiority of Knowledge
Rulings on consideration, right-holder, and ranking of kafa'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three core Hanafi rulings on kafa'ah: (1) it is considered only at the time of the contract, (2) it is a right of the woman, and (3) knowledge surpasses lineage in determining suitability.

Core Idea
Kafa'ah is a woman's right judged at marriage inception; knowledge honor outweighs lineage honor.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira distills essential Hanafi principles regarding suitability (kafa'ah) in marriage. It clarifies when kafa'ah is relevant, whom it benefits, and how competing factors like lineage and knowledge are weighed. These rulings protect the woman's choice while ensuring that her guardians can object only at the start, not later.

Guided Path
1

A client's marital status matches the nobility of their master — mawali of Banu Hashim are not equal to those of common Arabs.

This passage from Al-Jundi qualifies the general rule that mawali are equal in marriage. It explains that the nobility of the master determines the client's social standing for kafa'ah. Thus, a female client of Banu Hashim cannot be married to a common Arab mawla without her guardians' consent.
