Liability for Returning Borrowed Animal via Slave or Servant
Ruling on returning borrowed animal with owner's trusted person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a borrower is not liable when returning a borrowed animal to the owner's slave or hired servant, and apply the rule to differentiate between long-term and daily servants.

Core Idea
Returning to owner's slave or long-term servant removes liability; owner's implied consent suffices.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a specific scenario in the contract of i'arah (gratuitous loan). When a borrower returns the borrowed animal, the mode of delivery matters. If he entrusts it to the owner's slave or a permanent hired servant, the owner's satisfaction is presumed, and the borrower is discharged. However, this concession does not extend to daily workers or strangers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? The borrower returns the borrowed animal to the owner's slave or servant. The ruling: the borrower is not liable (no daman).
2
Identify the exceptional condition
The recipient must be a slave of the owner or a hired servant who is contracted by month or year (long-term). Daily servants are not included.
The text uses 'ajir' meaning long-term; daily 'ajir' is excluded.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is the owner's implied consent: if the owner himself received the animal, he would hand it to his slave. Thus the owner is satisfied with delivery to his slave.
This is qiyas based on custom and reasoning (talil).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Some restrict the ruling to slaves who tend animals; the correct view (asahh) is that any slave of the owner suffices.
The text adopts the general view.
5
Apply to the deposit analogy
The passage also implies that a borrower cannot deposit the borrowed item with a stranger; if he does, he remains liable.
Some scholars argue deposit (ida') is permissible since it is less than i'arah, but the text's ruling supports the opposite.

Returning Borrowed Items: Liability and Customary Exception
Ruling with proof from qiyas and custom exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on liability when a borrower returns an item to the owner's house, distinguish between customary and non-customary items, and apply the principle of 'urf in mu'amalat.

Core Idea
Returning a borrowed item to the owner's house suffices only if custom permits; otherwise, handover is required to avoid liability.

Imagine you borrow a kitchen blender from your neighbor and leave it in their garage without telling them. Is that enough to discharge your responsibility? According to Hanafi fiqh, the answer is: it depends on custom. In this lesson, we explore a ruling that contrasts with the stricter rules for deposits and stolen goods, showing how Hanafi school uses custom to moderate liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The borrower returns the borrowed object to the owner's house but does not hand it over personally. According to the main ruling (qiyas), the borrower is liable (daman) for loss or damage because he neither delivered to the owner nor his agent.
This qiyas is based on analogy with deposit (wadi'ah) and usurpation (ghasp).
2
Note the variant transmission
Some copies of the text state that the borrower is NOT liable, but the preferred view distinguishes based on custom.
The variant indicates an early intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Examine the custom-based exception
For items customarily left in the house (e.g., household utensils), placement in the house suffices and the borrower is not liable. For items like a slave or garment, handover is required.
Custom ('urf) is recognized as a legal basis for dispensation in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Contrast with deposit and usurped goods
For a deposit (wadi'ah) or usurped item, returning to the house never discharges liability because the owner's consent is explicit and limited; custom does not override.
This contrast highlights the role of custom: in ariyah, owner's implied consent based on custom is considered.
5
Apply the rule to a borderline case
If a pearl necklace is borrowed, it must be returned to the lender's hand because there is no custom of leaving such items in the house.
The custom must be established for the specific type of item.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from analogy (qiyas) and discussion of customary exceptions. The sharh explains that returning a borrowed item to the owner's house without personal delivery generally imposes liability (daman) on the borrower. However, there is a difference in the relied-upon ruling for items customarily left in the house, such as household utensils: for these, placement in the house suffices and the borrower is not liable. For items like a slave or garment, which are not customarily left unattended, physical delivery to the owner is required. The passage then contrasts this with the ruling for deposits (wadi'ah) and usurped goods (ghasp): for a deposit, returning to the house never discharges liability because the owner's consent is explicitly limited to the deposit contract. For borrowed items, custom ('urf) is considered, so if it is customary to leave such items in the house (e.g., tools), the borrower is excused. The example of a pearl necklace shows that if no custom exists, the strict rule applies.

عرف (urf): Customary practice that is recognized as a legal basis for dispensation.
وديعة (wadi'ah): Deposit for safekeeping; trust-based contract.

Wording of a Land Loan Contract: It'am vs I'ara
Intra-Hanafi dispute on contract wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the preferred wording for a land loan contract and identify the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prefers 'it'am' for land loans; the Sahibayn prefer 'i'ara'—both agree the contract is valid.

When drafting a contract for lending agricultural land, which word should you use? Abu Hanifa and his two companions disagree on whether 'it'am' (feeding) or 'i'ara' (lending) is the better choice. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and shows that the dispute is about clarity, not validity.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa holds that the borrower should write 'إنك قد أطعمتني' (you have fed me). Identify the key term 'it'am' and its agricultural connotation.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue for 'إنك قد أعرتني' (you have lent me) using the standard term 'i'ara'. Note their reliance on the literal coinage of the word.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn use qiyas: lending land is like lending a house, so the same term 'i'ara' should apply. Understand the analogy and its basis.
They argue that the literal coinage (al-mawdu') is superior.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes land from a house: 'it'am' is more specific to agriculture, avoiding ambiguity. Houses are only lent for dwelling, so 'i'ara' suffices there.
The specificity of 'it'am' prevents confusion with non-agricultural uses like building.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is the wording of the contract; the validity is unaffected. In case of dispute, the chosen wording may affect the burden of proof or clarity of terms.
Both wordings lead to a valid contract; the dispute is about recommended practice.

Presumption of Freedom and the Foundling's Status
Rulings with reasoning on ownership and paternity claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on claims of ownership and paternity regarding a foundling, and explain the legal reasoning using the presumption of freedom and the principle of preferring benefit over harm.

Core Idea
A foundling is free by default; only clear evidence can overturn this presumption.

When a foundling of unknown parentage is discovered, his legal status is presumed free. This lesson examines two claims that affect that status: a claim of ownership and a claim of paternity. The Hanafi rulings are based on the manifest state (al-zahir) and the maxim that benefit is preferred over harm in ambiguous situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims
The passage addresses two types of claims about a foundling: (1) someone claims he is his slave; (2) a slave claims he is his son. Each has a distinct ruling.
2
Determine the default presumption
The foundling is presumed free (al-zahir) because he is found in a society where the default status is freedom. This presumption can only be overturned by evidence.
In Hanafi usul, al-zahir is a probable proof that stands until a stronger proof overrides it.
3
Apply the rule for ownership claim
If a person claims the foundling is his slave without evidence, the claim is rejected. The foundling remains free. The passage adds a nuance: if the foundling affirms the claim after maturity, his affirmation is ineffective if free-person rulings (e.g., testimony, hadd) have already been applied to him; otherwise, he becomes the claimant's slave.
The elaboration from Al-Yanabi' shows the importance of prior legal status.
4
Apply the rule for paternity claim
If a slave claims the foundling is his son, the lineage is established and the foundling becomes free. The reasoning: establishing lineage is beneficial for the foundling (he gains a father and freedom), while slavery is harmful. The law prefers the beneficial outcome.
This uses the principle that benefit (manfa'ah) takes precedence over harm (darar) when both cannot be fully realized.
5
Note the maturity nuance from Al-Yanabi'
When a foundling reaches maturity and affirms a claim of ownership, the efficacy of his affirmation depends on whether he has already been treated as free. This illustrates that legal presumptions can become entrenched over time.
This is a secondary ruling that expands the main position.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Foundling's Religion: Three Narrations from Imam Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on foundling's religion criterion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the three transmitted narrations from Imam Muhammad regarding the foundling's religion when found by a Muslim or dhimmi in a Muslim land, and explain the rationale behind each.

Core Idea
Three narrations from Imam Muhammad: place, Islam, or finder's status — no single view is preferred here.

When a foundling is discovered in a Muslim land, its religion must be determined for legal purposes like inheritance, marriage, and custody. The Hanafi school transmits three different narrations from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani on the criterion. This lesson unpacks each narration and the reasoning behind it, showing the rich internal diversity within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
The question is: when a finder (al-multaqit) is Muslim in a Muslim place, or dhimmi in a Muslim place, what determines the foundling's religion? The answer varies according to different narrations from Imam Muhammad.
The term 'dhimmi' refers to a non-Muslim subject residing permanently in Muslim lands under state protection.
2
List the three narrations
The passage presents three narrations: (1) The narration of the Book of Foundlings (Kitāb al-Laqīṭ): considers the place. (2) The narration of the Book of Claims (Kitāb al-Da‘wā): considers Islam (i.e., the finder's faith). (3) A narration from Ibn Sama‘a from Muhammad: considers whichever the finder is (i.e., the finder's status as Muslim or dhimmi), based on the principle of quwwat al-yad (strength of possession).
Each narration is attributed to Imam Muhammad, showing that he transmitted multiple views.
3
Understand the rationale for each
First narration (place): The child is presumed to follow the dominant religion of the location. Second narration (Islam): The religion of the finder directly influences the foundling, especially since the finder becomes the guardian. Third narration (finder's status): Quwwat al-yad gives weight to the actual custodian's authority, so the foundling takes the religion of the finder.
The term 'quwwat al-yad' means the controlling power or authority of the hand (possession).
4
Recognize the absence of tarjih
The passage simply transmits these three narrations without indicating which one is preferred (muftā bihī). This is a memorization anchor: the student must know that multiple views exist, but the preferred view may be found elsewhere in later works.
This reflects the early transmission stage before tarjīh was established.

Foundling Rulings: Property, Guardianship, and Conflicting Claims
Expansion on foundling rulings with an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between property belonging to the foundling and lost property, explain why the finder cannot marry the foundling, and state the Hanafi dispute regarding a foundling claimed by two slaves.

Core Idea
The foundling's physical attachment determines property ownership, and the presumption of freedom prevails unless clear evidence of slavery.

A foundling (laqit) is an abandoned child with an unknown lineage. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses several practical issues: how to determine what property belongs to the foundling, why the finder cannot marry him, and what happens when two slaves or a Muslim and Christian each claim the foundling. We will examine each ruling and the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the effect of slave claimants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rulings covered
The passage contains three distinct rulings: ownership of property found with the foundling, prohibition of marriage by the finder, and the outcome when conflicting claims are made by two slaves or a Muslim and a Christian.
The dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the central legal issue.
2
Distinguish bound property from nearby property
Property that is physically bound or attached to the foundling (e.g., tied to his body or on an animal he rides) is considered his. If the property is merely placed near him, it is treated as a lost item (luqtah) for the finder to handle as a trust.
The key term is 'mashdud 'alayh' (bound on him).
3
Understand the marriage prohibition
The finder (multaqit) has no guardianship (wilayah) over the foundling for marriage because the foundling's lineage is unknown and the finder is merely a caretaker. Therefore, the finder cannot marry the foundling himself or arrange marriage for him.
This applies to both male and female foundlings; the finder's role is custodial, not parental.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on two slave claimants
When two slaves both claim the foundling as their son, Abu Hanifa rules that the foundling becomes their slave, reasoning that slaves cannot own property or grant freedom, so they cannot effectively claim fatherhood resulting in freedom. Muhammad rules that the foundling is free, prioritizing the presumption of freedom and the benefit of establishing lineage.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the child is enslaved or free.
5
Apply the principle of al-zahir to the Muslim/Christian case
If a Muslim claims the foundling is his slave and a Christian claims he is his son, the Christian's claim prevails because it leads to freedom (al-zahir), which aligns with the default presumption. The foundling is thus free and the son of the Christian.
The principle al-zahir (the apparent or preponderant presumption) favors freedom over slavery.

Ruling on ownership through revival and the permission dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ownership through revival, explain the intra-madhhab dispute on the need for imam's permission, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Who revives dead land owns it: but Abu Hanifa requires the imam's permission; the Sahibayn do not.

This passage presents the core legal ruling on reviving mawat land: the act of bringing it to productivity can lead to ownership. However, a significant intra-Hanafi dispute arises over whether the imam's permission is required. Abu Hanifa insists on permission, citing a hadith, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rely on a more general hadith. The passage also provides the proofs and notes the mufta bihi position.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling on ownership with permission
Whoever revives mawat with the imam's permission owns the land. This is agreed upon by all three imams.

Abu Hanifa interprets the permission hadith as specific (khass) and qualifying the general ('amm) hadith. The Sahibayn treat the general hadith as operative without restriction.

Conclude the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn: revival alone grants ownership. This is the zakir riwayah and the fatwa today.

العَادِيّ (al-'adi): Land that was originally without an owner, not associated with any ancient tribe ('Ad); it is ancient waste.
الإِمَام (al-imam): The ruler or his representative who authorizes the revival of mawat land.

The Validity of Sharecropping (Muzara'ah) in Hanafi Fiqh
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sharecropping validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi relied-upon position on muzara'ah and explain the intra-madhhab reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
The Hanafi mufta bihi holds sharecropping valid due to public necessity, despite Abu Hanifa's initial ruling of fasid.

This passage presents a dispute within the Hanafi school on the validity of muzara'ah (sharecropping). Abu Hanifa considered it invalid due to uncertainty, while his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani deemed it permissible, and their view became the operative fatwa. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how later Hanafi scholars prioritized public need over strict analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa rules muzara'ah as fasid (defective/void), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule it as ja'iz (permissible). Recognize the difference in hukm level.
Fasid in Hanafi terminology means the contract is defective but may still have legal effects, unlike batil which is void.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's Proofs
Abu Hanifa cites the hadith forbidding muhaqalah and muzabanah, and uses qiyas to the miller's qafiz (hiring for an uncertain measure) and hiring a shepherd for a share of offspring.
The hadith is sahih but its interpretation is contested.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's Proofs
The Sahibayn rely on custom (urf) and the principle that necessity permits (al-darurah tubih al-mahzurat). They argue that people commonly practice sharecropping and that strict analogy would cause hardship.
The text plainly states 'وعليه الفتوى' (and the fatwa is upon it).
4
Understand the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the contract is fasid; the worker gets ujr al-mithl (equivalent wage) and the produce goes to the seed owner. Under the Sahibayn, the contract is valid and the agreed share is legally binding.
This practical difference matters for dispute resolution.
5
Recognize the Tarjih
The Sahibayn's view is declared mufta bihi (the relied-upon position). The justification is public need: landowners may not find cash to hire workers, so sharecropping meets a necessity.
Tarjih is based on maslahah, not merely on strength of evidence.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of muzara'ah (sharecropping). The author begins by stating the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) that the contract is permissible (ja'iz). He then explains that the restriction to one-third or one-fourth mentioned in some narrations is due to custom. Next, he explains Abu Hanifa's view that the contract is invalid (fasid) by clarifying that 'bāṭila' here means defective. If the contract is fasid and no crop is produced, the worker gets the equivalent wage (ujr al-mithl) for his work. If seed came from the worker, he owes rental for the land and the crop goes to the seed owner. The proof for invalidity is that it is hiring for an uncertain wage, analogous to the miller's qafiz or hiring a shepherd for a share of offspring. The text then presents the Sahibayn's view as the fatwa-ready position (mufta bihi) based on necessity and custom. Abu Hanifa's proof is the hadith prohibiting muhaqalah and muzabanah; the author explains these terms: muhaqalah likely means sharecropping or barter of crops, and muzabanah is selling fresh fruit for dry dates by estimation. The fatwa follows the Sahibayn due to public need.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzāra‘ah): A contract of sharecropping where land is given to another to cultivate in exchange for a share of the produce.
المحاقلة (al-muḥāqalah): From haql (crop); said to be selling crop for crop, or possibly sharecropping. Used in the hadith prohibiting it.
المزابنة (al-muzābanah): Selling fresh dates on trees for dried dates by estimation, a prohibited form of sale.
