Rulings on Pre-existing Defects in Usurped Property
A ruling on pre-existing defects in usurped property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a usurper is not liable for death or defects caused by pre-existing conditions and apply the principle to new scenarios.

Core Idea
A usurper is not liable for loss caused by conditions that existed before the usurpation.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the law of ghasb (usurpation): when a usurped slave dies due to a hadd punishment for a crime committed before the usurpation, or due to pregnancy from a pre-existing marriage. The Hanafi school rules that the usurper is not liable because the cause of loss is attributed to the owner's possession, not the usurpation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
Examine the case: a female slave is usurped, but she had already committed zina or theft while with the owner, or she had a husband from before the usurpation. The death occurs from the hadd or pregnancy.
2
Isolate the cause of death
Determine whether the cause of death is a result of the usurpation or existed prior. Here, the hadd penalty or pregnancy originates from actions/status before the usurpation.
The text states 'because she perished in the hand of the owner' — the cause is attributed to the owner's possession.
3
Apply the liability principle
In Hanafi law, liability for loss follows the cause. Since the cause predates the usurpation, the usurper is not liable. He is only liable for new defects he introduces.
This principle prevents unjust enrichment and ensures fair allocation of risk.
4
Contrast with new defects
If the usurper himself causes a defect (e.g., beating the slave), he would be liable. This highlights the boundary: no liability for pre-existing conditions.

Calculating Guarantee of Diminution in Usurpation
States the method for calculating and guaranteeing diminution value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the compensation for diminution in usurped property by applying the two-step valuation method at the time of usurpation.

Core Idea
The usurper pays only the drop in value, not the full value.

When usurped property suffers a defect, the usurper must compensate for the diminution in value. This lesson explains the precise method: value the property as sound at the time of usurpation, then as defective, and pay the difference. This ruling from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar ensures fairness by pegging compensation to the moment of wrongdoing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Obligation
The passage begins 'When guarantee of the diminution is obligatory' — this condition is met when the usurped property becomes defective.
The obligation is triggered by the defect, not by the usurpation itself.
2
First Valuation: Sound Value
Value the property as if it were sound on the exact day the usurper took it. This is the baseline value.
Use market value at that time.
3
Second Valuation: Defective Value
Value the same property with the defect, also at the time of usurpation. This is the diminished value.
Both valuations use the same point in time.
4
Calculate the Difference
Subtract the defective value from the sound value. The result is the amount the usurper must pay.
This is the daman al-nuqṣān.

Liability for Sickness (Humma) in a Usurped Animal
Ruling on usurper's liability for death from fever after return or in his hand.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios of liability when a usurped animal dies from fever and calculate the amount owed by the usurper in each case.

Core Idea
A usurper pays only the diminution if the animal dies after return; he pays full value if it dies in his hand.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a usurper's liability when a usurped animal contracts a fever (humma) and later dies. The fever is a progressive condition that began in the usurper's custody, so the liability depends on where the death occurred and whether the fever started before or during the usurpation. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying the principles of daman (guarantee) in cases of progressive harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage describes two distinct situations: (a) animal becomes feverish in usurper's hand, returned, dies from that fever; (b) animal already feverish when usurped, dies in usurper's hand.
2
Determine the ruling for scenario one
Usurper owes only the diminution in value caused by the fever, not the full value. Reason: death was caused by a condition that began in his hand but culminated after return.
3
Determine the ruling for scenario two
Usurper owes the full value of the animal as it was at the time of usurpation (i.e., its feverish value). Reason: the animal died in his hand, so he is responsible for its entire loss.
4
Understand the rationale
The key distinction is that the fever is a progressive, ongoing harm; the usurper's liability is limited to the harm that originated during his custody. In the first scenario, the death itself occurred in the owner's possession, but the cause (fever) began with the usurper, so he compensates the loss he directly caused (the fever's effect on value).
5
Apply the principle to similar cases
Consider other progressive defects (e.g., a wound that becomes infected) and how liability would be apportioned using the same causation-based reasoning.

Ruling on slaughtering another's sheep with owner's option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the owner's two options when his sheep is slaughtered without permission and apply the rule to scenarios involving pregnant animals and non-edible animals.

Core Idea
Slaughtering another's sheep gives the owner a choice: demand full value (hand over carcass) or demand only the deficiency.

This passage from the chapter on ghasb (usurpation) presents a nuanced ruling. At first glance, it might seem that the usurper must always pay full value, but the Hanafi school gives the owner an option, recognizing that the carcass still has value. The text then extends the principle to complex scenarios involving pregnant animals and non-edible animals, showing how causation affects liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The matn states that the owner has an option: either demand full value (qimah) and hand over the carcass, or demand the deficiency (nuqsan). Note that this applies to edible animals.
2
Trace the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
The commentator notes that this ruling is the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya) and the view of Muhammad. There is another transmission (likely from Abu Yusuf) that only allows deficiency. Understand that the mufta bihi is the option.
3
Apply causation principle to pregnant animals
If the pregnancy was caused by the owner, and the animal dies from it in the usurper's hands, no liability. If pregnancy not from owner, usurper is liable for full value. This shows that liability is linked to who caused the destruction.
4
Distinguish edible vs. non-edible animals
For non-edible animals, cutting off a part results in full value liability because of complete consumption. For edible animals, only deficiency is owed if the carcass is left usable.

Tearing Another's Garment: Liability for Small vs Large Tear
Ruling on liability for tearing a garment, small vs large tear

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a small tear and a large tear for liability purposes and state the Hanafi ruling for each, including the owner's options and conditions for claiming deficiency only.

Core Idea
Small tear = deficiency; large tear = full value due to consumption.

When someone damages another's property, Hanafi law distinguishes between minor and major damage. In the case of tearing a garment, if the tear is small and does not destroy utility, the wrongdoer pays only the decrease in value. But if the tear is large and renders the garment unusable, he must pay its full value and then becomes the owner of the damaged garment. The owner, however, also has the option to claim only the deficiency and keep the garment, provided certain conditions are met.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two tear scenarios
The passage contrasts a 'small tear' (kharq yasīr) with a 'large tear' (kharq kathīr). Recognize that the classification depends on the effect on the garment's utility.
A small tear does not destroy general utility; a large tear destroys most of the benefits.
2
State the liability for each
For a small tear: liability is only for the deficiency (the reduction in value). For a large tear: the owner may claim the full value because it is considered consumption (istihlāk).
The matn uses 'yubṭilu ʿalayhi ʿāmmat manāfiʿih' to define a large tear.
3
Understand the reasoning behind full value for large tear
A large tear that destroys most utility is treated as consumption (istihlāk), similar to destroying the item entirely. Therefore, the wrongdoer pays full value and then owns the garment.
Consumption triggers liability for full value, and the owner cannot have both the garment and its price.
4
Analyze the owner's option to claim deficiency only
The owner may choose to take the deficiency and keep the garment, but only if three conditions hold: (1) the garment is not completely consumed (e.g., not burned), (2) it has not been increased in value (e.g., not dyed), (3) the item is not from food or weighed goods where similar replacement is required.
These conditions ensure that claiming deficiency does not lead to injustice.

This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar Al-Quduri clarifies a crucial distinction in Hanafi law: the usurper does not automatically own the usurped property simply by taking it. We will explore the three conditions for ownership and the ruling on benefit, addressing a common misunderstanding.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage is a series of rulings on how usurpation of movable property can lead to transfer of ownership from the original owner to the usurper, along with the corresponding obligation to compensate. Discharge of the Usurper by Owner's Consumption: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Dispute on when usurper is freed from liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on discharge of the usurper when the owner consumes the usurped property without knowledge, and explain the Shafi'i objection based on ghurur.

Core Idea
Actual delivery of the property to the owner discharges the usurper, even without the owner's knowledge.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: if a usurper feeds the owner's own food to him without his knowledge, is the usurper free from liability? The Hanafi school holds that the actual delivery of the property (ada' haqiqa) suffices, regardless of the owner's awareness. The Shafi'i school argues that such an act is deceptive (ghurur) and not a valid return of property. This lesson unpacks the reasoning on both sides and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Recognize the situation: a usurper (ghasib) takes food, then either the owner eats it (knowingly or unknowingly) or the usurper feeds it to the owner without the owner's knowledge. Also consider the variation with clothing.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis
The Hanafi position is that the usurper is discharged (bari'a) because the owner's property has reached him (ada' haqiqa). Ignorance does not nullify the legal effect of receipt.
Key principle: actual delivery of the 'ayn is sufficient.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi nuance
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold a different view if the usurper first transforms the property (e.g., grinding wheat). Then the usurper owns the transformed item, and feeding it to the owner is a voluntary act, not a discharge. But the relied-upon view overrides this.
'Owned by grinding' is a technical point about when liability shifts.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i argues that the usurper's act is not a commanded performance (ada' ma'mur bih) because it involves deception (ghurur), which the Law prohibits. Therefore the usurper remains liable.
Shafi'i focuses on the intention and method of return.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response and tarjih
The Hanafi response relies on the example of a buyer freeing a slave without knowledge: the freeing is valid and possession is deemed. The actual delivery of the owner's property is effective. The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school.
Thamarah al-khilaf: if the owner later discovers, Hanafi says the usurper is free; Shafi'i says he still owes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a Muslim consumes a dhimmi's wine or pig, explain the reasoning based on the prohibition of ownership, and apply the rule to distinguish between guaranteeing value versus a like-for-like replacement.

Core Idea
A Muslim must pay the value (qima) of a dhimmi's wine or pig because he cannot own them, even if they are standard commodities.

This passage from Kitab al-Ghasb addresses a unique scenario: a Muslim consumes the wine or pig of a dhimmi. Since these items are lawful for the dhimmi, they are considered his property. But because the Muslim is prohibited from owning them, the usual rule of replacing a standard commodity (mithli) with its like is set aside, and value (qima) must be paid. The commentary also discusses a related issue of feeding usurped wheat to the owner and the Shafi'i disagreement.

1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a Muslim consuming (istihlak) a dhimmi's wine or pig. The ruling: the Muslim must guarantee the value (qima) of those items.

Learning Objective
Apply the Hanafi rule regarding liability and ownership rights when a usurper tans a dead animal's hide, distinguishing between valueless and valuable tanning materials.

Core Idea
Tanning with valueless material is not consumption; liability depends on whether the skin still exists and the value added.

When a person unlawfully takes the skin of a dead animal and tans it, the fiqh ruling is not simply that he must return it or pay compensation. The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the tanning added value to the skin. This passage from the Book of Usurpation explains the precise conditions under which the owner can reclaim the skin, and when compensation is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
Determine that the act is usurping (ghasb) the skin of a dead animal (maytah) and then tanning it. Note that the skin is from an animal not Islamically slaughtered, so it is initially impure and not owned as wealth.
2
Distinguish between valueless and valuable tanning materials
Check whether the usurper tanned the skin with something that has no value (like sun and dust) or with something of value (like alum or bark). This distinction affects liability if the skin perishes, and also affects the owner's right to reclaim.
Tanning with valueless material is not considered consumption (istihlak) of the skin.
3
Apply the rule for existence vs. destruction of the skin
If the skin still exists, the owner may take it back. If tanned with valueless material, he takes it for free; if with valuable material, he must pay the increase in value caused by the tanning. If the skin perished after tanning with valueless material, there is no liability; if consumed (e.g., destroyed intentionally), liability by consensus.
The increase is measured by the difference between the value of the skin if the animal were properly slaughtered (untanned) and its value after tanning.
4
Handle the special case of abandonment on the road
If the owner threw the dead animal on the road and someone took and tanned it, there are two opinions: one says the owner loses the right to reclaim because abandonment implies permission; another says he may still reclaim.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Usurpation deals with the specific case of a usurper who tans the skin of a dead animal (meytah). If tanned with valuable materials, the owner must compensate the usurper for the increase in value (the difference between the value of the skin if the animal were properly slaughtered and untanned, versus its value tanned). The usurper has a right to detain the skin until he receives this compensation. Profit from Usurped Capital: Charity in Action
Ruling on profit from successive trades with usurped capital

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on profit from usurped capital and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Profit from usurped capital is tainted — all of it must be given in charity.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a sequence of trades using usurped money. The issue is not whether the original capital must be returned (it must), but what to do with the profit generated. The Hanafi school's majority view is that all profit is unlawful and must be disposed of in charity, while Abu Yusuf held a different opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The text describes a person who usurps 1000 dirhams, buys a slave girl, sells her for 2000, then buys another and sells for 3000. Trace the sequence.
2
Determine the ruling on profit
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the majority), the entire profit (2000 dirhams in this case) must be given in charity.
Profit = final amount minus original capital = 3000 - 1000 = 2000.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Yusuf differed, though his exact view is not elaborated here. This is a known ikhtilaf within the school.
The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
4
Extend the ruling to the depositary
The same rule applies to a depositary who uses entrusted money without permission and generates profit.
The depositary is liable for the deposit and must also give profit in charity.

Liability for Breaking Musical Instruments: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for breaking musical instruments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on liability for breaking musical instruments, identify the basis of the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and evaluate which position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Musical instruments are property with protected value; liability exists because of potential lawful use.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute on whether breaking a Muslim's musical instrument (like a lute, drum, flute, or tambourine) incurs liability (daman) and whether such instruments can be sold. Abu Hanifa holds that they are property (mal) with value, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue that they are akin to wine—void of value because they are prepared for sin. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi jurists weigh the potential for lawful use against the predominant unlawful use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Determine what act is being discussed (breaking instruments) and the ruling (liability or no liability) according to each side.
Focus on the legal consequences: compensation and validity of sale.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa rules that the breaker is liable and sale is valid because instruments can be used lawfully. He analogizes them to a singing slave-girl, which has lawful uses despite potential misuse.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
The Sahibayn rule no liability and sale is void because the instruments are primarily for sin, so they lose monetary value like wine.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the breaker must pay compensation; under the Sahibayn, there is no compensation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi authorities, such as al-Hidayah, favor Abu Hanifa's view, making it the relied-upon position for fatwa.
This is an example of tasarruh (preference) in the school.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Liability for Umm Walad and Mudabbarah
Dispute on compensation for two types of slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on compensation for an umm walad and a mudabbarah, and explain the qiyas used by the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Compensation depends on marketability: Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on whether an umm walad has recognized value.

Within the Hanafi school, the ruling on compensation for usurping a slave woman with a child (umm walad) versus one promised freedom (mudabbarah) is disputed. Abu Hanifa holds that only the mudabbarah is compensable, while the Sahibayn extend liability to both through analogy. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the two slave types
The passage deals with a usurper (ghasib) who takes an umm walad or a mudabbarah, and the slave dies in his possession. Determine the key characteristics of each slave type.
Umm walad: has born a child to master, cannot be sold. Mudabbarah: promised freedom at master's death, can be sold during his life.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules that the usurper must compensate for the mudabbarah but not for the umm walad. The reason is that the umm walad's value is not established (mutaqawwim) because she cannot be sold or seized for debts.
Marketability is key: only property that can be sold has a recognized monetary value for compensation.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that both slaves are compensable. They argue that both have monetary value because they are owned and have a right to freedom; the mudabbarah's value is agreed upon, and the umm walad is analogous.
Their evidence includes that the mudabbarah can be taken for creditors and heirs, and the umm walad shares the right to freedom.
4
Analyze the qiyas used by the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn analogize the umm walad to the mudabbarah. The asl (original case) is the mudabbarah, the far' (new case) is the umm walad. The illa (effective cause) is the right to freedom established for both, implying that both have monetary value.
Abu Hanifa does not accept this qiyas because the illa is not operative: inability to sell negates marketability.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the usurper pays less (only for mudabbarah); under Sahibayn, pays for both. Later fatwa often follows the Sahibayn, so compensation for both is the operative ruling today.
This shows how later scholars may adopt a view different from the founder.

Dispute on liability for breaking instruments used for sin

Musical instruments are property because of lawful uses, so breaking them incurs liability — Abu Hanifa.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: should a person be liable for breaking a musical instrument belonging to another Muslim? Abu Hanifa holds yes, because the instrument is property (mal) with potential lawful uses. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that since the instrument is intended for sin, its valuation is void like wine. The dispute turns on whether the presence of any lawful use preserves property status.
