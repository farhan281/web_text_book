Core Idea
A total exception ('three except three') does not retract the divorce — the exception is void, and three divorces occur.

A man declares 'You are divorced three times except three.' Does the exception negate the entire divorce? Hanafi jurists clarify that such total exception is invalid, preserving the original pronouncement. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the dispute over its legal nature, and the analogy to wills that supports the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is saying 'three except three' in a divorce context. The ruling is that three divorces occur.
Total exception is not valid in Hanafi law.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to wasiyya (will). In a will, if the testator excludes the entire bequest, the exception fails and the bequest is valid.
The same reasoning applies to divorce.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
If total exception were retraction, the will would be void because retraction is allowed. Since the will remains valid, the exception is merely corrupt, not retraction.
This proves that total exception does not nullify the original statement.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars consider total exception a retraction (ruju‘) that entirely cancels the divorce. The Hanafi school holds it is a corrupt exception and not retraction.
The Hanafi view is deemed correct by the author.

Despite Abu Yusuf's logical reasoning, the fatwa in the Hanafi school relies on Abu Hanifa's position. This is based on caution in matters of divorce and the principle that multiple exceptions in one utterance are considered cumulatively.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Raj‘ah (Book of Revocable Return) discusses the actions that constitute a valid return (raj‘ah) after a revocable divorce (talaq raj‘i). In Hanafi law, a husband can revoke the divorce during the ‘iddah period without a new marriage contract, and this revocation can be by either explicit words or certain actions done with desire. The base text (from al-Quduri) lists four actions: intercourse, kissing, touching, and looking at the private part—all must be accompanied by desire (shahwah). The commentator (Ibn Nujaym?) explains each. First, intercourse establishes raj‘ah by consensus. Kissing on the mouth is also by consensus, but on other body parts there is difference; the preferred view is that kissing anywhere with desire creates the prohibition of marriage affinity (hurmah al-musaharah), meaning the wife becomes permanently forbidden if not returned. However, the text notes that some scholars restrict it to the mouth. Second, touching with desire: Abu Hanifah and Muhammad consider it raj‘ah regardless of whether the husband initiated or the wife initiated, while Abu Yusuf distinguishes based on the husband's ability to prevent. Looking at the private part with desire: Abu Hanifah considers it raj‘ah; Abu Yusuf disagrees. Imam al-Shafi‘i's position is that raj‘ah must be by words only. The passage also covers the validity of using kinayah (implied) words for raj‘ah, and the issue of testimony: witnesses cannot testify to internal desire, so they cannot prove raj‘ah through kissing unless they observe external signs. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school is the view of Muhammad that marriage wording suffices, and the chosen view is that any action with desire (including looking) constitutes raj‘ah according to Abu Hanifah.

Touch with desire is raj'ah; but if the wife initiates, Abu Yusuf requires the husband's non-prevention.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, addresses the rulings of raj'ah (revocable return to marriage) during the 'iddah period after a revocable divorce. Requirement of Witnesses for Raj'ah
Inter-madhhab dispute on the necessity of witnesses for raj'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the necessity of witnesses for raj'ah and evaluate the evidence and counterarguments between the Hanafi and Maliki schools.

Core Idea
Witnesses for raj'ah are strongly recommended but not required; the Quranic command is for guidance, not obligation.

The question of whether witnesses are necessary for a valid raj'ah is a key point of difference between the Hanafi and Maliki schools. This lesson examines the Hanafi position that witnesses are mustahabb (recommended), not wajib (obligatory), based on a careful reading of the Quranic texts. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents both the Hanafi argument and the Maliki critique.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
The Hanafi school holds that having two witnesses to a declaration of raj'ah is recommended (mustahabb) but not obligatory. The raj'ah remains valid even without witnesses.
2
Identify the Maliki Objection
Imam Malik argues that witnesses are obligatory, citing the command in Quran 65:2: 'And call to witness two just persons among you.' The imperative (ashhidu) indicates obligation.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Proof
Hanafis rely on other verses that are absolute (mutlaq) regarding the act of taking back: 'so retain them with kindness' (2:231) and 'there is no sin upon them if they reconcile' (2:230). These do not mention witnesses, so the command in 65:2 is interpreted as recommendation.
4
Understand the Hanafi Response
The imperative can be overridden by other evidence showing recommendation, and the context of retaining wives does not require witnesses for validity. The command serves to preserve rights, not to invalidate the act.
5
Apply the Practical Consequence
In practice, Hanafis consider a raj'ah valid even without witnesses; Malikis require two witnesses for validity. This difference affects the reliability of the marital status.

Intra-Hanafī dispute on tayammum and ʿiddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abū Ḥanīfah, Abū Yūsuf, and Muḥammad on whether tayammum alone ends the ʿiddah, and identify the muftā bihī position.

Core Idea
Tayammum alone does not end ʿiddah; prayer after tayammum does, according to the majority.

In this lesson, we delve into an intra-Hanafī dispute regarding a woman in travel who performs tayammum (dry ablution) due to lack of water. The question is whether tayammum alone is sufficient to end her ʿiddah, or whether she must also pray. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to distinguish between rulings based on the nature of purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context
The woman is a traveler who cannot use water, so she performs tayammum. The dispute is about the effect of tayammum on the ʿiddah.
This only applies to Muslim women; Kitābiyyah are not addressed.
2
State the position of the majority (Abū Ḥanīfah and Abū Yūsuf)
They hold that tayammum alone does not end the ʿiddah; she must pray after tayammum.
The right of revocation ceases only with prayer, not with tayammum alone.
3
State the position of Muḥammad
He holds that tayammum alone ends the ʿiddah, even without prayer.
He equates tayammum with ghusl.
4

Majority: tayammum is void upon seeing water, so it is not equivalent to ghusl; prayer, however, remains valid even after finding water. Muḥammad: tayammum permits what ghusl permits.
The key is whether the ruling (permitting prayer) is permanent or conditional.
5

The fatwā is that the right ceases upon commencing the prayer, not upon tayammum alone.
This is the relied-upon position in later works.

Forgetting a complete limb invalidates ghusl; forgetting less than a limb does not.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Qudūrī discusses the rulings on when a woman's ʿiddah (waiting period) ends and consequently the husband's right of revocation (rajʿah) ceases. The base text (matn) is presented first, then the commentator expands on each part. The first ruling: if the blood from the third menstrual cycle stops after exactly ten days (which is the maximum duration of menstruation in the Hanafī school), then the ʿiddah ends immediately upon cessation, even if the woman has not performed ghusl (ritual bath). The reason is that menstruation cannot exceed ten days, so the blood's stop at that point conclusively indicates the end of the ʿiddah. The second ruling: if the blood stops at less than ten days, the ʿiddah does not end until she performs ghusl or a complete prayer time passes without her having prayed (due to her being in a state of impurity). This is because when blood stops before ten days, it may return within the menstrual period; thus, the ʿiddah is not considered ended until either ghusl confirms the cessation (as the blood does not return after ghusl) or the passage of a prayer time indicates that she has become pure. The commentator then distinguishes a Muslim woman from a Kitābiyyah (Scripture woman): for a Kitābiyyah, the ʿiddah ends with the mere cessation of blood, whether at the maximum or minimum, because ghusl is not obligatory upon her, so no further sign is needed. However, if she prays, the right ceases because the prayer is valid and not invalidated by later water. Muhammad argues that tayammum itself permits what ghusl permits, so the right ceases upon tayammum regardless of prayer. The later fatwā supports that the right ceases upon commencing the prayer. Permissibility of Remarriage After Ba'in Divorce
States the ruling for ba'in divorce and its basis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on remarriage after a ba'in divorce less than three and explain the legal proof (dalil) that supports it.

Core Idea
A ba'in divorce less than three does not sever the marital bond completely; the woman remains lawful (hilla) for the husband to remarry.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, a commentary on Al-Quduri, addresses a crucial point in family law: what happens after an irrevocable divorce that is not the third? Many assume that an irrevocable divorce ends the marriage entirely, but the Hanafi school distinguishes between ba'in divorces less than three and the full three divorces. The ruling is clear and practical, and the proof offered relies on the nature of revocable divorce. We will walk through the ruling and its legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
First, locate the specific act being ruled upon: a man who has divorced his wife with a ba'in divorce that is less than three divorces. The ruling is that he 'may marry her' during her iddah or after it ends. This is a permission (ibaha).
The act is remarriage to the same woman after a ba'in divorce.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The commentator provides a proof: 'Indeed, revocable divorce does not remove ownership, nor does it annul the contract.' The proof is based on the continued effects of a revocable divorce, such as the right of the husband to take her back without her consent, and the wife remaining subject to zihar, ila', and li'an. This shows that the marital bond is not fully severed.
The proof is a logical inference (dalil aqli) from the characteristics of revocable divorce.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The link between the proof and the ruling: If a revocable divorce does not remove ownership despite dissolving the marriage, then a ba'in divorce less than three also does not completely sever the bond. The third divorce is the only one that removes all connection. Therefore, the woman remains lawful (hilla) for the husband, and he may remarry her.
The key is the distinction between ba'in and the final three divorces.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf (Disagreement)
There is no significant disagreement among the four Sunni schools on this particular ruling. All schools permit remarriage after a ba'in divorce less than three. The only point of discussion is whether the husband can remarry during iddah or only after, and the Hanafi school permits both.
This unanimity (ijma'?) strengthens the ruling.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih (Preference)
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is exactly as stated: it is permissible to remarry during or after iddah. This is the position of the school and is based on the clear proof that the marriage bond is only severed completely by the third divorce.
The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah as it comes from Al-Quduri.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Marriage contract plus intercourse, not contract alone, makes a woman lawful after three divorces.

This lesson examines a fundamental ruling in family law: what must happen for a woman divorced three times to become permissible to her first husband again? The answer involves both a valid marriage to another man and actual consummation. We will explore the textual proofs and address a historical dissenting opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a woman divorced three times (or twice if a slave). The ruling is that she is unlawful (haram) for her first husband until two conditions are met: marrying another man and consummating the marriage.
The ruling applies whether the second marriage ends by divorce or death.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith: 'She is not lawful for the first until she tastes the sweetness of the other.' The Quranic verse (2:230) is also cited: 'until she marries another husband.'
The hadith is sahih (Bukhari and Muslim).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith's metaphor 'taste sweetness' indicates actual intercourse, as sweetness refers to pleasure experienced during intercourse. The Quran mentions both 'nikah' (marriage) and 'zawj' (husband), implying two distinct requirements: contract and consummation.
Hanafi usul holds that when two terms are used, each carries independent meaning.
