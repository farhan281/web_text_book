Definition of Khuntha Mushkil and Prayer Positioning
Defines khuntha mushkil and details prayer positioning rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khuntha mushkil and apply the Hanafi prayer positioning rules, including consequences for incorrect row placement.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil must stand between rows to safeguard the validity of everyone's prayer.

What happens when a person's gender cannot be determined? Islamic law prescribes precaution. This passage defines the khuntha mushkil and explains the careful rules for his prayer in congregation. Understanding these rules prevents unknowing invalidation of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of khuntha mushkil
Note the condition: none of the established gender signs appear. The author uses the masculine pronoun to avoid assigning a gender.
Emission of semen is not a sign because it occurs in both genders.
2
State the overarching principle
The khuntha mushkil is treated with caution (ahwat) in all matters. This principle drives the prayer ruling.
3
Determine the prayer positioning
Apply ahwat: stand between men's and women's rows. This avoids the risk of invalidating others' prayers (if actually a woman) or one's own prayer (if actually a man).
4
Analyze consequences of wrong placement
If he stands in the women's row, he repeats his prayer. If in the men's row, his prayer is valid but those adjacent repeat theirs as a precaution.
The repetition by neighbors is due to the possibility he is a woman.
5
Identify preferred practices
He is encouraged to wear a qina' (head cover) and sit like a woman. Al-Hidayah confirms this is mustahab (recommended), not obligatory.
Omission of qina' does not invalidate his prayer.

Circumcision of the Hermaphrodite: Purchase of a Slave-Girl
Ruling on circumcision of the hermaphrodite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on procuring circumcision for a khuntha and explain the conditions for purchasing a slave-girl from his wealth or the treasury.

Core Idea
A slave-girl is bought for the khuntha's circumcision; look is permissible due to sex doubt.

This lesson addresses the practical obligation of arranging circumcision for a hermaphrodite (khuntha). The Hanafi fiqh provides a detailed procedure to ensure that the circumcision is performed while maintaining modesty and legality, given the ambiguity of the khuntha's sex. The ruling explains who bears the cost and under what circumstances the state treasury intervenes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is arranging circumcision for the khuntha. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) upon the guardian if the khuntha has wealth; otherwise upon the imam from the treasury.
Circumcision itself is sunnah, but ensuring it for the khuntha is a necessity.
2
Locate the condition for purchase
If the khuntha has wealth, a slave-girl is bought from his money. If not, the imam buys from the public treasury.
Financial capability determines the source.
3
Understand the permissibility of looking
The female slave is permitted to see the khuntha's private parts because of the doubt: if he is male, she is his slave; if female, a woman looks at a woman.
This resolves the normal prohibition of looking at awrah.
4
Note the post-circumcision procedure
After circumcision, the slave-girl is sold and the price returned to the treasury (if bought from it) or to the khuntha's estate (if from his wealth).
The purchase is only for the need; after need, she is sold.
5
Apply the ruling to modern context
Today, a professional female doctor or nurse may be hired instead of a slave-girl; the principle of necessity and sex doubt still applies.
The underlying illa is the need for circumcision and the permissibility of looking due to doubt.

Dress Code for the Hermaphrodite: Precautionary Ruling of Imam Muhammad
Ruling on dress of the hermaphrodite according to Imam Muhammad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Muhammad's ruling on dress for the khuntha and explain why wearing men's clothing incurs a dam.

Core Idea
Wear women's clothing to avoid doubt; if you wear male, offer a dam.

This lesson examines the dress ruling for a hermaphrodite (khuntha) according to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Due to the ambiguity of sex, he recommends wearing women's clothing out of caution, and imposes a blood sacrifice (dam) if the khuntha wears men's clothing, as a precaution against violating gender-specific norms. This ruling highlights the Hanafi principle of ihtiyat (precaution) in matters of doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the khuntha's choice of clothing. Muhammad's ruling: wearing women's clothing is recommended, and no penalty; wearing men's clothing requires a dam.
This is an intra-Hanafi opinion; the main school view may differ.
2
Understand the reasoning for women's clothing
Because it is not safe that the khuntha is a woman, so covering her (by wearing female dress) is better than exposing her (by wearing male dress).
The precaution is to assume the more covering option.
3
Analyze the dam ruling
If the khuntha wears men's clothing, a dam is required out of precaution (ihtiyatan) because he might be a woman, and thus violating proper dress.
Dam is an expiation, not a punishment, but indicates the act is considered violation of a recommendation or prohibition.
4
Identify the legal category
The dam for wearing men's clothing shows it is considered makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) or haram due to the possibility of crossing gender lines.
It is a precautionary measure to safeguard against sin.
5
Contrast with the majority Hanafi view
The main Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) might not require a dam; Muhammad's view is an ihtiyat (precautionary) opinion.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) may be the more lenient view.

Ruling on Washing a Deceased Hermaphrodite
Ruling on washing a deceased hermaphrodite with tayammum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for washing a deceased hermaphrodite with unknown sex and explain the rationale for replacing washing with tayammum.

Core Idea
When sex is unknown, precaution dictates tayammum, not washing, to avoid unlawful touching.

This lesson addresses a unique scenario in funeral rites: a person born with ambiguous genitalia (khuntha) who dies before their sex is determined. Since direct washing would require looking at and touching the body, and it is unknown whether the washer is of the same sex, the Hanafi school mandates a special procedure. We will explore the ruling, the reasoning behind it, and a scholarly alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The khuntha's sex is unknown. Washing requires uncovering the body; if the washer is of the opposite sex, it would be haram. Thus, a default solution is needed.
The ruling applies only to adults who are sexually desired.
2
Recall the default solution
The default for the dead is washing (ghusl). Here, it is replaced by tayammum (dry ablution) to avoid forbidden contact.
Tayammum is normally for living persons lacking water, but applied here as a substitute for purification.
3
Distinguish the washer's relationship
If a non-mahram (stranger) performs tayammum, he must use a cloth as a barrier. If a mahram relative does it, no barrier is needed because looking/touching is permissible.
Mahram includes father, brother, son, etc. – those within prohibited marriage degrees.
4
Consider the objection and response
An interlocutor asks: why not buy a slave-girl to wash the body, analogous to the ruling for circumcision? The reply: the deceased cannot own property, so a purchased slave-girl would be a non-mahram stranger, invalidating the solution.
This shows the principle that ownership ends at death.
5
Note the alternative opinion
Shams al-A'imma suggests placing the body in a basket (kuwwara) and washing it indirectly, allowing full washing without direct contact.
This is a secondary view; tayammum remains the primary relied-upon method.
6
Apply the age distinction
For a child (before puberty), there is no sexual desire, so direct washing by either men or women is permissible without restriction.
The ruling is about preventing fitna; children are excluded.

Ghusl and the Revocable Divorce Right (Raj'ah)
Ruling on ghusl and raj'ah with intra-Hanafi details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on how incomplete ghusl affects raj'ah and explain the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf between Muhammad al-Shaybani and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The right of raj'ah is cut off only when purification is certain; in doubt, it is preserved by istihsan or cut off by caution.

A revocably divorced woman’s ghusl can affect her husband’s right to take her back. But what if the ghusl is incomplete or uses doubtful water? The Hanafi school applies istihsan and caution to determine the ruling, leading to a nuanced dispute between its leading authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A revocably divorced woman performs ghusl but leaves some parts unwashed. Consider the amount left: most of the body, less than a limb, a complete limb, or doubtful purity.
Each scenario has a different ruling.
2
Apply the principle of istihsan
When most of the body is washed, by analogy the raj'ah should be cut off, but by istihsan it remains because the state of impurity persists.
Istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf on mouth and nose rinsing
Imam Muhammad holds that leaving madmadah and istinshaq cuts off raj'ah because their obligation is disputed, so caution dictates cutting off. Abu Yusuf has two narrations: one that it does not cut off (since mouth is a complete limb) and another agreeing with Muhammad.
This intra-madhhab difference turns on whether these acts are wajib.
4
Examine doubtful water (su'r al-himar)
When ghusl is performed with water of doubtful purity (donkey's leftover), caution is applied in both directions: raj'ah is cut off and she cannot remarry.
This case reinforces the preference for the precautionary view.
5
Assess the practical consequence
If raj'ah is cut off, the husband cannot take her back without a new marriage contract. If not cut off, he can revoke the divorce during the iddah.
The thamarah al-khilaf is significant for the couple's rights.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of a revocably divorced woman's ghusl on the husband's right of raj'ah. The general ruling is that if she performs a complete ghusl with pure water and her entire body is washed, the raj'ah is cut off because she becomes ritually pure and the marriage bond effectively ends.
