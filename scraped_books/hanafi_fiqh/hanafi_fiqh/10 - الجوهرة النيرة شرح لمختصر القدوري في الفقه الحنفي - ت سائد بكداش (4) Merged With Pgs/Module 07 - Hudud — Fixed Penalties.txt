Compromise Not Allowed in Hudud Claims and Diya Cases
States general prohibition of compromise in hudud and discusses diya cases after judge's decree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when compromise (sulh) is invalid in hudud claims and identify the conditions under which compromise after a judge's decree on diya is permissible.

Core Idea
Compromise in hudud is invalid because it is a right of Allah; but after judgment, compromise on diya can be valid as an exchange.

This lesson from the Book of Compromise (Kitab al-Sulh) addresses a key limitation: compromise is forbidden in hudud claims but permitted in diya cases after a judicial decree, with specific conditions. Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the rules of settlement in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish rights of Allah from rights of individuals
Identify that hudud penalties are fixed punishments for offenses against Allah, while diya is a financial compensation for the victim or heirs. Compromise in rights of Allah is generally invalid.
This distinction is the basis for the prohibition.
2
Understand the 'illa (effective cause) for prohibition in hudud
The text states hudud are rights of Allah, so exchanging them for worldly benefit is impermissible. Recognize this as a talil (rationale) grounded in the nature of the right.
Some other schools may allow compromise; note the ikhtilaf.
3
Note the effect of a judge's decree on diya
After a judge specifies the amount and type of diya (e.g., 100 camels), the right becomes determined. Compromise then becomes an exchange (mubadala) rather than a waiver of an undetermined right.
This is key: before judgment, compromise on diya may be different.
4
Apply conditions for permissible compromise after decree
Check the type of alternative compensation (e.g., cows instead of camels) and the amount (increase should not be excessive). Also ensure the exchange is not a debt-for-debt sale (e.g., deferred payment in measured/weighed items).
The text gives examples: more than 200 cows for 100 camels is valid if the killer has them; deferring payment in kilos/weights is invalid.
5
Recognize the prohibition of debt-for-debt exchange
If the compromise involves a deferred payment of measured or weighed items (other than gold/silver), it becomes a sale of debt for debt (dayn bi dayn), which is forbidden under Hanafi rules of sarf.
This is separate from the hudud/diya distinction but appears in the same passage.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Compromise (Kitab al-Sulh) addresses several rulings. Finally, if the compromise is for an amount equal to the value of the camels or slightly more (within the range of negligible difference), it is permissible because the increase is not certain; but if the increase is clearly excessive, it is invalid.

Sulh in Hudud: The Hanafi Distinction for Qadhf
Ruling on sulh in hudud for theft, slander, and adultery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in hudud and distinguish the effect of sulh before and after court for the hadd of qadhf.

Core Idea
Sulh cannot extinguish Allah's right: the hadd of qadhf drops only if settled before court, but never through compensation.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explores a nuanced point: can a private settlement (sulh) extinguish a fixed penalty (hadd) that is considered a right of Allah? The Hanafi school maintains that hudud are fundamentally rights of Allah, so they cannot be bargained away. However, for qadhf (slander), which involves a personal accusation, there is a subtle distinction: if the settlement occurs before the case reaches the judge, the hadd is dropped because the claimant abandons his claim; but no financial compensation is due. After litigation begins, even dropping the claim does not cancel the hadd. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three hudud and their common basis
The passage lists theft (sariqa), slander (qadhf), and adultery (zina). For theft and adultery, there is consensus (bila khilaf) that they are rights of Allah. For qadhf, the Hanafi view also considers it a right of Allah, though it involves a private claim.
2
Distinguish the agreed-upon (theft, adultery) from the disputed (qadhf)
The text states 'بلا خلاف' for theft and adultery, meaning all schools agree. For qadhf, it says 'عندنا' (in our view), indicating a Hanafi-specific position.
The Shafi'i school considers qadhf a right of the individual, which leads to a different ruling.
3
Analyze the two scenarios for qadhf: before court vs. after court
The text gives two cases: (1) sulh occurs before the case is raised to the judge (قبل أن يُرفع إلى القاضي) – the hadd is dropped (يسقط الحد) and no compensation is due (لا يجب بدل الصلح). (2) sulh occurs after litigation (بعد الترافع) – the hadd is not dropped (لا يسقط الحد) and no compensation is due.
4
Explain the rationale: public right vs. private claim abandonment
Before court, the claimant has the right to initiate the case. By abandoning the claim (أعرض عن الدعوى), the hadd drops because the public right is not yet engaged. After court, the public right overrides, so the judge must enforce the hadd regardless of settlement.
The text says 'المغلب فيه حق الشرع' – the prevailing aspect is the right of the Shari'ah.
5
Note the inter-madhhab difference
Shafi'is argue that qadhf is a right of the individual, so sulh is always valid: the hadd drops and compensation is due. The Hanafi view prioritizes the public dimension.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different categorizations of the right.

Gift to an Orphan: Valid Possession by Guardian
Ruling on gift-to-orphan possession and guardians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify who may validly take possession of a gift for a minor orphan according to the Hanafi school and state the conditions for such possession.

Core Idea
Only four specific guardians may take possession for an orphan; others need de facto care authority.

Giving a gift to an orphan is commendable, but who can legally accept it on the orphan's behalf? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi position: only certain guardians have the authority to take possession (qabd) for the minor. The ruling ensures the orphan's property is protected by reliable persons. We will identify the four authorized guardians and two exceptional cases where the mother or a stranger caregiver may act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
The passage begins with the general rule: if a gift is given to an orphan, and his guardian takes possession, it is valid. Note that possession (qabd) by the guardian is a condition for the gift to be binding.
The guardian must be someone with legal authority over the orphan's property.
2
List the four authorized guardians
The commentary from al-Nihayah specifies exactly four persons who may take possession: the executor (wasi) of the father, the executor of the grandfather, the judge (qadi), or the judge's appointee. No other relatives may do so unless they are actually supporting the child.
These four are the default authorized persons.
3
Analyze the exception for the mother
If the orphan is in the care (hijr) of his mother, she may take possession. This applies when the father is dead or absent with no communication. Her authority derives from her role in preserving the child and his property.
The mother's authority is specific to her custody, not general guardianship.
4
Analyze the exception for a stranger caregiver
If the orphan is being raised by a stranger (ajnabi), that stranger may also take possession. This is because he has a recognized hand over the child — no other stranger can take the child away. This applies only when none of the four guardians are present.
If the stranger is also the donor, the ruling is the same after making the gift distinct.
5
Summarize the conditions for validity
The passage shows that possession is valid if done by: (1) one of the four guardians, or (2) the mother if she has care, or (3) a stranger caregiver if he has de facto custody. In all cases, the person must have a recognized connection to the orphan's care and preservation.
The key principle is that authority stems from either explicit appointment or practical necessity.

Gift to a Discerning Minor: Validity of Direct Possession
Ruling on validity of gift to a minor boy who is discerning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a valid gift to a minor and apply it to scenarios where the minor takes possession himself.

Core Idea
A gift to a discerning minor is valid; his own possession suffices because it is a pure benefit.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses a key question in Hanafi gift law: when does a gift to a minor boy become valid? The answer hinges on the boy's mental capacity (ya'qil). We will explore the two scenarios: the donor handing over the gift directly, and the minor taking possession himself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? A gift (hiba) given to a minor boy. The ruling: valid if the minor is discerning, otherwise invalid.
The act is the transfer of possession (qabd) to or by the minor.
2
Isolate the condition (shart)
What condition determines validity? The minor must be 'ya'qil' – discerning, i.e., able to understand the nature and benefit of the gift.
The text repeats the condition for both scenarios: donor delivery and minor's own possession.
3
Analyze the rationale
Why does discernment matter? Because the minor is acting to secure a benefit (naf'). The law presumes he would not harm himself. If he lacks discernment, the benefit is not assured.
The commentary adds: it is a benefit for him – so his acceptance is valid without guardian.
4
Distinguish from other contracts
Gift is a purely beneficial contract. For contracts with obligations (like sale), a minor cannot act alone even if discerning. Here, the minor's possession is effective because no counter-obligation exists.
This distinction is crucial for understanding Hanafi minor capacity rules.

Possession of a Gift for a Minor Married Girl
Ruling on who may take possession of a gift for a minor girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state who may validly take possession of a gift for a minor married girl and identify the conditions that determine validity.

Core Idea
Who takes possession of a gift for a minor wife depends on whether she has moved to her husband's home.

When a minor girl receives a gift, the law must protect her property. But who has the authority to take possession for her? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on possession by the husband, father, the girl herself, and other caretakers, focusing on the key condition of her marital status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
List all potential possessors mentioned: husband, father, the minor girl herself, mother, and other caretakers.
2
Apply the condition for the husband
Check if the minor wife has been physically handed over to her husband (zuffat). If yes, he may take possession; if no, he cannot.
The husband's authority comes from being placed in the father's position for preservation.
3
Apply the rule for others
The father always retains authority. The mother and other caretakers can only take possession if the father is dead or absent without communication.
Their authority arises from necessity, not delegation.
4
Check the girl's own capacity
The minor girl herself may take possession if she is discerning (ta'qil) – able to understand the transaction.

Joint Gifts: One Donor to Two Donees – Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on validity of joint gifts and intra-Hanafi dispute on one donor to two donees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the validity of a gift from one donor to multiple donees and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A gift to two donees is valid according to the Sahibayn but invalid according to Abu Hanifa due to shuyūʿ.

In Hanafi fiqh, the permissibility of joint gifts (hiba) turns on the issue of shuyūʿ (undivided shares). While a gift from two donors to one donee is straightforward, a gift from one donor to two donees is disputed. This lesson examines the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and the practical implications of their difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is a gift (hiba) of a single property from one donor to two donees. The dispute is whether such a gift is valid or not.
The same gift from two donors to one donee is unanimously valid.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa holds the gift is invalid (lā taṣiḥḥ). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold it is valid (taṣiḥḥ).
This is an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab dispute.
3
Explain Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that a gift to two donees creates shuyūʿ (undivided joint ownership) without specification of shares, which he considers impermissible in gifts.
Shuyūʿ is disliked in gifts but allowed in inheritance and other transactions.
4
Explain the Sahibayn's reasoning
The Sahibayn argue that the donor can transfer ownership of the entire property to both donees jointly, as a single unit. Joint ownership is permissible in gifts.
They consider the gift as a transfer of ownership en masse, not as two separate transfers.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the donor must either specify each donee's share or give separate gifts. Under the Sahibayn, a single gift to multiple donees is acceptable.
Later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn's view for ease of transactions.

Rulings on liability when death occurs from pre-existing zina/theft or pregnancy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of causation (sabab) to determine the liability of a usurper when a female slave dies from pre-existing zina, theft, or pregnancy.

Core Idea
Liability follows the cause: if the cause of death began in the owner's hand, the usurper is not liable.

This lesson examines a set of scenarios from the Jawharah al-Nayirah that test the boundaries of causation (sabab) in liability for a usurped slave's death. The key is identifying whether the fatal event originated before or after the usurpation. Understanding these cases sharpens your ability to attribute legal responsibility based on the origin of the cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act leading to death
Determine whether the slave died from zina, theft, or pregnancy. This is the immediate cause of destruction (talaf).
The text specifically mentions these three scenarios.
2
Locate where the cause originated
Ask: Did the zina, theft, or pregnancy occur while the slave was in the owner's hand or in the usurper's hand? For pregnancy, also consider whether the husband belonged to the owner's household.
The 'hand' (yad) signifies possession or control.
3
Apply the attribution principle
If the cause (sabab) of the fatal condition originated in the owner's hand, then the destruction is attributed to the owner, and the usurper is not liable. If the cause originated in the usurper's hand, he is liable for the full value.
This is analogous to the owner killing the slave in the usurper's hand — no liability for the usurper.
4
Consider the exception for crimes committed in the usurper's hand
If the slave committed zina or theft while in the usurper's hand, and is later returned to the owner but then executed by the authorities for that crime, the usurper remains liable because the cause (the crime) was in his hand.
This shows that the attribution is to the origin of the cause, not the location of death.

Core Idea
The khuntha's ambiguous gender reduces capital punishment and diyah but not hudud for slander and theft.

This passage from al-Yanabi' presents a concise set of rulings on how the Hanafi school applies hudud, qisas, and diyah to a hermaphrodite (khuntha). 1
Identify the apostasy ruling
The khuntha is not killed for apostasy because capital punishment for riddah is only for males; the khuntha may be female.

Gender ambiguity leads to the safer, lighter penalty except where gender is irrelevant (hudud that apply to both sexes).

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a spouse who refuses li'an and identify the scribal error in the matn regarding the wife's punishment.

Core Idea
Imprisonment compels li'an; a copyist's error wrongly added a hadd for the wife.

Li'an is a solemn oath procedure to resolve accusations of adultery between spouses. But what happens if either spouse refuses to participate? The Hanafi school prescribes imprisonment to compel compliance, but a scribal error in some manuscripts created confusion about the wife's fate if she confirms the accusation. This lesson clarifies the correct ruling and the evidence for it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage deals with refusal to perform li'an by either the husband or wife after the accusation has been made. Determine the legal consequence for each.
The husband initiates; the wife then must respond.
2
Trace the ruling for the husband's refusal
If the husband refuses li'an, the judge imprisons him until he performs li'an or confesses lying (takdhīb al-nafs). Confession triggers hadd al-qadhf.
Imprisonment is coercive, not punitive.
3
Trace the ruling for the wife's refusal
If the husband performs li'an, the wife must also perform li'an. If she refuses, she is imprisoned until she performs li'an or confirms the accusation. The text originally added 'fatuhadd' (then she is punished) for confirmation, but this is a scribal error.
The correct ruling is no hadd for the wife upon confirmation.
4
Analyse the scribal error correction
The author al-Haddād notes that 'fatuhadd' is a mistake. The reasoning (talil) is that confirming the husband is less than confessing zina herself, and a single confession does not lead to hadd. Therefore, the wife should not be punished.
Manuscript evidence supports the correction.
5
Apply the ruling to an exceptional case
The passage also notes a woman who has a child of unknown father — her accuser is not given hadd due to ambiguity, so no li'an is required upon her accusation.
This shows the scope of 'mimman yuhadd qādhiṭuhā'.

Commentary

Li'an as a Substitute: Hadd Applies When Li'an is Precluded
Ruling with proof that li'an is substitute for hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that hadd applies when li'an is precluded due to the husband's status, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Zufar regarding partial hadd followed by conversion.

Core Idea
Li'an is a substitute for hadd; when li'an is impossible, the original hadd returns.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: li'an (mutual imprecation) serves as a substitute for the fixed punishment (hadd) of qadhf (false accusation of adultery). When the husband is legally unable to perform li'an due to his status as a slave, disbeliever, or one already punished for qadhf, the original hadd punishment applies. The passage also presents an important intra-madhhab disagreement about the effect of partial execution of the hadd on subsequent li'an eligibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
Read the opening ruling: husbands who are slaves, disbelievers, or have been hadd-punished for qadhf — when they accuse their wives without witnesses — are subject to hadd. Note that li'an is not an option for them.
These three categories block li'an entirely, so the hadd is the default.
2
Analyse the legal basis
The text provides the proof: li'an is a substitute (khalaf) for the hadd commanded in Qur'an 24:4. When the substitute cannot function, the original obligation applies. Understand this substitution logic.
This is a form of 'al-badal' reasoning: if the alternative procedure fails, the primary rule re-emerges.
3
Examine the disbeliever husband scenario
The passage expands on the case of a disbeliever husband: if the wife converts and he accuses her before Islam is presented to him, hadd is obligatory. Then explore what happens if he converts after partial hadd and accuses again.
This scenario triggers the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Follow the intra-Hanafi dispute
Two major Hanafi authorities differ: Abu Yusuf holds that the remainder of the hadd is completed first, then li'an can occur. Zufar holds that the first lash invalidates the testimony, so no li'an is possible ever. Identify the basis of each view.
The dispute hinges on whether the accuser's testimony is invalidated after the full hadd or from the first stroke.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice: if a husband receives 40 lashes, converts, and then accuses again, Abu Yusuf would finish the hadd (40 more lashes) and then allow li'an; Zufar would reject li'an entirely. Understand the real-world difference.
This ikhtilaf persists within the madhhab; both views are recorded without definitive tarjih in this passage.

When the Wife is Ineligible: No Hadd, No Li'an
Ruling when wife is not muhsana: no hadd, no li'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a husband is exempt from hadd and li'an when accusing his wife, and identify the wife's deficiencies that block the procedure.

Core Idea
If the wife is not muhsana, the husband's accusation incurs neither hadd nor li'an.

This lesson examines an exception in the law of li'an. When a husband accuses his wife of adultery but she lacks the legal status of muhsana (e.g., she is a slave, disbeliever, minor, or previously punished for qadhf), the normal consequences—hadd punishment or li'an procedure—are both dropped. The reason lies in her deficiency, not his. Understanding this ruling clarifies the intersection of personal status and criminal procedure in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the husband's eligibility
Confirm that the husband is from ahl al-shahada (free, Muslim, not previously punished for qadhf). This is a precondition for li'an.
If he is not, the ruling differs.
2
Examine the wife's status
Determine if the wife is muhsana. If she is a slave, disbeliever, previously punished for qadhf, minor, insane, or a known fornicator, she is not muhsana.
3
Apply the ruling: no hadd, no li'an
If the wife is not muhsana, the husband does not receive the hadd punishment for qadhf, and there is no li'an. The accusation is valid but the legal consequence is dropped due to her deficiency.
The text analogizes it to the wife affirming the accusation.
4
Note exceptions regarding the husband's past
If the husband was previously punished for zina or drinking wine, he can still perform li'an. Only a prior punishment for qadhf blocks his eligibility.
This is a key distinction.
5
Consider additional cases
The commentary extends the ruling to mudabbara, mukataba, umm walad, and mute wives.
These are cases where the wife is not fully eligible for testimony or muhsana status.

The Procedure of Li'an: Oaths and Curses
Description of li'an procedure: four oaths, fifth curse, pointing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the steps of the li'an procedure as per the Hanafi school, including the number and content of oaths and the pointing gesture.

Core Idea
Li'an involves four oaths of truth and a fifth curse by the husband, who points to his wife throughout.

This lesson details the precise method of li'an. Once the conditions are met (husband eligible, wife muhsana), the judge initiates the procedure with the husband. The husband swears four times by Allah that he is truthful in his accusation, then a fifth time invoking the curse of Allah upon himself if he is lying. He points to his wife throughout. Understanding the exact wording and order is essential for legal validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initiator
The judge begins with the husband, not the wife. This is a sunnah (recommended) practice.
2
Recite the four oaths
The husband says four times: 'I bear witness by Allah that I am among the truthful in what I accused her of adultery.' Each is considered a testimony (shahada).
The phrasing is fixed and must be in Arabic.
3
Recite the fifth curse
The husband then says once: 'The curse of Allah be upon him if he is among the liars in what he accused her of adultery.' This is a curse, not an oath.
This is the fifth utterance.
4
Perform the pointing gesture
During all oaths and the curse, the husband points to his wife. This is wajib (obligatory) in the Hanafi school.
Pointing indicates the target of the accusation.
5
Note the sequence and completeness
The procedure must be done in this order, with all five utterances and the pointing. Any deviation may invalidate the li'an. The wife's corresponding response (four oaths and a fifth invoking anger) follows after, but is not detailed here.
The wife's part is covered in other sources.

وإن كان الزوج من أهل الشهادة وهي أمَةٌ، أو كافرة، أو محدودة في قذف، أو كانت ممَّن لا يُحَدُّ قاذفها : فلا حد عليه في قذفها، ولا لعان. وصفة اللعان : أن يبتدىء القاضي بالزوج، فيَشْهَدَ أربع مرات، يقولُ في كل مرَّةٍ : أشهدُ بالله إني لمن الصادقين فيما رميتها به من الزنا . ثم يقولُ في الخامسة : إن لعنة الله عليه إن كان من الكاذبين فيما رماها به من الزنا، ويشير إليها في جميع ذلك . وقيد بقوله : أو محدوداً في قذف إذ لو كان محدوداً في زناً، أو شرب خمر: فإنه يلاعن.
* قوله: (وإن كان الزوج من أهل الشهادة، وهي أمة أو كافرة أو محدودةٌ في قذف، أو كانت ممن لا يُحد قاذفها)، بأن كانت صبيةً أو مجنونة أو زانية: (فلا حد عليه في قذفها، ولا لعان). لأن القذف قد صح من جهته، وإنما سقط موجبه لمعنى من جهتها؛ لأنها ليست من أهل الشهادة ولا محصنة، فصار كما لو صدقته. وكذا إذا كانت مدبرة أو مكاتبة أو أم ولد، أو خرساء.
قوله: (وصفة اللعان أن يبتدئ القاضي بالزوج، فيشهد أربع شهادات بالله، فيقول في كل مرة : أشهد بالله إني لمن الصادقين فيما رميتها به من الزنا. ثم يقول في الخامسة : إن لعنة الله عليه إن كان من الكاذبين فيما رماها به من الزنا، ويُشير إليها في جميع ذلك).
And if the husband is from those eligible for testimony, and she is a female slave, or a disbeliever, or one who has been punished for qadhf, or she is one whose accuser is not subject to hadd: then there is no hadd on him for accusing her, and no li'an. The description of li'an: The judge begins with the husband, and he bears witness four times, saying each time: 'I bear witness by Allah that I am among the truthful in what I accused her of adultery.' Then he says in the fifth: 'The curse of Allah be upon him if he is among the liars in what he accused her of adultery,' and he points to her in all of that. And he restricted by his statement: 'or one who has been punished for qadhf', for if he had been punished for adultery or drinking wine: then he does perform li'an.
* His statement: (And if the husband is from those eligible for testimony, and she is a female slave, or a disbeliever, or one who has been punished for qadhf, or she is one whose accuser is not subject to hadd), such as if she is a minor, or insane, or a fornicator: (then there is no hadd on him for accusing her, and no li'an). Because the accusation is valid from his side, but its consequence is only dropped due to a meaning from her side; because she is not from the people of testimony and not muhsana, so it is as if she affirmed him. And likewise if she is a mudabbara, or mukataba, or umm walad, or mute.
His statement: (The description of li'an: The judge begins with the husband, and he bears witness four times by Allah, saying each time: 'I bear witness by Allah that I am among the truthful in what I accused her of adultery.' Then he says in the fifth: 'The curse of Allah be upon him if he is among the liars in what he accused her of adultery,' and he points to her in all of that.)

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the conditions for li'an. It first states the ruling: if the husband is eligible to bear witness (i.e., he is a free, Muslim, not previously punished for qadhf) but the wife is a slave, a disbeliever, or had been punished for qadhf, or she is one whose accuser would not be punished (e.g., a minor, insane, or known fornicator), then the husband does not receive the hadd punishment for accusing her, and there is no li'an. The reason given is that the accusation is valid from the husband's side, but the legal consequence (hadd or li'an) falls away because of a deficiency on the wife's side: she is not eligible for testimony (ahl al-shahada) because for hadd and li'an purposes, the wife must be muhsana (free, Muslim, chaste). The commentary then lists additional cases such as mudabbara, mukataba, umm walad, or mute. Finally, it describes the procedure of li'an: the judge begins with the husband, who swears four times by Allah that he is truthful in his accusation of adultery, then a fifth time invoking the curse of Allah upon himself if he is lying. He points to her throughout. A restriction is noted: if the husband was previously punished for qadhf, he cannot perform li'an; but if he was punished for adultery or drinking wine, he can still perform li'an.

Key Terms
أهل الشهادة (ahl al-shahada): Those eligible to bear legal testimony: free, Muslim, adult, sane, not convicted of slander.
محصنة (muhsana): A chaste, free, Muslim woman who is not a fornicator.
لعان (li'an): Mutual imprecation procedure consisting of oaths and curses between spouses.
