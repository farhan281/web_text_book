Surety's Right of Recourse and Mutual Imprisonment
Explanatory commentary on surety's recourse and mutual imprisonment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a surety (kafīl) has the right of recourse against the debtor and apply the ruling of mutual imprisonment.

Core Idea
A surety who guarantees without order is a volunteer with no recourse; only a paying surety with order can claim.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains the Hanafi rulings on a surety's right to claim reimbursement from the debtor and the right of mutual imprisonment. The key distinction is whether the guarantee was made with or without the debtor's order, which determines whether the surety is a volunteer or an agent. Understanding these rules is crucial for anyone acting as a guarantor or seeking to enforce a guarantee.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of guarantee
First, distinguish between guarantee with the debtor's order (bi-amrihi) and without order (bighayri amrihi). This is the foundational distinction that determines all subsequent rulings.
The passage explicitly contrasts these two cases.
2
Determine the right of recourse
If the guarantee was with order, the surety has recourse after paying. If without order, he is a volunteer and has no recourse, even after payment.
The inheritance example illustrates the without-order case.
3
Understand the timing of recourse
The surety cannot demand payment from the debtor before he himself has paid the creditor. This is based on the analogy (qiyas) of a lender who declines to lend: no compensation before disbursement.
The passage says 'ليس للكفيل أن يطالب المكفول عنه بالمال قبل أن يؤديه عنه'.
4
Apply the condition for mutual imprisonment
The surety can press (mulāzamah) the debtor only if he himself is being pressed by the creditor. This right exists to free himself from liability, but only after he has been pressured.
This is conditional: 'فإن لوزم بالمال كان له أن يلازم المكفول عنه'.
5
Synthesize the complete ruling
Combine the above: guarantee with order → after paying, surety can claim reimbursement; if pressed, he can press debtor. Guarantee without order → no claim, no pressing (since he is a volunteer).
The inheritance scenario shows extinction of debt if surety inherits from creditor.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings of suretyship (kafalah), specifically addressing the surety's right of recourse and mutual imprisonment. The text first states the ruling: if a person guarantees a debt without the debtor's request (bighayri amrihi), he cannot claim reimbursement from the debtor. The reason is that he is a volunteer (mutabarri'), and any payment made is considered a donation. The commentary elaborates with an example: if the surety inherits the debt from the claimant (e.g., the claimant dies and the surety is his heir), the debt is extinguished because it comes into his ownership. In contrast, if the guarantee is with the debtor's order (bi-amrihi), the debtor remains liable, and the surety can claim reimbursement. The commentary also clarifies that the surety cannot demand payment from the debtor before paying the creditor himself, drawing an analogy to a lender: just as a person who declines to lend cannot later demand compensation, similarly the surety cannot claim until he actually pays. Finally, the passage addresses the situation where the surety is pressured by the creditor for payment: in such a case, the surety has the right to pressure the debtor (e.g., by demanding that the debtor free him from liability, or even seeking imprisonment of the debtor) until the debtor relieves him. This right of mutual imprisonment applies only when the guarantee was made with the debtor's order.

Discharge of Surety and Debtor by Creditor's Action
Explanatory commentary on discharge rules in suretyship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on discharge of surety and explain why discharge of the principal frees the surety but discharge of the surety does not free the principal.

Core Idea
Discharge of the principal debtor automatically frees the surety; discharging the surety alone leaves the debtor liable.

In suretyship (kafāla), the surety's liability is accessory to that of the principal debtor. This means the surety's obligation rises and falls with the principal's debt. The commentator explains how various creditor actions—discharge (ibrāʾ), payment, deferment (taʾkhīr)—affect both parties. Understanding these rules is crucial for creditors and lawyers to avoid unintended outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Rule
State the basic rule: if the creditor discharges or receives payment from the debtor, the surety is automatically discharged. If the creditor discharges only the surety, the debtor remains liable.
This applies whether the guarantee was given with or without the debtor's order.
2
Trace the Legal Reasoning
The surety only guaranteed what was in the debtor's liability. Once the principal debt is extinguished (by payment or discharge), there is nothing left for the guarantee to attach to. Hence the surety is freed.
This is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) based on the accessory nature of suretyship.
3
Examine the Condition of Acceptance
A discharge (ibrāʾ) offered to the debtor requires the debtor's acceptance to take effect. If the debtor rejects it, the discharge is revoked. If the debtor dies before acceptance, death is treated as acceptance.
This condition is specific to ibrāʾ; payment does not require acceptance.
4
Analyze the Internal Dispute on Debt Return
If the debtor rejects the discharge, does the debt return to the surety? Some Hanafi scholars say yes (the surety remains liable), others say no (the surety is permanently discharged). The text notes the difference but does not give a definitive tarjīḥ.
Later fatwa works often adopt the view that the debt does not return.
5
Apply the Rule to Deferment
Deferment (taʾkhīr) is a temporary discharge. Analogous to permanent discharge: deferring from the principal is a deferment from the surety, but deferring from the surety is not a deferment from the principal.
The commentator al-Khujandī summarizes: 'Discharge of principal necessitates discharge of surety; discharge of surety does not necessitate discharge of principal.'

Hawalah: Transfer of Debt Absolves the Original Debtor – Hanafi Position and Refutation
Defines hawalah, its effects, and refutes opposing views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hawalah, explain the difference between hawalah and kafalah, and apply the conditions for recourse (tawa).

Core Idea
Hawalah transfers the debt and absolves the original debtor; kafalah only adds a guarantor.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi doctrine of hawalah (transfer of debt). The central ruling is that once a hawalah is completed, the original debtor is fully absolved and the debt becomes binding on the transferee. We will explore the linguistic basis for this ruling, distinguish it from kafalah (suretyship), and understand when the creditor may revert to the original debtor due to loss (tawa). The passage also refutes the views of Zufar and Imam Malik, providing you with a clear understanding of the Hanafi position and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Define the three parties: muheel (original debtor), muhtal (creditor), and muhtal 'alayh (transferee). Note that the transferor's consent is not required.
2
Understand the absolution effect
When hawalah is completed with the transferee's acceptance, the original debtor is freed from the debt. The debt becomes the transferee's liability.
3
Distinguish hawalah from kafalah
Hawalah derives from 'tahwil' (transfer) and empties the first liability. Kafalah derives from 'dam' (joinder) and does not absolve the original debtor. Legal rulings follow linguistic meanings.
4
Apply the conditions for recourse (tawa)
According to Abu Hanifa, the creditor may revert to the original debtor only if the transferee denies the hawalah with an oath and no evidence, or dies insolvent. This is the definition of tawa.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hawalah (Transfer of Debt) presents the Hanafi ruling on the nature of hawalah and its effects, along with a refutation of opposing views from Zufar and Imam Malik. The passage begins by stating the central ruling: when a hawalah is completed with the acceptance of the transferee (muhtal alayh), the original debtor (muheel) is absolved of the debt, and the debt becomes binding on the transferee. The transferor's consent is not required for validity because the transferee is undertaking a debt in his own right, which benefits the transferor by relieving him without harm. This is supported by references to al-Hidayah and al-Nihayah, which affirm that the debtor's order or consent is not a condition; even if the debtor initiates the transfer and the creditor agrees, the hawalah is valid and the original debtor is free.

Zufar argued by analogy to kafalah (suretyship) that hawalah does not absolve the original debtor, and that the creditor can be compelled to accept if the transferor pays cash. The Hanafi response distinguishes the two contracts linguistically and legally: hawalah is derived from 'transfer' (tahwil), so the debt moves from one liability to another, leaving the first empty; kafalah is derived from 'joining' (dam), which does not empty the first liability. Hence, legal rulings follow linguistic meanings. Al-Khujandi clarifies: hawalah is absolving; kafalah is not, unless it is conditional upon absolution, in which case it becomes hawalah.

The passage then addresses recourse after tawa (loss). According to Abu Hanifa, the creditor (muhtal) may revert to the transferor only if his right suffers loss, defined as either the transferee's denial with oath and lack of evidence, or his death insolvent (with no property, debt, or surety). Zufar maintained equality of hawalah and kafalah (both non-absolving), while Malik considered both absolving, arguing that a single right cannot become two. The Hanafi rebuttal reiterates the linguistic basis: transfer empties the first liability; joinder does not. Al-Shafi'i's position is that the creditor never has recourse even with loss. The definition of tawa is further clarified by al-Tamurtashi, specifying that there must be no evidence for the creditor, transferor, or anyone else. Overall, the passage establishes the Hanafi doctrine that hawalah is a transfer that absolves the original debtor, with limited recourse in cases of proven loss.

المحيل (al-muheel): The original debtor who transfers the debt to another.
المحتال (al-muhtal): The creditor who accepts the transfer (also called al-muhal lahu).
المحال عليه (al-muhtal 'alayh): The person who accepts liability for the transferred debt.

Core Idea
Settling a lower quality debt with higher quality currency of a different quantity is riba al-fadl; only spot exchange of equal quantity is permissible.

In this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the commentator explains a ruling from al-Quduri on settlement (sulh) when the debt and the settlement currency differ in quality. The key is that any increase in quality is considered an excess (fadl) if the quantity is not the same. However, if the quantity is equal and the settlement is conducted spot, it is valid as a currency exchange (sarf). This rule prevents riba in debt settlements.

Since both are dirhams (same genus), any increase in quality without a corresponding increase in quantity constitutes an excess (fadl). Here, the creditor receives a decrease in quantity but an increase in quality, which is a net increase in value → riba.
Riba al-fadl prohibits excess in spot exchanges of the same genus.

Deferment in sarf is prohibited.

The settlement is invalid (batil) unless it falls under one of the valid scenarios (quantity reduction or equal quantity spot).

Conditional Discharge in Sulh: Ibra Muqayyad
Analyzing conditional discharge in sulh transaction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional discharge in sulh and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The order of statements determines the binding nature of a condition in ibra: discharge first = absolute; payment first = conditional.

In a sulh agreement, a creditor may discharge the debtor conditionally upon future payment. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute: if the discharge is stated first, it remains absolute; if payment is mentioned first, the discharge becomes conditional. This lesson analyzes that ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction
Recognize that this case involves a sulh (compromise) where the creditor agrees to discharge part of the debt in exchange for a payment of a different type or amount. For example, discharging 1000 black dirhams in exchange for 500 white dirhams.
The sulh is valid as long as it avoids riba; here the difference in quality is permissible.
2
Understand the two scenarios
There are two possible sequences: (a) the creditor first declares discharge ('I discharge you from 500') then states the condition ('on condition that you pay me 500 tomorrow'), or (b) the creditor first demands payment ('you give me 500 tomorrow') then declares discharge ('and I discharge you from 500').
The order is crucial for the ruling.
3
Compare the two views
Abu Hanifa (via the preferred view) holds that if discharge is stated first, it is absolute and not invalidated by a failed condition. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if payment is mentioned first, the discharge is conditional and lapses if the payment is not made.
The dispute centers on the interpretation of the word 'على' (upon) and the nature of the payment as a condition or a countervalue.
4
Apply the distinction
To determine the ruling, check the order of statements. If discharge precedes payment, the condition does not affect the validity of the discharge. If payment precedes discharge, the discharge is conditional and becomes void if the payment is not fulfilled.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school.

Riba Prohibition in Sulh with Different Quality Currency
Explaining the invalidity of sulh with inferior-to-superior currency exchange due to riba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh involving different quality currency and explain the qiyas reasoning that makes it riba.

Core Idea
Exchanging a debt for less quantity of a better quality currency is riba — quality matters.

Sulh (compromise settlement) is a recognized method to resolve debts, but it must avoid riba. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes a key rule: when a debt of lower quality dirhams is settled with a smaller number of higher quality dirhams, the transaction is invalid due to riba. The commentator uses analogy (qiyas) to justify this prohibition and also highlights two exceptions where sulh is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is a sulh settlement: a debtor owes 1000 black (low quality) dirhams and settles with 500 white (high quality) dirhams. The ruling is that this is not valid (la yajuz).
The ruling is from the matn itself; the commentator expands on the reasoning.
2
Trace the Qiyas (Analogy)
The commentator explains that white dirhams are not owed (the debt was black), and they are superior in quality. The exchange becomes: 1000 black for 500 black + an increase in quality. This is analogous to exchanging gold for gold with an excess (riba al-fadl), which is prohibited.
The effective cause (illa) is the combined reduction in quantity and increase in quality, creating excess value.
3
Identify the Exceptions
Two exceptions are given: (1) If the settlement is from white to black (lower quality) and reduced amount, it is valid because the creditor forgives both quantity and quality. (2) If the same quantity of a better quality is settled, it is valid but requires hand-to-hand delivery like a currency exchange (sarf).
The first exception is a remission; the second treats the exchange as sarf due to change in quality despite equal quantity.

Settlement by One Partner of a Joint Debt for a Specific Item
Ruling on partner's settlement of his share of joint debt for a specific item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one partner settles his share of a joint debt for a specific item and identify the options of the other partner.

Core Idea
When a partner receives a specific item as settlement, it is from both shares; the other partner may take his share or pursue the debtor.

In Hanafi fiqh, when two partners jointly own a debt and one settles his share for a specific item, the receipt is considered from both shares because the debt cannot be divided while in the dhimmah. The other partner therefore has a choice: either pursue the debtor for his half or participate proportionally in the received item. This ruling ensures fairness and prevents one partner from unilaterally appropriating the settlement.

Guided Path
1

There are two partners sharing a single debt claim. One partner agrees to accept a specific item (e.g., a garment) from the debtor as settlement for his share of the debt.
The debt is in the debtor's dhimmah, i.e., an intangible liability.
2
State the ruling
The other partner has an option (khiyar): he may either pursue the debtor for his half of the debt, or participate in the received item proportionally.
The text explicitly gives the partner the choice.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The received item is considered to come from both partners' shares because it is impermissible to divide a debt while it remains in the dhimmah (division requires specification). Hence the item represents a portion of the joint right.
This prevents unfair distribution.
4
Note the consequence of disposal
Before the other partner participates, the receiving partner owns the item and can dispose of it, but he remains liable to compensate his partner for the partner's share.
The receiver's disposal is valid but creates a guarantee obligation.
5
Apply to practice
The option is triggered immediately upon the receiver's acceptance of the item. The other partner must be informed and can choose within a reasonable time.
This is an immediate khiyar.

Explains partner's options in compromise of joint debt

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A joint debt remains undivided in the liability; any receipt is shared until the other partner exercises his option.

When two people share a debt and one partner settles his share by receiving a specific item, the other partner does not lose his right. This passage from Kitab al-Sulh explains the partner's options, the underlying principle of joint debts, and a notable intra-Hanafi dispute about deferring one partner's portion.

Identify the scenario
Understand that there is a joint debt owed to two partners, and one partner compromises his share and receives a specific item (e.g., a garment).

Rights of Partners in Joint Debt When One Collects
Expansion on joint debt rules in sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules governing a partner's rights when one collects a joint debt, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on deferring one's share.

Core Idea
A partner who collects part of a joint debt must share it proportionally; deferring one's share is invalid according to Abu Hanifa.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyirah expands on the rules of sulh concerning joint debt. When two partners are owed money from a single cause, and one collects from the debtor, the other partner has specific legal rights to share in what was collected or to claim compensation. The text also addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether one partner may unilaterally defer his share. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone dealing with joint claims or settlements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the joint debt scenario
Recognize that the debt is owed to two or more persons from a single cause, such as a joint sale price or inherited debt. This is the context for all subsequent rules.
The text defines joint debt as 'due to a single cause, like the price of a sold item if it was one transaction'.
2
State the partner's options when one collects
When one partner collects part of the debt (e.g., cloth), the other partner may either take half of the received item or demand that the collector guarantee him a quarter of the total debt.
The quarter debt guarantee is not a penalty but a proportional right.
3
Explain the default rule if the partner does not take the item
If the partner does not take half of the received item and wants to pursue the debtor, he may recourse to the collecting partner for half the value of what was received, because the received item was originally joint.
This applies when the partner chooses not to exercise his option to take the item.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on deferral
Abu Hanifa holds that one partner may not unilaterally defer his share of the debt; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow it by analogy to absolute discharge (ibra).
The Sahibayn argue that deferral is like ibra, while Abu Hanifa says it leads to division of debt before receipt (qismat al-dayn qabl al-qabd), which is invalid.
5
Apply to special cases
Two scenarios: (a) partial collection of half of one's share – the partner shares in what was collected and both pursue the debtor for the remainder; (b) one partner buys a commodity with his share (set-off) – the other partner may guarantee him a quarter of the debt.
In the purchase case, the buyer is deemed to have received his share in full via set-off, so the partner's right is to demand the quarter debt.

Ruling on partner's liability via muqassa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a partner uses his debt share to buy a commodity, explain the reasoning based on muqassa and the distinction between sale and ṣulḥ, and apply it to determine the other partner's claim.

Core Idea
A partner using his debt share via offset becomes a complete receiver, entitling his co-partner to monetary compensation, not the commodity.

Partnership in a debt can lead to complex transactions when one partner uses his share to purchase a commodity. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling: the buying partner may be held liable for a quarter of the total debt, and the reasoning hinges on the nature of sale (bargaining) versus compromise (concession).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Ruling
Two partners share a joint debt. One partner uses his half of the debt to buy a commodity for himself. The ruling: the other partner may hold him liable for a quarter of the total debt (i.e., half of the buying partner's share).
The amount is a quarter when the price equals half the debt.
2
Understand the Mechanism of Muqassa
The buyer pays for the commodity through set-off (muqassa), effectively receiving his entire debt share. This makes him a ‘complete receiver’ (qābiḍan kāmilan).
Muqassa extinguishes the debt pro tanto.
3
Distinguish Sale from Ṣulḥ
Sale (bayʿ) involves bargaining (mumākasa), so the buyer is not harmed by compensating his partner. In contrast, ṣulḥ (compromise) involves concession; forcing payment would harm the receiver, so he has a choice.
This distinction explains why the rule differs for ṣulḥ.
4
Determine the Partner's Options
The partner cannot claim the commodity (it belongs to the buyer). He can demand a quarter of the debt from the buyer or pursue the original debtor for his share.
The partner may also choose not to participate.

Compromise in Salam Between Partners: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compromise validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on compromise in salam between partners and explain the reasoning behind each view, including the use of qiyas and the concept of qisma al-dayn.

Core Idea
Compromise by one partner in salam is invalid per majority due to prohibition of dividing a debt in the liability.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a salam (forward sale) contract shared between two partners. When one partner attempts to settle (ṣālaḥa) his share by returning the advanced principal, the school is divided. Understanding why Abu Hanifa and Muhammad prohibit it while Abu Yusuf permits it requires analyzing the nature of joint debts and the limits of analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contract and the Issue
Recognize that the dispute concerns a salam contract between two partners. The 'salam' is a forward sale with advance payment. The issue: can one partner unilaterally settle his share by returning the principal, effectively exiting the contract?
Salam creates a single liability for the commodity; joint debts are not divisible at the liability stage.
2
State the Positions of the Imams
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the compromise is not permissible (invalid). Abu Yusuf says it is permissible (valid). Note that Muhammad's position is recorded in two versions: one agreeing with Abu Yusuf, another with Abu Hanifa.
The majority opinion (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is the relied-upon position.
3
Understand Abu Yusuf's Qiyas
Abu Yusuf analogizes to compromises in ordinary debts (where ṣulḥ is allowed) and to the case of two partners buying a slave where one partner rescinds his share (iqāla). He argues that since salam debt is like any other debt, compromise should be allowed.
This is qiyas (analogical reasoning).
4
Analyze the Counter-Argument of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They argue that allowing a compromise on one partner's share would constitute 'qisma al-dayn fī al-dhimma' (division of a debt while it is a liability), which is invalid. The debt is a single obligation; dividing it without the consent of the other partner is not permitted. Also, it would rescind the contract on the partner without his permission.
This highlights a key principle: joint debts cannot be partitioned until they are collected.
5
Note the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If a partner compromises, according to Abu Yusuf the compromise is valid and he takes back his share of the principal. According to the majority, the compromise is invalid, and the contract remains in force for both.
The mufta bihi is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Gift of Debt: Ruling and Revocability in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on gift of debt with rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the gift of debt is irrevocable in Hanafi law and distinguish the three scenarios of acceptance, rejection, and death of an absent donee.

Core Idea
Gift of debt is an isqat (dropping) not a transfer, so once accepted it is irrevocable.

In this passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the author explains the unique nature of gifting a debt. This transaction is not like gifting a physical object; it is a dropping of the claim. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct application in financial dealings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal nature
Recognize that the gift of a debt (hibat al-dayn) from creditor to debtor is treated as an isqat (dropping) and bara'ah (discharge) of the claim, not a transfer of ownership.
This is the foundational concept; all rulings flow from it.
2
Determine the hukm of revocation
Since the transaction is an isqat, there remains no physical object (ayn) to take back. Therefore, the donor cannot revoke the gift.
Compare with gift of a tangible object, which is revocable under certain conditions.
3
Examine the scenario of rejection
If the donee (debtor) explicitly rejects the gift by saying 'I do not accept,' the debt remains as it was. The gift is invalidated by rejection.
Rejection must occur before acceptance or without acceptance.
4
Examine the scenario of an absent donee who dies
If the donee is absent and dies before learning of the gift, the gift is valid and the debt is discharged. This is because a gift is completed by the offer (ijab) alone, and rejection is no longer possible after death.
This shows the gift is 'completed by one party' — the donor's offer is sufficient in the absence of rejection.
5
Note cross-madhhab implications
The Hanafi school treats hibat al-dayn as isqat (irrevocable). The Shafi'i school treats it as a transfer of ownership, allowing revocation under certain conditions.
This difference stems from the conceptualization of debt as a property right versus a personal obligation.

Possession (Qabd) in Gifts: Conditions and Proofs
Rulings on possession (qabd) in gifts: in the session, after separation, with permission, death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid possession (qabd) in a gift under Hanafi law and apply the rule of istihsan to determine validity in various scenarios.

Core Idea
A gift is incomplete without possession; possession in the session is valid by istihsan, but after separation requires explicit authorization.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift (hibah) is not completed by offer and acceptance alone; it requires actual possession (qabd). This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the key conditions: when qabd is valid in the session, when it is invalid after separation, and how authorization (taslit) can extend the time. The ruling is supported by istihsan (juristic preference) and analogy (qiyas) with sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage begins with the ruling: if the donee takes possession in the session without the donor's command, it is valid by istihsan. Compare this to the invalidity of possession after separation without permission.
Note that the default is that gift completion requires qabd.
2
Understand the proof: istihsan and qiyas
The author compares qabd in gift to qabul (acceptance) in sale. In sale, acceptance does not need the offeror's permission after the offer. By analogy, qabd in gift also does not need permission in the session. Istihsan overrides strict qiyas that might require permission.
Istihsan is used because strict qiyas could lead to hardship.
3
Distinguish between session and separation
Qabd after separation without permission is invalid because qabd is analogous to qabul, which is limited to the session. If the donor authorizes (taslit), it becomes like an agency (tawkil) and remains valid after separation.
Taslit is a delegation that persists beyond the session.
4
Apply the death rulings
If the donor dies before qabd, the gift is void because ownership ceases and authorization is lost. If the donee dies, he never became owner, so the gift is not inherited.
Both cases illustrate that property remains with the donor until qabd.
5
Examine the slave case and revocation
A gift to a slave: the slave accepts, not the master. The donor can revoke before qabd because the slave's ownership is unstable. This reinforces the principle that without qabd, the gift is revocable.
Revocation is valid before possession even if the donee is a slave.

Why Conditional Discharge of Debt is Invalid
Ruling on conditional discharge of debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional ibra', explain why it is invalid by distinguishing tamlik from iskat, and apply this rule to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Conditional discharge is invalid because ibra' is a property transfer, and only pure cancellations can be suspended.

When a creditor says to a debtor, 'If you pay me half, you are free from the rest,' many assume this is a valid agreement. But Hanafi fiqh declares it invalid. Why? Because discharging a debt is not merely a cancellation—it is a transfer of ownership. And a transfer cannot be made conditional on a future event. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Focus on the act: a creditor makes the discharge of a debt conditional on a future event (e.g., 'if tomorrow comes' or 'if you pay half'). The ruling is that this conditional discharge is invalid (batil).

Understand the legal nature of ibra'
The source states the reason: 'because ibra' is a transfer of ownership (tamlik).' In Hanafi law, ibra' is not a mere cancellation (iskat) of the debt; it is a transfer of the debt from the creditor to the debtor, making the debtor the owner of that sum. The condition does not need to be fulfilled; the mere act of conditioning invalidates the ibra'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A contested waqf exits the waqif's ownership but does not enter the beneficiary's—it is suspended, not transferred.

If the waqf includes slaves, the waqif cannot free them; the beneficiary cannot sell the property. These follow from the ownership status.
The waqif loses all rights of ownership; the beneficiary does not gain full ownership.
3

The text says 'on their difference'—Abu Hanifa considers it mustahaqq by judgment or ta'liq bi-mawt; the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it by formal waqf and delivery.

This is analogous to other gratuitous transfers during illness.

Commentary
This passage discusses the situation where the usurped item is later found after the usurper has already paid compensation. Two scenarios are distinguished. First, if the compensation was determined based on the owner's statement, or on evidence, or because the usurper refused to take an oath (nukul), then the owner has no right to reclaim the item even if its current market value exceeds the compensation. The commentator explains that this is because the usurper's ownership is established with the owner's consent (rida), as the owner himself claimed that amount. Second, if the compensation was based on the usurper's own statement with his oath, the owner does have a choice: either to confirm the transaction and leave the item with the usurper, or to take the item back and return the compensation. The reason is that the owner's consent was not fully given to that amount; he had claimed a higher value, so now he may rescind. The passage further notes a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school: if the reappearing item's value is equal to or less than the compensation, the relied-upon view (according to Zahir al-Riwayah, and considered al-asahh) is that the owner still has the option (because his consent was incomplete regarding his claim), but al-Karkhi held that he has no option in that case. Thus, an intra-Hanafi dispute is recorded.

Key Terms
خيار (khiyar): Option or choice given to the owner to either confirm the compensation or take back the item.
نكول (nukul): Refusal of the defendant to take an oath when required.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The apparent/well-known transmission in the six core books of Imam Muhammad.
الأصح (al-asahh): The most correct view (used in tarjih).

Usurper's Profit from Trade with Usurped Money: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi ruling on profit from usurped trade

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on profit from trading with usurped property, identify the dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and apply the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
All profit from trading with usurped property must be given in charity; keeping it is unlawful.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the profit earned by a usurper who trades with the usurped property. The passage from al-Hidayah presents the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad that all profit must be given in charity, opposed by Abu Yusuf. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi position on unlawful gains and the concept of tainted profit.

Guided Path
1
State the case
Identify the exact scenario: a person usurps 1000 dirhams, uses it to trade in slave-girls, making a profit. The total profit is 2000 (3000 revenue minus 1000 cost).
The original usurped amount is returned? The passage does not mention returning the principal; the focus is on the profit.
2
Identify the ruling
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the usurper must give all the profit in charity (sadaqah). This is wajib (obligatory) because the profit is unlawful gain.
The ruling is conditional on the profit being derived from the usurped capital.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Yusuf opposes this view, though his exact alternative is not stated in this passage. He may have permitted keeping the profit with compensation or other conditions.
This is an example of legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: all profit must be given in charity.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah presents this as the prevailing view.
5
Extend to the depositary
The same ruling applies to a depositary (mūda‘) who uses the deposit in trade without permission — any profit must also be given in charity.
The depositary becomes a usurper by using the deposit without permission.
