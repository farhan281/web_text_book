Divisibility of Emancipation in Zihar Kaffarah
Intra-Hanafi ruling on zihar kaffarah: freeing half then intercourse then freeing rest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on divisible emancipation (tajazzu al-iʿtāq) and apply it to the zihar kaffarah case, and distinguish the counterposition of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Freeing half a slave before intercourse does not fulfill kaffarah because emancipation is divisible per Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn hold the whole is freed.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: if a person frees half his slave for zihar kaffarah, then has intercourse, then frees the remaining half, is the kaffarah valid? The answer depends on whether emancipation is considered divisible (yatajazzā) or indivisible. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply Quranic conditions precisely within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Case and Quranic Condition
The scenario: a man pronounces zihar, then frees half his slave, then has intercourse, then later frees the remaining half. The Quran (58:3) requires freeing a slave before sexual contact. The key operative phrase is 'raqabah' (a slave), implying an entire slave.
The condition is temporal: freeing must precede intercourse.
2
State Abu Hanifa’s Position and Reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules the kaffarah invalid (lā yajūz). His basis: emancipation is divisible. Freeing half is not freeing a full slave. Since intercourse occurred after only half was freed, the condition is not met. The freeing of the remaining half after intercourse does not retroactively satisfy the condition.
Tajazzu al-iʿtāq: each part of the slave’s freedom is separate.
3
State the Sahibayn’s Position and Reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) rule the kaffarah valid (yajūz). They hold that emancipation is indivisible (lā yatajazzā) for the purpose of kaffarah. Freeing half is equivalent to freeing the whole slave; thus, the full condition is considered fulfilled before intercourse.
This is a legal fiction (takhrīj) based on the idea that ownership is indivisible in expiation.
4
Analyze the Proofs (Istidlal)
Both sides rely on the same Quranic verse. The dispute is over the interpretation of 'raqabah' and the nature of emancipation. Abu Hanifa emphasizes the literal divisibility of property; the Sahibayn emphasize the purpose of kaffarah (to free a human being, not part of one).
No additional proof other than the Quran and linguistic/legal reasoning.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If the kaffarah is invalid per Abu Hanifa, the person must free another full slave. If valid per the Sahibayn, no further action is needed. The mufta bihi in most later Hanafi works is Abu Hanifa’s view, requiring a full slave before intercourse.
This is a disagreement over a subsidiary matter (furūʿ), not a fundamental principle.

Fasting Two Consecutive Months as Expiation for Zihar: Excluded Days and Conditions
Ruling on the expiatory fast and excluded days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of zihar expiation fasting, identify the condition of consecutiveness, and list the days excluded from the two-month period.

Core Idea
For zihar expiation, fasting two consecutive months is mandatory, but it cannot include any day on which fasting is prohibited or obligatory.

This lesson covers the ruling on the expiatory fast for zihar. When a person cannot free a slave, they must fast two consecutive months. However, not every day counts: this passage explicitly excludes Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Nahr, and the days of Tashriq. Understanding these exclusions is crucial to correctly performing the kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is fasting two consecutive months as kaffarah for zihar. This is a fard obligation (fard 'ayn) when the alternative of freeing a slave is not available.
The Quranic command (Surah Al-Mujadila 58:3-4) establishes the ruling.
2
Note the primary condition: tatabu'
The two months must be consecutive. Any interruption without a valid excuse (such as menstruation or travel) breaks the consecutiveness and requires restarting the fast from the beginning.
Hanafi school is strict on this condition.
3
Identify the excluded periods
The text lists four specific periods: the month of Ramadan, the day of Eid al-Fitr, the day of Eid al-Nahr (10th Dhul-Hijjah), and the three days of Tashriq (11th-13th Dhul-Hijjah). These cannot be included in the two-month count.
These days cannot validly be fasted for expiation either because they are already obligatory (Ramadan) or because fasting is prohibited (Eid and Tashriq).
4
Understand the rationale
Consecutiveness is textually required (Quran: 'fast two consecutive months'), and fasting on prohibited days would not fulfill a wajib because those days are legally unsuitable for fasting.
If one starts the expiatory fast and a prohibited day occurs, that day is simply omitted—it does not break consecutiveness because it was never part of the valid fast.
5
Apply to a scenario
Plan the two months to avoid these days entirely. For example, start after Ramadan and ensure the period does not include Eid or Tashriq. If the period inadvertently includes an excluded day, skip that day and continue; consecutiveness is preserved.
Intentionally fasting on a prohibited day invalidates that day and may affect the overall validity.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of intercourse on the consecutiveness of the expiatory fast.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether intercourse during the zihar expiatory fast requires restart, and explain why Abu Yusuf disagrees.

Core Idea
Intercourse during the expiatory fast breaks consecutiveness and requires restart—the prohibition is intrinsic to the fast.

This section deals with a specific scenario in zihar expiation: if the husband has intercourse with the wife from whom he made zihar during the two-month fast, must he restart? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say yes; Abu Yusuf says no. The dispute turns on the underlying logic of the prohibition—whether it is intrinsic to the fast (like i'tikaf) or extrinsic (like the kaffarah for killing). Understanding this distinction deepens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists reason about conditions and consecutiveness.

Guided Path
1

They rule that any such intercourse (night intentional or day forgetful) breaks consecutiveness, so the entire two-month fast must be restarted. Their evidence: Quran commands fasting two consecutive months with no sexual contact, and analogy to i'tikaf.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position.
Abu Yusuf holds that the man continues his fast without restart. He argues that since such intercourse does not invalidate the fast itself, it should not break consecutiveness, analogizing to the kaffarah for killing where similar rules apply.
4
Analyze the proofs.
The majority rely on the Quranic text ('la masisa') and compare to i'tikaf (where intercourse nullifies consecutiveness). Abu Yusuf relies on analogy to kaffarah for killing. The key difference: in zihar expiation, the prohibition is intrinsic to the act of fasting; in kaffarah for killing, it is not.
5
Determine the mufta bihi.
The position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the Hanafi school, due to the stronger dalil from Quran and the more appropriate analogy.

Li'an: Definition, Conditions, and Nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of li'an, identify its triggering conditions, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding its legal nature and its procedural consequence.

Core Idea
Li'an is wajib when a husband accuses his chaste wife of adultery or denies paternity; its nature as testimony or oath determines whether proceedings restart if the judge changes.

The Book of Li'an opens with a concise definition and the conditions that make li'an obligatory. The passage then reveals an important intra-Hanafi dispute: is li'an a form of testimony (shahada) or an oath (yamin)? You will learn the core definition, the conditions, and the two views within the Hanafi school.

Conditions for Li'an and When It Is Dropped

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven conditions for li'an and identify which defects in the spouses' testimonial capacity cause li'an to be dropped, applying the rule that li'an is replaced by hadd or dropped entirely based on which spouse lacks capacity.

Core Idea
Li'an is wajib only when both spouses are legally competent witnesses; otherwise, it is replaced by hadd or dropped.

The procedure of li'an (mutual imprecation) is a unique remedy for a husband who accuses his wife of adultery without four witnesses. However, not every husband and wife are qualified. This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri details the exact conditions that must be met for li'an to be valid and the consequences when those conditions fail. Understanding these conditions helps you see how the Hanafi school maintains strict consistency with the principle that li'an substitutes for testimony (shahadah).

This sets up the entire framework.

Extract from the passage the seven conditions: both spouses must be free, adult, sane, Muslim, not previously punished for qadhf, married with a valid (sahih) marriage, and physically capable of oath (not mute).

Exceptional Cases Where Li'an Is Not Possible and Hadd Is Applied
Rulings on when li'an is invalid and hadd applies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the cases where a husband cannot perform li'an and identify whether he is liable for the hadd of qadhf.

Core Idea
When the husband is disqualified from li'an, the original hadd for qadhf becomes wajib.

Li'an is a special procedure that allows a husband to accuse his wife of zina without four witnesses, but it is a substitute for the hadd punishment for false accusation (qadhf). For li'an to be valid, the husband must meet certain conditions. If he does not, the substitute falls away and the original hadd applies. This lesson explores those exceptional cases based on the matn and its commentary.

Guided Path
1
Understand li'an as a substitute
Recognize that li'an is a substitute (khalaf) for the hadd of qadhf. When it is not possible, the original ruling applies.
The Quranic basis is Surah al-Nur 24:4.
2
Identify conditions for li'an validity
The husband must be free, Muslim, and not previously punished for qadhf. The wife must be of good character (ahl al-shahadah).
These conditions are derived from the exceptions listed.
3
Match each exceptional case to a missing condition
A slave lacks freedom, a disbeliever lacks Islam, and a previously punished person (mahdud) lacks credibility (his testimony is rejected).
The text explicitly mentions these three.
4
Apply the ruling: Hadd is wajib
When the husband is disqualified, he is liable for the hadd of qadhf (80 lashes) instead of li'an.
This is the operative ruling (mufta bihi).

Abu Yusuf vs Zufar: Li'an After Partial Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on effect of partial hadd on subsequent li'an

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi positions of Abu Yusuf and Zufar regarding li'an after partial hadd and identify the underlying principle that causes their difference.

Core Idea
Li'an after partial hadd is disputed: Abu Yusuf requires completing the hadd first; Zufar forbids li'an entirely because testimony is invalid from the first lash.

This lesson dives into a specific intra-Hanafi disagreement: can a husband who was partially punished for qadhf (false accusation) and then converted to Islam perform li'an with his wife after repeating the accusation? The core issue is when the testimony of a qadhif becomes invalid. Abu Yusuf and Zufar offer conflicting answers based on different interpretations of the same principle.

Guided Path
1
1. State the scenario
Identify the sequence: a non-Muslim husband commits qadhf, receives partial hadd (some lashes), converts to Islam, then accuses his wife again. The question is whether li'an is possible.
2
2. Identify Abu Yusuf's position and his rationale
Abu Yusuf holds that the remaining hadd must be completed, then li'an can occur. His rationale: testimony of a qadhif is invalidated only after the full hadd.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
3. Identify Zufar's position and his rationale
Zufar holds that li'an is not possible at all. His rationale: testimony is invalidated from the first lash, so the husband's testimony is already invalid.
Zufar's view is marjuh (weaker) but methodologically interesting.
4
4. Recognize the underlying principle
The disagreement stems from the timing of invalidation of the qadhif's testimony: after completion of hadd (Abu Yusuf) vs. after the first lash (Zufar).
This is a matter of interpretation of the same legal effect.
5
5. Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: According to Abu Yusuf, the couple can separate via li'an after completing hadd; according to Zufar, they remain married and the husband only faces hadd.
Understanding the consequence clarifies the importance of the dispute.

Li'an and Hadd: Timing of Accusation and Divorce
Rulings on when hadd or li'an applies after different divorce types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which li'an is applicable and when hadd is imposed for qadhf based on the timing of divorce.

Core Idea
Li'an requires an existing marriage; after irrevocable divorce, the wife is a stranger and accusation triggers hadd.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira presents a set of nuanced rulings linking divorce and the accusation of zina. The core principle is that li'an (mutual cursing) is only possible while the marital bond exists. Once the marriage is irrevocably terminated, the husband's accusation is treated as qadhf against a stranger, making him liable for hadd. Understanding the sequence and type of divorce is essential for a judge or jurist.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage deals with rulings on hadd (fixed punishment) and li'an (mutual cursing) in various scenarios. We need to determine which applies based on timing.
Hadd here refers to the 80 lashes for false accusation of zina.
2
Distinguish revocable vs irrevocable divorce
Revocable (raj'i) divorce maintains the marital bond; the wife remains in the husband's house and can be taken back without a new marriage. Irrevocable (ba'in) divorce ends the marriage immediately, making the wife a stranger.
In Hanafi, triple talaq is also irrevocable.
3
Apply the principle: li'an requires marriage
Li'an is only valid when the marriage exists at the time of accusation. If the marriage has ended irrevocably, li'an is impossible, and the husband's accusation is treated as qadhf against a non-mahram, incurring hadd.
If the accusation precedes revocable divorce, the marriage is considered ongoing, so li'an applies.
4
Analyze word order in triple talaq
The phrase 'O adulteress, you are thrice divorced' means accusation before divorce: no li'an (because divorce ends marriage) and no hadd (because accusation was during marriage). Conversely, 'You are thrice divorced, O adulteress' means accusation after divorce: hadd applies.
The sequence determines the ruling.
5
Derive the ruling for each scenario
From the passage, we can list the outcomes: (1) accusation then irrevocable divorce: no hadd, no li'an; (2) accusation then revocable divorce: li'an; (3) irrevocable divorce then accusation: hadd; (4) accusation during marriage with immediate triple talaq: no hadd, no li'an; (5) triple talaq then accusation: hadd.
The principle of each marriage being independent also applies when remarriage occurs.

Ruling on Husband Retracting Accusation in Li'an
Intra-Hanafi dispute on retraction and associated rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a husband retracting his li'an accusation and distinguish between the views of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf regarding the permissibility of remarriage.

Core Idea
Retracting a li'an accusation triggers hadd of qadhf; remarriage is permitted per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but permanently forbidden per Abu Yusuf.

This passage from the Book of Li'an presents the ruling when a husband retracts his accusation after completing li'an. The main issue is whether the retraction and punishment nullify the eternal separation caused by li'an or if the separation stands. The passage also addresses collateral consequences for the husband if he accuses another woman, and for the wife if she commits zina after li'an.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The husband retracts by saying 'I was lying' – the judge imposes hadd of qadhf and remarriage is permissible.
2
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that remarriage is permissible after hadd; Abu Yusuf holds eternal prohibition.
3
Analyze the opposing proofs
Abu Yusuf argues li'an creates an irrevocable separation; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad counter that punishment nullifies the legal effect of the false accusation.
4
Apply to related cases
If the husband accuses another woman, he is flogged; if the wife commits zina, she is flogged (with special conditions regarding her status).
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, if a Hanafi judge follows Abu Yusuf, the couple cannot remarry; if he follows Abu Hanifa, they can after hadd.

Procedure and Effects of Li'an for Denial of Paternity
Describes the procedure and legal effects of li'an for denying paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the procedure of li'an for denying paternity and identify which lineage rulings are severed and which remain.

Core Idea
Li'an for denial of paternity cuts inheritance and maintenance but keeps all other lineage rulings intact.

When a husband denies paternity of his wife's child, Islamic law prescribes a specific oath-swearing procedure called li'an. This passage walks you through the exact form and the judge's declaration, then clarifies which paternal rights and obligations vanish and which stay — a nuance many overlook.

Guided Path
1
Read the li'an procedure
The judge orders the husband to swear four times that he is truthful in denying the child. The wife swears similarly. Note the wording: 'Ashhadu billahi inni lamina al-sadiqin fima ramaytuka bihi min nafy al-walad.'
If the husband also accuses the wife of zina, he mentions both matters in his oaths.
2
Understand the judge's pronouncement
After the oaths, the judge severs the child's paternal lineage and links him to the mother only, saying: 'I have attached the child to his mother and removed him from the father's lineage.'
This phrase (alzamtu al-walada ummah) is the formal judicial declaration.
3
Identify what is severed (inheritance and maintenance)
The text explicitly states that only inheritance (mirath) and maintenance (nafaqa) between father and child are cut. All other rulings of lineage remain.
This is the Hanafi view; other schools may hold that all lineage effects are severed.
4
List what remains despite the severance
The passage enumerates four rulings that continue: (1) testimony of one for the other is not accepted, (2) payment of zakat from one to the other is invalid, (3) if the child is a daughter, the father cannot marry her off, (4) the child cannot marry the husband's daughter (from another wife).
These remaining rulings indicate the child is still considered a mahram to the father's family.
5
Note the prohibition of later claims
No one else – not even someone who believes the husband was lying – can later claim the denied child. The li'an establishes the denial authoritatively.
This prevents further disputes and upholds the finality of the li'an.

Li'an with a Minor or Insane Wife: Conditions for Validity
Ruling on li'an when wife is minor, insane, or in different status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on li'an when the wife is a minor or insane, and distinguish cases where li'an is required from those where it is invalid.

Core Idea
Li'an is invalid if the wife lacks legal capacity at the time of the alleged act; it is required only if zina is conceivable from her.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a critical condition for li'an: the wife must be capable of committing zina at the time of the accusation. The commentator explains why li'an is not performed if the wife is a minor or insane, but is required if she was a slave or non-Muslim at the time. Understanding this distinction prevents misapplication of the li'an procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act in question is a husband accusing his wife of zina. The passage examines how the wife's status at the time of the alleged zina affects the validity of li'an.
Li'an is a mutual oath procedure that leads to separation when a husband accuses his wife of zina without witnesses.
2
Analyze the two scenarios
First scenario: wife is a minor (ṣaghīrah) or insane (majnūnah) at the time of the alleged zina. Second scenario: wife was an amah (slave) or kāfirah (non-Muslim) at the time of the alleged zina.
The passage states that in the first scenario, no li'an is possible; in the second, li'an is required.
3
Determine the ruling for each scenario
For minor/insane: li'an is invalid (bāṭil). For amah/kāfirah: li'an is required (wājib) if the husband persists.
The hukm is derived from the possibility or impossibility of zina from the wife given her legal capacity.
4
Understand the reasoning (illah)
The illah is that zina is legally inconceivable from a minor or insane person because their actions are not valid (ghayr ṣaḥīḥah). For a slave or non-Muslim, zina is legally possible.
This is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) based on legal capacity (ahliyyah).
5
Note the practical consequence
If the husband accuses while the wife was minor/insane, the accusation itself is treated as false qadhf, potentially subjecting him to hadd. If she was slave/kāfirah, li'an proceeds as usual.
The husband's accusation must be evaluated based on the wife's status at the time of the alleged act, not at the time of the accusation.

A husband says to his wife, 'This pregnancy is not from me.' Does this statement trigger li'an (mutual imprecation) or not? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani differentiate based on the time of birth: if she gives birth in less than six months, then certainty of pregnancy at the time of statement is established, so li'an applies.
If she gives birth at six months or more, they agree with Abu Hanifa.

The Sahibayn argue that if the birth occurs within the minimum pregnancy period (six months lunar), we are certain the pregnancy existed at the time of the husband's statement, so he is a qadhif and li'an is wajib.
