Core Idea

This passage addresses a dispute within the Hanafi school about a married couple who both leave Islam and then both return. The mainstream Hanafi view, represented by Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, holds that the marriage remains intact. Zufar, another prominent Hanafi jurist, argues that since the apostasy of even one spouse dissolves the marriage, the apostasy of both must do so as well. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to how the school treats joint actions versus individual ones.

3
Understand Zufar's reasoning
He logically extends the individual rule: if apostasy of one spouse dissolves marriage, then apostasy of both includes that of each, so it should also dissolve.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response (implied)
The majority treats joint apostasy and return as a single event that does not sever the marital bond, contrasting with individual apostasy where there is no immediate return to signal continuity.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under the majority view, the marriage continues without renewal; under Zufar, a new contract is needed. The mufta bihi is the majority view.

This lesson addresses a scenario where one spouse in a non-Muslim marriage living in non-Muslim territory (dar al-harb) accepts Islam. According to Imam Abu Hanifa and his school, the marriage is not annulled immediately. Conversion can occur at any moment before the period expires.

The dissolution is effective at the end of the period, not retroactively.

The reason is not based on religious incompatibility alone, but on the unique legal status of the apostate: a person condemned to death with a temporary reprieve only for the purpose of reflection and repentance. Book of Suckling. And what becomes prohibited by suckling is what becomes prohibited by lineage, except the mother of his brother from suckling, for it is permissible for him to marry her, and it is not permissible for him to marry the mother of his brother from lineage. Our companions differed concerning one who weaned during the period of suckling and became independent of suckling within the period according to the statement of each one of them: Muhammad narrated from Abu Hanifa that whatever suckling occurs within thirty months, before weaning or after it, is suckling that prohibits, and the fatwa is upon it. Al-Hasan narrated from Abu Hanifa that if he weaned him within the two years until he became independent by food, then he suckled after that within the two years or thirty months, it is not suckling because there is no suckling after weaning. And if she weaned him and he ate weak eating not sufficient to make him independent of suckling, then he returned and suckled, it is suckling that prohibits. And Muhammad did not consider weaning before two years. His statement: (And what becomes prohibited by suckling is what becomes prohibited by lineage, except the mother of his brother from suckling: it is permissible for him to marry her, and it is not permissible for him to marry the mother of his brother from lineage.) Because she is his mother or the mistress of his father, unlike suckling. And it is not permissible for him to marry the woman of his father from suckling. And if he married a woman and divorced her before entering her, it is not permissible for him to marry her mother from suckling, because the contract on the woman prohibits her mother from lineage, and likewise from suckling. And the sister of his son from suckling it is permissible for him to marry, and it is not permissible for him to marry the sister of his son from lineage. And the woman of his father from suckling and the woman of his son from suckling it is not permissible for him to marry them, just as that is not permissible from lineage. And the milk of the male carries prohibition, and that is: a woman suckles a girl, so this girl becomes prohibited to her husband and to his fathers and his sons, and the husband from whom the milk came becomes a father for the wet nurse. And it is not permissible for him to marry the daughter of his wife from suckling if he has entered her, because the prohibition of the stepdaughter from lineage is tied to intercourse with the mother, and likewise the stepdaughter from suckling.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Rada' (Book of Suckling) begins by stating the foundational Hanafi principle: the prohibitions of marriage arising from suckling (rida') are identical to those arising from lineage (nasab), with one explicit exception—the mother of one's brother by suckling may be married. The commentary explains that this is because in lineage, she is either one's own mother or a woman with whom one's father has had intercourse (hence permanently prohibited), but this reasoning does not apply in suckling. The passage then addresses the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the effect of weaning (fisam) on the prohibition. Muhammad ibn al-Hasan narrates from Abu Hanifa that any suckling within thirty months—whether before or after weaning—is prohibitive, and this is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad narrates a contrary view: if the child is fully weaned within two years and becomes independent of milk, subsequent suckling does not create prohibition; however, if the child is not fully weaned (eats weakly), subsequent suckling remains prohibitive. Muhammad himself disregards weaning before two years entirely. The commentator (likely Ibn al-Humam or the author of al-Jawharah) then elaborates on various applications: (1) The stepdaughter (rabibah) by suckling is prohibited if the husband has consummated the marriage with her mother, by analogy to lineage. (2) The sister of one's son by suckling is permissible to marry, unlike in lineage, because the prohibition in lineage arises from intercourse with the mother. (3) The wife of one's father or son by suckling is prohibited as in lineage. (4) The concept of 'milk of the male' (laban al-fahl): when a woman suckles a girl, the girl becomes prohibited to the woman's husband and his ascendants and descendants, and the husband becomes a father to the wet nurse. The entire discussion is consistent with the Hanafi school's reliance on the principle of analogy (qiyas) from lineage to suckling, with the acknowledgment that the underlying cause ('illa) of prohibition in lineage (intercourse or blood relation) is absent in some specific cases for suckling.

Key Terms
رضاع (riḍāʿ): Suckling (breastfeeding) that creates marital prohibitions. The text defines its effect as analogous to lineage.
فطام (fiṭām): Weaning; the act of separating the child from breastfeeding. The text discusses whether fisaam ends the prohibitive effect of subsequent suckling.
لبن الفحل (laban al-faḥl): Milk of the male; the legal concept that the milk comes from the husband's semen, so the husband (the 'fahl') is considered a parent to the child suckled by his wife.
