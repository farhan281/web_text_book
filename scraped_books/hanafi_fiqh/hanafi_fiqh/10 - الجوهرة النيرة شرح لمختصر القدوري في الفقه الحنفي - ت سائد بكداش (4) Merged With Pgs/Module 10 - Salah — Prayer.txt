Substituting Gold for Silver in Estate Settlement: The Sarf Requirement
Ruling on exchanging estate metals with requirement of immediate possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on substituting metals in estate settlement and explain why hand-to-hand possession is required.

Core Idea
A settlement substituting gold for silver is valid only if hand-to-hand possession occurs immediately, as it is treated as a currency exchange (sarf).

When an estate consists of one metal (silver or gold) and the heirs agree to pay the beneficiary in the other metal, the settlement is valid but must comply with the rules of currency exchange (sarf). This lesson examines the required condition of immediate hand-to-hand possession and the consequence of separation before possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? What is the legal ruling (sahih/fasid/batil)?
The act is a settlement (sulh) where the estate's metal differs from the settlement payment.
2
Locate the underlying principle
The ruling is based on qiyas (analogy) to the rules of sarf (exchange of gold/silver). Understand why this analogy is drawn.
The text explicitly says 'فهو معتبر بالصرف' (it is considered like sarf).
3
Identify the condition for validity
What specific condition is required? Hand-to-hand possession (taqabud) in the same session.
Taqubud fi al-majlis is a core requirement in sarf contracts.
4
Determine the consequence of non-compliance
What happens if the condition is not met? The contract becomes void (batal) if the parties separate before possession.
The text says 'وإن افترقا قبل القبض: بطل'.
5
Examine the historical precedent
The footnote relates a case of Uthman (rad) settling with Tamadir for a different amount, illustrating the practical application.
This shows the ruling is derived from practice and analogy, not just theoretical.

Shopkeeper's Liability for Deposits When Leaving for Prayer
Ruling on liability of a shopkeeper for deposits lost while he attends prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that removes liability for a shopkeeper leaving deposits unattended, and apply the Hanafi principle that negligence (taḍyīʿ) is the basis for liability.

Core Idea
A shopkeeper is not liable for lost deposits if custom ensures protection, because liability requires negligence.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Waqi'at about a shopkeeper who goes to prayer, leaving deposits inside his shop. The deposits are lost, but the Hanafi school holds him not liable. The reason is that he is not considered negligent, as the custom of the marketplace is that neighboring shopkeepers guard each other's shops. This illustrates the broader principle that in amanah contracts, liability arises only from negligence (taḍyīʿ).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and scenario
The act is the shopkeeper leaving his shop to attend prayer. The deposits are lost while he is away.
The ruling is about liability, not the validity of prayer.
2
Determine the default ruling on liability
Normally, a depositary (mustawdaʿ) is liable for loss if they are negligent (muḍayyiʿ). Here, the key question is whether the shopkeeper was negligent.
Negligence means failing to take customary precautions.
3
Apply the custom of the marketplace
The text states he is not negligent because his neighbors guard the shop. This is a customary practice that counts as sufficient care.
ʻUrf (custom) is considered a valid indicator of reasonable care in Hanafi fiqh.
4
State the ruling and principle
Ruling: no liability. Principle: liability requires taḍyīʿ (negligence); here no taḍyīʿ due to neighbor's watch.
The shopkeeper's act of going to prayer itself is not negligence if custom provides protection.

Ruling for the Ambiguous Hermaphrodite (Khuntha Mushkil) in Congregational Prayer
Ruling and reasoning for khuntha mushkil in salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for the positioning of the khuntha mushkil in congregational prayer and explain the precautionary reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil stands between rows to avoid invalidating the prayers of others.

The ambiguous hermaphrodite (khuntha mushkil) is a person whose gender cannot be determined by any signs. In congregational prayer, where rows are segregated by gender, this poses a problem. The Hanafi school resolves it with a precautionary rule: the khuntha mushkil stands between the male and female rows to guarantee that neither the men's nor the women's prayer is invalidated.

Guided Path
1
Define the khuntha mushkil
Identify the criteria: a person who shows no clear signs of being male or female upon puberty. The passage explains why the masculine pronoun is used (to avoid specification or because masculine is default).
2
State the ruling
The khuntha mushkil stands between the row of men and the row of women when praying behind the imam. This is the mufta bihi position.
The ruling is wajib to avoid corruption of prayer.
3
Explain the precautionary principle (ahwat)
The underlying principle is to take the safest course in religious matters. If the khuntha stands in the men's row and is actually female, the men's prayer is invalid. If in the women's row and is male, his own prayer is invalid. Standing between avoids both risks.
4
Apply the principle to other situations
Consider how the precautionary approach (ahwat) is used in other cases of doubt in Hanafi fiqh, such as performing wudu when uncertain about its validity.
Optional extension.

Disliked Acts for the Khuntha Mushkil
Lists disliked acts for khuntha mushkil: silk, jewelry, exposure, seclusion, travel

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four disliked acts for the khuntha mushkil and explain the rationale of caution (ihtiyat) that governs these rulings.

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil must avoid silk, jewelry, exposure, seclusion, and travel without mahram — caution due to ambiguous gender.

The khuntha mushkil (hermaphrodite of unresolved gender) occupies a unique position in Hanafi fiqh. Since their legal gender is unknown, actions that are clearly permitted for one gender but prohibited for the other become ambiguous. This passage lists four actions that are disliked (makruh) for the khuntha mushkil as a precautionary measure. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the principle of ihtiyat (legal caution) when definitive gender classification is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
The subject is the khuntha mushkil — a person whose gender cannot be determined. This ambiguity triggers precautionary rulings.
Khuntha mushkil is distinct from khuntha ghayr mushkil (where gender can be determined).
2
List the disliked acts
From the text: wearing silk, wearing jewelry, exposing oneself before men and women, being in seclusion (khalwa) with a non-mahram, and traveling without a mahram.
These are makruh, not haram, because the prohibition is based on doubt, not certainty.
3
Apply the rationale of caution
Since the true gender is unknown, any act that might be forbidden for a man or a woman is disliked for the khuntha. This avoids potential sin.
The principle is ihtiyat — erring on the side of caution.

Rulings on khuntha's prayer in rows and covering face

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A khuntha's prayer in the men's row is valid; others repeat out of caution — uncertainty triggers precaution, not nullification.

In this passage, the author of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the rulings for a hermaphrodite (khuntha) in congregational prayer. Because the khuntha's gender is unknown, special precautions apply to ensure the prayer's validity for all worshippers. The ruling distinguishes between the khuntha's own prayer and that of those standing near him.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the khuntha's gender ambiguity
The khuntha is a person whose gender is unknown. The ruling depends on which row he stands in.
2

If in women's row, his prayer is invalid (batil) because he might be a man. If in men's row, his prayer is valid (sahih).
3
Understand the precaution for adjacent worshippers
When khuntha prays in men's row, those on his right, left, and directly behind must repeat their prayers as a precaution because he might be a woman.

Analyze the recommendation to wear qina' and sit like a woman
Wearing a face-cover (qina') and sitting like a woman is recommended (mustahabb) to remove doubt. If omitted, the khuntha is commanded to repeat, but this is istihbab, not wajib — his prayer remains valid.
The source from al-Hidayah clarifies it is on the level of recommendation.
5
Distinguish between the khuntha's own ruling and the rulings of others
The khuntha's prayer is valid in men's row; the repetition requirement is only for those around him.

Find the two specific cases where the text states tahrim attaches: (1) milk mixed with medicine where milk is predominant, (2) milk milked from a deceased woman and fed to an infant.
2
Examine the proofs for each
For the mixed case, the commentator says milk remains intended; medicine aids delivery. For the deceased case, he gives three proofs: milk after death is same as before, death does not affect milk, and the wet nurse's action is not required (as with a sleeping woman).
The proof uses istihsan (juristic preference) by analogy.
3
Analyze the distinction with intercourse
The commentator contrasts this with intercourse with a dead woman, where no prohibition of affinity attaches by consensus. The difference: milk's purpose is nourishment (death does not prevent it), while intercourse's purpose is pleasure (absent after death).
This distinction clarifies the rationale.
4
Apply the ruling to a practical consequence
Consider the benefit (fa'idah): if a small girl suckles from a dead woman who has a husband, the dead woman becomes the husband's foster mother, making him a mahram. He can then perform tayammum and burial.
This shows the legal effect on mahram relations.

And if it [milk] is mixed with medicine, and the milk is predominant: the prohibition attaches to it. And if milk is milked from a woman after her death, and the infant is made to swallow it: the prohibition attaches to it. His statement: (And if mixed with medicine, and the milk is predominant: the prohibition attaches to it) because the milk remains intended in it, since the medicine strengthens it for reaching. * His statement: (And if milk is milked from a woman after her death, and the infant is made to swallow it: the prohibition attaches to it) because milk after death is as it was before it, except that it is in an impure container, and that does not prevent prohibition. And because the dead woman lacks action, and the action of the wet nurse is not considered, as indicated by the infant suckling from her while she sleeps. And the difference is that the purpose of milk is nourishment, and death does not prevent it, while the purpose of intercourse is customary pleasure, and that does not exist in intercourse with a dead woman.

Commentary
This passage presents two rulings from the matn, expanded by the commentator. First ruling: If human breast milk is mixed with medicine, but the milk is the predominant substance (al-ghalib), then the prohibition of marriage (tahrim al-rida') attaches. The reason given is that the milk remains the intended substance; the medicine merely aids its delivery. Second ruling: If milk is collected from a woman after her death and fed to an infant, the prohibition also attaches. The commentator explains three proofs: (1) Milk after death remains the same as before; the container (the body) is impure but that does not prevent prohibition. (2) Death does not affect the milk itself; it remains capable of nourishing. (3) The action of the wet nurse is not a condition; as evidenced by an infant suckling from a sleeping woman. Then the commentator adds a practical benefit (fa'idah): If a small girl suckles from a dead woman while having a husband, the dead woman becomes the husband's mother by fosterage, making him a mahram for the dead woman. This allows him to perform tayammum for her (since she is a mahram) and to bury her. The commentator then distinguishes this from intercourse with a dead woman: It does not establish the prohibition of affinity (hurmah al-musaharah) by consensus. The difference is that the purpose of milk is nourishment, which continues after death, whereas the purpose of intercourse is pleasure, which does not exist after death.

Key Terms
الرضاع (al-rida'): Fosterage: the act of an infant suckling human milk, which establishes familial prohibitions.
الوطء (al-wat'): Intercourse, specifically with a deceased woman in this context.
