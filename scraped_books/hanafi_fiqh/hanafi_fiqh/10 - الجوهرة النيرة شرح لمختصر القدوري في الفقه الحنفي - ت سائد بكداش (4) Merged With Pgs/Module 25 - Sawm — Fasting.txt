Breaking Consecutiveness in Dhihar Expiation: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling with proof and intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on breaking the consecutive fast in dhihar expiation and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the effect of intercourse.

Core Idea
Any break in the dhihar fast requires restarting; intercourse (even forgetful) breaks consecutiveness according to Abu Hanifa, but Abu Yusuf differs.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a critical condition in dhihar expiation: consecutiveness (tawātu'). While the basic rule is clear — any break, with or without excuse, requires restarting — a specific issue arises regarding intercourse: does forgetful or nocturnal intercourse break consecutiveness? The text presents the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad) and the dissenting view of Abu Yusuf, using qiyas. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school balances textual analogy and practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling on breaking the fast
The text states that breaking one day of the two-month fast, with or without excuse, requires restarting. What is the underlying condition being violated?
Consecutiveness (tawātu') is a condition for validity.
2
Distinguish the specific case of intercourse
According to the majority, intercourse during the fasting period of dhihar (even forgetfully by day or intentionally by night) breaks consecutiveness and requires restarting. The proof: it is analogous to i'tikaf, where the prohibition of intercourse is not specific to the fast.
This is contrasted with kaffarat al-qatl, where such acts do not break consecutiveness.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues that any intercourse that does not nullify the fast (forgetful by day, intentional by night) should not break consecutiveness. He analogizes to the expiation for killing.
The text rejects this, affirming the analogy with i'tikaf.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The relied-upon position is the majority view. Thus, in practice, any intercourse during the dhihar fast requires restarting, regardless of forgetfulness. The only exception is natural female bleeding (menstruation/postpartum).
The dispute remains an important usul example within the school.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira begins the chapter on dhihar. The first ruling is that if a person performing dhihar expiation — which requires fasting two consecutive months — breaks the fast of either day, whether intentionally or due to an excuse, they must restart the entire two-month fast. This is because the condition of consecutiveness (tawātu') has been lost while they were capable of maintaining it. The commentator then expands on a specific case: intercourse during this fasting period. The main position is that intercourse — even by day forgetfully or by night intentionally during dhihar expiation — does not require restarting, because the prohibition of intercourse in dhihar expiation is not specific to fasting (unlike Ramadan or kaffarat al-qatl). The text distinguishes: in kaffarat al-qatl, if one has intercourse forgetfully by day or intentionally by night, they do not restart; but if intentionally by day, they do restart by consensus. For dhihar, the comparison is to i'tikaf, where intercourse does not break consecutiveness. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that any intercourse that does not nullify the fast (like forgetful or nocturnal intercourse) does not break consecutiveness. His evidence is the case of kaffarat al-qatl. The text then clarifies that the immunity from restarting is only for intercourse with the wife from whom one performed dhihar; intercourse with any other woman requires restarting, by consensus. Finally, the passage notes that if a woman menstruates or experiences postpartum bleeding during the consecutive fasts, she does not restart; the consecutiveness is broken by that excuse, but she continues from where she left off after purification.

Key Terms
التتابع (al-tatābu'): The condition of consecutiveness; fasting the two months without interruption.
الوطء (al-waṭ'): Sexual intercourse; in this context, it refers to intercourse during the expiatory fast.

Feeding the Poor When Unable to Fast in Dhihar: Conditions and Dispute
Conditions for moving from fasting to feeding, amount, and dispute on recipients

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for transitioning from fasting to feeding in dhihar expiation and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether expiation food may be given to dhimmi poor.

Core Idea
Feeding 60 poor is the final tier; current inability triggers it, and the majority permits giving to dhimmis.

The Quran prescribes a three-tier expiation for dhihar: freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor people. This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira explains the specific rules for the feeding option: when it becomes obligatory, how much to give, and a point of internal disagreement within the Hanafi school over who may receive the food.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three tiers of dhihar expiation
Recall that expiation for dhihar proceeds in order: freeing a slave, then fasting two consecutive months, then feeding sixty poor. This lesson focuses on the third tier.
2
Determine the condition for moving from fasting to feeding
The passage states that the person must be 'unable to fast' at the time of expiation. This inability is current (not future); if one later becomes able, fasting becomes obligatory again. This differs from a decrepit old man where perpetual inability is considered.
Time of expiation, not time of dhihar, determines wealth status.
3
Apply the timing rule for wealth
Whether the person is considered rich (must feed) or poor (may fast) is evaluated at the time of expiation, not when the dhihar was uttered. Example: rich at dhihar but poor at expiation → fasting suffices; poor at dhihar but rich at expiation → must feed.
4
Determine the amount and type of food
Each poor person receives half a sa' of wheat (or its flour or sawiq) or one sa' of dates, barley (or their flour or sawiq). One sa' equals four manns.
These measures are specific to the Hanafi school.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on recipients
According to Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, the sixty poor may be Muslims or dhimmis (non-Muslim citizens of the Islamic state). Abu Yusuf restricts it to Muslim poor only. The majority view is mufta bihi.
The text says 'عندهما' (according to the two) to indicate the majority.

Commentary
This passage explains the third option in dhihar expiation: feeding sixty poor people. The transition from fasting to feeding is permitted only when the person is currently unable to fast (due to illness, old age, etc.). This is based on current inability, not future possibility; unlike the case of a decrepit old man who is unlikely to recover, where perpetual inability is considered. The text also clarifies that the relevant time for determining whether a person is wealthy or poor for the purpose of expiation is the time of actual expiation, not the time of uttering dhihar. For example, if someone was rich when he performed dhihar but poor when he expiates, he may fast (without feeding). Conversely, if he was poor at the time of dhihar but rich at expiation, he must feed, not fast. Regarding the recipients, the majority (Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad) hold that the sixty poor people may be Muslims or dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects of the Islamic state). Abu Yusuf restricts it to Muslim poor only. The amount of food is half a sa' of wheat (or its flour or sawiq) or one sa' of dates, barley (or their flour or sawiq). A sa' is defined as four manns (a measure of weight).

Key Terms
الصاع (al-ṣā'): A dry measure; in this context, a sa' is four manns (approximately 3.25 liters or 2.5 kg for wheat).
السويق (al-sawīq): Parched barley or wheat flour (a type of food).
الظهار (al-ẓihār): A form of divorce-like statement where a husband says to his wife 'You are like my mother's back', requiring expiation.
الذمي (al-dhimmī): A non-Muslim citizen of the Islamic state who pays jizya and is entitled to protection and welfare.
