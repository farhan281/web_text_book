Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ihram Clothing for Khuntha Mushkil
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha ihram dress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on what a khuntha mushkil wears in ihram, explain the reasoning behind each position, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
When gender is unknown, two Hanafi imams disagree: Muhammad prioritizes avoiding the greater obscenity, while Abu Yusuf refrains from any ruling.

This passage records an internal Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani on a unique case: a khuntha mushkil (hermaphrodite whose gender is unclear) who enters the state of ihram. Since men and women have different ihram dress codes (men cannot wear stitched garments, women can and must cover), the khuntha presents a dilemma. Both imams base their reasoning on conflicting prohibitions, but they reach opposite conclusions: Abu Yusuf gives no ruling, while Muhammad gives a definite answer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the problem: the khuntha's gender is unknown, and ihram dress rules differ by gender. What should he/she wear?
The khuntha mushkil is near puberty (rahiq) or past puberty.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Note that Abu Yusuf says 'I have no knowledge' (la 'ilma li) regarding the clothing. He refrains from giving a ruling because both wearing stitched garments (if male) and leaving them off (if female) involve a prohibition.
Abu Yusuf prioritizes uncertainty: since both choices entail a forbidden act, no ruling can be given.
3
State Muhammad's position and reasoning
Muhammad rules that the khuntha should wear women's clothing (stitched garments). His reasoning: if the khuntha is a woman, not wearing stitched garments is more obscene (afhash) than wearing them if the khuntha is a man.
Muhammad prioritizes avoiding the greater obscenity, even at the risk of a lesser one.
4
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider the practical difference: Abu Yusuf's view leaves the khuntha without guidance; Muhammad's view provides clear instruction. In later Hanafi fatwa, precaution often favors Muhammad's position or suggests a middle path.
The passage does not specify a mufta bihi; the dispute remains unresolved in this text.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad regarding what the khuntha mushkil should wear when in the state of ihram. Abu Yusuf abstains from giving a ruling, stating that he does not know what the khuntha should wear because of the conflicting prohibitions: if male, wearing stitched garments (makhit) is prohibited for men in ihram; if female, leaving them off (i.e., not wearing them) is prohibited for women. Since we cannot determine the gender, we cannot decide. Muhammad, however, gives a definite ruling: the khuntha should wear women's clothing (i.e., stitched garments). His reasoning is that if the khuntha turns out to be a woman, not wearing stitched garments (i.e., wearing ihram like a man) would be more obscene (afhash) than wearing them if he turns out to be a man. He also notes that if the khuntha is near puberty (rahiq) but has not yet reached it, there is no penalty (la shay'a 'alayh) for any mistake. The commentary adds that if the khuntha has already reached puberty, Abu Yusuf still has no opinion. The relied-upon position is not stated here; in later fatwa, the precautionary approach is often followed.

Key Terms
راهق (rahiq): A person approaching puberty; in the context, the khuntha is near the age of puberty.
مخيط (makhit): Stitched garments; men in ihram are forbidden from wearing them, while women may wear them.
خنثى مشكل (khuntha mushkil): Hermaphrodite with ambiguous gender, where the gender cannot be determined.

Ihram Attire for a Hermaphrodite: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha's ihram attire; additional ruling on circumcision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the ihram attire of a hermaphrodite and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Covering is better than uncovering: a khuntha should wear women's clothes in ihram.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on what a hermaphrodite (khuntha) approaching puberty should wear in ihram. Abu Yusuf declines to give a definitive ruling due to uncertainty, while Muhammad argues for women's clothing based on precaution and the principle that covering is better. The dispute illustrates how the Hanafi school handles ambiguity through tarjih and ihtiyat.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the exact question: what should a khuntha who is rahiq (approaching puberty) wear in ihram?
The khuntha has both male and female characteristics, making a clear ruling difficult.
2
Understand Abu Yusuf's argument
Abu Yusuf says he has no knowledge because both options (wearing sewn garments or not) have potential dislike depending on the actual gender.
He prioritizes avoiding both forms of karaha.
3
Understand Muhammad's argument
Muhammad argues for women's clothing (sewn garments) because leaving a woman uncovered is more disgraceful than a man wearing sewn garments, and covering is better.
He prioritizes covering (satr) over the risk of violating male ihram rules.
4
Identify the tarjih
The text adopts Muhammad's view as the relied-upon position (bihi na'khudh), requiring women's clothing and a dam as precaution.
This shows the Hanafi preference for precaution in cases of ambiguity.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ihram attire of a hermaphrodite (khuntha) who has not yet reached puberty (rahiq). Abu Yusuf refrains from giving a definitive ruling due to the uncertainty: if the khuntha is male, wearing sewn garments (al-makhit) in ihram is disliked; if female, not wearing them (i.e., leaving them off, as women do) is disliked. Thus, he cannot determine what is best. Muhammad takes a different approach: the khuntha should wear women's clothing (i.e., sewn garments) because the disgrace of a woman appearing without sewn garments (as a man does in ihram) is greater than a man wearing them. Since the khuntha has not reached puberty, no penalty applies. The text then interjects a ruling that a slave girl may be purchased to circumcise the khuntha, as she is permissible to look at him regardless of his gender. Muhammad reiterates his opinion that women's clothing should be worn, emphasizing that covering (satr) is better than uncovering due to the possibility of being female. Finally, Muhammad adds that a blood sacrifice (dam) is obligatory as a precaution, likely because if the khuntha is male, wearing sewn garments would violate ihram rules requiring compensation. This illustrates how the Hanafi school handles ambiguity through precaution (ihtiyat) and weighs different possibilities.

Key Terms
راهق (rahiq): Approaching puberty, close to the age of majority.
المخيط (al-makhit): Sewn garments that are tailored to the body (e.g., a shirt or trousers), which are prohibited for men in ihram.
الدم (al-dam): A blood sacrifice (usually a sheep) offered as compensation for violating an ihram restriction.
