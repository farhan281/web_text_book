The Khiyar of a Freed Slave Wife in Hanafi Law
Ruling on khiyar for freed slave wife and its details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the khiyar of a freed slave wife and identify the conditions under which she may exercise or lose it.

Core Idea
A freed slave wife married by her master has the option to annul the marriage; the option is tied to the council and lost by standing up.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific scenario: a master marries his female slave and then frees her. Upon freedom, the slave gains a special right – the khiyar – to either remain in the marriage or seek annulment without divorce. The commentary clarifies the temporal and procedural limits of this option, including the requirement of knowledge and the role of the council (majlis).

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
The passage deals with a female slave married by her master who is later freed. She has the option (khiyar) to annul or keep the marriage.
2
Determine the scope of the ruling
The option applies whether the husband is free or slave, and also to a mukataba.
3
Examine the conditions for exercising the option
She must learn of both the manumission and the existence of the option in a council. If she learns only of manumission, then later learns of the option in another council, she has the option there.
4
Identify the consequence of exercising the option
The separation is not a divorce but a 'firqa bi-ghayr talaq'.
5
Note the invalidator of the option
Standing up from the council invalidates the option, similar to the option of the mukhayyara.
6
Consider the dissent
Zufar denies the option because the contract was valid with her consent. The Hanafi position is with the majority (Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn).

Slave Marries Without Permission: Validity and Mahr
Ruling on validity and lack of option for slave who married without permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a slave's marriage without master's permission, identify who receives the mahr in various scenarios, and explain why the freed slave has no option.

Core Idea
A slave's marriage without master's permission is valid, and the mahr follows ownership at consummation.

This lesson examines the case where a female slave marries without her master's permission and is later freed. The Hanafi school holds that the marriage is valid, and she does not gain an option to annul upon freedom. The distribution of the mahr depends on whether the master permitted the marriage and the timing of consummation, reflecting the rule that mahr belongs to the owner of the slave at the time of consummation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its primary ruling
The act is a female slave marrying without her master's permission. The matn rules that the marriage is valid (sahih) and she has no option (khiyar) upon freedom. Note that the same ruling applies to a male slave, but the text specifies the female because the earlier case had established an option based on the master's action.
2
Understand the impact of the master's permission on mahr
If the master permitted the marriage (jāza), the mahr belongs to him regardless of whether he freed the slave or the timing of consummation. This is because the mahr is a right that follows ownership of the slave at the time the marriage is concluded with permission.
The commentator al-Khujandi clarifies this.
3
Analyze the scenario without master's permission
If the master did not permit the marriage until after he freed her, the contract becomes valid upon manumission. In this case, the mahr is determined by the timing of consummation: if antes of freedom, the master receives it; if after, the freed woman receives it.
The rationale is that at the moment of consummation, the mahr becomes due to the owner of the concubine (the master if she is still a slave, or herself if free).
4
Apply the rule to a concrete example
A slave marries without permission. The master does not permit. She consummates the marriage while still a slave. Then she is freed. The marriage is valid, she has no option, and the mahr belongs to the master. If consummation occurred after freedom, the mahr is hers.
5
Note the absence of option upon freedom
Unlike the previous case where a slave given in marriage by her master with a condition had an option, here the slave acted independently without any condition, so freedom does not grant her an option to annul.

Marrying Two Women in One Contract: Mahr Distribution Dispute
Ruling on split marriage contract and division of mahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on mahr distribution when two women are married in one contract and one marriage is void, and apply the rule to determine each woman's entitlement.

Core Idea
In a single contract marrying two women where one is unlawful, Abu Hanifa gives all mahr to the valid wife; the Sahibayn divide it proportionally.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies an intra-Hanafi dispute on mahr distribution when a man marries two women in a single contract, one of whom is unlawful for him. The matn establishes that the lawful marriage is valid and the other is void. The commentary then expands on the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding how the named mahr (musamma) should be allocated. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi fiqh handles contracts with mixed validity and the practical consequences for the women's financial rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the matn's ruling
Recognize that the man marries two women in one contract, one lawful and one unlawful. The matn rules the lawful marriage valid and the unlawful one void.
The void marriage is batil from the outset.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position on the mahr
According to Abu Hanifa, the entire named mahr goes to the valid wife, because the void marriage is as if it never existed.
This is based on the principle that a void contract yields no rights.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the mahr should be divided between the two women proportionally according to their respective mahr al-mithl (typical dower). The portion allocated to the void marriage is cancelled.
They treat the contract as a single transaction encompassing both women.
4
Note the additional case of consummation of the void marriage
If the man consummates the void marriage, she is entitled to mahr al-mithl. For Abu Hanifa, she gets full mahr al-mithl. For the Sahibayn, she gets mahr al-mithl but capped at her share of the named mahr.
This shows the practical difference even when a void marriage is consummated.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Compare the outcomes: Under Abu Hanifa, the valid wife gets the full named mahr; under the Sahibayn, she gets only a portion. If consummated, the void wife gets more under Abu Hanifa but is capped under the Sahibayn.
Later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn for practicality.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Apostasy before consummation is like divorce: wife gets half mahr.

This short passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a specific scenario: the husband leaves Islam before consummating the marriage. The Hanafi school rules that the marriage is dissolved, but the wife is not left empty-handed. Instead, she receives half the mahr. The reasoning is based on qiyas (analogical deduction) to the case of divorce before consummation, which is explicitly mentioned in the Quran. Understanding this qiyas helps us see how Hanafi jurists extend rulings from clear textual sources to similar situations.

1
Identify the case and ruling

2

The original case is divorce pronounced before consummation. In that case, Quran 2:237 establishes that the wife receives half the mahr. This is the clear text-based ruling that serves as the foundation for the qiyas.

Because the illa matches, the ruling follows: the wife receives half the mahr. The Hanafi school uses this qiyas to fill a gap not explicitly covered by the Quran or hadith.
