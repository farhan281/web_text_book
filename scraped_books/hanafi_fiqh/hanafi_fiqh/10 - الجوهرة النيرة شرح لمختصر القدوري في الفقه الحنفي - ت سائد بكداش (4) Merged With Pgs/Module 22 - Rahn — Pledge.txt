Ma'dhun Cannot Execute Kitaba
States that the ma'dhun cannot make a kitaba contract with his slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a ma'dhun making a kitaba contract and apply the condition that the master's approval is necessary for validity.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun's permission is limited to trade; kitaba is not trade, so he cannot make it without the master's approval.

A ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade) enjoys broad authority for commercial transactions, but not all contracts fall within that scope. Kitaba—a contract of manumission by installment payments—is fundamentally different from trade. This lesson clarifies the boundaries of the ma'dhun's capacity and the master's role in validating such contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the agent
The act is a ma'dhun (a slave permitted by his master to engage in trade) making a kitaba contract with his own slave. Identify that the ma'dhun is acting as principal, not agent.
2
Determine the default ruling
The text states 'ولا يكاتب' (he does not make kitaba). This is a prohibition; the ma'dhun is not allowed to initiate kitaba. The default ruling is that such an act is invalid without the master's consent.
The prohibition is because kitaba is not a trade contract.
3
Understand the reason
Kitaba is a contract of manumission, not an exchange of property for property. The ma'dhun's permission is specifically for trade (buyu'), so kitaba falls outside his scope.
In Hanafi fiqh, trade is defined as exchanging property for property; kitaba exchanges property for freedom.
4
Apply the exception
If the master approves the kitaba after it is made, it becomes valid. This shows that the prohibition is not absolute; it is curable by the master's ratification.
The master's approval transforms the contract from invalid to valid.
5
Identify the consequence of approval
Upon approval, the payments (the kitaba wealth) belong to the master, not the ma'dhun. The ma'dhun acts as an intermediary (safir); the rights of the contract return to the master.
The ma'dhun has no claim to the payments, and the slave cannot pay the ma'dhun directly.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement: a ma'dhun (permitted slave) cannot make a kitaba contract (a contract of manumission by payment) with his own slave. The reason is that kitaba is not trade; trade is the exchange of property for property, whereas in kitaba, the payment is in exchange for removing the restriction of slavery. Since the ma'dhun's permission is only for trade, this falls outside his authority. However, if the master approves the kitaba, it becomes valid. In that case, the ownership of the kitaba payments goes to the master, not the ma'dhun, because the ma'dhun acts merely as an intermediary. The passage also notes that the ma'dhun has no debt upon him (meaning no liability for the slave's price) because the master already owns the slave.

Key Terms
كتابة (kitaba): A contract between a master and his slave whereby the slave pays a sum in installments to earn freedom; here, the ma'dhun cannot execute it because it is not a trade contract.
سفير (safir): Intermediary; the ma'dhun acts as an intermediary for the master in the kitaba contract, so the master receives the payments.
