Conditions for Tahlil: Intercourse Required
Ruling on necessity of intercourse for tahlil marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for tahlil (actual penetration), explain the proof from hadith, and rebut the opposing view of Ibn al-Musayyib using the Quranic double indication.

Core Idea
Actual intercourse is required for tahlil; marriage alone does not suffice according to the Hanafi school.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies a critical condition in family law: what makes a thrice-divorced woman permissible to remarry her first husband? The answer lies in a famous hadith and a near-consensus of scholars. We will examine the proof, the lone dissenting view of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib, and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that after a triple divorce, the woman must marry another man and consummate the marriage before she can remarry her first husband. The dispute is whether consummation is required or marriage alone suffices.
This is known as tahlil (making permissible).
2
State the Hanafi position and its primary proof
The Hanafi school requires actual intercourse (ilaj) during a valid second marriage. The proof is the hadith: 'She is not permissible for the first until she tastes the honey of the other.' The word 'tastes' implies actual penetration.
The hadith is narrated in Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Examine the opposing argument
Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib argued from the apparent meaning of Quran 2:230: 'until she marries another husband.' He took 'marriage' (nikah) as sufficient, without requiring intercourse.
This view is considered weak and not accepted by the majority.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis point out that the verse uses two terms: 'marriage' (nikah) and 'husband' (zawj). This indicates two conditions: a valid marriage and actual cohabitation. If marriage alone sufficed, only one term would have been used.
This is a linguistic argument about the Quranic wording.
5
Apply the ruling to special cases
The passage specifies that penetration is sufficient even without ejaculation, and that intercourse during menstruation, fasting, or ihram still qualifies. It also covers adolescents, slaves, and women with a fused vagina (muftada), where pregnancy becomes evidence of vaginal penetration.
These case studies reinforce the general rule.

Multiple Talaq and the Effect of a Third Marriage
Explains a scenario where a woman becomes lawful for two previous husbands after triple divorces

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the permissibility of a woman remarrying her first two husbands after a triple divorce, when the second husband divorced without consummation and the third husband consummated.

Core Idea
A woman becomes lawful for all previous husbands if a later marriage includes consummation, even if an intermediate husband did not consummate.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Karkhi regarding a complex marriage scenario. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi position on tahlil and the conditions required for a woman to become permissible for her previous spouses after multiple divorces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sequence of events
Trace the chain: First husband divorces three times → marries second → second divorces three times without consummation → marries third → third consummates.
Note that second husband did not consummate.
2
Recognize the tahlil requirement
Understand that tahlil (making permissible for first husband) normally requires a valid marriage with consummation. Here, the third husband's consummation fulfills that.
The second husband's marriage was valid but lacked consummation.
3
Apply the principle to previous husbands
The third husband's consummation removes the prohibition (hurmat) for all previous husbands who gave three irrevocable divorces, including the second even without consummation.
The ruling relies on the concept that consummation in a later marriage benefits all prior husbands.
4
Conclude the ruling
State that she becomes lawful (halal) for both the first and second husbands.
This is the mufta bihi position according to al-Karkhi.

Explains the condition for a mafdah woman's tahlil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on when a mafdah woman becomes lawful after tahlil and identify the basis for requiring pregnancy.

Core Idea
Pregnancy is the only certain proof of valid vaginal intercourse for a mafdah woman.

This lesson addresses a special case in tahlil: a woman with a fistula (mafdah) who has been divorced three times and remarries. Since it is uncertain whether intercourse occurred in the vagina or anus, the Hanafi school requires pregnancy as a condition for her becoming lawful to the first husband.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and scenario
The woman is mafdah (fistula) and has been divorced three times. She marries a second husband who consummates the marriage. The question is whether she becomes halal for the first husband.
The fistula makes it impossible to ascertain the site of intercourse without external evidence.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
She does not become lawful for the first husband unless she becomes pregnant. This is the operative ruling (mufta bihi).
The ruling is based on ihtiyat (precaution) due to doubt.
3
Analyze the dalil (proof)
The proof is istihsan and the indication (qarina) of pregnancy. Pregnancy resolves the doubt by confirming vaginal intercourse.
No explicit text; the reasoning is based on likelihood (ghalabat al-zann).
4
Recognize the ikhtilaf
This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school. Other schools may accept other forms of evidence or not require pregnancy.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.

The commentary then draws out the consequence for purification: the woman must perform ghusl due to actual intercourse, while the minor is not obligated because he is not legally responsible.

Tahlil and Physical Defects: Maslul vs. Majbub
Distinguishes between maslul and majbub in tahlil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling for tahlil with a maslul and a majbub second husband, and apply Abu Yusuf's view regarding pregnancy as evidence.

Core Idea
For tahlil, effective penetration matters, not ejaculation; a majbub's act may be validated by pregnancy.

Tahlil (marrying a woman to make her lawful for her first husband after a triple divorce) requires actual consummation. But what if the second husband has a physical defect? This lesson examines two cases: a man with atrophied testicles (maslul) and one with amputated penis (majbub). The Hanafi school agrees on the maslul but differs on the majbub, especially when pregnancy occurs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the maslul case
Identify the ruling for a second husband whose testicles are atrophied. The text says if he can achieve an erection and have intercourse, the tahlil is valid because penetration (mukhalata) occurs, even without ejaculation. Ejaculation is not a condition.
This is analogous to a normal man who has intercourse without ejaculating.
2
Understand the majbub case
Identify the default ruling: the majbub's intercourse does not make the woman lawful because only surface contact (mulasaqa) occurs, not the meeting of circumcised parts (iltiqa' al-khatanayn), which is the requirement.
The condition for consummation is the meeting of the two circumcised parts, which is impossible for a man without a penis.
3
Abu Yusuf's view on pregnancy
State that if the woman becomes pregnant from the majbub and gives birth, she becomes lawful and is considered muhsana according to Abu Yusuf. He uses pregnancy as a qarina (evidence) that effective penetration must have occurred.
Pregnancy is a strong indicator that the meeting of circumcised parts happened, even if the penis is amputated.
4
Zufar and al-Hasan's objection
State that Zufar and al-Hasan (ibn Ziyad) disagree: they hold that even with pregnancy, she does not become lawful nor muhsana because the original act lacked the required meeting of circumcised parts.
They maintain the strict requirement; pregnancy could be due to other causes or a miracle, not necessarily effective penetration.
5
Analyze the practical consequence
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: if the woman becomes pregnant, under Abu Yusuf she becomes lawful for the first husband and is muhsana (affecting her legal status for zina punishment and future marriages). Under Zufar's view, she remains unlawful and not muhsana.
This dispute impacts the woman's ability to remarry her first husband and her criminal liability for zina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given oath constitutes Ila' by analyzing the duration and phrasing of the oath, applying the Hanafi rule that Ila' requires a continuous prevention period of four months or more.

Core Idea
Ila' requires a continuous four-month prevention period; oaths for less or with gaps do not trigger it.

This passage from the Jawahirah al-Nayyirah explains a key condition of Ila': the oath must prevent intercourse for four months or more continuously. The author presents several examples of different wordings to illustrate when the prevention period is considered continuous. As you read, focus on how the scholars calculate the total period of prevention and whether it meets the four-month threshold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the duration of each oath

2
Determine if oaths are separate or combined
Examine the connecting words. 'Wa' (and) or 'thumma' (then) with no gap may combine. Repeated oaths with a pause (e.g., one day) are separate. The particle 'la' (nor) can cause intermingling when the negative is repeated.

For combined oaths, add the durations to get total continuous prevention. For separate oaths, the continuous period is the longer single duration (if the gap is not part of the oath period) or the sum if there is no gap.

If the continuous prevention period is four months or more, the husband is a mūlī; otherwise, he is not.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ila' requires a single oath of abstinence for at least four continuous months; shorter or interrupted periods are not Ila'.

In Hanafi family law, not every oath of abstinence by a husband is considered Ila'. The duration of the oath is critical. This lesson explains that only oaths of four months or more trigger the legal category of Ila', and shows how to handle multiple or overlapping oaths that may not meet the requirement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
Determine the core legal statement: an oath of abstinence less than four months does not make the husband a muli (one who has taken Ila').
The passage begins with the matn: 'And if he swears to abstain for less than four months: he is not a muli.'
2
Understand the rationale
The reason is that the husband can have intercourse within that period without incurring a legal violation (hanth). Ila' only applies when the oath makes intercourse impossible without breaking the oath for at least four months.
The commentary explains: 'because he can have intercourse with her in that period without incurring any violation that obligates him'.
3
Analyze consecutive oaths
If the husband says 'two months and two months' (totalling four), he is a muli because the combined period is four months without an interruption. But if he says 'two months', waits a day, then adds another two months, the total halting period is less than four months (the day breaks continuity), so he is not a muli.
The second oath is a new obligation, not a continuation; thus the prohibition is interrupted.
4
Distinguish overlapping oaths
When the husband says 'nor two months' with the negation particle repeated, the two oaths overlap, and the effective period is the shorter one (two months). This is analogous to saying 'I will not speak to him for a day nor two days' – the oath expires in two days.
Overlapping durations merge, and the longer period governs, but here the negation creates separate obligations that collapse.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prayer is not a deterrent in Ila' — Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf distinguish it from Hajj and fasting.

The passage establishes that swearing by acts of worship involving hardship or financial burden (Hajj, fasting, charity, etc.) makes a husband a muli. If the object returns to the husband's ownership before intercourse (e.g., repurchasing the slave), the Ila' is renewed and the period starts afresh.

Core Idea
Ila' is valid only when the oath is over something that can be fulfilled by expiation and only from a wife who retains marital rights to intercourse.

This passage expands on two specific cases of ila'. First, a husband who swears to sacrifice his child if he approaches his wife: is this a valid ila'? Second, ila' from a woman divorced revocably is valid because the marriage persists, but from an irrevocably divorced woman it is not, as she no longer has a right to intercourse. Understanding these conditions clarifies the boundaries of ila' in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The validity of ila' depends on the slave being in the husband's ownership at the time of intercourse.

This lesson examines how a change in the ownership of a slave vowed as expiation affects the validity of ila'. The ruling turns on a simple condition: the expiation object must be available at the moment of intercourse. We trace the logic through sale and reacquisition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow and its condition
The husband vows to free a specific slave if he has intercourse with his wife. The vow creates a contingent obligation.
The slave must be owned at the time of the conditioning event (intercourse).
2
Understand the effect of sale before intercourse
If the husband sells the slave before intercourse, the vow is nullified because the slave is no longer owned. No expiation is required after intercourse.
Sale before intercourse removes the possibility of freeing that slave.
3
Analyze reacquisition before intercourse
If the husband buys back the slave before intercourse, the vow revives. The slave is once again owned and available for expiation.
Reacquisition restores the condition of ownership.
4
Analyze reacquisition after intercourse
If the husband buys back the slave after intercourse has occurred, the vow does not revive. The act has already taken place without a valid expiation object.
The condition (ownership) must be present at the moment of intercourse, not after.
5
State the general principle
The ruling depends solely on whether the expiation object is in the husband's ownership at the time of intercourse. Continuous ownership is not required.
Interruption of ownership before intercourse voids the vow, but restoration before intercourse revives it.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ila' remains valid as long as at least one slave remains for expiation.

When a man vows to free two slaves if he has intercourse, what happens if one or both slaves die or are sold before intercourse? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the continuity of ila' based on the possibility of fulfilling the expiation. The key principle is that ila' is not invalidated as long as there remains a viable means of expiation, even if only one of the two slaves survives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow and its components
The husband vows to free two specific slaves if he has intercourse. Note that the expiation is a single act: freeing a slave, but he mentions two slaves. In Hanafi law, specifying two slaves means each is a potential expiation; the fulfilment requires freeing one slave, but the vow names two as an emphasis or to cover both possibilities. However, the effect is that if both are available, he may free either; if one is lost, the other suffices.
The text assumes the vow is an oath (yamīn) or ila'? Actually it is ila' (vow to abstain) with a conditional freeing.
2
Determine the effect of losing one slave
If one slave dies or is sold, the ila' remains valid because the remaining slave can still be freed as expiation. The obligation lingers as long as there is a slave to free.
The ruling relies on the principle that inability to fulfil a vow in part does not nullify the entire vow if partial fulfilment remains possible.
3
Determine the effect of losing both slaves
If both slaves die or are sold (whether together or consecutively), the ila' is invalidated because there is no slave left to free. The expiation becomes impossible, so the vow collapses.
Death and sale are equally effective in removing ownership for the purpose of the vow.
4
Analyze the case of reacquisition
If one of the lost slaves re-enters the man's ownership before intercourse, the ila' is concluded (revived) at that moment. If the second also re-enters, the ila' is considered concluded from the time of the first re-entry. This ensures the earliest possible conclusion, treating the vow as effective continuously from the first available expiation.
Reacquisition can be by any means: purchase, gift, inheritance, etc.

Core Idea
An ila' validly initiated in marriage continues even after an irrevocable divorce.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a husband makes ila' (swears to abstain from intercourse) and then divorces his wife irrevocably. Does the ila' become void, or does it still take effect? The Hanafi position is clear: the ila' remains valid and can produce a divorce if the four-month period expires during the iddah. Identify the legal scenario

2

One divorce occurs due to ila' at the end of the four months.
The ila' is not nullified; its consequence (talaq) takes effect even after the irrevocable divorce.
3

The ila' was initiated while the wife was still legally married. The validity of the ila' is established at its inception; subsequent events do not undo it.
The proof is rational (dalil 'aqli): once a valid cause (sabab) is set, it continues unless explicitly abrogated.
4

A legal act validly initiated remains effective for its duration. The irrevocable divorce does not erase the past contractual bond that supported the ila'.
This is consistent with the Hanafi maxim: 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' but also relies on the continuity of legal effects.
5
Contrast with a hypothetical
If ila' were attempted after the irrevocable divorce, it would be invalid because no marital bond exists.
The key is the timing: the ila' must be initiated while the marriage is intact.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Legal incapacity (ihram/fasting) does not remove physical ability; fi' must be by intercourse, unlike Zufar's analogy to physical incapacity.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a second type of incapacity in ila' — legal incapacity, such as being in a state of ihram or fasting. The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa) rules that fi' must be by intercourse because the husband is physically capable, while Zufar argues that speech suffices because the prohibition is from Allah, similar to physical impossibility. We will analyze the reasoning behind each position and the practical outcome.

The text introduces 'al-ajz min tariq al-hukm' — legal incapacity, with examples: being in ihram, fasting, or similar.

State the Hanafi position
The text says: 'فهذا فيه : الوطء عندنا' — 'then in this [case] the fi' is intercourse according to us [Hanafis].'

The husband could break the fast/have intercourse and then make expiation.

Under Abu Hanifa, the husband must actually have intercourse to avoid divorce. Under Zufar, a simple statement suffices. The majority follows Abu Hanifa.

A husband's declaration of harām is fundamentally an oath (yamin) in the Hanafi school; only an explicit intention of divorce turns it into talaq.

When a husband says 'You are harām for me', the legal outcome depends critically on what he intended in his heart. Book of Khul'. If the spouses quarrel and fear that they will not uphold the limits of Allah, then there is no harm for her to ransom herself from him with wealth by which he divorces her. It is the Book of Khul'. In language, it is derived from 'khala'a' (to remove), as in 'he removed the sandal and shirt'. In sacred law, it is an expression for a contract between the spouses, with wealth from the woman which she gives, so he divorces her. Its ruling from her side is the ruling of a compensatory contract (mu'awada), so she is permitted to revoke it, and it becomes void if she abandons it. She is allowed in it the condition of option (khiyar) according to the correct view. It is not valid to suspend it on danger. Its ruling from the husband's side is the ruling of a suspended (ta'liq) divorce, i.e., a divorce suspended on a condition, so he is not permitted to revoke it. He is not allowed in it the condition of option, and it does not become void by his abandonment, and it is valid to suspend it on danger. He (the author) said, may Allah have mercy on him: 'If the spouses quarrel and fear that they will not uphold the limits of Allah, then there is no harm for her to ransom herself from him with wealth by which he divorces her.'

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Khul' with a definition and key legal characteristics. Linguistically, khul' means to remove; legally, it is a contract where the wife gives money to the husband in exchange for divorce. The commentator then distinguishes two perspectives: from the wife's side, it resembles a sale (mu'awada) —she can revoke it and stipulate a condition of option (khiyar). From the husband's side, it is a conditional divorce (ta'liq)—he cannot revoke it unilaterally, cannot stipulate a condition of option, and it can be suspended on a contingent event (danger). The correct view (al-sahih) is that the wife may stipulate khiyar. The passage concludes by quoting the matn of al-Quduri stating the basic permissibility of khul' when spouses fear transgression.

Key Terms
الخلع (al-khul‘): A contract between spouses where the wife gives money to the husband in exchange for her release (divorce).
المعاوضة (al-mu‘āwaḍah): A compensatory exchange, like a sale, where each party gives and receives something of value.
التعليق (al-ta‘līq): Suspending a divorce on a condition; the divorce takes effect only if the condition occurs.

Khul': Ba'in Talaq and the Role of Intention
Commentary on the matn ruling of khul' and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khul' and identify when intention is required.

Core Idea
Khul' with compensation effects a ba'in divorce without intention; without compensation, intention is needed.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on khul' (divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for compensation). The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that when the husband pronounces khul' with property, a ba'in divorce occurs immediately and the wife must pay the compensation. The commentary clarifies the role of intention: it is not required when property is mentioned because the exchange itself makes the dissolution intended. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the rules of khul' correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its key elements
Note that khul' with property results in a ba'in talaq and the wife owes the compensation. The passage states this emphatically: 'waqa'a bil-khul' tatliqah bā'inah wa lazima-hā al-māl.'
Ba'in means irrevocable.
2
Understand the condition of mushāqqah
Mushāqqah is marital discord where the source of the problem is unknown. The passage explains that if there is no discord from the wife, it is disliked for the husband to take anything. This condition makes khul' recommended but not obligatory.
The condition affects recommendation, not validity.
3
Distinguish between khul' with property and without
When property is mentioned, the husband's intention is automatically inferred; no separate intent is needed. Without property, the pronouncement is a kināyah (allusive expression) and requires the husband's intention for divorce.
Kināyah: a word that can mean divorce but needs intention.
4
Analyze the rationale for omitting intention
The wife gives property to free herself. That purpose (baynūnah) is clear from the exchange, so the husband's inner intent is irrelevant. The property itself indicates the dissolution.
This is a practical ruling based on clear indication.

Conditions for Valid Khul': Session, Acceptance, and Absence
Rulings on acceptance in the same session and absent parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid khul' acceptance, explain the binding nature of the husband's offer, and apply the rules for absent parties.

Core Idea
In khul', the wife's session is the only relevant session for acceptance; the husband's offer is binding like an oath but the wife may revoke.

This passage expands on the rules of acceptance (qabul) in khul'. While the basic principle is that khul' is an offer and acceptance, the Hanafi school specifies that acceptance must occur in the same session as the offer, and that session is the wife's, not the husband's. Additionally, the husband's offer becomes irrevocable like an oath, whereas the wife retains the right to revoke until acceptance. The passage also addresses scenarios where one party is absent and the permissibility of conditional khul'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Khul' and divorce for property require acceptance in the session (majlis). If the wife leaves before accepting, the khul' is invalid.
This contrasts with ordinary sales where acceptance can occur later.
2
Determine which session is relevant
The session considered is the wife's session, not the husband's. If the husband leaves but the wife accepts in her session, it is valid.
The wife's session is the time and place where she hears the offer.
3
Analyze binding nature from each side
From the husband's side, the offer is like an oath (yamin) – he cannot revoke. From the wife's side, it is like an exchange of property – she can revoke before acceptance.
The husband cannot withdraw his offer; the wife can.
4
Apply to absent parties
If husband offers while wife is absent, the khul' is suspended until she accepts in her session of knowledge. If wife offers while husband is absent, it is invalid even if he later accepts.
The wife's session is key; the husband's absence does not create a valid offer from her side.
5
Note the permissibility of conditional khul'
The husband can attach conditions (e.g., 'if tomorrow comes'), but the wife cannot. If she accepts before the condition is met, the khul' is invalid.
Conditional khul' from the wife is not valid.
