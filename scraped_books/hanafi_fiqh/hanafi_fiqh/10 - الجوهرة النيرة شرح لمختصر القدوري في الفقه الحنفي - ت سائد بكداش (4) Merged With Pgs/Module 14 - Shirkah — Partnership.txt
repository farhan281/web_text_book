Dispute over Mixed Lost Dirhams: Abu Hanifa vs. Ibn Shubruma
Story of a dispute over division of a remaining dirham from mixed and lost coins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on division of mixed lost assets and contrast it with the opposing view, applying the principle of proportional ownership after mixing.

Core Idea
Mixing creates proportional partnership; loss is shared proportionally, not by allocating specific lost coins.

This lesson presents a famous legal debate between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Kufan judge Ibn Shubruma over mixed coins after partial loss. The disagreement illustrates a core Hanafi principle: that mixing of indistinguishable assets creates joint ownership with proportional shares, affecting how losses and remaining assets are distributed. Understanding this debate strengthens your grasp of partnership law and the logic behind Hanafi rulings on commingled property.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa ruled regarding the remaining dirham. He said it is divided as one-third to the owner of one and two-thirds to the owner of two.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Ibn Shubruma argued that the remaining dirham should be divided equally (half and half). Understand his reasoning.
Ibn Shubruma was a prominent Kufan scholar and judge, not a founder of a separate madhhab.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Ibn Shubruma allocated the two lost dirhams first: one certainly belongs to the owner of two, the other is uncertain, so the remaining is split equally.
This method treats the lost coins as specific losses before considering joint ownership.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa countered that mixing creates proportional joint ownership in every coin; each lost coin carries its share, so the remaining is still owned proportionally.
The key is that after mixing, no coin is individually owned.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: the owner of one dirham gets 1/3 under Abu Hanifa vs. 1/2 under Ibn Shubruma.
This shows how a different legal conception of mixing changes the outcome.

Commentary
This passage narrates a famous dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Kufan judge Ibn Shubruma concerning a case of mixed coins with partial loss. The problem: Three dirhams were mixed, belonging to two owners (one with one dirham, the other with two). Two dirhams were lost; one remained. How should the remaining dirham be divided? Ibn Shubruma argued that the lost dirhams must be allocated first: one lost dirham certainly belongs to the owner of two, and the second lost dirham is uncertain between the two owners, so the remaining dirham is divided equally. Abu Hanifa countered that once mixed, the coins become a joint ownership (shirka) in proportion to each owner's share. Thus, each of the three dirhams is owned jointly: the one-dirham owner has 1/3 of each, the two-dirham owner has 2/3 of each. When a coin is lost, it is lost proportionately. Hence the remaining dirham is still jointly owned in the same proportions: 1/3 and 2/3. The Hanafi position is based on the principle of mixing causing undivided shares (shuyū‘), as opposed to earmarking specific coins. This is the relied-upon view in the school.

Key Terms
شركة (shirka): Partnership, here meaning joint ownership of the mixed coins.
أثلاثاً (athlāthan): Into three parts; here meaning one-third and two-thirds.
نصفان (nisfān): Two halves, i.e., equal division.

Interdicted Slave's Acknowledgment and Manumission: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi disputes on interdicted slave's iqrar and manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the validity of an interdicted slave's acknowledgment and manumission, and identify the consensus exception when debts encompass the property.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment by an interdicted slave: valid for Abu Hanifa, invalid for Sahibayn unless debts cover the property.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah examines two points of disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the effects of interdiction on a permitted slave. The first concerns the slave's acknowledgment (iqrar) of debts or deposits; the second concerns his manumission of his own slaves when debts encumber both his property and his person. Understanding these disputes reveals how the Hanafi school balances the slave's capacity to transact with the creditors' rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disputed rulings
The text presents two separate issues: (1) the validity of the slave's acknowledgment (iqrar) concerning property in his hand, and (2) the effect of debts that encompass both the slave's property and his person on his manumission of his own slaves.
Both issues arise in the context of a ma'dhun (permitted) slave who is interdicted due to debt.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa
For acknowledgment: Abu Hanifa holds it valid, so the slave's iqrar is enforceable against the property in his hand. For manumission: if debts encompass property and person, the master does not own the property, so the slave's manumission of his own slaves is invalid.
Abu Hanifa's view treats the slave as having capacity over the property in his hand, subject to creditors' rights.
3
State the position of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that acknowledgment is invalid because interdiction removes legal capacity. For manumission, they say the master owns the property, so the slave's manumission is effective, but the master is liable for the slave's value.
The Sahibayn emphasize the master's ownership over the slave's property.
4
Note the consensus exception
Both disputes share a common exception: if the debts already encompass all the property in the slave's hand, then acknowledgment is invalid (by consensus), and manumission is similarly invalid (if debts encompass both property and person). If debts do not encompass, manumission is valid by consensus.
This exception shows that when creditors' rights fully attach, even Abu Hanifa's position aligns with the Sahibayn.
5
Distinguish the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave's valid acknowledgment can deplete the property in his hand, reducing what creditors can claim. Under the Sahibayn, the acknowledgment is void, so the property remains intact for creditors. For manumission, Abu Hanifa's view prevents the slave from freeing his own slaves, preserving their value for creditors; the Sahibayn allow the manumission but make the master liable for the value.
The thamarah directly affects the distribution of assets among creditors and the master.

Commentary
This passage addresses two disputes between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding the effect of interdiction on a permitted slave. First, the validity of the slave's acknowledgment (iqrar): Abu Hanifa considers it valid for the property in his hand, while the Sahibayn consider it invalid. However, there is consensus that if the slave's debts already encumber all the property in his hand, then his acknowledgment is invalid because the creditors' rights have attached. Second, when debts encumber both the slave's property and his own value (i.e., he is insolvent), Abu Hanifa holds that the master does not own the property in the slave's hand, so any manumission by the slave of his own slaves is ineffective. The Sahibayn hold that the master does own the property, so the manumission is effective but the master is liable for the slave's value. If debts do not encumber the property, manumission is valid by consensus.

Harvesting and Threshing Expenses in Sharecropping: Ruling and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute on expense allocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on sharing harvesting expenses in sharecropping and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, identifying the role of custom (ta'amul) in the tarjih.

Core Idea
In sharecropping, expenses after maturity are shared; stipulating them on the worker alone invalidates the contract per Zahir al-Riwayah.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses the allocation of harvesting and threshing expenses in a sharecropping (muzara'ah) contract. The author presents the default rule from Zahir al-Riwayah and then introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute where Abu Yusuf allows stipulation based on custom. Understanding this dispute clarifies how custom ('urf) can influence fiqh rulings within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling for post-maturity expenses
Begin by locating the statement that expenses of harvesting, threshing, and winnowing are shared between the parties. Note that this is the position of Zahir al-Riwayah.
The phrase 'عليهما بالحصص' indicates proportional sharing.
2
Understand the effect of stipulating expenses on the worker
Read the clause: 'فإن شرطا ذلك في المزارعة على العامل : فسدت'. Recognize that stipulating these expenses solely on the worker renders the contract invalid (fasid) according to Abu Hanifa.
This applies only after the contract term ends; otherwise the worker bears them.
3
Distinguish between temporal periods
Divide the contract timeline into three segments: before maturity (worker alone), after maturity before division (shared), after division (shared). Understand why each segment has a different ruling.
The worker's obligation ends at maturity; later expenses are joint.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Identify Abu Yusuf's position that stipulation is permissible due to custom (ta'amul). Note that the Mashayikh of Balkh and later Sarakhsi preferred this view for their regions.
This shows how local custom can override a qiyas-based ruling.
5
Recognize the role of custom in tarjih
Observe that the author (al-Sarakhsi) declares Abu Yusuf's view 'al-asahh' for his region. Conclude that the mufta bihi may vary based on established practice.
This is a key principle in Hanafi ifta: custom can shift the relied-upon position.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses the expenses of harvesting and related activities in a sharecropping (muzara'ah) contract. The author first states the basic rule: the wages of harvesting, reaping, threshing, and winnowing are to be shared between the landowner and the worker in proportion to their respective shares of the crop. This is the position of the Zahir al-Riwayah. However, if the parties stipulate in the contract that the worker bears all these expenses alone, the contract becomes invalid (fasid). This ruling applies only when the contract term has ended; if the term has not yet expired, the worker bears these costs alone because the worker's duty is to tend the crop until maturity. The author then discusses an exception: if they take the crop as green fodder (qasil) before maturity and sell it, the harvesting cost is shared according to their rights. On the second ruling (stipulation causing invalidity), the text explains that harvesting and threshing are not obligatory on the worker; the worker only must care for the crop until it ripens. Then an intra-Hanafi dispute is reported: Abu Yusuf holds that such a stipulation is permissible due to common practice (ta'amul), and this was chosen by the scholars of Balkh. Imam al-Sarakhsi considered Abu Yusuf's view as the more correct (asahh) in his region (Khurasan). The passage then summarizes the temporal division: work before maturity (e.g., watering, guarding) is on the worker; work after maturity but before division (e.g., harvesting, threshing) is shared; work after division (e.g., carrying, guarding) is also shared. This is analogously applied to musaqah (sharecropping of trees). Finally, two cases are ruled invalid by consensus: stipulating picking of fruit on the worker (no custom), and stipulating harvesting on the landowner (no custom). The teacher should emphasize the importance of custom ('urf) in validating such stipulations and the hierarchy of sources: the Zahir al-Riwayah vs. practice-based opinions.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzāra‘ah): A contract of sharecropping where one party provides land and seed, the other provides labor, and the crop is shared proportionally.
ظاهر الرواية (ẓāhir ar-riwāyah): The well-known transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad's six books, considered authoritative in the Hanafi school.
التعامل (at-ta‘āmul): Common practice or custom among people in a particular region, used as a basis for legal ruling.
