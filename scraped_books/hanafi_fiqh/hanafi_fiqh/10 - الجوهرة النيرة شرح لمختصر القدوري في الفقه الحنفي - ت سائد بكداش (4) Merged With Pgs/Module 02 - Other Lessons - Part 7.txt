Rulings of the Foundling (Laqit)
Defines the foundling and states its legal status and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal status of a foundling (laqit) in the Hanafi school and apply the rulings on picking up, maintenance, and freedom.

Core Idea
A foundling is presumed free and Muslim, with maintenance from Bayt al-Mal.

This lesson opens the Book of the Foundling in the Hanafi text. It defines the laqit as an abandoned child and establishes its fundamental legal status: free and Muslim. The rulings that flow from this status are detailed, including the obligation to pick up a foundling in the wilderness, the prohibition on claiming it as a slave without evidence, and the responsibility of the public treasury for its maintenance.

Guided Path
1
Define the laqit and its derivation
Identify the linguistic and legal definition. Note the term 'laqit' comes from iltiqat (picking up). The passage defines it as an abandoned human child discarded out of fear of poverty or accusation.
2
Determine the ruling on picking up
Distinguish between town (mandub) and wilderness (wajib). Understand the basis: saving the child's life.
This distinction is based on the likelihood of others finding the child.
3
Establish the default legal status
Explain why the foundling is free and Muslim: original human freedom, Dar al-Islam, found among Muslims.
The default for all humans in Hanafi law is freedom unless proven otherwise.
4
Identify the consequences of freedom
List: qadhf penalty applies, no slavery claim without evidence, testimony valid, no manumission/tadbir/kitabah.
5
Analyze maintenance and liability
Maintenance from Bayt al-Mal; injury treated as free person; finder's spending without court order is voluntary and non-reimbursable.
If the judge orders spending, it becomes a debt on the foundling.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of the Foundling (Kitab al-Laqit). It defines the laqit as an abandoned child, typically discarded due to poverty or fear of accusation. The ruling: one who loses such a child is sinful, while one who picks him up gains reward; taking him is better than leaving him. The term 'laqit' derives from the act of picking up (iltiqat). The ruling on picking up depends on location: it is recommended (mandub) in towns, but obligatory (wajib) in the wilderness because it saves the child's life. The author then states that the foundling is considered free and Muslim in all legal aspects: if someone slanders him (accuses him of illegitimate birth without proof), the hadd penalty for qadhf applies, because the default ruling for humans is freedom, and the land is Dar al-Islam, a land of free people. If the finder or another claims the child is his slave, the claim is invalid without evidence (bayyinah). The foundling's testimony after puberty is valid if he is upright. He cannot validly perform manumission (i'taq), tadbir (deferred manumission upon master's death), or kitabah (contract of manumission) because he is not a slave. His maintenance (nafaqah) is from the public treasury (Bayt al-Mal). Injury caused to him or by him is treated like injury to free people. The foundling is judged Muslim because he was found among Muslims, analogous to Muslim children. The passage then cites two narrations: one where Ali ruled a foundling free, and another where Umar said, 'Perhaps the Ghawayr [place] has misfortunes?' (a proverb implying suspicion that the child might be the finder's own), then declared the child free and that his maintenance is upon the state. Finally, if the finder spends his own money on the foundling without court authorization, he is a volunteer and cannot reclaim the expense unless the judge ordered him, making it a debt.

Key Terms
اللقيط (al-laqit): An abandoned human child discarded out of fear of poverty or to avoid accusation, picked up by someone.
الالتقاط (al-iltiqat): The act of picking up a foundling; recommended in towns, obligatory in wilderness.
بيت المال (Bayt al-Mal): The public treasury of the Muslims, responsible for the foundling's maintenance if he has no wealth or relatives.

Ruling on Foundling: Maintenance, Crime Liability, and Islam Status
Presents rulings on foundling and supports them with a companion report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings concerning a foundling and explain the basis for each ruling from the proof cited.

Core Idea
A foundling's maintenance is from Bayt al-Mal, he is free, and judged Muslim because of his environment.

In this lesson, we examine the concise rulings of Mukhtasar al-Quduri regarding an abandoned child (laqit). The text presents three key legal positions: maintenance from the public treasury, equal crime liability to a free person, and presumption of Islam. These rulings are supported by a proof from the practice of Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, which establishes the principle of communal responsibility and default religious status based on environment.

Identify the three rulings
Read the matn and list the three distinct rulings given for a foundling: maintenance source, crime liability, and religious status.
The rulings are stated briefly; the commentary expands on the maintenance ruling.
2
Understand the condition for maintenance
Note that the maintenance from Bayt al-Mal is conditional: the foundling must have no personal wealth and no relatives obligated to support him.
This condition is derived from the commentary, not the matn itself.
3
Analyze the proof

The phrase 'نفقته علينا' indicates obligation; 'وهو حر' establishes freedom.

Connect to underlying principles
Relate the ruling to broader Hanafi principles: inheritance going to Muslims and blood money liability (aql) justify the maintenance obligation.
The commentary explicitly makes this connection.

The Finder as Volunteer: Reimbursement Only by Judge's Stipulation
Ruling statement on finder's right to reimbursement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling that the finder is a volunteer and identify the only two ways he can reclaim expenses from the foundling.

Core Idea
The finder spends at his own risk unless the judge explicitly makes it a debt.

When someone finds a lost child and spends on him from his own pocket, Islamic law does not automatically give him a right to be reimbursed. The finder acts as a volunteer. In this passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, we learn the precise conditions under which he can reclaim those expenses—only a judge's explicit stipulation or the foundling's later confirmation creates a binding debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the first sentence: the finder is a volunteer (mutatawwi‘) and cannot reclaim. Understand that this is the baseline — no reimbursement without an external legal cause.
Volunteer = no right to compensation in Hanafi law.
2
Locate the exception
Find the phrase 'إلا أن يأمره القاضي' (unless the judge orders him). This is the first exception. But note: the commentary immediately adds that a mere order is not enough.
The order must be specific.
3
Distinguish a mere order from a debt stipulation
The commentary explains that a judge's absolute command may be for encouragement. Only if the judge explicitly says 'this is a debt on the foundling' does the finder gain the right to reclaim. This is the key condition.
Hanafi: the wording matters — 'dainan‘alayhi' is required.
4
Recognize the second exception
Even without a judge's order, if the foundling after majority (bulugh) confirms the expenses and agrees to repay, the finder can reclaim. This is a voluntary acknowledgment by the now-adult.
The foundling's verification (tasdiq) substitutes for the judge's stipulation.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn with commentary. It addresses the financial relationship between the finder (multaqit) and the foundling. The general rule is that if the finder voluntarily spends on the foundling from his own money, he is a volunteer (mutatawwi') and cannot reclaim the money from the foundling later. The reason is that he has no legal guardianship over the foundling to make such expenses binding. However, there is an exception: if the judge orders the finder to spend, and specifies that the expenditure is a loan (debt) on the foundling, then the finder can reclaim it. The commentary clarifies that a mere general order from the judge is not enough because it could be interpreted as encouragement, not a binding loan. Only an explicit condition makes it a debt. Additionally, if the judge did not order it but the foundling, after reaching adulthood, confirms the expenses and agrees to repay, then the finder can reclaim. The passage also addresses the situation where the finder refuses to continue maintenance. The judge can transfer the foundling to another trustworthy person if the finder provides evidence (bayyinah) that the child is truly a foundling. The condition of evidence is to ensure the child is not actually the finder's son or slave, for whom maintenance would be obligatory on the finder. If the finder later changes his mind and wants the child back, the judge has discretion—he may return the child to him or keep him with the other person.

Key Terms
متطوع (mutatawwi‘): A volunteer; one who does an act without legal obligation to do so and without right to compensation.
ديناً عليه (daynan ‘alayhi): A debt that becomes incumbent upon the foundling to repay, established by the judge's explicit stipulation.

Transferring the Foundling: Conditions and Judge's Discretion
Ruling statement on transfer of foundling when finder refuses to continue.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the procedure for transferring a foundling from a reluctant finder to a trustworthy person, including the evidentiary requirement and the judge's discretion.

Core Idea
The judge may transfer the foundling only upon evidence that he is a foundling, and retains discretion to return him.

What happens if the finder no longer wishes to care for the foundling? The Hanafi madhhab provides a clear procedure: the finder must request the judge to transfer the child, and the judge can only do so after the finder produces evidence that the child is truly a foundling—not his own son or slave. Even after transfer, the judge has the option to return the child to the original finder if he later changes his mind.

Guided Path
1
Read the scenario

The finder is not forced to continue caring.
2
Identify the condition for transfer
The judge may transfer to a trustworthy person (عدل) only if the finder produces evidence (بينة) that the child is a foundling. Why? Because the child might be the finder's own son or slave, for whom maintenance would be obligatory.
The evidence prevents the finder from abandoning his own dependents.
3
Recognize the judge's role
The judge is not obligated to transfer; he has discretion. He may transfer or keep the child with the trustworthy person based on what is best.
The judge's primary concern is the child's welfare.
4
Understand the finder's request to return
If the finder later returns and wants the child back, the judge has full discretion: he may return him to the finder or leave him with the trustee.
No automatic right of return; the judge evaluates the situation.

Priority of the Finder and Paternity Claims for a Foundling
States rulings on finder's priority and paternity claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the finder's priority over a foundling and apply the rule for accepting a paternity claim with an oath, distinguishing between cases where the finder claims paternity and when a third party does.

Core Idea
Finder's prior possession gives him custody unless a stronger right (like proven lineage) appears; a claimant's word is accepted with oath to benefit the child.

This lesson covers two interconnected rulings from the chapter on foundlings (al-taqatt). First, the finder's right to keep the foundling is protected by his prior possession. Second, if someone claims the child is his son, his statement is accepted if he swears an oath—but only if the finder himself is not also claiming paternity. Understanding these rules clarifies the balance between physical custody and biological lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal issues
The passage addresses: (1) the finder's right to retain custody of a foundling he has picked up, and (2) the procedure when a person claims the foundling is his son.
Both issues are sequential: first, who can hold the child; second, how lineage is established.

Core Idea
Intention transforms the finder into a usurper; only delivery to the owner discharges liability.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and Zufar regarding a finder who takes a luqata intending to eat or keep it. The key issue is whether returning the item to the original spot suffices to discharge liability. The majority argues that because the finder took for himself, he becomes a usurper (ghasib) and must return to the owner or agent. This shows the burden remains on the usurper until the item reaches the owner's hand.

If a finder returns the item to the original spot, Zufar says he is free from liability; the majority says he remains liable. The mufta bihi (operative fatwa) follows the majority view.

The Judge's Options for Found Livestock

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the three options a judge has for found livestock and EXPLAIN the finder's right to detain the animal until expenses are repaid.

Core Idea
A judge must preserve the owner's property; he may hire, sell, or fund short-term care of found livestock.

When a found animal is brought before a judge, he acts as a guardian for the absent owner. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the judge's three discretionary options based on the animal's benefit and expenses. Guided Path

The judge chooses the most beneficial course (aslah).
2
Understand the guardian role
The judge acts as a guardian (wasi) for the absent owner, with authority to make decisions to preserve the property. This is a duty (wajib) that stems from his position.
The judge's authority is limited to what is truly beneficial.

Before authorizing expenses, the judge must require proof from the finder that he is not a usurper. If no proof, the judge may still allow spending on the condition that the finder is truthful, but the finder bears the risk if he is a usurper.
This protects the owner from fraudulent claims.

Identify the finder's right to detain
When the owner appears, the finder may detain the animal until his expenses are repaid. If the animal is destroyed before detention, the debt remains; if after detention, it falls because the finder then holds it as a pledge (rahin).
The finder's right is analogous to a sale or pledge.

Compare the Haram ruling
The Hanafi school holds that luqata in the Haram (sanctuary of Mecca) is identical to elsewhere: the finder announces for one year and may then use it. This differs from the Shafi'i view that requires perpetual announcement.

Core Idea
When gender is unknown, tayammum replaces ghusl; shrouding follows the female pattern.

When a person dies before their gender becomes apparent, neither a man nor a woman may perform ghusl due to the prohibition of looking at the 'awrah of the opposite sex. This lesson explains the Hanafi solution—tayammum—and also covers shrouding, a child exception, and the permissibility of an alternative method proposed by Shams al-Aimma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
The deceased is a khuntha whose gender is unknown. Ghusl by either a man or a woman would risk looking at the opposite gender's 'awrah, which is prohibited. Therefore, ghusl cannot be performed.
2
Determine the primary ruling
Tayammum (dry purification) is performed instead of ghusl. This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school (zahir al-riwayah).
Tayammum is a valid substitute when water cannot be used due to prohibition.
3
Apply the conditions to tayammum
If the person performing tayammum is a non-mahram (e.g., an ajnabi), they must do so through a cloth to avoid direct touch. If the person is a mahram (close blood relative), they may perform tayammum without a cloth, as direct touch is permitted.
Mahram includes relatives with whom marriage is permanently prohibited.
4
Consider the child exception
If the deceased is a young child (not of an age to be desired), the gender ambiguity is irrelevant. A man or woman may wash the child without prohibition.
The ruling of tayammum applies only to adults who are sexually attractive.
5
Address the objection and alternative view

Rulings on the Missing Person (Al-Mafqud): Guardian Appointment and Legal Status
Defines al-mafqud, rules on guardian appointment, sale of property, and legal status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of a missing person, explain the obligation of appointing a guardian (nazir) based on qiyas, identify the limits of the nazir's authority, and apply the dual legal status of the missing person regarding inheritance and marriage.

Core Idea
Certainty preserves property and marriage; doubt blocks inheritance for the missing.

Imagine a person disappears without a trace—neither his location nor whether he is alive or dead is known. What happens to his property, debts, and marriage? The Hanafi school provides a comprehensive framework. This lesson explores the definition of the missing person (al-mafqud), the obligation of the qadi to appoint a guardian (nazir) to manage his affairs, and the nuanced legal status that balances certainty and doubt.

Guided Path
1
Define al-mafqud and identify the ruling
Start by understanding who qualifies as a missing person: one whose location and life/death status are unknown. Then note the ruling: the qadi must appoint a guardian (nazir) to protect and manage the missing person's wealth.
The condition for this ruling is the absence of knowledge, not mere absence.
2
Trace the proof (dalil): qiyas to the minor and insane
The author proves the obligation by analogy: just as the qadi appoints a guardian for a minor or insane person who cannot manage their own affairs, the same applies to the missing person who is similarly incapable. This is a clear analogy (qiyas jaliy).
The effective cause (illa) is incapacity to protect one's own property.

The nazir can collect debts, receive revenues, and litigate for debts arising from contracts. However, he cannot litigate for matters the missing person himself undertook or for shares in property held by others, because he is an agent from the judge, not a general deputy from the owner.
The limitation is significant: the nazir does not have full disposal rights.

If goods are likely to spoil (e.g., fruits), the judge orders their sale. If they are durable, they are not sold, even for maintenance, because they are safe as they are and the judge's authority is only to preserve, not to trade.
This preserves the missing person's wealth without unnecessary intervention.

According to al-Khujandi, the missing person is considered dead regarding his own inheritance (he does not inherit from others) but alive regarding others' rights (his property is not inherited, his wife remains married). The Missing Person's Dual Status: Dead for Himself, Alive for Others
Explains the dual legal status of the missing person with two intra-Hanafi views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi views on the legal status of the missing person and apply the principle of istishab to each.

Core Idea
Certainty is not overridden by doubt: the missing person is alive for others' rights, dead for his own benefits.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling regarding the missing person (mafqud). Al-Khujandi presents a dual status: the missing person is considered dead in matters that benefit him (like inheriting) and alive in matters that affect others (like his marriage and property). An alternative view reverses this. The reasoning relies on the usul principles of istishab (presumption of continuity) and the maxim that certainty is not removed by doubt. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply legal presumptions in cases of uncertainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two views
State the first view (al-Khujandi): dead for himself, alive for others. State the second view: alive for himself, dead for others.
The first view is more widely accepted (zahir riwayah).
2
Understand the reasoning for the first view
For 'dead for himself': inheritance requires certainty of life at the testator's death; doubt prevents it. For 'alive for others': property and marriage are established with certainty, and doubt does not terminate them.
Relies on the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk'.
3
Understand the reasoning for the second view
'Alive for himself': istishab al-hayah (presumption of life) preserves his ownership. 'Dead for others': no certainty of life when he would inherit from others.
This view applies istishab differently.
4
Apply the principle of istishab
Recognize that both views use istishab but apply it to different aspects: the first view preserves rights of others against him; the second preserves his own rights.
Istishab is the key tool in both views.
5
Evaluate the practical difference
The first view allows the wife to be considered married (she cannot remarry) and property intact, but he cannot inherit. The second view would allow his property to be inherited after a ruling of death, but he could inherit from others if he returns.
The mufta bihi is the first view, protecting his property and marriage.

This illustrates the principle that the finder's claim is a secured debt.

Reward Eligibility for Relatives and Spouses
Ruling on reward eligibility for relatives and spouses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reward eligibility for a mahram relative or spouse who returns a runaway slave, and identify when household status affects entitlement.

Core Idea
A relative in the master's household returns out of familial duty, not for reward.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies the Hanafi position on who is entitled to a reward (ja'l) for returning a runaway slave. The key factor is whether the returner is a mahram relative living in the master's household ('iyal). The ruling also covers children returning the father's slave and spouses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the returner's relationship
Determine if the returner is a mahram relative (e.g., brother, uncle) or a child/spouse of the master.
Mahram relatives are those with a permanent marriage prohibition.
2
Check residence status
If the returner is a mahram relative, ask: Is he in the master's household?
Household means those under the master's maintenance (nafaqa).
3
Apply the general rule
If in the household: no reward. If not in the household: reward is due.
4
Apply the special case for child returning parent's slave
A child returning the father's slave gets no reward, regardless of residence.
This extends to any descendant.
5
Apply the spouse rule
A wife or husband returning the other's slave also gets no reward.

Commentary
This ruling distinguishes between categories of relatives. A mahram relative (one with a permanent marriage prohibition) who lives in the master's household ('iyal) is presumed to act out of family duty, not for reward. If he does not live in the household, he is entitled to the reward. However, a son returning his father's slave gets no reward regardless, as does a spouse returning the other spouse's slave.
