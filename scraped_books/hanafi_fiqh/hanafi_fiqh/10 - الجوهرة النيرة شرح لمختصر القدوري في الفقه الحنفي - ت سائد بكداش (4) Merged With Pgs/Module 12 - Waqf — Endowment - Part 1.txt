Waqf: Definition and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership
Definition of waqf and intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of waqf and explain the key difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding whether ownership of the waqf founder ceases.

Core Idea
Waqf's bindingness is disputed: Abu Hanifa requires a judge's ruling; the Sahibayn hold it immediately binding.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah opens the book of Waqf with a definition and a foundational intra-Hanafi dispute. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for grasping the legal nature of charitable endowments in Hanafi fiqh. The difference hinges on whether the founder's ownership transfers to Allah upon declaration or only after a judicial decree.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
The passage gives two definitions: Abu Hanifa's (ownership remains with founder) and the Sahibayn's (ownership transfers to Allah). Compare the wording.
Both agree waqf means 'confinement of the asset', but differ on the legal consequence.
2
Pinpoint the core disagreement
The dispute is about whether the founder's ownership (milk) ceases immediately. For Abu Hanifa, it does not; for the Sahibayn, it does.
This leads to different rulings on bindingness and revocability.
3
Analyse the practical effect
Under Abu Hanifa, the waqf is revocable until a judge rules it binding; under the Sahibayn, it is immediately irrevocable (cannot be sold, pawned, or inherited).
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is significant for waqf administration.
4
Understand the judicial mechanism
The passage explains that for Abu Hanifa, a judge's ruling (of the ruler, not an arbitrator) is needed to make the waqf binding. The founder delivers the property to the mutawallī, then later retracts, and the judge rules for bindingness.
This procedural step is key to converting a non-binding waqf into a binding one in Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Assess the tarjih
Although the passage does not give tarjih, later Hanafi fatwa works (e.g., al-Hidayah) often prefer the Sahibayn's view as the operative position (mufta bihi).
Students should know which view is acted upon today, while respecting both as valid ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Waqf presents the definition of waqf and the well-known intra-Hanafi dispute. Linguistically, waqf means 'confinement' (habs). Legally, Abu Hanifa defines it as confining the physical asset (al-'ayn) while the ownership remains with the waqf founder (al-wāqif), and the benefit (manfa'ah) is given in charity, akin to a loan (ariyah). Thus, the waqf is not immediately binding (lāzim) in Abu Hanifa's view; the founder can revoke it unless a judge rules for its bindingness. In contrast, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) define it as confining the asset to the ownership of Allah Almighty, so the founder's ownership ceases to Allah, making it immediately binding, non-revocable, and immune from sale, pawn, or inheritance. The author then clarifies that 'unless a judge rules it' means the ruler (al-mawlā), not an arbitrative judge (muhakkam), as the latter's ruling is not valid according to the leading scholars (mashāyikh). The practical method: the founder delivers the waqf to the administrator (mutawallī), then later seeks to revoke it claiming non-bindingness; they go to a judge who then rules it binding, thus transferring ownership in Abu Hanifa's view.

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): In Hanafi law: confining an asset; Abu Hanifa: ownership remains with founder; Sahibayn: ownership transfers to Allah.
الحبس (al-habs): Linguistic meaning: confinement; also a synonym for waqf in some contexts.
المتولي (al-mutawallī): The administrator or trustee of the waqf property.

Definition of Waqf and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership
Defines waqf and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on when ownership ends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on when ownership of a waqf ceases and explain the practical legal consequences (thamarah) of each position.

Core Idea
Waqf bindingness depends on which imam's condition is met: judge's ruling, utterance, or delivery.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition of waqf (endowment) and the internal dispute among the three primary imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—on the precise moment the waqif's ownership ends. Understanding this dispute is crucial for determining when an endowment becomes irrevocable, how it can be revoked, and the rights of trustees and heirs.

Guided Path
1
Definition of Waqf
Contrast the two Hanafi definitions (Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn). Identify the key difference: under whose ownership is the corpus detained?
Abu Hanifa: waqf is detention under waqif's ownership; Sahibayn: under Allah's ownership.
2
Locate the Three Positions
Extract the conditions for bindingness from each imam: Abu Hanifa (judge's ruling), Abu Yusuf (utterance), Muhammad (appointment of guardian + delivery).
The text explicitly states each position.
3
Identify the Proof
Recognize the analogical reasoning (qiyas) used by each imam: Abu Yusuf likens waqf to manumission (immediate), Muhammad to gift (requires delivery), Abu Hanifa focuses on the possibility of revocation before court involvement.
The proof is based on qiyas, not textual evidence.
4
Examine the Thamarah
Determine the practical outcome: when can the waqif revoke? What are the implications for heirs and trustees?
According to Abu Hanifa, revocation is possible until a judge rules; according to Muhammad, until delivery; according to Abu Yusuf, never after utterance.
5
Apply the Mufta Bihi
Note that the fatwa follows Abu Yusuf on ownership leaving and Muhammad on dismissal of trustee without stipulation.
The text explicitly mentions 'وعليه الفتوى' for these positions.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature and bindingness of waqf (endowment). The commentator first gives the linguistic and legal definitions, noting two positions. Abu Hanifa defines waqf as detaining the corpus under the waqif's ownership while giving the usufruct as charity, likening it to a loan (ariyah). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) define it as detaining the corpus under Allah's ownership, so the waqif's ownership ceases and the endowment becomes binding (lazim) and cannot be sold or inherited. The text then explains the condition for the waqf to leave the waqif's ownership. According to Abu Hanifa, ownership does not leave until a judge issues a ruling; the waqif remains owner and can revoke it. The procedure: the waqif hands it to a trustee, then tries to reclaim it, and the judge rules it binding. This applies to the ruling judge (al-hakim), not an appointed arbitrator (al-muhakkam) according to the correct view. The passage then discusses attaching the waqf to death (ta'liq bi mawt), which is valid and treated as a will (wasiyyah), thus limited to one-third of the estate. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership leaves immediately upon uttering the waqf, analogizing it to manumission ('itq), and this is the relied-upon fatwa. Muhammad holds that ownership does not leave until a guardian (wali) is appointed and possession is given, analogizing it to a gift (hibah) which requires delivery. The text also addresses whether the waqif can dismiss the appointed trustee: if he stipulated the right to dismiss in the waqf deed, he may; otherwise, Muhammad says it is not valid, and this is the fatwa, while Abu Yusuf allows dismissal during the waqif's lifetime. The passage thus provides a comprehensive view of the Hanafi positions on the requirements for a valid and binding waqf.

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Detaining a specific corpus (usufruct) and giving its benefit as charity, with the corpus itself remaining unsold and uninherited.
الحكم (al-hukm): A judicial ruling by a qualified judge (mawlā) that makes the waqf binding, according to Abu Hanifa.
المتولي (al-mutawalli): The trustee or administrator appointed to manage the waqf property.

Effect of Valid Waqf on Ownership and Trustee Authority
Explains ruling on ownership and trustee authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two effects of a valid waqf on ownership and trustee authority, and explain the exception for executorship.

Core Idea
A valid waqf removes property from the donor's ownership without transferring it to the beneficiary.

Understanding the legal effects of a valid waqf is essential for both donors and trustees. The Hanafi school holds that once a waqf is properly established, the endowed property ceases to be owned by the donor, yet it does not become the property of the beneficiary. This unique status preserves the perpetual nature of the endowment. Additionally, the authority of a trustee appointed by the donor is tied to the donor's life; after death, the trustee's role ends unless special provision is made.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'When the endowment is established, it leaves the ownership of the donor and does not enter the ownership of the beneficiary.' This is the core principle.
Notice the double negation: neither donor nor beneficiary owns it.
2
Understand the consequence for the donor
The passage gives an example: if the donor owned slaves and later freed them after establishing the waqf, the freeing is invalid because the slaves were no longer his property.
This shows that the waqf immediately removes ownership rights.
3
Understand why the beneficiary does not become owner
If the beneficiary owned the property, he could sell it like any other property, which would defeat the purpose of the perpetual endowment.
This explains the rationale behind the second part.
4
Identify the ruling on trustee authority
The passage states that upon the donor's death, the trustee's (qayyim) authority ends because the trustee is like a representative (wakil).
Representation ends with the principal's death.
5
Note the exception
If the donor explicitly appointed the person as trustee both during his life and after his death, then that person becomes an executor (wasi) and continues to manage the waqf.
This requires a clear appointment in the waqf deed or will.

Waqf in Health vs Death Illness: From All Wealth or One Third
Ruling on waqf during health vs death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for waqf made during health and during death illness, and explain why death-illness waqf is limited to one third.

Core Idea
A waqf in health consumes the entire property; a waqf in death illness is limited to one third, like a will.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi waqf law. The ruling depends on the endower's state: a healthy person may endow as much as they wish from their wealth, but a person in death illness (marad al-mawt) is restricted to one third. This is because donations made during death illness are treated as wills (wasiyyah), which Islamic law limits to one third of the estate to protect the rights of legal heirs. Understanding this distinction is essential for proper estate planning and avoiding disputes after death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct situations: (1) waqf made during health (fi sihhatihi), and (2) waqf made during death illness (fi marad al-mawt). Note the Arabic terms used.
Health and death illness are the only conditions discussed here.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For health: the waqf is valid from all the wealth (min jami' al-mal). For death illness: the waqf is valid only from one third (min al-thuluth).
The phrase 'k al-hibah' (like a gift) indicates the legal treatment is identical to a gift during death illness.
3
Understand the rationale
In Hanafi fiqh, any donation (including waqf) made during a death illness is treated as a will (wasiyyah) because the endower is considered to be in a state where his actions may affect the rights of heirs. The one-third rule, derived from the hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, limits bequests to one third of the estate to prevent deprivation of heirs.
This rationale is not explicit in the passage but is the basis for the ruling.
4
Apply to a hypothetical
Consider a person who endows his entire house during a terminal illness. After death, heirs object. Apply the ruling: only one third of the house's value is bound by the waqf; the rest remains part of the estate.
If the house exceeds one third, the excess is subject to heirs' consent.

Core Idea

This passage from al-Waqiat presents a nuanced ruling that hinges on the difference between making waqf conditional and making the appointment of an agent conditional. Waqf requires immediate and unconditional transfer of ownership to Allah. Attaching a condition like death suspends the transfer, making it resemble a will. Agency is a flexible contract in Hanafi fiqh, unlike waqf which has strict perpetuity conditions.

Core Idea
Waqf must be unconditional and immediate; a condition suspending it invalidates, but a condition on agency is fine.

This short passage from Al-Waqi'at illustrates a critical distinction in Hanafi waqf law. A dying man's words matter: if he says 'I have made it a waqf if I die,' the waqf is invalid; but if he says 'make it a waqf if I die,' it is valid. The difference hinges on whether the condition attaches to the waqf itself or to the appointment of an agent. Let's parse the ruling step by step.

Guided Path
1
