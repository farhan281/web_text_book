Obligation to Produce the Principal
Ruling on the guarantor's obligation to produce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the guarantor's duty to produce the principal and explain the legal consequences of non-compliance.

Core Idea
The guarantor must produce the principal; failure with ability leads to imprisonment.

In kafalah bi al-nafs, the guarantor voluntarily undertakes to ensure the principal's attendance. The law backs this commitment with judicial enforcement. This lesson examines the obligation and its sanctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is producing the principal (ihdar al-makful bih). The text states it is binding (lazim) on the guarantor.
2
Determine the consequence of non-compliance
If the guarantor is able but refuses, the judge first orders him; if he still fails, imprisonment is imposed.
Imprisonment is a form of legal compulsion, not punishment.
3
Understand the legal basis
The obligation arises because attendance was incumbent on the principal, and the guarantor voluntarily undertook it.
The guarantor steps into the principal's duty.
4
Know the school's position
The Hanafi school holds imprisonment as permissible and mufta bihi.
Some other schools may not allow imprisonment in this context.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the rulings of kafalah bi al-nafs. The commentator first clarifies the obligation of the guarantor: he must produce the principal (al-makful bih). If he is able but fails, the judge orders him to produce the person; if he still refuses, the judge can imprison him because attendance is a right that the guarantor has undertaken. Next, the commentator explains the valid wordings for concluding the contract. The contract is concluded if the guarantor says 'I guarantee (takaffaltu) the person of so-and-so,' or uses terms like 'his neck,' 'his spirit,' 'his body,' 'his head,' 'his face,' or 'his body' because these refer to the entire person. Similarly, saying 'his half' or 'his third' is valid because the self is indivisible, so mentioning a part is like mentioning the whole. However, saying 'I am a guarantor for his acquaintance' is invalid because 'acquaintance' is not a concrete subject of guarantee.

Valid Wordings of Kafalah
Valid and invalid wordings for kafalah contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify expressions of guarantee as valid or invalid based on Hanafi principles.

Core Idea
A guarantee is concluded by words that refer to the whole person; mentioning a part like hand is invalid, but half/third is valid due to indivisibility.

The Hanafi school specifies clear verbal formulas to ensure the guarantee is properly undertaken. This lesson explores the reasoning behind valid and invalid expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the valid wordings
Wordings like 'takaffaltu bi-nafsi fulan', 'bi-raqabatihi', 'bi-ruhihi', etc., are valid because they refer to the entire person.
These terms are understood as referring to the whole body.
2
Apply the principle of indivisibility
The self (nafs) is not divisible; mentioning a part that implies the whole (like 'his half') is valid.
This is based on the legal maxim that the self cannot be divided.
3
Distinguish between parts
'His head' or 'his face' are valid because they imply the whole person, while 'his hand' or 'his leg' are invalid because they do not.
The key is whether the part is commonly used to refer to the whole.
4
Handle self-reference
If the guarantor says 'my half' (attributing to himself), it is invalid because it does not refer to the principal.
The guarantee must be about the principal, not the guarantor.
5
Recognize other acceptable formulas
Phrases like 'damintuhu', 'huwa 'alayya', 'ana za'im', 'ana kafil', 'ana qabil' are valid; 'ana damin bi ma'rifatihi' is void.
The formula must clearly indicate guarantee of the person.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Charity to Two Rich Persons
Intra-Hanafi dispute on charity/gift to two rich persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute regarding giving charity or a gift to two rich persons, and identify the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).

Core Idea
Charity is for Allah (one); a gift is for the donee (two) – intention determines validity when giving to two rich persons.

This passage from Al-Jami' al-Saghir presents a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The issue is whether giving a single charity or gift to two rich persons is valid. The difference hinges on the underlying intention: charity is solely for the sake of Allah, while a gift is directed toward the recipient. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to distinguish between legal categories based on intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the base text
The matn (Al-Jami' al-Saghir) states that giving charity or a gift to two poor persons is valid, but to two rich persons is not valid. Note that the passage says 'for rich persons also' referring to the Sahibayn's view.
2
State the two opposing views
Abu Hanifa holds it is not valid (la yajuz); Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold it is valid (yajuz).
3
Analyze the reasoning of each side
The Sahibayn argue that both charity and gift are transfers without compensation (tamlik bi-ghayr badal), so no distinction. Abu Hanifa distinguishes by intention: charity is for Allah (one), gift is for the donee (two). Splitting charity between two recipients contradicts divine unity.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning is based on the concept of niyyah (intention) and tawhid (unity of Allah).
4
Understand the practical consequence
In practice, giving a lump sum to two rich persons is fasid according to Abu Hanifa (unless split into two separate acts), while the Sahibayn consider it valid.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The commentator says 'al-sahih' (the correct view) is Abu Hanifa's, so that is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Al-Khujandi also affirms Abu Hanifa's view.

Conditions of Valid Sadaqah and the Exception for Undivided Shares
Ruling statement on sadaqah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the validity conditions of sadaqah and identify when an undivided share (musha') is permissible.

Core Idea
Sadaqah requires possession; undivided shares are invalid except to multiple poor recipients.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra clarifies how sadaqah (charity) parallels hibah (gift) in requiring possession and prohibiting undivided shares, but adds a special relaxation for the poor. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly executing charitable gifts in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary validity condition
Note the first statement: sadaqah is not valid without possession (qabd), just like hibah. This means the recipient must take physical or constructive possession.
Possession is a common condition for all voluntary transfers.
2
Apply the prohibition of undivided shares
Sadaqah in an undivided share (musha') that can be divided is invalid. This applies when the donee is not poor (e.g., rich individuals).
The rationale is that sadaqah is a donation; undivided shares create uncertainty.
3
Recognize the exception for the poor
If sadaqah is given to two poor people jointly in a single undivided item, it is valid. This relaxation does not apply to hibah.
The text says 'idha tasaddaqa 'ala faqirayni bi shay'in: jaaz'.
4
Contrast with hibah
Recall from earlier discussion that hibah to two rich people in an undivided share is invalid. The difference here is the status of the recipients.
This highlights the Hanafi principle of easing charity for the needy.

Sadaqah Requires Possession and Exception for Poor Recipients
Ruling on sadaqah's similarity to hibah regarding qabd and mushā'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of sadaqah regarding possession and undivided shares, and apply the exception for poor recipients.

Core Idea
A charitable gift requires possession; giving an undivided divisible share is invalid unless the recipient is poor.

Sadaqah (charitable gift) shares many rulings with hibah (gift), including the requirement of possession (qabd) for validity. However, there is an important exception: sadaqah of an undivided divisible share is valid if given to the poor, even though the same gift would be invalid if given to the rich. This leniency facilitates charity for those in need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is sadaqah (charitable gift). The general ruling is that it is not valid except with possession (qabd), because it is a gratuitous transfer (tabarru') like hibah.
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand the proof (dalil)
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to hibah: both are tabarru' transactions, so possession is required. No explicit text is cited.
The analogy is based on the common cause (illah) of gratuity.
3
Analyze the condition of divisible undivided shares (mushā')
Sadaqah of a divisible undivided share is not valid, analogous to hibah. The example: giving such a share to two rich persons is invalid.
This applies when the property can be divided.
4
Note the exception for poor recipients
If the recipients are poor, the sadaqah of a divisible undivided share is valid, unlike hibah. This is a concession to encourage charity.
The exception is based on need (ḥājah).
5
Apply the rule to practical scenarios
Test your understanding: a man gives his undivided share in a field to two rich brothers (invalid) vs. to two poor families (valid). Also, any sadaqah without possession is invalid.
Possession can be constructive in some cases, but physical delivery is typical.

Major and Minor Defects of a Slave for Kaffarah
Listing major and minor defects for kaffarah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the major and minor defects of a slave according to Hanafi fiqh for the validity of kaffarah, and apply the rule to new cases.

Core Idea
A slave with major defects (e.g., missing both hands) is invalid for kaffarah; minor defects (e.g., missing one hand) are acceptable.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra lists the physical conditions of a slave that affect his suitability for expiation (kaffarah). The general principle is that the slave must be capable of providing benefit; major defects that severely impair utility render the freeing invalid, while minor defects do not. You will learn to recognize these defects and the intra-school difference on deafness.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general principle
The slave must be capable of providing benefit (manfa'ah). Defects that substantially impair this capability are major and invalidate the kaffarah; defects that only partially impair are minor and do not invalidate.
This is the underlying rationale for the list.
2
Memorize the list of major defects (invalid)
Review the first list in the passage: both hands/feet cut off, hemiplegia, paralyzed both hands, chronic illness, one hand and one foot from same side, both thumbs cut off, three fingers from each hand (excluding thumbs), blindness, insanity, muteness.
These defects make the slave practically unable to work.
3
Memorize the list of minor defects (valid)
Review the second list: missing one hand or one foot, hand and foot from opposite sides, one paralyzed hand, two fingers from each hand (excluding thumbs), one-eyed, weak sight, missing ears, missing nose, impotence, castration, amputated penis, hermaphrodite, female with blocked vagina, bone deformity.
These defects do not severely impair overall utility.
4
Note the intra-school difference on deafness
The apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) considers deafness a minor defect and allows the freeing. However, another narration says if the slave is totally deaf (cannot hear even loud sounds), it is invalid.
The relied-upon position is the apparent narration.
5
Apply to new cases
Given a specific defect, classify it as major or minor by comparing to the lists. If a slave has any major defect, the freeing is invalid. If only minor defects, it is valid.
Multiple minor defects do not combine to become major.

Commentary
This passage details the physical conditions of a slave that affect his suitability for kaffarah. The general principle is that the slave must be capable of providing benefit (manfa'ah). Severe defects that completely or substantially impair the slave's ability to work (e.g., missing both hands, hemiplegia, blindness, insanity) render the freeing invalid for expiation because the slave is considered 'damaged' (ma'yub) to the extent that he resembles a dead person in terms of utility. Minor defects that do not severely impede utility (e.g., missing one hand, squint, impotence) do not prevent validity. The case of deafness is disputed within the school: the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) allows it, but some scholars (qil) reject it if the deafness is total (cannot hear even loud sounds). The list is exhaustive and based on the judgments of Imam Abu Hanifa.

عنِّين (innin): Impotent male (cannot engage in sexual intercourse).
رتقاء (ratqa'): A female slave with a closed or blocked vagina (cannot engage in intercourse).

Freeing a slave for compensation and by a sick person
Conditions: freeing must be without compensation; freeing by a sick person has additional rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that invalidate freeing a slave for kaffarah and apply them to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Freeing for kaffarah must be purely for Allah, without compensation, and if done in terminal illness, within one-third of the estate.

This lesson examines two critical conditions for the validity of freeing a slave as expiation (kaffarah). The Hanafi school insists that the freeing must be entirely for Allah, without any compensation, and if performed during a terminal illness, must not exceed one-third of the estate. Understanding these conditions prevents common errors in fulfilling kaffarah obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is freeing a slave as a form of kaffarah (expiation). It is an act of worship with specific conditions for validity.
Kaffarah is required for certain violations like breaking an oath or zihar.
2
Recognize the first condition: no compensation
The freeing must be without any compensation (badal). If the master frees the slave on condition of payment, even if later forgiven, the freeing is invalid for kaffarah.
Compensation includes any price or service agreed upon.
3
Recognize the second condition: terminal illness limit
If the master is terminally ill (marad al-mawt), the freeing must not exceed one-third of his estate. If it does and he dies, the freeing is invalid for kaffarah, even if the heirs consent.
However, if the master recovers, the freeing becomes valid.
4
Understand the consequence of violation
Violating either condition makes the freeing batil (invalid) for kaffarah. Subsequent acts like forgiveness of debt or heir consent do not cure the defect.
The ruling is clear: the act remains invalid for its original purpose.
5
Note the exceptional case: recovery
If the sick person recovers before death, the restriction of one-third no longer applies, and the freeing becomes valid for kaffarah.
Recovery removes the 'terminal illness' condition retroactively.

Commentary
This passage covers two conditions: (1) The freeing must be without compensation (ghayr badal). If the slave is freed on the condition of payment (even if later forgiven), it is not valid for kaffarah because the freeing is not purely for Allah. (2) A person who is terminally ill (marad al-mawt) and frees a slave that exceeds one-third of his estate: if he dies, the freeing is not valid for kaffarah because it is subject to the rules of bequest (wasiyyah) which are limited to one-third. Even if the heirs consent, the freeing does not count as kaffarah because the act was originally defective. However, if he recovers, the freeing becomes valid retroactively because the illness no longer restricts transactions.

Key Terms
بدل (badal): Compensation or price paid for the slave's freedom.
ثلث المال (thuluth al-mal): One-third of the estate; limit for bequests in terminal illness.

The Condition of Fasting Before Touch in Zihar Expiation
Gloss on matn regarding fasting expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that fasting two consecutive months in zihar expiation must be completed before the spouses have physical contact (tamass).

Core Idea
The fasting expiation for zihar is not valid unless completed before any intimacy.

This gloss from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies a key condition in the Hanafi ruling on zihar expiation. When a person cannot free a slave, they must fast two consecutive months. However, this fast must be fulfilled before the spouses engage in any sexual contact; otherwise, the expiation must be repeated. This ensures the seriousness of the zihar prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Condition
The matn states the ruling: if he does not find a slave, then fasting two consecutive months. The gloss adds 'before they touch one another', which is a condition on the fasting.
2
Understand the Condition's Legal Effect
This condition is a shart (condition) for the validity of the expiation. If physical contact occurs before completing the fast, the expiation is not fulfilled and must be restarted.
3
Recognize the Source of the Condition
The condition is derived from the Hanafi interpretation of the Quranic verses on zihar (58:3-4), requiring expiation before returning to normal marital relations.

This is a brief gloss (probably from the sharh or a marginal note) on the matn phrase: 'فإن لم يجد فصيام شهرين متتابعين' (for if he does not find [a slave], then fasting two consecutive months). The added phrase 'من قبل أن يتماساً' (before they touch one another) indicates that this fasting is required before the spouses engage in intercourse; otherwise, they would have to start over. This condition is well-known in the Hanafi school: the expiation must be performed before any physical intimacy after the declaration of zihar. This gloss simply reminds the reader of that condition from the matn.
