Learning Objective

Core Idea
A conditional 'if you pay' is limited to the sitting; payment in that session frees the slave.

This passage addresses a common conditional phrase used in manumission. The particle 'in' (if) imposes an immediate restriction. We will explore the legal effect and the master's obligation to accept payment if tendered in the sitting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional phrase
The master says 'If you pay me a thousand, then you are free' using the particle 'in' (if). This particle indicates a condition that must be fulfilled.
2
Determine the temporal scope
The text says 'this is restricted to the sitting.' The condition must be fulfilled in the same contractual session (majlis).
3
Apply the condition
If the slave pays in that sitting, he becomes free immediately upon payment.
4
Consider the master's refusal
If the master refuses to accept the payment, the law compels him to accept. The slave's right to freedom is not defeated by the master's obstinacy.
This shows the Hanafi emphasis on enforcing conditions fairly.

Freeing by Giving Unrestricted Access to Money
Ruling on freeing by handing over money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tacit freeing (ʿitq bi al-dalāla) when a master gives a slave unrestricted access to money, and identify the key condition that triggers the freeing.

Core Idea
If the master gives the slave unrestricted access to money, the slave is freed immediately — even without taking the money.

This short ruling may seem simple, but it establishes an important Hanafi principle: that freeing can occur through implicit action when the master's conduct clearly indicates an intention to free. The key is the master's act of letting go of control, not the slave's acceptance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is the master giving the slave unrestricted access to money (takhliya). The legal consequence is that the slave becomes free.
This is a form of implied freeing, not an explicit declaration.
2
Determine the nature of the act
The master's action is a type of 'tacit freeing' (ʿitq bi al-dalāla) because his conduct reveals his intention to relinquish ownership.
The Hanafi school recognizes such conduct as evidence of freeing.
3
Identify the condition for freeing
The condition is the master's act of 'letting him have access' (takhliya), not the slave's acceptance. Even if the slave does not take the money, the freeing is effective.
This contrasts with any potential requirement of possession.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
If another school required actual possession to confirm freeing, the ruling would differ. In the Hanafi school, the master's act alone suffices.
The practical effect is that the slave's status changes immediately.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa rules sharecropping for a fixed share invalid—uncertainty in the crop's value voids the contract.

This lesson begins the Book of Sharecropping (Muzāraʿa). We will first define what muzāraʿa means in language and law, and then encounter Abu Hanifa's famous ruling that a sharecropping contract for a fixed fraction of the crop (e.g., one-third or one-fourth) is invalid. The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Muzara'ah: From Invalidity to Permissibility by Need
Intra-Hanafi dispute on crop-sharing validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on muzara'ah, explain the proof for each, and identify the mufta bihi (operative fatwa).

Core Idea
Muzara'ah is invalid by analogy but permissible by need – the Hanafi fatwa follows the Sahibayn.

Muzara'ah, or crop-sharing, is a vital contract for agricultural societies where cash is scarce. Yet the Hanafi school witnessed an internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa deemed it invalid due to uncertainty, while his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allowed it based on necessity. This lesson unpacks the arguments and the final fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa's view)
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling that muzara'ah for a share (e.g., 1/3 or 1/4) is batil/fasid. Note the consequences: if the contract is fasid and no crop is produced, the worker gets an equivalent wage; if seed is from the worker, the produce belongs to him and landowner gets rent.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold the opposite view: muzara'ah is ja'iz (permissible). Their position is the mufta bihi.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
Examine the dalil: analogical reasoning (qiyas) to the prohibited muhaqalah and muzabanah, and to hiring for an unknown or non-existent wage (e.g., miller's qafiz, grazing sheep for offspring). The wage (share of produce) is unknown or non-existent at contract time.
The hadith 'He forbade muhaqalah and muzabanah' is cited as evidence.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's response
Understand their istihsan based on need (hajah): people rely on crop-sharing when cash wages are unavailable. Necessity overrides the analogical objection. Also, the Prophet's contract with Khaybar is cited as precedent.
The text states 'upon it is the fatwa' (wa 'alayhi al-fatwa).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical difference: under the mufta bihi (Sahibayn), muzara'ah contracts are valid and enforceable; under Abu Hanifa, the worker claims wages and produce belongs to seed owner. The tarjih (preference) is given to Sahibayn due to overwhelming need.

Explanation of the first valid form with analogical proof

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tools subsidiary to labor, like a tailors needle, do not create independent hire.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) recognized four valid forms of muzara'ah. The first form is where the land and seed come from the owner, and the labor and oxen come from the worker. This lesson examines this form and the legal reasoning (qiyas) that upholds its validity, addressing a common objection about the inclusion of the oxen.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contributions
Note who provides what: owner gives land and seed; worker gives labor and oxen.
This is the first of four forms.
2
State the ruling
The contract is ja'iz (permissible) according to the Sahibayn.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Recognize the objection

Understand the analogy
The text compares the oxen to a tailor's needle: when hiring a tailor, the needle is subsidiary to the labor, not separately hired.
The illa (effective cause) is that the tool is subsidiary to the work.

Since the oxen are tools subsidiary to the worker's labor, they do not require separate compensation; thus the contract remains valid.
This qiyas is jali (evident) and establishes the ruling.

The Four Forms of Muzara'ah: Valid and Invalid According to the Sahibayn
Explains four types of muzara'ah contract with conditions and rulings using qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four faces of muzara'ah according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, state which are valid and invalid, and explain the reasoning using qiyas.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Post-ripening expenses before division are shared; custom invalidates contrary stipulations.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies how expenses in sharecropping (muzara'ah) are allocated between landowner and worker. It distinguishes between expenses incurred before division and after division, and extends the same logic to irrigation contracts (musaqah). Custom (urf) plays a key role: any condition that contradicts established custom renders the contract invalid by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the temporal categories
The passage divides expenses into two periods: before division (qabl al-qisma) and after division (ba'd al-qisma). For musaqah, before fruit ripens (qabl idrak al-thamar) and after ripens (ba'dahu). Identify these categories in the text.
2
State the default expense rule for each period
According to zahir al-riwayah, expenses before division (e.g., harvesting, threshing) are shared between landowner and worker. After division (e.g., carrying, storing), each bears their own. For musaqah, work before ripening is on the worker; after ripening is shared.
This is the baseline rule.

Custom (urf) is the basis for this allocation. Stipulations that depart from custom are invalid. The passage gives two examples: stipulating picking (jidadh) on the worker in musaqah, and stipulating harvesting (hasad) on the landowner in muzara'ah. Both are invalid because no custom supports them.

The text says the first stipulation is invalid 'by agreement' (bil-ittifaq) and the second 'by consensus' (bil-ijma'). Both mean the stipulation is not permissible and renders the contract defective (fasid).
The terms indicate the strength of the ruling.

Apply the analogy to musaqah
The author explicitly extends the muzara'ah rule to musaqah by qiyas. The underlying rationale is the same: the time of ripening is the dividing point for responsibility. Practice applying this to hypothetical scenarios.
The analogy is based on illa of maturity.

A note on multiple declarations: if a man declares zihar multiple times in one sitting, he owes expiation for each declaration because each is a separate oath; but if he claims they were in separate sittings, he is not believed (i.e., he is treated as having made multiple declarations). The commentary explains that comparing the wife to a body part that is permissible to view does not constitute zihar. The passage records a disagreement: Al-Sha'bi restricts zihar to comparison with the mother, while Imam Malik permits it with any non-mahram woman (ajnabiyyah). The Hanafi position is that zihar is established by comparing the wife to any permanently forbidden relative, not just the mother. Further, if the husband says 'you are to me like the back of your mother' (i.e., the wife's own mother), that is zihar because the wife's mother is his mahram by marriage. If he says 'like the back of your daughter' (i.e., the wife's daughter from another marriage), it is zihar only if he has consummated the marriage with his wife (making the daughter his step-daughter and permanently forbidden). Similarly, comparing to a son's wife (daughter-in-law) or father's wife (step-mother) is zihar because they are permanently forbidden. Lastly, a unique view of Abu Yusuf: if the husband compares his wife to a woman with whose mother he committed adultery, or to a woman with whom his father committed adultery, those women become permanently forbidden to him due to the zina, so the comparison constitutes zihar. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that zihar is tied to permanent prohibition, not merely to consanguinity.

مظاهر (muzahir): A man who has declared zihar upon his wife, thereby triggering the prohibition and obligation of expiation.
محارم (mahram): Relatives whom one is permanently forbidden to marry due to blood, marriage, or fosterage.

Intra-Hanafi Disputes on Freeing a Slave for Zihar Kaffarah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on freeing a slave for kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a valid manumission as kaffarah for zihar and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn, and Imam al-Shafi'i on partial ownership and intention disputes.

Core Idea
A slave freed for kaffarah must be fully owned and freed with intention; partial or joint ownership raises school disputes.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra examines the requirements for freeing a slave as expiation for zihar. It clarifies what constitutes a valid 'complete slave' (raqabah kamilah) and explores internal Hanafi disagreements between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, as well as a cross-madhhab difference with Imam al-Shafi'i on the timing of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary conditions
The author states three conditions: full ownership (raqabah kāmilah), intention (niyyah) accompanying the freeing, and the slave being a source of lasting benefit without exchange. Also, the slave must not be a mahram relative entering ownership without action (by consensus).
These conditions are derived from the phrase 'رقبة كاملة الرق في ملكه، مقروناً بنية الكفارة'.
2
Analyze the first dispute: freeing half a slave
Abu Hanifa distinguishes between freeing the second half before cohabitation (valid) and after cohabitation (invalid). The Sahibayn treat freeing half as equivalent to freeing the whole because manumission is indivisible for them, so it is valid even after cohabitation.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf based on the nature of manumission.
3
Analyze the second dispute: jointly owned slave
Abu Hanifa invalidates freeing one's share of a jointly owned slave entirely due to the slave's obligation to work for the other owner (sa'āya). The Sahibayn allow it if the freeder is wealthy (since wealth prevents sa'āya) but disallow if poor.
Wealth vs. poverty determines whether the freeing is with compensation.
4
Examine the role of intention and mahram relatives
Intention must accompany the act of freeing; post-freeing intention is invalid. A mahram relative entering ownership by inheritance is not valid for kaffarah by consensus. However, if entering by one's own action (e.g., purchase), it is valid with intention at the time of action according to Hanafis.
This introduces the cross-madhhab dispute with al-Shafi'i.
5
Understand the Shafi'i dispute on acquired slave
Al-Shafi'i holds that a slave acquired by one's own action (e.g., purchase) cannot be used for kaffarah even with intention at acquisition. The Hanafi position is that it is valid because the slave is fully owned by the time of freeing, and the intention at acquisition suffices.
The practical difference affects planning: Hanafis may intend at purchase; Shafi'is require pre-existing ownership.

Conditions for the Slave in Zihar Expiation
Expanding on permissible defects for slaves freed in zihar expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a slave to be validly freed in zihar expiation, identify defects allowed by istihsan, and explain why the Hanafi school prefers istihsan over qiyas.

Core Idea
A slave for zihar expiation must be capable of utility; istihsan permits certain defects that analogy would forbid.

In zihar expiation, freeing a slave is a required act. However, not any slave suffices. The Hanafi madhhab lays out specific conditions regarding the slave's physical and legal status. This lesson unpacks those conditions, showing how the school uses istihsan to allow some defects while rejecting others based on loss of utility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The default requirement is that the slave must be free of major defects that eliminate utility, such as blindness or missing both hands/feet.
The matn states these as impermissible.
2
Understand the role of istihsan
Despite qiyas (analogy) from zakat expiation requiring perfect soundness, the Hanafi school applies istihsan to allow some defects like deafness.

Distinguish permissible vs. impermissible defects

4
Recognize the status of ownership
The slave must be fully owned. Slaves with incomplete ownership (mudabbar, umm walad, mukatab who has paid some) are invalid because their emancipation is already restricted.
A mukatab who has paid nothing is still fully a slave and valid.

Gathering All Expiation Food in One Day vs Distributing
Rulings on giving all sixty meals in one day or spreading over days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the distribution of expiation meals to various scenarios involving one-day or multi-day feeding.

Core Idea
Giving all sixty meals in one day only counts for one day; two separate meals per poor are required.

In expiation for zihar and other kaffarat, the Hanafi school requires feeding sixty poor with two satiating meals. This lesson clarifies a common question: can you give all the food in one day? The answer is no—each poor must receive two meals on separate occasions. The passage expands on the matn to illustrate how different distribution methods are evaluated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
The matn states that giving the food of sixty poor in one day only suffices for that one day. Understand that the requirement is not a single meal per poor but two separate meals.
This is the foundational rule: one day's worth of feeding only covers one day of the kaffarat.
2
Understand the requirement of two meals per poor
The kaffarat requires each poor to receive two satiating meals. These meals must be given on two distinct occasions, not all at once.
The meals could be lunch and dinner on two different days or spaced over time.
3
Apply to feeding 120 at once
If someone feeds 120 poor at once (double the number), each poor receives only one meal. Therefore, he must feed one of the two groups another satiating meal.
The total quantity does not replace the condition of two meals per recipient.
4
Apply to lunch and dinner for different groups
If he feeds lunch to sixty poor and dinner to sixty different poor (total 120), each of the original sixty gets only one meal. He must feed one of the groups another meal.
The meals must be given to the same individual twice.
5
Apply to daily half sa' distribution
Giving half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates/barley per poor over sixty days suffices for that day's meal. This meets the requirement if each poor receives two such distributions.
This method ensures each poor gets two separate meals on different days.

Intercourse During Zihar Feeding and the Two-Zihar Dispute
Ruling on intercourse during feeding and dispute on feeding for two zihar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on intercourse during zihar feeding expiation and explain the Sahibayn’s position regarding two separate zihar expiations.

Core Idea
Intercourse during feeding does not restart the expiation; each zihar requires its own expiation.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies two distinct issues related to zihar expiation. First, it addresses a common practical question: if a man has intercourse with his wife while he is in the middle of feeding the poor for expiation, does he need to start over? The text clearly says no. Second, it presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether one distribution of sixty sa' of wheat can fulfill two separate zihar expiations. The relied-upon view is that it only covers one.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the ruling on intercourse during feeding
First, identify the act (intercourse during feeding) and the ruling: it does not require restarting the expiation. Note the reason: unlike freeing or fasting, the feeding verse does not contain the condition 'before they touch' (min qabli an yatamāssā). Then understand the prohibition: intercourse before completing expiation is discouraged because one might switch to freeing or fasting, which would then require restarting.
The key difference is the presence/absence of the 'before they touch' condition in the Quranic verses.
2
Identify the dispute on two zihar expiations
Focus on the scenario: a person gives sixty poor each one sa' of wheat intending it for two zihar expiations. State the positions: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) say it counts only for one; Muhammad alone says it suffices for both.
Each zihar is a separate legal cause (sabab) requiring its own kaffarah.
3
Evaluate the proofs and tarjih
Consider the dalil for each position: Sahibayn reason that each zihar is independent, so one distribution cannot cover two. Muhammad reasons that the quantity is double, so it covers both. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn, as indicated by the commentary.
The practical consequence: if a person gave sixty poor for two zihar, according to Sahibayn he must give another sixty for the second zihar.
