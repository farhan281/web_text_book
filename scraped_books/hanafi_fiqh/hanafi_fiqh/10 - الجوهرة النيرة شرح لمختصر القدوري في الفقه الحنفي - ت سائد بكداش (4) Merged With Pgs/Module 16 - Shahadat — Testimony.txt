Deposit Dispute: No Liability for Unspecified Contents
Ruling on liability and oath when depositor claims missing item not mentioned at deposit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the depositary's liability and the requirement of an oath when the depositor claims a missing item not mentioned at the time of deposit.

Core Idea
No liability for contents unknown; no oath without claim of wrongdoing.

This lesson examines a specific deposit dispute: a basket is deposited without specifying its contents. Later, the depositor claims a valuable tool is missing. The depositary admits the basket but denies knowledge of the tools. What is the ruling? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer grounded in the principle that liability arises only from an alleged act of negligence, not from a simple claim about the original state of the deposit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is depositing a basket containing unspecified tools. The ruling: the depositary is not liable for the missing tool.
Liability is negated because the depositary only undertook custody of the basket as a whole.
2
Understand the condition for liability
Liability in deposit (wadi'ah) requires the depositor to prove an act of wrongdoing (ṣunʿ) by the depositary.
A mere claim about the original contents does not constitute an accusation of wrongdoing.
3
Identify the ruling on the oath
The depositary is not required to take an oath because the depositor did not allege any specific wrongful act.
The phrase 'lam yadʿu ʿalayhi ṣunʿan' is key: no oath is needed without a claim of wrongdoing.
4
Contrast with a case where oath is required
If the depositor had accused the depositary of negligence or theft, the depositary would need to swear an oath to deny liability.
This contrast highlights the importance of the type of claim in determining procedural requirements.

رجل أودع رجلاً زنبيلاً فيه آلات النجارين، ثم جاء يسترده، وادعى أنه كان فيه قدوماً، فذهبت منه وقال المودع: قبضتُ منك الزنبيل ولا أدري ما فيه : لا ضمان عليه. وهل تجب عليه اليمين؟ قالوا: لا تجب عليه؛ لأنه لم يدع عليه صنعاً.
A man deposited with another a basket containing carpenter's tools. Then he came to reclaim it, and claimed that there was an adze (qadum) in it, and it was lost. The depositary said: I received from you the basket and I do not know what was in it: there is no liability upon him. And is an oath obligatory upon him? They said: not obligatory upon him, because he did not claim against him an act (of wrongdoing).
Source pages p. 163

Commentary
This is a ruling on a dispute over the contents of a deposit. The depositor claims the basket contained an adze (a tool) that is now missing. The depositary admits receiving the basket but denies knowledge of its contents. The depositary is not liable because he only undertook custody of the basket as a whole, not its specific contents. Furthermore, he is not required to take an oath because the depositor's claim is not about a specific wrongful act by the depositary, but about the original state of the deposit. The phrase 'لم يدع عليه صنعاً' means the depositor did not accuse him of negligence or wrongdoing, so no oath is needed.

Key Terms
زنبيل (zunbīl): A basket or container used for carrying items.
قدوم (qadūm): A carpenter's adze or axe.
صنع (ṣun'): An act (of wrongdoing or negligence).

Women's Testimony in Breastfeeding Cases: Hanafi Ruling
Rule on the admissibility of female witnesses in rada‘

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the admissibility of women's testimony alone in rada‘ cases and identify the general principle that male testimony is required.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, women's testimony alone is invalid for breastfeeding cases.

This lesson examines a concise yet important ruling from the Hanafi school regarding the admissibility of evidence in breastfeeding (rada‘) disputes. While women's testimony is accepted in many financial and property matters, this specific area requires male participation. Understanding this exception prevents procedural errors in family law cases.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Read the Arabic text and identify the exact legal statement: women's testimony alone is not accepted in rada‘.
The key phrase is 'لا تقبل' (is not accepted).
2
Identify the missing element
What is required for the testimony to be accepted? The text implies the presence of a male witness is necessary.
General Hanafi rules require either two men or one man and two women for matters like marriage, divorce, and lineage.
3
Connect to broader Hanafi principles
This ruling aligns with the principle that certain matters (like establishing permanent prohibition) require stronger evidence.
Breastfeeding creates a permanent mahram relationship, so the standard of proof is higher.
4
Apply to a scenario
Consider a case where a woman claims breastfeeding but only brings female witnesses. What is the outcome? The testimony is rejected.
The claimant must bring a male witness or one man and two women.

Source Text

ولا تقبل في الرضاع شهادة النساء منفردات،
And the testimony of women alone is not accepted in breastfeeding [cases].
Source pages p. 386

Commentary
This brief ruling states a procedural point: in cases involving breastfeeding (الرضاع), the testimony of women by themselves (without male witnesses) is not admissible. This is consistent with the general Hanafi rule that in certain matters like marriage, divorce, and lineage, the testimony of two women is accepted, but in others it is not. The specific scope is not elaborated here, but it indicates that rada‘ cases require either two men or a man and two women. The ruling guards against weak evidence in a matter that establishes permanent prohibition.

Key Terms
شهادة النساء منفردات (shahādat al-nisā’ munfaridāt): Testimony given by women alone, without any male witness; not accepted in rada‘ cases according to this text.
