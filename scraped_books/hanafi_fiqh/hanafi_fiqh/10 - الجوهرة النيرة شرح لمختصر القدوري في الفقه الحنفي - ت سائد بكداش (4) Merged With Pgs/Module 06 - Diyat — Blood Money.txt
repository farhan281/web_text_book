Sulh in Injury Cases and Prohibition in Hudud
Ruling statements with conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on sulh in claims of diyah, qisas, and hadd, and identify the conditions that apply to each type of claim.

Core Idea
Sulh in fixed claims cannot exceed the prescribed amount; sulh in qisas can exceed but must avoid riba; sulh in hudud is entirely prohibited.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains the nuances of settlement (sulh) in injury cases. While compromise is generally permissible in property and injury claims, strict limits apply when the diyah is fixed by Shariah or when the claim involves a right of Allah. Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying Hanafi fiqh in criminal and civil disputes.

Guided Path
1
Categorise the claim
First, identify whether the claim is about wealth (amwal/manafi), intentional injury (qisas), unintentional injury (diyah), or a fixed punishment (hadd). Each category has different rules for sulh.
Claims of benefits (manafi) are treated like wealth claims.
2
Analyse the diyah vs qisas distinction
For unintentional injury (khatā'), the diyah is fixed by Shariah, so sulh cannot exceed the prescribed amount. For intentional injury (qisas), since qisas is not property, sulh can involve more than the diyah.
The rationale: qisas is a right of the victim/guardian, not money.
3
Examine the conditions for exchange
When sulh involves exchanging camels for cows or other goods, spot payment (qabd fī l-majlis) is required to avoid debt-for-debt (riba). If the qadi has already ruled, the right is determined, so exchange is like a sale.
Debt-for-debt is prohibited in Hanafi law.
4
Identify the prohibition in hadd and lineage
Sulh is not permissible for hadd punishments (rights of Allah) or for claims of lineage (right of the child, not the mother).
These are rights that cannot be waived or sold.

Commentary
The commentator elaborates on the permissibility of sulh in claims of benefits, giving an example of a bequest of service of a slave. He then discusses the rules for sulh in cases of intentional and unintentional injury, noting that in cases of unintentional homicide (khatā'), the compensation cannot exceed the prescribed diyah because it is fixed by Shariah. However, in cases of intentional homicide (qasās), one may settle for more than the diyah since qasās is not a property right. The passage also addresses situations where the qāḍī has already ruled, and the distinction between exchanging one type of property for another. Finally, it states that sulh is not permissible in claims of fixed corporal punishments (ḥadd) because these are rights of Allah, and similarly, a woman cannot settle for compensation in a claim of lineage of her child because it is the child's right.

Key Terms
الدية (al-diyah): Blood money, the fixed compensation paid to the victim's family in unintentional homicide or injury.
القصاص (al-qisas): Retribution, the right to exact equivalent punishment in intentional homicide or injury; can be commuted via sulh.
الحد (al-hadd): A fixed corporal punishment prescribed by Shariah for specific crimes (e.g., theft, fornication, false accusation).
القبض في المجلس (al-qabd fī l-majlis): Spot payment (taking possession) in the same session to avoid debt-for-debt.

Liability for Death of a Free Minor Under Usurpation
Details liability for death of a free minor in usurper's hands under various causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of liability for a free minor dying in the hands of a usurper and identify the ruling for each based on the cause of death.

Core Idea
Liability for a free minor in usurpation depends on the cause of death: natural causes no liability, external attacks liability on the usurper's 'aqila.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a nuanced set of rulings on the liability (daman) of a usurper (ghasib) when a free minor dies while in his possession. The rulings differ based on the cause of death and the intent of any third party. Understanding these distinctions is key to grasping the Hanafi theory of wrongful possession (yad) and its consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and act
The passage discusses a free minor boy (sabiyy hurr) who is usurped (ghasb). Note that the minor is free, not a slave, so ownership rules do not apply.
The ruling hinges on the minor being free and not owned.
2
First scenario: natural illness
If the minor falls ill and dies naturally in the usurper's hand, according to Abu Hanifa there is no liability. The usurper did not cause the death.
This is because the death is attributed to divine decree, not the usurper's act.
3
Second scenario: animal attack
If a wild beast mauls or a snake bites the minor, causing death, the diya is upon the usurper's 'aqila (paternal relatives). The usurper's possession created the opportunity for harm.
The usurper is considered a cause (sabab) even without direct action.
4
Third scenario: accidental killing by third party
If a man kills the minor accidentally while the minor is in the usurper's possession, the guardians may claim diya from either the usurper or the killer at their choice. If they claim from the usurper, he can recoup from the killer; if from the killer, no recourse.
All liability is on the respective 'aqila.
5
Fourth scenario: intentional killing by third party
If the third party kills intentionally, the guardians may either demand qisas (retaliation) against the killer, absolving the usurper, or demand diya from the usurper's 'aqila, who then recover from the killer's wealth.
The usurper is not liable if qisas is chosen.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the liability (daman) of a usurper (ghasib) when a free minor (sabiyy hurr) dies while in his possession. It presents four scenarios with differing rules. First, if the minor dies of natural illness, Abu Hanifa holds no liability because the minor is a free person not owned, and death from illness is not caused by the usurper. Second, if the minor is killed by a wild animal or snake, the usurper's 'aqila (paternal relatives) must pay the diya (blood money) because the usurper's act of taking the minor created the situation where the animal could attack. Third, if a third party accidentally kills the minor, the guardians (awliya') have the option to claim diya from either the usurper or the killer. If they claim from the usurper, he can then recover from the killer; if they claim from the killer, he cannot recover from the usurper. In both cases, the 'aqila bears the diya. Fourth, if the third party kills intentionally, the guardians can either demand qisas (retaliation) against the killer, absolving the usurper, or demand diya from the usurper's 'aqila, who then recover from the killer's wealth. The reasoning underlying these distinctions is that the usurper's wrongful possession (yad) creates a form of guarantee, but this guarantee is limited compared to actual ownership. The free minor is not property, so liability depends on causation. This is from the chapter of Ghasb in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('aqila): The paternal male relatives (asaba) who bear the diya collectively for accidental killings.
غاصب (ghasib): A person who wrongfully takes and possesses property (or a free person) without right.
دية (diya): Blood money paid to the heirs of a victim in cases of accidental or intentional killing (if qisas is waived or not applicable).
ضمان (daman): Liability; financial responsibility for loss or damage.

Usurper's Non-Liability for a Free Minor's Murder
States the non-liability of a usurper for a free minor's independent act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a usurper's possession (yad) does not create liability for a free person's independent criminal acts.

Core Idea
A usurper is not liable for a free minor's murder of a third party because the minor's own liability is not established by the usurper's hand.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific scenario in diyat: a free minor, while in the unlawful possession (yad) of a usurper (ghasib), kills a man. The minor's 'aqila (paternal relatives) pay the diya. The text rules that the 'aqila cannot seek compensation from the usurper. The reasoning is a cornerstone of Hanafi law: the usurper's guarantee (daman al-yad) covers injuries to the minor but not injuries caused by the minor. This distinction highlights that free persons are not treated like property under usurpation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and actions
List the actors: the usurper (ghasib), the minor (sabi), the victim (a man killed), and the 'aqila (who pay diya). Identify the key action: the minor killed the victim while in the usurper's hand.
2
Identify the legal claim and ruling
The 'aqila pay diya for the minor. They seek to recover that payment from the usurper. The ruling: they have no recourse (la yumkinuhum al-ruju').
The ruling is a clear negative: no liability on the usurper for this act.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text gives the reason: because the minor does not become liable by virtue of being in the usurper's hand (al-sabi la yadman bi al-yad). Therefore, his criminal act (jinaayah) is not guaranteed by the usurper. The usurper only guarantees injury inflicted upon the minor, not by him.
This rests on the distinction between a free person and property: yad over a free person does not create property-like guarantee.
4
Distinguish the scope of usurper's liability
The usurper is liable for damage to the minor (e.g., if someone else injures the minor while in usurpation, the usurper may be liable). But the minor's own independent acts do not transfer liability to the usurper.
This is a key limit on daman al-yad: it covers the object's value or injuries to it, not the object's actions.
5
Apply to analogous scenarios
Consider if the minor acted under coercion (ikrah) from the usurper. In that case, the usurper might be liable as the direct cause. Here, the minor acts independently, so the ruling stands.
This step prepares for the application bridge.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific scenario: a free minor in the usurper's possession commits murder (kills a man). The usurper then returns the minor to his father. The 'aqila (paternal relatives) of the minor pay the diya for the murder. The text rules that the 'aqila cannot then claim compensation from the usurper. The reason given is that the minor's own liability (daman) is not established by being in the usurper's hand (yad); the minor is not a guaranteed object (like property) due to his freedom. The usurper only guarantees damages inflicted upon the minor, not those inflicted by him. This is a clear illustration of the Hanafi principle that a usurper's guarantee (daman al-yad) covers the object's value or injuries to it, but not acts of the object that is a free person. The minor's act of murder is his own responsibility (though as a minor, diya is on his 'aqila).

يد (yad): Possession, which in ghasb creates a guarantee for the object but for free persons only for injury to them.
