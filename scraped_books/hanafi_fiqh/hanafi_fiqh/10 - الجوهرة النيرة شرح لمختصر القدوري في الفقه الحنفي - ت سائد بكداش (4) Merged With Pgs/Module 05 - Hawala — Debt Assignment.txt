Definition and Ruling of Hawala
Author defines hawala and states its permissibility for debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hawala, identify its four essential parties, and state that it is permissible only for debts, not for physical assets or rights.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers a debt from one person to another and is valid only for debts, not physical assets.

Hawala is a contract that facilitates the transfer of a debt from the original debtor to a new debtor. This lesson introduces the definition, the ruling, and the parties involved, as explained in the classical Hanafi text. Understanding these fundamentals is essential before exploring further rulings.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and legal definition
Read the definition: linguistically it means transferring something; legally it is the transfer of debt from one liability to another as a means of security.
The legal definition specifies 'debt', not physical items.
2
Identify the four parties
The text lists the muhil (original debtor), muhal lahu (creditor), muhal alayh (new debtor), and muhal bihi (the debt itself). Remember each role.
These names are essential for later rulings.
3
State the ruling and its restriction
The author explicitly says 'Hawala is permissible for debts.' This means it is mubah (permissible) but only for debts, not for physical assets or rights.
The restriction to debts is because only debts can move from one liability to another.
4
Note the exception regarding debts invalid for kafala
The text mentions that debts for which kafala is invalid, like the debt of kitaba, are still valid for hawala. This shows the distinct nature of hawala.
This nuance highlights that hawala has a broader scope than kafala.

The Validity of a Judge's Declaration of Insolvency: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on judge's insolvency declaration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the legal effect of a judge's declaration of insolvency and explain the underlying principle (talil) for each.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling cannot change a debtor's financial reality if wealth is inherently unstable.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute about the legal validity of a judge declaring a debtor insolvent. The Sahibayn hold that such a declaration is effective, while Abu Hanifa argues that insolvency cannot be established by a judge's ruling because a person's sustenance is always changing. Understanding this disagreement illuminates the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to judicial authority and financial status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third view mentioned
Locate the passage that states 'and a third aspect: that the judge passes a ruling on his insolvency during his lifetime.' This is the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
The two earlier views are not detailed here; focus on this third one.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's principle
Understand that they consider a judge's ruling on insolvency as valid and effective. The text says 'this is according to their principle.' Their dalil is that a judge's ruling is authoritative in establishing legal statuses unless there is a specific prohibition.
They do not cite an explicit text, but rely on the general authority of the judge's decree.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's principle
Abu Hanifa argues that insolvency is not established by a judge's ruling because sustenance (rizq) comes and goes. The reality of a person's wealth is known only to Allah, and a judge's declaration cannot fix it.
The phrase 'rizq Allah ta'ala ghadi wa ra'ih' (sustenance comes and goes) is key.
4
Compare the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if a judge declares insolvency, according to Sahibayn the debtor's assets are seized and his sales are invalid. According to Abu Hanifa, the debtor retains legal capacity to transact, and his previous sales remain valid.
This affects creditors' rights and the validity of contracts made after the declaration.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text does not state which position is relied upon. In later Hanafi fatwa works, Abu Hanifa's view is generally preferred unless hardship arises that requires judicial intervention.
This reflects the cautious approach of the school in limiting the judge's power over financial status.

Ruling When the Muhal 'Alayh Demands from the Muhil
Ruling on claim by muhal alayh against muhil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when the muhal 'alayh demands repayment from the muhil and distinguish the two Hanafi narrations regarding the death of the muhal 'alayh.

Core Idea
In hawala, the muhal 'alayh can seek reimbursement from the muhil; the muhil's claim of a prior debt against him is not accepted without proof.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a key dispute scenario in hawala: what happens when the muhal 'alayh, after paying the muhtal, demands the same amount from the muhil? The text clarifies that the muhil cannot simply deny liability by alleging a counter-debt. Furthermore, a secondary dispute within the Hanafi school arises if the muhal 'alayh dies—should the word of the muhtal or the muhil be accepted? We will unpack the ruling and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Recognize the muhal 'alayh (new debtor), muhil (original debtor), and muhtal (creditor). Understand that the muhal 'alayh has paid the debt to the muhtal on the muhil's behalf.
2
Analyze the muhil's defense
The muhil claims that he transferred because the muhal 'alayh owed him a debt. This is a claim of set-off. Note that the muhil is essentially saying he had a prior claim against the muhal 'alayh.
3
Understand why the muhil's statement is not accepted
The text states that the cause of recourse (sabab al-ruju') is already established: the muhal 'alayh paid at the muhil's command. The muhil's denial of a prior debt is a new claim that requires evidence; his statement alone is insufficient.
4
Examine the death scenario and the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
If the muhal 'alayh dies, the muhtal claims he died insolvent, so the muhil should bear the loss. The muhil disputes. In al-Mabsut, the muhtal's word is taken; in other sources, the muhil's word is taken. Both with oaths. This shows the existence of divergent narrations within the school.

Commentary
This lesson covers two related rulings. First, the basic rule: When the muhal 'alayh (the person on whom the debt was transferred) demands the same amount from the muhil (the original debtor), and the muhil claims that he transferred because the muhal 'alayh owed him a debt, his claim is not accepted. The muhil must pay the muhal 'alayh the full amount. Ruling When the Muhil Sues the Muhtal
Ruling on a dispute between muhil and muhtal over the nature of a hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the muhil and muhtal dispute the nature of a hawala and explain why the muhil's word with oath is accepted.

Core Idea
When muhil and muhtal dispute hawala's nature, the muhil's word with oath prevails because the muhtal claims a debt and has no proof.

In hawala transactions, disputes can arise about whether the transfer was a true assignment of debt (hawala) or merely an agency to collect (wakala). This lesson examines the Hanafi evidentiary rule when the muhil (transferor) sues the muhtal (transferee) for the transferred amount. The ruling hinges on who is claiming a debt and who denies it, and on the linguistic flexibility of the term 'hawala' itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The muhil (transferor) demands the amount from the muhtal (transferee). The muhil claims the transfer was an agency (wakala) to collect money, not a discharge of a debt. The muhtal claims it was a true hawala to settle a debt the muhil owed him.
2
Determine who is plaintiff and who is defendant
The muhtal is asserting a claim (that the muhil owes him a debt), making him the plaintiff. The muhil is denying the debt, making him the defendant. The default rule in Islamic evidence law is that the defendant's denial is accepted with an oath.
This is based on the maxim 'al-bayyina 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' (the burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier).
3
Apply the principle of defendant's denial with oath
Since the muhil is the defendant, his word is taken with an oath. The sharh confirms: 'فالقول قول المحيل مع يمينه' – the word is the word of the muhil with his oath.
4
Note the linguistic ambiguity of 'hawala'
The sharh states that 'hawala' can also mean 'wakala' (agency). This linguistic ambiguity allows the muhil's interpretation to be plausible, reinforcing why his claim is accepted.
This is a subtle point: the very term used for the transfer can be interpreted in two ways, so the denier's interpretation is given weight.
5
State the evidentiary burden on the muhtal
If the muhil swears, he takes back the amount. The muhtal's claim of debt is not accepted without proof (bayyina). This protects the muhil from false claims.
The passage ends by noting the muhil may have transferred merely to collect, so the muhtal must prove the debt.

The sharh explains that the term 'hawala' is used here in the sense of agency (tawkīl). Since the muhtal is claiming a debt against the muhil (that the muhil owed him), and the muhil denies it, the muhil's denial is accepted. Once the muhil swears, he can take back the amount that was paid. The muhtal's claim of a debt is not accepted without evidence. Definition and Ruling on Sufaja (Letter of Credit)
Defines sufaja and states its dislike

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sufaja and state its ruling (makruh) in Hanafi fiqh, identifying the reason for dislike (conditional loan providing benefit).

Core Idea
A loan with a condition to write a letter of credit is disliked because it gives a benefit to the lender, resembling riba.

This lesson introduces the term 'sufaja' (letter of credit) and explains why the Hanafi school considers it makruh. It is placed in the context of hawala because both involve transfer—hawala transfers debt, while sufaja transfers the risk of loss. Understanding the ruling requires recognizing the problematic condition attached to the loan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Read the Arabic text and translation to understand what sufaja (plural safatij) is. Note that it is a loan with a condition that the borrower writes a letter to his agent to pay the lender in another city, avoiding the risk of carrying cash.
The term is derived from suftaja, meaning a paper or document.
2
Identify the ruling and the reason
The text states it is makruh. Analyze why: the condition (to write the letter) provides a benefit to the lender (safety from road risk), making the loan a conditional loan that gives a benefit, which is discouraged in Hanafi fiqh as it may lead to riba.
The principle: any loan that brings a benefit to the lender is considered problematic.
3
Understand the connection to hawala
The author links sufaja to hawala because both involve transfer. In hawala, debt is transferred; in sufaja, the risk of loss (tawa) is transferred from the lender's wealth to the borrower's wealth through the loan.
This connection helps in organizing fiqh chapters.

Ruling on Conditional Benefit in Loan (Qard)
Ruling on conditional benefit in loan (qard jarra manfa'ah) with Hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling of a conditional benefit in a loan (qard jarra manfa'ah) and identify the Hadith evidence that prohibits it.

Core Idea
A conditional benefit from a loan is prohibited as riba; an unconditional gift is permissible.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi fiqh regarding loans (qard). The source text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies when a benefit derived from a loan becomes prohibited. The ruling distinguishes between conditional and unconditional benefits, grounded in a Hadith that forbids loans that bring benefits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a lender giving a gift to the borrower on condition of safety from road danger (e.g., accompaniment). The ruling is makruh tahrimi.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the Hadith: 'Every loan that brings a benefit is riba.'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Arabic phrase 'جر منفعة' (draws a benefit) indicates that any extra benefit attached to a loan is considered riba. The prohibition is derived from this causal connection.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools may permit certain benefits, but Hanafis strictly prohibit any conditional benefit, upholding the general prohibition.
5
Evaluate the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that loans with conditional benefits are makruh tahrimi, as stated in later fatwa works.

Source Text

وأما إذا أعطاه من غير شرط، وسأله ذلك، ففعل: فلا بأس به. وإنما يكره: إذا كان أمن خطر الطريق مشروطاً؛ لأنه نوع نفع استفيد بالقرض، وقد نهى النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم عن قرض جر منفعة
As for when he gives it without a condition, and he asks him for that, and he does: then there is no harm. It is only disliked (makruh) when safety from the road danger is conditional, because it is a type of benefit derived from the loan, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade a loan that brings a benefit.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the chapter of hawala concerning the permissibility of a lender giving a gift to the borrower. It distinguishes two scenarios: first, if the lender gives a gift without any prior condition, even if the borrower requested it, it is permissible and not makruh. Second, if the lender makes the loan conditional upon the borrower providing security from road danger (e.g., accompaniment), then it is makruh tahrimi because it is a benefit derived from the loan, and any loan that brings a benefit is riba as per the hadith. The Hanafi school holds this as a form of riba al-fadl or riba al-qard.

Key Terms
قرض جر منفعة (qard jarra manfa'ah): A loan that provides some additional benefit to the lender, which is prohibited as riba.
