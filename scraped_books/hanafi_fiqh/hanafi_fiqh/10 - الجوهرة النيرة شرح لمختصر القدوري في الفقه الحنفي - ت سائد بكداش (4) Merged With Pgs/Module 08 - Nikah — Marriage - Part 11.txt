Li'an for Denial of Paternity: Conditions and Effect of Confirmation
Conditions for li'an to deny paternity and ruling if wife confirms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid li'an to deny paternity and explain why the wife's confirmation prevents li'an.

Core Idea
Li'an to deny paternity requires the wife to be eligible for testimony from conception to delivery.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a specific scenario: a husband accuses his wife of adultery and denies paternity of the child. The ruling establishes that li'an can negate lineage, but strict conditions apply regarding the wife's status at the time of conception. Additionally, if the wife confirms the accusation, li'an is impossible and the child remains his. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying Hanafi family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the husband's accusation of zina combined with denial of paternity (nafy al-walad). The ruling: if valid, the judge negates the child's lineage from the father and attaches it to the mother.
The ruling is conditional on the proper performance of li'an.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'The two mutual accusers never come together' (المتلاعنان لا يجتمعان أبداً). This indicates permanent separation after li'an, implying lineage negation.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud, grade: hasan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'never come together' signifies that li'an terminates the marriage permanently. Since the child's lineage is tied to the marriage, its negation is a necessary consequence.
Hanafi usul: the hadith is zanni al-thubut but relied upon in this context.
4
Examine the condition for negating paternity
The wife must be from 'ahl al-shahada' (qualified to testify: Muslim, free, adult, sane) from conception until delivery. If she was a Kitabiyyah or slave at conception, even if later Islam/freedom, the lineage is fixed and cannot be negated.
The condition is linked to the wife's capacity to perform li'an (which requires shahada qualification).
5
Analyze the effect of wife's confirmation
If the husband accuses and the wife confirms (i.e., admits to zina), then no hadd on husband, no li'an, and the child remains his. The couple cannot deny the child's lineage because lineage is a right of the child, and the mother cannot forfeit it.
Li'an requires the wife to swear that the husband is lying; if she confirms, that oath would be contradictory.

Retraction of the husband's oath after li'an cancels the eternal prohibition according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

While the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that the wife becomes lawful again, Imam Abu Yusuf argues that li'an creates an eternal bond of prohibition. Procedure for Li'an in Denial of Paternity
Procedure for li'an when husband denies paternity or also accuses zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the procedure of li'an for denial of paternity and identify which rulings of lineage remain after severance.

Core Idea
Li'an severs lineage for inheritance and maintenance only; all other kinship prohibitions persist.

Li'an is a solemn oath procedure used when a husband accuses his wife of zina or denies paternity without witnesses. This passage details the specific steps the judge and parties follow, and clarifies the limited effect of genealogical severance. Understanding these details prevents confusion about the legal consequences of li'an.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note that the passage gives two cases: (1) husband only denies paternity without accusing zina, and (2) husband both accuses zina and denies paternity. In the latter, both matters must be mentioned in the oath.
The judge orders the husband to swear specifically about denial of paternity.
2
Outline the oath procedure
The husband swears four times: 'I testify by Allah that I am indeed among the truthful in what I have accused you of regarding denial of paternity.' The wife performs the same oaths in response.
The wording is fixed and must be repeated exactly.
3
Understand the judge's role
After the oaths, the judge formally severs the child's lineage from the father and attaches it to the mother. He announces: 'I have attached the child to his mother and removed him from the father's lineage.'
This is a judicial declaration, not a separate marriage or adoption.
4
Distinguish between severed and preserved rulings
Only inheritance (mirath) and maintenance (nafaqa) are severed. All other rulings remain: the child and father cannot testify for each other, zakat transfers between them are invalid, and marriage prohibitions (e.g., father marrying the child's daughter, child marrying the husband's daughter) continue.
Preserved rulings are based on kinship through birth, not legal lineage.
5
Apply the restriction on claiming the child
No one other than the husband who performed li'an may later claim the negated child as his own, even if the child affirms such a claim.
The child remains exclusively attached to the mother's lineage.

Silence upon Congratulation: Acknowledgment for Wife's Child but not Slave-Girl's
Ruling on the legal effect of silence upon congratulation for paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on silence upon congratulation for a wife's child versus a slave-girl's child, and explain the role of firash and da'wa in establishing lineage.

Core Idea
Silence on congratulation acknowledges paternity only when lineage is already presumed by firash; it cannot replace an active claim for a slave-girl's child.

In Hanafi fiqh, congratulation on a newborn is not merely a social custom—it has legal weight. A husband's silence when congratulated can be interpreted as an acknowledgment of paternity. But this only applies if the child's lineage is already presumed by the marital bed (firash). For a slave-girl's child, where no firash exists, silence carries no such effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage contrasts the wife's child with the slave-girl's child. Note the third case of umm walad which is treated like a wife's child.
2
Understand the basis of lineage (firash)
For the wife, the marital bed (firash) establishes a presumption of paternity. For a slave-girl, no firash exists; paternity requires an explicit claim (da'wa).
Firash is the key concept; it is the legal foundation for automatic lineage.
3
Analyze the legal effect of silence
Silence upon congratulation implies acceptance of the presumed lineage for the wife's child. For the slave-girl, silence cannot replace the required claim.
Silence is interpreted as consent only when there is a pre-existing basis.
4
Apply the exception: umm walad
The child of an umm walad (a slave-girl who has borne her master's child and been declared umm walad) is treated like a wife's child because she now has a form of firash.
This shows that firash is not exclusive to marriage; it can arise from certain relationships.
5
State the Hanafi ruling
Silence upon congratulation constitutes acknowledgment for the wife's child and the umm walad's child, but not for a slave-girl's child.
The ruling is a settled principle in the Hanafi school (zahir al-riwayah).

Twin Birth: Acknowledgment of First, Denial of Second
Ruling when husband acknowledges first twin but denies second.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a husband acknowledges one twin and denies the other, and explain why li'an is required.

Core Idea
Acknowledging one twin establishes both; denying the second is slander requiring li'an.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario in lineage (nasab) and slander (qadhf). When a husband acknowledges only one of twin children and denies the other, the law must balance the presumption of a single pregnancy with the husband's retraction of his acknowledgment. The key insight is that the denial amounts to an accusation against the wife, triggering the process of mutual imprecation (li'an).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The husband acknowledges the first twin and denies the second. The ruling: both children are established as his, and he must perform li'an.
The denial is ineffective and becomes slander.

The text states that twins are one pregnancy (حملا واحد). Therefore, acknowledging the first establishes both, and the denial is a retraction that is invalid.
The proof is based on the nature of twin pregnancy and the principle of binding acknowledgment.
3

The denial is legally equivalent to accusing the wife of adultery (qadhf) because it implies the second child is not from the husband. The text says 'لأنه صار قاذفاً للزوجة بنفي الثاني' — he becomes a slanderer.
This triggers the obligation of li'an.
4

Some manuscripts read 'ولا عن' (no li'an). The commentary clarifies that this is incorrect; the correct ruling is with li'an.
The variant is not a recognized scholarly position.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position is that li'an is obligatory. The husband's denial after acknowledgment is a retraction and an accusation, so li'an is required.
This is the mufta bihi view.
