Since the stillborn is a child, the condition 'if you give birth to a child' is met, and the divorce (or manumission) takes effect.
The ruling applies to both free and slave wives.

Divorce and 'Iddah: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on the Wife's Status
Inter-madhhab dispute on divorce and 'iddah for slave and free women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the number of divorces and 'iddah for slave and free women, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the dispute.

Core Idea
The number of divorces and 'iddah depend on the wife's status, not the husband's.

This passage presents the foundational Hanafi position that both the count of divorces (talaq) and the waiting period ('iddah) are determined by whether the woman is free or a slave, regardless of her husband's status. It then contrasts this with Imam al-Shafi'i's view that the divorce count follows the husband's status while 'iddah follows the wife's. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correctly applying rulings in marriages involving free and slave parties.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that both the number of divorces and the 'iddah period are based on the wife's status: a slave woman gets two divorces and two menstrual periods, a free woman gets three divorces and three periods, regardless of the husband's freedom or slavery.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position, agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Identify the opposing school and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the number of divorces depends on the husband's status (since he initiates divorce), while 'iddah depends on the wife's status (since she undergoes it). Thus, a free woman under a slave gets two divorces (not three), and a slave woman under a free man gets three divorces (not two).
The 'iddah remains agreed upon: three periods for a free woman, two for a slave woman.
3
Follow the implications across four scenarios
Analyze the four possible combinations: (1) free woman under slave: Hanafi says three divorces, Shafi'i says two; (2) slave woman under free man: Hanafi says two, Shafi'i says three; (3) slave under slave: both agree on two divorces and two periods; (4) free under free: both agree on three divorces and three periods.
The cases of agreement (ijma) strengthen the Hanafi argument that the wife's status is the primary factor.
4
Analyze the evidence
The Hanafi school relies on the principle that marriage rights and obligations are tied to the wife's condition. The Shafi'i school argues that divorce is an act of the husband, so his status should govern. The text does not provide explicit scriptural evidence but indicates consensus on mixed cases supports the Hanafi view.
The debate is methodological: which status (husband or wife) is the 'illa (effective cause) for the ruling.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference occurs in mixed-status marriages. For example, a free woman married to a slave: under Hanafi she can be divorced three times, making her irrevocably divorced after the third; under Shafi'i she would be irrevocably divorced after the second.
This affects the possibility of remarriage and the number of times the husband can take her back.

Triple Divorce in One Utterance Before Consummation
Explains ruling and linguistic reasoning for triple divorce in one expression

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on triple divorce before consummation when uttered as one expression and explain the linguistic reasoning that 'three times' is a description (sifah) of the divorce.

Core Idea
Saying 'You are divorced three times' is one speech act; all three divorces take effect.

This lesson examines the first part of the passage, where the commentator explains the Hanafi position on a man who says to his wife before consummation, 'You are divorced three times.' The ruling is that all three divorces occur. Why? Because 'three times' is not a separate divorce but a description of the one divorce utterance. Understanding this linguistic principle is key to applying the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a man saying to his wife before consummation: 'You are divorced three times.' The ruling in the Hanafi school is that all three divorces take effect, making the wife bain (irrevocably divorced).
This applies only when the marriage is not yet consummated.
2
Locate the key linguistic argument
The commentator argues that the phrase 'three times' (ثلاثاً) is a description (sifah) and interpretation (tafsir) of the divorce, not an initiation of a new divorce. Thus the entire statement is considered one utterance.
The text uses the term 'ṣifah' (صفة) to describe 'three times'.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle of speech acts
In Hanafi usul, when a description is attached to a verb, the description and the described are considered one speech act. Therefore, 'You are divorced three times' is analogous to saying 'You are divorced with a bain divorce' or 'You are divorced twice' – all are single utterances.
This principle prevents fragmentation of the divorce into separate incidents.
4
Contrast with separated utterances
The second part of the passage will cover what happens if the divorces are uttered separately (e.g., 'You are divorced, divorced'). For now, note that the one-utterance case yields a different result.

The act is conditional talaq using the conjunction 'thumma' (then) between two talaq pronouncements. The key dispute is whether 'thumma' severs the second talaq from the first, making the first immediate and the second conditional, or whether the entire utterance is a single conditional sentence.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling when a husband pronounces talaq with the condition of 'tomorrow' (ghadan). The classical commentators explain that the divorce occurs at the very beginning of the day, at dawn. Suspended Divorce to a Future Time: Valid Phrasings and Their Consequences
Explaining Hanafi rulings on talaq suspended to a future time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when a suspended divorce takes effect and explain the Hanafi rules regarding contradictory time expressions and judicial credibility of intention.

Core Idea
A suspended divorce takes effect at the specified time; contradictory phrasings are resolved by the first uttered word.

The Hanafi school recognizes that a husband may suspend the effect of divorce to a future time, but the precise wording matters greatly. Depending on how the time is expressed—whether with 'in,' 'and,' or sequentially—the number of divorces and the moment they take effect can differ. This lesson walks through the key scenarios from al-Jawhara al-Nayira and unpacks the intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two students on when a husband's claimed intention is accepted in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling for simple 'tomorrow'
When a husband says 'You are divorced tomorrow,' the divorce occurs at dawn (fajr) of the next day, because the first moment of the day is considered the entire day's beginning. Note that if he later claims he intended the end of the day, he is believed only religiously, not in court.
This establishes the baseline: suspension to a day takes effect at its earliest part.
2
Analyze contradictory time phrases
If he says 'today tomorrow' or 'tomorrow today,' the first time word governs. 'Today' is immediate (tanjiz), cannot be suspended; 'tomorrow' is suspension (idafa), so the second word is void. Understand the reasoning: the immediate overrides the suspended.
The order of utterance determines the ruling.
3
Apply the rule to conjunction phrases
When using 'and' (waw), 'today and tomorrow' yields one immediate divorce (the description of being divorced covers both times). But 'tomorrow and today' yields two divorces: one tomorrow and one today, because the order implies sequence.
Zufar's minority view: only one divorce in the latter case.
4
Examine the dispute on intention with 'in tomorrow'
If he says 'in tomorrow' (using fi) and then claims he intended the very end of the day, Abu Hanifa accepts this both religiously and judicially, comparing 'in' to a container that does not require covering all parts. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) only accept it religiously; they hold that the apparent meaning of 'in tomorrow' covers the whole day, so judicial verdicts follow the apparent.
The practical consequence: in court, the Sahibayn's position is generally followed.
5
Synthesize the underlying principle
The dispute hinges on how much weight is given to the literal container (zarf) and the role of intention. Hanafi usul teaches that specification of a general expression by intention is valid only religiously, not judicially, unless the expression itself can bear that specification. Here, 'in tomorrow' can bear either 'any part' or 'the whole,' hence the ikhtilaf.
Remember: judicial matters rely on apparent evidence (zahir).

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The wife has khiyar (option) after talaq. Certain actions may nullify it if they indicate turning away (i'rad).
