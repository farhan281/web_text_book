Spending on Dependents from the Missing Person's Wealth
Details who is entitled to maintenance and what property may be sold

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which dependents are entitled to maintenance from a missing person's wealth without a court order and state the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the sale of movable and immovable property for maintenance.

Core Idea
Maintenance from a missing person's wealth is only for those already entitled without court order; the father may sell only movables according to Abu Hanifa, but nothing according to the Sahibayn.

When a person goes missing, their dependents still need support. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi rules: who gets maintenance automatically, who needs a court order (which is impossible for an absentee), and what property can be sold. It also highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on whether the missing person's father may sell any property at all.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
The author gives a rule: those who are already entitled to maintenance during the missing person's presence without a court order continue to receive it from his wealth during absence. Identify this principle and its rationale.
The court order is merely assistance (i‘āna) for those already entitled, but it creates the obligation for others.
2
Identify the two categories of dependents
List who falls into the first category (entitled without court order) and who falls into the second (needs court order). Use the examples given: young children, adult daughters, chronically ill adult males vs. siblings, aunts, uncles.
Also includes parents and all relatives in the direct lineage (awlād).
3
Identify the property types
The passage specifies that maintenance is taken from movable property (manqul): cash, food, clothing. Immovable property (ghayr manqul) like houses, land, animals, slaves is generally not sold.
This protects the missing person’s long-term assets.
4
State Abu Hanifa's exception for the father
Abu Hanifa allows the father of the missing person to sell movable property (only) for maintenance. He does not allow selling immovable property.
The father is considered to have a special duty and authority.
5
State the Sahibayn's contrary view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that even the father cannot sell anything at all. This is the more cautious position and often preferred in later fatwa.
The dispute is only about the father; other relatives have no sale authority.

Commentary
This passage details the rules of spending maintenance (nafaqah) from the wealth of a missing person. The general principle is that those who were already entitled to maintenance without a court order before the person went missing continue to receive it from his funds. This includes his wife, young children, adult daughters, and chronically ill adult sons. Also parents and all blood relatives in the direct line are included. However, siblings and more distant relatives like aunts and uncles do not receive maintenance from his wealth in his absence because they would need a court order to claim it even in his presence, and a court order against an absentee is not valid (la yajuz). Also, the passage clarifies what types of property can be used for maintenance: cash, clothing, and food—i.e., movable assets. Immovable property like houses, real estate, animals, and slaves cannot be sold for maintenance according to the majority view. However, Imam Abu Hanifa makes an exception for the father of the missing person: he may sell movable property to cover maintenance, but not immovable. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that even the father cannot sell anything. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance including food, clothing, and housing for dependents.
منقول (manqul): Movable property such as cash, food, and clothing that can be transported.
غير منقول (ghayr manqul): Immovable property such as land, houses, and buildings.

Key Takeaway
Maintenance from a missing person’s wealth is wajib only for those who were already entitled without a court order; and while Abu Hanifa allows the father to sell movable property, the relied-upon view is that he may sell nothing.

Maintenance from a Missing Person’s Wealth: Who Gets What
Ruling on who receives nafaqah and what property can be sold

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that distinguishes dependents who automatically receive nafaqah from a missing person's wealth from those who require a judge's order, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the sale of movable property.

Core Idea
Automatic nafaqah continues for dependents who would get it without a judge when the person is present.

When a person goes missing (mafqud), his dependents still need support. The Hanafi school has a clear principle: if a dependent would be entitled to maintenance without a judge's order when the person is present, they continue to receive it in his absence. If they would need a judge's order, they cannot get it because a judge cannot pass an order against an absent person. This lesson unpacks this principle and also examines which assets can be used for maintenance, including a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions.

1
Identify the base ruling
The passage begins by stating that the wife and young children receive nafaqah from the missing person's wealth. Note the extension to parents and all blood relatives.
2
Understand the key principle (al-asl)
The commentator states a rule: those who are entitled to nafaqah without a judge's order when the person is present continue to get it in his absence; those who need a judge's order do not. This is because a judge's order against an absent person is not valid.
3
Apply the principle to the categories
Identify examples from the first category (young children, adult daughters, disabled adult males) and the second category (brother, sister, maternal uncle, aunt).
4
Analyze the type of wealth used

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the ruling on maintaining dependents of a missing person (mafqud). It begins by stating the base ruling: the missing person's wife and young children receive maintenance from his wealth. The commentator clarifies that 'first young children' are meant, and then extends this to parents and all blood relatives. The key principle (al-asl) is given: if a dependent would be entitled to maintenance without a judge's order when the person is present, they continue to receive it in his absence; otherwise, they need a judge's order, which is not valid against an absent person. Examples of the first category are young children, adult daughters (as they are typically dependent and do not require a judge's order by default), and disabled adult males. Examples of the second category are adult siblings, aunts, and uncles who are not direct descendants. As for the type of wealth: only liquid assets like money, clothing, and food are used. Real estate, animals, and slaves are not sold for maintenance. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa allows the father (the missing person) to sell movable property to provide maintenance, but not immovable property. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two students) hold that no property at all is sold by the father; maintenance is only from existing moveable wealth. This is a known difference in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
الزَّمنى (az-zamanā): Disabled adult males who are unable to earn and are thus dependent on the missing person. They receive nafaqah without a judge's order.
المنقول (al-manqūl): Movable property such as dirhams, dinars, clothing, and food. According to Abu Hanifa, the father may sell this to provide nafaqah for the missing person's dependents.
غير المنقول (ghayr al-manqūl): Immovable property such as houses, real estate, animals, and slaves. Not sold for nafaqah according to all Hanafi imams.

Key Takeaway
