Core Idea
Movement nullifies khiyar only if the wife can control it; animal movement is her action, ship movement is not.

In classical Hanafi fiqh, when a husband gives his wife the option of divorce (khiyar), certain actions by the wife can nullify that option. One subtle scenario is when the woman is on a moving animal or ship. The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the distinction based on whether she has control over the movement. Understanding this ruling requires applying the principle of attribution of action (idafat al-fi‘l) to the wife.

Guided Path

Apply the control criterion
Determine whether the wife can stop the movement. For an animal, she can; for a ship, she cannot. This determines whether the movement is attributed to her action.
The animal's movement is attributed to her because she can stop it; the ship's movement is not.
3
Derive the ruling
If the movement is her action (e.g., letting the animal walk), the khiyar is nullified. If not (ship), the khiyar remains. Also note the exception when the husband is present.

Khiyar and Prayer: When the Wife's Option Is Nullified
Ruling on khiyar when wife starts or is in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a wife's khiyar is nullified by prayer and apply them to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Starting a prayer nullifies khiyar; completing fard preserves it; completing nafl beyond two rak'ats nullifies it.

The right of khiyar (option to divorce) is a significant right granted to the wife. However, certain actions—especially entering prayer—indicate a change of intention and affect this right. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies exactly when starting or completing a prayer nullifies the khiyar and when it does not, based on the nature of the prayer and the reasoning behind the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act under consideration
Recognize that the subject is the wife's khiyar (option of divorce) and how it is affected by her prayer.
The source distinguishes between when she initiates prayer (ruling: nullifies) and when she is already in prayer when given the choice.
2
Distinguish between starting prayer and being in prayer
If she begins prayer (fard or nafl) after being given khiyar, it is nullified. If she was already in prayer when given the choice, the ruling depends on the type and completion.
Starting prayer is a positive act indicating disinterest, whereas continuing an existing obligation does not imply rejection.
3
Differentiate fard/witr from nafl
For fard or witr: completing the prayer preserves khiyar. For nafl: completing only two rak'ats preserves it; adding more nullifies it.
The reasoning (ta'lil) is that exceeding two rak'ats in nafl is akin to starting a new prayer, which would nullify the option.
4
Apply the ta'lil (analogical reasoning)
Understand the underlying principle: 'what exceeds two rak'ats in nafl is like entering another prayer.' This analogy explains why the khiyar is nullified.
This is a clear example of qiyas: the original case (entering prayer) is extended to the case of adding extra rak'ats.
5
Note the exception for sunnat al-zuhr al-ula
If she is praying the first two rak'ats of the sunna of zuhr and transitions to the next two, this does NOT nullify her khiyar because it is considered a single supererogatory act.
This highlights that the ruling hinges on intention: the two pairs of rak'ats are part of one prayer sequence, not two separate prayers.

Ruling on 'ikhtari nafsaki' as Delegation of Divorce
Defines effect of 'ikhtari nafsaki' as one ba'in divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the phrase 'ikhtari nafsaki' and explain why it results in one ba'in divorce regardless of the husband's intention.

Core Idea
When a husband says 'choose yourself,' the wife's choice yields one irrevocable divorce — his hidden intention does not increase the count.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in delegation of divorce (tafwid al-talaq): when a husband uses the ambiguous phrase 'ikhtari nafsaki' (choose yourself), what is the effect if the wife chooses herself? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that protects the wife from unintended multiple divorces and emphasizes the primacy of the wife's choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its legal nature
Recognize that 'ikhtari nafsaki' is a kinayah (ambiguous phrase) in talaq. It conveys an option but not an explicit divorce.
Kinayah requires intention (niyyah) to effect divorce, but here the husband's delegation gives the wife the right to trigger the divorce.
2
State the ruling when the wife chooses
If the wife says 'I choose myself' (i.e., accepts the divorce), it counts as one ba'in (irrevocable) divorce.
Ba'in means the wife becomes forbidden to the husband without a new marriage and consummation.
3
Evaluate the effect of the husband's intention
Even if the husband intended three divorces, the ruling remains one ba'in. The wife's choice determines the count, not the husband's hidden intention.
This differs from the Shafi'i school, where the husband's intention can increase the number.
4
Note conditions for the wife's option (khiyar)
The wife's option remains valid unless she engages in prolonged activity like extended tasbih or reading. Short acts do not invalidate it.
The passage mentions that tasbih or short recitation is fine, but lengthy activity nullifies the option.
5
Clarify that the wife chooses only once
The wife may exercise her option only once; after choosing, she cannot revoke or choose again.
This ensures finality in the process.

Ruling on 'Amruki bi-Yadik' with Kullama and Idha
Distinguishes effect of 'kullama' vs 'idha' in delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi rulings when 'kullama' vs 'idha' is used in delegating divorce, and state the consequences for the wife's right to divorce.

Core Idea
Kullama gives one divorce per gathering up to three; idha gives only one ever.

The phrase 'amruki bi-yadik' (your affair is in your hand) is a kinayah expression that delegates the power of divorce to the wife. The word following it determines whether the delegation is ongoing or one-time. Hanafi jurists carefully parse these condition words, leading to different outcomes in the number of divorces the wife may effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition word
Read the husband's statement: does it contain 'kullama' (whenever) or 'idha' (if/when)? This determines the legal effect.
2
Determine linguistic implication
In Hanafi usul, 'kullama' implies repetition (takrar) each time the condition occurs; 'idha' and 'mata' indicate a single point in time (waqt).
3
Apply the Hanafi ruling
If 'kullama', the wife may divorce herself once per gathering (majlis) up to three total. If 'idha', she may only divorce once in her lifetime from that delegation.
The right is exhausted after three with 'kullama'; after one with 'idha'.
4
Note the requirement of intention (niyyah)
Both 'amruki bi-yadik' and 'ikhtari' are kinayah; thus, the husband must intend divorce for the delegation to be valid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A wife's response in delegated divorce must explicitly mention her 'nafs' (self); otherwise, no divorce occurs.

This lesson from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira examines the precise wording required when a husband delegates the choice of divorce to his wife. Not every expression of choice is valid; Hanafi jurisprudence insists on a specific condition. Additionally, we explore how qiyas and istihsan govern the validity of future tense responses.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the Essential Condition
The husband's delegation or the wife's response must mention 'nafs' (self). If either omits it, no divorce takes effect.
2
2. Classify Valid vs. Invalid Responses
Examine phrases like 'ikhtartu nafsi', 'abantu nafsi', 'harramtu nafsi', 'tallaqtu nafsi'—all valid. 'Ikhtartu' alone is invalid.
3

If she initially chooses herself then retracts to husband, divorce still occurs. If she initially chooses husband then retracts to self, nothing occurs and the option is lost.

For 'ana akhtaru nafsi', qiyas says it's a promise (invalid), but istihsan validates it. For 'ana utalliqu nafsi', both qiyas and istihsan rule it invalid.

Words like 'abantu', 'harramtu', and 'tallaqtu' when followed by 'nafsi' are valid responses indicating divorce.

Delegating Divorce with 'اختاري': Rulings, Conditions, and Disputes
Fleshes out tafwid using 'اختاري' with examples and a dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tafwid using 'اختاري', apply the condition of mentioning the self, and explain why the husband's intention of three does not result in three divorces.

Core Idea
When the husband says 'اختاري', the wife's choice results in one ba'in divorce, regardless of his intention, but she must mention herself in her response.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on the mechanism of tafwid al-talaq when the husband uses the word 'اختيار' (choice). The commentator clarifies the required form of the wife's response, addresses the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i on the number of divorces, and distinguishes between present and past tense replies using the tools of qiyas and istihsan. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly applying delegated divorce in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The husband delegates the choice to the wife using 'اختاري'. The ruling: if the wife responds correctly, one ba'in (irrevocable) divorce occurs.
This is a valid form of talaq in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the condition
The wife must mention herself (النفس) in her response. If she says only 'اخترت' without specifying herself, the response is invalid and no divorce occurs.
Examples include: 'اخترت نفسي', 'اخترت أبي', 'اخترت أمي', etc., all indicate divorce.
3
Analyze the dispute with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that if the husband intended three divorces, three occur. The Hanafi position is that only one ba'in divorce occurs, regardless of the husband's intention.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is the number of divorces: one vs. three.
4
Distinguish between past and future responses
A past tense response (e.g., 'اخترت نفسي') is valid and immediate. A future tense response (e.g., 'أنا أختار نفسي') is a promise: by qiyas no divorce occurs, but by istihsan it does. However, for 'طلقي نفسك' with future response, both qiyas and istihsan agree it is invalid.
The use of qiyas and istihsan here shows the Hanafi method of reasoning.

Commentary
This passage expands on the rules of tafwid (delegation of divorce) when the husband uses the word 'اختيار' (choice). The commentator begins by noting a dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i: if the husband says 'اختاري' intending three divorces, and the wife chooses herself, Shafi'i holds that three divorces occur. The Hanafi position, detailed later, is that only one ba'in divorce occurs, regardless of the husband's intention. The commentator then addresses the requirement that the wife must mention herself (النفس) in her response. If she says 'اخترت' without mentioning herself, it is invalid. Examples show that mentioning herself, her father, mother, etc., all indicate divorce. However, if she chooses her husband, it is not a valid response because she did not choose herself. The text distinguishes between present and past tense responses. If she says 'أنا أختار نفسي' (I will choose myself), by qiyas (analogy) no divorce occurs because it is a future promise, but by istihsan (juristic preference) it does occur, as it is common usage. Similarly, if the husband says 'طلقي نفسك' (divorce yourself) and she replies 'أنا أطلق نفسي' (I will divorce myself), both qiyas and istihsan agree it does not occur because it is a future action. Finally, if the husband says 'اختاري' and she responds with past tense forms like 'اخترت نفسي' (I have chosen myself), 'أبنت نفسي' (I have separated myself), 'حرمت نفسي' (I have forbidden myself), or 'طلقت نفسي' (I have divorced myself), it is a valid response and a ba'in (irrevocable) divorce occurs. This is because the word 'اختيار' itself leads to a ba'in divorce, unlike 'طلاق' which leads to raj'i divorce.

Key Terms
اختيار (ikhtiyar): The husband's delegation to the wife giving her the choice to divorce herself, using the word 'اختاري'.
بائن (ba'in): A type of divorce that is irrevocable; the husband cannot take back his wife without a new marriage contract and mahr.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning: here used to derive a ruling because the wife's statement in future tense is a promise, not a present act.

Delegating Divorce with 'طلقي نفسك' – Valid Responses and Number Scenarios
Details tafwid using 'طلقي' with responses and intra-Hanafi disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for various responses to 'طلقي نفسك' and apply the relied-upon Hanafi position when the wife exceeds the delegated number of divorces.

Core Idea
In tafwid, the wife's response must match the delegation; exceeding the number voids it per Abu Hanifa, but one divorce occurs per the mufta bihi view of Sahibayn.

When a husband delegates divorce to his wife with the phrase 'طلقي نفسك' (divorce yourself), specific verbal responses are valid and others are not. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra details which words constitute a valid divorce, how numbers affect the outcome, and an intra-Hanafi dispute on what happens when the wife exceeds the delegated number. Understanding these nuances prevents misapplication in marriage contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify valid responses to 'طلقي نفسك'
Read the opening lines: the wife must use past-tense forms like 'طلقت', 'أبنت', or 'حرمت'. These produce a raj'i divorce. 'اخترت' is not a valid response.
The word 'أبنت' (I separated) is considered a valid divorce word here.
2
Analyze the 'اختيار' (choice) scenario
If the husband says 'اختاري نفسك' (choose yourself) and intends three, the wife's divorce results in one ba'in divorce, not three. Distinguish this from 'طلقي' which yields raj'i.
The key difference: 'اختيار' gives the wife choice, not delegation of divorce itself.
3
Examine number scenarios in delegation
If the husband specifies a number (e.g., 'واحدة' or 'ثلاث') and the wife responds accordingly, the specified number applies by agreement. If she divorces once when told 'ثلاث', it is one by agreement.
4
Understand the intra-Hanafi dispute on exceeding the number
When the husband says 'واحدة' and the wife says 'ثلاث', Abu Hanifa says nothing occurs; the two disciples (Sahibayn) say one occurs. The mufta bihi is the disciples' view.
This dispute arises from different interpretations of delegation: whether the wife is bound by the delegation's limit.
5
Apply rulings on intention and slave wife
If the husband has no intention or intends one, the result is one raj'i. If he intends two, it is valid only if the wife is a slave woman, because that is the kind of her right (maximum of two divorces for a slave wife).
This shows consideration of the wife's status in Hanafi fiqh.

Delegation of Divorce: Wife's Valid and Invalid Responses
Rulings on tafwid al-talaq: wife's responses and husband's intentions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid and invalid responses when a husband delegates divorce to his wife and apply the Hanafi rulings on tafwid, including the intra-Hanafi dispute on 'ibān'.

Core Idea
Not every word the wife says in response to tafwid effects divorce—only explicit divorce terms or recognized metaphors like 'ibān'.

When a husband says 'Divorce yourself,' the wife's response is not free to be any expression. The Hanafi school carefully delineates which words effect divorce and which do not, based on the nature of the delegation. This lesson walks through the various responses and the intentions behind them, including a key intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether 'I have separated myself' counts as divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of delegation
Determine whether the husband said 'Divorce yourself' (tafwid) or 'Choose yourself' (tamlik al-khiyar). The rulings differ based on the wording.
2
Classify the wife's response
Is her response an explicit divorce word (tallaqt, abantu, harrimtu) or a non-divorce word (ikhtartu)? Explicit words effect raj'i divorce; 'ikhtartu' only works in tamlik al-khiyar.
3
Check the husband's intention when specified
If the husband intended a specific number of divorces, that affects the validity of the delegation and how many occur. For example, intending two only works for a slave wife.
4
Analyze the tamlik al-khiyar case
If the husband gives choice (ikhtari nafsaki), the wife's response 'I choose myself' effects only one ba'in divorce, no matter how many she intended.
5
Evaluate the dispute on 'abantu'
Understand the difference between Abu Hanifa (no divorce) and the Sahibayn (divorce occurs) regarding 'I have separated myself,' and know that the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah covers multiple rulings on tafwid al-talaq (delegation of divorce). The author first states that when a husband says 'Divorce yourself' and the wife responds with 'I have divorced myself,' 'I have separated myself,' or 'I have made myself forbidden,' these are valid responses and a single raj'i divorce occurs. However, if she says 'I have chosen myself,' it is not a valid response and no divorce occurs. Next, the author discusses tamlik al-khiyar (granting the right of choice). If the husband says 'Choose yourself' intending three divorces, and the wife chooses herself (i.e., divorces herself) three times or once, only one ba'in divorce takes place, not three. This is a well-known Hanafi ruling. Then, the text addresses scenarios where the husband specifies the number of divorces in his delegation. If he says 'Divorce yourself three times' intending three, and she divorces herself three, all three occur; if she divorces herself once, it is one by consensus. If he says 'Divorce yourself once' but she divorces herself three, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says nothing occurs, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say one occurs. If the husband has no intention or intends one, and she says 'I have divorced myself,' it is one raj'i divorce. If she divorces herself three and the husband intended that, all three occur. If the husband intends two divorces, the delegation is invalid unless the wife is a slave, because the number two is part of her right (slave women are only subject to two divorces). Finally, the passage elaborates on the distinction between 'ibanah' (separation) and 'ikhtiyar' (choice). It argues that 'ibanah' is a word of divorce, supported by the fact that if the husband says 'I separate you' intending divorce, it takes effect. In contrast, 'ikhtiyar' is not a word of divorce; if the husband says 'I choose you' or 'Choose,' it does not effect divorce. If the wife initiates 'I have chosen myself' and the husband says 'I accept,' no divorce occurs because it is only recognized as divorce when it is a response to a delegation of choice (tamlik). Abu Hanifa has an opinion that 'I have separated myself' does not effect divorce because the wife exceeded the delegated authority, as 'ibanah' is different from divorce. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that it does effect divorce, as it is a valid metaphor for divorce.

Key Terms
تفويض الطلاق (tafwid al-talaq): Delegation of divorce: the husband authorizes the wife to divorce herself.
أبانة (ibanah): Separation; a word that can be used as a metaphor for divorce.
اختيار (ikhtiyar): Choice; not a word of divorce unless used in the context of tamlik al-khiyar (granting the right of choice).
تمليك (tamlik): Granting ownership; in tafwid, the husband gives the wife ownership of the right to divorce, confined to the session.

When a wife says 'I chose the first' of three options, Abu Hanifa counts three divorces; the Sahibayn count one.

This passage addresses a common scenario in tafwid al-talaq (delegation of divorce) where the husband gives the option multiple times. Identify the husband's delegation formula

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divorce during marad al-mawt entitles the wife to inheritance only if death occurs before recovery and she did not consent.

The passage addresses a key exception: if a husband divorces his wife irrevocably during a terminal illness (marad al-mawt) without her consent, she inherits from him to prevent evasion (firar). Majority: no inheritance because recovery removes evasion suspicion. Zufar: she inherits because the divorce was given during illness.

The Effect of 'In Sha Allah' on Divorce: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Audibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce conditional on Allah's will and distinguish between the positions of al-Karkhi and al-Hindwani regarding the required audibility of the exception.

Core Idea
If a husband says 'You are divorced, in sha Allah' immediately, the divorce does not occur — but Hanafi jurists differ on whether he must hear his own utterance.

A husband who attaches 'in sha Allah' to a divorce statement intends to condition the divorce on Allah's will. In the Hanafi school, this exception (istithnā') negates the divorce if uttered immediately and connected to the oath. However, a subtle disagreement arose among later Hanafi jurists: does the husband need to audibly pronounce the exception, or is mere tongue movement sufficient? An exception in a divorce pronouncement reduces the count; 'three except one' means two.

In Hanafi law, when a husband pronounces a specific number of divorces and then immediately makes an exception (illā), the divorces take effect only for the remainder. This lesson shows how the arithmetic works for simple cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pronouncement
Note the stated number of divorces and the excepted number after 'illā'.
The exception must be immediate and connected (muttasil).
2
Apply the exception
Subtract the excepted number from the stated number.
This is simple arithmetic: excepted number cannot exceed stated number (otherwise the exception is invalid, but that case is not covered here).
3
State the result
The remaining number is the actual number of divorces that occur.
The divorces are valid (sahih) and effective immediately.

This applies in the Hanafi school as a straightforward application of the principle that exceptions subtract from the stated number.

Conditional Divorce on the Will of a Third Party
Ruling on the validity and limits of a conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a conditional divorce on another's will is valid and identify the limits of the assembly.

Core Idea
A divorce conditioned on another's will is limited to the immediate gathering — if he leaves without willing, it is void.

Divorce can be made conditional on the will of a third party, such as Zaid. The Hanafi school specifies that the condition is tied to the assembly (majlis) where the pronouncement is made or where the third party learns of it. Understanding the temporal and spatial limits is crucial to avoid unintended effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional formula
The husband uses the phrase 'You are divorced if Zaid wills.' The divorce is suspended on Zaid's will.
2
Determine the assembly (majlis)
The condition is limited to the specific gathering in which the pronouncement is made. If Zaid is present, it must be in that same gathering. If absent, it is limited to the gathering where he learns of the condition.
The majlis is a classic Hanafi concept; leaving the gathering voids the condition.
3
Identify the form of Zaid's will
Zaid must express his will by saying 'I will what so-and-so has referred to me.' He does not need to mention divorce or intend it specifically.
The acceptance is not a divorce pronouncement; it is a simple expression of will.
4
Recognize the consequence of leaving
If Zaid leaves the gathering without expressing his will, the condition becomes void (batal) and the divorce does not occur.
The condition is not perpetual; it is confined to the immediate assembly.
5
Apply the ruling to a variation
Consider if Zaid is absent at the time of pronouncement. The condition is tied to the assembly where Zaid learns of it. He must will in that same assembly or the condition is void.
This ensures the condition remains limited and certain.

Commentary
This passage deals with a conditional divorce where the condition is the will of a third party (e.g., Zaid). The divorce is suspended and becomes effective only if Zaid declares his will in the same assembly (majlis) where the condition was pronounced, or, if Zaid is absent, in the assembly where he learns of the condition. If Zaid leaves the assembly without expressing his will, the condition is void and the divorce does not occur. The form of Zaid's expression is to say 'I will what so-and-so has referred to me,' and he does not need to mention or intend the divorce specifically.

Key Terms
المجلس (al-majlis): The meeting or session in which the condition is pronounced; the condition is limited to that session.
