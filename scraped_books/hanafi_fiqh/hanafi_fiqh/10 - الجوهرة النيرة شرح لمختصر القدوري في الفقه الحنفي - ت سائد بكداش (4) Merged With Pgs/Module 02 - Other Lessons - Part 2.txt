Ruling on Gifting Non-Existent Items (Flour in Wheat, etc.)
Ruling with rational proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on gifting a non-existent item, explain why it is fasid, and distinguish it from common property, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding milk in udder.

Core Idea
A non-existent item cannot be gifted; the contract is void ab initio and requires renewal.

Gifting something that does not yet exist as a separate object—like flour still inside wheat—raises the issue of ma'dum (non-existence). The Hanafi school rules such a gift fasid (invalid) because the object of the contract is not capable of ownership. However, what about milk inside the udder? Is it like non-existent flour or like common property? This lesson unpacks the ruling and the nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that gifting flour in wheat or oil in sesame is fasid (invalid). Identify the act: gift (hiba), and the ruling: fasid.
Fasid in Hanafi terminology means invalid due to a defect, but not void (batil); however here it is explained as void because the object is non-existent.
2
Locate the dalil aqli (rational proof)
The proof given is rational: the gifted thing is ma'dum (non-existent), and the non-existent cannot be owned. Therefore the contract is void. This is a rational argument (dalil aqli) based on the nature of ownership.
No naqli (Quran/Sunnah) proof is cited; the reasoning is purely rational.
3
Contrast with common property (musha')
The text contrasts with musha' (undivided share), which is existent and can be gifted or sold. Understand why musha' is valid: it exists as a share, only possession is impeded temporarily.
The impediment for musha' is qabd (possession), not existence.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on analogous cases
The passage presents two positions: (a) from al-Hidayah: items like milk in udder are like musha', valid if possession is authorized; (b) from al-Karkhi: they are like non-existent, always invalid. Identify the basis of each view.
This shows that 'existence' can be interpreted differently: physical separation vs. potential existence.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Given a scenario where someone gifts 'half the milk from this cow next month', determine the ruling using the Hanafi principle and then note the conflicting views.
Practical thamarah al-khilaf: whether the gift is valid or not depends on which view is followed.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the commentator on the matn regarding the gift of non-existent items. The ruling states that gifting flour that is still within wheat, or oil still within sesame seeds, is a fasid (invalid) gift. The reason given is that the gifted object is ma'dum (non-existent) at the time of the contract, and the non-existent cannot be an object of ownership. Therefore the contract is batil (void) and cannot be validated except by a new contract after the item comes into existence. The contrast is made with common property (musha'), which, although undivided, is existent and thus a valid object of gift or sale. Gift When Item Is Already in the Donee's Hand
Ruling on gift with existing possession and substitution principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for gift possession when the donee already holds the item and apply the principle of substitution of possessions.

Core Idea
Existing possession suffices if it matches the gift's required possession type.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains a key rule in Hanafi gift law: if the gifted item is already in the donee's possession, no new physical taking is needed. However, the type of existing possession matters—trust possessions (e.g., deposit, loan) can substitute for trust, and liability possessions (e.g., usurped goods) can substitute for liability or even trust, but trust cannot substitute for liability. Let's analyse the logic and examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule on possession
The passage states that if the gifted property is in the donee's hand, he owns it without renewing possession. Note that possession (qabd) is a condition for the gift to become binding.
The rule applies whether the existing possession is trust (amanah) or liability (daman).
2
Understand the principle of substitution
The principle: 'whenever the two possessions are of the same type, one substitutes for the other; if they differ, liability possession substitutes for trust, but not vice versa.' This is the key legal logic.
This is a qiyas-based reasoning (dalil aqli).
3
Analyse the examples for same-type possession
Example 1: If an item is usurped (liability) and then sold by a valid sale, no new possession is needed because both are liability. Example 2: If an item is a loan (trust) and then gifted, no new possession is needed because both are trust.
In both cases the possessions are of the same type, so substitution is automatic.
4
Analyse the examples for different-type possession
If an item is deposit (trust) and then sold (which requires liability possession), a new possession is needed because trust cannot substitute for liability. Conversely, if an item is usurped (liability) and then gifted (trust), no new possession is needed because liability substitutes for trust.
This asymmetry is crucial: liability possession can replace trust, but trust cannot replace liability.
5
Note the exception for pledge (rahn)
The passage begins to discuss the case of a pledge (rahn), but the text ends mid-sentence. Typically, a pledge is a liability possession, so if a pledged item is later gifted, the existing possession may suffice depending on the type.
The full rule for rahn is covered in other texts, but the principle of substitution still applies.

Commentary
This passage explains a key rule in Hanafi gift law: if the item being gifted is already in the possession of the donee (e.g., as a deposit, loan, or even as a usurped item), then the gift is valid without requiring a new physical taking of possession. The reason is that the condition of qabd (possession) is already fulfilled. However, the type of existing possession matters. Substitution of Possession in Gifts: When Prior Possession Suffices
Ruling with proof using qiyas and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of substitution of possession to determine when a new qabd is required for a gift, and identify the exception for pledges based on qiyas.

Core Idea
If the donee already holds the object under a possession of the same nature, no new physical takeover is needed for the gift.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi rule regarding the gift contract (hiba): when the object being gifted is already in the possession of the donee, the condition of possession (qabd) may be satisfied without a new act of taking possession. The text elaborates on the principle that two possessions of the same type can substitute for each other, and provides illustrative cases from sale, trust, and pledge. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly applying the condition of qabd in gift transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Condition
The passage states that if the gifted item is already in the donee's hand, he owns it by the gift even without renewing possession. Identify that the condition for a gift is possession (qabd), and here it is already fulfilled.
The ruling is based on the fulfillment of the condition, not the absence of it.
2
Understand the Principle of Substitution
The commentator introduces the principle: when two possessions are of the same type, one substitutes for the other. If they differ, the guaranteed (madhmun) substitutes for the non-guaranteed, but not vice versa. Grasp this hierarchy.
Types: trust (amanah) vs. guaranteed (daman).

The asl is a sale after usurpation: no new qabd needed because both possessions are guaranteed. The far' is a gift after trust: no new qabd needed because both are trust. Identify the illa: agreement of possession types.
The qiyas is jaliy (clear) because the illa is explicit.

Note the Exception for Pledge (Rahn)
According to the relied-upon position, a pledge requires new possession because the possession types differ. However, there is a narration that it does not. Understand this intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The mufta bihi is that new qabd is needed for rahn.

Father's Gift to Minor Child: Ownership Without Possession
Ruling on gift to minor without possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father's gift to his minor child, identify the conditions for ownership without possession, and apply the exceptions.

Core Idea
A father's gift to his minor child is owned by contract alone, without physical delivery.

In Hanafi fiqh, gifts generally require possession (qabd) for ownership to transfer. However, the father's guardianship over his minor child's property creates a constructive possession, making the gift valid immediately upon contract. This lesson explores the scope, conditions, and exceptions of this important rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The matn states that the son owns the gift by the mere contract (bil-'aqd). The commentary explains the basis: the father has constructive custody (fi qabd al-ab), so he deputizes for the minor's possession.
This is a special dispensation due to wilayah (guardianship).
2
Distinguish between items in father's control and those held by others
The rule applies whether the item is physically with the father or with his depositary (muda'), as the depositary's hand is like the father's. But it does not apply if the item is mortgaged, usurped, or sold with a corrupt sale, because those are in the hand or ownership of another.
These exceptions prevent the father from effectively taking possession.
3
Extend the ruling to other caregivers
The same ruling applies to the mother (when the father is dead and no executor exists) and to anyone who supports the child (mu'il). The key is that the caregiver has de facto guardianship and custody.
This extension is based on need and the same rationale of constructive possession.
4
Apply the recommended procedure for the father
The father should publicize the gift or have witnesses to avoid future denial. Since the minor cannot assert ownership, external evidence protects the gift.
This is a cautionary measure (sadd al-dhari'ah) for certainty.

2

Abu Hanifa holds the invalid position for rich; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold it is valid for rich as well.

Abu Hanifa argues that sadaqah is purely for Allah's reward (one), so it must be given to the needy; giving to rich turns it into hibah. Hibah to two rich is invalid because the intended recipient (the rich) are two, and a single gift to two non-relatives is problematic.
The intention (niyyah) is the key differentiator.
4

They argue that both sadaqah and hibah are tamlik bi-ghayr badal (transfer without compensation), so they should be treated identically. If hibah to two rich is valid, then sadaqah to two rich should also be valid.
They focus on the form rather than the intention.

The relied-upon position (as supported by Al-Khujandi) is the distinction: charity to two rich is invalid, but if intended as a gift, it may be valid subject to other rules. The thamarah al-khilaf is whether the recipients become owners.

Attached Increase Bars Gift Revocation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An attached increase makes a gift irrevocable because the increase cannot be returned.

The Hanafi school permits revocation of a gift unless certain conditions bar it. One key bar is an 'attached increase' (ziyadah muttasilah) – an inseparable enhancement created by the donee. This lesson explores what counts as attached, why it blocks revocation, and where scholars differ on specific cases like intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Classify the increase
Determine if the increase is physically inseparable (attached) or separable. Examples: fattening, building, dyeing are attached; a newborn calf is separate.
Attachment is based on physical inseparability, not value alone.
2
Apply the barring principle
If the increase is attached, the donor cannot revoke because returning the increase alone is impossible (it cannot be removed) and returning both was not part of the original contract.
The increase was not gifted, so it cannot be taken back without a new agreement.

Pregnancy before birth is an attached increase – no revocation until birth. If the gifted animal was already pregnant, revocation is allowed only before the pregnancy becomes evident. Transformation (eggs to chicks) is also attached.
Once the increase becomes manifest, the gift becomes irrevocable.

Some Hanafi scholars argue that intercourse alone (even without pregnancy) bars revocation due to its legal effects. The author states the correct view is that revocation remains possible unless pregnancy occurs.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf; the relied-upon view is that revocation is allowed.
5
Distinguish separate increases
If the increase is separate (e.g., a calf born after the gift), the donor may revoke the original but not the increase. The contract covers only the mother, not the offspring.
The donee keeps the separate increase.

The ruling is grounded in the principle that in gratuitous contracts, no guarantee of safety is implied. The commentator uses an analogy to inheritance to reinforce the point.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage deals with a gift (hiba). If the gifted property is destroyed or claimed by a rightful owner (mustahiqq), the donee is liable for its value (damān) but cannot seek compensation from the donor.
The ruling is declaratory: no right of recourse.
2
Understand the reasoning
Two reasons are given: (1) the donor did not guarantee the safety of the property; (2) the donee acquired ownership without paying any consideration ('iwad).
Gratuitous contracts do not carry an implied warranty of title.
3
Analyze the qiyas (analogy)
The commentator draws an analogy to inheritance: if an heir inherits property and it is later claimed, the heir cannot recourse to the estate. The common illa (effective cause) is 'ownership acquired without compensation'.
The analogy is a qiyas jali (clear analogy).
4
Note the extension to borrowing
The same ruling applies to a borrower (musta'ir) under a contract of gratuitous loan: if the borrowed item is claimed, the borrower cannot recourse to the lender.
Reinforces the principle for all gratuitous transfers.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the donor is liable because the gift implies a guarantee of title. The Hanafi response is that the donor never guaranteed safety, and the donee accepted the risk.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul principles.

The Nature of Gift Conditional on Compensation: Dispute with Zufar
Ruling on gift with condition of compensation and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi majority view on hiba bi-shart al-iwad and compare it with Zufar’s view, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Hiba bi-shart al-iwad is a gift initially, a sale after mutual possession – mutual possession flips its ruling.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira delves into the unique contract of a gift with a condition of compensation (hiba bi-shart al-iwad). The Sharh clarifies the hybrid nature: initially it is a gift, but after mutual possession it acquires the rulings of a sale. This leads to a significant intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar ibn Hudhayl, who treats it as a sale from the outset. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it affects multiple practical rulings such as validity of shared property, option of inspection, and pre-emption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the matn statement: 'وإذا وهب بشرط العوض: اعتبر التقابض في العوضين'. The Sharh explains that mutual possession is required. Note the immediate consequence: before possession each party can withhold and the contract is invalid if the property is shared (musha').
This is the foundation – the condition of possession is what differentiates it from a pure gift.
2
Unpack al-Khujandi’s formula
Al-Khujandi summarizes: 'الهبة بشرط العوض: هبة في الابتداء، بيع في الانتهاء'. Analyze what 'ibtida’an' and 'intiha’an' mean for legal effects. For example, gift rulings apply before possession (no immediate ownership transfer for shared property), and sale rulings apply after possession (defect return, option of inspection, pre-emption).
This hybrid formula is the key to understanding the Hanafi position.
3
Contrast with Zufar’s view
Zufar ibn Hudhayl maintains that hiba bi-shart al-iwad is a sale from beginning to end (كالبيع ابتداء وانتهاء). Identify what this changes: he would allow immediate transfer even if the property is shared, and apply sale rules from contract formation.
Zufar’s view aligns more closely with the Shafi’i school.
4
Derive the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
List the differences: (1) validity of shared property (musha‘) – under majority view, invalid until possession; under Zufar, valid immediately. (2) Timing of khiyar al-ru’ya and radd bi al-‘ayb – only after possession in majority view, from contract in Zufar’s. (3) Shuf‘a – only after possession according to majority, immediately according to Zufar.
These differences determine the operative fatwa.
5
Apply the father-minor son scenario
Read the additional ruling: a father cannot compensate for a gift given to his minor son from the son’s property, even if the gift was conditional on compensation. This is because the father would be spending the child’s property as a gratuitous donation, which is impermissible. This illustrates that the gift aspect remains relevant even in the hybrid.
This furu‘ shows the Hanafi commitment to the gift nature in specific contexts.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling of a gift given with a condition of compensation (hiba bi-shart al-iwad). The matn states that mutual possession (taqabud) of both compensations (the gift and the counter-value) is required. The sharh explains that the compensation itself is considered a separate gift (hiba mubtada’a), so until both parties possess their respective items, each can withhold, and the contract is invalidated by partial possession (shuyu‘). Once both possess, the contract becomes valid and then takes on the legal rulings of a sale (bay‘), including: return for defect (radd bi al-‘ayb), option of inspection (khiyar al-ru’ya), and pre-emption (shuf‘a). This hybrid nature is summarized by al-Khujandi: it is a gift initially but a sale ultimately. The difference matters for issues like validity when the item is shared (musha‘): in a gift, shared property is not valid without division, but in a sale it is valid. Here, since it is a gift initially, if the property is shared, the contract is invalid until possession occurs, unlike a pure sale. Zufar ibn Hudhayl differed, holding that it is a sale from the outset in all respects. The passage also notes that a father cannot compensate from his minor child's property for a gift given to the child, even if the gift was conditional on compensation, because that would be using the child's property for gratuitous donation, which is impermissible.

الهبة بشرط العوض (al-hiba bi-shart al-‘iwaḍ): A gift where the donor stipulates that the donee must give something in return; it is a conditional gift that resembles a sale.
التقابض (al-taqābuḍ): Mutual physical possession of the two compensations (gift item and counter-value) by both parties.
خيار الرؤية (khiyār al-ru’ya): The option to rescind a contract after seeing the item if it was not seen before contracting.

Conditions for Revocation of a Gift
Rulings on revocation of gifts: conditional revocation invalid, mutual agreement valid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional revocation of a gift and explain the effect of mutual agreement to revoke.

Core Idea
Conditional revocation of a gift is invalid; mutual agreement to revoke is valid, even where unilateral revocation is prohibited.

In Hanafi fiqh, the revocation of a gift (ruju‘) is governed by specific rules. This passage clarifies two key scenarios: a donor cannot make revocation contingent on a future event, and if both parties agree to revoke, the gift returns to the donor even in cases where unilateral revocation is forbidden. Understanding these distinctions prevents common errors in gift transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios presented
The passage covers (1) a conditional revocation statement and (2) a mutual agreement to revoke. Read each carefully.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each scenario
For conditional revocation: it is not valid (batil). For mutual agreement: it is valid (sahih) and effective.
The ruling on mutual agreement applies even when unilateral revocation is prohibited (e.g., gifts to relatives).
3
Understand the rationale for conditional revocation being invalid
The reason given is that terminations (fusukh) involving ownership transfer do not accept suspension (ta‘liq). This is a general principle in Hanafi law.
Ta‘liq is not allowed in contracts of tamlik (ownership transfer).
4
Note the effect of a valid revocation
Upon valid revocation (by court order or mutual consent), ownership returns to the donor without requiring new possession (qabd), similar to rescission of a sale (iqala).
Possession is not a condition for the transfer of ownership in such cases.
5
Compare with the Shafi‘i position
Imam al-Shafi‘i allows limited conditional revocation, unlike the Hanafi school. Understand the basis of disagreement.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue; both positions are respected in their respective schools.

Commentary
This passage presents two rulings on revocation of gifts. First, a conditional revocation (ta‘liq al-ruju‘) is invalid. For example, if the donor says, 'When the month starts, I have revoked the gift,' the revocation does not take effect. The reasoning is that acts of termination (fusukh) that involve transfer of ownership cannot be made conditional on a future event (ta‘liq), similar to how one cannot say 'If Zayd comes, I divorce my wife' in a way that makes divorce conditional. This is a general principle in Hanafi law: contracts that transfer ownership (tamlik) cannot be suspended on a condition. Second, if both the donor and donee mutually agree to revoke the gift in a situation where unilateral revocation is prohibited (such as a gift to blood relatives or a gift with compensation), the mutual agreement is valid. The gift then reverts to the donor. Upon such revocation, whether by court order or mutual consent, ownership returns to the donor without requiring a new possession (qabd). This is analogous to a sale: when a sale is rescinded by mutual agreement (iqala), the ownership returns to the seller without the need for fresh possession.

Key Terms

التعليق (al-ta‘līq): Suspending a legal effect on the occurrence of a future condition.
الفسوخ (al-fusūkh): Terminations or cancellations of contracts.
