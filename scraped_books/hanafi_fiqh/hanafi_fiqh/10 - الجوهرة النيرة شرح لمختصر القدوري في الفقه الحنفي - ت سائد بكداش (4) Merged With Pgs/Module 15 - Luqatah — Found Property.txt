Selling Deposit When Spoilage Feared: Analogous to Luqatah
Ruling on depositor selling deposit to prevent spoilage, with analogy to found property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on selling a deposit when spoilage is feared, distinguishing between scenarios with and without a judge, and extend the ruling to found property (luqatah) via qiyas.

Core Idea
A depositor may sell a perishing deposit with judicial permission or independently if no judge exists; the same rule applies to found property by analogy.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: a depositor holds an item that is at risk of spoilage. Islamic law provides a clear procedure to preserve the owner's wealth. The ruling also extends by analogy to found property (luqatah). Understanding this ruling demonstrates how necessity permits sale without explicit authorization, but with safeguards.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is the depositor selling the deposit due to fear of spoilage. The ruling is that it is permissible (jā'iz) but subject to conditions.
The ruling is not absolute; it depends on the presence of a judge.
2
Determine the condition – existence of a judge
If a judge exists in the city, the depositor must raise the matter before the judge and seek permission. If no judge exists, he may sell directly without permission.
These are the two distinct scenarios outlined in the text.
3
Recognize the obligation after sale
After selling, the depositor must preserve the sale price (thaman) for the owner. This is a wajib (obligatory) step.
Preserving the price ensures the owner's right is protected.
4
Extend by analogy to found property (luqatah)
The passage states 'wa ʿala hadha al-luqatah' – the same ruling applies to found property that is at risk of spoilage. The common illa is the fear of loss of value.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
5
Verify the illa (effective cause)
The illa is the risk of spoilage (khawf al-fasād). Both deposit and found property share this cause, so the ruling is transferred by analogy.
The illa is jaliyyah (evident), making the qiyas strong.

Ruling and its qiyas derivation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A poor person may take a small ownerless estate by analogy to found property (luqatah).

This lesson examines a specific case: a stranger dies in someone's house, leaving no known heirs and only a small amount of property. The house owner is poor. The Hanafi school permits the house owner to take this property for himself. The reasoning is based on analogy (qiyas) to luqatah (found property). Understanding this ruling illuminates how Hanafi fiqh balances property rights, need, and practicality in cases where ownership is unclear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Read the passage and note the key facts: a stranger (gharib) dies in a man's house, no known heir, leaves a small amount (around five dirhams), the house owner is poor. The ruling: he may take it for himself.
The ruling is affirmative: 'فله أن يأخذها لنفسه' (he may take it for himself).
2
Understand the basis: analogy to luqatah
The text says 'لأنه في معنى اللقطة' (because it is in the meaning of luqatah). Luqatah is found property. Recognise that Hanafi fiqh treats this case like a lost item when there is no apparent owner and the value is small.
The qiyas is clear: the illa (effective cause) is the absence of a known owner and the small value.

Identify the four elements: Asl (original case) = luqatah; Hukm of asl = permissible for the finder to keep (after proper procedure); Far' (new case) = small estate of deceased stranger; Illa = lack of known owner and small value. Confirm the analogy is valid (qiyas jaliy).
The Hanafi school often uses qiyas jaliy when the illa is clear and explicit.
4
Note the conditions and implications
The ruling is conditional: the deceased must be a stranger, no known heir, estate small (fuindrant of five dirhams), and the house owner poor. If any condition changes, the ruling may differ. For example, if the estate is large, it goes to the public treasury.
The phrase 'ونحوها' indicates that five dirhams is a guideline for 'small'.
5
Consider the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require that such property be handed over to the state or used for charity. The Hanafi view is based on need and the absence of any claimant. Recognise that this is a legitimate difference of opinion.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi within the school, but other views are respected.

The Finder's Authority to Hire Out the Foundling: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Commentary on Al-Quduri's rulings on finder's authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on hiring out a foundling, apply the qiyas to the uncle, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The finder cannot hire out the foundling because he lacks ownership of his usufructs, unlike the mother.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses the scope of the finder's guardianship over a foundling. While the finder may accept gifts and apprentice the child, hiring him out is disputed within the Hanafi school. Al-Quduri permits it, but the sounder view in Al-Jami' al-Saghir prohibits it, based on an analogy to the uncle. Understanding this ruling requires analyzing the concept of ownership of usufructs (manafi‘).

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissible and prohibited actions of the finder
Read the passage and list what the finder may and may not do: cannot dispose of property, may accept gifts, may apprentice, and the dispute on hiring out.
This sets up the scope of the finder's wilayah.
2
Locate the disputed act and the two riwayas
The disputed act is hiring out (i'jarah). Al-Quduri's riwaya permits it; Al-Jami' al-Saghir prohibits it. Note which is called 'asahh (soundest).
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf, not an inter-madhhab one.
3
Analyze the qiyas arguments
The prohibiting view uses qiyas: the finder is like the uncle (cannot hire out), while the mother is unlike because she owns the usufructs. Trace the illah: lack of ownership of manafi‘.
The permissive view likely considers hiring out as a beneficial act, but the text does not elaborate its qiyas.
4
Determine the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The commentator states that the view from Al-Jami' al-Saghir is 'asahh, so the mufta bihi is prohibition. Confirm that this is the operative position for fatwa.
Authority weight: Al-Jami' al-Saghir is a Zahir al-Riwayah source.

Foundling's Injury and Inheritance: The Public Treasury's Right
Rulings on foundling's injury and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on a foundling's injury and inheritance, and explain why the finder has no claim.

Core Idea
The foundling's injury and inheritance belong to the public treasury, not the finder.

When a foundling is discovered, the finder assumes care but does not acquire any property rights over the foundling. This lesson clarifies that any injury caused by the foundling, as well as his inheritance, are the responsibility and right of the Muslim public treasury (bayt al-mal). Understanding this prevents the common misconception that the finder inherits or is liable for the foundling's actions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the foundling's legal status
The foundling is considered a free Muslim with no known lineage. Therefore, he has no specific heir except the Muslim community represented by the state treasury.
This status determines all subsequent rulings.
2
Identify who bears the foundling's injury
If the foundling causes injury to another (jinaayah), the compensation is paid from bayt al-mal, not by the finder.
The text states: 'وجناية اللقيط : في بيت المال'
3
Determine the inheritance of a foundling
If the foundling dies, his inheritance goes to bayt al-mal, not to the finder.
The text: 'وميراثه : لبيت المال، لا للذي التقطه'
4
Apply the ruling on accidental killing
If the foundling is killed accidentally, the killer's 'aqilah must pay diyah to bayt al-mal because the foundling is free and has no heir.
The text: 'فإذا قتل اللقيط خطأ : فالدية على عاقلة القاتل لبيت المال'

Definition and Ruling of Luqatah
Definition of lost property and ruling on picking it up with witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define luqatah, state its default ruling (mandub/wajib), and identify the condition (witnesses) that makes it a trust (amanah).

Core Idea
Taking lost property with witnesses makes it a trust; without witness, you may be liable.

This passage from Kitab al-Luqatah defines lost property and outlines the ruling for the finder. The author explains that picking up an item is generally better than leaving it, but becomes obligatory if loss is feared. The key condition for the finder to be a trustee (amin) is to take it with witnesses, intending to preserve and return it. This lesson breaks down the definition, conditions, and legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Define luqatah linguistically and technically
Identify the Arabic term (luqatah) and note its pronunciation variants. Understand that it refers to property found and picked up.
The word may be read as 'luqta' or 'luqata'.
2
Identify the default ruling on taking luqatah
The text states that taking it is better (afdal) than leaving it, except for camels and cattle. Under what condition does it become obligatory?
It becomes wajib when the finder fears loss or destruction of the property.
3
Analyze the condition for amanah status
The matn requires the finder to make witnesses (ishhad) that he takes it to preserve and return. Why is witness required?
This protects the finder from liability; without witnesses, the item may be considered a guaranty (daman).
4
Distinguish between types of property
Camels and cattle are excluded because they can protect themselves. Other items (e.g., money, jewels) are more vulnerable to loss.
The ruling varies based on the nature of the item.
5
Derive the legal effect (athar) of proper taking
If taken with witnesses and with intention to return, the item is an amanah. The finder is not liable for loss without negligence.
This is a key Hanafi distinction: amanah versus maddaman.

Finder's Liability Without Declaration: Abu Yusuf vs. The Two
Intra-Hanafi dispute on finder's liability without declaration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on finder's liability without declaration and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Confessing to the cause of guarantee requires proof of exemption to avoid liability.

In this lesson, we examine a dispute among the three Hanafi imams regarding the liability of a finder who takes lost property without making a declaration (ishhad). The finder claims he took it for the owner, but the owner denies, and the item is lost. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the finder liable, while Abu Yusuf exempts him. By understanding the arguments, you will grasp how Hanafi scholars weigh admissions and claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
A finds a lost item (luqata), does not make a declaration (ishhad), later claims he took it for the owner, but the owner denies this, and the item perishes in the finder's possession. What is the ruling?
This sets the stage for the dispute.
2
State the majority position (the Two)
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the finder is liable because he admitted to the cause of guarantee (taking) and his claim of taking for the owner is an unproven exemption.
The majority view is that the finder must compensate the owner.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds the finder is not liable, applying the principle that the denier's word is accepted (al-qawl qawl al-munki).
Abu Yusuf sees the finder as denying the owner's claim of a guaranteed taking.
4
Analyze the core disagreement
The disagreement hinges on whether the finder's admission to the taking makes him a confessor or a denier of liability. Abu Yusuf sees the finder as denying the owner's claim of a guaranteed taking, while the Two see the finder as already having confessed to the taking itself, so he must prove his good intent.
This is a methodological difference in interpreting the finder's statements.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The majority position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is relied upon in fatwa, making the finder liable in this scenario.
Thus, in practice, the finder must compensate the owner unless he proves his claim.

Intent and Liability in Luqata: The Finder Who Takes for Himself
Intra-Hanafi dispute on finder's liability after self-interested taking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a finder who takes luqata for himself and explain why Zufar's view is not the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A finder who takes with bad intent becomes a usurper; only return to the owner exonerates.

When someone finds lost property and takes it with the intention of keeping or consuming it, does returning it to the original spot clear him? This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and Imam Zufar. The key is the shift from trustee to usurper based on intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two intents
The passage describes two scenarios: taking luqata 'to eat it' or 'to keep it for himself'. Both share the intent to appropriate.
Intent distinguishes this from taking to return.
2
State the majority Hanafi rule
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad rule that such a finder is not exonerated until he delivers the item to the owner or the owner's agent.
The rule is based on the finder becoming a usurper (ghasib).
3
Analyze Zufar's opposing view and analogy
Zufar argues that returning to the place is sufficient because it resembles the case where the finder takes to return and then returns to the place; both involve returning to the original location.
Zufar's analogy (qiyas) focuses on the physical act of returning.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response to Zufar
The majority responds that the analogy fails because the intent differs. The finder who takes for himself becomes a usurper; a usurper is only exonerated by returning to the owner or his agent.
The ruling is thus based on the nature of possession (ghasb), not mere return to place.

Rules of Announcing and Disposing of a Lost Item
Rulings on announcement, joint finding, and disposal of lost items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on announcing a lost item, determine the finder's obligations, and apply the rule for charity when the owner does not appear.

Core Idea
Announce the lost item publicly; if the owner appears after charity, the finder may confirm the charity or compensate.

Finding a lost item (luqata) triggers a set of obligations and options. The finder must announce it publicly, and if the owner does not appear, charity may be given on the owner's behalf. But what happens if the owner shows up later? The Hanafi school provides a balanced ruling that respects the owner's rights while recognizing the finder's goodwill.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method of announcement
The text states that announcement must be made aloud (jahren) in markets, mosque doors, the place of finding, and gatherings. Understand that this is a public, vocal announcement, not a silent notice.
The phrase 'إنما يكون جهراً' emphasizes the vocal nature.
2
Handle joint finders
If two people find the item together, they both announce it and share in the ruling. If the owner appears, they hand it over jointly; otherwise, they both give charity or decide together.
The ruling implies partnership in obligation and discretion.
3
Distinguish the options after the owner fails to appear

Spending on Found Animals: Court Permission Required for Reimbursement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on spending by a finder on a found animal: without court permission the finder is a volunteer; with permission the expense becomes a debt on the owner.

Core Idea
Spending on a found animal without court permission makes the finder a volunteer; with permission it becomes a debt.

When you find a lost animal, you must care for it. But who pays? The Hanafi school draws a clear line: if you spend without asking the qadi, you are a volunteer. If you get his permission, the owner owes you. This lesson explains the ruling and its rationale, including the role of the judge’s authority over the absent owner’s property.

Guided Path

Identify the two cases
The passage presents two scenarios: spending without court permission and spending with court permission. Recognize the key phrases 'بغير إذن الحاكم' and 'بأمره'.
2

Without permission, the finder is 'متبرع' (volunteer) – he cannot claim repayment. With permission, the expense becomes 'ديناً' (a debt) on the owner.
The status affects the right to reimbursement.
3
Understand the underlying reason
The finder lacks 'ولاية' (legal authority) over the owner's property, so his act is voluntary. The judge has authority over the absent owner's property, so his order creates a binding debt.
This is the core ta'lil (reasoning) of the ruling.

If the finder guarantees the found animal (e.g., to a poor person), he becomes its owner, and his subsequent charity is from his own property – he cannot reclaim from the poor. Similarly, if the poor person guarantees it, he cannot reclaim from the finder because charity is a gratuitous contract.
This sub-ruling reinforces the no-recourse principle.

Condition of Evidence and Luqatah in the Haram

Core Idea
Evidence protects against false claims; the Haram does not change the rules of luqatah in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A correct description permits, but does not compel, the finder to hand over a lost item.

When someone finds a lost item and a claimant appears, how much proof is enough? The Hanafi school holds that a correct description makes handover permissible, but stops short of compulsory return. Identify the two rulings

This ruling is not disputed among the schools.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Mukhtasar al-Quduri regarding the rulings of luqatah (lost property). The author explains that when a claimant appears, he must provide proof (bayyinah) of ownership. If he cannot give proof but gives a correct description (alamah), the finder is allowed to hand over the item, but is not compelled by the court to do so. This is the Hanafi position, opposed by Imam Malik and Imam al-Shafi'i who hold that the finder is compelled once the description matches. The description includes details like weight, count, tie, and container of the item. There is a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school: some analogize it to a wakil (agent) who is not compelled to hand over a deposit, while others say compulsion because ownership is not as clear as in a deposit. The author then states that charity from luqatah cannot be given to a rich person. If the finder is rich, he cannot use the item for himself; if poor, he may use it. A rich finder may give charity from the luqatah to his poor relatives (father, son, mother, wife) by analogy to his own permission to use if he were poor. The hadith quoted is from Sahih al-Bukhari, indicating the permissibility of handing over upon recognition of the item's description.

Ruling on the Finder of Lost Property: Rich vs Poor and Charity to Relatives
Ruling on finder's use and charity to relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a finder's use of lost property based on the finder's wealth, and explain the exception for charity to poor relatives.

Core Idea
A rich finder cannot use lost property, but can give it in charity to his poor relatives; a poor finder may use it due to need.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a person who finds lost property (luqatah). The school distinguishes between a rich finder and a poor finder. While a rich finder is forbidden from benefiting from the found item, a poor finder is permitted. Additionally, the rich finder may give the item in charity to his needy father, son, mother, or wife. The rationale combines property rights, need, and juristic preference (istiḥsān).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the finder's benefit from the lost item (al-intifāʿ bihā). The ruling differs based on the finder's wealth.
The act is the finder using the property for himself or giving it in charity.
2
Determine the legal basis for the prohibition on the rich finder
The text states the item is the property of another (māl al-ghayr), so using it without consent is not permissible. The exception for the poor is based on need (ḥājah) and the consensus (ijmāʿ) that the poor are eligible for charity.
The prohibition is rooted in ownership rights; permission is granted only to the poor.
3
Analyze the exception for charity to poor relatives
The rich finder may give the found item in charity to his father, son, mother, or wife if they are poor. This is justified by qiyās: just as a poor finder can benefit himself, so too can a rich finder benefit his needy relatives.
The analogy (qiyās) extends the permission from self-benefit to benefit to needy relatives.
4
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi position allows charity to poor relatives without owner's consent. Other schools (e.g., Shafiʿi) may require the finder to wait for the owner or hand the item to authorities, and not permit charity.
This is a legitimate ikhtilāf based on differing views on the scope of permission.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Luqatah (lost and found property) presents the ruling on a finder (māltāqit) handling the found item. The commentator expands on the base text (matn) which states two scenarios. First, if the finder is wealthy (ghani), he may not use the property for himself because it is the property of another (māl al-ghayr) and permission is only given to the poor who are eligible for charity (maḥall li al-ṣadaqah bil-ijmā'). Second, if the finder is poor (faqīr), he may benefit from it because he is in need (dhū ḥājah). The commentator then explains that if the finder is rich, he is permitted to give the found item in charity specifically to his father, son, mother, or wife if they are poor. The rationale is that since a poor finder can benefit himself, a rich finder can transfer that benefit to his needy relatives by analogy (qiyās). The ruling is based on istiḥsān (juristic preference) and the principle of need.
