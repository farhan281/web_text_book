Heirs Buying Out a Co-Heir: Valid as a Sale
Ruling on validity of buyout as sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of heirs buying out a co-heir from an estate consisting of real estate or goods, and explain the rationale that it is validated as a sale.

Core Idea
Buying out a co-heir is valid as a sale, regardless of payment amount.

When an estate is to be divided among heirs, it is sometimes practical for the remaining heirs to buy out one co-heir by giving him a payment in exchange for his share. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on such a transaction when the estate consists of real estate (ʿaqār) or goods (ʿurūḍ). The key principle is that the transaction is validated as a sale (baiʿ), which permits any agreed price, whether less or more than the share's actual value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The estate is among multiple heirs. The heirs want to remove one co-heir by giving him money.
2
Determine the estate type
The estate must be real estate (ʿaqār) or goods (ʿurūḍ, movable property). If it is cash (naqd), different rules apply.
3
State the ruling
The transaction is valid (jāʾiz) regardless of whether the payment is less or more than the share's value.
The word 'jāz' in the text indicates validity.
4
Understand the rationale
The transaction is validated as a sale (baiʿ). In a sale, consideration can be any amount agreed upon; there is no requirement of equivalence.
The text explicitly says 'because it can be validated as a sale'.
5
Note the legal significance
This ruling provides flexibility in estate division, avoiding forced partition or complex valuation issues.

Heirs Excluding a Co-Heir by Buyout: Validity and Currency Exchange Condition
Ruling on the validity of a buyout among heirs and the requirement of taqabud in currency exchange

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of heirs buying out one heir and identify when hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) is required.

Core Idea
A buyout of an heir is valid as a sale; if the estate and payment are different currencies, hand-to-hand exchange is mandatory.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a common scenario in inheritance distribution: the remaining heirs agree to exclude one by giving him some property. The Hanafi school validates this as a type of sale (bay'). However, when the payment is in a different currency than the estate's composition, the strict rules of currency exchange (sarf) apply, particularly the condition of immediate hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) to avoid riba.

Identify the transaction
The heirs are giving property to one heir to exclude him. This is a type of sulh or sale.
The transaction is essentially a settlement (sulh) or sale (bay') depending on the assets.
2
Determine when it is permissible
If the estate is real estate or goods, it is always permissible regardless of amount.
The text says 'jā'iz' and does not require any special condition.
3
Analyze the currency subcase
If the estate is silver and they give gold, or vice versa, it is also permissible but with conditions.
This involves a change in currency type.
4
Apply the sarf rule
Since gold and silver are different currencies, hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) in the session is required; otherwise it becomes riba.
This is derived from the rules of sarf.
5
Examine the evidence
The athar of Caliph Uthman shows the permissibility of such buyouts even with large sums of currency.
The athar is a report from a Companion, considered a source of law in Hanafi usul.

Foundling's Crimes and Inheritance: Liability to the Public Treasury
Stating that liability for crimes and inheritance go to the public treasury

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the foundling's criminal liability and inheritance and explain why both go to the public treasury.

Core Idea
Both the foundling's liability and inheritance belong to the Muslim community's treasury, not to the finder.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two key issues regarding a foundling (laqiṭ): who bears financial responsibility for his crimes and who inherits from him. The finder might assume he becomes the guardian and thus the heir, but Hanafi law clearly separates these. The ruling is based on the foundling's freedom and lack of known relatives—making the entire Muslim community responsible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and state the two clear rulings: (1) the foundling's liability for crimes is on the Bayt al-Mal, and (2) his inheritance goes to the Bayt al-Mal, not to the finder.
2
Understand the condition
Note that the foundling is free (ḥurr). This condition is essential because the blood of a free person is protected, and diya is due if killed accidentally.
If the foundling were a slave, the ruling would differ.
3
Trace the reasoning
The passage gives two reasons: (a) diya is imposed because he is free, and (b) the payment goes to the treasury because he has no heir except the Muslims collectively.
This reasoning belongs to dalīl ʿaqlī (rational evidence).
4
Apply the rule to an example
If the foundling is killed accidentally, the killer's ʿāqila pays the diya to the Bayt al-Mal. This shows the practical application.
The ʿāqila is the group responsible for accidental killings.
5
Distinguish the finder's role
The finder does not inherit or bear liability. The passage explicitly says 'not for the finder' (la lilladhī iltaqaṭahu).
The finder is merely a caretaker, not a legal heir.

Definition and Gender Determination of the Hermaphrodite (Khunthā) by Urination
Defining hermaphrodite and gender determination via urination order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term 'khunthā' in Hanafi law and apply the rules of gender determination through urination to classify a newborn as male, female, or indeterminate.

Core Idea
Gender of a hermaphrodite is determined by the source and precedence of urination, not by anatomy alone.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, a newborn with both male and female genitalia is called a khunthā (hermaphrodite). Since many rulings—especially inheritance—depend on gender, the school provides a practical method based on urination. This lesson unpacks the definition and the step-by-step determination process, including how to deal with ambiguous cases and the exceptional scenario of absent genitalia.

Guided Path
1
Define the khunthā
Identify the key components: a newborn having both a vulva (farj) and a penis (dhakar). This is the starting point.
The term applies only when both organs are present.
2
Apply the urination test
Observe from which organ the urine exits: if only from the penis, the child is male; if only from the vulva, female. If from both, check which stream comes first (sabaq).
Precedence is decisive.
3
Handle simultaneous or equal urination
If urine comes from both at the same time or precedence cannot be determined, the child's gender remains ambiguous. The text does not specify a secondary sign here, so one moves to the next step.
The passage implies that if precedence is unclear, the case falls under ambiguity.
4
Apply the inheritance rule for ambiguous cases
When the gender is uncertain, the child is given inheritance (īrāth) based on the most cautious (aḥwaṭ) treatment—meaning the child receives the share that is definitely owed, and the remainder is held until clarification.
This ensures no one is deprived due to uncertainty.
5
Recognize the exceptional case of absent genitalia
If the child has neither organ, and waste exits from the anus or navel, the rule for determining gender is not given here but referenced from al-Yanābīʿ. Such a child is treated as ambiguous until further signs appear.
This case is mentioned to show the limits of the urination test.

Punishments and Inheritance of the Hermaphrodite
States rules on apostasy, qadhf, theft, qisas, diyah, and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi treatment of the khuntha in criminal and inheritance law, and apply the default rule of treating the khuntha as female in inheritance calculations.

Core Idea
The khuntha is treated as female by default in inheritance and receives half-blood money, but is exempt from hadd for apostasy.

The jurists faced a unique challenge: how to apply fixed punishments (hudud) and inheritance shares to a person whose sex is uncertain. This passage from al-Yanabi' outlines Abu Hanifa's approach: in criminal law, the khuntha is sometimes treated as male, sometimes as female, and sometimes exempt entirely due to doubt. In inheritance, the default is female, but with a safeguard against giving a smaller share than a male would receive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criminal rulings
List each crime mentioned (apostasy, slander, theft, qisas) and note the specific ruling for the khuntha. Ask: why is the khuntha exempt from execution for apostasy but subject to hadd for slander?
The key is the principle of doubt (shubha) in apostasy, and analogy (qiyas) with the eunuch (majbub) in qadhf.
2
Understand the diyah rule
Note that the khuntha receives the diyah of a woman (half of a man's) when killed by mistake. This is because the default is female until proven otherwise.
Qisas is not applied due to uncertainty in equivalence.
3

Guided Path
1
Identify the start of the wife's iddah
The commentary states: 'when his death is ruled, his wife must observe the waiting period of death from the time of the ruling.' This means the iddat al-wafat (4 months 10 days) begins at the moment the judge declares him dead, not at some hypothetical time of actual death.
2
Determine how the estate is divided
The matn says: 'his wealth is divided among his heirs present at that time.' The commentary adds: 'as if he died at that time visibly.' Only those heirs who are alive at the moment of the ruling inherit. If an heir died before the ruling, he does not inherit from the missing person.
3
Understand the missing person's inability to inherit others
The text states: 'the missing person does not inherit from anyone who dies during his absence.' Because he is legally considered dead in relation to others (since his status is unknown), he cannot inherit. However, his share (if any) is suspended until his status becomes known.
4
Apply the same suspension to bequests
Similarly, if someone bequeaths something to the missing person, that bequest is suspended. The reason given: it might be valid (if he is alive) or invalid (if he is dead). So it is held in abeyance until his status is determined.
5
Recognize the legal principle of suspended rights
The suspension of inheritance shares and bequests reflects the Hanafi principle of caution (ihtiyat). No final decision is made on rights that depend on the missing person's status until it is resolved.

Inheritance of the Missing Person: Suspension of Share
Ruling on inheritance of a missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance of a missing person and explain the legal fiction underlying the suspension of his share.

Core Idea
A missing person does not inherit, but his share is held in abeyance pending his return.

This lesson addresses a common but puzzling scenario in inheritance law: what happens to the share of a person who is missing when a relative dies? The Hanafi school provides a balanced ruling that neither forfeits the share nor distributes it prematurely. Instead, the share is suspended (yūqaf) until the missing person's status becomes known.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states clearly that the missing person does not inherit from anyone who dies during his absence. This is a negative ruling—denying inheritance.
2
Understand the legal basis
The reason given is that the missing person is considered 'dead with respect to others' (hukman). This means that, for the purpose of rights and obligations affecting others, he is treated as if dead, so inheritance—which requires the heir to be alive at the testator's death—is impossible.
3
Recognize the suspension mechanism
Despite not inheriting, his potential share is not forfeited. Instead, it is held in abeyance (yūqafu naṣībuhu). This protects his interest if he returns alive, while also preventing the share from being distributed incorrectly.
4
Extend to bequests
The same logic applies to a bequest (waṣiyyah) made to a missing person: it is suspended because it is possible he is dead (invalidating the bequest) or alive (making it valid).
