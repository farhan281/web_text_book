Liability for Breaking a Seller's Cup
Ruling on accidental breakage of a seller's cup due to permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a customer is not liable for accidentally breaking a seller's cup when taken with permission, and identify the conditions for this ruling.

Core Idea
No liability for accidental breakage when the customer had permission to use the item.

In Hanafi fiqh, liability for damage hinges on authorization. When a seller hands a cup to a customer for drinking, the customer has implicit permission to hold it. If the cup accidentally breaks, the customer is not liable, as the text states: 'because he took it with his permission.' This ruling facilitates ordinary transactions by removing fear of accidental damage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states that if a customer is given a cup by the seller and it accidentally falls and breaks, there is no liability (la daman).
This is the clear legal conclusion.
2
Extract the legal cause (illa)
The reason given is 'li-annahu akhadhahu bi-idhnihi' (because he took it with his permission). Permission is the key factor that removes liability.
Permission can be explicit or implied by the act of handing over.
3
Identify the conditions
For the ruling to apply, the breakage must be accidental (not intentional or due to gross negligence) and the permission must cover the manner of use (i.e., the customer was using it as intended — here, drinking).
If the customer misuses the item or acts recklessly, liability may arise.
4
Consider the opposite scenario
If the customer took the cup without permission (e.g., grabbed it from the counter), he would be liable for any damage even if accidental. Permission is the distinguishing factor.
This contrast reinforces the importance of idhn.

The Ruling on the Buffer Zone (Hareem) of a Canal in Another's Land
Intra-madhhab dispute on the hukm of a canal's buffer zone with proofs and practical consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the hareem of a canal and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) regarding planting rights.

Core Idea
A canal's buffer zone is not automatic; Abu Hanifa requires proof, while Sahibayn grant it by analogy.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a canal owner automatically has a buffer zone (hareem) when the canal runs through another's land. The disagreement turns on whether the well's hareem, established by tradition, can be analogized to a canal. The practical consequence affects who may plant trees on the buffer strip.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's view: the canal owner has no hareem unless he proves a prior right or agreement.
This is the default position in the matn.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the canal owner automatically has a path (musannah) for walking and throwing mud.
The size differs: Abu Yusuf says half the canal width on each side, Muhammad says full width.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's reasoning: necessity (cannot walk in the canal bed, cannot throw mud far) and analogy (qiyas) to the well, which has a known hareem.
They argue the canal cannot be maintained without a hareem.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes the well from the canal: the well's hareem is known by tradition (athar), not qiyas, and canal water can be used without a hareem, unlike well water which requires a draw area.
Thus, the analogy fails.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical consequence: the right to plant on the buffer zone belongs to the landowner according to Abu Hanifa, but to the canal owner according to the Sahibayn. Later authority Abu Ja'far adopts Abu Hanifa's view for planting and the Sahibayn's for mud disposal.
This selective adoption is a common Hanafi practice.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the buffer zone (hareem) of a canal that runs through another person's land. The buffer zone is a strip of land adjacent to the canal needed for maintenance, such as walking along the canal to manage water flow and for depositing mud dredged from the canal. The matn states that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the canal owner has no right to a buffer zone unless he can prove it (e.g., by a prior agreement or prescription). However, the two companions (Sahibayn) – Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani – hold that the canal owner automatically has a path (musannah) to walk on and to throw mud. Their reasoning is practical: without such a buffer zone, the canal cannot be properly maintained. Walking in the canal bed itself is impractical, and carrying mud far away would cause hardship. They analogize (qiyas) to the well, for which a buffer zone is known. Abu Hanifa counters that the well's buffer zone is established by transmitted tradition (athar), not analogy, and crucially, benefiting from a canal's water is possible without a buffer zone, whereas drawing water from a well requires a buffer zone for the draw operation itself. The passage then details the size of the buffer zone according to each of the Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf assigns half the canal's width on each side, while Muhammad assigns the full width on each side. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is highlighted: the right to plant trees on the buffer zone belongs to the landowner in Abu Hanifa's view, but to the canal owner in the Sahibayn's view. Furthermore, regarding Abu Hanifa's view on mud disposal, later scholars differed: some say the canal owner must move the mud to unowned land, others allow throwing it on the path as long as it does not become excessive. Similarly, for walking on the land, some say the canal owner is prevented, others permit it due to necessity. Finally, Abu Ja'far (a later Hanafi authority) gives a pragmatic ruling: adopt Abu Hanifa's view on planting rights and the Sahibayn's view on mud disposal. This selective adoption reflects a common practice of following the view that best serves maslahah (public interest) in different aspects.

ولاية الغرس (wilayat al-ghars): The right to plant trees on the buffer zone.
ثمرة الخلاف (thamarah al-khilaf): The practical legal consequence arising from a jurisprudential disagreement.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Slave's Sale at a Loss
Intra-Hanafi dispute on scope of trade permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a permitted slave's sale at a loss and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A slave's general trade permission does not extend to gross loss; it is like a gift.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the scope of a slave's trade permission. While a slave with general permission may buy and sell, there is a dispute over selling at a loss. Abu Hanifa allows any loss short of egregious overpayment, while the Sahibayn restrict it to only a slight unavoidable loss. Understanding this dispute illustrates how Hanafi jurists balance the slave's legal capacity with the master's rights.

Guided Path
1
Define the nature of permission (idhn)
Idhn lifts the restriction (hajr) on the slave, allowing him to transact for himself. The slave retains his legal capacity (ahliyyah).
The master's right is protected because the permission is conditional.
2
State the dispute on selling at a loss
The matn says 'he sells and buys' — but what does that include? Abu Hanifa says any loss not amounting to ghaban fahish; Sahibayn say only slight unavoidable loss.
Ghaban fahish is defined as a loss that custom would not tolerate.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
Sahibayn analogize gross loss to a gift (tabarru'), which is not covered by trade permission. Abu Hanifa argues the slave transacts by his own capacity, like a free person.
The dispute hinges on whether the slave's act is deemed trading or giving.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is the Sahibayn's view, as it is more cautious and protects the master's property.
This is the view preferred by later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani.

Prohibited Transactions for the Ma'dhun: Kitabah, 'Itq 'ala Mal, and Qard
Listing of prohibited non-trade transactions for the ma'dhun with details on kitabah validity conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a ma'dhun cannot perform kitabah, 'itq 'ala mal, or qard, and state the conditions under which kitabah can be valid.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun's permission excludes donations; kitabah, freeing for compensation, and lending are all forms of donation or non-trade contracts.

The same passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah continues to list transactions that are prohibited for the ma'dhun. These include entering a kitabah contract (contract of manumission by installments), freeing a slave for compensation ('itq 'ala mal), and giving a loan (qard). The reasoning is that these are not trade but forms of donation or non-exchange contracts. The passage also details the conditions under which a kitabah by a ma'dhun might be validated.

1

The text prohibits kitabah, 'itq 'ala mal, and qard. Understand what each is.

All are prohibited because they are not trade. Trade is exchange of property for property. Kitabah exchanges freedom for money, 'itq 'ala mal is a donation, qard is a donation.

Understand the effect of debt
If the ma'dhun has debt, even the master's permission does not make kitabah valid because the creditors have rights to the slave's earnings.
Text: 'فالكتابة فاسدة وإن أجازها المولى'.

A hadith (though weak) equates lending twice to charity once, showing it is a donation.

Legal Capacity of the Ma'dhun (Permitted Slave) in Hanafi Trade Law
Explaining capacity limits of the ma'dhun

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which transactions are permissible and prohibited for a ma'dhun and apply the distinction between agency-based and suretyship-based contracts.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun may trade freely but cannot give gifts or stand surety without permission.

The ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade) holds a unique position in Hanafi fiqh: he enjoys legal capacity for trade-related transactions but is restricted from gratuitous acts. This lesson examines the boundaries of his capacity, including gifts, suretyship, partnerships, and debts, and highlights a minor dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the restricted slave's hospitality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Start by noting the default prohibition: the ma'dhun cannot give gifts (with or without compensation) or charity. Then note the exception for small food hospitality.
The exception is based on trade custom.
2
Analyze suretyship (kafalah)
Understand that suretyship requires explicit master permission and only if the ma'dhun has no debt. Mukatab cannot do it even with permission.
Liability arises only after emancipation for unauthorized suretyship.
3
Distinguish partnership types
Compare 'inan (agency-based, allowed) and mufawada (suretyship-based, disallowed). The key is whether the contract requires kafalah.
Agency (wakalah) is within his capacity; suretyship is not.
4
Examine debt attachment and sale of the slave
Trade debts attach to the slave's person (he may be sold), while non-trade debts attach only to his liability after emancipation. The master cannot sell without creditor consent.
This protects creditors' rights.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Ma'dhun details the legal capacity of a slave who has been permitted by his master to engage in trade (ma'dhun). The opening states that a ma'dhun cannot give gifts, whether compensatory or gratuitous, except for minor hospitality like inviting someone to share a small amount of food, as this is customary among merchants. Charity is also prohibited as it is a pure gratuitous act. The ma'dhun may lend items like animals and clothes because such lending is part of trade custom. He may also engage in mudarabah (silent partnership) both as investor and as manager. He can enter into an 'inan partnership (limited partnership based on agency) because agency is within his capacity, but he cannot enter into mufawada partnership (unlimited partnership based on mutual suretyship) because he lacks the authority to stand surety for others. He can also authorize his own slave to trade.

Non-trade debts like dower or injury are attached only to his liability and collected after emancipation. The passage includes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf allows a restricted slave (mahjur) to invite others to the food given by his master if it is daily sustenance, but not if given for a month. The general position restricts such hospitality for longer periods to avoid harming the master. Finally, it is stated that a woman may give small food items from her husband's house in charity by custom, but not money or valuables.

This lesson examines a foundational ruling from the Book of the Authorized Slave (Kitab al-Ma'dhun). This distinction highlights that permission (idhn) is the operative cause.

Definition and expansion of which debts bind the slave's value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish trade debts that attach to the slave's raqabah from personal debts that attach to the dhimma, and explain the legal rules for selling the slave to satisfy creditors.

Core Idea
Only trade-related debts attach to the slave's value; personal debts like mahr are collectible after freedom.

This commentary expands the matn's statement that a slave's debts are attached to his neck (value). It clarifies that this refers only to debts arising from trade activities or analogous transactions. Other debts, such as marriage dower or injury liability, are personal and can only be collected after emancipation. The passage also details the procedure for selling the slave to pay creditors and the master's options to ransom or sell with permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of debts
Distinguish trade debts (dayn al-tijarah) and analogous liabilities (e.g., sale, lease, liability for usurpation) that attach to raqabah from personal debts (mahr, jinayah) that attach only to dhimma.
Trade debts can be satisfied by selling the slave; personal debts are collected after freedom.
2
Understand the effect of attachment to raqabah
Creditors can demand the judge sell the slave to recover their debts from the proceeds. The master may also ransom the slave by paying all debts, preventing the sale.
If the master sells without permission, creditors have the right to rescind the sale and instead make the slave work to pay debts (istis'a').

Apply the rules of sale by judge vs master
Only the judge may sell the slave to satisfy trade debts. If the master sells without creditors' permission, the sale is suspended on their approval, analogous to a pledge (rahn).
Proceeds are divided proportionally. If some creditors approve and some refuse, the sale is invalid unless all agree.

Distinguish from the guardian's sale

Creditors' Rights in the Sale of the Authorized Slave
Expansion of rulings on sale and creditor rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which creditors can rescind a master's sale of an authorized slave and explain the difference between a sale by the judge and a sale by the master.

Core Idea
Creditors can rescind a master's unauthorized sale unless the price covers their debts or the master ransoms the slave.

In Hanafi fiqh, an authorized slave (al-ma'dhun) can trade, but his debts are attached to his person. When the master sells him, creditors have certain rights to protect their claims. This passage explains the division of the sale price among creditors, the requirement of unanimous consent, and the crucial difference between a sale by the judge (binding) and a sale by the master (subject to rescission).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on price division
The sale price of the authorized slave is divided among his creditors proportionally, regardless of how the debt was proven (confession or evidence).
This ensures all creditors receive their fair share.
2
Unanimous consent rule
If some creditors permit the sale and some refuse, the sale is invalid unless all agree. This protects minority creditors from being forced into a disadvantageous sale.
3
The master's ransom (fida')
If the master pays all the slave's debts (ransom), the slave's liability is cleared and nothing remains to be sold. This is an exception to the general rule.
The term 'fida' implies full payment of all debts.
4
Sale by judge vs. sale by master
A judge's sale of the slave is binding and creditors cannot rescind. But if the master sells without creditors' permission, they have the right to rescind unless the price suffices to cover their debts, the master pays them, or they release the slave from debt.
This distinction protects creditors from unilateral actions by the master.
5
Distinction from executor's sale
Unlike a master's sale of a slave, when an executor sells the deceased's estate for debts, creditors cannot rescind because they cannot claim labor (istis'a') from the estate.
The rationale is that pursuing labor from the estate delays payment of the deceased's debts.

Gift of an Authorized Slave to a Creditor and the Revival of Debt
Expansion of a specific issue on gift and debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) that the gift of a slave to a creditor extinguishes the debt, and the debt does not revive upon return of the gift, and contrast this with Abu Yusuf's view.

Core Idea
Gifting a slave to a creditor extinguishes the debt; returning the gift does not revive the debt (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad).

A special issue arises when a master gifts his authorized slave to a creditor who has a debt against that slave. Upon acceptance, the debt is extinguished because one cannot own oneself. But what if the master later returns the gift? This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the debt does not return, analogizing to a husband gifting a slave to his wife which dissolves the marriage. Abu Yusuf argues that the debt returns, while another narration from Muhammad prevents the return altogether due to an increase in value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
If a master gifts an authorized slave to his creditor and the creditor accepts, the debt is immediately extinguished. This is because the creditor now owns the slave, and a man cannot have a debt against himself.
This is a general principle: ownership and debt in the same person cancel each other.
2
Analyze the master's return of the gift
If the master later returns the gift (i.e., the creditor returns the slave), the question is whether the debt revives. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no, because the debt was permanently discharged by ownership, akin to a release (ibra').
They analogize to a husband who receives a female slave as a gift from his wife; the marriage dissolves, and returning the gift does not revive the marriage.
3
Consider Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf argues that the debt returns because the debt was merely suspended due to ownership; when ownership reverts to the master, the debt revives.
This view treats the debt as a property right that is not permanently extinguished but only in abeyance.
4
Examine the alternative narration from Muhammad
Another narration from Muhammad holds that the master cannot even return the gift because the removal of the debt is an increase in the slave's value (the slave is now free of debt). Any increase in the gifted object prevents the donor from revoking the gift.
This is based on the general rule of gifts: a gift with an increase cannot be returned.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa's view, the creditor keeps the slave free of debt and the master cannot reclaim it. Under Abu Yusuf, if the slave is returned, the debt revives and the creditor can pursue the slave after freedom.
The difference affects the financial liability of the slave and the master.

Interdiction (Hajr) of the Authorized Slave and Public Notification
Expansion on the condition for valid interdiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid interdiction of an authorized slave and explain why knowledge of the majority of the market is required.

Core Idea
An interdiction (hajr) on an authorized slave is not effective until it is publicly known to the majority of the market.

A master may revoke his permission (idhn) and place an interdiction (hajr) on his authorized slave to prevent further trade. However, for the interdiction to be legally effective, it must be made known to those who deal with the slave. This passage stipulates that the interdiction does not take effect until it becomes apparent among the people of the slave's market, and specifically requires that the majority of the market know about it. A mere announcement to one or two people is insufficient. This rule is based on the principle of maintaining trust and preventing harm to third parties who have been dealing with the slave in good faith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal effect
The act is the master's interdiction (hajr) on his authorized slave. The legal effect is that the slave's authority to trade is revoked, and future transactions he undertakes are invalid.
Before interdiction, the slave's contracts are valid and binding.
2
Notice requirement for interdiction
The interdiction does not take effect until it is made known among the people of the slave's market. This is because the public has a legitimate reliance on the slave's apparent authority.
The term 'market' refers to the commercial community where the slave normally transacts.
3
Specify the required degree of knowledge
It is not enough that a few individuals know. The majority of the market's people must become aware. This ensures the interdiction is 'notorious' (mashhur).
Minority knowledge does not achieve the purpose of preventing further dealings.
4
Rationale for the requirement
The public has come to believe in the permissibility of dealing with the slave and lending to him. This belief cannot be dispelled except by widespread knowledge.
Hanafi fiqh considers 'urf (custom) and public trust.
5
Edge case: small market
If the market has only one or two people, interdiction through them alone does not suffice. The interdiction is ineffective until it spreads more widely, essentially requiring the same standard of notoriety.
This reinforces that the purpose is public awareness, not mere notification to individuals.

Debt vs. Criminal Liability of a Slave Woman's Offspring
Comparative ruling with reasoning (ta'lil) on debt vs. crime.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling on the extension of a slave woman's debt to her child born after the debt versus the treatment of her child when she commits a crime, and explain the role of possession (yad) in this distinction.

Core Idea
A slave woman's debt attaches to her child born after the debt, but criminal liability does not—the key is possession (yad).

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies a subtle point in Hanafi fiqh: when a slave woman has debts, the child born after the debt is also liable (the debt extends to the child), but if she commits a crime, the liability does not extend to the child. The difference hinges on whether the item is in her possession (yad). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi rules on slave transactions, debts, and crimes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage contrasts a slave woman with a debt (الدرن) and a slave woman who committed a crime (الجانية). Label each and note the different consequences for the child.
The first case is not explicitly named but is referred to as 'الأولى' (the first).
2
Apply the ruling to the child
In the debt case, the debt is established in her neck (raqabah) and extends to the child born after the debt. In the criminal case, the liability does not attach to the neck; the master must hand her over or pay ransom. The child is not affected.
3
Distinguish timing of child's birth
The child born before the debt does not enter into the debt, unlike earnings and gifts acquired before the debt, which go to the creditors if the master hasn't taken possession.
4
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
Earnings are in her hand (yad) because she can dispose of them before the master takes them. The child is not in her hand (she cannot sell or dispose of it), so it is like earnings already taken by the master.
Possession (yad) is the key factor.
5
Synthesize the principle
The general principle: If the slave woman has control (yad) over an asset, creditors can claim it; if not, it remains with the master. The child is never in her control, so it is not subject to debt claims, except when the debt already attached to her neck before the child's birth.
