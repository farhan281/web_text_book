Milk Mixed with Food: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Fosterage
Intra-Hanafi dispute on milk-food mixture and prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on when mixing breast milk with food does or does not cause prohibition of marriage, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: mixing negates prohibition even if milk dominant. Sahibayn: prohibition if milk is dominant.

The prohibition of marriage through fosterage (riḍā‘) is established when a child drinks a woman's breast milk under certain conditions. But what if the milk is mixed with food? Does the mixture still create a foster relationship? This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on this question, exploring the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, the criteria for milk dominance, and the nuances added by later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Pinpoint the exact point of disagreement: does mixing milk with food negate the prohibition even if milk is dominant (Abu Hanifa) or does prohibition attach when milk is predominant (Sahibayn)?
The dispute hinges on whether the mixture retains the legal identity of milk.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's proof
Examine the analogy (qiyas) to an oath: if one swears not to drink milk and drinks a mixture where water is dominant, the oath is not broken. Similarly, mixing milk with food changes its nature so it is no longer consumed as pure milk.
The key concept is that the act of consumption must be of milk alone for prohibition to attach.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's proof
They rely on the dominance of milk: if taste, color, and smell of milk are perceivable, the mixture is effectively milk. This is analogous to pure milk, so prohibition attaches.
Dominance is defined differently by each of the Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf uses change of color/taste; Muhammad uses removal from the name ('milk').
4

From later texts (al-Hidayah, al-Mustasfa), if the mixture is cooked, even the Sahibayn agree no prohibition, because cooking alters the essence. Also, if the food is negligible so the liquid is essentially drinkable milk, prohibition may attach even according to Abu Hanifa.
These nuances are important for fatwa application.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars have favored the Sahibayn's view as more cautious and in line with the objective of fosterage. Thus, the operative ruling is that if milk is dominant and uncooked, prohibition attaches.
However, in cases of doubt, Abu Hanifa's view may be considered.

Mixed Milk and Virgin’s Milk: Conditions of Fosterage Prohibition
Three rulings: mixing with goat milk, two women's milk, and virgin's milk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules of predominance in mixed milk, distinguish the positions in the intra-Hanafi dispute on two women's milk, and state the evidence for virgin's milk establishing fosterage prohibition.

Core Idea
Human milk must be predominant for tahrim; mixed milks attach to the majority woman; virgin’s milk is valid by general text.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra expands on the matn of Al-Quduri, addressing three key scenarios in rada‘ (fosterage suckling): when human milk is mixed with goat’s milk, when two women’s milks are mixed, and when a virgin produces milk. These rulings clarify that the prohibition of marriage (tahrim) depends on the predominance of human milk and that virgin’s milk is included under the general Qur’anic command, resolving common doubts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of predominance (ghalaba)
Identify that when human milk is mixed with another substance (goat’s milk, water, oil), the prohibition attaches only if the human milk is the majority component. If the other substance prevails, no fosterage relationship is established.
This principle is analogized from water mixtures.
2
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on two women’s milk
Examine the positions: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf state tahrim attaches to the woman whose milk is more abundant; Muhammad al-Shaybani says it attaches to both. If equal, all agree both become mahram. Note the practical difference: if a man is married to one woman, only the majority woman becomes mahram according to the first view.
The tarjih (preferred view) is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
3
Examine the proof for virgin’s milk
The commentator cites Qur’an 4:23, 'your mothers who suckled you', which is general and includes any woman who breastfeeds an infant, regardless of marital status or prior lactation. Thus a virgin’s milk is valid for fosterage.
This counters any notion that only previously married women can produce milk for rada‘.
4
Synthesize the rulings into a complete picture
Combine the three scenarios: the key determinant for mixed milks is the predominance of human milk (or the majority woman in case of two women). Virgin’s milk is treated the same as any woman’s milk. The common thread is the general evidence of the Qur’an and the principle of ghalaba.
This synthesis helps in real-life applications where milk mixtures or unusual situations arise.

Identify the exact requirement: two men, or one man and two women, all being upright (ʿudūl). Note that women alone are not accepted.
This is the mufta bihi position from al-Quduri.
2
Locate the dalil and identify the apparent problem
Read the hadith of 'Uqba ibn al-Ḥārith. The Prophet (peace be upon him) told him to separate from his wife based on the testimony of a single woman (the black wet nurse). This seems to contradict the requirement of two men or one man and two women.
The hadith is sahih but its meaning is debated.
3
Apply the Hanafi interpretation of tanzuh
Understand that the Prophet's command is interpreted as a recommendation of pious caution (tanzuh), not a legal ruling. The command did not establish a new evidentiary rule; rather, it urged scrupulousness in a doubtful situation.
This preserves the general principle that women's testimony alone is insufficient in matters men can observe.
4
Consider the cross-madhhab dispute
Note that the Shafiʿi school accepts the testimony of one reliable woman for fosterage. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in a Hanafi court, such testimony would not establish the fosterage, while in a Shafiʿi court it might.
The dispute hinges on the interpretation of the hadith and the definition of 'matters men can observe.'
5
Apply the ruling to the consequences
If fosterage is proven through valid testimony, the judge separates the couple. Determine whether dower is owed based on consummation, and note that no maintenance or housing is due during ʿiddah.
These consequences follow from the establishment of fosterage.

Proof of Fosterage: Testimony Requirements and Financial Consequences
Explaining the requirement of testimony to prove fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the proof of fosterage, explain the requirement of specific witnesses, and apply the financial consequences of separation upon proven fosterage.

Core Idea
Fosterage is proven only by two men or one man and two women; a single woman's claim is insufficient for obligatory separation.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains how fosterage (radāʿ) is established in court according to the Hanafi school. The ruling distinguishes between the proof required for fosterage and the financial effects if a marriage is annulled due to proven fosterage. We also see the Hanafi interpretation of a key hadith, where the Prophet's command to separate was precautionary (tanzih), not obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Understand that the ruling is about the proof of fosterage (radāʿ) in court. The act to be proven is the existence of a fosterage relationship, which creates marriage prohibitions.
Fosterage itself is a cause of prohibition; here we focus on how it is established judicially.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites the hadith of Uqbah ibn al-Harith from Sahih al-Bukhari. Identify that the dalil is a specific incident where a single woman claimed fosterage.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for this procedural ruling.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze the Prophet's behavior: he turned away twice before saying 'fad'aha' (leave her) on the third or fourth time. The Hanafi school interprets the delay as evidence that separation was not obligatory upon a single woman's claim; it was merely recommended (tanzih).
If it were obligatory, he would have ordered immediately. The imperative is overridden by the qarinah (context) of hesitation.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Recognize that the Shafi'i school holds that two women alone can prove fosterage. Understand the Hanafi counter: fosterage is a matter men can observe (through mahram looking at the breast), so a man is required.
The difference stems from the nature of the evidence and who can access it.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Confirm that the Hanafi position (two men or one man and two women) is the mufta bihi (relied upon) in the school, based on the hadith interpretation and the rationale of male observation.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is mentioned, so the ruling is stable.

Commentary
This passage, from the commentary Al-Jawhara al-Nayra on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, states the Hanafi ruling on proving fosterage. The core ruling: fosterage is established only by the testimony of two upright men, or one man and two women (with a man being present among them). The reason given is that fosterage is something which men can observe—specifically, the mahram relative (a man who is permanently forbidden to marry the woman) is allowed to look at her breast, and his testimony regarding that is accepted. If such witnesses testify to fosterage between a married couple, the court must separate them. Then the commentary explains the financial consequences: if the separation occurs before consummation, the wife gets no mahr; if after consummation, she receives the lesser of the agreed mahr and the mahr al-mithl (the standard mahr for women of her status). She is also not entitled to nafaqah (maintenance) or sakan (housing) during the 'iddah (waiting period). The text then presents evidence from the hadith of Uqbah ibn al-Harith, narrated in al-Karkhi and recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari. In that hadith, a single black woman claimed to have nursed both Uqbah and his wife, and the Prophet initially turned away when Uqbah mentioned it. Only on the third or fourth time did the Prophet say 'Leave her' or according to another version 'Separate from her.' The commentary interprets: the Prophet's hesitation and eventual command was on the way of tanzih (religious caution/recommendation), not obligation (wujub). If separation were obligatory, the Prophet would have ordered it immediately. Moreover, the fact that he said 'Separate from her' (using the imperative) implies that the marriage was still valid at that point; he was not confirming an automatic annulment. Thus the Hanafi position: fosterage requires conclusive proof (two men or a man with two women), and a single woman's claim is insufficient. The Prophet's command was a recommendation to avoid even the appearance of impropriety.

رضاع (raḍāʿ): Fosterage; the relationship created by a woman breastfeeding a child, which creates marriage prohibitions.
تنزه (tanzuh): Religious caution; a recommended abstention or action to avoid even a possibility of sin.

Ownership of Spouse Causes Separation (Hanafi Rule)
Ruling that acquisition of spouse ownership leads to immediate separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that ownership of a spouse dissolves the marriage and identify the three exceptional slave categories.

Core Idea
Complete ownership of a spouse dissolves marriage; partial ownership does the same.

This passage from Al-Jawharah Al-Nayirah presents a fundamental rule of Hanafi family law: when a husband or wife acquires ownership of the other, the marriage ends immediately. This rule reflects the principle that a person cannot own what they are married to, as ownership implies full control, contradictory to the equality required in marriage. However, the rule has exceptions for slaves with incomplete ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering event
Acquisition of ownership (milk) of the spouse, whether full or partial (shiqs). This can happen through purchase, inheritance, or any lawful means.
Even a partial share triggers the rule.
2
Note the consequence
Separation (furqa) occurs immediately and automatically. No additional legal action is required.
3
Recognize the exception
Three types of slaves have an exception: ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade), mudabbar (slave whose freedom is deferred), and mukatab (slave under contract of manumission). If they buy their spouse, the marriage remains valid.
The exception applies because these slaves have a right but not complete ownership (milk tamm).
4
Understand the rationale
Complete ownership (milk tamm) is incompatible with marriage (nikah). The exception confirms that incomplete ownership does not dissolve the marriage.
Thus, the critical factor is the completeness of ownership.

Witnessing and Conditions in Raj'ah – Hanafi vs Maliki
Ruling with proof on witnessing, condition, and post-iddah dispute in raj'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witnessing in raj'ah, explain the Hanafi dalil against the Maliki position, and apply the rule that conditional raj'ah is invalid.

Core Idea
Witnesses for raj'ah are recommended, not required; conditional raj'ah is void by consensus.

This lesson examines three key rulings from the Book of Raj'ah: whether witnesses are required for a valid revocation (raj'ah), whether conditional revocation is valid, and how disputes after the waiting period are resolved. The Hanafi school holds that witnessing is recommended (mustahabb) but not a condition, based on a careful reading of Quranic texts and a refutation of the Maliki position. By the end, you will understand how the Hanafi jurists derive these rulings and why they differ from Imam Malik.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on witnessing
Read the matn: 'Yuastahabb lahu an yushhid 'ala ar-raj'ati shahidayn. Fa-in lam yushhid, sahat ar-raj'ah.' State: witnessing is mustahabb; omitting it does not invalidate.
The commentator strengthens this with the verse 'wa ashhidoo' but explains it as recommendation.
2
Locate the Hanafi dalil against Maliki
The Hanafi response lists multiple Quranic verses (al-Baqarah 231, 230, 229, 228) and the hadith of Ibn Umar – none mention witnesses. Also qiyas: raj'ah is continuation of marriage, which does not require witnesses.
The key argument: the imperative 'ashhidoo' is paired with separation, and witnessing for separation is mustahabb, so by analogy it is mustahabb for raj'ah.
3
Understand the Maliki position and Hanafi response
Malik holds that 'ashhidoo' is a command of obligation, making witnesses a condition. The Hanafi rebuttal uses the pairing argument and the unrestricted nature of retention texts.
The thamarah al-khilaf: In Maliki, raj'ah without witnesses is invalid; in Hanafi, it is valid.
4
Note the consensus on conditional raj'ah
The passage states by ijma' that ta'liq (suspending raj'ah on a condition like 'if you enter the house') is not raj'ah. This is because raj'ah is an immediate reinstatement.
Examples from the text: 'idha ja'a ghad', 'idha dakhalti ad-dar'.
5
Apply the post-iddah dispute rule
After iddah, if the husband claims he performed raj'ah but the wife denies, her word is taken (without oath, per Abu Hanifa). Reason: he is claiming an action he cannot now prove, so he is suspect.
If she confirms, it is valid. This protects the wife from a false claim.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Raj'ah addresses three key rulings about revocation after a revocable divorce. First, the matn states that it is recommended (mustahabb) for the husband to have two witnesses when he revokes his wife. The commentator explains that the husband should say to them, 'Bear witness that I have revoked my wife so-and-so.' The proof is the verse in Surah at-Talaq (65:2) which says to call witnesses, and also the practical reason that if the iddah ends, the wife might deny the revocation. However, if the husband does not have witnesses, the revocation is still valid (sahih). This is the Hanafi position. A dispute with Imam Malik is presented: Malik holds that witnesses are obligatory based on the imperative form of the verse ('ashhidoo'), considering it a command indicating obligation (wujub). Thus, by analogy (qiyas), witnessing for revocation is also recommended. Marriage with Explicit Tahlil Condition: Valid but Makruh

Core Idea
A marriage with an explicit tahlil condition is valid but makruh tahrimi; intention alone does not affect the contract.

The Hanafi school holds that a marriage contract conditioned upon tahlil (making a woman permissible for her first husband) is legally valid because it fulfills all essential conditions. However, it is prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) due to the prophetic curse on the muhallil and muhallal lahu. Importantly, if the condition is merely intended in the heart and not verbalized, the marriage is perfectly valid and not disliked. Dislike here is makruh tahrimi, not haram.

The proof is the hadith: 'Allah has cursed the muhallil and the one for whom tahlil is done' and the saying about the 'borrowed he-goat.' These indicate severe disapproval.
Hadith is hasan sahih, ahad, but still authoritative.

Implicit intention does not affect the contract.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tahlil marriage is valid but disliked; whether it makes the woman permissible splits the Hanafi school.

We now examine a sensitive issue: a man marrying a woman with the explicit condition of making her permissible (tahlil) for her first husband after three divorces. Dislike is makruh tanzihi, not tahrimi.

If the condition is explicitly stated in the contract, the marriage is valid but disliked. If the muhallil only intends tahlil in his heart without mentioning it, and consummates, she becomes permissible by consensus.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hulla requires a valid second marriage; but Hanafi imams differ on validity and its effect.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute about the effect of a second marriage and divorce on the permissibility for the first husband. The issue arises when a woman divorced by her first husband marries someone else, consummates, and is then divorced. While the general principle requires a valid second marriage for hulla, the Hanafi masters disagreed on what constitutes validity and whether the objective of hulla is automatically achieved. We will examine each imam's position and their underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The question is whether a woman who has been divorced by a second husband after consummation becomes halal to her first husband. This is the context of the dispute.
The first husband's divorce must have been irrevocable (bain) for hulla to be needed.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa holds that the second marriage is valid and she becomes halal for the first husband. No additional condition is needed beyond a valid marriage and consummation.
This is the default position; the marriage is assumed sahih.
3
State Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf considers the second marriage fasid because it is in the meaning of a temporary marriage (mu'aqqat), i.e., the purpose is only to make her halal, which renders the contract corrupt. Therefore, she does not become halal.
He focuses on the intention behind the marriage, likening it to mut'ah.
4

Muhammad says the marriage is sahih, but she does not become halal as a punishment for hastening what the Shariah delayed. She is prevented from the objective of hulla, analogous to a killer being deprived of inheritance.
He uses qiyas with qatl al-muwarrith to justify the penalty.
5
Compare the practical outcome
Depending on which view is followed, the first husband may or may not be able to remarry her. The difference is known as thamarah al-khilaf.
The passage does not specify which view is relied upon in the school.

Definition and Conditions of Ila'
Defines Ila', its linguistic and legal meaning, and rulings on wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Ila' and distinguish between unambiguous and ambiguous oath wordings regarding belief in court and conscience.

Core Idea
Ila' is a valid oath to abstain from intercourse; unambiguous words bind in court, ambiguous words bind only if intention is admitted.

This passage from Kitab al-Ila' explains the Hanafi legal concept of a vow of abstinence. It defines Ila' linguistically and technically, establishes that the prohibition must stem directly from the oath, and draws a critical distinction between explicit and euphemistic expressions for intercourse, which affects whether the husband is believed in court or only in conscience.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of Ila'
Identify the linguistic meaning (oath) and the legal meaning (an oath to abandon intercourse with one's wife for a specified period). Note that Ila' is derived from 'ala and pronounced with elongation.
The four-month period is typical but not exclusive.
2
Identify the condition for valid Ila'
The prohibition must be directly caused by the oath. If the husband swears not to approach his wife while she is menstruating, it is not Ila' because the prohibition exists even without the oath.
This ensures the oath is the cause of the abstention.
3
Classify the oath wording as unambiguous or ambiguous
Unambiguous words (e.g., 'I will not have intercourse with you') clearly denote intercourse. Ambiguous words (e.g., 'I will not touch you') could mean intercourse or other things. The text lists examples of both.
The Quranic use of 'qurban' (approach) is cited as evidence that 'approach' in Sharia means intercourse.
4
Apply the ruling on belief in court vs. conscience
For unambiguous words, the husband is not believed in court if he claims he did not mean intercourse, but is believed between himself and Allah. For ambiguous words, he is believed both in court and conscience unless he admits intending intercourse.
This affects whether he is legally considered a muli.
5
Recognize valid and invalid oath formulas
Ila' is only contracted by valid oaths such as 'By Allah,' 'By the Might of Allah,' etc. Invalid formulas like 'By the knowledge of Allah' do not constitute Ila'.
Only oaths by Allah or His attributes are valid in Hanafi fiqh.

Clear Words of Intercourse and the Distinction Between Qada' and Diyanah
Ruling on husband's claim regarding intention in clear words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband claims a different intention for clear words of intercourse in an oath and distinguish between judicial (qada') and religious (diyanah) consequences.

Core Idea
Clear words for intercourse bind the husband in court; his inner intention is only between him and Allah.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are interpreted by their outward meaning when the words are unambiguous. This lesson examines a specific case: a husband uses a clear term for intercourse in an oath but later claims he intended something else. The passage establishes that such a claim is rejected in court (qada') but accepted between him and Allah (diyanah), reflecting the fundamental Hanafi principle that judicial rulings are based on apparent evidence, while internal states are left to the individual's conscience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term and its Quranic basis
Recognize that 'wat'' (الوطء) is defined by the Quran (2:222) as a clear, unambiguous term for sexual intercourse. This sets the foundation that the word itself is unequivocal.
The verse uses 'hatta yathurna' which implies intercourse until purity.
2
Analyze the husband's claim
If the husband says he did not intend intercourse when using a clear word, the claim is about his inner intention, which contradicts the apparent meaning. The text distinguishes between the outward and inward dimensions.
The husband might be truthful inwardly, but the law looks at the external expression.
3
Apply the ruling: qada' vs diyanah
The ruling states that the husband is not believed in the judiciary (qada'), meaning the court will treat the oath according to its clear meaning. However, he is believed between himself and Allah (diyanah), so he is not sinful if his intention was different.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi jurisprudence: court decisions are based on al-zahir.
4
Extend to synonyms
The same ruling applies to any clear synonym for intercourse, such as 'I will not have intercourse,' 'I will not copulate,' or 'I will not perform a major bath due to ritual impurity.' The key is the clarity of the word.
The principle is that unambiguous words cannot be overridden by a later claim of different intent.
