Kafalah with Absent Creditor: The Requirement of Acceptance and the Exception for the Sick Person's Heir
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kafalah acceptance with an exception for the sick person's heir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the necessity of creditor acceptance in kafalah, identify the single exception for the sick person's heir, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Kafalah needs the creditor's okay, unless a dying man's heir guarantees — that's a will.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar introduces a key exception to the general rule that a kafalah (suretyship) contract requires the creditor's acceptance. While the general rule is that kafalah is invalid without the creditor's consent, the Hanafi school recognizes one exception: when a terminally ill person instructs his heir to stand surety for his debts, the guarantee is valid as a will (wasiyyah) even if the creditors are absent and have not accepted. The passage also records a significant intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the two) on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other regarding whether acceptance is needed at all when the creditor is absent. Understanding this ruling and its reasoning deepens your appreciation for how the Hanafi school balances contractual principles with the special circumstances of the dying debtor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule of kafalah regarding creditor acceptance
Locate the statement that kafalah is a contract that requires the creditor's pleasure and acceptance, analogous to a sale. The text states it is 'suspended on what is beyond the session' (يقف على ما وراء المجلس), meaning it is not immediately binding and requires later acceptance.
The general rule applies when the creditor is absent from the contract session.
2
Note the single exception: the sick person's heir
Find the exception: 'except in one matter' (إلا في مسألة واحدة) when a sick person says to his heir: 'Stand surety for me for my debts' and the heir does so in the absence of the creditors. This is valid.
The exception is only for the heir of a person in mortal illness (marad al-mawt).
3
Understand the reasoning: why it is treated as a will
The commentary explains that this is essentially a will (wasiyyah), and therefore valid even if the creditors are not named. The sick person's dhimma is about to end, so the debt is considered transferred to the estate; his instruction is like an outsider's command.
The key is the legal reclassification: from kafalah to wasiyyah.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on acceptance in kafalah
The text presents Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's view that acceptance is required (making the contract invalid without it), and Abu Yusuf's two narrations: one that no acceptance is needed (analogy to iqrar), and another that acceptance is needed (analogy to marriage with an absent party).
This step requires careful differentiation: the exception is not part of this dispute; it applies regardless.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The text indicates that the view of the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is the one followed in the matn. The mufta bihi is that acceptance is required for kafalah with an absent creditor, except in the specific case of the sick person's heir where it is treated as a will.
Abu Yusuf's narrations are recorded but not relied upon for fatwa.

It is suspended on what is beyond the session, and because kafalah is a contract to which the right of the beneficiary (makful lahu) is attached, so it is suspended on his pleasure and acceptance like a sale. As for Abu Yusuf, it has been narrated from him that he does not need acceptance because kafalah is an obligation of wealth in the dhimmah by speech, so it becomes like an iqrar (confession). And if someone accepts on behalf of an absent person, it is suspended according to all of them. He (the author) said: (Except) in one matter, which is that the sick person says to his heir: 'Stand surety for me for what I owe of debt,' and he stands surety for him in the absence of the creditors: it is valid. Meaning if the creditor accepts after that, and that is because this is essentially a will (wasiyyah), and therefore it is valid even if the beneficiaries are not named. And therefore they said: it is only valid if he has wealth, or it is said: he stands in the place of the creditor due to his need for that, to free his dhimmah, and in it is benefit for the creditor, just as if he himself attended. And because when he is sick with a mortal illness, he becomes like a stranger in the debt, because his dhimmah is on the verge of destruction, and it is as if the debt transferred from his dhimmah to the estate, so his address becomes like the address of a stranger, and we have mentioned that when the addressee is a stranger, the guarantee is suspended.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the requirement of creditor's acceptance (ijazah) in a kafalah (suretyship) contract when the creditor is absent. The general rule, as stated, is that kafalah is suspended beyond the session (majlis) and requires the creditor's consent, similar to a sale contract. An exception is introduced: if a person in mortal illness (marad al-mawt) instructs his heir to stand surety for his debts, the guarantee is valid even without the creditors' immediate acceptance, because it is essentially a will (wasiyyah). The passage then details the views of the Hanafi imams. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that in the absence of the creditor, the guarantee is invalid unless the creditor later accepts. Abu Yusuf has two narrations: one that acceptance is not needed (analogizing to iqrar), and another that it is needed (analogizing to a marriage contract with an absent party). The text also provides examples: a debtor asking a third party to stand surety for a creditor absent, or a fuduli (unauthorized person) guaranteeing a debt for an absent party. According to the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), both are invalid until acceptance, while Abu Yusuf holds them valid. The commentary explains the exception for the sick person's heir as a disguised will, which is valid even without naming the creditors, and it is effective because the sick person's dhimma is about to expire, effectively transferring the debt to the estate. Thus, the heir stands in the place of the creditor for the purpose of freeing the dhimma.

Key Terms
الكفالة (al-kafalah): A contract of suretyship whereby a person (kafil) guarantees to pay a debt or fulfill an obligation on behalf of another (asil) to a beneficiary (makful lahu).
الإجازة (al-ijazah): The subsequent consent or ratification given by the creditor to a kafalah contract made without his prior approval.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A will or bequest; in this context, the sick person's instruction to his heir to guarantee his debt is legally treated as a wasiyyah, not a kafalah.
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): A mortal illness that renders the sick person's transactions regarding more than one-third of his estate or debts subject to the rules of wasiyyah.

Two Joint Debtors as Mutual Guarantors: Ruling on Payment and Recourse
Ruling on when one of two joint debtors who are mutual guarantors pays more than half.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on joint debtors who are mutual guarantors to determine the amount of recourse and the condition of request for repayment.

Core Idea
A joint debtor who pays more than half his share can only claim the excess if he guaranteed by the other's request.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in kafalah: two persons jointly liable for a debt, each also acting as guarantor for the other. The key question is: when one pays more than his share, can he recover the excess from his co-debtor? The Hanafi ruling introduces a distinction based on whether the guarantor acted at the request of the other party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Understand the scenario: two persons are joint debtors and mutual guarantors. The ruling states that if one pays an amount exceeding half of the total debt, he may claim the excess only if he became a guarantor at the other's request.
The act is the payment of the debt, and the ruling concerns the right of recourse for any excess over the paying party's own share.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the rational division of liability: each debtor owes half as his own debt (mudayanah) and half as a guarantor (kafalah). This division is not from a textual source but from the nature of joint liability and suretyship.
This is a ruling based on talil (causal reasoning) rather than explicit scriptural evidence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reasoning works as follows: the total debt is split equally between the two debtors. Each debtor's liability consists of two halves: his own debt and his guarantee for the other. Therefore, any payment up to half is attributed to the paying debtor's own debt. Payments beyond half are attributed to his guarantee liability. Recourse for the guarantee portion depends on whether the guarantee was at the request of the co-debtor.
This step shows how the division determines the extent of recourse.
4
Note the distinction between requested and unrequested guarantee
If the surety was given at the request of the co-debtor ('an amrihi), the paying party can claim the excess amount back from the co-debtor. If the surety was given without request (bi ghayri amrihi), the payment is considered voluntary, and no recourse is allowed.
This distinction is critical for the outcome.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical effect: in a joint purchase of 1,000 units, each owes 500. If one pays 600, the excess 100 is recoverable only if he was a guarantor by request. Without request, he loses the 100. This ruling protects co-debtors from unwanted liability.
Understanding this helps in structuring joint financing arrangements.

He said: (And when the debt is on two persons, and each of them is a surety and guarantor for the other) — for example, when they buy a slave for one thousand and each guarantees for his companion: whatever one pays, he cannot take recourse against his partner until what he pays exceeds half, then he takes recourse for the excess. Because the money on each of them is two halves: half from the aspect of debt (mugharama) and half from the aspect of surety (kafalah). So if he pays half or less, it falls for himself due to the debt; and what is more than that, he becomes liable for it due to the surety. If he became surety upon his (the other's) request, and he paid, he takes recourse against him because he (the other) brought him into the guarantee. But if he became surety without his request, he does not take recourse against him.

Commentary
This passage explains a specific ruling in joint suretyship. When two persons (e.g., two buyers jointly purchasing a slave for 1,000) are both debtors and also mutual guarantors for each other (each is kafil and damin for the other), the debt is divided into two halves: each owes 500 as a debtor and 500 as a guarantor. Therefore, if one of them pays up to 500, that payment is deemed to be for his own debt (from the mugharama aspect), and he cannot claim reimbursement from his partner. If he pays more than 500, the excess (above 500) is attributed to his liability as a guarantor. Whether he can recover that excess from his co-debtor depends on whether he became a guarantor at the other's request (kafalah bi-amrih). If yes, he can claim the excess back because the other brought him into the guarantee. If he guaranteed without request (kafalah bi-ghayr amrih), he cannot claim the excess; it is a voluntary payment. This ruling is based on the concept that the debt is shared, and the suretyship adds a secondary liability. The reasoning (talil) divides the payment proportionally.

Key Terms
كفيل ضامن (kafil damin): A person who is both a surety (kafil) and a guarantor (damin) for another, taking full responsibility for the debt.
المداينة (al-mudayanah): The relationship of debt; here it refers to the portion of the payment that is attributed to one's own debt obligation.

Recourse of Joint Debtors Who Are Mutual Guarantors
Ruling on recourse of mutual guarantors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a joint debtor who is also a guarantor can recover payment from his co-debtor and explain the rationale based on direct liability (mudayana) versus guarantee (kafala).

Core Idea
A joint debtor can only recover from his partner what he pays beyond his own half, and only if he guaranteed at the partner's request.

This passage from the Kitab al-Kafala addresses a common scenario in joint transactions: two people jointly purchase an item and each guarantees the other's payment. The ruling clarifies that the amounts paid are legally allocated first to the payer's own direct share, and only the surplus is governed by guarantee rules. Understanding this allocation prevents unjust enrichment and preserves the proper order of liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their liabilities
Recognize that each debtor owes the creditor in two capacities: as a direct debtor for his share (e.g., half the price) and as a guarantor for the other's share.
In the example, two people buy a slave for 1000, each directly liable for 500 and each a guarantor for the other's 500.
2
Determine the amount paid relative to half
Compare the sum paid by one debtor to half of the total debt. Half is the threshold because that is each debtor's direct share.
If total debt = 1000, half = 500. Check whether the payment is ≤500 or >500.
3
Apply the rule for recovery
If the payment is ≤ half, it is entirely attributed to the payer's own direct liability—no recovery from the partner. Only the excess over half is potentially recoverable as a guarantee payment.
E.g., paying 600: 500 is own share, 100 is as guarantor.
4
Check the basis of the guarantee
The recoverability of the excess depends on whether the guarantee was given at the request of the partner (bi amrihi) or without request (bi ghayri amrihi). If requested, the payer can recover the excess; if not, he cannot.
This distinction stems from the principle that a guarantor who volunteers without request bears the loss.

Commentary

The logical basis is that each debtor is directly liable for half the debt (as a co-purchaser) and additionally liable as a guarantor for the other's half. Payment first covers the payer's own share, and only the surplus is subject to guarantee rules.

Two Independent Guarantors with Mutual Guarantee: Recourse Between Co-Guarantors
Ruling on recourse for independent guarantors with mutual guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on recourse when two persons each independently guarantee a debt and each guarantees the other, and distinguish it from the case of joint guarantee.

Core Idea
When guarantors are independently liable for the full debt, they share any payment equally.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a key distinction in Hanafi kafalah. Two persons guarantee a debt, each for the full amount, and additionally guarantee each other's liability. The question is: if one pays, how much can he recover from the other? The answer depends on whether their initial liability was independent or joint.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial guarantee structure
Determine whether each guarantor is independently liable for the full debt (infirad) or whether they jointly guarantee the debt (ma'an). The passage presents two scenarios.
The key phrase is 'each one of them guarantees the entire money individually' vs 'they both guarantee jointly'.
2
Apply the mutual guarantee rule
If they mutually guarantee each other, the effect on recourse depends on the initial liability. For independent liability, any payment is split equally; for joint liability, only the excess over half is shared.
Mutual guarantee means each is a guarantor for the other's liability.
3
Calculate recourse for a given payment
In the independent scenario, the paying guarantor recovers half of what he paid from the co-guarantor, regardless of the amount. For example, paying 300 dirhams yields recovery of 150 dirhams.
This applies whether the payment is little or much.
4
Distinguish from the joint guarantee case
If the initial guarantee was joint (mudayana), the paying guarantor recovers only the amount exceeding half of the total debt. For a 1000 dirham debt, paying 300 dirhams recovers nothing; paying 800 recovers 300.
In joint guarantee, up to half is considered the payer's own share.

Core Idea
Kafala for kitaba money is invalid because the slave can annul the debt without payment, making it not a true debt.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding guarantees for kitaba (manumission) contracts. The passage clarifies that kafala for the money owed under kitaba is invalid, whether the guarantor is free or a slave. The underlying reasoning is that the kitaba obligation is not a valid debt (dyn ghayr sahih) because the slave can unilaterally terminate it by declaring inability to pay.

One condition of valid kafala is that the liability of the principal debtor (asil) and the guarantor (kafil) must be identical. Since the slave's liability is not a fixed debt, this condition is not met.
If the debt is not valid in the asil's liability, it cannot be transferred to the kafil.

Address the objection about free vs slave

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In cross-suretyship, recourse depends on whether each surety guaranteed the entire debt independently or jointly.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the matn of al-Quduri regarding two sureties who guarantee each other. The key is understanding the difference between independent suretyship (each liable for the whole individually) and joint suretyship (liability shared). The commentator expands the base text to clarify when the paying surety can recover half or only the excess from his partner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two suretyship structures
Read the passage and note the two different arrangements: first, each surety independently guarantees the full debt and also guarantees his companion; second, they both jointly guarantee the debt and also guarantee each other.
The first is like 'kafalah mufradah' (independent suretyship), the second like 'kafalah mushtarakah' (joint suretyship).
2
Apply the recourse rule to the first structure
In the first structure, whatever one pays, he recovers half from his partner, regardless of amount. This is because each is liable for the whole, and the cross-guarantee creates equal sharing.
The text says: 'فما أداه أحدهما : يرجع بنصفه على شريكه، قليلاً كان أو كثيراً'.
3
Apply the recourse rule to the second structure
In the second structure (joint suretyship), the paying surety does not recover half until he pays more than half. He recovers only the excess over his share.
The text compares it to joint debt (مداينة): 'فما أداه أحدهما لا يرجع على صاحبه بنصفه حتى يزيد ما أداه على النصف'.
4
Distinguish from ordinary joint debt
The analogy to joint debt means that among themselves, sureties are each responsible for an equal share. Recourse is only for amounts paid beyond that share.
This follows the Hanafi principle that joint suretyship is treated like joint liability (مداينة).
5
Summarize the practical difference
The two scenarios lead to different outcomes: in independent cross-suretyship, half is always recoverable; in joint cross-suretyship, only the excess over half is recoverable.
The key factor is whether the suretyship is 'على الانفراد' (individually) or 'معاً' (jointly).

Book of Hawala. Hawala linguistically: derived from tahwil (transfer) and naql (movement), meaning: moving something from one place to another. Legally: it is an expression for transferring debt from the liability of the principal debtor to the liability of the person charged (the acceptor), by way of securing it. And one needs to know four names: al-muhil (the transferor): the one who originally owes the debt. Al-muhal lahu (the creditor): the one who claims. Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Hawala. It first gives the linguistic and legal definitions. Legally, hawala is the transfer of a debt from the original debtor's liability to another person's liability as a form of security. The text then identifies the four parties: the transferor (muhil), the creditor (muhal lahu), the drawee (muhal 'alayh), and the transferred debt (muhal bihi). The core ruling from the matn is that hawala is permissible only with debts. The commentary explains that this restriction excludes physical assets and rights. The reason is that debts are inherently transferable between liabilities. An interesting point: even a debt that cannot be guaranteed by kafalah, such as the obligation of kitabah (where a slave contracts to purchase his freedom), can be transferred via hawala. This shows that hawala is more flexible than kafalah in some respects.

Types of Hawala: Absolute and Restricted
Explains the two types of hawala and the effect of debt voidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between absolute and restricted hawala and determine the validity of a restricted hawala when the underlying debt is void or falls.

Core Idea
In restricted hawala, the debt must exist; if it is void, the hawala is void.

Hawala (debt transfer) is a common transaction where a debt is shifted from one person to another. The Hanafi school classifies it into two types: absolute and restricted. Understanding the difference is crucial because the validity and consequences differ when the underlying debt is compromised.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type
Determine if the transferor specified a debt owed by the drawee. If yes, it is restricted (muqayyadah); if no, it is absolute (mutlaqah).
The wording 'by the thousand that I owe you' indicates restricted.
2
Check the underlying debt (restricted only)
For restricted hawala, verify that the specified debt actually exists and is valid. If the debt is void or the drawee is exonerated, the hawala becomes void.
Examples: sold item reclaimed, slave found to be free, deposit perishes.
3
Distinguish voidance from incidental fall
If the debt falls due to an incidental cause (e.g., goods destroyed before delivery) without exonerating the original debtor, the hawala remains valid. The drawee who pays may recourse to the transferor.
Key difference: voidance (batal) annuls the hawala; incidental fall (sqt) does not.
4
Apply the rule
Given a scenario, identify the type, check the status of the underlying debt, and state whether the hawala is valid or void, and the consequences for the parties.
Use the examples from the text as templates.

And hawala is of two types: absolute (mutlaqah) and restricted (muqayyadah). The absolute is that a man says to another: 'Transfer for this person from me one thousand dirhams,' and he says: 'I have transferred.' The restricted is that he says: 'Transfer with the thousand that I owe you,' and he says: 'I have transferred.' Both are permissible, and in both, the transferor (muhil) is discharged from his debt. However, there is a difference between absolute and restricted: when it is restricted, the right of claim of the transferor from the drawee is cut off. So if the debt in the restricted type is void, or the drawee is exonerated from the debt with which the hawala was restricted, the hawala becomes void. For example, a man buys something from a man for one thousand, and paid not the thousand until he transferred it to a man upon him, and he accepted, then the sold item is reclaimed, or the sold item was a slave who turned out to be free, then the hawala becomes void in these two cases, and the creditor may return to the transferor for his debt. Similarly, if he restricted the hawala to a thousand dirhams deposited with a man as a trust, and the thousand is destroyed with the depositary before delivery to the creditor, then the hawala becomes void. And as for if the debt with which the hawala was restricted falls due to an incidental cause, and the original debtor's exoneration is not established, the hawala does not become void. For example, he transfers with one thousand from the price of a sale, then the sold item is destroyed before delivery to the buyer: the price is remitted from him, and the hawala does not become void, but if he pays, he may recourse to the transferor for what he paid, because he discharged his debt by his command.

Commentary
This passage details the two types of hawala. The absolute (mutlaqah) is where the transferor asks the drawee to transfer a debt without specifying that the drawee owes him something. The restricted (muqayyadah) is where the transferor tells the drawee to transfer the debt that the drawee owes him. Both are valid, and in both the transferor is freed from the debt. The key difference: in restricted hawala, the transferor loses the right to claim from the drawee directly; his claim is now with the creditor. The passage then explains the effect of voidance of the underlying debt. If the debt that was the basis of a restricted hawala becomes void (e.g., the sold goods are reclaimed or a slave turns out to be free), the hawala becomes void and the creditor can revert to the transferor. Similarly, if the debt was a deposit that perishes before transfer, the hawala is void. However, if the debt falls (sqt) due to an external reason without the exoneration of the original debtor (as when the sold goods are destroyed before delivery, remitting the price), the hawala remains valid. In that case, if the drawee pays, he can recoup from the transferor.

Key Terms
الحوالة المطلقة (al-hawalah al-mutlaqah): Absolute hawala: where the transferor asks the drawee to transfer a debt without specifying that the drawee owes him anything.
الحوالة المقيدة (al-hawalah al-muqayyadah): Restricted hawala: where the transferor specifies that the drawee owes him a debt, and transfers that debt to the creditor.
براءة (bara'ah): Exoneration/discharge: the drawee being free from the debt that was the basis of the hawala.
