Dower of an Unspecified Animal: Validity and Husband's Option
Ruling on unspecified animal dower with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a dower consisting of an unspecified animal and explain why the husband has the option to give its value.

Core Idea
An unspecified animal dower is valid; the husband may substitute its value because an animal cannot be a precise debt.

The Hanafi school holds that when a man marries a woman and specifies the dower as an animal without further description, the dower is valid. The wife receives an average specimen of that animal, but the husband retains the choice to pay its cash value instead. This flexibility stems from a key principle: animals cannot be established as a precise liability in the dhimmah, as evidenced by the rule for consumption of borrowed animals. Let us examine the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is specifying an animal dower without description (e.g., 'a horse' without breed). The ruling: the specification is valid (sahih).
This is the primary ruling being established.
2
Understand the entitlements
The wife is entitled to an average (al-wasaṭ) of that animal. The husband has a choice: either give the animal itself or pay its market value.
Average means a mid-quality specimen.
3
Note the effect of pre-consumption divorce
If divorce occurs before consummation, she gets half of the lesser value (half of either the animal's value or the average, whichever is less). This is by consensus (ijma).
This applies to all dowers.
4
Trace the proof for the husband's choice
The author provides a qiyas: animals cannot be validly established as a debt in the dhimmah, because if someone consumes a borrowed animal, he is not liable for its like but only its value. Thus the obligation to deliver a specific animal is not a precise debt, giving the husband the option to pay value.
This proof is a dalil aqli (rational evidence) based on analogy.

Conditional Dower: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditional dower

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on conditional dower based on the husband's marital status and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa invalidates the condition that increases dower if the husband has a wife; the Sahibayn validate both conditions.

This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a marriage contract where the dower is contingent on a condition: a lower dower if the husband has no wife, and a higher dower if he does. Abu Hanifa deems the higher condition invalid but the lower condition valid, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider both conditions valid. We will explore the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A marriage contract stipulates: dower is 1000 if the husband has no wife, and 2000 if he has a wife. This is a conditional dower.
The condition relates to the husband's existing marital status.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa: the first condition (no wife → 1000) is valid (jāʼiz); the second condition (has wife → 2000) is invalid (fāsid).
Invalid condition means the condition itself is void, but the marriage may still be valid.
3
Understand the consequence under Abu Hanifa
If he has no wife, she gets 1000. If he has a wife, she gets mahr al-mithl (dower of her peers), but capped between 1000 and 2000.
Mahr al-mithl is not less than 1000 nor more than 2000.
4
State the Sahibayn's position
Both conditions are valid. Whichever condition is met, that dower applies.
If he has a wife, she gets 2000; if not, she gets 1000.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
If the husband divorces before consummation, under Abu Hanifa she gets half of the lesser (half of 1000 = 500) regardless of his marital status. Under the Sahibayn, she gets half of the applicable amount (half of 1000 or half of 2000).
Also, if he has a wife, under Abu Hanifa she gets mahr al-mithl (not necessarily 2000), while under Sahibayn she gets 2000.

Conditional Dower and Polygyny: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dower conditional on marital status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on a marriage dower conditioned on the husband's existing wife and apply the ruling to determine the dower amount in a given scenario.

Core Idea
A condition penalizing polygyny is invalid; the dower reverts to mahr al-mithl within the named bounds.

This passage explores an intra-Hanafi dispute about a marriage contract where the dower amount depends on whether the husband already has a wife. Abu Hanifa invalidates the condition that increases the dower for a husband with a wife, seeing it as contrary to the permissibility of polygyny. His two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, uphold both conditions as valid. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between permissible and impermissible conditions in marriage contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed conditions
Read the source passage and list the two conditions: one for when the husband has no wife (dower = 1000) and one for when he has a wife (dower = 2000).
The conditions are contingent on an existing fact, not a future event.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds the first condition valid and the second invalid. Ask: why does he invalidate the second? Because it discourages polygyny, which is permissible in Shari'ah.
The condition is fasid because it contradicts the objective of the law.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and reasoning
The Sahibayn consider both conditions valid. They see both as simple price variations based on a factual contingency, not as a prohibition.
Their reasoning focuses on the condition being attached to an existing state, not a forbidden act.
4
Trace the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, if the husband has a wife, she gets mahr al-mithl (bounded by the two amounts: min 1000, max 2000); under the Sahibayn, she gets the specified 2000.
Note also the rule for divorce before consummation: half of the lesser amount.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Given a similar conditional dower (e.g., 'if you are a virgin, 500; if not, 300'), determine which condition, if any, is valid per each imam.
Apply the same principle: conditions that penalize a permissible state are invalid.

Validity of an Unspecified Animal Dower and the Middle Value
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute on grades

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dower specified as an unnamed animal, explain why it is valid, and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the definition of the 'middle' grade.

Core Idea
An unspecified animal as dower is valid because the genus is known; the wife gets the middle value, and the husband may choose the animal or its cash value.

The Sharh of Al-Quduri clarifies that if a man marries a woman on the condition of an animal without describing its quality, the dower specification is valid. The wife is entitled to the middle grade of that animal. The husband has the option to give the actual animal or its monetary value. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling and the internal Hanafi differences in determining 'middle' for slaves—a key example that illustrates the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is marrying a woman on the condition of an animal without describing its quality (e.g., a horse, donkey). The ruling is that the dower specification is valid (sahih), and the wife gets the middle (al-wasaṭ) of that animal.
The validity depends on the genus being named—if only 'riding beast' is said, the tasmiyah fails and she gets mahr al-mithl.
2
Understand the husband's option
The husband may choose to give the actual animal or its market value. This is because animals are not considered soundly established as a debt in the dhimmah; a person who consumes an animal is liable for its value, not its like.
This is based on qiyas: the ruling for consumption of an animal (value) is applied to dower.
3
Examine the definition of 'middle' for slaves
The commentator uses slaves as an example to define grades. If the dower is a slave without specifying white or black, the wife gets the middle grade. He then presents a dispute: Abu Hanifa defines the good as Roman, middle as Sindhi, poor as Indian with fixed values; the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) define good as Turkish, middle as Saqlabi, poor as Indian, with values based on market fluctuations.
The author of al-Mustafa favors the view of the two.
4
Recognize the tarjih
The author cites al-Mustafa: 'Wa qawlahuma huwa al-sahih'—the opinion of the two is correct. This means the operative fatwa is that values depend on market conditions, not fixed numbers.
This tarjih applies to the definition of grades, not to the basic validity of the animal dower.

Resolving Disputes Over Gifts vs. Dower: The Makuul Exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for determining whether a husband's sent item is part of the dower or a gift, distinguishing perishable from non-perishable goods.

Core Idea
When husband and wife dispute over a sent item, the husband's word is taken, except for perishable food—there the wife's word prevails.

In marriage, the husband pays a dower (mahr) to the wife. Sometimes a husband sends gifts or items that could be part of the dower. When they disagree, Hanafi fiqh provides a clear rule: the husband's claim is generally believed, because the husband is expected to pay mahr. However, for perishable food items, the wife's claim is believed because such items are usually gifts. This lesson explains the rule and its exception.

Guided Path

State the general rule
The default presumption: when a husband sends something and disputes arise, his word is taken because he is the one obligated to pay the dower.
This is based on the principle that the party who bears the burden of proof often has the stronger claim.

For items classified as 'makuul' (perishable food), the wife's word is taken instead.
The rationale: such items are typically given as gifts, not as formal dower payments.
3

Makuul includes items prepared for immediate consumption that spoil quickly, such as bread, fresh fruits, milk, and cooked dishes.
These contrast with staple goods like wheat, barley, and live animals which are non-perishable.
4

Work through the examples: bread, dates, watermelon (wife's word); wheat, flour, live sheep (husband's word).
The key is whether the item is likely to be a gift due to its perishability.
5

Obligatory items like the head covering (khimar) and clothing (kiswa) cannot be counted as part of the dower, regardless of dispute.
These are considered maintenance (nafaqa) items, not dower.
