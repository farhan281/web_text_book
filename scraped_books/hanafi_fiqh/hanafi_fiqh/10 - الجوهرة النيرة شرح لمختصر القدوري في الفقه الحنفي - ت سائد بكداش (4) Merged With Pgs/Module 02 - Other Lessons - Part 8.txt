Dispute on Minimum Reward for Low-Value Slave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on reward amount when slave value under 40 dirhams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the reward for returning a slave worth less than 40 dirhams and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The reward for returning a lost slave is reduced when value is under 40 dirhams, so owner retains something.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and Abu Hanifa (or as recorded, Abu Yusuf appears on both sides—likely an OCR ambiguity). The issue: if a slave worth less than 40 dirhams is found and returned, should the finder get 40 dirhams (as the Companions originally fixed) or a reduced amount to benefit the owner? We will analyze the proofs and the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
State the two conflicting positions: (1) the view of 'the two' (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): value minus one dirham; (2) Abu Yusuf's separate view (likely Abu Hanifa's): full 40 dirhams.
The text attributes both views to Abu Yusuf, which is likely a scribal error.
2
Analyze the proof of the first view
The two argue that the purpose (maqsid) is to motivate return while preserving the owner's property; reducing by one dirham ensures the owner retains a benefit.
This relies on ta'līl (teleological reasoning).
3
Analyze the proof of the second view
Abu Yusuf (or Abu Hanifa) argues that the Companions fixed 40 dirhams without differentiating value, so the text (nass) is general and binding.
This relies on the literal generality of the Companions' determination.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The two's response: the text does not explicitly prohibit reduction; reducing achieves the goal while being consistent with the text.
5
Determine the practical difference
Thamarah al-khilaf: finder gets either 40 dirhams or (value minus one), affecting the owner's net benefit from recovery.
Mufta bihi: value minus one dirham.

Reward for Returning a Runaway Slave: Special Cases (Father, Minor’s Guardian, Ruler)
Ruling statements on reward exceptions for father, guardian, and ruler.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which special returners receive reward for returning a runaway slave and identify the legal reasoning (customary obligation or return to one’s own hand) for denying reward.

Core Idea
No reward for returners who act out of customary obligation or who return the slave to themselves.

The general rule is that a stranger who returns a runaway slave is entitled to a reward (ja'l) from the owner. However, when the returner has a special relationship with the owner or acts in an official capacity, the reward is withheld. This lesson examines the rulings for the father, son, spouses, guardian (wasi) of a minor owner, and the ruler (sultan), all of whom receive no reward. The reasoning is either that they are customarily expected to return the slave voluntarily, or that the legal effect of their return is as if they returned the slave to themselves. Understanding these exceptions reinforces the principle that the reward is only given for a genuine favor beyond ordinary expectation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the special returner categories
The passage discusses five categories: father of the owner, son of the owner, spouses, guardian of a minor owner, and the ruler. For each, note the ruling on reward entitlement.
The father and son are further subdivided based on whether they are in the household of the owner.
2
Extract the ruling for each category
State clearly: father (if in household – no reward; if not – reward), son (same as father), spouse (no reward for either returning the other’s slave), guardian of minor (no reward), ruler (no reward).
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (illah)
For father, son, and spouse: the reasoning is customary obligation – they are expected to return the slave voluntarily. For guardian: returning to his own hand is not a true return to the minor. For ruler: it is his obligatory duty (fard kifayah).
The Hidayah and Yanabi' are cited as sources for these rulings.
4
Compare with the general rule
The general rule for a stranger is that reward is given. These exceptions show that reward is only for a genuine service above what is owed by custom or duty.

When a slave’s value is low, Sahibayn reduce the reward by one dirham; Abu Yusuf insists on forty — the purpose of the reward vs. the Companions' text.

Core Idea
Entitlement to reward depends on delivery; liability depends on having witnesses.

This passage continues the discussion on the finder's reward (ju'l). It examines nuanced scenarios: what happens if the found slave runs away again, if the owner frees or sells the slave, and the finder's liability for loss. The key distinction is whether the finder had witnesses when taking the slave, which determines if his holding is a trust (amānah) or a guarantee.

The Finder of a Fugitive Slave: Reward and Liability
Commentary on Kitab al-Ibaq regarding the finder's rights and duties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the finder of a fugitive slave is entitled to the reward (ju'l) and determine his liability for loss based on the requirement of witnessing (ishhad).

Core Idea
Witnessing at the time of taking a fugitive slave is the decisive factor for both reward and liability.

In Hanafi fiqh, finding a runaway slave (abiq) and returning him to his master is a meritorious act that may entitle the finder to a reward (ju'l). However, entitlement to the reward and protection from liability depend on the finder's adherence to specific procedures, particularly the requirement of having witnesses at the moment of taking the slave. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah elucidates these rules and the intra-madhhab disagreement surrounding them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal nature
The finder takes the slave as a trust (amanah) if witnesses are present. Without witnesses, the taking is considered for the finder's own benefit, making him a guarantor (damin).
2
Determine the conditions for the reward
The finder must take possession with intention to return, and the master must not have emancipated the slave before return. Also, the slave must be successfully returned.
3
Analyze the witnessing requirement
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, witnessing (ishhad) is obligatory (hatm) at the time of taking. Without it, the finder loses the reward and becomes liable for the slave's value.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf disagrees on liability, holding that the finder is not liable even without witnesses. However, the relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
5
Apply the correct ruling
The mufta bihi (operative) position is that witnessing is essential for both reward and non-liability. If the finder omits witnesses, he is liable and gets no reward.

Core Idea
Mawat is land that is both unproductive and ownerless; reviving it grants ownership.

In Hanafi law, reviving dead land (iḥyāʾ al-mawāt) is a legitimate means of acquiring ownership. But what exactly qualifies as dead land? This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah lays out the definition and conditions with precision. Understanding these criteria is essential before any discussion of ownership through revival.

Guided Path

Benefit here primarily means cultivation, but construction also qualifies.
2
Determine the ownership status
The land must be either originally ownerless (ʿādī) with no known owner, or previously owned in the Islamic era but the owner is no longer known. If a known owner exists, it is not mawat.

The land must be far enough from the built-up area that a shout from the farthest edge of the village is not heard in the land. This ensures it is not part of the village's common land (murfāq).
The exact distance may vary but the test is practical.

If all three conditions are met (unproductive, no known owner, far from village), the land is legally mawat. Reviving it (e.g., by irrigation or construction) gives ownership to the reviver.
This is the mufta bihi view in the Hanafi school.

Ruling on Taḥjīr (Marking Land) Without Cultivation for Three Years

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Marking land does not give ownership; only cultivation does. Three years neglect triggers removal.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafī ruling on dead land (mawāt) that has been marked for cultivation but left uncultivated. The matn gives the rule: after three years, the imām may take it and give it to someone else. Core Idea
Tahjir does not own land; only ihya does, and three years' neglect triggers Imam's seizure.

In Hanafi fiqh, reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat) grants ownership, but merely marking it (tahjir) is only an expression of intent. This lesson examines a specific ruling: if a person marks land but leaves it uncultivated for three years, the Imam must take it and give it to another. We will explore the reasoning based on customary practice (urf) and the distinction between diyanah (religious duty) and qada (legal enforceability).

Identify the act and ruling

2

Three years is chosen because lands are typically cultivated once a year, and three years is the maximum period to indicate lack of interest. Leaving it idle for that long shows intent to destroy, so the Imam must intervene for public benefit (tax revenue).
The proof is based on customary practice (urf), a zanni dalil.

This ruling operates diyanatan (religiously). If someone else revives the land before three years, they own it, though it is makruh (like istiyam). The Imam's duty is religious, not necessarily enforceable in court.
Diyanah vs. qada is a key Hanafi concept.

Remedy for Digging a Well in the Harim: Qiyas and the Correct Opinion
Remedy for encroachment on harim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the remedy for encroaching on a harim and distinguish between compelling the digger to fill and requiring compensation.

Core Idea
If someone digs a well in the harim, the first owner may fill it voluntarily; the correct view is to make the digger pay the deficiency.

After defining the harim, the passage addresses what happens if someone violates it. The author presents a qiyas analogy to a trespasser dumping garbage in someone's house, leading to two opinions: compelling the digger to fill or, more correctly, compensating the loss. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the violation and default remedy
Digging a well in the harim is an encroachment. The first owner has the right to fill it voluntarily (تبرعا). This is his default option.
The word 'تبرعا' indicates he may do so out of his own initiative, without compelling the digger.
2
Examine the first opinion: compel the digger to fill
Some scholars say the first owner can compel the second to fill the well because the digging is a trespass (جناية). They analogize to throwing garbage in another's house: the trespasser is forced to remove it.
The qiyas is based on the illa of causing harm to another's property.
3
Examine the second opinion: compensation and then filling
Other scholars say the correct view (الصحيح) is that the digger must compensate the deficiency (النقصان) in the value of the land/water right, and then the first owner fills the well himself.
This balances the harm: the digger's well may have been expensive, but it still harms the first owner's right.
4
Understand the tarjih
The text explicitly states 'وهو الصحيح' (and that is the correct opinion). So the mufta bihi is the compensation opinion.
This tarjih is from the author of al-Jawhara or the later Hanafi tradition.

Rulings on reclaiming abandoned riverbeds and the protected area (harim) around wells and trees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reviving land abandoned by a river, identify the requirement of Imam's permission according to Abu Hanifa, and apply the rules of harim to wells and trees.

Core Idea
Land abandoned by a river is public property if water may return; otherwise it is dead land, but revival requires Imam's permission.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, providing rulings on water rights and land ownership. The river's change of course raises questions of ownership and public need. Additionally, the concept of harim protects the utility of wells and trees. Understanding these rules prevents disputes over water and land use in agricultural and residential contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the riverbed scenario
Determine whether the water can return to the abandoned tract (e.g., seasonal flooding) or not (permanent shift).
This is the key factual condition that determines the ruling.
2
Apply the ruling based on return possibility
If water can return, revival is prohibited due to public need. If not, the land is like dead land (mawat) and may be revived subject to conditions.
The prohibition is based on maslahah (public interest).
3
Note the condition of Imam's permission
According to Abu Hanifa, revival of dead land requires the Imam's permission for ownership. The Sahibayn do not require this.
This is a valid intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Understand harim for wells
A well has a protected area (harim) around it. Digging beyond that area causing the first well to dry up does not incur liability. The second digger gets harim on three sides.
The exact measurement of well harim is not given here but is typically 40 cubits in other texts.
5
Extend to trees
A tree planted in dead land also has a harim of five cubits, preventing others from planting within that radius.
This is from Al-Hidayah.

Definition and Basis of Idhn (Permission for a Slave to Trade)
Definition and rationale of idhn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define idhn in the Hanafi school and explain why a slave requires permission to trade.

Core Idea
Idhn removes the master's right to block trade, restoring the slave's inherent capacity to contract.

This lesson opens the chapter on the 'ma'dhun' – a slave permitted to trade. The author first defines idhn as the removal of restriction and dropping of the master's right. Understanding this definition is essential because it explains why the slave, who otherwise has legal capacity, is barred from trading without permission. The permission is not a grant of capacity but a removal of the master's right to object.

Break down the definition: (1) 'fakk al-hajr' – lifting the restriction that prevents the slave from transacting, and (2) 'isqat al-haqq' – dropping the master's right to prevent the slave's transactions.

The text explains that the slave's restriction is due to the master's right over his person and earnings. The slave's capacity (ahliyyah) is intact, but the master's right blocks exercise.

After permission, the slave 'يتصرف لنفسه بأهليته' – he acts for himself with his own capacity. The permission does not create capacity but removes the obstacle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that conditional phrasing (ta'liq) is valid for establishing permission and agency but invalid for revoking rights, and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
You can grant a right conditionally, but you cannot revoke it conditionally.

Can a master say 'when tomorrow comes, you are permitted'? Yes. But if he says 'when tomorrow comes, you are restricted,' that is ineffective. Conditional Future Acts: Authorization Valid, Dismissal Invalid
Ruling on validity of conditional future acts: authorization vs. dismissal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on conditional future acts and distinguish between valid and invalid conditional statements based on whether they create or revoke legal capacity.

Core Idea
Authorization can be conditional on the future; but dismissal, restraint, and revocable return cannot — because creating capacity is valid, revoking is not.

In Hanafi fiqh, the validity of a conditional future statement depends on what it does: creating a new capacity (like permission to trade or agency) can be suspended, but revoking or changing an existing capacity (like dismissing an agent or restraining a slave) must be immediate. Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage presents two sets of conditional statements: those that create capacity (authorization for trade, appointment as agent) and those that revoke or change capacity (dismissal, restraint of ma'dhun, taking back revocable divorcee).
Note that the first two examples create new rights; the last three affect existing rights.
2
Apply the creation vs. revocation principle
In Hanafi fiqh, acts that create capacity (like granting permission or appointing an agent) are valid when conditional because they bring about a new legal state. Acts that revoke or alter existing capacity are invalid when conditional because they involve an immediate right that cannot be suspended.
The underlying reason: creating capacity is a form of 'initiation' (insha'), while revocation is 'removal' (izala) which must be instantaneous.

4

A conditional future statement is valid if it creates a new legal capacity; it is invalid if it revokes or modifies an existing capacity.
This distinction is well-established in Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi position.

The ma'dhun (a slave permitted to trade) has limited legal capacity. He can engage in trade-related acts but cannot bind himself as a surety without special permission. Core Idea
Sharing daily food is permissible; sharing monthly food is not, because harm to the master differs.

Imam Abu Yusuf, one of the great Hanafi jurists, gives a precise ruling on the extent of a restricted slave's authority over his sustenance. The key distinction turns on the amount—daily or monthly—and the potential harm to the master. This lesson clarifies the legal reasoning behind this distinction.

Identify the subject and act
The subject is a restricted slave (mahjur 'alayh) who is given food by his master. The act is inviting companions to share that food.

Core Idea

Some phrasings like 'in adayta ilayya alfan fa anta hurr' are restricted to the session (majlis). If payment occurs in that same session, freedom is effective.
The master must accept the payment if offered in the session.

The key is whether the statement creates a conditional suspension or a direct command. Hanafis use dalalah (implication) to infer permission for trade when the condition requires earning.
