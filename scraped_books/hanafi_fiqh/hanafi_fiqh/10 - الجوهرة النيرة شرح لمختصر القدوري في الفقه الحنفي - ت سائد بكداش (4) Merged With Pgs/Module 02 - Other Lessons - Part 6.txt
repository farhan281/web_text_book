Joint Depositary Handing to Co-Depositary: The Sahibayn vs Abu Hanifa
Intra-Hanafi dispute on joint depositaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on one joint depositary handing a divisible deposit to the other, and apply the distinction to determine liability.

Core Idea
The owner's consent is limited to joint trusteeship; one alone cannot transfer the whole deposit without making himself liable.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the scope of a depositary's authority when two people are entrusted with a single deposit. The matn states a rule based on divisibility, but the Sahibayn challenge it. The key is whether the owner's satisfaction with both trustees implies permission for one to act alone. We'll analyse the positions and the practical consequence (daman).

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The passage asks: when two men receive a deposit, can one of them hand the entire deposit to the other without the owner's consent? The answer differs for divisible vs indivisible items according to the matn, but the Sahibayn have a uniform rule.
2
State the matn's ruling
For divisible items: it is not permissible to hand over; they must divide and each keep half. For indivisible items: one may keep it with the other's permission (since division is impossible).
Note that the matn's rule is the default position, but the dispute emerges in the commentary.
3
Present the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that in both cases one may hand over with the other's permission. Their proof: the owner was satisfied with the trusteeship of both, so each has the authority to delegate to the other, analogous to the indivisible case where permission is obviously intended.
The analogy (qiyas) from indivisible to divisible is the core of their argument.
4
Present Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that for divisible items, the owner's consent is limited to joint preservation. Handing over to one alone violates that consent, making the giver liable (daman). The receiver, as a sub-depositary (mūdiʿ al-mūdiʿ), is not liable unless negligent.
The distinction is based on the owner's implied expectation: if the item can be divided, he expects each to keep his share; if not, he trusts their judgement.
5
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, if one joint depositary hands a divisible deposit to the other without permission and the item is lost, the giver is liable for the value. Under the Sahibayn, he is not liable because he acted with permission (implied by the co-depositary's agreement).
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi; it presents the dispute as unresolved.

Depositary's Compliance with Owner's Instructions: Wife and Storage Location
Rulings on depositary's compliance with owner's instructions about delivery and storage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings of Abu Hanifa regarding a depositary's compliance with the owner's instructions, explain the talil (reasoning) behind each, and distinguish the Sahibayn's differing position.

Core Idea
Depositary may override owner's instruction if compliance conflicts with necessity or if alternative storage is equally secure.

A depositary is generally bound by the owner's instructions, but Abu Hanifa recognizes two exceptions: when the instruction involves the depositary's wife, and when it involves moving the deposit within the same dwelling. In both cases, the depositary is not liable even though he acted contrary to the instruction. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, especially regarding the wife, holding that the depositary must follow the instruction. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's position and the practical consequences of the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Each sub-paragraph presents a scenario: (1) owner says 'do not deliver to your wife', depositary delivers; (2) owner says 'keep in this house', depositary keeps in another house of the same dwelling. The ruling in both cases: depositary is not liable (lam yadman).
The ruling is about absence of liability, not about obligation to obey.
2
Locate the talil (reasoning) for each
For the wife case: 'because he must necessarily deliver to her' (necessity of household interaction). For the house case: 'because two houses in one dwelling do not differ in terms of being a secure place (ḥirz)'.
The talil is based on custom and the purpose of the deposit.
3
Note the conditions specified in the commentary
For the wife case: (a) the depositary has no other wife besides the one to whom delivery is prohibited, and (b) the deposit is of a type normally handled by women. For the house case: the two houses must be within the same dwelling (compound).
These conditions prevent misuse of the ruling.
4
Understand the Sahibayn's disagreement
The Sahibayn analogize this to the earlier case of two depositaries: if the owner consents to both, each may hand over to the other. Here, by prohibiting delivery to the wife, the owner did not consent to her, so delivery to her is a breach. Abu Hanifa distinguishes because the wife is not a co-depositary; she is a household member, and delivery to her is unavoidable.
The ikhtilaf is over whether the prohibition overrides necessity.
5
Apply the ruling to new scenarios
Consider: owner says 'do not keep in my wooden chest', depositary keeps in his own steel safe. If both are equally ḥirz, no liability. Or owner says 'do not give to your mother', but mother is the one who handles household goods. Abu Hanifa's reasoning would extend to such cases.
The key is whether the depositary's disobedience is required by necessity or whether the alternative is equally safe.

Depositary's Liability for Minor Instruction Violations
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on depositary liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on depositary liability when instructions are violated and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding two depositaries.

Core Idea
Not every instruction violation triggers liability; minor deviations preserving security are excused.

In Hanafi fiqh, a depositary is liable only when he transgresses (ta'addi) or is negligent. This lesson examines two scenarios where the depositary disobeys the owner's explicit instructions yet is not held liable. It also explores the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Sahibayn) on whether one of two joint depositaries may hand the deposit to the other without the owner's permission. Understanding these nuances prevents over-extension of liability in deposit contracts.

Identify the two scenarios

2
Analyze the rationale
For the wife scenario: the depositary inevitably needs to hand things to his wife for household management, so the prohibition is ineffective. For the house scenario: both houses are in the same compound and are equally secure (hirz), so no negligence.

This dispute is about a different scenario: two depositaries, not the original two examples.

The practical difference: if one of two depositaries hands the deposit to the other without the owner's explicit permission, the deliverer is liable according to Abu Hanifa but not according to Sahibayn.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The matn presents Abu Hanifa's view as the primary statement, and the commentary does not override it. Thus, the relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: no liability in the two original cases, and liability if one of two depositaries transfers without permission.

Liability for Moving Deposit and Related Rulings
Details rulings when deposit is moved to another house and additional practical scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a depositary is liable for moving a deposit, and apply the rulings on negligence, treatment of animals, and sale due to spoilage fear.

Core Idea
Moving deposit to a different house causes liability only if security is reduced; negligence always incurs liability.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah addresses several practical scenarios involving a depositary's liability. The first ruling clarifies that moving a deposit to a different compound does not automatically impose liability; it depends on relative security. The subsequent issues cover leaving a door open (negligence), hiring a healer for an animal, and selling perishable deposits. These rulings illustrate how the Hanafi school balances the duty of care with practical necessities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on moving deposits
The passage begins with the general rule: moving the deposit to a different house makes the depositary liable. Then it adds the critical condition: only if the new house is less secure. Determine the rationale: different compounds have distinct security contexts.
Note the exception: if the second house is equally or more secure, no liability.
2
Analyze the negligence scenario
The scenario of leaving the door open is presented as tadyi' (negligence). Identify that this is a clear breach of duty, and liability is unconditional.
No exception for equal or better security when the door is left open.
3
Examine the animal treatment case
When the depositary hires a healer for a sick/injured animal that later dies, the owner has a khiyar (option) to hold either the depositary or the healer liable. Trace the recourse rules depending on the healer's knowledge of ownership.
This khiyar is unique to this situation.
4
Understand the sale upon fear of spoilage
If the deposit risks spoilage, the depositary must seek a judge's permission to sell. If no judge exists, he may sell independently and keep the price for the owner. This is extended to luqatah (lost property).
The principle of darurah (necessity) permits sale without prior owner consent.
5
Synthesize the common theme
All rulings revolve around the depositary's duty to preserve the property. Liability arises from reducing security or negligence, while risk of total loss justifies proactive sale.

The Legal Nature of Ariyah: Tamlik vs Ibahah
Definition of 'ariyah and intra-Hanafi dispute on its nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi views on the nature of 'ariyah, identify the evidence for each, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Ariyah transfers ownership of benefits (tamlik), not mere permission, allowing sub-lending but not subleasing.

The contract of 'ariyah (gratuitous loan for use) is a common transaction in daily life, yet its precise legal nature is debated even within the Hanafi school. Does the borrower merely have permission to use the item, or does he own the right to its benefits? This passage defines 'ariyah and presents the arguments of two major Hanafi authorities: Abu Bakr al-Razi and al-Karkhi. Understanding this dispute clarifies what the borrower may do with the borrowed item—especially whether he can lend it to someone else.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, determine what act is being discussed ('ariyah) and its overall ruling (mubah/permissible). Note that 'ariyah is a non-binding contract revocable at will.
2
State the majority view (Abu Bakr al-Razi)
The majority of Hanafis hold that 'ariyah is tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad—ownership of the benefits without compensation. This means the borrower owns the right to use and can transfer that right to another.
3
State al-Karkhi's opposing view
Al-Karkhi defines 'ariyah as ibahah al-manafi'—mere permission to use the benefits owned by another. Under this view, the borrower has no ownership rights and cannot transfer the benefit.
4
Analyze the proofs (qiyas)
The text provides qiyas for both views: The majority argues that a borrower can sub-lend, which is only possible if he owns the benefit (like one who owns food can share it). Al-Karkhi argues that if it were ownership, the borrower could lease the item, but he cannot; thus it must be mere permission.
The Hanafi response to al-Karkhi explains that leasing is prohibited not because of lack of ownership, but because the borrower's right is revocable.
5
Identify the thamarah (practical consequence) and the mufta bihi
The practical difference: Under tamlik, the borrower may re-lend (but not lease); under ibahah, he may neither. The commentary declares the tamlik view more correct (al-awwal asahh), and it is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the school.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement followed by an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal nature of 'ariyah (borrowing/lending). The author begins with the linguistic derivation: 'ariyah either comes from 'ariyyah (gift) or from 'ar (disgrace), the latter due to the shame of asking for a loan. In legal terminology, it is defined as tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad (ownership of benefits without compensation). Key conditions include that the object must be consumable in use while its substance remains (e.g., money cannot be borrowed as 'ariyah but only as qard). 'Ariyah is non-binding (ghayr lazimah); the lender may revoke at any time, and it becomes void by the death of either party. The author then lists valid expressions: 'a‘tuka, at‘amtuka, manaḥtuka, etc., clarifying that if the word 'hibah' is intended, it is a gift not a loan. The commentary then focuses on the dispute: Abu Bakr al-Razi and the majority of Hanafis hold that 'ariyah is tamlik al-manafi' (ownership). Al-Karkhi holds that it is ibahah al-manafi' (permission to use benefits belonging to another). The text argues that the first view is more correct (al-awwal asahh). The proof: a borrower can lend the item to a third party; if it were mere ibahah, he could not permit another, just as one who is permitted to eat food may not permit another. Al-Karkhi's proof: if it were ownership, the borrower could lease (ijarah) the item, as a lessee can sublease. The Hanafi response: the prohibition of leasing a borrowed item is not due to lack of ownership of benefits, but because the borrower's right is conditional and revocable; leasing would create a fixed right that cuts off the lender's ability to reclaim. Thus the distinction between tamlik and ibahah has practical consequences: tamlik allows sub-lending but not subleasing, while ibahah would permit neither. The Hanafi position (tamlik) is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi).

العارية (al-'āriyah): In this text: tamlik al-manāfi' bi-ghayr 'iwaḍ (ownership of benefits without compensation), not mere ibāḥah.
تمليك المنافع (tamlik al-manāfi'): Transfer of ownership of the right to utilize benefits, as opposed to ownership of the substance ('ayn).
إباحة المنافع (ibāḥat al-manāfi'): Permission to use the benefits of an object owned by another, without transferring ownership.

Legal Nature of 'Ariyah: Tamlik vs Ibaha
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the legal nature of 'ariyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two views on the legal nature of 'ariyah, identify the qiyas evidence for each, and apply the Hanafi tarjih to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
'Ariyah is tamlik (ownership) of benefits, not mere ibaha — the borrower may lend the item to another.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah defines al-‘Ariyah and immediately presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between two towering figures: Abu Bakr al-Razi (the majority view) and al-Karkhi. The essence of the disagreement is whether the borrower owns the benefits (tamlik) or merely has permission to use them (ibaha). This seemingly technical distinction has a concrete practical effect: can the borrower lend the item to a third party? By tracing the qiyas arguments of each side and the commentator's tarjih, you will learn how Hanafi scholars resolve disputes within the school using rational proofs.

Guided Path
1
State the definition and ruling of 'ariyah
Identify the act (loan of benefits) and its ruling (ja'iz/permissible). Note the definition: tamlik al-manafi' bi ghayr 'iwad.
This establishes the baseline from which the dispute arises.
2
Identify the two positions
Recognize that al-Razi and the majority hold it is tamlik; al-Karkhi holds it is ibaha. Understand that both are Hanafi views.
Intra-madhhab ikhtilaf requires careful handling.
3
Analyze the evidence for tamlik
Trace the qiyas: the borrower may lend the item to another. If it were mere ibaha (like permission to eat food), the borrower could not permit another. Therefore, it must be tamlik.
The asl here is 'permission to eat' (ibaha), the far is 'ariyah, and the illa is lack of ownership to transfer.
4
Analyze the evidence for ibaha
Trace al-Karkhi's qiyas: if it were tamlik, the borrower could rent it out, like ijarah. Since renting is not allowed, it must be ibaha.
The asl is ijarah (tamlik with compensation), the illa is ownership of benefits.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response and tarjih
The response: the inability to rent is not due to lack of ownership but because the owner's right to reclaim is retained, which would be cut by a rental contract. The commentator declares the tamlik view 'asahh' (more correct).
This shows how a counter-argument is answered within the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah begins the section on loans of benefits (al-‘Ariyah). The author first quotes the matn stating that ‘ariyah is permissible and defines it as 'ownership of benefits without compensation.' The commentator then clarifies that this definition is from Abu Bakr al-Razi and the majority of Hanafis, but al-Karkhi disagreed, saying it is merely permission (ibaha) to use benefits of another's property. The commentator declares al-Razi's view more correct and provides the proof: a borrower can lend the item to someone else, which would not be possible if it were mere permission, just as one permitted to eat food cannot permit another to eat it. Al-Karkhi's proof is that if the borrower owned the benefits, he could rent the item like a lessee. The Hanafi response is that the inability to rent is not due to lack of ownership but because the owner's right to reclaim the item at any time would be cut off by a rental contract. This is a precise difference: ownership of benefits in ‘ariyah is conditional and revocable, unlike ijarah where the term is fixed. The madhhab follows the view that it is tamlik, which allows the borrower to lend to another, but not to rent.

Valid Expressions for Creating a Valid ‘Ariyah Contract
Lists and explains valid verbal expressions for creating an ‘ariyah contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify valid verbal expressions for establishing an ‘ariyah contract, explain why certain phrases are interpreted as loans of benefit, and apply the hadith of al-manhah mardudah to determine the legal classification.

Core Idea
A valid ‘ariyah can be expressed by many phrases; the hadith 'al-manhah mardudah' proves that a grant is a loan, not a gift.

In this passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, the commentator explains the various expressions that create a valid ‘ariyah (loan of benefit) contract. While the explicit term 'a‘artuka' is straightforward, other phrases like 'at‘amtuka al-ard' and 'manahṭuka' require interpretation. The proof from the Sunnah clarifies that a 'grant' (manhah) is a loan because it must be returned. This lesson will help you distinguish between valid and invalid expressions and understand the underlying principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit expression
Recognize that 'a‘artuka' is the direct and explicit term for ‘ariyah.
2
Analyze ambiguous expressions
Understand why 'at‘amtuka al-ard' is considered ‘ariyah: land is not eaten, so the intent must be to transfer benefit.
Compare with 'at‘amtuka al-ta‘am' which is a gift of the item.
3
Examine the hadith proof for manhah
The hadith 'al-manhah mardudah' proves that a manhah (grant) is a loan because the item must be returned.
If it were a gift, return would not be obligatory.
4
Understand time-limited expressions
Phrases like 'ʿumrā suknā' specify the benefit as lasting for the recipient's lifetime.
Upon death, the property returns to the owner.
5
Differentiate service expressions
'Akhdamtuka hadha al-ʿabd' explicitly conveys ownership of the benefit (service), making it a valid ‘ariyah.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the valid expressions for creating an ‘ariyah contract. The phrase 'I lend you' (a‘artuka) is explicitly for ‘ariyah. The phrase 'I have given you this land' (at‘amtuka hadhihi al-ard) is also ‘ariyah because land is not literally eaten, so the intended meaning is benefit; compare with 'I fed you this food' which would be a gift of the item itself. The phrase 'I have granted you this garment' (manahṭuka hadha al-thawb) is also ‘ariyah, supported by the hadith: 'The granted (manhah) is to be returned,' indicating that the item must be returned, hence it is not a gift of ownership. The word 'manhah' is defined as a gift. The phrase 'life-dwelling' (ʿumrā suknā) specifies the benefit for the lifespan of the recipient. The commentator also addresses a grammatical point: when the author says 'if he did not intend a gift' referring to both 'manahṭuka' and 'hamaltuka', he should have said 'bihima' (by both) but he said 'bihi' (by it) perhaps intending each one, analogous to a Quranic usage. Finally, 'I have made this slave serve you' is explicit for ownership of benefits because it is permission to use the slave's services.

Core Idea
The lender may revoke a loan at any time, and the borrower holds as a trust — no guarantee unless transgression or condition.

In this lesson, we examine a clear ruling from the Hanafi school: the lender has an unqualified right to revoke a loan ('ariyah) whenever he wishes. Core Idea
Once a borrower transgresses, liability persists — returning to the permitted zone does not restore trust status.

In this passage, we examine a specific case of transgression in 'ariyah: the borrower exceeds the agreed destination with a borrowed animal. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Transgression in 'ariyah makes the borrower like a usurper; liability persists until return to owner, not rectification of the transgression.

This lesson delves into a critical intra-Hanafi dispute regarding borrowed items. We will examine the majority Hanafi position, the opposing view of Imam Zufar, and the reasoning behind each.

If the borrower exceeds the permitted use (ta'addi), he becomes like a usurper (ghasib) and liability attaches permanently.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibitions and permissions of a borrower regarding a borrowed item, and determine when liability arises.

Core Idea
Borrowing does not give the right to lease or pawn; lending to another is only allowed if the item's usufruct is uniform and the original borrowing was unrestricted.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nairah expands on the rulings of 'ariyah, focusing on what the borrower can and cannot do with the borrowed item. The key tension is between the owner's right to reclaim and the borrower's temporary usufruct. Understanding these limits is crucial to avoid liability and to correctly categorize fungible items as qard rather than 'ariyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify prohibited acts
Locate the explicit prohibition: the borrower may not lease (ujr) or pawn (rahn) the borrowed item. Note the rational proof given (lending is below leasing).
The commentator uses qiyas (analogy) to the usurper (ghasib) for liability.

Understand liability scheme
If the borrower leases/paws and the item perishes, he is liable (daman). The owner may hold either the borrower or the sub-lessee/pawnee liable. Trace the recourse rights.
The sub-lessee can recourse to the borrower if unaware of the borrowing, to prevent harm from deception (ghurur).
3

The borrower may lend (i'arah) to another if (1) the borrowed item's usufruct does not vary with different users (e.g., a house), and (2) the original borrowing was unrestricted. If restricted, lending for a different use incurs liability.
Condition: item must be uniform in usufruct; otherwise, the owner's consent is exceeded.

This is a critical classification: in 'ariyah the very item is returned; in qard the equivalent is returned.
