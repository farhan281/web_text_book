Commentary
This passage from the sharh (commentary) of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands upon the rulings of divorce (talaq) when the wording is attached to the woman's entire body or to a part of it. The author states that if the husband attaches the divorce to the whole woman (jumlatiha) or to something that idiomatically expresses the whole (like 'your neck,' 'your soul,' 'your body,' etc.), the divorce occurs. This is because in Arabic usage, these terms are metonyms for the entire person. The same applies to an undivided share (juz' sha'i') such as 'half of you' or 'a third of you.' 

However, if the husband specifies a limb like hand, foot, or breast, divorce does not occur, because such limbs are not conventionally used to denote the whole person. The commentator explains that this rule is supported by analogy (qiyas) to sale (bay'): just as saying 'I sold you the neck of this slave girl' is a valid sale, the same phrase in divorce causes it to take effect.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh (commentary) of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands upon the rulings of divorce (talaq) when the wording is attached to the woman's entire body or to a part of it. The author states that if the husband attaches the divorce to the whole woman (jumlatiha) or to something that idiomatically expresses the whole (like 'your neck,' 'your soul,' 'your body,' etc.), the divorce occurs. This is because in Arabic usage, these terms are metonyms for the entire person. The same applies to an undivided share (juz' sha'i') such as 'half of you' or 'a third of you.' 

However, if the husband specifies a limb like hand, foot, or breast, divorce does not occur, because such limbs are not conventionally used to denote the whole person. The commentator explains that this rule is supported by analogy (qiyas) to sale (bay'): just as saying 'I sold you the neck of this slave girl' is a valid sale, the same phrase in divorce causes it to take effect. 

There is a notable intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the word 'blood' (dam). In one transmission (riwaya), saying 'your blood is divorced' does not cause divorce, but the sounder opinion (al-sahiha) is that it does, because 'blood' can idiomatically refer to the person (e.g., 'his blood went in vain'). 

Also, if the husband says 'the head from you is divorced' or points to a limb and says 'this limb is divorced,' divorce does not occur because he did not attach it to her directly. The rule for manumission ('itaq) is analogous to divorce.

A separate discussion involves the wife's request for divorce. If she repeats 'divorce me' three times with the conjunction 'wa' (and), the husband's response 'I have divorced you' effects three divorces regardless of his intention. Without the conjunction, the divorce depends on his intention. Similarly, if she says 'divorce me three times' and he says 'you are divorced,' it is one divorce unless he says 'I have divorced you,' which makes it three.

The passage also addresses ambiguous gestures: if the husband indicates with his fingers while saying 'you are divorced such,' the number of divorces is determined by the number of fingers he closes, not those released. And if he intends the 'closed two' (maddumma may refer to a specific gesture), he is not believed in court but can be truthful before Allah.

Divorce by Reference to a Specific Limb: Hanafi Position and Dispute
Dispute on whether divorcing a specific limb is effective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorcing a specific limb, explain the dispute with Zufr and al-Shafi'i, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Saying 'your hand is divorced' does not count as divorce because the limb stands for the whole, not a valid locus.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a subtle question in the law of talaq: does divorcing a specific part of the wife's body, like her hand, effect a divorce? The Hanafi school says no, but Zufr and al-Shafi'i disagree. Let's explore the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on common shares and specific limbs
Identify the two categories: common shares (e.g., half, third) where divorce occurs, and specific limbs (e.g., hand, foot) where it does not. Note the exception for 'blood' where the correct narration says divorce occurs.
The source begins by affirming that a common share works, then contrasts it with limbs.
2
Identify the opposing school's position
Zufr (a Hanafi dissenter) and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that divorce does occur for specific limbs and spreads to the whole person.
The commentary mentions 'وقال زفر والشافعي : يقع'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Their basis is that a limb is a part enjoyed in the marriage contract, making it a valid locus for divorce, analogous to an undivided share. They also cite the hadith 'The hand is responsible for what it takes' to argue that 'hand' means the whole person.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawood and Tirmidhi.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response argues that a specific limb is not a common share; it is a distinct part that idiomatically stands for the whole. Attaching divorce to it is like adding it to saliva or a nail—void. The hadith is interpreted as referring to the owner, not the limb.
The analogy to saliva/nail is crucial: those are not valid loci, so the divorce is لغو (void).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In a Hanafi jurisdiction, the husband incurs no divorce; in a Shafi'i jurisdiction, one divorce is counted. This affects marital status and subsequent remarriage.
Understanding this difference is essential for cross-madhhab fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute combined with ruling statements. It begins by establishing the general rule: if the husband issues a divorce by referring to an undivided share of the wife (e.g., half, third), the divorce occurs because it applies to the entire person. This is analogous to sales, where selling a common part of a slave-girl is valid.

Divorce by Referring to Body Parts and Fractions: The Hand, Limbs, and Fractional Divorces
Rulings on talaq when directed to limbs, fractions, and body parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on talaq when directed to a limb (e.g., hand) and explain the dispute with Zufar and al-Shafi‘i, including the qiyas with a common part.

Core Idea
A hand is not a valid subject for talaq — only the whole woman or a common part can be divorced.

This lesson explores a key point of difference between the Hanafi school and other scholars (Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl and Imam al-Shafi‘i) regarding divorce by referring to body parts. We will examine why the Hanafis hold that saying 'your hand is divorced' does not effect a divorce, and how they reconcile this with the concept of a common part (juz’ shayi‘). We will also cover the rulings on fractional divorce and other limbs.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on talaq by limb
Identify the ruling: if a husband says 'your hand is divorced' (or tongue, nose, etc.), the talaq does not occur according to Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad. The statement is void (batil) because the hand is not a valid subject for talaq.
Contrast with the opposing view of Zufar and al-Shafi‘i.
2
Identify the opposing view and its analogy
Zufar and al-Shafi‘i argue that the hand is a part enjoyed in marriage (juz’ mustamta‘ bih), so it is a valid locus for talaq, and the divorce extends to the whole woman, like a common part (juz’ shayi‘).
The common part (e.g., half the woman) is a valid subject for both nikah and talaq.
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafis respond that the hand is not a valid subject for nikah by consensus. Since talaq follows nikah in its subject, the hand cannot be a valid subject for talaq. The analogy to the common part fails because the common part can be a subject of nikah.
The Hanafis also interpret the hadith 'upon the hand is what it takes' as referring to the owner, not the limb.
4
Note the exception: intention of the owner
If the husband says 'your hand is divorced' and he intended his wife (the owner), then talaq occurs. The limb expression can figuratively refer to the whole if the intention is clear.
This is an exception, not the rule.
5
Apply to fractional talaq and other body parts
If a man says 'half a divorce' or 'a third of a divorce', it counts as one full divorce because talaq is indivisible. For other body parts like back, belly, hair, nail, etc., the ruling is that talaq does not occur (by consensus for hair/nail, and most apparent for back/belly).
The principle is that the subject of talaq must be something that has the restraint of marriage.

Another source, 'al-Yanabi', states a broader rule that the divorce of any drunkard is effective regardless of how he became drunk—voluntarily, under coercion, or in necessity—thus supporting Karakhi's view. The passage then lists a mas'ala (issue): ten things that remain valid even when done under coercion (ikrah). These include marriage, divorce, manumission, ruj'a (taking back a wife within iddah), ila' (vow of abstention from intercourse), returning from ila' (fi'), zihar (injurious comparison), oath (yamin), vow (nadhr), and pardon for qisas (retribution). The idea is that these actions are so serious that external pressure does not invalidate them, as they rely on the utterance rather than free will in these contexts.

Ten Acts Valid Despite Coercion
List of ten acts valid despite coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten acts that remain valid under coercion in Hanafi fiqh and explain the rationale behind their inclusion.

Core Idea
Ten specific acts – including marriage, divorce, and manumission – are valid even under coercion due to their reliance on utterance.

The second part of the passage enumerates ten legal acts that are considered valid even when performed under coercion. This list is a staple in Hanafi fiqh, illustrating a special exception to the general rule that coercion invalidates acts. Let's explore each act and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the ten acts
The acts are: marriage (nikah), divorce (talaq), manumission (itaq), revocation of divorce (ruj'a), ila' (vow of abstention), return from ila' (fi'), zihar, oath (yamin), vow (nadhr), and pardon for retaliation (afw an al-qisas).
These are all verbal acts that create or dissolve relationships or obligations.
2
Identify the common characteristic
All ten acts are purely verbal (qawl) or involve a statement that brings about a legal effect. Coercion does not negate their validity because the utterance itself is considered the operative element, not the free will behind it.
In contrast, physical acts like sale or gift are invalid under coercion because they require willing consent.
3
Understand the Hanafi principle
The Hanafi school holds that for these ten acts, the spoken word (sighah) is sufficient; the absence of free will due to coercion does not void them. This is a concession to maintain legal stability.
This principle is based on the idea that these acts are 'ihraz' (acquisition) or 'isqat' (relinquishment) and the utterance is the decisive factor.
4
Distinguish from the coerced drinking scenario
Note that this list does NOT include acts performed under the influence of intoxication; it covers direct coercion for the specific act. The earlier dispute about coerced drinking is a separate issue.
In the coerced drinking case, the divorce is pronounced after intoxication, not directly coerced as an act.
