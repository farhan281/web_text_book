Conditions for Valid Ila': Oath Form, Time Limit, and Istihsan
Valid oath forms for Ila' and rulings on time conditions with istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify valid oath forms for Ila', apply the ghayah condition to determine if the husband is a mul'i, and distinguish between the rulings of qiyas and istihsan regarding impossible conditions.

Core Idea
Ila' requires a valid oath; impossible conditions trigger Ila' via istihsan, overriding qiyas.

In this passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, the author explains the conditions for a valid Ila'. The first condition is the form of the oath: only oaths by Allah's names or attributes are valid. The second condition involves the time limit (ghayah): if the husband sets a condition unlikely to occur within four months, Ila' takes effect immediately. A key feature is the use of istihsan to override qiyas: even physically impossible conditions are ruled as Ila' because custom treats them as permanent abstinence. This lesson will walk you through these conditions and the Hanafi method of istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify valid oath forms
List the expressions that constitute a valid oath for Ila': any oath by Allah's names (e.g., 'By Allah') or His attributes (e.g., 'By His glory'). Exclude invalid forms like swearing by Allah's knowledge or invoking His anger.
The full list is in the first sentence of the passage.
2
Analyze the ghayah (condition) for timing
Examine the condition set for resuming intercourse. If it is not expected to occur within the four-month Ila' period, the husband is a mul'i immediately. If it is expected within four months, he is not a mul'i.
Examples: fasting in Muharram while in Rajab (>4 months) makes him a mul'i; weaning a child within 3 months does not.
3
Understand the qiyas ruling on impossible conditions
By strict analogy (qiyas), a condition that could theoretically occur at any moment (e.g., the sun rising from the west) would not make the husband a mul'i because the condition may be fulfilled soon.
This is the qiyas position, which is rejected by the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the istihsan override
The Hanafi school departs from qiyas and uses istihsan (juristic preference) based on custom ('urf): such expressions in common usage intend permanent abstinence, so the husband is a mul'i.
This is a classic example of istihsan overriding qiyas in Hanafi usul.
5
Examine special cases: conditions ending marriage
Conditions like death, killing, or irrevocable divorce also make the husband a mul'i by consensus, even though they could occur within four months.
The passage lists these separately; note the consensus (ijma') on these cases.

Istihsan over Qiyas for Impossible Conditions in Ila'
Ruling on ila' with impossible conditions: istihsan over qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ila' when an impossible condition is attached and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
Custom treats impossible conditions as perpetual; hence ila' applies despite qiyas.

This passage examines a specific case in the law of ila' (oath of abstinence). When a husband swears to avoid intercourse until an impossible event—like the sun rising from the west—does that constitute ila'? Strict analogy (qiyas) says no, because the event could theoretically happen at any moment. But the Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference), relying on custom: such expressions are understood as permanent. Let's unpack this ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is an oath (ila') in marriage. The condition attached is something impossible or extremely unlikely: sunrise from the west, appearance of the Dajjal, establishment of the Hour, or a camel passing through a needle's eye.
All these conditions are conventionally impossible, even if some may be theologically possible (e.g., the Hour will come, but not at a predictable moment).
2
State the qiyas ruling and its reasoning
By strict analogy (qiyas), such an oath is not ila' because the condition could theoretically occur at any moment, so the oath is not undeniably perpetual. The possibility of occurrence ('hoped hour by hour') prevents it from being a definitive indefinite abstention.
This is the apparent (jali) qiyas.
3
State the istihsan ruling and its basis
The Hanafi school adopts istihsan: the oath does constitute ila'. The basis is custom ('urf): in common usage, people employ such expressions to mean 'forever' or 'never again.' Since the condition is understood as a permanent bar, it triggers the rules of ila'.
This is the hidden (khafi) qiyas that gives preference to custom.
4
Understand the role of custom (urf)
Custom is a recognized source in Hanafi fiqh. Here, it overrides the literal possibility of occurrence. The legal effect depends not on physical possibility but on societal understanding.
This illustrates that usul al-fiqh considers 'urf as a legitimate source for deriving rulings.
5
Apply to similar cases
The same istihsan applies to all expressions that conventionally denote permanence, even if the literal condition is impossible. The text gives examples: 'until the camel goes through the eye of a needle' and 'until the Hour is established.'
The ruling is uniform: the oath is ila' regardless of the specific impossible condition.

Ila' with a slave wife only occurs when the husband vows to own her fully and take possession.

The text carefully distinguishes between promising to own, promising to buy, and promising to buy for oneself with possession.

The Hanafis argue that he is not considered a muli (one who makes ila') because the exception makes every day of the year potentially the excepted day, so the duration is uncertain and ila' does not take effect. They give an analogy: if someone says, 'I fasted one day this year', it could be any day. So the uncertainty prevents the fixed period necessary for ila'. On the other hand, if he says, 'except the deficiency of a day' (meaning he swears for a year except one deficient day at the end), then he becomes a muli, because the deficiency represents what remains at the end, so the period is effectively the full year minus a definite number of days at the end, and ila' is established.

Commentary

Core Idea
Zihar prohibits intercourse, touching, and kissing until expiation; even means to intercourse are forbidden.

Zihar is a pre-Islamic practice that Islam regulated. When a husband compares his wife to his mother, she becomes forbidden to him in certain ways. This lesson explains the immediate prohibition and the legal reasoning behind it, including the analogy to ihram and the distinction from menstruation and fasting.

Details of Zihar: temporary, wording, and validity conditions

Learning Objective

Temporary Zihar expires if the period passes without intimacy; explicit wording makes intention irrelevant; Zihar from a child or insane person is invalid.

Conditions and Legal Framework of Li'an for Spousal Accusation of Adultery
Explanation of conditions obligating li'an for a husband's accusation of adultery or denial of paternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for li'an, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature of li'an (shahadat vs ayman), and derive the ruling from the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Li'an replaces the hadd for qadhf when a husband accuses his wife — but only if both are competent witnesses and she is muhsan.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains the necessary conditions for li'an (mutual cursing) to be triggered when a husband accuses his wife of adultery or denies the paternity of her child. The author also clarifies an important intra-Hanafi difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding whether li'an is a set of testimonies (shahadat) or oaths (ayman), which has practical implications if the judge is replaced mid-procedure. The proof is drawn from Surah al-Nur, where Allah calls the husband's repeated statements 'shahadat' (testimonies).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
The act is li'an. The three conditions: (1) both spouses must be ahl al-shahada (competent witnesses), (2) the wife must be muhsan (so that her accuser would receive hadd), (3) the accusation must be explicit (e.g., 'You are an adulteress' or 'This child is not from me').
2
Understand the intra-Hanafi dispute
According to the text, Abu Yusuf considers li'an as testimonies (shahadat) strengthened by oaths; Muhammad considers it as oaths that contain the meaning of hadd. Note the practical difference: if the judge is replaced, Abu Yusuf requires restarting the li'an, while Muhammad allows building upon it.
3
Locate the dalil and derive the ruling
The Quranic proof from Surah al-Nur 24:6 is cited. The verse calls the husband a 'witness' and orders four testimonies by Allah. This establishes that the pillar of li'an is testimony, hence both must be competent witnesses. Also, because it replaces the hadd for qadhf, the wife must be muhsan.
4
Apply the conditions to specific expressions
The passage lists explicit phrases that trigger li'an (e.g., 'You are an adulteress') and distinguishes vague phrases that do not (e.g., 'You were subjected to unlawful intercourse').

The passage establishes the foundational framework: li'an substitutes for the hadd punishment in cases where direct proof of zina is lacking.

Conditions for Li'an and Exceptions
Lists conditions for li'an validity and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for li'an and identify the exceptions that drop the requirement.

Core Idea
Li'an is wajib only between free, adult, sane, Muslim spouses in a valid marriage.

Li'an is a special procedure that allows a husband to deny paternity or accuse his wife of zina without facing the hadd for qadhf. However, it is only available under strict conditions. This lesson enumerates those conditions and the specific exceptions where li'an is not applicable, based on the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for li'an
List the required attributes for both spouses: free, adult, sane, Muslim, and not previously punished for qadhf. Also, the marriage must be valid (sahih).
These conditions are cumulative; if any is missing, li'an is not valid.
2
Recognize exceptions from the husband's side
If the husband is a slave or lacks any condition, li'an is dropped and he must receive the hadd for qadhf.
The defect in the husband triggers punishment.
3
Recognize exceptions from the wife's side
If the wife is a minor, insane, kitabiyya, slave, etc., li'an is dropped and the husband does not face hadd.
The wife's disqualification removes both li'an and hadd.
4
Understand the effect of a fasid marriage
A marriage that is fasid (invalid) does not support li'an. The husband's accusation is treated like accusing a stranger.
Fasid marriage is valid for some purposes but not for li'an.
5
Note the non-expiry of the wife's right to demand
The wife can demand li'an at any time, even after a long delay, because the right to demand hadd of qadhf does not lapse.
Silence does not forfeit her right.

Procedure of Li'an and Its Conditions
Step-by-step description of li'an procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the step-by-step procedure of li'an and identify the required wording of shahada.

Core Idea
Li'an requires precise wording of testimony (shahada) from both spouses; using an oath invalidates the entire process.

This lesson walks through the exact sequence of li'an as prescribed by the Hanafi school. The text outlines the specific statements and actions required for the procedure to be valid. Pay careful attention to the distinction between shahada (testimony) and yamin (oath) — this is a key Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actor and sequence
The qadi begins with the husband, then the wife performs her part. The order is fixed: husband first, then wife.
The qadi initiates the process, but the statements are made by the spouses.
2
Extract the required wording
Husband: four times 'Ashhadu billahi inni la-min al-sadiqin...' (I testify by Allah that I am truthful) and fifth 'La'natu Allahi alayhi in kana min al-kadhibin...' (Curse of Allah upon him if he is lying). Wife: four times 'Ashhadu billahi innahu la-min al-kadhibin...' (I testify by Allah that he is lying) and fifth 'Ghadaba Allahi alayha in kana min al-sadiqin...' (Wrath of Allah upon her if he is truthful).
The wording 'shahada' (testimony) is mandatory; any other phrase like an oath invalidates.
3
Note the actions
The husband must point to his wife during his statements to remove ambiguity. Both should be standing, though standing is not a condition.
Al-Karkhi holds that standing is sunnah, not a condition.
4
Recognize the Hanafi condition
Li'an depends on the wording of testimony (shahada). If the husband says 'I swear by Allah' instead of 'I testify by Allah', the li'an is invalid.
This is a specific Hanafi requirement; other schools may accept an oath.
5
Understand the consequence
After both spouses complete their statements, the qadi separates them permanently. The separation is final and irrevocable.
Li'an results in permanent separation, even if later proven false.
