Talaq bi Mal: Divorce in Exchange for Payment
Matn stating three legal effects of talaq bi mal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three consequences of a valid talaq bi mal and identify when the divorce becomes ba'in (irrevocable).

Core Idea
Divorce for property is valid, the payment is due, and the separation is irrevocable (ba'in).

A husband may pronounce divorce contingent upon the wife paying him a certain sum. This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling on this transaction, known as talaq bi mal. The passage from al-Quduri clearly spells out the three immediate effects: the divorce takes effect, the wife owes the payment, and the divorce is irrevocable (ba'in). Understanding this helps distinguish it from a revocable (raj'i) divorce and from khul' (divorce initiated by the wife).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
The husband pronounces divorce 'on property' (i.e., in exchange for payment). The wife must accept the condition. Without her acceptance, the divorce might not take effect or the payment might not be due. Identify that acceptance is a condition.
The text says 'faqabilat' (and she accepted) – acceptance is essential.
2
Determine the legal effects
When the condition is fulfilled (husband's pronouncement + wife's acceptance), three effects occur simultaneously: (1) the divorce occurs (waqa'a al-talaq), (2) the payment becomes obligatory on the wife (lazimaha al-mal), and (3) the divorce is ba'in (irrevocable). List each effect.
The order in the matn is not chronological; all three are immediate.
3
Understand the ba'in nature
A ba'in divorce means the husband cannot take the wife back without a new marriage contract and new mahr. This is different from a raj'i (revocable) divorce where he can take her back within the waiting period without a new contract. Note the consequence: the marriage ends completely.
The payment is not like mahr; it is a separate obligation.
4
Distinguish from other types
Compare talaq bi mal with khul' (divorce initiated by wife for compensation) and regular talaq. In khul', the wife pays and divorce is ba'in; in talaq bi mal, the husband initiates the divorce but conditions it on payment. The result is similar, but the process differs.
Both result in ba'in divorce, but the initiating party and the manner of payment may differ.

Invalid Consideration in Khul' and Talaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the consideration is invalid in khul' and in talaq, and explain why the divorce is ba'in or raj'i.

Core Idea
Khul' with invalid consideration yields ba'in; talaq with invalid yields raj'i — the contract type determines the outcome.

In this lesson, we examine a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: what happens when the consideration (iwad) in a divorce transaction is invalid — such as wine, pork, or an unenforceable debt. The source text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that the divorce itself remains valid, but the type of divorce (ba'in vs raj'i) differs based on whether the transaction is khul' or talaq. We will also explore the intra-Hanafi disagreement on certain phrasings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main cases
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) invalid consideration in khul', (2) invalid consideration in talaq. Note the consequence for each.
Invalid consideration includes wine, pork, carrion, or anything not owned or not lawful.
2
Analyse the ruling for khul'
In khul' with invalid consideration, the husband gets nothing and the divorce is ba'in (irrevocable). Understand the reasoning: the wife did not specify lawful property, so no payment is owed; and khul' is a kinayah form of divorce, which always results in ba'in.
Kinayah: figurative expressions that require intention.
3
Analyse the ruling for talaq
In talaq with invalid consideration, the divorce is raj'i (revocable). The condition for payment fails, but the divorce itself remains valid and revocable, as long as the number of divorces is not exhausted.
4
Contrast with analogous cases
The text compares to: (a) if the wife specified vinegar but it turned out to be wine — husband gets mahr because he was deceived; (b) in kitaba or 'itq for wine, the value of the slave is due because the master's property right is valuable.
This highlights that the property of the vulva (bud') is not valuable in the context of khul' exit.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
When the husband says 'Ant taliq wa 'alayki alf', Abu Hanifa rules she owes nothing; the Sahibayn disagree. Understand that Abu Hanifa ties the acceptance to the session, and the wording does not make divorce contingent on payment.
The dispute affects whether the wife must pay or not.

Invalid Compensation in Khul': Ruling and Qiyas
Ruling with proof: invalid compensation in khul' leads to ba'in separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when compensation in khul' is invalid, and explain the reasoning using qiyas with kitaba/manumission.

Core Idea
An invalid compensation does not void the divorce; the husband gets nothing because his right over the wife's vulva is not valuable wealth.

Khul' is a divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for compensation. What happens if that compensation is not valid Islamic wealth, like wine or pork? This passage from the Hanafi commentary clarifies that the divorce still takes effect irrevocably (ba'in), but the husband receives no payment. The commentator draws a clever qiyas to show why the husband cannot claim compensation, contrasting with cases where a valid item was intended or where ownership is valuable.

Guided Path
1
State the core ruling
Identify the ruling: when a Muslim wife makes khul' for wine, pork, or carrion, the husband gets nothing and the separation is ba'in.
Note that the khul' itself is valid; only the compensation fails.
2
Understand why no compensation is due
The wife did not name valid wealth (mal), and there is no basis to obligate the named item (it's haram) or any alternative (no commitment to pay).
In Hanafi, wine and pork are not mal mutaqawwim.
3
Contrast with the vinegar case
If she named vinegar (a valid mal) but it turned out to be wine, he is deceived and gets the dower (mahr). This shows the key is whether a valid mal was intended.
The difference lies in the wife's specification of a valid item.
4
Contrast with kitaba/manumission on wine
If a master frees a slave or makes kitaba in exchange for wine, he is owed the slave's value because his ownership is valuable (mutaqawwim) and he did not consent to lose it for free.
This highlights the illa: the value of what is being given up.

Identify the illa and hukm
The husband's ownership of the wife's vulva (bud') during exit (the moment of khul') is not valuable, so no compensation is due. The separation is ba'in because khul' uses kinayah, which are ba'in in Hanafi.

The Principle of Equivalence: Valid Dower as Khul' Compensation
Definition ruling with talil: equivalence of dower and khul' compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that valid dower is valid khul' compensation, apply it to the case of an unspecified animal, and distinguish the exception for invalid items.

Core Idea
What is valid as a dower is valid as compensation in khul' — but invalid items require no payment.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle: the same rules that govern the validity of dower (mahr) in marriage apply to the compensation (badal) in khul'. However, a crucial exception arises when the wife names an intrinsically invalid item like wine or pork. Understanding this principle clarifies how khul' contracts operate and how they differ from the marriage contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its scope
The author states: 'Whatever is valid as a dower is valid as compensation in khul'.' This means any item or benefit that can lawfully serve as mahr can also be used as the wife's payment to obtain khul'.
The principle is based on the similarity of both contracts being 'on the vulva' (bud').
2
Examine the application: khul' on an unspecified animal
The text gives the example of khul' on an animal without specification. The husband receives a middle-quality animal, and the wife has the choice between giving that specific animal or its value.
Unspecified items are valid because they can be determined later.
3
Understand the talil (rationale)
The rationale is that khul' is a contract on the vulva, just like nikah. Therefore, what is valid as consideration in nikah is also valid in khul'. Both involve the vulva as the object of the contract.
This connects to the broader Hanafi theory of 'iwad (exchange).
4
Distinguish the exception for invalid items
If the wife specifies wine, pork, or something of no value, the khul' is still valid, but the husband receives nothing. In contrast, in nikah, if such invalid dower is named, the husband must pay mahr al-mithl (proper dower).
The difference arises because in nikah the husband is acquiring a right to the vulva, so he must give valuable compensation; in khul' he is relinquishing his right, so no compensation is due if the named item is worthless.
5
Conclude the practical ruling
The operative rule (mufta bihi) is that khul' compensation follows the same validity conditions as dower, with the exception that naming invalid items results in a valid khul' without compensation.
This is a zahir al-riwayah position with consensus across schools in principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that valid dower is valid consideration in khul', and distinguish when the husband receives compensation based on the value of the item.

Core Idea
In khul', any property that can be dower can be consideration; but if it has no value, the husband gets nothing.

This lesson details a key Hanafi principle from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: the consideration (badal) in a wife-initiated divorce (khul') is governed by the same rules as dower (mahr) in marriage. The commentary provides the rationale and an important exception: if the wife offers something without value, the divorce is valid but the husband receives nothing. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying khul' in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The matn states: 'Whatever is permissible as a dower is permissible as consideration in khul’.' This is the foundational principle. First, ensure you understand what constitutes a valid dower in Hanafi fiqh (e.g., any valuable property).
The rule is a one-to-one mapping; no new restrictions are added.
2

The commentator justifies the rule by qiyas: khul' is a contract on the buda' (marital rights) just like marriage. Therefore, the rules for what can be given as dower apply analogically to khul' consideration. Identify the asl (marriage), far‘ (khul‘), and illa (both involve buda‘).
This analogy is considered jali (clear) because the subject matter is identical.

Despite the analogy, there is a critical exception: if the wife offers something without value (e.g., wine, pig), the khul' is still valid, but the husband receives nothing. This is contrary to marriage, where non-valuable items would trigger mahr al-mithl.
This shows that the analogy is limited by the nature of ownership in khul' vs. marriage.

The commentator explains the distinction: the exit of buda' from the husband's ownership is not valuable, while its entry into his ownership (in marriage) is valuable. Thus, in khul', the husband loses rights that he did not pay for initially, so no compensation is due if the specified consideration is valueless.
This illa restricts the analogy: it only applies when the consideration is valuable.
5

Practice applying the rule: e.g., a car is valuable → valid consideration; a house → valid; wine → valid khul' but no payment; a Quran (valuable) → valid. Ensure you can explain each outcome using the principles above.
Always check whether the item would be valid as dower. If yes, the husband receives it. If not, the khul' is valid but he receives nothing.

When a wife initiates khul' by offering 'what is in my hand,' the compensation depends on whether she adds specifying terms like 'wealth' or 'dirhams.' The ruling is that the husband receives nothing, and the divorce is revocable because it is explicit divorce without compensation. The commentator explains that she did not deceive him since she did not name specific wealth. The second case: she says 'for what is in my hand of wealth' and has nothing; she must pay the husband her dower (mahr) because she named a general valuable thing (mal). Since the thing is unknown, imposing the named thing or its value is impossible due to uncertainty, and imposing the dower of the like is not appropriate because the vulva's exit is not valuable. Therefore, the court returns to what the husband originally gave (her dower). If she had already discharged him from the dower, he cannot claim anything further. The third case: she says 'for what is in my hand of dirhams' and has nothing; she must pay three dirhams because she named the plural (dirhams), and the minimum of the plural is three. If she actually had dirhams totaling three or more, they go to the husband. The passage also notes that the same ruling applies if she says 'what is in my house' with nothing in the house: khul' is valid, and he gets nothing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on khul' offers using 'bā' and '‘alā', and apply the rule to determine payment and raj'ah status.

Core Idea
The word ‘alā indicates a condition, not compensation, so partial performance does not trigger payment – a linguistic-legal distinction with real consequences.

In khul', the wife offers compensation in exchange for divorce. A subtle difference in Arabic prepositions – 'bā' vs '‘alā' – leads to a sharp intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students. This lesson unpacks the dispute, the linguistic evidence, and the practical outcome for the couple.

Guided Path

Identify the agreed scenario with 'bā'

He cites Quran 60:12 where '‘alā' introduces a condition (pledge not to associate). He argues that '‘alā' in khul' likewise imposes a condition that all three divorces occur before any payment is due.
Dalīl is dari (transmitted) from the Quran.
4

They claim that '‘alā' and 'bā' are interchangeable in compensation contexts, e.g., 'carry this for a dirham' uses either preposition. Thus, partial divorce triggers proportional payment.
Their proof is linguistic usage, not textual.
5

Under Abu Hanifa, one divorce is revocable and no money changes hands. Under Sahibayn, one divorce is irrevocable and the wife owes one-third. The husband's right to return is lost in the latter view.
This directly affects the couple's post-divorce options.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A condition is indivisible; no divorce or liability unless the full consideration is paid.

In conditional divorce with payment, the husband consents to separation only upon full receipt of the consideration. This lesson examines a common scenario: the husband delegates the right to divorce three times in exchange for a fixed sum, but the wife divorces only once. State the Ruling
The ruling is that no divorce occurs and the wife owes nothing. This is because the condition (full payment) was not fulfilled.

The author draws an analogy to a conditional divorce: if a husband says 'If you enter the house three times, you are divorced three times,' and she enters once, no divorce occurs. The illa (effective cause) is that a condition is indivisible—it must be completely satisfied.

The same logic applies even if the wife intended to pay later—immediate fulfillment is required.
5

The wife remains married, and she is not liable for any partial payment. The husband cannot demand a proportionate amount.
This protects the wife from unintended divorce or debt.

Freeing Half a Co-Owned Slave for Kaffarah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of freeing a co-owned slave for kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on freeing a co-owned slave for kaffarah and explain the basis of the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Freeing half a co-owned slave for kaffarah is invalid per Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn permit it if wealthy.

Expiations (kaffarah) in Islamic law often require freeing a slave. But what if you only own half a slave? The Hanafi masters disagree: Abu Hanifa holds that partial ownership can never be completed retroactively, while the two students (Sahibayn) consider a guarantee sufficient for the wealthy. Let's unpack the ruling and the reasoning behind each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Question
Determine the legal issue: can a person who owns only half of a slave fulfill the kaffarah by freeing the whole slave and promising to pay the co-owner the remaining half's value?
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
According to Abu Hanifa, the expiation is not valid under any circumstances. Why? Because complete ownership of the freed slave is required at the moment of freeing, and a mere promise to pay the co-owner does not transfer ownership retroactively.
The text says: 'lam yajuz' (it is not valid).
3
State the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad distinguish based on the person's wealth: if he is wealthy (mūsar), the act is valid because the guarantee effectively completes ownership; if insolvent (muʿsir), it is invalid because the debt remains unpaid and ownership is not truly complete.
Wealth here means ability to pay the guarantee immediately.
4
Compare the Underlying Rationale
Abu Hanifa focuses on the strict requirement of full ownership at the time of the act; Sahibayn consider that a wealthy person's guarantee effectively makes the slave fully his from the start, while an insolvent's guarantee is mere debt.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference is whether the expiation is accepted.
5
Note the Practice Impact
In the Hanafi school, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is often the view of Abu Hanifa in such matters of kaffarah, but the Sahibayn's view may be considered when needed. Always check the specific context of fatwa.
The text does not give a tarjih, so students should be aware of both.

Freeing a Slave in Two Installments for Kaffarah
Ruling that freeing half then remaining is valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing a slave in two installments for kaffarah and explain the reasoning based on ownership.

Core Idea
Freeing a wholly-owned slave in two installments for kaffarah is valid because the deficiency remains attached to one's own property.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a person frees half of his slave for kaffarah and later frees the remaining half. Some might assume this invalidates the expiation because the freeing was not a single act. The Hanafi school, however, validates it based on a clear legal reasoning rooted in ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage clearly states that freeing half then the remainder for kaffarah is valid (jāz). Note that this is a permissive ruling; the expiation is fulfilled.
2
Understand the Condition
The validity hinges on the slave being wholly owned by the person making the kaffarah. The passage contrasts this with a co-ownership case mentioned earlier.
Ownership here means exclusive ownership (milk khāliṣ).
3
Grasp the Reasoning (Ta‘līl)
The commentator explains: because the person freed the slave with two utterances, and the ‘deficiency’ (al-nuqṣān) that results from freeing half remains within his own property (mumakkin ‘alā milkihi). This does not prevent validity.
The deficiency refers to the partial ownership remaining after the first freeing.
4
Distinguish from Co-Ownership
The passage notes a contrary case: when a slave is co-owned, freeing a half would create a deficiency on the partner’s property, which is not permissible. This distinction confirms the condition of sole ownership.
The earlier case (mā taqaddam) likely involved a co-owned slave.
5
Apply the Principle
For any kaffarah requiring emancipation (e.g., ẓihār, murder, broken oath), the full emancipation can be completed over multiple utterances as long as the slave is solely owned and the intention for kaffarah is present each time.
The cumulative effect suffices; a single continuous act is not required.
