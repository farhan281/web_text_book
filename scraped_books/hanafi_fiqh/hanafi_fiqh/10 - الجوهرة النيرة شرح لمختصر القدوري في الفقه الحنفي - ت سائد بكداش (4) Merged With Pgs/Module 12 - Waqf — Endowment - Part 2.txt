Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Status of an Abandoned Mosque
Dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the status of an abandoned mosque.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the status of an abandoned mosque and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
A mosque remains a mosque forever even if abandoned – its purpose is not limited to local residents.

When a mosque is built as a waqf, it is dedicated to Allah forever. But what happens if the area around it becomes deserted and no one prays there? Does it cease to be a mosque? Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani disagreed on this exact question, and their debate reveals a deeper principle about the nature of waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger of the dispute
The scenario: the mosque's surrounding area falls into ruin, and no inhabitants remain near it. This raises the question: does the mosque's waqf status continue?
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf: the mosque remains a mosque until the Day of Resurrection. His evidence (dalil) is the possibility that travelers and wayfarers may still pray there, so the purpose of prayer has not entirely ceased.
His argument emphasizes the perpetual nature of mosque waqf.
3
State Muhammad's position and dalil
Muhammad al-Shaybani: the ownership of the builder reverts to his heirs. His evidence: the mosque was dedicated for a specific type of worship (prayer by local residents), and that purpose has ceased entirely.
He views the waqf as conditional on the continuity of local prayer.
4
Analyze the key difference in reasoning
The dispute hinges on how broadly one defines the 'purpose' of the mosque. Abu Yusuf sees it as any prayer (including travelers); Muhammad limits it to the original intended worshippers.
This reflects different understandings of the intention (niyya) of the founder.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its basis
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Yusuf, because mosques are established as perpetual waqf and the possibility of use by travelers preserves the purpose. This view protects the waqf from dissolution.
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah) confirm this tarjih.

Ruling on Private Endowments of Public Facilities Without Judge's Decree
States that these endowments do not transfer ownership without a judge's ruling according to Abu Hanifa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when ownership of a built public facility ceases and identify the key condition (judge's decree) that transfers it.

Core Idea
Building a public facility does not automatically make it waqf; ownership persists until a judge decrees it.

In this passage, we encounter a specific ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa regarding charitable endowments of public utilities. While building a water station or inn for travelers is a noble act, the legal consequence is that the builder's ownership does not immediately transfer to the public. This highlights a key principle in Hanafi waqf law: the need for a formal judicial decree to establish an irrevocable waqf. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the distinction between intention and legal effect in charitable dedications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The passage lists four acts: building a saqaya, building a khan, building a ribat, and making land a cemetery. Identify each act and the immediate legal consequence stated.
The consequence is that ownership does not cease (لم يَزُلْ ملكه).
2
Locate the key condition
Abu Hanifa attaches a condition for ownership to cease: 'until a judge rules on it' (حتى يَحكُمَ بـ حاكم). Identify this condition and note its role.
The judge's decree is a necessary condition for the waqf to become effective.
3
Understand the nature of these facilities
These facilities (saqaya, khan, ribat, cemetery) are intended for public or religious benefit. Recognize that they are considered potential waqf properties, but their status is pending judicial validation.
Without a decree, the builder retains ownership and can revoke the dedication.
4
Contrast with other schools (optional)
Note that other schools may not require a judge's decree for a valid waqf. This distinction illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on formality and judicial oversight.
The text only presents Abu Hanifa's view; the ikhtilaf is implied.
5
Apply the ruling to a modern scenario
Consider a person who builds a public park on their land. According to this ruling, until a judge declares it a waqf, the builder can still sell or use the land as they wish.
This demonstrates the practical significance of the judge's decree.

Disposal of Surplus Mosque Items and Debris
Rulings on transferring surplus mosque items and restricting debris use.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on transferring surplus mosque items and explain why debris cannot be used for a different type of endowment.

Core Idea
Mosque surplus must be used for like-purpose; debris cannot cross endowment types.

Mosque endowments are dedicated to specific purposes. When items become surplus or a structure is demolished, their use is restricted. This lesson examines the Hanafi rule on transferring surplus mats, wood, and grass, and the prohibition on using mosque debris for a well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the surplus items
The text mentions mats (حصُر), wood (خشب), and grass (حشيش) used for the mosque floor. These are no longer needed.
Surplus means they are still usable but not required at this mosque.
2
State Abu Yusuf's ruling
According to Abu Yusuf, these items should be transferred to another mosque. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
Transfer preserves the endowment's benefit for a similar purpose.
3
Note the alternative opinion
Some (other Hanafi scholars) said they should be sold and the proceeds spent on the mosque's needs.
The mufta bihi is transfer, as it keeps the endowment intact.
4
Apply the rule on debris (نقض)
When a mosque is demolished, its building materials (debris) cannot be used to build a well because a well is not of the same genre (جنس) as a mosque. Similarly, well debris cannot be used for a mosque.
Endowment property must be replaced with property of the same type and function.
5
Explain the rationale
The principle is that an endowment (waqf) is restricted to its specific purpose. Using mosque debris for a well would change the endowment's character.
This preserves the donor's intent.

Rules governing the use of endowment oil for mosque lamps.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the permissible extent of using endowed lamp oil for prayer and for studying, based on the intent of the endowment.

Core Idea
Endowed lamp oil is restricted to prayer needs; studying is allowed only incidentally.

Endowments for mosque lighting are dedicated to enable prayer. This lesson clarifies that the oil should not be burnt the entire night unnecessarily; it is limited to the time needed for worship. Additionally, using the lamp for studying is only permissible if the lamp was placed for prayer and during the time of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the endowed item
Oil endowed specifically for the mosque lamp (دهن السراج).
This is a waqf of consumable property.
2
State the general rule
The oil is not to be used for the entire night but only according to the need of worshippers.
Extravagance is avoided; the endowment is for utility, not decoration.
3
Determine the permissible duration
Up to one-third or half of the night if needed for prayer (e.g., Isha and night prayers).
The exact duration depends on the necessity of the worshippers.
4
Apply the rule for studying
If the lamp was set up for prayer, then studying a book using its light is permissible until the people finish prayer.
After prayer, the oil should be extinguished or kept only for its original purpose.

When Does Ownership Cease in Waqf of Cisterns, Hostels, and Cemeteries?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership cessation in public utility waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding the removal of ownership in waqf of public utilities, identify the dalil for each, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf in practice.

Core Idea
Ownership in waqf of public facilities does not automatically cease; it requires either a judge's ruling, mere declaration, or actual public use depending on the Hanafi imam.

This passage from Sharh Mukhtasar al-Quduri presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute about when an owner's ownership ends when he dedicates a cistern, hostel, or cemetery for public use. While all agree such waqf is valid, they differ on the precise moment ownership transfers. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi internal reasoning on the role of delivery (taslim) and the waqif's retained rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the three positions
Read the initial ruling: whoever builds a cistern, khan, ribat, or makes land a cemetery does not lose ownership according to Abu Hanifa until a judge rules. Note the two other positions: Abu Yusuf says ownership ceases by mere statement; Muhammad says it ceases when people actually use the facility.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that the waqif retains a right of benefit (he can still use the facility), so his ownership continues until a judge rules (hukm al-hakim) or he attaches the waqf to death (idafa ila ma ba'd al-mawt). This is contrasted with a mosque where the waqif loses all benefit.
The analogy to mosque shows that where the waqif retains any benefit, ownership does not cease automatically.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's dalil
Abu Yusuf holds that declaration alone suffices because delivery is not a necessary condition in his usul. He analogizes this to the freeing of a slave (itq), where mere statement effects emancipation.
This position is based on his principle that taslim (delivery) is not a required condition for the validity of the waqf.
4
Examine Muhammad's dalil
Muhammad requires actual delivery (taslim) to the beneficiaries. For him, ownership ceases only when people drink from the cistern, dwell in the hostel, or bury in the cemetery. One instance of use suffices because gathering all possible users is impractical.
This aligns with his general view that delivery is a condition for waqf effectiveness.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a person who builds a public drinking fountain and declares it waqf. Under Abu Hanifa, he can revoke until a judge confirms or he dies. Under Abu Yusuf, it is immediately binding. Under Muhammad, it becomes binding after someone drinks. Identify which view is most practical and the mufta bihi.
The later Hanafi school often adopts Abu Yusuf's view for ease of practice.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Mukhtasar al-Quduri covers the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the removal of ownership when endowing certain public utilities: cisterns (suqaya), hostels (khan), lodging houses (ribat), and cemeteries (maqbara). The core issue is whether mere declaration suffices to make the waqf effective or whether additional conditions are needed. According to Abu Hanifa, ownership does not cease unless a judge rules on it or the waqf is attached to death (as a will). His reasoning is that the waqif retains a right of benefit in these structures—he can drink from the cistern, stay in the hostel, etc.—unlike a mosque where the waqif has no residual benefit. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership ceases by mere statement (al-qawl), because in his view delivery is not a necessary condition; the waqf is analogous to freeing a slave (itq) where the declaration alone is sufficient. Muhammad al-Shaybani requires actual possession by the beneficiaries: people drinking from the cistern, dwelling in the hostel, burying in the cemetery. For him, delivery is a condition, and it is fulfilled by such usage. The text also discusses subsidiary rulings: surplus mosque materials (mats, wood, grass) are transferred to another mosque according to Abu Yusuf; some say they should be sold and the proceeds used for the mosque. Debris from a mosque cannot be used for a well because they are not of the same type. Oil for mosque lamps should be used only as needed for prayer, not all night. Using the lamp to study is permissible only if it was primarily intended for prayer and only until the prayer finishes. Footnote identifies 'hashish' as grass used as matting in Upper Egypt, from Ibn 'Abidin.

مقبرة (maqbara): A cemetery; making land a cemetery for public burial.

Ruling on Transfer of Waqf Ownership: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Presents intra-Hanafi dispute on when waqf ownership transfers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on when waqf ownership transfers, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) of each view.

Core Idea
In Hanafi waqf, ownership transfer requires either judge's ruling (Abu Hanifa), mere statement (Abu Yusuf), or actual use (Muhammad).

The Hanafi school's three founding imams agree that waqf is a valid and commendable act, but they differ on the precise moment ownership of the endowed property transfers from the donor to Allah, making the endowment irrevocable. This lesson unpacks their proofs and the practical consequences of each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common issue
All three imams agree that waqf is valid; the dispute is about when the property becomes permanently owned by Allah and the donor loses the right to revoke.
The ruling of validity (sahih) is unanimous within the school.
2
Outline Abu Hanifa's view and proof
Abu Hanifa holds that ownership transfers only by a judge's ruling or by adding the waqf to one's will (deathbed endowment). His proof: the donor retains the right to benefit from the property himself, indicating ownership has not fully left.
Mosque waqf is an exception because the donor cannot use it after dedication.
3
Outline Abu Yusuf's view and proof
Abu Yusuf holds that utterance alone suffices to transfer ownership, analogizing to freeing a slave where the statement immediately effects emancipation.
Delivery (qabd) is not a necessary condition in his view.
4
Outline Muhammad's view and proof
Muhammad al-Shaybani requires actual delivery (qabd) through use by the beneficiaries. He argues that delivery is a condition, and since universal use is impossible, one person's use suffices.
If no beneficiary has used the property, the donor may still revoke.
5
Determine the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether a donor can revoke a waqf declared verbally before any judge's ruling or actual use. Under Abu Hanifa, revocation is possible until a judge rules; under Abu Yusuf, it is immediate; under Muhammad, it becomes binding only after use.
This affects cases like endowing a well no one has yet drunk from.

Who May Use a Public Waqf? Ibaha vs Tamlik
Explains permissibility of use of endowed property by rich and poor, and liability for lost items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish ibaha from tamlik in waqf and apply the liability rules for items placed on endowed property.

Core Idea
Public waqf use is ibaha for all, but damage without transgression is not compensable.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi waqf law: endowments like cemeteries, mosques, and watering places grant mere permission to use (ibaha), not ownership (tamlik). This means rich and poor alike may benefit. The passage also addresses liability when items (e.g., jugs) are lost—no compensation unless the caretaker misuses them. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between sadaqah and public waqf.

Apply the rule of equal access
