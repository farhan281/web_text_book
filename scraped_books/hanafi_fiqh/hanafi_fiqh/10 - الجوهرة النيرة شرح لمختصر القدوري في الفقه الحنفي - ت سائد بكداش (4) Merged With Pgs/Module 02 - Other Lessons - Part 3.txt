Hibah bi Shart al-Iwad: The Hybrid Contract and Zufar's Objection
Explains ruling of hibah with condition of compensation and Zufar's disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hibah bi shart al-iwad and explain why Zufar disagrees with the majority position.

Core Idea
A gift with compensation is a gift initially, a sale ultimately—Zufar calls it a pure sale throughout.

This lesson examines the unique Hanafi treatment of hibah bi shart al-iwad, a contract that blends elements of gift and sale. The majority of Hanafi imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) view it as a gift in the beginning and a sale upon completion, while Zufar considers it a pure sale from start to finish. Understanding this distinction has practical consequences for possession, defects, and preemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type and its dual nature
Determine that hibah bi shart al-iwad is neither a pure gift nor a pure sale; it is a hybrid. The wording is that of a gift, but the meaning includes a sale.
2
State the beginning phase: gift rules apply
Without possession of the gift, the contract is not binding and the gift of an undivided share (musha') is invalid. This is the 'gift in the beginning' aspect.
Musha' invalid for gifts; not for sales.
3
State the completion phase: sale rules apply
Once both parties exchange possession (gift and compensation), the contract becomes like a sale, carrying all sale consequences: return for defect, option of sight, preemption.
4
Contrast Zufar's position: pure sale from the start
Unlike the majority, Zufar treats it as a pure sale from inception, so no initial gift restrictions apply.
Zufar's view is noted but not relied upon.
5
Apply the distinction to practical cases
Work through the examples: invalidity of musha' in the beginning, and the father's inability to compensate from the minor's property even if the gift was conditioned.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi ruling on hibah bi shart al-iwad (gift with condition of compensation). The author clarifies that such a transaction is treated as a gift at its inception and a sale upon completion. Therefore, at the beginning, it is not valid if the gifted item is undivided (musha') and ownership does not transfer without possession, unlike a pure sale. However, once both parties exchange the gift and the compensation, the transaction takes the full effect of a sale: each party has the right to return due to a defect (radd bi al-‘ayb), the option of sight (khiyar al-ru’yah), and the right of preemption (shuf‘ah) applies. The passage also includes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Zufar considers it a pure sale from start to finish, thus disagreeing on the initial phase. The text further notes a father cannot compensate for a gift given to his minor child using the child’s property, even if the gift was conditioned on compensation, as this would amount to a donation from the minor’s wealth, which is impermissible. Finally, a citation from al-Nihaya states that the dispute (between viewing it as a sale or a hybrid) only applies when the compensation is mentioned with the word ‘ala; if the compensation is mentioned with the letter ba (e.g., “I gifted you this for that”), it is unanimously a sale from beginning to end.

Key Terms
الهبة بشرط العوض (al-hibah bi shart al-iwad): A gift given with the condition that the recipient compensates the giver with something in return.
المشاع (al-musha'): An undivided share in a jointly owned property.

'Umra and Ruqba: Valid Gift vs. Void Condition
Defines 'umra and ruqba with rulings and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'umra and ruqba and state the Hanafi ruling on each, including the intra-Hanafi disagreement on ruqba.

Core Idea
'Umra is a valid gift with a void condition; ruqba is void due to uncertainty (gharar), but Abu Yusuf differs.

Gifts can be complicated when conditions are attached. This lesson discusses two special gift forms: 'umra (a gift for the recipient's lifetime) and ruqba (a conditional gift depending on who dies first). The Hanafi school has a clear ruling on 'umra but an internal disagreement on ruqba. Understanding this distinction is crucial for property transfers and inheritance planning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the wording of 'umra
Identify the phrases that create a life interest: 'I give you this house for your lifetime' or similar. Note the condition that upon death it returns to the donor.
The gift wording makes ownership effective immediately; the condition is a separate invalid clause.
2
Apply the rule to 'umra
Apply the Hanafi principle: the gift is valid, the condition is void. The recipient becomes absolute owner, and the property passes to his heirs.
Compare with regular gifts: invalid conditions do not invalidate the gift itself.
3
Understand the wording of ruqba
Identify the conditional transfer: 'if I die before you, it is yours; if you die before me, it returns to me.' This creates uncertainty (gharar) about ownership.
The outcome depends on an unknown future event, making the transaction contingent.
4
Apply the majority Hanafi rule to ruqba
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule ruqba void; if the property is handed over, it is a loan ('ariya) revocable at will.
The gift is not perfected due to the conditional nature.
5
Note Abu Yusuf's dissent
Abu Yusuf treats ruqba like 'umra: the gift is valid, the condition is void, and the recipient owns the property absolutely.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under the majority, the donor can reclaim; under Abu Yusuf, the recipient keeps it.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two special types of gift: ‘umra and ruqba. ‘Umra is a gift of property for the recipient's lifetime, with a condition that upon the recipient's death the property returns to the giver. The Hanafi ruling makes the gift valid and the condition void; the property becomes the absolute possession of the recipient and passes to his heirs after his death. This is because gifts are not invalidated by invalid conditions. The text provides examples of wording from al-Yanabi‘. Ruqba is a conditional gift where ownership depends on who dies first: if the donor dies first, the recipient gets it; if the recipient dies first, it returns to the donor. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider ruqba void; the property remains with the donor as a loan (‘ariya), revocable at any time. Abu Yusuf, however, treats it like ‘umra: the gift is valid and the condition is void, making it an absolute transfer. The difference stems from how they view the condition: the majority sees the uncertainty (gharar) as fatal, while Abu Yusuf sees the gift as independent of the condition.

العمرى (al-‘umra): A gift of property for the recipient's lifetime, with a stipulation that it returns to the donor upon the recipient's death.
الرقبى (al-ruqba): A conditional gift where ownership depends on who dies first: if the donor dies first, the recipient gets it; if the recipient dies first, it returns to the donor.

Ruling on ʿUmrā (Life Grant) – Valid Gift with Void Condition
Explains the Hanafi ruling on ʿumrā and the validity of the gift despite the void condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of ʿUmrā in the Hanafi school and explain why the condition of return is void.

Core Idea
ʿUmrā is a valid gift; the condition of return upon death is void and the gift passes to heirs.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain forms of property transfer during one's lifetime carry conditions regarding death. One such form is ʿumrā (life grant). This lesson explains the Hanafi position that ʿumrā is a valid gift and that any condition requiring return upon death is disregarded, making the gift absolute and inheritable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that ʿumrā is permissible (jāʾiz) for the recipient and his heirs.
2
Understand the meaning of ʿumrā
The commentary defines it as giving property for the lifetime of the recipient or giver with a condition of return.
The Arabic word 'ʿumrā' derives from 'ʿumr' (life).
3
Determine the legal effect of the condition
The condition is void (bāṭil), but the gift remains valid. The principle: a gift is not invalidated by an invalid condition.
This is a key Hanafi rule: al-hibah la tabtulu bi al-shurūṭ al-fāsidah.
4
Apply the general rules of gifts
Since it is a gift, it must meet all requirements of hibah (offer, acceptance, delivery, etc.).
The passage says: 'wa idhā kānat hibatan, iʿtubira fīhā mā yuʿtabaru fī al-hibah'.
5
Conclude the ruling for the heirs
The gift passes to the recipient's heirs after death; the giver cannot reclaim it.
This contrasts with ruqbā (see next lesson).

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ruqbā (Survival Grant)
Explains the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the validity of ruqbā.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority position on ruqbā, explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Ruqbā is invalid per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (treated as a loan), but Abu Yusuf considers it a valid gift with a void condition.

While ʿumrā is a permissible gift, a related concept called ruqbā (survival grant) divides the Hanafi school. The core difference lies in whether the conditional nature of the transfer prevents it from being a valid gift. This lesson examines the arguments of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad versus Abu Yusuf and the practical implications of their disagreement.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
What is ruqbā? It is a grant where ownership depends on who dies first.
The word ruqbā comes from muraqabah (watching), as each party watches for the other's death.
2
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it is invalid (bāṭil) and treat it as a loan; Abu Yusuf says it is a valid gift.
The text: 'wa al-ruqbā bāṭilatun ʿinda Abī Ḥanīfah wa Muḥammad, wa qāla Abū Yūsuf: jāʾizah.'
3
Understand the reasoning
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad focus on the conditional future transfer; Abu Yusuf focuses on the immediate words of transfer (tamlik).
For the majority, the condition is integral; for Abu Yusuf, the condition is separable.
4
Analyze the difference
The key is whether the condition (ruqbā) is inherent to the transfer or merely an invalid addition.
Abu Yusuf treats it like ʿumrā; the majority sees it as fundamentally different because of the mutual waiting.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under the majority view, the giver can take it back anytime; under Abu Yusuf's view, it is an irrevocable gift.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Usurpation of Misl Property and Increase as Amanah
States ruling for misl property and discusses increase as amanah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on usurpation of misl property and explain why both separate and attached increases are considered a trust (amanah) rather than guaranteed.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the usurper of misl property guarantees its like, but any increase is a trust, not guaranteed.

The Book of Ghasb begins with a foundational distinction: the type of property determines the mode of guarantee. This lesson focuses on misl property—goods that have a market equivalent, like grains or standard items. The passage then addresses a key legal effect (faa'idah): what happens to increases, such as offspring or fatness, that arise while the property is in the usurper's hand. The Hanafi ruling treats both types of increase as a trust (amanah), contrary to Imam al-Shafi'i's view. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying liability in cases of wrongful possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states: whoever usurps something that has a like (misl), and it perishes, must guarantee its like. Note that the obligation is to return the like, not the value.
Misl property includes commodities that are typically traded by weight, measure, or count of identical units.
2
Classify the increase
The increase is divided into two types: separate (e.g., offspring) and attached (e.g., fatness). Both are treated similarly in the ruling.
Separate increase is physically distinct; attached increase is intrinsic to the property.
3
State the Hanafi position
According to the Hanafi school, both types of increase are a trust (amanah) in the hand of the usurper. This means the usurper is not liable for them unless he transgresses (e.g., by negligence or intentional harm).
The increase remains the property of the original owner and must be returned with the original property.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that both types of increase are guaranteed (madhmun), because the usurper established a hand (yad) over the increase, making him responsible for it.
The point of dispute: whether the usurper's possession over the increase is legitimate in the context of usurpation.
5
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response: 'according to us, there is no removal of the rightful hand.' The usurper's hand is not a legitimate removal of the owner's possession; therefore, any increase remains a trust, not subject to guarantee.
This argument is based on the nature of usurpation (ghasb) as a wrongful act that does not transfer ownership.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Valuation Date When Misl Property Becomes Unavailable
Intra-Hanafi dispute on valuation of misl property when unavailable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the valuation date when a misl property becomes unavailable and explain the reasoning for each.

Core Idea
When a misl property becomes unavailable, the valuation date is disputed: lawsuit (Abu Hanifa), usurpation (Abu Yusuf), or last availability (Muhammad & Zufar).

The Hanafi school distinguishes between misl properties (those with market substitutes) and qimi properties (unique items). This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a usurer takes a misl property that later becomes unavailable in the market. The three leading Hanafi jurists offer different rulings on when to assess its value, each based on the nature of the liability and the moment the obligation shifts from returning the like to paying value. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze how legal reasoning (qiyas) operates within a single madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property type and the condition
Recognise that the property is misl (has a market substitute) and that it becomes unavailable (inqita') after the usurpation. This triggers the dispute.
The general rule for misl is that the usurper must return the like; value is only assessed when the like is unavailable.
2
State the three positions
Memorise the three views: Abu Hanifa: value at litigation (yawm al-khusumah); Abu Yusuf: value at usurpation (yawm al-ghasb); Muhammad and Zufar: value at the last moment of availability (akhir ma inqata'a).
3
Analyse the reasoning for each position
Work through the arguments: Muhammad/Zufar: the like was in liability until cut-off, then obligation shifts to value at that time. Abu Yusuf: once unavailable, the property is treated like a qimi, so value is at the cause (usurpation). Abu Hanifa: the right to the like persists until lawsuit; only at lawsuit does the transfer to value occur.
The key difference lies in when the liability for the like is considered extinguished.
4
Identify the practical consequence
Understand that the valuation date can significantly affect the amount payable, especially if prices fluctuate. Abu Hanifa's view is often preferred in fatwa as it gives the owner the most current value.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is Abu Hanifa's view, as stated in al-Hidayah.

Liability for the mithl remains until litigation, so value is set on the day of court – Abu Hanifa.

When a fungible item (mithli) is usurped and later becomes unavailable before the case reaches court, a question arises: on which day should its value be assessed? The later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Karkhi) support Abu Hanifa's view, and it is the relied-upon position in the school.

The ruling is based on rational principles: the owner's right is attached to the specific item, and the purpose of the law is to remove the injustice (zulamah). Thus, forcing a replacement without consent would be a second injustice. The time for valuing the item is the day of seizure (yawm al-qabd), not later changes in market price. The commentary then discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute: some ('ala ma qalu) hold that the primary obligation (wajib asli) is to return the specific item, and paying value is merely a substitute (makhlus khalafan). Others (qil) say the primary obligation is the value, and returning the specific item is the substitute. The practical difference (fa'idah) appears in cases where the owner releases the usurper from liability, pledges the item, or guarantees it while the item still exists. For example, if the owner releases the usurper while the item is in his possession, and the usurper claims it is destroyed, the judge will imprison him until he proves he cannot produce it. According to the view that the primary obligation is value, the release is valid and drops the guarantee; according to the other view, it is not valid. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the primary obligation is to return the specific item, as stated in the matn and supported by the majority.

وإن غصب عبداً، فأبقَ من يده ولم يكن أبق قبل ذلك، أو كانت أمةً، فزنت في يده ولم تكن زنت قبل ذلك، أو سرقت: فعلى الغاصب ضمان ما نقص من العبد والجارية من السرقة والإباق والزنا. - وإن أصابها في يد الغاصب، فردها محمومة، فماتت عند صاحبها ضمن الغاصب ما نقصتها الحُمَّى، دون قيمتها؛ لأن الموت كانت في يد الغاصب، وإنما هو من الحُمَّى التي ليس من الحمى التي حدثت في يد صاحبها؛ لأن الحُمَّى يحصل منها الألم جزءاً فجزءاً، تتكامل بما يتجدد من الحُمَّى من بعده، فتموت من ذلك. - ثم وإن غصبها محمومة، فماتت في يد الغاصب: ضمن قيمتها محمومة يوم غصبها.
And if he usurps a slave, and he runs away from his hand and had not run away before that, or a female slave, and she commits adultery in his hand and had not committed adultery before that, or steals: upon the usurper is the guarantee of what decreased from the slave and the female slave due to the theft, running away, and adultery. - And if a fever befalls her in the hand of the usurper, and he returns her with fever, and she dies in the possession of her owner: the usurper guarantees what the fever decreased her value, but not her full value; because the death occurred in the hand of the usurper, and it was from the fever that did not originate from the fever that occurred in the hand of her owner; because the fever causes pain part by part, accumulating from the renewed fever after that, so she dies from that. - Then if he usurps her with fever, and she dies in the hand of the usurper: he guarantees her value at the time of usurpation, with fever.

Commentary
This passage presents specific applications of the general rule on diminution. If a usurped slave runs away, or a female slave commits adultery or theft while in the usurper's hand, and these actions were not habitual before usurpation, the usurper is liable for the diminution in value caused by these acts. The rationale is that the usurper's hand holds the slave, and the defect occurred during his tenure, so he is responsible. The next case involves an animal that catches a fever in the usurper's hand. If he returns it with fever and the animal later dies at the owner's place from that same fever, the usurper only compensates for the diminution (the loss of value due to the fever), not the full value. The reason is that the death itself is attributed to the usurper's hand because the fever that caused it originated in his hand. However, if the animal was already feverish when the usurper took it and then dies in his hand, he must pay its full value at the time of usurpation (i.e., the value it had in its feverish state), not the sound value.

Key Terms
إباق (ibaq): A slave's act of running away from the master or usurper.
حمى (humma): Fever; a disease that can cause a progressive loss of value and eventual death.

Key Takeaway
The usurper is liable for diminution caused by new defects arising in his hand, but for pre-existing defects that cause death, he pays the value at time of usurpation.
