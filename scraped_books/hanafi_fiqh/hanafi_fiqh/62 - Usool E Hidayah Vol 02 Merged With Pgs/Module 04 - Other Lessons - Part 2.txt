Prohibition of Killing Non-Combatants in War
Inter-madhhab dispute on non-combatant killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing non-combatants in war, identify the cause (illah) as hirab, and distinguish the Shafi'i position based on kufr.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, non-combatants are protected because the cause for killing is fighting, not disbelief.

This passage presents a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the permissibility of killing in war. While both schools agree that war is declared against disbelievers, they differ on who may be targeted. The Hanafi school restricts killing to those who actively fight, relying on the hadith prohibiting the killing of women and children. The Shafi'i school extends permission to all disbelievers based on the cause of kufr.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling and its illah
Identify the Hanafi position: it is generally haram to kill non-combatants; the cause for permissible killing is fighting ability (hirab).
Note the categories of non-combatants: women, children, elderly, blind, etc.
2
Identify the Hanafi dalil
Locate the hadith evidence: the Prophet forbade killing women and children and condemned the killing of a non-fighting woman.
The hadith is ahad, but its prohibition is clear.
3
State the opposing Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school holds that the cause is kufr, so any disbeliever may be killed in war.
This results in a broader permission.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and exception
The Hanafi response: the hadith explicitly prohibits killing non-combatants. However, if a non-combatant is a strategist or leader, killing becomes permissible because they effectively participate in war.
This exception aligns with the illah of hirab.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, non-combatants are spared unless they pose a threat; under Shafi'i, any disbeliever can be targeted.
This difference affects military ethics and rules of engagement.

Ownership of Booty: The Rule of Ihraz and Its Branches
Establishing a principle and its practical branches

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on when booty ownership is established and apply it to six derivative scenarios, identifying the Shafi'i counterposition.

Core Idea
Booty ownership requires removal to Dar al-Islam; without it, no sale, inheritance, or liability.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh of siyar: the timing of ownership of booty (ghanima). The rule that ownership is contingent upon ihraz (securing in Muslim territory) triggers a chain of practical consequences. By examining the six masa'il that follow, you will see how a single legal concept shapes multiple rulings and how the Shafi'i school derives opposite results from a different starting point.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi principle
Identify the core rule: ownership of booty (milk) is not established until the booty is brought to Dar al-Islam and secured (ihraz).
This is the foundation for all that follows.
2
Identify the Shafi'i counterprinciple
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i holds ownership is established immediately upon seizure, even before removal.
The key difference: timing of milk.
3
Examine the six derived issues (masa'il)
Go through each of the six branches listed in the passage and see how each ruling flows from the Hanafi principle. For each, note the opposite Shafi'i ruling.
This step builds your ability to trace a principle to its furu'.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Reflect on the practical significance: the difference in ownership timing affects division, inheritance, sale, liability, slave status, and the imam's division authority.
Knowing the thamarah helps you predict rulings in new cases.

Chapter of Booty and its Division

(154) According to us, ownership for the recipients of booty is not established before securing it in Dar al-Islam.

Translation: According to us, ownership for the recipients is not established before securing in Dar al-Islam.

Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that as long as the Mujahideen do not bring the wealth of the disbelievers to Dar al-Islam and secure it, ownership is not established. According to Imam Shafi'i, ownership is established even before securing (qabl al-ihraz). The author of Hidayah, after mentioning the principle, explains that booty will not be divided among the fighters in Dar al-Harb until it is transferred to Dar al-Islam, because before transfer to Dar al-Islam, the fighters' ownership of the booty is not established. Therefore, booty will not be divided in Dar al-Harb. According to Imam Shafi'i, division of booty in Dar al-Harb is valid; the reason is the same rule: according to us, before bringing the booty to Dar al-Islam and securing it, the ownership of the fighters is not established, whereas according to Imam Shafi'i, ownership is established even before securing.

Section on the Manner of Division

(155) According to us, the state of crossing the border is considered; according to him, the state of the end of war is considered.

Translation: According to us, the state of crossing the border is considered; according to Imam Shafi'i, the state of war ending is considered.

The author of Hidayah mentions this principle and explains that the fighter is in which state...

And the division of booty in their land is not for storage, i.e., it is prohibited to divide booty in Dar al-Harb for other than storage. Al-Shafi'i said: division in Dar al-Harb is permissible after the defeat is established. This is based on the fact that ownership is not established before removal to Dar al-Islam according to us, while according to him it is established. Upon this principle are built issues: among them, if reinforcements join them before removal to Dar al-Islam, they do not share according to him, while according to us they share. And among them, if one of the recipients fornicates with a slave girl from the captives and she gives birth, and he returns her, his lineage from him is established according to him, and she becomes umm walad for him; according to us, it is not established due to lack of ownership, and the 'uqr (compensation for intercourse) is obligatory, and the slave girl, child, and 'uqr are divided among the fighters. And among them, permissibility of selling it: according to him it is permissible, according to us not. And among them, if one dies before removal to Dar al-Islam, his share is inherited according to him, according to us not. And among them, if one of the fighters destroys something from the booty, he does not guarantee it according to us, and according to him he guarantees. And among them, if the Imam divides the booty without ijtihad and without need of the fighters, it is not valid according to us, and according to him it is valid.

Al-Hidayah: Kitab al-Siyar, chapter of Booty, section on Manner of Division, vol. 2, p. 560, Rahmaniyyah.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute on the ownership of booty (ghanima) before it is brought to Dar al-Islam. The author states the Hanafi principle: ownership of booty is not established for the fighters until it is secured (ihraz) in Muslim territory. The Shafi'i position is that ownership is established immediately upon seizure, even before removal. The passage then explains the practical consequence: booty cannot be divided in Dar al-Harb according to Hanafis, while Shafi'is allow it. A subsection on the manner of division adds that Hanafis consider the state of crossing the border (mujawaza) as the point for division, while Shafi'is consider the end of war. The Arabic quote from Tabyin al-Haqa'iq and Hidayah lists six derived issues (masa'il) that follow from this principle: (1) reinforcements who join before ihraz share in booty for Hanafis but not for Shafi'is; (2) if a soldier has intercourse with a captive slave girl, lineage and umm walad status are established for Shafi'is but not for Hanafis; (3) selling booty before ihraz is invalid for Hanafis but valid for Shafi'is; (4) inheritance of a deceased soldier's share is not upheld for Hanafis before ihraz; (5) if a soldier destroys booty, he is not liable for Hanafis but liable for Shafi'is; (6) imam's division without ijtihad or need is invalid for Hanafis but valid for Shafi'is. These examples illustrate how the foundational difference in the timing of ownership affects many fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
غنیمت (ghanima): Booty or spoils of war taken from the enemy; the subject of ownership disputes between Hanafi and Shafi'i.
احراز (ihraz): Securing the booty by bringing it to Dar al-Islam; the condition for ownership to be established.
دار الحرب (dar al-harb): Territory under non-Muslim control where warfare is ongoing; booty division here is not allowed according to Hanafis.

Condition for Imposing Kharaj: Landowner Must Remain a Disbeliever
States the condition for imposing kharaj: landowner must remain a disbeliever.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for kharaj and explain why the landowner's disbelief is a prerequisite.

Core Idea
Kharaj is only imposed on land whose owner persists in disbelief; conversion lifts the tax.

This lesson presents a key principle from the Hidayah regarding kharaj land tax. Kharaj is a levy on agricultural land in conquered territories where the owners remain non-Muslim subjects. The condition is clear: the landowner must persist in disbelief. Understanding this condition is essential for correctly applying Islamic fiscal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Read the Arabic text: إن وضع الخراج من شرطه أن يقر أهلها على الكفر. The condition is that the landowners remain firm on disbelief.
The term 'يقر' implies persistence, not mere existence of disbelief.
2
Understand the implication
If the condition is not met (i.e., the landowner converts to Islam), kharaj is dropped and the land becomes subject to ushr.
This is a direct consequence of the condition's absence.
3

Istishab al-Hal: Proof for Defense, Not for Claim
Defines istishab al-hal and its Hanafi application to missing persons.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of istishab al-hal, explain its scope as a proof for defense only, and apply it to the missing person (mafqud) case.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal maintains an existing state but cannot create new rights.

Istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a foundational tool in Hanafi legal reasoning. It presumes that a previously established state continues until evidence proves otherwise. However, the Hanafi school strictly limits its use to defending existing states, not to claiming new entitlements. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows its practical effect in the case of a missing person (mafqud).

Guided Path
1
Define istishab al-hal
Read the passage and formulate the definition: 'continuation of what was, as it was, due to the absence of a removing evidence.'
The Arabic phrase is 'بقاء ما كان على ما كان لعدم الدليل المزيل'.
2
Identify its scope: defense vs. claim
The text explicitly states that istishab is valid 'as a proof for defense, not for entitlement.' Understand that it repels a change (daf‘) but does not establish a new right (istihqaq).
Memorize: 'يصلح الاستصحاب عندنا حجة للدفع لا للاستحقاق'.
3
Analyze the missing person application
For a missing person, istishab presumes he is alive regarding the wealth of others (defense of his property). Therefore, he does not inherit from a deceased relative (no new claim), nor do others inherit from him. His potential share is suspended.
The suspended share goes to the testator's heirs once death is confirmed or the statutory period passes.
4
Contrast with other schools
The Shafi'i school allows istishab for both defense and claim. Understand the practical difference: for a missing person, a Shafi'i judge might allow inheritance based on istishab, while a Hanafi judge would suspend it.
This ikhtilaf shows how the same tool can yield different rulings.
5
Summarize the legal ruling
Consolidate: the Hanafi ruling is that istishab is a valid proof only for defending an existing state. It is not a basis for asserting new rights.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Appointing an Agent to Take Permissible Property is Void
States the invalidity of agency for permissible things

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for permissible property and explain the underlying principle of benefit (manfa'ah) in agency.

Core Idea
Agency for mubah is void because there is no benefit – the principal can take it himself.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a key principle: appointing an agent to take permissible property (mubah) is void. The reason is that agency requires a benefit or necessity. Since the principal can personally take such property without needing representation, the agency serves no purpose and is invalid. This principle also affects partnerships in mubah activities like collecting firewood or hunting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on tawkil (appointing an agent) to take mal mubah (permissible property). The ruling is 'batil' (void).
Batil means invalid ab initio, not just defective.
2
Understand the underlying reason
The rationale is that agency must provide a benefit (manfa'ah). Since the principal can take the mubah property directly without an agent, there is no benefit, making the agency void.
This is a Hanafi rule: agency is only valid when the principal cannot perform the act personally or needs representation.
3
Apply to specific examples
The text mentions collecting firewood and hunting. If two people agree to share in such activities, the collection or catch belongs solely to the actual collector or hunter. The other person's verbal agreement does not confer ownership.
The same ruling applies to every mubah thing.
4
Generalize the principle
Any agency for acquiring property that anyone can freely take is void. This includes wild animals, water from public sources, or abandoned items not owned.
The property must be acquired by actual taking, not by appointment.
