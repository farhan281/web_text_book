The Maxim of Indivisible Matters (Mā lā Yatajazza')
Legal maxim on indivisible matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'ذكر ما لا يتجزى كذكر الكل' and apply it to a case of partial divorce.

Core Idea
Mentioning an indivisible part is legally the same as mentioning the whole.

The Hanafi school employs this concise maxim to resolve ambiguity when a person mentions only part of an indivisible legal act. It is most frequently encountered in divorce (talāq), where the marital bond cannot be divided. Understanding this axiom prevents common errors in interpreting expressions like 'half a divorce.'

Guided Path
1
Memorise the Arabic Maxim
Repeat the maxim verbatim: 'إن ذكر ما لا يتجزى كذكر الكل'. This is the foundation.
This is a textual anchor – commit it to memory.
2
Understand Indivisibility (Mā lā Yatajazza')
Identify which legal acts or objects are indivisible – acts that cannot be split without destroying their legal essence. Classic examples: divorce (talāq), manumission (itq), oath (yamīn), and sale (bay') regarding the price.
Divisible objects can be split into valid parts (e.g., money, land).
3
Apply to a Divorce Example
If a husband says 'I divorce you with half a divorce,' the maxim dictates that mentioning 'half' is like mentioning the whole. Therefore, one complete divorce occurs, not half.
The ruling applies regardless of the husband's intention – the act is indivisible.
4
Recognise the Scope and Exceptions
This maxim applies only to indivisible legal acts. For divisible matters (e.g., gifting part of a house), the partial mention does not imply the whole. Also, if the maxim conflicts with a specific ruling (e.g., provision for partial manumission in case of partnership), the specific ruling prevails.
Scope is critical – do not extend to divisible contracts without caution.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Divorce by Comparison - Bā'in or Raj'ī?

Core Idea
Comparison to an object triggers divorce; irrevocability depends on the imam's criterion.

When a husband compares his wife to an object, does the divorce become irrevocable (bā'in) or revocable (raj'ī)? This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Zufar. The ruling affects whether the couple can reconcile without a new marriage, making the practical consequence significant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and type of divorce
The act is a husband saying to his wife something like 'You are like meat' – a comparison (tashbīh) that implies divorce. The type of divorce is disputed: bā'in (irrevocable) or raj'ī (revocable).
Bā'in ends the marriage immediately; raj'ī allows reconciliation during the waiting period.
2
Locate the three positions
Abu Hanifa: always bā'in regardless of mentioning bone. Abu Yusuf: bā'in only if 'bone' is explicitly mentioned. Zufar: bā'in if the object is commonly known to have a bone (e.g., a leg); otherwise raj'ī.
All three are within the Hanafi school; this is not a cross-madhhab ikhtilaf.
3
Trace the basis for each position
Abu Hanifa focuses on the inherent separation implied by comparison. Abu Yusuf considers explicit wording. Zufar relies on common custom (urf). The source does not elaborate on the proofs, but these are understood from their general principles.
Understanding the basis helps in applying the correct view.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
The text does not specify the mufta bihi, but later Hanafi works often adopt Abu Hanifa's view. Students should note that the relied-upon position affects fatwa practice.
In exam contexts, unless specified, Abu Hanifa's position is often considered the school's authoritative view.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario (e.g., 'You are like a chicken'), determine the ruling according to each imam. Then state the practical consequence for the couple's ability to remarry.
Practice ensures mastery of the ikhtilaf's thamarah.

Core Idea
A second marriage resets the divorce count only for Abu Yusuf, not for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This passage presents a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: if a woman receives fewer than three divorces from her first husband, then marries another man (with consummation), does that second marriage wipe the slate clean? The text records a difference of opinion between the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on one side, and Muhammad (again) with Zufar on the other. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for correctly applying the rules of talaq, especially when a woman returns to her first husband after an intervening marriage.

Identify the core issue
The passage asks whether a second husband's marriage 'destroys' (yahdimu) the legal effect of divorces that are fewer than three. This affects the number of divorces still available to the first husband if the couple reunites.
2
Understand the term 'yahdimu'
In Hanafi terminology, 'hadm' means nullifying the past divorces so they are no longer counted. This only occurs under specific conditions, such as consummation of the second marriage.

The text says 'indahuma' (according to them two: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) but then 'ind Muhammad wa Zufar' (according to Muhammad and Zufar) contradicting. Condition Already Present: Immediate Divorce
Ruling on conditional divorce when the condition already exists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that a conditional statement with a condition already true at the time of utterance takes effect immediately, and distinguish it from a valid suspension on a future condition.

Core Idea
A condition that is already true makes the statement immediate, not suspended.

This short maxim from the Hanafi school addresses a subtle but critical point in conditional divorces: what happens when the condition is already fulfilled before the speaker finishes speaking? The rule cuts through the confusion—if the condition is already a reality, there is no suspense; the divorce occurs at that very moment. This lesson will parse the Arabic maxim, apply it to a common scenario, and solidify the principle that a condition must be genuinely uncertain for ta‘līq to be valid.

Guided Path
1
Parse the Arabic maxim
Break down the sentence: ‘إِنَّ التَّعْلِيْقَ بِشَرُطٍ كَائِنِ تَنْجِيُز’. Identify the subject (التعليق), the prepositional phrase (بشرط كائن), and the predicate (تنجيز). Note that the word ‘تَنْجِيُز’ (corrected: تَنْجِيز) means ‘immediate’ or ‘making instantaneous’.
The OCR may show ‘تَنْجِيُز’ but the correct classical spelling is ‘تَنْجِيز’.
2
Understand the legal implication
The maxim states that if a condition (شرط) is already existent (كائن) at the time the statement is made, the suspension (تعليق) fails and the pronouncement is treated as unconditional and immediate. There is no waiting for an event that has already occurred.
This applies to all forms of ta‘līq (divorce, emancipation, vows, etc.) but is most common in talaq.
3
Apply to a talaq scenario
Take the example: a husband says to his wife who is already inside the house, ‘If you enter the house, you are divorced.’ Since the condition (entering the house) is already satisfied, the divorce takes effect immediately upon completion of the sentence.
The key is that the condition must be objectively true at the moment of utterance, not just likely or assumed.
4
Distinguish from a future condition
Contrast with: ‘If you enter the house tomorrow, you are divorced.’ Here the condition is future and uncertain, so the divorce is suspended until the event occurs. The maxim only overrides suspension when the condition is already present.
This distinction is the entire basis of the maxim.

The Maxim of Attribution to Cause of Ownership
Legal maxim on equivalence of attribution to cause and owner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim that attribution to the cause of ownership is equivalent to attribution to the owner in contexts of oaths and divorce.

Core Idea
Attribution to the cause of ownership carries the same legal weight as attribution to the owner himself.

This lesson examines a concise but powerful Hanafi legal maxim from the book of oaths in divorce. The maxim establishes that when someone makes a statement or oath that references the cause of ownership (e.g., 'by the One who made this my property'), it is legally treated as if they referenced the owner directly. This principle extends to divorce, where referencing the cause of marriage can effect a valid divorce. Understanding this maxim is crucial for correctly interpreting conditional statements in oaths and family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Identify iḍāfah (attribution) and sabab al-milk (cause of ownership). Understand their meanings and how they relate to the owner.
iḍāfah can be to the owner or to the cause.
2
Understand the equivalence
Recognize that the maxim states iḍāfah to the cause is like iḍāfah to the owner. 'Like' means legally equivalent in terms of validity and effect.
The cause (sabab) is the legal reason for ownership, e.g., purchase.
3
Apply to an oath example
Consider an oath: 'By the One who made this my property.' Apply the maxim: since the speaker attributed to the cause of ownership, the oath is valid as if he swore by Allah.
The maxim validates the oath without needing to mention Allah directly.
4
Extend to divorce context
Consider a divorce statement: 'You are divorced by the reason I married you.' The cause of marriage is the marriage contract. Attribution to that cause validates the divorce.
This shows the maxim applies beyond ownership to any legal cause.
5
Note the scope
The maxim is not limited to ownership; it covers any sabab (cause) that leads to a legal effect. Examples include the cause of marriage for divorce or the cause of purchase for oaths.
A common misunderstanding is restricting it to ownership.

The Trustee's Word is Accepted upon Oath
Ruling on the trustee's word being accepted with oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that the trustee's word is accepted with oath and apply it to disputes regarding talaq and agency.

Core Idea
A trustee's claim is preferred when he takes an oath — trustworthiness is presumed.

The Hanafi school upholds the principle that a person entrusted with a task or property (amīn) is considered trustworthy. Therefore, in disputes over the discharge of that trust, the trustee's statement is given precedence, provided he swears an oath. This maxim appears in classical texts like al-Hidayah and governs many cases in divorce, agency, and bailment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trust relationship
Determine if the party claiming to be the trustee (amīn) is indeed entrusted with a specific matter (e.g., delegation of divorce, agency for sale).
2
Identify the disputed claim
Establish the exact claim the trustee is making (e.g., 'I did delegate divorce to her' or 'I sold it at this price'). The claim must be about the discharge of the trust.
3
Require an oath from the trustee
The trustee's word is not automatically accepted; he or she must take a solemn oath (yamīn) affirming the claim. Without oath, the claim is not given weight.
The oath must be taken in the name of Allah, and if the trustee refuses, the opposing party's claim may be accepted.
4
Accept the trustee's statement as determinative
Once the oath is taken, the trustee's statement is considered truth and the dispute is resolved in his/her favor. The burden of proof shifts to the other party.
This is based on the principle of amānah — trustworthiness.

This is a general principle in Hanafi fiqh applicable to various contexts like divorce, agency, and trusts. For example, if a wife delegates divorce and husband claims she revoked it, her word as trustee (amīn) is accepted upon oath. The maxim establishes that the amīn is trustworthy, so his/her claim is preferred.

The Particle of Adverb and Its Reference in Talaq Statements
Linguistic rule for interpreting adverbial clauses in talaq statements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of adverbial particle attachment to determine which object is described in a divorce statement.

Core Idea
If an adverbial particle is tied to a pronoun, it describes the last object; otherwise, the first.

In Hanafi divorce law, the precise wording of a husband's statement determines the legal effect. This lesson focuses on a key linguistic rule from the Hidayah: when two things are mentioned and an adverbial particle (like 'in' or 'at') is inserted, its reference depends on whether it is attached to a pronoun of allusion (هاء الكناية). Understanding this rule helps resolve disputes about what was actually intended in conditional divorce pronouncements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objects mentioned
In a divorce statement, the husband often mentions two things, such as the divorce and a location or condition. Determine the first and last objects in the Arabic sentence.
The order matters: first object vs. last object.
2
Locate the inserted adverbial particle (حرف الظرف)
Find the word that indicates a time or place relationship (e.g., 'in', 'at', 'when'). This is the adverbial particle.
The particle may be a single word or phrase.
3
Check if the particle is connected to a pronoun of allusion (هاء الكناية)
Look for the pronoun 'it' (ha') attached to the particle. If present, the particle describes the last-mentioned object. If absent, it describes the first-mentioned object.
The pronoun is typically a suffix on the particle.
4
Apply the rule to determine the attribute
Based on the presence or absence of the pronoun, decide which object is qualified by the adverbial phrase.
This determines the legal meaning of the conditional statement.
5
Test with a divorce scenario
Use a hypothetical statement like 'You are divorced in the house' and apply the rule: if 'in the house' has a pronoun (e.g., 'in it'), what does it refer to?
Practice with variations to solidify the rule.

When Intention Specifies: Same Category vs Different Categories
Principle on the effect of intention in general statements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when intention (niyyah) is considered in specifying a general statement according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Intention specifies when categories differ, but is ignored within the same category.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi principle: the legal effect of intention (niyyah) depends on whether the items covered by a general statement are of the same type (jins wāḥid) or of different types (jinsayn). When the items are of one type, the intention is irrelevant (laghw); when of two types, the intention is given legal weight (muʿtabarah). Mastering this rule is essential for correctly applying oaths, divorces, and other utterances in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Statement
Consider an utterance that, grammatically, applies to multiple possible items (e.g., 'You are divorced,' said to multiple wives, or 'I swear by Allah,' covering multiple acts).
The statement must be general and capable of applying to each item.
2
Classify the Items by Type
Determine whether all items belong to the same legal category (jins wāḥid) or to two different categories (jinsayn). For example, two wives both being Muslim women of equal status are the same type; a Muslim wife and a Jewish wife are different types.
Jins here refers to the legal category, not necessarily biological or social identity.
3
Apply the Rule
If the items are of the same type, the intention to specify one is ignored (laghw) and the statement applies to all. If they are of two different types, the intention is valid (muʿtabarah) and the statement applies only to the intended item.
This rule is a default; it may be overridden by other evidence (e.g., context or oaths).
4
Verify with an Example
Take a man with two wives of the same religion: he says 'You are divorced,' intending only the older one. Since the wives are of the same type, the intention is irrelevant and both are divorced. If one wife were Muslim and the other Jewish (different types), the intention would be valid and only the targeted wife would be divorced.
This example is from the chapter on ṭalāq in Al-Hidāyah.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Divorce (Kitab al-Talaq), Chapter of Sunnah Divorce (Bab Talaq al-Sunnah), presents two usul (principles) applied to the ruling of divorcing a woman during her menstrual period. The first principle (numbered 44) states that prohibition for an external reason does not negate the legality of the act itself. This reinforces the idea that the divorce remains effective even if done sinfully.

Divorce During Menstruation: Ruling and Prohibition
Explains that divorce during menstruation is valid but prohibited

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce during menstruation and explain why the prohibition does not invalidate the divorce.

Core Idea
Divorce during menstruation is valid but prohibited because the prohibition is for an external reason, not the essence.

This lesson addresses a common question: if an act is prohibited (haram), is it automatically invalid? The Hanafi school distinguishes between the essence of an act and external factors. Here, divorce during menstruation is prohibited due to the harm of lengthening the iddah, but the divorce itself remains valid. We will examine the proof from the Quran and the usul principle of 'nahy li ma'nan fi ghayrihi'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is divorce pronounced during menstruation. State the ruling: the divorce occurs (valid) but the act is prohibited (haram).
The prohibition is not on the essence of divorce but on the timing.
2
Understand the basis of the prohibition
The prohibition is due to an external reason: it lengthens the woman's iddah because the menstruation in which divorce is given does not count toward the three cycles. Thus, the woman suffers harm.
This external reason is key to the ruling's validity.
3
Examine the primary dalil
The Quranic verse (65:1) 'So divorce them for their iddah' indicates the proper time for divorce, but does not make it a condition. The Hadith where the Prophet told Umar to order his son to take back his wife shows the divorce occurred but was disliked.
The command to take back implies the divorce was valid; otherwise, no taking back would be needed.
4
Apply the usul principle
The Hanafi principle 'al-nahy li ma'nan fi ghayrihi' holds that when a prohibition is for an external reason, the act remains valid in its essence. Here, the prohibition is for lengthening iddah, not for the act of divorce itself.
This distinguishes Hanafi from Shafi'i, who sometimes consider prohibition as indicating invalidity.

Legal Principle: Sinful Cause Leads to Legal Continuity as Deterrent
States a legal maxim and applies it to the case of drunkard's divorce, with proof and distinction from narcotic user.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal principle that a thing removed by a sinful cause is legally considered remaining, and apply it to determine the validity of a drunkard's divorce.

Core Idea
A sinner cannot benefit from his sin: loss of reason due to intoxication is legally treated as intact to punish him.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: when a person’s legal capacity (like sanity) is lost through a sinful act, the law treats it as still present as a deterrent. The classic application is the divorce of an intoxicated person (sakran). In Hanafi fiqh, such a divorce is valid despite the lack of a sound mind, because the loss of intellect was caused by the sin of drinking. Understanding this principle is crucial for grasping how the Hanafi school balances justice and deterrence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal principle
Read the Arabic maxim 'إن الشيء إذا زال بسبب هو معصية يجعل باقيا حكما زجرا له' and understand its meaning: if a thing is removed by a sinful cause, it is legally considered remaining as a deterrent.
This is a qa'idah fiqhiyyah (legal maxim).
2
Apply to drunkard's divorce
The passage applies this maxim to a sakran who divorces. The intellect (reason) is removed by intoxication (sinful cause), so legally intellect is considered present. Therefore the divorce is valid.
3
Examine the proof (dalil) of taklif
The text argues that the drunkard is mukallaf because he is obligated to perform fara'id and liable for hadd and qisas. Since a mukallaf's divorce is valid, the drunkard's divorce is valid.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on legal responsibility.
4
Distinguish the narcotic user (mubannaj)
The maxim does not apply to a person intoxicated by a non-sinful cause (like medical banj). Such a person is not considered mukallaf, so his divorce is not valid.
The cause (sabab) being sinful is the key condition.
5
Note cross-madhhab dispute
Some Shafi'i scholars hold that intention is required for divorce, so the drunkard's divorce would be invalid. The Hanafi position is based on the principle of deterrence and the status of taklif.
This illustrates thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) between schools.

The Masdar Implies Generality and Multiplicity: Application in Divorce
Explanation of a Hanafi usul principle with application to divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the masdar implies generality and multiplicity to determine the number of divorces in ambiguous pronouncements.

Core Idea
The masdar as a generic noun can mean one or three, not two; intention specifies.

This lesson examines a key usul al-fiqh principle: the masdar (infinitive) inherently carries the possibility of generality and multiplicity because it is a generic noun (ism jins). The Hanafi school applies this to divorce: when a husband uses a masdar form like 'talaq', the word can mean one divorce or three, depending on his intention. However, the intention of two is invalid. You will learn the linguistic basis and the practical ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Recognize that the masdar (infinitive) can imply generality (ʿumūm) and multiplicity (kathrah). This is a principle of Hanafi usul.
The principle is used to interpret ambiguous words in legal contexts.
2
Understand the Linguistic Basis
The masdar is a generic noun (ism jins). Like other generic nouns, it includes all instances of the action—both minimal and maximal. Therefore, it can refer to one or many.
For example, 'talaq' as a masdar can mean one divorce or three.
3
Apply to Divorce Wording
When a husband says 'anti taliq al-talaq' or 'anti al-talaq', the word 'talaq' is a masdar. If he intends three, three divorces occur. If he intends one or has no intention, one revocable divorce occurs.
The intention is effective because the masdar's capacity allows specification.
4
Recognize the Limitation
The masdar cannot imply the number two. Hence, the intention of two is invalid—only one or three are possible. This is a unique Hanafi position.
The principle holds that the masdar covers the true individual (one) and the complete individual (three), but not the intermediate.

Core Idea
Divorcing a part divorces the whole when the part represents the person in custom.

In Hanafi law, the effectiveness of divorce wording relies on common linguistic usage (ʿurf). This lesson presents a key qāʿidah: if a husband says 'Your hand is divorced' or similar, the body part is understood to refer to the entire wife. Effect of Divorce When Attached to Body Parts
Ruling with proof on the condition of body part in divorce pronouncement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that divorce is effective only when attributed to the entire body or a comprehensive part, and identify when a divorce statement is valid or invalid based on the body part mentioned.

Core Idea
Divorce is only effective when attributed to the whole body or a comprehensive part that contains the marriage bond.

Divorce is defined as removing the restriction (qayd) of marriage. This restriction resides in the entire person, not in isolated limbs. Therefore, the words a husband uses to divorce must target the proper place (mahall) of divorce. The Hanafi school has a clear rule: if the divorce is attributed to a part that can represent the whole (like head, neck, or body), it is effective; if attributed to a part that cannot (like hand or foot), it is not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divorce statement and classify the body part
Determine whether the husband said 'your hand is divorced' or 'your head is divorced'. The body part mentioned determines the ruling.
The passage lists comprehensive parts: head, face, neck, private part, body. Non-comprehensive: hand, foot, saliva, nail.
2
Apply the principle of mahall al-talaq
Recall that divorce removes the marriage bond. That bond exists only in the whole body or in a part that can stand for the whole. If the attributed part contains the bond, divorce occurs; if not, it does not.
The principle from the text: 'The place of divorce is that in which the restriction exists.'
3
Decide the outcome
If the part is comprehensive, divorce occurs. If not, no divorce occurs. This is based on rational analogy: just as marriage is not contracted by referring to a hand or foot, divorce cannot be effected by such references.
There is consensus (la khilaf) among Hanafis on this point.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Recognize that the Shafi'i school disagrees: they hold that even saying 'your hand is divorced' results in divorce, because any part of the body is a valid mahall.
This difference is based on different understandings of where the marriage bond resides.

The Non-Divisibility of Divorce: Fractional Talaq
States that mentioning a non-divisible thing is like mentioning the whole, applied to divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that divorce is non-divisible and apply it to cases where a fractional talaq is uttered.

Core Idea
Divorce is indivisible; uttering a fraction still effects a full single divorce.

This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces a crucial Hanafi principle: mentioning a non-divisible thing is like mentioning the whole. The immediate application is divorce (talaq). If a husband says 'half a divorce' or 'one-third of a divorce,' it counts as one full divorce because divorce is a single, indivisible act. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying talaq rulings and avoiding confusion about partial expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The passage states: 'Indeed, the mention of something non-divisible is like the mention of the whole.' Recognize this as a general legal maxim.
The principle applies to any act that cannot be divided.
2
Locate the Application
The author applies this principle to divorce. Divorce is classified as non-divisible because it is a single act of dissolving the marriage bond.
3
Trace the Analogy (Qiyas)
Divorce is analogous to any other non-divisible act: just as mentioning a fraction of an indivisible thing counts as mentioning the whole, so too does uttering 'half a divorce' count as a full divorce.
4
Consider Cross-Madhhab Variation
Some other schools may treat fractional talaq differently, e.g., as no divorce or as one divorce depending on intent. The Hanafi position is based on the indivisible nature.
The text notes that this is mukhtalaf fih (disputed).
5
Derive the Ruling
Regardless of the fraction mentioned (half, third, quarter), the result is one full, valid divorce.
This has consequences for the count of revocable divorces and the possibility of return.

Divorce Occurs in All Places
Explains that talaq is effective everywhere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that talaq is not place-specific and apply it to a scenario where the husband specifies a location.

Core Idea
Divorce is not tied to a place; specifying a location does not delay or restrict its occurrence.

The principle 'Divorce, whenever it occurs, occurs in all places' is a key Hanafi rule. It prevents a husband from manipulating divorce by tying it to a location. For instance, if he says 'You are divorced in Mecca', the divorce takes effect immediately, regardless of where the wife actually is. This lesson unpacks that rule and its application.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Principle
Identify the core rule: talaq is not specific to any place. It is a legal act that is independent of location.
2
Consider the Example
Examine the scenario: a husband says to his wife 'You are divorced in Mecca'. Note that he has attached a location (Mecca) to the divorce.
3
Apply the Principle
Apply the rule: because divorce is not place-specific, the utterance causes immediate divorce. The wife is divorced at that moment, even if she is not in Mecca.
4
Understand the Rationale
The rationale is that talaq is a legal concept that does not require a physical container (zarf). Specifying a place is like specifying a container for something that cannot be contained.
