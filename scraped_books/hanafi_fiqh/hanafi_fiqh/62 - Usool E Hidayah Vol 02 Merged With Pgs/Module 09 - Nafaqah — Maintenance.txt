Nafaqah is Compensation for Confinement
Ruling principle defining nafaqah as compensation for confinement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that maintenance (nafaqah) is compensation for the wife's confinement (ihtibas) and identify the condition that triggers the obligation.

Core Idea
Maintenance is not a gift — it is compensation for the wife's confinement.

This passage establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi family law: the husband's obligation to provide maintenance (nafaqah) to his wife arises as a direct consequence of her being confined (ihtibas) to the marital home. Understanding this rationale is essential because it explains why the wife loses her right to maintenance if she leaves without a valid reason. This principle anchors many detailed rulings in the chapters of nafaqah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal characterization
Recognize that nafaqah is defined as 'jaza'' (compensation), not as a gift or charity.
2
Understand the cause (sabab)
The wife's confinement (ihtibas) is the direct cause that triggers the obligation of maintenance.
Confinement means the wife is legally bound to remain with her husband and not go out without permission.
3
Recognize the effect
The wife is entitled to maintenance as long as she is confined. If confinement ends, maintenance may cease.
4
Apply the reverse
If the wife leaves without valid reason (nushuz), confinement is breached, and maintenance is no longer owed.
This shows the conditional nature of nafaqah.

إِنَّ النَّفَقَةَ جَزَاءُ الْإِحْتِبَاسِ

Source pages p. 9

Commentary
This is a key principle: the wife's right to maintenance (nafaqah) from her husband is considered as a compensation (jaza') for her being confined to the marriage (ihtibas). Since the wife is legally bound to remain with her husband and not go out without his permission, she receives nafaqah in return. If she is not confined (e.g., if she leaves without cause), she may lose the right to maintenance.

Key Terms

Key Takeaway

What is Obliged as Sufficiency is Not Fixed in Amount
Principle: sufficiency obligations are not Shariah-quantified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that obligations of sufficiency are not quantified by Shariah and apply it to the example of nafaqah.

Core Idea
An obligation of sufficiency is not quantified by Shariah; its amount is determined by custom and need.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle: when the Shariah obliges something as sufficiency (kifayah), it does not fix a specific quantity. Instead, the amount is left to custom (urf) and circumstance. This is crucial for understanding rulings on maintenance (nafaqah) and other similar obligations.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the type of obligation: kifayah (sufficiency)
Identify that the obligation is for sufficiency, not a fixed performance. This triggers the principle.
Kifayah implies enough to meet basic needs.
2
Understand the principle: Not quantified by Shariah
The Shariah does not set a specific measure for obligations defined by sufficiency. The text says: 'la yataqaddaru shara'an'.
3
Identify the default: Custom (urf) determines the amount
In the absence of a fixed measure, the amount is determined by prevailing custom and the specific circumstances of the parties.
Custom is authoritative in non-fixed matters.
4
Apply to nafaqah: Maintenance varies
For example, the maintenance a husband must provide for his wife is not a fixed sum; it varies based on his means and her needs, as evaluated by custom.

Indeed, what is obliged as sufficiency is not quantified in itself by Shariah.
Source pages p. 9

Commentary
When an obligation is to provide sufficiency (kifayah) – such as maintenance for family – the amount is not predetermined by the Shariah. Instead, it is left to custom (urf) and the assessment of the judge or the needs of the recipient. For example, the amount of nafaqah for a wife varies based on the husband's means and the wife's needs.

Key Terms

Maintenance Right Depends on Who Causes Lack of Confinement
Ruling on when the wife's lack of confinement affects maintenance right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for when a wife who is not confined still deserves maintenance, and identify the decisive factor.

Core Idea
Maintenance right hinges on fault: if husband causes lack of confinement, wife still gets nafaqah.

A wife's right to maintenance is normally tied to her being confined (ihtibas) in her husband's home. But what if she is not confined? This lesson clarifies that the determining factor is not the mere fact of confinement, but who is responsible for its absence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage states a conditional rule: if the lack of confinement (fawt al-ihtibas) is caused by the husband, she deserves maintenance; otherwise, she does not.
2
Understand the cause
The cause (sabab) for the ruling is attribution: 'min qibal al-zawj' (from the husband's side). This includes acts like expelling her from the home or traveling without providing accommodation.
The wife's own fault (e.g., leaving without permission) does not trigger maintenance.
3
Apply to scenarios
Test the rule: If the husband forces her out, she gets nafaqah. If she leaves without a valid reason, she loses it.

Source Text

But if she leaves without a valid reason (like going out without permission), she loses her right to maintenance. The cause matters.

من قبل الزوج (min qibal al-zawj): From the husband's side; due to his action or omission.

The Condition for Obligatory Maintenance: Confinement for Marital Purpose
Definition of the confinement that obligates maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of ihtibas that obligates nafaqah and identify whether a given confinement meets the condition of being a means to the marital objective.

Core Idea
Only confinement that enables the marital purpose of intimacy and companionship obligates maintenance.

This passage establishes a crucial condition for the obligation of maintenance. Not all confinement of a wife triggers nafaqah; it must be confinement that serves the objectives of marriage. Understanding this distinction prevents misapplication of the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defined term
Locate the term 'al-ihtibas al-mujib' in the passage. This is the confinement that is being defined as obligating maintenance.
The term 'ihtibas' means confinement, but the additional word 'mujib' indicates that it causes obligation.
2
Extract the condition
The passage states that the ihtibas must be 'wasilah ila maqsud mustahaqq bil-nikah' – a means to a goal entitled by marriage. Identify this condition as the core of the definition.
The word 'wasilah' (means) implies that the confinement must have a causal link to the marital objective.
3
Understand the marital objective
The 'maqsud' (objective) of marriage, in Hanafi fiqh, primarily includes intimacy, companionship, and the procreation of children. Confinement must facilitate these ends.
If the confinement prevents rather than enables these objectives, it does not obligate nafaqah.
4
Apply to a counterexample
Consider a wife who is imprisoned for a crime. This confinement is not a means to any marital objective; therefore, it does not obligate nafaqah. Contrast with a wife confined to the marital home who is available for companionship.
The Hanafi school uses this condition to distinguish cases where nafaqah is due from those where it is not.

The Hanafi View: Nafaqah is Silah, Not 'Iwad
Hanafi view that nafaqah is a gift, not a compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that nafaqah is a gift (silah) and not a compensation ('iwad), and identify the legal implications of this distinction.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is a husband's obligation, not a transactional payment—it is a connective gift (silah), not a compensation ('iwad).

This short passage from the Hidayah encapsulates a fundamental Hanafi principle: that the husband's maintenance obligation (nafaqah) is not a form of compensation like a wage or price, but a gift that signifies connection and obligation. Understanding this distinction affects how we view the rights of the wife and the nature of the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Extract the core claim: Nafaqah is silah, not 'iwad according to the Hanafis.
2
Define Silah and 'Iwad
Silah means a gratuitous gift that fosters connection; 'iwad means compensation or countervalue in an exchange.
These definitions are crucial to applying the distinction.
3
Consider the Implications
If nafaqah were 'iwad, it would be transactional—like a salary for the wife's services. As silah, it is a pure obligation imposed by the shari'ah, not contingent on reciprocity.
For example, nafaqah is not forfeited if the wife does not perform household services.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi or Cross-Madhhab Dispute
Other schools may view nafaqah differently (e.g., as compensation for tamkin). This difference affects rulings like waiver and revocation.
5
Apply to a Practical Scenario
Consider: Can a wife waive future nafaqah before it becomes due? As a silah, prepaid gratuitous gifts are generally revocable, but nafaqah is an accrued right, so the ruling may differ.
This shows how the classification shapes fiqh rules.

Indeed, maintenance is a gift and is not a compensation, according to us.
Source pages p. 9

Commentary
The Hanafis hold that nafaqah is a silah (connective gift) rather than an 'iwad (compensation/consideration). This is important for the nature of the obligation. If it were compensation, it might fall under contracts of exchange. As a gift, it is based on the husband's obligation. This affects rulings like whether nafaqah can be waived? Possibly it cannot be like a debt?

صلة (silah): Gift, donation, or connection.
عوض ('iwad): Compensation; countervalue in exchange.

Nafaqah and Inheritance: The Textual Link

Core Idea
Nafaqah is tied to inheritance by text: those who inherit often bear maintenance obligations.

This brief but foundational statement from early Hanafi sources establishes a key link between two important areas of family law: maintenance (nafaqah) and inheritance (irth). Understanding this link helps clarify who is responsible for supporting whom, based on a deeper textual connection rather than mere social custom.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the linking term
The Arabic phrase 'مُتَعَلِّقَةٌ' indicates a relational bond. Identify that nafaqah and irth are not separate but connected.
This is a legal connection, not a mere coincidence.
2
Identify the two elements
First, 'النَّفَقَةَ' (nafaqah, maintenance) refers to financial support for dependents. Second, 'بِالْإرْثِ' (inheritance) refers to the transfer of property after death.
The text uses 'al-' (definite article) for both, indicating general categories.
3
Note the evidential basis
The phrase 'بِالنَّصِّ' means the connection is established by explicit texts from Quran or Sunnah, not by mere analogy. Recall Quranic verses on inheritance (e.g., 4:7) and maintenance (e.g., 2:233).
This elevates the link from a juristic construction to a direct scriptural principle.
4
Apply to a classic example
Consider the case of a father and his minor son. The son inherits from the father, and the father is obligated to provide nafaqah for the son. The textual link justifies this.
Similarly, a husband inherits from his wife and is obligated to maintain her during marriage.

Ruling that earning capability constitutes wealth for nafaqah

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a person capable of earning is considered wealthy, and apply it to determine eligibility for maintenance (nafaqah).

Core Idea
Earning capacity makes one legally wealthy—actual assets are not required.

In Hanafi fiqh, maintenance obligations depend on need. This short but powerful principle establishes that a person who can work is not considered needy, even if they currently have no wealth. Understanding this helps us correctly assign nafaqah responsibilities in families.

Condition: the person is physically and legally able to earn (al-qadir 'ala al-kasb). Consequence: they are wealthy by their own earning (ghani bi kasbihi), meaning they are not entitled to nafaqah from others.
The ability must be real and lawful; it does not include those who are sick or disabled.
3
Apply to common scenarios
An adult son who can work but prefers not to is not entitled to maintenance from his father. Similarly, a husband may stop nafaqah to a wife who is capable of earning but refuses.
Earning capacity is assessed at the time of need; past ability is irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage from Usool al-Hidayah establishes a key Hanafi principle regarding the quantification of nafaqah (maintenance). The author begins by explaining the general basis for nafaqah: it is wajib (obligatory) due to confinement (ihtibas). The commentary applies this to the wife's nafaqah: Imam al-Shafi'i held that it is fixed — two mudd for wealthy, one and a half for middle, one for poor. This is a clear intra-madhhab (Hanafi vs Shafi'i) dispute on a practical ruling.

Loss of Confinement and Nafaqah: Who is Responsible?
Explaining how loss of confinement affects nafaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of fawt al-ihtibas to determine when a wife retains or loses her right to nafaqah.

Core Idea
Nafaqah compensates confinement; if the husband causes its loss, nafaqah continues; if the wife causes it, she loses it.

Nafaqah in Hanafi fiqh is fundamentally tied to the wife's confinement (ihtibas) in the marital home. This lesson unpacks a key principle from al-Hidayah: when the loss of that confinement is due to the husband's action or omission, the wife remains entitled to nafaqah; but when she herself causes the loss by leaving without right, her entitlement ceases. We will also explore a crucial nuance: refusing intimacy while staying home does not break confinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Cause of Nafaqah
State the Hanafi position that nafaqah is wajib as compensation for the wife's confinement (ihtibas).
Confinement means the wife is physically available in the husband's home for him to access.
2
Classify the Reason for Loss of Confinement
Determine whether the loss of confinement (fawt al-ihtibas) originates from the husband or the wife. The principle states that if from the husband, nafaqah remains; if from the wife, it drops.
Examples: husband's travel or failure to provide mahr causing lawful refusal vs. wife's unauthorized departure.
3
Distinguish Nushuz from Refusal of Tamkin
Learn that nushuz (disobedience) in this context is defined as leaving the home without permission. Mere refusal of intimacy (tamkin) while remaining in the home does not constitute nushuz and does not affect nafaqah.
Confinement (ihtibas) is physically present even if tamkin is refused, because the husband can enforce his right.
4
Apply the Principle to a Practical Scenario
Given a case where the wife withholds herself due to unpaid mahr, rule that nafaqah is due if she stays in the home, because the cause of confinement loss is the husband's failure to pay mahr.
The wife is justified (mahirah) in refusing submission until mahr is paid.

Commentary

Nafaqah Amount Is Not Fixed but Based on Sufficiency
States that nafaqah is wajib as kifayah and thus not fixed in quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why the Hanafi school does not fix the amount of nafaqah and apply the principle of kifayah to determine maintenance.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib as kifayah, so its amount is not fixed but varies with need.

This passage clarifies a key Hanafi principle: when an obligation is defined as 'sufficiency' (kifayah), it cannot be fixed to a rigid quantity. We'll explore why the amount of a wife's maintenance is determined by her actual needs and the husband's capacity, not by a predetermined measure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What is the dispute about nafaqah? Is it a fixed amount or variable? The passage states that it is wajib as kifayah and therefore not fixed.
This is a definitional ruling – understanding the nature of the obligation determines its quantity.
2
Extract the ruling
State the Hanafi position: nafaqah is wajib, but its quantity is not fixed by law; it depends on what suffices the wife.
Contrast with a fixed measure (e.g., two mudd) which is mentioned but not the binding rule.
3
Understand the reasoning (qiyas)
The proof is rational: because nafaqah varies with age, season, health, and circumstances, fixing it would cause harm. Obligations based on sufficiency cannot be fixed.
This is a qiyas derived from the nature of kifayah, not from a specific textual proof.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the amount is fixed (e.g., two mudd for rich, etc.). The Hanafi school rejects this because it ignores variability.
This difference stems from different interpretations of the nature of nafaqah.
5
Apply the principle
In practice, a judge determines nafaqah based on the wife's need and husband's means, not a fixed schedule.
This ensures justice and avoids hardship.

Loss of Retention (Fawt al-Ihtibas) and Nafaqah Entitlement
Sets the rule for when loss of retention affects nafaqah entitlement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that nafaqah is contingent on ihtibas and apply it to cases where retention is lost due to the husband versus the wife.

Core Idea
Nafaqah belongs to retention: when the loss is from the husband, it continues; from the wife, it stops.

In the Hanafi school, a wife's right to maintenance (nafaqah) is directly linked to her being in a state of retention (ihtibas) in the marital home. But what happens if that retention is broken? This lesson unpacks the key rule from al-Hidayah that distinguishes between loss caused by the husband and loss caused by the wife, and shows how it applies to common scenarios like withholding for mahr or leaving without permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
First, find the rule stated at the beginning: if the loss of retention is from the husband's side, nafaqah is due; otherwise, it is not. This is the benchmark.
The principle is numbered (17) in the original text.
2
Understand what retention (ihtibas) means
Ihtibas is the wife's availability for sexual access in the marital home. It is the basis for nafaqah. Recognize that this is a condition, not a pillar.
Compare to tamkin in other schools; the concept is similar.
3
Apply the principle to the first scenario
Consider the case where the wife withholds herself because the husband has not paid the mahr. The text says she is justified—the loss of retention is from the husband, so nafaqah is wajib.
The fault is not hers; she is not being disobedient.
4
Apply the principle to the second scenario
Now consider nushuz: the wife leaves the house without permission. The loss of retention is from her, so nafaqah stops until she returns. The text clarifies that even if she refuses submission inside the house, retention is present, so nafaqah continues.
This shows the nuance: leaving vs. merely refusing submission.

The author of al-Hidayah mentions this principle to explain that if a woman refuses to hand herself over to her husband until he pays her mahr, she is justified in retaining herself, so nafaqah for that period is wajib on the husband, because the cause came from the husband. When she returns, retention is restored and nafaqah becomes wajib, unlike the case if she refuses to submit in the husband's house because retention is present and the husband can have intercourse forcibly. Ihtibas Must Be a Means to the Marital Purpose
Defines that only ihtibas leading to marital intimacy obligates nafaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which ihtibas obligates nafaqah and apply it to cases of incomplete ihtibas.

Core Idea
Only ihtibas that enables marital intimacy obligates nafaqah; incomplete ihtibas does not.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a crucial principle: not every form of ihtibas (confinement/retention) obligates nafaqah. Only ihtibas that serves as a means to the marital purpose—intercourse—triggers the obligation. The author applies this to a minor wife who cannot consummate, concluding she is not entitled to nafaqah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
Read principle (11): 'Indeed, the ihtibas that obligates nafaqah is what is a means to a purpose established by marriage.' Note that ihtibas alone is insufficient; it must lead to the marital purpose.
The marital purpose is defined as intercourse (وطی) and enjoyment.
2
Distinguish Complete from Incomplete Ihtibas
The passage distinguishes complete ihtibas (iḥtibās kāmil), which enables intercourse, from incomplete ihtibas, which does not. Only the former obligates nafaqah.
Incomplete ihtibas can arise from the wife's minority, illness, or refusal to relocate.
3
Apply to the Minor Wife Case
Consider a wife too young for intercourse. Though she lives with her husband (ihtibas exists), it is incomplete because it cannot achieve the marital purpose. Therefore, nafaqah is not wajib.
The inability is from the wife's side, not the husband's.
4
Note Cross-Madhhab Difference
The Hanafi ruling is mukhtalaf fih. The Shafi'i school holds that nafaqah is due even for a minor wife because she is available for other benefits (e.g., companionship).
This difference stems from defining the 'purpose of marriage' more broadly.

Nafaqah as a Gift: The Hanafi Principle and Past Maintenance
Explains Hanafi principle that nafaqah is a gift, not consideration, and its implication for past maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that nafaqah is a gift (silah) not consideration (iwad) and explain that past maintenance is not a debt unless fixed by a judge or agreement.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is a gift, not a debt — it binds only after court order or agreement.

The author of al-Hidayah establishes a fundamental Hanafi principle: maintenance (nafaqah) is a silah (gift), not an iwad (consideration). This classification determines when past maintenance becomes a binding debt on the husband. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying nafaqah law correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi classification of nafaqah
The passage states explicitly that according to us (Hanafis), nafaqah is a gift (silah) and not a consideration (iwad). Mark this statement: 'إن النفقة صلة وليست بعوض عندنا'.
2
Understand the logical reasoning behind the classification
The author argues that mahr is the consideration for marriage. If nafaqah were also a consideration, there would be two considerations for one subject, which is impossible. Therefore nafaqah must be a substitute for detention (ihtibas) rather than a consideration for ownership.
This is a qiyas-type argument: asl = mahr as consideration; far' = nafaqah; illa = impossibility of two considerations.
3
Derive the ruling for past maintenance
Since nafaqah is a gift, it does not automatically become a debt in the past. It only becomes obligatory when fixed by a judge (qada) or agreed upon by the spouses (sulh). Without fixation, past maintenance is not owed.
Contrast with a debt like a loan which becomes binding from the start.
4
Compare briefly with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i treats nafaqah as a consideration for ownership, similar to the case of a slave-girl. Under his view, past maintenance is automatically owed as a debt from the moment of marriage. This leads to different outcomes.
The detailed comparison on the minor wife case will be covered in the next lesson.

Nafaqah for a Minor Wife: Hanafi vs Shafi'i Dispute
Presents the Hanafi ruling on nafaqah for a minor wife with the Shafi'i dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on nafaqah for a minor wife who cannot be sexually enjoyed, and explain the rational proof for it, contrasting with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
A minor wife gets no nafaqah because detention (ihtibas) is absent — the basis for nafaqah.

This passage applies the Hanafi principle that nafaqah is a gift for detention to the specific case of a minor wife who cannot be sexually enjoyed. The Hanafis deny her nafaqah because the detention that justifies maintenance is not fully realized. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, treating nafaqah as consideration for ownership. The author presents the Hanafi proof using the mahr argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specific case and Hanafi ruling
The passage begins: 'وَصَغِيرَةٍ لَا تُوطَأُ أَى لَا نَفَقَةَ لِلصَّغِيرَةِ' — a minor girl who cannot be sexually enjoyed has no maintenance. This is the Hanafi ruling.
2
Understand the Hanafi proof (dalil)
The Hanafi proof is that mahr is the consideration for marriage, and two considerations cannot coexist for one subject. Therefore, nafaqah cannot be a consideration; it is a substitute for detention. Since the minor wife cannot be detained for sexual enjoyment, the basis for nafaqah is absent.
This is a continuation of the earlier logical argument.
3
Understand the Shafi'i proof and Hanafi response
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that nafaqah is a consideration for ownership, just as for a possessed slave-girl. The Hanafi response: 'For us, mahr is the consideration for ownership, and two considerations cannot come together for one subject. So she gets the mahr but not the nafaqah.'
The Hanafi response distinguishes between mahr as consideration and nafaqah as gift.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Hanafi law, the minor wife receives no nafaqah. Under Shafi'i law, she does receive nafaqah.
This shows the real-world impact of the classification.

Gifts Fall Away with Death: The Principle Applied to Nafaqah
Principle statement with qiyas argument and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that silat (gifts) fall away with death and apply it to the case of nafaqah (maintenance), identifying the qiyas to hibah.

Core Idea
Gifts are voided by death; unpaid nafaqah cannot be claimed after either spouse dies.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle: all gifts (silat) are automatically voided by the death of either party before execution. The passage from Al-Hidayah applies this principle to nafaqah (maintenance). Even if a judge fixed the amount, if the husband dies before paying, the wife cannot claim it from his estate because nafaqah is a gift, not a debt. We will dissect the reasoning and the qiyas to hibah.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the rule: 'Al-silat tasqutu bi al-mawt' – all gifts without compensation fall away with death. Understand what qualifies as silah: any gratuitous transfer of ownership.
Silat includes hibah, sadaqah, atiyyah, and nafaqah (in Hanafi view).
2
Identify the original case (asl)
The asl is hibah: if a donor gives a gift but dies before the donee takes possession, the gift is void. This is because possession (qabd) is required for completion.
Ijma establishes that hibah is not binding until qabd.
3
Apply qiyas to nafaqah (far')
Nafaqah is classified as a silah (gift) under Hanafi law. Therefore, by analogy to hibah, if either spouse dies before the nafaqah is paid, the obligation falls away.
The illah (effective cause) is that both are silat – gratuitous transfers that do not become binding debts until executed.
4
Consider the exception: judicial decree or agreement
The passage notes that if a judge fixed nafaqah or the husband agreed to a sum, the nafaqah becomes a debt while both are alive. However, even that debt is voided by death because it remains a silah at its core.
This guards against the misunderstanding that fixation makes it a permanent debt surviving death.
5
State the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that all past unpaid nafaqah – even if decreed – falls away with the death of either spouse. This is from zahir al-riwayah.
Some other schools treat fixed nafaqah as a debt that enters the estate.

Property of an Absent Person Cannot Be Sold
States the agreed principle and applies it to the case of spousal maintenance from an absentee's property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the property of an absent person cannot be sold, and explain the two different proofs given by Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An absent person's property cannot be sold without his presence — the ruling is unanimous, the proof differs.

This lesson examines a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh: the inviolability of an absentee's property. While the ruling itself is agreed upon, the underlying reasoning reveals a subtle difference in legal methodology between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples. We'll see how this principle applies to the case of a wife seeking maintenance from her absent husband's estate.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the agreed ruling: the property of an absent person cannot be sold in his absence.
The term 'ittifaq' indicates unanimity among the three Hanafi imams.
2
Identify the context
The passage applies this principle to spousal maintenance: if a husband is absent and his property is held by a third party, the qadi can allocate maintenance only from property of the same type as the wife's right (e.g., cash, food). If it is of a different type, sale would be required, which is prohibited.
The focus is on the type of property relative to the wife's right.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues by qiyas (analogy): if it is not permissible to sell the property of a present person without his permission, then it is even more impermissible to sell the property of an absent person.
This is an a fortiori (qiyas al-awla) argument.
4
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue from a procedural perspective: a judge can pass judgment against a present person because he can verify his defense, but he cannot judge against an absent person because his defense cannot be heard.
This proof focuses on the impossibility of adjudication without the absentee's presence.

Taking Payment for Obligatory Acts and Nafaqah for Absent Persons
Ruling with proof on payment for wajib acts and nafaqah for absent persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that taking payment for an obligatory act is invalid and apply it to determine which relatives can receive maintenance from the property of an absent person.

Core Idea
An obligatory act cannot be compensated with payment; only pre-existing obligations like nafaqah for wife/children justify ex parte orders.

This lesson examines a fundamental Hanafi principle: that taking payment for an act already required by Shari'ah is impermissible. This principle governs both personal obligations and judicial rulings on maintenance (nafaqah) for absent persons. Through concrete examples, including the hiring of a wife to breastfeed, you will learn how the school distinguishes between obligations fixed by nass and those requiring a court order, and why this distinction matters in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle and its rationale
Identify the legal maxim: taking payment for a wajib/fard act is haram because the act is already due from the person; accepting payment would constitute taking wealth without valid exchange (gharar) and corrupts the intention (niyyah).
This principle is not universally accepted outside the Hanafi school; it is based on the idea that obligations are due to Allah alone.
2
Identify the exception for nafaqah of wife, children, and parents
Note that maintenance for these three categories is wajib even before any court order (qada'). Therefore, a judge can order it from an absent person's property because the ruling merely assists in collecting a pre-existing debt, not initiating a new obligation.
This exception is derived from the Quran and Sunnah; the obligation is established by nass.
3
Explain why non-lineal relatives cannot claim from absent person's property
For other relatives (siblings, uncles, etc.), maintenance is only wajib after a court order (qada'). Since qada' ala al-gha'ib (ruling against an absent person) is invalid in Hanafi law, no order can be made. Thus, they cannot take anything without the person's consent.
The difference hinges on the timing of obligation: pre-qada' vs. post-qada'.
4
Analyze the breastfeeding example as a concrete application
If a husband hires his wife (who is not divorced) to breastfeed their child, the contract is invalid and she is not entitled to any fee. This is because breastfeeding is a religious duty (diyanatan) on the mother based on Quran 2:233, and one cannot take payment for a wajib act. However, she is not compelled to breastfeed if she is unable, as compulsion requires certainty of ability.
The verse is a command in khabar form, which in Hanafi usul indicates obligation (wujub).
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Shafi'is hold that maintenance for all mahram relatives is obligatory on general grounds and can be adjudicated even in absence. The Hanafi response is that only those obligations fixed by nass are pre-existing; others require a judicial determination, which cannot happen if the defendant is absent.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that a Hanafi judge will not order maintenance for a sibling from an absent person's property, while a Shafi'i judge may do so.

Commentary

The General Principle of Nafaqah: From One's Own Wealth
Explaining that a person's maintenance is from his own wealth, not from others, even if he is minor or adult

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding nafaqah and apply it to determine the obligation of maintenance when a minor owns property.

Core Idea
Your maintenance comes from your own wealth first; others owe you only if you have none.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone maxim in Hanafi fiqh: every person's maintenance is taken from his own property, irrespective of age. While many assume a father is always obliged to support his minor child, the law first looks to the child's own resources. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly assigning financial responsibility in family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
Locate the key statement in the Arabic text: 'إن نفقة الإنسان في مال نفسه صغيرًا كان أو كبيرًا'. This is the core principle. The translation: 'The maintenance of a person is from his own wealth, whether he is minor or adult.'
Memorize this phrase – it is a foundational rule in nafaqah (family support).
2
Understand the scope of the maxim
The maxim applies to every person, regardless of age. 'Minor' and 'adult' are both included. It means that if a person possesses wealth, his own wealth must be used to cover his expenses before anyone else becomes liable.
It does not exempt the father from all obligations – only when the child has no wealth.
3
Apply to the case of a minor child
The author of al-Hidayah explains that a father is obligated to provide nafaqah for his minor children, but this obligation is conditional: it exists only if the child does not own property. If the child has inherited or received property as a gift, that property is used first.
This is a direct application of the maxim: the child's own wealth takes priority.
4
Contrast with a common misconception
Many people think the father must always support his minor child simply because the child is young. The maxim corrects that: age does not shift financial liability; only lack of wealth does.
If the child has wealth, the father's obligation becomes subsidiary (ma'dhum).

Hiring a Wife to Breastfeed Her Child is Invalid

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a wife to breastfeed her child and explain why the contract is invalid based on the Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
A wife cannot be paid for breastfeeding her own child because it is already obligatory upon her.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah, the author rules on a specific contractual scenario: a husband hiring his wife to breastfeed their child. At first glance, one might think such a contract is permissible since it involves services. However, the Hanafi school declares it invalid due to the prior obligation of breastfeeding. Let's examine the reasoning and the Quranic proof.

What exactly is being ruled upon? It is the contract of hiring (isti'jar) a wife (or woman in revocable iddah) to breastfeed her child. The ruling is that the contract is invalid (batil) and she does not deserve the wage.
The ruling applies specifically when the woman is the biological mother and still in the marriage/revocable iddah.

What proof does the author cite? The Quranic verse: 'And mothers shall suckle their children for two whole years' (2:233). This is the primary textual evidence.

How does this verse prove the ruling? The verse is in the form of an indicative statement (khabar) but is interpreted as a command (amr) because the intent is to obligate breastfeeding. In Hanafi usul, a command expressed as a khabar is even more emphatic. Thus, breastfeeding is wajib diyaanatan.
The term 'أمر بصيغة الخبر' – a command in the form of a news report – is a key usul concept.
4
Apply the general rule
The author states a general principle: hiring a person for an act that is already obligatory upon them is not permissible. Since breastfeeding is wajib on the mother, she cannot receive compensation for it.
This principle applies to any obligatory act, not just breastfeeding.
5
Understand the nuance of diyaanatan vs. qada'an
The mother is not compelled (jabr) to breastfeed because of the possibility of her inability (e.g., illness). So if she refuses, she is excused legally. But if she does it voluntarily, her ability is confirmed, and she cannot claim wages because the act was obligatory on her.
This explains why the hire is invalid even though she is not forced to breastfeed.

Core Idea
Maintenance comes from your own wealth first; only when you are needy does it shift to another.

This passage from Al-Hidayah lays down a foundational principle of Islamic family law: every person's maintenance (nafaqah) is primarily from their own property. This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi principle governing nafaqah obligations. The maxim 'One able to earn is wealthy by his earning' shifts the focus from actual wealth to earning capacity. We will explore how this maxim is applied to determine when a mahram relative is entitled to financial support.

Liability is Proportionate to Benefit: The Maxim Al-Ghurm Bil-Ghunm in Nafaqah
Maxim applied to nafaqah amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-ghurm bil-ghunm' to determine the amount of nafaqah owed to a mahram relative based on inheritance share.

Core Idea
Liability for nafaqah mirrors the inheritance share — you owe maintenance in proportion to what you stand to inherit.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling that a mahram relative's nafaqah is calculated according to his inheritance share. The author of Al-Hidayah uses the well-known maxim 'al-ghurm bil-ghunm' (liability is proportionate to benefit) together with the Quranic verse 'Wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' to establish this proportional obligation. Understanding this link helps avoid the common mistake of thinking nafaqah is a fixed or need-based amount only.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its literal meaning
Begin by reading the maxim 'al-ghurm bil-ghunm'. 'Ghurm' means liability or compensation; 'ghunm' means profit or benefit. The maxim means that financial responsibility is proportional to the benefit received.
This is a general legal maxim, not limited to nafaqah.
2
Identify the ruling and its scope
The author applies the maxim specifically to nafaqah for mahram relatives (those within prohibited degrees). The ruling: nafaqah is wajib in the same proportion as the relative's inheritance share from the person being maintained.
The obligation of nafaqah itself is established elsewhere; this passage determines its amount.
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
Two proofs are combined: (1) the Quranic verse 'Wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (And upon the heir is like that) which implies a similar obligation, and (2) the maxim that links liability to benefit.
Wajh al-dalalah: 'mithlu dhalik' indicates equality in proportion; the maxim provides the underlying rationale.
4
Apply the maxim to a concrete example
Suppose a brother's inheritance share is one-third of the deceased's estate. According to the maxim, his nafaqah obligation toward the deceased's dependent (e.g., mother) is also one-third of the total maintenance required. The remaining two-thirds are borne by other heirs proportionally.
This ensures fairness: those who gain more from inheritance bear more of the maintenance burden.

The Capable Earner is Wealthy: Nafaqah and the Hanafi Maxim
States a legal maxim about capability and nafaqah obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-qadir ala al-kasb ghani bi-kasbihi' and apply it to determine the obligation of nafaqah for a capable relative.

Core Idea
A capable earner is legally wealthy, so nafaqah is not due on him.

In Hanafi fiqh, the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) on relatives hinges on the recipient's need and inability to earn. This lesson introduces a pivotal maxim from Al-Hidayah that defines wealth not by actual possessions but by the capacity to earn. Understanding this maxim is essential for determining who qualifies for support and who does not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
Read the Arabic/Urdu statement: 'إن القادر على الكسب غني بكسبه'. Understand its literal meaning: the one capable of earning is wealthy by his earning.
This is a legal presumption, not a factual statement about assets.
2
Define 'Capability of Earning'
Clarify the conditions: the person must be healthy, have the tools/means of earning, and possess ability/capacity. The text mentions health, tools of earning, and capacity.
The capability must be actual, not theoretical.
3
Apply to Nafaqah
Understand that this maxim is invoked in the context of nafaqah for dhi rahm mahram relatives. If the potential recipient is capable of earning, he is considered wealthy (ghani) and nafaqah is not obligatory on the relative.
This is a default rule; exceptions follow.
4
Recognize Exceptions
The obligation of nafaqah becomes wajib when the recipient is incapable: minor, poor (faqir), disabled (zamanah), blind, or any genuine excuse preventing earning.
These excuses prove the inability, thus triggering the obligation.

The Maxim of Compensation Commensurate with Gain
States a legal maxim linking compensation to proportion of benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' and apply it to determine the proportional nafaqah obligation of an heir based on inheritance share.

Core Idea
Compensation is proportional to benefit — the amount of nafaqah an heir owes matches their inheritance share.

In this lesson, we examine the classical Hanafi legal maxim 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' (compensation is commensurate with gain). This principle is not only for financial transactions but also governs family maintenance obligations. The author of Al-Hidayah uses it to derive the amount of nafaqah due from an heir, linking it directly to the inheritance share received.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its literal meaning
Write down the Arabic maxim 'الغرم بالغنم' and understand that it means compensation (tawān) is obligatory in proportion to the benefit received.
2
Identify the context of application
The author applies this maxim to nafaqah for a dhi rahm mahram (close relative who is a mahram). The gain (ghunm) here is the inheritance share, and the compensation (ghurm) is the nafaqah obligation.
3
Apply the maxim to determine proportion
If the inheritance share is one-third, then the nafaqah obligation is also one-third of the total required maintenance. This is derived from the maxim.
4
Cite the Quranic evidence
The Quranic verse 'وَعَلَى الْوَارِثِ مِثْلُ ذَلِكَ' (and upon the heir is the like of that) is used as the primary dalil, indicating that nafaqah is equivalent to the inheritance share.
5
Derive the ruling and note its binding force
Based on the maxim and the verse, the ruling is that an heir's nafaqah obligation is wajib proportional to the inheritance. This is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi.
Some schools may not tie nafaqah to inheritance, but this is the relied-upon Hanafi position.
