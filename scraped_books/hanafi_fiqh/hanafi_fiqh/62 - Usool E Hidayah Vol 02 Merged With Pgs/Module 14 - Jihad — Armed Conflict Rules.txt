The Principle of Non-Interference: Slave Ownership and Marital Authority Do Not Affect Fixed Property Duties
States a Hanafi legal maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim regarding milk al-yamin and milk al-nikah in relation to furud al-a'yan and apply it to concrete fiqh scenarios.

Core Idea
Slave ownership and marriage do not create obligations on the owner/husband regarding the slave's/wife's specific property duties.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain types of ownership and authority have limited scope. This maxim establishes that owning a slave or being a husband does not make one liable for the fixed property duties (like zakat) on the slave's or wife's property. Understanding this principle helps avoid incorrectly assigning obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two ownership types
Milk al-yamin (slave ownership) and milk al-nikah (marital authority) are specific legal relationships. Note that they confer rights over persons, not over their property.
2
Define furud al-a'yan
Furud al-a'yan are obligations tied to specific property, such as zakat on livestock, crops, or business inventory. These duties are attached to the property itself, not to the person who owns the property.
3
Understand why they do not manifest
The maxim states that these two types of ownership do not 'manifest' in the realm of furud al-a'yan. This means the owner/husband is not responsible for paying zakat on the property of the slave or wife, even though he has authority over them.
The underlying principle is that the owner/husband does not own the slave's or wife's property; the property remains separate.

General Harm is Warded Off Even if It Involves Specific Harm
States a legal maxim on public benefit over individual harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-darar al-'amm yudfa'...' and apply it to a fiqh scenario involving public welfare and individual harm.

Core Idea
Public interest prevails over individual harm — a core principle of collective fiqh.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim: general harm must be repelled even if it inflicts specific harm on individuals. This principle governs situations where a collective benefit or prevention of widespread harm requires actions that may hurt some. It is frequently applied in jihad (military strategy) and public law, such as imposing a quarantine to stop an epidemic. Understanding this maxim helps you balance individual rights against public welfare in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Maxim
Read and commit to memory the Arabic text: الضَّرَرُ الْعَامُ يُدْفَعُ وَإِنْ كَانَ فِيْهِ ضَرَرٌ خَاصٌ (note: the source has الغام but the standard is العام).
The maxim is usually quoted with 'al-''amm' not 'al-ghamm'.
2
Break Down the Key Terms
Identify 'al-darar al-'amm' (general harm) vs 'darar khas' (specific harm). Understand that 'yudfa'' means 'is repelled/warded off'.
The contrast is between public and private harm.
3
Understand the Legal Rule
The maxim establishes a priority rule: preventing widespread harm takes precedence over avoiding harm to specific individuals.
This is not an absolute license; the specific harm must be outweighed by the public benefit.
4
Apply to a Fiqh Example
Consider a scenario from jihad: the army must send a scout on a deadly mission to warn of an ambush. The scout faces certain death (specific harm) but the army is saved (general benefit). The maxim justifies the sacrifice.
Other examples: quarantine during plague, demolishing a property to stop a fire.
5
Recognize the Boundary
The specific harm must be proportional and necessary. If a less harmful alternative exists, it must be used. The maxim does not permit arbitrary harm.
This relates to the principle of 'al-darar yuzal' (harm must be removed).

The Spear as the Sole Permissive Cause for Killing in Jihad
Inter-madhhab dispute on the permissive cause for killing in jihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on what permits killing in jihad and explain the Shafi'i counterposition with the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Killing in jihad is only permissible through direct weapon use, not by indirect means.

This brief ruling from the Hidayah states a core Hanafi principle in jihad: the permissive cause (mubih) for killing an enemy combatant is the use of a weapon like a spear in direct confrontation. The Shafi'i school differs, allowing killing by other means such as traps or poison. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how each school derives rulings from the same sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the exact legal question? The passage defines the 'mubih' (what makes killing permissible) in jihad.
The issue is: what act renders killing of an enemy combatant lawful?
2
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa holds that only direct weapon use (e.g., spear, sword) makes killing permissible.
The term 'al-hirab' is generic for weapon but in context means direct combat tools.
3
Identify the opposing school and its view
Imam al-Shafi'i permits killing by any means, including traps, poison, or indirect methods.
The passage explicitly mentions 'khilafan lil-Shafi'i'.
4
Analyze the basis of disagreement
The Hanafi school requires a direct act of combat (qital) as the basis for killing, while Shafi'i focuses on the intent of jihad itself.
This reflects different understandings of the Quranic verse 'قاتلوا المشركين' (Fight the polytheists).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If an enemy is killed by a trap or poison, the Hanafi considers it unlawful (haram) with possible blood money; Shafi'i considers it permissible.
This affects liability for retaliation and distribution of booty.

States that ownership of booty is conditional on ihraz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that ownership of spoils is conditional upon ihraz and identify the legal consequence of failure to complete ihraz.

Core Idea
No ownership of booty until it reaches Islamic soil.

This short but foundational passage from the Hidayah states a core Hanafi principle regarding the spoils of war (ghanima). The school holds that the combatants (ghanimun) do not acquire full legal ownership of movable booty until it is safely brought into Islamic territory (Dar al-Islam). This condition – called ihraz – is crucial for the validity of the distribution. Understanding this helps clarify the precise moment ownership transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is taking possession of spoils. The ruling is that ownership is not established before ihraz.
2
Locate the condition for validity
The condition is ihraz – securing the booty by bringing it into Dar al-Islam. Without it, ownership remains with the enemy.
Ihraz is a shart (condition) for ownership.
3
Determine the consequence of missing condition
If ihraz is not performed, the spoils are still considered enemy property; the ghanim does not own them and bears no liability if lost before ihraz.
After ihraz, full ownership is established.
4
Apply the rule to a simple scenario
A soldier captures a sword in enemy territory. He keeps it but loses it before reaching Islamic land. According to the Hanafi school, he does not owe compensation because ownership had not transferred to him.
Contrast with after ihraz where he would be liable.

Defines spoils by method of acquisition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ghanima and distinguish it from unlawful seizure based on the Hanafi criterion of overpowering force.

Core Idea
Ghanima is only what is taken by open force, not by stealth or theft.

This lesson examines the precise meaning of ghanima (spoils of war) in Hanafi fiqh. The defining characteristic is that the property must be taken through overpowering force (qahr) and overcoming (ghalaba) in a legitimate military context. If a soldier obtains enemy property covertly—by stealth or theft—it does not qualify as ghanima and may be subject to the rulings of theft or looting. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of spoils distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core term and its ruling
The term is 'ghanima'. The passage defines it as a specific category of property acquisition. Note that it is a definition, not a command, so the ruling is that such property is lawful spoils.
Ghanima is a subset of lawfully acquired war booty.
2

Break down the definition: 'taken by force and overcoming' (qahran wa ghalabatan) are the positive elements. 'Not by stealth and theft' (la ikhtilasan wa sariqatan) are the negative exclusions.
The Arabic uses 'ma' (that which) to introduce the definition, and 'la' to negate the excluded methods.
3
Understand the exclusionary conditions
Identify what is excluded: stealth (ikhtilas) and theft (sariqa). These are methods that lack the element of open combat. The Hanafi school emphasizes that ghanima must be acquired in the context of active fighting, not by cunning or surprise.
This exclusion prevents looting or theft from being treated as legitimate spoils.

Condition for Kharaj: Inhabitants Must Remain Non-Muslim
States condition for imposing kharaj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for imposing kharaj and distinguish it from 'ushr based on the inhabitants' religious status.

Core Idea
Kharaj requires the people to remain non-Muslim; their conversion ends kharaj and triggers 'ushr.

This brief maxim from Hanafi fiqh of jihad lays down a critical condition for the land tax (kharaj) on conquered territories. Understanding that kharaj is conditional on the inhabitants' non-Muslim status distinguishes it from 'ushr, which applies to Muslim-owned land. This principle shapes Hanafi rulings on taxation and land ownership after conquest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act being discussed is the imposition of kharaj (land tax) on conquered lands. Kharaj is a tax levied on the land itself, not on the produce, and is distinct from 'ushr (tithe).
Kharaj is a fixed annual tax per land unit; 'ushr is 10% of produce.
2
Identify the condition
The maxim states that the condition (shart) for imposing kharaj is that the inhabitants are allowed to remain on their unbelief (kufr). They must not be forced to convert to Islam.
The Arabic phrase 'yuqirru ahluhā 'alā l-kufr' means 'they are confirmed on unbelief'.
3
Understand the implication
Since kharaj is tied to the inhabitants' non-Muslim status, if the inhabitants all convert to Islam, the condition disappears. The land then becomes 'ushr land, and the tax changes from kharaj to 'ushr.
This is a key distinction from jizya, which is a head tax on non-Muslims that ends upon conversion.
4
Distinguish from jizya
Kharaj is on land; jizya is on the person. Both are imposed on non-Muslims, but kharaj is conditional on the inhabitants' continued non-Muslim status, while jizya is a personal obligation that lapses upon conversion.
If inhabitants convert, kharaj ceases, but jizya also ceases (though jizya is not conditional on remaining non-Muslim per se; it is only for non-Muslims).
5
Recognize legal consequence
If the condition is not met (e.g., inhabitants are forced to convert), kharaj cannot be imposed. Attempting to impose kharaj under such circumstances would be invalid, and the land must be classified as 'ushr land from the start.
This ruling aligns with Hanafi emphasis on intention and status at the time of imposition.

When Individual Obligation Overrides Marital and Master Rights
Applies the principle to defensive jihad: wife and slave can go without permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that fard 'ayn overrides the rights of husband and master, and apply it to defensive jihad by identifying the qiyas to salah and siyam.

Core Idea
The rights of master and husband are overridden when an obligation becomes fard 'ayn.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle from al-Hidayah: the rights of a husband over his wife and a master over his slave do not take effect when an obligation becomes fard 'ayn (individual binding duty). The ruling is most vividly applied to defensive jihad: when enemies suddenly attack a Muslim city, every capable person – including women and slaves – must go out to defend, even without permission. The principle is derived by qiyas (analogy) from salah and fasting, which are also fard 'ayn and require no external consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is defensive jihad (sudden enemy attack). The ruling: it becomes fard 'ayn (individual obligation) upon every capable Muslim, including women and slaves. The hukm level is fard 'ayn (or wajib according to Hanafi terminology here).
Al-Hidayah uses 'wajib' interchangeably with fard 'ayn in this context.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to salah and siyam (fasting). Both are fard 'ayn and do not require permission from a husband or master. Since defensive jihad shares the attribute of being fard 'ayn, it likewise overrides those rights.
The text does not quote a Quranic verse or hadith directly; it relies on analogical reasoning.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The common element (illa) is 'being an individual obligation (fard 'ayn)'. The ruling in the original cases (salah and siyam) is that permission is not needed. The same illa applies to defensive jihad, so the same ruling follows.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) with a shared illa.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools may differ on when women are obligated to participate actively in armed combat. Some hold that women need husband's permission even in defense. The Hanafi position here is definitive for fard 'ayn situations.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih (disputed).
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that permission is not required. The authority is from Zahir al-Riwayah (al-Hidayah). The principle is supported by the overriding nature of fard 'ayn over subordinate rights.
This ruling is relied upon for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
General harm is repelled even at the cost of specific harm.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi legal maxim: general harm must be repelled even if doing so causes specific harm. The maxim is placed in the context of jihad, where defending the community from enemy attack (general harm) may require actions that risk injury to individual Muslims (specific harm). The ruling clarifies that such collateral harm is tolerated because preventing the greater evil takes precedence.

The commentary explains that specific harm is tolerated to eliminate general harm. This is a principle of priority: the greater (general) harm outweighs the lesser (specific) harm.

The author of al-Hidayah applies this maxim to the permissibility of shooting arrows at the enemy during battle, even if some Muslims may be accidentally harmed. There is 'no objection' (lā muḍāʾiqah) to such actions.

4

Permissibility of Bombarding Fortresses with Muslim Prisoners
Ruling with proof on bombarding while Muslims are human shields.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bombarding enemy fortresses when Muslims are present as human shields and explain the principle that no diyah or kaffarah is due.

Core Idea
General harm outweighs specific harm; compensations do not attach to obligations.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a difficult scenario in jihad: when the enemy uses Muslim prisoners or civilians as shields, is it permissible to bombard the fortress? The Hanafi school rules that it is permissible, and if Muslims are inadvertently killed, no financial penalty (diyah) or expiation (kaffarah) is required. This ruling is built on two key foundations: the priority of repelling general harm (ḍarar ʿāmm) over specific harm (ḍarar khāṣṣ), and the principle that compensations (gharamāt) are not attached to obligations (furūḍ). Let's walk through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1

2

3
Analyze the first dalil: repelling general harm
Understand the argument: stopping the fortress removes the general threat of disbelief (ḍarar ʿāmm). Killing a Muslim is a specific harm (ḍarar khāṣṣ). General harm takes priority.
This is based on the principle that necessity overrides individual rights.
4
Analyze the second dalil: the principle of gharamāt
Jihad is a farḍ (obligation). The act of bombarding is part of fulfilling this farḍ. The rule 'al-gharamāt lā taqtarin bi al-furūḍ' means compensation is not linked to commanded acts.
The distinction between ma'mūr bihi (commanded) and manhī ʿanhu (prohibited) is key.
5

Even if a Muslim dies, the mujāhid is not liable because the act was part of an obligatory struggle. Intention matters: they target the disbelievers, not the Muslims.
Intent is possible even if discrimination in action is difficult.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof (hukm with dalil) that details which non-combatant disbelievers are prohibited from being killed in jihad. The Hanafi position is that the cause (mubīḥ) for killing is ḥirāb (actual fighting), so those who cannot or do not fight—women, children, the insane, the blind, the decrepit old, the paralyzed, the amputee, monks, and reclusive hermits—are not permissible to kill. The exception is if they have a role in war (e.g., providing strategic advice or leadership). The proof is the authentic hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) expressly forbade killing women and children, and his remark upon seeing a slain woman: 'She was not fighting, so why was she killed?' This demonstrates that the operative cause is fighting, not mere disbelief. The Shafi'i position is contrasted: they hold that kufr is the cause, so all disbelievers are permissible to kill unless there is a specific exemption (women/children by this hadith). The thamarah al-khilaf is that Hanafis extend the exemption to more categories (monks, blind, etc.) based on the principle that those incapable of fighting are not targeted. The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah. The mufta bihi position is that non-combatants are not killed unless they contribute to the war effort.

غير مكلف (ghayr mukallaf): A person not legally responsible (e.g., children, insane) – thus not a combatant.
صاحب رأي (ṣāḥib ra'y): A person whose opinion (strategic advice) is sought in warfare; such a person becomes a legitimate target even if not physically fighting.
شيخ فاني (shaykh fānī): A very old, decrepit man who cannot fight.

Timing of Ownership in War Booty: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Hanafi vs Shafi'i on when ownership of ghanimah is established

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the timing of ownership of war booty and explain how it differs from the Shafi'i view, and identify the practical consequences of this difference in at least four subsidiary issues.

Core Idea
Ownership of ghanimah is not established until transfer to Dar al-Islam; division before ihtiyaz is invalid.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding when ownership of war booty (ghanimah) is established. The difference is not merely theoretical; it affects practical matters such as inheritance, sale, liability, and family law. Understanding this principle will help you see how a single legal distinction can generate a cascade of different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Recognize that the Hanafi school prohibits division of booty in Dar al-Harb, while the Shafi'i school permits it. The core difference is the condition of ihtiyaz (transfer to Dar al-Islam).
2
Understand the basis
Know that the Hanafi condition is ihtiyaz (transfer to Dar al-Islam), while the Shafi'i condition is the establishment of defeat. This stems from different views on when ownership vests.
3
Analyze the subsidiary issues
Work through the six issues listed in the text to see how the core principle applies: reinforcements joining, intercourse with captives, sale, death, destruction of booty, and arbitrary division by the Imam.
4
Evaluate the practical differences
For each issue, note the different rulings and their impact. For example, in the Hanafi school, a warrior's share is not inherited if he dies before ihtiyaz; in the Shafi'i school it is.
5
Assess the tarjih
Recognize that the Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school, as presented by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the timing of ownership of war booty (ghanimah). The Hanafi position is that ownership of booty is not established until the booty is transferred (ihtiyaz) to Dar al-Islam. Therefore, dividing booty in Dar al-Harb is prohibited because ownership has not yet been established. The Shafi'i school holds that ownership is established immediately upon capture, even before transfer, so division in Dar al-Harb is valid. This fundamental difference gives rise to six specific subsidiary issues (masa'il) listed by the author: (1) If reinforcements join the army before the booty is brought to Dar al-Islam, do they share? Hanafi says yes (since ownership not yet fixed, all fighters share equally), Shafi'i says no (ownership fixed for original fighters). (2) If a warrior has intercourse with a captive slave girl and she bears a child, is paternity established and does she become umm walad? Shafi'i says yes (ownership established), Hanafi says no (no ownership yet), and the 'uqur (compensation for intercourse) and the slave girl and child are divided among all. (3) Is it permissible to sell booty before transfer? Shafi'i says yes, Hanafi says no. (4) If a warrior dies before transfer, is his share inherited? Shafi'i says yes, Hanafi says no. (5) If a warrior destroys booty, is he liable? Hanafi says no (since not his property), Shafi'i says yes. (6) If the Imam divides the booty arbitrarily without need, is it valid? Hanafi says no (since ownership not established), Shafi'i says yes. The passage ends by citing Tabyin al-Haqa'iq and al-Hidayah.

الاحراز (ihtiyaz): Transferring war booty to a safe place (Dar al-Islam) after capture; the act that establishes ownership according to the Hanafi school.
العقر (al-'uqur): Compensation paid for unlawful intercourse with a slave woman; in this context, it is divided among the warriors.

The Criterion for Cavalry Share: Crossing the Border vs. Actual Fighting
Hanafi vs Shafi'i on condition for cavalry entitlement in booty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds the state of crossing (mujawaza) as the decisive criterion for cavalry booty entitlement and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the moment of crossing into enemy territory determines booty shares, not the actual fighting.

This passage presents a classic difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the distribution of war booty. The key question: should a soldier's share depend on his state when he first crosses the border into enemy land, or on his state during the actual battle? Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi emphasis on intention and the moment of engagement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
What exactly is in dispute? The ruling concerns the condition for a soldier to receive a cavalry share (two shares) of booty: does it depend on the state upon crossing into Dar al-Harb or on the state during actual combat?
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi school holds that the decisive moment is the state upon crossing (mujawaza). The proof: fear is inflicted on the enemy from the moment of crossing, and the soldier's intention upon crossing determines his entitlement. The horse's death later does not change this.
3
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i considers the state during actual combat (harb) because that is the cause (sabab) of booty. If the soldier fights on foot at the time of battle, he gets a foot soldier's share regardless of how he entered.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that crossing (mujawaza) is a type of fighting that already causes fear. The initial intention is the basis for entitlement. Continuation (of the horse) is not required.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: If a soldier enters on horseback but his horse dies before combat, Hanafis give him a cavalry share; Shafi'is give him a foot soldier's share. If he enters on foot and later acquires a horse, Hanafis give foot soldier share; Shafi'is give cavalry share.

Basis for Cavalry Shares: Entry vs Fighting
Dispute on basis for shares: entry vs fighting state

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on determining spoils shares, explain the Hanafi proof based on mujawazah, and distinguish the Shafi'i counter-position.

Core Idea
Spoils shares hinge on the fighter's state when crossing the border, not on later changes.

This passage presents a classic inter-madhab dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding the allocation of shares in spoils of war (ghaneemah). The central question is whether the fighter's share is determined by his status at the moment of entering enemy territory or by his status at the time of battle. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to identify how different legal schools derive rulings from the same principles and evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Issue
State the precise legal question: what is the basis (sabab) for determining whether a fighter gets a horseman's share (two shares) or a foot soldier's share (one share)?
The cause is either mujawazah (crossing) per Hanafis or qital (fighting) per Shafi'is.
2
State Each School's Position
Clearly articulate the Hanafi position (state at crossing) and the Shafi'i position (state at fighting).
Hanafis look at the initial entry; Shafi'is look at the actual combat.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Dalil
Understand the Hanafi proof: crossing the border instills fear in the enemy, which is the real cause of victory. Continued presence is a mere extension (dawam), which has no independent legal effect.
This is a rational argument (talil) based on the purpose of jihad.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i Dalil and Hanafi Response
Shafi'is argue that fighting is the direct cause of victory. Hanafis respond that the initial entry is the cause, and later changes (e.g., horse dying) do not alter the established right.
The Hanafi response emphasizes the principle that existence (wujud) at the time of the cause is what counts.
5
Apply the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: if a horseman enters but his horse dies before battle, Hanafis give him a horseman's share; Shafi'is give him a foot soldier's share. Vice versa for a foot soldier who later acquires a horse.
This illustrates how the dispute affects actual distribution of spoils.

Core Idea
Penalties overlap: when multiple penalties combine, one execution suffices.

This lesson examines a concise yet powerful legal principle from al-Hidayah: when multiple penalties converge, they overlap (tadakhul). The passage states this principle and applies it to jizyah, the poll tax on non-Muslims. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly determining obligations and penalties in Hanafi fiqh.

The principle applies to all penalties in Hanafi fiqh, including financial penalties and corporal punishments, as long as they are of the same nature. Overlap does not necessarily occur across different categories (e.g., jizyah and hadd).
The text says 'penalties' generally.

A common mistake is to think that unpaid jizyah accumulates like a debt. The principle clarifies that it is a penalty, not a debt, so it overlaps.
Distinguish penalty ('uquba) from financial obligation (dayn).

جزیہ لیا جائے گا، کیونکہ جزیہ لینا یہ ان کے کفر پر اڑے رہنے کی سزا ہے اور سزاؤں کا حکم یہ ہے کہ اگر یہ جمع ہو جائیں تو تداخل ہوتا ہے، جیسے حدود کا معاملہ ہے کہ اگر ایک شخص نے کئی لوگوں پر تہمت لگائی اور اس پر کئی حد قذف واجب ہوئیں تو صرف ایک ہی مرتبہ اُسے حد لگائی جائے گی اور حدود میں تذاخل ہوگا ، اسی طرح جزیہ سزا ہے جب کئی جزیہ جمع ہو جائیں گے تو ان میں تداخل ہوگا۔ الْوَاجِبُ عَلَيْهِمُ بِطَرِيقِ الْعُقُوبَةِ وَالْعُقُوبَاتُ الَّتِى تَجِبُ لِحَقِّ اللَّهِ تَعَالَى إِذَا اجْتَمَعَتْ تَدَاخَلَتْ كَالْحُدُودِ وَفِي حَقِّنَا خَلَفْ عَنْ التَّصْرَةِ وَهَذَا الْمَعْنَى يَتِمُّ بِاسْتِيفَاءِ جِزْيَةٍ وَاحِدَةٍ مِنْهُ فَلا حَاجَةَ إِلَى اسْتِيفَاءِ مَا مَضَى وَلَانَّ الْمَقْصُودَ لَيْسَ هُوَ الْمَالُ بَلْ الْمَقْصُودُ اسْتِذْلَالُ الْكَافِرِ وَاسْتِصْغَارُهُ لِأَنَّ إصْرَارَهُ عَلَى الشِّرْكِ فِي دَارِ التَّوْحِيدِ جِنَايَةٌ فَلَا يَنْفَكُ عَنْ صَغَارٍ يَجْرِى عَلَيْهِ وَهَذَا الْمَقْصُودُ يَحْصُلُ بِاسْتِيفَاءِ جِزْيَةٍ وَاحِدَةٍ فَلَوْ أَخَذْنَاهُ بِالْمَوَانِيذِ لَمْ يَكُنْ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا لِمَقْصُودِ الْمَالِ وَقَدْ بَيَّنَّا أَنَّ الْمَالَ غَيْرُ مَقْصُودٍ وَلِهَذَا لَا يَبْقَى بَعْدَ مَوْتِهِ وَإِسْلَامِهِ.
Jizya will be taken because taking jizya is a punishment for their persistence in disbelief, and the ruling of punishments is that if they accumulate, they overlap (tadakhul), like the case of hudud: if a person accuses several people of slander and multiple hadd penalties for qadhf become obligatory, he is given only one hadd, and there is tadakhul. Similarly, jizya is a punishment; when multiple jizya obligations accumulate, they overlap. What is obligatory upon them by way of punishment, and punishments that are obligatory for the right of Allah the Exalted, when they accumulate, they overlap, like hudud. And concerning our case, it differs from [؟]. And this meaning is fulfilled by collecting one jizya from him, so there is no need to collect what is past. And indeed the purpose is not wealth; rather the purpose is to humiliate and belittle the disbeliever, because his persistence on shirk in the abode of tawhid is a crime, so he is not free from the disgrace that befalls him. And this purpose is achieved by collecting one jizya. If we were to take him [with?] penalties, that would be only for the purpose of wealth, and we have explained that wealth is not the purpose, and for this reason it does not remain after his death and his Islam.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining the principle of tadakhul (overlapping) of punishments and its application to jizya. The author states that jizya is a punishment (uquba) for the disbeliever's persistence in kufr. Punishments that are rights of Allah, such as hudud, overlap: if multiple hadd penalties are incurred, only one is carried out. The same applies to jizya—if multiple jizya obligations accumulate (e.g., for past years), only one is taken. The proof is qiyas on hudud: both are punishments for Allah's right. The 'illa (effective cause) is being a punishment (uquba). The author further explains that the purpose of jizya is not wealth but humiliation (istidhlal) of the disbeliever, which is achieved by taking one jizya. Taking multiple jizya would indicate a focus on wealth, which is not intended. This ruling is from the Hanafi school as found in al-Mabsut and al-Hidayah, thus from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
تداخل (tadakhul): The principle that when multiple punishments of the same type become obligatory, they overlap and only one is executed.
جزیہ (jizya): A tax taken from dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects) as a punishment for their disbelief and as a sign of humiliation.
