The Agent as Principal in Rights
States that an agent is considered the principal regarding rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding agency and apply it to determine who bears the rights and obligations in a contract of agency.

Core Idea
The agent is the source of rights in contracts, not the principal.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi agency law: the maxim 'al-wakil huwa al-asl fi al-huquq.' At first glance, it may seem counterintuitive that an agent, who acts on behalf of another, is treated as the principal with respect to rights. Understanding this maxim is crucial for determining contractual liabilities and who can sue or be sued in agency relationships.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Commit the Arabic phrase to memory: الْوَكِيلُ هُوَ الْأَصْلُ فِي الْحُقُوقِ.
2
Understand its literal meaning
Translate: 'The agent is the origin (principal) in rights.' The agent is treated as the direct source of rights and obligations from his actions.
3
Recognize its application
In contracts, rights arising from the agent's actions are against the agent himself. For example, if an agent buys goods, the seller's claim for payment is against the agent.
4
Distinguish from the principal's ultimate liability
The maxim addresses procedural rights, not ultimate ownership. The principal may still be liable indirectly, but the immediate rights are against the agent.
This is a nuance of Hanafi law; other schools may differ.

Ruling statement: This maxim indicates that when an agent (wakil) acts on behalf of a principal, he is considered as the direct source (asl) with respect to the rights and obligations that arise from his actions, especially in contractual rights. For example, if an agent buys something, the rights of the seller are against the agent (unless the agency is disclosed). This is a nuance in the law of agency (wakala).

States that profit-sharing is based on agreement, not investment ratio

Core Idea
Profit in a Hanafi partnership follows agreement, not capital ratios.

This lesson explains a foundational Hanafi principle in partnership law. Unlike some other schools, the Hanafi madhhab holds that the distribution of profit is based on what the partners agree in the contract, not solely on how much capital each contributed. This gives flexibility to partnerships and reflects the importance of mutual consent.

Agency to Take Mubah Property is Invalid
States the invalidity of agency for mubah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for mubah property and explain why it is invalid.

Core Idea
Appointing an agent to take ownerless property is invalid; the taker becomes the owner.

This lesson addresses a fundamental principle in Hanafi contract law: agency (wakala) requires that the principal actually needs representation. For mubah property—things not owned by anyone, like wild animals or water—anyone can acquire them by physical taking. Therefore, appointing an agent for such acquisition is legally meaningless. Understanding this helps avoid confusion in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the ruling from the source: The appointment of an agent to take mubah property is invalid (batil).
Memorize the Arabic phrase: التَّوْكِيلُ فِي أَخْذِ الْمَالِ الْمُبَاحِ بِاطِلٌ
2
Define mubah property
Mubah property is that which is not owned by anyone and is permissible to acquire, such as wild animals, fish, water, and firewood.
Ownership is acquired only through physical possession (ihraz).
3
Understand the reason for invalidity
Since anyone can take mubah directly, there is no need for agency. The 'agent' would be acquiring the property for himself, not for the principal, so the contract serves no purpose and is invalid.
If the principal later receives the property, it is through a separate gift or sale, not through the agency.

Agency Terminates upon Death
Ruling statement: agency voided by death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that an agency contract becomes void upon the death of either the principal or the agent.

Core Idea
Agency ends with death – personal trust cannot survive the person.

In Islamic law, contracts of agency are built on personal trust and confidence. This trust is inherently tied to the living parties. Therefore, when either the principal or the agent dies, the foundation of the contract dissolves. This lesson presents the clear ruling from the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is 'al-wakala' (agency). The ruling is that it becomes void (tabtulu) upon death.
Void means the contract has no legal effect from the moment of death.
2
Recognize the trigger condition
The trigger is death (al-mawt) of either party. No other condition is required.
Both the principal (muwakkil) and the agent (wakil) are included.
3
Understand the rationale
Agency is based on personal confidence, which ceases with the life of either party. The deceased's estate is managed by heirs or executors, not by the agent.
This is a general Hanafi principle: contracts based on personal confidence end with death.
4
Apply the ruling consistently
If the principal dies first, the agent's authority ends immediately. If the agent dies, the principal must appoint a new agent.
The ruling applies equally to both sides, maintaining symmetry.

The Agent is the Principal in Rights
Principle that the agent is primarily liable in partnership transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'the agent is the principal in rights' and apply it to determine liability in partnership transactions.

Core Idea
In Hanafi partnership, the buying partner alone is liable to the seller — agency does not imply suretyship.

This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces a foundational legal maxim governing liability in partnership. The maxim clarifies that when one partner transacts on behalf of the partnership, all rights and obligations flow to that partner alone, not to the other partners. Understanding this distinction between agency and suretyship is key to correctly assigning liability in Hanafi commercial law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
Read the Arabic maxim: 'الوكيل هو الأصل في الحقوق'. Understand its literal meaning: the agent is the principal (primary party) in rights.
The maxim is stated at the beginning of the passage.
2
Understand the Application in Partnership
The author applies the maxim to partnership: when one partner purchases for the partnership, he is the agent, so the seller demands the price only from him.
The partnership implies agency (wakalah), not suretyship (kafalah).
3
Distinguish Agency from Suretyship
If suretyship were implied, all partners would be jointly liable. The passage explicitly states that partnership includes agency only.
This is the key legal distinction that determines liability.
4
Derive the Practical Ruling
The practical ruling: the seller can claim the price only from the purchasing partner. That partner may later seek reimbursement from the other partner(s).
This avoids uncertainty (gharar) in transactions.

The Agent is the Principal in Legal Claims
States and explains the ruling that an agent is the primary party in rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the agent is the principal in rights and apply it to determine the direct liability of a buying partner in a partnership.

Core Idea
The agent is the primary party in rights; the seller claims only from the buying partner.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, the great Hanafi jurist al-Marghinani articulates a foundational principle of agency and partnership law. The rule 'al-wakil huwa al-asl fi al-huquq' determines who bears direct liability in transactions. Understanding this principle is essential to correctly allocate rights and obligations in partnerships and other agency relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
Read the statement 'الْوَكِيلُ هُوَ الْأَصْلُ فِى الْحُقُوقِ' and recognize it as a core principle of Hanafi fiqh.
This is a qawida (legal maxim) that governs agency.
2
Understand its meaning
The maxim means that in any transaction carried out by an agent, all rights and obligations arising from that transaction are primarily attached to the agent, not the principal.
The agent is the direct party vis-à-vis third parties.
3
Apply to partnership (shirkah)
In a partnership, each partner is an agent for the others in commercial transactions. If one partner buys goods, he is the agent for that purchase. Therefore, the seller demands payment from him, not from the other partners.
The buying partner then recovers from the non-buying partners their share.
4
Trace the practical consequence
This rule simplifies litigation: the third party only needs to sue the agent, not all partners. It also clarifies that the agent must be solvent.
The non-buying partners are not directly liable to the seller.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that profit in a partnership is attributed to the contract and evaluate the Shafi'i/Zufar counter-argument.

Core Idea
Profit follows the contract, not capital — Hanafi partnership law allows flexible profit-sharing.

This passage from al-Hidayah states a core Hanafi principle: profit in partnerships is based on mutual agreement, not capital ratios. This has significant practical consequences for structuring business ventures. We will examine this principle, its justification, and the opposing view of Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
State the ruling: profit attribution is based on the contract (musta'idatan ila al-'aqd), not capital ratio.
Partners can agree on any profit split regardless of capital.

Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Zufar hold that profit must be proportional to capital.

Their dalil: profit is a natural increase of capital, so it should accrue proportionally.

Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis argue that partnership can exist without capital (e.g., service partnerships), so profit must be tied to the contract and work/liability.
The contract is the source of entitlement.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Hanafis allow unequal profit shares even with equal capital and permit partnerships without capital mixing.
Shafi'is require proportionality and capital mixing.

Explanation: The meaning of this principle is that if the agent dies, the agency becomes void due to the agent's death, because a dead person does not have the capacity for agency, nor is there any benefit in the agency since the agent cannot perform any task. The author of Al-Hidayah mentions this principle to explain that if one of the two partners dies or apostatizes and goes to the abode of war (Dar al-Harb), and the judge rules on his going to Dar al-Harb, then the partnership becomes void, because partnership includes agency, and agency becomes void due to death. When the agency becomes void, that which includes agency also becomes void, so the partnership becomes void. (His statement: "And partnership in woodcutting, hunting, and grass-cutting is not permissible") because partnership includes the meaning of agency, and appointing an agent to take permissible (mubah) property is invalid because the matter for which the principal appoints the agent is not valid, and the agent owns what he takes without the principal's order, so he cannot serve as a deputy for him. And because each of them owns what he takes by taking it, so the other has no claim over it. Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, Book of Partnership, Partnership of Faces, vol. 1, p. 290, al-Matba'a al-Khayriyya. Al-Hidayah: Book of Partnership, Chapter on Invalid Partnership, vol. 2, p. 615, Rahmaniyya: (His statement: "And partnership becomes void by the death of one of them, even if legally") because it includes agency, and agency is necessary for the realization of partnership as previously mentioned, and agency becomes void by death, and legal death is joining the abode of war as an apostate when the judge rules so, because it is like death. Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: Book of Partnership, Chapter on Invalid Partnership, vol. 5, p. 199, Dar al-Kitab al-Islami.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal principle from the Hanafi school: agency terminates upon the death of the agent. The author then applies this principle to partnership (shirkah), because partnership inherently includes the concept of agency—each partner acts as an agent for the other. Therefore, if a partner dies (or is legally deemed dead, such as apostatizing and joining the abode of war with a judge's ruling), the partnership becomes void. The passage also addresses a related rule: partnership in collecting permissible (mubah) items like wood, grass, or hunting is not valid because such actions do not require agency; each person independently owns what they collect, so the mutual agency necessary for partnership is absent.

Key Terms
وکالت (wakālah): A contract by which a person appoints another to perform a lawful act on their behalf.
شرکت (shirkah): A partnership contract where two or more individuals contribute capital or labor for profit, which inherently includes mutual agency.
موتِ حکمی (mawt hukmī): Legal death, such as when a person apostatizes and joins the abode of war and the judge rules accordingly, making them like a dead person for legal purposes.
