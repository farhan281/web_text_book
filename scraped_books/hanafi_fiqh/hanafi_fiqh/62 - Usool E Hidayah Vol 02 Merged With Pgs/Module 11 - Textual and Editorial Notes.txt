The Scope of Metaphor (Majaz) in Hanafi Usul
Definition ruling on the scope of metaphorical usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a metaphorical usage as valid or invalid according to the Hanafi principle that majaz can only apply to meanings lower than the literal.

Core Idea
Metaphor can downgrade meaning but not upgrade it.

This short passage lays down a core principle of figurative language in Hanafi legal theory: a word used metaphorically can only refer to something lesser in degree than its literal meaning (haqiqah), never something higher. This rule is crucial when interpreting ambiguous terms in the Quran and Sunnah, ensuring that metaphorical readings do not elevate a concept beyond its original scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning (haqiqah)
Determine the real, primary meaning of the word as understood in the language. This is the benchmark against which the metaphorical usage is measured.
For example, the literal meaning of 'asad' (أسد) is a large feline predator.
2
Assess the metaphorical meaning
Compare the metaphorical meaning to the literal. Is it lower in value, status, or degree? If yes, the metaphor is valid. If equal or higher, it is invalid.
Calling a brave man 'asad' is valid because bravery is a lesser attribute than the full essence of a lion. Calling an angel 'asad' would be invalid.
3
Apply to legal context
When encountering a word that may be metaphorical in a Quranic verse or hadith, use this rule to decide whether the metaphorical reading is permissible. If the intended meaning is higher, the literal meaning must be retained.
This prevents stretching language to inflate legal obligations or concepts.

Commentary
A linguistic principle in usul: when a word is used figuratively (majaz), it can be applied to a concept that is lower in value or degree than the literal meaning, but not higher. This affects legal interpretation.

The Rule of Exception from Negation
Definition of the linguistic and legal rule of istithna' from nafi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that exception from negation (istithna' min al-nafy) is affirmation on the way of emphasis, and apply it to interpret legal texts containing negation followed by exception.

Core Idea
Exception from negation is emphatic affirmation of the excepted item.

In Arabic grammar and Hanafi usul, the construction of a negation followed by an exception (e.g., 'No one came except Zaid') has a specific legal effect: it affirms the excepted item emphatically. This principle is crucial for interpreting Qur'anic verses, hadith, and legal contracts. In this lesson, you will internalize this rule and learn to apply it accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
Recognize a sentence that contains a negation (like ما, لا, ليس) followed by an exception using إلّا.
Example: لا أحد قام إلا زيد (No one stood except Zaid).
2
State the rule
The rule: 'Exception from negation is affirmation on the way of emphasis' (الإستثناء من النفي إثبات على وجه التأكيد). That is, the excepted item is affirmed with strength.
This is a settled principle in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply to a simple example
Apply the rule: in 'لا أحد قام إلا زيد', the negation (no one stood) is qualified by the exception (except Zaid). The result is that Zaid definitely stood (affirmation).
The affirmation is emphatic—no doubt that Zaid stood.
4
Understand legal implications
In legal texts, this principle resolves apparent conflicts. For instance, if a ruling negates a general category and then excepts a subset, that subset is ruled positively.
This is used in interpreting exceptions in Quran, hadith, and contracts.

The exception from negation is an affirmation in the manner of emphasis.

Commentary
When a sentence is negated and then an exception is made, the result is an affirmation of the excepted item. For example, 'No one came except Zaid' means Zaid came. This is a principle of Arabic grammar and usul, used in interpreting legal texts. The exception affirms the excepted item strongly.

Metaphorical Usage Limited to Lesser Meaning
Principle of metaphorical usage limits extension to lesser meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of metaphorical usage and apply it to determine whether a metaphorical interpretation in a legal text is valid.

Core Idea
A word's metaphor can extend to lesser, not greater, meanings.

This passage introduces a key linguistic principle in Hanafi usul: when a word is used metaphorically in a legal context, its meaning can only be less than the literal scope, not more. This limits how far metaphorical interpretation can go. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly applying texts on manumission and beyond.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the Arabic maxim and its translation. The key is 'دُونَ حَقِيْقَتِهِ' (less than its literal) and 'فَوْقَهُ' (above it).
2
Understand the limit
A metaphor can only reduce the scope of a word, not increase it. So 'slave' can refer to a partially freed slave (lesser status) but not to a free person (greater).
3
Apply to legal examples
Think of other words like 'wine' (khamr): literal is fermented grape drink; metaphor for any intoxicant? That is expanding, not reducing, so not allowed per this rule.
Hanafi view on khamr is that it specifically means wine from grapes; other intoxicants are not called khamr by metaphor but by qiyas.

Languages Are Not Known by Analogy
A principle on how languages are known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-lughat la tudraku qiyasan' and apply it to determine the linguistic meaning of words in oaths and contracts.

Core Idea
Language meanings are conventional, not derivable by analogy.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh: that linguistic meanings are not subject to analogical reasoning. While qiyas is a valid source for legal rulings, it cannot be used to define what words mean. This has direct application in oaths and contracts, where the intended meaning must be determined by custom (urf) rather than logical extension.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Write down the Arabic text 'اللغات لا تدرك قياسًا' and its English translation 'Languages are not known by analogy.' This is the core principle.
Memorize this as a fundamental rule of Hanafi methodology.
2
Identify the ruling: convention-based, not analogy-based
Understand that the maxim declares that linguistic meanings are established by convention (wad'), not by logical deduction or analogy. Qiyas applies to legal rulings (ahkam), not to language itself.
This limits the scope of qiyas.
3
Apply the principle to the dirham example
In an oath involving 'dirham', the term refers to the conventional silver coin. One cannot use qiyas to extend it to other currencies or values.
Custom (urf) defines the conventional meaning.
4
Extend to other linguistic contexts
Consider how this principle applies in contracts, bequests, and other speech acts where word meanings are at issue.
Always refer to the conventional usage at the time and place.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement from the chapter on oaths about paying dirhams. When interpreting words in oaths or contracts, one cannot use analogical reasoning (qiyas) to determine linguistic meanings. Language is conventional, not logical. For example, the word 'dirham' in an oath refers to the coin known by custom; one cannot analogically extend it to other currencies.

The Maxim: Al-Ghalib Ka Al-Waqi' (The Predominant is Like the Actual)
Stating a legal maxim about predominant assumption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-ghalib ka al-waqi'' in Arabic and English, explain its meaning, and apply it to a fiqh scenario involving predominant assumption.

Core Idea
A predominant assumption in law is treated as reality.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: 'The predominant is like the actual.' This maxim allows jurists to base rulings on strong probability when certainty is unattainable. Understanding this principle is essential for navigating cases where doubt arises, such as purity of water or validity of contracts.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
Recite: إِنَّ الْغَالِبَ كَالْوَاقِعِ. Its literal meaning: 'Indeed, the predominant is like the actual.'
2
Analyze the term 'ghalib'
'Ghalib' means predominant or most likely, as opposed to rare (nadir). The maxim does not apply to mere possibility, only to something that is dominant in probability.
Often used in istihsan.
3
Interpret the legal effect
'Like the actual' means that the predominant assumption carries the same legal consequences as if the matter were certain. For example, if water is predominantly pure, it is legally considered pure.
4
Apply to a taharah scenario
Scenario: A person sees water in a vessel and is predominantly sure it is rain water. He may use it for wudu, even if there is a small chance it was contaminated. The maxim overrides the doubt.
Contrast: If doubt is equal (50/50), the maxim does not apply.
5
Evaluate boundaries
The maxim requires that the predominance be strong and reasonable. It does not apply to weak or speculative assumptions. The exact threshold is not fixed but is left to the mufti's judgment.
Some later scholars set the threshold at 70% or 80%.

Indeed, the predominant is like the actual. Translation: The predominant is like the actual.
Source pages p. 119

Commentary
This is a standalone legal maxim (qawida) that states: in Islamic law, a predominant assumption is treated as if it were actual reality. This maxim is used to derive rulings in situations where certainty is not attainable but the prevailing probability is strong. For example, in matters of taharah, if a person is predominantly sure that water is pure, they can use it even if there is a slight doubt.

Key Terms
الغالب (al-ghalib): The predominant or most likely situation, as opposed to rare or doubtful.

The Downward Rule of Majaz in Hanafi Fiqh
States a Hanafi linguistic-legal principle on the direction of metaphorical usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of downward majaz to determine whether a metaphorical interpretation is valid in legal declarations such as divorce and emancipation.

Core Idea
A word may only be used metaphorically for something less than its literal meaning—never for something higher.

In legal texts, words often carry both literal and metaphorical meanings. The Hanafi school has a precise rule governing when metaphorical usage is valid: a word can only be extended to a meaning that is lower in intensity or legal status. This lesson unpacks that principle as stated by Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah, and applies it to real cases in divorce and emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning (haqiqah) of the word in question.
Determine what the word normally means in legal context. For example, 'Hurrah' literally means 'free' (emancipated), and 'Taliq' literally means 'divorced'.
Literal meanings are the starting point for any metaphorical analysis.
2
Determine the intended metaphorical meaning (majaz) and its relative value.
Assess whether the intended meaning is higher or lower than the literal in terms of legal effect or conceptual intensity. For instance, being 'free' (Hurrah) is a higher status than being 'divorced' (Taliq).
The relative hierarchy is key; it may be based on shar'i ranking or common understanding.
3
Apply the principle: if the intended meaning is lower than the literal, majaz is valid; if higher, invalid.
The Hanafi rule states that a word can be used metaphorically for what is beneath its literal meaning, not for what is above it. Thus, 'Hurrah' (higher) can be used metaphorically for 'Taliq' (lower), but not vice versa.
This is a binding rule in the school, not a mere preference.
4
Test with examples from the source: Taliq vs Hurrah, and milk al-yameen vs milk al-nikah.
In divorce, saying 'Anta Hurrah' intending divorce is valid because 'free' is higher. In emancipation, the ownership of a slave (milk al-yameen) is higher than marriage (milk al-nikah), so releasing a slave is stronger, and the same linguistic principle applies.
The metaphor is only permissible in one direction: from higher literal to lower metaphorical.
5
Generalize: this principle governs interpretation in wills, oaths, and other legal declarations.
Any time a speaker uses a word that could be literal or metaphorical, the Hanafi jurist checks the direction. The principle prevents arbitrary reinterpretation and ensures legal predictability.
The principle is a foundational tool for extracting rulings from ambiguous statements.
