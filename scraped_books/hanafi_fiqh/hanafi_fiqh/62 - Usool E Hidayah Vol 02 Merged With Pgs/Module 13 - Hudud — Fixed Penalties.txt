Ruling statement on admissibility of evidence in hudud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that lapse of time prevents testimony but not confession in hudud cases, and explain the rationale for the distinction.

Core Idea
Lapse of time invalidates testimony in hudud but leaves confession fully valid.

This lesson presents a brief but important rule from Hanafi fiqh: the passage of time can bar testimony in hudud cases, but it does not affect the validity of a confession. Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating how the law treats external evidence versus self-incrimination, especially when memories may fade or motives become suspect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage addresses two different legal acts: giving testimony (shahada) and making a confession (iqrar). For each, the text states whether lapse of time (taqadum al-'ahd) is an impediment.
Testimony is prevented; confession is not.
2
Note the condition of timeliness
Understand that in Hanafi hudud jurisprudence, timeliness is a condition for the validity of testimony. If a long period has passed since the event, doubts may arise about the witness's memory or motives, so the testimony is not accepted.
This condition does not apply to confession because it is self-incriminating and thus presumed truthful.
3
Apply to hudud cases
Recall that hudud are fixed punishments (e.g., for zina or theft). In such cases, the law is strict about evidence. Lapse of time makes testimony unreliable, but a person's own admission is always accepted, even after many years.
The ruling underscores the high evidentiary standard for testimony in hudud versus the strength of confession.

Hadd Punishment as Deterrent: The Maxim 'Hadd is a Deterrent, Not a Destroyer'
Axiomatic statement on the purpose of hudud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on hadd punishment, define its key terms, and explain its practical implication in the application of fixed punishments.

Core Idea
Hadd punishments deter crime but must not cause destruction or death.

This lesson examines a foundational maxim from the Hanafi school: 'Indeed, the hadd punishment is a deterrent, not a destroyer.' This principle shapes how fixed punishments (hudud) are carried out, emphasizing that their primary purpose is to prevent future crimes, not to inflict permanent harm. Understanding this maxim helps clarify why the Hanafi school imposes strict conditions on the execution of hadd penalties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its context
Read the Arabic maxim: 'إِنَّ الْحَدَّ زَاجِرٌ لَا مُثْلِفٌ'. Recognize that it is a statement about the purpose of hadd punishments (fixed penalties for crimes like theft, zina, false accusation, and drinking).
2
Define the key terms
Break down 'زاجر' (zajir) meaning deterrent, and 'مثلف' (muthlif) meaning something that causes destruction or harm. Understand that the maxim explicitly negates destruction as a goal.
The term 'hadd' itself refers to a fixed punishment determined by Shariah.
3
Derive the legal implication
If hadd is a deterrent, then its application must be severe enough to discourage but not so severe as to kill or cause permanent damage. This leads to specific rules: e.g., whipping must be with a medium whip, avoiding vital organs; amputation must be done in a manner that preserves life.
4
Apply the maxim to a specific hadd
Consider the hadd for drinking alcohol: 80 lashes. The Hanafi school holds that the lashes must not be so hard as to injure the spine or head, and the whip should be of moderate weight. This ensures deterrence without destruction.
5
Recognize the boundary
Note that if a hadd punishment inadvertently causes death, the executor is not liable for homicide because the act was legally sanctioned, but the principle emphasizes avoiding such outcomes.
This is distinct from retaliation (qisas) where the intent is proportional harm.

Core Idea
Hudud that are solely Allah's right are dropped if much time has passed.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi principle: Fixed penalties that are purely the right of Allah become void if a long time elapses since the crime. This is based on the idea that the purpose of deterrence may no longer be served and evidence may become unreliable. We will examine the scope and implication of this rule.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the rule
The text states that hudud that are pure rights of Allah are voided by lapse of time (taqādam).
This is a definitive ruling in the Hanafi school.
2
Distinguish pure from mixed
Some hudud involve a personal claim (e.g., qadhf involves defamation of a person). Only those without personal claims are voided.
Pure hudud: zina, drinking. Mixed: qadhf, theft.
3
Identify the condition
The condition is the passage of a long time (taqādam), meaning a duration significant enough to warrant dropping the penalty.
No specific fixed duration is given; it is left to the judge's discretion.
4
Apply the consequence
If the condition is met, the punishment is not carried out. The hadd becomes void (bāṭil).
This does not affect other consequences like restitution if a private right is involved.

Core Idea
Disbelief requires conscious conviction; intoxication negates it.

This passage states a fundamental Hanafi legal principle: kufr (disbelief) is not a mere utterance but a matter of inner conviction (i'tiqad). Therefore, any statement of disbelief made while intoxicated is legally ineffective. This has implications for hudud rulings on apostasy.

The condition for valid apostasy is conscious conviction (i'tiqad). Intoxication destroys this condition, so the act fails to meet the requirement.
Intoxication (sukr) impairs the mental faculty required for genuine conviction.
3
Understand the legal consequence
Since the condition of conviction is absent, the apostasy is not legally effective. Therefore, no hadd punishment for apostasy applies, and other consequences (inheritance, marital status) are not triggered.

This brief but powerful maxim from the Hidayah establishes a cornerstone of Islamic law: legal personality is not conditional upon faith. In a few words, it guarantees that non-Muslims under Islamic rule possess full capacity to own, inherit, contract, and be subject to criminal penalties. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying hudud and muamalat to all residents of the Islamic state.

Contrast with the misconception that disbelief entirely nullifies legal standing.
3
Apply to concrete cases
Consider property ownership, inheritance, and hudud penalties. In each, the non-Muslim's disbelief is irrelevant to their capacity.
This does not mean all acts of disbelief are permitted; only that capacity remains.

Core Idea
Hudud punishments are personal divine claims that lapse upon the offender's death.

This short but powerful maxim captures a cornerstone of Hanafi penal theory: fixed punishments (hudud) are rights of God that cannot be transferred to heirs. While the Shafi'i school permits inheritance of certain financial hudud, the Hanafis maintain that all hudud are purely personal obligations that expire with the offender. Understanding this rule clarifies how the school balances divine justice with individual responsibility.

Guided Path

State the ruling

Identify the dispute
The text notes khilaf (disagreement) with al-Shafi'i. Understand that the Shafi'i school allows some hadd penalties to be inherited if they involve financial components.
The dispute is inter-madhhab, not intra-Hanafi.
3
Connect to Hanafi theory
Hanafis classify hudud as pure divine rights (huquq Allah) that are not owned by anyone; hence they cannot be inherited like private property.
This contrasts with financial rights which can be inherited.
4
Analyze practical effect
If a person is liable for a hadd punishment and dies, no substitute punishment applies to heirs. The claim is extinguished.
Example: a thief sentenced to amputation dies; his heirs face no punishment.

Core Idea
Hudud are fixed by clear texts; qiyas cannot extend them.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi principle: hudud punishments are determined exclusively by explicit Quranic and prophetic texts, and analogical reasoning (qiyas) has no role in expanding or deriving new hudud. Understanding this restriction is crucial for correctly applying penal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the maxim: 'There is no entry for qiyas in the hudud.' This is a clear prohibition of analogical expansion in fixed punishments.
2
Understand the scope
Hudud are specific punishments prescribed by the Qur'an and Sunnah for major offenses. They are not open to human reasoning to add new cases.
3
Recognize the rationale
Because hudud are based on clear texts (nusus), they are qat'i (definitive) and not subject to ijtihad via qiyas. Certainty in punishment requires limiting them to their textual instances.
4
Apply the rule
When faced with a new offense, do not attempt to derive a hadd punishment by analogy. Instead, consider tazir or other discretionary penalties.

It means that when assessing a legal ruling, the primary object (asl) determines the outcome, not its subordinate attachments. This ensures consistency in applying the hadd punishment.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A conditional accusation of adultery is void—qadhf must be immediate and explicit.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain legal acts like divorce and manumission can be suspended on future conditions. However, this principle from Usool-e-Hidayah clarifies that qadhf (accusation of zina) is not among them. Why? Because qadhf is a definitive, immediate accusation that cannot be delayed or made contingent. Understanding this distinction protects the integrity of hadd penalties and prevents frivolous or manipulative accusations.

The commentary introduces the term makhluq bihi—something that can be made the object of a condition. Acts that can be intentionally performed or withheld (like talaq) can be conditioned. Qadhf is not such an act.

Hadd is a Deterrent, Not a Destroyer
States and explains the principle with application to flogging

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that hadd is a deterrent (zajir) not a destroyer (muthlif) and apply it to the proper execution of flogging by distributing lashes.

Core Idea
Hadd punishments deter, not destroy; lashes must be spread to avoid death.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone of Hanafi criminal jurisprudence: the fixed penalties (hudud) are legislated to reform and deter, not to kill. The author of al-Hidayah uses this principle to explain why lashes must be distributed over the body during flogging. Understanding this principle is key to grasping the balance between justice and mercy in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the exact Arabic phrase and its meaning: 'al-haddu zajirun la muthlif' – the hadd is a deterrent, not a destroyer.
2
Understand the Rationale
Explain why the lawgiver intends deterrence over destruction: to preserve life and reform the offender.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on maqasid al-sharia (preservation of life).
3
Apply to Flogging
The principle directly dictates that during flogging, lashes must be distributed over different body parts, not concentrated on one spot, because concentration could kill.
The Hidayah cites the practice of Caliph Ali (ra) for stripping and distributing.
4
Note the Exception
The head, face, and private parts are excluded from striking, and the awrah must be covered.
This balances severity with modesty and protection of vital areas.
5
Recognize the Ikhtilaf
Other madhhabs may have different rules on the manner of flogging; the Hanafi view is based on the principle of deterrence.
This distinction shows how usul principles generate fiqh differences.

Pure Hudud are Invalidated by Passage of Time
States the rule that pure hudud are invalidated by taqaddum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a hadd as either a pure right of Allah or a right of a human being, and determine whether its testimony is invalidated by taqaddum.

Core Idea
Hudud that are purely Allah's right are nullified by delay in testimony; human-right hudud are not.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a fundamental classification of hudud based on whose right they uphold. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the rule that the passage of time (taqaddum) can invalidate testimony in certain hudud cases but not others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of hudud
Understand that hudud are divided into those that are pure rights of Allah (e.g., zina, theft, drinking) and those that involve human rights (e.g., qadhf).
Pure rights of Allah are those where no individual human has a personal claim; the right is entirely for public order.
2
Define taqaddum
Taqaddum means a significant passage of time between the commission of the act and the testimony, without a valid excuse.
What constitutes 'significant' is left to the judge's discretion, but it implies a delay that suggests possible concealment or fask.
3
Apply the rule to pure Allah-right hudud
For hudud that are pure rights of Allah, if witnesses testify after a long delay without excuse, that testimony is invalid and the hadd cannot be enforced.
The rationale is that delay may indicate the witnesses are not upright or the act may have been concealed.
4
Distinguish human-right hudud
For hudud that involve a human right (e.g., qadhf where the victim has a right to demand punishment), taqaddum does not automatically bar acceptance of testimony.
The human right element gives the victim a stake, so the delay may not prejudice the public interest in the same way.
5
Note the practical consequence
A judge must examine each case to determine if the hadd is pure or mixed, and whether the delay is excusable.
If the hadd is pure and delay is unreasonable, the judge rejects the testimony and does not enforce the hadd.

Pure Hudud Void by Passage of Time: The Principle of Taqadum
Establishes that pure hudud are invalid if testimony is delayed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that pure hudud are invalidated by taqadum, explain the rationale based on the witness's conflicting duties, and apply it to scenarios involving delayed testimony.

Core Idea
Pure hudud rights of Allah are void by delay; the witness's choice to conceal or delay triggers suspicion.

This lesson explores a key distinction in Hanafi criminal law: the difference between hudud that are pure rights of Allah and those that involve personal rights. The passage from Al-Hidayah and Al-Bahr al-Raiq establishes that when a significant time passes between a crime and the testimony, the hadd punishment is no longer imposed. The reasoning is rooted in the witness's moral obligation to either testify or conceal — delay undermines the required certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Start by stating the rule: Pure hudud (zina, theft, drinking) are void if testimony is delayed (taqadum). Note the exception: hadd of qadhf (false accusation) is not affected because it involves a personal right.
2
Recognize the two categories of hudud
Understand that hudud are split into pure rights of Allah (haqq Allah) and rights of servants (haqq al-'abd). The rule only applies to the former.
Hadd of qadhf is shared between Allah and the victim, so it is not voided by time.
3
Analyze the rationale: the witness's choice
The witness had two options: testify (reward) or conceal (reward for covering a sin). Delaying indicates either initial concealment (later testimony is suspect due to spite/enmity) or a sinful delay without cause. Both scenarios prevent the certainty required for hadd.
This reasoning is from Al-Bahr al-Raiq.
4
Apply the principle to a concrete case
Consider a theft witnessed three years ago; the witness only comes forward now. According to Hanafi law, the testimony is rejected and no hadd is imposed because the delay triggers suspicion or sinfulness.
This demonstrates the practical outcome.
5
Note the authority and cross-madhhab status
The rule is well-established in the Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi. Some other schools may accept delayed testimony, but Hanafis reject it based on this reasoning.
Al-Hidayah presents it without opposition.

Kufr Requires Genuine Belief, Not Intoxication
States the legal principle that kufr requires belief, thus cannot occur during intoxication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that kufr requires genuine belief and apply it to determine the ruling on an intoxicated person's utterances of kufr.

Core Idea
Kufr requires genuine belief; intoxication prevents legal validity of disbelief.

This lesson presents a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh: disbelief (kufr) is an internal conviction, not a mere utterance. Therefore, if a person utters words of kufr while intoxicated, he is not considered an apostate. This principle highlights the importance of intention and mental capacity in legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Terms
Understand the three key terms: kufr (disbelief), i'tiqad (belief), and sukr (intoxication). Kufr is a state of rejection that must be based on conviction, not a slip of the tongue.
The term i'tiqad is central: it means firm belief in the heart.
2
Understand the Causal Relationship
The principle states that kufr belongs to the category of belief. This means that for an act or utterance to be considered kufr, it must spring from genuine internal conviction.
3
Recognize the Consequence of Intoxication
Intoxication negates sound belief and mental capacity. Since kufr requires i'tiqad, and sukr prevents i'tiqad, the two cannot coexist. Thus, utterances made while drunk cannot be considered kufr.
This does not mean the intoxicated person is excused from all liability; he may still be punished for drinking.
4
Apply the Principle
In a case where a drunkard utters words of disbelief, the ruling is: he is not deemed a kafir (apostate), and he is not subject to the laws of apostasy. He is, however, liable for the hadd punishment for drinking (flogging).
The same principle applies to other states that negate mental capacity, such as coercion (ikrah) or insanity.

Delay in Testimony Invalidates Hadd for Pure Rights of Allah
Explains principle that delayed testimony for theft, zina, drinking is rejected

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that delayed testimony invalidates hadd for pure rights of Allah and explain the rationale based on the witness's choice between testifying and concealing.

Core Idea
Delayed testimony invalidates hadd for pure rights of Allah because the witness is suspected of enmity or sin.

In this lesson, we examine a key principle from Al-Hidayah: when witnesses delay testifying about theft, zina, or drinking, their testimony is rejected and the hadd punishment is not applied. The reason lies in the nature of these crimes as pure rights of Allah and the underlying rationale about witness motives. We'll trace the proof and identify the important exception for qadhf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is testimony (shahada) about a hadd crime after a long delay (taqadum). The ruling is that the testimony is not accepted and the hadd is not applied (lam yuhadd).
The ruling is specific to hadd crimes that are pure rights of Allah: theft, zina, drinking.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a rational argument (dalil aqli) based on the witness's original choice: he could testify and gain reward, or conceal and gain reward of covering. Delay indicates either ill intent (if he originally chose concealment) or sinfulness (if he delayed without cause).
This is not a revealed text but a juristic deduction.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (reasoning link)
If the delay was for concealment, later testimony shows enmity (the witness is suspected). If the delay was without concealment, the witness becomes sinful (fasiq). In both cases, the testimony is unreliable, so the hadd is invalidated.
The hadd is a pure right of Allah, so it is forgiven when certainty is lost.
4
Note the exception
The exception is hadd al-qadhf (slander). Qadhf involves a right of the individual (the slandered person), so delay does not invalidate it.
This distinction is crucial: pure rights of Allah are treated differently from mixed rights.

The reason is that the witness had a choice between two things: (1) to give testimony and gain reward, or (2) to conceal the testimony and gain the reward of covering. If the witness initially did not give testimony intending concealment, then after some time he proceeds to testify, this action is based on malice and enmity, so the witness is suspected. Kufr Requires Conscious Belief: Apostasy During Intoxication
States the principle that kufr depends on belief and applies it to prove apostasy during intoxication is invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that kufr depends on belief (i'tiqad) and apply it to determine that apostasy during intoxication does not dissolve the marriage.

Core Idea
Kufr is a matter of belief, so acts of apostasy while intoxicated are not valid in worldly rulings.

The principle that kufr is rooted in conscious belief has a crucial practical consequence: a person who speaks words of apostasy while intoxicated does not become a valid apostate in the eyes of the court. Therefore, his marriage remains intact. This lesson unpacks the principle and its proof, along with the important distinction between worldly and afterlife rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The text states: 'Indeed kufr is from the chapter of belief (i'tiqad).' Recognize that this is the foundational legal maxim governing the ruling.
This principle is taken from Al-Hidayah and is central to Hanafi jurisprudence on apostasy.
2
Understand the Implication
Since kufr requires conscious belief, external actions alone—especially those done without intentional conviction—do not constitute disbelief. The legal ruling follows the internal state.
This aligns with the broader Hanafi emphasis on intention (niyyah).
3
Apply to Intoxication
Intoxication (sukr) impairs consciousness and free will. Therefore, a person who utters apostasy while drunk lacks the required i'tiqad, so the apostasy is invalid (batil) in worldly terms.
The marriage is not dissolved because the apostasy is not legally recognized.
4
Distinguish Worldly and Afterlife Rulings
Fath al-Qadir clarifies: This is the worldly ruling (hukm). In the sight of Allah, if the person consciously intended the meaning of his words, he is a disbeliever in the afterlife. The two domains are separate.
This prevents a misunderstanding that intoxication completely excuses apostasy before Allah.

Kufr Does Not Negate Eligibility to Demand Hadd
States a legal principle with concrete application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a non-Muslim relative may demand hadd for slander against a Muslim relative and explain the principle that kufr does not negate eligibility to demand a right.

Core Idea
Kufr does not sever family relationships for demanding hadd punishment.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi principle from Al-Hidayah: disbelief (kufr) does not nullify a person's legal capacity to demand a right (ahliyyat al-istihqāq). Specifically, a non-Muslim son may demand that the hadd penalty be enforced against someone who slandered his Muslim father, because the slander directly affects his honor and the father-son relationship remains intact despite difference in faith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The text states: 'And kufr does not negate the eligibility of demanding.' Recognize that this is a general legal maxim (qa'ida) in Hanafi fiqh: disbelief does not destroy a person's capacity to claim a right.
The principle applies not only to hadd but to other legal claims where personal status is relevant.
2
Understand the Concrete Example
The example: a Muslim father is slandered; his son is a non-Muslim. According to the Hanafi school, the son can demand the hadd punishment for his father. The basis is the son's relationship to the victim and the injury to his honor.
The relationship (father-son) is not dissolved by kufr; thus the son retains standing to demand hadd.
3
Analyze the Reasoning
The slander (qadhf) harms the honor of both the victim and his close relatives. The son, though a kafir, is directly affected. His eligibility to demand hadd arises from his relational tie, not from his faith.
This reasoning is part of the broader Hanafi concept that eligibility (ahliyyah) for demanding rights is based on objective cause and effect, not on the demander's personal religious status.
4
Note Cross-Madhhab Difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require the demander to be Muslim to seek hadd. The Hanafi position is that kufr does not block such claims. This is a legitimate area of ikhtilaf.
The practical consequence: in a Hanafi jurisdiction, a non-Muslim heir can still trigger the hadd penalty for a Muslim relative's slander.

Hadd Qadhf Is Not Inherited: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Dispute on inheritance of hadd qadhf with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on inheritance of hadd qadhf, explain the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on the dominance of haqq al-shar'.

Core Idea
Hadd qadhf is a right of Allah—it cannot be inherited, so it lapses upon the victim's death.

When a person slanders another (qadhf), the hadd punishment of 80 lashes is prescribed. But what happens if the slandered person dies before the penalty is carried out? Can the heirs demand execution? In this lesson, we examine the classic inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether hadd qadhf is inheritable, and how the Hanafi school grounds its ruling on the primacy of rights belonging to Allah (haqq al-shar').

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
State the core question: Does the hadd qadhf punishment survive the death of the maqdhuf (the slandered person) so that heirs can demand its execution?
2
Recognize the dual nature of hadd qadhf
Understand that hadd qadhf contains both a right of Allah (haqq al-shar')—public benefit—and a right of the individual (haqq al-'abd)—protection of honor. Both schools agree on this duality.
The disagreement is on which right dominates.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
In the Hanafi school, the right of Allah is dominant. Rights of Allah are not inheritable, not subject to waiver, and cannot overlap. Therefore, upon the death of the maqdhuf, the hadd becomes void.
The Hanafi view is built on the maxim: 'حُقُوقُ اللهِ لَا تُورَثُ' (Rights of Allah are not inherited).
4
Compare with the Shafi'i principle
Imam al-Shafi'i gives greater weight to the personal right (haqq al-'abd). Since the victim's honor is a personal entitlement, it passes to the heirs, so the hadd can still be claimed after death.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The concrete difference: If the slandered person dies before hadd is executed, the Hanafi school says the hadd is void; the Shafi'i school says the heirs can demand it. Also, if part of the hadd was executed before death, the remainder is void in Hanafi, but not in Shafi'i.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A hadd punishment is primarily a right of Allah; therefore, it cannot be inherited by human heirs.

The hadd punishment for false accusation of zina (qadhf) involves both a divine right and a personal right. But which aspect governs inheritance? The Hanafis and Shafi'is differ. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical consequences.

Al-Shafi'i emphasizes the right of the slave (haqq al-'abd) in hadd qadhf—protection of honor and repelling disgrace—which is a personal right inheritable like financial rights.

The passage from al-Hidayah explains this rule and its application to insults that do not fall under qadhf.

Application: Ta'zīr for Insults Outside Qadhf
Application of the principle to a list of insults, differentiating between qadhf cases with impediment and non-qadhf cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of no qiyas in hudud to determine that specific insults (e.g., 'Yā fāsiq') incur ta'zīr, and explain the two categories of cases where hadd is prevented.

Core Idea
Insults like 'O faasiq' incur ta'zir, not hadd, because qiyas cannot extend the fixed punishment.

Building on the principle that qiyas has no entry in hudud, this lesson examines the practical application: a long list of insults provided in al-Hidayah. Understanding which cases fall under ta'zīr versus hadd is essential for correctly implementing Islamic criminal law. The passage distinguishes two categories: those where the hadd of qadhf is prevented due to lack of iḥṣān (slave and disbeliever), and those where no hadd exists at all.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Categories
The passage divides the cases into: (1) accusing a slave or disbeliever of zinā, where hadd is prevented due to lack of iḥṣān; (2) other insults (e.g., 'Yā fāsiq'), where no hadd exists.
In both categories, ta'zīr is the result.
2
Analyze the First Category
For a slave or disbeliever, the act is still qadhf (false accusation of zinā), but the hadd is not applied because the victim lacks iḥṣān. Thus, ta'zīr substitutes the hadd.
The text says: 'لأنه جناية قذف في المسألتين الأوليين'.
3
Analyze the Second Category
For the remaining insults (e.g., 'Yā fāsiq'), they are not qadhf but general harm (adhā). Since no hadd is prescribed, qiyas cannot create one, so ta'zīr is imposed.
The text says: 'وفيما عداهما قد آذاه وألحق الشين به ولا مدخل للقياس في الحدود'.
4
Confirm the Proof for Ta'zīr
The passage concludes that ta'zīr is established by the Quran, Sunnah, and consensus (ijmāʿ).
The text: 'وهو ثابت بالكتاب والسنة وإجماع الأمة'.

The Commanded Person Is Not Liable for Unintended Harm
Urdu commentary on the maxim that a commanded act is not bound by safety condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that a commanded person is not liable for unintended death and apply it to distinguish between official executioners and husbands disciplining wives.

Core Idea
A commanded person is not liable for accidental death because his action is not conditioned on safety.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim from the Hanafi school: when a person acts under a valid command, they are not responsible for unforeseen harm. The classic example is an executioner flogging on a judge's order; if the person dies, no blood money is due. We will examine the proof, the analogy to cupping, and the important exception for a husband disciplining his wife.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the maxim: 'The action of the commanded person is not restricted by the condition of safety.' Understand that it applies when a person is commanded by a legitimate authority to perform a specific act.
The commanded person only does what is within his power.
2
Locate the proof and analogy
Find the dalil in the Hidayah: the analogy with cupping and phlebotomy, known medical practices where the practitioner is not liable for accidental death.
These customary practices establish the principle by consensus (or implied Sunnah).
3
Analyze the qiyas
Break down the analogy: asl = cupping/phlebotomy (no liability), far' = executioner flogging (same ruling), illa = both acts are performed under command.
The illa is 'command', a clear attribute (jaliyyah).

The Follower Has No Consideration in Theft
Explaining the rule that subordinate items do not affect hadd

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The ruling follows the principal; a subordinate item does not trigger hadd.

This passage from al-Hidayah explains a key principle in Hanafi theft law: when a free child is stolen along with valuable jewelry, the hand is not cut because the child, being free, is not property, and the jewelry is merely a follower. The principle illustrates that the object of theft must itself be property; subordinate items attached to a non-property principal do not count. We will unpack the legal reasoning and explore its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principal and Follower
Distinguish between the main object taken (the free child) and the attached item (jewelry). The principal (matbū‘) is the child; the follower (tābi‘) is the jewelry.
In Hanafi terminology, the follower is something that is subordinate and not independently intended.
2
Determine the Legal Status of the Principal
A free child is not considered property (mal) in Islamic law. Theft of a free person is not subject to the hadd of hand-cutting because the crime of theft requires taking property.
3
Apply the Rule 'La I‘tibār bi al-Tābi‘'
Since the principal is not property, the follower (jewelry) does not receive independent consideration. The hand is not cut, even if the jewelry alone meets the nisab threshold.
The rule prevents the follower from converting a non-hadd case into a hadd case.
4

One might object: what if the thief intended only the jewelry? The text replies: if that were true, he would have taken only the jewelry and left the child. His act of taking the child shows the child was the intended principal.
The Hanafi position focuses on the physical act, not mere intention.
5

If the child were a slave (property), the jewelry would still be a follower, but the principal is property, so the total value (child + jewelry) could reach nisab and trigger hadd. The rule would not block hadd because the principal qualifies.
This contrast highlights the pivotal role of the principal's legal status.

Ownership of an Apostate's Property Suspended
States the ruling that an apostate's ownership is suspended according to Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa regarding the ownership of an apostate's property and explain why it is considered suspended (mawquf).

Core Idea
An apostate's ownership is suspended, not immediately terminated.

This concise ruling from Al-Hidayah presents Imam Abu Hanifa's unique position on the property of an apostate. Understanding that ownership becomes suspended rather than completely removed is crucial for determining the validity of the apostate's transactions and the fate of his wealth if he returns to Islam or dies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject and predicate
The subject is the ownership (milk) of the apostate (murtadd). The predicate is that it is removed (yazulu) by his apostasy (bi-riddatihi). Recognize that 'removed' has a specific Hanafi interpretation.
The Arabic phrase 'inda-hu indicates this is Abu Hanifa's view.
2
Interpret the legal effect of 'yazulu'
According to Abu Hanifa, 'yazulu' here means the ownership is suspended (mawquf), not completely terminated. The apostate retains de facto possession but cannot validly dispose of property until he repents or dies.
Suspension is a middle state between full ownership and no ownership.
3
Understand the consequence of repentance vs. death
If the apostate returns to Islam, his ownership is restored and all suspended transactions become valid. If he dies or is killed as an apostate, the suspension is lifted and the transactions become void.
This outcome distinguishes suspension from immediate termination.
4
Compare with the Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that ownership is immediately and completely removed upon apostasy. Their view leads to different rulings for the apostate's transactions.
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's suspension view.

The Apostate's Property: Suspended Ownership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on apostate's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the apostate's property, explain the concept of zawal mura'an (suspended removal), and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The apostate's ownership is suspended, not immediately removed; its fate depends on the final outcome.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute about the legal status of an apostate's property. The key question is whether ownership is removed immediately upon apostasy or remains in a state of suspension. Abu Hanifa introduced the unique concept of zawal mura'an (suspended removal), which has significant practical consequences for inheritance and the public treasury. Let's explore the positions and their proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of zawal mura'an
Grasp that under Abu Hanifa, the apostate's ownership is not permanently removed; it is suspended until his situation becomes clear—either he returns to Islam or dies/persists in apostasy.
This suspension is the core of the dispute.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that just as the apostate's blood becomes permissible (no longer inviolable), so does his property. The removal is suspended because he may return to Islam.
The dalil is qiyas from blood inviolability to property inviolability.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that ownership is not removed at all upon apostasy; the apostate retains ownership until death or execution.
Their opposing dalil is not explicitly stated in this text.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If the apostate returns to Islam, Abu Hanifa's position restores ownership as if he never apostatized; the Sahibayn's position means ownership was never disturbed. If he dies in apostasy, Abu Hanifa distinguishes property acquired during Islam (goes to Muslim heirs) and during apostasy (goes to Bayt al-Mal); under the Sahibayn, possibly all goes to heirs.
This difference affects inheritance and treasury revenues.
5
Recognize the mufta bihi and its authority
The text declares Abu Hanifa's view as correct (sahih), supported by later authorities like al-Burhani and al-Nasafi. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin.
