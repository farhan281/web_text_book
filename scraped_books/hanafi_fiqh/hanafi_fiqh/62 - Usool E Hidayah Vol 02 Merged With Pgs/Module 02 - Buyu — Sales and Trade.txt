Sale is strict, marriage is lenient
Contrasting principles of sale and marriage contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the foundational principles of sale and marriage contracts and apply the maxim to distinguish their legal consequences.

Core Idea
Sale requires strict precision; marriage demands forgiving leniency.

This maxim by Imam al-Haskafi encapsulates a core Hanafi contract theory distinction. Understanding this principle helps explain why many minor irregularities in a marriage contract are overlooked while similar issues in a sale would render it invalid. The maxim governs how scholars derive rulings for both contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts
Locate the terms 'bay'' (sale) and 'nikah' (marriage) in the maxim. These are the two contracts being contrasted.
2
State the foundation of sale
The maxim says sale is founded on 'mudayaqah' (strictness) and 'mumaksah' (haggling). This means precise terms and negotiation are essential.
3
State the foundation of marriage
Marriage is founded on 'musamahah' (leniency). This means forgiveness and flexibility are prioritized.
4
Note the contrast
The word 'bikhilaf' signals an opposition. Sale and marriage are treated oppositely due to their different natures.
5
Apply the principle
Consider how this affects rulings: in sale, ambiguity (gharar) often voids the contract; in marriage, similar ambiguity (e.g., unclear dowry) is tolerated to preserve the relationship.

Core Idea
You cannot collect two compensations for the same lost thing or right.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi contract law: one compensated matter cannot yield two compensations. Understanding this principle prevents invalid dual recovery in sales, debts, and other transactions.

4
Apply to sales
If a seller receives the price and also claims the goods after sale, that is prohibited dual compensation.
The seller must choose either the price or the goods.

Property of an Absentee Not Sold by Consensus
Ruling that an absentee's property cannot be sold without his consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling on selling an absentee's property and identify the exceptions that permit it.

Core Idea
An absentee's property cannot be sold without his consent — this is a consensus among scholars.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Islamic sales law: the property of a person who is absent (al-gha'ib) is protected from unauthorized sale. The ruling is established by consensus (bil-ittifaq), meaning all schools of thought agree on the prohibition. As you read, focus on the absolute nature of the rule and the conditions under which exceptions may apply, such as with power of attorney or court order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its subject
The act is selling (bay') the property (mal) of an absentee (al-gha'ib). The absentee is a person who is not present to give consent.
Absence can be physical or legal (e.g., inability to consent).
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that such a sale is not permitted (la yuba'). The phrase 'bil-ittifaq' confirms this is a matter of scholarly consensus.
The prohibition is default; it does not mean the sale is impossible, but that it is not valid without authorization.
3
Recognize the exceptions
The rule has exceptions: if the absentee appointed a representative (wakil) or if a judge orders the sale (e.g., to settle debts), it becomes permissible.
Exceptions are not mentioned in the text but are derived from other sources.
4
Understand the wisdom
The ruling protects ownership rights. Without it, an absentee's property could be usurped or misused by others.
The underlying rationale is the sanctity of private property in Islamic law.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, any sale of an absentee's property without proper authorization is void (batil), and the seller is liable for any loss.
This applies to both movable and immovable property.

Core Idea
Liability follows benefit—whoever gains the profit bears the risk.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone legal maxim that governs many financial transactions in Hanafi fiqh: 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' – liability is in return for profit. Understanding this principle helps you see why the seller bears the risk of damage before delivery and why partners share both profit and loss.

These terms appear in many fiqh texts.

Explore the relationship
The maxim asserts that the party who receives the financial benefit (ghunm) must also bear the liability (ghurm) for any loss or damage.
It is a principle of fairness and equity.
4
Apply to a fiqh example
In a sale, the seller benefits from the price, so he is liable for the goods until delivery. If the goods are damaged before delivery, the seller bears the loss.
This is the standard rule in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Note limitations and exceptions
The maxim generally applies, but parties may contract out of it under certain conditions (e.g., seller stipulates non-liability for defects in some cases).
Such exceptions are limited and must be clearly proven.

Disposition is Limited to the Domain of Attribution
A ruling stating the scope of legal disposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal maxim 'al-tasarruf yaqtasiru 'ala mawdi' al-idafah' and apply it to a guardian's sale of a minor's property, explaining how the scope of authority limits the disposition.

Core Idea
A guardian's power over a ward's property is confined strictly to the scope granted by law.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that governs all forms of delegated authority. Whether you are a guardian, an agent, or a trustee, your power to act (tasarruf) is not absolute—it is bounded by the exact scope of your authorization (idafah). Understanding this maxim helps prevent invalid transactions and clarifies when a legal act is effective.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Core Terms
Examine the Arabic phrase: 'إِنَّ التَّصَرُّفَ يَقْتَصِرُ عَلَى مَوْضِعَ الْإِضَافَةِ'. Break it down into 'al-tasarruf' (disposition), 'yaqtasiru' (is limited), 'ala mawdi'' (to the place/domain), and 'al-idafah' (attribution/authorization). Understand that 'mawdi' al-idafah' refers to the specific scope of permission granted by law or contract.
This maxim is a qa'idah kulliyyah (universal principle) but is presented here as a single ruling statement.
2
Analyze the Ruling: What Does 'Limited' Mean?
The maxim states that any legal act (e.g., selling, buying, leasing) performed by a person with delegated authority is valid only if it falls within the boundaries of that authority. If the act exceeds those boundaries, it is either void (batil) or subject to ratification depending on context. Example: A father selling his minor child's property without necessity exceeds his authority.
In Hanafi fiqh, the guardian's tasarruf is restricted to what is in the ward's best interest and must not involve gross negligence.
3
Apply the Maxim to a Concrete Scenario
Consider a father appointed as guardian over his minor son's inherited property. The father's authority (idafah) allows him to manage and preserve the property. He may sell the property only if there is a genuine need (e.g., to pay the child's debts or for maintenance). If he sells without need, the sale is not effective because it exceeds 'mawdi' al-idafah'.
If the sale is made with a valid need, the disposition is valid and binding.
4
Distinguish from Absolute Ownership
Contrast this with a full owner (malik). An owner's disposition is not limited by any external attribution; he may sell, gift, or dispose freely. The maxim applies only when the person's right to act is derived from another's authority (e.g., wali, wakil, wasi).
The same person can have both roles: e.g., a father as owner of his own property and as guardian of his child's property—different scopes apply.

Source Text

Indeed, the action (disposition) is limited to the place of attribution.
Source pages p. 9

Commentary
When someone is given authority over something (like a guardian over a ward's property), their power of disposition is limited to what they are authorized for. For example, a father can sell his minor child's property only in specific circumstances; he cannot dispose beyond that. The 'mawdi' al-idafah' refers to the domain covered by the authority.

Wealth of Manumitter Does Not Prevent Slave's Earnings
Ruling on partial freedom and earnings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a wealthy master does not prevent the slave's sa'ayah, and distinguish it from the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Master's wealth does not cancel slave's duty to earn his freedom.

This lesson presents a key maxim from the chapter on partial manumission (kitab al-i'taq). Imam Abu Hanifa holds that even if the master is wealthy, a slave who has been partly freed must work (sa'ayah) to pay off the remaining value. The master is not obliged to compensate the slave with his own wealth. This ruling highlights the primacy of the slave's obligation over the master's financial capacity.

Guided Path
1
Define sa'ayah
Understand that sa'ayah is the slave's earning activity to complete his freedom. It is the means by which the slave pays the unfreed portion of his value.
Sa'ayah is a form of kasb (earning) mandated by the shari'ah for the partially manumitted slave.
2
State the ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the master's wealth ('ysar al-mu'tiq) does not prevent (la yamna') the slave from performing sa'ayah. The slave must earn the remaining value; the master is not required to pay it.
This is indicated by the phrase 'indahu' (in his opinion), referring to Abu Hanifa.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if the master is wealthy, he must pay the slave's value for the unfreed portion, and the slave does not have to work. This is a valid ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's view, as it is stronger in evidence and more commonly relied upon.

The Maxim 'Al-Kharaj bi al-Daman': Profit Follows Liability
Axiomatic legal maxim

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Profit is inseparable from liability: whoever enjoys the yield must bear the risk.

This lesson introduces one of the most famous legal maxims in Hanafi fiqh, 'al-kharaju bi al-daman'. This maxim establishes a fundamental principle of risk and reward in commercial transactions, particularly in sales and tenancy. Understanding it is essential for correctly assigning liability for profits and losses.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
Commit the Arabic phrase 'الْخَرَاجُ بِالضَّمَانِ' to memory, along with its literal translation: 'Revenue is paired with liability.'
This maxim is often cited in Hanafi texts as a concise rule.
2
Define the key terms
Understand 'al-kharaj' here means yield, revenue, or profit from land (not the tax). 'Al-daman' means liability for loss or damage, i.e., guarantee.
In other contexts, kharaj can mean land tax, but here it is the produce or benefit.
3
Grasp the relationship
The maxim asserts a pairing: whoever is entitled to the revenue (kharaj) must also bear the risk of loss (daman). Conversely, whoever bears the liability is entitled to the revenue.
This is a reciprocal relationship: the right to profit comes with the duty to bear risk.
4
Apply to a sale
Consider a sale of land: if the buyer takes possession and collects the harvest (kharaj), he is also responsible if the land is destroyed or damaged. If the seller has guaranteed the land (e.g., against defects), he is entitled to the harvest until delivery.
This principle is used to determine who is liable for accidental destruction after sale.

Ruling statement: A famous legal maxim (qā'ida) meaning that the person who enjoys the revenue (kharaj) from land must also bear the risk of damage or loss (daman). (134) Indeed, the property value of umm walad is not compensable according to him (Abu Hanifa). Translation: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the property value of umm walad is not compensable. Explanation: The meaning of this principle is that according to Imam Sahib, umm walad is not compensable property, so just as in case of destroying non-compensable property no guarantee (daman) is obligatory, similarly for umm walad, if destroyed or in case of truth, no guarantee comes. The author of Hidayah mentions this principle and explains that if umm walad is jointly owned by two persons and one partner frees her while he is wealthy, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa there is no guarantee because umm walad is non-compensable and cannot be sold. After the master's death, 'And the manumitter does not recourse against the slave if he guaranteed; and the wala belongs to him, i.e., the manumitter in both cases. And the basis of this dispute is on two principles: one is the divisibility of manumission and its absence as we have established, and the second is that the wealth of the manumitter does not prevent the slave's earning (sa'y) according to him, but it prevents according to them.' Majma' al-Anhur: Kitab al-I'taq, Bab 'Ittq al-Ba'd, vol. 1 p. 517, Dar Ihya al-Turath al-Arabi. Al-Hidayah: Kitab al-'Itaq, Bab al-'Abd yu'tiqu ba'dahu, vol. 2 p. 462, Rahmaniyya. [Page 138] ... She cannot earn for any creditor or heir. Destroying non-compensable property incurs no guarantee, so freeing umm walad also incurs no guarantee. And they (the two) say: he guarantees half of her value, because the property value of umm walad is non-compensable according to him (Abu Hanifa) and compensable according to them. And this is the principle in the matter, and the statement of all other jurists is like their statement. And on this principle are based several issues that we have mentioned in Kifayat al-Muntaha, but Kifayat al-Muntaha is lost today; however, the issues based on this principle are well known and mentioned in books. Among them: if one of them dies, she does not earn for the other according to him, and according to them she earns. Among them: if she gives birth after that and one of them claims the child, the lineage is established from him and the child is free, and he does not guarantee anything of his value to his partner according to him; according to them he guarantees half of his value to his partner if he is wealthy, or the child earns for the half if he is poor. Among them: if an aggressor seizes her and she dies in his hand, he does not guarantee according to him, and he guarantees according to them. And in the matter of slaves attached to land, he guarantees according to him in aggression as he guarantees a free child, such that if he brings her near a beast and it devours her, he guarantees because it is a guarantee of injury, not a guarantee of seizure. And he guarantees by killing by agreement, because it is a guarantee of injury. Among them: if he sells her and hands her over and she dies in the buyer's hand, he does not guarantee according to him, and according to them he guarantees. Among them: if a pregnant slave woman is sold and gives birth before six months, then the mother dies with the buyer, and the seller claims the child, the matter is sound, and he must return the entire price according to him; according to them, he withholds what is due to him from the price. Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute centered on a principle: according to Abu Hanifa, the property value (maliyyah) of umm walad (a female slave who bears her master's child) is non-compensable (ghayr muttaqawwim), meaning she cannot be sold and her destruction does not incur financial liability. The two disciples (Sahibayn) hold that she is compensable (muttaqawwim). The author then lists several practical consequences of this difference: (1) If one co-owner dies, she does not earn for the other according to Abu Hanifa, but does according to them. (2) If she gives birth after the incident and one co-owner claims the child, lineage is established and the child is free without compensation according to Abu Hanifa, but half the value must be paid by the claiming owner (or the child earns if poor) according to them. (3) If someone seizes her (ghasb) and she dies, no compensation is due according to Abu Hanifa, but is due according to them. However, in cases of injury (jinayah) such as causing her death by a wild animal or homicide, compensation is due by agreement because that is a guarantee of injury, not of property. (4) If she is sold and dies with the buyer, the seller does not guarantee the price according to Abu Hanifa, but does according to them. (5) If a pregnant slave is sold, gives birth shortly after, and the mother dies with the buyer, the seller must return the entire price according to Abu Hanifa, while they withhold part of the price.

Key Terms
مالیة (māliyyah): The property value or legal monetary worth of a thing.
متقوم (muttaqawwim): Property that has legal monetary value and is compensable in transactions and torts.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who bears a child to her master, thereby gaining special status: she becomes free upon master's death and cannot be sold.
ضمان (ḍamān): Financial liability or guarantee; compensation for loss or damage.
