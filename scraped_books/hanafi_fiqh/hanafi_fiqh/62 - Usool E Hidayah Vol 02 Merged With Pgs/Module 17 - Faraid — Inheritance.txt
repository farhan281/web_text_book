Ownership of Booty Requires Iḥrāz to Dār al-Islam
States the Hanafi rule on ownership of spoils with a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for ownership of spoils (iḥrāz) and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Ownership of booty is not final until secured in Dār al-Islam – iḥrāz is a necessary condition for milk.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a key principle in the law of ghanimah: the right of individual ownership does not crystallize the moment a soldier captures enemy property. Instead, the Hanafi school requires that the spoils be brought into Muslim territory (iḥrāz) before any combatant can claim ownership. This doctrine contrasts sharply with the Shafi'i view, which recognizes immediate ownership upon possession. Understanding this rule clarifies why early authorities were cautious about disposing of spoils on the battlefield.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being ruled upon? The act of acquiring spoils of war (ghanimah). The ruling: ownership (milk) is not established before a specific condition is met.
The ruling is not about the permissibility of taking spoils, but about when private ownership arises.
2
Locate the condition (iḥrāz)
Iḥrāz means securing the spoils by bringing them into Dār al-Islam and protecting them from recapture. The Hanafi school makes this a prerequisite for individual ownership.
This condition ensures that the spoils are safe from being retaken and can be distributed fairly.
3
Compare with the opposing view (Shafi'i)
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that ownership is established immediately upon possession (qabd) in Dār al-Harb, without requiring iḥrāz.
The dispute hinges on whether the risk of recapture affects the validity of ownership.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Hanafis: spoils cannot be sold, used, or distributed until iḥrāz. Shafi'is: soldiers could potentially dispose of items before returning to Dār al-Islam.
The difference is material: a Hanafi soldier who sells a captured item before iḥrāz commits a void act.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an inter-madhhab dispute (though the dispute is mentioned briefly). The Hanafi position is that ownership (milk) of spoils of war (ghanim) does not become established for the combatants (ghanimin) until the spoils are secured (iḥrāz) in Dār al-Islam. This means that while the spoils are still in enemy territory, no individual soldier has a property right over them; they are still in the nature of a common fund. Only after bringing them safely into Muslim land does ownership accrue. The Shafi'i position is that ownership is established immediately upon possession in Dār al-Harb. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is in the permissibility of using the spoils before iḥrāz: Hanafis prohibit using or selling them until iḥrāz, whereas Shafi'is allow it. The dalil (though not quoted here) is based on the principle that Muslim land provides safety for distribution; before that, the spoils are at risk. The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
الإحراز (al-iḥrāz): The act of securing spoils by bringing them into Dār al-Islam and protecting them from recapture.
الغانمين (al-ghānimīn): The combatants who actually acquire the spoils during jihad.

Istishab al-Hal: Defense Only, Not Entitlement
Usul principle: istishab as defensive proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istishab al-hal in inheritance and explain why a missing person does not inherit from a deceased relative.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal repels harm but cannot grant a right—the missing person does not inherit.

The principle of istishab (presumption of continuity) is often misunderstood as a tool to claim rights. In Hanafi fiqh, it serves only as a shield, not a sword. This lesson clarifies its scope, focusing on the inheritance case of the missing person (mafqud).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the principle
Identify istishab al-hal as the presumption that a state (e.g., life) continues until proven otherwise.
This is a default assumption, not a positive proof.
2
Understand the Hanafi limitation
In the Hanafi school, istishab is only a defensive proof (hujjah dafi'ah) to prevent harm, not a constitutive proof (hujjah lazimah) to establish a new right.
The text explicitly states it 'does not suffice for entitlement'.
3
Apply to the missing person
For a missing person (mafqud), we presume he is alive to protect his own interests (e.g., marriage, property), but we cannot use that presumption to grant him a claim over another's inheritance.
Thus, if his relative dies, he does not inherit.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
The Shafi'i school allows istishab to be used both defensively and to establish rights, so a missing person could inherit in their view.
This cross-madhhab difference highlights the practical impact of usul principles.
5
Derive the mufta bihi ruling
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the missing person does not inherit from a relative who dies before the missing person's death is confirmed.
This ruling applies across Hanafi fatwa practice.

Commentary
This passage from the Urdu sharh discusses a key usul principle regarding istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity of a previous state). The text states that in the Hanafi school, istishab is a valid proof for repelling harm (hujjah dafi'ah) but not for establishing a right (hujjah lazimah). The context is the missing person (mafqud). If a missing person's relative dies, the missing person does not inherit from that relative because we cannot affirm his life at the time of death solely by istishab. Istishab only allows us to treat him as alive for his own benefit (e.g., to maintain his marriage), not to give him a claim over another's property.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istiṣḥāb al-ḥāl): Presumption that a state or condition continues until proven otherwise.
المفقود (al-mafqūd): A person who is missing, whose life or death is uncertain.

Istishab al-Hal: Defense, Not Entitlement
Definition and application of istishab al-hal to missing person's inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi limitation of istishab al-hal and apply it to the inheritance case of a missing person.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal defends the status quo but cannot create new legal entitlements.

Istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a foundational tool in Islamic legal theory. The Hanafi school, however, limits its scope: it repels harm but does not establish claims. This lesson unpacks that limitation through the concrete case of a missing person's inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Define istishab al-hal
Understand that istishab means a state is presumed to continue until evidence of change appears.
Key: 'بقاء ما كان على ما كان لعدم الدليل المزيل'.
2
Identify the Hanafi restriction
Recognize that in the Hanafi school, istishab is a 'hujjah dafi'ah' (defensive proof) not a 'hujjah lazimah' (obligatory proof).
It prevents loss but does not grant gain.
3
Apply to the missing person (mafqud)
If an heir of the mafqud dies, the mafqud does not inherit because that would be a claim of entitlement based on presumption.
The mafqud's own share from a predecessor is suspended (defense), but he does not inherit from a relative who dies later.
4
Contrast with other schools
Note that other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) accept istishab for both defense and entitlement, leading to different rulings.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

كتاب المفقود
(١٦٢) إِنَّ اِسْتِصْحَابَ الْحَالِ لَا يَصْلُحُ حُجَّةً فِي الاسْتِحْقَاقِ . ترجمہ : استصحاب حال استحقاق کے لئے حجت نہیں بن سکتا۔
تشریح: مذکورہ اصول کا مطلب یہ ہے کہ ہمارے ہاں استصحاب حال حجبت دافعہ ہے نہ کہ حجبت لازمہ، یعنی استصحاب حال کی وجہ سے ضر رکو تو دور کیا جائے گا لیکن کسی پر کوئی چیز لازم نہیں کی جائے گی۔ صاحب ہدایہ مذکورہ اصول ذکر کر کے یہ بتلا رہے ہیں کہ اگر مفقود کا کوئی وارث اس کے مفقود ہونے کی حالت میں مرجائے تو یہ مفقود کسی کا وارث نہیں بنے گا، کیونکہ اس وقت میں مفقود کو زندہ شمار کرنا استصحاب حال کی وجہ سے ہے، اور استصحاب حال استحقاق میں حجت بننے کی صلاحیت نہیں رکھتا۔ استصحاب حال ہمارے یہاں دفع کے لئے حجت بن سکتا ہے لیکن استحقاق کے لئے حجت نہیں بن سکتا ، اس لئے ہم نے کہا کہ مفقود کی موت کے حکم سے پہلے مرنے والے اس کے ورثہ کا حق اس کی میراث سے دفع کر دیا اور ان لوگوں کی میراث سے اس کا استحقاق ختم کر دیا۔
لا يصلح حجة في الاستحقاق واستصحاب الحال عبارة عن بقاء ما كان على ما كان لعدم الدليل المزيل فيصلح الاستصحاب عندنا حجة للدفع لا للاستحقاق. فلهذا اعتبر المقصود حياً في مال غيره حتى لا يرث من المفقود في حال فقده، ولا يرث المفقود عن أحد بل يوقف نصيبه من حال مورثه، فإذا مضت المدة أو علم موته يرد الموقوف لأجله إلى وارث مورثه الذي ورث من ماله. (البناية: كتاب المفقود، ج ص ۳۶۷، ط: دار الكتب العلمية) (الهداية كتاب الشركة، ج ۲ ص ۶۱۰، ط: رحمانيه)
[Book of the Missing Person] (162) Indeed, istishab al-hal is not valid as a proof in entitlement. Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that with us, istishab al-hal is a defensive proof, not an obligatory proof; i.e., via istishab al-hal, harm is repelled, but nothing is made obligatory upon anyone. The author of Al-Hidayah mentions this principle to explain that if an heir of a missing person dies during the period of his disappearance, this missing person does not inherit from him, because considering the missing person alive at that time is due to istishab al-hal, and istishab al-hal does not have the capacity to be a proof for entitlement. With us, istishab al-hal can be a proof for defense, not for entitlement. Thus we said that the right of those who died before the death of the missing person is repelled from his inheritance, and his entitlement from their inheritance is terminated. It is not valid as a proof for entitlement. Istishab al-hal is the continued existence of what was upon what it was due to absence of evidence removing it. So with us, istishab is a proof for defense, not for entitlement. For this reason, the missing person is considered alive regarding the property of others so that he does not inherit from the missing person in his state of disappearance, and the missing person does not inherit from anyone, but his share from his predecessor is suspended. When the period passes or his death is known, what was suspended is returned to the heir of the deceased.

Commentary
This passage defines the scope of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) within the Hanafi school. It clarifies that istishab is valid as a proof for repelling harm (daf) but not for establishing a claim (istihqaq). The author applies this to the case of a missing person (mafqud): if an heir of the missing person dies, the missing person does not inherit from that heir, because treating him as alive is based on istishab, which cannot grant entitlement. Conversely, the missing person's share from a deceased relative is suspended. The ruling demonstrates that istishab prevents loss of rights (defense) but does not create new rights.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istishab al-hal): Presumption that a state of affairs continues until evidence shows change.
مفقود (mafqud): A person who disappears and whose death is not confirmed.
حجة دافعة (hujjah dafi'ah): A proof that repels harm or protects a status quo.
