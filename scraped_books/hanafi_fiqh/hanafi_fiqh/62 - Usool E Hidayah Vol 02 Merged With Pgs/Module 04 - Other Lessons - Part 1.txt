Irrelevance of Cause When Purpose Is Achieved
Legal maxim on cause vs. purpose

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim and apply it to a scenario where the cause differs but the purpose is achieved.

Core Idea
When the purpose is fulfilled, the specific cause becomes irrelevant in Islamic law.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim: once the intended objective (maqsud) is attained, any variation in the cause (sabab) leading to it does not invalidate the act. This principle appears in many areas of fiqh, from contracts to purification, ensuring legal pragmatism and avoiding unnecessary technicalities.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Read the Arabic maxim carefully and memorise it. Understand its literal meaning: difference in the cause is not considered when the purpose is achieved.
The maxim is a qawida kulliya (universal principle) in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the key terms
Identify 'sabab' (cause) and 'maqsud' (purpose) as the two central concepts. Note that the maxim relates to the relationship between them.
A sabab is the means that triggers a legal effect; the maqsud is the Shariah-intended outcome.
3
Understand the legal logic
Reflect on why the purpose overrides the cause: Islamic law focuses on substantive outcomes (maqasid) over formalities. If the intended effect is realised, the precise method becomes secondary.
This aligns with the principle of ease (taysir) in the Shariah.
4
Apply to a Hanafi example
Consider a marriage contract. If the purpose (valid union) is achieved through a different wording than the standard formula, the marriage is still valid because the cause (exact wording) is irrelevant once the purpose is attained.
This application is found in Hanafi works on nikah.

Maxim: Self-Inflicted Injury Not Considered in Worldly Rulings
States a legal maxim on self-inflicted harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on self-injury, explain its scope, and apply it to distinguish between self-harm and harm to others in fiqh rulings.

Core Idea
Self-harm does not create worldly legal consequences, but may be sinful in the Hereafter.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim that governs many cases where a person's own action causes harm to themselves. Understanding this maxim helps clarify why certain actions, like consenting to injury or damaging one's own property, do not trigger worldly legal liability even though they may be morally wrong. We will unpack the maxim's meaning, its scope, and its practical applications in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim's key terms
Break down the Arabic phrase: 'jināyah' (injurious act), 'al-mar'' (the person), 'nafsihi' (himself), and 'ghayr mu'tabarah' (not considered).
Note that 'ghayr mu'tabarah' means not legally effective in worldly courts.
2
Contrast worldly vs. hereafter consequences
The maxim only negates worldly rulings, not Hereafter accountability. Understand that self-harm may be sinful but not punishable by human courts.
3
Apply the maxim to a simple scenario
Consider a person who deliberately injures themselves. According to this maxim, they cannot claim damages from themselves, nor does the state punish them for self-harm.
This is different from harming another person, which is jināyah actionable in court.
4
Distinguish from cases where self-injury has legal effect
Identify exceptions: e.g., a person who mutilates themselves while under contract (e.g., military service) may face consequences, or if the self-harm violates public order. The maxim applies generally but has limits.
These exceptions are rare; the default is no worldly consideration.

The Maxim of Progeny Preservation Over Self-Preservation
Establishes a priority of preserving progeny over self

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on preservation of progeny over self and apply it to family law scenarios.

Core Idea
Preserving one's lineage is a higher legal priority than preserving one's own life.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that establishes a hierarchy of needs: the preservation of offspring takes precedence over self-preservation. Derived from the objectives of Islamic law (maqasid al-shari'ah), this maxim shapes rulings in marriage, maintenance, and guardianship. Understanding it helps you see why family obligations often override personal preferences in Hanafi fiqh.

Memorize the maxim

Pay attention to the word 'duna' (more than) which indicates priority.
2
Understand the legal implication
Grasp that this maxim establishes a hierarchy of needs: preservation of lineage (nasl) is a higher legal priority than preservation of self (nafs). This does not mean one can recklessly endanger life, but in legal conflicts, obligations toward progeny prevail.
It is a principle of tarjih (preference) used by Hanafi jurists.
3
Apply to family law
Identify scenarios where this maxim governs rulings, e.g., nafaqah (maintenance) of children takes precedence over personal desires, marriage is encouraged to secure lineage, and in cases of necessity, child support obligations cannot be neglected for personal convenience.
The maxim is applied in the chapters of nikah, nafaqah, and hadanah.

Foundational principle of Islamic supremacy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'Islam prevails and is not prevailed over' and apply it to at least two fiqh rulings (marriage and inheritance).

Core Idea
Islam's legal supremacy is a foundational Hanafi principle governing marriage and inheritance.

This brief maxim carries immense weight in Hanafi fiqh. It establishes a clear hierarchy in legal interactions between Muslims and non-Muslims. By mastering this principle, you will unlock many specific rulings in family law and inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Maxim
Commit the Arabic to memory: 'إِنَّ الْإِسْلَامَ يَعْلُو وَلَا يُعْلَى'. This is a foundational principle derived from Quran (3:139) and hadith.
The maxim is short but legally powerful.
2
Understand the Legal Implication
The principle means that in any conflict between Islamic law and other systems, Islamic law prevails. It also means Islam holds a higher status in personal status matters.
This is about legal superiority, not political dominance or oppression.
3
Apply to Marriage
A Muslim man may marry a Kitabiyya (Christian or Jewish woman), but a non-Muslim man cannot marry a Muslim woman. Why? Because the husband's faith dominates the household; Islam prevails.
This ruling directly flows from the maxim.
4
Apply to Inheritance
A Muslim can inherit from a non-Muslim relative, but a non-Muslim cannot inherit from a Muslim. The higher faith inherits from the lower.
Another key application.
5
Recognize Limits
The principle does not justify oppression. It is a legal maxim for specific rulings, not a blanket license for discrimination.
Context is crucial to avoid misuse.

Maxim: Prohibition for External Reason Does Not Negate Inherent Permissibility
Legal maxim clarifying the scope of prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim, explain its meaning, and apply it to distinguish between an act's inherent permissibility and its temporary prohibition.

Core Idea
A prohibited act may remain valid in essence if the prohibition is due to an external cause.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim that prevents confusion between two distinct legal states: the inherent nature of an act and a temporary prohibition arising from an external factor. Understanding this maxim is crucial for correctly classifying acts that are valid in principle but restricted in certain circumstances, such as prayer during forbidden times.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim's components
Break down the Arabic: 'Al-nahy' (the prohibition), 'an al-shay' (of the thing), 'li ma'na fi ghayrihi' (for a meaning in something else), 'la yunafi' (does not oppose), 'mashru'iyyatahu' (its legality). The key distinction is between a prohibition rooted in the act itself and one rooted in an external factor.
The 'meaning in something else' refers to an external cause like time, place, or circumstance.
2
Understand the legal consequence
The maxim asserts that the act remains legally valid (mashru') in principle. The prohibition does not make the act inherently invalid or sinful; rather, it restricts performance under specific conditions.
Example: Salat is inherently valid, but prohibited during sunrise due to the time, not the act itself.
3
Apply to a concrete fiqh case
Take the example of fasting on the two 'Eid days. The act of fasting is generally permissible (even meritorious), but it is prohibited on 'Eid due to the occasion. The maxim confirms that the prohibition does not negate the inherent legality of fasting.
This contrasts with acts prohibited intrinsically (e.g., zina), where the prohibition negates mashru'iyyah entirely.
4
Distinguish from other maxims
Compare with the principle 'al-nahy yufsid' (prohibition renders invalid) which applies when the prohibition is intrinsic to the act itself (e.g., selling alcohol). This maxim carves out an exception for external prohibitions.
Hanafi usul treats commands (amr) as indicating obligation unless specified; similarly, prohibition indicates invalidity unless the cause is external.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
إِنَّ النَّهُىَ عَنِ الشَّيْء لِمَعْنِّي فِي غَيْرِهِ لَا يُنَافِي مَشْرُوعِيَّتَهُ
Indeed, the prohibition of a thing for a meaning in something else does not oppose its legality.
Source pages p. 4

Commentary
This maxim means that if an act is prohibited because of an external factor (e.g., time, place, circumstance), the act itself remains legally valid in essence. For example, salah during prohibited times (like sunrise) is forbidden for the time being, but the prayer itself is still valid if performed at other times. Similarly, fasting on 'Eid is forbidden, but the act of fasting is generally permissible.

Key Terms
مَشْرُوعِيَّة (mashrū'iyyah): Legality, permissibility in principle, indicating that an act is recognized by the Shari'ah as a valid form of worship or transaction in its essence, even if temporarily restricted.

Definition of it'am as enabling access to food

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of it'am as enabling access to food and apply it to distinguish valid feeding obligations from misconceptions.

Core Idea
It'am means enabling access to food, not necessarily placing it in the mouth.

This short but foundational usool defines the term 'it'am' (feeding) in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this definition is crucial for correctly fulfilling obligations like kaffarah (expiation) where feeding is required. The precise meaning determines what actions count as fulfilling the command.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Term and Definition
Read the Arabic usool: 'الإطعام هو حقيقة في التمكين من الطعام'. The term is 'it'am' and its true (haqiqi) meaning is 'tamkin min al-ta'am' - enabling access to food.
This is a lexical definition based on Hanafi usul al-fiqh.
2
Understand 'Tamkin' (Enabling)
'Tamkin' means to put food within a person's reach so they can eat if they choose. It does not require physically placing food into the mouth or forcing the person to eat.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from a more literal interpretation.
3
Apply the Definition to a Scenario
Consider a person who must feed 10 poor people as kaffarah. If he places a meal before each poor person such that they can eat freely, he has fulfilled it'am. He does not need to spoon-feed them.
The key is enabling, not direct feeding.
4
Differentiate from Common Misconception
A common mistake is to think it'am requires hand-feeding or cooking. The Hanafi ruling broadens the application, making it easier to fulfill obligations.

Taking Payment for an Obligatory Act is Not Permissible
Ruling that wages cannot be taken for performing a wajib act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking payment for a wajib act and apply it to distinguish between acts that are personally obligatory (wajib 'ayni) and communal obligations (wajib kifayah).

Core Idea
You cannot charge for what you are already obligated to do.

This lesson presents a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: one cannot receive payment for performing an act that is wajib (obligatory) upon them. This principle governs many transactions, from hiring an imam to paying for acts of worship. We will explore the ruling, its scope, and how it distinguishes between personal and communal obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its obligation status
Determine whether the act in question is wajib 'ayni (personally obligatory) or wajib kifayah (communal obligation). The ruling applies primarily to wajib 'ayni.
Wajib 'ayni: must be performed by every individual (e.g., five daily prayers). Wajib kifayah: sufficient if some perform it (e.g., funeral prayer).
2
Apply the rule: no payment for wajib acts
If the act is wajib 'ayni, taking payment for performing it is not permissible (haram). If it is wajib kifayah, payment may be permissible under certain conditions.
The default position is that receiving wages for wajib acts is prohibited to avoid commodifying religious duties.
3
Recognize exceptions and nuances
Payment may be acceptable if it is not directly for the wajib act itself but for a separate service (e.g., a hired imam is paid for leading the prayer, not for the prayer itself). Also, acts that are not wajib (sunnah, mubah) can be compensated.
The commentary uses the example of nafaqah: a husband's maintenance of his wife cannot be placed as payment for her obligatory act of confinement (iddah).
4
Distinguish from other contractual obligations
The principle is specific to wajib acts. For non-obligatory acts or acts that are not personally required, payment is generally permissible under the rules of ijarah (hire).
E.g., teaching Quran is typically fard kifayah, so a teacher may receive a salary.

The Maxim 'Al-Hukmu Li Al-Ghalib': The Ruling Follows the Predominant
States a legal maxim for resolving ambiguity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-hukmu li al-ghalib' and apply it to resolve ambiguity in fiqh cases such as the foundling example.

Core Idea
Legal rulings presume the predominant circumstance when the actual fact is unknown.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim of the Hanafi school: 'The ruling follows the predominant.' It is used to resolve cases where direct evidence is absent, by assuming the most common situation. For instance, the religious status of a foundling (laqīṭ) is determined by the majority religion of the area where he was found. This maxim simplifies many ambiguous rulings.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
Commit to memory the exact Arabic text: 'إِنَّ الْحُكْمَ لِلْغَالِبِ' and its English translation: 'Indeed, the ruling is for the predominant.'
This is a concise, powerful principle.
2
Identify the key term 'al-ghalib'
Understand that 'al-ghalib' means that which is most common or predominant in a given context. It refers to the prevalent situation, not the exceptional.
3
Explain the legal presumption
When the actual fact of a case is unknown, the law presumes the state of affairs that is most common. This is a tool of legal certainty (taghlib al-ghalib).
4
Apply to the foundling example
If a child is found in a city where the majority are Muslims, he is presumed Muslim. If the majority are non-Muslim, he is presumed non-Muslim. This affects inheritance, marriage, and other rulings.
5
Note the scope and limits
This maxim applies only when the actual fact is unknown. If direct evidence exists, it takes precedence. Also, the presumption may be rebutted by strong indications.

Istishhab and Its Limitation in Claims of Entitlement
Ruling statement on the limitation of istishhab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on istishhab and identify when it cannot be used as proof.

Core Idea
Istishhab cannot establish a right; it only preserves a known state against change.

This lesson presents a key ruling from Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the presumption of continuity (istishhab) is not a valid proof when someone tries to use it to claim a right (istihqaq). While some schools accept istishhab as a fallback proof, the Hanafi school restricts it – it can rebut an alleged change, but cannot create a new right. Understanding this distinction prevents misapplication in fatwa and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the Arabic phrase: istishhab al-hal is not a valid proof for istihqaq. Note the term 'istishhab' – presumption of continuity of a past state.
Memorize this as a core Hanafi usul rule.
2
Understand the limitation
Istishhab is only valid to rebut a claim of change, not to prove a substantive right. For example, if someone was known to be Muslim, we presume they remain Muslim unless proven otherwise. But istishhab cannot be used to claim ownership without evidence.
This limitation is unique to Hanafis compared to Shafiʿis.
3
Apply to a case
Given a dispute: A claims B owes him money because B owed him last year. Can istishhab prove the debt still exists? No, because istihqaq (entitlement) requires fresh evidence.
Think of istishhab as a shield, not a sword.

The Dominated is Legally Non-Existent: Applied to Fosterage
Stating and applying the usul principle to milk mixed with water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi principle of the dominated being legally non-existent and APPLY it to determine whether the prohibition of fosterage is established when milk is mixed with water.

Core Idea
The dominated element in a mixture is legally non-existent; only the dominant determines the ruling.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal principle: المغلوب غير موجود حكما (the dominated is legally non-existent). This principle is not about physical reality but about how the law treats mixed substances. We will see how al-Hidayah applies it to a classic case: a woman's milk mixed with water and fed to an infant. The ruling on fosterage prohibition hinges on which component is dominant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Extract the legal maxim: المغلوب غير موجود حكما. Understand that it means the dominated element in a mixture is disregarded for legal purposes.
The maxim is not about physical annihilation but legal treatment.
2
Understand the Application Context
The passage applies the principle to a mixture of milk (from a woman) and water, fed to an infant. The issue is whether this feeding establishes the prohibition of fosterage (hurmat al-rada').
3
Determine Dominance
If milk is dominant (more than water), the mixture is legally treated as pure milk, and the prohibition is established. If water is dominant, the mixture is legally water, and no prohibition arises.
The dominated element (water or milk) is legally non-existent.
4
Examine the Proof and Objection
The proof uses analogy to oaths: if someone swears not to drink milk and drinks milk dominated by water, they do not break the oath. An opponent objects that oaths are based on custom, but prohibition is based on the existence of milk.
5
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response refines the reasoning: prohibition is not based on mere existence but on the effect of the milk — causing bone growth and flesh development. Dominated milk cannot achieve this effect because it does not provide sufficient nutrition.
Thus, the principle still holds, and the ruling distinguishes between the basis for oaths (custom) and prohibition (causal effect).

(20) Indeed, the dominated is non-existent legally. Translation: The dominated is legally non-existent.
Explanation: The meaning of this principle is that when of two things one is dominant and the other is dominated, then this dominated thing, in terms of ruling, will be considered non-existent. That is, the ruling will attach to the dominant thing and the ruling will not apply to the dominated thing.
The author of al-Hidayah, by mentioning this principle, is explaining that if a woman's milk mixes with water and then the child is fed, and the situation is that the milk is dominant, i.e., the milk is more than the water, then in this case the prohibition of fosterage is established. Because when the milk is dominant and the water is dominated, and the dominated thing is not existent in terms of ruling, then in this case, as if the water does not exist, rather it is all milk. So in such a case, the prohibition of fosterage is established. And if the situation is opposite, i.e., the water is dominant and the milk is dominated, then the prohibition of fosterage is not established, because the dominated thing, although existent in reality, is legally nonexistent and not present. While for the establishment of prohibition, its existence is necessary both in reality and in ruling.

The Principle of Same Genus Not Dominating (الجنس لا يغلب الجنس)
Stating the principle without application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-jins la yaghlib al-jins' and explain its basic implication in legal rulings concerning mixtures and transactions.

Core Idea
When two substances belong to the same genus, legal dominance does not apply.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim in Hanafi fiqh: 'Indeed, the same genus does not dominate another of the same genus.' This principle appears in discussions of mixtures, transactions, and other areas where determining which substance is dominant affects the ruling. Understanding this maxim is essential for applying correct rulings in many later contexts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term 'jins' (genus)
Identify what constitutes a genus in fiqh—a category grouping substances or items sharing a core nature (e.g., milk from different animals are all milk).
Genus is not based on quantity or strength but on essential similarity.
2
State the principle in Arabic and English
Recite the maxim: 'إِنَّ الْجِنْسَ لَا يَغْلِبُ الْجِنْسَ' meaning 'The same genus does not dominate another of the same genus.'
Memorize the wording; it is a key qa'ida.
3
Explain the legal consequence
When two items are of the same genus, neither legally dominates the other. This means that in rulings based on dominance (e.g., which substance's ruling applies to a mixture), genus membership overrides considerations of quantity or other factors.
Contrast with principles like 'al-ghalib yuglab' (the dominant prevails) which apply across different genera.
4
Consider a simple application
Imagine mixing two types of milk (e.g., cow's milk and goat's milk). According to this principle, one does not dominate the other, so the mixture is treated as a blend of the same genus rather than one overriding the other.
This principle prevents automatic dominance based on volume or taste.

The Subsidiary is Like the Overcome: Milk Mixed with Food
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fosterage with mixed milk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifah rules that milk mixed with food does not establish fosterage prohibition and evaluate the Sahibayn's counter-argument.

Core Idea
Subsidiary is legally overcome: milk mixed with food is nonexistent for fosterage because growth depends on food, not milk.

This lesson explores the Hanafi legal maxim 'the subsidiary is like the overcome' through a classic dispute between Abu Hanifah and his two principal students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The specific case is whether a mixture of milk and food can create a fosterage prohibition (ḥurmat al-riḍāʿ). Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi jurists prioritize the effective cause (ʿillah) over physical quantities, and why the relied-upon position ultimately disregards even dominant milk when food is present.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Begin by articulating Abu Hanifah's ruling: when milk is mixed with food, even if the milk is dominant in quantity, the mixture does not establish fosterage prohibition. The prohibition depends on growth (nashw wa numū), which comes from food, not milk.
This is the muftā bihī position.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's opposing view
Next, state that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the milk is dominant at the time of consumption (e.g., it drips when lifting the morsel), then the prohibition attaches to the mixture, considering the dominant element.
Both agree that cooked mixtures never cause prohibition.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Understand their reasoning: they look to the outward dominant component at the moment of drinking. Since the milk is greater in quantity, it should be the effective cause.
This is a literal, quantity-based approach.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifah's response
Now examine Abu Hanifah's counter: the true effective cause (manāṭ al-taḥrīm) is growth, and growth is primarily caused by food, not milk. Moreover, at the stomach stage, food becomes dominant regardless of initial quantities. Milk, when mixed, is subsidiary (tābiʿ) to food and is legally treated as nonexistent.
The principle 'the subsidiary is like the overcome' is applied here.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifah, a child who drinks milk mixed with food does not become a foster child of the milk-mother; according to the Sahibayn, if the milk is dominant and uncooked, he does. The text declares Abu Hanifah's view as ṣaḥīḥ (correct) and it is the operative position in the school.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah confirms this tarjīḥ.

The Same Genus Does Not Overcome Its Own Kind: Mixed Milk of Two Women
Applies the maxim that mixing same genus does not cause absorption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, according to Muhammad and Zufar, mixing same-genus milk from two women establishes fosterage prohibition from both, and apply the maxim 'al-jins la yaghlib al-jins' to analogous scenarios using the oil usurpation analogy.

Core Idea
Mixing same-genus substances does not cause absorption; each retains its legal effect.

This passage from Al-Hidayah and Bada'i' al-Sana'i' tackles a practical question: if milk from two women mixes, does the fosterage prohibition attach to both women or only to the one whose milk is physically dominant? The answer hinges on a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: 'The genus does not overcome the same genus.' Let's unpack the reasoning of the Hanafi masters and see how they apply this maxim to determine the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
First, clearly state the rulings of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (with Zufar). According to Abu Yusuf, only the woman whose milk is dominant in quantity becomes a foster mother. According to Muhammad and Zufar, both women become foster mothers equally.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
2
Understand the Maxim
Grasp the meaning of 'al-jins la yaghlib al-jins': when two items are of the same genus (jins), mixing them does not cause one to be absorbed (istihlak) into the other. Each retains its independent legal identity and effect.
Same genus means sharing the essential nature, e.g., milk from different women.
3
Trace Muhammad's Reasoning
Follow Muhammad's argument: both milks are from one genus, so mixing is not absorption. Each milk nourishes the child proportionally (cause flesh, bone, or satisfy hunger). One does not rob the other of its strength. Therefore prohibition attaches to both.
The effective cause (illa) is growth; every drop contributes.
4
Examine the Analogy
Analyze the oil usurpation case: if someone usurps oil from another and mixes it with his own oil, both become co-owners. This proves that mixing same-genus substances does not cause one to vanish; each portion remains legally attributable.
This qiyas (analogy) is the key dalil for Muhammad's position.
5
Determine the Practical Difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Muhammad's view, the child cannot marry any of the two women (both become foster mothers). Under Abu Yusuf, only the woman with dominant milk is a foster mother.
The later school (mufta bihi) follows Muhammad and Zufar.

Breastfeeding via Enema Does Not Establish Mahram Relationship
Ruling on breastfeeding via enema with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) holds that rada' via enema does not create a mahram relationship, and analyze Imam Muhammad's opposing analogy to fasting.

Core Idea
Rada' requires growth (nushu') through oral ingestion; enema from below does not create growth, hence no prohibition.

This lesson examines a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: does administering a woman's milk to a child via enema establish a fosterage relationship that prohibits marriage? The author of Hidayah rules it does not, because the legal cause (illa) of the prohibition is the child's growth from the milk, which only occurs when milk is ingested through the mouth. Imam Muhammad dissents, drawing an analogy to fasting. Understanding this dispute sharpens our grasp of how effective causes are identified in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: rada' is not established via enema. Note that this is the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
The text explicitly says 'النشوء لا يوجد فيه' (growth is not found in it).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam Muhammad holds that rada' is established via enema, analogizing to fasting.
Opposing dalil: 'كما يفسد به الصوم' (just as fasting is broken by it).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Muhammad's analogy: if enema breaks fasting by reaching the cavity, then it should also establish rada'.
This is a qiyas on the illa of 'wusul ila al-jawf'.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The response differentiates the illa: fasting relates to 'wusul ila al-jawf', while rada' relates to 'nushu''. Enema reaches the cavity but does not cause growth.
Thus the analogy fails due to difference in effective cause.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, no mahram relationship; according to Muhammad, marriage is prohibited.
The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

This principle directly supports the Hanafi ruling that feeding the poor morning and evening without gift of ownership fulfills the expiation of ẓihār. Understanding this definition is essential for distinguishing it from related rulings in zakāt and fiṭrah.

A Word Can Be Majāz for a Lower Meaning, Not a Higher

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi linguistic principle that a word can be used figuratively (majāz) for something lower than its literal meaning, but not for something higher, and identify its application in cases of manumission and divorce.

Core Idea
A word can be a metaphor for a weaker meaning but not for a stronger one.

The Hanafi school recognizes that language operates in a hierarchy: a word's literal meaning has a certain strength, and metaphorical usage can only extend to weaker meanings. This principle is crucial for determining what a speaker's utterance legally implies, especially in oaths, divorce, and manumission. In this passage, the author states the rule and then illustrates it with the case of 'anta tāliq' (you are divorced) versus 'anta hurra' (you are free).

Guided Path
1
State the Linguistic Principle
The rule: lafz (a word) can be used as majāz (figuratively) for a meaning that is lower (dūn) than its haqīqah (literal meaning), but not for a meaning that is higher (fawq).
This is a principle of Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh derived from customary usage.
2
Understand the Rationale
The stronger meaning cannot be conveyed by a weaker term because the weaker term lacks the necessary force. Conversely, a stronger term can encompass a weaker meaning, similar to how a lion can be called a brave man (since bravery is found in lions) but a brave man cannot be called a lion.
The rationale is based on the hierarchy of strength in meaning.
3
Apply to the Divorce/Manumission Case
'Anta tāliq' (you are divorced) is weaker than 'anta hurra' (you are free) because divorce removes marital ownership, while manumission removes ownership of a slave, which is considered a stronger form of ownership. Therefore, saying 'anta tāliq' cannot metaphorically mean freedom.
The Hanafi justification: milk al-yamīn (ownership of a slave) is superior to milk al-nikāḥ (marriage ownership), so its removal (freedom) is a stronger act.
4
Identify the Reverse
The reverse is allowed: saying 'anta hurra' can metaphorically mean divorce, because the stronger term (freedom) can encompass the weaker (divorce). This has practical implications for oaths and declarations.
This asymmetry is a key feature of the principle.

Condition for Oath Validity: Possibility of Fulfillment
States a condition for oath validity and gives an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that an oath requires possibility of fulfillment and apply it to determine whether a given oath is valid or invalid.

Core Idea
An oath on an impossible act is void — it never takes effect.

Oaths are serious legal commitments, but not every uttered condition creates a binding oath. The Hanafi school teaches that for an oath to be valid (in'aqad) and to remain in effect (baqa'), fulfillment must be conceivable at the time of swearing. This lesson unpacks that principle using the classic example from al-Hidayah: swearing to drink from an empty bowl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the Arabic phrase: 'إن من شرط انعقاد اليمين وبقائه التصور عند الطرفين'. Identify the key condition (shart) stated: the oath must be conceivable (tasawwur) from both sides (the one swearing and the context).
Tasawwur here means legal possibility, not merely logical possibility.
2
Apply the Principle to the Example
Consider the scenario: a person swears 'If I do not drink the water in this bowl, my wife is divorced' while the bowl is empty. Since drinking from an empty bowl is impossible, the condition for oath validity fails. Therefore, the oath does not take effect (in'aqad).
The impossibility is at the time of swearing, not later.
3
Distinguish from a Possible Act
Contrast with an oath to do something possible, e.g., 'If I do not drink this water' when the bowl is full. Here the oath is valid because fulfillment is conceivable. If the person does not drink, they break the oath (hanth) and the divorce occurs.
Possibility is assessed at the moment the oath is made.
4
Understand Legal Consequences
If the oath is invalid, no legal effects follow: no divorce, no expiation (kaffarah) required. The speaker is not considered to have broken an oath. This protects people from unintended consequences.
The Hanafi school emphasizes avoiding hardship (raf' al-haraj).

Irrelevance of Attributes in Oaths on Present Objects
Rule about ignoring attributes in present-object oaths unless motivating.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that an attribute of a present object in an oath is ignored unless it was the motivating factor, and identify when the attribute becomes relevant.

Core Idea
In oaths, describing a present thing is legally irrelevant unless the description was the reason for the oath.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi principle regarding oaths: when a person swears about something that is present, any attribute mentioned (e.g., young, black) is ignored unless that attribute was the actual reason for swearing. This prevents oath violations from being decided by changes in attributes, ensuring that the essence of the subject remains the focus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the attribute
Read the oath carefully and note both the subject (the thing or person) and any descriptive attribute mentioned (e.g., 'young,' 'black').
The attribute may be an adjective describing the subject.
2
Determine if the subject is present or absent
Check if the subject of the oath is physically present at the time of swearing. If it is present, the attribute is presumptively irrelevant. If absent, different rules apply.
Present means the subject is in front of the swearer or immediately accessible.
3
Evaluate whether the attribute was the motivating factor
Ask: Did the swearer swear because of that specific attribute? If yes, the attribute is considered and changes may affect the oath. If no, the attribute is ignored and only the essence matters.
The motivation is determined by context and intention.

Dispute on Condition of Oath Validity and Continuation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on condition of conceptual possibility for oath formation and continuation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi position on the condition of conceptual possibility for oaths, compare Abu Yusuf's opposing view, and determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
An oath on a truly impossible action is void; the majority requires real possibility at swearing and afterward.

Oaths on future actions are common in daily life and legal disputes. This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi disagreement: must the action be conceptually possible both when sworn and later? The source text from Al-Hidayah presents the views of the three major Hanafi imams and highlights the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Majority Position
State the condition required by Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Zufar: the action must be conceptually possible in reality (haqiqatan) at the time of swearing and must remain possible for the oath to continue.
This is the dominant view in the school.
2
Identify Abu Yusuf's Position
Abu Yusuf holds that only the future nature of the oath matters; possibility is not a condition for formation or continuation.
He is the sole dissenter among the early Hanafi masters.
3
Compare the Rationale
The majority argues that an oath to do something impossible cannot be binding by its nature. Abu Yusuf focuses on the oath's textual condition: it is about a future matter.
Both use logical necessity (talil) but reach different conclusions.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Apply the dispute to a scenario: swearing to give a drink from a dry well (truly impossible). The majority says the oath is void; Abu Yusuf says it is valid, requiring expiation.
This practical difference matters for fatwa.
5
Note the Agreed Point
All three imams agree that customary impossibility ('adatan) is not a condition; the oath is valid if the action is not truly impossible.
This distinguishes real impossibility from mere custom.

A Defect Does Not Annihilate the Genus
Explaining the maxim with oath fulfillment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-ayb la yu'dimu al-jins' to determine whether an oath to repay a debt is fulfilled when defective coins are used.

Core Idea
A defect in an item does not remove it from its legal genus.

In Hanafi fiqh, categories (jins) are determined by essential nature, not accidental defects. This principle governs many transactions and oaths. In this lesson, we examine the maxim 'Al-ayb la yu'dimu al-jins' through a concrete case: a man swears to repay a debt on a specific day, then pays with defective coins. Does he fulfill his oath? The answer hinges on whether the defect changes the genus of the coins. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the key maxim: 'Al-ayb la yu'dimu al-jins' (A defect does not annihilate the genus). Understand that it applies to any item whose genus remains despite flaws.
2
Identify the case
The case is an oath: 'By Allah, I will repay my debt today.' The debtor repays with coins that are defective—counterfeit (zuyuf), debased (nabharajah), or legally claimed (mustahaqqah).
The debt is assumed to be in silver dirhams.
3
Apply the maxim
Determine if the defective coins are still of the same genus as the debt (silver dirhams). If yes, the oath is fulfilled. If paid with a different substance like lead or copper, the genus changes and the oath is not fulfilled.
Copper (sutuqah) is a different genus.
4
Understand the reasoning
The maxim is supported by the fact that in transactions, accepting defective coins is considered valid receipt (istifa). This proves that defects do not alter the legal category; the coins remain dirhams.
The proof is from practice and consensus.
5
Conclude the ruling
The swearer is absolved (barra) when paying with defective coins of the same genus. He is not absolved if the substance is different.
No sin on him, and the oath is fulfilled.

This passage presents a legal maxim from the Hanafi school: 'A defect does not eliminate the genus.' The author of Usool al-Hidayah explains this maxim and provides an illustration from Al-Hidayah. The maxim means that if an item has a defect, its essential legal category (genus) remains intact. For example, if a person swears to repay a debt and pays with counterfeit dirhams (which have the defect of being fake), the oath is still fulfilled because the genus 'dirham' is still present despite the defect. The text quotes Al-Hidayah: if one swears to pay a debt and pays with nabaherejah (a type of acceptable coinage) or zuyuf (counterfeit) or coins claimed by someone else, the oath is fulfilled. But paying with lead or plated coins does not fulfill the oath because those are a different genus. The distinction is that lead is not of the genus of dirhams. This principle is used in the Hanafi school to determine fulfillment of oaths and other obligations.

Permissions Are Conditioned by Safety
Explaining the principle of conditional safety in permissible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that absolute permissions are restricted by the condition of safety and apply it to determine when liability (damān) arises.

Core Idea
A permissible act becomes liable for harm unless another liability already attaches.

This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces a key Hanafi principle: even acts that are generally permissible (mubah) carry an implicit condition of safety. If harm results, the doer becomes liable for compensation. However, if the act already carries a known liability (like mahr in marriage), imposing additional liability would be double compensation. We'll explore how this principle applies to walking, hunting, disciplining a wife, and marital intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The author states that 'absolute permissions are restricted by the condition of safety.' Understand that every mubah act has an implicit condition: it must not cause harm. If harm occurs, liability (damān) is triggered.
This principle is a manifestation of the broader maxim 'lā ḍarar wa lā ḍirār' (no harm and no reciprocating harm).
2
Analyze the liability-generating examples
The author gives examples: walking on a road and hunting. If these permissible acts cause destruction, the actor is liable because the permission was conditioned on safety. The harm violates the condition.
These are cases where no pre-existing liability exists.
3
Examine the exception: marital intercourse
Contrary to the previous examples, if a man has intercourse with his wife and she dies or suffers injury (ifḍā'), he is not liable for diyah according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. This is despite intercourse being permissible.
The exception is based on a specific legal reasoning, not a general exemption from the safety condition.
4
Understand the reasoning behind the exception
The text explains: the husband is already liable for the mahr (dower) as a result of the marriage contract. If he were also liable for diyah, it would mean two compensations for a single act (the act of intercourse). This is avoided.
The principle of avoiding double liability overrides the general restriction by safety condition in this specific context.
5
Recognize the authority and limitation
This ruling is cited from al-Muhit and is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. It is the muftā bihī position. The passage does not mention Muhammad al-Shaybani's view, but it may differ, indicating an intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf.
The authority weight is ẓāhir al-riwāyah, and the ruling is mukhtalaf fīh across schools.

Ownership Does Not Affect Personal Obligations
A legal maxim on ownership and personal obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim regarding ownership and personal religious duties, and explain its implications for slaves and married women.

Core Idea
Personal religious duties cannot be delegated to owners or spouses.

In this passage from Kitab al-Siyar, the Hanafi school articulates a key principle: the two types of ownership—milk al-yamin (slave ownership) and milk al-nikah (marital authority)—do not affect an individual's personal obligations (furud al-a'yan). This means that obligations such as prayer, fasting, zakat, and hajj remain personally binding, regardless of these relationships. Understanding this maxim prevents the misconception that a master or husband can fulfill religious duties on behalf of another.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forms of ownership
Recognize milk al-yamin (slave ownership) and milk al-nikah (marital authority) as mentioned in the maxim.
2
Define farḍ al-‘ayn
Understand that farḍ al-‘ayn are individual obligations that each person must perform personally.
Contrast with farḍ al-kifāyah (communal obligations).
3
Analyze the negation
The phrase 'لا يظهر' means these ownerships have no legal effect in the realm of personal obligations.
They do not transfer, delegate, or fulfill these duties.
4
Apply to examples
Consider a slave's obligation to pray, or a wife's obligation to fast. Neither the master nor the husband can perform these acts for them.
5
Recognize broader application
This maxim applies to all farḍ al-‘ayn and underscores personal responsibility in Islam.
No one can stand in for another in these duties, even under ownership.
