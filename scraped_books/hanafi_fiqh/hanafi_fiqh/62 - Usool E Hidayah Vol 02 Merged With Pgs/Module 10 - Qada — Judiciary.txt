Judgment Against an Absentee Not Permissible
Ruling that a court cannot give judgment against an absent party.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on judging an absent person and identify the exceptions to this principle.

Core Idea
A judge cannot pass a verdict against an absent party without giving them a chance to defend.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, the presence of the defendant or their legal representative is a fundamental safeguard of justice. This ruling protects absent persons from being condemned without a hearing. While the general principle is strict, exceptions exist for cases where the absentee has appointed a representative or has been properly notified with clear evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is 'judging against an absentee' (al-qada' 'ala al-gha'ib). The ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuz). Determine the hukm level: it is haram (prohibited) because it violates the right to defense.
The ruling is absolute in the text but has exceptions.
2
Understand the Rationale
Justice requires that both parties be heard. An absent party cannot present their case, so a judgment against them would be inherently unjust.
This is a procedural safeguard.
3
Note the Exceptions
Exceptions exist when the absentee has a representative (wakil) who appears, or when the claim is proven by clear evidence and the absentee has been notified and had opportunity to respond.
These exceptions are based on later Hanafi fatwa.

The Denier's Word with His Oath
States the rule that the denier's statement is accepted with an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi judicial maxim regarding the denier's oath and apply it to a simple dispute scenario.

Core Idea
The denier's statement is accepted with his oath – burden of proof on the claimant.

This maxim is a cornerstone of Islamic judicial procedure. It establishes that when a claim is made and denied, the defendant's denial is given precedence, provided he confirms it with an oath. This principle ensures that disputes are resolved fairly, protecting the defendant from baseless claims while requiring a solemn commitment to truth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the claimant (mudda'i) who brings the suit and the denier (munkir) who denies the claim.
In Hanafi fiqh, the burden of proof is on the claimant.
2
State the default rule
The denier's word (his denial) is accepted. He is not required to prove his denial, only to swear an oath affirming it.
This is the foundational maxim: 'al-qawl lil-munkir ma'a yaminihi'.
3
Note the condition of the oath
The denier must take an oath (yamin) to strengthen his denial. Without the oath, his statement alone may not suffice.
The oath is a solemn religious affirmation; lying under oath is a major sin.
4
Recognize the exception
If the claimant produces clear evidence (bayyinah), the denier's word is overridden and the claimant prevails.
Evidence (shahadah, documents, etc.) takes precedence over the denier's oath.
5
Apply to a simple scenario
Example: Claimant says 'You owe me $100', denier says 'I do not owe anything'. Without evidence, the judge asks the denier to swear, then dismisses the claim.
If the denier refuses to swear, judgment may be given against him in some cases.

Repelling Harm vs. Imposing Obligation: The Role of Judicial Decision
Explaining the principle that defending from harm does not need a judge's ruling, unlike imposing obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between daf' (repelling harm) and ilzam (imposing obligation) and state which requires a judicial decision, citing the Hanafi principle from Al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Repelling harm requires no judge; imposing a claim does.

In Islamic law, not every action needs a court order. Some actions, like defending oneself from harm, are permissible without prior judicial approval. Others, like claiming a debt or enforcing a liability, require a judge's ruling. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi principle that distinguishes between these two categories, drawing from Al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Legal Categories
The passage introduces two key terms: daf' (repelling or warding off harm) and ilzam (imposing an obligation on another). Note that daf' is a self-help action, while ilzam involves a claim against someone else.
Think of daf' as defensive, ilzam as offensive in a legal sense.
2
Understand the Core Distinction
According to the principle, daf' does not require a judicial decision (qada) because it is an immediate response to harm. Ilzam, however, requires a judge's ruling to be binding and enforceable. This prevents arbitrary imposition of liabilities.
The distinction protects individuals from self-help in claiming rights but allows self-defense.
3
Anchor the Principle in Its Source
This principle is cited from Al-Hidayah, a foundational Hanafi text, specifically in the context of marriage (Kitab al-Nikah). Its authority in the Hanafi school makes it a guiding rule for similar cases across fiqh.
The reference to Al-Hidayah indicates this is a relied-upon (mufta bihi) principle.
4
Apply the Principle to a Scenario
Consider a physical attack: you may defend yourself (daf') without a court order. But if you want to sue the attacker for damages (ilzam), you need a judge's ruling. Similarly, removing a trespasser from your property is daf', while claiming rent arrears is ilzam.
The key is whether the action repels immediate harm or enforces a pre-existing obligation.
5
Recognize the Scope and Limits
Daf' is limited to urgent, immediate harm. Once the harm has passed or becomes a liability claim, the action shifts to ilzam and requires judicial oversight. Also, daf' must not be excessive; if it causes disproportionate harm, it may become unlawful.
The principle has exceptions, e.g., when daf' itself causes harm, it may require compensation.

Lapse of Time: Testimony vs. Confession
Stating a legal maxim on evidence admissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim on lapse of time and apply it to distinguish between the admissibility of testimony and confession.

Core Idea
A long delay bars testimony but not confession because confession is against self-interest.

In Islamic court proceedings, the reliability of evidence is paramount. The Hanafi school recognizes that human memory fades and motives shift over time. This lesson presents a foundational maxim from Kitab al-Hudud: while a witness's testimony may be rejected due to delay, a person's own confession remains admissible regardless of when it is made. Understanding this distinction is key to evaluating evidence in qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
Read the maxim in Arabic and its translation. The phrase 'inna taqaduma al-ahdi yamna'u al-shahadata duna al-iqrari' states the ruling concisely.
This is a ruling statement – no proof is cited here.
2
Contrast testimony and confession
Note that the maxim treats the two differently. Testimony (shahadah) is prevented by time; confession (iqrar) is not. Ask yourself: why would the law distinguish?
Confession is against one's own interest, so it is considered more trustworthy.
3
Understand the rationale
The Hanafi reasoning: a witness may forget or develop bias after a long time, making testimony unreliable. But a person is unlikely to falsely confess against their own interest, even after many years.
This rationale is based on human psychology and the strength of iqrar as proof.
4
Apply the principle to a scenario
Consider a case: ten years after a debt transaction, two witnesses come forward. How does the maxim apply? What if the debtor himself admits the debt? Apply the rule.
The witnesses are rejected; the debtor's confession is accepted.

The Act of the Commanded Person Is Not Conditioned on Safety
States a legal maxim and applies it to judicial punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'Fi'l al-Ma'mur la yataqayyad bi-shart al-salama' and apply it to determine liability in cases of judicial punishment and other commanded acts.

Core Idea
An act performed under a valid Shari'ah command carries no liability for unintended harm.

When a person carries out a lawful command and unintentionally causes harm, who is responsible? The Hanafi school answers with a powerful legal maxim: the act of the commanded person is not conditioned on safety. This principle protects judges and executors of punishment from liability, based on the nature of a Shari'ah command. In this lesson, we will unpack the maxim, its proof, and its limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its legal effect
State the maxim in Arabic and English. Understand that it removes liability from the commanded person for unintended harm caused while executing a valid command.
The maxim is: فعل المأمور لا يتقيد بشرط السلامة.
2
Examine the dalil from Al-Hidayah
Read the text from Al-Hidayah: the judge's command is a command of the Shari'ah, and the act of the commanded person is analogized to cupping and bleeding, where no liability attaches.
Dalil: the practice of the Imam and qiyas.
3
Apply to the case of judicial punishment
If a judge orders a flogger to execute a hadd or ta'zir punishment and the convict dies, no compensation is due from the flogger or the judge. The blood is 'wasted' (hader).
4
Contrast with unrestricted permissions
Compare with the case of a husband disciplining his wife: he acts under a permission, not a command. Permissions are conditioned on safety, so if death results, compensation is due.
The distinction is between 'ma'mur bihi' and 'mutlaq'.

Commentary
This passage introduces the first of two related legal maxims (usul) from the Hanafi school, as expounded in Al-Hidayah. The maxim is: 'The act of the commanded person is not restricted by the condition of safety.' The commentator explains that when a person is lawfully commanded to perform an act (by a judge, for instance), the command does not carry an implicit condition that the act must be carried out without causing harm. Therefore, if harm or death results from the commanded act, the commanded person is not liable for damages (daman). The authority for this principle is the practice of the Shari'ah: the judge acts on behalf of the law, and the executor of the punishment is merely an instrument. The commentator draws an analogy to cupping (hijama) and bloodletting (fasd): if a doctor performs these on a patient or an animal and death ensues, no compensation is due because the doctor was commanded (or authorized) to perform the act. The key distinction is that in the case of a judge's order, the act is purely by command of the Shari'ah, so liability is completely removed (the blood is wasted, i.e., no diyya). However, the principle is contrasted with the case of a husband disciplining his wife: the husband's permission to strike is not a command from the Shari'ah but a personal right, so it is restricted by the condition of safety. If the wife dies, the husband must pay compensation. This contrast sets up the second maxim about unrestricted permissions being conditioned on safety. The passage concludes with citations from Al-Hidayah and Al-Lubab.

ضمان (ḍamān): Legal responsibility to compensate for loss or injury, e.g., paying diyya or damages.

Unrestricted Permissions Are Conditioned on Safety
States a complementary maxim on liability for permissible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-Iṭlāqāt tataqayyad bi-shart al-salāma', apply it to permissible acts, and identify the exception for marital intercourse.

Core Idea
Permissible acts carry an implicit condition of safety; if harm results, liability arises — except in marital intercourse.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi legal maxim that governs liability for harm caused during permissible acts. You will see how the maxim applies to everyday actions like walking or disciplining a wife, and you will examine a notable exception for marital intercourse based on juristic preference (istiḥsān).

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Memorize the Arabic maxim: الإطلاقات تتقيد بشرط السلامة (al-Iṭlāqāt tataqayyad bi-shart al-salāma) and its translation: 'Unrestricted permissions are conditioned on safety.'
This is the core principle of the lesson.
2
Identify the ruling on permissible acts
Understand that any act which is merely permitted (mubāḥ) is implicitly conditioned on safety. If safety is violated (harm occurs), the actor becomes liable (ḍamān).
This distinguishes mubāḥ from commanded acts (ma'mūr) where liability is not automatic.
3
Apply the maxim to examples
Consider the two examples from the text: (1) disciplining a wife, and (2) walking on a road. Both are permitted, but if death or injury results, the actor must pay diyya because the condition of safety was breached.
These examples illustrate the practical consequence of the maxim.
4
Analyze the exception for marital intercourse
The text notes an exception: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, a husband whose wife dies or is injured during consensual intercourse is not liable for diyya. The reason is that he is already liable for the mahr (dower), and imposing double liability for one act would be unjust.
This exception is based on istiḥsān (juristic preference) and is specific to the Hanafi school.
5
Evaluate the legal reasoning
Contrast the general rule (qiyās: analogy to walking on the road) with the exception (istiḥsān for intercourse). Note that the maxim is applied strictly to most permissible acts, but the husband's unique relationship (milk al-mut'a) justifies the exception.
This step builds the skill of distinguishing between rule and exception based on underlying rationale.

Commentary
This passage presents the second legal maxim: 'Unrestricted permissions are restricted by the condition of safety.' The commentator explains that any act which is merely permitted (mubāḥ) carries an implicit condition that it must be performed without causing harm to others. If harm results, the actor becomes liable (ḍāmin) for compensation. The first example is the husband disciplining his wife: this is a permitted act (ta'dīb), but it is conditioned on safety. If death results, the husband must pay diyya. The second example is walking on a public road: it is permissible, but if one negligently causes harm (e.g., by kicking someone), liability arises. This exception is noted, and the commentator suggests that perhaps this case should also be conditioned on safety, but the established position is as stated. The passage concludes with a citation from Al-Binayah.

The Denier's Statement with Oath is Accepted
States and proves a procedural rule of evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that the denier's word with oath is accepted, identify its proof from the hadith, and apply it to a simple dispute between two parties.

Core Idea
Without evidence, the judge accepts the defendant's sworn denial.

In Islamic judicial procedure, the burden of proof lies on the claimant. If the claimant fails to produce evidence, the defendant's denial under oath is taken as decisive. This lesson presents this fundamental rule as found in Al-Hidayah, with its proof from the hadith, and illustrates it with a partnership example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the rule: the denier's (defendant's) statement, when accompanied by an oath, is accepted as the basis for judgment if the claimant lacks evidence.
The rule is procedural, not substantive.
2
Locate the Dalil
Find the hadith cited: 'al-bayyinatu 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yaminu 'ala al-mudda'a 'alayhi' (The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the defendant). This is from Sunan al-Tirmidhi.
The hadith is ahad (single-chain) but accepted by all schools.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Show how the hadith proves the rule. The phrase 'al-yaminu 'ala al-mudda'a 'alayhi' directly assigns the oath to the defendant, implying that his denial (with oath) prevails over the bare claim of the plaintiff.
The word 'al-mudda'a 'alayhi' = the one against whom the claim is made, i.e., the defendant.
4
Apply to the Given Example
Consider the partnership: one partner claims the other bought an item and owes a share; the other denies. Since the claimant has no witnesses, the defendant's denial under oath is accepted, and no liability is imposed.
The example clarifies how the rule works in a common dispute.
5
Note the Condition of Oath
The rule explicitly requires that the denier take an oath. Without the oath, the denial is not automatically accepted – the judge would ask the defendant to swear.
The oath legitimizes the denial; mere assertion is insufficient.
