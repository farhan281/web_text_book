Able to Earn is Considered Rich
Ruling on ability to earn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling that an able-bodied person is considered rich for the purposes of zakat and obligatory maintenance.

Core Idea
Ability to earn makes one legally rich, even without actual wealth.

This passage from Usul al-Hidayah is a pivotal maxim in Hanafi fiqh. It establishes that legal wealth (ghina) is not limited to material possession but includes the capacity to earn. This affects who is eligible to receive zakat and who is entitled to obligatory support (nafaqah). Let's unpack its meaning and application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and predicate
The statement: 'The one who is able to earn is rich by his earning.' The subject is 'the one able to earn', the predicate is 'rich by his earning'.
2
Understand the legal consequence
This attribution of 'rich' (ghani) has fiqh effects: such a person is not eligible for zakat (since zakat is for the poor and needy) and is not entitled to receive nafaqah (maintenance) from others (since they can earn their own livelihood).
3
Recognize the condition
The ability must be genuine (physically and mentally capable). If a person is unable to work (e.g., disabled, dependent), they are not considered rich by earning.
4
Note the distinction from actual wealth
The individual may have no cash or property, yet they are still legally 'rich' due to earning capacity. This is a unique Hanafi perspective.
5
Apply to typical scenarios
e.g., a healthy young man who is unemployed but can work is considered rich for zakat eligibility; he cannot receive zakat.

(128) Indeed, the one who is able to earn is rich by his earning.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement: a person who has the ability to earn a living is considered wealthy (ghani) by virtue of that ability, even if they do not possess actual wealth. In Hanafi law, this affects eligibility for zakat and maintenance support: a capable person is not entitled to receive zakat or mandatory support from others unless they are unable to work.

Key Terms
القادر على الكسب (al-qādir ʿalā al-kasb): One who is physically and mentally capable of earning a livelihood.

Key Takeaway
The ability to earn makes a person legally rich in Hanafi fiqh, barring them from receiving zakat or obligatory maintenance.

The Foundling's Maintenance from the Treasury
Ruling with dual proofs: naqli (companion practice) and aqli (qiyas and maxim).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the foundling's maintenance and explain the proofs: the practice of the Companions, the analogy to the cripple, and the maxim al-kharaj bi al-daman.

Core Idea
The foundling's maintenance is wajib upon the Bayt al-Mal because profit accompanies liability – the treasury inherits his estate.

When a foundling (laqit) is discovered, the immediate question is who is responsible for his care. The Hanafi school establishes a clear public duty: the Bayt al-Mal (state treasury) must provide maintenance. This lesson unpacks the textual and rational proofs that lead to this ruling, grounding it in companion practice, analogical reasoning, and a foundational legal maxim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Obligated Party
Read the first sentence: 'His maintenance is in the public treasury.' Determine the hukm (wajib) and who is obligated (the Bayt al-Mal, not the finder).
The ruling is a collective obligation on the state.
2
Examine the Textual Proof (Naqli)
The text cites a narration from Umar and Ali (RA). In Hanafi usul, the practice of the Companions (qawl sahabi) is a valid source of law when not contradicted. Recognize this as a dalil naqli.
The narration is not given verbatim; its authority is from the consensus of the Companions.
3
Analyze the Analogical Proof (Qiyas)
The text compares the foundling to a cripple (maq'ad) who has no wealth or relative: just as the cripple's maintenance is from the treasury, so too is the foundling's. Identify the asl (cripple), far' (foundling), and illa (helplessness and need without provider).
Both are dependent on the state due to lack of personal means and relatives.
4
Apply the Maxim al-Kharaj bi al-Daman
The text states that since the foundling's inheritance (if he dies) goes to the treasury, his maintenance is due from it. This applies the maxim 'one who enjoys the profit must bear the liability' – the treasury inherits, so it must support.
This is a rational proof (talil) supplementing the naqli proof.

Source Text

وَنَفَقَتُهُ فِي بَيْتِ الْمَالِ رُوِيَ ذَلِكَ عَنْ عُمَرَ وَعَلِيٍّ رَضِيَ اللَّهُ عَنْهُمَا وَلِأَنَّهُ عَاجِرٌ مُحْتَاجٌ لَا مَالَ لَهُ وَلَا قَرِيبَ وَمَالُ بَيْتِ الْمَالِ مُعَةٌ لِلصَّرْفِ إِلَى مِثْلِهِ فَصَارَ كَالْمَقْعَدِ الَّذِي لَا مَالَ لَهُ وَلَا قَرِيبَ وَلَأَنَّ مِيرَاثَهُ لِبَيْتِ الْمَالِ فَتَجِبُ نَفَقَتُهُ مِنْهُ لِأَنَّ الْخَرَاجَ بِالضَّمَانِ وَلِهَذَا كَانَتْ جِنَايَتُهُ فِيهِ.
His maintenance is in the public treasury. That is narrated from Umar and Ali (may Allah be pleased with them), and because he is helpless, needy, with no wealth and no relative, and the wealth of the public treasury is prepared for spending on such a person, so he is like a cripple who has no wealth and no relative. And because his inheritance goes to the public treasury, so his maintenance is obligatory from it, for 'profit accompanies liability'; and for this reason his crimes are also against it.
Source pages p. 162

Commentary
This passage from Tabyin al-Haqa'iq provides the ruling and its proofs for the maintenance (nafaqah) of a foundling. The text states that his maintenance is from the Bayt al-Mal (public treasury). This is supported by a narration from Umar and Ali (RA). The reasoning: the foundling is helpless and has no wealth or known relatives; the treasury is designated for such needy persons, analogous to a disabled person without means. Additionally, since the foundling's inheritance (if he dies) goes to the treasury, the treasury must also bear his expenses during his life, following the maxim 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' (profit accompanies liability). Similarly, any crimes he commits are also paid by the treasury.

Key Terms
الخراج بالضمان (al-kharāj bi al-ḍamān): The legal maxim meaning 'profit accompanies liability'; one who enjoys a benefit must also bear the associated risks or expenses.
بيت المال (bayt al-māl): The public treasury of the Islamic state.

Key Takeaway
The foundling's maintenance is wajib upon the Bayt al-Mal, proven by companion practice, analogy to the disabled, and the maxim that profit accompanies liability.
