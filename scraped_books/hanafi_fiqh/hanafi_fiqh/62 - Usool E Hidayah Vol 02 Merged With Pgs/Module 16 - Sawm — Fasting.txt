Intention in Single vs. Multiple Genera of Obligations
Defines the rule on intention within same versus different genera

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that intention is lughw within the same genus and mu'tabara across different genera to cases of qaḍā’ fasts and expiation.

Core Idea
Intention only distinguishes between different categories of obligation, not within the same category.

This lesson explains a foundational Hanafi principle regarding intention (niyya). When you owe multiple obligations of the same type, a general intention suffices; but when obligations are of different types, you must specify. Understanding this prevents overcomplication and ensures validity in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus (jins) of the obligations
Examine each obligation you intend to fulfill. Are they all of the same legal category? For example, multiple qaḍā’ days from Ramaḍān are one genus; nadhr (vowed) fasts are another genus.
Genus is determined by the underlying cause (sabab) and legal nature of the obligation.
2
Check if all obligations are of the same jins
If all obligations share the same jins (e.g., all qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān), then a general intention suffices; you do not need to specify which particular day. If they belong to different jins (e.g., qaḍā’ and nadhr), then you must specify which one you intend.
Intention's function is to distinguish between different types; when types are identical, no distinction is needed.
3
Apply the ruling to the scenario
If you have only qaḍā’ fasts, intending 'fast of qaḍā’' is sufficient. If you have both qaḍā’ and nadhr, you must intend either 'qaḍā’' or 'nadhr' – a vague intention would be invalid because it fails to separate the two genera.
The same principle applies to expiations: multiple ẓihār expiations are one genus, so feeding the poor with a general intention only counts for one expiation even if the amount given exceeds the minimum.

(100) Indeed, intention in one genus is futile, and in two genera it is considered. Translation: Intention becomes futile in one genus, and in two genera intention will be considered. Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that making separate intentions for two things of the same genus is not correct; rather, intention will be futile, because intention is for distinction between different genera. Since both are of the same genus, each will be performed even without intention. If a person owes qaḍā’ days of Ramaḍān and no other fasts are obligatory upon him, then if he merely intends 'fast of qaḍā’', it is sufficient, even if he does not say the word 'Ramaḍān'; specification is not necessary. However, if there are two things of different genera, then intention in them is considered. For example, a person has qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān and nadhr fasts; if he intends one of them, that intention will be correct and the fasts will be performed according to his intention. The author of al-Hidāyah mentions this principle to explain that if a muthāhir feeds sixty poor people as expiation for two ẓihārs by giving each poor person one ṣā‘ of wheat, whereas in one expiation half a ṣā‘ is given, still the expiation will be counted only for one ẓihār, not both, because this person intended in a single genus, and intention in a single genus is futile. When intention became futile, the amount of food given has the capacity of one expiation, because half a ṣā‘ is the minimum amount for expiation; less than that would prevent expiation, but more than that is not prohibited. So by giving one ṣā‘, he will be discharged from only one expiation. [From al-Bināyah: 'Intention in one genus is futile because intention is for distinction between different genera or for distinguishing what is shared, and that does not exist in one genus. In two genera, intention is considered. Do you not see that if someone owes qaḍā’ of days from Ramaḍān and intends fasting of qaḍā’, it is permissible and specification is not required; but in qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān and nadhr fasts, specification of intention is required due to the difference of their genera?']

Commentary
This passage presents a key Hanafi principle regarding intention (niyya) when multiple obligations of the same or different categories exist. The principle: intention is irrelevant (lughw) when the obligations are of the same genus (jins wāḥid), but it is valid and necessary (mu'tabara) when they belong to different genera (jinsayn). The reason: intention serves to distinguish between different types of acts or their categories. If all obligations are of the same type (e.g., all are qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān), a general intention suffices without specifying which particular fast. But if they are of different types (e.g., qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān and nadhr fasts), specification is required. The application in the text is to ẓihār expiation: if a man owes two expiations (both of the same genus — kaffārat ẓihār) and feeds each of sixty poor people a full ṣā‘ (double the minimum half ṣā‘), only one expiation is discharged because his intention to fulfill both in the same feeding is futile; the single genus does not allow separate intentions. The extra amount given does not count as a second expiation because the minimum (niṣf ṣā‘) is already exceeded, but the excess does not create a separate obligation. The Arabic quote from al-Bināyah confirms this reasoning.

Key Terms
نِيَّة (niyya): Intention; the mental resolve to perform a specific act of worship. In this principle, its function is to distinguish between different categories of obligation.
جِنْس (jins): Genus or category of a legal obligation. E.g., qaḍā’ of Ramaḍān is one genus; nadhr fasts are another genus. Obligations of the same type share the same jins.
لَغْو (lughw): Futile or irrelevant; an intention that has no legal effect because it does not serve the purpose of distinguishing between different obligations.
مُعْتَبَرَة (mu'tabara): Considered or legally relevant; an intention that is valid and has effect, required when obligations are of different genera.

Intention Disregarded Within the Same Category
Explaining that intention is irrelevant within same category, necessary between different categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that intention is disregarded within the same category to determine the validity of multiple expiations or fasts.

Core Idea
Intention is disregarded when acts are of the same category; it only matters to distinguish different categories.

In Hanafi fiqh, intention (niyyah) primarily serves to differentiate between acts of worship. When multiple obligations fall under the same legal category, intention loses its differentiating function and becomes irrelevant. This lesson explores the practical implications of this principle using examples from expiation (kaffarah) and fasting.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle
Grasp that intention is for distinguishing between different categories (tamyiz) and is unnecessary when all acts belong to the same category (jins wahid).
The principle is: 'al-niyyah fi al-jins al-wahid laghw' – intention in a single category is disregarded.
2
Apply to the kaffarah example
Consider a person who owes two kaffarahs for two separate zihar oaths. He feeds sixty poor people one sa' each, intending to fulfill both. Since both kaffarahs are of the same type, intention is disregarded, and the feeding fulfills only one kaffarah.
The extra food given beyond the minimum does not count toward the second kaffarah because the intention was ineffective.
3
Apply to the fasting example
For multiple qada days of Ramadan, simply intending qada is sufficient without specifying which days. But if qada of Ramadan and a vow (nadhr) are owed, specification of intention is required because they are different categories.
This shows the principle: intention matters only when categories differ.

Commentary
This passage states a key Hanafi principle regarding intention (niyyah) in acts of worship and expiation. The principle is that when multiple acts belong to the same category (jins wahid), intention is not required to distinguish them; the mere act suffices for any single instance. For example, if a person owes multiple kaffarahs of the same type, feeding each poor person one sa' without specifying which kaffarah is enough for one kaffarah. Similarly, for fasting, if one owes only qada of Ramadan, simply intending qada is sufficient without specifying the exact days. However, if the acts are of different categories (e.g., qada of Ramadan and a vow), then intention and specification are required. This is derived from the idea that intention serves to differentiate, and where no differentiation is needed, it is disregarded. The example given is of a man who has to expiate two zihar oaths. He feeds sixty poor people one sa' each, but only one expiation is fulfilled because the category is the same (kaffarah), and the extra food is considered surplus but not for the second expiation. The reason is that the intention, being disregarded, does not allocate the extra amount to the second expiation.

Key Terms
النية (al-niyyah): Here, intention is the mental determination to perform a specific act of worship.
الجنس الواحد (al-jins al-wahid): Acts that belong to the same legal category, e.g., multiple kaffarahs of the same type.
