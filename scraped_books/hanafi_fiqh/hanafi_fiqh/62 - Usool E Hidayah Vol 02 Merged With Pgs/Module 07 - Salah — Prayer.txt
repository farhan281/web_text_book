Valid Excuses Affect Acts of Worship
General maxim affecting ibadat rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that valid excuses (a'dhar) can modify the rulings of worship in concrete scenarios.

Core Idea
Valid excuses transform rigid worship rules into flexible concessions.

Is the obligation to pray standing absolute? Not when illness strikes. This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle: valid excuses (a'dhar) such as travel, illness, or coercion can alter the rulings of acts of worship (ibadat). This principle unlocks the entire system of rukhsah (concessions) in Islamic law, allowing believers to fulfill their duties without undue hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Recognize the maxim: 'Acts of worship are affected by valid excuses.' This means the default ruling (azimah) can be lifted or modified due to a legitimate reason.
This is a general maxim (qa'idah) in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Define Valid Excuses
List the classical excuses: travel (safar), illness (marad), coercion (ikrah), forgetfulness (nisyan), and necessity (darurah). Each has specific conditions and impacts.
The exact scope varies across schools; Hanafis include more excuses than some others.
3
Apply to Worship Scenarios
For each excuse, state how it modifies the ruling. For example: a sick person may pray sitting (qaidan); a traveler may shorten (qasr) four-rak'ah prayers to two; a forgetful person performs sajdah al-sahw.
These applications are detailed in later lessons on prayer, fasting, etc.

Prayers Fall at Death
Ruling that missed prayers are not obligated after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the obligation of prayer terminates at death and distinguish it from financial obligations that survive death.

Core Idea
Prayer is a personal obligation that ends with death; no qada or compensation is required from the estate.

This lesson presents a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh: the obligation of salah (prayer) is lifted upon death. Unlike debts or zakat, which remain due from the deceased's estate, missed prayers are not transferable. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly advising on the duties of the deceased and their heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Recognize that the ruling concerns the obligation of prayer, not the act of prayer itself. The statement 'Prayer falls with death' means the duty to perform (including missed prayers) ceases upon death.
Personal physical acts of worship are not inheritable.
2
Understand 'tasqutu'
The verb 'tasqutu' (تسقط) means 'falls, is dropped, becomes void.' In legal context, it indicates the obligation is completely removed with no remaining liability.
Contrast with obligations that are 'thabit' (established) after death.
3
Contrast with financial obligations
Debts, zakat, and other financial rights of others remain due from the estate. This ruling applies specifically to purely personal acts of worship where no financial right is involved.
Hanafi scholars derive this from hadith: 'When a person dies, all his deeds cease except three...' (excluding prescribed acts).

No Compensation for Missed Prayers After Death
Ruling that compensation for prayer is not possible after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that missed obligatory prayers cannot be compensated after death and explain the rationale based on the physical nature of salah.

Core Idea
No fidyah or proxy can substitute for missed prayers after death.

The Hanafi school holds a firm position: once a person dies, there is no way to make up for missed obligatory prayers. This lesson explores the basis of this ruling, distinguishing prayer from other acts of worship like fasting where compensation is accepted. Understanding this helps avoid common misconceptions about post-mortem obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is the compensation (ruju') for missed obligatory prayers after death. The ruling is that it is not permissible; there is no return.
This is a negative ruling: compensation is invalid.
2
Understand the rationale
Salah is a physical act of worship ('ibadah badaniyyah) that requires the person's own body and intention. After death, the body is gone, so proxy or monetary fidyah cannot fulfill the obligation.
This contrasts with fasting, where fidyah is accepted for missed fasts if death occurs before making them up, because fasting is considered a 'partial' physical act.
3
Recognize the implications for practice
Upon death, the family cannot offer fidyah to compensate missed prayers. However, they can give charity and make dua for the deceased, which may benefit the deceased's rank but does not erase the prayer obligation.
This ruling prevents the misconception that wealth can substitute for personal worship.

Prayer Obligation Lapses at Death
Ruling on missed prayers after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the obligation of prayer lapses with death and explain why it cannot be compensated after death.

Core Idea
Salat is a personal physical obligation that ceases entirely at death; no qada or fidyah is permitted.

This lesson addresses a fundamental distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the nature of the prayer obligation. Unlike financial duties such as zakat or debts, prayer is a direct bodily act that cannot be performed or substituted after death. This ruling is concise but carries significant implications for inheritance, estate planning, and the understanding of personal responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
Recognize that the passage refers to the ritual prayer (salat), which is a fard 'ayn obligation on every adult Muslim. The ruling given is about its termination upon death.
The passage does not define salat; it assumes this knowledge.
2
Understand the meaning of 'lapses' (tasqutu)
The verb 'tasqutu' indicates a complete fall/removal of the obligation. There is no transfer, substitution, or pending liability after death.
In Arabic, suqut implies the obligation is lifted permanently.
3
Contrast with other obligations that survive death
Compare with zakat, debts, or expiations: these may remain as claims on the estate. Prayer, being purely physical, cannot be delegated or compensated.
This distinction is unique to ibadat that require physical action with no financial alternative.
4
State the practical consequence
Heirs are not required to pray for the deceased, nor can they pay any penalty. The missed prayers are simply waived by Allah.
This reinforces the personal nature of the spiritual responsibility.

The Maxim of the Vanquished: Application to Breastfeeding
Explaining the principle 'al-maghlub ghayr mawjud hukman' applied to breastfeeding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-maghlub ghayr mawjud hukman' to determine the establishment of hurmat al-rada' in mixed breastfeeding scenarios.

Core Idea
When milk is predominant in a mixture, the vanquished water is legally nonexistent—so the prohibition of marriage due to breastfeeding is established.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a fundamental legal maxim of the Hanafi school: the vanquished element in a mixture is treated as nonexistent for legal rulings. The author applies this to a practical question of breastfeeding (rida'). When a woman's milk is mixed with water and fed to a child, does the prohibition of marriage (hurmat al-rada') arise? The answer depends on which component is predominant.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its meaning
Identify the principle 'al-maghlub ghayr mawjud hukman' and understand that the predominant element governs the ruling while the vanquished is disregarded.
This is a key legal maxim in Hanafi fiqh for mixtures.
2
Apply to breastfeeding mixture
Consider the scenario: milk and water are mixed and fed to an infant. Determine which is predominant: if milk, the mixture is legally all milk; if water, legally all water.
The ruling depends on quantitative predominance, not just presence.
3
Identify the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi ruling: hurmat al-rada' is established only if milk is predominant. Proof: the maxim itself, reinforced by an analogy (qiyas) with oaths—swearing not to drink milk and consuming a water-dominant mixture does not break the oath.
The analogy shows that custom treats the vanquished element as nonexistent.
4
Respond to the opponent's objection
An opponent (possibly Shafi'i) argues that oaths are based on custom, so the analogy is weak. The better Hanafi response is to refine the effective cause ('illa).
The opponent's objection forces a deeper analysis.
5
Refine the argument: effective cause is nourishment
The prohibition in breastfeeding is not due to mere existence of milk but to its effect: inshaz al-'azm wa inbat al-lahm (growing bones and developing flesh). Vanquished milk does not provide sufficient nourishment to achieve this effect, so no prohibition arises.
This demonstrates Hanafi istihsan and attention to the underlying purpose.

Nafaqah for a Minor Wife: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i Dispute
Establishes principle that nafaqah is for habs enabling marital intimacy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on nafaqah for a minor wife, explain the rationale based on the principle of complete ihtibas, and distinguish the Shafi'i position with its counter-argument.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is compensation for the wife's complete availability for intimacy, not just for the marriage contract.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) for a minor wife who is too young for sexual intercourse. The Hanafi school, based on the principle that nafaqah is tied to complete ihtibas (availability) enabling the marital objective, holds that no nafaqah is due. The Shafi'i school, viewing nafaqah as a compensation for ownership, argues that it is obligatory regardless of age. Understanding this difference reveals the foundational legal theories underlying the rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Ruling
Recall the issue: Is a husband obligated to provide nafaqah for his minor wife who cannot yet engage in intercourse? The Hanafi answer is no; the Shafi'i answer is yes.
This is a practical difference that affects family law.
2
State the Hanafi Position and Its Proof
The Hanafi position is derived from the principle that nafaqah is due only when the wife's ihtibas is complete and serves as a means to the objective of marriage (intimacy). Since a minor's ihtibas is incomplete (she cannot engage in intercourse), nafaqah is not obligatory.
The proof is based on the meaning of ihtibas in the hidayah text: 'the intended meaning of habs is that which is a means to the objective of marriage'.
3
State the Shafi'i Position and Its Proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that nafaqah is a compensation for the husband's ownership of the wife (milk al-nikah) itself, analogous to a master's obligation to provide for his slave. Thus, ownership alone triggers nafaqah, regardless of age.
The Shafi'i proof analogizes nafaqah to the maintenance of a female slave owned outright.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi school responds by distinguishing between mahr (dower) and nafaqah. Mahr is the compensation for ownership; nafaqah is compensation for ihtibas. Combining two compensations for the same thing (ownership) is invalid in Hanafi law. Therefore, the minor wife receives mahr but not nafaqah.
This response hinges on the Hanafi principle that two compensations cannot be due for a single exchange.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: in a Hanafi jurisdiction, a minor wife does not receive nafaqah; in a Shafi'i jurisdiction, she does. This affects the husband's financial obligations.
The dispute illustrates the importance of identifying the legal basis (illah) for a ruling.

(11) Indeed, the habs that necessitates [nafaqah] is that which is a means to an objective deserved through marriage. Translation: The habs that makes nafaqah obligatory is that which is a means to obtain the objective established through marriage. Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that habs does not obligate nafaqah absolutely, rather the habs that obligates nafaqah is that which is a means to the objective that the husband has become entitled to due to marriage, i.e., it is a means to intercourse. And if there is habs but it is not a means to the objective, then it does not obligate nafaqah, i.e., due to such habs nafaqah will not be obligatory upon the husband. The author of al-Hidayah, by mentioning the mentioned principle, is conveying that if the wife is so young that benefit cannot be taken from her, i.e., she is not capable of intercourse, then nafaqah is not obligatory upon the husband for her, because here although there is habs, it is not the habs that obligates nafaqah, because this habs is deficient; it is not a means to the objective of marriage, i.e., intercourse. Such habs does not obligate nafaqah. In the case under discussion, the inability to have intercourse is on the part of the woman, as she is a minor, so she will not be entitled to nafaqah, because nafaqah becomes obligatory from habs that is complete and in which there is ability for the objective of marriage, i.e., intercourse and enjoyment, as is proper. And here such complete habs does not exist, so this also does not obligate nafaqah. Al-Hidayah: ... and as for the second, it is a woman who is minor and cannot be enjoyed, because the intended meaning of habs is that which is a means to the objective of marriage, and that [enjoyment] is prevented due to a reason from her side, so it becomes like non-existence. Al-Ikhtiyar ... Al-Hidayah ... His statement: 'and a minor woman who is not to be penetrated, i.e., there is no nafaqah for the minor when she cannot endure intercourse, because the prevention of enjoyment is due to a meaning in her, and the habs that obligates is that which is a means to the objective deserved through marriage, and it is not found, contrary to the sick woman as will come. And al-Shafi'i said: she has nafaqah, because it is a compensation for ownership according to him, just as in the case of a female slave owned by right hand. And we [Hanafis] hold that the mahr is the compensation for ownership, and two compensations cannot be combined for one thing, so she gets the mahr but not the nafaqah.'

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) for a minor wife who is too young for intercourse. The Hanafi school holds that nafaqah is not obligatory because the legal basis for nafaqah is the wife's complete habs (restraint/availability) that enables the husband to achieve the primary objective of marriage, which is intimacy. If the wife is a minor and cannot engage in intercourse, her habs is incomplete; it is not a means to the marital objective. Thus, nafaqah is not due. The author quotes from al-Hidayah and other sources to support this. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i argues that nafaqah is a compensation for the ownership of the wife (milk al-nikah) itself, similar to a slave's nafaqah being due from the master. Therefore, according to him, nafaqah is obligatory for a minor wife because the husband owns her. The Hanafi response is that mahr (dower) is the compensation for ownership, and two compensations (mahr and nafaqah) cannot be combined for one thing (ownership). Hence, the wife receives mahr, not nafaqah. This illustrates a fundamental difference in the legal theory of nafaqah between the schools.

Key Terms
احتباس (ihtibās): The wife's restraint or being at the husband's disposal; her availability for marital intimacy.
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance payments owed by husband to wife, including food, clothing, and shelter.
عوض (ʿiwaḍ): Compensation or exchange; something given in return for something else.
مهر (mahr): The dower given by husband to wife as a mandatory gift upon marriage.

The Foundling is Free and Muslim: Three Proofs from Al-Hidayah
Applying maxim al-hukm lil-ghalib to foundling's status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that a foundling in Dar al-Islam is free and Muslim and identify the three proofs from the passage.

Core Idea
A foundling is presumed free and Muslim based on the land, the majority, and the origin of humanity.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a fundamental Hanafi ruling about the status of an abandoned child (al-laqiṭ) found in a Muslim land. The school does not leave the child's status uncertain but gives clear reasoning: the child is free and Muslim. The author provides three independent proofs, each reinforcing the same conclusion. Pay close attention to how the maxim 'al-hukm lil-ghālib' (the ruling follows the majority) operates here.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the Arabic passage and note that the ruling is about a foundling (laqiṭ) discovered in Dar al-Islam. The text begins with 'wa huwa al-madhhab' indicating this is the relied-upon position.
The ruling applies regardless of the finder's status.
2
Analyze the first proof: consideration of the land
The land (dar) is characterized as free and Muslim. The author argues that whoever is in it is free Muslim by the apparent (zahir) condition. Understand that this is a presumption based on the dominant character of the territory.
This is a form of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity).
3
Analyze the second proof: consideration of the majority
The text invokes the maxim 'al-hukm lil-ghalib': the ruling follows the majority. Since most residents of Dar al-Islam are free Muslims, the foundling is adjudged free Muslim. This is a probabilistic reasoning.
This proof relies on empirical observation, not a direct text.
4
Analyze the third proof: consideration of origin
All humans descend from Adam and Eve, who were free. Therefore, freedom is the original state (asl) of every human. The foundling inherits this default status.
This is a stronger proof because it goes back to creation.
5
Synthesize and note the school's reliance
All three proofs lead to the same ruling. Al-Hidayah explicitly states that this is the madhhab. Understand that the Hanafi school prefers these presumptions over potential doubts.
The ruling is mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a ruling with proof. It establishes that a foundling (laqit) discovered in Dar al-Islam is considered free and Muslim. The author provides three lines of reasoning: (1) Consideration of the land: Dar al-Islam is characterized by freedom and Islam, so its inhabitants are presumed to be free Muslims in the apparent sense. (2) Consideration of the majority: Most people in Dar al-Islam are free Muslims, and the legal maxim 'al-hukm lil-ghalib' (ruling follows the majority) applies. (3) Consideration of origin: All humans descend from Adam and Eve, who were free, so freedom is the default. Thus, the foundling is adjudged free.
