Purification of Visible and Non-Visible Impurities
This passage explains the purification methods for the two categories of impurity: visible and non-visible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between visible and non-visible impurity and state the Hanafi method for purifying each, including the ruling on traces that are difficult to remove.

Core Idea
Visible impurity is purified by removing its essence, even once; non-visible requires three washes with wringing.

The Hanafi school classifies impurities into two types: visible and non-visible, each with a distinct purification method. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying taharah rulings in daily life. This passage from Nur al-Idah, with commentary from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, lays out the rules clearly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the impurity type
Determine if the impurity is visible (seen after drying) or non-visible (not seen after drying). The text gives examples: blood and feces are visible; urine is non-visible.
2
Apply the purification method
For visible: remove the essence even once. For non-visible: wash three times with wringing each time.
Note that if the essence is not removed after three washes, continue until it is removed.
3
Handle traces
If a trace (color or odor) remains that is difficult to remove (requires soap or special effort), it is excused and does not affect purity.
The text specifies that odor is excused absolutely; color is excused only if removal is difficult.
4
Account for intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There is a difference of opinion on the number of washes after the essence is removed. The correct view is that once is sufficient; some said twice or thrice.
The mufta bihi position is the correct one.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive ruling on how to purify items that have become impure. It distinguishes between visible impurity (like blood and feces) and non-visible impurity (like urine). For visible impurity, it is enough to remove the essence itself, even once; if a trace remains that is difficult to remove (like color or odor that cannot be eliminated without special effort), it is excused. For non-visible impurity, washing three times with wringing each time is required, based on the dominant opinion. The passage then discusses purification with liquids: water (both pure and used) is valid, as are other liquids that actively remove impurity, such as vinegar and rose water. Oils and milk do not qualify. There is a difference of opinion about purification with urine; the correct view is that it is not effective. Finally, the purification of the khuff (leather sock) is addressed: if the impurity has a mass (something that remains visible after drying), rubbing on the ground suffices; if it is non-visible (like urine), washing is necessary. The same applies to similar items. The ruling is specific to the khuff because garments and body are not purified by rubbing except in the case of semen.

Key Terms
مرئية (mar'iyyah): Visible impurity that can be seen after drying, e.g., blood, feces.
غير مرئية (ghayr mar'iyyah): Non-visible impurity not seen after drying, e.g., urine.
جرم (jurm): Mass or substance of impurity that remains after drying on the surface (e.g., on a khuff).
مائع مزيل (mā'i' muzīl): Liquid that removes impurity, like water, vinegar, rose water. Excludes oils, milk.

Purification with Liquids: Water and Other Removers
This passage discusses the liquids that can be used for purification, the conditions for their validity, and the controversy around urine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which liquids are valid for purifying impurity and evaluate the Hanafi position on the use of urine for purification.

Core Idea
Any liquid that actively removes impurity suffices for purification; water is primary, but other liquids like vinegar are also valid, while urine is not.

The Hanafi school permits purifying impure items with any liquid that effectively removes the impurity, not just water. This passage discusses the types of liquids that can be used, including the controversial issue of using urine. Understanding this expands the practical options for taharah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the impurity type
The text states that the ruling applies to actual impurity (najisah haqiqiyyah), not hukmiyyah. Impurity can be on garment or body.
2
Recognize valid liquids
Valid liquids are those that are 'muzil' (removing). Water (absolute and used) and other liquids like vinegar and rose water are valid. Oils, ghee, and milk are invalid.
The condition is the liquid's ability to remove, not its purity.
3
Analyze the urine dispute
There is a difference of opinion on purifying with urine. Some consider it valid (the weaker view), but al-Sarakhsi corrected that it is not valid. The correct view is that purification by urine does not occur.
The practical consequence: if someone swore there was no blood in something and washed it with urine, on the correct view he breaks his oath.
4
Apply the ruling
When an item is impure, use water or any removing liquid. If only urine is available, purification is not considered effective according to the correct view.

Purification of the Khuff: Rubbing vs. Washing
This passage gives the ruling for purifying a khuff, distinguishing between impurities with and without mass.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how a khuff (leather sock) is purified when affected by impurity with or without mass (jurm).

Core Idea
For a khuff, rubbing suffices if the impurity has mass; otherwise washing is required.

The khuff (leather sock) has a special ruling for purification due to its material and usage. The key factor is whether the impurity has mass (jurm) — something that remains visible after drying. This passage from Nur al-Idah explains the distinction clearly, along with qualifications and examples.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the impurity has mass (jurm)
If the impurity leaves a visible residue after drying (e.g., feces, blood), it has mass. If it does not leave a visible residue (e.g., urine), it has no mass.
The text defines mass as what remains after drying on the surface of the khuff.
2
Apply the correct purification method
If the impurity has mass, rubbing (dalk) on the ground suffices to remove it. If no mass, washing with water is necessary.
3
Note the scope of the ruling
This ruling is specific to the khuff and similar items (e.g., shoes). Garments and body are not purified by rubbing except in the case of semen.
The text explicitly says 'qayyada bil-khuff' (qualified by khuff) because garments and body are not purified by rubbing except for semen.
4
Understand the extension of 'mass'
Mass can come from the impurity itself or from something else, e.g., wine on a khuff that mixes with sand and solidifies. Rubbing off the solidified mass purifies the khuff.
This is the correct view according to al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Purification of Visible and Non-Visible Impurities (Najasah)
Defining visible and non-visible impurity and their purification methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings for purifying visible and non-visible najasah, including the treatment of difficult-to-remove traces.

Core Idea
For visible impurity, removal of substance suffices; for non-visible, three washes with squeezing are required.

This passage from Nur al-Idah, with commentary from later Hanafi authorities, establishes the fundamental distinction between visible and non-visible impurities. The ruling hinges on whether the impurity can be seen after drying and whether the trace is difficult to remove. Understanding this distinction is essential for correct purification before prayer.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the impurity
Determine if the najasah is visible (seen after drying, e.g., blood, feces) or non-visible (not seen after drying, e.g., urine). This classification dictates the purification method.
The criterion is visibility after the impurity dries, not wetness.
2
Apply the purification method for visible najasah
For visible najasah, the correct view is that removal of the substance ('ayn) purifies, even with one wash. If a trace (colour or smell) remains but is difficult to remove (requires soap, hot water, etc.), it is excused and does not prevent validity. Repetition of washing is not required.
Difficult removal is defined as needing something other than water alone.
3
Apply the purification method for non-visible najasah
For non-visible najasah, three washes with squeezing each time are required. This is based on predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann) that cleanliness is achieved.
If three washes do not remove the trace, continue until predominant probability is reached.
4
Recognise exceptions where washing is not needed
Some items are purified by rubbing, wiping, or drying: the khuff (leather sock) by rubbing, semen by rubbing, a sword by wiping, and the earth by drying. The principle is removal of the substance, not necessarily washing with water.
These exceptions are based on the concept of removal of 'ayn without water.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which rubbing (dalk) suffices for purifying a khuff and distinguish when washing is required.

Rubbing suffices for khuff if impurity leaves a trace after drying.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi ruling on purifying the khuff (leather sock). The key distinction is whether the impurity has a visible substance (jirm) after drying. If it does, simple rubbing on the ground suffices; otherwise washing is necessary. This ruling is unique to the Hanafi school and is supported by the principle of ease in purification.

1
Define the object and impurity
The khuff is a leather sock; identify whether the impurity has substance (jirm) – that which remains after drying.

Apply the ruling for jirm
If the impurity has jirm, rubbing (dalk) suffices even if the impurity is wet initially.

Garment and body are not purified by rubbing except in the case of semen.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a sword and similar solid objects are purified by wiping (mash) and identify the conditions for this method.

Core Idea
Wiping cleans the sword; no water needed.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling for purifying smooth, hard items like swords and sandals. Unlike absorbent materials, these can be purified simply by wiping with a dry cloth or dust. This ruling is based on the principle that the impurity does not penetrate and can be removed mechanically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object and its material
The sword and similar items (e.g., knife, smooth stone, sandal) are non-absorbent, hard surfaces.
Also includes metal tools.
2
State the ruling
These items are purified by wiping (mash) with a dry cloth, dust, or soil. The majority of Hanafi scholars support this.
The impurity is removed by the friction of wiping.
3
Specify the method
Wipe with a rag, dust, or directly on the ground. The goal is to remove the impurity visibly.
If the impurity is solid, it may be brushed off.
4
Note the distinction from absorbent items
Absorbent items like cloth or the khuff require washing or rubbing; the sword is smooth and non-porous.
5
Recognize the authority
This ruling is from the majority of scholars (mashayikh) and is considered reliable (mufta bihi).
It is part of the zahir al-riwayah.

Four Methods of Purification in Hanafi Fiqh
Rulings on purification by drying, chemical change, rubbing semen, and tanning hide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four Hanafi methods of purification (drying earth, istihalah, rubbing dry semen, and tanning hide) to correctly determine when an object becomes pure for prayer.

Core Idea
Purification occurs by drying, chemical change, rubbing, or tanning—each with its own conditions.

This passage from Nur al-Idah presents four distinct scenarios of purification. Each method—drying of earth, chemical transformation of an impurity, rubbing of dry semen, and tanning of animal hide—has specific conditions that must be met. Understanding these will help you avoid common mistakes in practical purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four purification scenarios
Scan the text and list the four cases: earth drying, chemical change of impurity, semen (dry/wet), and hide tanning. Note that each has a separate ruling and condition set.
The passage is structured as a series of rulings linked by 'wa' (and).
2
Analyze earth drying conditions
The text states that earth is purified when the trace (taste, color, smell) disappears and the earth dries. However, this only makes the earth tahir (pure for prayer), not tahur (purifying for tayammum). Also, only earth is purified this way—not garments, mats, or bodies.
The drying can be by sun, fire, wind, or shade; no specific method required.
3
Understand chemical change (istihalah)
If an impurity undergoes a complete chemical transformation (e.g., becomes salt or burns to ash), it becomes pure without washing. This is a Hanafi principle accepted by later scholars.
Some other schools do not accept istihalah as a purifier.
4
Examine semen purification
Dry semen is purified by rubbing (fark) until it crumbles; even if a trace remains. Wet semen requires washing. The ruling applies equally to men and women, and to garments including linings.
There is a minor ikhtilaf on woman's semen, but the relied-upon view is equality.
5
Study hide tanning
The hide of a dead animal (including elephant, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is purified by genuine tanning using astringent substances like qaraz. Imam Muhammad holds the elephant is najis al-ayn, but the mufta bihi is that tanning purifies it.
Tanning only purifies the hide; the flesh remains impure.

Purification by Drying, Wiping, Rubbing, and Transformation
Rulings on purification of earth via jufaf, swords via wiping, and semen via rubbing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purification of earth by drying, swords by wiping, and semen by rubbing, and distinguish between tahir and tahur.

Core Idea
Earth dried after impurity is pure for prayer but not for tayammum; dried semen is purified by rubbing.

In this lesson, we examine several distinct rulings on purification that rely on physical change rather than water. These rulings highlight the Hanafi school's nuanced understanding of purity and its degrees—tahir (pure) versus tahur (purifying). Understanding these concepts is essential for correctly applying taharah in everyday situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the different items and their purification methods
List each item: earth, sword/similar, trees/plants, transformed substances, semen. Note the purification method for each.
The passage covers multiple rulings; treat each as a separate case.
2
Understand the condition of jufaf for earth
Earth is purified by drying (jufaf) only after all traces of impurity are gone. The outcome: the earth becomes tahir (pure for salah) but not tahur (cannot be used for tayammum).
This is a unique Hanafi distinction.
3
Recognize the rule for swords and similar objects
Swords and similar non-porous items are purified by wiping (mas-h) on earth or with a cloth. This is based on the majority of shaykhs.
The method is akin to cleaning a sandal on the ground.
4
Analyze istihalah (transformation)
If a najasah changes into a completely different substance (e.g., becomes salt or ash), it becomes pure. This is because the original impurity is no longer present.
This principle applies to many modern scenarios, like burning najis material.
5
Apply the ruling for semen
Dry semen is purified by rubbing (dalk) until it flakes off; wet semen must be washed. This applies to both garment and body.
This is specific to the Hanafi school; Shafi'is require washing even when dry.

Purification of Dead Animal Hide by Tanning and Slaughter
Rulings on purification of hide by tanning and slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of tanning that purify hide, identify exceptions, and explain the effect of Islamic slaughter on the hide of non-edible animals.

Core Idea
Tanning purifies any dead animal hide except pig and human; slaughter purifies the hide of non-edible animals but not their flesh.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on how the hide of a dead animal can become pure. The process of tanning—whether real or effective—removes impurity, but with exceptions. Furthermore, Islamic slaughter has a specific effect on the hide of animals not permissible to eat. Understanding these rulings is essential for using animal products in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is purifying the hide of a dead animal (maytah). The ruling: it becomes pure (tahir) by dibagh (tanning).
This applies to all dead animals except pig and human.
2
Distinguish types of tanning
Real tanning (dibagh haqiqi) uses astringent substances like qaraz. Legal/effective tanning (dibagh hukmi) includes dusting (tatrib) and sun-drying (tashmis) if they act as a tanning agent.
Both methods yield purification.
3
Note exceptions
Pig hide and human hide are not purified by tanning. Pig is intrinsically impure (najis al-ayn); human hide is excluded due to dignity.
These remain impure even after tanning.
4
Apply the rule of Islamic slaughter
For non-edible animals (e.g., beasts of prey), if slaughtered Islamically (dhabh), the hide becomes pure, but the flesh does not.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Recognize the authority level
These rulings are from Zahir al-Riwayah and are the relied-upon fatwa.
Intra-Hanafi differences exist (e.g., elephant), but the above is standard.

This passage from Nur al-Idah addresses a practical concern: how to pray when impurity is present on one's clothing or prayer mat. The act is performing prayer while there is impurity on a surface (e.g., prayer mat, garment, turban) that is not on the worshipper's body but on the material used in prayer.

If a garment's lining is impure but the outer is pure, prayer is valid only if the garment is not mudarab (stitched such that the sides and middle are joined). If mudarab, it becomes like one garment and the impurity transfers, invalidating prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of awrah for a man, a slave woman, and a free woman, and explain the recommended method for a naked person's prayer with gestures.

Core Idea
Covering awrah is a condition for prayer; a naked person should pray sitting with gestures to maximize cover.

In this passage from Nur al-Idah, the author first defines the boundaries of awrah for different categories of people: free man, slave woman, and free woman. Then, he addresses the exceptional case of a person who has no clothing, providing the ruling for how such a person should pray. The rulings emphasize the principle of covering as much as possible while preserving the essential acts of prayer. Through this lesson, you will gain precision in applying these definitions and understand the legal reasoning behind the recommended posture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three awrah definitions
Parse the passage to extract the awrah boundaries for a free man (navel to knee), a slave woman (same as man plus belly and back), and a free woman (entire body except face, hands, feet).

2
Understand the naked prayer ruling
Read the ruling: it is recommended (mandub) to pray sitting with gestures (ima'), stretching legs towards the qiblah. This is because it provides maximum covering. Standing with gestures or actual bowing and prostrating is valid but less preferred.

3
Examine intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Note the disagreement: some scholars say the naked person should stand at night because darkness covers. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is to sit in both cases, as per al-Bahr al-Raiq.
The key principle: covering awrah outweighs performing pillars standing.
4
Apply the principle of covering
Recognize that the ruling prioritizes covering awrah over standing. If the naked person chooses to stand, the prayer is still valid because the three pillars (qiyam, ruku, sujud) are completed, but sitting with gestures is better because it ensures more covering.
This shows the hierarchy: condition (shart) of covering takes precedence when possible.
5
Map to modern context
Consider how these definitions apply today: for example, a free woman must cover all except face, hands, feet for prayer validity, but due to fitna, later scholars recommend covering the face. The naked prayer rule applies in situations of complete lack of clothing (e.g., emergency).
The ruling is not abrogated; it remains for genuine necessity.

Gesturing While Lying and the Obligation of Qada for the Sick
Primary ruling on the prayer of the sick with gesturing and the qada dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid postures for a sick person's prayer by gesturing and apply the Hanafi ruling on whether qada is required if inability lasts more than five prayers.

Core Idea
Inability to gesture for over five prayers drops the obligation of qada entirely.

This lesson addresses the core ruling for a sick person who cannot sit: he prays by gesturing while lying down. We also explore a key dispute within the Hanafi school regarding whether missed prayers must be made up if the inability continues beyond five prayer times. Understanding this exception is crucial for understanding the school's approach to hardship (mashaqqah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary method of prayer when sitting is impossible
The passage states that if sitting is difficult, the sick person gestures (īmā') while lying on his back or side, with preference for the back.
The gesture substitutes for bowing and prostration.
2
Understand the necessary adjustments for facing the qibla
A pillow is placed under the head so the face faces the qibla, not the sky, and knees are raised to avoid pointing feet towards the qibla.
This avoids makruh.
3
Recognize the condition when even gesturing is impossible
If gesturing is impossible, prayer is deferred as long as the person understands speech.
This is the correct position according to al-Hidayah.
4
Analyze the dispute on qada when inability lasts more than five prayers
The passage presents the position that if inability to gesture continues for more than five prayers, qada is dropped, even if the person understood speech. This is the chosen view of al-Hidayah, Qadi Khan, and others.
There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: some scholars require qada for up to a day and a night.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position is that if inability lasts more than five prayers, no qada is required; if five or fewer, qada is required.
This is based on the principle of extended hardship.

Gesturing with Eyes, Heart, and Other Parts; Building on Prayer; and Rulings for Insanity and Death
Detailed rulings on alternative gestures, building on prayer, and final rules for insurmountable inability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on gesturing with eyes, eyebrows, and heart, apply the rule for building on a prayer when ability changes, and state the rulings on qada for insanity/unconsciousness and bequests upon death.

Core Idea
Gesturing with the heart alone invalidates the prayer; for eyes, it's permissible but disputed; extended insanity (>5 prayers) drops qada; no bequest needed if death occurs in continuous inability.

This lesson covers the detailed rulings for cases where a sick person cannot gesture with his head: can he use his eyes, eyebrows, or heart? We also explore what happens if a person's condition improves or worsens during prayer, and the final rules for those who die or become insane without being able to pray. These details ensure that the obligation of prayer is fulfilled to the maximum extent possible while respecting genuine hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute on gesturing with eyes, eyebrows, and heart
The passage presents the views of Zufr, al-Shafi'i, al-Hasan ibn Hayy, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on whether a sick person can gesture with these parts when unable to gesture with the head.
Abu Yusuf permits eyes only; Muhammad doubts eyes and rejects heart.
2
Understand the Hanafi consensus on heart gesture
All Hanafi authorities agree that gesturing with the heart alone is invalid because it is an internal action, not a physical movement resembling ruku' and sujud.
This contrasts with some other schools.
3
Learn the ruling on building (bina') upon a prayer when ability changes
If a person starts prayer standing and then becomes unable, he continues according to his new ability (sitting, gesturing). But if he starts with gestures and then recovers, he does not build on that prayer but starts anew.
This is based on the idea that the initial valid part is only valid under the original ability.
4
Apply the rule for insanity and unconsciousness
If a person becomes insane or unconscious for five prayers or less, he must make them up. If more than five, no qada is required.
This is analogized to the sick person's qada rule.
5
Determine the ruling on bequest (wasiyyah) after death
If a sick person dies without being able to pray with gestures, no bequest is required for missed prayers. Similarly, if a traveler or sick person dies after breaking a fast in Ramadan before recovery, no bequest for fidyah is needed.
This is because the inability was continuous and insurmountable.

Sunnah actions for the dying including turning to qibla, talqin, and recitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah actions for the dying person and distinguish between the prescribed and disputed forms of talqin.

Core Idea
Turn the dying to qibla, prompt gently with shahada, and recite Yasin — sunnah acts that bring mercy at the moment of death.

This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the recommended (sunnah) practices for the dying person (muhtadar). These acts—turning to qibla, gentle prompting of the shahada, and recitation of specific surahs—are meant to ease the transition and remind the dying of faith. The text also touches on a subtle ikhtilaf regarding whether talqin (prompting) should be performed after burial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah acts
List all the recommended actions mentioned: turning to qibla, talqin, visitation, recitation of Yasin and Ra'd.
2
Understand the method of tawjih
Note that the dying should be turned on his right side facing qibla; if not possible, lying on the back with head raised is allowed so the face points to qibla.
This shows flexibility in applying the sunnah.
3
Analyze the proper talqin
Talqin is done gently without insistence (ghayri ilhah) and the dying is not commanded to say the shahada. It is a reminder, not a demand.
Common mistake: forcing the shahada contradicts the sunnah.
4
Examine the ikhtilaf on talqin after burial
The text presents three views: mashru (prescribed), la yulaqqan (not prompted), or neither commanded nor forbidden. The relied-upon Hanafi view is that it is mashru.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf among scholars.
5

Surah Yasin is recommended; Surah al-Ra'd is also considered good (istihsan). These surahs are believed to ease the soul's departure.
Recitation should be done quietly in the presence of the dying.

Dispute on Exiting Menstruating and Junub from the Dying: Preference, Not Obligation
Disagreement on exiting the dying person's room

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi positions on whether menstruating, postpartum, and junub individuals should leave the dying person, and identify the dispute as one of priority (awlawiyya) rather than obligation (wujub).

Core Idea
The dispute is about recommended or merely permissible, not wajib or haram.

This passage addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi disagreement: should a menstruating woman, a postpartum woman, or a person in a state of major ritual impurity (junub) leave the room of someone who is dying? The answer is not a single ruling but a difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars. Importantly, the disagreement is not about obligation (wajib) or prohibition (haram), but about what is preferable (awlawiyya). Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating how the Hanafi school accommodates multiple valid views within its fold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed parties
Notice that the text mentions three categories: menstruating woman (ha'id), postpartum woman (nafsa'), and the one in major impurity (junub). The act in question is their exit ('ikhraj) from the presence of the dying person.
The term 'min 'inda al-mukhtasar' indicates the passage is from Al-Mukhtasar by Al-Quduri.
2
Locate the two positions
One position recommends that they leave because the angels of mercy avoid places where such ritually impure persons are present. The other position allows them to stay out of compassion or need (shafaqah or ihtiyaj).
Both views are attributed to Hanafi scholars; no external madhhab is mentioned.
3
Understand the basis for each view
The first view relies on the hadith about angels not entering a house with a picture or a dog, extended to impurity by analogy. The second view prioritizes the emotional and practical needs of the dying person who may require comfort or care from these individuals.
The commentary calls this 'ikhtilaf fi al-awlawiyya wa 'adamiha'.
4
Note the nature of the dispute
The text explicitly states this is a difference in priority (awlawiyya), not in obligation. This means neither view declares the other's action sinful or invalid.
This is a hallmark of intra-madhhab flexibility.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In practice, a Hanafi may follow either opinion. Some recommend sending them out; others allow them to stay. A mufti can give either ruling based on circumstances.
The more cautious view is to send them out if possible, but staying is not prohibited.

Washing the Deceased: Rulings and Procedure
Exposition of funeral washing procedures and related rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sequence of actions in washing the deceased and identify the Hanafi rulings on each step, distinguishing between fard, wajib, and sunnah.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a communal obligation with detailed steps; every action has a specific ruling from fard to sunnah.

This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the Hanafi procedure for ghusl al-mayyit. It begins immediately after death with preparing the body, then moves to washing, perfuming, and drying. Understanding these steps ensures the obligation is fulfilled correctly according to the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify post-death actions
Read the opening lines: tying the jaw, closing the eyes, reciting the supplication. Note the ruling: these are recommended (mustahabb).
The supplication is specific; memorize it for practice.
2
Learn the placement and prohibitions
The hands are placed by the sides (not on chest). Reciting Quran is makruh until after washing. Informing people is mustahabb.
The iron piece on the stomach prevents bloating.
3
Understand the wudu and washing process
The deceased is given wudu like the living, but without rinsing mouth and nose unless junub. Washing is done with sidr water, three times, turning the body.
If impure water is used, the ghusl is invalid.
4
Apply the perfuming rules
Hanut (perfume) on head and beard, camphor on prostration points. Nails and hair are not cut.
Using cotton for orifices is discouraged in the apparent narrations.
5
Recognize who may wash
A wife may wash her husband; a husband may not wash his wife. Female slaves may not wash their masters.
If the deceased is a young child who does not understand prayer, wudu is omitted.

The Method of Washing the Deceased
Method and materials for washing the deceased

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi method of washing the deceased, including the water used, the sequence of washing, and the application of hanut and camphor.

Core Idea
Wash the deceased with pure water and katm, turning the body, and apply perfume to the beard and camphor to the prostration points.

This lesson details the prescribed method for giving the deceased their final purification. The Hanafi school emphasizes the use of natural cleansing agents and a specific sequence to ensure thorough washing while maintaining dignity and following the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of water
If sidr or khardal are available, use them; otherwise, use pure water (qarah). The text states 'وإلا فالقراح' meaning otherwise pure water is used.
The preferred cleanser is katm (khatmi) for head and beard.
2
Describe the washing sequence
First wash the head and beard with katm. Then lay the deceased on the left side and wash until water reaches the area adjacent to the bed. Then turn to the right side and wash similarly. Finally, sit him up, support him, and gently wipe the belly.
This ensures complete coverage and expels any remaining contents.
3
Note the drying and perfuming
After washing, dry the body with a cloth. Apply hanut (perfume) to the beard and head, and camphor to the seven prostration points (forehead, nose, hands, knees, feet).
Hanut is a compound perfume; camphor is placed on each limb that touches the ground in sujud.
4
Recognize the prohibition of cotton
The text says 'there is no use of cotton in washing according to the apparent narrations'. Avoid using cotton in the ghusl itself, though it may be used for necessity per later Hanafi scholars.
The sunnah is to avoid cotton; the mufta bihi allows it if needed.

A) It is always disliked regardless of security.
B) It is disliked only if one fears desire or it is lewd.
C) It is never disliked because it is not explicitly mentioned in the Quran.
D) It is disliked only during the day but permissible at night.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school provides a nuanced list of disliked and permissible actions during fasting, with conditions that turn an action from disliked to permissible or even invalidating.
