The Basmalah: Grammatical and Theological Analysis
Linguistic and theological parsing of the basmalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five words of the basmalah, explain the grammatical possibilities for the attached ba, and state the derivation of the word ism according to the Basran and Kufan schools.

Core Idea
The basmalah is not merely a formula; its every word carries precise grammatical and theological weight.

This lesson unpacks the opening section of Nur al-Idah, where the author dissects the basmalah word by word. You will explore why the ba is attached to a specific implied verb, how the word ism is derived, and why the names al-Rahman and al-Rahim differ in comprehensiveness. This analysis is essential for understanding the depth behind even the most familiar Islamic phrase.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Five Words
The basmalah consists of ba, ism, Allah, al-Rahman, and al-Rahim. Recognize each as a distinct component.
These are the five words the author enumerates.
2
Analyze the Ba Preposition
The ba requires a verb or noun to attach to. The author lists eight possibilities based on verb/noun, specific/general, and advanced/delayed. Determine which is preferred.
The preferred view is that the implied verb is specific and delayed, e.g., 'u'allif' (I author).
3
Examine the Derivation of Ism
The word ism is derived either from sumuw (height) according to Basrans or from wasm (mark) according to Kufans. Understand the morphological process for each.
Both derivations are linguistically valid; no tarjih is given.
4
Analyze Lafz al-Jalalah (Allah)
Allah is a proper noun for the Necessary Being, with no predominance (ghalabah). Understand why it is unique.
Unlike terms like 'al-najm' (star) which can predominate, Allah never does.
5
Differentiate al-Rahman and al-Rahim
Al-Rahman is more comprehensive due to its augmented form (increase in letters indicates increase in meaning).
The full discussion resumes later in the text.

Since it is professionally obligatory for every author to do three things: the basmalah, the hamdalah, and praying upon the Prophet, the Shaykh opened his book with it, and gave precedence to the basmalah over others due to the strength of its hadith and in accordance with the style of the Quran. The best is that it is a verb because the basis in actions is verbs, and what acts as a noun like the infinitive and the noun of action is by way of being carried on verbs; and that it is specific because everyone who embarks on something conceals in himself the wording of what he made the basmalah a start for. So the traveler when he says 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' the meaning is 'I travel', and the eater when he says 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' the meaning is 'I eat', and so on; and that it be delayed to indicate restriction, i.e., restriction of exclusivity if addressed to one who believes in partnership in the ruling, so the intended meaning is to refute those polytheists who believe that he starts with the names of their gods while His name is meant, and this is apparent; or restriction of reversal if addressed to one who believes contrary to the ruling, so the intended meaning is to refute those who start with the name of other than Him, not with His name, and this is far; or restriction of specification if addressed to one who is uncertain about the ruling, so the intended meaning is to specify the one who starts with His name for those who are uncertain and doubt whether to start with His name or with another's, and this is also far. Allah al-Rahman al-Rahim. So the truth in my view is to say that its estimation is 'with the name of Allah I author' because you have learned that the best is that it be specific, and so that the blessing encompasses all authorship, unlike the estimation 'I begin' for then blessing is specific to the beginning. And its meaning is seeking assistance or accompaniment in a blessed manner. The best is to make it for accompaniment in the mentioned manner because making it for seeking assistance implies that His name, exalted, is an instrument for the thing, and that contains disrespect, even if it is answered by saying that the intended meaning is that beginning a thing is dependent on His name as a thing is dependent on its instrument. So its origin... The second: al-ism (the name), and its meaning is what indicates a named entity. It is derived, according to the Basrans, from al-sumuw (height) because it rises above its named entity according to them: 'sumuw' on the pattern of 'fu'ul', then it was lightened by dropping its final part, and its first letter was made sukun, and a hamzat al-wasl was introduced to facilitate pronunciation of the sukun, so its pattern became 'if'; and according to the Kufans, from 'wasm' meaning 'mark' because it is a sign for its named entity. We say this and do not say 'from al-simah al-alamah (the mark)' as is well-known because derivation according to them is from verbs, so its origin with them is 'wasm' on the pattern of 'fa'l', then the waw was deleted and replaced by the hamza, so its pattern became 'i'l'. So it is among the nouns whose final parts are deleted according to the first view, and among the nouns whose initial parts are deleted according to the second view. The third: the word of majesty (Lafz al-Jalalah), and it is a proper noun for the essence that necessarily exists. So it is a personal, particular proper noun, and there is no predominance in it at all, neither actual nor estimative. As for actual predominance, it is that the general term was previously used for other than the individual it predominates over, like 'al-najm' (the star) which is a name for every nocturnal star, then it came to predominate for al-Thurayya (the Pleiades) after previous use for other stars. As for estimative predominance, it is that the general term was not previously used for other than the individual it predominates over, but that is estimated, like 'al-alah' (the instrument) for it was not used for other than it. The word of majesty does not contain any of that in reality. The fourth and fifth: 'al-Rahman' and 'al-Rahim', and discussion on them will come. Al-Rahman: Know that al-Rahman is more comprehensive than al-Rahim because increase in structure generally indicates increase in meaning. So the first means 'the Bestower of blessings'

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to the book, where he discusses the basmalah in detail. The author states that it is professionally obligatory for every author to begin with the basmalah, praise of Allah, and salawat upon the Prophet. He then analyzes the basmalah's five words: the letter 'ba', 'ism', 'Allah', 'al-Rahman', and 'al-Rahim'. For the 'ba', he explores eight possibilities regarding what it attaches to, concluding that the best interpretation is that the implied verb is specific and delayed, indicating restriction. He proposes that the implied verb should be 'u'allif' (I author) so that the blessing covers the entire work. Next, he discusses the word 'ism', explaining its derivation: Basrans derive it from 'sumuw' (height), Kufans from 'wasm' (mark). Then he examines 'Lafz al-Jalalah' (Allah), stating it is a proper noun for the Necessary Being with no predominance. Finally, he introduces 'al-Rahman' and 'al-Rahim', noting that 'al-Rahman' is more comprehensive due to its longer form. The discussion of 'al-Rahman' is cut off at the end of the passage.

Key Terms
البسملة (al-basmalah): The phrase 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' (In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful).
الاسم (al-ism): A word that indicates a named entity; derived from 'sumuw' (height) according to Basrans or from 'wasm' (mark) according to Kufans.
لفظ الجلالة (lafz al-jalalah): The proper noun 'Allah', referring to the necessary existent essence, a personal particular proper noun without predominance.
الرحمن (al-rahman): A name of Allah meaning 'the Most Gracious', more comprehensive than al-Rahim due to its augmented form.
الرحيم (al-rahim): A name of Allah meaning 'the Most Merciful', less comprehensive than al-Rahman.

Rules for Carrying the Funeral Bier: Sunnahs and Etiquette
Explains the recommended method of carrying the bier and related etiquette

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnahs of carrying the bier, distinguish between recommended and disliked actions, and apply the ruling that it is a communal obligation.

Core Idea
Carrying the bier is sunnah with specific etiquettes; hasten but do not shake the deceased.

The funeral bier (janazah) is carried from the place of prayer to the grave. The Hanafi school provides detailed guidelines on how to carry it with reverence and speed, balancing respect for the deceased with fulfilling the communal obligation. This lesson examines the recommended method, the disliked actions, and the underlying principle that carrying and burying is fard kifayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall obligation
Recognise that carrying and burying the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). If some Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved.
This principle underlies the entire section.
2
Examine the sunnah method of carrying
Learn that it is sunnah to have four carriers, take forty steps, and begin with the right front corner. Understand the sequence of corners: right front, right rear, left front, left rear.
The carrier's right and left are defined relative to his position facing the bier.
3
Recognize recommended actions
Note that it is mustahabb to hasten without khabab (jostling) and to walk behind the bier. Walking behind is better than walking in front.
Haste should not disturb the deceased.
4
Identify disliked actions (makruh)
It is makruh to raise the voice in dhikr, sit before placing the bier down, and carry it on an animal without excuse. With an excuse, the latter is not makruh.
Dislike is due to disrespect or undue delay.
5
Note grave digging dimensions
The grave should be dug to half a man's height or up to the chest. Additional depth is good.
This ensures proper burial.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement that details the sunnah and recommendations for carrying the funeral bier. It states that it is sunnah to have four carriers, and it is recommended to take forty steps, starting with the right front corner. The order of corners is explained. It is recommended to walk at a brisk pace but not so fast that it jostles the deceased. Walking behind the bier is better than walking in front, analogous to the preference of obligatory prayer over voluntary. Burial Rites: Grave Construction and Placing the Body
Rulings on grave construction and burial procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for placing the deceased in the grave, constructing the grave, and identify which actions are sunnah, wajib, haram, or makruh.

Core Idea
Lahd is sunnah; shaq only when necessary. Facing qibla is crucial. Building on graves for adornment is haram.

Burial is a sacred act with detailed sunnah guidelines from the Hanafi school. This passage from Nur al-Idah explains how to dig the grave, place the deceased, and construct the grave, specifying what is recommended, obligatory, disliked, and prohibited. Understanding these rulings helps preserve the dignity of the deceased and avoid innovations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grave type
Determine whether to use lahd (niche on qibla side) or shaq (trench in middle). Lahd is preferred (sunnah); shaq is only permissible when the soil is too soft to hold a lahd.
2
Place the deceased
The bier is brought from the qibla side. The deceased is placed on the right side facing the qibla. The one placing recites 'Bismillah wa ala millati Rasulillah'. Knots of the shroud are untied.
Opinions differ on whether facing qibla is wajib or sunnah mu'akkada.
3
Cover and construct the grave
Unbaked bricks (laban) are placed flat over the lahd. Baked bricks (ajur) and wood are disliked. For a woman, the grave is temporarily covered with a cloth until the bricks are set; for a man, it is not covered.
4
Raise the grave (tasnim)
The grave is raised about a handspan above ground, shaped like a camel's hump (musannam), not flattened. This is recommended (awlawiyyah), not obligatory. Earth is thrown by hand or shovel.
There is an intra-Hanafi difference on obligation vs recommendation.
5
Prohibitions and disliked acts
Building on the grave for adornment (e.g., domes) is haram. Reinforcement with building after burial is makruh tahrimi. Before burial, building in the grave is not disliked. Plastering the grave is permissible per later fatwa.

Rulings on Transporting a Corpse: Prohibitions and Exceptions
Rulings on transporting a corpse and exceptions to the prohibition of exhumation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on transporting a corpse before and after burial and identify the exceptions to the prohibition of exhumation.

Core Idea
Transporting a corpse after burial is haram by consensus, except when the land is usurped or taken by pre-emption.

In this passage from Nur al-Idah, we learn the detailed rules for handling a deceased person's body: before burial, limited transport is permissible; after burial, it is absolutely prohibited unless specific property rights override the sanctity of the grave. The passage also covers burial at sea and compensation for wrongful burial. Understanding these distinctions is essential for proper funeral practice in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling for transporting the body before burial
Read the text: 'فإن نقل قبل الدفن قدر ميل أو ميلين لا بأس به وكره نقله لأكثر منه'. This shows that transport up to one or two miles is permissible, but beyond that it is disliked (makruh tanzihi).
The mile is approximately 2 km in Hanafi measure.
2
Identify the default ruling for transporting the body after burial
Read: 'ولا يجوز نقله بعد دفنه بالإجماع' — it is not permissible by consensus (ijma'). This is a definitive prohibition (haram).
Ijma' is a binding proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Note the exceptions to the prohibition
The text says: 'إلا أن تكون الأرض مغصوبة أو أخذت بالشفعة'. Exhumation and transport are allowed only if the land was usurped (maghsubah) or taken by pre-emption (shuf'a).
These exceptions prioritize property rights over the sanctity of the grave.
4
Identify additional rulings in the passage
The passage also covers: separating corpses with soil, burial at sea when land is far and decomposition feared, recommendation to bury in the local cemetery, and compensation for burying in a grave dug for another.
These are separate but related rules for managing funerals.

Lawful Disinterment: Permissible and Impermissible Grounds
Ruling on lawful and unlawful grounds for disinterment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three permissible reasons for disinterring a grave according to Hanafi law and identify which reasons do not justify disinterment.

Core Idea
A grave is inviolable; it may be opened only to retrieve property rights, not to correct orientation errors.

The sanctity of the grave is a foundational principle in Islamic law. However, property rights persist even after burial. This brief passage from Nur al-Idah clearly delineates when the inviolability of the grave may be lifted and when it must be upheld. Understanding these exceptions is essential for applying the correct ruling in real-life situations of accidental loss or theft involving a burial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The passage begins with 'ولا يخرج منه' (he is not taken out). This establishes the default: the body is not to be removed from the grave after burial.
This is a general prohibition based on the sanctity of the deceased.
2
List the three permissible exceptions
The text then states 'وينبش لمتاع سقط فيه ولكفن مغصوب ومال مع الميت'. Memorize these three: (1) an item that fell into the grave, (2) a stolen shroud, and (3) wealth buried with the deceased.
These exceptions are based on the necessity of returning property to its rightful owner.
3
Identify the impermissible reasons
The text negates permission for two cases: 'لا ينبش بوضعه لغير القبلة، أو على يساره'. Exhuming due to incorrect direction (non-qibla) or wrong positioning (left side) is not allowed.
These errors, while regrettable, do not involve property rights and thus do not override the grave's sanctity.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The permission hinges on the existence of a property right (mal) that can be reclaimed. Orientation mistakes are not property issues.
This reflects the Hanafi prioritization of property rights (ghasb) while maintaining dignity of the deceased.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Given a scenario, decide if it fits one of the three exceptions. If yes, disinterment is permitted; if no, it is prohibited.
Example: a lost ring falling into the grave is like 'متاع سقط فيه'.

Fasting the Day of Doubt: Permissibility and Dislike
Rulings on sighting the crescent and fasting the day of doubt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fasting the day of doubt and distinguish when it is permissible and when it is disliked.

Core Idea
Fasting the day of doubt is disliked except when firmly intended as nafl or habitual; the mufti and judge fast secretly.

This passage from Nur al-Idah explains the establishment of the Ramadan crescent and the rulings on the day of doubt (yawm al-shakk). You will learn when fasting is makruh, when it is permissible, and how the mufti and judge should act, with evidence from the practice of Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling context
Recognize that the day of doubt is the 30th of Sha'ban when the crescent is not seen. The passage gives the general rule: Ramadan is confirmed by sighting or completing 30 days of Sha'ban.
2
Determine the default ruling for fasting
The default ruling for fasting on the day of doubt is makruh tanzihi (disliked). This applies to any fast unless it is a supererogatory fast firmly intended without hesitation.
The term 'jazam' (firm intention) is key.
3
Identify the exceptions
Fasting is not disliked if the person makes a firm intention (jazam) for a nafl fast, or if the day coincides with a habitual fast (e.g., Mondays). The fast counts if it later turns out to be Ramadan.
4
Understand the mufti and judge's practice
The mufti orders the public to wait until the time for intention passes, then break if still unclear. The mufti and judge themselves fast secretly with nafl intention, as shown by Abu Yusuf's action.
Secrecy avoids accusations of disobedience.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Scholars differed on the better course for a non-habitual person: some said breaking is better to avoid the prohibition; others said fasting is better following Ali and Aisha. The preferred view depends on habit.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Idah deals with the establishment of the crescent moon for Ramadan and the rulings on the day of doubt (yawm al-shakk). It is a ruling statement with some proof (dalil) in the form of a narration about Imam Abu Yusuf. The author states that Ramadan is confirmed either by sighting the crescent on the 29th of Sha'ban or by completing 30 days of Sha'ban if the moon is obscured. The day of doubt is the 30th of Sha'ban when the sky is cloudy and the new moon is not seen. Fasting on this day is generally disliked (makruh) except if one intends a supererogatory fast without hesitation (jazam). If it later becomes clear that the day was actually from Ramadan, the fast suffices. However, if one is hesitant (e.g., saying 'if it is Ramadan I am fasting, otherwise not'), he is not considered a fasting person. The mufti (jurisconsult) should instruct the general public to wait until the time for intention (before half the day) and then break if still uncertain. The mufti and the judge themselves should fast secretly with the intention of nafl, as shown by the practice of Imam Abu Yusuf. The passage also clarifies that fasting one or two days at the end of Sha'ban is disliked, but more than that is not, because it might be from Ramadan. The rule of istihsan (juristic preference) may allow fasting on the day of doubt if it coincides with one's habit (e.g., fasting Mondays and Thursdays). The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah, as the base text is from Nur al-Idah (matn), with later commentary from al-Tahtawi and Maraqi al-Falah expanding on the rulings.

يوم الشك (yawm al-shakk): The 30th day of Sha'ban when the crescent for Ramadan is not seen due to clouds, making it uncertain whether it is Ramadan or not.
التلوم (al-talawwum): Waiting until the time for intention (before half the day) to see if the situation becomes clear.
جزم (jazam): Firm intention without hesitation, required for the fast to be valid on the day of doubt as nafl.

Twenty-Four Actions That Do Not Invalidate the Fast
Listing twenty-four actions that do not break the fast with embedded commentary on three intra-Hanafi disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the twenty-four actions that do not break the fast according to the Hanafi school and distinguish the intra-Hanafi differences regarding kohl, crescent sighting, and vomiting.

Core Idea
Many actions do not break the fast if lacking intent; only intentional consumption or intercourse invalidates.

This passage from Nur al-Idah lists twenty-four actions that a fasting person may do without invalidating their fast. The Hanafi school draws a sharp line between intentional and unintentional acts. Behind the list lie important intra-Hanafi disagreements on points like kohl, sighting of the crescent, and vomiting. We will work through the list and then examine these disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching principle
The fast remains valid unless there is intentional eating, drinking, or sexual intercourse. All twenty-four actions are exceptions to this principle.
The principle is derived from the hadith: 'If one forgetfully eats or drinks, let him complete his fast, for Allah fed him and gave him drink.'
2
Enumerate the 24 items from the passage
Notice the common themes: forgetfulness, involuntary actions, medical procedures, backbiting, etc. The list includes acts like applying kohl, cupping, swallowing a fly, etc.
The twenty-four are not listed one by one in the translation but can be extracted from the Arabic.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on kohl
Abu Hanifa holds that kohl does not break the fast even if taste reaches the throat; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
The dispute hinges on whether the substance enters through an open passage (manfadh) intentionally.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on crescent sighting
If the crescent is sighted during the day on the 30th of Sha'ban, does that day become Ramadan? Abu Yusuf says yes; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no (the sighting is for the next night). Fatwa is on the latter view.
The practical consequence (thamarah): if Friday is the 30th and sighted, Abu Yusuf would consider Friday Ramadan, so fasting and Friday prayer are valid; the Sahibayn say Saturday is Ramadan.
5
Examine the detailed rules on vomiting
From the commentary: vomiting breaks the fast only if intentionally induced and amounting to a mouthful. Unintentional vomiting does not break, even if a mouthful. Swallowing back vomit intentionally breaks if it was a mouthful.
The commentary from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi enumerates twelve cases; the key is the distinction between intentional and unintentional.

The Four Conditions for a Valid Vow (Nadhr)
Conditions for valid vows with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four conditions for a valid nadhr and apply them to classify whether a given vow is binding.

Core Idea
A vow is invalid if the act is already wajib, merely a means, or impossible — only acts with a wajib jins are binding.

The Hanafi school sets precise conditions for a vow to be binding. This passage explains why some acts (like wudu) cannot be vowed, while others (like manumission) can. Understanding these prevents innovation and ensures sincerity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the passage type
Recognize that this is a definitional ruling (hukm taklifi) listing conditions for nadhr. Note that it combines the matn and commentary.
The passage opens the chapter on fulfilling vows, immediately stating the conditions.
2
Isolate the conditions
Extract the three conditions stated in the matn: (1) from a jins that has a wajib li-dhatihi, (2) intended as an end (maqsūd), (3) not already wajib. Then note the fourth condition from the commentary: (4) not inherently impossible.
The fourth condition is added from al-Kifāyah and Maraqi al-Falāh.
3
Apply conditions to examples
Work through each example in the commentary: wudu fails condition 2 (not maqsūd), sajdat al-tilāwa fails condition 3 (already wajib), visiting the sick fails condition 1 (no wajib in its jins).
The author uses these to illustrate each condition.
4
Analyze the problematic case
Consider the fast of Yawm al-Nahr (Eid al-Adha). The commentary explains that the essence (fasting) is from a jins that is fard, but the attribute (turning away from Allah's hospitality) makes it prohibited. The vow is on the essence, so it is valid in principle but must be broken due to prohibition.
This shows the nuance of condition 1: the jins criterion is about essence, not incidental rulings.

إذا نذر شيئاً لزمه الوفاء به إذا اجتمع فيه ثلاثة شروط: أن يكون من جنسه لذاته لا لغيره واجب، وأن يكون مقصودا، وأن يكون ليس واجبا فلا يلزم الوضوء بنذره، ولا سجدة التلاوة، ولا عيادة المريض، ولا الواجبات بنذرها، ويصح بالعتق أي فرض يصح لأنه جنسه إذا نذر إلخ: اعلم أن الأصل في صحة النذر أن لا يكون المنذور واجباً، ولكن من لله تعالى واجب قصداً لا تبعاً؛ لأن الأصل في العبادة: الدوام لتواتر نعمه في كل لحظة، وتتابع إحسانه في كل لمحة إلا أن الله تعالى اكتفى بإيجاب خمس صلوات في كل يوم وليلة تيسيراً للأمر على عباده والعبد بنذره يريد أن يتمسك بالعزيمة، ويلحق المنذور بما هو الواجب، ومن شرط إلحاق الشيء بالشيء أن يتحقق ذلك الشيء. وقولنا: "قصداً لا تبعاً وهذا؛ لأن ما يكون واجباً تبعاً يكون مباحاً لعينه، فلم يكن النذر به إلحاقاً بالواجب، بل يكون نذراً بالمباح، والنذر بالمباح لا يصح، فلذا لا النذر بعيادة المريض؛ واجب، ولا بالوضوء ولا بقراءة القرآن؛ لأنهما وجبا للصلاة وليس من جنسهما واجب لعينه ولا يلزم صحة النذر بالاعتكاف؛ لأن من جنسه وهو اللبث واجبا على العباد لعينه، وهو الوقوف في الصلاة، والثاني أن النذر بالاعتكاف إنما صح؛ لكونه إدامة للصلاة، وإنها واجبة لعينها، ولهذا لم يصح الاعتكاف في غير المسجد. (الكفاية بزيادة) ثلاثة شروط: زيد شرط رابع أن لا يكون المنذور محالاً كقوله: علي صوم أمس اليوم؛ إذ لا يلزمه، وكذا لو قال: اليوم وكان بعد الزوال. مراقي الفلاح واجب فإن قلت: فكيف يصح النذر بصوم يوم النحر، وهو حرام؟ قلت أراد أن يكون واجبا بأصله وإن حرم إرتكابه لوصفه، فإن الصوم من جنسه فرض، ولكن بوصفه، وهو الإعراض عن ضيافة الله تعالى حرام.
ليس واجبا أي لا يكون واجبا قبل نذره بإيجاب الله تعالى كالصلوات الخمس. (مراقي الفلاح) فلا يلزم الوضوء إلخ : أما عدم لزوم الوضوء؛ فلكونه ليس مقصوداً بالذات؛ لأنه شرع شرطاً لغيره كحل الصلاة، وأما عدم لزوم سجدة التلاوة؛ فلأنها واجبة بإيجاب الشارع، وأما عدم لزوم عيادة المريض؛ فلأنه ليس من جنسها واجب، وإيجاب العبد معتبر بإيجاب الله تعالى، فما كان من جنسه عبادة أوجبها الله تعالى صح نذره، وإلا لا؛ إذ له الاتباع لا الابتداع وأما عدم صحة نذر الواجبات؛ فلأن إيجاب الواجب محال. ويصح بالعتق إلخ: أما صحة النذر بالعتق فلافتراض التحرير في الكفارات نصاً ، وأما صحته بالاعتكاف؛ فلأن . واجباً، وهو ا القعدة الأخيرة في الصلاة، فأصل المكث بهذه الصفة له نظير في الشرع، والاعتكاف انتظار للصلاة فهو كالجالس في الصلاة، فإذن صح نذره، وأما صحته بالصلاة غير المفروضة والصوم فظاهر.
Chapter on what must be fulfilled from vowed fasts, prayers, and the like.
If one vows something, it becomes obligatory to fulfill it if three conditions are met: that it be from a category whose essence (not for something else) is wajib, that it be intended (as an end in itself), and that it not be already wajib. Thus, wudu is not obligated by vowing it, nor is the prostration of recitation, nor visiting the sick, nor acts already wajib by vowing them. It is valid with manumission, i.e., it is valid because its category is wajib. When he vows, etc. Know that the basis for the validity of a vow is that the vowed thing not be wajib, but rather from acts that are wajib in themselves (primary), not subsidiary. Because the basis in worship is continuity due to the succession of His blessings every moment and the sequence of His beneficence every glance; however, Allah sufficed with obligating five prayers every day and night for ease upon His servants. The servant by his vow wants to adhere to resolve and annex the vowed act to what is wajib. One condition of annexing something to something is that that thing be verified. Our saying: 'primarily, not subsidiarily' is because what is wajib subsidiarily is in itself permissible, so vowing it is not annexation to the wajib, but rather vowing the permissible, and vowing the permissible is not valid. Hence vowing visiting the sick is not wajib, nor wudu, nor reading Quran, because they become wajib for prayer, and from their category there is no act wajib in itself. And the vow of i'tikaf is not necessary? Rather, because from its category, i.e., staying, is wajib upon servants in itself, which is standing in prayer. And second, the vow of i'tikaf is valid because it is persistence in prayer, and prayer is wajib in itself. Thus i'tikaf is not valid outside a mosque. (al-Kifaya with addition) Three conditions: a fourth condition is added: that the vowed thing not be impossible, like saying: upon me is fasting yesterday today, for it is not binding; similarly if he said: today after midday. Maraqi al-Falah: 'wajib'. If you say: how is the vow to fast on the day of Nahr valid, while it is prohibited? I say: he intended it to be wajib in its essence, even though performing it is prohibited due to its attribute, because fasting is from a category that is fard, but with the attribute of turning away from Allah's hospitality, it is prohibited.
'Not wajib' means it should not be wajib before the vow by Allah's command, like the five prayers. (Maraqi al-Falah) Thus wudu is not binding, etc. As for wudu not being binding, it is because it is not intended in itself; it is legislated as a condition for something else like the validity of prayer. As for the prostration of recitation not being binding, it is because it is already wajib by the Lawgiver's command. As for visiting the sick not being binding, it is because from its category there is no wajib. The servant's obligation is considered by Allah's obligation, so whatever from its category is an act of worship that Allah made obligatory, his vow is valid; otherwise not, for he is following, not innovating. As for the invalidity of vowing the already wajib, it is because making the wajib obligatory is impossible. 'And it is valid with manumission' etc. As for validity of vowing manumission, it is because freeing slaves is prescribed in expiations textually. As for its validity with i'tikaf, it is because ... wajib, and it is the last sitting in prayer, so the essence of staying in this manner has a parallel in the law, and i'tikaf is waiting for prayer, so it is like one sitting in prayer, thus his vow is valid. And its validity with supererogatory prayer and fasting is obvious.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions for a valid vow (nadhr) in the Hanafi school. It opens the chapter on fulfilling vowed acts. The matn states three conditions: 1) The vowed act must be of a category (jins) that has an obligatory act in itself (li-dhatihi) — meaning the genus includes some act that is wajib by its own essence, not wajib for the sake of something else. 2) The vowed act must be intended as an end in itself (maqsūdan), i.e., it is a primary act, not merely a means. 3) The vowed act must not already be wajib by Allah's command (e.g., the five prayers). The commentator explains the rationale: a vow is a voluntary addition to what Allah has obligated; you cannot vow something already obligatory (that would be redundant), nor can you vow something that is merely permissible or a means to an obligation. He gives examples: wudu is not valid to vow because it is a condition for prayer (not an end), sajdat al-tilāwah is already wajib, and visiting the sick is not from a category that has a wajib in itself. Acts like manumission and i'tikaf are valid to vow because their genus has analogous obligations (manumission in kaffārāt, i'tikaf as a form of prayer). The commentary also adds a fourth condition from al-Kifāyah: the vowed act must not be inherently impossible (like fasting yesterday). It addresses a potential objection about vowing the fast of al-Nahr (Eid al-Adha, where fasting is prohibited) by explaining that the genus (fasting) is fard, but the specific attribute (turning away from Allah's hospitality) makes it prohibited; the vow is on the essence, not the attribute. The relied-upon position is that stated conditions make a vow valid and binding.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow: making an act of worship obligatory upon oneself by one's own utterance, intending closeness to Allah.
جنس (jins): The legal category or genus of an act; a category that includes some act that is wajib li-dhatihi.
مقصود (maqsūd): Intended as an end in itself; the act is the purpose, not a means to another act.
