Compensation for Grave Digging and Conditions of Permissibility
Ruling on compensation and conditional permissibility for burial in a grave dug for another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on burying in a grave dug for another and identify the conditions that affect its permissibility.

Core Idea
Burying in a grave dug for another requires compensation; permissibility depends on cemetery space.

This lesson addresses a common concern in funeral practices: what happens when a person is buried in a grave that was originally dug for someone else? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that balances the rights of the deceased with practical necessity. You will learn when such burial is permissible, when it is disliked, and who bears the cost of digging the grave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Obligation
First, recognize that the act in question is burying a person in a grave that was dug for another. The text states that the person buried owes compensation for the digging (ḍamān qīmat al-ḥafr). This is a financial obligation that arises from the burial.
The obligation is on the deceased's estate, not on the living heirs personally.
2
Determine the Source of Compensation
If the deceased has an estate, the compensation is taken from it. If there is no estate, the obligation falls on the public treasury (bayt al-māl) or the Muslim community collectively. This ensures that the person who dug the grave is not left unpaid.
bayt al-māl is the state treasury responsible for communal needs.
3
Apply the Condition of Cemetery Space
The ruling on permissibility depends on whether the cemetery is spacious or narrow. If spacious, the burial is makrūh tanzīhī (disliked) because it may cause distress to the original owner of the grave. If the land is narrow and there is no alternative, it is permissible without dislike.
The dislike is due to the potential distress to the dead (iḥtishāḥ), not any inherent prohibition.

Rulings on Visiting Graves (Ziyarat al-Qubur)
Rulings, proofs, and intra-madhhab difference on grave visitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on visiting graves, identify the recommended and disliked actions, and explain the difference in rulings for men and women.

Core Idea
Visiting graves is recommended for all, but women's visitation is restricted in later Hanafi fatwa due to social change.

Visiting graves (ziyarat al-qubur) is a recommended act with great reward, but it comes with specific etiquettes. This lesson from Nur al-Idah presents the foundational ruling, proof from hadith, and the nuanced Hanafi position for women.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall ruling and its proof
Read the opening of the passage: 'ندب زيارتها للرجال والنساء على الأصح'. Note that the ruling is nadb (recommended) for both men and women. The proof is a hadith promising reward for reciting Surah Yasin at the graves.
The hadith is ahad, so the ruling is zanni al-dalala, hence nadb not wujub.
2
List the recommended and disliked actions
The text states that reciting Yasin is recommended. It lists disliked acts: sitting on graves without recitation, stepping on graves, sleeping, relieving oneself, and uprooting fresh plants. Uprooting dry plants is permissible.
Disliked (makruh) here means makruh tanzihi according to the madhhab.
3
Understand the exception for exhumation
The passage includes a note from Marqi al-Falah that exhumation is permitted for valid reasons: to retrieve a fallen item, a usurped shroud, or buried wealth, if the owner insists.
This is an exception to the general prohibition of disturbing graves.
4
Analyze the different views regarding women
The text cites strong warnings against women visiting graves, with some scholars (like al-Badr al-Ayni) saying it is prohibited in this era. However, the relied-upon view (al-ashahh) is that it remains recommended.
This intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf shows how later authorities considered changing circumstances.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
For men: recommended. For women: the prevalent fatwa is makruh tanzihi (disliked), though some contemporary scholars lean towards prohibition (haram). The original recommendation is still a valid view.
The tarjih authority is al-Badr al-Ayni in Sharh al-Bukhari.

The Hanafi Definition of Shahid al-Dunya
Definition and conditions of the martyr with rulings on burial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a shahid al-dunya, list its conditions, and apply them to determine burial rulings.

Core Idea
Not every Muslim who dies tragically is a shahid al-dunya; Hanafi law defines precise conditions for the special burial.

Martyrdom carries immense spiritual reward, but in Hanafi fiqh, a specific legal category—shahid al-dunya—dictates how the body is handled: no washing and burial in clothes. This lesson dissects the definition and conditions from Nur al-Idah, distinguishing between worldly rulings and afterlife reward. You will learn which deceased are to be buried without washing and in their clothes, and why.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
Recognize that the passage defines 'shahid al-dunya' (worldly martyr) for burial rulings, not shahid al-akhira (who is washed normally).
The definition is purpose-specific: it governs the absence of washing and removal of clothes.
2
List the conditions for martyr status
Extract from the text the specific scenarios that confer martyr status: killed by warring disbelievers, rebels, highway robbers, thieves at night, found dead on battlefield with a trace, or killed wrongfully intentionally by a Muslim with a sharp tool.
Indirect killing (tasbib) is included; tools include any weapon, even heavy ones for enemies.
3
Understand the preventers (mawani)
Identify when the martyr status is negated: if blood money (diya) is due (e.g., killing by mistake with heavy weapon) or if the killer is unknown (slaughtered body).
The principle: no financial liability must arise from the act of killing.
4
Apply to scenarios
Test different cases against the conditions and preventers to determine if the deceased qualifies as shahid al-dunya.
Pay attention to weapon type, killer identity, and certainty of circumstances.

Rulings of the Martyr (Shahid) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines the martyr and states burial rulings without washing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that make a killed person a martyr in the Hanafi school and state the burial rulings (no washing, shrouded in blood) and their exceptions.

Core Idea
A martyr is buried without washing and in his blood-stained clothes if he meets specific conditions: killed unjustly with a sharp tool, Muslim, adult, pure, and not found alive after battle.

The martyr (shahid) holds a special status in Islamic law. In the Hanafi school, a person killed unjustly in war or by certain means is considered a martyr and receives a unique burial: no washing (ghusl) and shrouded in his blood-stained clothes. However, not every death on the battlefield qualifies. This lesson examines the precise conditions for this ruling and the important exceptions that all students of fiqh must know.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
This passage is about the burial of the martyr (shahid) – a specific ruling within the chapter of prayer (janazah). The ruling is that a martyr is not washed but is shrouded in his blood and clothes.
The ruling applies only to the worldly treatment of the body; spiritual martyrdom is broader.
2
List the conditions for martyrdom
The text gives a detailed definition: killed by enemies, rebels, highway robbers, thieves at night (even with a blunt tool), or found dead in battle with a trace of violence, or killed by a Muslim unjustly with a sharp tool.
The key principle: no monetary compensation (diyah) is due for the killing itself.
3
Understand the default ruling
If the killed person is Muslim, adult (baligh), free from menstruation/postnatal bleeding/janabah, and not 'murtath' (no signs of life after war ends), then he is shrouded in his clothes and blood, prayed over without washing.
Items not suitable for shroud (fur, padding, weapons, armor) are removed. Shroud may be added or reduced to meet sunna.
4
Recognize exceptions requiring ghusl
The text lists cases where the special ruling is overridden and ghusl is required: if the killed was a child, insane, menstruating, or in postnatal bleeding, or if he became murtath (e.g., ate, drank, slept, treated, prayed, made a will, spoke much after the war).
If these actions occurred before war ends, he remains a martyr. Also, one killed in the city with unknown circumstances, or by hadd/qisas, requires ghusl.
5
Note the dislike of removing all clothes
It is disliked (makruh) to remove all the martyr's clothes; the trace of blood should remain as a sign of martyrdom.
This is sunnah-based, not obligatory.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Idah presents the Hanafi rulings on the martyr (shahid). It begins by defining the martyr: a person killed unjustly by enemies, rebels, or highway robbers, or found dead in battle with signs of violence. The opening line affirms Sunni creed—the martyr dies at his appointed time, refuting the Mu'tazilite view that the killer shortens the victim's life. The text then explores the linguistic derivations of the word 'shahid' (witness) and its various interpretations: active participle (one who witnesses), passive participle (one witnessed for), etc. The commentator stresses that this definition is not absolute but pertains specifically to the worldly rulings that follow (not washing, not removing clothes). The commentary expands on what constitutes killing: direct or indirect causation (e.g., throwing rocks, releasing water), and any tool including water and fire. It clarifies that being found in the battlefield with an effect (wound, fracture, burn) qualifies, but blood from mouth, nose, or privates does not count as 'trace'. A person killed by a Muslim unjustly with a sharp tool is a martyr; but if killed by mistake or with a heavy blunt object (where diyah is due), he is not a martyr in worldly rulings. The principle is: for one to be a martyr in the worldly sense, no monetary compensation (diyah) should be obligatory. Then the passage lists conditions for the martyr to receive the special burial (no ghusl, shrouded in his blood-stained clothes): the killed must be Muslim, adult (baligh), free from menstruation, postnatal bleeding, and janabah, and not 'murtath' (i.e., not showing signs of life after the war). The special ruling is: he is shrouded in his clothes and blood, prayed over without washing. Items not suitable for shroud (like fur, padding, weapon, armor) are removed. If his clothes are insufficient, they are added to; if excessive, reduced. Removing all his clothes is disliked. The passage then gives exceptions requiring ghusl: if the killed was a child, insane, menstruating, or in postnatal bleeding; or if after battle he shows signs of life (eats, drinks, sleeps, treats himself, passes a prayer time while conscious, is moved from field, makes a will, buys, sells, or speaks many words). If such actions occur before battle ends, he remains a martyr. Also, one killed in a city with unknown circumstances, or by hadd penalty or qisas, requires ghusl and regular burial. The commentator adds details: the need for alternative cloth, definition of fur, and that 'murtath' is derived from 'rathith' (worn out, weak). The overarching lesson: Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between the martyr's special status in worldly rites (no ghusl) and his spiritual rank. The exceptions ensure that the ruling applies only to those who fully meet the criteria.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahīd): One killed unjustly in war or by enemies/rebels/robbers, or found dead in battle with signs of violence; he is buried without washing.
مرتث (murtath): A wounded person carried from battle who shows signs of life after war (eats, drinks, sleeps, etc.), losing the worldly martyr's ruling; he requires ghusl.
مثقل (muthaqqal): A heavy blunt object; killing with such tools does not make one a martyr in worldly ruling because diyah is obligatory.

Rulings Concerning the Martyr (Shahid)
Details the conditions and rulings for a martyr killed in battle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a martyr to be buried without ghusl and identify the exceptions that require ghusl.

Core Idea
A martyr's blood is honored without washing, but only if he meets five conditions — otherwise, he is washed like any other deceased Muslim.

Martyrdom in the Hanafi school carries a unique set of burial rulings that honor the sacrifice of the shahid while maintaining the dignity of Islamic funeral rites. This lesson unpacks the precise conditions for treating a deceased person as a shahid and the critical exceptions that change the ruling from no washing to required ghusl. Understanding these details ensures that funeral rites are performed correctly according to the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is handling the body of a martyr: shrouding, prayer without washing. The ruling is that washing is not required if conditions are met.
2
List the conditions for martyrdom without ghusl
The martyr must be Muslim, adult (baligh), free from menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and major impurity (janabah), and must not show signs of life (irtith) after the war ends.
All conditions must be present simultaneously.
3
Identify the exceptions that necessitate ghusl
Washing is required if the deceased was a child, insane, menstruating, or in postpartum bleeding, or if irtith occurred after war (e.g., eating, drinking, sleeping, etc.), or if killed in the city, or by hadd/qisas.
These exceptions override the default no-wash ruling.
4
Distinguish irtith actions
Irtith includes specific actions after war: eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving treatment, a prayer time passing while conscious, being moved from battlefield, making a will, selling, buying, or speaking many words.
If these occur before war ends, he is not murtath and remains unwashed.
5
Apply the ruling to non-battlefield cases
Those killed in the city (even if unjustly with a sharp instrument) or by a fixed punishment or retaliation require full ghusl and prayer.
The honor of battlefield martyrdom does not extend to these cases.

Commentary
This passage details the rulings (ahkam) concerning a martyr (shahid) killed in battle. The text first enumerates the conditions for a person to be treated as a martyr: he must be Muslim, adult (baligh), free from menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and major impurity (janabah), and must not have 'iratth' (i.e., remained alive after the cessation of hostilities with signs of life). If these conditions are met, the martyr is shrouded with his blood and clothes, the funeral prayer is performed without ritual washing (ghusl), and items not suitable for a shroud—such as fur, padding, weapons, and armor—are removed. Additional cloth may be added or removed from his clothing to meet the requirements of a proper shroud, and it is disliked (makruh) to remove all his clothes. However, the martyr is washed (ghusl) if he was killed while being a child, insane, menstruating, or in postpartum bleeding, or if he exhibited signs of life (irtith) after the war—such as eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving medical treatment, a prayer time passing while conscious, or being moved from the battlefield even slightly (unless moved to avoid horses trampling), making a will, selling, buying, or speaking many words. If these signs occur before the war ends, he is not considered 'murtath' and is washed. Additionally, a person killed in the city (not on the battlefield) is washed, as is someone killed by a fixed penalty (hadd) or retaliation (qisas). The commentary clarifies terms: 'irtith' refers to being carried from the battlefield wounded with life remaining; the phrase 'with his blood' means the blood is not washed off; the removal of items is conditional upon the availability of other suitable shrouding material; the clothing of the martyr should not be entirely removed so that the trace of his sacrifice remains. The passage also addresses the distinction between menstruation and istihadah and the definition of 'many words' as opposed to few.

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahid): One killed unjustly in battle with a sharp instrument while Muslim, adult, free from impurities, and not having irtith after war.
المرتث (al-murtath): One who is wounded and carried from the battlefield with life remaining, and performs worldly acts after the war ends.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Idah deals with the legal rulings for confirming the new moon (hilal) for the months of Ramadan and Shawwal, as well as other months. The text is a matn (core text) with interspersed commentary. The opening states that for Ramadan, the testimony of a witness who is female, a slave, or one who has been punished for false accusation (qadhf) but has repented is acceptable. Interestingly, the formal utterance of testimony (lafz al-shahada) and the claim (da'wa) are not required conditions. For the crescent of Shawwal (Eid al-Fitr), if the sky is clear (no obstruction), the same leniency applies, requiring two free men or a man and two free women, without a claim. However, if the sky is cloudy (with an obstruction, 'illa), then a large multitude (jam' 'azim) is needed for both months. The size of this multitude is not fixed in the zahir al-riwayah; Abu Yusuf estimated it at fifty (like qasama), while others suggested five hundred or the residents of the locality. The most correct view, attributed to Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, is that the criterion is tawatur (widespread transmission) from all sides, and the determination is left to the Imam's judgment. If Ramadan is completed (30 days) based on the testimony of a single witness and the Shawwal crescent is not seen while the sky is clear, it is not permissible to break the fast; the witness may be punished (ta'zir). There is a dispute among later authorities about whether two upright witnesses suffice for breaking fast when it is clear; some say yes, others say no (a second witness does not override the clear sky). For the crescent of Dhu al-Hijjah (Adha), the ruling is the same as for Shawwal. For all other lunar months (e.g., for marriage or iddah purposes), the standard requirement of two male witnesses or one man and two women applies, but only when the sky is cloudy; if clear, a multitude is needed. The passage also addresses the issue of sighting in different regions: the apparent position of the Hanafi school is that if the moon is established in one region, it binds all other regions (no consideration of differing rising places). However, the more likely view (ashbah) is that each region follows its own sighting because the separation of the crescent from the sun differs by longitude. Finally, the text discusses the ruling of seeing the crescent during the day: the chosen position (of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that it relates to the following night, so that day is not part of the new month. Abu Yusuf differentiates: if seen before noon, it is for the past night, making that day the first of the month; if after noon, it is for the following night. The practical consequence is whether one must fast or break fast on that day.

Key Terms
علة (illa): Obstruction in the sky such as clouds, dust, or haze that prevents clear sighting of the crescent.
جمع عظيم (jam' 'azim): A large multitude whose report produces predominant conjecture (ghalabat al-zann) about the sighting; not defined by a fixed number in the zahir al-riwayah.

Regional Sighting and Daytime Crescent
Rules on sighting horizons and daytime crescent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on whether a moon sighting in one region obligates other regions (ikhtilaf al-matali') and state the ruling for seeing the crescent during the day.

Core Idea
A moon sighting in one region does not necessarily bind others, and a daytime sighting is for the next night.

This part of the passage addresses two important issues: whether the sighting of the moon in one locality requires fasting in another locality (ikhtilaf al-matali'), and what to do if the crescent is seen during the day. The Hanafi school has an apparent position that sighting binds all, but the more likely view (ashbah) is that each region follows its own sighting. For daytime sightings, the chosen position (mukhtar) is that it relates to the following night.

Guided Path
1
State the apparent Hanafi position on regional sighting
The zahir al-madhhab is that if the moon is established in one region, it binds all people; this is the position upon which fatwa is given and most shuyukh hold.
2
Present the more likely view (ashbah)
Most shuyukh hold that ikhtilaf al-matali' is considered; each region follows its own sighting. This is based on the difference in moonrise times by longitude.
The more likely view is that rising places differ, so each region acts on its own sighting.
3
Explain the ruling on daytime sighting
The chosen position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that a daytime sighting is for the following night, so that day is still part of the current month.
No fasting or breaking fast is established by daytime sighting.
4
Distinguish Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf says if seen before noon, it is for the past night; if after noon, for the following night. But the mufta bihi is the majority view.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Idah deals with the legal rulings for confirming the new moon (hilal) for the months of Ramadan and Shawwal, as well as other months. The text is a matn (core text) with interspersed commentary. The opening states that for Ramadan, the testimony of a witness who is female, a slave, or one who has been punished for false accusation (qadhf) but has repented is acceptable. Interestingly, the formal utterance of testimony (lafz al-shahada) and the claim (da'wa) are not required conditions. For the crescent of Shawwal (Eid al-Fitr), if the sky is clear (no obstruction), the same leniency applies, requiring two free men or a man and two free women, without a claim. However, if the sky is cloudy (with an obstruction, 'illa), then a large multitude (jam' 'azim) is needed for both months. The size of this multitude is not fixed in the zahir al-riwayah; Abu Yusuf estimated it at fifty (like qasama), while others suggested five hundred or the residents of the locality. The most correct view, attributed to Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, is that the criterion is tawatur (widespread transmission) from all sides, and the determination is left to the Imam's judgment. If Ramadan is completed (30 days) based on the testimony of a single witness and the Shawwal crescent is not seen while the sky is clear, it is not permissible to break the fast; the witness may be punished (ta'zir). There is a dispute among later authorities about whether two upright witnesses suffice for breaking fast when it is clear; some say yes, others say no (a second witness does not override the clear sky). For the crescent of Dhu al-Hijjah (Adha), the ruling is the same as for Shawwal. For all other lunar months (e.g., for marriage or iddah purposes), the standard requirement of two male witnesses or one man and two women applies, but only when the sky is cloudy; if clear, a multitude is needed. The passage also addresses the issue of sighting in different regions: the apparent position of the Hanafi school is that if the moon is established in one region, it binds all other regions (no consideration of differing rising places). However, the more likely view (ashbah) is that each region follows its own sighting because the separation of the crescent from the sun differs by longitude. Finally, the text discusses the ruling of seeing the crescent during the day: the chosen position (of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that it relates to the following night, so that day is not part of the new month. Abu Yusuf differentiates: if seen before noon, it is for the past night, making that day the first of the month; if after noon, it is for the following night. The practical consequence is whether one must fast or break fast on that day.

Key Terms
المختار (al-mukhtar): The chosen position, here the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad that a daytime sighting is for the following night.
اختلاف المطالع (ikhtilaf al-matali'): Difference in rising places (longitudes) of the moon, i.e., whether a sighting in one region obligates other regions.

Vomiting and Its Effect on the Fast: Hanafi Rulings
Detailed breakdown of vomiting scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between compulsive and induced vomiting, and apply the Hanafi rulings for each scenario.

Core Idea
Only induced vomiting of a mouthful that is intentionally returned breaks the fast; other vomiting scenarios do not nullify.

The passage includes a detailed discussion on vomiting, breaking down twelve possible scenarios based on whether the vomiting is involuntary or induced, the amount (mouthful or less), and what happens to the vomit (returns by itself, is returned intentionally, or exits). Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The author divides the discussion into 'qā'a' (vomiting involuntarily) and 'istaqā'a' (inducing vomiting intentionally). Each is further split by the amount: 'ملء الفم' (mouthful) or 'دونه' (less).
Mouthful is defined as what a person would naturally expel when vomiting.
2
Consider the three outcomes for each
After vomiting, the vomit can either: (a) return to the stomach by itself, (b) be returned intentionally by the person, or (c) exit the body. Each outcome has a different ruling.
3
Apply the 'sound opinion' (as-sahih)
The text states: 'لا يفطر في الكل على الأصح' (none break the fast according to the sound opinion), with one exception: returning induced vomiting of a mouthful.
Exception: if he induces vomiting a mouthful and intentionally returns it, the fast breaks.
4
Note the nuance on repeated induced vomiting
If he induces vomiting a mouthful repeatedly in one sitting, the fast breaks. If done in separate sittings (e.g., morning, noon, evening), it does not break.
This is according to the second saying, indicating a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school.

Conditions for Obligation of Qada and Kaffarah in Fasting
Conditions for kaffarah when breaking the fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the eight conditions for kaffarah when a voluntary fast is broken and apply them to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for breaking a Ramadan fast is only wajib when eight conditions are fully met.

This passage elaborates on the conditions that must be present for kaffarah to be obligatory when a fasting person intentionally breaks a Ramadan fast. The base text lists the nullifying acts, but the commentary specifies the prerequisites for kaffarah. Understanding these conditions is crucial for correctly applying the ruling, as missing even one condition reduces the obligation to only qada (make-up).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage deals with breaking a Ramadan fast requiring both qada and kaffarah. Start by noting the base list of nullifiers: intercourse, eating, drinking, swallowing rain. Then focus on the conditions.
2
List the conditions from the commentary
The commentator enumerates eight conditions: 1) the action must be by the fasting person, 2) the person must be mukallaf (legally responsible), 3) intention must have been made from the night (mabīt al-niyya), 4) the nullification must occur in an actual Ramadan fast (not a make-up), 5) no permissible cause for breaking the fast arises after the nullification, 6) the act must be voluntary, 7) intentional, 8) not under duress. Write them down.
3
Understand the effect of each condition
For each condition, consider: if it is missing, kaffarah is not due. For example, if the person is a child, kaffarah is waived. If the person had not intended from the night, Abu Hanifa says no kaffarah (though Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree).

Given a scenario like 'a Muslim woman intentionally eats a meal during Ramadan after having intended from night, but later that day she menstruates', determine if kaffarah is due. The Definition of Taghaddhi and Its Impact on Kaffarah for Smoking
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the definition of taghaddhi for kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi definitions of taghaddhi and apply them to determine whether smoking or eating hashish incurs kaffarah.

Core Idea
Whether smoking tobacco requires kaffarah depends on whether 'nutritious' means satisfying desire or providing health benefit.

This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi debate over the meaning of taghaddhi (being nutritious). The ruling on kaffarah for eating non-nutritive substances like tobacco, hashish, or habshi paper hinges on this definition. The commentator notes that the most correct view (al-asahh) considers taghaddhi as what the natural disposition inclines to and satisfies the stomach's desire, thus requiring kaffarah for such substances. This lesson unpacks the debate and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The issue is whether eating a substance that does not provide nutritional value but is consumed for pleasure (like tobacco or hashish) requires kaffarah.
The term taghaddhi is derived from ghidha' (nourishment).
2
State the two opinions
First definition: taghaddhi is what nature inclines to and satisfies stomach desire. Second definition: it must provide bodily benefit (islah al-badan).
The second definition is more restrictive.
3
Analyze the evidence
The first definition is supported by the example of chewing then spitting out a morsel: when spat out, the soul detests it, so it cannot be considered want. The second definition focuses on benefit to the body.
The commentator cites al-Muhit.
4
Evaluate the tarjih
The commentator says the first definition is the most correct (al-asahh). Thus, substances that satisfy desire, even if harmful, incur kaffarah.
This tarjih affects fatwa on smoking.
5
Apply to modern cases
Provide examples: smoking tobacco, eating qatat, hashish: according to first opinion, kaffarah; according to second, only qada. Also consider same rule for other addictive substances.
The commentator explicitly mentions 'the innovation that has appeared now, which is smoke (tobacco).'

Actions Nullifying Fast Without Expiation
List of 57 actions that invalidate fast without kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which actions invalidate the fast without expiation and explain the Hanafi rationale for each category.

Core Idea
Not everything that breaks the fast requires expiation – many actions nullify without kaffarah due to doubt, duress, or the substance not being normal food.

This passage from Nur al-Idah lists fifty-seven specific actions that invalidate the fast of Ramadan but do not obligate expiation (kaffarah). Understanding which actions fall into this category is crucial for distinguishing between mere nullification (fasād) and the severe penalty of kaffarah, which is only required for deliberate, complete violation without excuse. The list includes eating inedible substances, medical procedures, accidental ingestion, and coerced actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Read each item in the list and classify it: is it a substance that is not normally food (e.g., raw rice, clay), a medical procedure (e.g., enema, snuff), an accidental occurrence (e.g., water from rinsing), or a case of duress (e.g., coercion)?
2
Determine if the substance reaches the stomach or brain
For a substance to invalidate the fast, it must reach the internal cavity (jawf) or brain. Check each item: does it typically reach the stomach? For wounds (ja'ifah, ammah), medicine that reaches the stomach or brain nullifies.
Rain, snow, and water from rinsing also reach the stomach unintentionally.
3
Check the absence of kaffarah conditions
Kaffarah is only due for deliberate eating/drinking/intercourse that is ordinarily nutritive, without any doubt. For each item, ask: Is this normally eaten or drunk? Was the act intentional? Is there a valid scholarly difference (shubhah) that prevents kaffarah?
Intra-Hanafi differences (e.g., enema) and inter-madhhab differences (e.g., forgetfulness) create doubt that nullifies without kaffarah.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf held that enema (ihtiqan) and snuff (su'ut) require kaffarah, but the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is no kaffarah. Understand why the soundest opinion (al-asahh) prevails.
The principle: kaffarah is waived when there is doubt about the ruling itself.
5
Apply to a novel scenario
Given a new action not listed (e.g., inhaling steam), use the principles to deduce whether it invalidates with or without kaffarah.

Commentary

Does vowing 'days' of i'tikaf also obligate the nights? The Hanafi school answers with a firm 'yes', and adds that consecutiveness is required even if not stipulated. If he is able to get it after shaving before he fasts the seven in the days of slaughter or after them, the hadi does not become obligatory; because tahallul has occurred by shaving, so the existence of the original after it does not nullify the substitute, like the tayammum one seeing water after prayer. Similarly if he does not find until the days of slaughter have passed, then finds a hadi; because slaughter is tied to the days of Nahr, so if they pass the purpose has been achieved, which is permitting tahallul without a hadi, as if he had performed tahallul then found it. If he fasts in its time while having a hadi, it is considered: if the hadi remains until the Day of Sacrifice, it is not sufficient; because of ability to do the original. If it perishes before slaughter, it is permitted; because of inability. So the considered time is the time of tahallul. [Fath al-Qadir 2/418, and al-Bahr al-Raiq] Contrary to one who fasts the miqat: it is to avoid Mecca, because its inhabitants are not mutamatti' or qarin, not to avoid his dwelling or other places. Its ihram is not restricted to the months of Hajj because it is not a condition, but performing most of its tawaf during them is a condition. So if he performs the lesser tawaf in Ramadan for example, then performs the rest in Shawwal, then performs Hajj in that year, he is a mutamatti'. [al-Bahr al-Raiq abridged 2/552] Exposition of Umrah. He says after praying the two rak'ahs of ihram: O Allah, I intend Umrah, so facilitate it for me and accept it from me. Then he recites talbiyah until he enters Mecca, performs tawaf for it, cuts the talbiyah at the beginning of his tawaf, does ramal in it, then prays two rak'ahs of tawaf, then runs between Safa and Marwah after standing on Safa—as previously—seven circuits, then shaves his head or cuts his hair if he did not bring a hadi, and everything becomes permissible for him including intercourse. He remains halal. If he brought a hadi, he does not become halal from his Umrah. When the Day of Tarwiyah comes, he enters ihram for Hajj from the Haram and goes out to Mina. When he throws the Jamrat al-Aqabah on the Day of Sacrifice, slaughtering a sheep or one-seventh of a camel becomes obligatory for him. If he does not find, he fasts three days before the Day of Sacrifice, and seven when he returns like the qarin. If he does not fast the three until the Day of Sacrifice comes, it becomes obligatory upon him to slaughter a sheep, and neither fasting nor charity suffices him.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the rules of the compensatory fasts for a mutamatti' (one who performs tamattu' hajj). The base ruling is from the matn: if a person cannot find a sacrificial animal (hadi), he must fast three days during the months of Hajj before the Day of Sacrifice, and seven days after completing Hajj. The commentary then clarifies several points. First, the definition of tamattu': entering ihram for Umrah only from the miqat. The three days of fasting: the best practice is to fast on the 7th of Dhu al-Hijjah, the Day of Tarwiyah (8th), and the Day of Arafah (9th), but the permissible time extends from the start of ihram for Umrah in the months of Hajj (Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, Dhu al-Hijjah) until the Day of Sacrifice. The evidence is the Quranic verse 'fasting three days during Hajj'—the word 'during Hajj' refers to the time of Hajj, not the actions, because actions cannot be a container. Delaying is better because fasting is a substitute for the hadi, hoping to get the original. If one delays until the last moment and still cannot get a hadi, the fast is valid; but if the Day of Sacrifice enters without having fasted, the fast is no longer valid and a sacrificial animal becomes obligatory. The seven fasts after returning: they cannot be performed before returning from Mina after completing the rites, because they are conditioned on return (Quran: 'and seven when you return'). The passage also discusses the effect of later ability to procure a hadi: if one becomes able to get a hadi during the three fasts or before the Day of Sacrifice, the hadi becomes obligatory and the fasts drop; if after shaving (tahallul) but before the seven fasts, the hadi does not become obligatory, analogous to the one performing tayammum who finds water after prayer. If the hadi was available at the time of fasting but perished before slaughter, then the fast was valid; if it remained until the Day of Sacrifice, it was not. The note about 'contrary to one who fasts the miqat' clarifies that the miqat is specified to avoid residents of Mecca, who cannot perform tamattu' or qiran. The exposition of Umrah then describes the actions of Umrah for a mutamatti' who does not bring a hadi: he performs Umrah, becomes halal, and then on the Day of Tarwiyah enters ihram for Hajj. If he brought a hadi, he does not become halal from Umrah and remains in ihram until after Hajj. The passage concludes by equating the ruling of the qarin and mutamatti' regarding the compensatory fasts.

Key Terms
تمتع (tamattu'): A form of Hajj where one performs Umrah first in the months of Hajj, becomes halal, then enters ihram for Hajj on the Day of Tarwiyah.
هدي (hadi): A sacrificial animal (sheep, goat, or share in a camel/cow) offered as a ritual obligation for tamattu' and qiran.
إحرام (ihram): The state of ritual consecration for Hajj or Umrah, marked by special garments and prohibitions.
