Greetings to the Caliphs Abu Bakr and Umar during Ziyarah
Practical instructions for greetings to the caliphs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recite the proper greetings to Abu Bakr and Umar during ziyarah and explain the ruling on conveying salaam.

Core Idea
Conveying salaam to the Prophet is wajib, and greeting the Caliphs with specific praise honors their legacies.

After supplicating at the Prophet's grave, the pilgrim moves to greet Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and Umar ibn al-Khattab with specific invocations. This passage details the sequence and wording, and the hashiah notes that conveying salaam from others is wajib because it is an amanah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sequence of movements
After supplication at the Prophet's grave, the pilgrim moves a cubit (dhira') to the right to face Abu Bakr's grave, then a cubit more to face Umar's grave, then steps back half a cubit.
The distances are symbolic and ensure proper orientation.
2
Recite the greeting for Abu Bakr
The greeting acknowledges his caliphate, companionship, intimate in the cave, journeys, and trustee of secrets. It praises his successorship and fighting against apostasy.
The greeting emphasizes his role in preserving Islam.
3
Recite the greeting for Umar
The greeting acknowledges him as the manifestor of Islam, breaker of idols, conqueror of lands, sponsor of orphans, and one who strengthened Islam.
It highlights his contributions to the expansion of Islam.
4
Note the ruling on conveying salaam
The hashiah states that conveying the salaam of others is wajib because it is a trust (amanah). The pilgrim should deliver the message exactly as entrusted.
This applies even if the person who sent salaam is not present.

Commentary
This passage is from the Book of Hajj in Nur al-Idah, detailing the recommended acts (sunan) of visiting the Prophet's grave in Medina. It begins with a supplication seeking the Prophet's intercession, citing Quran 4:64 as evidence that visiting the Prophet and seeking forgiveness through him is a means to Allah's mercy. The text instructs the visitor to say specific invocations three times, then convey greetings from others. After supplicating at the Prophet's grave, the visitor moves a cubit to the right to face the grave of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, offering a lengthy greeting praising his caliphate and role. Then moving a similar distance to face Umar ibn al-Khattab, greeting him and acknowledging his contributions. Finally, stepping back half a cubit. The hashiah of al-Tahtawi provides lexical definitions and notes that conveying salaam is wajib (obligatory) as it is an amanah. This reflects the Hanafi position that ziyarat al-Nabi is mustahabb (recommended), and respect for the Companions is emphasized.

Key Terms
الشفاعة (al-shafa'ah): Intercession; the Prophet’s act of pleading with Allah on behalf of sinners, which he is promised to have on the Day of Judgment (shafa'ah 'uzma).
الزيارة (al-ziyarah): Visitation, specifically to the Prophet's grave in Medina, performed as a recommended act during or outside of Hajj.
الوسيلة (al-wasilah): A high rank in Paradise uniquely granted to the Prophet, mentioned in the Quran (5:35) and sought in the tashahhud.

Etiquette of Visiting the Prophet's Grave and the Two Caliphs
Describes recommended supplications and movements for ziyarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended supplications and movements when visiting the graves of the Prophet, Abu Bakr, and Umar, and identify the sunnah actions at the Rawdah, pillar of Abu Lubabah, and minbar.

Core Idea
Visiting the Prophet's grave with prescribed salam and du'a is a confirmed sunnah (sunnah muakkadah) in the Hanafi school.

After completing the Hajj, the pilgrim is encouraged to visit the Prophet's mosque and grave in Medina. This passage from Nur al-Idah lays out the recommended etiquette, including specific salams for Abu Bakr and Umar, the recitation of a Quranic verse for intercession, and visiting the blessed sites within the mosque. Understanding this procedure helps the visitor perform the ziyarah correctly, seeking the spiritual benefits and blessings associated with these sacred places.

Guided Path
1
Sequence the visit
Trace the physical movements: first face the Prophet, then move one cubit to the right to face Abu Bakr, then similarly to face Umar, then step back half a cubit to greet both, then return to the Prophet's head for the final du'a. Then go to the pillar of Abu Lubabah, the Rawdah, and the minbar.
The order is important to maintain adab.
2
Extract the key supplications
Identify the opening and closing phrases of each salam: for Abu Bakr, 'السلام عليك يا خليفة رسول الله...'; for Umar, 'السلام عليك يا أمير المؤمنين...'; and the joint greeting, 'السلام عليكما يا ضجيعي رسول الله...'. Note the Quranic verse recited at the Prophet's head (Surah al-Nisa 4:64).
Memorizing these supplications is recommended for the visitor.
3
Locate the Quranic verse used
Recognize that the verse 'وَلَوْ أَنَّهُمْ إِذْ ظَلَمُوا أَنْفُسَهُمْ جَاءُوكَ...' is recited as a plea for intercession, demonstrating the concept of tawassul through the Prophet.
This verse is often cited in discussions on the permissibility of seeking intercession.
4
Map the additional sites
Understand the significance of the pillar of Abu Lubabah (a place of repentance), the Rawdah (a garden of Paradise), and the minbar (where the Prophet's hand may have touched). Each has a recommended action: prayer at the pillar, prayer and du'a at the Rawdah, and placing the hand on the pomegranate of the minbar.
These are additional sunnah acts that enhance the visit.
