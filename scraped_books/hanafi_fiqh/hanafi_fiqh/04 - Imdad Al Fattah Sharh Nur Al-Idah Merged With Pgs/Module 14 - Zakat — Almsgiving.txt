Definition and Conditions of Zakat Obligation
Opening of zakat chapter with definition, conditions, and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of zakat, list the conditions for its obligation and validity, and apply the rule of intention to a payment scenario.

Core Idea
Zakat is a transfer of ownership of growing wealth to a deserving recipient after the passage of a lunar year.

This passage opens the Book of Zakat with a precise definition and enumerates the conditions that make zakat obligatory and valid. Understanding these conditions is essential for every Muslim to fulfill this pillar correctly. The commentary adds Quranic proofs and clarifies key terms like tibr and hilyah.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
Identify the three components: tamlik (transfer of ownership), mal makhsus (specific wealth – the nisab), and shakhs makhsus (specific person – the deserving recipient). Note that mere permission to use (ibahah) is insufficient.
The definition distinguishes zakat from sadaqah: zakat must be given as ownership, not just allowed to be consumed.
2
List the shurut al-wujub (conditions of obligation)
From the text: free (not slave), Muslim, mukallaf (adult, sane), owner of nisab (gold/silver/trade goods) free of debt and basic needs, wealth must be growing (actual or estimated). Also, the passage of a full lunar year (hawl) on the original nisab.
'Free from debt and basic needs' means the nisab must exceed the minimum necessities and any debts due.
3
Identify shart al-ada' (condition of validity)
The text states the condition for valid payment is intention (niyyah) concurrent with giving the zakat to the poor or his agent, or setting aside the amount. The concurrence can be legal (hukmi) as when one gives without intention then intends while the money is still in the poor's hand.
The poor need not know it is zakat; giving as a gift or loan with intention of zakat is valid.
4
Analyze special rulings
Wealth acquired during the year is added (yudamm) to similar wealth and zakat is due at the end of the original year. Advance payment (ta'jil) for multiple years is valid if one owns nisab at the time. If one gives all his wealth as charity without intending zakat, the zakat obligation for that year is discharged (saqata).
The ruling on charity covering the obligation is based on the principle that general charity can fulfill the specific duty if it meets the criteria.
5
Review the Quranic proofs
The footnote cites verses 2:43, 9:103, and 34:39. According to Hanafi usul, the imperative 'wa atu al-zakah' indicates obligation (wujub). The other verses commend zakat and promise reward, reinforcing its obligatory nature.
The widespread pairing of zakat with prayer in the Quran (82 places) shows its fundamental importance.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Zakat from the matn (likely Nur al-Idah) along with its commentary from Imdad al-Fattah. The passage begins by defining zakat as the transfer of ownership (tamlik) of specific wealth to a specific person. This definition distinguishes zakat from other forms of spending: it must be given as ownership to a recipient; mere use (ibahah) is not sufficient. The obligation of zakat is upon a free (not a slave), Muslim, legally accountable (mukallaf: adult and sane) person who owns a nisab (minimum threshold) of currency (gold or silver) — even if it is in the form of uncoined metal (tibr), jewelry (hilyah), or utensils (aniyah) — or trade goods of equivalent value. The wealth must be free from debt and from basic needs (hajah asliyyah) and must be growing (nami), either actually (through trade, reproduction) or potentially (as with gold/silver that can be invested). A key condition for zakat to be obligatory (shart al-wujub) is the passing of a full lunar year (hawl) on the original nisab. If additional wealth is acquired during the year, it is added (yudamm) to similar wealth and its zakat is due at the end of the original year. For example, if one has 200 dirhams and gains another 100 during the year, the 100 is added and zakat on 300 is paid at the end of the year. The text also permits paying zakat in advance for up to several years, provided one owns the nisab at the time of payment. For validity (sihhah) of payment, intention (niyyah) is required concurrent with giving. The intention can be legal (hukmi), e.g., one gives without intention and then intends while the money is still in the poor's hand. It is not required that the poor know it is zakat; if one gives a gift or loan and intends zakat, it is valid. If one gives all his wealth as charity without explicitly intending zakat, the obligation of zakat is still discharged (saqata 'anhu farduha) because the intention is subsumed? Actually the text says 'sadaqa bi-jami‘i malihi' — if he gives all his wealth as charity and does not intend zakat, the obligation falls. This is based on the idea that general charity can cover the obligation if it is given with the intention of discharging the duty? The Hanafi position here is that if one gives all his wealth away, there remains no wealth on which zakat is due for future years, but for the current year's zakat, has it been paid? The text says 'saqata 'anhu farduha' meaning the obligation for that year falls, but careful: if he had zakat due and gave all his wealth as charity without specifying, the zakat is considered fulfilled because the charity may include the amount due. This is a lenient ruling. The footnote explains the legislation of zakat: it was made obligatory in the second year of Hijra, and it is linked with prayer in many Quranic verses. Zakat can mean growth (nama’) or purification (taharah). The author quotes verses: 'Establish prayer and give zakat' (2:43), 'Take from their wealth charity to purify them' (9:103), 'Whatever you spend He will replace' (34:39). The passage emphasizes that worship is of three types: bodily, financial, and combined (hajj). Hajj is the fifth pillar because it combines both. The footnote also defines tibr, hilyah, and the ruling on mixing assets.

Key Terms
الزكاة (al-zakah): A financial act of worship involving transfer of ownership of specific wealth (nisab) to a specific recipient, obligatory on eligible Muslims.
النصاب (al-nisab): The minimum amount of wealth (e.g., 20 dinars gold or 200 dirhams silver) that triggers zakat obligation.
التبر (al-tibr): Gold or silver in uncoined form, before being minted into coins.
الحَوال (al-hawl): The passage of a full lunar year over the nisab, a condition for zakat obligation.

Definition and Conditions of Zakat
Defines zakat and lists its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of zakat and identify the conditions for its obligation and validity.

Core Idea
Zakat is the transfer of ownership of specific wealth to a specific person, obligatory upon meeting nisab, freedom from debt, growth, and a full year.

This lesson presents the foundational definition of zakat as a mandatory act of worship. The passage from Nur al-Idah lays out precisely who must pay zakat, what wealth is subject to it, and the conditions that trigger its obligation. Understanding these conditions is essential for correct application.

Guided Path
1
State the definition
Identify the two key components of the legal definition: tamlik (transfer of ownership) and mal makhsus (specific wealth) to a shakhs makhsus (specific person).
This is the core legal definition used in the Hanafi school.
2
Determine the hukm level
From the word 'فرضت', classify the hukm as fard 'ayn. List the five qualifications of the obligated person: free, Muslim, accountable (mukallaf), owner of nisab, and free from debt and basic needs.
The hukm is established from the matn's explicit statement.
3
Analyze the conditions for obligation
Break down the conditions on wealth: nisab threshold (gold/silver/equivalents), growth (actual or potential), and the passing of a full lunar year (hawl).
The condition 'free from debt' applies at the time the nisab is held.
4
Examine the rules for acquired wealth
Understand that wealth acquired during the year is added to its like (e.g., gold to gold) and zakat is paid at the end of the original year, not from the acquisition date.
This applies whether the wealth is from trade or other sources.
5
Understand the conditions for validity
Focus on intention (niyyah) concurrent with payment or setting aside. Note that lacking intention initially can be rectified later if the wealth is still in the recipient's hand. Also, advance payment (ta'jil) is valid if the payer possesses nisab.
The poor person need not know it is zakat; a gift or loan suffices with intention.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of Nur al-Idah, defining zakat and enumerating its conditions for obligation and validity. The author defines zakat legally as the transfer of ownership of specific wealth (mal makhsus) to a specific person (shakhs makhsus), i.e., the eligible recipients. It is fard upon every free, Muslim, accountable (mukallaf) individual who owns a nisab (minimum threshold) of currency (gold or silver) or trade goods equal in value to that nisab. The conditions for obligation include: (1) the wealth must be free from debt and from one's basic needs (hajah asliyyah), (2) it must be growing (nam) either actually (through trade or breeding) or potentially (i.e., capable of growth), (3) a full lunar year must pass over the original nisab. If wealth is acquired during the year, it is added to its like (e.g., gold to gold) and zakat is paid at the end of the original year. Advance payment (ta'jil) of zakat for future years is valid if the payer possesses the nisab. For validity of payment, intention (niyyah) is required concurrently with payment to the poor, the poor's agent, or when setting aside the zakat. If no intention is made initially and then is made while the wealth is still in the poor's hand, it suffices (ruling-based concurrence). The poor need not know it is zakat; even if called a gift or loan, the intention of zakat is sufficient. If one gives all his wealth without zakat intention, his fard zakat is not discharged.

Key Terms
تمليك مال مخصوص (tamlik mal makhsus): Transfer of ownership of specific wealth to a specific person (the recipient).
نصاب (nisab): The minimum amount of wealth that makes zakat obligatory. For gold: 20 mithqal (approx. 85g), for silver: 200 dirhams (approx. 595g).
حولان الحول (hawalan al-hawl): The passing of one full lunar year on the nisab wealth.

Zakat on Debts: Strong, Medium, Weak — and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Classifies debts for zakat purposes into strong, medium, weak

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify debts for zakat purposes into the three Hanafi categories and explain the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding weak debts.

Core Idea
Debts for zakat differ by source: strong (loan/trade), medium (non-trade sale), weak (non-wealth) — and the hawl depends on the category.

Zakat on debts is not a single rule. The Hanafi school distinguishes debts based on the nature of the underlying transaction. This passage from Nur al-Idah and its commentary classifies debts into strong, medium, and weak, and then presents a well-known intra-madhhab dispute: did the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree with Imam Abu Hanifa on the start of the hawl for weak debts? Understanding this classification is essential for applying zakat correctly when receiving long-standing debts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage explicitly names din qawi, wasat, and da'if. For each, note the underlying transaction (e.g., loan, trade vs. personal goods vs. mahr, bequest).
2
Extract the ruling for each category
State the rule: strong: zakat due for past years upon receipt of nisab; medium: zakat due only upon receipt of nisab, considering past year (sahih al-riwayah); weak: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, a new hawl starts after receipt; according to the two companions, zakat is due proportionally for all past years upon receipt.
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf
The dispute centres on weak debts. Understand each side's dalil: Abu Hanifa says weak debts are not fully owned until receipt, so hawl begins after; the Sahibayn say the essence of wealth exists from contract, so past years count.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: e.g., a woman receiving deferred mahr after 5 years pays zakat for only 1 year (Abu Hanifa) vs. for all 5 years proportionally (Sahibayn).
5
Note the mufta bihi
Later fatwa collections prefer the view of the two companions for convenience and public interest.

The nisab must be complete at both the beginning and end of the lunar year. A drop below nisab mid-year does not nullify the obligation.
Exception: If the nisab is not complete at the start, no zakat is due for that year even if it reaches nisab later.
3
Learn the specific nisab values
Nisab of gold: 20 mithqals (approx. 85-87g). Nisab of silver: 200 dirhams (approx. 700g). Excess above nisab is subject to proportional zakat when it reaches 5 dirhams or half a mithqal.
Adulterated coins are treated as pure if the pure substance predominates.
4
Apply the rules for payment
If paying from the same kind (e.g., grain), pay 2.5% (1/4 of 1/10). If paying from value, use the market price on the day the haul completes (day of obligation).
Price fluctuations after the haul do not affect the valuation.
5
Recognize the effect of destruction and legal tricks
If wealth is destroyed after the haul, the obligation drops proportionally. The destroyed portion is considered from the exempt (afuw) part. Legal tricks (hila) to avoid zakat are permitted by Abu Yusuf but disliked by Muhammad (the relied-upon view).
Tricks for a valid need are allowed; tricks out of miserliness are disliked by consensus.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the commentary (sharh) on Nur al-Idah. The author explains key rules of zakat regarding nisab and the calculation of zakatable amounts. First, the values of trade goods (urud) are added to gold and silver to complete the nisab. Gold and silver themselves are combined by value, not weight. For example, if one has 150 dirhams of silver and 5 mithqals of gold worth 50 dirhams, the total is 200 dirhams, and zakat of 5 dirhams is due. A temporary drop below nisab during the year does not break the continuity if the nisab is complete at both the beginning and end. However, if one acquires goods for trade not worth a nisab and its value later reaches nisab at the end of the year, zakat is not due for that year because the nisab was not complete at the start. The nisab of gold is 20 mithqals (approximately 85-87 grams today), and of silver 200 dirhams (approximately 700 grams today). Excess above nisab is subject to zakat proportionally, with a minimum of five (i.e., an increase of five dirhams or half a mithqal triggers zakat on the excess). Adulterated coins are treated as pure if the pure substance predominates. Jewels and pearls are not subject to zakat unless held for trade, like other merchandise. If the year completes on a measurable or weighable commodity (e.g., grains or gold), and its price fluctuates, paying from the same kind (e.g., one quarter tenth of the grain) is sufficient; if paying value, the market price on the day of obligation (completion of the year) is considered. The author then discusses destruction of wealth after the year: if the entire wealth is destroyed, the zakat obligation drops; if part is destroyed, only that part's zakat is dropped, and the destroyed amount is considered from the exempt (afuw) portion (what remains is still zakatable). Zakat is not taken forcibly from a reluctant person, nor from their estate after death unless they bequeath it, in which case it comes from the third of the estate. The passage ends with a difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on using legal tricks (hila) to avoid zakat: Abu Yusuf permits it, Muhammad dislikes it. The footnote elaborates on the trick of gifting the nisab before the year ends. The schools agree that tricks to escape an obligation are disliked if done out of miserliness, but allowed if for a valid need.

الحيلة (al-hilah): A legal stratagem to avoid an obligation or achieve a permissible end through a different legal means. Here, used to avoid zakat before it becomes due.

Categories and Prohibitions of Zakat Recipients
Opening of the chapter on zakat disbursement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the eight prescribed categories of zakat recipients, state which persons are prohibited from receiving zakat per the Hanafi school, and apply the recommended priority order for distribution.

Core Idea
Zakat must be distributed to the eight specified categories; giving to Banu Hashim is invalid in the relied-upon Hanafi view.

This passage from the chapter on disbursement (masrif) defines each of the eight categories of zakat recipients mentioned in the Quran. It also clarifies who may not receive zakat—including the rich, Banu Hashim, and one's own dependents—and discusses a notable intra-Hanafi dispute on the Banu Hashim issue. Understanding these rules is essential for valid zakat disbursement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Eight Categories
Read the definitions of faqir, miskin, the zakat collector, the one whose heart is to be reconciled, the slave, the debtor, the one in the path of Allah, and the wayfarer. Note the specific Hanafi definitions for faqir (owns less than nisab) and miskin (owns nothing).
These correspond to the eight asnaf in Quran 9:60.
2
List Prohibited Recipients
The text lists categories to whom zakat is not valid: disbelievers, the rich (who own nisab), a rich child, Banu Hashim and their clients, and also one's own dependents (such as spouse, children, parents, slaves).
Prohibition is strict for some; for others, it is due to dependency.
3
Analyze the Banu Hashim Dispute
Note that Imam al-Tahawi differed, permitting zakat to Banu Hashim after the Prophet's death, but the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) maintains the prohibition. Understand the evidence on both sides.
The hadith 'Sadaqah is not lawful for us' is the key proof.
4
Apply Prioritization Rules
The text gives an order of preference: relatives of prohibited degree, neighbors, people of the same district, same profession, same town. Also, it is disliked to transfer zakat to another town without reason, and it is recommended to give enough to make the recipient needless of asking.
These rules maximize benefit and fulfill religious obligations.

Obligation and Scope of Sadaqat al-Fiṭr
Defines obligation conditions and dependents for Sadaqat al-Fiṭr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fiṭr and identify for whom it must be paid according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is wajib upon every free Muslim who has surplus nisab at dawn of Eid, even without a year.

Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is a unique obligation tied to the completion of Ramadan. Unlike zakat, it does not require the passage of a year or the condition of growth. In this lesson, we examine who must pay it, when it becomes obligatory, and for whom the head of household must pay. The text from Nur al-Idah with its commentary lays out these rules clearly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for obligation
List the conditions mentioned: free, Muslim, mukallaf, owner of nisab, free from debt and basic needs.
Note that nisab here is surplus wealth beyond essentials, not the zakat nisab.
2
Determine the time of obligation
The obligation attaches at dawn of the day of Eid al-Fitr. What happens if someone dies or becomes poor before dawn?
The text states it does not become obligatory in that case.
3
Identify for whom the head of household must pay
The head pays for himself, his minor poor children, his slaves for service, mudabbar, umm walad. He does not pay for his adult child, wife, mukatab, jointly-owned slave, absconding slave, etc.
The rationale is guardianship and expenses.
4
Examine the proofs
The hadith from Ibn Abbas and the Prophet's command are cited. Note that the evidence is ahad, hence the obligation is wajib not fard.
The Hanafi principle that ahad hadith gives wujub, not fard.

Chapter of Sadaqat al-Fitr: It is obligatory upon a free Muslim, accountable, owner of nisab (or its value) even if a year has not passed over it, at the dawn of the day of Fitr, and it is not for trade, free from debt and his basic needs and needs of his family. And considered in it is sufficiency, not estimation, and it is his residence, furniture, clothes, horse, weapon, and slaves for service. He gives it for himself and his minor poor children, and if they are rich, he gives it from their wealth. It is not obligatory upon the grandfather in the apparent narration. And it is chosen that the grandfather is like the father when he [the father] is absent or poor. And for his slaves for service, his mudabbar, and his umm walad, even if they are disbelievers; not for his mukatab, nor his adult child, nor his wife, nor a jointly-owned slave, nor an absconding slave except after his return, and likewise the usurped and the captive. And it is: half a sa' of wheat, or its flour, or its saweeq, or a sa' of dates, or raisins, or barley, and it is eight ratls by Iraqi measure. And it is permissible to pay the value, and it is better when one finds what he needs because it is quicker to fulfill the need of the poor. And if it is a time of hardship, then wheat and barley and what is eaten are better than dirhams. And the time of obligation: at the dawn of the day of Fitr. So whoever dies or becomes poor before it, or becomes Muslim, or becomes rich, or is born after it, it is not necessary upon him. It is recommended to give it before going out to the prayer place. And it is valid if he gives early or delays, and delay is disliked. And each person gives his fitrah to one poor person. And there is disagreement on the permissibility of dividing one fitrah among more than one poor person. And it is permissible to give the amount due from a group to one person according to the correct view. And Allah is the granter of success to the truth.

(1) It was commanded in the year in which Ramadan was prescribed before zakat, and he used to deliver a sermon two days before Fitr commanding its payment, and it is not forfeited by the destruction of wealth after obligation, contrary to zakat. And the reason for its legislation is what Ibn 'Abbas narrated: 'The Messenger of Allah prescribed zakat al-fitr as a purification for the fasting person from idle talk and obscenity, and as food for the needy. Whoever pays it before the prayer, it is an accepted zakat, and whoever pays it after the prayer, it is a charity among charities.' Reported by Abu Dawud in Zakat, chapter Zakat al-Fitr (1609).
(2) And it is obligatory due to his saying in his sermon: 'Pay on behalf of every free person and slave, young and old, half a sa' of wheat, or a sa' of barley, or a sa' of dates.' Reported by Abu Dawud in Zakat, chapter of those who narrated half a sa' of wheat (1622).
(3) Know that the nisabs are three: a nisab in which growth is required and zakat and all rulings related to growing wealth are attached to it. And a nisab for which four rulings become obligatory: the prohibition of charity, the obligation of sacrifice, sadaqat al-fitr, and maintenance of relatives; growth by trade is not required, nor the passage of a year. And a nisab by which the prohibition of begging is established, which is when one has provision for his day according to some. And some say: it is that he owns fifty dirhams.
(4) Because guardianship and expenses are not nullified by tadbir and istilad, but their marketability is impaired by them because they cannot be sold. Fath Bab al-'Inaya (1/550).
(5) His statement: 'not for his mukatab', due to lack of guardianship, and it is not obligatory upon the mukatab because what is in his hand belongs to his master. His statement: 'nor his adult child', i.e., the poor one, even if he is under his care, due to lack of guardianship. His statement: 'nor his wife', due to lack of complete guardianship over her. His statement: 'nor a jointly-owned slave nor an absconding slave except after his return', due to deficiency of guardianship and expense in respect of each of them. His statement: 'and likewise the usurped and the captive', so it is not obligatory upon their master except after their return, then it becomes obligatory for the past.
(6) The Iraqi ratl is one hundred and thirty dirhams, so the sa' is what contains one thousand and forty dirhams. And Abu Yusuf's statement: the sa' is what contains five and one-third ratls, his meaning is by ratl the ratl of Medina, which is thirty ustars, and the Iraqi ratl is twenty ustars, so the total according to both opinions is one hundred and sixty ustars. And the ustar: six and a half dirhams.

(1) Because of what al-Hakim narrated in 'Ulum al-Hadith (1/131) from Ibn 'Umar: 'The Messenger of Allah used to command us to give sadaqat al-fitr before the prayer, and he used to distribute it before going to the prayer place and say: 'Make them independent of asking on this day.'
(2) Al-Harawi said in Fath Bab al-'Inaya (1/554): 'It is not forfeited if he delays beyond the day of fitr according to the most correct opinion, even if he becomes poor, because it is a financial act of worship, so it is not forfeited after obligation except by payment, like zakat, because the aspect of worship in charity is understood, which is filling the need of the needy.'

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on Sadaqat al-Fiṭr, defining its obligation, conditions, amount, and related rulings. It begins by stating that Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is obligatory upon every free Muslim who is legally responsible (mukallaf) and owns nisab, even if that nisab has not been in his possession for a full lunar year. The nisab here is not the same as zakat nisab; it does not require growth or the passage of a year. The person must be free from debt and from his essential needs (like housing, furniture, clothing, horse, weapon, and slaves for service). The obligation is according to sufficiency of basic needs, not estimation. The person gives for himself and his minor poor children; if the children are rich, he gives from their wealth. The grandfather is not obligated in the apparent narration, but it is chosen that he is like the father when the father is absent or poor. The person gives for his slaves for service, his mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death), and his umm walad (slave mother of his child), even if they are non-Muslims. He does not give for his mukatab (slave under contract of freedom), nor for his adult child (even if poor), nor for his wife, nor for a jointly-owned slave, nor for an absconding slave until he returns, nor for a usurped or captive slave until they return. The amount is half a sa' of wheat, or its flour, or its saweeq (parched barley), or a full sa' of dates, raisins, or barley. A sa' is eight Iraqi ratls. Paying the value (in money) is permissible and is better when one finds what he needs because it fulfills the poor's need faster. However, in times of hardship, grains and what is eaten are better than dirhams. The time of obligation is at dawn of the day of Fitr; if one dies or becomes poor before dawn, or accepts Islam, or becomes rich, or is born after dawn, it is not obligatory. It is recommended to give it before going to the Eid prayer, but it is valid if given early or delayed, though delay is disliked. Each person should give his fitrah to one poor person, but there is disagreement on dividing one fitrah among multiple poor people; however, it is permissible for multiple people to pool their fitrah and give it to one needy person. The footnotes provide the hadith proofs: the order was instituted in the year Ramadan was prescribed, and the Prophet's hadith about paying half a sa' of wheat or a sa' of barley/dates. They also explain the three types of nisab: one for zakat (with growth and haul), one for sadaqat al-fitr, sacrifice, and family maintenance (without growth or haul), and one for prohibition of begging (having day's provision or fifty dirhams). The footnotes also detail that guardianship and expenses are not nullified by tadbir and istilad, and give specific rulings for mukatab, adult child, wife, jointly-owned slave, and absconding slave. A note clarifies the Iraqi ratl (130 dirhams) and Abu Yusuf's opinion on the sa' (5 1/3 ratls of Medina, which equals 160 ustars, with an ustar being 6.5 dirhams). Finally, a hadith from al-Hakim recommends giving before the prayer, and al-Harawi explains that the obligation does not fall due if delayed, as it is a financial worship like zakat.

Key Terms
نصاب (niṣāb): The minimum amount of wealth that triggers legal obligations; here specifically the nisab for sadaqat al-fitr, which does not require growth or haul.
صاع (ṣāʿ): A measure of volume; for sadaqat al-fitr, one sa' of dates/barley/raisins or half sa' of wheat, equivalent to 8 Iraqi ratls or 1040 dirhams weight.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose emancipation is deferred until the master's death; the slave remains under ownership but cannot be sold.
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave who has made a contract with his master to purchase his freedom; he is to be treated like a free person in some matters.
فطرة (fiṭrah): The obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan; also called zakat al-fitr or sadaqat al-fitr.

Key Takeaway
Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is wajib on every free Muslim who has surplus nisab at dawn of Eid; the head of household pays for himself, his minor children, and his non-absconding slaves, but not for his mukatab, adult child, wife, or jointly-owned slaves.

Amount, Payment, and Timing of Sadaqat al-Fiṭr
Specifies the amount, measure, permissibility of value payment, and timing rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the required amount of Sadaqat al-Fiṭr, determine the recommended and permissible times of payment, and apply rulings on paying value and distribution.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of other grains; paying its value is permissible and often better for quick relief.

The amount of Sadaqat al-Fiṭr is specified by the Prophet ﷺ, but the Hanafi school permits paying its value in money to ease the need of the poor. In this lesson, we analyze the exact measures, the time of obligation, and the rulings on early or delayed payment, as well as how to distribute it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prescribed amounts
Note that it is half a sa' of wheat or its flour/saweeq, or one sa' of dates, raisins, or barley. Understand the sa' measurement: 8 Iraqi ratls.
Abu Yusuf has a different definition of sa' but the volume is the same.
2
Understand the permissibility and preference of paying value
Paying the cash value is permissible and is better when it fulfills the poor's need quicker, except in times of scarcity when grains are better.
This is a distinctive Hanafi ruling.
3
Determine the time of obligation and recommended timing
Obligation attaches at dawn of Eid. It is recommended to pay before the Eid prayer, but paying early (even before Ramadan?) or later is valid, though delay is disliked.
The obligation is not forfeited by delay; it remains a debt.
4
Apply rulings on distribution
Each person's fitrah is optimally given to one poor person, but it is permissible to give multiple fitrahs to one person. There is disagreement on dividing one fitrah among multiple poor.
The correct view (sahih) is that a group can pool to one.

The Binding Time of Zakat al-Fitr and Who Is Exempt
Ruling with proof on timing and exemptions of Zakat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the time of obligation for Zakat al-Fitr and identify who is exempt from paying it.

Core Idea
Zakat al-Fitr becomes wajib at dawn of Eid; anyone dead, poor, or newborn before that is exempt.

This lesson explains the precise moment when Zakat al-Fitr becomes binding (waqt al-wujub) and the categories of people who are not required to pay. Understanding this timing is crucial for correct observance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqt al-wujub
The passage states that the time of obligation is at dawn of Eid al-Fitr. This is a key Hanafi position: the obligation is tied to the moment of dawn, not the prayer or moon sighting.
For Hanafis, this means whoever is alive and affluent at dawn must pay; others are exempt.
2
List the conditions for obligation
At dawn, the person must be alive, Muslim, and possess sufficient wealth (ghina) to be liable. The exemptions listed (death, poverty before dawn, becoming Muslim after dawn, etc.) are cases where these conditions are not met.
Note that becoming wealthy after dawn does not create obligation because the condition was absent at the binding moment.
3
Apply the exemptions to scenarios
Consider scenarios: if a person dies before dawn, no fitrah is due from his estate; if he dies after dawn, it must be paid. Similarly, a newborn after dawn is not obligated.
4
Recognize the recommended timing
The text says it is mustahabb to give before leaving for Eid prayer. This follows the Prophet's practice of distributing before the prayer.
5
Understand the ruling on delay
It is valid to give early or late, but delay is makruh. The hadith emphasizes early giving to enrich the poor on Eid day.
The obligation remains even if delayed; it does not lapse.

Distribution of Zakat al-Fitr: Single vs Multiple Recipients and the Non-Lapse Ruling
Ruling with proof on distribution and non-lapse of Zakat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred ruling on distributing one fitrah among multiple recipients and explain why Zakat al-Fitr does not lapse if delayed.

Core Idea
One fitrah should go to one poor person, but a group's fitrah may be given to one; delay does not cancel the wajib.

This lesson addresses the distribution of Zakat al-Fitr: whether one person's fitrah can be split among several poor recipients, and whether the obligation falls away if payment is postponed. The text presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on splitting, and a clear ruling on non-lapse supported by qiyas to zakat.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule of distribution
The text begins: 'Each person gives his fitrah to one poor person.' This is the default: one fitrah per poor person.
The term 'فقير واحد' indicates a single recipient for each giver's fitrah.
2
Identify the ikhtilaf on splitting one fitrah
The text says there is disagreement on whether one fitrah can be divided among more than one poor person. This is an intra-Hanafi difference; some scholars allowed splitting, others did not.
3
Analyze the tarjih: group fitrah to one person
The text concludes: 'It is permissible to give what is due from a group to one person according to the correct view.' This means the combined fitrah of multiple people can be given to a single poor person, but splitting one individual's fitrah among many is not mentioned as preferred.
The phrase 'على الصحيح' indicates the relied-upon view.
4
Understand the non-lapse ruling
Footnote (2) from al-Harawi states that Zakat al-Fitr does not fall due (i.e., does not become invalid) if delayed after Eid day, even if the payer becomes poor later. It is like zakat – a financial devotion that must be fulfilled.
This is the 'asahh' (most sound) view.
5
Connect to the evidence
The proof for non-lapse is qiyas to zakat: financial acts of worship do not lapse after obligation; they must be fulfilled. The text also implies the hadith from the first part, but the qiyas is explicit.
