Navigating the Hanafi Fiqh Tradition: The Bibliography of Imdad al-Fattah (Books 28-83)
Bibliography of Hanafi works from 28 to 83

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify key Hanafi works from the bibliography, classify them as primary (Zahir al-Riwaya) or secondary (commentaries, fatwa collections), and state the death dates of major authors.

Core Idea
A Hanafi scholar's library is a living chain: from the Zahir al-Riwaya core to the commentaries that illuminate it.

This passage is a bibliography from the commentary Imdad al-Fattah Sharh Nur al-Idah, listing 56 Hanafi works (entries 28-83). It is not a random list but a carefully curated map of the sources the commentator relied upon. Understanding this map helps you see how the Hanafi tradition transmits authority: from the original compilations (like al-Shaybani's works) through commentaries (like al-Kasani's Bada'i' al-Sana'i') to later authoritative summaries (like al-Durr al-Mukhtar). In this lesson, you will learn to read this bibliography as a window into the school's textual hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Zahir al-Riwaya books
Scan the list for works explicitly noted as part of Zahir al-Riwaya. The text mentions al-Jami' al-Saghir (entry 42) and al-Jami' al-Kabir (entry 45) by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani as such. These are two of the six core transmitted works.
Zahir al-Riwaya works form the highest authority for fatwa in the Hanafi school.
2
Differentiate matn from sharh
Many entries are commentaries (sharh) on earlier texts. For example, entry 46 is Jami' al-Kardari, a commentary on al-Jami' al-Saghir. Identify at least three such pairs.
A commentary explains and sometimes expands the original matn, but the matn's authority remains primary.
3
Note death dates and chronology
Each entry typically includes the author's death date (hijri). Observe the range from 187 AH (al-Shaybani) to 1088 AH (al-Haskafi). This shows the development of the school over centuries.
Later authors often build on earlier ones, creating a chain of transmission (isnad) in fiqh literature.
4
Recognize fatwa collections
Look for titles containing 'Fatawa' or 'Jami' al-Fatawa'. Examples: Jami' al-Fatawa (entry 43), Fatawa Qadikhan (entry 62), Fatawa al-Khabbaziyya (entry 63). These compile legal opinions of later scholars.
Fatwa collections are practical references but rank below Zahir al-Riwaya in authoritative weight.
5
Spot textual corrections
Entry 60 (Hulbat al-Mujalli) includes a note about a corrupt title corrected by Hajji Khalifa. This shows the commentator's attention to accurate citation, a hallmark of scholarly rigor.
Such corrections reflect the living tradition of manuscript study and transmission.

Commentary

Such bibliographies are common in later Hanafi commentaries and supercommentaries, as they demonstrate the breadth of sources the commentator relied upon and serve as a quick reference for the reader. The list includes foundational texts of the Hanafi school such as the “Jami’ al-Saghir” and “Jami’ al-Kabir” of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (which are part of the Zahir al-Riwaya), commentaries like “Bada’i’ al-Sana’i” of al-Kasani, and later works like “al-Durr al-Mukhtar” of al-Haskafi. Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwaya): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that form the foundation of the Hanafi school's authoritative transmissions.
شرح (Sharh): A commentary that explains, expands, or justifies a base text (matn).
فتاوى (Fatawa): A collection of legal opinions (fatwas) issued by a Hanafi jurist, often compiled by students.

Core Idea
Fatwa collections preserve the operative rulings (mufta bihi) of the Hanafi school for practical application.

In addition to theoretical works, the Hanafi school produced many fatwa collections that record the rulings relied upon in judicial practice. This lesson highlights the most prominent fatwa compilations listed in the passage and explains their role in the school's legal system.

Core Hanafi Fiqh Texts: Matn and Sharh (Entries 167–186)
Bibliographic list of early Hanafi texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the authors and key features of foundational Hanafi matn and sharh works such as al-Mukhtar, al-Hidayah, and al-Multaqa.

Core Idea
A Hanafi jurist's library begins with matn (al-Mukhtar, al-Kanz, al-Wiqayah) and climbs to sharh (al-Hidayah, al-Ikhtiyar).

This lesson introduces the first twenty entries of the bibliography, covering the most essential texts of the Hanafi school. These include concise primers (matn) like al-Mukhtar and al-Kanz, and their authoritative commentaries (sharh) like al-Hidayah and al-Ikhtiyar. Understanding these works and their authors is the first step in navigating Hanafi legal literature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genre of each work
Determine whether the entry is a matn (concise text), a sharh (commentary), a fatwa collection, or a hadith work. This helps in understanding its function in the school.
For example, al-Mukhtar (entry 167) is a matn, while al-Ikhtiyar is its sharh by the same author.
2
Note the author and his death date
Record the author's full name and year of death. This places the work chronologically and shows the chain of transmission.
Al-Marghinani (d. 593) is a key figure; his al-Hidayah is a cornerstone of Hanafi fiqh.
3
Recognize cross-references to other works
Some entries explicitly mention other texts they comment on or compile. For instance, al-Multaqa (entry 186) encompasses issues from al-Quduri, al-Mukhtar, al-Kanz, and al-Wiqayah.
This shows how later works summarize earlier ones.
4
Group works by their role in the school
Categorize: (a) matn for memorization, (b) sharh for detailed reasoning, (c) fatwa collections for applied rulings, (d) usul works for methodology.
Al-Hidayah is a sharh; al-Mustasfa is a usul work; Mukhtarat al-Nawazil is a fatwa collection.
5
Memorize the five most important works from this list
Focus on al-Mukhtar, al-Hidayah, al-Kanz, al-Wiqayah, and al-Multaqa as the core reference texts.
These are frequently cited in later Hanafi literature.

Fatwa Collections and Supplementary Sources (Entries 187–215)
Bibliographic list of later Hanafi texts, including fatwa collections and lexical works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify major Hanafi fatwa collections, commentaries, and reference works from the later period, and explain their role in the school.

Core Idea
Later Hanafi works compile fatwa precedents (al-Nawazil, al-Waqi'at) and provide lexical tools (al-Misbah, al-Nihayah) to support accurate legal reasoning.

This lesson covers the latter part of the bibliography, from al-Muntaqa to al-Yanbu'. These works include fatwa collections (e.g., al-Nawazil, al-Waqi'at), comprehensive commentaries (e.g., al-Nahr al-Fa'iq), and lexical aids (e.g., al-Misbah al-Munir, al-Nihayah fi Gharib al-Hadith). Understanding these texts allows a student to appreciate how the Hanafi school developed over centuries, moving from matn to applied fatwa and linguistic precision.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between fatwa collections and commentaries
Some entries are collections of actual fatwas (e.g., al-Waqi'at, al-Nawadir), while others are commentaries (e.g., al-Manh, al-Nahr al-Fa'iq). Identify which is which.
Fatwa collections document rulings on real cases; commentaries explain existing texts.
2
Note the author's position in the school
Authors like al-Hakim al-Shahid (d. 334) and al-Sadr al-Shahid (d. 536) were prominent Hanafi judges. Their fatwa collections carry weight.
Martyred scholars (شهيد) indicate high respect.
3
Recognize lexical works and their purpose
Entries like al-Misbah al-Munir and al-Nihayah fi Gharib al-Hadith are language references. They help jurists understand terms in the Quran and hadith.
Al-Misbah is a dictionary; al-Nihayah explains rare hadith terms.
4
Identify cross-references to earlier matn
Some works are explicitly commentaries on earlier matn, e.g., al-Manh is a commentary on Tanwir al-Absar; al-Nahr al-Fa'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq.
This shows the hierarchical relationship of texts.
5
Memorize the most cited fatwa works from this list
Focus on al-Waqi'at (both al-Natifi and al-Sadr al-Shahid), al-Nawazil (Abu al-Layth), and al-Fatawa al-Wilwaljiyyah.
These collections are frequently referenced in later fatwa compilations.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography of Hanafi fiqh works, likely from the introduction or appendix of Imdad al-Fattah. It lists books from al-Mukhtar to al-Yanbu', providing author names and death dates. These are standard references in the Hanafi madhhab, including foundational texts like al-Hidayah, al-Mukhtar, al-Kanz, and al-Wiqayah, as well as collections of fatwa and commentaries. The list reflects the scholarly chain and sources used by the author. Note that some entries include corrections (e.g., entry 194 corrects a common misattribution).
