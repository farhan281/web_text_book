Conflicting Reports and the Principle of Tahātur
Ruling on conflicting reports about meat and water, with tahātur and continuity of original ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of tahātur to conflicting reports and state the resulting ruling based on the original presumption.

Core Idea
When two reliable reports cancel, the original ruling stands – prohibition for meat, purity for water.

This lesson addresses a recurring fiqh scenario: two trustworthy witnesses give contradictory information about the same object. How does a Muslim determine the ruling? The Hanafi school applies the principle of tahātur (mutual nullification), whereby the reports cancel each other out, and the original ruling is presumed to continue. This preserves legal certainty in the face of conflicting evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of dispute
Determine what the conflicting reports are about – in this passage, meat (or water). Each report gives a legal classification (e.g., halal vs haram).
The classification must be directly about the object's legal status.
2
Determine the original ruling (aṣl)
What is the baseline ruling for this object in the absence of any report? For meat, the original is prohibition (haram) unless proven to be properly slaughtered. For water, the original is purity (tahir).
The original ruling is established by sharīʿah, not by the reports.
3
Apply tahātur to the reports
Since both reports come from reliable (ʿadl) sources and contradict each other, they cancel out. Neither can be given preference over the other.
This assumes no additional information that would create a tarjīḥ (preference).
4
Return to the original ruling
After the reports cancel, the legal situation reverts to the original ruling. For meat, it remains haram until the correct slaughter is confirmed. For water, it remains pure.
This is an application of istiṣḥāb (presumption of continuity).

ولو أخبره عدل، أن هذا اللحم ذبيحة مجوسي أو ميتة، وعدل آخر أنه ذبيحة مسلم، فإنـه لا يحل له، لأنه لما تهاتر الخبران) بقي على الحرمة الأصلية؛ لأنه لا يحل إلا بالذكاة الشرعية، ولو أخبرا عن ماء وتهاترا بقي على الطهارة الأصلية، كما تقدم. [ تنبيه ]: مثل تعارض الخبرين الشك، وقالوا: إن الشك على ثلاثة أضرب: شك طرأ على أصل حرام. وشك طرأ على أصل مباح، وشك لا يعرف أصله. فالأول: مثل أن يجد شاة مذبوحة في بلد فيها مسلمون ومجوس، فلا تحل حتى يعلم أنـهـا ذكـاة مسلم، لأن الأصل فيها الحرمة، إذاً حل الأكل يتوقف على تحقق الذكاة الشرعية فصار حل الأكل مشكوكاً، فلو كان الغالب فيها المسلمين جاز الأكل عملاً بالغالب المفيد للحل. والثاني: أن يجد ماء متغيراً واحتمل أن يكون تغيره بنجاسة أو طول مكث يجوز التطهير به عملاً بأصل الطهارة. والثالث: مثل معاملة من أكثر ماله حرام لا تحرم مبايعته، حيث لم يتحقق حرم حرمة ما أخذه منه، ولكـن يـكــره خوفاً من الوقوع في الحرام، ط.
And if a reliable person informs him that this meat is from a Magian slaughter or carrion, and another reliable person informs him that it is from a Muslim's slaughter, then it is not permissible for him, because when the two reports cancel each other out, it remains on the original prohibition, as it is only permissible through shar'i slaughter. And if they both inform about water and cancel each other, it remains on original purity, as before. [Note]: An example of conflicting reports is doubt. They said doubt is of three types: doubt that arises on a basis of prohibition, doubt that arises on a basis of permissibility, and doubt whose origin is unknown. The first: like finding a slaughtered sheep in a town containing both Muslims and Magians, it is not permissible until knowing it is a Muslim's slaughter, because the origin is prohibition; therefore eating becomes dependent on verification of shar'i slaughter, so its permissibility becomes doubtful. If Muslims are the majority, eating is permissible by acting on the majority which yields permissibility. The second: like finding changed water and it being possible that its change is due to impurity or long duration; purification with it is permissible by acting on the original purity. The third: like dealing with someone most of whose wealth is forbidden; his transaction is not forbidden because the prohibition of what one takes from him is not confirmed, but it is disliked out of fear of falling into the forbidden.

Commentary
This concluding section addresses conflicting reports from reliable witnesses. When one says meat is forbidden and another says it is permissible, the reports cancel out, and the original ruling (prohibition for meat unless properly slaughtered) prevails. For water, the original ruling is purity. The note then categorizes three types of doubt: (1) doubt about something originally forbidden (e.g., a slaughtered animal in a mixed population), (2) doubt about something originally permissible (e.g., changed water), and (3) doubt where the origin is unknown (e.g., dealing with someone who has mixed lawful and unlawful wealth). The Hanafi principles of istishab (presumption of continuity) and majority are applied.

Key Terms
تهاتر (tahātur): Mutual nullification of two reports due to equal credibility.
أصل (aṣl): The original ruling (prohibition or permissibility) that is presumed to continue until evidence changes it.

Checkpoint
Two reliable witnesses conflict about water: one says it is pure, the other says it is impure. What is the ruling?
A) The water is impure because the witness for impurity is more cautious.
B) The water is pure because the reports cancel and the original ruling is purity.
C) The water is impure because the number of witnesses is equal.
D) The water is pure because purity is always preferred.

Key Takeaway
When reliable witnesses conflict, the reports cancel, and the original ruling (prohibition for meat, purity for water) remains in effect.

The Three Types of Doubt (Shakk) and Their Rulings
Classification of doubt into three types with examples and rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given scenario of legal doubt into one of three types and apply the appropriate Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
Doubt is classified by the original basis – prohibition, permissibility, or unknown – each with a distinct ruling.

Doubt (shakk) permeates many fiqh scenarios, but not all doubts are treated equally. The Hanafi school delineates three categories: doubt arising from something originally prohibited, doubt arising from something originally permissible, and doubt with an unknown origin. Each category triggers a different presumption. This lesson systematically unpacks these types using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of doubt
What is the thing about which there is doubt? It could be meat, water, or a transaction.
The object determines which category of doubt applies.
2
Determine the original ruling (aṣl) of that object
What is the baseline sharʿī ruling for this object? For meat: prohibition. For water: purity. For transactions: permissibility unless proven otherwise.
The original ruling is known from general principles of fiqh.
3
Classify the doubt into one of three types
Type 1: Doubt that arises on a basis of prohibition (e.g., meat in a mixed town). Type 2: Doubt that arises on a basis of permissibility (e.g., changed water). Type 3: Doubt whose origin is unknown (e.g., dealing with someone whose wealth is mixed).
The classification depends on whether the origin is known and what that origin is.
4
Apply the ruling corresponding to the type
Type 1: Remain on prohibition until certainty of permissibility. Type 2: Remain on permissibility (purity) unless impurity is certain. Type 3: The act is permissible but disliked (makruh) if it involves clear risk of haram.
The ruling may be modified by circumstances like majority.

Key Terms
شك (shakk): Doubt about a matter of fiqh, classified into three types as explained.
غالب (ghālib): The predominant or majority situation, which can tip the ruling towards permissibility in Type 1 doubt.
أصل مباح (aṣl mubāḥ): Original permissibility, e.g., water is originally pure.
أصل حرام (aṣl ḥarām): Original prohibition, e.g., meat is originally haram until slaughtered properly.

Key Takeaway
Doubt is not monolithic; its ruling depends on the original basis (prohibition, permissibility, or unknown), and in Type 1, the majority can shift the ruling to permissibility.
