Understanding Hamd: Its Definition and Distinctions
Defining hamd and its linguistic and technical relationships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hamd in the Hanafi tradition and distinguish it from madh and shukr.

Core Idea
Hamd is obligatory praise for Allah's voluntary beneficence, narrower than madh but more comprehensive than some forms of shukr.

This lesson unpacks the technical definition of hamd and its relationship with madh (general commendation) and shukr (gratitude). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the opening verse of the Quran and fulfilling the wajib of praise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Terms
Start by locating the three key terms: hamd, madh, and shukr. Note their basic definitions as given in the text.
Hamd is a branch of madh; madh is more general.
2
Analyze the Relationships
Work through the six relationships outlined: e.g., linguistic hamd vs. customary hamd, and linguistic hamd vs. linguistic shukr. Understand when they are identical and when they differ.
Pay attention to the condition of 'reaching the thank-giver' (qayd al-wusul).
3
Recognize the Obligation
Identify the claim that hamd is wajib (obligatory) for the essence of Allah. Note the proof: the performative nature of 'al-hamdu lillah' and the necessity of acknowledging Allah's attributes.
The text explicitly states 'الحمد واجب للذات'.
4
Examine the Grammatical Aspect
Consider why the sentence 'al-hamdu lillah' is declarative in form but performative in meaning. This reinforces the exclusive assignment of hamd to Allah.
Uttering it with conviction actualizes the praise.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification text from the commentary on Nur al-Idah. It opens by concluding the previous discussion (انتهى) and then explains the relationships between the terms hamd (praise), shukr (thanks), and madh (commendation) in both their linguistic and technical (shar'i) senses. The author, following the nazim (the poet), identifies six relationships. For example, linguistically hamd relates to three entities (perhaps the later-discussed meanings), while technically hamd relates to two. Shukr linguistically relates to shukr technically. The relationship between customary shukr and other than it is absolute generality and specificity (one is more general in every instance). Similarly, between linguistic hamd and customary hamd, the relationship is general-specific from one perspective (they share some meanings but not all). Linguistic hamd and linguistic shukr are identical if we do not consider the condition that shukr involves reaching the benefactor; if we do, then hamd is more general. Then the author provides his own poetic summary. The text then quotes from Shaykh Ali al-Ajhuri's commentary that hamd linguistically is a branch of madh (commendation) because madh is more general: madh can be for any quality, voluntary or not (like beauty or lineage), while hamd is specifically for voluntary actions. A key distinction: every hamd is madh, but not vice versa. Then the author discusses the grammatical nature of 'al-hamdu lillah': it is declarative in form but performative in meaning—uttering it with conviction brings the praise into existence. He chooses hamd over madh for praise of Allah because hamd is specifically for actions following beneficence, whereas madh is broader. He emphasizes that hamd is wajib (obligatory) for the essence of Allah, as the essence is deserving of all praise, not just for specific attributes. The passage concludes with a poetic reminder: even gratitude for blessings is itself a blessing requiring further thanks, so ultimate thanks is impossible without Allah's grace.

Key Terms
الحمد (al-hamd): Praise of Allah for His voluntary beneficence, exclusive to Him, and obligatory for His essence.
المدح (al-madh): General commendation of good qualities, both voluntary and involuntary; broader than hamd.
الشكر (al-shukr): Gratitude for a favor received, involving acknowledgment through heart, tongue, and limbs.

Lexical and theological commentary on the phrase 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the meaning and derivation of 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn' and its significance in Hanafi theology and prayer recitation.

Core Idea
Rabb is absolute lordship and care; ʿālamīn encompasses all creation, especially rational beings.

This passage unpacks the Qur'anic phrase 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn' (Lord of the worlds). You already know this phrase from al-Fatiha. Now we dive into its precise linguistic meaning, how 'Rabb' is used only for Allah in an absolute sense, and how 'al-ʿālamīn' includes all beings, highlighting Allah's universal Lordship. Understanding these terms strengthens your tawhid and enriches your prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key terms
The passage focuses on 'Rabb' and 'al-ʿālamīn'. Locate their definitions and derivations.
They are the subject of the commentary.
2
Analyze the derivation of 'Rabb'
The text explains 'Rabb' comes from tarbiyah (upbringing) and means the caretaker who brings each individual to perfection. Note that it is used absolutely only for Allah; for others it is restrictive (e.g., rabb al-dār).
This is a key theological point.
3
Examine 'al-ʿālamīn' as a plural
The word is the plural of ʿālam, originally a sign or a proper noun. It includes all created beings, primarily rational ones (humans, angels, jinn). Two derivations are mentioned: from ʿalam (sign) or ʿilm (knowledge).
The sound masculine plural form emphasizes rational beings.
4
Understand the iqtibās (quotation)
The author notes that using 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn' is a subtle quotation from the Quran without explicitly stating it, which is permitted to open a book.
This shows the author's respect for the Quran.
5
Connect to fiqh and practice
The phrase is recited in every rakʿah of prayer. Knowing its meaning enhances khushūʿ (concentration) and reinforces the belief in Allah's exclusive Lordship.
This is the practical takeaway.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and theological commentary on the phrase 'Rabb al-ʿālamīn' from the Qur'anic verse (al-Fatiha: 2). The author begins by quoting a poetic line about thankfulness, then explains that mentioning blessings after affirming the essence's deservingness highlights two deserving qualities: the essence and the blessings. The word 'Rabb' means caretaker of each individual's interests, bringing them to perfection. It is an attribute of exaggeration from tarbiya (upbringing). Rabb is applied absolutely only to Allah; for others, it is used in a restricted sense (e.g., 'rabb al-dār', owner of the house). The word 'al-ʿālamīn' (the worlds) is analyzed: it is the plural of ʿālam, originally a proper noun but treated as a common noun. It includes all created beings, which by their contingency demonstrate the necessity of the Creator. The derivation is either from 'alam (sign) meaning that creation is a sign of Allah, or from 'ilm (knowledge) meaning intelligent beings. The plural form is sound masculine, used predominantly for rational beings. Then the author notes that the phrase 'al-hamdu lillahi rabbi al-ʿālamīn' is a subtle quotation from the Quran used to open the book without explicitly stating it is a quotation, which is the rule for such quotations (iqtibās). The lesson concludes by mentioning that prayer (al-salāh) is mercy from Allah.

الرب (al-rabb): The Lord, the caretaker, owner, and educator; applied absolutely only to Allah.
العالمين (al-ʿālamīn): The worlds, referring to all created beings, primarily rational ones (humans, angels, jinn) but including others by extension.

Oath Rulings: When Does the Majority Replace the Whole?
Discusses rulings on swearing oaths and what actions fulfill or break them

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the majority does not replace the whole in oaths and identify the exceptions (such as reading a surah or catching prayer).

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, the majority does not replace the whole—except for specific cases like reading a surah.

Oaths (ayman) require precise fulfillment. A common question is whether doing most of an action counts as doing it all. This passage resolves that issue: the default ruling is that the full act is needed, but there are important exceptions based on linguistic usage and scholarly consensus. We will examine the proof, the disagreement with Shams al-A'immah, and the practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The majority of Hanafi scholars hold that in oaths, the majority does not stand in place of the whole. This is supported by consensus (ijma) on the loaf example.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the agreed-upon principle: if one swears not to eat a loaf, he does not break his oath unless he eats all of it. This shows the majority does not replace the whole.
The dalil is ijma (consensus) of the Hanafi school.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi argued that the majority has the ruling of the whole. The author weakens this by citing the consensus.
The first view (al-zahir) is preferred, as in al-Fath.
4
Examine the exceptions
Despite the general rule, there are exceptions: (a) Swearing not to read a surah: missing a single letter breaks the oath, but missing a long verse does not. (b) Swearing to 'catch' a prayer: catching one rak'ah or even the tashahhud suffices because 'catching' linguistically refers to the end.
These exceptions are based on 'urf and linguistic convention.
5
Apply the rule to new cases
The key is to identify the specific wording and the common understanding of the oath. For food oaths, the whole is required. For actions like 'reading' or 'catching', partial completion may be sufficient.
Each case is judged on its own merit.

Commentary
This passage discusses the rulings regarding the fulfillment of oaths (al-ayman). The central issue is whether performing the majority of an action constitutes fulfillment or violation of an oath. Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi held that the majority has the ruling of the whole, so eating most of a loaf would break an oath not to eat it. However, the preferred view (al-zahir) is the opposite, as stated in al-Fath: the majority does not replace the whole in oaths. This is supported by the agreed-upon rule that swearing not to eat a loaf requires eating all of it to break the oath. Nevertheless, exceptions exist: in oaths to read a surah, omitting a single letter still breaks the oath, but omitting a long verse does not. In oaths related to prayer (like 'if he catches Dhuhr'), catching one rak'ah is sufficient because 'catching' is understood as catching the latter part. Similarly, entering prayer with the imam in tashahhud fulfills the condition. This is based on the principle that 'catching a thing is by catching its end.' The passage relies on authorities like al-Fath, al-Bahr, al-Khulasa, and Sharh al-Maqdisi.

الحنث (al-hanath): Breaking an oath by doing what one swore not to do, or failing to do what one swore to do.
الأكثر (al-akthar): The majority or greater part of something, debated whether it substitutes for the whole in oaths.

Using the Hadith Index (Fihris al-Ahadith) in Hanafi Fiqh Works
Alphabetical hadith index for reference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate a specific hadith in the commentary using the fihris, and explain the purpose of such indexes in classical Hanafi texts.

Core Idea
The fihris is a navigational tool, not a hadith collection; it guides you to where the full hadith and its discussion appear.

Classical Hanafi fiqh works often include a comprehensive index of hadith (fihris al-ahadith) at the end. This index lists hadith identifying phrases alphabetically, along with page numbers where the hadith is cited or discussed in the main text. Understanding how to use this index is a practical skill for quickly locating evidence for rulings without flipping through hundreds of pages. In this lesson, we will examine the structure of such an index and practice using it.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the alphabetical arrangement
The index is ordered by the first word of each hadith (e.g., 'أبردوا', 'أتى', 'أتدْرُونَ'). Note that the definite article 'أل' is ignored for ordering purposes, but here the entries are listed as they appear.
In classical indexes, the first letter determines the order, but words prefixed with 'أ' (like 'إذا') may be grouped together.
2
Identify the hadith snippet
Each entry gives a short phrase from the hadith, enough to identify it. For example, 'أبردوا بالظهر' is a snippet from the hadith about delaying Zuhr in extreme heat.
The full text of the hadith is not given here; you must go to the indicated page.
3
Locate the page number
In the original printed edition, a page number follows each snippet. Although the page numbers are missing from this OCR version, a typical index would have them. You would then open the book at that page to find the hadith with its chain and commentary.
If the page number is missing or unclear, you can scan the surrounding pages for the hadith.
4
Understand the purpose
This index is not a substitute for the actual hadith. It is a reference tool to quickly find where a specific hadith is discussed. In a dense sharh like Imdad al-Fattah, the index saves time when researching a particular proof for a Hanafi ruling.
Use the index as a gateway, not as a source itself.

Hadith Fragments Beginning with 'La' as Hanafi Proof Anchors
A listing of memory-anchor hadith fragments for Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the function of 'la'-based hadith fragments in Hanafi fiqh and match each fragment to its corresponding ruling.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, 'la' fragments are concise proof anchors, each negating validity or permissibility without the proper condition.

This passage is a list of hadith fragments, each starting with the negation particle 'la', compiled as a memory aid for scholars and students. They are not full narrations but extracts that quickly recall key rulings. Understanding these fragments helps you instantly connect a Hanafi ruling with its prophetic evidence, particularly in areas like prayer, fasting, and purity.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Negation Pattern
Every fragment begins with 'la' which, in this context, negates the validity or permissibility of the action that follows. For example, 'لا صلاة' means 'there is no (valid) prayer'.
The negation applies to the act itself, not to its reward.
2
Identify the Subject
The word immediately after 'la' indicates the subject—'salah' for prayer, 'siyam' for fasting, 'i'tikaf' for seclusion, etc. This gives you the topic.
This allows quick cross-referencing with fiqh chapters.
3
Understand the Condition Implied
The fragment often omits the condition but implies it. For instance, 'لا صلاة إلا بفاتحة' means 'no prayer without Surah al-Fatiha'—the condition is recitation of Fatiha.
The complete hadith would include the condition; the fragment serves as a mnemonic for the rule.
4
Apply to Hanafi Rulings
Use these fragments to recall the Hanafi legal position. For example, 'لا صلاة لجار المسجد إلا في المسجد' indicates the Hanafi view that prayer at home is not valid for a neighbour of the mosque without excuse.
These fragments are used as concise evidence (dalil) in Hanafi fiqh works.
5
Note the Scope and Limitations
Not every 'la' hadith is absolute; some are conditional or abrogated. The Hanafi school interprets them according to usul principles.
Always verify the full hadith and its context before applying.

Understanding the Fihrist (Hadith Index) in Hanafi Fiqh Works
Alphabetical index of hadith excerpts with page numbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use an alphabetical hadith index (fihrist) to locate a hadith and identify the corresponding discussion page in 'Imdad al-Fattah Sharh Nur al-Idah'.

Core Idea
A fihrist is a finding tool, not a ruling—knowing how to use it efficiently saves hours of searching.

Classical fiqh commentaries often include a hadith index (fihrist) arranged alphabetically for quick reference. The passage you are about to study is exactly such an index from 'Imdad al-Fattah Sharh Nur al-Idah'. While it may look like a list of fragmented hadith texts, it is a powerful navigational tool. This lesson will teach you how to read and use a fihrist to locate the full discussions in the body of the book.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the structure of a fihrist entry
Each entry consists of a short hadith fragment (usually the first few words) followed by one or more page numbers. The fragment is not the full hadith, but a pointer.
The index is typically arranged in Arabic alphabetical order by the first letter of the hadith.
2
Identify the hadith fragment and its page reference
Find a hadith you are looking for by its first word. The page number(s) tell you where in the commentary the complete discussion of that hadith can be found.
Multiple page numbers indicate multiple occurrences or cross-references.
3
Navigate from index to the full discussion
Once you have the page number, go to that page in the commentary. There you will find the full hadith text along with its fiqh analysis.
Do not rely on the fragment alone for legal rulings; always consult the main text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major Hanafi primary sources and commentaries listed in this index and classify them by authority weight (Zahir al-Riwaya, commentaries, fatwa collections).

Core Idea
The index of a Hanafi text reveals the school's hierarchy of sources — from the six books of Zahir al-Riwaya to later commentaries and fatwa collections.

This passage is a bibliography ('fihr al-kutub') from the end of 'Imdad al-Fattah Sharh Nur al-Idah', listing 27 works cited in the commentary. These include foundational texts like al-Athar and al-Jami' al-Saghir by Imam Muhammad, important commentaries like al-Ikhtiyar and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, and fatwa collections like al-Bazzaziyya. Understanding this list helps you appreciate the breadth of sources a Hanafi scholar consults and the relative authority of each work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of each source
Scan the list and categorize each entry: is it a primary text (matn), a commentary (sharh), a fatwa collection, a usul work, or a non-Hanafi source? Note the descriptions like 'Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq' or 'al-Fatawa al-Tatarkhaniyya'.
This builds awareness of genre.
2
Recognize authors and their madhhab

Cross-madhhab citations are common in Hanafi texts.
3
Trace the authority chain

Zahir al-Riwaya texts carry the highest authority.

Understanding the Hierarchy of Hanafi Sources: A Bibliography Walkthrough
List of authoritative Hanafi and related sources referenced in the commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Hanafi work from this bibliography into its proper authority tier (Zahir al-Riwayah, later commentary, fatwa collection) and explain its role in fiqh derivation.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh rests on a pyramid: Zahir al-Riwayah at base, then later commentaries, then fatwa collections for contemporary practice.

A Hanafi faqih or mufti must know the reliability of each source. The bibliography in Imdad al-Fattah is not random; it is a carefully curated list reflecting the school's hierarchy. This lesson unpacks that hierarchy so you can evaluate the weight of any cited work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source category
For each work in the list, determine if it is a primary transmitted text (Zahir al-Riwayah), a commentary (sharh), a fatwa compilation, or a work from another madhhab.
Al-Mabsut (entry 158) is Zahir al-Riwayah; Fatawa Qadi Khan (entry 142) is a fatwa collection; al-Muwatta' (191) is from Imam Malik.
2
Assess its authority level
Determine whether the work belongs to the core Zahir al-Riwayah (e.g., al-Mabsut), the reliable later compilations (e.g., al-Hidayah, al-Kanz), or the fatwa collections (e.g., Fatawa Qadi Khan) that are used for tarjih.
Al-Kafi fi al-Furu' (150) is described as containing al-Mabsut but is a later summary, so authority is derivative.
3
Link to the school's tarjih mechanism
Understand how later scholars (e.g., Ibn Abidin) weigh these sources to determine the mufta bihi ruling. Fatwa collections are often consulted for contemporary practice but cross-checked with primary texts.
A mufti may rely on Fatawa Qadi Khan, but ultimate authority lies with Zahir al-Riwayah and the later accepted compilations like al-Durr al-Mukhtar.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography of classical works, primarily Hanafi, listed by the commentator of Imdad al-Fattah as references or sources consulted in his commentary on Nur al-Idah. It spans from entry 140 to 192, including numerous fatwa collections, commentaries, and primary texts. The list demonstrates the breadth of sources a Hanafi scholar relies upon: foundational texts like al-Mabsut (al-Asl) by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (d. 189 AH), which is a core book of Zahir al-Riwayah; later commentaries and compilations such as Fatawa Qadi Khan (d. 592 AH) and al-Hidayah commentaries; and works from other schools like the Muwatta' of Imam Malik. The inclusion of works by Shafi'i scholars like al-Baghawi and al-Bayhaqi shows the Hanafi method of engaging with broader Sunni scholarship. The list is arranged in alphabetical order by book title (except for some inconsistencies like the number 100 for al-Kanz). The commentator uses these works to derive rulings, strengthen arguments, or note ikhtilafat (differences of opinion).

Key Terms
الفتاوى الكبرى (al-Fatāwā al-Kubrā): A major fatwa collection by 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz (al-Sadr al-Shahid), a Hanafi authority.
زاهر الرواية (Zāhir al-Riwāyah): Refers to the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, such as al-Mabsut, which form the authoritative transmitted rulings of the Hanafi school.
المبسوط (al-Mabsūṭ): Also known as al-Asl, the foundational fiqh work of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, part of Zahir al-Riwayah.
فتاوى قاضي خان (Fatāwā Qāḍī Khān): A renowned Hanafi fatwa collection by Imam Fakhr al-Din Hasan ibn Mansur al-Uzjandi al-Farghani (d. 592 AH).
