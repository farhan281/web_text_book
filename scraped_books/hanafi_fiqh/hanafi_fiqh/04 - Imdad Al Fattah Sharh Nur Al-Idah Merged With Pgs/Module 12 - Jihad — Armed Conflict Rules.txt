Definition and Conditions of Shahid (Martyr)
Defines the legal category of shahid in Hanafi fiqh with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of a shahid and list its conditions, distinguishing between types of killers and circumstances.

Core Idea
A shahid is defined by specific killers and circumstances, not just any battlefield death.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi legal definition of a shahid (martyr). Unlike popular understanding, the school has precise conditions that determine who receives the special treatment of a martyr. This passage from Imdad al-Fattah explains the categories of killers, the required instruments, and the state of the victim. Understanding these conditions is crucial for properly applying the rulings of washing, shrouding, and prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being defined
Recognize that the passage defines 'shahid' in the legal sense, distinct from the theological concept. The definition sets conditions for outward treatment like that of the martyrs of Uhud.
2
List the categories of killers
The passage specifies four groups: people of war (ahl al-harb), rebels (ahl al-baghy), highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq), and thieves in the house at night (lusus fi manzilihi laylan). Note that for thieves, even a heavy object qualifies at night.
3
Examine the conditions of the killing
When killed by a Muslim, the act must be unjust ('udwan), intentional ('amdan), with a sharp instrument (bi-muhaddad). Excluded: accidental, semi-intentional (heavy object), and lawful killings (fixed punishment, retribution).
4
Examine the conditions of the victim
The victim must be Muslim, adult (baligh), and free from major ritual impurity (menstruation, postpartum bleeding, janabah). Also, the victim must not be carried alive from the battlefield after the war ends (irtithath).
5
Understand the legal consequence
Those who meet the definition are given the outward ruling of the martyrs of Uhud: not washed, buried in their bloodied clothes, and the funeral prayer is offered (except for shahid al-ma'rakah where there is a known difference).

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the rulings of the shahid (martyr) in Hanafi fiqh. It first affirms the Sunni creed that every person dies at their appointed term. Then it defines the shahid in the legal context (as opposed to the hereafter): one who is killed by specific groups (people of war, rebels, highway robbers, thieves at night) or found dead in a battle with an effect (wound, etc.), or killed unjustly by a Muslim with a sharp instrument intentionally, provided the person is a Muslim of sound mind (adult, free from states of major impurity) and did not relapse (i.e., die after being carried from the battlefield alive). The commentary explains each condition, distinguishing between direct and indirect killing, specifying that killing at night by thieves is included even with a heavy object because during the day help may come, but at night it is feared. Also, being killed by one's father or master is included. The passage emphasizes that the shahid is treated outwardly as a martyr of Uhud (i.e., not washed, buried in his bloodied clothes, etc.), and the conditions are meant to ensure that the person died in a state of honor for the religion.

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahid): In the legal definition: one who is killed by specific enemies (war, rebellion, highway robbers, thieves at night) or found in battle with an effect, or killed unjustly by a Muslim with a sharp instrument, provided the person is Muslim, adult, pure, and does not die after the war from a wound.
بِمُحَدَّد (bi-muhaddad): With a sharp instrument (e.g., sword, knife), as opposed to a heavy object (muthaqqal) which results in semi-intentional killing.
الارتثاث (al-irtithath): Being carried from the battlefield while still alive with wounds; if one dies after the war ends from these wounds, they are not a shahid in this context.

Definition and Conditions of the Martyr (Shahid) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defining the legal concept of shahid and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a shahid and identify the conditions that qualify a person as a shahid.

Core Idea
Being a shahid requires specific killers, a bodily trace, and a pure adult Muslim victim.

This passage from Imdad al-Fattah opens the chapter on martyrs. It first clarifies the theological backdrop—everyone dies by their appointed term. Then it provides the legal definition of a shahid, listing six categories of killers and additional conditions. Understanding this definition is essential because it determines whether the deceased receives the special treatment of a martyr: no washing, shrouded with his blood, and not prayed upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the theological premise
The author states that all killed die by their appointed term, affirming Sunni doctrine. This is not a legal condition but a theological foundation.
The quote from al-`Inayah reinforces this.
2
List the categories of killers
The shahid is killed by: ahl al-harb (warring non-Muslims), ahl al-baghy (rebels), qutta' al-tariq (highway robbers), thieves at night, or found dead on a battlefield with a trace. Additionally, a Muslim who kills another Muslim unjustly with a sharp instrument qualifies.
Each category has its own subconditions (e.g., night for thieves).
3
Examine the conditions for the Muslim killer case
When a Muslim kills another Muslim intentionally with a sharp instrument, the victim must be: Muslim, adult, free from hayd/nifas/janabah, and must not have retreated from the battlefield (if in a battle context).
This excludes accidental killings and those by heavy objects (shibh al-amd).
4
Understand the trace condition
On the battlefield, the corpse must show a trace like a wound, fracture, burn, or blood from ear/eye (not mouth/nose/anus). This indicates death was caused by the battle.
Blood from mouth/nose/anus is usually from natural causes, not battle.
5
Summarize the Hanafi position
The Hanafi definition is broader than other schools, including those killed unjustly by Muslims. This definition determines the fiqh rulings (no ghusl, burial with blood, etc.).
The position is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage is a definition_ruling that opens the chapter on the martyr (shahid) in Hanafi fiqh. The author first explains the linguistic and theological background: the martyr is so called because angels attend him, or he is testified for Paradise, or he is present with his Lord alive. He then states the Sunni doctrine that every killed person dies by his appointed term. The legal definition of a martyr in the Hanafi school is someone killed by specific categories of aggressors: people of war (harbis), rebels (baghi), highway robbers, or thieves attacking at night, even with a heavy object. Also included is one found dead on any of these battlefields with a physical trace (wound, burn, blood from ear/eye but not from mouth/nose/anus). Additionally, a Muslim killed unjustly and intentionally by a sharp instrument by another Muslim (even a father or master) qualifies if the victim is Muslim, adult, free from menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and janabah, and did not retreat from the battlefield after the war ends. The key point is that the martyr receives special treatment: no washing, shrouded with his blood and clothes. This definition sets the foundation for the subsequent rulings.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): Legal term for one killed in specific circumstances as defined by the Hanafi school, treated like the martyrs of Uhud in rulings of washing, shrouding, and prayer.
أهل الحرب (ahl al-harb): Non-Muslims with whom Muslims are in a state of war.
أهل البغي (ahl al-baghy): Rebellious Muslims who reject the legitimate ruler's authority.
قطاع الطريق (qutta' al-tariq): Highway robbers who attack travelers.

Rulings on Shrouding and Washing the Martyr
Rulings on shrouding, praying, washing, and clothing the shahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shrouding the martyr and explain the exception for washing when the martyr was in a state of janabah, a child, or insane, citing the proofs from hadith.

Core Idea
The shahid is shrouded with his blood and not washed, except if he was junub, a child, or insane — then he must be washed, per Abu Hanifa.

The martyr holds a special rank in Islam, and his funeral rites reflect that honor. This lesson examines the rulings on how the martyr is shrouded and whether he is washed, with particular attention to the exception for those in major impurity (janabah). We will explore the scriptural proofs and the internal Hanafi debate between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the martyr is shrouded with his blood and clothes, and prayed over without washing. This is the general rule based on the command of the Prophet.
The act is obligatory on the community (fard kifayah).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith 'Shroud them with their blood' is the proof that the martyr's blood is left on and he is not washed. Understand how this hadith indicates the ruling.
The hadith is narrated in Musnad Ahmad and others.
3
Identify the exception to the rule
The text states that the martyr is washed if he was in a state of janabah, a child, or insane. Note that this exception is only according to Abu Hanifa.
The Sahibayn disagree.
4
Analyze the proof for the exception
The hadith of Hanzalah, where angels washed him despite his martyrdom, proves that janabah requires washing even for the shahid. Trace the logic: martyrdom is a preventer of new obligations but does not remove existing ones.
Abu Hanifa views this as a normative proof, not a special case.
5
Note the ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that the martyr is not washed under any circumstance, including janabah. They argue that the rank of shahadah exempts from washing entirely.
The text briefly mentions their view but focuses on Abu Hanifa's proof.
6
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi sources generally prefer Abu Hanifa's view, so the relied-upon position is to wash a shahid who was junub, a child, or insane. Confirm the tarjih in your own study.
The hadith of Hanzalah is strong evidence supporting this.

Commentary
This passage contains rulings regarding the treatment of the martyr's body. The key points: The shahid is shrouded without washing, with his blood and clothes left on, based on hadith commanding to shroud them with their blood. Items unsuitable for the shroud (fur, padding, weapons, armor) are removed. The shroud may be adjusted to meet the sunnah standard for a shroud. It is disliked to remove all the clothes he was wearing. The worship of the funeral prayer is performed over the shahid without ghusl, as the Prophet himself prayed over the martyrs of Uhud and Badr. However, an exception is made: if the shahid was in a state of janabah (major impurity), was a child, or was insane, then Abu Hanifa rules that he must be washed. His proof is the incident of Hanzalah, who was in janabah when martyred and was washed by angels. Abu Hanifa argues that martyrdom prevents further obligations but does not remove the obligation of ghusl for janabah. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, stating that the child and the one in janabah are not washed, because the rank of shahadah exempts them from washing. The text cuts off, but the full dispute is recorded in other sources.

Key Terms
تكفين (takfin): Shrouding the deceased; for the shahid done with his blood and clothes.
غسل (ghusl): Ritual washing of the deceased; usually required for all Muslims except shahid, but disputed for janab etc.
جنب (junub): State of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl.
حشو (hashw): Padding or stuffing used in garments, not suitable for shroud.
