Wudu for the Deceased and Pouring Warm Water
Rulings on wudu for the deceased and pouring water.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of performing wudu for the deceased, identify the exceptions (child, junub), and explain the omission of madmadah and istinshāq as well as the requirement of warm water.

Core Idea
Wudu is performed for the deceased except for young children; madmadah and istinshāq are omitted unless the deceased was junub; warm water is poured.

After covering the private parts and stripping clothes, the washer performs wudu for the deceased, starting with the face. However, a small child who does not understand prayer is exempted. The usual rinsing of the mouth and nose is omitted because water cannot be expelled, except when the deceased was in a state of major impurity (junub, menstruating, postpartum), in which case the washer makes an effort to perform them to complete purification. Finally, warm water is poured over the body.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on wudu for the deceased
The text says wudu is performed (yuwaddā) in the correct view. Understand that this is wajib for the deceased (except small children).
Wudu here is a wajib part of the washing process.
2
Recognize the exceptions
A small child who does not understand prayer is not given wudu. Also, madmadah and istinshāq are normally omitted because they are impossible; but for a junub (and by analogy, menstruating/postpartum women), the washer must attempt them to complete purification.
The exception for junub is explicitly stated; the commentator extends it to other states of major impurity.
3
Understand the pouring of warm water
After wudu, warm water is poured over the body. The word 'maghli' means water that has been intentionally heated (from aghlaytu), not just boiling water.
The water should be warm, not hot, to facilitate washing.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary details the procedure for washing the deceased, following the matn of Nur al-Idah. First, the deceased is placed on a bier that has been fumigated with incense an odd number of times (1, 3, 5, or up to 7) to hide odor and honor the deceased. The orientation of the deceased on the bier is flexible: the most correct view is that it can be any direction, though some said widthwise or towards the qiblah. Next, the private parts (from navel to knee) are covered by tightening a cloth (izār). The correct view is to cover the entire area, though a more lenient view suffices with covering only the major private parts (al-‘awrah al-ghalīẓah) for ease, as in al-Hidāyah, supported by the hadith forbidding looking at the thigh. Then the clothes are stripped from the deceased, unless he is a hermaphrodite (then special rules apply). The washer's hand should be wrapped in a cloth when washing the private parts, and he does not insert his uncovered hand under the cover. After that, wudu is performed for the deceased: starting with the face, then hands to wrists, and wiping the head. This is the correct view, but exceptions: a small child who does not understand prayer is not given wudu; others are. However, rinsing the mouth (madmadah) and sniffing water (istinshāq) are omitted because water cannot be expelled easily. There is an exception for a junub (person in state of major impurity), for whom the washer should make an effort to do these acts to complete purification. The same applies to a menstruating or postpartum woman. Finally, warm water that has been boiled (but not necessarily boiling hot) is poured over the deceased. The commentary clarifies that 'maghli' is from 'aghlaytu' (I made it boil), not 'ghalā' (it boiled intransitively), indicating the water is intentionally heated. These rulings are based on Hanafi fiqh, with references to hadith and multiple authoritative texts.

Key Terms
مضمضة واستنشاق (madmadah wa istinshāq): Rinsing the mouth and sniffing water into the nostrils as part of wudu; these are omitted for the deceased except when the deceased was in a state of major impurity (junub, etc.).

Procedure of Washing the Deceased (Ghusl al-Mayyit)
Detailed ritual procedure with evidence and intra-madhhab preferences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sequence of washing the deceased according to the Hanafi school and identify which steps are obligatory (wajib) versus sunnah based on the evidence provided.

Core Idea
The Hanafi washing of the deceased prioritises ease: cover only the genitals, give wudu without mouth/nose, and use available cleansers.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). This passage from Imdad al-Fattah details each step, clarifying the correct views and their proofs. Understanding the procedure ensures you can perform this duty correctly or guide others. The Hanafi school intentionally selects easier options where differing opinions exist, based on the prophetic command of ease and respect for the deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall obligation
Recognise that ghusl al-mayyit is fard kifayah. The passage details the procedure within that obligation.
2
Trace the sequence of actions
Follow the order: placement, fumigation, covering ‘awrah, removing clothes, performing wudu, washing with sidr/khitmi/water, washing sides, seating and belly rub, final wash, drying.
3
Analyse the disputed points
Note where intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf exists (e.g., number of fumigations, direction of placement, extent of ‘awrah, wudu for the junub). Identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
4
Examine the evidence (dalil)
See how the hadith about not looking at the thigh (for covering) and the command to wash with sidr (for cleansing) support the rulings.
5
Action the fiqh in practice
Imagine you are the ghasil (washer). Apply the sequence step by step, noting what is wajib and what is sunnah.

This passage from Imdad al-Fattah explains the detailed procedure for washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit), which is a fard kifayah in the Hanafi school. The author systematically goes through each step, clarifying the correct positions and evidence. 

First, the deceased is placed on a fumigated bed (sarir mujammar) – fumigated with incense an odd number of times (one, three, five, or seven according to some) to mask odors and honor the deceased. Intention in Washing the Deceased: Validity vs. Discharging Obligation
Discussion on the necessity of intention for washing the deceased and related intra-Hanafi disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position that intention is not required for the validity of washing the deceased but is necessary to discharge the collective obligation, and identify the mufta bihi position from the ikhtilaf within the school.

Core Idea
Intention for washing the deceased is needed to discharge the obligation, not for the act's validity.

This passage from Imdad al-Fattah clarifies a critical nuance in the Hanafi school: while washing a living person does not require intention, washing the deceased does — but only to fulfill the communal duty, not to make the washing itself valid. The commentator introduces the view of al-Sighnaqi, who insists on intention, and reconciles it with the base text. This lesson unpacks that distinction, along with related issues of tayammum and recommendations for the washer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text's claim
The base text (Nur al-Idah) implies no intention needed; the commentator clarifies that intention is not a condition for validity but for obligation.
The base text is not directly quoted, but the tanbeeh addresses it.
2
Understand al-Sighnaqi's critique
Al-Sighnaqi asserts that intention is necessary in washing the deceased; without it, moving the body in water does not count as washing.
Mentioned in the source as 'في السغناقي، لابد من النية'.
3
Reconcile the two positions
The commentator harmonizes: intention is not for sihha but for isqat al-wajib (discharging duty). The prayer over the deceased is valid regardless.
Key phrase: 'وهذا لإسقاط الواجب عنا، لا لصحة الصلاة عليه'.
4
Apply to a concrete case
If a body is found in water, the washer must move it with intention of washing at the moment of removal to fulfill the obligation.
Example from the text: 'إلا أن يحركه في الماء بنية الغسل وقت الإخراج'.
5
Note the related ikhtilaf on tayammum and re-washing
Abu Yusuf has two narrations: one requires washing and repeating prayer if water is found later; another says wash but do not repeat prayer (analogous to a junub).
Reference from al-Hujja and al-Burhan.

Applying Hanut and Camphor, and Prohibitions After Washing the Deceased
States the manner of applying hanut and camphor, and what is not done

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended acts and prohibitions after washing the deceased, and identify the condition regarding intention.

Core Idea
The deceased is honoured with perfume on head and camphor on prostration points; nails and hair are not cut.

This passage from the commentary expands on rulings after washing the deceased. It specifies the placement of hanut and camphor, prohibits cutting nails and hair, and addresses the use of cotton. It also clarifies that intention is not required for validity but is needed to discharge the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the post-washing acts
List what is done (hanut on head/beard, camphor on prostration points) and what is prohibited (cutting nails/hair, combing).
Also note the recommended person to wash the deceased.
2
Understand the placement specifics
Hanut is placed on the head and beard (avoid saffron/wars for men). Camphor goes on the seven body parts that touch the ground in prostration (forehead, nose, hands, knees, feet).
This is to honor the deceased and repel worms.
3
Examine the rulings on cotton
According to zahir al-riwayah, no cotton is used in washing. However, some scholars permit it for the face and orifices, but most disapprove of inserting it into the anus or genitals.
The relied-upon position is no cotton.
4

Intention (niyyah) is not a condition for the validity of washing the deceased, but it is required to discharge the obligation. This is similar to the living.
If the deceased is found in water, intention at the time of removal suffices.

Conditions for a Wife to Wash Her Deceased Husband
Explains the permissibility and limitations of a wife washing her deceased husband.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a wife may wash her deceased husband and identify the exceptions that prevent her from doing so.

Core Idea
A wife may wash her husband because the marriage bond persists through the 'iddah, but certain events sever that permission.

In the Hanafi school, the permissibility for a wife to wash her husband's body is grounded in the ongoing marital relationship even after death. However, this permission is not absolute; several factors can remove it, such as the type of divorce, the birth of a child immediately after death, or certain prohibitions. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying the funeral rites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its rationale
The text begins by stating that a woman washes her husband because it is permissible to touch and look at him due to the remaining 'iddah (waiting period). The rationale is that the marriage bond is not fully severed until the 'iddah ends.
This rationale applies even if the husband dies during the 'iddah of a revocable divorce.
2
List the exceptions mentioned in the text
The passage gives several cases where the wife may not wash her husband: if she gives birth immediately after his death, if she is irrevocably divorced (mubānah), if she became forbidden due to apostasy, foster relationship, or in-law relationship, and in the case of zihar there is a difference of opinion.
Each exception has its own legal basis, often related to the severance of the marriage bond.
3
Analyze the scenario of ambiguous divorce statement
The text presents a case where a man says 'One of you is divorced thrice' to his two wives, has entered with both, then dies without clarification. Neither wife may wash him, but both inherit and observe the 'iddah. This shows how ambiguity blocks the permissibility.
This is because the wife who was actually divorced is unknown, and the remaining marriage bond cannot be assumed for washing.
4
Distinguish between revocable and irrevocable divorce
A revocably divorced woman (raj'ī) washes her husband because the marriage is not yet dissolved; an irrevocably divorced woman does not, as the bond is severed immediately.
The same logic applies to other prohibitions that terminate the marriage.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Consider a husband who divorces his wife revocably, then dies during the 'iddah. She may wash him. If the divorce was irrevocable, she may not. This direct application reinforces the condition.

A wife may wash her husband because the marriage bond persists through the waiting period ('iddah). Special Cases in Washing the Deceased: Umm Walad, Tayammum, and Related Rulings
Explains additional rulings: umm walad, tayammum for non-mahram and khuntha, washing children, kissing deceased, and husband's obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rulings for umm walad, tayammum for non-mahram and khuntha mushkil, and state the husband's obligation to prepare his wife's funeral.

Core Idea
The rulings for washing the deceased vary based on the relationship: an umm walad does not wash her master, non-mahram use tayammum with a cloth, and the husband must prepare his wife's funeral even if poor.

The rules of washing the deceased extend beyond the husband-wife relationship. A slave woman who bore a child to her master (umm walad) does not wash him, as the bond is one of ownership not marriage. When no mahram is available, tayammum with a cloth is performed. Similarly, an ambiguous hermaphrodite requires tayammum. Additionally, the husband is obligated to prepare his wife's funeral even if he is poor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for umm walad
The text states that an umm walad (slave mother of a child) does not wash her master (the father of her child). The reason is that the relationship is one of ownership, not marriage, and she is not considered a wife.
This contrasts with a free wife who washes her husband.
2
Apply the tayammum rulings for non-mahram cases
When a woman dies among men who are not mahram, they perform tayammum for her by wrapping their hands in a cloth to avoid direct skin contact. The same applies when a man dies among women. If a mahram relative is present, tayammum can be done without a cloth.
The cloth serves as a barrier to prevent forbidden touching.
3
Handle the case of an ambiguous hermaphrodite (khuntha mushkil)
The khuntha mushkil, whose gender is unknown, is given tayammum in the apparent narration, to avoid the risk of prohibited touching.
This shows how the school prioritizes caution in ambiguous situations.

Responsibility for the Shroud of a Destitute Muslim
Ruling on kafan responsibility for the poor and handling leftover funds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of responsibility for providing a shroud to a destitute deceased and apply the ruling on leftover funds.

Core Idea
The shroud of a destitute Muslim is a communal obligation, first on relatives, then the treasury, then the community.

This lesson examines who is obligated to provide the shroud (kafan) when a deceased Muslim has no wealth. The Hanafi school establishes a clear hierarchy: relatives who owed maintenance (nafaqah) are first, then the public treasury (bayt al-mal), and finally the Muslim community. It also addresses what to do with leftover collected funds.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary obligor
Determine who is responsible for the kafan: relatives who were obliged to provide nafaqah (maintenance) to the deceased during life. If multiple, the cost is divided proportionally to inheritance shares.
This mirrors the obligation of nafaqah.
2
Identify secondary obligors
If no such relatives exist, responsibility falls on the bayt al-mal (public treasury) from estates without heirs. If the treasury fails (empty or unjust), then the capable Muslims are collectively obligated.
The obligation is fard kifayah: sufficient if some fulfill it.
3
Distinguish the living person's case
Note the contrast: a living person who lacks a garment for prayer must ask for himself; it is not obligatory for others to collect for him. For the deceased, it is obligatory for the community to collect.
Reason: the deceased cannot ask for himself.
4
Handle leftover funds
If collected funds exceed the cost, return the excess to the donor if known; otherwise, use it for another needy deceased's shroud, or give as charity if not possible.
This ensures the donation is used for the intended purpose.
5
Analyze the donated shroud's ownership
A donated shroud remains the property of the donor. If the body is devoured by a beast, the shroud returns to the donor, not the heirs.
This is based on a ruling in al-Fath.

The Sunnah Shroud for a Man and Rulings on Partial Remains
Description of the sunnah shroud for a man and rulings on partial remains.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the sunnah shroud for a man and apply the rulings on washing and burying partial remains.

Core Idea
The sunnah shroud for a man is three pieces; partial remains (less than half) are buried without washing or prayer.

This lesson focuses on the specific description of the recommended (sunnah) shroud for a man, consisting of three pieces. It also addresses the procedure when only part of a deceased person's body is found, which varies based on how much is recovered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general categories of kafan
The kafan is obligatory in principle, but can be of three levels: sunnah (recommended), kifayah (sufficient), and darurah (necessary). The text begins with the sunnah.
Sunnah is the best; kifayah is a single sheet covering the body; darurah is what is available.
2
List the three pieces for a man's sunnah shroud
The sunnah for a man is three garments: a shirt (qamis) from neck to feet without a slit or sleeves, a wrapper (izar), and a sheet (lifafah) – though the lifafah is not fully described in this passage.
The shirt is not like a regular shirt; it has no sleeves and no opening other than the neck.
3
Describe the ruling when a limb is found after washing and shrouding
If a limb (e.g., a finger) remains unwashed or found later, it is not washed separately; it is placed with the body.
The partial body is not washed or prayed over if less than half.
4
Differentiate based on quantity of remains
If more than half the body is found (or half with the head), it is washed and funeral prayer is performed. If less than half, it is buried without washing or prayer.
This is consistent with treating the majority as the person.
5
Determine the ruling for an unknown religion
If it is uncertain whether the deceased was Muslim or non-Muslim, but found in a Muslim village with Muslim signs, it is washed and prayed over.
Presumption favors Islam in such contexts.

Fumigation of the Shroud and the Shroud of Necessity
Ruling and proof on fumigation and shroud of necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fumigating the shroud and identify what suffices as a shroud of necessity.

Core Idea
Fumigation is sunnah mu'akkadah performed an odd number of times; necessity allows any cloth covering the whole body.

After learning the order of the shroud, we now examine two important practical rulings: the recommended fumigation of the shroud and the dispensation of the shroud of necessity. These rulings are based on specific prophetic commands and practices, and the Hanafi school differs from the Shafi'i school on the extent of coverage required in necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage discuses two distinct rulings: (1) tajmir (fumigation) of the shroud, and (2) kafan al-ḍarūrah (shroud of necessity). Note the ruling level for each.
Tajmir is a sunnah act; shroud of necessity is a dispensation.
2
Analyze the evidence for fumigation
The hadith 'If you fumigate the deceased, fumigate an odd number of times' is cited. Determine its implication in Hanafi usul: the command indicates recommendation (nadb), not obligation, because it is not a matter of worship obligation.
Also note the condition: odd number, not exceeding five, and before wrapping.
3
Understand the conditions of fumigation
The text specifies that fumigation is done at three times: at death, at washing, and at shrouding. It is not done behind the funeral procession or in the grave, as those are disliked.
This shows the practice is regulated by sunnah, not mere custom.
4
Examine the shroud of necessity
When the proper shroud is unavailable, any cloth that covers the body suffices. The evidence comes from the story of Hamza (shrouded in one garment) and Mus'ab ibn 'Umayr (cloak that did not cover all, so Prophet ordered covering head and legs).
This demonstrates that the entire body must be covered, not just the 'awrah.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
The Hanafi school argues that the entire body must be covered, contrary to Imam al-Shafi'i who held that covering the 'awrah suffices in necessity. The passage cites al-Zayla'i to support the Hanafi view.
The practical effect (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a Hanafi would require a larger cloth than a Shafi'i in necessity.

Washing a Kafir's Corpse: Permissible but Not Purificatory
Hanafi ruling on washing, shrouding, and burying a kafir's corpse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing a kafir's corpse and explain the legal distinction from washing a Muslim.

Core Idea
Washing a kafir is a cleaning, not a purification; the water becomes najis.

This passage from Imdad al-Fattah clarifies a common point of confusion: how should a Muslim relative handle the funeral rites of a non-Muslim relative? The Hanafi school permits it out of kinship rights, but with key differences from Muslim washing that reflect the lack of ritual purification. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the ruling and avoiding ritual impurity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is washing, shrouding, and burying a kafir's corpse by a Muslim relative. The ruling is permissible (mubah), not obligatory or prohibited.
2
Distinguish from Muslim washing
The washing is likened to cleaning a dirty cloth – no sunnah method, water becomes impure (najis), and the body is simply thrown into a pit without a niche. This shows it is not a ritual purification.
3
Understand the legal basis
The permissibility is based on a general sunnah for all children of Adam (human dignity) and the right of kinship (haqq al-qarabah). The washing becomes a proof against the kafir on the Day of Judgment, not a purification.
4
Note the recommended delegation
If the deceased has a kafir relative, it is better for the Muslim not to undertake the washing, leaving it to the kafir relatives. This avoids unnecessary impurity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A Muslim's funeral is a communal obligation; non-Muslim relatives have no authority over it.

What happens when a Muslim dies and has no Muslim relatives? Can a non-Muslim father handle the funeral? This passage from Imdad al-Fattah clarifies the Hanafi position: the non-Muslim relative is not permitted to take charge; instead, the Muslim community must fulfill the funeral rites as a fard kifayah. The ruling is grounded in a hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) personally led the funeral of a convert, preventing his Jewish father from doing so.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states that when a Muslim has only a non-Muslim relative, the relative cannot be given the deceased. The Muslim community must take over the funeral preparation.
2
Recognize the legal category
Preparing the deceased (ghusl, kafan, salat, burial) is a fard kifayah—a communal obligation. If some Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved; if none do, all sin.
Fard kifayah is distinct from fard ayn; the obligation is on the community as a whole, not on each individual.

The author cites a hadith: the Prophet (peace be upon him) came upon a Jew mourning his son, who had just converted to Islam. The Prophet asked the Jew about prophecies, then after the son affirmed Islam, the Prophet said, 'Remove the Jew from your brother,' and led the funeral prayer himself.
The key phrase: 'Remove the Jew from your brother' indicates the non-Muslim is not allowed to participate in the funeral leadership.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet’s action of removing the Jewish father and himself leading the prayer demonstrates that non-Muslims have no authority over a Muslim’s funeral. The Muslims present collectively took charge, illustrating the communal obligation.
This is a direct proof that even a non-Muslim mahram (close relative) cannot handle a Muslim’s funeral.
5

The passage adds from al-Tajnis: a non-Muslim should not bury a Muslim because curses descend upon the non-Muslim while the Muslim needs mercy, especially at death. It also clarifies that an apostate (who left Islam) has no religion and is not washed at all—thrown in a pit like a dog. This distinguishes original kufr from apostasy.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prayer is prohibited over rebels and highway robbers killed in action to deter corruption, based on the consensus of the Companions.

This passage addresses a sensitive area of funeral jurisprudence: what rites are performed for those who die while actively committing major crimes against the Muslim community? The Hanafi school, drawing on the practice of Caliph Ali (RA) and the consensus that followed, rules that the janazah prayer is not offered for such individuals. If the rebel or robber is killed after being captured (under imam's control), they are washed and prayed over. This detail from al-Zayla'i is adopted by senior scholars as the mufta bihi position.
The capture changes the legal category: they are now treated as ordinary Muslims who have been punished.

Commentary
This passage from Imdad al-Fattah is a commentary on a ruling about exhuming graves. The passage type is a ruling with proof and conditions. It begins by stating exceptions to the general prohibition of exhumation: if the land was usurped, the body may be removed for the rightful owner; if the land was taken by pre-emption (shuf'a), the pre-emptor has the option to remove the body. Then it addresses the case of burying in a grave dug for someone else: the one buried guarantees the value of the digging, which is taken from his estate or bayt al-mal. The person is not removed from the grave because his right to be there is established and his sanctity takes precedence. Exhumation is permitted for retrieving a fallen item (like a book or coin), a usurped shroud if the owner insists on its return, or wealth that was buried with the deceased. The proof cited is the hadith of Abu Righal, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitted exhumation to recover a golden branch buried with him. However, exhumation is not permitted solely because the body was placed facing the wrong direction (other than qiblah) or on its left side, or even if the head and feet positions are reversed; instead, only the bricks are removed and proper Sunnah practice is followed. The commentator also discusses the issue of narrow vs. wide cemeteries, using the analogy of a prayer mat in a mosque: if space is wide, one may not displace another's property; if narrow, it is permissible. Additionally, digging one's own grave before death is permissible and even rewarded, following the practice of pious predecessors like Umar ibn Abdul Aziz. The ruling establishes a hierarchy of rights: the inviolability of the deceased's grave is strong but yields to higher rights like ownership of land or property.

Key Terms
نبش (nabsh): Exhumation of a grave, removing the body.
شفعة (shuf'a): Pre-emption right to purchase land from a buyer, applicable to real estate in Hanafi fiqh.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation of property without right, which obligates return or compensation.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) from Imdad al-Fattah on the rulings of the martyr (shahid). It expands the matn of Nur al-Idah with proofs and intra-madhhab disputes. The author first states the general rule: the shahid is shrouded with his blood and clothes, not washed, and prayer is performed over him. The author then discusses exceptions to the no-wash rule. According to Abu Hanifa, if the martyr was junub, a child, insane, menstruating, or postpartum, he should be washed. Abu Hanifa responds that the sword purifies sins, but a child has no sins, so the exception for junub does not extend to children; Hanzalah was washed for janabah specifically. For the murtaath (one carried from the battlefield alive and later dies after worldly benefits), he is washed as a martyr in the afterlife but not in worldly rulings. The author details conditions for irtithath (eating, drinking, sleeping, treatment, time passing) and exceptions (e.g., moving out of fear of trampling does not make one murtaath). This showcases careful Hanafi legal reasoning distinguishing between worldly honor and afterlife reward.

مرتث (murtaath): A wounded person carried from the battlefield alive who later dies after receiving worldly benefits such as food, drink, or treatment.
جنب (junub): A state of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl, e.g., after sexual intercourse or seminal emission.

Mixed and Unknown Dead: Washing and Prayer Rulings
Rulings for mixed and unknown dead bodies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a mixed or unknown dead body is washed and prayed over in the Hanafi school, applying the rule of majority and the principle of signs (sima).

Core Idea
When Muslim and non-Muslim dead are mixed, the ruling follows the majority or known signs; if equal, no prayer is offered.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings for washing and praying over deceased individuals when their religious identity is uncertain or when Muslim and non-Muslim bodies are mixed. The passage from Imdad al-Fattah expands on the core rulings from Nur al-Idah, introducing key principles: the reliance on majority, the use of distinctive signs (sima), and the invalidity of prayer when halal and haram combine equally. These rules ensure that the obligations of washing and prayer are fulfilled without violating prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Washing and praying over a Muslim deceased is fard kifayah; for a non-Muslim, it is haram. The underlying act is the funeral prayer (janazah) and washing (ghusl) of the dead.
These are collective obligations for Muslims.
2
Determine the conditions for an unknown single body
If a single body is found and its religion is unknown, check for signs of Islam (circumcision, henna, wearing black) or location: if in a Muslim village, treat as Muslim; if in a non-Muslim village without signs, do not wash or pray.
The principle is adopting the apparent (zahir) based on majority context.
3
Apply the majority principle to mixed dead
When Muslim and non-Muslim dead are mixed: if Muslims are more numerous, all are washed and prayed over (with intention for Muslims); if non-Muslims are more numerous, only those known to be Muslim by signs are prayed over; if equal, none are prayed over because the prohibition (prayer over non-Muslims) overcomes the permission.
The hadith 'When halal and haram combine, haram prevails' supports this.
4
Note the hadith evidence
The cited hadith (though weakly transmitted) provides a general principle used here: in cases of equal doubt, the haram side takes precedence, so prayer is withheld.
This is a usul principle applied to a fiqh ruling.
5
Consider burial location
If the dead are not prayed over (e.g., equal numbers), scholars differ: some say bury with polytheists, others say a separate graveyard. The analogy is the case of a pregnant Christian woman married to a Muslim; the Companions differed based on whether the child's status is considered.
The preferred view is to make a separate graveyard.

Commentary
This passage covers several rulings on washing and praying over dead bodies when the identity or religion is uncertain. First, if Muslim and non-Muslim dead are mixed together in battle, they are not prayed over individually; instead, if the Muslim dead are more numerous, they are all prayed over with the intention for Muslims. The commentator adds a note that the same applies to mixing with rebels and highway robbers (who are considered Muslims despite sin). Next, if someone is killed in a city (misr) and it is unknown whether it was by legal punishment or unjustly, or by retaliation, he is washed and prayed over (since martyrdom is not certain). Then, if a single dead body is found and it is unknown whether it is Muslim or non-Muslim, the ruling depends on location and signs: if found in a Muslim village or with signs of Islam (circumcision, henna, wearing black), he is treated as Muslim; otherwise not. When Muslim and non-Muslim dead are mixed, the majority determines the treatment: if Muslims are more numerous, all are prayed over with intention for Muslims; if non-Muslims are more numerous, only those known to be Muslim by signs are prayed over; if equal, none are prayed over because prayer over non-Muslims is forbidden. The hadith cited ('When halal and haram come together, haram prevails') supports the cautious approach. Finally, regarding burial location if not prayed over, scholars differ: some say bury in polytheist graveyards, others say separate graveyard. This is analogous to the case of a Christian pregnant woman married to a Muslim who dies; the Companions differed whether she is buried in Muslim or non-Muslim graveyard.

Key Terms
سيما (sima): Distinctive signs of Muslims, such as circumcision, henna, and wearing black.
مصر (misr): A city or town where one is killed; as opposed to battlefield.

Ruling on Washing and Praying over a Slain Muslim and Unknown Deceased
Explains washing and praying rulings for slain and unknown deceased persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing and praying over a slain Muslim and apply the conditions for treating an unknown deceased based on signs, location, and majority.

Core Idea
The religion of an unknown deceased is determined by signs or location; when mixed, the majority governs.

This passage addresses two related scenarios: washing and praying over a slain Muslim in the city, and handling a deceased person of unknown religion. In the latter case, the Hanafi school relies on external indicators—such as circumcision, henna, and black clothing—or the location of discovery. When a mixed group of dead Muslims and disbelievers is encountered, the majority principle applies, supported by a hadith that the unlawful prevails over the lawful. These rulings ensure that the obligations of washing and prayer are fulfilled while avoiding prohibited acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core obligation
The passage begins by stating that a person killed in the city—whether unjustly or by retaliation—must be washed and prayed over. This establishes the default ruling for a known Muslim.
The ruling applies regardless of the manner of death within Islamic legal categories.
2
Determine the religion of an unknown deceased
When the religion of a single deceased is unknown, check for Muslim signs (circumcision, henna, black clothing) or the location (abode of Islam vs. abode of disbelief). If either is present, treat as Muslim.
Signs are cumulative; any one may be sufficient.
3
Apply the majority principle for mixed groups
When Muslim and disbeliever dead are mixed, look at the majority. If Muslims outnumber, wash and pray over all with intention for Muslims. If disbelievers outnumber, do not pray except for known Muslims. If equal, do not pray.
The hadith that the unlawful prevails over the lawful is the proof for refraining when numbers are equal.
4
Consider the burial place
If prayer is not performed, the deceased is buried either in disbelievers' graveyards or in a separate graveyard, reflecting a difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars.
The origin of this difference is the case of a pregnant Christian wife of a Muslim who died.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary deals with rulings on washing and praying over the deceased. It begins by stating that a person who is killed in the city, regardless of whether it was unjust or by retaliation, is washed and prayed over. The commentary then addresses the case of a single deceased person of unknown religion: if he bears Muslim signs (circumcision, henna, black clothing) or is found in a Muslim village, he is treated as Muslim. When Muslims and disbelievers are mixed, the majority determines the ruling: if Muslims are more numerous, all are washed and prayed over with the intention for Muslims; if disbelievers are more numerous, no prayer is performed except for those known to be Muslim by signs; if equal, no prayer because of the prohibition on praying over disbelievers. A hadith is cited: 'When the lawful and unlawful meet, the unlawful prevails.' The burial place is then discussed: if they are not prayed over, some say bury in disbelievers' graveyard, others say a separate graveyard.
