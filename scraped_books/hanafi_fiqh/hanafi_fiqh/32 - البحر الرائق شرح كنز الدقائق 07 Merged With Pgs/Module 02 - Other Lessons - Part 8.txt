Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Revocation of Gift After Sacrificial Slaughter
Intra-Hanafi dispute on revocation after udhiya/tamattu'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on revocation of a gifted animal when slaughtered for udhiya, and identify the preferred view within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Gifting an animal and then sacrificing it for 'Eid: can the donor take it back? Two Hanafi giants disagree.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school. The ruling hinges on whether the donee's act of sacrificing the gifted animal for udhiya or tamattu' changes the nature of the gift such that revocation is barred. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi principles in the intersection of gifts and sacrifices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Start with the undisputed rule: if the donee simply slaughters the animal (for regular consumption), the donor can revoke. This is the baseline from al-Khaniyya.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
For sacrificial slaughter (udhiya/tamattu'), Abu Yusuf bars revocation. Consider possible reasoning: the sacrifice is a religious act that finalizes the gift's purpose.
3
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad allows revocation, and the sacrifice remains valid. His reasoning likely treats the sacrifice as not altering the donor's right to revoke.
4
Note the absence of Abu Hanifa's explicit view
The text notes that scholars differed; some follow Muhammad. Abu Hanifa's opinion is not recorded, showing the non-static nature of Hanafi fiqh.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah)
If the donor revokes after sacrifice, under Abu Yusuf the donor cannot claim the animal; under Muhammad, the donor can reclaim it (likely its value). The udhiya remains valid in both views.

Partial Revocation and Compensation in Gift
Commentary on partial entitlement of gift or compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partial revocation when part of the gift or compensation is reclaimed by a third party, and explain the adjustment required before revocation.

Core Idea
Partial loss of gift or compensation revives the donor's revocation right, but adjustments must be made proportionally.

When a gift is revoked, the donor may receive compensation from the donee. But what happens if a third party claims part of the gifted property or part of the compensation? This lesson unpacks two symmetrical rulings from the Hanafi school that govern such situations, ensuring fairness and legal precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) half of the gifted property (mawhub) is taken by istihqaq, and (2) half of the compensation ('iwad) is taken by istihqaq. Note that the donor wants to revoke the gift.
Istihqaq here means a third party's valid legal claim over the property.
2
Understand the legal mechanism
In case 1, the donor must return half the compensation to the donee, because only half of the gift was effectively transferred. In case 2, the donor cannot revoke the gift until he first returns the remaining half of the compensation to the donee; then he can revoke. The reasoning: the compensation was for the entire gift, so partial compensation revives the revocation right.
The compensation is treated as a sulh (settlement) for the whole gift from the beginning.
3
Apply to a hypothetical
Consider: A gives a car to B in exchange for $10,000. Later, a third party proves ownership of half the car. A must return $5,000 to B. If instead, $5,000 of the compensation is reclaimed (e.g., stolen before delivery to A), A must return the remaining $5,000 to B before revoking the gift.
The donor cannot retain any benefit from the partial compensation while revoking.

Third-Party Compensation and Gift Revocation
Ruling on third-party compensation and its effect on revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation from a third party for gift revocation and apply the principle of enforceability to determine the compensator's right of recourse.

Core Idea
Compensation from a third party drops the donor's right to revoke, and the compensator’s recourse depends on whether the obligation was legally enforceable.

This lesson explores a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding gifts. When a third party provides compensation to the donor to waive the right to revoke a gift, the revocation right is dropped upon possession. The passage also introduces a general principle to determine when the third party can recover compensation from the donee – a key distinction based on whether the underlying obligation was enforceable by law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The passage begins with the statement from Kanz al-Daqa'iq: 'And it is valid from a third party.' The ruling is that compensation from a third party (ajnabi) is valid and drops the donor's right to revoke when the third party takes possession.
2
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The commentary provides the legal reason: compensation is for dropping the right, so it is valid from a third party just like the substitute in khul' (divorce by compensation) or sulh 'an inkār (settlement for denial). This analogy shows that the right being dropped is not personal to the donee.
3
Examine conditions and exceptions
The ruling is absolute regarding the donee's order – it applies whether or not the donee ordered the third party. The compensator has no recourse against the donee unless they stipulated guarantee. This is contrasted with enforceable obligations like debt repayment.
4
Apply the general principle
The passage cites al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah: For matters that can be compelled by imprisonment and suing (e.g., debts, maintenance), an order to perform creates a right of recourse. For non-enforceable actions (like gifts), no recourse without a guarantee.
5
Consider noted exceptions
The commentator notes that orders for spending on construction or buying a prisoner may be exceptions to the principle, requiring further consideration.

Partial Claim of Gift or Compensation: Proportional Rulings
Rulings on partial claim of gift or compensation with proportional effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings when part of the gifted property or compensation is legally claimed, and derive the proportional effect on revocation and return.

Core Idea
Partial claim of gift or compensation proportionally affects the right to revoke: the missing part governs the return.

When a third party successfully claims ownership of part of a gift or part of the compensation given in exchange, the Hanafi school applies proportional adjustments. The donor's right to revoke and the duty to return are tied to the fraction lost, not the whole. This lesson walks through the key scenarios and the underlying reasoning (ta'lil) that makes the rulings consistent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
Distinguish between claim on the gifted property (al-hiba) and claim on the compensation (al-'iwad). Each triggers a different proportional response.
The passage treats these separately.
2
Apply the proportional rule to each scenario
If half the gift is claimed, the donor returns half the compensation. If half the compensation is claimed, the donor must return the remaining half of compensation before revoking the gift.
The same proportion applies: the claimed fraction determines the return fraction.
3
Consider the effect of increase (ziyada)
If the gifted property has increased (e.g., animal gave birth), the donor cannot revoke even if the compensation is claimed. The increase attaches to the gift and blocks revocation.
Increase here means natural growth or offspring.
4
Recognize the mnemonic letters
The passage hints at letters Kha (sale), Ba (marriage), Za (blood relation) as conditions affecting revocation. They will be detailed in later lessons; for now, note they are factors that may prevent or allow revocation.
These letters are from the classical texts as memory aids.
5
Derive the underlying principle
The core principle is that the compensation and the gift are considered proportional. A partial claim on one reduces the other proportionally, and the rights of revocation and return are adjusted accordingly.
This is a ta'lil (reasoning) based on fairness and analogy.

The Five Conditions Preventing Gift Revocation (Mawani' al-Ruju')
Lists and explains the five mnemonic letters indicating conditions that permanently bar revocation of a gift, with detailed examples and an intra-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five conditions that prevent revocation of a gift in Hanafi fiqh and identify which condition is subject to an intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
Five conditions permanently prevent gift revocation: exit, increase, sale, marriage, and blood relationship.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift (hibah) is normally revocable after possession, but certain conditions permanently bar revocation. These are memorized using the mnemonic letters Kha', Ya', Ba', Za', and Qaf. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains each condition with examples and highlights a dispute between Abu Hanifah and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other regarding the sacrifice of the gifted animal. Understanding these conditions is crucial for determining the validity of a donor's attempt to revoke a gift.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five conditions from the mnemonic letters
Each letter corresponds to a condition: Kha' for exit of the gifted property from the donee's possession, Ya' for increase in the property, Ba' for sale, Za' for marriage, and Qaf for blood relationship.
The letters are derived from the first letter of the Arabic word for each condition.
2
Explain the legal reasoning behind each condition
For each condition, understand the rationale: exit and sale transfer ownership to a third party, increase makes the gift a new entity, marriage creates a bond of kinship, and qarabah prevents revocation to maintain family ties.
The exit condition is explained with references to the story of Barirah.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on the sacrifice case
Abu Yusuf holds that sacrificing the gifted animal (e.g., for 'udhiyah) prevents revocation because it leaves ownership to Allah, while Abu Hanifah and Muhammad say it does not because the meat remains owned by the donee.
This dispute illustrates the difference between complete transfer (sale) and partial disposal (sacrifice).
4
Apply the rule to determine revocation rights in given scenarios
Use the conditions to decide whether a donor can revoke: check if any of the five conditions exist. If yes, revocation is prohibited; if no, it is permissible.
Remember that if only part of the gift is sold, only that part becomes immune; the rest can still be revoked.

Commentary
This passage explains the five conditions that prevent the donor from revoking a gift, summarized by mnemonic letters in the Hanafi tradition. The letters are: Kha' (خ) for khuruj (exit of the gifted property from the donee's possession), Ya' (ي) for ziyadah (increase), Ba' (ب) for bay' (sale), Za' (ز) for zawaj (marriage), and Qaf (ق) for qarabah (blood relationship). The text details each condition with examples and ikhtilaf. For exit (Kha'), if the donee transfers ownership to another (by gift, sale, etc.), the original donor loses revocation right unless the property returns to the donee by rescission (faskh). There is a dispute between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding a gift to a mukatab who then becomes insolvent. For sale (Ba'), the donor cannot revoke the sold portion. For marriage (Za'), if the donor marries a relative of the donee or vice versa, revocation is barred because the purpose is to maintain familial ties. For relationship (Qaf), gifts between blood relatives (mahram) cannot be revoked. The increase (Ya') condition is also mentioned: if the gifted property increases (e.g., gives birth, grows), revocation is prevented. The passage ends abruptly during the discussion of marriage with a case from Fatawa Qadikhan about spouses exchanging gifts.

Key Terms
خروج عن الملك (khuruj 'an al-milk): The gifted property leaving the possession of the donee, e.g., by the donee giving it away or selling it.
زيادة (ziyadah): Increase in the gifted property, such as birth of offspring, growth, or attachment of something inseparable.
الهبة (hibah): A gift; voluntary transfer of ownership without compensation.
الرجوع (ruju'): Revocation; taking back the gifted property by the donor.

The Five Impediments to Revocation of a Gift
Commentary on the mnemonic letters indicating impediments to revoking a gift.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five impediments to revoking a gift in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rules to practical scenarios involving compensation, exit, marriage, kinship, and destruction.

Core Idea
Five letters block a gift's revocation: compensation, exit, marriage, kinship, destruction.

The Hanafi school permits a donor to revoke a gift unless one of five specific impediments is present. This lesson unpacks each impediment, drawing from the commentary of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. You will learn the legal reasoning behind each rule and how intra-madhhab differences affect the outcome.

Guided Path
1
List the five impediments from the mnemonic
Identify the five letters (kha', za', qaf, ha') and the word 'iwad' as standing for compensation, exit from ownership, marriage, mahram kinship, and destruction.
The impediments are exhaustive; no others prevent revocation in the Hanafi school.
2
Analyze each impediment's conditions
For each, determine when it applies and when it does not, based on the text. Note intra-Hanafi disagreements (e.g., Abu Yusuf vs. Abu Hanifa on sacrifice).
Pay attention to the timing of marriage and the definition of mahram.
3
Examine the evidence for the kinship impediment
The hadith from al-Hakim and the athar of Ibn Umar provide the proof. Understand how mafhum al-mukhalafah is used.
Shafi'i does not accept mafhum, but the Hanafi school relies on the explicit athar.
4
Apply the rules to practical scenarios
Use the guided examples: sale of half, gift to a mukatab, sacrifice, marriage timing, etc.
Each scenario tests your ability to identify which impediment is present or absent.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq on the chapter of revocation of gifts. The author organizes the impediments to revocation using mnemonic letters: kha' (khurūj, exit from ownership), za' (zawjiyyah, marriage), qaf (qarābah, kinship), and ha' (halāk, destruction). First, regarding compensation (iwad): if the donor receives compensation for the gift, he loses the right to revoke. If the compensation is only partial, he may revoke the part not compensated. If the compensation is subsequently rescinded (mustahaqq), the donor may revoke the gift if it still exists. The gift must not have increased in value; if it increased and the compensation is rescinded, no revocation. Similarly, if the gift is rescinded, the donee may reclaim the compensation. Exit from ownership: if the gifted property leaves the donee's ownership due to an act of the donee (selling, giving away, etc.), the donor cannot revoke unless the property returns to the donee by a voidable transaction (faskh). If it returns by a new contract (e.g., purchase), the donor cannot revoke because it is a new ownership. Sacrificing the gifted animal or vowing to give it in charity does not remove it from ownership according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, so revocation remains possible; Abu Yusuf disagrees, saying it has left ownership to Allah. Slaughtering without sacrificial intent leaves the right to revoke by agreement. Selling half the gift allows revocation of half. Marriage: if the donor marries the donee after the gift, he may still revoke because the marriage impediment did not exist at the time of the gift. If he married first and then gave, he cannot revoke because the impediment existed at the time of the gift. Kinship: gifts to mahram relatives (those with both blood relation and marriage prohibition) cannot be revoked, based on a hadith from al-Hakim. Non-mahram relatives (e.g., cousins) allow revocation. The commentary clarifies that mahram includes Muslims, dhimmis, and musta'mins, and excludes foster siblings, in-laws, etc. If the donee is a slave who is a mahram relative, Abu Hanifa says revocation is allowed because the slave does not own property absolutely. If the donee is a mukatab slave who becomes unable to pay, Muhammad says no revocation; the gift passes to the master. Destruction: if the gifted object is destroyed, revocation is impossible. The donee's claim of destruction is accepted without oath because he denies the obligation to return the gift.

الهبة (al-hibah): A gift; the transfer of ownership of property without compensation, made voluntarily during one's lifetime.

The Hybrid Legal Nature of Hibah Bishart al-Iwad
Explains hybrid classification of hibah bishart al-iwad with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a gift with a condition of compensation is a gift initially and a sale terminally, explain the reasoning behind the hybrid treatment, and identify the practical consequences of this classification.

Core Idea
A gift with compensation bridges both categories: it starts as a gift but ends as a binding sale.

The Hanafi school encounters a borderline contract: a donor says, 'I give you this property on the condition that you pay me a specified compensation.' Is this a gift or a sale? The school brilliantly resolves the dilemma by treating it as a gift in its inception and a sale in its completion. This classification affects every ruling from possession requirements to pre-emption rights. In this lesson, we will unpack the reasoning behind this hybrid treatment and explore the internal dispute with Zufar, who insists it is a pure sale from start to finish.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn ruling
Extract the core ruling from the base text (Kanz al-Daqa'iq): 'The gift with a condition of compensation is a gift initially... and a sale terminally.' Then note the two immediate consequences: (1) as a gift, mutual possession in the assembly is required, and the gift is void if the item is undivided; (2) as a sale, it is subject to return for defect, option of inspection, and pre-emption.
These consequences are listed directly in the matn.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The commentator explains why the hybrid treatment is adopted: the contract has two aspects (wording of gift, involvement of compensation). Since both similarities exist, we act on both as much as possible. The ruling of a gift delays ownership until possession, while the ruling of a sale creates bindingness. By combining them, the contract is gift-like initially (ownership delayed, possession required) and sale-like terminally (becomes binding once compensation is received).
This is the key analytical move: combining two sets of rules where possible.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Identify Zufar's opposing view: he holds it is a sale from beginning to end, because the compensation makes the transaction effectively a sale regardless of wording. Examine the response: the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) argue that the wording matters and ownership delay is characteristic of a gift, so the initial stage remains a gift.
Zufar's view is a minority within the school; the relied-upon position is the hybrid one.
4
Examine additional contexts
The commentator cites other works that apply the same rule in special circumstances: coercion (the coerced party in such a gift is treated as coerced in a sale, indicating the contract is akin to a sale), waqf with compensation (stipulating compensation makes it like a sale), and a father gifting minor's property (Muhammad allows it with equivalent compensation, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in another narration disallow it, requiring a distinction).
These applications show the practical scope of the hybrid classification.
5

The practical difference between the majority and Zufar: under the majority view, possession must occur in the assembly (gift condition), and the contract is not binding until compensation is paid; under Zufar's view, the contract is binding immediately and possession is mandatory at once. Also, the hybrid view allows the donor to retain ownership until possession, while Zufar's view gives immediate ownership to the donee subject to payment.
This affects risk and revocability.

Suspended Gift of Debt to Debtor is Invalid
Ruling on suspended gift of debt with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a suspended gift of debt to the debtor is invalid in the Hanafi school and distinguish between tamlik and isqāt.

Core Idea
Suspended gifts of debt are invalid because suspension only applies to pure isqātāt, not to mixed tamlik/isqāt.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq clarifies the ruling on conditional statements about debt forgiveness. Many assume that any promise of debt release tied to a future event is valid, but the Hanafi school strictly limits suspension to specific contract types. Here, we will unpack the legal reasoning behind the invalidity and the subtle difference between a suspended condition and a conditional release.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific statement being evaluated? The text lists several examples like 'if tomorrow comes, it is yours' or 'if you pay half, you have half'. The ruling is that such statements are invalid (bāṭil).
2
Locate the legal proof (dalīl)
The author explains the reasoning: a gift of debt to the debtor is essentially an ibrā' (release). This ibrā' has a dual nature – it is tamlik (transfer of ownership) from one perspective and isqāt (dropping) from another. Suspension (ta'līq) is only valid for pure isqātāt that are sworn upon, like divorce and manumission.
The proof is based on the principle that suspension is not valid in tamlikāt.
3
Analyze the dual nature of ibrā'
Why does the dual nature matter? Tamlik requires acceptance and can be rejected; isqāt does not require acceptance. Since suspended tamlik is invalid, and this act has tamlik aspects, the suspension is void.
This analysis shows the intricate fīqh reasoning distinguishing between types of legal actions.
4
Note the exceptions and distinctions
The text contrasts with other cases: if phrased as 'on condition that you pay', it is a conditional release (not suspension) and valid. Also, gifting the debt to a guarantor is pure tamlik, while releasing the guarantor is pure isqāt. Additionally, in contracts like ṣarf and salam, ibrā' requires acceptance.
These distinctions are crucial for correct application.
5
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilāf)
The Hanafi position that suspension is invalid means that statements like 'if you die, you are free' are not binding unless it is a will (when the creditor says 'if I die'). This prevents unintended consequences and gambling.
Other schools may allow such suspended releases, leading to different legal outcomes.

The Validity of 'Umra and Ruqba: Text vs. Qiyas

Learning Objective

Core Idea
'Umra is a valid absolute gift; ruqba is invalid due to explicit prophetic prohibition.

This lesson examines two conditional gifts: 'umra (life grant) and ruqba (a gamble on survival). The Hanafi majority, based on a sound hadith, uphold 'umra as an absolute transfer, while invalidating ruqba because it involves gambling and uncertainty. However, Imam Abu Yusuf dissents, allowing ruqba by analogy to 'umra. Understanding this dispute shows how the Hanafi school prioritizes explicit textual evidence over analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1

2

According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, 'umra is valid (sahih) and the property passes absolutely; ruqba is invalid (batil).

The hadith of Ahmad, Abu Dawud, and al-Nasa'i: 'Do not practice ruqba... it is invalid.' This is the primary evidence.
The hadith is ahad, but its meaning is clear (dalala qat'iyya).

Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf allows ruqba by analogy to 'umra, voiding the condition. The majority responds that the explicit prohibition overrides qiyas.
This is a classic case of textual evidence (nass) vs. analogical reasoning.

Under the majority view, the ruqba gift is void entirely. Under Abu Yusuf, the gift is valid but the condition is void, making it like 'umra.

Conditional Discharge of Debt and the Requirement of Acceptance
Commentary on conditional ibra' and acceptance exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid and invalid conditional discharges in ibra' and identify when acceptance (qabul) is required.

Core Idea
Conditional discharge of debt is invalid unless structured as a bequest – acceptance is required only in special contracts.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi ruling on statements like 'you have half' or 'you are free from half' made to a debtor. The commentary clarifies the boundary between invalid conditional discharges (ta'liq) and valid restricted discharges (taqyid), and highlights the general rule that ibra' does not require acceptance, with exceptions for sarf and salam contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The specific statement 'you have half of it' or 'you are free from the remaining half' is ruled invalid (batil). Determine what legal act is being evaluated and its hukm.
The ruling is on a conditional discharge (ta'liq) of debt, not on ibra' itself.
2
Understand the reasoning behind invalidity
The commentator explains that conditional suspension (ta'liq) is only valid for pure discharges that people swear by, such as divorce (talaq) and manumission ('itaq). Ibra' is not of that type, so ta'liq is invalid.
The basis is qiyas: ibra' is analogous to other discharges like pardon, which do not accept conditions.
3
Distinguish ta'liq from taqyid
A statement like 'you are free from half on condition that you pay me the other half' is valid because it is a restriction (taqyid) not a conditional suspension (ta'liq). The condition is attached to the performance, not to the discharge itself.
The key: in ta'liq, the effect is suspended on a future event; in taqyid, it is an immediate obligation subject to a condition.
4
Identify the exception to acceptance requirement
The general rule is that ibra' does not require acceptance (qabul). However, when the debt arises from a currency exchange (sarf) or salam contract, acceptance is necessary because these contracts require delivery (qabd) and cannot be annulled unilaterally.
The exception preserves the contractual structure that requires mutual consent for dissolution.
5
Apply the death condition ruling
If the condition is on the debtor's death (with fatha on the ta of 'tamut'), it is invalid and a gamble. But if on the creditor's death (with damm on the ta of 'amut'), it is valid as a bequest (wasiyya).
The distinction turns on whose death is the condition: the debtor's death creates uncertainty; the creditor's death is a known future event that can be a bequest.

Core Idea
Sadaqah is like hibah in requiring delivery and excluding undivided shares, but it is irrevocable because the reward has been attained.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on sadaqah (charity) as compared to hibah (gift). The matn states three rules: sadaqah is not valid without possession, not valid in an undivided share that can be divided, and once given, it cannot be revoked. The commentator expands these rules, resolves an apparent contradiction about charity to two poor persons, and addresses a practical dispute over whether a transfer is a gift (revocable) or charity (irrevocable). Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi fiqh of gratuitous transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three central rulings
The matn states three rules for sadaqah: it requires possession (qabd), it is not valid in an undivided share (musha') that can be divided, and it cannot be revoked. Know these at the outset.
These rules mirror hibah except for revocation.
2
Understand the proof: qiyas to hibah
The commentator states that sadaqah is a gratuitous act (tabarru') like hibah, so the same conditions apply by qiyas. Identify the shared illa: both are voluntary transfers without compensation.

The commentator anticipates an objection: earlier it was said that sadaqah to two poor persons is valid in a divisible undivided share. This distinction prevents confusion.

The reason for no revocation (la ruju') in sadaqah is that the donor's intended reward (thawab) has been obtained. Once the donee possesses the property, the reward is secured, so revoking would nullify it. In hibah, revocation is possible because the donor may not have intended reward.

Commentary
This brief passage asserts a crucial principle in Hanafi fiqh: the property of an orphan cannot be acquired by someone through prolonged possession or adverse possession (da'wa al-mulk bi tul al-mudda). This protection is based on texts that prohibit encroaching on orphan's wealth. The author extends the same protection to Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) property, meaning that no one can claim ownership of state property simply by occupying it for a long time. The phrase 'فليكن عليه المعوّل' indicates this is the relied-upon position.

Key Terms
دعوى الملك بطول المدة (da'wa al-mulk bi tul al-mudda): A claim of ownership based solely on the passage of time and continued possession.

Key Takeaway
