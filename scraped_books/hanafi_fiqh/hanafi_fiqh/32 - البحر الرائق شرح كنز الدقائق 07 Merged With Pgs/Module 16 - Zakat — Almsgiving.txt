Charity Declaration vs. Will: The Hanafi Distinction by Istihsan
Explains distinction between charity declaration and will via istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a charity declaration applies only to zakat-wealth, while a will applies to all property, and identify the istihsan reasoning that distinguishes them.

Core Idea
Charity mirrors zakat; will mirrors inheritance – one is limited, the other universal.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq teaches a crucial distinction in Hanafi fiqh. When someone says 'my wealth is charity,' it does not obligate giving away all assets, only those on which zakat is due. But a will ('I bequeath one-third of my wealth') applies to everything. The difference is not by analogy but by istihsan, rooted in the nature of obligations: divine duties versus human arrangements.

Guided Path
1
State the two cases and their apparent analogy
Identify the two scenarios: (1) someone declares 'my wealth is charity' and (2) someone bequeaths one-third of their wealth. By pure analogy (qiyas), both would obligate all wealth.
Zufar held this qiyas-based view.
2
Identify the ruling by qiyas
Understand that Zufar's position uses strict analogy: if charity and will are both dispositions of property, they should be treated equally.
3
Recognize the istihsan distinction and its rationale
The Hanafi school abandons qiyas for istihsan. The rationale: the servant's obligation is measured by Allah's obligation. Charity resembles zakat (a divine duty) and is thus limited to zakat-wealth; a will resembles inheritance (a human arrangement) and applies to all property.
This is the core of the passage.
4
Apply the definition of zakat-wealth and note intra-Hanafi differences
Zakat-wealth includes grazing animals, gold/silver, trade goods, and according to Abu Yusuf, 'ushri land. Excluded: kharaj land, domestic slaves, houses, and necessities. Muhammad excludes 'ushri land; Abu Hanifa agrees with Muhammad.
The mufta bihi position follows al-Hidayah and al-Majma'.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter on arbitration, but it digresses into the distinction between a charity declaration and a will. The author states that if someone says 'my wealth is charity for the poor', it applies only to zakat-wealth (mal al-zakat), while a bequest of one-third applies to all property. By analogy (qiyas), both would obligate all wealth, as held by Zufar. However, the Hanafi school adopts istihsan based on the idea that a servant's obligation is measured by Allah's obligation: charity is like zakat (a divine duty), so it is confined to zakat-wealth, whereas a will is like inheritance (a human arrangement) and applies to all. The passage then elaborates on what constitutes zakat-wealth: grazing animals, gold and silver, trade goods, regardless of nisab or debt. 'Ushri land is included according to Abu Yusuf, but Muhammad excludes it due to the maintenance element; Abu Hanifa agrees with Muhammad. Kharaj land is excluded. Domestic servants, houses, furniture, and basic needs are excluded. The author equates 'my wealth' and 'what I own' as synonymous. The commentator also includes a side note about the treasury representative, citing al-Ramli that if the sultan appoints a representative to both collect/hold and to sue/be sued, he has full authority. This is relevant to the earlier discussion about the ninth admonition.

Key Terms
مال الزكاة (mal al-zakat): Wealth on which zakat is due, including grazing animals, gold and silver, trade goods, and 'ushri land (according to Abu Yusuf). Excluded are kharaj land, domestic slaves, houses, and basic necessities.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A will or bequest; applies to all property as opposed to a charity declaration which is limited to zakat-wealth.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juridical preference; used here to give a ruling different from qiyas, based on the distinction between divine and human obligations.

Validity of Charity and Gift to Two Persons: The Poverty Condition
Ruling on charity and gift to two persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving ten dirhams as charity or gift to two persons and identify the condition that determines validity.

Core Idea
Charity or gift to two persons is valid only if they are poor; giving to the rich defeats the purpose.

This lesson addresses a specific ruling from the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: the validity of giving ten dirhams as charity (ṣadaqah) or as a gift (hibah) to two persons. The ruling distinguishes between poor and rich recipients. For charity, the recipient must be poor by definition; for a gift, the invalidity when given to two rich persons is explained by the Hanafi principle that a gift to two persons without division is not valid unless the recipients are poor, due to the resemblance to a bribe or the problem of undivided share (shuyūʻ).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is giving ten dirhams as either charity or gift. The recipients are two people, either both poor or both rich.
The ruling applies specifically to ten dirhams, a common monetary amount in classical texts.
2
State the ruling for each combination
For charity to two poor: valid. For charity to two rich: invalid. For gift to two poor: valid. For gift to two rich: invalid.
The ruling is the same for both charity and gift in this case.
3
Identify the underlying condition
The condition for validity is that the recipients are poor. For charity, this is inherent; for gift, it is a required condition derived from the principle that a gift to two rich persons is akin to a bribe or involves an undivided share.
The passage is cut off but the commentary explains the difference.
4
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Consider giving ten dirhams to two persons where one is poor and one is rich. The ruling likely follows the same logic: the charity/gift is invalid because not all recipients are poor.
This is an extension; the text only mentions both rich or both poor.

Commentary
This is a brief ruling from the matn stating that giving ten dirhams as charity or as a gift to two poor persons is valid, but to two rich persons is not. The commentary explains that charity is intended for the needy, so giving to the rich defeats the purpose. The reason for the difference between charity and gift may be that charity to the rich is invalid because it is not charity; as for a gift to the rich, the invalidity may be due to the undivided share issue (shuyu') or because it resembles a bribe. The text is incomplete, but the core ruling is clear: charity to the rich is not valid, and similarly a gift to two rich persons is not valid. This applies when the donor gives ten dirhams to two rich persons without specifying shares, following the earlier ruling on one-to-two gifts.

Key Terms
صدقة (ṣadaqah): Charity given for the sake of Allah, typically to the poor.
فقير (faqīr): A poor person who does not have enough wealth to meet basic needs.
