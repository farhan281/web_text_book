Proving Agency Without the Opponent: Hanafi Ruling and Indirect Methods
Ruling on accepting agency evidence without opponent, followed by three practical solutions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a representative's testimony about his own agency is rejected without the opponent present, and explain the three indirect methods to establish agency through the opponent's actions.

Core Idea
A wakil cannot prove his own agency by witnesses alone; but he can force proof through the opponent's need.

In Hanafi law, a person who claims to be a litigation agent cannot simply bring witnesses to prove his agency if the opposing party is absent. This lesson examines why such evidence is inadmissible and then explores three practical methods from the later Hanafi tradition (Jami' al-Fusulayn) that allow the agent to indirectly establish his authority. These methods turn the opponent's own actions—or inactions—into a means of proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Ruling
Read the opening line of the source: 'لا تقبل من الوكيل بالخصومة بينة على وكالته من غير خصم حاضر'. This states that the testimony (bayyinah) of a litigation representative (wakil bi al-khusumah) regarding his own agency is not accepted without an opponent (khasm) being present. Note that this applies even if the judge rules based on it; such a ruling is invalid because it is 'qada' fi al-mukhtalaf' (a ruling on a disputed matter).
The key term is 'qada' fi al-mukhtalaf'—the ruling is void because the opponent's absence means the matter is still contestable.
2
Understand the Rationale
The reason is that if the representative could prove his agency without the opponent, the opponent's right to challenge the agency would be undermined. The opponent might later deny the agency, and the ruling would have been given without hearing his side. This ensures fairness in litigation.
This is a procedural safeguard, not a reflection on the representative's honesty.
3
Explore Method One: Handing Over to a Third Party
The first method from Jami' al-Fusulayn: The representative hands over the item to a third person (e.g., for safekeeping). Then he claims that he is an agent to receive and sell it. The third party says, 'I do not know of your agency.' The representative then provides evidence of his agency. The judge orders the item to be handed to the representative for sale. Here, the third party's denial creates a dispute, allowing the representative to prove his agency.
The third party becomes a 'kind of opponent' by denying knowledge, even though he is not the principal.
4
Explore Method Two: Selling and Compelling Acceptance
The second method: The representative sells the item directly. The buyer refuses to take delivery, fearing the principal may deny the agency and the item may be lost or damaged. The representative then proves his agency to the judge, and the judge compels the buyer to accept delivery. The authority to compel is established by the evidence.
The buyer's fear and refusal transform him into an opponent whose objection must be resolved, allowing proof.
5
Explore Method Three: Buyer Proving Agency
The third method: The representative sells and then claims he is an unauthorized agent (fuduli), thus refusing to deliver the item. The buyer then seeks to prove that the representative is indeed an agent of the owner, because the buyer wants to enforce the sale. The buyer thus becomes the opponent who establishes the agency. The ruling confirms that the buyer can prove the representative's agency.
This method flips the burden: the opponent (buyer) proves the agency, not the representative himself.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the book of Agency (al-Wakalah). It states a fundamental principle: a person who claims to be a representative (wakil) in litigation cannot prove his own agency by presenting witnesses unless the opposing party (khasm) is present. If the judge were to rule based on such evidence without the opponent, the ruling would be invalid because it is a ruling over a contested matter (qada' fi al-mukhtalaf). The text then provides three practical methods from Jami' al-Fusulayn for a sale representative to establish his agency when there is a risk that the principal might later deny it. The first method involves the representative handing the item to a third party, then claiming agency to receive and sell it; the third party says he does not know of the agency, so the representative proves it by evidence, after which the judge orders the item to be handed to him for sale. The second method involves the representative selling directly and the buyer refusing to take delivery fearing the principal's denial; the representative then proves his agency and compels the buyer to accept delivery. The third method is when the representative claims to be an unauthorized agent (fuduli) and refuses delivery; then the buyer proves that the representative is indeed an agent, making the buyer the opponent who can establish the agency. This shows that a representative can indirectly prove his agency through the opposing party's need to defend the transaction.

Key Terms

فضولي (fuḍūlī): An unauthorized agent who acts without prior appointment but whose action may be ratified later.

Twin Representatives: Proof of Agency Covers Both
Ruling on joint representatives for collecting a debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one of two joint representatives proves the agency and the debt, and explain why the absent representative is bound.

Core Idea
Proof by one joint representative establishes agency for both; no re-proof needed if absent appears.

In matters of agency, it is common for a principal to appoint two representatives to act jointly. What happens when one representative disappears and the other needs to prove the agency? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses that scenario, establishing that the presence of evidence from one representative is sufficient for the judge to rule on the joint agency and the debt. The absent representative, if he later appears, is not required to re-prove his agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two people were appointed (by the principal) to collect a debt. The principal and one representative are absent. The present representative claims the debt and proves his own agency and the joint agency.
2
Determine the required proof
The present representative must provide evidence of two things: (1) that the principal appointed both of them as representatives, and (2) that the debtor owes the debt.
This is standard proof of agency and debt.
3
State the ruling
The judge rules on the validity of the agency for both representatives and on the debt. The ruling is effective for both the present and absent representative.
The judgment covers the absent representative's agency as well.
4
Note the implication
If the absent representative later appears, he is not required to repeat the evidence. The initial ruling suffices.
This shows efficiency: the absent rep benefits from the proof already established.

The Musakhkhar: Judge-Appointed Agent for Absent and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of judgment against an absent person via a judge-appointed representative (musakhkhar).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define musakhkhar, explain the two Hanafi narrations on judgment against absent persons via this agent, and identify the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Judgment against an absent person via a musakhkhar is generally impermissible; the mu'tamad forbids it except under strict necessity.

What does a judge do when a claimant sues a person who is absent? One mechanism is to appoint a representative (musakhkhar) for the absent defendant. However, Hanafi scholars are divided on whether a judgment can be issued against the absent person through this agent. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents the dispute, the conditions for permissibility, and the relied-upon position that such judgment is not allowed. Understanding this issue is crucial for grasping Hanafi procedural caution regarding due process.

Guided Path
1
Define musakhkhar
Musakhkhar is a person appointed by the judge to represent an absent party in litigation, so that the case can proceed against the absent person.
It is used when the defendant cannot be found or is hiding.
2
State the issue
The core question: Can the judge render a binding judgment against an absent person by using a musakhkhar?
3
Present the two views
One view (held by Khuwarizadah) permits it based on a narration and necessity. The other view (relied-upon) prohibits it, as upheld by Zahir al-Din to protect the madhhab's principles.
The prohibition is tied to the rule that judgment against an absent person without representation is generally invalid.
4
Identify conditions for those who permit
For those who permit it, a condition is that the absent person must be within the judge's jurisdiction. If the absent is outside the jurisdiction, the appointment is invalid.
This is from al-Khazanah.
5
Determine the mu'tamad
The relied-upon position (mu'tamad) is that judgment against the musakhkhar is not permissible. This is stated explicitly in the text.
The text says: 'والمعتمد أن القضاء على المسخر لا يجوز'.

Judicial Representation for the Absent: The Debtor's Oath and Buyer's Option
First two of four scenarios for judicial representation of an absent party.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on judicial appointment to prevent a debtor's oath from being violated, and identify the disagreement on the judge's role when a buyer with an option returns goods to an absent seller.

Core Idea
A judge may appoint a representative for an absent party to protect rights and oaths, but necessity is debated.

This lesson explores two scenarios from the Hanafi tradition where a party's absence creates procedural challenges. In the first, a debtor's conditional oath (e.g., divorce) is at risk because the creditor disappears. In the second, a buyer with an option to return goods cannot locate the seller. The Hanafi school offers solutions involving judicial intervention, but with significant differences of opinion among the early masters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the problem
In the first case, a debtor swears an oath contingent on payment today, but the creditor disappears, risking violation of the oath. In the second, a buyer with a khiyar al-shart wants to return goods but the seller hides.
The problem is the absence of the party with whom the legal act must be completed.
2
State the judge's action in the first case
The judge appoints a representative (wakil) for the absent creditor, the debtor pays the debt to the representative, and the oath is fulfilled. This is the mufta bihi position.
The commentary notes that paying directly to the judge is also sufficient according to many later scholars.
3
Understand the dispute in the second case
Some say the judge should appoint a representative for the seller to facilitate the return; others say he should not because the buyer neglected to secure a representative at the time of purchase.
If the judge does not appoint, the buyer may request a notice (i'dhar).
4
Analyze the two narrations from Muhammad
In one narration, the judge gives a three-day public notice at the seller's door before rescinding; in another, no notice is given. This reflects an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The practice may vary based on the qadi's preference.
5
Conclude with the practical import
The first case is a clear ruling; the second shows how procedural options differ. Both cases illustrate judicial intervention to prevent rights from being lost due to absence.

Judicial Representation in Surety Cases and General Principles of Representation
Last two of four specific scenarios and extension to general principles of representation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling on discharge of a surety when the claimant is absent, describe the procedure for an absent opponent, and analyze the extension of representation to partners and trustees.

Core Idea
A surety can be discharged by paying a judge-appointed representative; a hiding opponent is given a three-day notice before a representative is appointed.

The third and fourth scenarios address more complex situations: a surety's liability when the claimant disappears, and a formal procedure for dealing with a litigant in hiding. The discussion then extends to general principles: other parties like trustees and heirs can also represent absent parties, with important conditions regarding property in hand. Intra-Hanafi disputes on representation among partners are also highlighted.

Guided Path
1
Understand the third scenario (surety for person)
A surety (kafil) guarantees to produce a debtor by tomorrow or else he will pay the debt. The claimant disappears, so the surety cannot produce the debtor. The money becomes due. However, if the surety takes the matter to the judge, who appoints a representative for the claimant, and the surety hands over the debtor to that representative, he is discharged.
This ruling is from Abu Yusuf, not the apparent narration, indicating a weaker transmission.
2
Analyze the fourth scenario (hiding opponent)
If an opponent (defendant) goes into hiding, the judge sends a trustee to call at his door for three days. Then the judge appoints a representative for him to litigate. This is Abu Yusuf's view, based on istihsan.
The procedure ensures the absent party's right to be notified before a representative is appointed.
3
Identify the exception for property claims
If the plaintiff wants to take something from the hand of the absent defendant, the opponent's presence is required. But if the claim is against property that was in the absent's hand (e.g., price of goods), no representative is needed for the judge to give the plaintiff his right.
This distinction is important for practical litigation.
4
Generalize the principle: 'kalwakil'
The author uses 'ka' (like) to indicate that the examples (wakil, wasi) are not exhaustive. Thus, a trustee of a waqf (mutawalli) can represent, and one heir can represent others in claims for or against the deceased.
However, for claims to a specific property (ayn), the heir must have that property in his hand; otherwise, the suit is not heard. For debt claims, possession is not required.
5
Examine the partner-in-debt dispute
In inheritance, one partner in a debt can represent the other by agreement. In other matters, Abu Hanifa says no (qiyas), Abu Yusuf says yes (istihsan), and Muhammad agrees with Abu Yusuf. This shows the interplay of qiyas and istihsan.
The practical effect: in non-inheritance cases, a partner can represent the other according to the mufta bihi (istihsan) view.

Conditions for Representing an Absent Litigant
Expansion of a rule on representation in lawsuits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for allowing a present defendant to represent an absent party, distinguish necessary causality from mere condition or continuation, and apply the rule to given fiqh scenarios.

Core Idea
A present person can represent an absent only if the claim against the absent is an inseparable cause of the claim against the present.

The Hanafi school generally forbids judgment against an absent person, but necessity requires an exception: when a lawsuit against a present person necessarily involves an absent party whose action is the direct cause of the claim. This lesson unpacks that exception, its conditions, and its limits through a series of practical examples from sales, guarantees, pre-emption, homicide, and more.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage begins with the rule: if the claim against the absent is a necessary cause (sabab la mahalah) for the claim against the present, then the present can be made a representative (khasm) and judgment is valid against both.
The cause must be inseparable – the claim against the absent cannot be separated from the claim against the present.
2
Distinguish first and second categories
The rule applies whether the claims are the same thing (e.g., the absent sold the property to the present) or two different things (e.g., the absent freed a slave, and the slave claims against the present). In both, the necessary causality holds.
Examples for same claim: house purchased from absent, guarantee for debt, pre-emption. Examples for different claims: slander, emancipation divorce, homicide.
3
Analyze the limit: cause vs. condition
The passage then qualifies that the cause must be necessary, not merely a condition (e.g., 'if so-and-so divorces this wife, you are divorced') nor a continuing cause (e.g., a continued marriage after a sale). In these cases representation is not allowed.
This distinction is crucial: conditions and continuing causes do not justify judgment against the absent.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on conditional divorce
There is a disagreement: Shams al-A'immah al-Uzjandi allows representation even for a condition, but the majority (asahh) does not, because it initiates judgment against the absent on a mere condition.
The text says the first view (disallowance) is 'asahh' (more correct).
5
Review the objection from Marisi and Hanbalis and the Hanafi response
Bishr al-Marisi and some Hanbalis argued that allowing representation is a circumvention of the prohibition against judgment on the absent. The Hanafi response: necessity – without it, many rights would be lost. The condition of necessary causality ensures the absent is not unfairly bound.
This shows the Hanafi commitment to both principle and practical justice.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, expanding the rule about when a lawsuit can proceed against a present person as representative of an absent party. The core principle is that if the claim against the absent is a necessary cause (sabab la mahalah) for the claim against the present, then the present person stands in the place of the absent, and judgment is valid against both. The author illustrates this with a series of examples from various areas of fiqh: sale, guarantee, pre-emption, slander, emancipation, homicide, and inheritance. He also distinguishes cases where the causality is not necessary (e.g., when the cause is merely continuing, not initiating) and where the absent's involvement is conditional. The commentary further addresses a known objection from Bishr al-Marisi and Hanbalis, who argued that this practice contradicts the Hanafi prohibition of judgment against an absent person. The passage ends with practical advice: to prove a claim against an absent, one can appoint him as agent to collect rights, then sue the agent. This entire discussion is a model of Hanafi legal reasoning, balancing textual principles with practical justice.

Agent's Guaranty Invalidates Agency: The Kafalah-Wakalah Conflict
Ruling that an agent's guaranty for the debt invalidates the agency

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kafalah is stronger than Wakalah—an agent cannot be both representative and personal guarantor for the same obligation.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq establishes a key Hanafi principle: if an agent gives a valid guaranty (kafalah) for the same thing he is authorized to transact, his agency becomes void. The reasoning is that kafalah creates direct personal liability which conflicts with the representative role of a wakil. The author also notes exceptions and raises three practical questions about executors, waqf supervisors, and creditor-appointed agents, extending the same reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its scope
The passage states that an agent for sale who guarantees the buyer's price invalidates the agency. More broadly, kafalah for property (bil-mal) invalidates wakalah, regardless of whether the guaranty precedes or follows the agency. Identify this as the core principle.
The rule applies to any agent whose role directly involves the property being guaranteed.
2
Understand the exceptions for collection and marriage agents
The author notes that agents for collection and for marriage (with respect to dower) can initially give a guaranty without immediate invalidation, because the rights are directed away from them. However, if the guaranty becomes valid, the agency ends. Explain why these exceptions exist.
These exceptions do not violate the rule; they delay the effect until the guaranty is enforced.
3
Extract the underlying reasoning
The passage explicitly states: 'kafalah is stronger than agency' (li kawniha aqwa min al-wakalah). This is the legal rationale. Kafalah imposes a direct liability on the guarantor, which conflicts with the agent's role as a representative who does not personally owe the debt.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on the nature of the two contracts.

The author presents three unresolved questions: executor (wasi) guaranteeing a deceased's debt, supervisor (nazir) guaranteeing a tenant's rent, and a creditor appointing a debtor's executor to collect. Apply the same principle: if the guaranty is for an obligation that arose from the executor's/supervisor's own contract, it invalidates; otherwise it is valid. For the third, the agency itself is valid, but a separate executor must be appointed to hear evidence to avoid conflict.
The distinction hinges on whether the guaranty is for something arising from the agent's own contract (e.g., the executor's contract with the debtor) or something pre-existing.
5
Consider al-Ramli's refinement
Al-Ramli qualifies the third case: the appointment of a separate executor is only needed when all heirs are minors. If there is an adult heir, the executor can claim agency from the creditor for that adult directly, and this is the operative fatwa.
This shows how later scholars refine rulings based on context.

The author then raises three unanswered questions: 1) Can an executor (wasi) guarantee the debt of the deceased? 2) Can the supervisor (nazir) of a waqf guarantee the rent for a tenant? 3) Can a creditor appoint the debtor's executor to collect from the estate? Otherwise, it is valid, as they are agents. For the third question, the agency is valid, but the judge must appoint an executor to hear the claim and evidence to avoid conflict of interest. Al-Ramli adds that this applies only when all heirs are minors; if there is an adult heir, the executor can claim agency from the creditor without needing a separate executor.

كفالة (kafālah): A guaranty or surety for a debt, creating personal liability on the guarantor.
وصي (waṣī): Executor of a deceased person's will, managing the estate.
ناظر (nāẓir): Supervisor or manager of a waqf (endowment).

Granting a Surety for the Defendant
Expansion on the takfil ruling from a matn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking a surety from the defendant and identify the conditions under which it is permissible.

Core Idea
Taking a surety from the defendant is istihsan to protect the claimant's right, conditioned on the claimant having present witnesses.

This lesson examines a key procedural rule in Hanafi fiqh: the permissibility of taking a surety (al-takfil) from the defendant to ensure his presence in court. The passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains that this is allowed by istihsan when the claimant states he has present witnesses. We will analyze the conditions, the duration, and the qualities of an acceptable surety.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The ruling is that a surety can be taken from the defendant upon mere claim, and this is istihsan (juridical preference), departing from strict qiyas to protect the claimant.
The default qiyas might not allow it because the defendant is not yet proven liable.
2
Determine the conditions for taking a surety
The surety is only taken if the claimant says he has present witnesses in the city. If no witnesses or witnesses are absent, no surety is taken due to lack of benefit.
The phrase 'لي بينة حاضرة' is key.
3
Analyze the duration of the surety
Two views: three days (Abu Hanifa, considered correct) or until the judge sits another session (the second, i.e., Abu Yusuf or Muhammad). The mufta bihi is three days.
Tarjih is from al-Kafi and al-Khaniya.
4
Define the qualities of a surety
The surety must be trustworthy, have known assets (a house and shop), not likely to flee, and be a resident of the city (not a stranger).
Al-Bazzaziyya and al-Sughra elaborate on these conditions.
5
Understand the discharge of the surety
If the surety surrenders the defendant to the judge or his messenger, he is discharged. Surrendering to the claimant does not discharge him.
This is from Kafalah al-Fatawa al-Sughra.

Explains the Hanafi ruling on taking a kafil from the defendant upon mere claim, with conditions and intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking a kafil from the defendant upon mere claim, explain the proof (istihsan), and identify the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf concerning the duration.

Core Idea
Taking a kafil upon mere claim is based on istihsan, not qiyas, because it protects the claimant with little harm to the defendant.

How far can a judge go to secure a defendant’s presence? The Hanafi school uniquely allows the judge to demand a personal guarantor (kafil bil-nafs) immediately upon the claim, even without evidence. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its justification via istihsan, and the internal Hanafi debate on the duration of the guarantee.

Guided Path

What is the act being ruled upon? Taking a kafil from the defendant. What is the hukm? It is mustahabb (recommended) based on istihsan, not wajib.
The text says 'Akhdhu al-kafil bi-mujarrad al-da'wa istihsanan' – taking a kafil merely upon the claim is by istihsan.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is istihsan (juristic preference). The rationale given: 'li-anna fihi nazaran li-l-mudda'i wa laysa fihi kathir darar bi-l-mudda'a 'alayh' – because it considers the claimant and does not cause much harm to the defendant.
Istihsan overrides qiyas, which would not allow such interference without evidence.
3

The dalal is how istihsan is used: the attendance of the defendant is obligatory merely by the claim, so taking a kafil ensures this without unjust hardship.
The text: 'al-hudur mustahaqq 'alayhi bi-mujarrad al-da'wa' – attendance is due upon him by mere claim.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the duration. Abu Hanifa says three days; Abu Yusuf says until the next court session.
The mufta bihi is three days as per al-Kafi and al-Khaniyya.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical effect: the deadline for producing the defendant differs. Under three days, the kafil must surrender the defendant within three days; under the other view, it is until the next session.
The passage confirms that the three-day limit is the correct opinion.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on claims (Kitab al-Da‘wa) of Al-Bahr al-Ra’iq explains the rulings related to taking a guarantor (kafil) from the defendant in a claim. The commentary opens by elaborating the matn regarding the judge's action if the defendant refuses to provide a guarantor: the judge may follow him for up to three days (or one session for a stranger). This is a measure to ensure the defendant does not abscond. The passage then discusses the scenario where a claimant says he has no evidence, requests an oath, and then after the oath claims he has evidence; the Hanafi position is that the judge accepts this new evidence, though there is a dissent. The core discussion concerns the requirement for the defendant to give a guarantor for himself (kafil bil-nafs) merely upon the claim, as a matter of istihsan, because attendance is obligatory. The period is three days according to Abu Hanifa, but Abu Yusuf holds it is until the next court session. Details are given on who requests the guarantor: the judge does so only if the claimant is knowledgeable; if the claimant is ignorant, the judge initiates. The type of defendant (whether influential or insignificant) and the value of the claim do not matter; all are treated equally. However, the guarantor must be trustworthy, defined as having a known residence and business, not likely to flee. The passage also covers the delivery of the guarantor's ward: if the guarantor surrenders the defendant to the judge or his messenger, he is discharged; to the claimant directly, he is not. Extended discussions from various sources (al-Mujtaba, al-Kafi, al-Sughra) address related issues such as the buyer claiming payment, the killer claiming evidence of pardon, and the claimant's right to request an agent or sequestration of property. The passage concludes with mention that claims of divorce are treated like property claims in terms of evidence requirements, but blood matters receive greater reverence.

Key Terms
كفيل (kafil): A guarantor who undertakes to produce the defendant's person before the court if required.
لازمه (lazamahu): The judge follows the defendant wherever he goes if he refuses to provide a kafil.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference used to justify taking a kafil merely upon the claim without evidence, due to consideration for the claimant.

Oath Wording in Claims of Surety (Kafala)
Specifies oath wording for valid surety claims from Khizanat al-Muftin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid surety claim and apply the correct oath wording for each type of kafala (money, object, person).

Core Idea
The oath in surety claims must be specific to the type of surety to avoid including unrelated obligations.

In claims of surety (kafala), the validity of the claim depends on specific conditions and the oath wording must match the type of surety. This lesson from Khizanat al-Muftin details the required conditions and the precise wording for monetary, object, and personal sureties, ensuring clarity in legal proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses the oath in claims of surety (kafala). The ruling is that the oath must be administered only when the surety is valid.
Validity is a precondition for the oath.
2
List the conditions for validity
The surety must be immediate or suspended on a customary condition, and either authorized by the debtor or ratified in the same session.
These conditions ensure the surety is legally binding.
3
Note the oath wording for each type
For a monetary surety, the oath is: 'By Allah, you have no claim against me for this thousand due to this surety.' For an object: '…for this garment due to this surety.' For a person: '…for the delivery of so-and-so due to this surety.'
The wording excludes other sureties by specifying the cause.
4
Understand the purpose of specificity
The precise wording prevents the oath from covering other sureties the claimant might have against the same defendant.
This protects the defendant from multiple claims under one oath.

Commentary
This passage outlines the oath wording in claims of surety (kafala), but only when the surety is valid. Validity requires that the surety was either immediate (munjiz) or suspended on a customary condition, and that it was either authorized by the debtor or ratified in the same session. The oath wording is tailored to the type of surety: for a monetary surety, the surety swears that the claimant has no claim for that amount due to that surety; for a surety of a specific object, he swears similarly; for a surety of the person (kafala bi al-nafs), he swears regarding delivery of the person. The wording is designed to exclude other sureties.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafālah): A contract of guarantee, where one person becomes surety for another's debt or presence.
منجزة (munjizah): Immediate, not conditional.
معلقة بشرط متعارف (mu'allaqah bi-shart muta'arif): Suspended on a customary condition, i.e., a condition commonly accepted in transactions.
