Intra-Hanafi dispute on judge's ruling vs. personal ijtihad in divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on whether a judge's ruling overrides a husband's personal ijtihad in divorce matters and identify the practical consequences.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling binds externally, but whether it changes the husband's internal permissibility is disputed between Hanafi imams.

In Hanafi fiqh, the authority of a judge's ruling (qada) raises a subtle question: does it also override the personal ijtihad of a knowledgeable husband? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents three distinct Hanafi positions on whether the husband may lawfully continue marital relations after a judge's ruling that contradicts his own belief. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply rulings in cases where external verdicts conflict with personal conviction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Consider a knowledgeable husband whose personal ijtihad (e.g., that the divorce is ba'in) conflicts with a judge's ruling (e.g., that it is raj'i).
The husband must have his own ijtihad; a layman follows the judge unconditionally.
2
Extract the positions of the three imams
From the passage, determine the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on whether the husband may stay with his wife based on the judge's ruling.
Abu Hanifa's view is reported via Muhammad or directly; Abu Yusuf has two opinions; Muhammad allows staying.
3
Trace the evidence for each position
Identify the reasoning: Muhammad relies on the judge's authority to cut off dispute; Abu Yusuf upholds the husband's personal conviction.
The judge's ruling is binding in the external (zahir) sphere, but internal (batin) conviction is separate.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Muhammad and reportedly Abu Hanifa, the husband may lawfully stay. According to Abu Yusuf, he must separate in his own belief.
This affects whether marital relations are permissible from the husband's perspective.
5
Note the distinctions for laymen and fatwa
If the husband is a layman (no ijtihad), he follows the judge in all cases. If a mufti gives a fatwa, the same ikhtilaf applies.

Proof of Conditions Affecting an Absent Party
Procedural rulings on proof of conditions affecting absent parties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting proof of a condition that harms an absent party and apply it to the given cases.

Core Idea
Proof of a condition that harms an absent party is not accepted, even against the present party.

This lesson explores a procedural principle from Jami' al-Fusulayn: when a condition (shart) affects both a present and an absent party, proof of that condition is not accepted if it would harm the absent party. The principle is illustrated through two cases – one involving conditional divorce and another concerning surety for mahr. Understanding this helps you see how Hanafi fiqh safeguards absent parties' rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that the passage deals with the acceptability of proof when a condition affects a present and an absent party. The question is whether the court can hear evidence that would impact an absent party's rights.
The absent party cannot defend themselves, so special rules apply.
2
Analyse the first case
Study the scenario: a husband suspends divorce on his marrying another woman. The present wife proves the marriage to an absent woman. Determine the ruling: the more correct view rejects the proof, so no divorce or new marriage is established.
The condition would harm the absent woman (becoming a co-wife) and benefit the present wife (divorce).
3
Analyse the second case
Examine the branch: a woman claims a surety guaranteed her mahr if triple divorce occurs, and she proves the divorce. The surety admits the kafala but denies knowledge of the divorce. The ruling awards mahr against the present surety but does not rule on divorce against the absent husband.
The divorce is a condition affecting the absent husband; the surety is only a cause for mahr.
4
Extract the principle
Formulate the general rule: evidence establishing a condition that brings harm to an absent party is not accepted in court, even if it also concerns a present party.
This protects absent parties from judgments based on unverified claims.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Acknowledge that there are two narrations in the Hanafi school on this issue; the more correct (asahh) view is that proof is not accepted. The mufta bihi follows this view.
The ikhtilaf is intra-Hanafi, and the later authorities rely on the asahh.

Commentary
This passage provides examples from Jami' al-Fusulayn of cases involving a condition (shart) and its effect on judgment against an absent party. In the first case, a husband says to his wife, 'Your divorce is conditional upon my marrying another woman.' The wife proves that the husband married a specific woman who is absent from court. Hearing Evidence in Conditional Divorce with an Absent Co-wife
Intra-Hanafi dispute on accepting evidence of a condition when the co-wife is absent, with branch on kafalah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting evidence when a condition involves an absent party and distinguish between claims on the present and absent in a kafalah case.

Core Idea
Evidence of a condition's fulfillment does not bind the absent party; each party's rights are adjudicated separately.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi dispute regarding conditional divorce when the triggering event involves an absent person. The key principle is that the rights of an absent party cannot be determined based solely on evidence presented by the present party. We will explore the two narrations and the preferred ruling, as well as a branch case involving a guarantee (kafalah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition and its fulfillment
The husband made his wife's divorce conditional upon his marrying another woman. The wife proves that he did marry another, but that woman is absent from court.
2
Recognize the absent party issue
The co-wife is not present; her rights (in her marriage) are at stake. The question is whether the evidence can be accepted without her presence or representation.
3
Understand the two narrations
There are two narrations within the Hanafi school: one accepts the evidence (permitting divorce), the other rejects it. Identify the basis for each.
4
Apply the preferred ruling
The most correct (asahh) is that the evidence is not accepted for either wife; hence no divorce and no nullification of the co-wife's marriage.
The principle: a condition involving an absent party cannot be adjudicated without representation.
5
Distinguish the branch case on kafalah
In the branch, a guarantor (present) admits the guarantee but denies knowledge of the triple divorce. The wife proves the divorce. The court awards mahr against the guarantor but does not annul the marriage against the absent husband.
This illustrates that claims against a present party are separate from claims against an absent principal.

Conditions and Exceptions After Conjunctive Clauses
Expands on rules of conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a condition after conjunctive sentences applies to all, while an exception with 'illa' applies only to the last, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi dispute over 'in sha Allah' after conditional sentences.

Core Idea
A condition after a series binds all; an exception binds only the last.

In legal speech, the placement of a condition or exception can dramatically change the ruling. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the Hanafi position: when a condition follows multiple conjunctive clauses, it governs all of them, but an exception (istithna) using 'illa' only affects the last clause. A special rule applies to 'in sha Allah', which is treated as a condition, and here the school has an internal disagreement between Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf. Understanding these principles is essential for correctly interpreting contracts, divorce statements, oaths, and confessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of expression
Determine whether the text involves a condition (shart) like 'if...then' or an exception (istithna) like 'except...'.
A condition suspends a ruling; an exception excludes part of a general statement.
2
Check the clause structure
Are the clauses conjunctive (connected by wa 'and') and does the condition/exception directly follow them? If so, apply the general rule. If there is silence between, different rules apply.
Conjunctive clauses are called 'jumalan muta'atifa'.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule for conditions and exceptions
If it is a condition, it applies to all preceding conjunctive clauses. If it is an exception with 'illa' or its sisters, it applies only to the last clause.
This is based on custom and istihsan.
4
Handle the special case of 'in sha Allah'
After performative sentences (iqa'iyya), 'in sha Allah' applies to all by agreement. After conditional sentences (mu'allaqa), there is a dispute: Muhammad says it applies to all, Abu Yusuf says only to the last. The relied-upon view is Muhammad's.
Also note the ruling on oaths after silence: if the addition broadens, it is invalid; if it narrows, it is valid.

Disagreement on Number of Divorces in Testimony
Ruling on when two witnesses disagree on the number of divorces pronounced.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two witnesses disagree on the number of divorces and apply it to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
When witnesses disagree on divorce counts, the lower number is taken, preserving the husband's right of return.

In marriage, witnesses may testify about a divorce. But what happens when they disagree on the number? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that the husband retains the right to take back his wife during the waiting period even if one witness claims three divorces. The key is that the word 'al-battah' is superfluous in the context of three, so the disagreement is only about the number, and the lower number prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disagreement
Recognize that both witnesses agree a divorce took place but disagree on the count (one says three, the other says two).
2
Determine the accepted number
Apply the Hanafi principle: when testimony conflicts on the quantity, the lower number is established. Here, two divorces are accepted.
This is based on the rule that certainty (fewer) overrides doubt (more).
3
Consider the extra word 'al-battah'
The witness who said three also said 'al-battah' (irrevocably). But since three divorces are already irrevocable, the word is superfluous and does not affect the count.
This shows that superfluous language does not alter the legal outcome.
4
State the consequence
Since only two divorces occurred, the husband retains the right to take his wife back (raj'a) during the waiting period.

Witnesses' Liability for Retraction in Divorce and Manumission
Primary ruling on witness retraction liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on retracting witnesses in divorce and manumission and identify the conditions that affect liability.

Core Idea
Retracting witnesses guarantee half dower in pre‑consummation divorce and the full value in manumission.

This lesson focuses on the fundamental ruling: witnesses who testify to a divorce or manumission and then retract become liable for financial compensation. The amount depends on timing (before or after consummation) and the type of case. Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying the rules of testimony correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the ruling covers two scenarios: divorce before consummation (half dower) and manumission (value). After consummation, no liability.
2
Distinguish before and after consummation
Note that the dower is fully established after consummation, so witnesses cannot be held liable. Before consummation, the dower is not fully fixed, so half is due.
3
Apply to manumission
For manumission, witnesses guarantee the value of the slave regardless of the timing of the testimony.
4
Consider the effect of confession
If the husband or master later confesses to the divorce or manumission, the witnesses' liability is reversed.

The Reasoning Behind Witness Liability: A Hanafi Internal Debate
Expansion of the reasoning behind witness liability: intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi positions on why witnesses guarantee half the dower and identify which view was preferred by Fakhr al-Islam.

Core Idea
The ruling is agreed upon, but the reasoning differs: earlier scholars view the testimony as confirming a falling obligation; later scholars reject this and offer alternative reasoning.

While the ruling on witness retraction is settled, the underlying rationale was debated within the Hanafi school. This disagreement between early and later scholars is a classic example of ta'lil refinement, showing how usul al-fiqh evolves while preserving the ruling. Understanding this debate deepens your appreciation of Hanafi methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
The earlier scholars said the witnesses' testimony confirmed a guarantee that was about to fall (due to apostasy etc.). The later scholars rejected this reasoning.
2
Explain the earlier scholars' argument
They argued that before consummation, the entire dower could fall (e.g., if she apostatized). The testimony made half the dower obligatory as mut'a, so they created a new obligation.
3
Explain the later scholars' objection
They said the half dower was already confirmed by the marriage contract, not the testimony. Nothing remained except consummation, which is like receipt. Thus the testimony did not create a new obligation.
4
Analyze the gift analogy
The later scholars used the example of a donor: if witnesses testified that he received compensation, the judge voids his right to revoke, but if they retract, they owe nothing. This shows that confirming an existing obligation does not create liability.
5
Note the tarjih by Fakhr al-Islam
Fakhr al-Islam (al-Bazdawi) chose the later scholars' view as closer to verification, as stated in al-Taqrir.

Core Idea
The judge's ruling is either inwardly binding (Abu Hanifa) or only outwardly (Sahibayn)—this determines who bears the loss.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a nuanced scenario where witnesses retract their testimony after a divorce ruling, and later the husband admits the divorce occurred at a different time. 1

Core Idea
An act that does not require deliberation can be performed by one agent as if both were present.

When a principal appoints two agents for divorce or manumission without compensation, either agent may act alone. This is because these acts do not require careful thought or bargaining. However, this rule has important exceptions that modify its application.

The rule applies to divorce (talaq) and manumission (itaq) where the receiving party gives no compensation. These are acts that do not involve financial exchange or negotiation.

Exceptions to the General Rule: When Joint Action is Required
Exception scenarios requiring joint action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five exceptions where one of two agents cannot act alone in divorce or manumission without compensation, and apply the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
Even for acts without compensation, one agent cannot act alone when the subject is unspecified, the authority is conditional, the instruction requires togetherness, or joint action is explicitly mandated.

While the general rule allows one agent to act alone for divorce or manumission without compensation, the Hanafi jurists enumerated five specific exceptions. These arise when the act involves choice, conditional language, or explicit restrictions. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first exception: unspecified subject
If the principal says 'divorce one of my wives' or 'free one of my slaves' without specification, one agent cannot act alone because choosing requires deliberation (ra'y).
From al-Siraj al-Wahhaj: 'لأنه مما يحتاج إلى الرأي بخلاف المعين'.
2
Understand the second exception: conditional authority
If the principal says 'Divorce her if you wish' (condition), it is either a delegation requiring both agents to act in the same session, or a conditional suspension requiring both to act together.
The passage explains: 'يكون تفويضاً فيقتصر على المجلس ... أو يكون تعليقاً فيشترط فعلهما'.
3
Analyze the third exception: 'together' instruction
If the principal says 'Divorce her together', one agent alone cannot act. Even if one divorces once and the other twice (total three), it does not occur because the instruction requires simultaneous action.
This is based on the literal meaning of 'جميعاً'.
4
Examine the fourth exception: explicit prohibition of singularity
If the principal says 'Do not divorce without your companion', joint action is required. If one divorces and the other later ratifies, it is still not valid until both act together at the same time.
From Munyat al-Mufti: 'لا يقع ما لم يجتمعا'.
5
Apply the compensation exception
If the divorce or manumission involves compensation (e.g., khul'), one agent cannot act alone because it requires deliberation. This overrides the general rule entirely.

The Rule of Gender Suitability in Spousal Disputes Over Household Goods
Commentary on the matn ruling about spousal disputes over household goods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that each spouse's statement is accepted for items suitable to their gender, and identify when the exception for traders overrides it.

Core Idea
In disputes, the one whose gender typically uses the item gets to claim it—unless the other trades in such items.

When a husband and wife disagree over who owns the household contents, how does a judge decide? Hanafi law provides a clear framework: each spouse's statement is accepted for items that are 'suitable' to their gender, based on apparent usage and possession. However, if one spouse routinely sells items of the other type, the balance shifts. In this lesson, we unpack the ruling from Bahr al-Raiq, explore its reasoning, and learn to apply it to real cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The core ruling: in a dispute over household goods, each spouse's claim (with oath) is accepted for items that are suitable for their gender. The scope includes all items in the house, even gold and silver, and applies during marriage and after separation, regardless of house ownership.
The ruling is procedural—it guides the qadi on how to adjudicate without clear evidence.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The evidence is the 'zahir' (apparent circumstance): men typically use men's items, women use women's items. This creates a strong presumption that the person who uses an item is its owner. Additionally, physical possession (yad) supports the claim.
No Quran or hadith is cited; the proof is customary practice and rational inference.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how it proves)
The apparent circumstance acts as a witness. For items like turbans or swords, the fact that only men normally use them suggests the husband owns them. The wife's use of headcovers or anklets suggests her ownership. This is a form of qarinah (circumstantial evidence) deemed strong enough to decide.
This principle is called 'al-zahir yanfa' al-da'wa' – the apparent supports the claim.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
The exception: if one spouse trades in items suitable for the other, the conflicting circumstantial evidence (trade vs. use) cancels out. But the Hanafi majority holds that the husband's statement still prevails over the wife's when she sells men's items, because her hand (yad) is subordinate to his. Some scholars (al-Shurnublaliyyah) suggest there are two views on this.
There is also a conflicting report in al-Hukkam mentioned by al-Ramli.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view)
The majority of commentators follow the restricted exception: the exception only applies when it is the husband selling women's items (his statement accepted) or the wife selling men's items (husband's statement still accepted). The mufta bihi is as given in the text: gender-based suitability, with the trader exception limited by the principle of yad.
Al-Ramli endorses following the majority.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn phrase regarding disputes between spouses over household goods (mata' al-bayt). The commentator begins by defining 'mata'' linguistically as anything of benefit, then clarifies that in this context it includes all items in the house, even gold and silver. However, an exception arises if one spouse engages in business selling items suitable for the other; in that case, the conflicting circumstances cancel out, and the seller's statement may not be accepted. For instance, if a husband sells women's goods, his statement is accepted (because his hand of possession supports him?), while if a wife sells men's goods, the husband's statement prevails because the wife's hand is considered an extension of the husband's. It also addresses specific scenarios: if a wife brings a slave girl to the marital home, uses her for a year while the husband silently witnesses, then claims her after separation, the husband's statement is accepted because his possession (yad) was not removed. The passage ends with a cautionary note from al-Shurnublaliyyah that the exception should not be interpreted broadly, and from al-Ramli that there is a conflicting report in al-Hukkam. Confession of the Sick Person: Validity and Priority of Debts
Exposition of the core ruling on the sick person's confession of debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a sick person's confession of debt is valid from the entire estate and that health debts and obligations from known causes take priority over confessional debts.

Core Idea
A sick person's confession of debt is binding, but earlier health debts and liabilities from known causes come first.

When a person on his deathbed makes a confession of debt, Islamic law balances the rights of the confessor's creditors and heirs. This passage from Bahru al-Rāʾiq explains that while the confession is valid and effective from all wealth, it does not override debts that arose during health or obligations incurred due to well-known causes like marriage, sale, or damage. State the general ruling
The text begins with 'إقراره بدين نافذ من كل ماله' — the confession of a debt by a terminally ill person is valid and must be paid from the entire estate. This is the default rule.
No limit to one-third for debt confession, unlike gifts.

Identify the priority rule
Then it says 'ودين الصحة وما لزمه في مرضه بسبب معروف قدم على ما أقر به في مرض موته' — debts from health and obligations from known causes (e.g., dower, price of sale, compensation for damage) take precedence over the confessional debt. This means the confessional debt is only paid after those are satisfied.
This protects against fraudulent confessions.

The text states that the sick person may not pay some creditors over others, even if one is a dower or wage. All creditors of the same class have equal rights.

If the estate is insufficient, creditors share proportionally.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the rulings pertaining to the confession (iqrār) of a person who is terminally ill (marīḍ marad al-mawt). The text covers multiple interrelated issues: First, it clarifies that the confession of a sick person regarding a debt is valid and takes effect from all of his wealth, but debts of health and obligations arising from known causes during illness are given priority over confessions made in the terminal illness. This means that if a person has both a pre-existing debt and confesses a new debt during his terminal illness, the pre-existing debt is paid first. The passage then discusses the prohibition of favoring some creditors over others, except in specific cases where the debtor paid for a loan taken or the price of a purchase during illness with evidence. Next, it addresses the validity of confession to an heir (wārith) — it is invalid unless the other heirs confirm it. Confession to a non-heir is valid even if it encompasses all of the estate. The text also covers the interaction between confession of debt and deposit (wadīʿa), the effect of confession of lineage, marriage, and declaration of freedom. Furthermore, it deals with the scenario where a sick person confesses to a sale and receipt of price, and the limits of validity (e.g., the receipt is not valid except to the extent of one-third if the sale was in health). The passage includes rules on the confession of a person who is bankrupt (madyūn) and the acceptance of evidence. Overall, the commentary follows the author's order, explaining each sub-rule with attention to the nuances of Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): A legal confession or admission by a person which establishes a right against himself.
مريض مرض الموت (marīḍ marad al-mawt): A person suffering from a terminal illness from which death is expected within a year.
دين الصحة (dīn al-ṣiḥḥa): A debt contracted during a state of health before the terminal illness.

Acknowledgment to Heir vs. Stranger
Opening section of the chapter on the sick person's acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling for a sick person's acknowledgment to an heir and to a stranger, and identify the exceptions when marriage or subsequent events alter the ruling.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment to an heir is void unless all other heirs confirm; to a stranger it is valid even if it consumes the entire estate.

The Hanafi school protects heirs from potential fraud by voiding a sick person's acknowledgment to an heir unless the other heirs consent. However, an acknowledgment to a stranger is fully valid because it is treated like a debt. This lesson explores this fundamental distinction and its exceptions, including cases where the stranger later marries the patient or acknowledges paternity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that acknowledgments are divided into two broad categories: to an heir and to a stranger. The default ruling for each is different.
The key phrase: 'لوارثه بطل' vs 'لأجنبي صح'.
2
Understand the condition for the heir
Why is confirmation of other heirs required? The acknowledgment to an heir is treated as a potential disguised gift, which is restricted during illness.
The condition: 'إلا أن يصدقه البقية' – unless the rest confirm.
3
Note the exceptions for the stranger
If the stranger later has his lineage established by the patient's acknowledgment, the initial acknowledgment becomes void. If the patient marries the stranger, the acknowledgment becomes valid, unlike a gift or bequest.
Marriage validates; lineage establishment voids.
4
Apply to the thrice-divorced woman
Acknowledgment to a woman divorced three times during the waiting period is limited to the lesser of her inheritance share or the debt amount.
This prevents abuse: she cannot claim more than she would inherit.

Gifts with Void Conditions: The Hanafi Rule of Validity
Ruling on gifts with void conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a gift with a void condition (e.g., exception of pregnancy) and explain the basis for distinguishing between contracts that are and are not invalidated by void conditions.

Core Idea
A gift with a void condition remains valid; the condition is null, because gift is not an exchange contract.

The Hanafi madhhab has a distinctive approach to conditions in gratuitous contracts. Unlike sale, which is invalidated by void conditions, a gift (hibah) survives such conditions. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the rule and its rationale, using the example of excepting pregnancy from a gift of a slave-girl. Understanding this distinction is key to applying Hanafi contract law correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is giving a gift (hibah) with a void condition or exception. The ruling: the gift is valid (sahih) and the condition/exception is void (batil).
This is the foundational outcome of the passage.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: istithna' (exception) is only effective where the contract can operate on the excepted part. A gift cannot operate on pregnancy because it is a description, so the exception becomes a void condition. The gift is not invalidated by void conditions.
The passage uses talil (reasoning) rather than a textual source.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reasoning reveals a principle: gratuitous contracts (gift, charity, marriage, khul') are tolerant of void conditions because they are not exchange-based. In contrast, exchange contracts (sale, hire, pledge) are invalidated because conditions affect the exchange balance.
This principle drives the distinction between contracts.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that void conditions invalidate a gift entirely, analogizing it to sale. The Hanafi response is that gift is not an exchange, so the analogy fails.
This ikhtilaf has practical consequences for the validity of the transaction.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In Hanafi fiqh, the donee takes the property free of the void condition and is not obligated to fulfill it. In Shafi'i fiqh, the gift is void and the property must be returned.
The practical outcome differs: Hanafi upholds the gift; Shafi'i nullifies it.

Chapter: And whoever gives a female slave as a gift except her pregnancy, or on condition that she be returned to him, or that he manumit her, or that he seek offspring from her, or a house on condition that he return some of it to him, or that he compensate him with something from it, the gift is valid and the exception and condition are void, because the exception is effective only in a place where the contract is effective, and the gift is not effective in the pregnancy because it is a description, so it turns into a void condition, and the gift is not invalidated by void conditions. So it includes every contract that is not invalidated by void conditions, such as marriage (nikah), khul', charity (sadaqa), compromise for intentional homicide (sulh 'an dam al-amd), and emancipation (itq); it is valid, and the exception is void. And it excludes everything that is invalidated by them, such as sale (bay'), hire (ijara), pledge (rahn), kitaba, and what is valid with exception like bequest (wasiyya) and khul'. So it appears that the exception of pregnancy in contracts is of three levels. As for concluding the contract on it alone, it is not valid, like sale and kitaba, even if the mother is accepted; and gift and charity, even if the mother is delivered to the donee or the recipient of charity; and marriage, and the dower of the like is obligatory. And if one compromises for retaliation on what is in the womb, it is valid and nullifies the retaliation, and blood money (diya) is obligatory. And his emancipation individually is valid if its existence at the time is known, like bequest and khul', and if it does not exist at the time, then not. And she is entitled to the dower he gave her if she said: 'Divorce me on what is in the belly of my slave-girl from a child,' and if she did not say 'from a child,' then not; as in Ghayat al-Bayan briefly. And the author indicated that if he emancipated what is in her belly then gave her as a gift, it is permissible because the fetus did not remain in his ownership, so it resembles an exception. And if he made a tadbir for what is in her belly then gave her as a gift, it is not permissible because the pregnancy remained in his ownership, so it is not similar to an exception, and it is not possible to execute the gift in it due to the tadbir, so it remains a gift of common property or a gift of something occupied by the owner's ownership, unlike sale where it is not permissible in all chapters due to the prohibition of sale with a condition. And it has preceded that the compensation cannot be part of the gifted item, therefore his statement 'on condition that he return to him something from it' is void, whether the condition is with this expression or the thing is specified like one-third or one-fourth. As for his statement 'or that he compensate him from it with something,' it is also not valid because the condition of compensation in a gift requires that the compensation be known, as preceded because it is a new transfer of ownership, and this is unknown. And with this the objection of the commentator (may Allah have mercy on him) following the author of al-Nihaya is refuted, namely that if he meant by it the gift with a condition of compensation, then it and the condition are permissible, so his statement 'the condition is void' is not sound; and if he meant by it that he compensates him with something from the gifted property, it is pure repetition because he mentioned it in his statement 'on condition that he return to him something from it.' So his statement is not complete unless the compensation is specified, and this is not the author's intent; this became clear to me before seeing the statement of Sadr al-Shari'a, then I saw he stated it explicitly and said: I say their intent is when the compensation is unknown, and the compensation is only valid if it is known.

Commentary
This passage deals with gifts that include an exception (istithna') or conditions that are void. The key ruling: if someone gives a female slave as a gift but excepts her pregnancy, or imposes a condition that the donee must return the item or manumit the slave, etc., the gift itself is valid but the exception or condition is void. The reason is that istithna' only operates in a context where the contract itself can affect the excepted part; since a gift does not affect the fetus (as it is a description, not a separate entity), the exception turns into a void condition. However, the Hanafi rule is that a gift is not invalidated by void conditions. This rule also applies to other contracts like marriage, khul', charity, compromise for homicide, and emancipation — they remain valid despite void conditions. Contracts that are invalidated by void conditions, such as sale, hire, pledge, and kitaba, are excluded. The passage then categorizes contracts regarding exception of pregnancy into three levels. It also discusses the validity of tadbir (declaration of freedom after the owner's death) and emancipation before gifting. The last part clarifies that if a condition requires the donee to return part of the gifted item or compensate with something from it, the condition is void because compensation cannot be part of the gifted item (it would be like selling with a condition), and if the compensation is unspecified, it is also void due to ignorance (jahalah). The author resolves an objection by clarifying that the intended meaning is when the compensation is unknown.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna'): An exception clause in a contract, e.g., 'I give you this except that part.'
هبة (hibah): A gift; a voluntary transfer of ownership without compensation.
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A void condition that contradicts the essence of the contract or is legally invalid.
