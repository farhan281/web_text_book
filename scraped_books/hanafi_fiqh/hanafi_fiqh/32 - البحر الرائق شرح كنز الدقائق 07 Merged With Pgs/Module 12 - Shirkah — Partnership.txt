Permissible Unilateral Acts by a Co-Owner
Rulings on permissible unilateral acts in joint property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the actions a co-owner may perform without the other's consent and identify the condition of no harm.

Core Idea
A co-owner may use joint property without permission as long as no harm is caused to the other co-owner.

In a jointly owned property, daily use often requires one co-owner to act alone. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: certain actions like tying an animal, placing a beam, or performing ablution are permissible if they do not harm the partner. However, digging or altering the property may require compensation if damage occurs. This lesson explores which acts are allowed without consent and the principle of no harm that governs them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of joint property
Determine that the property is 'mushtarak' (shared ownership) and that no partnership contract exists, only common ownership.
2
List the permissible acts
From the source, identify acts like tying animals, placing beams, performing ablution, and accessing a neighbor's property for wall repair.
These acts are allowed if they do not cause harm or undue inconvenience.
3
Apply the condition of no harm
Each act must be done in a way that does not damage the property or inconvenience the co-owner. If harm results, liability may arise.
For example, digging must be leveled if it causes a deficiency.
4
Differentiate access for repair
If a co-owner needs to enter the partner's property to repair a shared wall or channel, the partner must allow access or bear the expense himself.
This is a ruling from Imam Muhammad adopted by Abu al-Layth.

The third matter is related to common property, and it has two types: the first is what one of them may do alone, and the second is its repair when it becomes dilapidated. As for the first, in Waqf al-Nawazil: If a house is shared among people, some of them may tie animals in it, and place a beam in a way that does not harm the co-owner, and perform ablution provided it does not narrow the path for their passage; if someone is harmed by it, they are not liable. If someone digs the ground, he is ordered to level it; if the digging causes a deficiency, he is liable for the deficiency. Likewise, if there is a path among people that is not a thoroughfare, except that in the path he is not liable for a deficiency caused by digging. If a man has a wall and its face is in another man's house, and he wants to plaster it, but there is no way to reach it except by entering the other man's house, or the wall collapses and its debris falls into his house, and he wants to enter to remove the clay and other things, but the owner of the house prevents him; or he has a water channel in his house and wants to dig and repair it, and it is not possible except by entering the other's house, and he is prevented; then he is told: Either let him enter and repair and do, or you do it at your own expense. This is narrated from Muhammad, and al-Faqih Abu al-Layth adopted it. In Jami' al-Fusulayn, from the chapter on walls: If one co-owner has a beam on the wall, the other may place a similar one if the wall can bear it; otherwise, his partner is ordered to raise some of the beam, etc. As for the second type, there is no compulsion on the one who refuses, because a person is not compelled to repair his property, whether it is a house, a bathhouse, or a wall. This is stated in most books. In Khizanat al-Akmal, from the book of partnership: If a bathhouse between two partners collapses and one refuses to repair, neither is compelled to build with his partner, but the partner may build, then rent it out and take from its rent to cover his expenses. Similarly, in the case of shifting the wells of a qanat or its canals. However, if the qanat needs repair by removing silt and opening blockages and springs, then he is compelled to assist his partner. There is no compulsion in these and similar cases. In Tahdhib al-Qalanisi, from the book of claims: In a shared well, waterwheel, and the like, the partner is compelled to repair. In a screening wall that has no building on it, if its fragility becomes apparent, fatwa is given for compulsion because it has no benefit preventing him other than screening, and that is achieved by building. This is when there is no orphan property or endowment. If it is orphan property, then in Wasaay al-Khaniyya: A wall between the houses of two minors has a load on it, feared to collapse, and each minor has a guardian; one guardian seeks repair of the wall, the other refuses. Imam Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn al-Fadl said: The judge should send a trustee to inspect; if he knows that leaving it would cause harm to both, he compels the refusing one to build with his partner. This is not like the refusal of one of the owners because there the refusing one is content with entering harm, so he is not compelled. But here, the guardian intended to bring harm to the minor, so he is compelled to repair with his partner. I say: The endowment should be like orphan property. If a house is shared between two endowments and needs repair, and one trustee wants to repair and the other refuses, he is compelled to repair from the endowment's money, and this has become a fatwa incident. And if it is known that there is no compulsion on the partner, then the one seeking repair may spend and repair, and he may recover if he is in a situation of necessity, such as if the shared property cannot be divided, e.g., a small house that cannot be divided, a bathhouse, or a wall that is not wide. If he is not in necessity, like a large house that can have its land divided and each build in his share, then he has no recourse. Al-Halwani mentioned a principle, saying: Whoever is compelled to do something with his partner, if he does it without the partner's permission, he does not have recourse. Our teacher's teacher, Mulla Ali al-Turkumani, said: Its summary is: If he is in necessity, then either the judge would compel him or not. If the judge would compel him, he has recourse; if it is something for which the judge does not compel, and he spent without the partner's order, he has recourse. But if he spent without his partner's permission, he does not have recourse.

Commentary
This passage collects rulings on joint property (musharrak) in two types. First: what one co-owner may do alone without needing permission, such as tying animals or placing beams, provided no harm to others. If harm occurs, they are not liable unless they exceed bounds. For repairs of a shared wall or water channel that requires entering a neighbor's property, the neighbor must allow access or bear the expense himself—a ruling from Muhammad al-Shaybani adopted by Abu al-Layth. Second: what to do when a shared property deteriorates and one partner refuses to repair. The general rule is no compulsion (la jabr) because one cannot be forced to fix his own property. However, exceptions exist: for a qanat needing cleaning, for a screening wall with no other benefit, for orphan property (guardians are compelled to avoid harm to minors), and for endowments (treated like orphans). The key distinction is necessity (idtirar): if the property cannot be divided (e.g., small house, bathhouse, narrow wall), the repairing partner can recover costs from the refusing partner even without permission. If division is possible (large house), no recovery. Al-Halwani's principle is that if the judge would compel the partner to repair, the one who spends may recover; if the judge would not compel, and he spends without order, he has no recourse—except if he is in necessity, then even without order he may recover. This applies when the refusing partner simply does not care about harm, but in orphan/endowment cases, the guardian's refusal is seen as introducing harm, so compulsion applies.

Key Terms
المشترك (al-mushtarak): Jointly owned property (shirkah in 'ayn).
الجبر (al-jabr): Legal compulsion to perform an action against one's will, typically by court order.
التعمير (al-ta'mir): Repair or reconstruction of a property to prevent dilapidation.

Compulsion to Repair Joint Property: General Rule and Exceptions
Rulings on compulsion to repair and recourse in joint property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule of no compulsion to repair joint property, identify the exceptions, and apply the principle of recourse for the repairing partner.

Core Idea
No co-owner is forced to repair joint property, but necessity, orphan protection, and endowments can trigger compulsion and recovery rights.

When a jointly owned property falls into disrepair, can one partner force the other to contribute to repairs? The Hanafi school gives a clear answer: generally, no one can be forced to repair their own property. However, this rule has important exceptions—for orphans, endowments, indispensable utilities like qanats, and when the property cannot be divided. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the exceptions, and the crucial distinction between when the repairing partner can recover costs and when he cannot, based on the concept of necessity (idtirar).

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
From the passage: 'There is no compulsion on the one who refuses, because a person is not compelled to repair his property.' This is the default in most Hanafi books.
Applies to houses, bathhouses, walls, etc.
2
Identify the exceptions where compulsion applies
The passage lists: (1) Qanat needing repair (clearing silt), (2) Screening wall with no other benefit, (3) Orphan property (guardians are compelled to avoid harm), (4) Endowments (treated like orphans), (5) Shared well and waterwheel per Tahdhib al-Qalanisi (though this is a minority view).
The exception for orphans is based on the guardian's duty; for endowments, the trustee's duty.
3
Understand the recourse principle
If one partner repairs voluntarily, he may recover costs only if he is in 'necessity' (idtirar), i.e., the property cannot be divided (small house, bathhouse, narrow wall). If division is possible, he cannot recover.
Al-Halwani's principle: if the judge would compel the partner to repair, the one who spends has recourse; if not, and he spends without order, no recourse.
4
Apply to a case
Given a scenario, determine whether compulsion applies and if the repairing partner can recover costs.
Use the rules: necessity + possible judicial compulsion = recourse; no necessity = no recourse.

Second: what to do when a shared property deteriorates and one partner refuses to repair. The general rule is no compulsion (la jabr) because one cannot be forced to fix his own property. However, exceptions exist: for a qanat needing cleaning, for a screening wall with no other benefit, for orphan property (guardians are compelled to avoid harm to minors), and for endowments (treated like orphans). The key distinction is necessity (idtirar): if the property cannot be divided (e.g., small house, bathhouse, narrow wall), the repairing partner can recover costs from the refusing partner even without permission. If division is possible (large house), no recovery. Al-Halwani's principle is that if the judge would compel the partner to repair, the one who spends may recover; if the judge would not compel, and he spends without order, he has no recourse—except if he is in necessity, then even without order he may recover. This applies when the refusing partner simply does not care about harm, but in orphan/endowment cases, the guardian's refusal is seen as introducing harm, so compulsion applies.

Key Terms
الجبر (al-jabr): Legal compulsion to perform an action against one's will, typically by court order.
التعمير (al-ta'mir): Repair or reconstruction of a property to prevent dilapidation.
الاضطرار (al-idtirar): A state of necessity where a person has no alternative but to act to prevent harm.

Termination of Agency by Separation of Partners
Expansion of the ruling on agency termination by partnership separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on termination of agency when partners separate, distinguishing between constructive and actual removal, and identify exceptions for prior transactions and debt collection.

Core Idea
Partnership separation terminates agency constructively, but prior transactions remain valid and debt collection rights of the lending partner are unaffected.

Agency in a partnership is often implied, allowing each partner to act on behalf of the other. When the partnership dissolves through separation, this agency ends automatically. However, the law does not invalidate actions already taken or rights that are personal to one partner. This lesson unpacks the details from the classical Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that separation of partners terminates the agency. This is a constructive removal (azl hukmi) that does not require the agent's knowledge.
Contrast with express removal which may require notification.
2
Understand the Reasoning
Constructive removal operates by law due to the dissolution of the partnership. The agency is dependent on the partnership, so its termination automatically ends the agency.
The author cites al-Kafi to support this.
3
Distinguish Partnership Types
The passage discusses two types: mufawada (equal agency) and 'inan (limited partnership). In mufawada, each partner's agency extends fully to the other; in 'inan, it is limited.
This affects the validity of actions after separation.
4
Examine Exceptions
Prior transactions (e.g., contracts executed before separation) remain valid. Also, if a partner personally lent a debt, the agent appointed by that partner can still collect it after separation, and the other partner's removal of the agent is void for that debt.
These exceptions protect vested rights.
5
Apply to Scenarios
Consider an agent appointed by one partner to sell joint property. If separation occurs, the agent is removed vis-à-vis the non-appointing partner, unless explicit authorization was given.
For 'inan, istihsan allows one partner's appointment to bind the other for selling joint property.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling that agency terminates upon the separation of partners in a partnership. The author distinguishes between constructive removal (which does not require knowledge) and actual removal. The separation can occur due to dissolution of the partnership (e.g., loss of capital). If the partners had appointed an agent, the agent is removed with respect to the non-appointing partner unless explicit authorization was given. The author quotes al-Hakim's Kafi with several scenarios: for a mufawada partnership, the agent's actions remain valid if executed before separation even if the agent did not know; this is because the agency of one partner is valid for the other. For a 'inan partnership, istihsan allows one partner's appointment of an agent to be valid over both for selling joint property. If the other partner later removes the agent, the removal is effective except for collecting debts owed to the appointing partner individually. This is because the agent's authority to collect that specific debt is not shared.

Key Terms
عزل حكمي (azl hukmi): Constructive removal of an agent without explicit statement, occurring by operation of law (e.g., death, separation).
