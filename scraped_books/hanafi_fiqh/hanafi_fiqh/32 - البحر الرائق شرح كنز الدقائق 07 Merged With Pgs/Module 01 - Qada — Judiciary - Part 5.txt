Principle of Division: 'Awliyya vs Niza'iyya
Expounding Abu Hanifa's principle for classifying division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's principle for classifying division as 'awliyya or niza'iyya, and identify the practical difference with Sahibayn when rights are established at different times.

Core Idea
Division is proportional when rights share equal strength; contestatory when rights are specified or time-differentiated.

The Hanafi school classifies the division of jointly owned property into two types: 'awliyya (proportional, like inheritance shares) and niza'iyya (contestatory, where each takes a specific portion). The classification depends on the nature of the rights, not merely the number of claimants. This passage presents Abu Hanifa's foundational principle and then shows how his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), apply it differently when the rights arise at different times.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two categories
First, grasp the definitions: 'awliyya division divides the property in proportion to each claimant's right, analogous to inheritance ('awl). Niza'iyya division gives each claimant his specific portion without proportional adjustment.
'Awliyya is based on the idea that rights of equal strength should share proportionally.
2
Identify the criterion for 'awliyya
According to Abu Hanifa, 'awliyya applies when rights are either (1) in the dhimma (debt-like) or (2) in an undivided common share of the 'ayn (the property itself). In both cases, the rights are equal in strength.
Rights in the dhimma are considered equal because the dhimma is broad enough to accommodate all claims proportionally.
3
Identify the criterion for niza'iyya
Niza'iyya applies when rights are either (1) specified to distinct parts of the property (tamyiz) or (2) one claimant has a right in a common share while the other has a right in the whole property. In these cases, rights are not equal in strength.
When one owns the whole and another only a part, the whole owner cannot be forced into proportional division.
4
Analyze the intra-madhhab difference
Sahibayn introduce an additional factor: the time when rights are established. In examples like a slave killing two victims at different times, the Sahibayn classify division as niza'iyya because the rights are established at different times. Abu Hanifa's principle suggests that if the rights are in the dhimma (as in jinaayah obligations), they remain equal in strength regardless of timing.
This ikhtilaf affects cases where rights are in the dhimma but arise sequentially.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the Hanafi principles of division (qisma) when multiple claimants have rights that are established at different times or in different manners. The author first gives several examples to illustrate the concept of niza'iyya (contested) division. In the example of a slave who kills one man intentionally and another by mistake, the intentional victim's guardian has a right to qisas (which becomes a monetary right after pardon), while the guardian of the mistaken victim has a right to value (the slave's price). Since these rights are established at different times (the time of killing vs. the time of handing over), the division is niza'iyya according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Similarly, with umm walad's aggression, the two killing events occur at different times, so division is niza'iyya according to them. The author then states Abu Hanifa's fundamental principle: division is 'awliyya (proportional) when rights are either in the dhimma or in the 'ayn on a common share; it is niza'iyya when rights are specified (e.g., one has right in a specific part) or when one has right in a common part and the other in the whole. The reasoning is that rights in the dhimma are equal in strength, as are rights in a common share; these are analogous to inheritance ('awl). When rights are specified, they are not analogous. The passage concludes that cases where rights arise from aggression (jinaayah) fall under 'awliyya because the obligation is in the dhimma.

Key Terms
قسمة عولية (qisma 'awliyya): Proportional division where each claimant takes a share of the property in proportion to his right, applicable when rights are equal in strength (in dhimma or common share).
قسمة نزاعية (qisma niza'iyya): Contested division where rights are distinct or specified, not analogous to inheritance; each claimant takes his specific portion without proportional adjustment.
شيوع (shuyu'): Common ownership; a right that is not specified to a particular part of the property.

Umm Walad's Multiple Jinaayaat and Compensation Division
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of umm walad's compensation for multiple homicides

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that multiple homicides by an umm walad impose only one payment of her value, and compare Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on dividing that value among the heirs of multiple victims.

Core Idea
An umm walad's multiple homicides merge into a single compensation value, divided between heirs by thirds or fourths.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the legal treatment of an umm walad who kills both her master and a stranger. It addresses why qisas is not applicable against her for killing her master (due to the child's inheritance), how the liability converts to a monetary value, and the intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing that value. The analysis also covers the effect of payment with or without a court order on the discharge of liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the crime
Determine who the umm walad killed and who the heirs are. Note the presence of the umm walad's child from the master, who inherits the master.
2
Establish why qisas is not applicable for the master
Understand the reasoning: the child cannot claim qisas against a parent, so the claim for the master's death becomes purely financial (value).
This is a legal impediment (mani') not from the killer, so the claim converts to property.
3
Recognize the single value rule
Multiple jinaayaat (homicides) by an umm walad only necessitate one payment of her value.
This rule applies regardless of the number of victims or jinaayaat.
4
Analyze the division dispute
Compare Abu Hanifa's division into thirds and the Sahibayn's division into fourths. Identify which heirs get which proportion.
The text suggests the stranger's heirs get one-third under Abu Hanifa and one-fourth under the Sahibayn.
5
Understand the payment scenarios
Differentiate between payment with a judge's order (discharge) and without a judge's order (option for stranger's heirs to invalidate).
This affects whether the stranger's heirs can claim directly from the umm walad again.

Commentary
This passage expands on the rulings regarding umm walad (a slave girl who bears a child to her master) who commits multiple homicides. The key point is that her value (price) is treated as a single compensation due from her, regardless of how many killings she committed. This value is divided among the heirs of all victims. The text discusses two scenarios: (1) when she has no child from the master, and (2) when she has a child who inherits the master. The latter case affects qisas: if she kills her master, her child (the master's heir) cannot claim qisas against her because a child does not take qisas for a parent. Therefore, the claim becomes purely financial. The passage then addresses an objection: since qisas is not applicable, should the killing be treated as accidental (hader)? The response explains that qisas was initially due but dropped due to the child's inheritance right, so it converts to a monetary claim (value). The passage also discusses the rights of heirs of a third party (ajnabi) who is also killed by the umm walad. They can still claim qisas or delay for payment. If a guardian of the ajnabi pardons, half the value is due to the silent one. The division of the value between the master's heirs and the ajnabi's heirs is disputed: Abu Hanifa says thirds, the Sahibayn say fourths. Finally, the text discusses the effect of paying the value with or without a judge's order. If paid by court order, the umm walad's liability is discharged, and the ajnabi's heirs must pursue the master's heirs. If paid without court order, according to Abu Hanifa the ajnabi's heir has an option to accept or revert. This is a detailed intra-Hanafi discussion.

Confession of Debt from Unreceived Slave Price: Four Scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for each of the four scenarios of confession regarding an unreceived slave price and explain the reasoning based on agreement on entitlement.

Core Idea
A confession of debt is binding only if the confesse confirms entitlement; differing on the cause does not matter.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into a nuanced fiqh issue: when a person confesses to owing the price of a slave that he never received, is the confession binding? The ruling depends entirely on what the confesse (the alleged seller) affirms. Through four distinct scenarios, the passage illustrates the Hanafi principle that mutual agreement on the substance of entitlement (istihqaq) suffices, even if the parties differ on the underlying cause. This lesson will guide you through each scenario and its legal consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
First, list the four possible responses of the confesse when the confessor says 'I owe you the price of that specific slave I never received'. Note the difference between confirming the sale with delivery, claiming a different cause, denying the sale, and claiming a different subject matter.
The four scenarios are clearly delineated in al-Zayla'i's analysis.
2
Analyze the ruling for each scenario
For each scenario, determine whether the confession is binding (lazim) or not. Consider the presence or absence of mutual confirmation (tasāduq) and the condition of the slave's existence.
Scenarios 1 and 2 are binding; 3 and 4 are not (or lead to tahāluf).
3
Understand the reasoning
Explore the underlying reasoning: tasāduq establishes proof like visual certainty (thābit ʿiyānan). The purpose (maqsūd) is to secure the entitlement; the cause is irrelevant once the substance is agreed upon.
This is a key Hanafi principle: the cause of entitlement need not be consistent.
4
Contrast with the exception examples
The passage begins with examples of exceptions in rings, belts, and gowns. Identify the shared principle: if removal of the excepted item harms the confessor, he pays its value. This parallels the slave price scenarios where the existence/condition of the slave matters.
The exception rulings from al-Tatarkhaniyya set the stage for the later analysis.

When Claims of Bad Coins Are Accepted in Confessions
Determines when claims of bad coins are accepted in confessions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on claims of bad coins in debt confessions and distinguish when such claims are accepted (in ghasb and wadi'a) versus rejected (in debt).

Core Idea
In debt confessions, claims of bad coins are rejected; in ghasb and wadi'a, they are accepted.

When someone confesses to a debt, the law treats that confession as definitive. But what if the confessor later claims the money was counterfeit or of poor quality? The Hanafi school draws a sharp line: in pure monetary debts, such claims are not accepted; in cases of usurpation or deposit, they are. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of confession
Determine whether the confession is for a debt (dayn) or for usurpation/deposit (ghasb/wadi'a). This is the decisive factor.
Debt confessions create a monetary obligation; ghasb/wadi'a involve returning a specific property.
2
Apply the general rule for debt
If the confession is of a debt, a later claim that the coins were bad (zuyuf, nubhiraja, sutuqa) is rejected. The confessor must pay good coins as confessed.
The confession is binding on quantity and quality.
3
Recognize the exception for ghasb and wadi'a
In confessions of usurpation or deposit, the confessor is believed if he claims the coins were bad. This is because the obligation is to return the specific property, not an equivalent.
The same rule applies to mudaraba (partnership).
4
Note additional distinctions
If the claim is made 'connected' (mawsul) to the confession, it may be accepted in some cases (e.g., sutuqa when alive). After death, the heir's claim is not accepted. In sale confessions with good still in possession, both parties take oaths.
Connected means stated in the same sentence or immediately after.
5
Apply to a concrete scenario
Given a case like 'I owe you 1000 dirhams, but they were counterfeit', state the ruling and justify it using the principles above.
Practice makes the distinction automatic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that gift revocation is only valid by mutual consent or a judge's decree, and identify the rationale and examples.

Core Idea
A donor cannot unilaterally revoke a gift; consent of the donee or a judge's order is necessary.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the text: revocation of a gift must occur either with the agreement of both parties or through a judicial decision. The reason is the scholarly disagreement on the matter, making a mutual or authoritative resolution necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The matn states revocation is only valid by mutual consent or judge's decree. Note that this is a procedural condition, not a substantive right.
2
Understand the reason
The commentator explains that because scholars differ on the permissibility of revocation, a definitive mechanism (mutual agreement or judicial order) is needed to avoid dispute.
The reason is talil based on ikhtilaf.

If the donee frees a slave before the judge's decree, the emancipation is valid and the donor cannot reclaim the slave. If the item perishes in the donee's hands without transgression, the donee is not liable.

Commentary
This passage discusses the procedure and consequences of revoking a gift. The matn states that revocation is only valid by mutual consent (both parties agree) or by a judge's decree. If the revocation was by judge, the heirs cannot recover from the donor. Additionally, if the donor consumes the gifted item after delivery, he must guarantee it. If he frees a slave that was gifted, the emancipation is valid. Finally, the concept of faskh min al-asl is clarified: it means the contract ceases to have effect for the future, but does not undo past effects completely (e.g., not to the extent of returning separate increments like offspring already born).

Detailed explanation of the effects of a valid revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of faskh min al-asl in gift revocation and distinguish between its past and future effects.

Core Idea
Faskh min al-asl annuls the gift contract for the future but does not reverse all past ownership effects.

This lesson delves into the consequences of a valid revocation. The passage clarifies that revocation is a rescission from the root (faskh min al-asl), meaning the contract has no effect moving forward. However, this does not reverse past ownership of separate increments. Additionally, special rules apply when a terminally ill person revokes a gift.

Guided Path
1
Understand faskh min al-asl
The term means the contract is annulled from its root, but only for the future. Past effects, such as ownership of separate increments, remain unaffected.
This is a key nuance: not a complete nullification ab initio.
2
Apply the principle
Consequences: no need for the donor to take possession again, revocation is valid even in an undivided share (shai').
3
Distinguish from return due to defect
Return due to defect (khiyar al-ayb) is not a rescission from the root; it addresses a defect in the item, not the contract itself.
The donor can return the gift to his seller only because the gift contract was permissive.
4
Analyze the sick person's case
If a terminally ill person revokes a gift, it is considered from the one-third of his estate. If revoked by a judge, the heirs cannot reclaim from the donor.
This is a special rule for deathbed transactions.
5
Note liability and emancipation
If the donor consumes the gifted item after revocation, he must guarantee it. If he frees a slave that was gifted, the emancipation is valid.
These show the donor's ownership is revived upon valid revocation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the procedure and consequences of revoking a gift. The matn states that revocation is only valid by mutual consent (both parties agree) or by a judge's decree. The reason is that scholars differ on the permissibility of revocation, so to avoid dispute, it must be settled by mutual agreement or judicial ruling. Examples: if the gift was a slave and the donor frees him before the judge's decree, the emancipation is effective (so revocation cannot undo it). If the donee prevents the donor from taking back the item and it perishes, the donee is not liable because he still has ownership. However, if he prevents after the donor's request, that is transgression and he becomes liable. Revocation, whether by consent or judge, is considered a rescission from the origin (faskh min al-asl), meaning the contract is annulled from the start. Therefore, the donor does not need to take possession again (no new qabd required) and revocation is valid even in a share (shai'). The donor may even return the item to his original seller (if he had bought it) because the gift contract was permissible (ja'iz) and gave him a right to rescind; this is equivalent to the right of rescission in a sale. This differs from returning a defective item (khiyar al-ayb) after possession, where the buyer cannot return it to the original seller without a judge. The passage also covers the sick person's revocation: if a terminally ill person revokes a gift, it is considered from his one-third (for inheritance purposes). If the revocation was by judge, the heirs cannot recover from the donor. Additionally, if the donor consumes the gifted item after delivery, he must guarantee it. If he frees a slave that was gifted, the emancipation is valid. Finally, the concept of faskh min al-asl is clarified: it means the contract ceases to have effect for the future, but does not undo past effects completely (e.g., not to the extent of returning separate increments like offspring already born).

Key Terms
فسخ من الأصل (faskh min al-aṣl): Rescission from the root; annulment of the contract ab initio, but only for future effects.
شائع (shā'iʿ): An undivided share in a common property; revocation is valid even if the gifted item is a share.
