Testimony in Marriage: Dispute on Validity When Witnesses Differ on Dowry Amount
Intra-Hanafi dispute on marriage validity when witnesses differ on dowry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the validity of marriage when witnesses disagree on the dowry amount and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Disagreement on a subsidiary term does not invalidate the marriage; the lesser agreed amount is taken.

When two witnesses testify about a marriage but differ on the dowry amount, does the testimony become invalid? In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa rules the marriage valid by istihsan, while the Sahibayn deem it invalid by analogy to sale. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi distinction between essential and subsidiary elements in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed element
The dispute is over the validity of the marriage contract when two witnesses agree on the marriage itself but disagree on the amount of the dower (mahr).
The dower is considered subsidiary (tabi') in marriage.
2
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa holds that the marriage is valid with the lesser amount of dowry, using istihsan (juristic preference).
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) view.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) hold that the marriage is invalid (batil), by strict qiyas to a sale contract.
They analogize the dower to the price in sale.
4
Analyze the illah (rationale)
Abu Hanifa: the dower is subsidiary (tabi'), so disagreement on a subsidiary does not affect the principal contract. Sahibayn: the dower is an essential term like price; disagreement invalidates the contract.
The difference hinges on whether the dower is considered essential or subsidiary.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the marriage stands and the wife receives the lesser amount. Under the Sahibayn, the marriage is void and must be recontracted.
In practice, fatwa is given on Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage addresses the validity of marriage when witnesses disagree on the dowry amount. In the example, one witness says the dowry was one thousand, the other says two thousand. The ruling: Abu Hanifa says the marriage is valid with the lesser amount (one thousand) by istihsan (juristic preference). The two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the marriage is invalid, just like in sale when witnesses disagree on the price. Their reasoning: marriage, like sale, is a contract, and disagreement in the essential term (the dowry) invalidates the contract. Abu Hanifa's reasoning: in marriage, the dower is subsidiary to the main purpose of the contract, which is permissibility and pairing. The primary subject is not disputed. Therefore, the marriage is valid, but since a dower is obligatory, the lesser amount is taken because both witnesses agree on it. The passage also notes that claiming the lesser amount does not discredit a witness, as the lesser could be the original named amount, and the greater could have been added later. Additionally, there is a difference of opinion on who the claimant is: if the husband is the claimant, some say there is consensus that the marriage is invalid because the husband's only interest is the contract, while the wife may seek money. The most correct view is that the ruling applies regardless of who is claimant.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): A Hanafi method of juristic preference; here, Abu Hanifa prefers the validity of marriage despite disagreement on the subsidiary dower, using istihsan over strict qiyas.
تبع (tabi'): Subsidiary or secondary; the dower is considered subsidiary to the main purpose of marriage.

Retraction from Testimony on Marriage and Dower
Ruling on witnesses retracting testimony about marriage and receipt of dower

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi witnesses retracting testimony about marriage and receipt of dower guarantee mahr al-mithl rather than the named dower, and evaluate the internal Hanafi dispute regarding the basis of liability.

Core Idea
Witnesses retracting testimony on marriage and dower compensate with mahr al-mithl, but the legal basis is debated within the Hanafi school.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced case from the Hanafi school where witnesses retract their testimony after a marriage and dower judgment. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents the ruling and then critiques an earlier explanation, offering a refined reasoning. You will learn not only the practical outcome but also how later Hanafi jurists reconciled conflicting principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
Read the scenario: witnesses testify that a woman married for 1000 with mahr al-mithl = 500, and that she received the 1000. She denies. Judge rules. They retract. The ruling is they pay mahr al-mithl (500) not the named dower (1000).
The woman's denial is key: she does not claim the named dower.
2
Examine the initial reasoning from al-Tahrir
Al-Tahrir explains that the testimony destroyed the sexual right (bud'), so witnesses pay its value (mahr al-mithl). This is because the judgment removed her ownership of the marital exchange, not the dower.
3
Critique from the madhhab's principle
The commentator objects: in the Hanafi school, benefits of the sexual right are not compensable when destroyed, only when taken possession of by the husband. So according to the madhhab, nothing should be owed.
This objection highlights a tension with established Hanafi rules.
4
Understand the author's resolution
The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq reconciles: the guarantee is not for the sexual right but for the dower itself. The testimony of receipt destroyed her right to the dower. Since she denies everything, she does not claim the named dower, so she gets mahr al-mithl.
This preserves the madhhab's principle while explaining the liability.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific case where witnesses testify against a woman regarding her marriage and receipt of dower. The ruling is a legal statement with proof. The author first presents the ruling from al-Tahrir: witnesses who testify that a woman married for 1000 (while her proper dower is 500) and that she received the 1000, and she denies everything, and the judge rules based on their testimony, then they retract — they must pay the woman her mahr al-mithl (500), not the named dower (1000). The reasoning is that the judgment for marriage with receipt of dower effectively transfers ownership of the woman's marital exchange (the sexual right) to the husband, not the dower amount itself. Since the named dower was never obliged by the court (because it was already received), the testimony of receipt does not destroy the named dower but destroys the sexual right, so they guarantee its value (mahr al-mithl). However, the author criticizes this from another Hanafi position: the benefits of the sexual right are not compensable when destroyed, only when taken possession of by the husband. So the madhhab's requirement is that nothing should be owed. The author then offers his own resolution: the guarantee is not for the sexual right but for the dower itself, since the witnesses testified to her receipt. The amount they pay is mahr al-mithl because the woman, by denying everything, does not claim the named dower, so she is entitled to the proper dower. He illustrates with a variation: if they had not testified to receipt initially but later did, and then retracted both, they would owe the full 1000. This shows the nuanced understanding of liability based on what exactly the testimony destroyed.

Key Terms
مهر المثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower appropriate for a woman of her status, determined by circumstances; used when the named dower is not claimed or is invalid.
بضع (bud'): The sexual right or marital exchange that the woman gives the husband in return for the dower; it is a right that can be owned.
إتلاف (itlāf): Destruction; legal concept of causing loss to someone's property or right, giving rise to liability for compensation.

Attribution in Agency: Marriage, Divorce, and Dropping of Rights
Rules for phrasing agency in marriage, divorce, khul', and compromise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct phrasing for an agent in marriage, divorce, khul', and sulh, and identify when attribution to the principal is required.

Core Idea
In contracts that drop rights (isqat), the agent must attribute the act to himself but mention the principal; in ownership transfers like marriage, the agent for the owner mentions the principal.

This lesson from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains how agents should phrase contracts to ensure the legal effect goes to the principal. The key distinction is between contracts of isqat (dropping rights) and tamlik (ownership transfer). We will apply these rules to marriage, divorce, khul', and compromise (sulh).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of transaction
Determine if the transaction is a dropping of rights (isqat) like divorce, khul', or compromise for murder, or an ownership transfer (tamlik) like marriage or emancipation.
2
Determine the side of the agent
Is the agent appointed by the person who owns the right (the husband in divorce, the wife in marriage) or the other party? This affects the phrasing.
3
Apply the rule for isqat transactions
For isqat, the agent attributes the action to himself but mentions the principal. E.g., the husband's agent in khul' says 'I gave khul' to so-and-so for a thousand.'
4
Apply the rule for tamlik transactions
For tamlik, if the agent is from the owner's side, he may act as an original party (asil) and speak in first person without mentioning the principal. If from the other side, he must act as a messenger.
5
Practice with examples from the text
Work through the examples: husband's agent in marriage says 'Zawwajtu bintaka li-fulan'; wife's agent says 'Zawwajtu'; agent in divorce says 'Tallaqtu fulanatan bi-alf'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Guardianship over a Muslim minor requires capability (freedom) and compassion (Islam); slavery and disbelief negate both.

In this passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq, the author establishes a foundational principle of Hanafi guardianship law: only a free Muslim can be a guardian over a free Muslim minor. The ruling is supported by a rational proof that appeals to the very nature of guardianship as a relationship requiring both ability and care. We will dissect this reasoning and see how it applies to slaves, disbelievers, and even apostates.

The passage notes that if an apostate performs guardianship, it is suspended (mauquf) by consensus. If he returns to Islam, it becomes valid; if he dies on apostasy, it is void. This illustrates that the key factor is the status at the time of the action's finality.

The Authority of the Mother’s Executor and the Later Fatwa on Necessity
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sale of minor's property at less than value with necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of guardians for a minor's property and evaluate the difference between the Zahir al-Riwayah position and the later mufta bihi position regarding sale at a loss due to necessity.

Core Idea
The weakest of the executors in the strongest situation is like the strongest in the weakest situation — but necessity can override the strict rule.

This lesson delves into the detailed hierarchy of guardians over a minor's property and a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether the mother's executor (or other weak guardians) can sell the minor's property below market value under certain urgent circumstances. The Zahir al-Riwayah strictly requires selling at the similar value, but later scholars permitted sale at half value or with excuses, and this later view is the operative fatwa. We will trace the hierarchy, understand the principle that determines authority, and see how the need for practical flexibility led to a refined ruling.

Guided Path
1
List the hierarchy of guardians
From the text: the hierarchy for a minor’s property is: father → his executor → executor of that executor → grandfather → his executor → judge → judge’s appointee. The mother’s executor is not included and has limited authority.
This hierarchy is from Khazanat al-Muftin and is standard in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the mother's executor's limited authority
When higher guardians are absent, the mother’s executor can preserve the property, sell movable items (but not real estate), and purchase for trade. He cannot sell real estate even if beneficial.
The principle: 'the weakest executor in the strongest situation is like the strongest in the weakest situation'.
3
State the Zahir al-Riwayah position
According to the early authorities (salaf), any sale of the minor’s property must be at a price similar to its market value (bi mithl al-qima). Selling below that is not permitted.
Al-Hulwani attributes this to the salaf.
4
Explain the later scholars' modification
Later scholars (muta'akhkhirin) permitted sale at half the value or less when there is a necessity, such as fear of loss, oppression, or the property being in danger of ruin. This is documented in al-Durar and al-Ashbah.
The necessity must be genuine; not every inconvenience qualifies.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The later view is the relied-upon position for fatwa. Al-Waqi'at states 'wa bihi yufta' (and by it fatwa is given), and Abu al-Su'ud confirms this in his hashya on Miskin.
The earlier view remains valid but is not the operative fatwa.

Evidence in Mahr Disputes: When One or Both Spouses Prove
Ruling on mahr dispute evidence hierarchy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for resolving mahr disputes when one or both spouses have evidence, and identify when the wife's or husband's evidence is preferred based on mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
The wife's evidence prevails when both bring proof, unless mahr al-mithl supports the husband's claim for a reduction.

In mahr disputes, evidence is the first step. The Hanafi school has detailed rules for whose evidence is accepted when both spouses present proof. The key is the relationship between the claims and the proper dower (mahr al-mithl). This lesson unpacks these rules and their exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three evidence scenarios
Recognize that the ruling covers three cases: only one spouse has evidence, both have evidence, or neither has evidence. This frames the entire procedure.
The general rule is: judgment for the one who proves.
2
Understand the wife's preference when both prove
When both have evidence, the default is that the wife's evidence takes precedence because she proves an increase in the mahr.
This is the entry rule – it will be qualified later.
3
Apply the condition based on mahr al-mithl
If mahr al-mithl (proper dower) supports the husband's claim (i.e., is equal to or less than his claim), then the wife's evidence is preferred. If mahr al-mithl supports the wife's claim, the husband's evidence is preferred because it proves a reduction.
If mahr al-mithl is between the two claims, neither evidence prevails – set-off (tehatur) and mahr al-mithl is applied.
4
Distinguish differences in amount vs. species
The same rules apply whether the dispute is about the amount (e.g., 1000 vs 2000) or the type (e.g., a slave vs. a slave girl). An exception: if the specified item is unique and mahr al-mithl equals its value, the wife gets value, not the item itself.
Cite the slave girl example from al-Zahiriyya and al-Hidaya.
5
Note special cases after divorce before consummation
If divorce occurs before consummation and the mahr is disputed, the wife gets half of what the husband claimed, or compensation (mut'a) in the slave/slave girl case unless they agree otherwise.
These are reported from al-Zahiriyya.

Core Idea

Ruling on Dispute Between Spouses Regarding Mahr
Explains the rules when spouses differ on dower amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving a mahr dispute between spouses, including the priority of evidence, the procedure of mutual oath (tahāluf), and the imposition of mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
In a mahr dispute, evidence rules first; if none, mutual oath voids the specified dower but not the marriage; mahr al-mithl then applies.

When spouses disagree on the mahr (dower) amount or type, the Hanafi school provides a structured resolution process. This lesson unpacks the passage from al-Bahr al-Rāʾiq which details the steps: judgment for the party with evidence, preference for the wife if both have evidence, and the procedure when neither can prove. The critical point is that mutual oath (tahāluf) does not annul the marriage; it merely voids the specified dower, leading to mahr al-mithl (dower of the like). We will also examine an intra-Hanafi dispute on the order of oaths and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
Evidence Priority
Identify the scenario where one spouse has evidence; judgment goes to that spouse. If both have evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred because she claims an increase (contrary to the default).
This is the first layer of dispute resolution.
2
Mutual Oath (Tahāluf)
When neither spouse can provide evidence, they perform mutual oaths. The husband swears first because the first submission (payment) is upon him. The marriage remains valid; only the specified dower is voided.
Tahāluf in marriage differs from tahāluf in sale, which invalidates the contract.
3
Imposition of Mahr al-Mithl
After the oaths, the specified dower is voided, and the wife receives mahr al-mithl, the dower of women of similar status. The final amount is determined by the mean of the two claims unless one is proven closer.
Mahr al-mithl ensures fairness without requiring specific evidence.
4
Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Note the disagreement: al-Karkhi holds that tahāluf precedes arbitration (the relied-upon view), while al-Razi's derivation and Abu Yusuf prefer arbitration first.
The muftā bihī (operative ruling) is tahāluf first per al-Karkhi.

Effect of Wife's Admission of Husband's Purchase
Exception to general rule: wife's admission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a wife's admission of her husband's purchase establishes his ownership and requires evidence for any claimed transfer to her.

Core Idea
A wife's admission of her husband's purchase defeats her claim; transfer requires evidence.

This lesson focuses on a specific exception: if the wife admits her husband purchased the disputed item, her claim to ownership is invalid. She must provide proof of a later transfer (e.g., gift) to regain ownership. Mere use or permission is insufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand the default rule: in a dispute between spouses over household goods, the husband's word is accepted for items suitable for both. The wife's admission changes this.
The general rule is that goods are presumed for the husband if they suit both.
2
Apply the effect of admission
If the wife admits the husband purchased the item, she has acknowledged his ownership. She cannot later claim transfer to herself without evidence.
Admission (iqrar) is binding against the admitter.
3
Evaluate evidence of transfer
The wife must produce clear evidence (beyyina) of a gift or sale from husband to her. Her enjoyment and his consent do not constitute proof.
This counters common misconceptions among women and commoners.
4
Distinguish from the case of her purchase
If she claims she bought it from him, the same rule applies: her admission of his purchase stands, and she needs evidence of the sale.
Al-Khaniya confirms this symmetry.

Exceptions for father-son, artisans, landlord-tenant, and goods outside the house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve disputes between father-son, artisans, landlord-tenant, and spouses over goods not in the house using the specific Hanafi rules from the passage.

Core Idea
Dependency determines father-son ownership; artisans share equally; tenant's word binds landlord; goods outside the house split as strangers.

This lesson covers four more exceptions to the general spousal dispute rule: (1) father-son disputes where dependency decides ownership, (2) disputes between artisans over tools, (3) landlord-tenant disputes over goods in the rental property, and (4) spouses disputing over goods not in the house. Each has a distinct rule reflecting different presumptions of possession and use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of dispute
Determine if the dispute is between father and son, two artisans, landlord and tenant, or spouses over goods not in the house.
Each type has a unique rule.
2
Apply the father-son rule
If the father is dependent (in the son's house), goods belong to the son; if the son is dependent in the father's house, goods belong to the father. This is based on possession and control.
Abu Yusuf's view is upheld in al-Khaniya; Muhammad has a different detail for a married daughter living with father.

Apply the artisan rule
If two artisans (e.g., shoemaker and perfumer) dispute over tools that could belong to either profession, the tools are divided equally. Suitability is not a factor because they may be for personal use or resale.

4

If landlord and tenant dispute over goods in the rented house, the tenant's word is accepted because the house is in his possession (sukna).
Possession due to lease creates presumption.
5

If spouses dispute over goods that are not in the house (e.g., in a store) and both have possession, they are treated as strangers and the goods are divided equally.
The default spousal rule only applies to goods in the house.

Rulings on Two Men Claiming Marriage to the Same Woman
Ruling on conflicting marriage claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for two men claiming marriage to the same woman with evidence, including the rule of tahaatur, consequences for mahr, inheritance, lineage, and the role of dating.

Core Idea
Equal evidence in marriage claims cancels out; but if both have dates, the earlier one wins, and even a prior judgment can be overturned.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a complex scenario in family law: two men each claim to have married the same woman, both with valid evidence. The Hanafi school has specific rules that balance the impossibility of shared marriage with the need to protect rights. We will walk through the main scenarios and their rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule: tahaatur
When both claimants provide evidence with no preference (no dates, or equal dates), the claims cancel each other because the woman cannot be married to two simultaneously.
This is unlike property claims where conflicting evidence can lead to division.
2
Apply consequences of tahaatur
Before consummation, no mahr is due. If the woman dies, each pays half the mahr and inheritance is split as if one husband. A child born after tahaatur has lineage to both and inherits from each as a full son.
The text in Munyat al-Mufti states 'acknowledgement and hand are not considered' in the initial tahaatur.
3
Consider the role of dates
If one claimant has an earlier date, his claim prevails exclusively. Even if a judgment was already given, a later claim with an earlier date can overturn it.
Marriage judgments, like freedom and lineage, are not absolutely final; they can be revisited with earlier proof.
4
Examine the effect of the woman's acknowledgement
If the woman acknowledges one claimant before evidence is brought, she is his wife. But if the other brings stronger evidence, that evidence outweighs her acknowledgement. After tahaatur, a subsequent acknowledgement evidence can break the tie.
Hanafi: evidence (bayyina) is stronger than iqrar, but after tahaatur, a new iqrar can tip the scale.
5
Understand the special finality in marriage
Unlike property, a marriage judgment can be invalidated if a later claimant provides evidence with an earlier date. This mirrors rules for freedom, lineage, and clientage.
This exception ensures that substantive truth prevails over procedural finality in matters of personal status.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the situation where two men each claim a woman as their wife. The commentator explains that the preferred factor for determining which husband she belongs to is her affirmation or the precedence of his evidence. However, this is nuanced: when both men have evidence with no dates, her affirmation decides. If one man's evidence predates, his claim prevails regardless of her affirmation. The passage establishes a hierarchy: (1) precedence of evidence date, (2) physical possession (hand) of the woman by one claimant, (3) consummation (dakhul), (4) her formal affirmation (iqrar), and (5) simply having a date without affirmation. Sub-cases are discussed: if she affirms one but the other has possession, possession overrides. If she marries herself to Zaid after Amr, Abu Yusuf says she belongs to Zaid (later marriage), and this is the fatwa, while Muhammad says the earlier marriage (to Amr) is valid. The passage concludes by comparing this to a man marrying two sisters in sequence.

Wife Demanding Money for Reconciliation
Ruling on permissibility of wife demanding money during mediation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a wife demanding money from her husband during marital mediation is permissible according to the Hanafi school and identify the basis of her right.

Core Idea
Demanding money in exchange for reconciliation is permissible because she has a prior right (mahr) against her husband.

Marital disputes often lead to mediation. A question arises: may the wife condition reconciliation on payment of money? This passage from Majmu' al-Nawazil clarifies that she may, because she holds a legal right (haqq) against her husband from the dower (mahr) and other obligations. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between demanding one's due and coercion without right.

Gift Before and After Marriage: Revocation Rules
Ruling on revocation when gift precedes or follows marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revocation of a gift when the donor marries the donee after the gift, and distinguish it from the case where the gift follows marriage.

Core Idea
Gift before marriage is revocable; gift after marriage is not — the key is marital status at the time of gift.

This lesson extracts a precise ruling from the Hanafi fiqh regarding gifts exchanged between a man and a woman who later marry. The timing of the gift relative to the marriage determines whether the donor can revoke it. We also examine a related principle from the Fatawa of Qadikhan concerning a wife's claim of coercion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First scenario: a gift is given before marriage. Second scenario: a gift is given after marriage (i.e., when the donee is already the spouse).
2
State the ruling for each
For the first, revocation is allowed; for the second, revocation is not allowed. The critical factor is the marital status at the time of the gift.
3
Understand the legal basis
The prohibition on revocation when the donee is a spouse is based on the special relationship of marriage. Even if the donor divorces the spouse later, the inability to revoke remains.
4
Apply to a related case
If a wife claims she was coerced into giving a gift to her husband, her claim is heard. This is a separate procedural ruling that protects against coercion.
5
Note the distinction from other areas
The author points out that the rules for gift revocation differ from those for testimony and will between spouses, which have different legal bases.

Commentary
This passage provides two separate rulings related to marriage and gift revocation. First, from the Fatawa of Qadikhan: if a wife claims she was coerced into gifting something to her husband, her claim is accepted by the court. This protects a wife from potential coercion. Second, the commentary on the matn addresses the scenario of gift and marriage. The ruling is: if a man gives a gift to a woman and thereafter marries her, he may revoke the gift because at the time of the gift she was a non-mahram (stranger). Conversely, if he marries her first and then gives her a gift, he cannot revoke because at the time of the gift she was already his wife, and a husband cannot revoke a gift to his wife. This distinction is based on the marital status at the moment the gift was made. Even if he later divorces her, the inability to revoke remains. The author notes that this principle differs from similar principles in zakat distribution, such as testimony and will, which are treated differently.
