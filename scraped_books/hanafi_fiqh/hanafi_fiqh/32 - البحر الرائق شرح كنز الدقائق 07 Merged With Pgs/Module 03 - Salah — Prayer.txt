Delegation by the Jumu'ah Imam: Implicit Permission and Necessity
Compares delegation in Jumu'ah vs. judging

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the Jumu'ah imam's delegation without explicit permission, explain the proof based on implicit permission (idhn dilalah) and necessity, and apply the conditions regarding when delegation is valid.

Core Idea
The Jumu'ah imam can delegate without explicit permission because Jumu'ah is time-sensitive and cannot be delayed.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: why the Jumu'ah imam can appoint a substitute without the sultan's explicit permission, whereas a judge cannot. The commentary from Bahr al-Raiq develops this ruling by analyzing the nature of Jumu'ah as a time-bound obligation and introducing the concept of implicit permission (idhn dilalah). We will explore the proof, the intra-madhhab dispute with Mulla Khusraw, and the precise conditions that affect the validity of delegation.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and the analogy
Identify the ruling: the Jumu'ah imam may delegate without explicit permission. Contrast with the judge who needs explicit permission. Understand the analogy: Jumu'ah is time-sensitive; judging can be delayed.
The base text phrase 'بخلاف المأمور في الجمعة' signals the comparison.
2
Identify the proof: implicit permission (idhn dilalah)
The passage argues that the sultan's original appointment implies permission to delegate, because obstacles (like hadath) may arise and Jumu'ah cannot be delayed. This is a form of qiyas based on necessity.
Imam al-A'zam delegated the function, not just the person.
3
Note the dispute with Mulla Khusraw
Mulla Khusraw holds that delegation requires explicit permission for each instance. The Hanafi majority rejects this, citing the principle that permission for the first establishment covers subsequent delegations.
Ibn Kamal Pasha's rebuttal emphasizes that the sermon (khutbah) is the part requiring permission, not the prayer.
4
Apply the conditions for valid delegation
If the khatib becomes impure after the sermon but before prayer, he can only delegate to one who attended the sermon. If he is already in prayer, he can delegate to one who did not attend the sermon, because the prayer is already valid by his own sermon.
The khutbah is a condition (shart) for Jumu'ah, so the substitute must have heard it if the original khatib did not yet pray.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute affects the validity of Jumu'ah led by a substitute without permission. According to the majority view, it is valid; according to Mulla Khusraw, it would be invalid.
The author (and later fatwa) relies on the majority view due to custom and necessity.

Commentary
This passage explains the difference between a judge and the imam of Jumu'ah regarding delegation. The base text states 'unlike the one appointed in Jumu'ah' (meaning the khatib). The commentary clarifies that the Jumu'ah imam can delegate even without explicit permission because the greatest Imam (caliph) implicitly granted that when he appointed him, due to the time-sensitive nature of Jumu'ah. Obstacles like impurity can occur suddenly, and waiting for permission would cause the prayer to be missed. In contrast, judicial matters can be delayed. The passage then discusses various opinions: some early texts suggest the khatib can delegate in any circumstance (illness, travel), while others require permission. The author defends the view that permission is not required for every new khatib if the first permission was given for the mosque. It also addresses the condition: if the khatib becomes impure after the sermon but before prayer, he can only delegate to one who attended the sermon because the sermon is a condition for the validity of Jumu'ah. If he is in prayer, he can delegate to one who missed the sermon because the prayer is already valid due to the original imam's sermon. Further discussion includes a rebuttal of Mulla Khusraw's view by Ibn Kamal Pasha, emphasizing that the sermon is the part requiring permission, not the prayer itself. The passage ends with an objection about someone who invalidates his prayer and then delegates to someone who did not attend the sermon.

Key Terms
الإمام الأعظم (al-Imam al-A'zam): The supreme ruler (caliph or sultan) who delegates authority for Jumu'ah.
إذن دلالة (idhn dilalah): Implicit permission inferred from the circumstances, as opposed to explicit verbal permission.
الخطبة (al-khutbah): The sermon delivered before the Jumu'ah prayer, which is a condition for its validity.
استخلاف (istikhlaaf): Delegation or appointment of a substitute to lead the prayer or deliver the sermon.

The Khatib's Authority to Appoint a Substitute for Jumu'ah
Commentary expanding on the khatib's substitution authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the khatib's authority to appoint a substitute for Jumu'ah and distinguish it from the judge's authority, citing the principle of darurah.

Core Idea
Necessity (darurah) allows the khatib to appoint a substitute without explicit permission for time-sensitive Jumu'ah.

In the Hanafi school, the khatib (Friday sermon giver) holds a unique authority to appoint a substitute to lead Jumu'ah without needing explicit permission from the ruling authority. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the rationale: Jumu'ah is time-bound and cannot be delayed, so the original delegation implicitly includes the power to substitute when necessity arises. This distinguishes the khatib from a judge, who must obtain explicit permission. We will explore the proof, the intra-madhhab dispute with Mulla Khusraw, and the practical conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the khatib appointing a substitute to deliver the sermon and lead the Jumu'ah prayer. The ruling is jā'iz (permissible) without explicit permission from the sultan.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is based on necessity (darurah) and the implicit nature of the original delegation. The text cites al-Mi'raj and Furūq al-Karābīsī to distinguish between the judge and the imam.
The dalil is aqli (rational) based on the principle that what is necessary for an obligation is itself obligatory.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The necessity is that Jumu'ah must be performed within its fixed time; if the khatib becomes unable (e.g., due to hadath, illness, travel), waiting for permission would cause the prayer to be missed. Therefore, the original permission from the sultan implicitly includes authority to substitute.
This is an istidlal bi al-darurah (reasoning by necessity).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Mulla Khusraw opposed the ruling, arguing by qiyas with a judge that the khatib cannot delegate without explicit permission. The text refutes this by showing the difference: Jumu'ah is time-sensitive; judgment is not.
The Hanafi response emphasizes the distinction based on time-sensitivity.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Ibn Kamal Pasha and later scholars (al-Ramli, al-Ghazzi) affirmed the majority view. The mufta bihi is that the khatib may appoint a substitute without explicit permission.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwa works).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the khatib's authority. The main ruling is that the khatib (Friday sermon giver) is permitted to appoint a substitute to lead the Jumu'ah prayer without needing explicit permission from the ruling authority, unlike a judge who generally cannot delegate his duties without permission. The reasoning is based on necessity (darurah): the Jumu'ah prayer is time-sensitive and must be performed within its fixed time. If the khatib becomes unable to lead (e.g., due to minor impurity or illness), waiting for permission from the sultan would cause the prayer to be missed. Therefore, the original delegation from the sultan implicitly includes the authority to appoint substitutes. The author cites al-Mi'raj and al-Karabisi's Furūq to establish the distinction between the judge and the imam. He then critiques the view of Mulla Khusraw, who held that the khatib cannot appoint a substitute without explicit permission. Ibn Kamal Pasha refutes this, arguing that the sultan's initial permission for establishing Jumu'ah covers subsequent substitutions. The passage also discusses conditions: if the khatib breaks his wudu after the sermon but before prayer, he can only appoint someone who heard the sermon (since the sermon is a condition for validity). If he breaks it after starting the prayer, he can appoint anyone, even someone who didn't hear the sermon, because the prayer was validly initiated.

الاستخلاف (al-istikhāf): Appointing a substitute to lead the prayer or deliver the sermon.
الضَّرُورَة (al-ḍarūrah): A pressing need that would cause loss of the Jumu'ah obligation if not addressed.
