Scope of Arbitration: Valid Only Where Rights of Servants Predominate
Explaining conditions for arbitration and who can serve

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid arbitration (tahkim) in Hanafi fiqh and explain why it is invalid in qisas and hudud.

Core Idea
Arbitration is valid only where the rights of servants predominate; it is invalid in hudud and qisas because of Allah's rights.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores the boundary of private arbitration (tahkim). While arbitration allows parties to choose a decision-maker, not every dispute can be arbitrated. The Hanafi school carefully delineates between rights of Allah (huquq Allah) and rights of servants (huquq al-‘ibad). Understanding this distinction is key to applying the rules of tahkim correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scope: The ‘Aqila Restriction
The passage begins by restricting arbitration to cases involving the 'aqila (blood-money payers). If the killer confesses, the arbitrator’s judgment is effective only if the blood money is less than what the 'aqila bears. This shows that arbitration is tied to financial liability, not to fixed punishments.
The 'aqila is the clan traditionally responsible for paying diyah on behalf of a member who commits accidental homicide.
2
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Qisas
Al-Khassaf prohibits arbitration in qisas; al-Muhit permits it. The correct view (as in Fath al-Qadeer) is prohibition. The passage explains why: qisas combines the rights of Allah and the servant, making it similar to hudud.
The proof for likening qisas to hudud: women's testimony is excluded in qisas, and a judge may not delegate jurisdiction via letter in qisas cases.
3
Determine Who Can Serve as Arbitrator
The text states that a woman or a sinner can serve as arbitrator because they are qualified to judge. However, it is preferable to avoid a sinful arbitrator. Two arbitrators must agree on the judgment; if they disagree, the ruling is void.
The example from al-Khassaf: a husband says 'You are forbidden to me' intending less than three divorces; two arbitrators give different rulings – the arbitration fails.
4
Generalize to All Hudud
The passage concludes that arbitration is invalid in all hadd punishments, including qadhf (slander). Al-Sarakhsi’s view that qadhf could be arbitrated is declared weak.
The text from al-Walwaljiya: 'The correct view is that it is not valid in all hudud.' The exception 'other than hadd' in the matn covers matters of marriage, divorce, and suspended oaths.
5
Examine the Effect of Delegation by an Arbitrator
If one arbitrator delegates the judgment to another person (e.g., the other party’s lawyer), he leaves the arbitral role and becomes a party, thus losing witness capacity. If he appoints someone whose testimony is disqualified, the delegation is invalid.
This condition ensures the arbitrator remains neutral and capable of witnessing.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter on Arbitration (Tahkim) in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. It begins by establishing that the arbitration is restricted to cases involving the 'aqila (the clan liable for blood money) because if the case concerns the killer himself, the arbitrator's judgment is effective only under certain conditions. The text then discusses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether arbitration is permissible in qisas (retaliation) cases. The correct position, according to al-Khassaf and affirmed by al-Marghinani in Fath al-Qadeer, is that it is not permissible. The opposing view from al-Muhit (that it is allowed because qisas is a right of the servant) is rejected as weak both in transmission (riwayatan) and in reasoning (dirayatan). The reasoning is that qisas combines both the rights of Allah and the rights of the servant, making it similar to hudud. The passage then clarifies that the matn's phrase 'if he is considered a judge' permits the appointment of a woman or a sinner as arbitrator, as they are qualified to judge, though it is preferable to avoid a sinner. A crucial condition is that when two arbitrators are appointed, their judgment must be unanimous; if they disagree, the judgment is void. This is illustrated with an example from al-Khassaf concerning a husband's ambiguous statement. Furthermore, an arbitrator cannot delegate the judgment to another person in a way that compromises his role. Finally, the text confirms that arbitration is not valid in any hadd punishment, including qadhf (slander), and that the matn's exception 'other than hadd' covers all ijtihad-based matters like marriage, divorce, and suspended oaths.

Key Terms
تحكيم (tahkim): The appointment of a private arbitrator by two disputing parties to judge between them.
محكم (muhakkam): An arbitrator appointed by the parties; considered like a judge for those parties.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury; a legal right involving both divine and human aspects.
حدود (hudud): Fixed punishments for crimes against Allah, such as theft and zina; not subject to arbitration.

When to Believe a Judge's Hadd Judgment
Fiqh ruling on accepting a judge's hadd judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge's statement about a hadd penalty is accepted in the Hanafi school, and explain the evolution from al-Maturidi's view to Imam Muhammad's retracted position.

Core Idea
A judge's bare statement is not enough for hadd: later Hanafis require evidence or a just witness due to corruption.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines the reliability of a judge's report that he has issued a hadd punishment. The issue touches on the tension between the general obligation to obey those in authority (Quran 4:59) and the need to guard against error and corruption in the judiciary. The Hanafi school's position evolved from a trusting stance to a stricter one, reflecting changing social realities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling and its basis
According to al-Maturidi, if a judge is just ('adl) and knowledgeable ('alim), his statement about a hadd judgment is accepted. The basis is the Quranic command to obey those in authority (uli al-amr).
The judge must be both just and knowledgeable to remove suspicion.
2
Note the conditions (justice and knowledge)
If the judge is just but ignorant, he must be questioned about the conditions; if he fulfills them, his word is accepted. If he is sinful (fasiq), his word is not accepted unless the evidence is directly seen.
This shows that character and competence are prerequisites.
3
Understand the retraction by Imam Muhammad
Later, Imam Muhammad retracted his earlier position and required either direct observation of the evidence or the testimony of the judge together with a just witness. This became the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view due to widespread corruption among judges.
The retraction is documented in al-Jami' al-Saghir.
4
Recognize the exception for judicial correspondence
An exception is made for a judge's written communication (kitab al-qadi ila al-qadi) because of the necessity to revive rights and the rarity of fraud in such cases.
This exception is noted in al-Hidayah.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
In practice, a judge's bare statement about a hadd penalty is insufficient. The executing authority must either see the evidence or hear testimony from the judge along with a just witness.
This protects the integrity of hadd punishments.

Commentary
This passage discusses the reliability of a judge's statement regarding a hadd penalty he claims to have imposed. The initial position, attributed to al-Maturidi, is that if the judge is just ('adl) and knowledgeable ('alim), his word is accepted, and the listener must carry out the penalty. This is based on the obligation to obey those in authority (uli al-amr). However, later scholars, following Imam Muhammad's retraction, require either direct observation of the evidence or the testimony of a just witness alongside the judge. This stricter position was adopted due to widespread corruption among judges. An exception is made for a judge's written correspondence (kitab al-qadi ila al-qadi) because of the necessity to revive rights and the rarity of fraud in such cases. The passage highlights the evolution of Hanafi fiqh in response to societal conditions.

Concealing Hudud: Recommended or Permissible?
Ruling that concealing hudud offenses is recommended, with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on concealing hudud offenses and explain the evidence from hadith that supports recommendation, while distinguishing when establishing the penalty is permissible.

Core Idea
Concealing hudud is recommended (ahabb), not obligatory—deterrence permits establishing the penalty.

When a crime carrying a fixed punishment (hadd) occurs, should Muslims conceal it or report it? The Hanafi school addresses this in a nuanced way. The matn states that concealment is 'ahabb' (liked), implying it is recommended rather than mandatory. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks the proofs, including the famous hadith of Ma'iz, and explains why establishing the hadd is also good when it serves to deter corruption. This lesson will guide you through the textual evidence and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling from the matn
Note the key phrase 'wastaruha fi al-hudud ahabb'. The word 'ahabb' indicates a recommended act (sunnah), not an obligation (wajib). Contrast with terms like 'wajib' or 'fard' that would indicate obligation.
In Hanafi usul, 'ahabb' is a term of preference, not compulsion.
2
Understand the first hadith proof
The author cites the Prophet's statement to Hazzal regarding Ma'iz: 'If you had covered him with your garment, it would have been better for you.' This is a direct textual proof for the virtue of concealment. Note that the hadith is about a confession, not a testimony, but the principle extends.
Some contest the inference because Ma'iz confessed voluntarily, but the Prophet's rebuke to Hazzal shows concealment was better.
3
Understand the second hadith and the repelling evidence
The hadith 'Whoever conceals a Muslim, Allah will conceal him...' is a general encouragement. Also, the practice of the Prophet and Companions in teaching avoidance (talqin al-dar') shows concealment is superior.
The term 'talqin al-dar' refers to teaching a way to avoid the hadd, which aligns with concealment.
4
Resolve the apparent contradiction with the Quranic verse
The verse 'Indeed, those who like that immorality should be spread...' (24:19) seems to condemn concealment. But the author clarifies it applies to those who desire the spread of immorality, not to those who establish hadd for reform. Thus, no contradiction.
This shows how a general verse can be restricted by other proofs (takhsis).

Commentary

Agency of a Secluded Woman (Mukhaddarah) in Litigation
Ruling on appointment of agent by a secluded woman and its acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on the validity of agency for a mukhaddarah and determine the oath procedure based on her social class.

Core Idea
A secluded woman's agency is valid without court appearance; her status and oath procedure depend on social class and custom.

The Hanafi school recognizes the difficulty a secluded woman (mukhaddarah) faces in attending court. This lesson explains the definition of mukhaddarah, the validity of her appointing an agent, and special procedures for administering oaths to her, with nuances based on social class.

Guided Path
1
Define mukhaddarah linguistically and legally
Identify the term mukhaddarah from the text: linguistically from 'khidr' (curtain), legally as a woman whose custom is not to appear or mix with men.
Her opposite is barizah, a woman who goes out for needs.
2
Recognize social class criteria for mukhaddarah status
Note that the acceptance of her claim of being mukhaddarah varies: noblewomen (virgin or not) are believed; middle-class only if virgin; lower-class no claim accepted.
This is based on apparent custom ('ala').
3
Understand the ruling on her agency
The passage states that the agency of a mukhaddarah is accepted without opponent consent. She does not need to appear in court.
This is indicated by the author accepting her tawkil.
4
Note the oath procedure for a mukhaddarah
If an oath becomes obligatory, the judge sends three trustworthy men to her home; one administers the oath, two witness.
This avoids her having to appear in court.
5
Apply the rules: litigation context
The plaintiff cannot directly litigate against her husband; the husband litigates with her agent or with her (via agent).
She remains party to the suit but through her agent.

Scope of Agency: Fulfilling and Receiving Rights, and the Hudud Exception
Ruling on scope of agency with proof and evidence rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that an agent can fulfill and receive all rights except hudud and qisas, explain the rationale based on shubuhat and qiyas, and apply the evidence rule for proving agency when the debtor admits agency but denies the debt.

Core Idea
An agent can fulfill and receive all rights except hudud and qisas, which are repelled by doubts and require direct principal involvement.

This lesson examines a fundamental ruling in Hanafi agency law: an agent's authority to pay and collect rights (iwfa’ wa istiwfa’) is comprehensive, covering all financial and personal rights—except two: hudud (fixed corporal punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The commentary explains why hudud and qisas are excluded using the principle that they are repelled by doubts (shubuhat) and cannot be delegated. The passage then shifts to a procedural nuance: how agency is proven when the debtor admits representation but denies the underlying debt. This nuance reveals important rules of evidence (bayyina) and the status of the agent as opponent. Mastering these points is essential for correctly applying agency law in both classical and contemporary settings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its exception
Read the matn: 'وبإيفائها واستيفائها إلا في حد وقود'. The rule is that an agent can fulfill (pay) and receive (collect) all rights. The exception is hudud and qisas. Understand that 'rights' here include financial debts, trusts, and also personal rights like retaliation (qisas) – but those are excluded.
The exception is categorical: no agency is valid for any hadd or qisas, whether execution or any step.
2
Explain the rationale for the exception
The commentary states: 'فإنها تندرئ بالشبهات' – hudud and qisas are repelled by doubts. This means any uncertainty in proof precludes their application. Therefore, delegating them to an agent increases the chance of doubt, so only the principal (or directly the ruler) can execute them. The principle is derived from a hadith: 'ادرؤوا الحدود بالشبهات'.
This rationale applies to both hudud and qisas, as both involve severe penalties and require direct authority.
3
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The commentary implies a qiyas: the principal himself can perform hudud/qisas (asl), so he can appoint an agent for them? No—because hudud are not like other rights. The effective cause (illa) is that hudud are repelled by doubts and require personal involvement of the one responsible. Since an agent is not the responsible party, the ruling does not transfer. Thus agency for hudud is invalid (batil).
Some might think agency for hudud is like agency for divorce or zihar, but those are not repelled by doubts; hudud are unique.
4
Apply the evidence rule for proving agency when debt is denied
The passage then gives a scenario: the principal appoints an agent to collect a debt. The debtor admits the agency but denies the debt. Can the agent produce evidence to prove the debt? No, because the debtor's admission of agency only proves that the agent is the agent, but it does not make the debtor an opponent (khasm) in the debt claim. Evidence (bayyina) is only accepted against an opponent who contests the claim. Since the debtor is not contesting the debt actively (he only denies it, but the dispute is on debt, not agency), the agent cannot prove the debt by his own evidence. The principal must be present or the agent must prove agency separately before litigating.
Contrast with executorship: if an executor proves his status, his evidence for the debt is accepted against the heir who admits the executorship but denies the debt. This is because the executor's authority is based on the deceased's will and is analogous to the deceased himself.
5
Distinguish between financial rights and hudud/qisas in practice
Financial rights (like debts, loans, deposits) can be collected or paid by an agent because they are pure property rights and do not involve the same level of personal responsibility. Hudud and qisas, on the other hand, involve the physical punishment or retaliation that must be administered with certainty; even a small doubt prevents their execution. Hence, only the one who is directly responsible (the principal or the state) can perform them.
This distinction is crucial when analyzing hybrid cases: e.g., an agent authorized to collect a debt and also to enforce a self-help remedy? The agent can collect the debt but cannot use force; that would be a hadd-like act.

Self-Help in Hadd, Qisas, and Ta'zir: What You Can Execute Without a Judge
Listing rulings on self-help in punitive and disciplinary claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on self-help for hadd qadhf, qisas, and ta'zir, and identify the conditions under which a right-holder may use force.

Core Idea
Self-help is permissible only for qisas with a sword; for hadd and ta'zir, state authority or mutual discipline apply.

The Hanafi commentators on al-Kanz did not cover self-help in this chapter. This passage collects rulings from various sources: the hadd of qadhf cannot be taken by the victim; qisas may be taken by the heir with a sword even without a court order; mutual assault leads to ta'zir starting with the aggressor; insults may be returned, but forbearance is better. Also, a husband may discipline his wife, and a person subject to ta'zir may allow the right-holder to execute it.

Identify the ruling for qisas
The heir of a victim of intentional murder may execute qisas himself with a sword, even without a judge's order. No other weapon is allowed.

When both parties assault each other, both receive ta'zir. The ta'zir is executed first on the initial aggressor because his wrong is greater.
The obligation of ta'zir is prior on the one who started.

The Oath of the Thief: Liability Without Hadd
Ruling with qiyas on the thief's oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school administers an oath to the thief and why refusal leads to financial liability but not the hadd penalty, and apply the qiyas to analogous cases.

Core Idea
The thief's oath establishes financial liability but never the cutting penalty — oath is for rights, not for hudud.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a key ruling on the thief's oath. The judge administers an oath; if the thief refuses, he must compensate the stolen property but is not subject to hand amputation. The proof is a qiyas to the testimony of a man and two women, which establishes financial claims but not hudud. The text also expands to related cases, such as the master's oath in a conditional emancipation scenario, highlighting the Hanafi distinction between financial rights and penal punishments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its two consequences
The passage states: the thief is administered an oath (yustahlif). If he refuses (nakala), he becomes liable (daman) but is not cut (qat'). Note that the same act — refusal — has different effects for liability and for the hadd.
The oath itself does not cause cutting; it only triggers liability.
2
Understand the proof: qiyas on testimony
The ruling is based on qiyas (analogy) to the case of testimony by a man and two women. That testimony suffices for financial matters (like debt) but not for hadd. Similarly, an oath (or refusal) can establish financial liability but never the cutting penalty.
The illa is that oaths and testimony are procedural tools valid for rights but not for bodily punishments.
3
Recognize the scope and exceptions
This oath is specific to the hadd of theft; by consensus, no oath is administered in other hudud. However, an exception arises in qadhf (false accusation) when it involves a private right, such as a slave's conditional emancipation. In that case, the master is oathed, and refusal frees the slave but does not establish zina.
The exception shows that oaths can still be used when a financial or personal right is intertwined with a hadd.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Al-Halwani corrected the position that the thief is oathed, contrary to al-Sarakhsi who may have opposed it. The mufta bihi is that the oath is administered, as recorded in al-Khaniyah.
This ikhtilaf does not affect the core ruling that refusal leads to liability, not cutting.
5
Apply the distinction to related scenarios
Consider the qadhf case: if a person repeats another's slander, he becomes a slanderer. But the slave who claims zina to trigger emancipation does not become a slanderer because he is merely invoking a condition, not directly accusing his master.
The key is whether the statement is an accusation or a conditional claim.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the oath of the thief. The text begins with a ruling: the thief is administered an oath (yustahlif). If he refuses to swear (nakala), he becomes liable for the value of the stolen property (daman) but he is not subjected to the cutting penalty (qat'). The reasoning is that two things are contingent on his act: liability and the cutting penalty. The oath (or refusal) can establish liability but cannot establish the cutting penalty. This is analogous to a case where a man and two women testify against someone: their testimony is sufficient for financial matters but not for hadd. The text then notes that this oath is specific to the hadd of theft; in other hadd penalties, no oath is administered by consensus, except in the hadd of qadhf (false accusation) when it involves a private right. An example is given: if a master says to his slave 'If you commit zina, you are free,' and the slave claims he did commit zina to gain freedom, the master is administered an oath. If the master refuses to swear, the slave is freed, but the master is not deemed to have committed zina. The passage then discusses a nuance: whether the slave becomes a slanderer of his master by making such a claim. Imam al-Khassaf indicates that the slave does not become a slanderer because he merely claims the occurrence of the condition, not direct accusation. Finally, the text mentions that in qadhf cases, if one person repeats another's slander, he becomes a slanderer himself.

الضمان (al-daman): Financial liability for the value of the stolen property.
القطع (al-qat'): The cutting of the hand as the hadd punishment for theft.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Oath-Taking: Cause vs. Result
Explains the preferred view on oath-taking and the intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples regarding the manner of administering oaths in judicial claims, and state the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) along with its justification from the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah).

Core Idea
The manner of oath depends on what the defendant denies—cause or effect—according to the relied-upon view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, the oath is a critical tool to settle disputes when evidence is lacking. But what exactly should the defendant swear on? Should it be on the underlying transaction (the cause) or on the resulting ownership (the ruling)? This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq reveals a delicate internal difference within the Hanafi school, where Imam Abu Hanifa's position appears at odds with that of his two closest disciples. Understanding this dispute will sharpen your ability to apply the correct ruling and appreciate how the school's authoritative hierarchy resolves such ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
The core question: When a defendant (munkir) denies a claim in court, should the judge ask him to swear on the cause (e.g., 'I did not sell') or on the legal result (e.g., 'This item is not yours')? The answer determines the wording of the oath and the legal consequence.
The cause (sabab) is the transaction that gives rise to ownership; the result (hukm) is the ownership itself.
2
State the Position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
The two disciples held that the judge should examine what the defendant actually denies. If he denies the cause, the oath is on the cause; if he denies the ruling, the oath is on the result. This is the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah) and the chosen position of the matn authors.
This view is attributed to Abu Yusuf explicitly: 'ينظر إلى إنكار المدعى عليه'.
3
State the Position of Imam Abu Hanifa
Imam Abu Hanifa appears to hold that the defendant should be sworn on the result (ownership) regardless of the nature of the denial. However, some scholars reinterpret his stance as applying only when a claim of wealth is combined with another matter (e.g., marriage). The commentator finds this reinterpretation far-fetched.
Abu Hanifa's exact view is not fully detailed in this passage, but the contrast is clear.
4
Understand the Tarjih (Preference)
The school's mufta bihi is the view of the two disciples because it is zahir al-riwayah and chosen by the authors of matn texts. However, Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi states that the matter is delegated to the judge's discretion, leaving some flexibility.
Note the tension: a clear textual preference (zahir al-riwayah) vs. judicial discretion.
5
Apply to a Hypothetical Scenario
Consider a case: The claimant says 'I sold you my watch; you must return it.' The defendant replies 'I never bought it.' According to the relied-upon view, the defendant swears on the cause: 'By Allah, I did not buy that watch.' Whereas according to Abu Hanifa's apparent view, the oath would be on the result: 'By Allah, this watch is not yours.'
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear in the wording and the legal effect.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute over the manner of administering oaths in judicial claims. The text begins by referencing Abu Yusuf's view that the oath should be tailored to the form of the claimant's claim. The commentator then explains that Imam Abu Hanifa sometimes derives rulings based on the views of his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) even though he himself holds a different opinion, similar to his derivation in the case of sharecropping. The relied-upon position (madhhab) in oath-taking is the view of the two disciples, and this is the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah) from the six core books of Imam Muhammad, as stated in Khizanat al-Muftin. Therefore, the authors of matn texts chose this view. However, Fakhr al-Islam (al-Bazdawi) states that the matter is delegated to the judge's discretion. According to Abu Yusuf, the judge should examine the defendant's denial: if the defendant denies the cause (e.g., the occurrence of a sale), the oath is on the cause; if he denies the legal effect (e.g., ownership), the oath is on the result. This is the position of most judges. The author (of Kanz al-Daqaiq) did not exhaust all sub-cases arising from this principle, mentioning only trust and debt. The two footnotes address further refinements: the first discusses an interpretation of Abu Hanifa's stance when a claim of wealth is combined with marriage, and the second refers to the previous page.

Legal Effect of Affirmation (Tasdiq) vs Acknowledgment (Iqrar)
Clarifies when 'tasdiq' is a binding acknowledgment and its limitation in hadd cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasdiq and iqrar, and state the Hanafi ruling on the binding effect of tasdiq in financial versus hadd cases.

Core Idea
Tasdiq (affirmation) is not a true confession; it binds in financial claims but does not trigger hadd punishment.

In Hanafi fiqh, saying 'sadaqtu' (I affirmed) can have different legal effects depending on the context. This lesson dissects the distinction between tasdiq (affirmation) and iqrar (direct acknowledgment), showing how words of affirmation are treated in hudud cases versus financial claims. By examining authoritative sources, we will understand when such words constitute a binding confession and when they do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of tasdiq
Tasdiq includes saying 'sadaqtu' or phrases like 'al-haqq' (the truth) or 'al-sidq' (the truth) in response to a claim. Note that the word itself is not a direct confession but an affirmation of the claim.
The source distinguishes tasdiq from iqrar based on intention and linguistic usage.
2
Distinguish iqrar from tasdiq
Iqrar is a direct, intentional confession of a right or obligation. Tasdiq is a weaker affirmation; it is considered an iqrar only when the context and custom customarily treat it as such.
Hanafis say: 'Tasdiq is not iqrar intentionally' – it may be iqrar implicitly in some contexts.
3
Apply the ruling in hudud cases
In hudud (fixed punishments), tasdiq does not establish the hadd, even if repeated four times. The source explicitly rejects the view that a single tasdiq can trigger hadd, and states that the same holds for multiple affirmations.
This is because tasdiq lacks the intentional confession required for hadd.
4
Apply the ruling in financial claims
In financial matters, saying 'sadaqtu' (or equivalent) after a claim of debt binds the person to the debt, unless it is said mockingly. The tone may indicate mockery, which nullifies the effect.
Sources quoted: al-Talkhis, al-Bazzaziyya, al-Khaniyya.
5
Consider special cases and testimony
If a witness testifies and the defendant says 'He is truthful' after testimony, it is an iqrar. If said before testimony, it is not. Similarly, conditional statements (e.g., 'If he testifies, it is upon me') are not binding because suspending iqrar is invalid.
Sharh Adab al-Qada' provides the distinction.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the legal meaning of 'tasdiq' (affirmation) versus 'iqrar' (acknowledgment). It originates from a note in the commentary on the marriage dispute ruling, where some scholars used the term 'iqrar' and others 'tasdiq'. The commentator clarifies that in the context of the marriage dispute, they are practically the same, but in general law they differ. The key distinction is that saying 'sadaqtu' (I affirmed) does not count as an iqrar for the purpose of establishing a hadd punishment, even if repeated four times, because it is not a direct confession. However, in financial matters, saying 'sadaqtu' or equivalent words like 'al-haqq' or 'al-sidq' is binding as an iqrar. The passage also discusses cases involving testimony: if a defendant says a witness is truthful after testimony, it is an iqrar, but if said before testimony, it is not. The commentator quotes various sources (al-Talkhis, al-Bazzaziyya, al-Khaniyya, Sharh Adab al-Qada') to establish these rules. The overall lesson is that the word 'tasdiq' is context-dependent: it is not an iqrar for hudud but is for financial obligations.

Key Terms
تصديق (tasdiq): The act of saying 'sadaqtu' or similar words of affirmation; not a direct confession but can have legal effects.
إقرار (iqrar): A formal acknowledgment of a right or obligation, binding in most cases.
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Islamic law, e.g., for zina or qadhf.
