Legal Tricks for Proving Debt and Pledge Against the Absent
Legal stratagems to obtain judgments on debt and pledge from an absent party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi tricks for proving rights against an absent party, explain the internal dispute on the pledge trick, and apply the conditions for the maintenance trick.

Core Idea
Hanafi hiyal overcome procedural obstacles to justice when a party is absent.

This lesson explores legal stratagems (hiyal) used in Hanafi courts to indirectly prove debts and pledges when the defendant is absent. These tricks are not deceptive but are procedural mechanisms to secure justice, often by using possession or conditional statements to obtain a judgment that can later be overturned if the absent party appears. We will examine three specific tricks, their reasoning, and their practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural problem
Each trick addresses the difficulty of litigating against an absent party who cannot defend themselves. Understand why direct judgment is problematic.
The Hanafi school generally avoids judgments against absent persons without representation.
2
Describe the trick's mechanism
For each trick, outline the parties involved and the sequence of claims that lead to a judgment. Focus on how the trick circumvents the absence.
The kafalah and hawala tricks are already presented; the text elaborates on the rahn and maintenance tricks.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning
Examine the justifications for and against the pledge trick: the rejected narration because it judges on an absent person, and the accepted narration because the possessor is a trustee and can represent the absent owner.
This internal Hanafi dispute shows the balance between procedural fairness and practical necessity.
4
Determine the operative ruling
Identify which position is mufta bihi (relied upon) for each trick, based on the text's mention of later practice and Jami' al-Fusulayn.
For the maintenance trick, the text states it is currently practiced and considered sound, though reversible.

Commentary
This passage outlines several legal tricks (hiyal) used to indirectly prove rights against absent persons. First, two well-known tricks for proving a debt: kafalah (guarantee) and hawala (transfer of debt). These are considered valid means. Second, a trick for proving a pledge (rahn) against an absent pledgor: the mortgagee gets a third party to claim ownership of the pledged item, then the possessor (who holds it as pledge) proves it is a pledge, thereby obtaining a judgment that indirectly proves the right against the absent owner. Third, a trick for dropping past maintenance and clothing: the judge uses a conditional divorce phrasing. The husband denies and is sworn, leading to a ruling that no maintenance is due and no divorce occurred. This is considered valid in practice, though if the woman later appears and proves the maintenance, the earlier judgment is overturned. This illustrates the nuanced approach of the Hanafi school in using procedural mechanisms to handle absent parties.

Ruling on Taking Debtor's Turban as Collateral
Ruling on validity of taking a debtor's turban as a lien

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking a debtor's property as collateral without his consent, and explain the condition that transforms such taking into a valid pledge.

Core Idea
Without the debtor's consent, taking his turban makes you a usurper, not a secured creditor.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores a nuanced scenario: a creditor lifts a debtor's turban while demanding payment. Is this a valid pledge? The author cites multiple Hanafi sources and reconciles them, revealing that the debtor's consent—explicit or implicit—is the decisive factor. This lesson walks you through the legal reasoning and the conditions for a valid rahn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage begins with a case from al-Bazzaziyya: a creditor lifts the debtor's turban and says he will not return it until paid. Determine the initial ruling given.
The initial ruling treats the turban as a pledge if destroyed, extinguishing the debt.
2
Examine the condition
Note that the ruling is conditional: it only applies if the debtor could retrieve the turban but left it willingly. If the debtor was unable to retrieve it, the case is questionable.
The ability to retrieve is key to inferring consent.
3
Understand the complication about excess value
The author points out that if the turban is worth more than the debt, the excess is a trust (amanah) while the creditor is also a usurper (ghasib). This highlights the problem of taking property without right.
The creditor is not allowed to take more than the type of his right.
4
Analyze the second al-Bazzaziyya case
In another case, the debtor voluntarily removes his turban and gives it, receiving a handkerchief. This is a valid pledge because of the debtor's consent.
Consent can be explicit or implicit (by leaving the item after ability to retrieve).
5
Reconcile the transmissions
The author cites Tanwir al-Absar and Jami' al-Fusulayn, which state that taking without consent is not a valid pledge. The reconciliation is that consent is the determining factor.
The apparent conflict is resolved by focusing on the debtor's agreement.

Commentary
This passage is a parenthetical commentary clarifying a statement in the original matn ('as in the ghasb of al-Bazzaziyya'). It presents various rulings from Hanafi sources regarding the situation where a creditor takes a debtor's turban as collateral without explicit agreement. Al-Ramli first cites a case from al-Bazzaziyya where the creditor lifts the turban while demanding payment and says he will not return it until paid; if the turban is destroyed, it is treated as a pledged item lost, i.e., it extinguishes the debt. However, this ruling is conditional: it only applies if the debtor could have retrieved it but left it willingly. If the debtor was unable to retrieve it (e.g., due to force), then there is a problem. The author then notes that the excess value of the turban over the debt is considered a trust (amanah), but the creditor is also a usurper (ghasib) because he has no right to take property of a type different from his right. Next, another case from al-Bazzaziyya on pledge: the debtor voluntarily removes his turban and gives it, and the creditor offers a handkerchief; this constitutes a valid pledge because of the debtor's consent. Jami' al-Fusulayn says it is not valid but if the debtor agrees to leave it as a pledge, then it is permissible and its loss is treated as a pledge. The commentator concludes that these apparently conflicting statements can be reconciled: the key factor is the debtor's consent. If the debtor agrees (expressly or implicitly by leaving it), the turban becomes a valid pledge; if taken forcibly without consent, it is not a pledge and the creditor is a usurper. This guidance is for a student of Hanafi fiqh to understand the nuanced conditions for a valid pledge.

Effect of Temporary Joint Ownership on Rahn, Hibah, and Sadaqah
Ruling on whether temporary joint ownership invalidates rahn, hibah, and sadaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether temporary joint ownership (shuyu' 'ari) invalidates rahn, hibah, and sadaqah, and explain the distinction based on each contract's ownership requirements.

Core Idea
Temporary joint ownership invalidates a pledge (rahn) but does not invalidate a gift (hibah) or charity (sadaqah) in Hanafi law.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author rules on a scenario where a claimant asserts both purchase and another contract (pledge or gift) over the same property. The key legal issue is the effect of shuyu' 'ari—joint ownership that arises after the contract. The correct Hanafi view distinguishes between contracts: rahn requires exclusive ownership at the time of contract, so temporary joint ownership renders it fasid, while hibah and sadaqah remain valid. The passage also clarifies an earlier opinion by Khwahirzadeh, ensuring you grasp the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contracts and Scenario
Notice that two parties claim ownership: one claims purchase (shira'), the other claims either a pledge (rahn) or a gift (hibah). The property may be divisible or indivisible.
The scenario is about conflicting claims, not actual transactions.
2
Understand the Ruling for Pledge (Rahn)
When purchase and pledge conflict, temporary joint ownership makes the pledge fasid (corrupt). The pledgor no longer has exclusive ownership, so his evidence is rejected, and judgment goes entirely to the claimant of purchase.
Rahn requires the pledgor to be the sole owner at the time of pledge.
3
Examine the Earlier Opinion on Gift (Hibah)
Khwahirzadeh held that for a divisible property, shuyu' 'ari invalidates the gift, so the purchase claimant gets everything. For an indivisible property (e.g., a slave), both share equally because the gift of an undivided share in an indivisible property is valid.
This was the earlier reasoning based on the invalidity of a gift of an undivided share in divisible property.
4
Apply the Correct View and Distinguish
The commentator corrects: temporary joint ownership does NOT invalidate a gift or charity. Hence, the gift claim remains valid regardless of shuyu' 'ari. For divisible property, the purchase claimant still wins because a gift of an undivided share in divisible property is invalid on its own (without partition). For indivisible property, both claims stand, so judgment is between them (they share equally).
The distinction hinges on whether the property is divisible and the nature of the contract, not on the mere existence of joint ownership.

Commentary

Ten Cases Where Possessor Cannot Transfer Possession
Lists five of ten cases where possessor cannot transfer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal principle that certain possessors cannot transfer possession and list the first five cases from the Hanafi list.

Core Idea
Possession does not grant ownership; special possessors cannot transfer without permission.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain individuals hold property in a limited capacity—they have possession but not full ownership. Qadi Khan's Fatawa lists ten such cases where the possessor cannot transfer possession to another, neither before nor after taking possession. This lesson examines the first five cases from that list.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal principle
Understand that for certain possessors, transferring possession to a third party is prohibited because they hold the property in a trust-like capacity, not as owners.
The prohibition applies both before and after the possessor takes physical possession.
2
List the first five cases
Recall the five possessors mentioned: mortgagee (al-murtahin), depositary (al-mudi'), agent for sale (al-wakil bil-bay'), lessee of an animal or garment (musta'jir al-dabbah aw al-thawb), and borrower for use (al-musta'ir).
Memorize these five: they form the beginning of the ten-item list.
3
Apply the principle
For each case, explain why the possessor cannot transfer: each has a limited right over the property, and sub-transfer would exceed their authority.
For example, the mortgagee holds the pledge only as security; re-pledging it would risk the owner's property.
4
Recognize the authority
Note that this ruling comes from Qadi Khan's Fatawa, a later Hanafi work (fatawa mutakhkhirin), and is the relied-upon position in the school.
Some earlier authorities may have differed, but the fatwa is settled.

This list is important for understanding the limits of different types of possession in Hanafi law.
