Whose Madhhab Counts? Oaths in Shuf'a and Nafaqa Claims
Dispute on whose madhhab governs oath administration in shuf'a and nafaqa claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on whose madhhab is considered when administering oaths in shuf'a and nafaqa claims, and state the preferred opinion (mu'tamad) regarding the claimant's madhhab.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the defendant's madhhab is irrelevant; the claimant's belief matters to prevent injustice.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq tackles a practical problem: when parties from different madhhabs are in court, whose madhhab should the judge consider when swearing the oath? The answer reveals a nuanced approach that prioritizes protecting the claimant's right while respecting the defendant's belief. The Hanafi school carefully balances substantive justice with procedural fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
Recognize that the core question is: when a claimant and defendant follow different madhhabs, whose madhhab governs the wording of the oath?
The issue arises in claims of pre-emption by neighborhood (shuf'at al-jiwār) and maintenance of the irrevocably divorced woman (nafaqat al-mabtūtah).
2
State the Hanafi Position on the Defendant's Madhhab
The passage explicitly says the defendant's madhhab is not considered. The oath must be administered in a way that does not allow the defendant to exploit his own madhhab to evade the claim.
If the defendant were allowed to swear according to his own school, he might be truthful in his belief but the claimant would lose her right.
3
Examine the Three Opinions on the Claimant's Madhhab
There are three views: (1) the claimant's madhhab is also ignored, only the judge's madhhab counts; (2) the claimant's madhhab is considered; (3) the judge asks the claimant if he believes the claim is obligatory.
The third opinion is the most correct (mu'tamad) according to Sharh al-Sadr al-Shahid.
4
Understand the Reason for Preferring the Third Opinion
Ignoring the claimant's madhhab can lead to injustice. The story of Qadi Abu 'Asim shows how he adjusted the oath based on the defendant's madhhab to protect the claimant.
The judge inquired about the defendant's locality to know his school, then tailored the oath accordingly.
5
Apply the Principle to the Example
If a Shafi'i claimant seeks pre-emption by neighborhood before a Hanafi judge, some say the judge hears the case (opinion 1), others say he does not (opinion 2). The mu'tamad is that the judge asks the claimant about his belief.
The practical outcome depends on which opinion is followed.

His statement: (If he claims pre-emption by neighborhood or maintenance of the irrevocably divorced woman, and the buyer or husband does not see them, he [the judge] administers an oath based on that, as if each of them is a Shafi'i for example, because of what we presented that if he were to swear from what is obtained, he would be truthful in his oath according to his belief, thus the consideration for the claimant is lost. In Sharh al-Sadr al-Shahid, the last is the most correct and best of the opinions. End. Correction: it is the relied-upon. And al-Sadr narrated from Qadi Abu 'Asim that he used to teach and the Caliph would judge, and it happened that a woman claimed against her husband maintenance during the 'idda, the husband denied, so the judge administered an oath: 'By Allah, you have no obligation to deliver maintenance from the aspect she claims.' If he is from our companions, administer an oath: 'By Allah, you have no obligation to deliver maintenance to her from the aspect she claims,' out of consideration for her.' End. Commentary
This lengthy passage deals with a crucial procedural issue: whose madhhab should be considered when administering an oath in claims of pre-emption (shuf'a) and maintenance (nafaqa) where the parties may follow different schools. The judge must ensure the oath is meaningful and does not harm the claimant by allowing the defendant to swear according to his own madhhab. The madhhab of the defendant is not considered. As for the claimant's madhhab, there are three opinions: (1) not considered, only the judge's madhhab matters; (2) considered; (3) the judge asks the claimant if he believes in the obligation. The third is the most correct according to Sharh al-Sadr al-Shahid. The author then narrates a story of Qadi Abu 'Asim who adjusted the oath based on the madhhab of the defendant to protect the claimant. The passage also addresses an objection: why not consider the defendant's madhhab? The answer is that the claimant's right is established by the original cause (e.g., purchase for pre-emption), and the defendant's defenses are accidental; the original is presumed until evidence of the accidental. Finally, the author notes that in his time, judges are bound by the madhhab of their sovereign, so this discussion may not apply directly.

Key Terms
شفعة الجوار (shuf‘at al-jiwār): Pre-emption based on neighborly proximity, recognized by Shafi'i but not by Hanafi.
نفقة المبتوتة (nafaqat al-mabtūtah): Maintenance of an irrevocably divorced woman during her 'idda.
معتمد (mu‘tamad): The relied-upon or preferred opinion in the school.
