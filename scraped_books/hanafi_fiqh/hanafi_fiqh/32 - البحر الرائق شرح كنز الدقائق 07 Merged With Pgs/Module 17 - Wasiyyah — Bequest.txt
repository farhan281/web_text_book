Inclusion of Debts in a Bequest of One-Third of Wealth
Ruling with proof on inclusion of debts in bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the inclusion of debts in a bequest of one-third of wealth and explain the legal rationale using the analogy with oaths and qiṣāṣ.

Core Idea
Debts are not wealth until collected — a bequest of one-third excludes uncollected debts but includes them as realized.

This passage from Bahr al-Rāʾiq addresses a nuanced issue in bequest (wasiyyah) law: if someone bequeaths one-third of his wealth, do debts owed to him count as wealth? The Hanafi school distinguishes between tangible cash (ʿayn) and debts (dayn). While the legatee initially takes only from existing cash, debts eventually become part of the bequest as they are collected. This principle is derived from the analogy with oaths and the treatment of qiṣāṣ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
The passage asks whether debts (dayn) owed to the deceased are included under a bequest of 'one-third of wealth.'
2
State the initial ruling
Debts are not considered wealth (māl) initially; the legatee takes one-third of the existing cash only, not of the debts.
Example from the text: if one has 100 dirhams cash and a 100-dirham debt, the legatee takes 33.33 dirhams from cash, not from the debt.
3
Trace the proof from oath-breaking
The ruling is derived from the principle that if a person swears he has no wealth while having debts, he does not break his oath. This proves debts are not considered wealth.
The same logic applies to bequests: debts are not part of 'wealth' at the time of bequest.
4
Analyze the expansion upon collection
As debts are collected, they become like cash, and the legatee takes one-third of each collected amount until the debt is fully recovered.
The collected debt 'joins what was cash in the beginning.'
5
Understand the reconciliation using qiṣāṣ analogy
The passage compares this to qiṣāṣ (retaliation): the legatee of one-third does not have a right to qiṣāṣ initially, but if it converts to wealth (e.g., diyah), his right is established. Similarly, debts are not included until they materialize.
This shows that a right can be suspended until a condition is met.

Commentary
This passage addresses a critical question in Hanafi inheritance and bequest law: whether debts (ديون) owed to the deceased count as wealth (مال) for the purpose of a bequest. The general principle stated is that a debt is not considered مال; for example, if someone swears that he has no wealth, and he has debts owed to him, he does not break his oath because debts are not tangible property. In the context of a bequest of one-third of one's wealth, if the deceased leaves both cash and debts, the legatee initially receives one-third of the cash only, not of the debts. However, as the debts are collected (i.e., they become specific cash), the legatee is entitled to one-third of the collected amount. This is analogous to the case of qiṣāṣ (retaliation): if the deceased had a right to qiṣāṣ, the legatee does not have a right to it, but if that right converts to wealth (e.g., through diyah), then the legatee's right is established. The passage provides a reconciliation between the two seemingly conflicting positions: debts are not included in the bequest initially, but as they materialize, they are retroactively included.

Key Terms
دين (dayn): A debt owed to the deceased by another person; it is considered not to be مال (wealth) for the purposes of oaths and bequests until it is actually collected.
عين (‘ayn): Cash or tangible property that is physically present and owned; distinguished from debt.
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation (eye for an eye); in this context, it refers to the right to demand retaliation, which is not considered wealth but can convert to wealth (diyah).

Core Idea
Debts are not initially wealth for bequests, but become so upon collection.

This passage examines a nuanced issue in Hanafi inheritance law: when a testator bequeaths 'one-third of his wealth,' does this include debts owed to him by others? The answer is not straightforward, as the definition of 'mal' (wealth) varies across legal contexts. The author reconciles conflicting Hanafi opinions, offering a practical resolution.

Note the two positions: some Hanafi scholars (e.g., Burhan al-Muhit) exclude debts entirely; others (e.g., Ibn Wahban) include them.
The author cites multiple sources to illustrate the split.

The author reconciles by stating debts are not initially included, but when collected, the collected portion becomes like cash and one-third of it goes to the legatee.
This respects both the general principle (debts not mal) and the practical need to fulfill the bequest.
5
Apply to a scenario

Executor's Actions Before Knowledge of Appointment
Ruling with proof on executor's validity before knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that executorship is succession (khilafah) to determine the validity of an executor's pre-knowledge actions, and distinguish it from agency.

Core Idea
An executor's actions are valid before knowledge because executorship is succession, not agency.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the executor (wasi) is like an heir, so his authority vests automatically upon appointment, while the agent (wakil) must know his role. The passage from Bahr al-Raiq explains the reasoning and explores exceptions, such as general authorization for a slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the validity of an executor's sale before knowing of his appointment. The hukm is sahih (valid).
Also note the contrasting ruling for an agent: not valid.
2
Locate the analogy (qiyas)
The proof is qiyas to inheritance: an heir's ownership vests immediately upon death without knowledge. Executorship is khilafah, so it shares this feature.
This is a dalil aqli.
3
Examine the distinction from agency
Agency (wakalah) is not succession; it establishes authority in the principal's property while the principal still has authority, so knowledge is required.
Also analogize: permission for a slave or minor to trade is like agency.
4
Understand the exceptions
If authorization is general (e.g., master tells market people to deal with his slave), some Hanafis say the slave becomes authorized without knowledge. Also, if the buyer knows of the agency, the sale may be valid even if the agent does not know.
These nuances show the scope of the principle.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There is a difference on the authorized slave: al-Ziyadat says knowledge is required; al-Jami' al-Saghir says general authorization suffices without knowledge. The commentator prefers the latter.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

Debts and Vows: When Does a Debt Count as Wealth?
Ruling on whether debts are considered wealth for vows and wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether debts are considered wealth for vows (nadhər) and wills (waṣiyya), and explain the role of receipt (qabḍ) in determining obligation.

Core Idea
A debt is not wealth for a vow until received — anticipation does not create obligation.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rāʼiq addresses a practical question: if someone vows to give 'his wealth' in charity, are the debts owed to him included? The commentator begins by noting an analogy with zakāt, which becomes obligatory on debts only upon receipt. He then cites a contrary view from al-Khāniyah that debts do not enter a vow of wealth, and provides a series of examples to clarify when the vow becomes binding. The lesson will help you navigate the rule and its applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Central Analogy
Read the opening line: 'There is no doubt that zakāt is obligatory on debts on condition of receipt, so it should enter under the vow of wealth.' This sets up an analogy: since zakāt requires receipt, debts should logically be included in a vow of 'wealth' as well. But the commentator immediately contrasts this with al-Khāniyah's saying that debts do not enter.
2
Understand the Distinction Between Vows and Wills
The passage mentions that in the context of wills, some authorities hold that debts do enter a bequest of wealth because the bequest operates on future collection (like inheritance). For vows, the rule is stricter: the vowed property must exist at the time the vow becomes binding. A debt does not exist as wealth until it is paid.
3
Examine the Examples from Al-Jāmi‘ li-l-Ṣadr
The passage lists several scenarios: (a) a vow to give the price of a slave sold on credit – valid but receipt is a condition; if the price is lost before receipt, the vow lapses; (b) a vow to give a specific load (determinate) versus money (indeterminate) – only the determinate item triggers the vow immediately; (c) a woman vowing her mahr (dower) if she marries – receipt is condition; apostasy or death before receipt voids the vow. These illustrate the principle that the vow attaches only to wealth actually possessed.
4
Apply the General Rule
The Hanafi muftā bihī view is: debts do not enter a vow of wealth until they are received (qabḍ). Commentary on the rule distinguishing executor from agent regarding knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that an executor's actions are valid before knowledge and distinguish this from agency.

Core Idea
Executorship is succession, not agency—knowledge is not required for the executor's authority to take effect.

This passage addresses a crucial distinction between an executor (wasī) and an agent (wakīl) in Hanafi law. While an agent must know of his appointment to act, an executor becomes validly appointed even without knowledge, and his first disposition constitutes acceptance. The commentator explains the underlying rationale: executorship is a form of succession (khilāfa) akin to inheritance, whereas agency is a contract with a living principal.

2

Commentary
This passage provides a comparative analysis of two legal concepts: executorship (wisāya) and agency (wakāla). The commentator lists several points of difference. First is the ruling in the book (the matter of knowledge, which was the earlier topic). Second, executorship cannot be restricted to specific tasks (takhṣīṣ) whereas agency can. Fourth, if an executor dies before completing his duties, the judge appoints a replacement; if an agent of an absent person dies, no replacement is appointed unless the absent person is missing (mafqūd) for preservation of property. Fifth, a judge can remove an executor for breach of trust or suspicion, but cannot remove an agent appointed by a living principal. Disagreement in Testimony: When Differences are Acceptable vs Void
Rulings when witnesses differ on the day of a will or on the means of payment transfer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between testimony that is acceptable due to non-essential differences and testimony that is void due to contradictory legal foundations, applying the Hanafi principle that speech acts do not vary by time or place.

Core Idea
Speech acts do not vary by time or place; what matters is the substance of the legal act being testified.

When witnesses differ in their testimony, the key question is whether the disagreement touches the essence of the legal act or only its non-essential details. This lesson, drawn from Ibn Nujaym's Bahr al-Raiq, explores that distinction through several cases: differing on the day of a will, differing on the amount of a dower, and describing a waqf in health versus a will after death. Understanding these distinctions is vital for correctly evaluating testimony in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Locate the statement 'kalamun la yakhtalifu bi zaman wa makan' (speech does not differ by time or place). This principle means that for speech-act based legal acts (like appointing an executor or making a will), a difference in the time or place of utterance does not change the act itself.
This principle applies to unilateral acts (wasiyyah, wakalah) and certain consensual acts (marriage, sale) only when the core is not changed.
2
Apply to Issue 28: Will on different days
One witness says the testator appointed an executor on Thursday, another says Friday. The testimony is accepted because the will is the same act; the day detail is irrelevant to the substance.
3
Analyze Issue 20: Marriage vs Sale – differing on amount
In sale, differing on the price (e.g., one says one thousand, another says two thousand) voids testimony because price is integral. In marriage, according to Abu Hanifa, differing on the dower does not void testimony because dower is not integral; the Sahibayn disagree, analogizing to sale.
The thamarah (practical outcome): under Abu Hanifa, the marriage is valid and the dower is set by mahr al-mithl; under Sahibayn, testimony fails and marriage is not proven.
4
Distinguish Issue 27: Waqf in health vs will after death
If one witness says the waqf was made in health and the other says in illness, testimony is accepted (the act is the same: waqf; timing affects execution from one-third). But if one says 'waqf in health' and the other says 'waqf after death' (i.e., a will for waqf), testimony is void because they describe different legal acts – one a waqf, the other a wasiyya.
This is a key boundary: the difference is not in secondary detail but in the nature of the legal act itself.

Debtor's Claim of Payment to Principal: Agent vs Executor
Ruling on debtor's claim and distinction between agent and executor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a debtor claims to have paid the principal, and distinguish between the authority of an agent (wakil) and an executor (wasi) in receiving payments.

Core Idea
A debtor's unproven claim of payment does not stop the agent from collecting the debt.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a principal appoints an agent to receive a debt, the debtor's mere claim that he already paid the principal is not accepted without proof. The agent must still collect. However, if the claimant is an executor rather than an agent, the rules differ because the judge has authority over executors. This lesson explains the ruling and the crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1

Recognize the principal, agent (wakil), and debtor. The agent is appointed to receive the property. The debtor claims he already paid the principal.
Agency must be established.

The debtor says 'the principal took the money' (i.e., debt paid). Consider the legal effect of this claim on the agent's duty.
The claim is a denial of current liability.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
Since agency is proven and the debtor's claim is unproven, the agent still has the right to collect. The debtor's claim is treated as an acknowledgment of the debt and agency in the sense that he engages with the agent.
Mere claim without proof does not waive the right.
4
Compare with the executor (wasi) case
If a person claims to be an executor and the possessor confirms it, the judge does not order the possessor to hand over unless the claim is proven. Why? Because the judge has authority to appoint executors, so a confirmation by the judge is needed. For an agent, the judge has no such authority, so the debtor's claim does not prevent collection.
The key distinction: judicial authority over appointment.
5
State the practical consequence
In the agent scenario, the debtor must pay the agent despite his claim. In the executor scenario, payment is not enforced without proof or judicial order.
Different outcomes due to different roles.

Sulh in Criminal Cases: Qisas and Diyah
Rulings on sulh for qisas and diyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on sulh in cases of qisas and diyah, including conditions for validity and consequences of subsequent events like death or recovery.

Core Idea
Sulh for qisas is valid for any amount of property; if the victim later dies, the sulh is void and diyah is owed.

This lesson covers the Hanafi rulings on compromise (sulh) in criminal matters, specifically where qisas (retribution) is possible. The passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains that sulh is valid for any property amount, but its effect changes if the victim dies or recovers. It also discusses the consideration (iwad) requirements and distinctions between intentional and accidental wounds.

Guided Path
1
Determine the nature of the crime
Identify whether the act is intentional (amdan) or accidental (khata). This affects who is liable for diyah (the killer or his aqila) and the validity of sulh.
2
Evaluate the effect of subsequent events
If the victim recovers after sulh, the sulh stands. If the victim dies, the sulh becomes void and full diyah is due from the killer (or aqila for accidental).
The passage: 'وإن مات بطل وعليه الدية' – if he dies, it becomes invalid and upon him is the blood-money.
3
Analyse the consideration (iwad)
The consideration must be valid like a dower (mahr). It must be specified; if it turns out to be free (a free person), diyah is owed. Disputes about the amount are settled by the killer's oath, unlike dower which defaults to mahr al-mithl.

Compromise by Executor with Heirs: Condition for Validity
Ruling on compromise by executor with heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on an executor's compromise with one heir and identify the condition that determines its validity or invalidity.

Core Idea
An executor's compromise with one heir is invalid if the estate is intact, but valid if the executor is personally indebted.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a critical issue in estate administration. The executor (wasi) is a trustee and must treat all heirs equitably. A compromise (sulh) with one heir that diminishes another's share is problematic. The key distinction is whether the estate assets are still in the executor's possession or have been consumed, making the executor a debtor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its two scenarios
Read the passage and note that there are two cases: (1) the executor admits he has the estate money, and (2) the executor has consumed (used) the money.
The ruling changes based on this condition.
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
In the first case, the compromise is 'la yajuz' (invalid). In the second case, it is 'jaza' (valid/permissible).
The Arabic terms are clear: لم يجز and جاز.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning
When the executor admits to holding the estate money, a compromise with one heir is like disposing of a shared asset without consent of the other co-owners – it harms the other heir. But if the executor consumed the money, it becomes a personal debt; the compromise is like settling a debt with a creditor, which does not involve the estate assets directly.
The executor's capacity changes from trustee to debtor.
4
Apply the rule to a similar case
Consider if the executor had mixed the estate money with his own, making it indistinguishable. Would the same rule apply? The Hanafi position would be similar: if the executor cannot return the specific asset, it becomes a debt.
This reinforces the principle of protecting heirs' shares.

Commentary
This passage addresses the validity of a compromise between an executor (wasi) and one of multiple heirs. If the executor admits to holding a specific sum (1000 dirhams) for the deceased and settles with one heir (say, for 400 dirhams to drop his share), the compromise is invalid because it unfairly diminishes the rights of the other heir who is not party to the settlement. However, if the executor had consumed the amount (istahlakaha), i.e., used it or made it his own liability, then a compromise with one heir is valid because the executor is now personally indebted, and the compromise is akin to settling a debt. The distinction is crucial: when the estate is still intact, the executor cannot preferentially treat one heir via compromise.

Key Terms
وصي (wasi): An executor appointed to manage the deceased's estate.
صلح (sulh): A compromise or settlement to resolve a dispute or claim.
استهلك (istahlaka): He consumed (used up) the property, making it his liability.
