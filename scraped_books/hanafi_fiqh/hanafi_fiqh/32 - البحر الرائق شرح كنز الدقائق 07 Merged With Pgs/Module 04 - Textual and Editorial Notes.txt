Inward Effect of a Qadi's Ruling Based on False Testimony
Expansion on inward effect of false testimony based judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for inward enforcement of a qadi's ruling based on false testimony, apply the distinction between absolute ownerships (amlak mursala) and other matters, and explain the Hanafi position using the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
A qadi's ruling based on false testimony is only outwardly binding; inwardly, the truthful party must avoid haram.

A qadi's ruling based on false testimony creates a dilemma: outwardly, the parties must comply, but inwardly, the truth remains unchanged. The Hanafi school has developed a nuanced distinction: some matters (like absolute ownership) are never enforced inwardly, while others (like lineage, divorce, and emancipation) generally are. This lesson unpacks the conditions and the intra-school disagreements, equipping you to apply this ruling in real fatwa scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions for inward enforcement
First, the qadi must not know the witnesses are lying. Second, the subject matter must be capable of being created by the judge's ruling (qabil li al-insha').
If the qadi knows of the lying, the ruling has no inward effect at all. Also, matters that cannot be created new (e.g., a woman already married) fail the second condition.
2
Distinguish absolute ownerships from other matters
Absolute ownership claims (amlak mursala) are never enforced inwardly, by consensus. Other matters (nasab, talaq, itq, waqf) are generally enforced inwardly, though there is ikhtilaf on nasab.
This is the core practical difference. The judge cannot create or transfer true ownership out of nothing.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on nasab and other matters
Abu Hanifa (one narration) and Abu Yusuf (first opinion) held that nasab is enforced inwardly; Muhammad (according to some versions) disagreed. There are also two narrations on hibah and sadaqah.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that only absolute ownerships are excluded; everything else is enforced inwardly.
4
Apply the proof: the hadith of the Prophet
The hadith 'I am but a human; if I give judgment for something of a brother's right, I am only cutting a piece of the Fire' shows that judgment does not change the truth inwardly. The Hanafi school uses this to argue that the qadi does not create ownership.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalala) for the Hanafis.
5
Derive the practical thamarah al-khilaf
If a qadi awards property based on false testimony, the recipient cannot use it (eat, wear, have intercourse) inwardly. For divorce, the parties may remarry outwardly but must avoid sin inwardly. The person against whom judgment is given may act secretly but not openly.
This shows the delicate balance between social order (outward enforcement) and individual conscience (inward truth).

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, specifically on the topic of the inward effect of a qadi's ruling based on false testimony. It is an expansion probe that examines the conditions under which such rulings are binding inwardly (batinan) and their practical implications.

The passage begins by establishing two essential conditions for inward enforcement: first, that the qadi must be unaware that the witnesses are lying; if he knows they are false, the ruling is not enforceable inwardly. Second, the subject matter of the ruling must be capable of being created by the qadi's judgment (qabil li al-insha'). For example, if a woman is already married or in her waiting period, the qadi's ruling cannot create a new marriage because the subject is not open to creation.

A key discussion is whether the presence of witnesses at the marriage ceremony is required for the qadi's ruling to be valid. There are two views: one (attributed to Muhammad al-Shaybani, according to some) does not require it; the other (attributed to al-Za'farani and adopted by most shaykhs) requires it. The relied-upon position is that witness presence is required. However, if we take the view that it is not required, the reasoning is that we consider the judge's ruling as creating an implicit effect (insha' muqtada) within the framework of valid judgment, and such an effect does not require all external conditions.

Similarly, for a valid sale or currency exchange (sarf), the ruling does not require actual possession of the price or counter-value before separation; the judge's ruling suffices. This shows that the judge's power is broad in matters of financial transactions.

Next, the passage restricts the discussion to false testimony. If the qadi rules based on testimony that later proves to be from slaves, disbelievers, or individuals who have been punished for slander (hadd al-qadhf), the ruling is not enforceable by consensus because such testimony is not legally valid. Sinners (fasiq) are different because their testimony may be accepted if their status is unknown.

A critical case is that of a wife who claims her husband divorced her with three talags, the husband denies, and the qadi makes him swear. If the husband swears falsely, the wife knows the truth. For a triple divorce, it is clear that she cannot stay with him because the marriage is irrevocably terminated. For a single or double divorce, there is a problem because the marriage could still be renewed. However, the commentator clarifies that the qadi did not rule on the marriage; he only swore the husband, so the wife's claim is not upheld by the qadi. She cannot live with him, and if she kills him in self-defense (to prevent rape), she is not liable.

The passage then generalizes to all contracts (including gratuitous ones like hibah and sadaqah). There are two narrations regarding whether such rulings are enforceable inwardly. The relied-upon (mu'tamad) is that they are enforceable inwardly because the enforcement is part of the validity of the judgment itself, and its conditions are not external. Even a sale at less than market value (which has an element of gratuity) can be enforced, as even someone who cannot do gratuitous acts (like a mukatib) can sell at a loss.

The discussion then turns to the meaning of 'faskh' (dissolution). The commentator in Idah al-Islah says that faskh includes any invalidation of contracts, including divorce. But the author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq notes a subtlety: divorce does not invalidate marriage; it removes its tie, so it is better to define faskh as 'removing the effect of the contract,' which includes divorce.

A significant point is made about ruling based on the defendant's refusal to swear (nukul). This is considered equivalent to ruling based on false testimony; the property becomes permissible for the claimant both dianatan (inwardly) and qada'an (outwardly).

Regarding lineage (nasab): the text presents a disagreement. According to al-Walwalji, a ruling on lineage based on false testimony is enforceable inwardly. However, others say it is not enforceable by consensus. Al-Khassaf reports that Abu Hanifa holds it enforceable. This created a stratagem: a person with no heir could establish lineage by producing false witnesses, but the inward permissibility of inheritance and other effects remains disputed.

The passage compares emancipation (itq) and talaq; testimony on these is treated similarly. The author suggests that waqf should be treated like emancipation, as it is a form of isqat (relinquishment of ownership).

Finally, the passage clarifies that inheritance is treated like absolute ownership (amlak mursala). Thus, a ruling on inheritance based on false testimony is not enforceable inwardly by agreement. However, there is disagreement on whether inheritance is an absolute right or a right due to a cause; the well-known view is that it is absolute. The author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq chose that it is due to a cause, and the commentator supports this with the hadith of the Prophet: "Whoever I give judgment for something of his brother's right, I am but cutting for him a piece of the Fire." The Prophet said this regarding lost inheritances, indicating that inheritance and absolute ownership are similar in claim.

A final intra-Hanafi disagreement is noted: after agreeing that judgments based on false testimony are not enforceable inwardly, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on the practical implication. In the case of a false testimony of divorce, Muhammad (according to one version) says the first husband cannot have intercourse with her, while Abu Yusuf says he can in the outward sense (zahiran) but not inwardly. The text notes variant readings, but the core is that outward permissibility differs from inward truth.

Key Terms
شهادة الزور (shahadat al-zur): Testimony that is false to the witness's knowledge; perjury.
نفاذ باطناً (nifadh batinan): The ruling being effective and binding on the conscience (dianatan); i.e., the person must act accordingly even if it contradicts the outward truth.
أملاك مرسلة (amlak mursala): Absolute ownership claims without specific cause (like inheritance or sale).
فسخ (faskh): In this context, dissolution of a contract by raising its effect; includes divorce (talaq) by interpretation.

Key Takeaway
A qadi's ruling based on false testimony is outwardly binding but does not change the inward truth; absolute ownerships are never enforced inwardly, while other matters like divorce and emancipation generally are.

Definition of al-Muhtajab and the Principle of Reconciling Contradictions
Definition of al-muhtajab and rule on contradictory statements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of al-muhtajab and apply the Hanafi principle of reconciling contradictory statements in legal claims.

Core Idea
Contradictory statements are rejected unless reconciliation is possible; the defendant's status matters.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq defines a technical term, al-muhtajab, and establishes a key procedural principle. Understanding who qualifies as a muhtajab helps determine when a contradiction in testimony can be overlooked. The Hanafi school prefers to reconcile statements where possible, rather than dismiss them outright.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Read the text to find the definition of al-muhtajab. Note that it refers to a defendant who does not personally manage his affairs or is of high status.
The term is defined in two ways in the passage.
2
Understand the principle of reconciliation
Grasp that if two contradictory statements can be harmonized, the contradiction is ignored. If reconciliation is impossible, the contradiction remains and the later statement is rejected.
The author emphasizes this principle with an example.
3
Apply to the example of payment
Work through the example: defendant says 'I did not pay' then 'I paid'. This is a direct contradiction that cannot be reconciled, so the later claim is rejected.
Note that claiming the plaintiff's acknowledgment is different.
4
Distinguish between direct contradiction and reported acknowledgment
If the defendant claims the plaintiff acknowledged payment, this is not a contradiction because it is not the defendant's own statement. He is merely reporting the plaintiff's action.
The passage uses this to show the limit of the principle.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The passage cites al-Shafi'i as possibly rejecting the claim outright, while Hanafis attempt reconciliation first.
This shows the ikhtilaf on this point.

Commentary

Distinguishing Vow of Charity from Bequest: Istihsan Over Qiyas
Ruling with proof using istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the scope of a vow of sadaqah versus a bequest, and explain the istihsan reasoning that overrides the qiyas.

Core Idea
Vow of charity is tied to Allah's obligation (zakat); bequest is tied to inheritance – different scopes by istihsan.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq, the commentator clarifies the matn ruling that a vow of charity applies only to zakatable wealth, while a bequest applies to all possessions. The default analogy would treat them equally, but the Hanafi school differentiates using istihsan, linking the servant's vow to divine obligation. This lesson explores the core distinction and the argument with Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts and default rulings
Recognize that the matn states: for a vow of sadaqah, it applies to zakatable wealth; for a bequest of one-third, it applies to all wealth.
2
Note the qiyas that would equate them
By pure analogy, both are obligations created by the servant, so they should apply equally to all wealth. This is the position of Zufar.
Imam Zufar is a Hanafi scholar who disagreed on this point.
3
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal via istihsan
The Hanafi school overrides the qiyas using istihsan: the servant's obligation (sadaqah) is measured by Allah's obligation (zakat), which is limited in scope. Bequest, however, is akin to inheritance, which covers all assets.
Istihsan is a Hanafi principle that allows departing from qiyas when necessary.
4
Observe the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Zufar's view, a person who vows all wealth must give all assets in charity. Under the Hanafi position, he gives only zakatable assets, retaining necessities.
This difference has significant financial and social implications.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It addresses the legal ruling when someone says 'my wealth is sadaqah for the poor' versus 'I bequeath one third of my wealth.' The matn ruling states that the sadaqah applies only to zakatable wealth, while the bequest applies to all wealth. The commentator explains the rationale: by qiyas (analogy), they should be equal, and Zufar held that view. However, the Hanafi school differentiates by istihsan (juristic preference). The reason is that a vow of charity is an obligation created by the servant, which is measured by Allah's obligation (zakat), whereas a bequest is like inheritance, covering all assets. There is further intra-madhhab difference: Abu Yusuf includes 'ushri land, Muhammad excludes it, and Abu Hanifa agrees with Muhammad. The passage also discusses that the agent of Bayt al-Mal is not a proper opponent unless specifically authorized. Defining Zakatable Wealth: Types and Intra-Madhhab Differences
Expansion on the definition of mal al-zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which assets are considered zakatable wealth (mal al-zakat) according to the Hanafi school, and state the disagreement over 'ushri land.

Core Idea
Zakatable wealth includes livestock, gold, silver, and trade goods, but excludes kharaji land and personal necessities.

The passage further specifies what constitutes 'zakatable wealth' (mal al-zakat) in the context of the vow. It lists all types that normally attract zakat, explains that nisab and debts are irrelevant for the scope, and includes an intra-madhhab dispute on whether 'ushri land falls under this definition. This lesson breaks down these categories and the points of difference within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
List the categories of zakatable wealth
Identify the assets explicitly mentioned: grazing animals (sawa'im), gold and silver (naqdayn), trade goods ('urud al-tijarah). Note that these are included regardless of nisab or debts.
2
Understand the effect of debts and nisab
The passage states that even if the wealth is encumbered by a debt that fully consumes it, it is still considered zakatable for the vow. If the debt is later paid, the person must give charity to the amount of the debt.
This is a leniency; normally zakat is not due on wealth that is net of debts, but for the vow, the obligation is tied to the genus of zakatable wealth, not its net worth.
3
Analyze the dispute on 'ushri land
Abu Yusuf includes 'ushri land because its produce (ushr) is spent on the same recipients as zakat. Muhammad excludes it because it has an aspect of maintenance (mu'nah). Abu Hanifa agrees with Muhammad.
'Ushri land is land that yields 'ushr (one-tenth) as a tax, considered a form of zakat by some.
4
Identify excluded assets
Kharaji land (subject to kharaj tax), slave-servants, residential houses, household furniture, and items of primary need are excluded from zakatable wealth.

Practical Considerations: Retaining Necessities and Using Legal Stratagems (Hilah)
Practical application and legal stratagem

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rule that a person may retain basic necessities from the vowed sadaqah, and describe the hilah that allows one to avoid the vow by selling assets with option of inspection.

Core Idea
Necessities are retained before sadaqah; a valid hilah can prevent the vow from binding.

The passage addresses two practical aspects of the vow of sadaqah. First, if a person has no wealth beyond what is required for basic needs, he may retain enough for his sustenance. Second, a legal stratagem (hilah) is presented: one can sell all his wealth with an option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'yah), then fulfill the condition of the vow, and then rescind the sale, thereby avoiding the obligation. This lesson explores these applications and also notes a critical point about the nature of bequest.

Guided Path
1
Understand the rule of retaining necessities
If the person has no wealth other than what falls under the vow (i.e., all his assets are zakatable and he has no other means), he may keep enough to cover his basic needs. When he later acquires additional wealth, he must give charity for the amount he retained.
The Hanafi school prioritizes necessity over the obligation to fulfill a vow immediately.
2
Learn the hilah using khiyar al-ru'yah
A person who made a conditional vow (e.g., 'If I do X, all I own is sadaqah') can sell all his possessions to another person for a nominal item (e.g., a cloth in a napkin) without seeing the item, thereby fulfilling the condition. Then he inspects the item and returns it by the option of inspection, so he does not actually lose his wealth.
This hilah is considered valid and prevents the person from sinning by breaking the vow or losing all wealth.
3
Identify the condition for the hilah
The vow must be conditional and the sale must be genuine with the intention to use the option of inspection. The buyer must be a willing participant.
The option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'yah) is established by custom ('urf) in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Note the critical point about bequest as succession
The commentator criticizes a statement in al-Hidayah that describes bequest as a 'succession' (khilafah) like inheritance. He argues that a legatee is not a true successor because he does not bear the same responsibilities (e.g., debts).
This is a subtle point about the legal nature of bequest.

Scope of Wealth in Oaths: Immediate vs. Conditional
Ruling on scope of wealth in oaths based on tunjiz and ta'liq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between immediate and conditional oaths regarding the inclusion of after-acquired property and apply the Hanafi rules to determine the scope of an oath.

Core Idea
In oaths, after-acquired property is included only if the oath is conditional; immediate oaths cover only existing wealth.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school defines the scope of wealth in oaths. A key distinction is between an immediate oath (munajjaz) and a conditional one (mu'allaq). Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining what property the oath covers and what obligations arise.

1
Identify the oath wording

2
Apply the rule on tunjiz
If the oath is immediate (munajjaz), only wealth existing at the time of the oath is included.

Commentary
This passage is a sharh (expansion) on the scope of wealth (مال) in oaths and its comparison with bequests (وصية). The author first discusses the rulings on oaths: if someone swears to give all his wealth in charity, the scope depends on the wording and whether the oath is immediate (tunjīz) or conditional. According to al-Tahawi's Mukhtasar, if the oath is suspended on a condition, it includes both wealth existing at the time of the oath and wealth acquired later up to the occurrence of the condition. If the oath is immediate, it only includes wealth existing at the time of the oath. However, if the wording is 'all my wealth' or 'all my property', by consensus it includes all he owns at the time, and he must give all except his basic sustenance. If he later acquires more, he gives charity for a similar amount. A legal trick (hīla) from al-Walwālijī shows how to avoid breaking the oath: sell all property without seeing the item bought, then exercise the option of inspection to nullify the sale, thus avoiding the obligation. The passage then clarifies that the definition of wealth excludes debts for the purpose of oaths, as per the maxim that debts are not considered مال. The discussion then shifts to bequests, where the rule is that a bequest of 'my wealth' or a fraction of it includes all property owned at the time of death, including after-acquired property. This is contrasted with the case of oaths. The author also addresses a problem in al-Hidāyah where it says bequest is a succession (خلافة) like inheritance; the correct view is that the legatee's ownership is not by way of succession, as evidenced by the fact that the legatee can buy property from the deceased's estate at a lower price, unlike an heir. The passage concludes with various rulings on specific wording in bequests, such as 'one-third of my wealth for Allah' being void according to the two (Abū Yūsuf and Muhammad) but redirected to good deeds according to Muhammad.

Key Terms
مال (māl): In oaths, مال refers to tangible property owned; it excludes debts. For oaths, it includes only existing property if immediate, or also after-acquired if conditional.
تنجيز (tunjīz): Immediacy of an oath; an immediate oath is not suspended on a condition.
خلافة (khilāfah): This term is mainly relevant to bequests, not oaths. In oaths, it is not discussed.

Scope of Wealth in Bequests and the Status of the Legatee
Ruling on scope of wealth in bequests and the nature of legatee's ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a bequest of 'my wealth' includes after-acquired property and state that the legatee is not a true successor (khalīfa) of the deceased.

Core Idea
A bequest of wealth covers all property owned at death, including after-acquired, and the legatee is not a successor like an heir.

This lesson examines the scope of wealth in bequests and the legal status of the legatee. Unlike oaths, a bequest automatically includes after-acquired property. Additionally, the passage clarifies a critical point: the legatee does not succeed the deceased in the same way an heir does, leading to different legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the rule for bequests
A bequest of 'my wealth' or a fraction thereof (e.g., one-third) includes all property the testator owns at the time of death, including property acquired after making the will.
This is based on the quoted ruling from al-Waṣāyā al-Khāniyyah.
2
Contrast with oaths
In bequests, after-acquired property is always included, unlike oaths where inclusion depends on tunjiz.
Bequests are treated like conditional matters (mu'allaq).
3
Identify the apparent conflict in al-Hidāyah
Al-Hidāyah states 'الوصية خلافة كالوراثة' (the bequest is a succession like inheritance).
4
Analyze the rebuttal
Al-Ṣadr al-Shahīd explains that khilāfah here only means ownership after death, not that the legatee stands in the testator's place.
Evidence: the legatee is not liable for the testator's debts.
5
Apply the practical differences
The legatee can buy property from the estate at a lower price than the deceased sold, and is not considered deceived if a purchased item is reclaimed.
These differences show the legatee is not a true successor.

Commentary
This passage is a sharh (expansion) on the scope of wealth (مال) in oaths and its comparison with bequests (وصية). The author first discusses the rulings on oaths: if someone swears to give all his wealth in charity, the scope depends on the wording and whether the oath is immediate (tunjīz) or conditional. According to al-Tahawi's Mukhtasar, if the oath is suspended on a condition, it includes both wealth existing at the time of the oath and wealth acquired later up to the occurrence of the condition. If the oath is immediate, it only includes wealth existing at the time of the oath. However, if the wording is 'all my wealth' or 'all my property', by consensus it includes all he owns at the time, and he must give all except his basic sustenance. If he later acquires more, he gives charity for a similar amount. A legal trick (hīla) from al-Walwālijī shows how to avoid breaking the oath: sell all property without seeing the item bought, then exercise the option of inspection to nullify the sale, thus avoiding the obligation. The passage then clarifies that the definition of wealth excludes debts for the purpose of oaths, as per the maxim that debts are not considered مال. The discussion then shifts to bequests, where the rule is that a bequest of 'my wealth' or a fraction of it includes all property owned at the time of death, including after-acquired property. This is contrasted with the case of oaths. The author also addresses a problem in al-Hidāyah where it says bequest is a succession (خلافة) like inheritance; the correct view is that the legatee's ownership is not by way of succession, as evidenced by the fact that the legatee can buy property from the deceased's estate at a lower price, unlike an heir. The passage concludes with various rulings on specific wording in bequests, such as 'one-third of my wealth for Allah' being void according to the two (Abū Yūsuf and Muhammad) but redirected to good deeds according to Muhammad.

مال (māl): In bequests, مال includes all tangible property owned by the testator at the time of death, including after-acquired property.
تنجيز (tunjīz): Immediacy; relevant to oaths but not bequests, as bequests always include after-acquired like conditional oaths.
خلافة (khilāfah): Succession; in this context, it refers to the idea that the legatee succeeds the deceased in ownership after death. The accurate view is that this is not true succession.

Reconciling Conflicting Testimonies on Quantity
Explains reconciliation of conflicting testimonies on quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for reconciling testimonies that differ in number, apply it to cases like 1000 vs 1500 and 1000 vs 2000, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two disciples on fractional divorces.

Core Idea
When witnesses differ on quantity, the common part is accepted only if agreed in wording and meaning; mere semantic inclusion is not enough.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq details the Hanafi rules for reconciling witness testimonies that disagree on numbers. The key distinction is between cases where the witnesses share a common verbal element (e.g., both say 1000) and cases where the numbers are different in wording (e.g., 1000 vs 2000). The text explores several variants, including divorces and fractional numbers, and reveals an internal Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples. Understanding this ruling is essential for evaluating witness testimony in financial disputes or marital cases.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the general principle
If witnesses differ in number but share a common part in wording and meaning, that common part is accepted. Otherwise, neither testimony is accepted because the common part must be explicitly agreed upon, not implied.
The common part must be agreed upon in both wording (لفظ) and meaning (معنى).
2
Apply to the primary example
One witness says 1000, the other says 1500, and plaintiff claims 1500. Only the 1000 is accepted because both witnesses said '1000' in the same words. The extra 500 is from only one witness.
The Arabic phrase 'قبلت على الألف لاتفاقهما على الألف لفظاً ومعنى' is the textual basis.
3
Contrast with 1000 vs 2000
When one says 1000 and the other says 2000, there is no common wording – 1000 is not the same as 2000. Neither is accepted, even though 1000 is included in 2000 meaning-wise.
The text argues: if 2000 is not proven, 1000 cannot be derived from it. The contained (الجزء) does not exist if the container (الكل) is unproven.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
In cases like 'half a divorce' vs 'one third of a divorce', or 'three divorces' vs 'one divorce', Abu Hanifa rejects the testimony entirely, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad accept it for the least common part.
The dispute is based on whether implication can be used to find a common part. Abu Hanifa requires explicit agreement in wording.
5
Recognize the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position according to Qadikhan and al-Kafi is Abu Hanifa's view in cases like 1000 vs 2000 (nothing accepted), but for 1000 vs 1500 the common part is accepted by agreement.
Qadikhan's Fatawa explicitly supports al-Kafi, making it the madhhab.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling_statement with detailed commentary from the Hanafi school on how to reconcile conflicting testimonies when they differ in number. The author, Zayla'i, explains the principle: when two witnesses agree on a common part of their testimony, that common part is accepted; when they disagree in wording without a common part, neither testimony is accepted. The discussion begins with the case of a husband divorcing his wife with a thousand divorces or similar numbers, noting that the wife can only effect what she has capacity for. Then it moves to the key ruling: if one witness testifies to 1000 and the other to 1500, and the plaintiff claims 1500, the testimony is accepted for the 1000 because they agree on that amount in word and meaning. The author emphasizes that the common part must be agreed upon both verbally and in meaning (لفظاً ومعنى). The reason is that '1000' is not the same wording as '2000' and the former is not implicitly proven because the larger amount is not established. This is contrasted with an example where one witnesses to 1000 and the other to '1000 and 1000' (i.e., 2000 expressed as two separate thousands), then the common 1000 is accepted. بإقرار فيما لا يحدث مثله وإن باع نسيئة فقال أمرتك بنقد وقال المأمور أطلقت فالقول للآمر لأن الأمر يستفاد من جهته ولا دلالة على الإطلاق. وفي كافي الحاكم: وإذا باع الوكيل العبد بخمسمائة فقال الآمر أمرتك بألف وقال أمرتك بدينار أو بحنطة أو شعير أو باعه بنسيئة فقال أمرتك بالحال فالقول قول الآمر، وكذلك هذا في النكاح والمكاتب والإجارة والعتق على مال ا هـ . ثم قال : ولو أمره أن يبيعه من فلان بكفيل فباعه بغير كفيل لم يجز، وإن قال الوكيل لم يأمرني بذلك فالقول للآمر ا هـ . فلو قال المؤلف لو اختلفا فيما عينه الموكل فالقول له لكان أولى ليشمل وكيل البيع والنكاح والإجارة والخلع والإعتاق والكتابة والمقدار والصفة من حلول وتأجيل والتقييد المقيد بمشتر ورهن وكفيل ووقت وقولي فيما عينه الموكل شامل لما إذا ادعى الموكل التقييد والوكيل الإطلاق، وما إذا ادعى الموكل تعيين شيء وادعى الوكيل تعيين آخر. قيد الاختلاف في الإطلاق والتقييد لأن الوكيل بالبيع إذا ادعى البيع وقبض الثمن وهلاكه وادعاه المشتري وكذبهما الآمر فالوكيل يصدق مع يمينه فإن كان الأمر قد مات فقال ورثته لم يبعه وقال الوكيل قد بعته من فلان بألف وقبضت الثمن وهلك وصدقه المشتري، فإن كان العبد قائماً بعينه لم يصدق الوكيل على البيع إلا أن تقوم بينة أنه باعه في حياة الأمر، فإن لم تكن له بينة رد البيع وضمن الوكيل المال للمشتري، وإن كان العبد مستهلكاً فالوكيل مصدق بعد الحلف استحسن ذلك، وإن قال الأمر قد أخرجتك من الوكالة وقال الوكيل قد بعته أمس لم يصدق الوكيل ولو أقر الوكيل بالبيع لإنسان بعينه فقال الأمر قد أخرجتك من الوكالة جاز البيع إذا ادعى ذلك المشتري ؛ كذا في كافي الحاكم وإنما يصدق الوكيل في البيع وقبض الثمن وهلاكه عنده إذا كان المبيع مسلماً في يده، فإن كان في يد البائع فلا وتمامه في البزازية وفيها أيضاً وكيل العتق قال أعتقته أمس وكذبه موكله لا يعتق. وكيل البيع قال بعته أمس وكذبه موكله فالقول للوكيل الوكيل بالكتابة وقبض بدلها إذا قال كاتبت وقبضت بدلها فالقول له في الكتابة لا في قبض بدلها أما لو قال كاتبته ثم قال قبضت بدلها ودفعت إلى الموكل فهو صحيح مصدق لأنه أمين ا هـ . وتقدم الاختلاف بين وكيل الشراء وموكله .

والهلاك، ولا يقبل قول وكيل النكاح والوكيل بقبض الذين إذا ادعى القبض والهلاك مصدق. وفي خزانة المفتين : وكل رجلاً بأن يشتري أخاه فاشترى فقال الأمر ليس هذا أخي فالقول له مع يمينه لأنه ينكر وجوب الثمن عليه ويلزم الشراء للوكيل لكن يعتق بقوله هذا أخوك ا.هـ. وفي كافي الحاكم في باب الوكالة بالعتق : وإن وكله أن يكاتب عبده يوم الجمعة فقال الوكيل يوم السبت قد كاتبته أمس بعد الوكالة على كذا وكذا وكذبه المولى فالقول للمولى في القياس ولكني أدع القياس وأجيزه، وكذلك البيع والإجارة والعتق على مال والخلع فإن الوكيل مصدق، ولو وكله أن يكاتبه فقال الوكيل وكلتني أمس وكاتبته آخر النهار بعد الوكالة وقال رب العبد إنما وكلتك اليوم فالقول قول رب العبد وتبطل المكاتبة، وكذلك البيع والنكاح والخلع والعتق ا هـ. وفي نكاح خزانة الأكمل : أمره بالنكاح ثم قال له ما أشهدت وقال الوكيل أشهدت يفرق بينهما وعليه نصف Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a commentary expanding on the rulings of agency for sale and purchase, specifically addressing disputes between principal and agent. The passage type is a series of rulings with underlying rational proofs, often citing earlier authorities like Kafi al-Hakim and al-Bazzaziyya. The author first establishes the principle: when the agent sells on credit but the principal claims he ordered cash, the principal's word is accepted because the order emanates from him and no evidence of absolute authority exists. This applies to variations in price, type of payment, and terms. The author then extends this to other contracts like marriage, kitaba, lease, and manumission for money. However, the agent's claim of having performed an act is treated differently depending on the context. For instance, if the agent claims he sold and received the price, he is believed only if the sold item was in his possession; otherwise, he is not. An important distinction is made between the agent's report before and after dismissal. The passage also covers the case of agency to pay a debt, where the agent cannot recover from the principal if the creditor denies payment, because the agent is considered a buyer of the debt from his own money. The text then summarises the rules for various agents: for sale, the agent's word is accepted when the item is consumed; for manumission, not accepted; for kitaba, accepted for the contract but not for receipt; for marriage, the agent's word is not accepted generally, but there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says the husband's word is taken, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the agent's word is taken. This dispute affects practical cases where the agent claims to have performed the marriage and the husband denies. The passage concludes with the statement that the agent is trusted in matters of receipt and loss because he is an amin (trustee). The overall method is to present the general principle, then detail exceptions based on istihsan (juristic preference) and the nature of the agent's role.

Agent’s Claim of Performance: Istihsan Over Qiyas
Explaining when the agent’s claim of performance is accepted and the role of istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain when the agent’s claim of having performed the act is accepted and how istihsan overrides qiyas in certain agency disputes.

Core Idea
In agency, the agent’s word is accepted for his own actions to facilitate transactions, overriding strict analogy.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses disputes where the agent claims to have performed the act (e.g., sold, manumitted, or made a kitaba contract) and the principal denies it. The Hanafi school applies a nuanced rule: initially, qiyas would favor the principal’s denial, but istihsan overrides this, leading to the agent’s word being accepted in many cases to avoid hardship and support trust in commercial dealings. The passage covers specific conditions like possession of the item, timing of the claim, and the type of agency.

Third-Party Compensation in Gift Revocation
Commentary on matn phrase 'valid from an ajnabi'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on compensation from a third party (ajnabi) to prevent gift revocation and identify when the third party has recourse against the donee.

Core Idea
A third-party compensation prevents gift revocation, but the payer has no recourse unless he guarantees repayment.

In this passage, the commentator explains that if a third party provides compensation to the donor to prevent revocation of a gift, the compensation is valid and the donor's right to revoke falls. However, the third party cannot seek reimbursement from the donee unless he expressly stipulated a guarantee. This ruling is contrasted with obligatory debts, where ordering payment creates a right of recourse. A general principle from al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah is presented to distinguish between enforceable and non-enforceable obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a third party (ajnabi) providing compensation to the donor of a gift to prevent revocation. The ruling: it is permissible (jāza) and the donor's right to revoke falls if the compensation is taken possession (qabd).
The compensation is valid even if the donee did not request it.
2
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The author analogizes to khul' (divorce by wife's compensation) and sulh 'an inkār (settlement of a denied claim). In those cases, a third party can provide compensation to drop a right. Here, the donor's right to revoke is dropped by compensation, so the analogy holds.
This is the proof for the validity of third-party compensation.
3
Note the default rule for recourse
The third party has no recourse (no right to reclaim) against the donee, even if the donee is his partner or if he paid with or without permission. This is because the compensation is not obligatory on the third party.
Exception: if the third party explicitly stipulates guarantee (daman), then he does have recourse.
4
Contrast with obligatory debts
If a debtor orders someone to pay his debt, the payer can seek recourse even without guarantee, because the debt is enforceable by law (imprisonment/coercion). Similarly, orders to spend on dependents or to ransom a captive create recourse.
The key difference: the underlying obligation is enforceable.
5
Apply the general principle from al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah
Whatever a person can be compelled to pay by imprisonment/coercion, ordering its payment gives the payer recourse without guarantee. Whatever cannot be compelled, ordering its payment does not give recourse unless guarantee is stipulated.
This principle helps decide cases like spending on building or captive redemption, which are exceptions to the rule and require careful reflection.

His saying: 'and is valid from an ajnabi' means compensation from an ajnabi is permissible and the donor's right to revoke falls when he takes possession, because compensation is to drop the right, so it is valid from an ajnabi like the substitute in khul' and sulh 'an inkār. He made it absolute, so it includes whether it is by the donee's command or not, and the compensator has no recourse against the donee, even if he is his partner, whether with his permission or not, because compensation is not obligatory on him. So it becomes like if he ordered him to donate to someone. Except if he said 'on condition that I am a guarantor'. This is unlike a debtor who ordered a man to pay his debt, where he can recourse even without guarantee, because the debt is obligatory on him. It is like his saying 'spend from your wealth on my dependents' or 'spend on building my house', or the captive who ordered a man to buy him and free him or pay the ransom and take from him, where he can recourse even without stipulating recourse. Mentioned by Qadikhan from kafalah bi'l-mal; its completion is in the Book of Zakat. In al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah, a good principle is mentioned for these issues, and it is the principle: whatever a person can be compelled to pay by imprisonment and coercion, ordering its payment is a cause for recourse without stipulating guarantee; and whatever a person cannot be compelled to pay, ordering its payment is not a cause for recourse except with a stipulation of guarantee. However, sometimes the order to spend on building and the order to buy a captive may be exceptions, so reflect.

Commentary
This passage explains that compensation from a third party (ajnabi) is valid to prevent the donor's revocation, and the third party does not have recourse against the donee unless he stipulated guarantee. This is because compensation is a voluntary act, not an obligation. The passage contrasts this with obligatory debts: if a debtor orders someone to pay his debt, the payer can demand reimbursement even without a guarantee, because the debt is enforceable. The passage then extracts a general principle from al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah: any payment that the person can be forced to make through legal coercion, if he orders another to pay it, the payer can seek recourse; if not, he cannot unless a guarantee was stipulated. This principle applies to gifts and compensations, with some exceptions needing careful consideration.

Key Terms
أجنبي (ajnabi): A third party who is a stranger to the gift transaction, not the donor or donee.
الرجوع (al-ruju'): The right to reclaim compensation from the donee (recourse) or the right of the donor to revoke the gift.
ضمان (daman): Guarantee or binding promise to be liable for the compensation.

Textual Criticism in Hanafi Fiqh: Al-Ramli's Note on Detention
Super-commentary interpreting phrase 'prefer prohibition' as meaning no right of detention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a scribal error in a legal text and explain how the correction alters the ruling on detention.

Core Idea
A single missing word can reverse a legal ruling — textual criticism is essential.

This marginal note from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq demonstrates the careful textual work that Hanafi scholars performed. Al-Ramli examines the phrase 'wa yanbaghi tarjih al-man' and suggests it may be a scribal corruption. Understanding such notes helps you appreciate how rulings are preserved and corrected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed phrase
Locate the phrase 'wa yanbaghi tarjih al-man' in the source. Understand its apparent meaning: 'it is appropriate to prefer prohibition'.
The phrase appears in the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq in the section on Judicial Matters.
2
Understand the broader legal context
Recall that the commentator (Ibn Nujaym) previously stated in Kitab al-Qada' that detention (habs) is prohibited. The current phrase should align with that position.
Consistency within a work is an important tool for textual criticism.
3
Analyze al-Ramli's suggested emendation
Al-Ramli proposes that the intended phrase might be 'tarjih 'adam al-man' (preferring absence of prohibition). He argues that the word 'adam was dropped by a copyist.
This changes the meaning from 'prefer prohibition' to 'prefer non-prohibition'.
4
Determine the legal effect
If the original phrase is kept, detention is prohibited. If the emendation is accepted, detention is not prohibited (i.e., it becomes permissible).
The ruling on the worker's right to detain the object switches.
5
Apply the method to other texts
Practice reading marginal notes in Hanafi commentaries and ask: Does the main text conflict with an earlier position? Could a scribal error be present?
This skill is vital for accurate fatwa and scholarly research.

Commentary
This is a marginal note (hashiya) by al-Ramli commenting on the author's phrase 'wa yanbaghi tarjih al-man' 'and it is appropriate to prefer prohibition.' Al-Ramli suggests a textual corruption: the commentator (al-Sharih, i.e., Ibn Nujaym) had earlier in the book on Judicial Matters (Kitab al-Qada') that detention (habs) is prohibited (man'). So perhaps the intended phrase here should be 'prefer the absence of prohibition' (tarjih 'adam al-man'), meaning that the worker does not have the right of detention. The copyist may have dropped the word 'adam (absence). Alternatively, the meaning is simply 'preference' without specifying. The note attempts to reconcile the statement with the author's earlier position.

Key Terms
ترجيح المنع (tarjih al-man'): Preferring prohibition; here the marginal note suggests it might be a scribal error for 'absence of prohibition'.
عدم المنع ('adam al-man'): Absence of prohibition; the emended reading that changes the legal implication.
الحبس (al-habs): Detention; the legal issue under discussion regarding a worker's right to detain an object.
