Judge Appointing a Representative for an Absent Party: Three Cases
Three scenarios of judge appointing representative for absent party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios in which a Hanafi judge appoints a representative for an absent party and identify the relied-upon opinion (mufta bihi) for each.

Core Idea
A judge can appoint a representative for an absent party to prevent harm, but the ruling depends on the case and transmission strength.

In the Book of Hawalah, we encounter three situations where a party's absence creates a legal problem: a debtor at risk of breaking an oath, a buyer unable to return goods, and a guarantor unable to deliver the principal debtor to the creditor. In each, the judge may intervene by appointing a representative for the absent party. The rulings, however, vary based on the circumstances and the authority of the narrations. This lesson unpacks each scenario, the differing opinions within the Hanafi school, and the operative position for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Scenarios
Read the passage and list the three distinct cases: (1) debtor with a conditional oath, (2) buyer with an option to return, (3) guarantor who personally guarantees to produce the debtor by a deadline.
2
Analyze the Legal Problem in Each
For each scenario, determine the harm that absence causes: perjury (hanth) for the debtor, inability to exercise the option for the buyer, immediate liability for the guarantor.
3
Extract the Judge's Intervention
Note what action the judge takes: appoints a representative, gives notice, or refuses intervention. Observe that in the first case the judge appoints; in the second there is disagreement; in the third the judge may appoint only in a weak narration.
4
Map the Ikhtilaf
For the buyer case, there are two narrations from Imam Muhammad—one where the judge gives notice before annulling, another where no notice is given. For the guarantor case, the ruling that discharge is possible is contrary to the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) and is attributed only to Abu Yusuf.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
State the relied-upon position: (1) the judge appoints a representative for the debtor's case and the debtor is not perjured—this is the fatwa; (2) for the buyer, the more cautious opinion is to give notice before annulment; (3) for the guarantor, the zahir al-riwaya holds that the guarantor remains liable unless he can deliver the debtor—the Abu Yusuf narration is not operative.

Commentary
This passage presents three distinct scenarios from the Book of Hawalah, all involving the appointment of a representative by the judge when a party is absent. In the first case, a debtor has made a conditional oath (either freeing a slave or divorcing his wife) if he does not pay by the end of the day. The creditor then disappears, putting the debtor in danger of breaking his oath. The Hanafi ruling is that the judge appoints a representative for the absent creditor, pays the debt to that representative, and the debtor does not break his oath. This is the relied-upon fatwa. In the second case, a buyer has a right to return a purchase (khiyar al-shart), but the seller hides. The buyer asks the judge to appoint a representative for the seller. The passage presents two opinions: one in favor (looking to the buyer's benefit) and one against (since the buyer failed to protect himself). If the judge does not appoint, there are two narrations from Imam Muhammad about giving notice ('udhr) to the seller via a caller. In one narration, the judge gives notice and if the seller does not appear, the judge may annul the sale, but not without notice. In the other, no notice is given. The third case involves a guarantor (kafil) who personally guarantees to produce the debtor by the next day, otherwise the debt becomes his. If the creditor disappears, the guarantor is liable. However, if the guarantor goes to the judge and the judge appoints a representative for the creditor, the guarantor is discharged. This ruling is not from the Zahir al-Riwayah but from some narrations of Abu Yusuf. All three cases illustrate the role of the judge in facilitating transactions and preventing harm when one party is absent.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawalah): Transfer of debt from one debtor to another, or the chapter title covering the legal rulings on hawalah.
وكيل (wakil): A representative appointed by the judge to act on behalf of an absent party to receive debt or handle legal matters.
الحنث (al-hinth): Breaking an oath, here specifically the debtor's conditional oath, which is avoided by paying through the judge's representative.
خيار (khiyar): The buyer's option to rescind a sale, here specifically khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation) within a set period.
كفيل (kafil): A guarantor who undertakes to produce the debtor or pay the debt if the debtor fails.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The apparent/authoritative narrations from Imam Muhammad's six books; rulings contrary to these are from other narrations.
الإعذار (al-i'dhar): Formal notice given by the judge to an absent party, via a caller, alerting them of a pending action (e.g., annulment of sale).

Procedure When Opponent Hides: Judge's Call and Representative
Ruling and its expansion by commentators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure when an opponent hides and explain when the judge's appointment of a representative is necessary.

Core Idea
When an opponent hides, the judge sends a trustee to call for three days before appointing a representative.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling for a judge when a defendant goes into hiding. The passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq outlines the procedure established by Abu Yusuf based on istihsan. We will also explore the nuances of when the claimant can proceed without a representative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedure
The judge sends a trustworthy person (amin) to call the opponent at his door for three days, then appoints a representative (wakil) for him.
2
Distinguish between types of claims
If the claimant wants to take specific property (ayn) from the opponent's hand, the opponent's presence is required. But if claiming from the price of property, no representative is needed.
This distinction is key.
3
Understand the commentator's expansion
Abu al-Sa'ud argues the procedure is not limited to the hidden opponent case, but the author restricts it because the earlier cases were time-sensitive.
This is an expansion probe.
4
Apply to other scenarios
The text analogizes to the mutawalli of a waqf and one heir representing others, provided the property (ayn) is in hand.
Example of application.

Commentary
This passage first presents the Hanafi ruling according to Abu Yusuf, who practiced it as an istihsan: when an opponent hides, the judge sends a trustee to call at his door for three days, then appoints a representative for him. This procedure is specific. However, the commentator Abu al-Sa'ud argues that this case is not exclusive; the judge can appoint a representative in other similar situations as well. But the responder (the main author) objects, noting that the previous three cases were time-sensitive (the oath would be broken, the return period would expire, etc.), which warranted immediate action, whereas here the procedure is more deliberate. The passage also clarifies that the need for the opponent's presence is only for claims of specific property; for claims against an absent person's property that is in the claimant's hand, no representative is needed. It further discusses that a partner in debt can represent the other in inheritance, confirming the opinion of Abu Yusuf (istihsan) preferred by Muhammad over Abu Hanifa's qiyas. The passage ends with examples from waqf where one beneficiary can represent others.

Key Terms
التوارى (al-tawarri): Hiding or disappearance of the opponent, making it difficult for the claimant to proceed with the lawsuit.
أمين (amin): A trustworthy person (trustee) sent by the judge to call the hidden opponent.
وكيل (wakil): A representative appointed by the judge for the absent opponent to facilitate the lawsuit.

Can One Partner in Debt Represent the Other? The Hanafi Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on representation of a partner in debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's qiyas and Abu Yusuf's istihsan regarding a partner in debt representing the other, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A partner in debt can represent the other in inheritance by consensus, and in other claims per Abu Yusuf's istihsan, which is the relied-upon view.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether one partner in a debt can represent the other in a claim. The passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq reports that Abu Hanifa rejected representation beyond inheritance, while Abu Yusuf allowed it based on istihsan, and Muhammad sided with Abu Yusuf. The practical consequence is that a single partner can sue and bind the others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed and disputed cases
In inheritance, one partner can represent the other by consensus. In other matters, there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa says no, Abu Yusuf says yes.
This is the core of the dispute.
2
Understand the proof methods
Abu Hanifa's view is based on qiyas (strict analogy): representation requires explicit authorization. Abu Yusuf uses istihsan (juristic preference) considering necessity and common interest.
Muhammad labels Abu Hanifa's as qiyas and Abu Yusuf's as istihsan.
3
Identify the tarjih
Muhammad states that he is with Abu Yusuf, thus establishing the mufta bihi position.
Tarjih based on Muhammad's endorsement.
4
Apply to related cases
The passage also gives examples: one who holds the deceased's property can represent even without being executor, and one waqf beneficiary can represent others if the waqf is a single entity.
These follow the same principle of istihsan.

Claim Against Absent as Cause for Claim Against Present: Six Cases
Detailed ruling with six subcases illustrating the principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that a claim against an absent party can serve as the legal cause (sabab) for a claim against a present party, and apply it to the six subcases provided.

Core Idea
When the claim against the absent is the cause of the claim against the present, judgment binds both.

In Hanafi litigation, a plaintiff may sometimes sue a present party to indirectly establish a claim against an absent party. This is only permissible when the claim against the absent is the legal cause (sabab) for the claim against the present. In this lesson, we examine the principle through six subcases divided into two categories: cases where both claims concern the same thing, and cases where the claims are different but causally linked.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Principle
State the rule: when the claim against the absent is the cause for the claim against the present, judgment is given against both, and the present party stands as a representative (khasm) for the absent.
This is not absolute; it only applies when the causal link is established.
2
Distinguish the Two Categories
The first category covers cases where the two claims are the same thing (e.g., a house claimed from both). The second covers cases where the claims are different but the absent's liability is the cause for the present claim.
In the first category, the present party's liability is directly tied to the absent's; in the second, the link is through a legal relationship.
3
Examine the First Category Subcases
List and understand the three subcases: (1) purchase of property from absent owner, (2) guarantee (kafalah) for a debt owed by absent, (3) pre-emption (shuf'ah) based on purchase from absent.
The absolute guarantee (kafalah mutlaqah) can be used as a stratagem to prove a debt against the absent.
4
Examine the Second Category Subcases
List the three subcases: (1) false accusation (qadhf) where the accused proves he was freed by absent master, (2) challenging a witness's status by proving absent owner emancipated him, (3) murder where one heir proves the absent heir forgave his share.
These cases involve different claims but a causal link between the absent's action and the present claim.
5

This shows the principle has limits when the result is uncertain.

Accepting Testimony Despite Differences on Hawala, Kafala, and Sale Terms
Rulings on testimony when witnesses differ on guarantee type or sale terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on when testimony is accepted despite witnesses differing on the legal form of a guarantee or on non-integral sale details.

Core Idea
Testimony is accepted if witnesses agree on the underlying debt, even if they differ on whether it was hawala or kafala.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents three scenarios where witnesses' testimonies appear to conflict but are accepted because they agree on the core fact. The Hanafi school prioritizes substance over form: as long as the existence of the debt or sale is undisputed, differing descriptions of the transaction's nature do not invalidate the testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common element
In each case, identify what the witnesses agree on: the existence of a debt (issue 29) or the fact of a sale (issues 30-31). This is the core fact that the plaintiff must establish.
The disagreement is only about the legal characterization (hawala vs kafala) or a non-integral detail (term, option).
2
Apply the principle of 'agreement on the essence'
The Hanafi rule is that testimony is accepted if the witnesses agree on the essence of the claim, even if they differ on secondary attributes. For hawala and kafala, both acknowledge the debt; for sale, the contract's existence is agreed.
This principle is derived from the purpose of testimony: to prove the right, not the precise legal form.
3
Distinguish from irreconcilable contradictions
Note the first line: if one witness says the debt is 1000 and another says 2000, they disagree on the amount—a core element—so testimony is rejected. Contrast this with the cases where the amount is agreed but the form differs.
This helps identify when a difference is material vs. immaterial.

The Legal Equivalence of Jirah and Wikalah
Commentary on acceptance of testimony with synonymous terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that jirah and wikalah are synonymous in Hanafi law and apply this principle to evaluate whether testimony is accepted when witnesses use differing but synonymous terms.

Core Idea
Jirah and wikalah are synonymous; testimony remains valid across varied terminology.

In Hanafi fiqh, the substance of testimony outweighs its form. When two witnesses describe the same legal transaction but one says 'jirah' and the other 'wikalah', the question arises: does the difference in wording invalidate their testimony? This lesson examines the ruling from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq that since the two terms are equivalent, the testimony is accepted. Understanding this principle helps clarify how Hanafi law treats linguistic variation in legal contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Two witnesses testify: one says the debtor transferred (hawala) the debt, the other says the debtor made him a jari (agent). The issue is whether the testimony is accepted despite the different words.
The scenario involves hawala, but the principle applies broadly to synonymous terms.
2
Analyze the synonymity argument
The commentator cites Sharh Adab al-Qadi: 'al-jirah wa al-wikalah sawa' — jirah and wikalah are the same. Jari and wakil are thus identical in legal effect.
This is a key qawl (statement) from a Hanafi source.
3
Apply to the ruling
Since the two terms mean the same thing, the witnesses agreed on the underlying fact. The difference in wording does not harm the testimony; it is accepted.
The ruling is that testimony is accepted when meaning is consistent.
4
Extend the principle
This principle extends to other synonymous terms in fiqh. As long as witnesses refer to the same legal event, their testimony is valid despite verbal variation.
For example, 'bai' and 'shira' both refer to sale.
