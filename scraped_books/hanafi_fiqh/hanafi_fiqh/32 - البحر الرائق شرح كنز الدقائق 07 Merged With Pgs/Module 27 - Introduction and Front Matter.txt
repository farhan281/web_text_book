The Making of Minhah al-Khaliq: From Marginal Notes to a Standard Hashiah
Biographical preface explaining the compilation history

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author and compiler of Minhah al-Khaliq and explain the process by which the hashiah was created.

Core Idea
Minhah al-Khaliq was born from Ibn Abidin's marginal notes, compiled posthumously by his nephew.

This passage is the author's preface to the hashiah 'Minhah al-Khaliq' on Ibn Nujaym's al-Bahr al-Raiq. It tells the story of how the original marginal notes were written by the great Hanafi jurist Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin (the uncle) while studying with his teacher. After Ibn Abidin's death, his nephew Ahmad bin Abd al-Ghani gathered these notes into the present work. Understanding this backstory helps us appreciate the chain of transmission and the scholarly effort behind a crucial Hanafi reference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original author of the marginal notes
The passage refers to 'our shaykh, my late master and uncle, the author of this hashiah.' This uncle is Muhammad Amin bin Umar bin Abidin, known as Ibn Abidin, the famous Hanafi jurist. He wrote the marginal notes during his study with al-Sayyid Sa'id al-Halabi.
Ibn Abidin's full name is Muhammad Amin bin Umar bin Abd al-Aziz bin Abidin.
2
Understand the sources used
Ibn Abidin's notes drew from several key works: the commentary 'al-Nahr' by the brother of the original commentator, the hashiah of Khayr al-Din al-Ramli, and other books he had collected. This shows the depth of his research.
The brother of the commentator of al-Bahr al-Raiq (Ibn Nujaym) wrote al-Nahr al-Faiq.
3
Recognize the compiler's role
After Ibn Abidin died in 1252 AH, his nephew Ahmad bin Abd al-Ghani compiled the notes into a single volume to prevent them from being lost. He also added a preface and drafted two booklets in his own handwriting.
The nephew is also known as al-Sayyid Ahmad bin Abd al-Ghani bin Umar Abidin.

Commentary
This is the preface (khutbah) to the hashiah 'Minhah al-Khaliq' by al-Sayyid Ahmad bin Abd al-Ghani bin Umar Abidin, the nephew of the famous Hanafi jurist Ibn Abidin (Muhammad Amin bin Umar abidin). The preface explains the history of the hashiah: the original marginal notes were written by his uncle (the author of the hashiah, i.e., Ibn Abidin himself) while studying the text al-Bahr al-Raiq with his teacher al-Sayyid Sa'id al-Halabi. The uncle compiled comments from various sources: from the commentary al-Nahr (by the brother of the original commentator of al-Bahr al-Raiq), from the hashiah of Khayr al-Din al-Ramli, and from other works. He intended to compile these notes into an independent volume but died in 1252 before doing so. His nephew, the writer of this preface, then undertook the task of collecting and organizing these marginal notes into the present hashiah. The preface includes prayers and praises, and details the method of compilation.

Key Terms
الحاشية (al-hashiyah): A marginal commentary or super-commentary on a primary work; here, 'Minhah al-Khaliq' is a hashiah on 'al-Bahr al-Raiq' by Ibn Nujaym.
منحة الخالق (Minhah al-Khaliq): The name of the hashiah, meaning 'The Gift of the Creator.' It is a super-commentary on Ibn Nujaym's al-Bahr al-Raiq.
الشيخ خير الدين الرملي (Shaykh Khayr al-Din al-Ramli): A prominent Hanafi jurist (d. 1081 AH) known for his fatwa collection and hashiah on al-Bahr al-Raiq.

The Scholarly Transmission of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: From Ibn Nujaym to the Printed Edition
Colophon and biographical notes on authorship and transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key figures in the transmission of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and explain the role of glosses (hashiya) in Hanafi scholarship.

Core Idea
A Hanafi text's authority depends on its chain of transmission and scholarly commentary.

This passage from the end of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is more than a closing remark—it documents the living chain of Hanafi scholarship. We see the author Ibn Nujaym complete his work, then his work is enriched by a hashya (gloss) from his student's shaykh, later compiled by Ibn Abidin's son, and finally printed in Cairo. Understanding this chain helps you assess the authority of the rulings in the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the final ruling and its significance
Locate the ruling on invalid Ijarah about not exceeding the named rent. Note how even at the end of a massive work, Ibn Nujaym adds a precise legal point.
This shows the thoroughness of Hanafi jurists.
2
Trace the author and his work
Recognize Ibn Nujaym as the author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. Note the praise and colophon.
Ibn Nujaym is a key figure in later Hanafi fiqh.
3
Follow the hashya's origin and compilation
Read Ahmad ibn Abd al-Ghani's account: his shaykh (the 'uncle') wrote marginal notes on Al-Bahr, drawing from al-Nahr, al-Ramli, and his own ijtihad. After the shaykh's death in 1252 AH, Ahmad compiled these into Minhat al-Khaliq.
This illustrates the collaborative nature of Hanafi scholarship.
4
Note the printed edition's history
Muhammad al-Zahri al-Ghamrawi's colophon states the printing was completed in 1333 AH and mentions that the takmila by al-Turi follows.
The 1333 AH edition is a standard reference.
5
Reflect on the chain's significance
Each step—author, glossator, compiler, printer—adds a layer of authentication and refinement. This chain is analogous to an isnad in hadith.
The chain ensures that the text we use today is reliable.

Commentary
This passage comprises the concluding sections of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq by Ibn Nujaym, including the final legal note on Ijarah, followed by extensive biographical and editorial remarks. The first part (page 531) completes the chapter on invalid Ijarah with a ruling: if a named rental amount is stipulated, the lessor should not exceed it. Then the author's colophon praises Allah and mentions the completion of the book. Next, Ahmad ibn Abd al-Ghani ibn Umar Abidin (known as Ibn Abidin) provides a detailed account of how his shaykh (the 'uncle') wrote the hashya titled Minhat al-Khaliq on the margins of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. He explains that the hashya was derived from the commentary of Ibn Nujaym's brother (al-Nahr), from Khayr al-Din al-Ramli, and from his own reasoning. The hashya was compiled after the shaykh's death in 1252 AH. Ibn Abidin then collected these marginal notes into a separate work to preserve them. Finally, Muhammad al-Zahri al-Ghamrawi, head of the correction committee at the Great Arabic Book House in Cairo, writes a colophon for the printed edition, praising Ibn Nujaym and Ibn Abidin, and noting that the eighth volume (takmila) by al-Turi follows, starting from the Book of Ijarah, not from where Ibn Nujaym stopped. This front matter provides essential historical context for the work's transmission and publication.

Key Terms
ابن نجيم (Ibn Nujaym): Zayn al-Din Ibn Nujaym, the author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a Hanafi commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq.
منحة الخالق (Minhat al-Khaliq): The hashya (gloss) on Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq written by Ibn Abidin's shaykh, referred to as 'the uncle'.
ابن عابدين (Ibn Abidin): Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin (d. 1252 AH) is the famous later Hanafi jurist; here his son Ahmad ibn Abd al-Ghani is the compiler of the hashya.
