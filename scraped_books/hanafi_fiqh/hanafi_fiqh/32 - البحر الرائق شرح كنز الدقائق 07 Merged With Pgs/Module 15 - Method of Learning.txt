The Hanafi Interpretation of 'Every Mujtahid is Correct'
Defends Abu Hanifa and explains the mu'tamad position on ijtihad and error.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi interpretation of 'kull mujtahid musib' and explain why it does not imply Mu'tazilism.

Core Idea
Every mujtahid is correct in ijtihad but the truth with Allah is one; error is rewarded.

This passage defends Imam Abu Hanifa against the accusation of Mu'tazilism by clarifying that his statement 'kull mujtahid musib' means every mujtahid is rewarded for his effort, not that every conclusion is equally true. The text provides the Hanafi usul position and refutes the alternative reading through logical proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and accusation
Some scholars accused Abu Hanifa of Mu'tazilism based on a reported statement 'kull mujtahid musib'. Understand what the accusation is.
The Mu'tazila held that every mujtahid is correct in an absolute sense.
2
Understand the Hanafi interpretation
The author explains that 'musib' refers to correctness in ijtihad effort, not hitting the actual divine ruling. The truth with Allah is one.
This interpretation avoids contradiction with the unity of truth.
3
Analyze the proof from contradiction
The text argues that literal reading would be contradictory because 'the truth with Allah is one' would be violated if multiple mujtahids could be correct in the absolute sense.
Logical proof: if A (truth is one) and B (every mujtahid is absolutely correct) then B entails multiple truths, contradicting A. So B cannot be literal.
4
Refute the Mu'tazili accusation
The text shows that Abu Hanifa's criticism of Ibn Abi Layla demonstrates that he recognized error, proving he did not hold the Mu'tazili view.
The Imam's description of the qadi's action as 'zulm' (deviation) indicates error.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: The Hanafi view allows that an erroneous mujtahid is rewarded and his ruling is considered a valid ijtihad, even if not the correct divine ruling. This promotes respect for scholarly disagreement.
In contrast, the Mu'tazili view leads to relativism of truth.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed defense and explanation of Imam Abu Hanifa's statement criticizing a qadi (Ibn Abi Layla) for taking a surety (kafil) from an heir, calling it 'oppression (zulm)'. The author, Zayla'i, clarifies that this criticism does not mean the qadi is sinful, but rather that his ijtihad led to error. The text addresses the famous principle 'every mujtahid is correct (kull mujtahid musib)' which some attributed to Abu Hanifa, causing accusations of Mu'tazilism (who held that every mujtahid is correct in the sense that error is impossible). The commentator explains that Abu Hanifa actually held that a mujtahid can err and be correct – correct in his ijtihad effort even if he misses the actual divine truth. The proof is that if taken literally, the statement 'the truth with Allah is one' would contradict multiplicity of truths. So 'musib' means 'correct in ijtihad', not in absolute terms. The text then refutes the allegation that the Sahibayn's view on taking kafil implies Mu'tazilism, showing that their ijtihad is based on valid analogy. The discussion concludes that describing the act as 'oppression' does not make the qadi a sinner, because he is a mujtahid and rewarded even for error, unless he was negligent. The historical anecdote from Manaqib al-Kardari confirms that Abu Hanifa persistently criticized Ibn Abi Layla until the caliph dismissed him. Finally, the author notes that in the chapter on nafaqah, the kafil is for money, but he is unsure whether the Sahibayn's kafalah in this issue is for money or for the person (i.e., guaranteeing the heir's presence).

Key Terms
كل مجتهد مصيب (kull mujtahid musib): Every mujtahid is correct in his ijtihad (effort) and rewarded, even if he does not attain the actual divine ruling. The truth with Allah is one.
الاعتزال (al-i'tizal): Mu'tazilism; the accusation here is based on taking 'kull mujtahid musib' literally, which would imply that every ijtihad is equally valid, a Mu'tazili view.

The Meaning of Zulm in the Context of Ijtihad and Qadi Criticism
Explains the technical meaning of zulm and defends the qadi's reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi concept of 'zulm' as 'placing something in the wrong place' and how it applies to a mujtahid qadi's action without implying sin.

Core Idea
Zulm in the Imam's criticism means deviation, not sinful oppression, because the mujtahid is excused.

This passage clarifies that when Imam Abu Hanifa called a qadi's action 'zulm', he did not mean the qadi was sinful. Rather, it was a metaphorical expression indicating error. The text shows how to reconcile the Imam's criticism with the principle that a mujtahid is rewarded even if mistaken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the description and context
Imam Abu Hanifa described the qadi's act of taking a kafil as 'zulm'. Understand the historical context: Ibn Abi Layla was a mujtahid qadi in Kufa.
The criticism is about a specific ijtihad, not about the person's character.
2
Define zulm in Hanafi legal usage
The author explains that 'zulm' here means deviation (al-mayl 'an sawa' al-sabil) or placing something in the wrong place (wad' al-shay' fi ghayr mawdi'ih). It is not necessarily sinful injustice.
This technical definition allows the term to be used for error.
3
Understand the context of the qadi's ijtihad
The qadi was a mujtahid; his action was based on ijtihad. The text argues that if a mujtahid errs, he is rewarded, so calling it 'zulm' cannot mean he is sinful.
The reward for ijtihad negates sin.
4
See the refutation of the sin implication
The author refutes the idea that the description implies sin by quoting the Imam's own question: 'If he did not find a kafil, would I prevent him from his right?' This shows the Imam was concerned about justice, not condemning.
The criticism is a technical observation of error, not a moral judgment.
5
Appreciate the adab of criticizing fellow mujtahids
The text from al-Talwih states that criticizing a mujtahid for error is acceptable if the evidence is clear in the critic's view, but the mujtahid is excused and not called misguided.
This adab is important for maintaining respect among scholars.
