Retraction of Witnesses in Qisas Cases
Explains ruling and Shafi'i dispute on retraction after qisas execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafis impose diyah instead of qisas on witnesses who retract after execution, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Doubt (shubhah) blocks qisas but not liability for property; retracting witnesses pay diyah, not death.

When witnesses retract after a qisas execution, the Hanafi school holds them liable for diyah (blood money) but not qisas (retaliation). This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this ruling, contrasting it with the Shafi'i position that qisas should apply. By examining concepts of causation (sabab) and doubt (shubhah), you will understand why Hanafi jurists distinguish between personal and property liability.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the explicit ruling from the matn: the witnesses are not subject to qisas but must pay the diyah of the executed person.
The ruling applies whether the testimony was about life or limb.
2
Examine the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i argues that qisas applies because the witnesses caused death indirectly, like a person who coerces another to kill.
The analogy is based on causation (tasabbub) being equivalent to direct action in some cases.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Hanafis respond that there is no causation because their testimony does not normally lead to death (pardon is recommended), and voluntary action severs causal attribution. Additionally, doubt (shubhah) prevents qisas but not property claims.
The doubt arises from the possibility that the witnesses might have been mistaken or that the guardian could have pardoned.
4
Understand the role of doubt (shubhah)
In Hanafi usul, shubhah blocks severe punishment (hadd/qisas) but allows property liability. This is a key distinction from the Shafi'i approach.
This principle is central to many Hanafi rulings in criminal law.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical outcome: under Hanafi law, the witnesses pay diyah; under Shafi'i, they could face qisas. The Hanafi view prevails due to the strength of the doubt argument.
The passage also notes intra-Hanafi differences about recourse between witnesses and the guardian.

His statement: (And in qisas, the diyah, and they are not subjected to qisas) i.e., the two witnesses of qisas become liable upon their retraction after execution for the diyah of the person testified against, and they are not subjected to qisas. Al-Shafi'i said: They are subjected to qisas because they caused death indirectly, so they resemble the coerced (mukrah), rather they are more deserving because the guardian (wali) is assisted, while the coerced is prevented. Our proof: Direct killing did not occur, nor indirect, because the cause (sabab) is that which normally leads to the result, and this does not normally lead because pardon (afw) is recommended, unlike the coerced who apparently preserves life; and because voluntary action cuts attribution; then there is at least doubt (shubhah) which prevents qisas, unlike property because it is established along with doubts. The ruling is general, covering whether the guardian retracted with them or not, but if he retracted with them, the guardian chooses between making the guardian liable for the diyah or the two witnesses, as if the person testified against came alive. Whichever is made liable does not have recourse against the other according to him (Abu Hanifa), but according to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) he has recourse because they were acting for him. They agreed on their recourse against him in cases of error (khata'). The proof from both sides is in al-Zayla'i's Sharh. And it includes cases where they testified regarding the person (nafs) or less than that. It is restricted to qisas because if they testified about pardon from qisas and then retracted, they do not become liable because qisas is not property. And if they testified that he settled (salaha) for deliberate bloodshed for a thousand and then retracted, they do not become liable regardless of which party denied the settlement. It is said: If the killer denies, the correct view is that they become liable for the thousand. The correct answer is the book's answer, and its completion is in al-Muhit, in which: They testified that he settled for twenty thousand while the killer denied, and the judge gave judgment, then they retracted; they become liable for the excess over the diyah. It is said: The correct view is that they become liable for the entire amount. The student said: 'I settled with you for a thousand,' and the opponent said: 'No, rather for five hundred.' The statement is that of the defendant.

Commentary
This passage explains the Hanafi ruling on retraction of witnesses in qisas cases after the execution has taken place. The matn states: 'In qisas, the diyah, and they are not subjected to qisas.' The commentator clarifies that when two witnesses retract after qisas execution, they must pay the diyah of the person executed, and they are not subjected to qisas in retaliation. This is the Hanafi position. The passage then presents the Shafi'i view, which argues for qisas against the witnesses because they indirectly caused death, similar to coercion. The Hanafi response is that there is no direct or indirect causation because the death was not the likely result of their testimony, as pardon is encouraged. Moreover, the presence of doubt (shubhah) prevents qisas, but property rights (diyah) are not negated by doubt. The commentator also discusses variations: if the guardian (wali) retracts along with the witnesses, the guardian may choose to hold the witnesses or himself liable. Recourse between them is also discussed according to Abu Hanifa and the two disciples. The ruling applies whether the testimony was about life or limb. Additional details: testimony about pardon from qisas does not entail liability, but testimony about a settlement involving money may entail liability for the excess over diyah. The passage ends with an example of conflicting claims about the amount of settlement, where the defendant's statement is taken with an oath.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retributive punishment in kind, e.g., death for intentional killing
دية (diyah): Blood money paid in lieu of qisas for homicide or bodily harm
شبهة (shubhah): Doubt or ambiguity that prevents application of hadd or qisas
سبب (sabab): Cause that normally leads to a legal effect
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion that negates free will

Consequences of Nukul in Qisas and Oath Procedures
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nukul in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the effect of nukul in qisas claims and distinguish between oaths on the cause versus the result.

Core Idea
Nukul in qisas: Abu Hanifa leads to imprisonment/retaliation; Sahibayn only compensation, because nukul is a confession with doubt.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines what happens when a defendant refuses to swear an oath (nukul) in a claim for retaliation (qisas). The Hanafi school has an internal dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that for murder, the defendant is imprisoned until he confesses or swears, and for injuries, retaliation is carried out. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that nukul is only a confession with doubt, so only financial compensation is due. The passage also details the two methods of administering oaths—on the cause or on the result—and applies them to different types of claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Central Dispute
Recognize that the disagreement is about the legal effect of nukul in qisas. Abu Hanifa treats nukul as sufficient to establish qisas for injuries, and for murder, as grounds for imprisonment to compel confession or oath. The Sahibayn treat nukul as a confession with doubt, so only compensation is owed.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, both positions are within the school.
2
Examine the Basis of Each Position
Note that Abu Hanifa's view is based on treating limbs like property, so retaliation for injuries can be executed. For murder, imprisonment is used because there is doubt. The Sahibayn's view is based on the principle that nukul is 'iqrar fihi shubhah' (confession with doubt), which cannot support the severe penalty of qisas.
The underlying legal reasoning (ta'lil) is key to understanding the difference.
3
Analyze the Manner of Oath-Taking
Distinguish between the two narrations: oath on the result (al-ḥāṣil) denies the existence of the right, while oath on the cause (al-sabab) denies the act itself. Understand when each is used based on the nature of the claim (financial vs. criminal).
The passage cites al-Khaniyah for these details.
4
Apply the Rulings to Different Scenarios
Consider how the ruling changes based on whether the claim is for murder or injury, and whether it is intentional or not. Also note the distinction made by Abu Yusuf regarding who is liable for diyah (defendant vs. third party).
This step tests your ability to operationalize the text.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the defendant may face indefinite imprisonment or immediate retaliation; under Sahibayn, only financial payment is required. This affects the defendant's rights and the plaintiff's remedy.
This is the real-world impact of the legal disagreement.

Commentary
This passage deals with the situation where a person denies a claim of retaliation (qisas) for murder or injury. According to Abu Hanifa, if the defendant refuses to take an oath (nukul) in a murder claim, he is not immediately subjected to retaliation but is imprisoned until he either confesses or swears. In claims for injuries less than death, retaliation is actually carried out upon his refusal. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they say that in both murder and injury, the defendant is only liable for financial compensation (arsh) because nukul is considered a confession with doubt, and retaliation requires certainty, so only monetary payment is due. They analogize to a case where the defendant confesses to manslaughter but the guardian claims intentional killing. The passage then discusses the manner of administering the oath: there are two narrations – one swears on the result (e.g., 'there is no right due to this blood'), the other on the cause (e.g., 'I did not kill him'). In cases involving financial claims like diyah for manslaughter, the oath is on the result. Abu Yusuf differentiates: if the blood money is owed by someone other than the defendant (e.g., the killer's tribe), the oath is on the cause; if it is owed by the defendant himself, it is on the result as in retaliation claims.

النكول إقرار فيه شبهة (al-nukūl iqrār fīhi shubhah): Refusal to swear is considered a confession with a degree of ambiguity
التحليف على الحاصل (al-taḥlīf ʿalā al-ḥāṣil): Administering an oath on the state of affairs (e.g., denying any right exists)
التحليف على السبب (al-taḥlīf ʿalā al-sabab): Administering an oath on the cause of action (e.g., denying the act itself)

The Form of Oath in Qisas and Diyat Claims: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath form in qisas/diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the oath on the result (al-yamin 'ala al-hasil) and the oath on the cause (al-yamin 'ala al-sabab) in qisas and diyat claims, and explain the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The oath in property-like claims is on the result; in retaliation claims, two views: on the result or on the cause.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on how the accused is made to swear in murder and injury claims. The core question is whether the defendant swears that the claimant has no right (oath on the result) or that he did not commit the act (oath on the cause). Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the correct legal procedure in qisas and diyat cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
The dispute is about the form of the oath (yamin) administered to the defendant in claims of murder (qisas) and injuries (diyat/arsh). Specifically, the wording of the oath differs.
The ruling itself (that the oath is wajib) is not disputed.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that in property-like claims (diyat, arsh), the oath is on the result: the defendant swears that the claimant has no right against him. In intentional murder claims (qisas), there are two narrations: one on the result, one on the cause.
The two narrations are both from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf distinguishes: when the right (e.g., diyat for accidental death) is due from a third party (like the 'aqila), the oath should be on the cause – the defendant swears he did not commit the act. When the right is directly on the defendant, he swears on the result as in qisas.
Abu Yusuf's view is based on the nature of the denial: denying the act vs. denying the right.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is the exact wording the defendant utters. Under Abu Hanifa’s result-based oath, the defendant says “You have no right against me”; under Abu Yusuf’s cause-based oath, he says “I did not kill him”. This affects the burden of proof and the claim’s framing.
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Yusuf in accidental death cases.
5
Note the tarjih
The passage does not give a clear tarjih (preference). However, the fact that Abu Yusuf’s view is explicitly mentioned suggests it is a significant alternative. In later Hanafi fatwa, both views are recognized, with a tendency to adopt Abu Yusuf’s for accidental deaths due to the involvement of the 'aqila.
Students should be aware that this is an unresolved ikhtilaf within the school.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq discusses the form of oath (yamin) in claims of qisas (retaliation) and diyat (blood money). The author begins by stating a general ruling: if the accused refuses to take an oath in a murder case, he is imprisoned until he confesses or swears; in injuries less than murder, retaliation is carried out. Then the text mentions that if the claimant says he has a present witness but demands an oath, he is not given the oath (this will be explained later). The passage then shifts to an important distinction: in bodily harm cases, if the defendant says 'cut off my hand' and the claimant does so, no compensation is due because the defendant's offer is operative, though it is not permissible due to lack of benefit. The author then cites Al-Khaniya, which provides two narrations on how the oath is administered in murder: one narration has the accused swear on the result (al-hasil), i.e., that the claimant has no right against him; the other has him swear on the cause (al-sabab), i.e., that he did not commit the act. In injuries other than murder, only the oath on the result is used. Then, for claims of accidental death or injury involving diyat or arsh (compensation), the oath is again on the result because it is a property claim. Abu Yusuf differs: he holds that where the right is due on someone other than the defendant (e.g., blood money in accidental death from the 'aqila), the oath should be on the cause. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but the text presents both views.

Sulh in Qisas and Diyat Cases: Validity and Conditions
Rulings on sulh regarding qisas and diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in intentional and accidental homicide, identify the conditions for its validity, and apply the consequences when conditions are not met.

Core Idea
Sulh in qisas is valid for any property amount; if the victim dies later, diyah from the killer's property is due.

Sulh (amicable settlement) is a central mechanism in Hanafi criminal law to avoid qisas (retaliation) and replace it with property compensation. This lesson unpacks the detailed rulings from Al-Bahr al-Raiq on when sulh is valid, what happens if the victim dies, how defects in the substitute are handled, and the special rules for accidental killings. Mastering these conditions is essential for applying fiqh in contemporary disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State that sulh in any criminal act with qisas is valid for any amount of property, large or small. This is the foundational principle.
Sulh converts a qisas claim into a property obligation.
2
Distinguish intentional vs. accidental liability
If the victim dies after sulh, the blood-money (diyah) is due from the killer's own property for intentional acts, and from the aqila for accidental acts, unless the sulh covered future consequences.
This affects who pays and from which assets.
3
Analyze the effect of death on sulh
If the victim dies from the injury after sulh, the sulh becomes invalid. The killer must pay diyah from his own property (for intentional) or from the aqila (for accidental).
The sulh is conditional on the victim surviving.
4
Understand rules for the substitute (badal)
The substitute must be valid as dower (sadaq). The killer may dispose of it before possession, but must pay its value if it perishes. The victim may return it for a major defect and claim value. If the substitute is a free person, full diyah is due.
These rules mirror those of marriage dower.
5
Recognize limitations and special cases
Sulh for accidental killing requires a specified consideration. Sulh for usufruct (residence, service) for a known period is valid, but not for unborn or unspecified produce. Sulh for wine or pig yields no obligation.
Some considerations are invalid, leading to diyah or no compensation.
