Judge's Authority to Lend Orphan's Wealth
Conditions and limits on judge lending orphan wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge may lend orphan's wealth and identify which transactions are prohibited for an executor or endowment supervisor.

Core Idea
A judge may only lend orphan's wealth when no better investment exists; executors and trustees are generally prohibited from lending.

This lesson examines the delicate balance between preserving orphan wealth and the duty of a judge to invest it productively. The Hanafi school permits a judge to lend orphan funds under strict conditions, prioritizing more beneficial investments like purchasing income-generating assets or mudarabah. We will explore the precise limits, compare the roles of different guardians, and note exceptions for emergencies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its General Ruling
Recognize that the act in question is the judge lending orphan's wealth. The default ruling is permissibility (mubah) but only under specific conditions; the judge does not have unlimited discretion.
2
List the Conditions for Lending
Extract from the text the key conditions: (1) no better investment available (purchase of asset or mudarabah), (2) borrower must be wealthy (mali'), (3) lending must be more beneficial than depositing, and (4) the judge must monitor the borrower and recover when solvent.
The condition 'no better investment' is primary and is derived from the phrase 'if he does not find something to buy or someone to do mudarabah'.
3
Compare with Other Guardians
Contrast the judge's authority with that of the executor (wasi), endowment supervisor (mutawalli), and father. The executor may deposit but not lend (except necessity); the mutawalli lends only at risk of personal liability; the father is prohibited from lending his child's wealth.
The text explicitly states: 'the executor may deposit but not lend' and 'the supervisor of the endowment does not have the right to lend the wealth of the mosque'.
4
Note Exceptions and Disagreements
Identify the exception for necessity (fire, plunder) where all guardians may lend by agreement. Also note the intra-madhhab difference on whether the executor can borrow orphan's wealth; the relied-upon view is prohibition.
The passage mentions 'if the executor borrows and profits... the most correct is that he cannot.'
5
Apply to a Practical Scenario
Using the conditions, evaluate a scenario: A judge has orphan cash, no mudarabah or asset for purchase, but a wealthy merchant requests a loan. The judge may lend because it is guaranteed and beneficial. If a poor person requests, the judge cannot lend.

Commentary
This passage discusses the extent of a judge's authority to lend the wealth of orphans, endowments, and absent persons. The author begins by citing the prohibition of a father lending from his minor child's wealth, but notes a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa that if the father takes it as a loan for himself, it is included in the prohibition. The judge must monitor borrowers and recover funds when they become solvent, as he cannot lend to the insolvent initially or leave wealth with them. The passage then expands to the judge's authority over endowment wealth (waqf), found objects (luqatah), and the property of absent persons. The judge may also seize property from a wasteful father and place it with a trustworthy person. A key condition is that the judge should only lend if there is no better investment, such as purchasing productive assets or entering into a mudarabah partnership. The text distinguishes between the judge's wider powers and those of an executor (wasi) or endowment supervisor (mutawalli), who are generally prohibited from lending but may deposit or sell on credit. There is a discussion on the grandfather's status, likening him to the father in permissibility. The passage concludes with exceptions for emergencies like fire or plunder, where lending is unanimously permitted, and a note on hiring out minors.

Key Terms
إقراض (iqrad): Lending money with the expectation of repayment; in this context, lending orphan/endowment wealth.
الوصي (al-wasi): The executor appointed by the father or judge to manage the minor's affairs.
المتولي (al-mutawalli): The supervisor of an endowment (waqf), responsible for its management.

Invalidity of Arbitrator's Ruling for Close Relatives and on Waqf
Ruling with proof by analogy (qiyas) and citation of fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two cases where an arbitrator's ruling is legally ineffective (void for immediate relatives, non-final for waqf) and apply the exceptions.

Core Idea
An arbitrator cannot rule for parents/children/wife; his waqf ruling does not settle the dispute.

This lesson examines two important limitations on the arbitrator's authority in Hanafi law: the prohibition of ruling in favor of close relatives and the non-finality of rulings on waqf. Both rules derive from the principle that an arbitrator holds a delegated judicial power subject to similar constraints. Understanding these limits is essential for evaluating the validity of arbitral decisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage presents two distinct rulings: (1) an arbitrator's ruling in favor of his ascendants, descendants, or wife is void (باطل); (2) his ruling on waqf does not remove the dispute (لا يرفع الخلاف). Note the difference in legal consequence: the first is outright void, the second is non-final.
The waqf ruling is not called void but is considered not binding if challenged.
2
Analyze the first ruling: prohibited relatives
The void ruling applies only to ascendants (parents/grandparents), descendants (children/grandchildren), and the wife. The ruling is analogized to a judge's disqualification. Ruling against them is valid. Siblings, their children, and paternal uncles are not included because testimony for them is allowed.
The key terms are أصول وفروع (ascendants and descendants).
3
Understand the second ruling: waqf
The correct view is that an arbitrator's ruling on waqf does not remove the dispute. If the case is brought to a judge who agrees with the arbitrator, the judge must rule anew on its bindingness, not merely enforce the prior decision.
This is based on al-Bazzaziyyah, a later fatwa source.
4
Note the exceptions and extensions
For the first ruling, exceptions include ruling for the father of one's wife (if alive) and the husband of one's daughter (if alive). For both rulings, the arbitrator retains the power to inform (e.g., of an admission or witness justice) but not to impose a final, binding ruling in these contexts.
The text contrasts informing (إخبار) with ruling (حكم).

Commentary

The Mutawalli: Agent or Executor? Intra-Hanafi Views
Discusses the legal nature of a waqf administrator (mutawalli) - whether he is a wakil or wasi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the legal nature of the mutawalli and state the thamarah al-khilaf regarding removal authority.

Core Idea
The mutawalli is a hybrid: agent during founder's life, executor after death; removal rights differ by view.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq probes a key intra-Hanafi dispute: is the mutawalli (waqf administrator) a wakil (agent) or a wasi (executor)? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani take different positions, with significant implications for who can remove the mutawalli and under what conditions. The commentator Ibn Nujaym offers a nuanced synthesis that bridges both views while acknowledging the practical differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Hanafi positions
Find in the passage the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the mutawalli's legal classification. Abu Yusuf sees him as an agent (removable by founder at will); Muhammad sees him as an agent for the beneficiaries.
The text states: 'على قول أبي يوسف... وأما على قول محمد فهو وكيل عن الموقوف عليهم'.
2
Examine the evidence for Muhammad's view
Muhammad's reasoning is that the mutawalli works for the benefit of the mawquf alayhim, so he should be considered their agent. Understand the logical implication: if he is their agent, they could remove him.
The author questions this implication.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response to Muhammad
The author objects: if the mutawalli were truly the beneficiaries' agent, they could dismiss him, but the school holds that dismissal by beneficiaries is not valid except for betrayal. This reveals the flaw in Muhammad's pure agency position.
The objection is in the line: 'قلت : وقول محمد مشكل...'
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Yusuf allows the founder to remove the mutawalli without cause; Muhammad restricts removal, requiring cause (e.g., betrayal). The passage also mentions that dismissal by a judge is analogous to agent removal.
The author concludes that the mutawalli is a hybrid: agent during founder's life, wasi after death.
5
Synthesize the final tarjih
The author favors Abu Yusuf's view on removal by the founder, but adopts a hybrid classification that respects the mutawalli's dual role. This synthesis reconciles the two positions.
The passage ends with an addition about the judge's removal, indicating the ongoing relevance of the agency analogy.

Hearsay Testimony on Waqf: The Origin-Conditions Distinction
Distinguishing between hearsay testimony on the original endowment and its conditions, with differing Hanafi opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hearsay testimony for the origin vs. conditions of a waqf, and apply the exception of necessity.

Core Idea
Hearsay proves a waqf's existence but not its detailed conditions—except when necessity demands.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between the enduring fact that a property is an endowment (asl al-waqf) and the specific conditions (shara'it) that govern its distribution. While hearsay testimony (tasamu') is accepted for the origin because it is a matter of continuous reputation, the conditions require more direct evidence. However, when original witnesses are dead and records lost, later scholars permit hearsay under necessity. This lesson examines the disagreement and its practical resolution.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Identify the baseline Hanafi position: hearsay accepted for origin, not for conditions. The origin endures over time (publicly known), conditions are changeable and not publicly attested.
Origin = the fact that the property is waqf. Conditions = details like beneficiaries and percentages.
2
Map the intra-madhhab dispute
List three positions: al-Fadli (reject all), al-Sarakhsi (accept origin only), al-Mujtaba/Fath al-Qadir (accept both under necessity).
The dispute is not about origin but about conditions.
3
Analyse the proof structure
Origin can be proven by reputation because it is observable and continuous. Conditions are internal arrangements not generally known; hearsay is unreliable for them.
Necessity (ضرورة) is the key: when witnesses are dead and records lost, hearsay becomes the only option.
4

The majority Hanafi view accepts hearsay for conditions only when necessity arises—e.g., death of original witnesses and loss of deeds.
This is not absolute acceptance but a practical solution to preserve the waqf.
5

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A waqf beneficiary's self-removal is effective only when his entitlement is contingent on appointment, not when it is inherent.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq refines the ruling on a waqf beneficiary's self-removal. The author distinguishes between two types of entitlement: inherent (like a son in a family waqf) and contingent (like a faqih appointed to a madrasa waqf). The key is whether the right is established by the waqf itself or by a subsequent appointment. Understanding this distinction is crucial for the practical administration of waqfs.

Testimony of Waqf Beneficiary Regarding Income
Ruling with proof and response to a potential objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the testimony of a waqf beneficiary regarding income is not accepted (ghayr maqbul) and identify the legal analogy used.

Core Idea
A waqf beneficiary's testimony about the waqf's income is rejected due to suspicion of bias, akin to a partner's testimony.

In Hanafi waqf law, a beneficiary who testifies about the income (ghallah) has a direct personal interest in the outcome. This raises tuhmah (suspicion), rendering his testimony legally unacceptable. The ruling is derived by analogy to the case of a partner testifying for his partner, or one creditor for another in a joint debt. Even if the judge appointed the beneficiary as a witness, his testimony remains rejected, but it can serve to remove the trustee's oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the testimony of a waqf beneficiary regarding income (ghallah). The ruling is that it is not accepted (ghayr maqbul).
The ruling applies even if the judge appointed the witness.
2
Locate the dalil (qiyas)
The ruling is based on qiyas: the beneficiary is like a partner, and the testimony of a partner for his partner is rejected due to tuhmah. It is also analogous to one creditor testifying for another in a shared debt.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illa) is the presence of a personal interest that creates suspicion of bias. In both the partner and creditor cases, the witness stands to benefit, so his testimony is unreliable.
The Hanafi school applies tuhmah broadly.
4
Note the benefit of having such a witness despite rejection
The testimony is not accepted for establishing the facts, but it can be used to remove suspicion from the trustee (mutawalli) so that he is not required to take an oath. This is supported by the principle that evidence is accepted to remove the oath.
This practical benefit preserves the role of waqf witnesses.

Sub-Delegation by an Agent: The Rule and Its Exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's sub-delegation, identify the two exceptions (explicit permission or 'act on your judgment'), and explain the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the guardian of a waqf.

Core Idea
An agent cannot sub-delegate unless permitted; the principal's trust is personal.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā’iq elaborates on a key principle of agency in Hanafi fiqh: the appointed agent is trusted for his own judgment, so he cannot delegate that trust to another without the principal's consent. We will examine the rule, its exceptions, and the subtle differences within the school on the status of a waqf guardian.

Guided Path
1
State the default rule
Identify the general principle: an appointed agent (wakīl) may not sub-delegate (tawkīl) unless specifically permitted.
The text begins with 'wa lā yuwakkilu illā bi-idhni aw iʿmal bi-ra’yika'.
2
Identify the two exceptions
Note the two conditions that allow sub-delegation: explicit permission from the principal, or the phrase 'iʿmal bi-ra’yika' (act on your judgment).

3

Examine the practical effects: if a sub-agent is appointed without authority, his actions are not binding on the principal; the principal may ratify, but until then the sub-agent's acts are suspended (mawqūf).

فرغ من بيان دعوى الواحد ذكر دعوى ما زاد عليه قوله: (برهنا على ما في يد واحد آخر قضى لهما لحديث تميم بن طرفة أن رجلين اختصما إلى رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم في ناقة أقام كل واحد منهما البينة فقضى بها بينهما نصفين. وحديث القرعة كان في الابتداء ثم نسخ، ولأن المطلق للشهادة في حق كل واحد منهما يحتمل الوجوه بأن يعتمد أحدهما الملك والآخر اليد فصحت الشهادتان فيجب العمل بهما ما أمكن وقد أمكن بالتنصيف إذ المحل يقبله، وإنما ينصف لاستوائهما في سبب الاستحقاق والضمير في قوله «برهنا» عائد على الرجلين أي الخارجين بقرينة على ما في يد أخر، والمعنى على ملك ما في يد الآخر ملكاً فالكلام في دعوى الخارجين الملك المطلق فخرج ما إذا ادعيا بسبب معين أو مقيد بتاريخ هذا القبيل ما في منية المفتي: أقاما بينة على عبد في يد رجل أحدهما بغصب وسيأتي. ومن والآخر بوديعة فهو بينهما ا هـ . وأطلقهما فشمل ما إذا ادعيا الوقف في يد ثالث فيقضى بالعقار نصفين لكل وقف النصف وهو من قبيل دعوى الملك المطلق باعتبار ملك الواقف ولهذا قال في القنية : دار في يد رجل أقام رجل عليه بينة أنها وقفت عليه وأقام تيم المسجد بينة أنها وقف المسجد، فإن أرخا فهي للسابق منهما، وإن لم يؤرخا فهي بينهما نصفان ا هـ . ذو اليد الملك فيها أو الوقف على جهة أخرى. والحاصل أن أن ذلك ولا فرق في بين يدعي فدفعه دعوى الوقف من قبيل دعوى الملك المطلق ولهذا لو ادعى وقفية ما في يد آخر وبرهن

ذو اليد بأنه مودع فلان ونحوه وبرهن فإنها تندفع خصومة المدعي كما في الإسعاف، فدعوى الوقف داخل في المسألة المخمسة، وكما يقسم الدار بين الواقفين كذلك لو برهن كل على أن الواقف جعل له الغلة ولا مرجح فإنها تكون بينهما نصفين لما في الاسعاف من باب إقرار الصحيح بأرض في يده أنها وقف لو شهد اثنان على إقرار رجل بأن أرضه وقف على زيد ونسله وشهد آخران على إقراره بأنها وقف على عمرو ونسله تكون وقفاً على الأسبق وقتاً إن علم، وإن لم يعلم أو ذكروا وقتاً واحداً تكون الغلة الفريقين إنصافاً، مات من ولد زيد فنصيبه لمن بقي منهم وكذلك حكم أولاد عمرو، وإذا انقرض أحد الفريقين رجعت إلى الفريق الباقي لزوال المزاحم وقيد بالبرهان منهما إذ لو برهن أحدهما فقط فإنه يقضى له اهـ.

ومن

قوله: (ولآخر بوديعة فهو بينهما) أي لأن المودع بالجحود يصير غاصباً ثم إن ما ذكره عن المنية
Chapter of the claim of two men: They both provided evidence for what is in the hand of another, the judge gave it to them both. And regarding the marriage of a woman, they fall. [Chapter heading] Purpose: He finished explaining the claim of one, he mentions the claim of more than one. His statement: (They both provided evidence for what is in the hand of a third, the judge gave it to them both) due to the hadith of Tamim ibn Tarafa that two men disputed before the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) over a she-camel, each of them produced evidence, so he judged it between them half and half. And the hadith of the lottery (qur'a) was initially [used] then abrogated. And because the absolute [nature] of testimony concerning each of them is plausible in ways: one may rely on ownership and the other on possession, so both testimonies are valid, so it is necessary to act upon them as much as possible, and it is possible by halving since the subject accepts it. It is halved because they are equal in the cause of entitlement...
Source pages p. 397

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the claim of two persons (daw'a al-rajulayn) in litigation. It states the basic ruling: if two claimants both present evidence (bayyina) that a property in the hand of a third person belongs to them, the judge divides it equally between them. The proof is the hadith of Tamim ibn Tarafa where the Prophet (peace be upon him) divided the camel between two claimants who each had evidence. The text notes that the practice of drawing lots (qur'a) was initially used but abrogated. The legal reasoning is that each testimony is possible (one indicating ownership, the other possession), so both must be given effect as much as possible, and halving is feasible. This applies to claims of absolute ownership (al-mulk al-mutlaq), not claims based on a specific cause (like ghasb or deposit) because those involve contradiction in the basis. The passage extends the ruling to cases of waqf (endowment): if two claimants each claim a property as waqf for themselves (e.g., one for himself, one for the mosque), and dates are unknown, it is divided half and half. It also discusses cases where the possessor defends by claiming deposit from a third party, which may dismiss the claim. The text clarifies that the ruling requires both to present evidence; if only one presents evidence, he wins entirely. This chapter demonstrates the Hanafi method of reconciling conflicting evidence through equitable division.

Key Terms
برهنا (barrahana): They both provided evidence (bayyina) meaning they presented witnesses or documentation.
تنصيف (tansif): Halving; dividing the property equally between the two claimants.
ملك مطلق (mulk mutlaq): Absolute ownership claimed without specifying a cause (e.g., purchase, inheritance, gift).

Key Takeaway
When two external claimants both provide valid evidence for property in a third party's hand and no date is known, the Hanafi judge divides the property equally.
