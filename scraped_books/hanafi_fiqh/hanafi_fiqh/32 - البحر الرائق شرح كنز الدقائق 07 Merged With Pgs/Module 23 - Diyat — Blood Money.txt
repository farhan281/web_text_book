Oath in Claim of Destruction (Itlaf) – Procedure for the Judge
Ruling on oath in destruction claim with present item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi procedural rule that the judge must examine the damaged item before administering an oath in a claim of destruction.

Core Idea
In destruction claims, examine the damage first – the defendant may have already paid.

When someone claims their property was destroyed by another and brings the damaged item to court, the natural instinct might be to immediately swear the defendant. However, Hanafi law directs the judge to first inspect the damage. This lesson explains why and when the oath is deferred, based on the ruling from al-Khizānah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Ruling
The act in question is the judge's procedure when a claimant brings a destroyed/damaged item to court. The ruling is that the judge should not administer an oath immediately; instead, he first examines the damage.
The text says 'لا يحلفه ما خرقت' (he does not administer an oath: did you tear it?).
2
Understand the Rationale
The reason for deferring the oath is the possibility that the defendant has already compensated the claimant for the damage. If the defendant had paid, swearing him would be unnecessary and might lead to a false oath.
The commentary mentions: 'لاحتمال أنه خرقه وأداء ضمانه' (because of the possibility that he tore it and paid its guarantee).
3
Determine the Course of Action
After the inspection, the judge assesses the damage. If it is minor and the defendant is liable (i.e., the tear matches the claim), the defendant must pay compensation. The oath may be administered later if needed.
The text says: 'ثم ينظر في الخرق؛ إن كان يسيراً وضمن هو' (then he examines the tear; if it was minor, and he is liable).
4
Apply the Rule to Similar Cases
This rule applies to any claim of destruction (itlaf) where the damaged item is present and visible. The judge must always prioritize physical evidence before calling for an oath.
The principle is that the judge should not rush to the oath when the evidence is available for inspection.

Commentary
This passage is problematic due to OCR corruption. The intended ruling for destruction claims appears at the beginning: when someone claims another tore his garment and the garment is present, the judge does not immediately swear the defendant; rather, he first inspects the tear. If the damage is minor, the defendant is liable (i.e., must pay compensation). The rest of the text seems to be a continuation of the earlier discussion about the oath for the deceased debtor, with comments from 'Nūr al-‘Ayn' and the author's own remarks. Because of the corruption, I will only base commentary on the clear first part. The author emphasizes that the oath is not administered immediately because the defendant might have already compensated the damage. This ruling highlights the Hanafi principle of examining evidence before oaths.

Key Terms
إتلاف (itlāf): Destruction of property, a tort claim requiring compensation.
يمين (yamīn): Oath. In this context, the oath administered to the defendant in denial of liability.
