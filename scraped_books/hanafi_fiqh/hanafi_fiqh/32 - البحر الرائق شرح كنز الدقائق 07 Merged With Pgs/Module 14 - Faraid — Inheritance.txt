Istishab in Inheritance Disputes: The Case of the Dhimmi's Wife
Ruling on wife's claim of conversion after dhimmi husband's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istishab to resolve a dispute over the timing of conversion in an inheritance case, and distinguish between daf' (defense) and istihqaq (entitlement).

Core Idea
When a dhimmi dies, istishab presumes the original state; the heirs' claim is accepted because they are defending the status quo.

A dhimmi dies, and his wife claims she converted to Islam after his death, while the heirs claim she converted before. Who inherits? The Hanafi school rules that the heirs' statement is accepted, based on the legal presumption of continuity (istishab). This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the qiyas to the flowing watermill, and the intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar, clarifying the critical distinction between using a presumption for defense (daf') versus for entitlement (istihqaq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Recognize that the wife claims conversion after death (so no inheritance for her), while the heirs claim conversion before death (so she would inherit). The ruling is about whose statement is accepted.
The husband is a dhimmi, so if the wife converted before his death, she would inherit as a Muslim. If after, she does not.
2
Apply istishab to the facts
Determine the original state: the husband died a dhimmi, and the wife was originally a dhimmi at marriage. Presume that this state continued unless proven otherwise. The claim of conversion before death is a change; the heirs deny it, maintaining the presumption.
The Hanafi maxim: 'Certainty is not overruled by doubt.' The original state is certain.
3
Identify the defender (daf')
The heirs are defending the original state (no conversion before death), so they are in a position of defense. Istishab is valid for defense, not for establishing a new right (istihqaq). The wife is seeking to establish a new right (inheritance based on conversion), so her claim requires proof.
The text states: 'This is a manifest (zahir) that we consider for defense (daf'), while what he (Zufar) mentioned he considers for entitlement (istihqaq).'
4
Compare with Zufar's view
Zufar argues that conversion is a new event, so it should be attributed to the nearest time (after death), making the wife's claim credible. The Hanafi response: this would use istishab to grant her a right (entitlement), which is impermissible; istishab only protects existing rights.
Zufar's view would mean the wife inherits, but the majority Hanafi opinion rejects this.
5
Note the practical consequence
The heirs' statement is accepted; the wife does not inherit from her dhimmi husband. If she had converted before death, she would inherit, but the default presumption overcomes her claim.
In practice, the burden of proof is on the wife to show she converted before death.

The dispute: she claims conversion after death (so she does not inherit), the heirs claim she converted before (so she does inherit, but they want to exclude her). The ruling is that the statement of the heirs is accepted, based on istishab (presumption of continuity): the husband died a dhimmi, and the original state is that the wife was also a dhimmi (since she married him as such). The claim that she converted before death is an innovation; the default is that she remained a dhimmi. The Hanafi response is that we consider the present state as evidence for defense (daf'), not for entitlement (istihqaq). Actually the text says: if a dhimmi dies and two sons dispute, one claiming the father died Muslim, the other claiming kafir, the Muslim son's claim is accepted even if his witnesses are dhimmis. The summary: the statement of the one who claims Islam is accepted. This is consistent with the principle that the default in human beings is not Islam, but when there is a conflict, the one claiming Islam is believed because it is the more favorable status. The commentary also clarifies that istishab is the correct term rather than zahir. The marginal notes at the end discuss the nature of 'in sha Allah' as a condition, not a true exception, and comment on earlier passages.

Key Terms
استصحاب (istishab): A legal presumption that a previously established state continues to exist until evidence to the contrary arises.
تحكيم الحال (tahkim al-hal): Applying the present state of affairs to determine the past in the absence of contrary evidence.
دفع (daf'): Defense: protecting an existing right or status from a claim of change.
استحقاق (istihqaq): Entitlement: claiming a right that did not previously exist.
ظاهر (zahir): Apparent: what appears to be the case based on external evidence.
ذمي (dhimmi): A non-Muslim subject living under the protection of an Islamic state.

Dispute over Wife's Conversion After Dhimmi Husband's Death
Intra-Hanafi dispute on istishab vs attribution to nearest time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istishab to resolve a dispute over the timing of a dhimmi widow's conversion and explain why Zufar's contrary view is not adopted.

Core Idea
Istishab preserves the past status of deprivation; thus the heirs' claim prevails over the widow's claim of conversion.

When a dhimmi dies, his widow may claim she converted after his death to inherit. The heirs may claim she converted before death, barring inheritance. This dispute turns on whether the widow's present claim of Islam can overcome the presumption that her non-Muslim status continued until death. The Hanafi school relies on istishab (presumption of continuity) to favour the heirs' statement, while Imam Zufar attributes the conversion event to the nearest time. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and its practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The widow claims conversion after the husband's death (so she inherits). The heirs claim conversion before death (so she does not inherit).
No external evidence is presented by either side.
2
Recognize the central issue
Which statement is preferred when there is no external evidence? The dispute is about the evidentiary weight of the present state (her being Muslim now) versus the presumption of continuity of the prior non-Muslim status.
3
Understand the Hanafi argument
The Hanafis argue that the cause of deprivation (her non-Muslim status at the time of death) is established in the present (she was non-Muslim before death), so through istishab it is presumed to have continued from the past.
This is like the flow of a mill: the present water flow indicates it was flowing in the past.
4
Contrast Zufar's argument
Zufar argues that conversion is a new event, so it should be attributed to the nearest possible time (after death). Thus the widow's claim should be accepted unless the heirs prove otherwise.
Zufar applies the principle of attributing new events to the present moment.
5
Apply the Hanafi maxim
The Hanafis state that apparent evidence (zahir) is used for prevention (daf') not for entitlement (istihqaq). Here, the heirs are preventing the widow from claiming inheritance, so their reliance on istishab is valid.
This maxim clarifies why the widow's claim, which would establish entitlement, is not given weight.

Commentary
This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) husband who dies. The wife claims she converted to Islam after his death, while the heirs claim she converted before his death. The Hanafi ruling is that the heirs' statement is accepted because the cause of deprivation (the wife's non-Muslim status at time of death) is established in the present, and through istishab (presumption of continuity) it is considered to have been the case in the past. Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl disagreed, arguing that since conversion is a new event, it should be attributed to the nearest possible time, i.e., after death. The Hanafi response uses the analogy of a mill's water flow to illustrate that the present condition informs the past. The text further clarifies that this applies specifically when the husband is a dhimmi; if the husband was Muslim and the wife was Christian, the same ruling applies but the reasoning differs: the apparent state (zahir) is not strong enough to establish entitlement, only to prevent it. The author prefers the term 'istishab' over 'zahir' because istishab is more accurate for establishing entitlement, as seen in the acceptance of solitary reports (akhbar al-ahad) which are also probable but can establish rights.

Key Terms
ذمي (dhimmi): A non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state, whose rights and obligations are regulated by the dhimma contract.
استصحاب (istishab): Presumption of continuity: the continued validity of a previously established legal state until a change is proven.
ظاهر (zahir): Apparent evidence or circumstantial indication, considered sufficient for prevention (daf') but not for entitlement (istihqaq) in this context.

When inheritance is disputed over the deceased's faith, the claim of Islam prevails regardless of who makes it.

Depositary's Acknowledgment of an Heir: Conditions and Exceptions
Ruling with proof on depositary's heir acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a depositary acknowledges a son as the sole heir and distinguish it from cases involving a brother, executor, agent, or buyer.

Core Idea
A depositary's acknowledgment of a son as sole heir obligates delivery; other relations require delay.

When a depositary (المودع) acknowledges that a specific person is the sole heir of the deceased depositor, does that force him to hand over the deposit? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks the ruling, revealing critical conditions such as sonship versus brotherhood, the requirement of declaring 'no other heir', and exceptions for executors, agents, and buyers. Understanding these nuances ensures that the depositary's acknowledgment aligns with the rights of all potential heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Core Condition
Start from the opening statement: if the depositary says 'this is the son of my depositor, he has no other heir', the property must be given wajib. Note that the ruling is conditional on the acknowledgment being of sonship and on the declaration of no other heir.
The word 'and' (و) in the source connects the two conditions.
2
Contrast with Brotherhood Acknowledgment
The passage contrasts with a brother acknowledgment: the judge must delay (talawwum). Why? Because a brother inherits only if there is no son, so verification is needed. A son inherits unconditionally, so no delay.
This distinction is rooted in the faraid principle of unconditional vs. conditional heirs.
3
Analyze the 'No Other Heir' Condition
If the depositary says 'he has another heir and I do not know if he died or not', nothing is given to the claimant until he brings evidence that no other heir is known. This protects potential missing heirs.
The condition is not just the acknowledgment but also the statement of sole heirship.
4
Explore Exceptions: Executor, Agent, Buyer
Acknowledgment of an executor, agent, or buyer does not force delivery because it would harm the depositor's right in the specific property. However, acknowledgment of an agent for debt collection does force delivery since debts are discharged by equivalents, not specific property.
The key is whether the acknowledgment affects the depositor's ownership in the same property.
5
Extend the Ruling to Other Heirs
The passage extends the 'son' ruling to daughters, parents, and any heir who inherits unconditionally. Conditional heirs (e.g., brother, uncle) follow the brother case requiring delay.
This extension is based on the illa of unconditional inheritance.
6
Synthesize Multi-Claimant Scenarios
When multiple claimants appear (e.g., one claims sonship, another claims marriage), the judge must prioritize heirs based on the strength of their claims and the depositary's acknowledgment, often requiring oaths.
The Fath al-Qadir excerpt shows the judge's role in resolving conflicts.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Taking a Surety from an Heir
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheritance surety with procedural details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on not taking a surety from an heir, explain the Sahibayn's opposing view, and apply the Hanafi procedure of talwum in inheritance distribution.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: no surety from heir, only judicial delay; Sahibayn: surety required.

When distributing an inheritance, a judge must balance the rights of the present heir against the possibility of absent claimants. This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that taking a surety (kafil) from the heir is an act of oppression, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it a permissible precaution. The resolution affects the judge's procedure and the heir's rights.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify that Abu Hanifa rules that no surety is taken from the heir; instead, the judge postpones the distribution (talwum) until confident there are no other claimants.
The judge's delay is precautionary, not a fixed period.
2
Identify the opposing view
The Sahibayn (and the earlier judge Ibn Abi Layla) hold that a surety should be taken from the heir to protect a potential absent claimant.
Their analogy: similar to taking surety for found property or maintenance for an absent person's wife.
3
Follow the opposing proof
The Sahibayn use qiyas: just as a surety is taken when returning lost property or supporting an absent wife's maintenance, it should be taken from an heir to guard against unknown claimants.
They argue that taking a surety does not delay the present heir's right.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa uses istihsan: the existence of another claimant is unlikely (mawhum), so delaying without surety is sufficient. Taking a surety is oppressive and departs from the straight path.
The judge's increased knowledge through waiting is a stronger precaution than burdening the heir with a surety.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa's view, the heir receives the property after the judge's delay and does not need a surety. Under the Sahibayn, the heir must provide a surety or risk the property being withheld.
Also, the duration of talwum is disputed: Abu Hanifa leaves it to the judge; the Sahibayn fix it at a year (Abu Yusuf at a month).

Present Heir Claims Inheritance: Imam vs Sahibayn on Absent Heir's Share
Intra-Hanafi dispute on handling absent heir's share.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the present heir taking his share, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the absent heir's share, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each position.

Core Idea
The present heir takes only his share; the absent's share stays with the possessor unless the possessor's denial triggers Sahibayn's precaution.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a specific procedural ruling in inheritance claims. When a present heir claims a property as inheritance for himself and an absent brother and proves it, he is entitled only to his own share. However, what happens to the absent brother's share? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn), differ on this point. Understanding their reasoning and the practical difference is essential for correctly applying Hanafi inheritance litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the basic ruling
The act is a present heir claiming a property as inheritance for himself and his absent brother, and proving his claim. The basic ruling from the matn: he takes only his own share, leaving the absent’s share in the hand of the current possessor (dhul yad).
This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa without any condition.
2
Understand the principle of representation
The present heir is not considered a representative (khasm) for the absent heir in taking possession (istifa’). Therefore, the judge cannot seize the absent’s share without a claimant. This is analogous to a judge seeing property in someone’s hand that he knows belongs to another – he does not take it without a claimant.
Imam’s reasoning: the present heir only represents the deceased for liabilities (debts, bequests), not for taking possession of property.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Abu Hanifa holds the absolute position: leave the absent’s share with the possessor. The two Shaykhs (Sahibayn) differentiate: if the possessor denies the claim (juhud), then the share is taken from him and placed with a trustee (amin); if he does not deny, it is left with him.
Sahibayn’s reasoning: denial indicates dishonesty (khiyanah), so the possessor cannot be trusted. Imam counters that the judgment itself removes the effect of denial, restoring trust.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the possessor denies, according to Imam the absent’s share remains with the possessor; according to Sahibayn it is secured with a trustee. The text does not give a final tarjih, but later works (e.g., Jami’ al-Fusulayn) lean towards Sahibayn’s precaution due to the betrayal element.
The dispute also extends to taking a surety for the person (kafil bi al-nafs): Imam says no, Sahibayn say yes, based on the same underlying principle of trust.

Executor's Purchase for Emancipation: Effects of Return or Reclamation
Analyzing a case where an executor's purchase for emancipation is reversed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine, in a case where an executor's purchase of a slave for emancipation is later reversed, whether the emancipation is valid on behalf of the deceased or the executor, by applying the condition of price equivalence.

Core Idea
If the purchased slave is returned, emancipation is on the deceased only if the new price matches the original; otherwise it is on the executor.

This lesson examines a detailed ruling from al-Hasiri's Sharh al-Jami' al-Kabir regarding an executor's purchase of a slave for emancipation (nasama) on behalf of the deceased. The ruling addresses the consequences if the slave is later returned due to a defect or reclaimed by a rightful owner. Understanding this case highlights the strict requirement that the executor must precisely follow the bequest, or face personal liability.

Guided Path

The deceased bequeathed that his slave be sold and the proceeds used to buy a nasama (a slave to be freed on his behalf).

The executor sold the slave, bought a nasama with the price, and freed it, assuming it is within one-third of the estate.
The purchase appears to fulfill the bequest initially.

The slave is returned because of a defect. The executor must sell it again and use the new price to buy another nasama.

If the slave is reclaimed by its true owner, the buyer recovers the price from the executor. The emancipation is on the executor because the purchased slave never belonged to the deceased.

The Requirement of Al-Jarr in Inheritance Testimony: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony for inheritance: requirement of al-jarr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the requirement of al-jarr in inheritance testimony according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, distinguish Abu Yusuf's opposing view with proofs, and apply the mufta bihi position to determine the validity of testimony.

Core Idea
Inheritance testimony requires explicit linking to death because the heir's ownership is renewed, not identical to the deceased's.

When an heir claims property from an estate, witnesses cannot simply state 'This belonged to the deceased.' They must explicitly link the property to the deceased's ownership at the moment of death. This requirement, called al-jarr, is disputed within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani insist on it, while Abu Yusuf deems it unnecessary. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each view and the practical impact on testimony.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on al-jarr
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: witnesses must explicitly link the property to the deceased's ownership at the time of death (al-jarr). Without it, testimony is invalid.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf holds that testimony to the deceased's ownership suffices without specifying 'at the time of death' – he considers the heir's ownership identical to the deceased's.
He is the sole dissenter among the three major Hanafi imams.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Abu Yusuf argues: if the property belonged to the deceased, then by inheritance it automatically transfers to the heir, so testifying for the deceased is testifying for the heir.
He relies on the continuity of ownership across death.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rebut that the heir's ownership is renewed and distinct, citing rulings on istibra' of inherited slave-girls and permissibility for wealthy heir to use charity of poor deceased.
These rulings show the heir's ownership is a new legal relationship.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if witnesses say 'this was for the deceased' without temporal link, Abu Yusuf accepts it; the other two do not. The heir must either have witnesses rephrase or bring additional evidence establishing ownership at death.
This directly affects the outcome in probate cases.

When a Depositary Must Hand Over to a Claimant of Inheritance
Ruling with conditions on delivery of deposit to inheritance claimant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on handing over a deposit to a claimant who claims inheritance and identify the conditions and exceptions.

Core Idea
A confirmed inheritance claim obligates the depositary to deliver, unless the estate has debt.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author explains the ruling when a person claims that the depositor died and left the deposit as his inheritance. Unlike cases of agency, here the depositary is ordered to hand over because the property becomes the heir's after death. However, this is subject to conditions, including the absence of encompassing debt and the need for caution (talammuh) to check for other heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and confirmation
The claimant says: 'The depositor died and left the deposit as inheritance for me.' The depositary confirms this statement.
2
Determine the ruling
The depositary is ordered to hand over (yu'mar bi al-daf') the deposit to the claimant.
This is wajib according to the Hanafi school.
3

After the depositor's death, the property is no longer his; both the depositary and the claimant agree it belongs to the heir. Thus, no competing claim from the deceased.
4
Note the condition of talammuh
There must be a delay to investigate whether there are other heirs, to avoid handing over to a wrong person.
This is a precautionary measure.
5

For a bequest (wasiyyah), the ruling is similar but the legatee is treated like an heir when there is no other heir. For an executor (wasi), the depositary is not ordered to hand over without evidence.
Claiming to be an executor is different from claiming inheritance.
6
Apply the debt exception
If the deceased had debt that covers the entire estate, the depositary must not hand over without a judge's order; otherwise, he is liable.
This is from Jami' al-Fusulayn.

The author points out that these rulings have appeared before in the Book of Judgement, albeit in different form: there the possessor acknowledged the claimant as heir, here the claimant asserts it and the possessor believes him. The ruling is conditioned on the trustee's belief: if he denies the death or is uncertain, he does not hand over without proof. If the claimant claims bequest, the trustee must state that the deceased left no heir. The passage then distinguishes between claiming to be an heir/legatee versus claiming to be an executor (وصي). If someone claims to be an executor, the trustee should not hand over even if he believes him, because that would be acknowledging agency after death, which is invalid. There is a disagreement between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf about the case where the money is a debt owed by the trustee: Muhammad's first view says the trustee is believed and must pay; his latter view (which is Abu Yusuf's) says he is not believed and should not pay. The passage concludes that neither the depository nor the debtor of the deceased should deliver to a claimant of bequest without evidence, and they are not discharged by doing so. Delivery to an heir is restricted by the condition that there is no debt encompassing the estate; otherwise, the depository should not deliver to the heir without a judge's order, or he becomes liable. However, if the depository is trusted, he may take from the estate and pay debts. Further details from جامع الفصولين are given about the heir's right to sue debtors, etc.

Oath Versus Confession in Inheritance Claims
Discussion on when a judge should swear a claimant heir despite confession of others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a judge must swear a claimant when the estate is fully indebted, distinguishing between cases where confession alone suffices and where it does not.

Core Idea
When the estate is fully consumed by debt, heirs' confession is ineffective, and the judge must swear the claimant.

In inheritance law, confession (iqrar) is generally a powerful proof that binds the confessor. However, this general rule has a critical exception: when the deceased's estate is completely absorbed by debt (dayn mustaghriq), the heirs' confession loses its validity. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this exception and the judge's duty to administer an oath to the claimant to protect the rights of creditors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule on Iqrar
Confession (iqrar) is normally a binding proof against the confessor, requiring no further evidence. This is a well-established Hanafi principle.
2
Recognize the Exception for Dayn Mustaghriq
When the estate is fully consumed by debt (dayn mustaghriq), the general rule does not apply because the heirs have no valid ownership to confess. The key condition is absolute insolvency of the estate.
Dayn mustaghriq = debt that completely consumes the estate.
3
Understand the Reasoning (Talil)
The heirs' confession is not valid because they do not truly own the estate; it belongs to the creditors. Therefore, the judge must take extra caution and swear the claimant to confirm the debt.
This balances the rights of creditors and heirs, preventing collusion.
4
Apply the Ruling
If a claimant creditor presents evidence or even if the heirs confess to a debt that absorbs the whole estate, the judge should administer an oath to the claimant before granting him a share. The oath is mandatory (wajib) to protect against collusion.
The judge may swear the claimant even without the creditors' request.

Including Discharge in the Heir's Oath: A Hanafi Refinement
Explains oath wording for creditor's claim and the dispute over including discharge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school includes 'discharge' in the heir's oath when the creditor claims payment, and evaluate the counter-argument from Jami' al-Fusulayn.

Core Idea
Include 'discharge' in the oath because the deceased may have forgiven the debt without the debtor's knowledge.

When a creditor claims he paid a debt directly to the deceased, the heir must swear an oath denying knowledge of payment. But should the oath also cover the possibility that the deceased discharged (forgave) the debt? This lesson examines a critical refinement within the Hanafi school: the author of Jami' al-Fusulayn argues it is unnecessary, while the author of al-Bahr al-Rā'iq defends its inclusion based on the nature of discharge (barā'a), which does not require acceptance.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the ruling from Khizanat al-Muftin
Identify the exact situation: a creditor claims payment to the deceased, the heir denies knowledge. The oath from Khizanat al-Muftin includes three elements: not knowing that the deceased received the debt, nor any part of it, nor that he was discharged from it.
This is the baseline ruling that triggers the dispute.
2
Present the objection from Jami' al-Fusulayn
The author of Jami' al-Fusulayn argues that the third element ('nor discharged') is unnecessary because the claim is specifically about payment (īfā'), not discharge (barā'a). Hence, including discharge goes beyond the scope of the claim.
This objection is a methodological challenge: should the oath cover only the fact asserted or also possible alternative grounds for the debt's extinction?
3
Analyze the Hanafi response from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq
The response relies on the Hanafi principle that barā'a (discharge) does not require acceptance by the debtor. Therefore, the deceased could have discharged the creditor without the creditor's knowledge. To avoid injustice, the oath must include discharge to account for this possibility.
The key legal point: discharge can be unilateral (īqā'), so the creditor may not know it occurred.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the oath omits 'discharge', a creditor who was actually discharged but claims ignorance could falsely press his claim. Including 'discharge' protects the heir by forcing the creditor to address that possibility, aligning with the cautious approach of the Hanafi school.
The dispute is not merely academic; it affects the outcome of litigation.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative opinion)
The position of al-Bahr al-Rā'iq, which includes discharge, is the relied-upon opinion (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school, as it is more comprehensive and safeguards against potential false claims.
This is the outcome of the critical refinement process.

Inheritance Distribution When an Heir Acknowledges a Sibling or Spouse
Calculates shares when one heir acknowledges an additional heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the share taken from an acknowledging heir when an additional sibling or spouse is acknowledged, and distinguish when deniers must swear.

Core Idea
An heir's acknowledgment of an additional sibling or spouse reduces only his own share, not the shares of other heirs.

When an heir acknowledges that the deceased had another sibling or a spouse, that acknowledgment does not establish full inheritance rights against all heirs. Instead, it only affects the acknowledging heir's share. This lesson walks through the specific fractions and calculations for such scenarios, as well as the effect of acknowledging a debt's fulfillment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledging heir and acknowledged person
Determine who is making the iqrār (e.g., son acknowledging a brother) and who is being acknowledged (e.g., brother). The acknowledgment only binds the acknowledging heir.
2
Determine the prescribed fraction
The fraction that the acknowledged person takes from the acknowledging heir's share is fixed: brother takes 1/2, sister takes 1/3, wife of father takes 1/8.
3
Handle multiple acknowledging heirs
When multiple heirs acknowledge, their shares are combined and divided proportionally. For a son and daughter acknowledging a brother, their shares are in a 3:2 ratio (son gets 3/5 of combined, daughter gets 2/5). The brother's share is taken from the total of their shares.
This applies only to the acknowledging heirs; denying heirs are unaffected.
4
Calculate the share of the acknowledged person
Multiply the prescribed fraction by the acknowledging heir's total share (or combined shares) to get the amount due to the acknowledged person.
The acknowledged person does not inherit from the rest of the estate.
5
Apply the iqrār of debt rule
If an heir acknowledges that the deceased's debt has been paid, that acknowledgment only affects his own share. Other heirs must swear that they have no knowledge of the payment, unless the acknowledgment is for a portion equal to the debt's value, in which case they are not sworn.
This prevents the acknowledging heir from imposing a loss on others.
