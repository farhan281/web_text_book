The Oath of the Possessor When Ownership Is Claimed (Ariyah, Ijarah, Wadi'ah)
Ruling statement on oath of the possessor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a possessor must take a specific oath before a claimant's ownership proof is accepted.

Core Idea
Even with proof, the trust-based possessor’s oath is required before the property is handed over.

In this lesson, we examine a ruling from Khizanat al-Muftin regarding cases where a person has possession of an item through borrowing, leasing, or deposit. A third party comes with evidence that the item is his. What does the law say? This lesson clarifies the procedural protection given to the trust-based possessor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three trust-based contracts
Recognize that the passage specifies borrowing (ariyah), leasing (ijara), and deposit (wadi'ah) as the contexts where this ruling applies.
These are amanah (trust) contracts, not ownership transfers.
2
Understand the claimant's action
The claimant (mustahaqq) brings evidence (bayyinah) that the item in the possessor's hands belongs to him.
3
State the ruling
The judge does not decide anything for the claimant until the possessor takes a specific oath.
"لا يقضى له بشيء حتى يحلف"
4
List the four elements of the oath
The possessor must swear: (1) I did not sell it, (2) I did not give it as a gift, (3) I did not give permission regarding it, (4) it is still in your ownership at present.
This covers both past transactions and current status.
5
Grasp the wisdom
The oath protects the trust-based possessor from being dispossessed by fraudulent claims, ensuring that the claimant is truthful before the possession is disturbed.

ومنها تحليف المستحق قال في خزانة المفتين : رجل أعار دابة أو أجرها أو أودعها فجاء مدع وأقام بينة أنها له لا يقضى له بشيء حتى يحلف بالله ما بعت ولا وهبت ولا أذنت فيها ولا هي خارجة عن ملكك للحال.
And among them is the oath of the rightful owner (mustahaqq). He said in Khizanat al-Muftin: A man lends a riding animal, or leases it, or deposits it, then a claimant comes and establishes evidence that it belongs to him – nothing is decided for him until [the possessor] swears: 'By Allah, I did not sell, nor grant, nor give permission regarding it, nor is it out of your ownership at present.'

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario where someone has possession of an animal through borrowing, leasing, or deposit, and a third party brings proof that the animal is his. Before the judge can rule for the claimant, the current possessor must take an oath denying that he sold, gifted, or permitted the animal to the claimant, and affirming that it still belongs to the possessor (i.e., not out of his ownership). This protects the possessor's trust-based possession, ensuring that even with evidence, the claimant does not automatically receive the property until the possessor's oath is taken.

Key Terms
مستحق (mustaḥaqq): The person claiming ownership of an item currently in another's possession.
