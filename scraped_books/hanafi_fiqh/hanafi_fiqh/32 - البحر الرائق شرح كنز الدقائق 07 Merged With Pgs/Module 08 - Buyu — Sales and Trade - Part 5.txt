Ruling on evidence after compromise with denial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two scenarios regarding evidence after a compromise and state the Hanafi ruling for each scenario.

Core Idea
The validity of a compromise depends on the timing and nature of the admission — prior denial does not invalidate, but subsequent admission voids.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a nuanced ruling about the effect of producing evidence after a compromise. It distinguishes between a plaintiff's prior statement of no claim and a subsequent admission. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining whether a compromise remains binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and identify the two different situations described. In the first, the plaintiff produces evidence that he had previously said he had no right. In the second, the plaintiff himself later says he had no right.
2
Determine the timing of each statement
Note when the plaintiff's statement was made relative to the compromise: the first statement is before the compromise, the second is after the compromise.
3
State the ruling for each scenario
For the first scenario (prior statement), the ruling is that the compromise stands (madi). For the second scenario (subsequent admission), the ruling is that the compromise is void (batal).
4
Understand the rationale
The distinction rests on whether the compromise was based on a false claim. A prior statement does not necessarily indicate that the claim was false at the time of compromise. However, a subsequent admission reveals that the claim was false from the beginning, making the compromise invalid.

Third Party Purchase After Claim: Substitution in Lawsuit
Ruling on third party purchase after claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a third party purchasing a disputed claim from the plaintiff and explain that the buyer steps into the plaintiff's position with no automatic ownership.

Core Idea
A plaintiff can transfer his claim by sale; the buyer acquires only the right to sue.

The Hanafi school recognizes the transferability of a claim (da'wa) as a property right. This lesson examines the ruling where after a claim is made, a third party purchases the disputed property from the plaintiff. What is the legal effect? The text from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq provides a clear answer: the purchase is valid vis-à-vis the plaintiff, and the buyer takes his place in the lawsuit. However, the buyer does not automatically own the property—he must prove his right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case: a plaintiff claims property (or similar) from someone. A third party then buys that property from the plaintiff. Note that the purchase is of the claimed object itself, not merely the lawsuit.
The plaintiff is the seller; the third party is the buyer; the defendant is not a party to the sale.
2
Determine validity of the purchase
The text states the purchase is permissible 'with respect to the plaintiff.' This means the sale is valid between plaintiff and buyer. The defendant's interests are not affected at this stage.
Validity is subject to general conditions of sale (e.g., offer, acceptance, known price).
3
Understand the substitution effect
The buyer 'takes the plaintiff's place in the lawsuit' (qama maqamahu fi al-da'wa). Legally, the buyer acquires the right to pursue the claim against the defendant, inheriting the plaintiff's legal position.
This is not ownership of the property, but a transfer of the right to litigate.
4
Analyze the outcome
If the buyer succeeds in proving his right (istahaqqa) to any part of the claimed property, it becomes his. If he cannot prove it, he gets nothing—the purchase does not guarantee ownership.
The burden of proof shifts to the buyer, who must establish his title.
5
Recognize the underlying principle
The Hanafi school treats a claim as a form of property (mal) that can be sold. This encourages the monetization of disputed rights and facilitates resolution.
Contrast with other schools that may restrict sale of disputed items due to gharar.

Core Idea
Compromise on a voidable claim is valid; on a void claim it is not.

In Hanafi law, the validity of a compromise (sulh) depends on the nature of the claim. This passage distinguishes between voidable claims—those that have some basis and can be corrected—and void claims, which have no legal foundation. It further expands the ruling to non-property rights such as the right of pre-emption, water rights, and even claims of ta'zir (discretionary punishment), establishing a general principle of ransoming the oath.

Guided Path

The text states that compromise on a fasid (voidable) claim is valid, while on a batil (void) claim it is not. Understand that a fasid claim is one that can be corrected, e.g., a claim with some basis but a procedural defect.

The passage gives examples: right to water, right of pre-emption (shuf'a), right to place beams. These are not monetary claims but are still valid for compromise because they involve a right that can be subject to an oath.
3
Understand the principle of oath-ransom
The underlying principle is that whenever an oath is directed at a person concerning any right, it is permissible to ransom that oath (i.e., pay to avoid swearing). This applies to any right, including non-financial ones.
This principle extends the validity of compromise beyond property claims.
4
Extend to claims of ta'zir
The text explicitly includes claims of disciplinary punishment (ta'zir) such as accusations of disbelief, misguidance, or slander. Even though these are punitive, they can be resolved by compromise because an oath is involved.
Some might think punishment claims cannot be settled, but the Hanafi position allows it.
5

A payment under an invalid condition is void; the payer may reclaim.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a scenario where a defendant conditions payment on the plaintiff swearing an oath. It rules that such a conditional payment is legally void and the money can be reclaimed. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that obligations cannot be created through unrecognized conditions.

A valid condition must be recognized by law; oaths alone do not create contractual obligations.

The payer (defendant) has the right to retrieve (yastarid) the money because it was given without legal basis.

The payment is deferred for the time it takes to travel to the nearest place where the currency is available, both going and coming back.
This is based on necessity and practicality.
4
Note the additional requirement: taking a guarantor
To secure the deferred payment, the debtor must provide a guarantor (kafil). This protects the creditor.
The guarantor is a safeguard against non-payment.

The ruling aims to honor the original contract terms.

Whose Word is Accepted: Capital and Profit Disputes
Ruling with proof on qawl in capital and profit disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of qawl to determine which party's statement is accepted in disputes over the amount of capital and profit in a mudarabah contract.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the receiver's word is taken on capital received; the owner's word is taken on profit earned.

When a mudarib and capital owner disagree over how much capital was given or how much profit was made, Hanafi law assigns the 'word' (qawl) based on who is claiming a departure from the default. This lesson explains the rationale and application of these procedural rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of dispute
Is the dispute about the amount of capital given or the amount of profit earned? The ruling differs accordingly.
The text explicitly distinguishes between capital and profit disputes.
2
Determine the receiver and the claimant
For capital, the mudarib is the receiver (qabid); for profit, the mudarib claims extra profit beyond what the owner acknowledges.
The default principle is that the receiver's word on quantity is taken.
3
Apply the qawl rule
If dispute is over capital, the mudarib's word is accepted. If over profit, the capital owner's word is accepted on the profit amount.
The text provides an analogy: if the mudarib denied receiving the capital entirely, his word would be taken.
4
Consider evidence (bayyinah)
If one party produces evidence, it is accepted. If both produce evidence, the owner's evidence prevails on capital, the mudarib's on profit.
This follows from the same principle: each party is considered credible within their domain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on qawl and bayyinah in disputes over the nature of the contract (mudarabah vs. bida'ah, etc.) and when both parties present evidence with or without timing.

Core Idea
The owner's word is default on contract nature, but the mudarib's evidence is preferred when he denies liability.

When the dispute is about whether the agreement was a mudarabah, bida'ah, loan, or deposit, Hanafi law assigns the qawl based on who is claiming a departure from the default. Additionally, rules for conflicting evidence involve timing and need. This lesson unpacks the scenarios and the rationale behind the rulings.

Guided Path
1

If the dispute is over quality of the received item (e.g., condition), the owner's word is taken. If over type of contract, further analysis is needed.

Apply the default for contract nature disputes

Borrower's Liability and Restrictions on Use in 'Ariyah
Rulings on borrower's liability for exceeding use

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a borrower is liable for damaging a borrowed item and apply the rules for re-lending and violating restrictions.

Core Idea
Exceeding permitted use in 'ariyah makes the borrower liable as a usurper.

Borrowing (al-‘āriyah) is a generous contract allowing free use of another's property. However, the borrower must adhere to the lender's restrictions. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies when re-lending is permissible and what constitutes a violation that triggers liability. Understanding these rules prevents unintended damage and ensures just compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, identify the specific act being discussed (e.g., hiring out a borrowed item, re-lending, or violating a restriction). Then state its legal classification: permissible (jā'iz) or prohibited (ḥarām), and whether liability (ḍamān) attaches.
The text treats each act separately: hiring out, re-lending, and exceeding restrictions.
2
Analyze the conditions for re-lending
Determine whether the borrowed item's benefit varies with use. Items like carrying capacity, employment, or dwelling are non-varying; the borrower may re-lend them. Items like clothing or riding vary; the borrower may not re-lend unless the original loan was unrestricted.
The key distinction is whether the item's benefit differs based on the user. If not, re-lending is allowed.
3
Evaluate liability for hiring out the borrowed item
If the borrower hires out the item and it perishes, he is liable because handing it over to the hirer constitutes exceeding the permitted use (ta‘addī), making him a usurper (ghāṣib). He may choose to hold the hirer liable, and if the borrower is sued, he can recover from the borrower only if the borrower did not know it was a loan.
The text also discusses the hire fee: it must be given in charity according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (contrary to Abu Yusuf).
4
Assess violations of restrictions
If the lender restricts use by time, type, or both, the borrower must not exceed. Violating to a similar or better use (e.g., lighter load) does not incur liability, but violating to a worse use (e.g., heavier load) does. Similarly, exceeding the permitted place or time triggers liability.
The underlying rationale is that the restriction aims to prevent harm; a harmless violation does not create liability.
5
Resolve the dispute on using after re-lending
The text presents a disagreement: if the borrower re-lends an unrestricted item (e.g., a riding animal) and then himself rides it, is he liable? Al-Bazdawi says yes (because permission transferred to the second user), while al-Sarakhsi and Khwahrizadeh say no (because his own permission remains). The relied-upon view is al-Bazdawi's (liability).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the first view.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the rulings of 'ariyah (borrowing for use). The author begins by stating that if the borrower hires out the borrowed item and it perishes, he is liable because he acted as a usurper (ghasib) by handing it over. He may choose to hold the hirer liable, and if he does, he can recover from the borrower unless the borrower knew it was a loan. The borrower, if sued, cannot recover from the hirer because the contract is between the borrower and the hirer; the borrower must give the hire fee as charity (according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, contrary to Abu Yusuf). Then the text discusses re-lending (i'arah) of the borrowed item. The borrower may re-lend only what does not vary with use, such as carrying, employing, or dwelling, because such items have a defined benefit. He may not re-lend items that vary with use, like clothes for wearing or animals for riding, unless the original loan was unrestricted. If the borrower rides after re-lending, there is a dispute: according to al-Bazdawi, he is liable; according to al-Sarakhsi and Khwahrizadeh, he is not. The first view is preferred. When restrictions are placed by the lender (time, type, or both), the borrower must not exceed them. If he violates to a similar or better use, he is not liable, but if to a worse use (heavier load), he is liable. If restricted by time, the borrower is free in other aspects but must return by the time; if he delays, he becomes liable for any loss. Similarly for place restrictions. If the borrower violates a restriction by giving to another person, he is liable for items that vary with use but not for those that don't, except when the lender explicitly forbade it. Finally, a fatwa from Qadikhan states that if someone borrows a camel to go to a specific place, he may go and return; if no place is specified, he may not leave the city.

Borrowing Fungibles as a Loan (Qard)
Ruling that borrowing fungibles is qard with exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qard and 'ariyah when borrowing fungible items, and apply the exceptions (familiarity, lender's statement) correctly.

Core Idea
Borrowing consumables is a loan: you own and must return the like, not the specific item.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle: when someone borrows an item that is typically consumed or cannot be returned intact, the contract is actually a loan (qard), not a loan for use ('ariyah). This affects ownership and liability. We will examine the items involved, the default ruling, and exceptions based on the lender's words or the nature of use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items
The text mentions thamanayn (gold/silver), makil (measured by volume), mawzun (weighed), ma'dud (counted). These are fungibles where using them normally consumes them.
These are examples; the principle applies to any consumable.
2
Understand the default ruling
Borrowing such items is qard. The borrower owns the item and must return its like (mithl) or value (qima). The lender cannot demand the specific item back.
Contrast with 'ariyah where the specific item must be returned.
3
Note the exception: 'I do not require its return'
If the lender says 'I do not require its return', then it becomes 'ariyah. The borrower may use it but must return the same object if it is not consumed (e.g., gold for display).
This exception changes ownership back to the lender.
4
Check the condition: usability with substance intact
If the item can be used while remaining intact (e.g., borrowing a dirham to calibrate a scale, or borrowing jewelry), it is 'ariyah, not qard.
The substance of the item is not consumed in such uses.
5
Consider familiarity (mabasita)
If there is a close relationship between lender and borrower, the transaction may be considered a gift (ibaha) rather than either contract.
This is a nuance to avoid strict liability in informal settings.

Borrowing Land for Building or Planting: Validity and Revocation
Ruling on borrowing land for building/planting: validity, revocation, and compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on borrowing land for building or planting, explain the right to revoke, and determine liability for compensation based on timing and harm.

Core Idea
Borrowing land for building is valid but revocable; timing affects the lender's liability for removal costs.

This lesson covers a common scenario: someone allows another to build a house or plant trees on their land. Is this a lease or a loan? Hanafi fiqh treats it as a loan for use ('ariyah), which is non-binding. The lender can revoke at any time, but if he set a time limit and revokes early, he must compensate the borrower for the loss caused by removal. We will also examine the rules regarding who pays for removal and what happens if removal harms the land.

Guided Path
1
Confirm the validity
The passage states that lending land for building or planting is valid (sahih) because the benefit (manfa'ah) is known and measurable.
This is a contract of 'ariyah, not ijarah.
2
Understand the right to revoke
Since 'ariyah is non-binding (ghayr lazim), the lender can revoke at any time before the borrower has taken full benefit.
This distinguishes it from a lease which is binding for the term.
3
Determine the obligation to remove
Upon revocation, the borrower must remove the building or trees (qal') at his own expense, unless removal would cause harm to the land.
If harm would occur, the borrower may leave the structure and pay its value as if removed.
4
Apply timing rules
If no time limit was set (ghayr muwaqqat), the lender can revoke without compensation. If a time limit was set and he revokes before expiry (muwaqqat), he must compensate for the decrease in value caused by removal (ma naqasa bi al-qal').
Compensation is the difference between the value of the land with the structure remaining for the remainder of the term and the value after removal.
5
Assess valuation and liability
The value is assessed on the day of retrieval (yawm al-istirdad). The borrower is not liable for any decline due to passage of time, only due to premature revocation.
This protects the borrower from being misled by the lender's promise.

Commentary
This passage addresses two separate but related issues. First, it clarifies that when a lender gives an item that is consumed by use (like food, money), this is actually a loan (qard), not a loan for use ('ariyah). The borrower is obligated to return the like or value. However, if there is a close relationship, it may be considered a gift (ibaha). The text gives examples: borrowing a patch for a shirt or timber for construction is a loan because the item becomes part of the borrower's property. If the lender says 'I do not require its return', it becomes 'ariyah because the specific object is to be returned. The distinction is that qard does not require the return of the specific object, while 'ariyah does. Then it discusses borrowing an animal for use: that is 'ariyah, not qard, because the animal is used but not consumed. Second, it addresses borrowing land for building or planting. The contract is valid because the benefit is known. The lender has the right to revoke at any time since 'ariyah is non-binding. If he revokes, the borrower is forced to remove the structure or plants, unless removal would harm the land, in which case the borrower may leave it and pay the value as if removed. If the lender set a time limit and revokes before the time expires, he must compensate the borrower for the decrease in value due to removal (the difference between the value as standing with the remaining time and the value as removed). The value is assessed on the day of retrieval. There is a comment about a potential contradiction: if removal does not harm the land, some say the borrower has the option to remove or pay full value, but the text of the book suggests only compensation for the deficiency. Finally, there is a brief comment on an earlier phrase about half the value when two ride an animal, explaining that both riders caused the damage.

Gift from One to Two: Conditions of Validity in Hanafi Law
Intra-Hanafi dispute on gift to two donees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions under which a gift from one donor to two donees is valid or invalid per the Hanafi school, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
A gift from one to two is invalid according to Abu Hanifa due to undivided share, but valid per the Sahibayn if possession is taken jointly.

In Hanafi gift law, a single donor giving an undivided item to two donees raises a key dispute about undivided shares (shuyuʿ). The matn states the reverse (one to two) is invalid, but the commentary reveals a nuanced intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with conditions and even a legal trick (hila). This lesson unpacks the ruling, the arguments, and the applied conditions.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling and the ikhtilaf
Read the matn: 'not the reverse' – one to two is invalid (Abu Hanifa). But the Sahibayn permit it. Identify the core issue: undivided share (shuyuʿ).
2
Identify the proofs for each position
Abu Hanifa argues it is two contracts (evidenced by partial acceptance invalidating for one donee). Sahibayn view it as one contract. Note the analogy to sale where one acceptance invalidates the whole.
3
Apply the conditions from the text
Conditions: donees must be adults (if one minor, invalid by consensus); property must be divisible (indivisible like a room is valid by consensus); if shares specified, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf still invalid, but Muhammad permits with possession.
4
Examine the legal trick (hila)
To avoid shuyuʿ, deliver the property to one donee first, then make the gift to both. This sequence ensures no undivided share at the time of the contract.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi scholars give fatwa according to the Sahibayn when possession is taken jointly. This is the practical ruling to follow.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the validity of a gift from one donor to two donees without specifying each share. The matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq states that a gift from two to one is valid, but the reverse (one to two) is not. The commentary explains the reasoning and conditions. According to Abu Hanifa, such a gift is invalid because it is effectively two separate contracts (half to each), and since the shares are undivided at the time of the contract, the gift is invalid. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it one contract and therefore valid, as no undivided share exists if the donor delivers the item and the donees take possession together. However, several conditions are imposed. First, the ruling applies only if the donees are adults; if one is a minor in the care of the donor, the gift is invalid by consensus because the donor becomes possessor of the minor's share immediately, causing undivided share. Second, if the donor specifies shares (e.g., one-third and two-thirds), Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say it is invalid, while Muhammad allows it if the donees take possession. Third, the gifted property must be divisible; something like a single room is unanimously valid because division is not possible. Fourth, if the donor is two persons giving to two donees with specified shares, it is invalid by consensus. The commentary also includes a refinement by al-Ramli, who notes that the restriction to adult donees actually points to a difference: if both minors are in the care of the donor, the gift is valid according to the Sahibayn due to immediate possession. The text discusses the cause (illa) of undivided share and offers a legal trick (hila): deliver the item to the adult first, then make the gift to both. This passage highlights the importance of shuyu' (undivided share) in Hanafi gift law.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the unique hybrid contract of a gift given with a condition of return compensation. The Hanafi school, using istihsan (juristic preference), treats it as a gift in its initial formation but a sale in its binding effect, combining the rules of both to honor its dual nature. Commentary
This passage, from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, addresses the legal nature of a gift given with a condition that the recipient must give something in return (hibah bishart al-'iwad). The author states that such a gift is considered a gift (hibah) in its inception, meaning at the moment of contract it is formed as a gift. Consequently, the normal rules of a gift apply initially: mutual taking of possession (qabd) of both the gifted item and the compensation is required, and the gift is invalid if the gifted property is undivided (musha'). However, in its ultimate effect, this transaction is treated like a sale. Therefore, it can be returned due to a defect (radd bil-'ayb), the buyer has the option of sight (khiyar al-ru'ya), and the property is subject to pre-emption (shuf'a). The commentator explains the reasoning: the contract has two aspects. It partakes of the nature of a gift (where ownership transfers only upon qabd and the gift is initially non-binding) and of a sale (which is binding). To respect both similarities, the Hanafis combine the rules as much as possible. Zufar disagrees, holding it a sale from start to finish. The passage cites several authorities: al-Haqa'iq, Ghayat al-Bayan, Fatawa Qadikhan, and al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyya to show the predominant view that it is essentially a sale. It also discusses a related issue: coercion in such a transaction is treated like coercion in a sale. The commentary by al-Nasihi and al-Majma' extend the discussion to endowment and gift of a minor's property. The bottom line is that when the 'iwad is specified, the transaction is akin to a sale; if the 'iwad is unknown, it remains a pure gift (and the condition is void).

Key Terms
هبة بشرط العوض (hibah bishart al-'iwad): A gift given on the condition that the recipient gives something in return.

Conditional Remission of Debt is Invalid
Ruling on conditional remission of debt: statement is void

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a statement of debt forgiveness is void due to conditionality, and explain the Hanafi reasoning based on the dual nature of ibra'.

Core Idea
A conditional promise to forgive a debt is void — ibra' must be unconditional.

We often hear creditors say 'if you pay half, the rest is forgiven.' The Hanafi school clarifies that such statements have no legal effect. Why? Because forgiving a debt (ibra') is a form of ownership transfer that cannot be suspended on an uncertain future event. This lesson examines the rule and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Recognize the statement as an attempt at ibra' (remission of debt) or hibat al-dayn (gift of debt).
The examples given: 'If tomorrow comes, it is yours', 'You are free from it', etc.
2
Detect the condition
Determine if the statement is conditional upon a future event (ta'liq).
E.g., 'If you pay half, you are free from the remaining half.'
3
Apply the ruling
State that such a conditional statement is batil (void) with no legal effect.
The debt remains as if the statement was never made.
4
Understand the reason
Analyze why: ibra' is both a discharge (isqat) and a transfer of ownership (tamlik). As a transfer, it requires certainty; conditionality introduces gharar (uncertainty), making it void.
The text says 'because a gift of a debt is a discharge, and it is a transfer of ownership in one sense, so it is revocable by rejection...'
5
Note the consequence
The debt remains intact; the creditor still has the right to demand payment.
No forgiveness occurs even if the condition is later fulfilled.

Commentary
This passage addresses statements made by a creditor to a debtor that appear to forgive or gift the debt conditionally. For example, 'If you come tomorrow, the debt is forgiven' or 'If you pay half, the other half is forgiven.' The author rules that such statements are void (batil). The reason: a gift of a debt (hibat al-dayn) is essentially a discharge (ibra'), and it is a transfer of ownership in one sense (the debt is owned by the creditor, and he is transferring it to the debtor by forgiving it). Because it is partly a transfer of ownership, it requires acceptance and is subject to rejection (radd) by the debtor even after the session (majlis). However, if the debtor rejects it, the debt remains. The conditional nature makes it invalid because ibra' cannot be suspended on a future condition; it must be unconditional. There is a difference of opinion on whether ibra' can be rejected after the session, but here the focus is that the condition renders the statement void. The phrase 'it is a form of relinquishment in another sense' indicates that ibra' is also an act of forfeiting a right, which does not require acceptance, but the combination makes it problematic.

Key Terms
هبة الدين (hibat al-dayn): A gift of a debt, i.e., the creditor giving the debt to the debtor (forgiving it).
