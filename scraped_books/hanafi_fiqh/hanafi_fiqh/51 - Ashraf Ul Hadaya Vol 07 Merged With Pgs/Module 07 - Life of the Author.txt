The Authority of the Imam in War: The Story of Thumamah
Relates the conversion of Thumamah and his refusal to send grain to Makkah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that individual Muslims cannot override the Imam's authority in military matters, using the story of Thumamah.

Core Idea
The Imam's authority supersedes individual initiative in war - only the Imam can permit or forbid actions against the enemy.

The story of Thumamah ibn Uthal is a powerful illustration of the balance between individual initiative and communal authority in Islam. After his conversion, Thumamah acted independently to blockade grain from the Quraysh, but when they appealed to the Prophet, he permitted the grain to pass. This shows that while a Muslim may take lawful action against enemies, the ultimate authority rests with the Imam (leader) who can override such actions based on public interest (maslahah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the historical context
Understand the events: Thumamah's capture, conversion, and subsequent blockade of grain to Makkah.
This sets the stage for the legal principle.
2
Recognize the legal principle
Thumamah's blockade was an independent act of war against the Quraysh, who were enemies of the Muslims.
The Hanafi school permits individual Muslims to harm the enemy, but it is not an absolute right.
3
Understand the Imam's authority
The Quraysh appealed to the Prophet, who had the authority to permit or forbid the blockade. The Prophet permitted it, showing his authority overrides individual action.
This establishes the principle that the Imam can restrict or allow what individuals do in warfare.
4
Apply to Hanafi fiqh
In Hanafi law, the Imam (or his deputy) has the final say in matters of war, including trade embargoes, blockades, and granting aman.
Individual Muslims must defer to the Imam's judgment.

Hazrat Thumamah ibn Uthal (may Allah be pleased with him) had been given permission to send grain to the people of Makkah for food and not to prevent it, even though at that time the people of Makkah were open enemies of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) and all Muslims. (The story is that once, when Thumamah was a disbeliever, he was captured somehow. By the order of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him), he was tied to a pillar of the mosque. After three days, he was untied. Then Thumamah (may Allah be pleased with him) left from there, after a short while he changed and arrived of his own accord, and accepted Islam at the hands of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him). Afterwards, he went to his people. The Quraysh began to taunt him, saying, 'You became apostate out of fear.' He sent back word: 'I did not become apostate, but I entered from a false religion into the true religion, which is the religion of the Messenger of Allah.' At the same time he swore, 'By Allah, now you will not get a single grain of food from our region, even though grain used to be sent from here to Makkah, until the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) permits it.' Upon this, the Quraysh became very distressed and remorseful, and wrote to the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) and adjured him by kindness and goodness. Then the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) permitted Hazrat Thumamah to send the grain, as reported by Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Hisham, and Waqidi.)

Commentary
This is a historical anecdote from the Siyar literature illustrating the principle that a Muslim may independently take action against the enemy, such as cutting off supplies, but ultimately the authority of the Imam (the Prophet in this case) prevails. The story shows that Thumamah, after his conversion, used his personal authority to block grain to Makkah, but when the Quraysh appealed to the Prophet, he allowed it. This establishes that individual Muslims have the right to harm the enemy, but the Imam may override or permit such actions based on maslahah (public interest). The story is cited as background for the rulings on aman (safe-conduct) that follow.

Key Terms
إمام (Imam): The leader of the Muslim community, who has authority over military and public affairs.
