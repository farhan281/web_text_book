The Principle of Maintenance from a Missing Person's Wealth
Ruling on maintenance from missing person's wealth for dependents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between relatives who are entitled to maintenance from a missing person's wealth without a judge's order and those who require a judge's order, and apply this principle to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Close dependents' maintenance is wajib without a judge's order; extended relatives need a judicial ruling.

This passage from the Book of the Missing Person (Mafqud) of al-Hidayah addresses a key question: when a husband/father disappears, who can claim maintenance from his estate? The author clarifies that the answer depends on the relationship and whether the entitlement existed before his absence. We will unpack the principle and see how it distinguishes between categories of relatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling's scope
Note that the ruling applies to maintenance (nafaqah) from the wealth of a missing person. The author initially mentions wife and children but expands to all blood relatives (qarabat al-walad).
The scope includes ascendants and descendants, not just nuclear family.
2
Extract the core principle
The principle: entitlement during absence mirrors entitlement during presence. If a relative could receive maintenance without a judge's order when the person was present, they can also receive it during absence. The judge's order is merely confirmatory.
The judge's order is 'i'ānah' (assistance), not a source of obligation.
3
Apply to the first category
Identify which relatives are entitled without a judge's order: minor children, adult daughters, and disabled adult sons. These have an inherent right to maintenance due to their dependency and close kinship.
Adult daughters are typically unmarried and without means; disabled sons cannot earn.
4
Apply to the second category
Identify relatives who require a judge's order: siblings (brother, sister), maternal uncle, maternal aunt. These have no automatic entitlement; the obligation arises only by judicial decree, which is impossible against an absent person.
The judge lacks authority to impose a new obligation on an absent person's wealth.
5
Note the practical consequence
The practical outcome: the judge-appointed administrator must pay maintenance to the first category automatically. For the second category, they cannot be paid from the missing person's wealth until his return or death is confirmed.
This protects the missing person's wealth from unauthorized depletion.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement with commentary from the Book of the Missing Person, addressing maintenance (nafaqah) from the wealth of a missing person. The author clarifies that maintenance is not limited to wife and children but includes all relatives connected by birth (walad), such as parents, grandparents, and grandchildren. The key principle is that entitlement to maintenance in the missing person's absence follows the same rules as in his presence. Those who are entitled to maintenance without a judge's order when the person is present (e.g., minor children, adult unmarried daughters, and disabled adult sons) are also entitled during his absence. The judge's order in such cases merely confirms a pre-existing obligation. On the other hand, those who only become entitled through a judge's order in the person's presence (e.g., siblings, maternal uncle/aunt) cannot claim maintenance during his absence because the judge cannot impose an obligation on an absent person. This distinction ensures that the judge does not overstep his limited authority over the absent person's wealth, which is restricted to preservation. The Hanafi school holds that maintenance for the first category is wajib (obligatory) even without a court order, based on the principle of necessity and kinship.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance, including food, clothing, and shelter, provided to dependents.
والد (wālad): Literally 'one who begets'; in context, all blood relatives (qarabat al-walad).
استحقاق (istiḥqāq): Entitlement to maintenance, which may be inherent or established by a judge's ruling.
زمنى (zamānā): Disabled adult males who are unable to earn a living.

Definition of 'His Wealth' for Maintenance Purposes
Defining what constitutes 'wealth' for spending from missing person's estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'wealth' in the context of maintenance from a missing person's estate and apply the ruling that cash or bullion is used when food or clothing are not available in kind.

Core Idea
For maintenance from a missing person's estate, 'wealth' means cash or bullion, not every asset.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri clarifies that when a judge orders maintenance from a missing person's estate, the term 'his wealth' does not include all property. Instead, it specifically refers to cash (dirhams and dinars) or bullion (tibr), because the dependents' right is ultimately to food and clothing, and cash is the means to provide them. This precision avoids confusion and ensures the obligation is fulfilled practically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key phrase
The key phrase being defined is 'from his wealth' (min mālihi) in the context of maintenance.
2
State the author's definition
The author states that 'wealth' here specifically means dirhams and dinars (cash).
The term 'dirhams and dinars' refers to silver and gold coins, i.e., cash money.
3
Explain the rationale
The rationale is that the dependents' right is to receive food and clothing. If these are not present in the missing person's estate, their value must be given, and that value is in cash.
Cash is the medium to purchase necessities.
4
Extend the ruling to bullion
Bullion (uncoined gold/silver) is treated like minted coins because it can serve as a price.
The text says 'bullion is like them in this ruling, because it can serve as a price, like minted coins.'
5
Note the condition
This ruling applies only when the wealth is in the judge's possession or control.
The judge can only order payment from assets he has custody of.

Commentary

This reflects the Hanafi pragmatic approach: maintenance is to be provided in the form of actual necessities or their cash equivalent, and bullion is considered equivalent to currency for fulfilling this obligation.

Conditions for Spending from Deposit or Debt and Liability for Unauthorized Payment
Ruling on conditions and liability for spending from deposit/debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge may order maintenance from an absent person's deposit or debt and identify the liability consequences of unauthorized payment by the depositor or debtor.

Core Idea
Judicial authority and acknowledgment of hidden facts are required to spend another's property.

When a person is missing (ghā'ib) and cannot provide for his dependents, the judge may order maintenance from his assets. But what if those assets are held by others as deposits (wadi'ah) or debts (dayn)? This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies two critical points: first, the conditions for such spending, especially the requirement for acknowledgment (iqrār) when facts are not apparent to the judge; second, the severe liability that falls on the depositor or debtor if they pay without judicial authority. Understanding these rules protects both the absent person's property and the rights of dependents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
The passage deals with a missing person (ghā'ib) whose wealth is held by others: either as a deposit (wadi'ah) with a custodian or as a debt (dayn) owed by a debtor. The judge intends to order maintenance for the dependents from that wealth.
The 'missing person' is one whose whereabouts are unknown and who cannot manage his own affairs.
2
Recognize the condition of acknowledgment
The depositor or debtor must acknowledge three facts: (1) the existence of the deposit or debt, (2) the validity of the missing person's marriage to the wife claimant, and (3) the lineage (nasab) of the other claimants. This is required only when these facts are not apparent (zāhir) to the judge.
Acknowledgment (iqrār) is a legal admission binding in court.
3
Distinguish between apparent and non-apparent facts
If the judge already knows these facts (they are 'apparent'), no acknowledgment is needed. If some are apparent and others not, acknowledgment is required only for the non-apparent ones.
This avoids unnecessary formality when the judge has independent knowledge.
4
Learn the liability ruling for unauthorized payment
If the depositor or debtor pays the dependents without the judge's order, the depositor becomes liable (ḍāmin) to compensate the missing person's estate, and the debtor is not discharged from his debt. Payment without authority is invalid.
The judge acts as the missing person's representative (nā'ib) only when he orders the payment.

Commentary
This passage contains two rulings. The first part addresses the situation where the missing person's wealth is held as a deposit (wadi'ah) or owed as a debt (dayn) by another person. The judge may order that maintenance be given to the dependents from that property, but only on condition that the depositor or debtor acknowledges the following: (1) that the deposit or debt exists, (2) that the missing person's marriage to his wife is still valid, and (3) that the claimants have proper lineage (i.e., they are the missing person's relatives). However, if these facts are already apparent (zāhir) to the judge—i.e., he has knowledge independent of the acknowledgment—then no acknowledgment is required. If only some facts are apparent, acknowledgment is needed for the rest. The second ruling states that if the depositor or debtor pays the dependents without being ordered by the judge, the depositor will be liable (he must replace the property from his own pocket) and the debtor will not be discharged from his debt. This is because they paid someone who is neither the rightful owner nor the judge acting as his representative. Conversely, if the judge orders the payment, the judge is the representative (nā'ib) of the missing person, so payment is valid and discharges the debtor and protects the depositor. The Missing Person's Wife: Hanafi Position on Judicial Separation
Hanafi rebuttal of Maliki's qiyas-based separation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on separation of a missing person's wife, explain the Hanafi proofs (hadith and saying of ʿAlī), and critique the Maliki qiyas argument.

Core Idea
A wife remains wed to her missing husband until certain proof of death or divorce arrives.

This passage presents a fundamental disagreement between the Hanafi and Maliki schools regarding the fate of the wife of a man who has disappeared without trace. Imam Malik allows separation after four years, while the Hanafi school upholds the marriage until definitive evidence emerges. We will examine the proofs on both sides and understand why the Hanafi view is more cautious.

Guided Path
1
State the Maliki position and its basis
Identify that Malik rules for judicial separation after four years, after which the wife observes ʿiddat al-wafāt and may remarry. His proofs: (1) the verdict of ʿUmar in the case of a man taken by jinn, and (2) qiyas on īlāʾ (vow of abstinence, 4 months) and ʿinnah (impotence, 1 year), combined to yield four years.
The qiyas combines two different periods to derive a new duration.
2
Extract the Hanafi counter-proofs
List the Hanafi proofs: (a) a prophetic hadith that the wife remains his wife until clear proof (al-bayān) comes; (b) the saying of ʿAlī advising patience until death or divorce is confirmed; (c) the report that ʿUmar himself reverted to ʿAlī’s view. These establish that certainty is required, not mere passage of time.
The hadith and companion saying are considered stronger than the qiyas.
3
Analyse the rebuttal of the qiyas
Understand why Hanafi scholars reject the analogy with īlāʾ and ʿinnah. Īlāʾ involves a vow that immediately triggers a deferred divorce after expiry; absence is not a vow. ʿInnah is a persistent physical defect, while absence can end with return. The two cases are legally distinct.
The Hanafi critique undermines the measure (miqdār) derived from the analogy.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under the Maliki rule, the wife can remarry after 4 years plus ʿiddah (4 months 10 days). Under the Hanafi rule, she must wait indefinitely until she receives confirmed news of death or divorce, or until the Qadi estimates that the missing person would have died naturally (usually 90 years from birth).
This is a major difference in outcome for the wife.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
In the Hanafi school, the operative ruling is that no separation is granted; the wife must be patient. Later scholars added the option of presumed death after 90 years, but the core principle remains: the marriage is not dissolved by mere absence.
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is authoritative in fatwa.

Because ʿUmar (may Allah be pleased with him) gave such a verdict regarding the one whom the jinn had taken away in Madinah, and he is sufficient as an imām. And because he prevented her right by absence, so the Qadi separates between them after a period has passed, by analogy with īlāʾ and ʿinnah. And after this analogy, the measure is taken from both: four from īlāʾ and years from ʿinnah, acting on the two analogies. And our (Hanafi) proof is the saying of the Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding the wife of the missing person: 'She is his wife until clear proof comes to her.' And the saying of ʿAlī (may Allah be pleased with him): 'She is a woman who has been tested; let her be patient until death or divorce becomes clear.' This explains the 'clear proof' mentioned in the marfūʿ hadith. And because marriage is known to have been established, and absence does not necessitate separation, and death is only a possibility, so marriage is not removed by doubt. And ʿUmar (may Allah be pleased with him) reverted to ʿAlī's (may Allah be pleased with him) saying. And analogy with īlāʾ is not valid, because īlāʾ is a divorce that is immediate in law but deferred in effect, so it necessitates separation. And nor with ʿinnah, because absence may end with return, while ʿinnah rarely resolves after [a year?].

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the fate of the missing person's wife. Imam Malik holds that after four years of disappearance, the Qadi should separate the couple, and the wife can remarry after an ʿiddah of four months and ten days (like a widow). His reasoning is based on the practice of Caliph ʿUmar in a specific case of a man taken by jinn, and a qiyas (analogy) with īlāʾ (vow of abstention) and ʿinnah (impotence). According to Malik, combining the periods from īlāʾ (four months) and ʿinnah (one year) gives a rough measure, but he ends up with four years. The Hanafi school, represented by Imam Abu Hanifa, rejects this view. Their proofs are: 1) A hadith of the Prophet: the wife of a missing person remains his wife until clear evidence (of death or divorce) comes. 2) The saying of Caliph ʿAlī advising patience until death or divorce is confirmed. 3) ʿUmar himself reverted to ʿAlī's opinion. 4) Marriage is established with certainty, and absence does not necessitate dissolution, nor does doubt. 5) The analogy with īlāʾ is invalid because īlāʾ includes a vow that leads to immediate divorce after a period, whereas absence is not a vow. 6) Analogy with ʿinnah is invalid because impotence usually persists, while absence may end with return. Therefore, Hanafi ruling: no separation is pronounced; the wife must wait until confirmed news of death or divorce, or until the Qadi estimates the missing person's lifespan (normally 90 years from birth) after which she may inherit and remarry.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): Missing person whose whereabouts are unknown.
تفریق (tafrīq): Judicial separation of spouses.
عدۃ وفات (ʿiddat al-wafāt): Waiting period of a widow: 4 months and 10 days.
ایلا (īlāʾ): A vow to abstain from marital relations for four months or more.
عنہ (ʿinnah): Impotence of the husband, grounds for separation in some schools.
