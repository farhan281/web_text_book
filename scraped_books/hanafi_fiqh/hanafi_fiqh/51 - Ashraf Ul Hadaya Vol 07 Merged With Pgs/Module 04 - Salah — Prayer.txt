Jihad Obligatory Even Without Aggression
States that fighting disbelievers is obligatory even if they do not initiate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on initiating jihad and identify its basis in the general texts of the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Fighting disbelievers is wajib even without aggression, because the Qur'anic commands are general and unconditional.

This passage from al-Hidayah lays down a fundamental ruling of Hanafi fiqh: jihad against non-Muslims is an obligation that does not depend on their initiation of hostilities. The text anchors this obligation in the general imperatives of the Qur'an and Sunnah, which command fighting until fitnah is removed and religion is for Allah. Understanding this ruling is crucial for grasping the Hanafi perspective on the scope of jihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its classification
Read the Arabic matn and note that the ruling is 'wajib' (obligatory). In Hanafi usul, 'wajib' is distinct from 'fard' — it is an obligation proven by zanni (probable) evidence, such as general commands.
The text explicitly uses 'wājib', not 'fard'.
2
Identify the condition under which this ruling applies
The matn states 'wa in lam yadū' (even if they do not initiate). This means the obligation is not contingent on the enemy's aggression. It applies to offensive jihad (jihad al-ṭalab) as well as defensive.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from those who consider jihad only defensive.
3
Locate the basis of the ruling
The text points to 'al-ʿumūmāt' (the general texts). These are Qur'anic verses and hadiths that command fighting disbelievers without qualification. The commentator explains that the purpose is to exalt Allah's word and establish justice.
General proofs (ʿumūm) in Hanafi usul indicate the ruling extends to all instances unless specified.
4
Understand the purpose behind the ruling
The commentary clarifies that the obligation aims at elevating Allah's word, establishing justice, and removing corruption, disbelief, and oppression — not merely retaliation.
This teleological dimension shows that jihad is a means to a moral end, not raw aggression.
5
Differentiate from defensive jihad
Defensive jihad (jihad al-dafʿ) becomes fard ʿayn when Muslims are attacked, but offensive jihad (jihad al-ṭalab) is generally fard kifayah (communal obligation). This passage refers to the latter variety.
Even offensive jihad is obligatory, but its implementation depends on the imam and community capacity.

Defensive Jihad: Fard 'Ayn Overrides Rights of Husband and Master
Explains that in defensive emergency, jihad becomes individual obligation overriding spousal and master rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that defensive jihad is fard 'ayn and explain the qiyas to salah and sawm that overrides spousal and master rights.

Core Idea
When enemy attacks, defensive jihad becomes fard 'ayn—no one's permission can stop you.

This passage from the sharh of al-Hidayah addresses the critical scenario of defensive jihad. When an enemy attacks a Muslim territory, the obligation becomes individual and absolute, overriding the normal authority of husbands over wives and masters over slaves. The author uses a powerful analogy to prayer and fasting to prove that ownership and marriage bonds cannot obstruct a personal religious duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is defensive jihad when an enemy attacks a Muslim city. The ruling is that it becomes fard 'ayn (individual obligation) on every capable person.
Fard 'ayn means the duty cannot be fulfilled by others.
2
Recognize the consequence of the ruling
Because it is fard 'ayn, normal rights of masters and husbands are suspended: a woman may go without her husband's permission, and a slave without his master's permission.
The text explicitly states that they go out without permission.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to salah and sawm, which are also fard 'ayn and cannot be prevented by any human authority.
The analogy highlights that ownership and marriage rights have no effect on individual obligations.
4
Distinguish from the normal state
Before the general call (nafir 'am), jihad is fard kifayah, and the husband's and master's rights take precedence because the duty can be fulfilled by others.
The exception shows that the ruling is limited to defensive emergencies.
5
Note additional conditions
The commentator adds that one must have physical capability, proper equipment, and not be certain of being killed without benefit.
These conditions are derived from the broader fiqh of jihad.

Fundraising for Jihad: When the Treasury Is Sufficient
Ruling statement with proof and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on collecting voluntary contributions (juʿl) for jihad when the bayt al-māl is solvent, and identify the condition that changes the ruling.

Core Idea
When the state treasury is full, fundraising for jihad is makruh—necessity alone permits it.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a practical question: Is it permissible to raise voluntary funds for jihad when the Islamic state already has money in its treasury? The Hanafi school gives a nuanced ruling based on necessity, distinguishing between a solvent and an empty treasury. The author provides the reasoning (taʿlīl) and supports it with Prophetic and Caliphal practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is collecting juʿl (reward/compensation) for jihad. The author states it is makrūh as long as the treasury has funds for the Muslims.
Makrūh here is likely makrūh tanzīhī (slightly disliked).
2
Understand the underlying reason (taʿlīl)
Why is it disliked? Because it resembles a wage (ajr) for a religious duty, and there is no necessity (ḍarūrah) since the treasury is meant for this purpose.
The spiritual reward of jihad should be sought for Allah, not for worldly compensation.
3
Identify the exception condition
If the treasury is empty, then there is no harm (lā baʾs) if Muslims support each other. This is because repelling a greater harm (the enemy) with a lesser harm (financial contribution) is justified.
The principle of repelling greater harm with lesser harm is key.
4
Note the supporting evidence
The author cites: (1) The Prophet ﷺ took armor from Ṣafwān (a wealthy Companion) for jihad. (2) ʿUmar would equip Bedouins with horses and gifts to motivate them to fight.
These show that when the state lacks resources, private contributions are accepted.
5
Apply the overall ruling
In summary: if the treasury has funds, fundraising is makrūh; if it is empty, it is permissible and even commendable to support the war effort.
Always assess the state of the bayt al-māl.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on the propriety of fundraising for jihad when the state treasury (bayt al-mal) is solvent. The matn declares it makruh (disliked) to collect voluntary contributions (ju'l) in such a case. The reason is twofold: it resembles a wage (which diminishes the spiritual reward), and there is no necessity since the treasury is meant to fund the Muslims' needs. However, if the treasury is empty, then it is permissible (la ba'sa) for Muslims to support each other, as this involves repelling a greater harm (the enemy's attack) with a lesser harm (financial contribution). Evidence is given from the Prophet's action of taking armor from Safwan (a wealthy companion) for jihad, and from Umar's practice of incentivizing the Bedouins to fight by giving them gifts and horses. The commentator explains that the treasury's wealth is designated for such purposes, so fundraising is unnecessary and therefore disliked.

Key Terms
جعل (juʿl): Reward or compensation given for performing a service; here referring to voluntary contributions collected for jihad.
مکروہ (makrūh): Disliked; an action whose omission is rewarded but whose commission is not sinful, though it is blameworthy.
بیت المال (bayt al-māl): The public treasury of the Islamic state.
ضرورت (ḍarūrah): Necessity; here the absence of necessity justifies the dislike.

Ruling on Taking Women and Mushaf to Jihad
Ruling on taking women and Quran into enemy territory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking women and the Quran into enemy territory, and explain how the hadith prohibition is interpreted (ta'wil) based on risk.

Core Idea
The permissibility of taking women and the Quran into jihad depends on the army's size: large armies are safe, small parties risk disgrace.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a practical concern in jihad: may Muslims take their women and the Quran with them into enemy territory? The answer is not absolute; it hinges on the security of the force. The author explains the conditional ruling and provides the correct interpretation of a relevant hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is taking women and copies of the Quran (mushaf) into enemy territory. The ruling varies: it is permissible (la ba'sa) with a large army, but disliked (yukrahu) with a small raiding party.
The terms 'la ba'sa' and 'yukrahu' are key indicators of the hukm level.
2
Note the conditions for permissibility and dislike
Permissibility depends on the army being 'askar 'azim (large) where safety is dominant. Dislike applies to sariyya (small party) where security is not assured, due to risk of women being disgraced and the Quran being desecrated.
The legal principle: 'the dominant is like certainty' (al-ghalib ka al-muhaqqaq) is used to justify permissibility.
3
Locate the hadith proof and the Hanafi ta'wil
The Prophet ﷺ said: 'Do not travel with the Quran in the enemy's land' (narrated by Bukhari and Muslim). The Hanafis interpret this as directed at situations where disrespect is feared, not as an absolute ban.
The ta'wil (interpretation) limits the apparent general prohibition to cases of risk.
4
Examine the exception: entering with aman
If a Muslim enters enemy territory with aman (safe conduct) and the enemy is trustworthy, it is permissible to carry the Quran, as there is no fear of disrespect.
This shows that the prohibition is based on the illa of risk, not on the act itself.
5
Note the practical details about women's roles
Old women may go out with a large army to perform necessary tasks (cooking, nursing), but young women should stay home to avoid fitna. Women should not fight except in dire necessity. For sexual service, slave women are preferred over free women.
There is a conflicting view from Al-Dhakhira that even old women cannot be taken out, but the relied-upon view is the one stated in the matn.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Al-Hidayah on the permissibility of taking women and copies of the Quran (mushaf) into jihad. The ruling is conditional on the size and security of the army. The author, Imam al-Marghinani, first presents the matn from Al-Quduri: there is no harm (la ba'sa) in taking them with a large army ('askar 'azim) because safety is the dominant expectation, and the dominant is treated as certain in legal reasoning. In a small raiding party (sariyya) where security is not assured, it is disliked (yukrahu) because of the risk of women being captured and disgraced, and the Quran being desecrated by the enemy. This understanding provides the correct interpretation (ta'wil sahih) of the Prophet's hadith 'Do not travel with the Quran in the enemy's land' – the prohibition is due to the risk of disrespect, not an absolute ban. If a Muslim enters enemy territory with aman (safe conduct) and the enemy is trustworthy, it is permissible to carry the Quran. The commentary then details the roles: old women may go out to perform essential tasks like cooking, nursing, etc., but young women should stay home to avoid fitna. Women should not directly fight except in dire necessity, as it signals weakness. Taking women for sexual service is not recommended; if necessary, slave women are preferred over free women. A later note from Al-Dhakhira states that even old women are not allowed, indicating a difference within the school.

Women and Slaves in Jihad: The Requirement of Permission
States ruling on permission for women and slaves in jihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on women and slaves participating in jihad, identify the condition of permission, and explain the exception of defensive necessity.

Core Idea
Without permission, a woman or slave cannot fight; but when the enemy attacks, necessity overrides.

This lesson addresses a specific fiqh ruling from the Hanafi school regarding who may participate in jihad. The central point is that a woman and a slave are not independent agents in this context; their participation is conditional upon permission from the person who holds authority over them. However, an important exception exists when the enemy attacks the city, at which point necessity permits them to fight without prior authorization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and subjects
The act is participation in jihad (fighting). The subjects are a woman and a slave. Note that the ruling applies to these two categories specifically.
Jihad here refers to offensive or defensive military expeditions.
2
State the default ruling
The default ruling is that it is impermissible (haram) for a woman to fight without her husband's permission, and similarly for a slave without his master's permission.
The prohibition is due to the rights of the husband and master being violated.
3
Identify the condition for validity
The condition that makes participation valid is obtaining explicit permission (idhn) from the husband or master. Without this condition, the act is legally prohibited.
Permission is a shart (condition) for the permissibility of the act.
4
Recognize the exception
The exception is when the enemy attacks the city (hujum al-'aduww 'ala al-balad). In that case, necessity (darurah) overrides the requirement of permission.
The exception is limited to defensive necessity; it does not apply to offensive expeditions.
5
Understand the rationale
The underlying rationale is that the rights of the husband and master over the woman and slave are otherwise nullified. In a defensive emergency, the necessity of repelling the enemy takes precedence over these rights.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle of ‘darurah yuzīlu al-haraj’ (necessity removes hardship).

Prohibition of Mutilation (Muthla) in Jihad
Narrates incident of 'Uraynah and establishes prohibition of muthla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that muthla is haram, identify the dalil from the Sunnah, and explain the abrogation or qisas exception from the 'Uraynah incident.

Core Idea
The Prophet's explicit prohibition of muthla after the 'Uraynah incident makes it permanently haram.

The early retaliation against the 'Uraynah tribe might seem to permit mutilation, but later prophetic sermons establish a clear ban. This lesson examines the hadith evidence and the Hanafi position that muthla is absolutely forbidden in jihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
State the act (muthla) and the ruling (haram) according to the Hanafi school.
The act is mutilation of the body, whether of a living or dead enemy.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the Prophet's prohibition in his sermons after the 'Uraynah incident, narrated by Anas and 'Imran ibn Husayn.
The hadith 'The Prophet forbade mutilation' is the key evidence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
In Hanafi usul, the imperative verb 'nahā' (forbade) indicates a clear prohibition, making the act haram.
The prohibition is explicit and applies generally.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars, including Qatadah, Ibn Sirin, and Imam al-Shafi'i, considered the earlier incident abrogated or a specific case of qisas.
The Hanafi position maintains the general prohibition is not contradicted.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi mufta bihi is absolute prohibition, supported by the consistent prophetic sermons.
The 'Uraynah incident is seen as a one-time retaliation (qisas) before the revelation of the prohibition.

The Prohibition of Killing Non-Combatants and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on the Effective Cause
States the Hanafi ruling and the inter-madhhab dispute on the effective cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on killing non-combatants, explain the illa of hirab, and compare it with the Shafi'i illa of kufr to identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Jihad permits killing only actual combatants, not women, children, or the incapacitated.

This lesson examines a fundamental inter-madhhab dispute in the law of jihad: what makes it permissible to kill an enemy combatant? The Hanafi school anchors permissibility in actual fighting (hirab), while the Shafi'i school anchors it in disbelief (kufr). This difference has profound practical consequences for who may be targeted in war. In this passage from al-Hidayah, the author presents the Hanafi position and its proof, then rebuts the Shafi'i view.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify which categories of people the Hanafi school forbids killing in jihad: women, children, the very old, the lame, the blind, and others who cannot fight.
The ruling is based on the illa of hirab (actual fighting).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees regarding the old, lame, and blind, allowing their killing because they are disbelievers.
The Shafi'i illa is kufr (disbelief).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i argument: killing is permitted because of kufr, which is present in all disbelievers regardless of combat status.
This leads to a broader permission to kill non-combatants.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds with explicit hadiths forbidding killing women and children, and the Prophet's disapproval of killing a non-fighting woman. These hadiths prove that the cause is hirab, not mere kufr.
The hadiths serve as evidence (dalil naqli) that restrict the permission.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi law, an old blind man may not be killed unless he fights or gives harmful advice; under Shafi'i law, he may be killed simply because he is a disbeliever.
This illustrates the real-world impact of the dispute over the illa.

Initiating Killing of Polytheist Father: Ruling and Proof
Ruling on initiating killing of one's polytheist father in battle, with proof and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim son initiating killing his polytheist father in battle, explain the Quranic proof (Q 31:15) and the obligation of nafaqah, and distinguish between prohibited initiation and permissible self-defense.

Core Idea
A Muslim son must never initiate killing his polytheist father—but may defend himself if attacked.

Jihad places Muslims in battle against polytheists, but what if one's own father is among the enemy? This lesson unpacks a nuanced Hanafi ruling: initiating the father's killing is prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi), yet self-defense is permitted. The proof lies in the Quranic command to treat parents with goodness and the son's duty to maintain the father's life. However, when the father attacks, the son's right to preserve his own life overrides.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the exact action being ruled upon: a Muslim son taking the initiative (ibtida) to kill his polytheist father in battle. The ruling is 'makruh' – here, makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked), near haram.
Do not confuse with self-defense scenarios; the ruling only applies to initiation.
2
Locate the first dalil (Quran)
The text cites Q 31:15: وَصَاحِبُهُمَا فِي الدُّنْيَا مَعْرُوفًا (Accompany them in the world with goodness). Consider how a general command to treat parents with ihsan can prohibit killing them, especially initiating.
The verse is qat'iy al-thubut but zanni al-dalala; it requires interpretation.
3
Trace the second dalil (qiyas based on nafaqah)
The son is obligated to spend (nafaqah) on the father to preserve his life. Since the son must maintain the father's life, he cannot be the one to end it by initiating. This is reasoning through talil (effective cause).
This is an istihsan-like argument: the illa (cause) is the duty to preserve life.
4
Apply the exception: self-defense (daf')
Read the passage: if the father attacks the son and the son cannot repel him except by killing, then it is permissible (la ba'sa). The son's intent is defense (daf'), not initiation. The text draws an analogy to a Muslim father attacking his son.
The Hanafi principle: al-daf' mubah (defense is permissible), and one's life takes precedence.
5
Note the practical ruling: restraint strategy
If the son encounters the father but is not attacked, he should restrain the father (e.g., cut his horse's leg) until another Muslim kills him. This avoids sin while achieving the military objective.
The same applies to other maharim (mother, grandfather, etc.).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hidayah, expanded by the commentator. It states that it is makruh (disliked and sinful) for a Muslim son to take the initiative and kill his own father who is among the polytheists fighting Muslims. The proof is the Quranic verse commanding good treatment of parents (31:15) and the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) that the son owes the father. Since the son must sustain the father's life, he cannot take the lead in ending it. However, if they meet face to face in battle and the son can prevent the father without killing (e.g., by cutting his horse's leg or pushing him off), he should do so until another Muslim kills the father. But if the father attacks the son and there is no other way to defend himself, the son may kill the father in self-defense. This is based on the principle that preserving one's own life is fard. The commentator analogizes to a case where a Muslim father draws his sword to kill his son—the son may kill in defense. The same ruling applies to other maharim (close relatives like mother, grandfather, grandmother). The son also cannot flee from the father because it would expose other mujahideen to danger, nor can he let the father escape; he must restrain him. The thirst analogy reinforces that saving one's own life takes precedence.

Killing a Father Who Insults the Prophet
Ruling with proof from a Companion incident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a son killing his father for insulting Allah or the Prophet and explain the evidence from the incident of Abu 'Ubaydah (RA).

Core Idea
The Prophet's tacit approval (taqrir) of Abu 'Ubaydah's act makes it permissible for a son to kill his father for blasphemy.

This passage addresses a highly sensitive case: a son hears his father insulting Allah or the Prophet (SAW). The Hanafi school, drawing on a specific Companion precedent, rules that the son may kill his father. The proof is not an explicit command but the Prophet's silence after the act, which in Hanafi usul constitutes tacit approval (taqrir) and thus a valid legal evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that it is permissible (ja'iz) for the son to kill his father if he hears him insulting Allah or the Prophet.
Note that 'ja'iz' here indicates permissibility, not obligation.
2
Identify the evidence
The evidence is the incident of Abu 'Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah (RA), who killed his own father for cursing the Prophet (SAW).
This is a sunnah ahad (single narration) and is zanni in both transmission and meaning.
3
Understand the modality of evidence
The Prophet (SAW) did not reprimand Abu 'Ubaydah or demand compensation. His silence is considered taqrir (tacit approval), which in Hanafi usul is a valid source of law.
Taqrir is a type of sunnah where the Prophet's silence indicates permission.
4
Note scope and limitations
The ruling applies specifically when the father verbally insults Allah or the Prophet directly. It does not apply to other forms of disrespect or to non-family members in the same way.
Some scholars restrict this to cases where the father is an apostate (murtadd) or blasphemer (sabb).

Ruling on Killing Non-Muslim and Rebel Relatives in Battle
Ruling statement on killing relatives in battle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on killing non-Muslim relatives and Muslim rebel relatives in battle, and apply the condition that determines whether the act is makruh or not.

Core Idea
Jihad against disbelief overrides kinship; but Muslim rebels should not be killed by relatives without necessity.

In the context of jihad and internal conflict, Muslims may encounter relatives on the opposing side. This lesson from Al-Hidayah clarifies the Hanafi position on when it is permissible (even not hated) to kill such relatives, and when it becomes disliked (makruh). The key distinction lies in the relative's faith—whether they are polytheists or Muslims rebelling against the legitimate caliph.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and actors
The ruling concerns a Muslim killing a relative during battle. Determine who the killer is and who the victim is.
The killer is always a Muslim soldier; the victim is a relative on the opposing side.
2
Classify the relative's status
Is the relative a non-Muslim (polytheist) or a Muslim rebel? This determines the ruling.
The text mentions polytheists (mushrik) and Muslim rebels (baghi) as two distinct categories.
3
Recall the default hukm for each class
For polytheist relatives, killing is not even disliked (mubah). For Muslim rebels, taking initiative to kill is makruh.
The wording 'not even makruh' indicates a stronger level of permissibility than ordinary mubah.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Jihad against disbelief is a collective duty that overrides family ties. With Muslim rebels, the aim is to minimize bloodshed, so relatives should avoid being the instigators.
The principle balances the obligation of jihad against the need to preserve family bonds in intra-Muslim conflict.

Ruling on Truce (Muwada'ah) with the Enemy
Ruling on the imam making a truce with the enemy for Muslim benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on truce with the enemy and identify the primary evidence from Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Truce is permissible when beneficial, as a form of jihad in meaning.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses the permissibility of the imam making a truce (muwada'ah) with hostile non-Muslims. It clarifies that the imam may agree to peace when it serves the welfare of the Muslim community, supported by the Quranic command to incline to peace and the Prophet's own treaty at Hudaybiyyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act under discussion? The imam making a truce (muwada'ah) with the enemy. Identify the ruling: it is permissible (la ba'sa bihi) when beneficial.
The ruling is conditional on benefit (maslahah).
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Two pieces of evidence are cited: the Quranic verse (Surah al-Anfal 8:61) commanding to incline to peace if the enemy does, and the Sunnah of the Prophet's treaty at Hudaybiyyah.
The verse is the primary textual proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the evidence proves the ruling)
The Quranic imperative to incline to peace implies permissibility (ibahah) in Hanafi usul. The Prophet's action at Hudaybiyyah confirms the applicability. The comment adds that truce is a form of jihad in meaning because it repels evil.
The ruling is derived from the apparent meaning (zahir) of the command.
4
Identify the condition and scope
The key condition is that the truce must bring benefit (maslahah) for the Muslims. The duration is not fixed to ten years; it can be extended based on need. If no benefit, it is not allowed.
This distinguishes from a blanket permissibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on jizya for reclusive monks and explain the qiyas that supports the view imposing jizya.

Core Idea
A monk's ability to work determines his jizya obligation—even if he isolates himself from society.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding jizya on reclusive monks (ruhban) who completely withdraw from society. The initial ruling exempts them, but a narration from Imam Muhammad through Abu Hanifa imposes it if they have earning capacity, supported by a strong analogy to kharaj land. Understanding this qiyas and its conditions is essential for determining the operative ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First, recognize that al-Quduri's statement (the base text) says jizya is NOT imposed on monks who do not mix with people. Then note that Imam Muhammad narrates from Abu Hanifa that jizya IS imposed if they are able to work, and Abu Yusuf agrees.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in al-Hidayah.
2
Examine the proof for imposing jizya
The proof is a qiyas to kharaj land: just as kharaj is due on productive land even if left idle, jizya is due on a monk who has earning capacity even if he does not work. The effective cause (illa) is the existence of productive capacity.
This is a clear jaliy qiyas.
3
Examine the proof for not imposing jizya
The opposing proof: jizya is a substitute for killing (qatl). Monks who do not interact are not subject to killing, so jizya is dropped. The Hanafi response: this only applies if they cannot work; if they can, the capacity creates obligation.
The initial ruling focuses on the absence of qatl obligation.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf

This is the dispute. Translation: He said, if an apostate reaches dar al-harb and the ruler gives a verdict of his having reached, then his mudabbir slave and umm walad all become free. And whatever time-bound debts (fixed term) he had, they become immediately due. And whatever he earned during Islam is transferred to his Muslim heirs. (And according to the two companions, earnings during apostasy also go to the heirs.) And Imam al-Shafi'i said that his property remains in his ownership but in a suspended state, just as before reaching dar al-harb, because reaching dar al-harb is like being absent on a journey; it is as if he became absent while traveling in dar al-islam. Our proof is that he joined the harbis and became apostate. In matters of Islamic rulings, harbis are considered like the dead, because they have no right to enforce anything upon them. Nothing can be imposed on them, just as this right ends with a dead person. However, immediately upon his reaching dar al-harb and mixing with its people, the verdict is not given until the qadi passes judgment and announces it, because there remains a possibility that he might return. Therefore, the qadi's verdict is necessary. When he issued the verdict of his death (i.e., transfer to dar al-harb), then all the rulings related to his death are established, i.e., the freeing of his mudabbir and umm walad, as previously explained, just as happens in actual death. Then, according to Imam Muhammad, consideration for inheritance is at the time he reached dar al-harb, because reaching itself is the cause of inheritance, and the qadi's verdict fully establishes it, so that the possibility of reaching or not is completely eliminated and certainty is achieved. (Conclusion: whoever becomes an heir at the time of reaching dar al-harb will also inherit.) And Qadi Abu Yusuf said that consideration for inheritance is at the time of the qadi's verdict, because this apostate's reaching among the harbis becomes, by the qadi's verdict, like his death. (Therefore, a child born after his reaching among the harbis but before the qadi's verdict will also inherit.) And if a woman (apostate) flees and reaches dar al-harb, the same dispute applies: according to Imam Muhammad, whoever exists until the qadi's verdict will be considered an heir.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the legal status of an apostate who reaches dar al-harb, followed by an intra-Hanafi dispute on the timing of inheritance. The Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions) is that once the qadi announces the apostate's arrival in dar al-harb, he is treated like a dead person: his mudabbir and umm walad become free, time-bound debts become immediately due, and his earnings during Islam go to his Muslim heirs. According to the Sahibayn, earnings during apostasy also go to heirs. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that the property remains in his ownership in a suspended state, as reaching dar al-harb is like being absent on a journey. The Hanafi response is that harbis are legally dead because they cannot be compelled, so joining them is like death, unlike a journey within dar al-islam. The intra-Hanafi dispute is between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf: Imam Muhammad considers the time of reaching dar al-harb as the moment for determining heirs, while Abu Yusuf considers the time of the qadi's verdict, because the verdict makes the reaching equivalent to death. Both agree that a qadi's verdict is necessary because of the possibility of return.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A person who abandons Islam for disbelief.
دار الحرب (dar al-harb): Territory under non-Muslim rule where Islamic law is not enforced.
مدبر (mudabbir): A slave who has been promised emancipation upon the master's death.

Repayment of an Apostate's Debts from His Earnings
Intra-Hanafi dispute on debt repayment from apostate's earnings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three transmissions from Abu Hanifa and the view of Sahibayn regarding the repayment of an apostate's debts from his earnings, and identify the reasoning (ta'lil) behind each position.

Core Idea
An apostate's debts are paid from earnings corresponding to the state when incurred, but Hanafi masters differ on prioritization based on ownership and inheritance rights.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on how to settle the debts of an apostate. Imam Abu Hanifa is reported to have three different positions, and his two foremost students—Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani—hold a fourth view. Understanding the reasoning (ta'lil) behind each view reveals deeper principles about ownership (milk), legal capacity (ahliyyah), and the rights of creditors versus heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common issue
All views address the same question: from which earnings (Islam or apostasy) should the apostate's debts be paid?
2
List the four views
Three transmissions from Abu Hanifa (via Zufar, Hasan, Abu Yusuf) plus the view of Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
The first transmission: each debt from its corresponding earning. Second: prioritize Islamic earnings. Third: prioritize apostasy earnings. Sahibayn: both earnings used equally.
3
Extract the reasoning (ta'lil)
For each view, identify the cause given in the text: (1) loss proportional to gain; (2) Islamic earnings owned, apostasy earnings not owned due to loss of capacity; (3) apostasy earnings are his pure right, Islamic earnings are heirs' right; (4) both earnings are owned and subject to inheritance.
4
Compare underlying principles
Contrast the principles of ownership (milk), capacity for ownership (ahliyyah al-milk), and the rights of heirs. Note how each view prioritizes different aspects.
E.g., the second transmission emphasizes clearing the testator's debt before inheritance; the third emphasizes the apostate's own right to his apostasy earnings.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: which creditors get paid first and from which pot. This affects the distribution among creditors and heirs.
E.g., under the second transmission, Islamic earnings are depleted first, potentially leaving Islamic creditors unpaid if those earnings are insufficient.

Foundling's Religion: Place vs. Finder
Intra-Hanafi dispute on foundling's religion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi mufta bihi ruling for a foundling's religion when location and finder differ, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute using the principles of precedence (sabq) and possession (yad).

Core Idea
A foundling's religion follows the place found, not the finder — Zahir al-Riwayah prevails.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the religion of an abandoned child (laqit) of unknown lineage. The default ruling in the Zahir al-Riwayah texts is that the child's religion follows the place where it was found. However, a narration from Imam Muhammad via Ibn Sama'a gives weight to the finder's religion based on the strength of possession. We will examine the proofs and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting narrations
The passage mentions two narrations within the Hanafi school: one from Kitab al-Laqit (Zahir al-Riwayah) considers the place; another from Kitab al-Da'wa (via Ibn Sama'a) considers the finder.
Both are attributed to Imam Muhammad but the first is the relied-upon position.
2
Understand the basis for each view
The location-based view relies on the precedence (sabq) of the place. The finder-based view relies on the strength of possession (qiwat al-yad).
The passage uses a qiyas to support the location-based view: following parents is stronger than following place.
3
Analyze the qiyas used
The commentator argues that if a child is captured with a non-Muslim parent, the child is considered non-Muslim, showing that parental following overrides place. By analogy, the place where a foundling is found has a stronger claim than the finder's possession.
This is a qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) because the effective cause is not explicit.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and thamarah al-khilaf
The mufta bihi position is the location-based ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah. The practical consequence: if a Muslim finds a child in a dhimmi village, the child is considered dhimmi, not Muslim.
A third narration in some copies simply considers Islam beneficial for the child, but it is not adopted.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the religion of a foundling. The default ruling as per Zahir al-Riwayah (Kitab al-Laqit) is that the foundling's religion follows the place where it is found: if in a Muslim area, it is Muslim; if in a dhimmi area, it is dhimmi. Another narration from Imam Muhammad via Ibn Sama'a gives weight to the finder's religion, based on the strength of possession (qiwat al-yad). The commentator then provides a qiyas: a child's following of parents (taba'iyya) is stronger than following a place, as evidenced by the rule that if a child is captured with a non-Muslim parent, the child is considered non-Muslim even if in a Muslim land. A third narration considers Islam beneficial for the child. The mufta bihi position is the first (location-based).

سَبْق (sabq): Precedence in time or legal priority.
قُوَّة الْيَد (qiwat al-yad): Strength of possession, a legal concept giving weight to the possessor's claim.
