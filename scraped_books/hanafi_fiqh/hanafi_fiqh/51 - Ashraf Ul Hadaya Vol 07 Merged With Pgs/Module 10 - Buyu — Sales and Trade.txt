Ruling on Imam's Discretion in Conquered Lands
Ruling with proof from Umar's practice regarding conquered lands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the imam's two options for conquered lands and identify the proof from Umar's practice with companion consensus.

Core Idea
An imam may choose to distribute conquered lands or levy jizyah and kharaj, following Umar's precedent.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a key question after military conquest: what to do with the land and its inhabitants? The text presents two valid options for the imam, each with prophetic precedent. The decisive proof is the practice of Caliph Umar in the Sawad of Iraq, carried out with the agreement of the Companions. Understanding this ruling clarifies that the imam's decision is based on public interest, not a fixed obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the imam's decision regarding conquered lands. The ruling is that he may choose between two options: distributing the land among the mujahideen or leaving the inhabitants with jizyah and kharaj. This is a discretionary (mubah) ruling, not an obligation.
The text states 'fayatahayyar' (so he may choose), indicating choice.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is the practice of Caliph Umar (ra) in the Sawad of Iraq, done with the agreement (muwafaqah) of the Companions. This is a form of ijma (consensus) – a strong source of law.
The text says 'ka dhalika fa'ala Umar bi-Sawad al-Iraq bi-muwafaqah min al-sahabah'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'ka dhalika fa'ala Umar' presents Umar's action as a binding precedent (sunnah). The addition 'bi-muwafaqah min al-sahabah' elevates this to a consensus, as the Companions did not oppose it after initial discussion. This makes the practice a definitive proof for the permissibility of the choice.
The commentary notes that those who initially opposed (like Bilal and Salman) later agreed, strengthening the consensus.
4
Note any ikhtilaf
There is no significant school disagreement on this ruling. The Hanafi reliance on Umar's practice is accepted across schools, though some may emphasise different aspects.
The text states 'wa lam yuhmad man khalafahu' – meaning the opposition was not approved.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Since the passage is from al-Hidayah (Zahir al-Riwayah), this is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position. The imam's discretion is established, and no stronger contrary proof overrides it.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi with consensus; no tarjih necessary.

Preferable Option for Conquered Lands and Response to Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on granting favor to conquered peoples on immovable property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on granting favor (mann) to conquered peoples on immovable property, explain the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on maslaha and precedent.

Core Idea
The imam may choose to leave conquered lands under jizya/kharaj for long-term community benefit, based on Companions' practice.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute about the disposition of conquered immovable property. The Hanafi school grants the imam discretion to either distribute the land as ghanimah or leave it in the hands of the conquered under kharaj, based on public interest and the precedent of Umar. Imam al-Shafi'i objects that this nullifies the rights of the mujahideen without equivalent compensation. We will analyze the arguments and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and the two options
Identify the two options available to the imam: distribution to mujahideen or leaving the conquered people on their lands with jizya/kharaj. Note which is preferred when Muslims are in need.
The preference shifts based on need (hajat al-ʿālimīn).
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that granting favor (mann) nullifies the right of the Muslims to the property without equivalent compensation, and kharaj is not equivalent.
The Shafi'i position is based on protecting the rights of the mujahideen.
3
Examine the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response has two parts: a transmitted proof (the practice of the Companions, which is a binding precedent) and a rational argument (maslaha: tenants are skilled farmers, kharaj is continuous wealth, and future generations benefit).
The text says 'al-ḥujjah ʿalayhi mā rawaynāh' and then mentions beneficial outcomes.
4
Determine the practical consequence
Under Shafi'i law, the imam must distribute conquered land as ghanimah; under Hanafi law, the imam has discretion to choose based on public interest.
This thamarah al-khilaf affects post-conquest land policy.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi because it is based on the consensus of the Companions and the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs.
The text implies that the Hanafi view is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Commentary
Passage type: Inter-madhhab dispute. The Hidayah text begins by stating that the first option (distribution) is preferable (awla) when the Muslims are in need, and the second option (leaving with jizyah/kharaj) is preferable when there is no immediate need, to serve as a resource for the future. This ruling applies specifically to immovable property (ʿiqār). As for movable property, granting favor (mann) by returning it to the conquered is not permissible because no such precedent exists in the Shari'ah. The text then presents the disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i, who argues that granting favor on immovable property is not permissible because it nullifies the right of the Muslims (ḥaqq al-ʿālimīn) to the property without equivalent compensation. Since the kharaj is not equivalent to the value of the lives (which could have been taken by killing), the nullification is unjust. The Hanafi response is twofold: (1) The transmitted proof (ma rawaynahu) — the practice of the Companions, which is a binding precedent. (2) Rational consideration (nazar) — leaving the conquered as tenant farmers (akarah) benefits the Muslims as they are skilled in agriculture, and the kharaj, though small in the short term, is continuous and accumulates, benefiting future generations. Furthermore, if the imam grants favor regarding the people and the lands, he should give them some movable property as a startup to avoid dislike.

Key Terms
مَنّ (mann): Granting favor or remission, here referring to returning property to conquered people without compensation.
عِقَار (ʿiqār): Immovable property such as land and buildings.
أَكَرَة (akarah): Tenant farmers or agricultural laborers, here referring to conquered people left on land to cultivate for Muslims.

Division of Spoils in Dar al-Harb: The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute
Dispute with Shafi'i on timing of spoils division and derivative rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on dividing spoils in dar al-harb, contrast it with the Shafi'i view, and apply the principle of ihraz to subsidiary rulings.

Core Idea
Ownership of spoils is not complete until ihraz — division before that is invalid (or makruh) according to Hanafis.

When Muslim fighters capture spoils in enemy territory, when do they truly own it? The Hanafi school teaches that ownership is suspended until the spoils are safely brought to dar al-Islam. This lesson examines the dispute with Imam al-Shafi‘i, who sees immediate ownership upon capture. The difference ripples through many practical rulings, from selling spoils to inheritance and liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the root difference
Pinpoint the key point of contention: the timing of ownership of spoils. Hanafis require ihraz (bringing to dar al-Islam); Shafi'is consider ownership upon capture (istila).
2
Examine both proofs
Analyze the Hanafi proof (hadith prohibiting sale, lack of full control) and the Shafi'i proof (analogy to hunting). Understand how each school justifies its position.
Hanafi hadith is gharib; the logical argument about risk of recapture is stronger.
3
Trace the furu'
See how the root difference affects specific cases: selling spoils, intercourse with slave-girls, inheritance, liability for destruction, and sharing with reinforcements.
Each case illustrates the practical consequences of the ownership principle.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi variation
Recognize that within the Hanafi school, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold division in dar al-harb is invalid, while Imam Muhammad considers it makruh tanzihi.
This distinction affects the degree of prohibition and legal effect.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Identify the relied-upon position for fatwa: the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf that division is not permissible.
Later scholars follow the majority; the cautionary position prevails.

The original owner may reclaim his property from the trader but must pay the price the trader paid to prevent harm.

The reasoning (ta'lil) balances the owner's right with the trader's interest, ensuring neither is unjustly harmed. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its rationale, and two additional variants.

Recovery of Property Before and After Division of Booty
Ruling on original owners taking property without or with compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim recovering his property from booty before and after division, and explain the proof and legal reasoning (istihsan).

Core Idea
An owner can reclaim his property from booty free before division, but must pay its value after division.

This passage from the Hidayah addresses a scenario from the law of warfare (siyar): property taken by disbelievers from Muslims is later recovered as booty. The original owner's right to reclaim it depends on whether the booty has been divided. The ruling combines a prophetic hadith (though transmitted with weak chains) with legal reasoning (istihsan) that balances fairness for both the original owner and the new owner (the fighter who received it in his share). Understanding this distinction clarifies how Hanafi fiqh protects property rights while preventing unjust harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between finding property before qismah (division among fighters) and after qismah. This timing is the key variable.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
Before division: original owner takes it free (without payment). After division: he may take it only by paying its current value (qimah).
The option to take it after division is conditional on the owner's wish; he is not forced to pay.
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
The hadith: 'If you find it before division, it is for you without anything; if after division, it is for you with its value.' Recognize that this is an ahad hadith with zanni thubut, but its dalalah is clear.
The hadith is reported by Bayhaqi, Darqutni, Tabarani, Tahawi (weak chains) and Abu Dawud (mursal with strong chain).
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative structure (فهو لك) indicates the ruling. The condition (before/after) directly determines the compensation. Hanafi usul treats this as a clear command (amr) that establishes the rule.
Even though the hadith is weak in transmission, it is supported by istihsan.
5
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Before division, all fighters share common ownership, so taking property from them causes only slight harm to each. After division, the property has become the specific ownership of one fighter; taking it free would cause him significant harm. To balance consideration for both sides, the original owner must pay the value.
This is an example of istihsan (juristic preference) overriding strict qiyas.

Merchant Buying Captured Property and Original Owner's Option
Ruling with proof on a merchant's purchase of captured property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for the original owner's option when a merchant buys captured property from Dar al-Harb, and apply it to different types of transactions (cash, barter, gift, spoils).

Core Idea
A merchant who pays for captured property must be reimbursed; the original owner cannot take it free.

This lesson extends the previous discussion on captured property. Here, a merchant enters Dar al-Harb, purchases the goods from the enemy, and brings them to Dar al-Islam. The original owner now has an option: either reclaim the property by paying the merchant exactly what he paid, or leave it. The key principle is that the merchant has expended value, so taking it without compensation would be unjust. The passage then explores variations: barter, gift, and spoils that are fungible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the transaction
A Muslim merchant buys captured property from non-Muslims and brings it to Muslim territory. The original owner is a Muslim who lost the property.
2
State the default ruling and its rationale
The original owner has a choice (khiyar): take it by paying the merchant's purchase price, or leave it. The rationale: the merchant paid consideration, so taking it free would harm him.
Illah: the merchant's payment establishes a right.
3
Apply the rule to different payment modes
If bought with goods (barter), the owner pays the value of those goods. If gifted, the owner pays the value. If spoils (ghanimah) and it is a fungible (mithli), before division the owner can take it free; after division, he cannot because taking by like is not beneficial.
Fungibles: items like gold, silver, wheat where like is available.
4
Consider the case where the merchant bought with its like
If the merchant bought a fungible with its exact like in quantity and description, the owner cannot take it, because the merchant already paid the like.
This prevents double recovery.
5
Recognize the underlying principle
The merchant's ownership is established by payment; it cannot be voided without compensation, except in the spoils-before-division case where original ownership is prioritized.
The school balances original ownership against the merchant's expenditure.

The Hanafi school rules that he may take the slave by paying the purchase price, but cannot take the diya. This ruling is justified by a critical qiyas comparing to shuf'ah (pre-emption), where the opposite rule applies. Let us unpack the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and ruling
Read the source passage carefully. Identify the key facts: a captured slave is bought by a Muslim, brought to Dar al-Islam, then injured; the buyer collects diya. The ruling: the original owner can take the slave for the price paid, but not the diya.
The text explicitly says 'la ya'khudh al-arsh' (he does not take the arsh).
2
Identify the basis of the ruling
The ruling is based on the validity of the buyer's ownership: 'li-anna al-milk fihi sahih' (because ownership in him is valid). The diya is a compensation for an injury that occurred while the slave was validly owned by the buyer, so the diya belongs to the buyer, not the original owner.
3
Contrast with shuf'ah
The author contrasts this case with shuf'ah (pre-emption). In shuf'ah, when the property is transferred to the pre-emptor (shafi'), the buyer's ownership becomes fasid (void). Therefore, the pre-emptor can take the property with all descriptions, including compensation for defects. Here, ownership is sahih, so the diya does not transfer.
Key difference: fasid vs sahih ownership.
4
Understand the qiyas illa
The effective cause (illa) that distinguishes the two cases is the validity of ownership. In shuf'ah, ownership is defective; here it is valid. This illa is jaliyy (clear) and is extracted by tahqiq al-manat.
Thus, the ruling changes based on this single factor.
5
Apply the principle
From this ruling, we derive the principle: when ownership is valid, the original owner reclaiming the item cannot also claim compensation for damage that occurred during the buyer's valid ownership. The buyer retains the compensation.
This principle applies to similar cases of valid acquisition.

Valid ownership at injury vests compensation in the owner; defects are not priced.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof by analogy (qiyas) from the previous principle and a distinction from shuf'a. Imam Muhammad states that if a captive slave is bought from harbis and brought to Dar al-Islam, and then someone injures his eye, the buyer collects the diyat (arsh). When the original owner reclaims the slave, he pays only the purchase price to the buyer, and cannot claim the arsh that the buyer received. The reason is that at the time of injury, the buyer had valid ownership of the slave, so the compensation belongs to him. The original owner reclaims the slave itself, not the compensation. Furthermore, the price is not reduced due to the defect (loss of eye) because defects (awsaf) are not offset against the price in a valid sale. The text contrasts this with shuf'a (pre-emption): in shuf'a, when the shafi' takes the property, the buyer's transaction is considered voidable (fasad), so the buyer must compensate for defects. But here ownership is valid, so the rule differs.

Ruling on successive capture and purchase of a slave

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Capture breaks ownership chain: original owner can only reclaim from first buyer, not the second.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from the chapter of buyu: a slave captured twice, purchased by two different buyers. The ruling clarifies the rights of the original owner and each buyer. At first glance, one might think the original owner can reclaim directly from the most recent buyer, but the text shows otherwise due to the timing of capture and ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and sequence of events
There are three relevant parties: the original owner (al-mawla al-awwal), the first buyer, and the second buyer. The slave is captured first, bought by the first buyer, captured again, then bought by the second buyer.
The key point is which capture occurred under whose ownership.
2
State the ruling for original owner vs second buyer
The original owner has no right to take the slave from the second buyer by paying the second purchase price (1000 dirhams). Reason: the second capture did not occur while the slave was in the original owner's ownership; it was in the first buyer's ownership.
This is the core prohibition in the first part of the text.
3
State the ruling for first buyer vs second buyer
The first buyer has the right to take the slave from the second buyer by paying the second purchase price (1000 dirhams). Reason: the second capture occurred while the slave was in the first buyer's ownership, so the first buyer's right to reclaim is established.
The first buyer can reclaim because the capture invaded his ownership.
4
State the ruling for original owner vs first buyer
The original owner may take the slave from the first buyer by paying both purchase prices (2000 dirhams) if he wishes. Reason: the slave became due to the original owner with two prices (the two amounts paid by the first buyer). The original owner's right to reclaim is only from the first buyer, not from the second.
The original owner's right is conditional on paying the combined price.

Milk Mahzur: Valid Ownership but Prohibited Use
Ruling on property obtained by a Muslim trader's treachery in Dar al-Harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on property acquired by a Muslim trader through treachery in Dar al-Harb and explain the distinction between valid ownership (milk) and prohibition of use (hazar).

Core Idea
Ownership can be valid but the property prohibited – the act taints the acquisition, not the property itself.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: a Muslim trader, who has entered Dar al-Harb with aman (safe conduct), commits treachery (ghadr) by taking property from the harbis and bringing it to Dar al-Islam. The ruling illustrates a key Hanafi principle: the cause (sabab) of ownership (taking possession of mubah property) remains valid, but the sinful means render the property mahzur (prohibited) for the taker to use. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act: Muslim trader commits treachery in Dar al-Harb, seizes property, and brings it to Dar al-Islam. The ruling: he becomes the owner (milk) but the ownership is mahzur (prohibited).
2
Distinguish cause and prohibition
The cause of ownership (sabab) is valid: the property was mubah (permissible) and he took possession. The prohibition (hazar) arises from an external factor (the treachery), not from the property itself.
This is the principle 'al-hazar li-ghayrihi la yamna' al-sabab' – an external prohibition does not invalidate the cause.
3
Define milkan mahzuran
Milkan mahzuran means valid ownership with prohibited benefit. The taker cannot use the property; he must dispose of it in charity.
This contrasts with batil ownership (void) or fasid (defective).
4
Apply the required disposition
The trader is commanded to give the property in sadaqah (charity). He cannot keep it, return it (since the harbis are in Dar al-Harb), or destroy it.
Sadaqah is the only permissible option because the ownership is valid but the property is tainted.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Other schools may require returning the property to the original owners if possible. Hanafi holds that once property is brought to Dar al-Islam and ownership is established, charity is the correct disposition.
This ikhtilaf stems from different views on the effect of the prohibition on the ownership.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jizyah varies by wealth because the Companions' consensus overrides a specific peace treaty hadith.

The amount of jizyah (poll tax) on dhimmis is a classic inter-madhhab dispute. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position that the amount varies by the dhimmi's wealth, based on the practice of Caliph Umar and the consensus of the Companions. We will also examine the Shafi'i argument for a fixed amount and the Hanafi response that interprets the Shafi'i proof as a special case of peace treaty.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
First, clearly articulate what the Hanafi school holds: jizyah is wajib on adult male dhimmis, with amounts differentiated by wealth—48 dirhams for rich, 24 for middle, 12 for poor annually.
The amount is per year, not a one-time payment.
2
Identify the opposing school and view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that jizyah is a fixed one dinar (or its equivalent) for every adult dhimmi, male or female, regardless of wealth.
The Shafi'i view includes women, which is a key point of contention.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i proof: a hadith of the Prophet instructing Mu'adh ibn Jabal to take one dinar from every adult male and female. The Shafi'i reasoning is that jizyah compensates for sparing life, which is equal for all.
The hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, and others, with variations in wording.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is twofold: (1) the hadith is interpreted as a specific peace treaty (sulh) with Banu Taghlib, not a general rule—evidenced by the inclusion of women; (2) the practice of Umar, endorsed by the Companions without objection, constitutes ijma' al-sahabah, which is a definitive proof (qat'i).
The Hanafis also make a qiyas on kharaj: land tax varies by productivity, so jizyah varies by wealth.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis collect jizyah according to wealth and exempt women and children; Shafi'is collect a fixed amount from all adults including women. This affects implementation in Islamic states.
The difference also impacts the overall tax burden on the dhimmi community.

Jizyah is imposed on all non-Muslims except Arab polytheists; Hanafis extend it to non-Arab idol worshippers via qiyas on enslavement.

قَالَ وَيَدْفَعُهُ مُضَارِبَةً لِإِنهَا دُونَ الشَّرْكَةِ فَيَتَضَمَّنْهَا وَ عَنْ أَبِي حَنِيفَة أَنَّهُ لَيْسَ لَهُ ذَالِكَ لأنه نوع شِرُكَةٍ. وَالأصَحُ هُوَ الأَوَّلُ وَرِوَايَةُ الأصل لأنَّ الشَّرْكَةَ غَيْرَ مَقْصُودٍ وَإِنَّمَا الْمَقْصُودُ تَحْصِيلُ الرِّبْح كَمَا إِذَا اسْتَأجَر بِأَجْرَبَلْ أولى لِأَنَّهُ تَحْصِيلُ بِدُونَ ضَمَانٍ فِي ذِمَّتِهِ بِخِلَافِ الشَّرْكَةِ حَيْثُ لَا يَمْلِكُهَا لِأَنَّ الشَّيْئَ لَا يَسْتَطِيعُ مِثْلَهُ
He said: And he gives it as mudarabah because it is lesser than partnership and so it is included in it. And from Abu Hanifa that he does not have that, because it is a type of partnership. And the more correct is the first, and it is the narration of the original [books], because partnership is not intended; rather what is intended is obtaining profit, just as when one hires with a wage; rather it is more fitting because it obtains profit without liability upon him, unlike partnership where he does not own it, because a thing cannot take its like.

Commentary
This passage addresses whether a partner in a shirkah has the authority to give the partnership capital to a third party on the basis of mudarabah (where the third party works with the capital for a share of profit). The text states the ruling from the matn: yes, the partner can do so, because mudarabah is a lesser form of partnership (since it involves only one party investing and the other working), and the greater (shirkah) includes the lesser. However, there is a divergent narration from Abu Hanifa that the partner may not do so, because mudarabah is itself a type of partnership, and a partner cannot make someone else a partner without the consent of the other partner. The preferred view (al-asahh) is the first view, which is the one recorded in the original sources (riwayat al-asl). The reasoning is that the goal of partnership is profit, not the partnership itself. Giving capital on mudarabah is even more appropriate than hiring a worker for wages, because in mudarabah there is no liability on the partner's part, whereas hiring for wages entails liability. This ruling shows that partners have authority to engage in arrangements that are beneficial for the business.

Key Terms
مُضَارِبَة (muḍārabah): A contract where one party provides capital and the other provides labor; profit shared as agreed; losses borne by capital owner.
شِرْكَة (shirkah): Partnership; a contract where two or more persons contribute capital and labor, and share profits and losses.
رِوَايَة الْأَصْل (riwayat al-asl): The original narration, i.e., the version recorded in the authoritative early Hanafi texts (Zahir al-Riwayah).

Key Takeaway
