Liability for Killing a Muslim Convert in Dar al-Harb
Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on liability for killing a convert harbi in Dar al-Harb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on liability for killing a Muslim convert in Dar al-Harb and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Protection of a Muslim's blood is not absolute—it requires enforceability.

This lesson examines a dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding a specific scenario: a Muslim kills another Muslim who had converted to Islam in enemy territory (Dar al-Harb) and whose Muslim heirs remain there. Does the killer owe qisas or diyah? The Hanafi school says no, only kaffarah for accidental killing; the Shafi'i school insists that the victim's Islam alone guarantees full protection. This passage from Al-Hidayah presents the Hanafi reasoning and the refutation of the opposing view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The victim was a harbi (enemy combatant) who converted to Islam in Dar al-Harb. The killer is a Muslim. The victim has Muslim heirs who also remain in Dar al-Harb. The killing could be intentional or accidental.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to the Hanafi school, the killer owes nothing except expiation (kaffarah) in the case of accidental killing. No qisas for intentional killing, no diyah for accidental killing.
3
State the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that qisas (for intentional) or diyah (for accidental) are obligatory because the victim's blood is inviolable (ma'sum) due to his Islam.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds that inviolability (ismah) has a foundation (asl) which is the barrier (al-ithmah) that prevents sins, and its purpose is deterrence (zajr). Since the heirs are in Dar al-Harb, the deterrent effect cannot be achieved, so the full penalty is waived. Only kaffarah remains because the killing is still a sin.
The text says: 'عصمة أصلها المؤثمة لتحصيل أصل الزجر' meaning the basis of ismah is the barrier to sin for achieving deterrence.
5
Apply the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: Under Hanafi law, a Muslim who kills such a convert would not face qisas or financial compensation, only a religious expiation (siyam). Under Shafi'i law, he would be liable for qisas or diyah.

Al-Shafi'i said: Diyah is obligatory in mistake and qisas in intentional killing, because he has shed protected blood due to the existence of the protector, which is Islam, because it is deserving of honor. That is because the basis of inviolability is the barrier, for the achievement of the basis of deterrence.

Commentary
This passage presents a dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the liability of a Muslim who kills another Muslim who was originally a harbi and converted in Dar al-Harb. The Hanafi position, as stated in the text, is that no financial compensation (diyah) or retribution (qisas) is due, only expiation (kaffarah) in the case of accidental killing. Their reasoning (implied) is that although the victim is Muslim, the heirs are in Dar al-Harb and not under Muslim jurisdiction, so the protection (ismah) is not fully enforceable. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that since the victim was Muslim, his blood is protected, and therefore qisas or diyah must be paid. The Hanafi response (here brief) is that the basis of ismah is the barrier (al-ithmah) which requires that punishment be enforceable; since the heirs are not present in Dar al-Islam, the deterrent purpose cannot be fulfilled.

Key Terms
عصمة (ismah): Inviolability of life and property, especially of a Muslim.
زجر (zajr): Deterrence; the purpose of punishments like qisas is to deter others from killing.

No Qisas for Killing Among Rebels After Imam Overcomes
Ruling on intra-rebel killing after imam's victory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate why qisas is not due when killing occurs among rebels before the Imam's victory, applying the Hanafi condition of effective wilayah.

Core Idea
Without the Imam's effective authority, a killing among rebels triggers no worldly liability.

This passage addresses a specific scenario from the law of rebellion (ahkam al-bughat). A killing occurs between two rebels before the Imam defeats their army. Does the killer face qisas or diyah once the Imam gains control? The Hanafi ruling is no, and the reasoning turns on a crucial condition: the existence of the Imam's effective jurisdiction at the time of the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Recognize that the passage describes a killing where both the killer and victim are rebels, and the killing occurs before the Imam overcomes them.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling is clear: 'there is nothing upon them'—no qisas and no diyah.
3
Extract the cause (illa)
The text gives the reasoning: 'because there is no authority of the just Imam at the time of killing'. The absence of effective wilayah prevents the act from creating a legal obligation.
4
Analyze the analogical proof (qiyas)
The passage compares this to a killing in dar al-harb, where Islamic authority is also absent. Identify the shared illa: lack of Imam's effective jurisdiction.
This is qiyas khafi (subtle analogy).
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school would hold qisas due because the killer is Muslim and the victim's family has a right; the Hanafi school responds that enforcement authority is a necessary condition.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in liability.

Commentary
This is a ruling about liability for killing among rebels when the Imam later prevails. The ruling: No qisas or diyah is due on the killer. The reason: At the time of the killing, the Imam's wilayah (authority/jurisdiction) was not effective over these individuals because they were in rebellion, operating outside legitimate authority. Since qisas and diyah are rights that depend on the existence of a valid Islamic judicial authority to enforce them, and that authority was absent at the time of the act, the killing does not create a legal obligation. The ruling is analogized (qiyas) to killings that occur in dar al-harb (non-Muslim territory) where, even among Muslims, there is no qisas because the Islamic legal system is not in force there. Note that this does not mean the killing is lawful; it means no worldly punishment is applicable. The killer's spiritual accountability remains with Allah.

Key Terms
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliatory punishment equivalent to the crime, e.g., death for murder.
دیت (diyah): Blood money paid to the victim's family as compensation in lieu of qisas.
ولایت (wilāyah): Legal authority and jurisdiction, here of the Imam, over the territory and its inhabitants.

Ruling on Ju'l for a Fugitive Slave Who Committed Homicide
Determining who pays the reward when the fugitive slave is liable for accidental killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine which party is obligated to pay the reward (ju'l) for returning a fugitive slave based on the master’s choice of ransom or surrender.

Core Idea
The ju'l is paid by the one who ultimately benefits from the slave’s return.

When a fugitive slave has committed accidental homicide, the master faces a choice: ransom the slave to retain ownership, or surrender the slave to the victim's heirs. This choice determines who bears the cost of recovering the slave. The principle is simple: the benefit of the slave's return dictates who must pay the reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The slave is a fugitive (abiq) and has committed accidental homicide (jani bi khata'). This creates liability on the master or the slave's capital.
Intentional homicide would follow different rules.
2
Recognize the master's options
The master may either pay ransom (fida) to keep the slave, or surrender (daf') the slave to the heirs as compensation.
The choice is the master's alone.
3
Apply the principle of benefit
Determine who gains the benefit of the slave's return. If the master ransoms, the slave's service and ownership return to him. If he surrenders, the heirs become owners and benefit.
Benefit is the operative cause (illa) for the obligation.
4
Derive the ruling
Whoever benefits must pay the ju'l for returning the slave. If master ransoms → master pays; if master surrenders → heirs pay.
The ruling is wajib (obligatory) on the benefiting party.

Source Text

وَإِنْ كَانَ جَانِيًا فَعَلَى الْمَوْلَى إِنِ اخْتَارَ الْفِدَاءَ لِعَوْدِ الْمَنْفَعَةِ إِلَيْهِ وَعَلَى الْأَوْلِيَاءِ إِنِ اخْتَارَ الدَّفْعَ لِعَوْدِ الْمَنْفَعَةِ إِلَيْهِمْ
ترجمہ: اور اگر غلام سے خطا کوئی قتل ہو گیا ہو تو دیکھا جائے گا کہ اگر اس کے مولیٰ نے اس کے قتل کا فدیہ دینا قبول کر لیا ہو تو اس غلام کو واپس لانے کا جعل اسی مولی پر لازم ہوگا۔ کیونکہ اس غلام کی واپسی سے غلام کا نفع اسی مولی کا ہوگا۔ اور اگر مولی نے قتل کے فدیہ میں یہ غلام ہی مقتول کے ورثہ کو دے دینے کا فیصلہ کر لیا ہو تو اب اس کی واپسی کا جعل بھی ان ورثہ پر ہی لازم ہوگا۔ کیونکہ موجودہ صورت میں اس غلام کی واپسی سے ان ورثہ ہی کو فائدہ پہنچے گا۔
And if he is a criminal (one who committed homicide), then it is upon the master if he chooses ransom, because the benefit returns to him; and upon the heirs if they choose surrender, because the benefit returns to them.
Source pages p. 204

Commentary
This ruling covers a fugitive slave who has caused an accidental killing (khata' qatl). The master has two options: (1) Pay the ransom (fidya) to the victim's heirs and keep the slave, or (2) Surrender the slave to the heirs as compensation (daf'). The ju'l for returning the slave is assigned accordingly: If the master chooses ransom and keeps the slave, he pays the ju'l because the benefit of the slave's return (his ownership and service) accrues to him. If the master chooses to surrender the slave to the heirs, then the heirs pay the ju'l because the benefit of the return (ownership of the slave) accrues to them. The underlying principle is that the ju'l is an expense of ownership or benefit: whoever ultimately gains the benefit of the slave's recovery must bear the cost.

Key Terms
جَانٍ (jānin): A slave who has committed a crime, specifically accidental homicide (khata').
فِدَاء (fidā'): Ransom; payment made by the master to the victim's heirs to retain the slave.
دَفْع (daf'): Surrender of the slave to the heirs as compensation for the homicide.
