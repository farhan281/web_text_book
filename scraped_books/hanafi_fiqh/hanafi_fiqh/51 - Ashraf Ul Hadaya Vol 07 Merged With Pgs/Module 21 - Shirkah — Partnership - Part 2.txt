Invalidity of Stipulating Fixed Profit in Partnership
Ruling that stipulating a fixed amount of profit for one partner invalidates the partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a fixed sum of profit is stipulated in a partnership and explain the illah (reason) for invalidity.

Core Idea
Stipulating a fixed profit sum in partnership is invalid because profit is uncertain by nature.

In a partnership, profit is by nature uncertain and shared according to actual earnings. If one partner demands a fixed amount of dirhams regardless of the outcome, that condition contradicts the essence of the contract. This lesson explains why such a stipulation invalidates the entire partnership, and how qiyas (analogy) to muzara'ah confirms the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
The passage states that if a partner stipulates a fixed number of dirhams as his profit share, the partnership is not valid (batil). Note the act: stipulating fixed dirhams. The ruling: invalidity.
The term 'darāhim musammāt' means specified dirhams.
2
Understand the reason (illah)
The condition risks that the actual profit may be less than the fixed amount, making the condition impossible to fulfill. This would necessarily terminate the partnership (shart yarhabbu inqitaʿ al-sharika).
Profit in partnership is inherently uncertain (gharar). A fixed claim negates that uncertainty.
3
Recognize the qiyas to muzara'ah
The author draws an analogy: just as stipulating a fixed amount of crop in a sharecropping contract (muzara'ah) is invalid, so is stipulating fixed dirhams in partnership. The underlying cause (illa) is the same: the contract requires sharing actual produce/profit.
Muzara'ah is an agricultural partnership; the same principle applies.
4
Apply to proper contract formation
To form a valid partnership, the profit share must be expressed as a proportion or percentage of the actual profit, not a fixed sum. The condition must be consistent with the nature of sharing.
The mufta bihi position is that this ruling is settled in the Hanafi school.

Permissibility of Bida'ah in Partnership
Ruling on bida'ah in partnership with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partners giving capital as bida'ah and explain its proof based on custom and qiyas.

Core Idea
Bida'ah is permissible in partnership because custom allows it and it is analogous to hiring without wage.

Partners often need to delegate trading to third parties. This lesson clarifies that each partner in a mu'awada or inan partnership may give the common capital as bida'ah (a consignment where the trader gets no profit share). The Hanafi school justifies this ruling through established trade custom and a powerful analogy to hiring a worker for a wage—if hiring with wage is allowed, then getting the same service without wage is even more appropriate. We will unpack both proofs and see how they reinforce the permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and its Scope
The passage states that each partner may give partnership capital as bida'ah. Note that this applies to both mu'awada (equal) and inan (unequal) partnerships. Determine the hukm level: it is permissible (mubah).
2
Analyze the First Proof: Custom (Urf)
The text says 'because it is customary in the contract of partnership.' Recognize that 'urf (custom) is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school for transactional matters. The established practice of merchants supports this permission.
Custom must be general and not contradict a clear text.
3
Analyze the Second Proof: Qiyas (Analogy to Hiring)
The passage argues: the partner is allowed to hire a worker for a wage to do work. Doing the same work without wage (bida'ah) is even more permissible because the partner gets the benefit without cost. This is a fortiori analogy (qiyas awla). Trace the illa (effective cause): the need for trading assistance.
This is considered qiyas khafi because it uses a hidden analogy based on priority.
4
Distinguish Bida'ah from Mudarabah
Bida'ah is agency without compensation; the trader receives no share of profit. This differs from mudarabah where the worker receives a share. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion with other partnership forms.
The common misunderstanding is equating bida'ah with mudarabah.
5
Conclude: Application and Custom
The ruling is mufta bihi (operative). In practice, partners may use bida'ah to delegate trading to third parties when needed, as long as it follows the local trade custom.
No intra-Hanafi dispute on this point.

Permission for Partners to Give Capital as Mudarabah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partner's authority to give capital as mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on a partner giving capital as mudarabah and identify the mufta bihi position with its supporting reasoning.

Core Idea
A partner may give capital as mudarabah because the objective is profit, not partnership itself.

In this passage, we explore a key intra-Hanafi disagreement about the scope of a partner's authority over shared capital. While Imam Abu Hanifa restricts the partner from creating a mudarabah with a third party, the later preferred view permits it, drawing on the principle that mudarabah is a subordinate contract focused on profit. This dispute is resolved by weighing the objectives of the partnership contract.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the permissive view: each partner may give the partnership capital as mudarabah. Note the key phrase 'yadfa'uhu mudarabah' from the matn.
This is the position preferred by the commentator.
2
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi view
Recognize that a narration from Abu Hanifa holds the opposite: the partner does not have that authority, because mudarabah is a type of partnership.
This shows the ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
3
Examine the proof for the permissive view
The passage argues that mudarabah is 'lesser than partnership' (dun al-shirkah), so it is included implicitly. The objective is profit, not partnership, analogized to hiring a worker.
The analogy to hiring strengthens the reasoning: if hiring is allowed, mudarabah (without wage) is even more suitable.
4
Examine the counter-proof
Abu Hanifa's view sees mudarabah as a type of partnership; one partner cannot create a new partnership without consent, as a thing cannot subjugate its like.
This argument emphasizes the integrity of the original partnership contract.
5
Apply the tarjih mechanism
The commentator declares the first view as the soundest (asahh) and labels it as riwayat al-asl (from the original books). The ruling is mufta bihi.
The tarjih relies on the principle that the objective (profit) overrides the form.

Permission for Partners to Appoint an Agent (Wakil)
Ruling on appointing an agent in partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a partner appointing an agent and distinguish it from a specific agency contract.

Core Idea
A partner can appoint an agent because agency is a necessary part of trade, which is the purpose of partnership.

In a general partnership, the partners jointly conduct trade. Trade naturally involves delegation — hiring salespeople, buying agents, and managers. The Hanafi school recognizes this practical reality and rules that each partner implicitly has the authority to appoint agents for trade-related transactions. This lesson examines the proof for this ruling and its limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Read the matn statement: a partner may appoint an agent who transacts in the partnership capital. The ruling is permissibility (mubah).
The ruling is from Al-Quduri's matn, a foundational Hanafi text.
2
Understand the proof (talil)
The proof is that agency for sale and purchase is among the requirements (tawābi') of trade. Since the partnership contract is for trade, this authority is implied.
Hanafis use the principle 'al-tābi' tabi'' – the accessory follows the main.
3
Contrast with the exception
The text contrasts with an agent appointed specifically to purchase a particular item. Such an agent cannot sub-delegate because the contract is limited to obtaining that specific object.
This highlights that the authority to delegate depends on the scope of the agency.
4
Apply the principle
Consider: Does the action fall within trade transactions? If yes, delegation is allowed. If not (e.g., litigation), explicit consent is required.
The partner's authority is not unlimited; it covers only acts ancillary to trade.

In labor partnerships, extra profit for equal work is valid because it is compensation, not profit.

This lesson examines a scenario from partnership law where partners work equally but agree to split profits unequally. While qiyas would invalidate such a condition, the Hanafi school applies istihsan to uphold it, distinguishing between profit and compensation for work.

Understand the qiyas analogy

Unequal Profit in Partnership: Istihsan Overrides Qiyas
Explaining permissibility by istihsan despite qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits unequal profit distribution in a work-only partnership by applying istihsan, and distinguish between profit (ribh) and compensation for work (badal al-'amal).

Core Idea
Unequal profit is valid when the excess is a wage for labor, not profit on capital.

When two partners contribute equal work but different capital, can they agree to split profits unequally? Qiyas forbids it due to the risk of riba—profit without guarantee. However, the Hanafi school permits it by istihsan. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains: the extra amount is not ribh but badal al-'amal (compensation for work) because labor (capital) and money (profit) are different genera. Thus, the imbalance is a negotiated wage, not a forbidden return.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and the stipulation
State the scenario: partners contribute equal work but agree that one takes 2/3 profit, the other 1/3, despite equal capital. Identify the act as a condition in a shirkah contract.
The capital is equal, but work is equal by condition.
2
Explain the qiyas argument against it
Qiyas would invalidate the contract: guarantee (daman) is proportional to work, so any excess profit is profit on unguaranteed capital, akin to shirkat al-wujuh, where profit on unguaranteed capital is prohibited except in mudarabah.
This is the 'dalil al-qiyas' that the text presents.
3
Show the Hanafi istihsan response using badal al-'amal
The Hanafi position is that the excess is not ribh but 'iwad (compensation) for work. Since capital (labor) and profit (money) are different genera (jins), the extra amount is a permissible wage, valued by mutual estimation. There is no riba because genera differ.
This is the core of istihsan: overriding qiyas for practical equity.
4
Contrast with shirkat al-wujuh
In shirkat al-wujuh, both capital and profit are money (same genus), so any excess would be riba. Therefore, the ruling differs.
This contrast highlights the importance of genus unity in riba determination.
5
Note the exception for mudarabah
Mudarabah is an exception: profit is allowed even without guarantee due to the nature of the contract. This does not apply to shirkah.
A useful boundary case.

Mutual Binding of Work in Partnerships: The Istihsan Exception
Ruling with proof: istihsan for mutual liability in work partnerships.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that work accepted by one partner binds both, and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in shirkat al-sanai'.

Core Idea
In a crafts partnership, each partner's accepted work binds both — istihsan overrides qiyas because partnership implies mutual guarantee.

This passage from Al-Hidayah, commenting on Quduri's matn, addresses a key question in partnership (shirkah) law: if one partner accepts a job, does the other partner become jointly liable? The text distinguishes between mufawadah (full partnership, where the ruling is clear) and other partnerships, where the ruling is established by istihsan (juristic preference) despite the contrary qiyas. Understanding this istihsan is crucial for grasping how Hanafi fiqh balances textual principles with practical needs in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
From Quduri's matn: 'Whatever work each of them accepts is binding upon him and upon his partner.' State the ruling clearly: both partners are liable for work accepted by either, both can demand wages, and payment to one discharges the customer.
This is the default rule for all partnerships in crafts (shirkat al-sanai').
2
Distinguish mufawadah from other partnerships
In mufawadah (full partnership with mutual agency and suretyship), this ruling applies by original rule (zahir) because kafalah is inherent. In other partnerships (e.g., inan, abdan, wujuh), it applies by istihsan. Understand why mufawadah is special: it automatically includes kafalah.
Mufawadah requires equal capital, labor, and profit; non-mufawadah partnerships are more restricted.
3
Understand the qiyas objection
Qiyas would hold each partner liable only for his own accepted work because the partnership contract does not explicitly include kafalah. Since the contract is 'absolute' (without guarantee), the general principle is that no one is liable for another's debts without consent. Identify this as the qiyas argument.
The text says: 'Qiyas is contrary to that because the partnership is absolute, and kafalah is only a requirement of mufawadah.'
4
Analyze the istihsan reasoning
Istihsan overrides qiyas because the nature of a work partnership (shirkat al-sanai') necessarily implies mutual guarantee. Each partner's acceptance of work is guaranteed by the other—otherwise the partnership would be impractical. The commentator points out: 'Do you not see that whatever work each of them accepts is guaranteed by the other?' Thus, both are entitled to wages because the other's acceptance is deemed as their own acceptance.
The istihsan argument is based on custom (urf) and necessity, common in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Consider a tailoring partnership: one partner accepts a suit order for 500. According to this ruling, both partners are liable to sew the suit, both can demand the 500, and the customer is discharged by paying either. Verify that this applies by istihsan (if not mufawadah) and explain why it is not unjust.
This ensures operational efficiency and protects customers from double liability.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partnership in mubah is invalid because agency for unowned property is void; each owns his personal catch.

This lesson explains why a partnership to collect freely available items like firewood or game is invalid in the Hanafi school. The key insight is that partnership implies agency, and you cannot appoint someone to acquire something that is not yet owned. We will explore the ruling and its application to different scenarios of joint and individual collection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a partnership (shirkah) to collect mubah property such as firewood or hunting. The ruling is that it is not permissible (invalid). Determine the precise legal classification (hukm) and scope.
The text opens with 'وَلَا يَجُوزُ' (not permissible) indicating invalidity.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is that partnership inherently includes agency (wakala), and agency to take mubah is invalid. Understand why: the principal cannot validly instruct regarding something not owned, and the agent can own it himself without the principal's order.
The phrase 'لِأَنَّ الشَّرِكَةَ مُتَضَمِّنَةٌ مَعْنَى الْوَكَالَةِ' is the key evidence.
3
Apply the ruling to joint taking
If both partners physically take the mubah together, they own it equally in halves because they are equal in the cause of entitlement (actual taking).
This is the exception: joint action produces joint ownership, but not by contract.
4

If only one partner works and the other does nothing, the product belongs entirely to the worker. The partnership agreement does not transfer ownership.
Contrast with valid partnership where work and profit are shared.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An assistant in collecting mubah deserves fair wage; Abu Yusuf caps it at half the value, while Muhammad awards full fair wage.

This lesson delves into the scenario where one person works and another assists in collecting mubah property. The Hanafi school agrees that the assistant is not a partner but a hired helper. Muhammad's view is based on general principles of hire: the assistant deserves fair compensation for his work. Abu Yusuf's view is based on the idea that the assistant's help cannot be more valuable than half the item itself, as the primary worker did the main job.
The ikhtilaf is over the quantum of compensation, not the entitlement itself.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative view)
Later Hanafi works generally prefer Imam Muhammad's view, awarding full fair wage. However, Abu Yusuf's view is also recognized as valid within the school.
In practice, a mufti would follow the majority view of the later scholars.

Section on Invalid Partnership. Translation: Section on invalid partnership. Partnership in collecting firewood and hunting is not valid. Whatever each one of them hunts or collects firewood is his alone, not his partner's. By analogy, this applies to partnership in acquiring any mubah (ownerless) thing, because partnership implies the meaning of agency, and appointing an agent to acquire mubah property is invalid, because the principal's command is not sound, and the agent can acquire it without his command, so he cannot act as his deputy. Ownership for them only occurs through taking and possession of the mubah. So if both take it together, it is half and half between them, because they are equal in the cause of entitlement. If only one takes it and the other does nothing, it is for the worker. Commentary
This is a section (faṣl) from Ashraf al-Hidayah on invalid partnerships (shirkah fāsidah). The matn begins with the ruling that partnership in collecting firewood (iḥṭiṭāb) and hunting (iṣṭiyād) is not valid. Each person owns what he personally collects or hunts. This ruling extends by analogy to all mubah (ownerless) property. The reason (ʿillah) is that a partnership inherently includes the meaning of agency (wakālah). But appointing someone as an agent to acquire mubah property is invalid, because the principal's command is not effective—the agent can acquire it without any command. Therefore, the partnership is invalid. The matn then outlines scenarios: if both physically participate in acquisition, ownership is shared equally (half and half) because they are equal in the cause. If only one works, the produce belongs solely to the worker. If one works and the other assists (e.g., uprooting vs. collecting), the assistant is entitled to a fair wage (ujr al-mithl). There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Muhammad says the assistant gets the full fair wage, while Imam Abu Yusuf limits it to half the value of the collected item. The commentary notes that Imam Muhammad's view is preferred (mukhtār). The text then presents another case: a partnership where one has a mule and the other a water-skin to draw water. The partnership is invalid because it is based on acquiring mubah (water). The earnings go entirely to the drawer, but he must pay the fair hire for the other's property (mule or water-skin). This is because he benefits from the property of another under a corrupt contract. The commentary explains the reasoning and confirms that the fair hire is due.

Key Terms
احتطاب (iḥṭiṭāb): Collecting firewood from the wilderness; considered acquiring mubah property.
اصطیاد (iṣṭiyād): Hunting; another form of acquiring mubah property.
مباح (mubāḥ): Ownerless property that anyone can acquire, e.g., firewood, wild game, water, minerals.
اجر مثل (ujr al-mithl): The fair market wage for a given service, determined by what is customary for similar work.

Partnership in Acquiring Mubah and Helper Compensation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on helper compensation in mubah acquisition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership for mubah property and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on helper compensation, including the positions of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf and the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Partnership to acquire mubah is invalid, but cooperative acquisition creates co-ownership; helper compensation is disputed between full wages and half-value cap.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: two people agree to jointly collect ownerless items (e.g., firewood or game). While the partnership contract itself is invalid, the actual cooperation in acquisition generates rights of ownership and compensation. The key dispute among the Hanafi imams concerns a helper who assists without independent acquisition—should he receive full market wages or only a portion?

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on partnership for mubah
Identify the ruling: the partnership contract (shirkah) for acquiring mubah items is fasid (invalid) because it includes an invalid agency component. Agency to acquire mubah is invalid because the agent can do it without a command.
The invalidity does not negate actual co-ownership if both work together.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Recognize that the disagreement is between Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf regarding the compensation of a helper who performs part of the work (e.g., loading or collecting) but does not independently acquire the property.
Both imams agree the helper is entitled to something; they differ on the quantum.
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad holds that the helper receives full ujrat al-mithl (market wage) for the work done, regardless of the value of the collected property.
This is based on the principle that wages are for work, not tied to the outcome.
4
Analyze Imam Abu Yusuf's position
Imam Abu Yusuf caps the helper's compensation at half the total value of the collected property. He reasons that the helper's role is secondary and should not exceed the owner's share.
His reasoning may be that the assistant's work is less than the primary acquirer's.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical effect
The commentator states Imam Muhammad's view is mukhtar (chosen). Thus, the relied-upon fatwa is that the helper gets full fair wages. The practical effect is that in a cooperation scenario, the assistant is entitled to full compensation, not limited by the property's value.
This thamarah al-khilaf: the helper's compensation is not capped by half the property value.

Commentary

Liability for Duplicate Zakat Payment in Partnership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for duplicate zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on liability for duplicate zakat payment and explain the underlying rationale using the analogy to dam al-ihsar.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the second payer of zakat is liable even if unaware, because the obligation was already discharged.

This passage deals with a subtle yet significant dispute within the Hanafi school: when two partners are authorized to pay each other's zakat and both end up paying, who bears the loss? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples disagree on whether the second payer's ignorance of the first payment absolves him of liability. The disagreement extends to the case of an agent (wakil) who pays zakat after the principal has already paid. Understanding the reasoning here sharpens your ability to apply the principles of obligation (wujub) and agency (wakalah) in financial matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Case
First, locate the primary scenario: two partners are each authorized to pay the other's zakat. Both pay, one after the other. What is the ruling on the second payer's liability? Recognize that this triggers an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The case assumes the partners gave explicit permission to each other.
2
State the Positions
State the position of Abu Hanifa: the second payer is liable (damin) whether he knew of the first payment or not. Then state the position of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): he is not liable if he was unaware of the prior payment.
Both positions agree if they paid simultaneously—each is liable for the other's share.
3
Compare the Rationales
Examine the underlying reasoning. Abu Hanifa holds that the purpose of the order to pay zakat is to discharge the obligation, which was already fulfilled by the first payment. The second payer's payment was ineffective, so he is a violator (mukhalif). The Sahibayn argue that the second payer was only required to give possession to the poor, which he did; whether it counts as zakat depends on the principal's intention, which is beyond his control.
This mirrors a deeper usul disagreement about the scope of authority.
4
Analyze the Analogy to Dam al-Ihsar
The Sahibayn analogize to a person ordered to slaughter a sacrifice (dam) for ihsar: if the principal performs Hajj first and then the agent slaughters, the agent is not liable. Abu Hanifa rebuts that in the zakat case, the obligation is already fixed (wajib), while the sacrifice was not obligatory at the time of slaughter (the person could have waited).
The key distinction is the timing and obligatory nature of the act.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the second payer must compensate the first; under the Sahibayn, no compensation if he was unaware. In contemporary practice, the mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa, so agents must be cautious.
This dispute also applies to an agent (wakil) paying zakat after the principal.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute in Hanafi law regarding liability when two partners are authorized to pay zakat on each other's property, and both pay. The ruling is that if they pay one after the other, the second payer is liable for the amount paid, according to Abu Hanifa, even if he was unaware of the first payment. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if he did not know of the prior payment, he is not liable. However, if both pay simultaneously, each is liable for the other's share according to all. The same disagreement applies to an agent (wakil) appointed to pay zakat: if the principal pays first and then the agent pays, the Sahibayn say the agent is not liable because he was only required to give possession, which he did. Abu Hanifa says the agent is liable because his payment did not actually fulfill the zakat obligation, which requires the principal's intention. The passage analogizes this to the case of a person ordered to slaughter a sacrifice (dam al-ihsar) after the ihsar has ended and the principal has already performed Hajj; in that case, the ordered person is not liable, according to all. Some say the same disagreement applies there, but others distinguish because the sacrifice is not obligatory (the person could have waited), whereas zakat is obligatory, so avoiding liability is the primary intent.

إحصار (iḥṣār): Prevention from completing Hajj or Umrah due to enemy, illness, or other obstacles, requiring a sacrifice (dam).

Authorization to Purchase a Slave-Girl in Mufawada Partnership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership of a slave-girl purchased with authorization in mufawada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position and the Sahibayn's position on ownership of a slave-girl bought with authorization in mufawada, and explain the legal reasoning behind each view.

Core Idea
Authorization for intercourse implies a gift of the partner's share – ownership passes without compensation.

In a mufawada partnership, partners have wide agency. But what happens when one partner authorizes the other to buy a slave-girl for personal use from partnership funds? Does the buyer own her outright, or must he compensate the authorizer? The Hanafi school splits: Abu Hanifa sees an implied gift in the authorization, while the Sahibayn analogize it to personal necessities like food and clothing. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
One mufawada partner authorizes the other to buy a slave-girl for intercourse. The purchase is made from joint funds.
The authorization is explicit for this specific purpose.
2
Identify the positions
Abu Hanifa says the buyer owns the slave-girl without compensation. Sahibayn say he must pay half the price.
The dispute is over ownership and reimbursement.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
The slave-girl enters joint property by the purchase, but authorization for intercourse implies a gift of the authorizer's share. Sale would violate partnership terms, so gift is the only lawful way.
The buyer must own fully to have intercourse; thus the authorization includes a gift.
4
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Analogous to food/clothing: the buyer discharged a personal debt from joint property, so he must reimburse. The purchase is for personal benefit, not partnership.
They apply qiyas to the established exception of necessities.
5
Understand the exception
Food and clothing are necessities excepted from partnership; slave-girl is not a necessity, hence gift is implied according to Abu Hanifa.
The distinction rests on necessity vs. non-necessity.

This passage deals with a scenario in a mufawada (unlimited) partnership where one partner authorizes the other to purchase a slave-girl from partnership funds and then have intercourse with her. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a specific transaction in a mufawada (equal) partnership. The case: one partner (A) gives permission to the other partner (B) to buy a slave girl (jariya) from joint funds for the purpose of intercourse. B does so. The dispute is about the ownership of the jariya and whether B owes A any compensation. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the jariya becomes B's property without any payment to A. His reasoning: In a mufawada partnership, any purchase made with joint funds automatically becomes joint property, because the nature of mufawada is that each partner is fully agent and surety for the other, and they cannot deviate from this requirement. Therefore, the jariya initially enters the partnership. However, since A gave explicit permission for B to have intercourse, and intercourse is only halal with full ownership, that permission implies A has gifted his share of the jariya to B. Thus, B becomes the sole owner through this implicit gift (hibah), and no reimbursement is required. Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree. They argue that the purchase was for B's personal benefit (intercourse), not for the partnership. The price was paid from joint funds, which constitutes B paying his personal debt from common property. Therefore, B must reimburse A for A's share of the price, i.e., half. They analogize this to the case where a partner buys food or clothing for his own dependents: the item becomes his personal property, but he must compensate the partnership for the cost. The text then clarifies that in the case of food and clothing, such purchases are exempted from the partnership due to necessity, so ownership vests immediately in the buyer. But in the jariya case, the purchase is not a necessity but a personal luxury, so the joint ownership initially attaches, and only the permission's implied gift transfers full ownership to B. The passage also mentions that the seller of the jariya can collect the full price from either partner, because in mufawada, each partner is a surety (kafil) for the other regarding trade debts. This is agreed upon by all (including the Sahibayn).

ملکیت کے مقابلہ میں ہوتی ہے۔ اور امام ابوحنیفہ رحمتہ اللہ علیہ کی دلیل یہ ہے کہ یہ باندی بلاشبہ مشتر کہ ملکیت میں آئی ہے۔ کیونکہ شرکت مفاوضہ اسی کا تقاضا کرتی ہے۔ کیونکہ دونوں شریکوں میں سے کسی کو بھی یہ اختیار نہیں ہے کہ شرکت کے تقاضہ کو بدل دیں۔ اس لئے یہ معاملہ اس حال کے مشابہہ ہوا کہ جس میں اجازت نہ ہو۔ پھر بھی اس میں کچھ اجازت پائی جاتی ہے کہ اجازت دینے والے نے اپنا حصہ اپنے شریک کو ہبہ کر دیا ہے۔ کیونکہ اسی نے وطی کرنے کی اجازت دی ہے اور یہ بات اس وقت حلال ہوتی ہے کہ اس پر ملکیت پائی گئی ہو۔ جبکہ اس صورت میں بیچ کے ذریعہ ملکیت حاصل ہونے کی کوئی صورت نہیں پائی جارہی ہے۔ کیونکہ ہم نے یہ پہلے ہی کہ دیا ہے کہ یہ بات تقاضائے شرکت کے خلاف ہے۔ اس لئے اس میں ملکیت کا ہونا اس ہبہ کے ذریعہ ثابت کیا جو اس کی اجازت دینے کے اندر پائی گئی ہے۔ بخلاف کھانے پینے اور کپڑے کے کیونکہ یہ چیزیں انتہائی ضروری ہونے کی وجہ سے شرکت سے مستفی ہوتی ہیں۔ لہذا خریدتے ہی اسی کی ملکیت میں داخل ہو جاتی اور مال شرکت سے اس کی قیمت ادا کرنے سے یہ ثابت ہوا کہ اس نے اپنا ذاتی قرض ادا کیا ہے ۔ بخلاف باندی کی صورت کے کہ اس نے اس باندی کے خریدنے کے بعد اس کی وہ قیمت ادا کی جو دونوں پر ادھار لازم ہوئی تھی ۔ کیونکہ اس باندی کی خریداری مشترک طور پر ہوئی تھی۔

Book of Partnership. 242. Ashraf al-Hidayah Urdu Sharh of Hidayah Volume Seven. ... (Urdu commentary on slave girl ownership dispute) ... The owner of the slave girl may receive the price from whichever of the two he wishes. Kafala. And the seller may take the price from whichever of them he wishes, by agreement, because it is a debt that became obligatory due to trade, and the mufawada partnership includes (kafala), so it becomes like food and clothing.
Source pages p. 243

Commentary
This passage concludes the discussion on mufawada partnership. The Urdu commentary explains an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a slave girl purchased jointly: Imam Abu Hanifa argued that the slave girl becomes common property from the moment of purchase, and if one partner permits the other to have intercourse, it becomes halal only through the first partner's gift of his share. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) held that the purchase is valid and the price is a joint debt. The Arabic matn then states the ruling agreed upon by all: the seller of the slave girl may demand the full price from either partner, because the debt arose from trade and mufawada inherently includes mutual guarantee (kafala). This is analogous to food and clothing purchases, which become the property of the purchasing partner but the price is a joint debt. Thus, the ruling is clear: in a mufawada partnership, debts from trade are jointly guaranteed, giving the creditor the option to recover from any partner.

Key Terms
مفاوضة (mufāwaḍah): A form of partnership where partners are equal in capital, profit, and liability, and each is an agent and guarantor for the other in trade.
كفالة (kafālah): Guarantee or suretyship; in mufawada, each partner is a guarantor for the debts arising from trade.
بالاتفاق (bil-ittifāq): By agreement; used here to indicate unanimity among the three Hanafi imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad).
