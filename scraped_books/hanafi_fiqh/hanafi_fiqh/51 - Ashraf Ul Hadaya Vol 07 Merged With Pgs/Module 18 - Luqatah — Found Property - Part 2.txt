Luqata in Hal and Haram: A Fundamental Ikhtilaf
Inter-madhab dispute: Hanafi holds Hal and Haram equal in luqata; Shafi'i requires indefinite ta'rif in Haram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that luqata in the Haram requires the same one-year ta'rif as in the Hal, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school treats luqata in the Haram exactly like outside—one year of ta'rif, then charity—based on the general hadith and a reinterpretation of the Haram-specific hadith.

In this lesson, we examine a classical ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding found property in the sacred precincts of Makkah. The Shafi'i school argues that because of the sanctity of the Haram, the duty to announce continues indefinitely. The Hanafi school, however, sees no difference in the rule. Let's explore the proofs and the reasoning behind the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: luqata in both Hal and Haram are treated equally. After one year of ta'rif, the finder may use or give in charity.
This is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that in the Haram, ta'rif must continue indefinitely until the owner appears.
Based on a hadith specific to the Haram.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i proof: the hadith 'Its luqata is not lawful except for one who announces it.'
This appears to command continuous announcement.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response interprets that hadith as meaning picking up is only for ta'rif, not that ta'rif is indefinite. The specification of the Haram is to emphasize ta'rif is not waived due to the place.
The general hadith commands ta'rif for one year without distinction.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Shafi'i requires ongoing announcement; Hanafi allows disposal after one year.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the school.

He said: The luqata of the Hal and the Haram are equal. Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhab dispute. The Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa) is that the rules of found property (luqata) are the same whether the item is found inside the Haram (sanctuary of Makkah) or outside (al-Hal). After one year of announcement (ta'rif), the finder may use the item or give it in charity, as in any other location. Al-Shafi'i argues that in the Haram, the finder must continue announcing indefinitely until the owner appears, citing the hadith 'its luqata is not lawful except for one who announces it.' The Hanafi response: our proof is the general hadith of the Prophet to 'identify its container and its tie, then announce it for a year,' which does not distinguish between Hal and Haram. Moreover, the Shafi'i hadith is interpreted as meaning that picking up is only allowed with the intention of announcing, and the specific mention of the Haram is to emphasize that ta'rif is not waived even though the item likely belongs to strangers (pilgrims) who may not return. Thus, after one year, the finder may give it in charity, preserving the owner's ownership in a sense, similar to other luqata.

Key Terms
الْحِلُّ (al-hill): The area outside the sanctuary (Haram) of Makkah.
التَّعْرِيفُ (at-ta'rif): The legal requirement to announce the found item publicly for a period.
اللُّقَطَةُ (al-luqatah): A lost item found by someone; the finder has the duty to announce and eventually may own it if conditions met.

Handover of Found Property Upon Description
Dispute on whether finder must hand over luqata to claimant who describes it correctly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether a finder must hand over luqata upon correct description, identify the opposing view, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Correct description permits, but does not obligate, handing over found property — Bayyinah required for forced delivery.

When someone finds a lost item, and a claimant appears who accurately describes it, what must the finder do? Must he hand it over, or can he wait for witnesses? The Hanafi school draws a sharp line between permission and obligation, based on the nature of possession (yad) and the requirement of proof (bayyinah). This passage examines the dispute with Malik and al-Shafi'i, who argue that description alone suffices to compel handover.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the core ruling: the finder may voluntarily hand over upon correct description but cannot be forced by a judge without bayyinah.
The passage uses 'halla li al-multaqit an yadfa'aha ilayhi wa la yujbar' – permissible but not mandatory.
2
Identify the opposing view
State that Malik and al-Shafi'i hold that the finder must be forced to hand over upon accurate description.
The text says 'qala Malik wa al-Shafi'i yujbar'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof (istidlal)
Understand why the opponents consider description sufficient: the dispute is over possession (yad), not ownership, so description resolves it without needing formal evidence.
The phrase 'sahib al-yadayn yunazi'uhu fi al-yad wa la yunazi'uhu fi al-milk' is key.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
See how the Hanafi school rebuts: yad is a substantive right akin to ownership, so it requires the same level of proof – bayyinah. The hadith permitting handover upon description grants permission, not obligation.
The comparative phrase 'al-yadu haqqun maqsudun ka al-milk' is central.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: in a Hanafi court, a judge will not order handover based on description alone; in Shafi'i/Maliki courts, he will.
This affects how a finder should respond to a claimant.

And when a man comes and claims the luqata, it is not handed over to him until he produces evidence (bayyinah). Then if he gives its sign (description), it becomes permissible for the finder to hand it over to him, but he is not forced to do that in the surplus. And Malik and al-Shafi'i said: He is forced. And the sign is like specifying the weight of the dirhams, their number, their tie, and their container. Their proof: the possessor of two hands disputes with him over the hand but does not dispute over ownership, so description is required due to the existence of dispute in one respect, and producing evidence is not required due to the absence of haste in one respect. And our proof: the hand is a right sought like ownership, so he is not entitled to it except by a proof, which is evidence, by analogy to ownership. Except that it is permissible for him to hand over upon matching the description, due to his saying (peace be upon him): 'If its owner comes and knows its container and its number, then hand it over to him.' And that is permissibility, acting on the well-known hadith, which is his saying: 'The burden of proof is on the claimant.'

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhab dispute. The Hanafi position: when a claimant claims a found item, he must produce witnesses (bayyinah) to prove ownership. If he merely describes the item correctly (giving signs like weight, number, tie, container), the finder may voluntarily hand it over, but the finder cannot be forced by a judge to do so. Malik and al-Shafi'i argue that correct description alone should obligate the finder to hand over, because the dispute is over possession (yad) not ownership, and description is sufficient to resolve it. The Hanafi response: the right to possession is a substantive right similar to ownership, so strong proof (bayyinah) is required. However, the hadith of the Prophet permits the finder to hand over upon correct matching of description, but this is a permission (ibahah), not an obligation. The well-known principle 'al-bayyinah 'ala al-mudda'i' (the burden of proof is on the claimant) also supports requiring evidence.

Key Terms
الْبَيِّنَةُ (al-bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically two male witnesses or one male and two females, to prove a claim.
الْعَلَامَةُ (al-‘alamah): The description of the found item (weight, number, tie, container) given by the claimant.
الْيَدُ (al-yad): Physical possession or control over an item.

Taking a Guarantor (Kafil) When Returning Luqata
Ruling on finder taking a guarantor from claimant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on taking a guarantor for luqata, distinguish it from taking a guarantor for an absent heir, and explain the two opinions on compulsion after the finder confirms the claimant.

Core Idea
Taking a guarantor for luqata is unanimously permissible; but after confirmation, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute on compulsion to hand over.

This passage addresses a secondary issue within the law of luqata: when the finder (multaqit) is ready to return the found property to the claimant, he may take a guarantor from the claimant to protect himself from future claims. While this is undisputed, the text contrasts it with the case of an absent heir and then presents a deeper disagreement on whether the finder's confirmation of the claimant's ownership compels him to hand over the property immediately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The text opens with the ruling that the finder may take a guarantor from the claimant before handing over the luqata. Note that this is by istihsan (juristic preference) and is unanimous.
Istihsan here means the finder is protecting his own interest, so there is no objection.
2
Contrast with the absent heir case
The text distinguishes this from taking a guarantor for an absent heir, which is disputed between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. Understand the point of difference: here the guarantor is for the finder himself, not for a third party.
3
State the two opinions on compulsion after confirmation
When the finder confirms (tasdiq) that the claimant is the owner, two opinions exist: (1) he is not compelled to hand over, by analogy with an agent for a deposit; (2) he is compelled because the owner is not apparent.
Both opinions are cited with the word 'qila' (it is said).
4
Analyze the analogy with the deposit
Understand the analogy: In the deposit case, the trustee (amin) is not compelled to hand over to an agent because the owner is known and the trustee can verify. In luqata, the owner is unknown, so the finder's confirmation may create a stronger obligation.
5
Recognize the practical consequence
The difference in opinion leads to different fatwa recommendations. Note that the text does not specify which view is mufta bihi.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Translation: 'He takes a guarantor from him' – The finder appoints someone as guarantor from the owner of the luqata, i.e., when he begins to hand over the property, to avoid any future liability, he asks the owner to appoint a guarantor. In this matter, there is no disagreement because he is asking for a guarantor for himself, unlike taking a guarantor for an absent heir, which according to Imam Abu Hanifa is not permissible (i.e., if Zayd dies and his inheritance is distributed among creditors and heirs, the Imam says that no guarantor is taken from creditors or heirs; but the Sahibayn say it is taken, in case another creditor or heir appears). And if the finder confirms the claimant that he is the owner, it is said that he is not compelled to return the luqata, like the agent for receiving a deposit when the trustee confirms him. For example, a person comes to the trustee and says, 'I have come from the owner of the deposit to take it back, and I am his agent for this purpose.' The trustee says, 'Yes, you are his agent.' Despite this statement, he is not compelled to hand over the deposit. And it is also said that the finder is compelled to return the property because in this case the owner is not apparent, whereas in the deposit the owner is apparent. (In the deposit, the owner is known, and the agent can be rejected, but in luqata, the owner is unknown, so when the finder confesses that the claimant is the owner, this confession becomes evidence and he must hand over.)

Commentary
This passage discusses a secondary issue: the finder (multaqit) may take a guarantor (kafil) from the claimant when returning the luqata. This is unanimously permissible (bila khilaf) because the finder is protecting his own interest. The text contrasts this with the case of taking a guarantor for an absent heir, which is disputed between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). The main point of difference arises when the finder acknowledges the claimant's ownership (if he says 'you are the owner'). Two opinions are presented: (1) He is not compelled to hand over, by analogy with a trustee (amin) of a deposit (wadi'ah) who confirms an agent of the owner – the trustee is not forced to hand over because the owner is known and present. (2) He is compelled because in luqata, the owner is unknown, so the finder's confession itself becomes proof. The text does not specify which view is relied upon but presents both as qeel (it is said). This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Key Terms
كفيل (kafil): A guarantor who becomes liable if the principal defaults.
تصديق (tasdiq): The finder's confirmation that the claimant is the true owner.
امانت (amanah): A deposit held in trust; the depositary must return it to the owner on demand.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that if a finder (multaqit) decides to give the luqatah in charity (after proper announcement and inability to find the owner), he must not give it to a rich person. The proof is the hadith: 'If the owner does not come, give it in charity,' and the principle that charity is not for the rich. Thus, luqatah given in charity becomes like zakat (obligatory charity) in this regard. The commentary elaborates: the finder should give to a poor person, even to a needy relative. The case of Ubayy ibn Ka'b, who was given a found item by the Prophet (ﷺ) to keep, is discussed. Some argued that Ubayy was rich, making this a special permission; others rebut that Ubayy was not rich but merely self-sufficient. The passage then relates the story of Abu Talha who gave his beloved garden in charity, and the Prophet advised him to give it to relatives, which Ubayy and Hassan were. This supports that Ubayy was a poor recipient, not rich. The commentary concludes that the term 'rich' in luqatah refers to one upon whom zakat is obligatory (possessor of nisab). The discussion ends with a narration about Ali and Fatima finding a dinar, illustrating ethical handling of found property.

Key Terms
الصدقة المفروضة (al-sadaqah al-mafrudah): Obligatory charity, i.e., zakat, which is prescribed for specific recipients and cannot be given to the rich.
الغني (al-ghani): A rich person; in the context of luqatah, it refers to one who possesses nisab (wealth liable for zakat).
اللقطة (al-luqatah): Found property that is not an animal; the finder must announce it for a year before disposing of it.

The Wealthy Finder and Luqatah: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on wealthy finder using lost property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a wealthy finder benefiting from luqatah, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and evaluate the Hanafi response regarding the hadith of Ubayy.

Core Idea
A wealthy finder cannot use lost property; only the poor may, by consensus and general texts.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a classical dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the permissibility of a wealthy finder (multaqit) benefitting from a lost item (luqatah). While Imam al-Shafi'i relies on the hadith of Ubayy ibn Ka'b, the Hanafi school restricts that hadith to Ubayy's specific case, upholding the general prohibition against using another's property without consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its basis
State the Hanafi position that a wealthy finder may not benefit from luqatah. Note that the ruling is based on the general texts prohibiting use of others' property and the consensus that the poor are excepted.
The hukm level is haram for the wealthy.
2
Recognise the Shafi'i argument and its dalil
Understand that Imam al-Shafi'i argues for permissibility based on the hadith of Ubayy, where the Prophet told him to benefit if the owner does not come. The Shafi'i also argues that the incentive of preservation applies to both rich and poor.
Ubayy was reportedly wealthy at the time (according to Shafi'i).
3
Examine the Hanafi response: two interpretations of Ubayy's hadith
The Hanafi school offers two responses: (1) Ubayy was not truly wealthy—he was needy (khushhaal) as shown by the story of Abu Talha's garden. (2) Even if wealthy, Ubayy's case was specific with explicit permission from the Prophet, not a general rule.
The general texts (إطلاق النصوص) remain the default principle.
4
Understand the Hanafi classification of wealthy vs. poor and the role of ijma
In Hanafi fiqh, 'wealthy' (ghani) means one liable for zakat. The poor (faqir) are excepted by consensus (ijma). The wealthy finder is expected to take the item for preservation, not benefit. The possibility of future poverty or wealth does not change the initial ruling.
The exception for the poor is established by narrated texts and ijma.
5
Apply the ruling to a hypothetical scenario
Given a wealthy person who finds a wallet, after announcing for one year he cannot spend the money. He must preserve it or give it as sadaqah if appropriate. A poor person may benefit after proper announcement.
This demonstrates the practical thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents an inter-madhhab dispute regarding whether a wealthy finder (multaqit) of a lost item (luqatah) may benefit from it before the owner is found. The Hanafi position, stated first, is that it is not permissible for a wealthy finder to benefit from the luqatah. Imam al-Shafi'i, however, argues that it is permissible for any finder, regardless of wealth, based on the hadith of Ubayy ibn Ka'b (may Allah be pleased with him), in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) instructed Ubayy to return the item if the owner comes, otherwise to benefit from it. The Shafi'i argument also includes the reasoning that the permission for the poor is to incentivize them to pick up and preserve the item, and the wealthy can also be motivated by the same incentive. The Hanafi response counters by emphasizing the general principle that another's property cannot be used without its owner's consent, as established by the general texts (Quran and Sunnah). The exception for the poor is based on explicit texts and consensus (ijma), and therefore remains specific to them. The Hanafi interpretation of the hadith of Ubayy is twofold: first, Ubayy was not truly wealthy at the time—he was in need, as indicated by the story of Abu Talha distributing his garden to him and Hassan ibn Thabit; second, even if he were wealthy, his case was specific and required the Prophet's explicit permission, which does not grant a general ruling. Furthermore, the Hanafi school argues that the wealthy person's taking of the item can be interpreted as taking it for safekeeping, with the possibility that during the one-year announcement period, he may become needy. Conversely, the poor person may become wealthy during that period. Thus, the ruling is based on the condition at the time of finding, not potential future states. The passage concludes that the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the Hanafi one: a wealthy finder may not benefit from the luqatah.

Sharaf al-Hidayah, Urdu commentary on Al-Hidayah, volume 7, page 195. Chapter of Luqatah. (Permissible) and Hadrat Ubayy bin Ka'b had permission from the Imam to benefit, i.e., the Messenger of Allah himself had given permission. And with the Imam's permission, even a rich person may benefit. Explanation: The scenario is that if a person finds a luqatah and its owner does not appear during the announcement period (one year), then the luqatah is given in charity. If the finder is rich, he is not permitted to benefit from the luqatah based on charity, because the luqatah is the property of another and does not permit benefit without the owner's consent. If the finder is poor, by consensus he is entitled to benefit from it. But if the finder is rich, he is not permitted to benefit. Imam Shafi'i says that after one year of announcement, even a rich person may benefit, citing the hadith of Ubayy in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitted him to benefit, though he was rich. Second, a poor person is allowed to benefit because he protected the luqatah, and this meaning is also present in the rich. Imam Abu Hanifa says that a rich person cannot benefit because the hadith absolutely negates the permissibility of luqatah, and its absolute forbiddance is established by consensus, and Ubayy was not rich, or someone asked Ubayy about luqatah and he gave that ruling. The reconciliation of these statements is that Imam Shafi'i's view is conditional on Qadi's permission, which is also accepted by the Hanafis. Otherwise, benefitting is not correct. A poor finder may benefit from the luqatah. And if the finder is poor, there is no harm in his benefitting from it, because that achieves consideration of benefit from both sides. That is why it is permissible to give it to another poor person. Translation: And if the finder is poor, there is no harm in his benefitting from it himself, because by doing so there is consideration of good and benefit for both the original owner and the finder. Therefore, it is also permissible for him to give it to another poor person. Likewise, if the poor person is his father, son, or wife, he may give to them even if he himself is rich, because there is benefit for both. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Shafi'i regarding the permissibility of a rich finder benefiting from luqatah after one year of announcement. The author first states the Hanafi position: a rich finder cannot benefit, but a poor finder can. Imam Shafi'i argues that a rich finder may also benefit, citing the hadith of Ubayy bin Ka'b. Imam Abu Hanifa responds by clarifying that the hadith is not absolute, and that Ubayy was not rich or that the permission was specific. The author reconciles by noting that Imam Shafi'i's view requires Qadi's permission, which the Hanafis also accept in some scenarios. The Arabic text from Al-Hidayah is then given: 'وَإِنْ كَانَ الْمُلْتَقِطُ فَقِيرًا فَلَا بَأسَ بِأنْ يَنْتَفِعَ بِهَا' confirming the poor finder's permission. The Urdu commentary explains that this is to ensure benefit for both parties. The ruling is clear: a poor finder may use the luqatah or give it to another poor person.

Rich Finder Giving Luqatah to Poor Relatives
Extends the ruling to the finder's poor father, son, or wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a rich finder gives luqatah to his poor father, son, or wife, and explain the rationale behind the permissibility.

Core Idea
A rich finder may give luqatah to his poor father, son, or wife, for mutual benefit.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a poor finder may benefit from luqatah after proper announcement. This lesson expands that ruling: even if the finder is rich, he may give the luqatah to his poor father, son, or wife. This is based on the same reasoning—benefit for both the poor relative and the original owner through reward.

Guided Path
1
Recall the base ruling
Remember that a poor finder, after proper announcement, may use the luqatah for himself. This is the foundation for the extension.
Refer to the earlier lesson on poor finder.
2
Identify the new case
Here the finder is rich, but his father, son, or wife is poor. The question is whether he can give the luqatah to them.
3
State the ruling
The Hanafi school rules that it is permissible (mubah) for a rich finder to give luqatah to his poor father, son, or wife.
This is from Al-Hidayah and is the relied-upon position.
4
Understand the rationale (talil)
The reason is the same as for the poor finder: there is benefit for both. The poor relative gets financial help, and the finder earns reward on behalf of the owner (which the owner would have gained if he gave charity himself).
The source says 'because of what we mentioned' referring to the earlier illa.
5
Confirm the authority
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is accepted without dispute within the Hanafi school.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf mentioned.
