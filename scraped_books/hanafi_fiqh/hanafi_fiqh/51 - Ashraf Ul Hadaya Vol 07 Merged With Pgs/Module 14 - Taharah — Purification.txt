Definition and Types of Ard al-Mawat (Barren Land)
Defines ard al-Mawat and enumerates its three main types with subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi Zahir al-Riwayah definition of ard al-mawat and identify its three main types with examples.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, ard al-mawat is ownerless, unclaimed, and non-beneficial land — no distance from habitation required.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the definition of barren land (ard al-mawat), which is central to rules of land ownership and taxation. You will learn the authoritative Hanafi definition from Zahir al-Riwayah and the differing views of Abu Yusuf and Tahawi. The passage then classifies barren land into three types, including khalisah land, which affects whether land is 'ushri or kharaji.

Guided Path
1
Extract the Zahir al-Riwayah definition
Find the definition given in the passage: land that is not owned, has no specific right, and is not beneficial.
Note that no distance condition is included.
2
Compare with alternative Hanafi views
Identify the conditions added by Imam Tahawi (nearness to habitation) and Imam Abu Yusuf (beyond earshot).
These are not from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Determine the mufta bihi (operative view)
The author of Bada'i' al-Sana'i' prefers the Zahir al-Riwayah, so that is the relied-upon definition.
4
Identify the three types of mawat
The passage lists permanently uncultivable, abandoned, and khalisah (state-owned) land.
5
Analyze the subcategories of khalisah
There are six subtypes, including lands of killed/fled owners, royal allocations, jungles, etc.
These relate to how land becomes state property after conquest.

It should be known that in the issue under discussion, five matters are clarified: 1. Barren land (ard al-mawat), 2. The ruling on barren land being 'ushri or kharaji, 3. The ruling on the lands of Basra, 4. The consensus of the Companions, 5. Analogy.

Barren Land (Ard al-Mawat)
In Urdu it is called 'non-cultivated' and 'wasteland'. In Arabic it is called 'ard al-mawat'. Barren land (non-cultivated, wasteland) is also called 'ard al-khalisah'. According to the Zahir al-Riwayah, barren land is defined as land that is not owned by anyone, nor is there any specific right over it, nor is it beneficial. According to Imam Tahawi, barren land is that which is not near habitation. Imam Abu Yusuf's statement is that barren land applies to a plot of land that is so far from habitation that a loud voice cannot be heard. In the Zahir al-Riwayah, the condition of Imam Tahawi and Imam Abu Yusuf is not present. According to the apparent tradition, land that becomes empty due to the sea receding also has the ruling of barren land, regardless of whether it is near or far from the city. An unowned large forest also falls under the category of barren land. According to the author of Bada'i' al-Sana'i', the correct view is that of the Zahir al-Riwayah. According to traditions and hadiths, barren and uncultivated lands are of three types:
1. Permanently uncultivable land
2. Abandoned land
3. Khalisah land
So the details of these three types of land are as follows. And with Allah is success.

- Permanently uncultivable land:
Such barren land that has always remained uncultivated, and due to its prolonged desolation, people generally fear and refrain from cultivating it. In Naqi', such land was given to Hazrat Zubayr.

2. Abandoned land:
Land that, despite being cultivable, has become uncultivable for some reason. For example,
- Land that remains useless after settlements are ruined and no one cultivates it.
- Lands that cannot be cultivated due to difficulty of irrigation, such as the lands of Medina that the people of Medina handed over to the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him). In Wadi 'Aqiq, the Prophet (peace be upon him) granted such land to Hazrat Hilal ibn Harith.

3. Khalisah land:
All those lands in the conquered territories that were designated as 'khalisah' (state-owned) for the Islamic government. Such lands are of six types:
First type: Lands whose owners were killed in war were considered khalisah.
Second type: Lands whose owners fled leaving their lands were considered khalisah.
Third type: Lands allocated solely for the king's expenses were called khalisah.
Fourth type: Lands that were fiefs of the royal family and officials were khalisah.
Fifth type: Land that includes jungles, lakes, thickets, etc. was considered khalisah.
Sixth type: Lands that were in the possession of a few individuals providing means of luxury, and after the dominance of the Islamic government, they had no owner or cultivator. All these lands were designated as khalisah.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of 'ard al-mawat' (barren land) from the commentary on Al-Hidayah. It begins by listing five key issues to be discussed, then focuses on the first: the definition of ard al-mawat. The author explains its meaning in Urdu and Arabic, and presents three views: the Zahir al-Riwayah (apparent transmission) from Imam Muhammad's six books, which defines it as ownerless, unclaimed, and non-beneficial; Imam Tahawi's view that it must be away from habitation; and Imam Abu Yusuf's view that it must be so far that a loud call cannot be heard. The commentary clarifies that the Zahir al-Riwayah does not include the conditions of distance mentioned by Tahawi and Abu Yusuf. Thus, even land near a city that becomes empty due to sea recession is considered mawat. The correct view is the Zahir al-Riwayah, as stated in Bada'i' al-Sana'i'. Then, based on traditions and hadiths, three types of barren land are given: (1) permanently uncultivable (e.g., land that has always been wasteland like that given to Zubayr in Naqi'), (2) abandoned land (land that was once cultivable but now barren due to ruined settlements or irrigation issues, as with Medinan lands given to the Prophet), and (3) khalisah land (state-owned land in conquered territories). The six subtypes of khalisah land are detailed, including lands of killed or fleeing owners, royal allocations, fiefs, jungles/lakes, and lands left ownerless after conquest. This classification is crucial for determining whether land is subject to 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax).

Key Terms
ارض موات (ard al-mawat): Land that is not owned by anyone, has no specific right attached, and is not beneficial (according to Zahir al-Riwayah).
ظاہر روایت (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-known and authoritative transmission from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani in his six core books.
ارض خالصہ (ard al-khalisah): State-owned land in conquered territories, often derived from mawat or lands of killed/fled owners.

Validity of Apostasy and Islam of an Intelligent Minor
Intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute on minor's religious acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, Abu Yusuf, and Zufar/Shafi'i on the validity of an intelligent minor's Islam and apostasy, and evaluate the Hanafi proof based on the precedent of 'Ali (RA).

Core Idea
An intelligent minor's Islam and apostasy are legally valid in the Hanafi school, though punishments are waived.

This lesson examines a classical debate within the Hanafi school (and with Imam al-Shafi'i) regarding the legal effect of an intelligent minor's actions in matters of faith. The central question is whether a child who has reached intellectual discernment can independently adopt or leave Islam. The positions range from full validity to complete rejection, with different implications for inheritance, compulsion, and punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the three positions
First, recognize that the debate concerns two acts: accepting Islam and apostatizing. Note the three distinct positions: (1) Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say both are valid; (2) Abu Yusuf says Islam is valid but apostasy is not; (3) Zufar and al-Shafi'i say neither is valid.
Abu Yusuf's position is an intermediate view.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof from 'Ali (RA)
The primary proof for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the historical fact that 'Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA) accepted Islam as a child and the Prophet (SAW) confirmed it. This demonstrates that a minor can be an independent agent (asl) in religion, not merely a follower (tabi').
This is a dalil naqli (transmitted proof) though not a specific hadith text.
3
Analyze the opposing argument (Zufar/Shafi'i)
The opponents argue that the minor is a follower (tabi') of his parents in religion, so he cannot be a primary agent. Additionally, recognizing his Islam leads to harmful consequences like losing inheritance from non-Muslim parents.
They consider the harm as a disqualifying factor.
4
Understand the Hanafi response on benefit and reality
Hanafis respond that the eternal benefit of Islam outweighs any worldly harm. The child's act of Islam is a reality (tasdiq and iqrar) and realities are not rejected. For apostasy, they argue it is also a real act, so it must be recognized, but the minor is compelled to return to Islam due to its benefit and not executed because punishments are waived for minors.
The principle of al-haqa'iq la turadd (realities are not rejected) is key.
5
Note the exceptions
The ruling applies only to a minor who has 'aql (intellectual discernment). A minor without intellect, a lunatic, or an intoxicated person without intellect cannot validly apostatize because their declaration does not indicate a change of creed.
This limits the scope of the debate.

A foundling is born free; his maintenance is a public responsibility, not the finder's.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes the legal status of an abandoned infant (laqit). Ruling on Capturing a Runaway versus a Stray Slave
States ruling on capturing runaway versus stray slaves with reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on capturing a runaway slave (ābiq) and a stray slave (ḍāll), and explain the procedural difference regarding the ruler's role.

Core Idea
A runaway slave must be handed to the ruler for imprisonment; a stray may be left because he does not flee.

This lesson examines a ruling from Al-Hidayah on the treatment of found slaves. The author distinguishes between a runaway (ābiq) who actively escapes and a stray (ḍāll) who has merely lost his way. Understanding this distinction helps us see how the school weighs practical security concerns in property law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Identify the two types of found slaves mentioned: ābiq (runaway) and ḍāll (stray). Note the preferred action for each.
2
Understand the rationale
Understand why the rulings differ: a runaway is likely to escape again, so he must be taken to the ruler for imprisonment; a stray remains in place, so the owner will find him.
3
Note the level of preference
Recognize that capturing a runaway is afḍal (preferred) but not obligatory. For a stray, there is a difference of opinion, but leaving him is considered better.
4
Review the procedure
Review the procedural steps: the finder brings the runaway to the sultan, who imprisons him; for a stray, no such action is required.

Digging a Well in a Mosque: Conditions of Permissibility
Ruling on digging a well inside a mosque

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which digging a well in a mosque is permissible according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A well in the mosque is allowed only if it benefits the people and harms no one.

Mosques are waqf properties dedicated to worship, but sometimes practical improvements like a well for wudu or drinking are proposed. This lesson examines a concise Hanafi ruling that permits such a modification when it serves the community without causing harm. The principle behind this is the legal maxim 'la darar wa la dirar' (no harm and no reciprocating harm).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is digging a well inside a mosque. The ruling is 'ja'iz' (permissible) — it is allowed.
Note that this is not wajib or mustahabb; it is simply permitted if conditions are met.
2
State the two conditions
Two conditions are required: (1) the well benefits people (e.g., provides water for wudu or drinking), and (2) it causes no harm (darar) to anyone — meaning no damage to the mosque structure, no obstruction, no uncleanliness, etc.
The Urdu phrase 'سی کا نقصان' likely means 'kisi ka nuqsan' (harm to anyone).
3
Apply the principle of harm avoidance
The condition 'no harm' reflects the Hanafi qa'ida: 'al-darar yuzal' (harm must be removed). Any modification that causes harm to the building or its users would render the act impermissible.
This principle is derived from the hadith 'la darar wa la dirar'.
4
Consider a counterexample
If the well leaks, causes dampness, or creates a safety hazard, it would become haram (prohibited) because harm outweighs benefit. The ruling is conditional.
The well must be maintained to avoid future harm.
