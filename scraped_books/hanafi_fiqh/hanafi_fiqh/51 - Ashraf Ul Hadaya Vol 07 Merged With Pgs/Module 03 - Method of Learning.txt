Definition of Siyar and Virtues of Jihad
Defines siyar and jihad, then cites hadith on virtues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define siyar and jihad according to the Hanafi school and state the virtue of jihad based on the hadith cited in Al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Jihad is the ultimate sacrifice in Allah's path, and its virtue is so great that even one day of guarding the frontier surpasses a month of worship.

This passage opens Kitab al-Siyar, one of the most important sections of Hanafi fiqh dealing with jihad and international relations. Al-Marghinani begins by defining the key terms siyar and jihad, then provides extensive hadith evidence to establish the immense virtue of jihad and ribat. Understanding these definitions is essential before learning the detailed legal rulings that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the lexical and legal definitions
Find how siyar is defined both in language and in sacred law. Note that siyar is the plural of sirah, meaning 'way' or 'conduct', and in shari'ah it refers specifically to the Prophet's conduct in military expeditions.
2
Extract the definition of jihad
Locate the definition of jihad: calling to the true religion and fighting those who reject it, with emphasis on expending all ability – including personal fighting, support with wealth, advice, or any means.
Ribat (frontier guarding) is introduced as subordinate to jihad.
3
Interrogate the hadith evidence
Review the hadith cited (e.g., from Ibn Mas'ud, Abu Hurairah, etc.) and note the ranking of deeds: jihad is among the best after faith and prayer. Also note the hadith on the virtue of ribat.
One day of ribat is better than a month of fasting and night vigil.
4
Identify the historical qualification
Notice that the text states jihad was fard 'ayn (individual obligation) in the time of the Prophet until the conquest of Makkah, implying that after that it became fard kifayah (collective obligation).
This is a key point in Hanafi fiqh.

The lexical and shar'i meaning of Siyar: Siyar is the plural of Sirah, which is a way in matters. In sacred law, it is specifically the way of the Prophet (peace be upon him) in his military expeditions. Commentary: Sirah is the plural of Sirah, and it refers to a way in matters. In sacred law, it refers to the specific way that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) adopted in his jihad. Explanation: In sacred law, jihad is called inviting to the true religion and fighting those who do not accept it. And it is apparent that besides Arabs, whoever is not satisfied with submission and paying jizyah should also be fought. The definition of this jihad is: expending all ability in fighting in the path of Allah, whether by fighting personally, or by helping those who fight, whether with wealth, or by giving opinions and advice, or by increasing their numbers and multitude, or by any other means. (Ibn al-Kamal). Subordinate to jihad is ribat. The meaning of ribat is staying at that frontier of Islam after which is the abode of disbelief. This is the chosen statement. The virtue of jihad is very great. Why not, since a person sacrifices his life and wealth while bearing many hardships to obtain Allah's pleasure. Even more difficult is to keep one's soul established on worship of Allah in both outer and inner states, happiness and sorrow. For this reason, the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) once said upon returning from a jihad: 'We have returned from the lesser jihad to the greater jihad.' For this reason, in the hadith of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him): I asked, 'O Messenger of Allah, which deed is best?' He said, 'Prayer at its time.' I asked, 'Then what?' He said, 'Obedience to parents.' I asked, 'Then what?' He said, 'Jihad in the path of Allah.' If I had asked more, he would have told me more. (Narrated by Bukhari). In the hadith of Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him): The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) was asked, 'Which deed is best?' He said, 'True faith in Allah and His messengers.' It was asked, 'Then what deed?' He said, 'Jihad in the path of Allah.' It was asked, 'Then what deed?' He said, 'An accepted Hajj.' (Narrated by Bukhari and Muslim). The meaning is that faith in Allah and His messenger includes prayer, fasting, and zakat. That is, when one has true faith, then abandoning prayer will not be possible. For this reason, in the hadith of Mu'adh (may Allah be pleased with him), he swore that after obligatory prayer, jihad in the path of Allah is (best). (Narrated by Tirmidhi). It should be known that all the Companions who performed jihad with the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) were fully observant of prayer and all other obligations. For this reason, in the hadith of Imran ibn Husayn (may Allah be pleased with him), the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Standing in the row of jihad is better than a year of worship.' (Narrated by Hakim). In the time of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him), jihad was an important matter and a personal obligation (fard 'ayn) until the conquest of Makkah. For this reason, in the hadith of Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him), when it was asked, 'O Messenger of Allah, what deed equals jihad?' He said, 'You cannot do it.' This was asked several times, and each time he said the same. Then he said, 'The example of a mujahid in the path of Allah is like a person who continuously fasts and prays all night, never being lazy in prayer and fasting until the mujahid returns.' (Narrated by Bukhari and Muslim). In the hadith of Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him): 'Whoever believes in Allah and sincerely trusts His promises, and dedicates his horse in the path of Allah, then its fodder, water, dung, and urine will be in the scale of his good deeds on the Day of Judgment.' (Narrated by Bukhari). Subordinate to jihad is also ribat. There are many hadiths about the virtue of ribat. In the hadith of Salman (may Allah be pleased with him), the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) used to say: 'One day of ribat in the path of Allah is better than a month of fasting and night vigil. And if he dies in that state, all the good deeds he used to do will continue, his provision will continue, and he will be safe from trials.' (Narrated by Muslim). And he will be raised as a martyr on the Day of Judgment, and he will be safe from the great terror of the Day of Judgment. (Narrated by Ibn Majah and al-Tabarani). In the hadith of Abu Umamah: 'One prayer of a murabit is equal to five hundred prayers, and spending one dinar or dirham is better than giving seven hundred gold coins in charity.'

Commentary
This passage introduces Kitab al-Siyar (The Book of Military Expeditions) from Al-Hidayah. The author first defines siyar lexically as 'manner' or 'way', and in Islamic legal terminology it specifically refers to the Prophet's conduct in his military campaigns (maghazi). Then jihad is defined as calling people to the true religion and fighting those who reject it. The definition emphasizes that jihad is not only personal fighting but also supporting fighters with wealth, opinion, or any means. The concept of ribat (frontier guarding) is introduced as subordinate to jihad. Extensive hadith are cited to show the great virtue of jihad: it is among the best deeds after faith and prayer, and one day of ribat is better than a month of worship. The passage clarifies that jihad was a personal obligation (fard 'ayn) in the Prophet's time until the conquest of Makkah, after which it became a collective obligation (fard kifayah). This sets the stage for the ruling that follows in the next section.

Key Terms
سیر (siyar): Plural of sirah; the Prophet's conduct in military expeditions.
جہاد (jihad): Struggling in Allah's path by fighting disbelievers or supporting fighters.
رباط (ribat): Stationing at a Muslim frontier to guard against enemy attack.
