Diyah and Kaffarah for Unintentional Killing Without Heir
Establishes the diyah and kaffarah obligations for specific unintentional killings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the diyah and kaffarah obligations for unintentional killing of a protected soul with no heir and apply the 'illa of ismah to analogous cases.

Core Idea
A protected soul with no heir still demands diyah: the Imam receives it.

This lesson examines a specific case: when a Muslim with no heir is killed unintentionally, or a harbi who had entered with aman and then embraced Islam is killed. The Hanafi ruling establishes that the 'aqila must pay diyah to the Imam, and the killer must offer kaffarah. The proof is based on analogy (qiyas) to all protected souls, because the 'illa (effective cause) is the sanctity of the slain person's life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Two acts are covered: unintentional killing of a Muslim with no heir, and unintentional killing of a harbi who became Muslim after aman. In both, the killer's 'aqila must pay diyah to the Imam, and the killer must perform kaffarah. The ruling level is wajib.
The diyah is wajib on the 'aqila; kaffarah is wajib on the killer individually.
2
Locate the dalil
The text provides the dalil: 'because he killed a protected soul unintentionally, so it is analogized to other protected souls.' This is a clear qiyas (analogical reasoning).
The dalil is not a direct verse or hadith but an inferred analogy from general principles of protected life.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The 'illa is the protected status (ismah) of the slain. Since a protected soul was killed unintentionally, the ruling of diyah and kaffarah applies to this case just as it applies to any other protected soul. The text compares this case to all other protected souls (sa'ir al-nufus al-ma'sumah).
The Hanafi position holds that the 'illa is established through tanqih al-manat (identifying the common attribute).
4
Note the recipient of diyah
The ruling specifies that diyah goes to the Imam, not the killer's family. The reason is that the victim has no heir. The Imam acts as administrator of the treasury.
This is a specific departure from the normal rule where diyah goes to the victim's heirs.
5
Examine the adjacent ruling
The commentary adds that if the killing was intentional, the Imam has the option of qisas or diyah. This is a transition to the next topic, but it highlights that the unintentional nature is critical.
The ruling in this lesson applies only to khata' (unintentional) killing.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It addresses the case of unintentional killing when there is no personal heir: either a Muslim with no heir, or a harbi (enemy combatant) who entered Muslim territory with a safe conduct (aman) and later embraced Islam. The ruling states that diyah (blood money) is due on the killer's 'aqila (agnatic helpers) and must be paid to the Imam (the Muslim ruler or treasury) because there is no specific heir to receive it. Additionally, the killer must perform kaffarah (expiation) for unintentional killing, which is freeing a slave or fasting. The reasoning given is that the slain person was a protected soul (ma'sumah), so the case is analogous to other protected souls. The proof uses analogy (qiyas) from general principles of protected life. The Urdu commentary clarifies that the right to collect diyah belongs solely to the Imam since there is no heir. It also notes a transition: if the killing was intentional, the Imam has the authority to either execute the killer or accept diyah, which is covered in the next lesson.

Key Terms
خطأ (khata'): Unintentional killing, without intent or mistake in action.
عاقلہ ('aqilah): The killer's agnatic relatives (tribe or clan) who are obligated to pay diyah in cases of unintentional killing.
دیت (diyah): Blood money paid to the victim's heirs for unintentional killing.
حربی (harbi): An enemy combatant from non-Muslim territory with whom no peace treaty exists.
مستأمن (musta'min): A non-Muslim who enters Muslim territory with a safe conduct (aman).

Imam's Authority in Intentional Killing Without an Heir
Ruling with proof on Imam's options in qatl amd without heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Imam's two permissible options in the case of intentional killing of a Muslim with no heir, and explain why forgiveness without compensation is not allowed, citing the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
The Imam may choose qisas or diyah, but cannot forgive, because he represents the public whose right cannot be surrendered without benefit.

When a Muslim is intentionally killed and leaves no heir, who holds the right to demand justice? The Hanafi school teaches that the Imam, as guardian of the public, steps in. This lesson unpacks his specific authority: he may execute the killer or accept blood money through reconciliation, but he cannot simply pardon. A hadith and the principle of public interest (maslahah) ground this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is intentional killing of a Muslim with no heir. The ruling is that the Imam has two options: qisas (execution) or diyah (blood money) via sulh (reconciliation). Forgiveness without compensation is not allowed.
The passage explicitly states the Imam does not have the right to forgive (laysa lahu an ya'fuwa).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'السُّلْطَانُ وَلِى مَنْ لَا وَلِى لَهُ' (The Sultan is the guardian of one who has no guardian). This is reported by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, and Ibn Majah.
This hadith is ahad in transmission (zanni al-thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith grants the Sultan general guardianship over those without a specific guardian. In this context, the slain has no heir, so the Imam becomes the guardian with authority to seek qisas or accept diyah in the public interest. The phrase 'wali man la wali lahu' establishes this authority.
The Hanafi position uses this hadith to expand the Imam's options beyond the default qisas.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that in intentional killing, qisas is the only right; the ruler cannot substitute diyah without the victim's consent. They argue the right belongs to the victim's heir, and the Imam is merely an executor.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on differing interpretations of the Sultan's guardianship.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Hanafis allow diyah via sulh because it may be more beneficial (anfa') for the public. The Imam's authority is supervisory (nazariyyah) and must serve the public interest. Dropping the right without compensation harms that interest, hence forbidden.
The principle of public interest (maslahah) overrides strict application of qisas in this specific scenario.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof addressing intentional killing (qatl amd) of a Muslim with no heir. The ruling gives the Imam two options: execute the killer by qisas (retaliation) or accept diyah (blood money) through sulh (reconciliation). The third option of forgiving without compensation is not available. The proof is the hadith 'The Sultan is the guardian of one who has no guardian' (reported by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi). The reasoning is that the right of retaliation belongs to the general public (al-'ammah) in the absence of a specific heir, and the Imam acts as their representative. Since his authority is based on public interest (maslahah), he may not forfeit their right without compensation. The Urdu commentary elaborates that 'taking diyah' means via sulh, not as an alternative penalty, because qisas is the primary penalty for intentional killing. However, because diyah may be more beneficial (e.g., for the community or public treasury), the Imam is permitted to accept it in lieu of execution. The commentary also emphasizes that the Imam cannot forgive unconditionally, as that would harm the public interest.

Key Terms
عمد ('amd): Intentional killing with the intent to kill or cause harm likely to result in death.
قود (qawad): Retaliation in kind; qisas for intentional killing.
صلح (sulh): A settlement between parties; here, the Imam agrees with the killer to accept diyah instead of qisas.
ولاية (wilayah): Authority or guardianship; here, the Imam's authority over the public interest in the absence of a personal heir.
