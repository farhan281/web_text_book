One Partner Guaranteeing a Stranger: Abu Hanifa's View
Dispute: one partner's guarantee of a stranger binds the other according to Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a kafalah by one mufawadah partner binds the other, and explain the basis for each position.

Core Idea
A gratuitous guarantee by one partner does not bind the other according to Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa holds it binds due to mutual agency.

This passage examines a critical question in partnership law: when does one partner's voluntary act create liability for the other? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on whether a guarantee for a stranger falls within the scope of mufawadah. Understanding this dispute clarifies the limits of agency in partnership.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: the guarantee binds the other partner.
This is based on the mutual agency and guarantee inherent in mufawadah.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
State that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) disagree: the guarantee does not bind.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's reasoning: the guarantee is a tabarru' (gratuitous act) not connected to trade, so it does not bind.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Note that Abu Hanifa's view is implicit: the guarantee is within the mutual agency of mufawadah, which covers all financial matters.
The text does not provide an explicit rebuttal but includes conditions that support Abu Hanifa's comprehensive view.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: if the partner is bound, he can be sued by the creditor; if not, only the guarantor is liable.

He said: If one of them guarantees a payment for a stranger, does his partner become bound? According to Abu Hanifa, yes. And they (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: It does not bind him, because it is a donation. And for this, it is not valid from a child, a slave who is permitted (to trade), nor a mukatab. And if it occurs from a sick person, it is valid from the third (of his estate), and it becomes like a loan and guarantee of the person.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a guarantee (kafalah) made by one mufawah partner for a stranger binds the other partner. Abu Hanifa holds that it does bind the other, because the guarantee is part of the mutual agency in mufawadah. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) disagree, arguing that such a guarantee is a gratuitous act (tabarru') and not part of trade, so it does not bind the other. The text also mentions that this type of guarantee is not valid from a child, a ma'dhun slave (one permitted to trade), or a mukatab (slave with a contract of manumission), and if it comes from a sick person (in death illness), it is valid only from one-third of his estate, similar to a loan or guarantee of the person.

Key Terms
تبرع (tabarruʿ): A gratuitous act, a voluntary donation without compensation.
مأذون (ma'dhūn): A slave permitted by his master to engage in trade.
مکاتب (mukātab): A slave who has made a contract with his master for manumission upon payment.

Kafalah in Mufawada: Does a Partner's Guarantee Bind the Partnership?
Intra-madhhab dispute on kafalah binding in mufawada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute on whether kafalah by one mufawada partner binds the others, and explain the talil based on the distinction between tabarru' and mu'awada.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: kafalah begins as donation, ends as exchange, hence binds partner; Sahibayn: pure donation throughout, so does not bind.

This passage delves into a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the binding effect of a kafalah (suretyship) undertaken by one partner in a mufawada partnership. At stake is whether the other partners become liable for the obligation. Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), hold opposing views. The disagreement hinges on the legal characterization of kafalah: is it purely a gratuitous contract (tabarru') throughout, or does it become a compensatory exchange (mu'awada) in its conclusion? Let us explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
What is the legal question? Does kafalah by one partner in mufawada bind the others?
The passage focuses on kafalah bi al-mal (property guarantee) with the order of the guaranteed person.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his talil
He says kafalah begins as tabarru' but becomes mu'awada because the guarantor gains a right to indemnity, thus it binds.
The key phrase: 'tabarru' ibtida'an wa mu'awada baqa'an' (donation initially, compensation eventually).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their talil
They say kafalah is entirely tabarru', so no binding on partners.
This is the default view often implied in the base text of the Hidayah.
4
Note the distinctions made in the text
The text differentiates kafalah bi al-mal from kafalah bi al-nafs, and also discusses iqrad and ghasb in a similar vein.
Kafalah bi al-nafs is agreed to be pure tabarru' (initial and final), so never binds.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: if Abu Hanifa's view is followed, the other partner must share the obligation; if Sahibayn's, only the guarantor is liable.
This affects risk allocation in mufawada partnerships.

And from Abu Hanifa: it is a donation initially and a compensation eventually, because he becomes entitled to indemnity for what he pays from the guaranteed person if the guarantee was by his order. So with regard to the eventual, the partnership (mufawada) encompasses it, and with regard to the initial, it is not valid from those mentioned. It is valid from one-third of the sick person, unlike guarantee of the person (kafalah bi-n-nafs) because it is a donation initially and finally. As for lending (iqrad), from Abu Hanifa it binds its owner. And if it is granted, it is an 'ariyah (loan for use), so its ruling is the ruling of its substance, not the ruling of the substitute, such that deferment is not valid in it, so compensation is not realized. If it was without his order, it does not bind its owner according to the sound opinion due to absence of the meaning of mufawada. The absolute answer in the book is interpreted as restricted. And guarantee of usurpation and destruction is like the guarantee according to Abu Hanifa because it is compensation eventually.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school. It discusses whether a kafalah (suretyship) undertaken by one partner in a shirkat al-mufawada (general partnership) for an outsider binds the other partner. Abu Hanifa holds that it does bind the other partner because although it is a tabarru' (donation) at its inception, it becomes a mu'awada (compensatory contract) at its conclusion: the guarantor becomes entitled to seek indemnity from the guaranteed person, turning it into an exchange. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, arguing that kafalah is entirely a tabarru', so it cannot bind other partners. The text then differentiates kafalah bi al-mal (guarantee of property) from kafalah bi al-nafs (guarantee of person), noting that the latter is pure tabarru' and never binding on partners. It also discusses iqrad (lending) and ghasb (usurpation) in a similar vein. The passage emphasizes the distinction between initial and eventual nature to determine whether a contract is a tabarru' or mu'awada, a key principle in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafalah): Guarantee or suretyship; undertaking to be responsible for a debt or obligation of another.
مفاوضة (mufawada): A full partnership where partners are general agents and guarantors for each other in all dealings.
تبرع (tabarru'): A gratuitous contract or donation without expectation of return.
