Inheritance of an Apostate's Earnings: A Three-Way Dispute
Presents a three-way dispute on apostate's earnings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the distribution of an apostate's earnings and evaluate the arguments of the Sahibayn and Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Earnings before apostasy go to Muslim heirs; after apostasy become fay'—time and attribution decide.

This lesson examines a classic dispute within the Hanafi school and between Hanafis and Shafi'is over the property of an apostate who dies or is killed in apostasy. The core question is whether ownership persists after apostasy and how to attribute earnings to the period before or after apostasy. We will explore the reasoning of Abu Hanifa, his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), and Imam al-Shafi'i, highlighting the practical consequences of each view.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa rules regarding earnings during Islam and during apostasy. Note the differentiation.
The matn attributes the view to Abu Hanifa alone.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold both earnings go to heirs, while al-Shafi'i holds both become fay'.
This is an intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the dalil for Sahibayn (ownership continues) and for al-Shafi'i (hadith 'Muslim does not inherit from kafir' and harbi status).
Al-Shafi'i uses a general proof; Sahibayn use a usul argument.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Explain how Abu Hanifa counters: attribution (isnad) is possible only if the earnings existed at the time before apostasy.
The condition for attribution is existence at the earlier time.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa only pre-apostasy wealth is inherited; under Sahibayn all is inherited; under Shafi'i none is inherited.
The commentary favours Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute on the distribution of an apostate's property when he dies or is killed. The matn states Abu Hanifa's view: what he earned before apostasy (as a Muslim) goes to his Muslim heirs, and what he earned after apostasy becomes fay' (booty for the public treasury). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that both categories of earnings go to his heirs, because ownership is not lost by apostasy alone. Al-Shafi'i holds that both become fay', because the apostate died as a disbeliever and a Muslim cannot inherit from a disbeliever, and the property is considered that of a harbi. The commentary then provides the proofs: Sahibayn argue that ownership persists after apostasy, so upon death it transfers to heirs, and since apostasy is the cause of death, it is as if it happened before death—hence Muslim inheriting from Muslim. Abu Hanifa counters that attribution to a time before apostasy is possible for earnings that existed then, but not for earnings that came into existence after apostasy, because the condition for attribution is existence at that time.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): One who leaves Islam after having been a Muslim.
فَيْء (fay'): Booty that belongs to the public treasury; here, property of an apostate that does not go to his heirs.
إِسْنَاد (isnad): Attribution of a ruling or effect to a specific time or cause.

Rules for Determining Heirs of an Apostate
Intra-Hanafi dispute on timing of determining heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi narrations on determining heirs of an apostate, identify the most correct (asahh) narration, and explain why the apostate's Muslim wife inherits if she is in her waiting period (iddah).

Core Idea
The apostate's heir is determined at death (asahh), but his wife inherits if in iddah because he is deemed to have fled from her.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the determination of heirs for an apostate. Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) has three different narrations on whether the identity of the heirs should be fixed at the time of apostasy, at death, or both. The commentary marks one narration as the most correct (asahh), which relies on analogy to a sale. Additionally, the passage addresses a separate but related ruling: the apostate's Muslim wife inherits from him if she is in her iddah at his death, regardless of his health at apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three narrated positions
List the three rulings attributed to Abu Hanifa regarding the timing of determining heirs: (1) heir must be heir at both apostasy and death; (2) heir at apostasy only, with substitution if that heir dies; (3) heir at death only.
Pay attention to the key difference: whether the heir can change between apostasy and death.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil) for each position
For each narration, identify the underlying principle: the first uses isnad (attribution), the second treats apostasy as death, the third analogizes to a sale where an event after contract but before completion is treated as before the contract.
These principles are foundational Hanafi usul concepts.
3
Compare the practical outcomes (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a scenario where a potential heir dies before the apostate. In the second narration, the deceased heir's own heir substitutes; in the first and third, no substitution occurs, so the estate goes to whoever is heir at the relevant time.
This difference affects estate distribution significantly.
4
Recognise the tarjih (preferment)
The commentary explicitly marks the third narration (by Muhammad al-Shaybani) as asahh (most sound), making it the mufta bihi (operative position) for Hanafi fatwa.
Tarjih is based on strength of analogy and consistency with usul principles.
5
Apply the separate ruling for the apostate's wife
Note that the apostate's Muslim wife inherits if she is in her iddah at his death, regardless of his health at apostasy. This is because the apostate is considered a farr (one who flees to deprive inheritance).
This ruling applies whether the apostate dies naturally or is executed.

Commentary
This passage deals with the determination of heirs for an apostate. It presents three narrations from Abu Hanifa. The first (attributed to Hasan ibn Ziyad) considers the heirs as those who were his heirs at the time of apostasy and remained heirs until his death, due to the principle of isnad (attribution) connecting the effect to the cause. Who Inherits from a Male Apostate? Three Narrations from Abu Hanifa
Explains three narrations from Abu Hanifa on who inherits from a male apostate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three narrations of Abu Hanifa on when the heir of a male apostate is determined and identify the most sound (asahh) narration.

Core Idea
The time for determining the heir of a male apostate is disputed among three narrations; the soundest ruling counts the heir at death.

This passage presents three narrations from Imam Abu Hanifa on the question of who inherits from a male apostate when he dies or is executed for apostasy. Each narration uses a different analogy to determine the heir: attribution to apostasy, apostasy as death, or considering the heir at death. The most sound (asahh) is Muhammad's narration. Additionally, the Muslim wife inherits if she is in her 'iddah, because he is considered a farr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three narrations
List the three narrations: (1) Hasan ibn Ziyad: heir must exist at apostasy and remain until death; (2) Abu Yusuf: heir at apostasy suffices, with substitution; (3) Muhammad: heir at death is considered.
Names: Hasan ibn Ziyad, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad.
2
Understand the analogical reasoning
For each narration, identify the underlying analogy or principle: (1) attribution to the time of apostasy; (2) apostasy is like death; (3) cause-inception analogy: heir born after the cause begins but before completion is like one born before the cause.
The third analogy uses the example of a child born from a purchased slave-girl before possession.
3
Recognize practical consequences
If the apostate's heir dies before the apostate, under the second narration that heir's own heir steps in; under the third, the original heir is excluded. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
The practical difference determines who eventually receives the inheritance.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text states that Muhammad's narration is the most sound (asahh). Therefore, the operative ruling for fatwa is that the heir at the time of death inherits. The wife in 'iddah also inherits as a farr case.
Tarjih: Muhammad's narration is preferred.

Earnings of a Female Apostate Go to Her Heirs
States the ruling for a female apostate's earnings with a causal explanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a female apostate's earnings and explain the legal cause ('illa) that distinguishes her case from a male apostate.

Core Idea
A female apostate's earnings are inherited, not fay', because she is not a combatant.

This lesson clarifies a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the earnings of a female apostate (murtaddah) go to her heirs as inheritance, not to the state treasury as fay'. The basis is the absence of the effective cause (sabab) that triggers fay' for a male apostate — namely, the potential for combat (harb). Understanding this distinction deepens your grasp of how the Hanafi school applies causal reasoning in inheritance and apostasy rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the clear ruling: a female apostate's earnings are inherited by her heirs, not taken as fay'.
2
Locate the legal cause
Find the sabab: the absence of harb (warfare) from her. The text says 'because there is no warfare from her, so the cause of fay' is not found'.
3
Distinguish the male apostate case
Contrast with the male apostate according to Abu Hanifa: his post-apostasy earnings become fay' because he is capable of harb.
This highlights that the 'illa (effective cause) for fay' is the possibility of combat, not apostasy itself.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Recognize that women are generally non-combatants in Hanafi law; thus the rule for fay' does not apply to them.
This is consistent with the broader Hanafi principle that women and non-combatants are not subject to the rules of fay'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the apostate's return to Islam after a judge has ruled him a murtadd and distributed his property. The ruling is that he can take back any property still in specie (ayn) from his heirs.
This applies only after judgment has been passed.
2
Locate the proof (ta'lil)
The rationale is that the heir inherited only because the apostate was considered dead and independent of the property. Upon return, the apostate is alive and in need, so he takes precedence.
This is a form of qiyas based on the concept of need (hajah).
3
Identify the exceptions
The right to reclaim is lost if the heir has disposed of the property (sold or consumed). Additionally, umm walad and mudabbar slaves are not reclaimed because the judge's ruling of their freedom is valid and irrevocable.
The exceptions are based on the finality of the judgment and the protection of third-party rights.
4
Distinguish before vs. after judgment
If the apostate returns before any judgment, it is as if he never apostatized; his property remains his entirely, and heirs have no claim.
The timing of the return relative to the judge's ruling is critical.

Core Idea
The child's inheritance from an apostate father hinges on the mother's religion, following the principle of the better parent.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced case: an apostate has a child with his concubine who was acquired during his Muslim period. Identify the scenario

Upon claiming the child, the concubine becomes an umm walad – she will be freed upon the master's death. The child is free and acknowledged as his son.

If the mother is Christian, the child does not inherit; if Muslim, the child does inherit. This is the critical point of the passage.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An apostate's first property becomes war booty; the second belongs to heirs if captured before division.

When an apostate flees to enemy territory with property, the fate of that property depends on when it was taken. The Hanafi school distinguishes between property taken on the initial flight and property taken after a subsequent return. For the first scenario, the property becomes fay' (war booty). For the second, if the heirs find the property before division, it is returned to them.
The ruling differs based on when the property was taken.

The first property was never inherited because the apostate's ownership was severed upon joining Dar al-Harb. The second property had already transferred to the heirs by inheritance when the apostate initially left.
The timing of the property's acquisition relative to the apostate's first flight is crucial.

If the second property is found before division, it is returned to heirs; if found after division, it would not be returned (implied).
The 'before division' condition protects the heirs' right.

Inheritance When a Just Person Kills a Rebel or Vice Versa: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheritance when rebels or just persons kill each other

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on inheritance when a just person or rebel kills the other, distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, and evaluate the role of corrupt interpretation (ta'wil fasid) and strength (man'a) in determining inheritance.

Core Idea
A justified killing does not prevent inheritance; a rebel's corrupt interpretation with strength can preserve his inheritance right.

This lesson examines a nuanced inheritance scenario from faraid: when killing occurs between a just person (ahl al-adl) and a rebel (baghi). The Hanafi school addresses both cases, but there is a significant intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other. We will unpack the rulings, the underlying principles of liability and sin, and the proofs from consensus.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
What exactly does the Hanafi school hold regarding inheritance when a just person kills a rebel, and when a rebel kills a just person? Identify the two scenarios and the ruling for each according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
Note the condition that the rebel must claim to have been on the truth.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Yusuf disagrees. State his position: the rebel never inherits from the just person, regardless of belief. Recognize that al-Shafi'i also holds this view.
3
Analyze the underlying principle (asl)
The passage explains that a just person is not liable and does not sin when killing a rebel, because he is commanded to fight. A rebel, however, sins but is not liable for damages. Understand why this distinction matters for inheritance.
Compare with the Shafi'i view that the rebel is liable.
4
Examine the proofs
The Hanafi proof for the rebel inheriting is the consensus of the Companions (ijma') as narrated by al-Zuhri, plus the reasoning that corrupt interpretation combined with strength (man'a) removes enforcement of liability. Abu Yusuf counters that ta'wil fasid only applies to repulsion (daf'), not to entitlement to inheritance.
The response of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the need to repel deprivation (hirman) is also valid, and kinship is the cause of inheritance.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: if a rebel kills his just father and claims he was right, he inherits according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but not according to Abu Yusuf. The relied-upon position in the madhhab is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
Abu Yusuf's view is also within the madhhab but considered weaker in this context.

Commentary
This passage addresses the complex issue of inheritance (irth) when a just person (ahl al-adl) and a rebel (baghi) kill each other. The ruling is presented and then the intra-Hanafi disagreement is detailed. First, if a just man kills a rebel relative, he inherits from him despite being the killer, because the killing is justified (bi haqq). Second, if a rebel kills his just relative, there are two scenarios: (a) if the rebel claims he was upon the truth at the time of killing and still believes so, he inherits; (b) if he admits he knew he was on falsehood, he does not inherit. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, holds that the rebel never inherits in either scenario, a view also held by Imam al-Shafi'i. The passage then explores the foundational principle (asl): a just person is not liable (daman) and does not sin when killing a rebel or destroying his property, because he is commanded to fight them. A rebel, however, commits a sin when killing a just person but is not liable for damages according to the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). Al-Shafi'i (in his old view) held that the rebel is liable. The same difference applies to an apostate who repents after killing or destroying property. The Hanafi proof is the consensus (ijma') of the Companions, as narrated by al-Zuhri, regarding the Khawarij: they were not subject to hadd or qisas due to their corrupt interpretation (ta'wil fasid) when they had strength (man'ah). The reasoning is that rulings require either enforcement (ilzam) by the Imam or voluntary acceptance (iltizam) by the subject. Since the rebel believes his action is permissible due to his interpretation, he does not consider himself sinful, and the Imam cannot enforce liability because the rebel has strength (man'ah) preventing enforcement. However, if the rebel admits he was on falsehood, then his action becomes unjustified, and liability (daman) applies. Abu Yusuf's argument is that the corrupt interpretation is only relevant for repulsion (daf'), not for entitlement to inheritance. But Abu Hanifa and Muhammad respond that the need to repel deprivation (hirman) from inheritance also applies, since kinship is the cause of inheritance; thus the corrupt interpretation can be considered, provided the rebel maintains his belief (diyaanah) that he was right. Once he admits falsehood, the excuse vanishes and liability returns.

إرث (irth): Inheritance, the transfer of property from a deceased to his legal heirs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A legal judgment is treated as reality: the estate is divided as if the missing person died at the moment of declaration.

The disappearance of a person (mafqud) raises complex legal questions about his property. How long should the estate remain undistributed? The Hanafi school answers that once a judge officially declares the missing person dead, the estate is immediately divided among the heirs present at that time. This lesson examines the ruling and its foundation in the principle that a legal judgment (hukmi) carries the same weight as actual reality (haqiqi) in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the distribution of the missing person's property. The ruling is that it is wajib to distribute upon the judge's declaration of death.
The distribution is not done at the time of disappearance but only after a legal judgment.
2
Understand the legal presumption
The division is treated as if the person died physically at that moment. This is because a legal judgment (hukmi) is given the same effect as actual reality (haqiqi).
This prevents indefinite suspension of inheritance.
3
Identify the heirs
The heirs considered are those alive at the time of the judgment, not at the time of disappearance.
Heirs born after disappearance but before judgment inherit; heirs who died before judgment do not.

No Inheritance Between Missing Person and Pre-deceased
Ruling on mutual inheritance exclusion with missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance between a missing person and those who die before the missing person's death is judicially declared, and explain why istishab al-hal does not suffice for entitlement.

Core Idea
A missing person and those who die before his judicial declaration do not inherit from each other.

When a person goes missing, his legal status is uncertain. The Hanafi school addresses inheritance rights between the missing person and others who die during his absence. This lesson examines the ruling that no mutual inheritance occurs until the missing person's death is judicially declared, and the rationale based on the weakness of istishab al-hal in establishing new rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The passage presents two symmetrical rules: (1) If a relative dies before the missing person is declared dead, that relative does not inherit from the missing person. (2) The missing person does not inherit from anyone who dies during his absence.
2
Analyze the reasoning for the first rule
The missing person's death has not been established by a judge, so he is treated as alive. Therefore, no inheritance can pass from him to the pre-deceased relative.
Inheritance requires that the heir survive the decedent.
3
Analyze the reasoning for the second rule
Although istishab al-hal presumes the missing person is alive, this presumption is not a sufficient proof (hujja) to establish entitlement (istihqaq) to inheritance. The missing person cannot inherit because there is no certainty that he survived the deceased.
Istishab can only preserve existing rights, not create new ones.
4
Recognize the epistemological principle
Inheritance rights require certainty or strong evidence. Istishab is a weak proof in Hanafi usul and cannot establish new claims. Therefore, both directions of inheritance are blocked.
If the missing person is later declared dead, the ruling applies from the date of the judicial declaration, not retroactively.

Inheritance with a Missing Heir: The Certain Minimum
Ruling on will for missing person and faraid example with missing son

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the immediate shares for heirs when a potential heir is missing, applying the Hanafi rule of giving the minimum certain share and stopping the remainder.

Core Idea
In Hanafi faraid, missing heirs cause shares to be reduced to the certain minimum; the rest is stopped.

When an heir is missing, we cannot know if they are alive or dead. The Hanafi school protects all potential heirs by giving each living heir the smallest amount they could possibly receive, holding the rest until the missing person's status is known. This lesson walks through a classic example with a missing son, two daughters, and grandchildren.

Guided Path
1
Identify all potential heirs
List every person who could inherit depending on the missing heir's status. In our case: two daughters, a missing son, a grandson, and a granddaughter.
Daughters are always heirs. Grandchildren are only heirs if the son is dead.
2
Determine who is excluded (hajb)
Check if any heir is totally excluded by the possibility of the missing heir being alive. The missing son, if alive, excludes his own children (grandson and granddaughter). So they get nothing now.
Hajb is hirman (total exclusion) here.
3
Calculate each heir's share in both scenarios
For the daughters: compute their share if the son is alive (1/2) and if he is dead (2/3). Their minimum certain share is 1/2.
The missing son's share is the residue (1/2 if alive, nothing if dead).
4
Give the minimum certain share to the non-excluded heirs
Daughters receive 1/2 immediately. The remaining 1/2 is stopped (mawquf) pending the son's status.
No other heir gets anything now.
5
State the final distribution
Daughters: half. Missing son: half stopped. Grandchildren: zero. If the son later appears alive, he takes the stopped half; if declared dead, the half goes to the daughters (as radd or asabah).
This protects all parties until certainty.

Core Idea
A missing person is presumed alive until proven otherwise; heirs who would be excluded by him receive nothing, and only the certain minimum is given to others.

When an heir disappears (mafqud), his status creates uncertainty in inheritance distribution. Hanafi law presumes him alive until his death is confirmed, affecting the shares of other heirs. In this lesson, we explore the principle: heirs who would be totally excluded by the missing person get nothing, while those who would only be reduced receive the lesser of their possible shares, with the remainder held pending the missing person's return or proof of death.

Similarly, if a bequest is made to a missing person and the testator dies, then the principle is that if with the missing person there is an heir who is not excluded by him, but his right is decreased by him: he is given the lesser of the two shares and the rest is stopped. And if with him there is an heir who would be excluded by him, he is not given anything at all. And the son's children are not given anything because they are excluded by the missing son: if he were alive, they would not inherit at all by the condition.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya explains inheritance rules when an heir is missing (mafqud). The basic principle is that the missing person is presumed alive until proven otherwise. Therefore, any heir who would be totally excluded (mahjub) by the missing person if alive does not receive any inheritance, because there is a possibility the missing person is alive. However, an heir who is not excluded (e.g., daughters who are not excluded by a son but rather their share is reduced) is given the minimum share that is certain—in this case, half of the estate—and the remaining half is reserved for the missing son. If the missing son later appears alive, he takes the reserved half; if he is confirmed dead, it goes to his children (as the residuary heirs by ta'sib). The example clarifies: a man dies with two daughters (who get half as Quranic heirs), a missing son, and a son's son and daughter (who would be excluded by the son if alive). Since the son's presence is possible, the grandchildren receive nothing. The bequest (wasiyyah) for a missing person is valid but suspended until the person appears or is confirmed dead.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): A person whose whereabouts are unknown and whose life or death is uncertain.
محجوب (mahjub): An heir who is excluded from inheritance due to the presence of a closer heir (hajib).
حاجب (hajib): An heir who prevents another heir from inheriting.
موقوف (mawquf): Suspended; the portion of the estate held back pending resolution of the mafqud's status.

If a stranger (non-heir) takes possession of that held portion, it is not taken away unless treachery is revealed.
