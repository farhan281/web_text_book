Imam's Option in Dividing Spoils from a Conquered City
Ruling statement on the imam's option after conquest by force

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the imam's options when a city is conquered by force (anwatan) and identify the basis for this discretion.

Core Idea
When a city is conquered by force, the imam may either distribute the spoils or leave the land as fay' for all Muslims.

This passage opens the chapter on spoils and their division in Al-Hidayah. It establishes a foundational principle: the imam has a choice (khiyar) when a city is conquered by force (anwatan). This discretion is rooted in the practice of the Prophet with Khaybar. Understanding this ruling is essential for grasping the broader Hanafi framework of ghanimah and fay'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the imam's disposition of a city conquered by force. The ruling: the imam has a choice (khiyar).
The choice is discretionary, not obligatory.
2
Understand the term 'anwatan'
Anwatan means conquest by overpowering the enemy in battle, as opposed to sulhan (peace treaty).
This condition triggers the ruling.
3
Note the two options
Option 1: Divide the spoils among the Muslim fighters. Option 2: Leave the land as fay' for the collective benefit of all Muslims.
The text mentions the first option explicitly, citing Khaybar.
4
Recognize the precedent
The Prophet ﷺ did similar with Khaybar, as referenced in the text.
This is a primary evidence for the ruling.
5
Appreciate the maslahah basis
The imam chooses based on what benefits the Muslims (public interest).
The choice is not arbitrary but guided by maslahah.

Reinforcements Share in Spoils Before Ihraz
Ruling on reinforcements sharing in spoils before ihraz, with basis in Hanafi principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reinforcements sharing in spoils before ihraz and explain the three events that cut off the right of partnership.

Core Idea
Reinforcements arriving before ihraz share in spoils, even after battle, because ownership completes only with ihraz, division, or sale in dar al-harb.

This ruling from Hanafi siyar addresses a critical question: when do reinforcements (madad) who join a Muslim army after the fighting has ended still get a share of the spoils? The Hanafi school, contrary to the Shafi'i, grants them a share as long as they arrive before one of three events that complete ownership of the spoils. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its basis in Hanafi usul, and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario involves reinforcements joining the original fighters in dar al-harb before the spoils are taken to dar al-Islam (ihraz). The passage states that these reinforcements do share in the spoils, even if they arrive after the battle ends.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi position is that reinforcements are entitled to a share if they join before any of the three cut-off events: ihraz (bringing to dar al-Islam), division by the imam in dar al-harb, or sale by the imam of the spoils in dar al-harb.
These events complete ownership and cut off the right of new arrivals.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that reinforcements who arrive after the fighting ends do not share, even if ihraz hasn't occurred. The Hanafi school's more inclusive view is based on the principle that ownership is not complete until a formal act (ihraz, division, or sale) takes place.
The point of ikhtilaf is whether the end of battle alone cuts off partnership.
4
Apply the principle to al-Quduri's addition
Al-Quduri notes that non-combatant market people (ahl al-suq) have no share. This reinforces that entitlement is tied to being part of the military expedition, not just being present in dar al-harb.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, following the earlier discussion. It addresses the scenario where reinforcements (madad) arrive and join the original army in dar al-harb before the spoils have been taken to dar al-Islam (ihraz). The ruling states that these reinforcements share in the spoils, even if they arrive after the fighting has ended. This differs from Imam al-Shafi'i, who would not give them a share. The basis is the Hanafi principle that ownership of spoils is not complete until ihraz, or until the imam divides or sells the spoils in dar al-harb (which also completes ownership). Until one of these three events occurs, the spoils remain common property (musharikah) among those who were part of the army for the purpose. Therefore, any new arrivals who join the army in dar al-harb before these cut-off points become partners. The passage ends by quoting al-Quduri that non-combatant market people (ahl al-suq) have no share in spoils, reinforcing that entitlement is based on being part of the military expedition.

Key Terms
مدد (madad): Reinforcements or additional troops that join the army after the initial expedition has set out.
إحراز (ihraz): Bringing spoils to dar al-Islam and securing them, completing ownership and cutting off partnership.

Khums Distribution for Relatives: Hanafi Position and Proofs
Hanafi ruling and proof for khums to relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khums distribution to relatives and identify the proofs from the verse, the practice of the caliphs, and the hadith.

Core Idea
Khums goes only to poor relatives of Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, as proven by caliphal practice and the substitution for zakat.

This lesson presents the foundational Hanafi ruling on the distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils) to the Prophet's relatives. The author clarifies that only Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib are entitled, and only if they are poor. The evidence includes the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs and a hadith where the Prophet explains that khums compensates for the prohibition of zakat, which is only for the needy. Understanding this ruling is essential before examining the disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State that khums is divided into three shares (orphans, needy, wayfarers) and that relatives entitled are Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, poor only.
2
Locate the first proof: caliphal practice
The author cites the four caliphs who divided khums into three shares, omitting a separate share for relatives, indicating that relatives are included only if poor.
3
Locate the second proof: the hadith
The hadith states that Allah substituted khums for zakat for Banu Hashim, implying that khums is for the needy as zakat is for the needy.
4
Analyze the reason for excluding other clans
The Prophet's interlocking fingers and statement about support indicate that 'nearness' in the verse means support, not blood relation.

Commentary
This passage explains the distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils) and the entitlement of the Prophet's relatives. The passage is a ruling with proof and includes an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, followed by an intra-Hanafi difference between Imam al-Karkhi and Imam al-Tahawi regarding the fate of the share for poor relatives after the Prophet's death. The author first states that the khums is divided into three shares for orphans, needy, and wayfarers, with the male receiving twice the female's portion. Only Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib are entitled among the Prophet's relatives, to the exclusion of Banu Abd Shams and Banu Nawfal, because they supported the Prophet consistently. The proof is the verse 'and for the near of kin' (Quran 59:7) and the practice of the four caliphs. A hadith is cited: the Prophet said Allah disliked for them the dirt of zakat and substituted khums. The compensation (khums) goes only to the needy, not the wealthy, because the hadith indicates that the share is for the poor. The author then addresses: the mention of Allah in the khums verse is for blessing; the Prophet's share ceased with his death just as the safi (his selection from booty) ceased, because he deserved it through prophethood and there is no prophet after him. Al-Shafi'i held that the Prophet's share goes to the caliph, but the author rejects this, noting that the Rightly Guided Caliphs did not take it. Finally, the passage discusses the post-Prophet entitlement of poor relatives: Imam al-Karkhi said they are entitled due to poverty, while Imam al-Tahawi said even the poor relatives' share fell because it is like sadaqah and forbidden for them. The first opinion (Karkhi) is considered stronger (asahh) because of the hadith that Umar gave to the poor among them, and consensus was that the rich are excluded, but the poor are included in the three categories.

Key Terms
خمس (khums): One-fifth of the spoils of war set aside for specified categories.
صفي (safi): Item the Prophet could select from spoils before division.
ذوي القربی (dhawi al-qurba): Near relatives; in khums context, Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib.

The Shafi‘i Dispute: Wealthy Relatives and the Prophet’s Share
Inter-madhhab dispute on khums details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on khums distribution to wealthy relatives and the fate of the Prophet's share, and evaluate the Hanafi rebuttal.

Core Idea
Shafi‘i holds that all relatives (rich and poor) get a share and the Prophet's share goes to the caliph; Hanafi rejects both.

This lesson delves into the inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding two key issues: whether wealthy relatives of the Prophet are entitled to a share of khums, and what happens to the Prophet's share after his death. The author presents the Shafi'i position and then provides strong Hanafi counterarguments based on the cessation of prophethood and the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that all relatives of the Prophet, rich and poor, are entitled to a share from khums, and that the Prophet's share should be given to the caliph after his death.
2
Analyze the Hanafi response to the relative's share
The Hanafi response is that the practice of the four caliphs and the hadith about zakat substitution exclude the wealthy. The verse is interpreted by these specific proofs.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Prophet's share
The Prophet's share fell with his death because it was linked to his prophethood, which ended. The caliphs did not take it, proving it ceased. The safi also ceased for the same reason.
4
Note the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Shafi'is would give a separate share to all relatives and give the Prophet's share to the caliph; Hanafis do neither.

Commentary
This passage explains the distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils) and the entitlement of the Prophet's relatives. The passage is a ruling with proof and includes an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, followed by an intra-Hanafi difference between Imam al-Karkhi and Imam al-Tahawi regarding the fate of the share for poor relatives after the Prophet's death. The author first states that the khums is divided into three shares for orphans, needy, and wayfarers, with the male receiving twice the female's portion. Only Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib are entitled among the Prophet's relatives, to the exclusion of Banu Abd Shams and Banu Nawfal, because they supported the Prophet consistently. The proof is the verse 'and for the near of kin' (Quran 59:7) and the practice of the four caliphs. A hadith is cited: the Prophet said Allah disliked for them the dirt of zakat and substituted khums. The compensation (khums) goes only to the needy, not the wealthy, because the hadith indicates that the share is for the poor. The author then addresses: the mention of Allah in the khums verse is for blessing; the Prophet's share ceased with his death just as the safi (his selection from booty) ceased, because he deserved it through prophethood and there is no prophet after him. Al-Shafi'i held that the Prophet's share goes to the caliph, but the author rejects this, noting that the Rightly Guided Caliphs did not take it. Finally, the passage discusses the post-Prophet entitlement of poor relatives: Imam al-Karkhi said they are entitled due to poverty, while Imam al-Tahawi said even the poor relatives' share fell because it is like sadaqah and forbidden for them. The first opinion (Karkhi) is considered stronger (asahh) because of the hadith that Umar gave to the poor among them, and consensus was that the rich are excluded, but the poor are included in the three categories.

Post-Prophet Entitlement: Karkhi vs. Tahawi on Poor Relatives
Intra-Hanafi dispute on poor relatives' entitlement after Prophet

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the views of Imam al-Karkhi and Imam al-Tahawi regarding the share of poor relatives after the Prophet's death, and explain why the Karkhi position is considered asahh.

Core Idea
After the Prophet, poor relatives still receive khums according to Karkhi; Tahawi says even that share fell.

This lesson addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute between two early Hanafi authorities, Imam al-Karkhi and Imam al-Tahawi. The question is whether the poor relatives of Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib continue to be entitled to khums after the Prophet's death. The author sides with Imam al-Karkhi, stating it is the more correct opinion (asahh). Understanding this nuance is crucial for the correct application of khums distribution today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the point of dispute
Both agree that rich relatives are excluded. The dispute is whether poor relatives also lose their entitlement after the Prophet's death.
2
State al-Karkhi's position and evidence
Al-Karkhi holds that poor relatives still receive khums because they are included in the three categories (orphans, needy, wayfarers). Evidence: Umar gave to poor relatives.
3
State al-Tahawi's position and evidence
Al-Tahawi holds that even the poor relatives' share fell, because khums to relatives is like sadaqah for them (due to need), and sadaqah is impermissible for Hashimites. Also, he relies on the consensus (ijma) of the caliphs dividing khums into three shares without a separate relative share.
4
Analyze the tarjih (preference)
The author states that the first opinion (Karkhi) is asahh (more correct) because Umar's practice supports it, and the consensus only excluded the rich, not the poor.

Khums for Poor Relatives: Karakhi vs Tahawi
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khums for poor relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions in the intra-Hanafi dispute over khums for poor relatives and apply the preferred opinion (mufta bihi).

Core Idea
Poor relatives still receive khums after the Prophet's death; the rich do not.

After the Prophet's death, the distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils) was adjusted. A key question arose: do poor relatives of the Prophet still receive a share? This lesson examines the internal Hanafi debate between Imam al-Karakhi and Imam al-Tahawi, and which view is considered strongest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Recognize that the passage presents two opposing views within the Hanafi school: al-Karakhi says poor relatives are entitled, al-Tahawi says their share is dropped.
This is an ikhtilaf within the madhhab, not between madhhabs.
2
Examine al-Tahawi's evidence
Al-Tahawi argues based on the consensus of the caliphs who divided khums into three shares (orphans, poor, travelers) and because the recipient's need resembles charity (sadaqah), which is forbidden for Hashimis.
The term 'sadaqah' is key: it is prohibited for Banu Hashim.
3
Examine al-Karakhi's evidence
Al-Karakhi's view is supported by the practice of Umar giving to poor relatives and the argument that the consensus only dropped the share of the wealthy, not the poor.
The shared illa (cause) is need, not wealth.
4
Understand the tarjih (preference)
The text declares al-Karakhi's view as 'asahh' (more correct). The mufta bihi is that poor relatives receive khums.
Tarjih based on action of a Caliph and consistency with general categories.
5

Ruling on determining kharaj via capacity

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kharaj on unrated lands is set by capacity, capped at half the produce—the essence of justice.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a gap in the kharaj system: what to do with crops like saffron or walled gardens for which Caliph Umar didn't set a fixed rate? The Hanafi solution uses the principle of capacity (ṭāqah), derived from Umar's general practice, with a maximum of half the produce. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning (qiyas khafi) behind this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the gap
Note that for certain crops (e.g., saffron) and land types (bustān), no fixed rate (tawẓīf) was set by Caliph Umar.
This creates a legal lacuna that needs a principle.
2
State the governing principle
The tax is imposed according to the land's productive capacity (ṭāqah).
Capacity is the key concept; it is not a fixed percentage.
3
Trace the proof
The proof is from Umar's practice: he considered capacity when setting rates for other lands, so by analogy (qiyas), the same applies here.
This is a qawl al-sahabi and a qiyas khafi.
4
Apply the maximum limit
The tax cannot exceed half of the produce, as half is the essence of justice (inṣāf).
Half is the upper bound, not a fixed rate.
5
Define bustān
A bostan is a walled enclosure containing date palms and various trees.
Definition clarifies which lands fall under this ruling.

Dropping Kharaj Due to Natural Calamities
Ruling on kharaj exemption due to natural calamities with later fatwa refinement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dropping kharaj due to natural calamities and apply the condition that crop presence for three months prevents the drop.

Core Idea
Kharaj drops when land loses productivity, but a three-month crop presence keeps it due.

Kharaj is an annual tax on agricultural land under Islamic state revenue. The Hanafi school ties the obligation to the land's potential productivity throughout the year. This lesson examines when natural disasters such as flood, drought, or blight excuse the farmer from paying kharaj, and a later fatwa that refines the rule when a crop appears briefly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Subject
Recognize that the passage rules on kharaj land when natural calamities destroy the crop or make cultivation impossible. The hukm is that kharaj is dropped (sāqiṭ).
2
Locate the Proof (Dalil)
The text cites an analogy (qiyas) with zakat wealth: just as zakat requires wealth to be productive for a full year, kharaj requires land to be potentially productive (samā' taqdīrī) for the whole year.
The proof is explicit: 'كما في مال الزكوة'
3
Trace the Legal Reasoning (Wajh al-Dalalah)
Analyze how the analogy works: Zakat obligation drops if wealth loses its productive capacity before the year ends. Similarly, kharaj drops if the land loses its notional growth at any point during the year.
The effective cause (ʿilla) is the loss of potential productivity for the entire year.
4
Note the Later Fatwa (Iftaa)
The text mentions a later fatwa: if the crop is present for at least three months, kharaj is not dropped. This restricts the general rule based on actual growth (samā' haqīqī) for that period.
This is a muftā bihī position from the later Hanafi authorities.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih
Understand that the three-month condition is a refinement (tarjih) to prevent abuse. The authority weight is 'fatawa mutakhkhirin' and it overrides the general principle in cases of partial survival.
The reasoning remains the same: kharaj is linked to actual cultivation once it appears.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kharaj and 'ushr are mutually exclusive in Hanafi; a land cannot bear both taxes.

This passage presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute over agricultural taxation. The Hanafi school holds that land subject to kharaj (land tax) is exempt from 'ushr (tithe on produce), while Imam al-Shafi'i argues both may be due. The disagreement hinges on whether kharaj and 'ushr are fundamentally the same type of tax or distinct obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling
First, determine exactly what each school claims. Hanafi: no 'ushr is due on produce from kharaj land. Shafi'i: both kharaj and 'ushr are due on their respective bases (land vs. produce).
The dispute is about cumulative taxation.
2
Analyze each school's proof
Examine the reasoning. Shafi'i argues that kharaj is a land tax and 'ushr is a produce tax; they are two different rights. Hanafi responds that both are mu'nah (burden) on the land and cannot be combined; the practice of the Companions supports this.
Hanafi's implicit rebuttal is that the two are not truly distinct in nature.
3
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference: In Hanafi, a Muslim landowner of kharaj land pays only kharaj, not 'ushr. In Shafi'i, he would pay both, increasing his tax burden.
This difference directly affects the farmer's finances.
4
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (relied upon for fatwa) based on the authority of the Zahir al-Riwayah texts. The reasoning that kharaj and 'ushr are both mu'nah is considered stronger.
Tarjih is given by later Hanafi jurists.

Women of Banu Taghlib and the Double Zakat: A Madhhab Dispute
Dispute on the legal nature of the double zakat treaty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school collects the double zakat from women of Banu Taghlib and evaluate the opposing view of Zufar and al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
The double zakat is property by treaty, not jizya, so women are liable just as for zakat.

This passage addresses a specific historical treaty with the Christian Arab tribe Banu Taghlib, imposing a tax double the standard zakat rate. The Hanafi school rules that this tax is collected from both men and women, but not from children. A minority Hanafi opinion (Zufar) and the Shafi'i school treat it as jizya, which excludes women. The dispute hinges on the legal nature of the payment: is it a form of jizya or a treaty-based property obligation analogous to zakat?

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: women of Banu Taghlib pay the double zakat; children do not. Note the analogy to zakat itself, which obligates women and not children.
The analogy to zakat is the key to the Hanafi reasoning.
2
Identify the opposing view
Zufar (Hanafi) and al-Shafi'i argue that women should not pay because the payment is essentially jizya, and jizya is not imposed on women. Their evidence includes Umar's statement: 'This is jizya, call it whatever you wish.'
Umar's statement is a strong textual proof for the opposition.
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response is twofold: (1) The payment is property (mal) that became obligatory by treaty (sulh), not by the rules of jizya. Women are legally capable of such obligations. (2) The spending of the payment is on general Muslim interests (masalih al-muslimin), not exclusively on jizya purposes. Moreover, the specific conditions of jizya (e.g., humiliation, collection through a deputy) are not observed.
The distinction between jizya and treaty-based wealth is central.
4
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether the tax is collected from women of Banu Taghlib. The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (operative ruling).
This shows how a conceptual dispute has real financial implications.

Imam Does Not Recollect Taxes from Rebel-Occupied Periods
Ruling on recollecting kharaj/ushr from rebel-occupied territories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on recollecting kharaj/ushr from rebel-occupied territories and explain the underlying legal illa (protection-based entitlement).

Core Idea
Tax is compensation for protection; no protection, no recollecting.

This passage addresses a practical fiscal issue: when rebels temporarily control Muslim lands and collect the state taxes (kharaj and ushr), does the Imam recollect those same taxes after regaining control? The Hanafi school answers with a clear 'no', grounded in the principle that the Imam's right to tax is paired with his duty to protect. The reasoning (talil) unfolds in several layers, considering whether the rebels spent the funds correctly or misappropriated them, and distinguishes between kharaj (spent on combatants) and ushr (right of the poor).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Scenario
The scenario: Rebels gain control over Muslim lands and collect kharaj and ushr from the inhabitants. The Imam later recovers the territory. The question: should he recollect those taxes from the payers for the period of rebel rule?
2
State the Hanafi Ruling
The Hanafi ruling: The Imam does **not** recollect the taxes already taken by the rebels. This applies to both kharaj and ushr.
The ruling is from the later mashā'ikh (Fatawa mutakhkhirin), not the Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Identify the Legal Illa (Effective Cause)
The illa: The Imam's authority (wilayah) to collect taxes is based on providing protection (himayah) to the inhabitants. Since he was unable to protect them during the rebel occupation, his claim to that period's tax lapses.
This is a case of 'al-wilayah muqabil al-himayah' – authority is paired with protection.
4
Apply the Illa to Determine Outcomes
If the rebels spent the collected funds on legitimate state purposes (e.g., defense, public works), then the right reached its due recipients, and no repetition is needed. If they misused the funds, the payers are religiously advised to repay directly to the rightful beneficiaries, but again, the Imam does not recollect.
For kharaj, even if the payers are wealthy, they do not have to repeat because kharaj is spent on combatants, and the rebels themselves were combatants. For ushr, if the rebels were poor, the same leniency applies because ushr is a right of the poor.
5
Distinguish Future vs. Past Periods
For future years after the Imam regains control and restores protection, he will collect as normal, because the condition of protection is now fulfilled.

One Partner Cannot Pay Zakat for Another Without Permission
States the restriction on paying zakat from partnership wealth without the partner's consent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a partner may not pay zakat on the other's share without permission and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) based on the scope of partnership agency.

Core Idea
Zakat is worship, not trade – partnership agency does not extend to it.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding zakat in partnerships. You will learn that the inherent agency in partnership is limited to trade-related acts, and because zakat is an act of worship, a partner cannot pay it on behalf of the other without explicit permission. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying zakat obligations in shared wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
The passage begins by stating a restriction: a partner cannot pay zakat from the other partner's wealth. Identify who is the subject (the partner) and what act is prohibited (paying zakat on behalf of the other).
2
Extract the legal reason (ta'lil)
The text gives the reason: 'because it is not of the type of trade' (laysa min jins al-tijarah). This is the ta'lil – the rationale behind the ruling. Understand that zakat is categorized differently from trade transactions.
3
Understand the scope of partnership agency
In Hanafi law, each partner is an agent (wakil) for the other only in matters related to trade. Acts of worship like zakat, sadaqah, or hajj are not included. This step connects the ruling to the general principle of limited agency in shirkah.
4
Identify the exception – permission
The passage ends with 'then if he permits...' (fa in adhina). This indicates that the prohibition is lifted if the other partner gives specific permission. The default is impermissibility, but consent makes it valid.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Consider two partners in a clothing business. One partner decides to pay zakat from the partnership cash on behalf of both. Without the other's permission, this is not valid. The partner must either pay from his own separate wealth or seek explicit permission.

Liability of a Partner Paying Zakat After the Owner
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for duplicate zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a partner's liability when he pays zakat after the owner, and state the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A partner may pay zakat with permission, but if the owner pays first, the later payer's liability depends on knowledge—Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn.

In a partnership, one partner may be authorized to pay zakat for the other. But what happens if both the owner and the partner end up paying? The Hanafi school has an internal difference: Imam Abu Hanifa holds the later payer liable regardless of knowledge, while the two companions (Sahibayn) excuse him if he was unaware. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the core legal question: Is the partner who pays zakat after the owner liable to compensate the owner?
The payment is made with permission, but the owner's prior payment discharges the obligation.
2
Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the later payer is always liable. His proof: the owner's order aims to relieve himself of the obligation; once that is achieved, the agent's payment is contrary to intent and legally dismissed (azl hukmi).
Ignorance does not excuse because the dismissal is automatic.
3
Sahibayn's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the later payer is not liable if he was unaware. Their proof: the agent was ordered to transfer ownership, which he did; what is beyond his capacity (the owner's intention) is not required of him.
They analogize to the dam of ihsar: the agent is not liable if unforeseen conditions change.
4
Hanafi response and rebuttal
Abu Hanifa responds that the purpose is discharging the obligation, which the owner already achieved; the agent's payment becomes wasted. The Sahibayn counter that the agent fulfilled his mandate as far as possible.
The disagreement lies in whether the agent's role is purely mechanical or purpose-driven.
5
Thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
Practical difference: per Abu Hanifa the partner must repay the owner; per Sahibayn he does not if unaware. The preferred view (mufta bihi) often follows Sahibayn for ease, but Abu Hanifa's view is also relied upon in some contexts.
The later Hanafi authorities lean towards the Sahibayn's position.

This is because he is capable of transferring ownership, not of making it count as zakat, as that depends on the intention of the principal; only what is within his capability is required of him. It becomes like the one ordered to slaughter the dam of ihsar (restriction) who slaughters after the restriction has ended and the orderer has performed Hajj — the ordered one is not liable, whether he knew or not. According to Abu Hanifa, he is ordered to pay the zakat, and what was paid did not count as zakat, so he became non-compliant. This is because the purpose of the order is to relieve oneself of the obligation; the apparent [intent] is that he does not undertake harm except to remove harm. This purpose is achieved by his own payment, and the payment of the ordered one is rendered irrelevant, so he is dismissed whether he knew or not, because it is a legal dismissal. As for the dam of ihsar, it is said that it is subject to this difference, and it is said there is a distinction between them: the dam is not obligatory upon him; he can wait until the restriction ends. In our issue, payment is obligatory, so cancellation is considered intended in it, unlike the dam of ihsar.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a partner paying zakat on behalf of his partner without explicit permission. The matn first states that it is not permissible for a partner to pay the other's zakat without permission because it is not part of trade (the scope of partnership). If permission is granted, and both the owner and the partner end up paying (perhaps due to confusion), who is liable? According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the later payer is liable (dhāmin) to compensate the other partner, even if he did not know the first had paid. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if the later payer did not know the first had paid, he is not liable; he acted within his authority to transfer ownership. However, if he knew, then he is liable (some texts say he remains not liable per a reliable view). The same dispute applies when someone appoints an agent to pay zakat and then pays himself; the agent is liable per Abu Hanifa but not per the Sahibayn if he was unaware. The Sahibayn argue that the agent was ordered to transfer ownership, which he did, so he is not liable. Abu Hanifa argues that the purpose is to discharge the obligation; since the owner's payment already achieved that, the agent's payment becomes ineffective, and he is liable for the amount. The passage also compares this to slaughtering the dam of ihsar (a sacrifice due for being prevented from Hajj) — if the orderer performs Hajj after the restriction ends, the agent is not liable per all because the obligation was not yet incumbent. The distinction is that in the zakat case, payment is obligatory (wajib) and the orderer's payment intends to cancel the obligation, so the agent's later payment is wasted.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Liability to compensate the other partner for the amount of zakat paid after the obligation was already fulfilled.
دم الإحصار (dam al-ihsar): A sacrificial animal required due to being prevented from completing Hajj; used here as an analogy.
عزل حكمي (azl hukmi): A legal automatic dismissal of agency due to the principal's prior performance.

Scholar Collecting Charity Without Authorization: Liability and Zakat
Ruling on a scholar collecting and mixing charity for the poor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a scholar collects charity for the poor without authorization and identify the condition for valid zakat fulfillment.

Core Idea
Without authorization, the scholar is liable and zakat remains unfulfilled.

Many assume that a trustworthy scholar collecting charity automatically discharges the donors' obligations. However, Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between a trustee and a guarantor. This passage from Al-Muhit clarifies that without explicit permission from each poor recipient, the scholar becomes a guarantor (damin) and the donors' zakat is invalid. Understanding this protects both the collector and the giver.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the three roles: scholar (collector), donors (givers), and poor (recipients). Determine who holds what liability.
2
Understand the collection scenario
The scholar asks for contributions for the poor, and donors give without specifying individual recipients. The funds become mixed.
Mixing is key—it prevents ownership from reaching specific poor persons.
3
Determine the scholar's status
Because the poor did not authorize the scholar, he is not a trustee (amin) but a guarantor (damin). He must compensate if the funds are lost.
Al-Muhit states: 'that scholar will be liable for all of it.'
4
Assess the effect on zakat
Since ownership did not transfer to the poor, the zakat obligation of the donors remains unfulfilled.
Zakat requires transfer of ownership to a valid recipient.
5
Apply the solution
To avoid this, the poor should give the scholar permission to collect and mix on their behalf. This transforms him into an agent, and zakat becomes valid.
Permission can be given before or after collection but before distribution.

Collecting for a Good Deed Without Permission
Ruling on a collector's liability and zakat validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the initial legal status of a collector without permission and identify the conditions under which he becomes a guarantor.

Core Idea
A collector without permission is a trustee; mixing funds makes him a guarantor for others' shares.

This lesson explains a specific scenario from Al-Mudmarat: a person stands up for a good cause and collects donations for a particular poor individual without that poor person's permission. We will examine the collector's liability, the validity of the donors' zakat, and the recommended precaution to avoid liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its initial status
The act is collecting funds for a specific poor person without that person's permission. Initially, the collector is a trustee (amin) — not liable for loss unless negligent.
Trusteeship is the default when someone undertakes a voluntary good deed.
2
Analyze the effect of mixing funds
If the collector mixes the collected wealth with funds intended for others (or with his own), he becomes liable (damin) for the shares belonging to other recipients. However, the poor person for whom he collected is considered to have received his share directly from the collector.
Mixing changes the legal relationship from trusteeship to guarantee.
3
Check the validity of donors' zakat
Despite the mixing, the zakat of the donors is fulfilled (valid). This is unlike a previous case where the collector was not authorized and zakat was not valid.
The validity of zakat does not depend on the collector's liability status.
4
Identify the recommended solution
To avoid becoming a guarantor, the collector should appoint the poor person as his agent (wakil) to receive the funds. This effectively authorizes the mixing from the poor person's side.
Agency (wakala) is a key tool in Hanafi fiqh to transfer authority and alter liability.

What is the initial status of a person who collects for a poor individual without permission?
A) Guarantor (damin)
B) Trustee (amin)
C) Borrower
D) Owner

Key Takeaway
A collector without permission starts as a trustee, but mixing funds makes him a guarantor for others; appointing the poor as wakil avoids liability.
