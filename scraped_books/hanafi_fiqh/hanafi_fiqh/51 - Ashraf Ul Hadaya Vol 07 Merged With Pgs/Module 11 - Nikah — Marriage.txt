Property of a New Muslim in Dar al-Harb: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on new Muslim's property in dar al-harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the property of a new Muslim in dar al-harb and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of their disagreement.

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, a new Muslim's property in dar al-harb is ghanimah; for the Sahibayn, it remains inviolable.

This passage from Ashraf al-Bidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute. At issue is whether the property of a person who accepted Islam while living in dar al-harb, but whose property has fallen into the hands of another Muslim or dhimmi, is considered spoils of war (ghanimah) or remains the new Muslim's protected property. The difference arises from how each authority weighs the inviolability of the person against the nature of property in dar al-harb. By working through this dispute, you will sharpen your ability to analyse the interplay between personal status and property rights under Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa)
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the usurped property is ghanimah (spoils) because the new Muslim lacks effective possession and his person is not valuable in property terms in dar al-harb.
The key concept is that property by origin is mubah (permissible) and becomes owned through overpowering (ghalbah).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that the property follows the person (al-mal tabi' li al-nafs). Since the new Muslim's person is inviolable due to Islam, his property is also inviolable and cannot be treated as ghanimah.
The Sahibayn's ruling protects the new Muslim's property rights even in dar al-harb.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn's proof: The person of a Muslim is mahram (inviolable) in life and limb; property is an extension of the person. Therefore, if the person is protected, the property is protected, regardless of who holds it in dar al-harb.
This proof relies on the principle of tabi'iyyah (following/subordination).
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Imam Abu Hanifa's counter: Property by its original nature is mubah and becomes owned through overpowering. The new Muslim's person, though inviolable from being killed, does not bestow inviolability on his property in dar al-harb because he lacks effective control. The evil of disbelief that justified taking his property has been removed, but the property remains mubah until he takes possession. Since it is in another's possession, that person gains ownership.
The focus is on possession and the original permissibility of property in dar al-harb.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: According to Abu Hanifa, the Muslim or dhimmi holding the property can keep it as spoils; according to the Sahibayn, it must be returned to the new Muslim. This difference affects the rights of new Muslims and the distribution of property in conquered lands.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the key takeaway for fatwa application.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the property of a new Muslim in dar al-harb when it is in the possession of a Muslim or dhimmi. The text first presents the position of Imam Abu Hanifa, then the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Imam Abu Hanifa rules that such property is considered ghanimah (spoils of war) because the person (nafs) of a new Muslim, while inviolable from being killed, is not valuable (ghayr qayyim) in terms of property rights in dar al-harb. Property, by its nature, is mubah (permissible) and becomes owned through overpowering (ghalbah). Since the new Muslim does not have effective possession (hukman), his property is not protected (mahram); it becomes part of the spoils. The Sahibayn, on the other hand, argue that property follows the person (al-mal tabi' li al-nafs). Since the new Muslim's person is inviolable (mahram) due to his Islam, his property is also inviolable, even if it is in dar al-harb. Therefore, it cannot be treated as ghanimah. The text elaborates on the reasoning: the purpose of jihad is to eliminate the evil of kufr; once the person becomes Muslim, that basis is gone. The authority weight: this is a known ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school. The mufta bihi (relied upon) position is not explicitly stated here, but from other sources, the view of Imam Abu Hanifa is often treated as the basis for fatwa in such matters. The key terms: nafs (person), ghanimah (spoils), dar al-harb (abode of war), mahram (inviolable), mubah (permissible), ghalbah (overpowering).

Key Terms
نو مسلم (na muslim): A person who has recently accepted Islam while in dar al-harb.
مال غنیمت (mal ghanimah): Spoils of war, property taken from combatants by force.
نفس (nafs): The person or self; in this context, the life and inviolability of a person.

Marriage and Dhimmi Status: Harbiya vs. Harbi
Explaining the gender-based asymmetry in dhimmi status via marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a harbiya woman becomes a dhimmiya by marrying a dhimmi while a harbi man does not, and apply the underlying principle of permanent residence.

Core Idea
Marriage makes a harbiya a dhimmiya because she follows her husband; a harbi man retains the option to leave.

This lesson explores a nuanced ruling in Hanafi siyar: how marriage to a dhimmi affects the legal status of non-Muslims who entered Dar al-Islam with aman. The key distinction is not the marriage contract itself, but the likelihood of permanent residence—a rational (dalil 'aqli) consideration. Understanding this principle helps you grasp how the Hanafi school balances intention and circumstance in determining dhimmi status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two cases: a harbiya woman entering with aman and marrying a dhimmi, and a harbi man entering with aman and marrying a dhimmiya.
The rulings are opposite despite similar starting points.
2
State the ruling for each case
For the harbiya: she becomes a dhimmiya. For the harbi: he does not become a dhimmi.
The text uses the word 'because' (li-anna) to introduce the reason.
3
Identify the effective cause (illa)
The illa is the undertaking of permanent residence. The harbiya, by marrying, is expected to follow her husband and thus intends to stay. The harbi can divorce and return, so he has not undertaken residence.
This is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) based on probability.
4
Apply the principle to other cases
Consider analogous situations: if a harbiya marries a harbi who later becomes dhimmi, or if a harbi marries a dhimmiya but is physically unable to leave (e.g., illness).
The principle revolves around the ability and likelihood of leaving Dar al-Islam.

Thus, his marriage does not necessarily indicate an intention to settle permanently. The ruling is based on the principle that the one who can easily depart (the man) is not considered to have undertaken residence, while the one who typically follows (the woman) is.

Status of a Musta'min's Family After Conversion and Conquest
Ruling on a musta'min's family and property after conversion and conquest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the status of a musta'min's family and property after his conversion and the conquest of his original dar, and explain the condition of taba'iyyah requiring same dar and custody.

Core Idea
Separation of dars breaks taba'iyyah — minor children do NOT follow father's conversion when in Dar al-Harb.

What happens to a non-Muslim who enters a Muslim land with safe conduct, converts, and later his home territory is conquered by Muslims? Are his family and property automatically protected? This passage from Al-Hidayah provides a nuanced ruling, distinguishing between wife, adult children, minor children, and property, and introducing the critical factor of the separation of abodes (ikhtilaf al-darayn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
A harbi (non-Muslim from enemy territory) enters Dar al-Islam with aman (safe conduct) and converts to Islam. Later, Muslims conquer his original Dar al-Harb. Determine the legal status of his family (wife, children) and property left behind.
The distinction between adult and minor children is key.
2
Distinguish between Wife/Adult Children vs Minors
Wife and adult children are independent persons; they do not automatically follow father's conversion. They remain harbis and become part of the booty (ghanimah). Minor children, however, could potentially follow the father if certain conditions are met.
The principle of taba'iyyah (following) applies only to minors.
3
Analyze the Condition for Taba'iyyah
A minor child follows the father's Islam only if: (a) the child is under the father's physical custody and authority, and (b) both reside in the same dar (abode). Here, the child is in Dar al-Harb while the father is in Dar al-Islam; thus taba'iyyah is not realized.
This is a condition based on the concept of darayn (two abodes).
4
Apply the Principle to Property
The father's property in Dar al-Harb does not become protected (muharram) simply because he himself is safe in Dar al-Islam. Since the property is in a different dar, it remains subject to the rules of war and becomes booty.
Even if some property was entrusted to Muslims or dhimmis, the location of the property matters.
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may hold that minor children follow the father regardless of dar, giving them Muslim status and protecting them from booty. The Hanafi position is that separation of dar prevents this, leading to a practical difference in the distribution of booty.
Understanding this difference helps in appreciating the role of dar in Hanafi jurisprudence.

The Missing Person's Wife: Hanafi vs. Maliki on Judicial Separation
Hanafi response to Maliki ruling on separation after four years

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the missing person's wife and evaluate the Maliki counter-argument using the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
Certainty is not removed by doubt: a missing person's marriage remains intact until proven otherwise.

When a husband goes missing and his whereabouts are unknown, his wife faces a dilemma. Can she remarry? The Hanafi school maintains that the marriage continues indefinitely until death or divorce is confirmed, while Imam Malik rules that after four years the qadi may separate them. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind both positions, focusing on the Hanafi response to the Maliki evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the core ruling: the qadi does not separate the missing person from his wife. This is based on the principle that marriage is established with certainty and cannot be undone by doubt.
The matn says: 'لا يُفَرَّقُ بَيْنَهُ وَبَيْنَ امْرَأَتِهِ'.
2
Identify the Maliki position and its evidence
Imam Malik says separation occurs after four years, after which the wife observes iddat al-wafat and may remarry. His evidence: practice of 'Umar and analogy to ila' and 'innah.
Malik's proof includes 'Umar's verdict and qiyas on two cases.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal: the primary evidence is the hadith 'She is his wife until evidence comes to her' and 'Ali's saying. 'Umar later returned to 'Ali's opinion. Ila' and 'innah are not analogous because ila' is a direct cause of separation in sharia, and 'innah is a permanent defect, unlike absence which usually ends in return.
The Hanafi response distinguishes each analogy.
4

Under Hanafi law, the wife cannot remarry unless clear evidence of death or divorce appears. Under Maliki, she can remarry after four years plus iddah. This creates a real difference in outcome.
Later Hanafi fatwa allows following Maliki in cases of necessity.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam Malik over the ruling of a missing husband's wife. The text begins by stating the Hanafi position: no separation is made between the missing husband and his wife. It then presents Imam Malik's view: after four years, the judge separates them, the wife observes the waiting period of death, and she may remarry. Imam Malik's evidence includes the practice of Umar (a person taken by jinn), and narrations from Ali and Uthman, even claiming consensus. The Maliki reasoning is that the missing person has prevented the wife's right, so separation is justified, drawing analogy to ila' (vow of abstinence) and 'unna (impotence), taking a four-year period from both. The Hanafi response follows: their proof is a weak hadith that she remains his wife until clarification, and Ali's statement that she must be patient. Marriage is established with certainty (yaqeen), and disappearance does not remove it because death is only probable. The Hanafis argue that analogy to ila' is invalid because ila' involves an oath and conditional suspension, which are absent here. Analogy to 'unna is invalid because an absent person may return, whereas an impotent man after a year has little hope. The text then notes that some later Hanafi scholars allow following Imam Malik in cases of necessity, citing Radd al-Muhtar and Fatawa Bazaziyyah. The passage concludes by reiterating the positions and the Hanafi rebuttal. The commentary should follow the author's order.

Time for Declaring Death of a Missing Person
Hanafi dispute on the period to declare a missing person dead

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi narrations on the period for declaring a missing person dead and apply the mufta bihi (90 years) in a practical scenario.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school blends principle (death of peers) with practicality (90-year fixed term).

This passage delves into an important intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: how long must a person be missing before we can presume them dead? The answers range from 'death of peers' to fixed numbers like 120, 100, or 90 years. Understanding which view is operative (mufta bihi) is crucial for inheritance and iddah rulings. Let's trace the narrations and the tarjih mechanism.

Guided Path
1
State the apparent madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah)
Identify that the authoritative doctrine of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani does not fix a number but relies on the death of the missing person's peers (al-aqran). This is based on the principle of presuming life until evidence of death.
2
Identify the variant narrations
Note three other narrations: Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa (120 years from birth), Abu Yusuf (100 years), and some later scholars (90 years). Recognize each as a valid but less authoritative transmission.
The 90-year view is not from the founding Imams but from later practice.
3
Analyze the basis of each narration
Consider the reasoning: the apparent madhhab is pure qiyas; the fixed numbers are based on transmitted reports (riwayah) or expedience. Evaluate which is 'al-aqbas' (most appropriate by qiyas) and 'al-arfaq' (easiest for people).
Al-aqbas is no fixed number; al-arfaq is 90 years.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Identify that later fatwa preferred 90 years for ease and consistency, making it the operative position. Understand the tarjih mechanism: practical need overrides theoretical purity.
This is the position you will use in practice.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given a missing person born in 1920, compute when he can be declared dead (2010 if 90 years from birth). Determine the wife's iddah from that date.
Ensure you start counting from birth, not from disappearance.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the time limit for declaring a missing person (mafqud) dead. The ruling begins with a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa that after 120 years from birth, a judgment of death is made. However, the apparent madhhab (Zahir al-Riwayah) follows the death of the missing person's peers (aqran) — i.e., when most people of his age have died. Another narration from Abu Yusuf sets 100 years. Some later scholars estimated 90 years as a practical measure. The text notes that the most consistent with qiyas (al-aqbas) is not to fix any number but rely on circumstantial evidence, while the easiest (al-arfaq) is 90 years. The mufta bihi (operative position) is 90 years, as stated in the Urdu commentary. Once judgment is passed, the wife begins her iddah of death (four months and ten days).

ظاهر المذهب (zahir al-madhhab): The apparent or authoritative doctrine of the Hanafi school, transmitted in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
