Son's Participation in Stoning His Father: Intention Requirement
Ruling on son's participation in stoning his father.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a son's participation in stoning his father and explain the role of intention in this act.

Core Idea
A son witnessing his father's stoning must participate but without intending to kill him.

This passage addresses a delicate scenario: a son is a witness to his father's adultery punishable by stoning. While the son must testify and even throw the first stones, Islamic law requires him to do so without the intention of killing his father, to preserve filial piety and avoid sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: a married (muhsan) father commits zina, his son is among the witnesses, and the punishment of stoning (rajm) is prescribed.
The son's role is as a witness, not as a prosecutor.
2
State the general rule for witnesses
In stoning cases, the witnesses are ordered to throw the first stones. This is a prescribed procedure to establish the punishment.
3
Apply the exception for the son
When the witness is the son of the convicted person, he must still throw the stone, but he must not intend to kill his father. The act of throwing is to fulfill the punishment, not to cause death intentionally.
The intention distinguishes permissible participation from sinful aggression.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The ruling prevents the son from committing an act of filial impiety (uquq al-walidayn) and ensures that the punishment is carried out without personal malice.
It balances the duty to uphold Hudud with the duty to respect parents.

Jizya Tadakhul and Timing of Obligation
Explains intra-Hanafi dispute on merging unpaid jizya across years

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the timing of jizya obligation and tadakhul, and explain the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Jizya is wajib at the year's start; if unpaid years accumulate, they merge (tadakhul) only if jizya is a punishment, not compensation.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a critical intra-Hanafi dispute: when does jizya become obligatory for a dhimmi, and if multiple years pass without payment, do the obligations merge into one? The answer hinges on the legal nature of jizya—whether it is a punishment (uqubah) or a compensation (badal). Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani each stake positions, and the text presents their proofs before settling on the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
According to Abu Hanifa, jizya is wajib at the beginning of the year. If two years pass without payment, the two years' jizya merge (tadakhul) into a single liability.
The text calls this the 'asahh' (most correct) view within the school.
2
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi view (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that jizya is due at the end of the year, and no tadakhul occurs; each year's obligation remains separate and collectable.
They consider jizya a compensation (badal) for protection, so multiple compensations can be collected together.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn argue that jizya is a financial exchange: the dhimmi pays for safety (tal) and the state provides support (nasr). Since it is compensation, multiple years' worth can be demanded when feasible.
They also note that if the dhimmi converts to Islam, collection stops—further evidence of a contingent exchange.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response (Abu Hanifa's proof)
Abu Hanifa counters that jizya is a punishment for persistence in kufr. Punishments of the same type merge, as in hudud. Moreover, jizya compensates for future protection, not past; you cannot pay for a service already rendered.
The humiliating method of payment (personally bringing it, being shaken) underscores its punitive nature.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if two years pass, under Abu Hanifa only one year's jizya is collected (tadakhul); under the Sahibayn, both years must be collected separately.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage is primarily an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding tadakhul (merging) of unpaid jizya when two or more years pass without payment. The text opens with the original Arabic from al-Hidayah discussing the interpretation of Imam Muhammad's statement 'the second year came.' Some early mashayikh interpreted it figuratively as the year having passed, while others took it literally as the onset of the second year. The core issue is the timing of jizya obligation. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that jizya becomes wajib at the beginning of the year, so the mere arrival of the second year causes both years' jizya to be due simultaneously, leading to tadakhul (merging). Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that jizya is due at the end of the year, so for tadakhul to occur, the second year must actually pass. The Sahibayn argue that jizya is compensation (badal) for protection (tal and nasr); multiple compensations can be collected when possible. Abu Hanifa argues that jizya is a punishment (uqubah) for persistence in kufr, and multiple punishments of the same type merge, analogously to hudud. The text then supports Abu Hanifa's view by citing that jizya must be paid personally in a humiliating manner, reinforcing its punitive nature. It also contrasts with kharaj, where there is consensus no tadakhul occurs. The passage also includes a comparison with Imam al-Shafi'i's view, who analogizes jizya to zakat and makes it due at year-end. The Hanafi response is that jizya compensates for future protection (support in battle, safety from execution), which cannot be backdated, so obligation must be at the start. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Abu Hanifa: tadakhul occurs, and jizya is wajib at the beginning of the year.

Key Terms
تداخل (tadakhul): Merging of multiple obligations of the same type into a single fulfillment; here, merging of jizya from multiple years into one payment.
جزیہ (jizya): Annual poll tax levied on adult free dhimmis in an Islamic state in exchange for protection and exemption from military service.
عوض (badal): Compensation; in this context, jizya as a financial exchange for safety (tal) and support (nasr).

Building and Rebuilding Churches in Dar al-Islam
States rulings on construction and maintenance of non-Muslim places of worship in Islamic territory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on new construction and rebuilding of non-Muslim places of worship in Dar al-Islam and apply the geographic conditions to determine when rebuilding is permitted.

Core Idea
New churches are prohibited in Dar al-Islam; old ones may be rebuilt but not expanded.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced area of fiqh: the status of non-Muslim places of worship under Islamic rule. The ruling balances the preservation of dhimmi rights to maintain their existing sites with the prohibition of establishing new ones. We'll unpack the proof from the hadith and the geographic distinctions that affect the application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage first states that it is not permissible to build a new church (bi'ah or kanisah) in Dar al-Islam. Determine the precise act: new construction of a place of worship for non-Muslims. The ruling is prohibition (haram).
The act is 'ihdat' (new establishment), not maintenance.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'There is no castration in Islam and no church' (لا خصاء في الإسلام ولا كنيسة). Note that the chain is considered weak (da'if), but the school accepts it supported by other considerations.
The hadith also prohibits castration, but the focus here is on 'no church'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The general negation 'لا كنيسة' is interpreted as prohibiting new establishment of churches. It does not negate the existence of old ones. The school argues that the prohibition is on initiating new ones, not on maintaining those that existed by covenant.
Hanafi usul: general negation (nafi 'amm) indicates prohibition of the genus, but here it is specified to new construction by context.
4

Core Idea
Open, habitual reviling of the Prophet nullifies the dhimmah covenant, making execution wajib.

This lesson examines the legal ruling concerning a dhimmi—a non-Muslim citizen of the Islamic state—who reviles the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The passage draws from later Hanafi fatwa works and historical precedents to establish that while the dhimmi's covenant generally protects his life, persistent public blasphemy removes that protection. Commentary
This is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the three-day imprisonment of an apostate. The Hanafi position, based on al-Quduri, states that imprisonment for three days is the practice, but the respite is not obligatory. According to one interpretation, it is only given if the apostate asks for it. The stronger Hanafi view (from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is that granting three days is mustahabb (recommended) regardless of request. Imam al-Shafi'i, however, holds it is wajib (obligatory) on the imam to grant three days, arguing that apostasy arises from doubt and reflection needs time. Killing an Apostate Before Presenting Islam: Ruling and Liability
Ruling statement with talīl: ruling and rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing an apostate before presenting Islam and explain why there is no qisas or diya liability.

Core Idea
Killing an apostate without presenting Islam is makruh tanzihi but incurs no liability because kufr permits killing.

The Hanafi school rules that an apostate's blood is permissible due to kufr, yet the recommended procedure before execution is to present Islam. What if someone kills the apostate without this presentation? This lesson unpacks the ruling of 'makruh tanzihi' and the complete absence of financial or penal liability, grounded in the principle that kufr itself renders the life lawful.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is killing an apostate before presenting Islam to him. The ruling is 'makruh' (disliked). Determine whether this is makruh tahrīmi or tanzīhi based on the text.

2
Understand the Absence of Liability
The text states 'there is nothing upon the killer' (lā shay'a). This means no qisas, no diya, and no kaffarah. Identify why: because the apostate's kufr makes his blood lawful (halāl al-dam).
Liability (ḍamān) only arises from unlawful killing; here the killing is lawful in essence.

The text provides two rationales: (1) Kufr permits killing (al-kufru muḥillun lil-qatl). (2) Presenting Islam after the da'wa has reached is not obligatory (ghayru wājib). Understand how the non-obligatory nature of presentation means its omission does not create liability.

The Hanafi view is muftā bihī in the school.

Effect of Apostasy on Property Ownership (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on apostasy's effect on property ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of apostasy on ownership of property and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Apostasy does not immediately terminate ownership; it is suspended until the apostate's fate is known.

This lesson examines a disagreement within the Hanafi school between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), over the legal consequence of apostasy on property ownership. The core question is whether a person who leaves Islam loses ownership of his property immediately, or whether ownership persists until death or execution. Understanding this dispute reveals the sophistication of Hanafi legal reasoning, particularly the use of analogy and the concept of suspended rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling: apostasy removes ownership, but this removal is suspended and conditional. If the apostate returns to Islam, ownership reverts; if he dies or joins dar al-harb, it becomes permanent.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
State the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani: apostasy does not affect ownership at all; the apostate retains full ownership until he is killed.
They base this on the apostate being a legally responsible person in need of his property.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
Examine their analogy: the apostate is like a person sentenced to stoning or retaliation—both are mukallaf and need their property until death, so ownership remains.
This is a qiyas to a criminal under capital punishment.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response
Understand Abu Hanifa's counter-analogy: the apostate is like a harbi (enemy combatant) under Muslim control, whose property is forfeited. Since the apostate's death is for harb (warfare), he is legally treated as an enemy.
The key difference is the cause of death: apostasy linked to harb, not ordinary crime.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference: if the apostate repents, Abu Hanifa says ownership reverts; Sahibayn say it never left. If he dies a murtad, Abu Hanifa says property is removed; Sahibayn say it passes to heirs.
This difference matters for inheritance and distribution of property.

The Hanafi mufta bihi position is the view of Abu Hanifa as recorded in zahir al-riwayah.

Key Terms
المرتد (al-murtadd): A male apostate from Islam.
الملك (al-milk): Ownership of property.
حربی (harbi): An enemy combatant from dar al-harb with whom Muslims are in a state of war.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, what happens to the ownership of an apostate's property when he apostatizes?
A) It is immediately and permanently taken away.
B) It remains unchanged until he is killed.
C) It is temporarily removed; if he returns to Islam it reverts.
D) It is transferred to his heirs.

Key Takeaway
Apostasy temporarily suspends ownership; it reverts upon repentance or is permanently removed upon death or joining dar al-harb.
