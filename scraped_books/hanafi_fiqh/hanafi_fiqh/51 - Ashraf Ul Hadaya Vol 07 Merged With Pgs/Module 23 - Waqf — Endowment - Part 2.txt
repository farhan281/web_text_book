The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ruined Mosques: Abu Yusuf vs Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ruined mosque and its items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on a ruined mosque, identify the mufta bihi, and apply the ruling to related items like mats and grass.

Core Idea
A ruined mosque remains a mosque forever per Abu Yusuf; Muhammad says it reverts to the owner if the purpose ceases.

When a mosque becomes ruined and its surrounding area is deserted, a split emerges between the two great Hanafi imams. This lesson walks through each position, the evidence each uses, and which view is relied upon for fatwa. We also extend these principles to the mosque's physical items: mats, grass, and straw.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the two positions
The passage presents: 'if the area around the mosque becomes ruined and it is no longer needed, it remains a mosque according to Abu Yusuf; according to Muhammad, it reverts to the builder or his heir.' Identify both positions clearly.
2
Analyze Abu Yusuf's proof
Abu Yusuf argues that the waqif's relinquishment (isqat) is absolute and cannot be reversed. Therefore, the mosque remains a mosque permanently, even if it falls into disuse.
He treats isqat as a complete cutting off of ownership.
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues that the mosque was designated for a specific devotion (prayer). When that devotion can no longer be performed (due to ruin and disuse), the purpose ceases, so the property reverts to the original owner or his heirs, analogous to the rule for mats and grass when no longer needed.
He uses qiyas: mats become useless → removed; similarly mosque becomes useless → reverts.
4
Note the fatwa and its application to mats/grass
The passage states that the fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view for both the mosque and for its mats/grass (transfer to another mosque). It also discusses disposal of worthless items: broken mats need judge's permission; worthless straw can be discarded.
The fatwa is 'wa 'alayhi al-fatwa' for Abu Yusuf on mosque and mats.
5
Distinguish between mosque and movable items
Note that even according to Abu Yusuf, the mosque's movable items (mats, grass) can be transferred to another mosque, but the land itself remains a mosque. The later commentary adds rules for selling worthless items with judge's permission.
This shows the practical implementation of the principle.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute followed by commentary. The author begins with the ruling from the matn: one who designates his land as a mosque cannot revoke it, sell it, or have it inherited. All Hanafis agree on this because the mosque is removed from human rights and becomes purely for Allah. An analogy with manumission is made: just as a freed slave returns to Allah's ownership and the master's authority ends, similarly a mosque becomes Allah's property and the waqif's authority ends. Then a dispute arises when the mosque area becomes ruined and no longer needed. Abu Yusuf holds it remains a mosque permanently because the waqif relinquished his ownership absolutely, so it cannot revert. Imam Muhammad holds it reverts to the builder or his heirs because the purpose (prayer) has ended, drawing an analogy with the mosque's mat or grass when no longer needed. However, Abu Yusuf also states that mats and grass should be transferred to another mosque, not to the owner. The commentary then notes that the fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view for mosques, and for mats/grass also transfer to another mosque. It further discusses the disposal of useless mosque items: broken mats cannot be given away or sold without judge's permission; worthless straw can be discarded and anyone can pick it up; if it has value, it must be sold with judge's permission.

Key Terms
اسقاط (isqat): Relinquishment of ownership; here it is absolute and irreversible.
قربة (qurba): Devotional act; here the mosque was designated for prayer.

Conditions for Irrevocable Waqf of Public Utilities
Ruling on when endowments for public utilities become irrevocable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a waqf of a public utility (sabil, inn, ribat, cemetery) to become irrevocable according to Imam Abu Hanifa, and explain the rational proof and exception for mosques.

Core Idea
A public utility waqf is not irrevocable until a judge rules or it is attached to death, due to the donor's retained benefit.

This passage from Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a critical distinction in Hanafi waqf law: when does a charitable endowment become permanently binding? The answer hinges on whether the donor retains any right to benefit from the property. Let's examine the ruling, its proof, and the unique case of mosques.

Guided Path
1
Identify the properties
List the four types of public utility waqf mentioned: sabil (watering place), inn (al-han) for travelers, ribat (border outpost), and cemetery (maqbara).
These are all for general public benefit.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the donor's ownership does not cease until a judge (hakim) issues a ruling on the waqf, or the donor attaches it to after his death (e.g., in a will).
Without one of these, the property remains in the donor's ownership.
3
Understand the proof (dalil aqli)
The donor retains the right to benefit from the property: he can stay in the inn, drink from the sabil, lodge in the ribat, and bury in the cemetery. Therefore, his ownership right is not fully severed, so the waqf is incomplete.
This is a rational proof based on the continuity of the owner's right.
4
Contrast with the mosque
A mosque is different: the donor cannot validly benefit from it in a way that preserves ownership (e.g., he cannot reserve a private prayer space). Thus, no judge's ruling is needed; the waqf becomes binding immediately upon declaration.
The mosque is an exception to the general rule.
5
Derive the general principle
The completeness of a waqf depends on the complete severance of the donor's right to benefit. If any right remains, a judge's ruling or death attachment is required.
This principle applies to all waqf except mosques.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Quduri (matn) explained by the commentator. It covers endowments for public utilities: sabil (water source), inn (for travelers), ribat (border fortress), and cemetery. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, such endowments do not automatically remove the property from the owner's possession; the owner can still benefit from them (e.g., stay in the inn, drink from the sabil). Therefore, the endowment is not complete until either a judge (hakim) issues a ruling that it is a valid waqf, or the owner attaches it to after his death (i.e., makes it effective upon his death). This is similar to waqf for the poor. The proof is that the owner retains the right to benefit, so his ownership is not fully severed. In contrast, a mosque requires no judge's ruling because the owner cannot benefit from it (he cannot pray in a way that excludes others or claim private ownership); thus it becomes exclusively for Allah immediately upon verbal declaration. The commentator emphasizes that the mosque's ruling is opposite to other endowments.

Key Terms
سقاية (siqaya): A public water source for Muslims.
رباط (ribat): A border outpost for defenders of the realm.
الحان (al-han): An inn or caravanserai for travelers.
مقبرة (maqbara): A cemetery, burial ground.
حكم الحاكم (hukm al-hakim): The ruling of a judge, which validates the waqf.

Disposal of Worn-Out Mosque Mats: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on disposal of obsolescent waqf items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disposal of worn-out mosque mats and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
Worn-out mosque mats remain waqf property and must be transferred to another mosque, not returned to the owner or sold freely.

This passage addresses a practical issue in waqf management: what to do with mosque mats that have become too worn for prayer use. The author presents an internal Hanafi debate between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, and clarifies the relied-upon position. Understanding this ruling illustrates the broader Hanafi principle that waqf property cannot revert to private ownership once designated for a charitable purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the legal question about mats that have served their purpose? The passage considers whether they revert to the original owner, are transferred to another mosque, or may be freely disposed of.
2
State the conflicting views
Note the views: The author's initial statement suggests reversion to heirs; Imam Abu Yusuf says transfer to another mosque; Qadi Khan reports Imam Muhammad agreeing with transfer. Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
Imam Abu Yusuf's view is the mufta bihi.
3
Identify the preferred ruling
The text explicitly says 'fatwa is upon Abu Yusuf's saying' for the mosque case, and extends it to mats. Also, disposal without qadi's permission is not allowed.
The rule: transfer to another mosque with qadi's permission.
4
Recognize exceptions
For straw bedding with no value, it may be discarded and anyone may take it. If it has value, qadi's permission is needed to sell.
Value determines the ruling.
5
Apply the principle
Understand that the ruling stems from the nature of waqf: property dedicated to Allah cannot revert to private ownership but must be used for similar benefit.

Commentary
This passage discusses the legal ruling regarding items endowed for a specific purpose that becomes obsolete, focusing on mosque mats and bedding. The author explains that if a mat was placed for prayer and the prayer purpose ends (e.g., the person dies), it does not revert to the owner's heirs but rather is treated like a mosque mat. For mosque mats that become worn out, Imam Abu Yusuf holds that they must be transferred to another mosque, and this is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The mat or its owner's heirs cannot dispose of it except with the qadi's permission. Qadi Khan attributes this view to Imam Muhammad, contrary to the author's statement. For straw bedding that has no remaining value, it can be discarded and anyone may take it. If it has value, the mosque administration should sell it with qadi's permission. The passage also cites al-Muhit and al-Jawahir as sources.

Key Terms
چٹائی (chattai): a mat used for prayer in a mosque
قاضی (qadi): the judge whose permission is required for disposing of waqf property

Endowing Non-Mosque Properties: Ownership and Conditions
States the ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on non-mosque endowments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on when ownership ceases in non-mosque endowments and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
For non-mosque endowments, Abu Hanifa requires a judge's decree; Abu Yusuf suffices by mere statement; Muhammad requires actual use.

In waqf, not all endowments become effective immediately upon declaration. When the endower retains the right to personally benefit from the property—as with a public drinking fountain (sabil), inn (khan), garrison (ribat), or cemetery (maqbara)—the Hanafi imams differ on when the endowment becomes binding and ownership ceases. This lesson unpacks the three intra-Hanafi positions and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four properties
Recognize that sabil, khan, ribat, and maqbara are distinct from mosques because the endower retains a right to benefit.
These are categorized as 'non-mosque' endowments in Hanafi fiqh.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa holds that ownership does not cease until a judge decrees or the endowment is attributed to after death. The reason (illa) is that the owner still has the right to benefit (e.g., drink from the sabil, stay in the khan).
This is analogous to waqf for the poor.
3
Contrast with the mosque
A mosque immediately becomes waqf because the endower loses all personal benefit; no judge's decree is needed.
Mosque waqf is unique in this respect.
4
Present Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf says ownership ceases by the mere verbal declaration (qawl), as delivery (taslim) is not a condition in his view.
This is his general principle for waqf.
5
Present Muhammad's position
Muhammad requires actual use by the beneficiaries—people drinking from the sabil, dwelling in the khan, etc.—to constitute delivery, which is a condition.
One instance of use suffices.
6
Determine the mufta bihi
The text indicates that fatwa is upon the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), so ownership ceases by statement or use.
Abu Hanifa's view is considered more precautionary but not followed in fatwa.

The proof is that the servant's right has not ended; we see that the owner himself has the right to dwell in that inn, stay in that fortress, drink from that sabil, and bury his own people in that cemetery. The condition is that the judge decrees or he attributes it to after his death, as in the case of endowment for the poor. Unlike a mosque, where there is no need for a judge's decree because the right to benefit is not reserved for him; thus it leaves his ownership and becomes purely for Allah without a judge's decree. (It should be known that the ruling of mosque endowment is opposite to other endowments. Thus, regarding a mosque, according to Imam Muhammad, delivery to a trustee is not a condition; and according to Abu Yusuf, being shared and undivided is also not a prevention. The mosque leaves the ownership of the endower even if no judge decrees, as mentioned explicitly in al-Durr etc. (Radd al-Muhtar). So for other endowments besides the mosque, based on Imam Abu Hanifa's saying, attributing the endowment to after death or a judge's decree is a condition for the ownership to cease.) (Imam Abu Yusuf's view) And according to Abu Yusuf, his ownership ceases by the mere statement, as is his principle, because delivery is not a condition in his view, and the endowment becomes binding. And according to Muhammad, when people drink from the sabil, dwell in the inn and fortress, and bury in the cemetery, ownership ceases, because delivery is a condition in his view, and the condition is delivery of its type, and that occurs by what we have mentioned. And one is sufficient because it is impossible to perform the whole genus. And likewise for the endowed well and tank. And if he delivers to the trustee, delivery is valid in all these cases because the trustee is a deputy for the beneficiaries, and the deputy's action is like the action of the principal. As for the mosque, it has been said that it is not delivery because the trustee has no control over it; and it has been said that it is delivery because it needs someone to sweep it and close its door; so when he delivers to him, delivery is valid. The cemetery in this regard is like the mosque according to some, because customarily there is no trustee for it; and according to some it is like the sabil and inn, so delivery to the trustee is valid because if he appoints a trustee it is valid, even if contrary to custom.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling for endowing four types of non-mosque properties: sabil (water fountain), khan (inn), ribat (fortress), and maqbara (cemetery). It begins by quoting the matn of Al-Quduri, stating that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the ownership of the endower does not cease by mere declaration; rather, a judge's decree or attributing the endowment to after one's death is required. The reason is that the owner still retains the right to benefit personally from these properties—he can drink from the sabil, stay in the inn, etc. This contrasts with a mosque, where the owner loses all personal benefit, so it becomes a dedicated waqf immediately. The commentary then notes that for mosque endowments, even delivery to a trustee is not required according to Imam Muhammad, and undivided share is not a prevention according to Abu Yusuf. The passage then presents the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership ceases by the mere statement (qawl) alone, as delivery is not a condition in his school. Muhammad holds that ownership ceases only after people actually use the property—drinking, dwelling, burying—which constitutes the required delivery (taslim). One such use suffices because completing all uses is impossible. The same applies to endowed wells and tanks. If the endower delivers to a trustee, it is valid in all cases because the trustee represents the beneficiaries. For a mosque, there is a difference whether delivery to the trustee is valid due to the trustee's lack of control; some say it is not valid, others say it is because the trustee can sweep and lock the door. The cemetery is similar to a mosque in some views (no trustee customarily) and to the sabil in others (delivery to trustee valid).

Key Terms
سقاية (siqaya): a place for drinking water, often a public fountain
حان (khan): an inn or caravanserai for travelers
رباط (ribat): a fortress or frontier post for guards
مقبرة (maqbara): a cemetery or burial ground
تسليم (taslim): delivery or handing over of the endowed property to the beneficiaries or trustee

Taslim in Waqf: The Difference Between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Explains role of taslim in waqf validity and views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding taslim in waqf and apply the ruling to various types of waqf properties.

Core Idea
Taslim is a condition for waqf according to Muhammad, but Abu Yusuf says declaration alone suffices — the view of the Sahibayn is relied upon.

In the Hanafi school, there is an internal difference on whether physical handover (taslim) is required for a waqf to become binding and remove ownership. This lesson examines the views of the two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and how they apply to different endowed properties such as watering places, inns, mosques, and graveyards.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Determine what exactly is contested: is taslim (handover) a condition for the removal of ownership and binding nature of waqf?
Abu Yusuf says no; Muhammad says yes, but with a flexible interpretation.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and proof
Abu Yusuf holds that mere declaration (qawl) removes ownership and makes waqf binding; taslim is not a condition. His principle is that waqf is binding by qawl alone.
This is based on the general principle that waqf is a form of ownership transfer that can occur by speech.
3
State Muhammad's position and proof
Muhammad holds that taslim is a condition, but it can be fulfilled by actual use of the waqf property by even one person. He argues that performing the entire type of benefit is impossible, so a single instance suffices.
For example, if one person drinks from a watering place, that constitutes taslim for the entire watering place.
4
Examine the practical application to different waqfs
The text discusses how the dispute applies to saqaya, khan, ribat, well, basin, mosque, and graveyard. Note the special cases of mosque and graveyard where custom and appointment of a trustee are considered.
For a mosque, there is a difference: some say handover to a trustee is not valid because the trustee has no authority; others say it is valid for maintenance purposes.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical consequence: under Abu Yusuf, waqf is binding immediately; under Muhammad, it binds only after actual use or handover to a trustee. The relied-upon position in later fatwa (Mabsut etc.) is the view of the Sahibayn, and there is consensus on that.
Thus, in practice, both views are considered valid, and fatwa may follow either depending on circumstances.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school on the condition of taslim (physical handover) for the validity of waqf. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that mere declaration (qawl) removes ownership and makes waqf binding; taslim is not a condition. Imam Muhammad holds that taslim is a condition, but it is fulfilled when people actually use the endowed property in its intended way (e.g., drinking from a saqaya, staying in a khan). One person's use suffices for the entire class. If the endower hands over to a trustee (mutawalli), it is valid because the trustee acts on behalf of all beneficiaries. For a mosque, there is a difference: some say taslim to a trustee does not suffice because the trustee has no authority in a mosque; others say it does because the mosque needs maintenance. The graveyard is similar: some treat it like a mosque (no trustee by custom), others like a saqaya where a trustee can be appointed. The relied-upon position in later fatwa is that of Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, as mentioned in Mughribat and Mabsut, and there is consensus on that.

المتولي (al-mutawallī): The trustee or administrator appointed by the endower to manage the waqf property.
الوقف (al-waqf): The endowment of property for charitable purposes, removing it from ownership.

Endowment for Pilgrims, Poor, and Warriors: The Role of Custom
States validity of specific endowments and differentiates beneficiaries based on urf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on endowments for specific groups and explain how custom (urf) differentiates between rich and poor in benefit distribution.

Core Idea
In waqf, custom reveals intent: produce goes only to the poor, but shelter and water are for all.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a key nuance in waqf: while an endowment is valid and irrevocable once handed over, the distribution of its benefits depends on the nature of the benefit and the endower's custom. The text distinguishes between consumable produce (like crops) and services (like housing or water), showing that urf determines whether rich and poor are treated equally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the endowments
List the four endowments mentioned: a house in Mecca for pilgrims/Umrah performers, a house elsewhere for the poor, a house at a frontier for warriors, and the produce of land for mujahideen.
All are specific beneficiaries allowed in waqf.
2
Note the ruling and exception
All are valid (ja'iz) and irrevocable after delivery. The exception: produce is only for the poor, not the rich.
The exception is the core distinction.
3
Examine the justification
The author cites two proofs: custom (urf) and need (haja). Custom shows the endower's intent; need explains why rich don't require produce but need shelter and water.
Urf is the primary proof.
4
Apply the reasoning
Understand that urf differentiates because people customarily intend produce for the needy, while basic needs like shelter and water are universal. This determines the ruling for similar endowments today.
The principle extends to modern endowments like scholarships or public facilities.
5
Evaluate the strength of proof
Urf is zanni in both transmission (thubut) and meaning (dalala). Yet it is sufficient here because it clarifies the endower's condition, which is binding in waqf.
This shows Hanafi reliance on custom in muamalat.

The ruling is consistent with the Hanafi principle that waqf operates according to the endower's conditions, and custom clarifies ambiguous conditions.

Tree in Mosque and Waqf of a Tree
States brief ruling on attached tree and waqf of tree for benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling regarding a tree planted in a mosque and explain the validity of waqf of a tree for its usufruct.

Core Idea
A tree becomes part of the mosque if planted there; waqf of a tree for its benefit is valid.

This lesson covers two concise but important rulings from Hanafi fiqh: the status of a tree planted inside a mosque, and the permissibility of endowing a standalone tree. Understanding these rulings clarifies that waqf is not limited to land or buildings—trees can be independent endowments. The passage also illustrates how property rights transfer when actions are taken on waqf land.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Read the Arabic/Urdu passage and note that it presents two separate rulings: (1) a tree planted in a mosque becomes part of the mosque, and (2) endowing a tree for its leaves, fruits, or trunk is a valid waqf.
The passage is a direct ruling statement without proof citation.
2
Understand the rationale for the mosque tree
The tree planted in mosque land becomes part of the mosque because the land is waqf; anything permanently attached to it merges into the endowment. The planter's intention does not override the nature of waqf property.
This prevents individuals from claiming ownership of improvements on waqf land.
3
Recognize the conditions for waqf of a tree
The passage states the waqf is valid if the intention is to benefit from the tree's usufruct (leaves, fruits, trunk). This implies the tree must be owned, capable of providing recurring benefit, and the waqif must have legal capacity.
The trunk (wood) can be used after the tree dies, so it is a form of durable usufruct.
4
Apply to new scenarios
Consider cases like planting a tree in a mosque courtyard vs. a tree donated but kept outside. Use the principle that attachment to waqf land transfers ownership; a standalone tree requires specific endowment intention.
The key distinction is whether the tree is physically on waqf land or not.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Custom ('urf) determines who benefits from a waqf when the terms are general.

The Hanafi school holds that a waqf is valid and irrevocable once handed over to a mutawalli. But when the beneficiary description is generic—such as 'for pilgrims' or 'for the poor'—custom clarifies the intended scope. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah illustrates how custom distinguishes between benefiting the poor exclusively (for produce) and benefiting all (for lodging and water), based on common usage and need.

The text sets apart produce (ghallah) from other usufructs (sukna, drinking water, etc.). For produce, only the poor may benefit; for others, rich and poor are equal. Identify this key distinction.
Produce is seen as charity for the needy; other usufructs are for general convenience.

Apply the Custom Principle
Understand that the differentiating factor is custom ('urf) and need. People of custom intend produce for the poor, but intend equality for lodging and water because both rich and poor need these amenities. Apply this reasoning to hypothetical waqf scenarios with general terms.

Digging a Well in a Mosque
Ruling on a beneficial alteration to mosque waqf property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on digging a well in a mosque and identify the condition that makes it permissible.

Core Idea
A beneficial alteration to a mosque is permissible if it does not harm the mosque's structure or function.

This short ruling addresses a practical question: can a mosque be modified for public benefit? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer based on the principle of public interest (maslahah) and the preservation of waqf property. Understanding this ruling helps us apply similar principles to modern situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is digging a well inside a mosque. The ruling is ja'iz (permissible), not obligatory or disliked.
Ja'iz is the same as mubah.
2
Identify the condition for permissibility
The condition is that the well must benefit the people (e.g., for wudu or drinking) and must not harm the mosque, meaning no damage to its structure or disruption of its use.
If harm occurs, the ruling changes to impermissible.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The Hanafi school allows modifications to mosque endowment property if they serve a legitimate need (maslahah) and do not undermine the original purpose of the mosque.
This aligns with the waqf rules: changes that preserve or enhance the benefit are allowed.
4
Note the scope of the ruling
The ruling applies to any similar beneficial change, such as installing water coolers, fans, or restrooms, provided they give benefit and cause no harm.
If the change is purely for personal benefit or causes harm, it is not permissible.

This is an application of the principle that the public interest (maslahah) can justify modifications to endowment property as long as the original purpose is preserved.
