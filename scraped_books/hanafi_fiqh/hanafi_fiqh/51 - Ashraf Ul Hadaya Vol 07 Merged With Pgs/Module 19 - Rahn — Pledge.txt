Who Bears the Ju'l for a Fugitive Mortgaged Slave?
Determining who bears the cost of returning a fugitive mortgaged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who bears the ju'l for a fugitive mortgaged slave and explain the istihsanic reasoning using analogy to medicine and ransom.

Core Idea
The mortgagee bears the cost of recovering a fugitive mortgaged slave because it preserves his collateral.

This ruling from Kitab al-Ibaq addresses a practical question: when a mortgaged slave flees and a third party recovers him for a reward, who must pay that reward? The Hanafi school, using istihsan (juristic preference), places the primary obligation on the mortgagee (murtahin), with adjustments based on the slave's value relative to the debt. Understanding this ruling helps illuminate the school's approach to preserving collateral and allocating expenses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
Confirm the roles: rahin (mortgagor) gives the slave as collateral to murtahin (mortgagee) for a debt. The slave flees, and a third party recovers him for a reward (ju'l). The ruling determines who must pay that reward.
2
Determine the default ruling
The text states: 'the reward is upon the mortgagee' (فَالْجُعْلُ عَلَى الْمُرْتَهِنِ). This is the base ruling: the mortgagee bears the full ju'l when the slave's value is equal to or less than the debt.
3
Apply the condition of value exceeding debt
If the slave's value exceeds the debt, the mortgagee only pays a portion proportional to the debt, and the mortgagor pays the remaining portion. The text says: 'if it is more, then the reward is proportionate to the debt upon him'.
Proportionality: mortgagee's share = (debt amount / slave's value) * total ju'l.
4
Analyze the istihsanic analogy
The ruling is based on istihsan: the ju'l is likened to the cost of medicine for a sick slave or ransom to free him from liability. These are necessary expenses to preserve the collateral, and since the mortgagee benefits from the collateral's preservation, he must bear the cost.
The text states: 'So it becomes like the price of medicine and the ransom to free the fugitive from liability'.
5
Note the duration of obligation
The obligation holds whether the mortgagor is alive or dead, because the mortgage contract does not become void upon death.

And if [the slave] is mortgaged, the reward is upon the mortgagee because in it is an enlivening of his wealth, so it is upon him. And the return during the life of the mortgagor and after it is the same, because the mortgage is not invalidated by death. And this is when its value is like the debt or less; if it is more, then the reward is proportionate to the debt upon him, and the remainder is upon the mortgagor, because his right is only to the extent of the guaranteed amount. Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Kitab al-Ibaq on who bears the ju'l (reward/expense) for returning a fugitive slave that is currently mortgaged as collateral for a debt. The Hanafi position, as stated in the text, is that the mortgagee (murtahin) is responsible for the full reward if the slave's value equals or is less than the debt. If the slave's value exceeds the debt, the mortgagee pays only a portion proportionate to the debt, and the mortgagor (rahin) pays the remainder. The reasoning is based on istihsan (juristic preference) by analogy to the costs of medicine and ransom: just as the mortgagee bears expenses to preserve the collateral (since he benefits from its security), he must bear the cost of recovering it. The reward is treated as an expense of preservation. The text also clarifies that the ruling applies whether the mortgagor is alive or dead, because the mortgage contract continues after death. The subsequent tashrih (explanation) provides a concrete example: Hamad (rahin) mortgages his slave Zahid to Khalid (murtahin) for three thousand rupees. The slave flees, and a third person Sajid retrieves him at a cost of three hundred rupees — this cost is the ju'l. Since the slave's value likely equals or is less than the debt (not specified, but assumed), the ju'l is on Khalid (murtahin).

Key Terms
رَهْن (rahn): A contract where property is held as collateral for a debt; the mortgagor (rahin) gives possession to the mortgagee (murtahin).
جُعْل (ju'l): The reward or expense paid to a person who returns a fugitive slave or performs a service; here it is the cost incurred to recover the slave.
مُرْتَهِن (murtahin): The creditor who holds the mortgaged property as security; the mortgagee.
إِحْيَاء الْمَالِيَّة (ihya' al-maliyyah): Enlivening or reviving the financial value of the property; the act of recovering the fugitive slave restores its value to the creditor.
