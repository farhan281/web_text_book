Ju'l for a Runaway Slave Belonging to a Minor
States ruling on ju'l when the slave belongs to a minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on ju'l for a runaway slave of a minor and identify the condition that disqualifies the guardian from receiving the reward.

Core Idea
A reward for retrieving a minor's runaway slave is charged to the minor's estate, but not to the guardian who is duty-bound to retrieve it.

This lesson examines a specific fiqh ruling from the Hanafi school regarding the reward (ju'l) offered for the return of a runaway slave belonging to a minor child (sabi). The key question is: who bears the cost and who is entitled to the reward? The text provides a clear answer based on the principle of mawnat al-milk (expenses of ownership).

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its ruling
The act is offering a reward (ju'l) for the return of a runaway slave. The ruling pertains to the validity of charging the reward to the minor's estate.
2
Determine the property status of the minor
In Hanafi law, a minor (sabi) owns property but lacks legal capacity; a guardian (wasi) manages it. The reward is an expense related to that property.
Mawnat al-milk: costs arising from ownership, such as maintenance and retrieval.
3
Apply the principle to the reward
Since the reward is an expense of ownership, it is a valid charge against the minor's wealth, similar to feeding or clothing the slave.
4
Note the exception for the guardian
If the guardian personally retrieves the slave, he is not entitled to the reward because it is his fiduciary duty (dhimmah) to care for the minor's property.
The guardian's duty supersedes any claim to reward.

Guardianship for the Missing Person: Appointment and Powers
Ruling on property guardianship for missing persons and guardian's authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a judge must appoint a guardian for a missing person's property and distinguish which actions the guardian may and may not perform.

Core Idea
The missing person's guardian is a preserver, not a representative – he can collect but cannot litigate pre-existing claims.

What happens to a person's property when they vanish without a trace? The Hanafi school addresses this through qiyas (analogy) to those who lack capacity, like children or the insane. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the precise limits on the guardian's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the appointment of a guardian (hafiz) for the missing person's property by the judge. The hukm is wajib (obligatory) because the judge must care for those unable to manage their own affairs.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to a child or an insane person. The illa (effective cause) is the inability to manage one's own affairs.
3
List the guardian's permissible actions
The guardian can collect revenues (ghallat) and acknowledged debts. He can litigate for debts he himself incurred as guardian.
These actions are considered preservation (hifz).
4
List the guardian's prohibited actions
He cannot litigate for debts the missing person incurred before disappearance, nor claim property in another's possession. He is not a deputy but merely an agent for collection by the judge.
5
Understand the judge's role for spoilage
If property is at risk of spoiling, the judge himself sells it; the guardian does not have independent sale authority.
Preserving the form is secondary to preserving the value.

Book of the Missing Person. When a man disappears and his location is not known nor is it known whether he is alive or dead, the judge appoints someone to preserve his property, manage it, and collect his rights, because the judge is appointed as overseer for everyone unable to manage for themselves, and the missing person is of this description; he becomes like a child and an insane person. In appointing a preserver for his property and manager over it, there is consideration for his benefit. And his saying 'collects his rights' — it is no secret that he collects his revenues and the debt that a debtor among his debtors acknowledged, because that is from the category of preservation. And he litigates concerning a debt that became obligatory by his (the guardian's) contract, because he is the original party in his rights. And he does not litigate concerning that which the missing person himself undertook, nor concerning a share for him in real estate or goods in the possession of another person, because he is neither the owner nor his deputy; rather he is merely an agent for collection from the direction of the judge. And he does not possess the right to litigation without disagreement; the disagreement is only concerning the agent for collection from the direction of the owner in the matter of debt. And when that is the case, it entails a ruling against the absent person, and it is not permissible except by the management of the judge and his judgment, because it is a matter of ijtihad. Then whatever is feared will spoil, the judge sells it, because preserving its form is difficult, so he looks after preservation of its meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling and explanation from the Book of the Missing Person (Kitab al-Mafqud). It establishes the legal position of a person who has disappeared such that neither his location nor his life/death status is known. The ruling states that the judge (qadi) must appoint a guardian (hafiz / qa'im) to protect the missing person's property, collect his revenues and debts, and generally manage his affairs. The proof for this is qiyas (analogy) to a child or an insane person, who also cannot manage their own affairs and thus have a guardian appointed by the judge. The guardian is specifically appointed to preserve the property; any actions that preserve the property are permissible. The guardian can collect rents (ghallat) and debts that the missing person's debtors have acknowledged (i.e., debts that are already established). He can also litigate (mukhasama) for debts that arise from his own contracts as guardian, because in such debts he is the original party (asil). However, he cannot litigate regarding debts that the missing person himself incurred before disappearing, nor can he claim a share in real estate or goods in the possession of another, because he is not the owner nor a deputy (na'ib) — he is merely an agent for collection from the judge's side. This type of agent does not have the power of litigation by consensus (bila khilaf). The disagreement among scholars is only about an agent for collection appointed by the owner himself. The text further notes that if the guardian's actions entail a ruling against the absent missing person (such as selling property to prevent spoilage), this is not valid unless it goes through the judge's own judgment. The judge himself may sell property that is feared to spoil, because preserving the substance (ma'na) is prioritized when preserving the form (sura) is impossible.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): A person who is absent from his people such that his whereabouts and whether he is alive or dead are completely unknown.
حافظ (hafiz): A person appointed by the judge to preserve and manage the property of a missing person.
قاضي (qadi): The judge who is responsible for appointing guardians for those unable to manage their own affairs, including the missing person.

The Judge's Duty to Appoint a Guardian for the Missing Person
Ruling on judge appointing guardian for missing person's property, supported by qiyas jaliy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on appointing a guardian for a missing person's property and explain the qiyas (analogy) that supports it.

Core Idea
A missing person's property must be preserved by a judge-appointed guardian, just as for a minor or insane person.

When a person disappears without trace, his property is left vulnerable. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling that obliges the judge to appoint a guardian (hafiz) to protect and manage the missing person's assets. The ruling is derived through clear analogy (qiyas jaliy) with minors and the insane, who also cannot manage their own affairs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the judge appointing a guardian for the missing person's property. The ruling is that it is necessary (fard kifayah) for the judge to do so.
Fard kifayah means a communal obligation; if some judges do it, the obligation is discharged.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is an analogy (qiyas): the missing person is compared to a minor (saghir) and an insane person (majnun). The text says: 'فَصَارَ كَالصَّغِيرِ وَالْمَجْنُونِ' (so he becomes like a minor and an insane person).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The illa (effective cause) is inability to manage one's own affairs. The judge is appointed as guardian for all who are incapable. The missing person is incapable due to absence, thus the analogy transfers the ruling from minors/insane to the mafqud.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) because the illa is explicitly stated.
4
Note any ikhtilaf
No disagreement is mentioned within the Hanafi school or across madhhabs on this basic ruling. It is considered muttafaq alayh among the Hanafis.
Later Hanafi sources may discuss details, but the core ruling is accepted.
5
Determine the thamarah (practical outcome)
The judge must actively appoint a guardian; failure to do so would be a dereliction of duty. The guardian preserves property and collects debts until the missing person returns or is legally declared dead.
This ensures the missing person's rights are protected.

Clarifying guardian's powers to collect and restrictions on litigation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a guardian's right to collect debts and his right to litigate, and apply the rules to determine when he may sue.

Core Idea
A judge-appointed guardian collects but cannot sue except for debts from his own contracts.

This passage defines the precise boundaries of a guardian's power over the missing person's assets. Understanding the distinction between the right to collect (qabd) and the right to litigate (khusumah) is crucial to avoid overstepping authority. We will walk through the types of claims the guardian can handle and those he cannot.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main rights: collection vs litigation
Recognize that the guardian has two distinct powers: collecting (qabd) what is due and suing (khusumah) to enforce rights. The text separates these clearly.
2
List what the guardian can collect without dispute
The guardian can collect the missing person's income (e.g., rents, crops) and any debts that debtors have acknowledged. This is considered preservation (hifz).
3
Determine when the guardian can sue
The guardian may sue for debts that become due from his own contracts (e.g., a sale he made for the missing person). In such cases, he is the principal (asil) in those rights.
4
Identify limitations on suing
The guardian cannot sue for debts the missing person incurred before disappearing, nor for real estate or movable goods in someone else's hand. He is neither owner nor deputy for litigation.
5
Note the exception: judge's ijtihad
If the judge, exercising ijtihad, rules that the guardian can sue in a particular case, that judgment is enforceable because the matter is subject to ijtihad.
This shows that the judge can override the default rule based on his own reasoning.

Commentary
This passage details the powers of the guardian appointed for the missing person. The guardian is permitted to collect the missing person's income (e.g., rents, crops) and any debts that debtors have acknowledged. He may also sue in court for debts that become due through his own contract (e.g., if he sells something on behalf of the missing person and the buyer fails to pay), because in those matters he is acting as a principal. However, he cannot sue for debts that the missing person himself incurred before disappearing, nor can he sue for any property (real estate or movable goods) that is in someone else's hand, even if it belongs to the missing person, because the guardian is not the owner and not a deputy authorized for litigation; he is merely an agent for collection appointed by the judge. For such a judge-appointed agent, there is no dispute that he cannot sue. The disagreement (ikhtilaf) is regarding an agent for collection appointed by the owner himself: can he sue? The text notes that if the judge, in his ijtihad, rules that the guardian can sue in such cases, that judgment is enforceable because the matter is subject to ijtihad.

Selling Property of the Missing Person: When Is It Allowed?
Ruling on sale of missing person's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a judge may sell a missing person's property and apply the principle of hifz al-surah vs hifz al-ma'na.

Core Idea
Preserve the form if possible; if not, preserve the value by selling.

This lesson examines a key fiqh rule for guardians of absent persons' property. The judge's authority is strictly limited to preservation. Understanding when sale becomes permissible is essential for correctly applying the principle of hifz al-surah (preserving the physical form) and hifz al-ma'na (preserving the value).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is the sale of the missing person's property by the judge. The default ruling is that it is not permitted because the judge's role is preservation, not conversion.
2
Spot the exception
The exception is when the property is 'feared to spoil' (yukhaf alayhi al-fasad). In that case, preserving the physical form (hifz al-surah) becomes impossible, so the judge sells to preserve the value (hifz al-ma'na).
The text says 'it is difficult to preserve its form' (ta'azzara alayhi hifz surah).
3
Apply the boundary condition
Even if the family needs maintenance (nafaqah), the judge cannot sell non-perishable property because the possibility of physical preservation remains. The need for maintenance does not override the prohibition.
This refutes a common misconception.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that the guardian's authority (wilayah) over the absent person's property is limited to preservation (hifz). Any action that changes the form is only allowed when preservation of the form is impossible.
This is based on the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar' and general qawā'id.

Commentary
This passage addresses the sale of the missing person's property. The principle is that the guardian (acting under the judge's authority) may only sell property if there is a genuine fear that it will spoil or deteriorate if kept in its physical form (e.g., fresh fruits, perishable goods). حفظ المعنى (hifz al-ma'na): Preserving the value or benefit of the property by converting it into a more durable form (e.g., selling perishables and keeping the money).

Commentary
This passage establishes a general rule for spending on dependents from the missing person's wealth. The guardian appointed by the judge is to provide maintenance (nafaqah) for the missing person's wife and children. This rule extends to all blood relatives (parents, grandparents, grandchildren, etc.). The underlying principle is: those relatives who are entitled to maintenance from the missing person's wealth during his presence without needing a court order (i.e., their right is established by shari'ah directly) will also be entitled in his absence. Examples: young children, adult daughters (who are unmarried and poor), and permanently disabled adult males (like a paralyzed father). For these, the court order is merely an 'assistance' (i'anah) to enforce a pre-existing right. On the other hand, relatives whose maintenance is only obligatory by a court order during the missing person's presence (e.g., a brother, sister, maternal uncle, maternal aunt) lose their entitlement in his absence, because the obligation would arise only from a court judgment, and a judgment cannot be passed against an absent person (since he is not present to defend himself).

Key Terms

Agent's Authority and Sale of Missing Person's Perishable Property
Ruling on agent's authority and sale of perishable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the limits of an agent's authority in litigation and identify the condition under which a judge may sell a missing person's property.

Core Idea
Agent cannot litigate without authorization; judge may sell only when property is at risk of spoilage.

This passage from the Book of the Missing Person clarifies two key issues: the scope of an agent's power to litigate, and the limits of a judge's authority to sell the absent person's property. Guided Path

This reinforces the principle that guardianship over an absent person's wealth is strictly for protection.

Ruling on Litigant Standing for Maintenance of a Missing Person
Ruling on who can be a litigant for missing person's maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a maintenance beneficiary cannot be a litigant for a missing person's property and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
A maintenance claimant cannot litigate if the custodian denies the underlying obligation, because the right is not specific to that property.

This lesson examines a procedural ruling from al-Hidayah concerning the missing person (mafqud). When a missing person's property is held by a trustee or a debtor, and those parties deny the deposit, debt, or the very relationship (marriage/lineage) that gives rise to a maintenance claim, the potential beneficiaries cannot step in as litigants. The rationale is that the right to maintenance is a general obligation on the missing person's wealth, not specially linked to that particular property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claims
The case involves a missing person (mafqud), a trustee (mudi‘) or debtor (madyun), and maintenance beneficiaries (e.g., wife, children). The trustee may deny the deposit, the debtor may deny the debt, or both may deny the marital or filial relationship.
This sets the factual context for the ruling.
2
Determine when beneficiaries cannot be litigants
According to the text, if the trustee or debtor are deniers (jahid) of the underlying obligation or the relationship, then none of the maintenance beneficiaries can become a litigant (khasm) against them.
The denial triggers the prohibition.
3
Understand the legal rationale (ta‘lil)
The reason given is that the beneficiary's claim for maintenance is not a specified cause (sabab mu‘ayyan) for the right of maintenance from that particular property. Maintenance is obligatory on the missing person's entire wealth, not just this specific deposit or debt.
This explains why the denial blocks standing.
4
Apply the rule to examples
Consider a wife seeking maintenance from a trustee who denies the deposit. She cannot sue because her right is general. But if the trustee admits the deposit, she could potentially be a litigant (though the text does not cover that).
Practice applying the condition and rationale.
