Acts That Do Not Break the Dhimmi Covenant
Hanafi view that major sins by a dhimmi do not nullify the covenant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on which acts do not break the dhimma contract and explain the reasoning against the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Major sins by a dhimmi do not break the covenant because it is a contract of security, not faith.

This passage addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute: what actions by a dhimmi void the pact of protection? The Hanafi school famously holds that even serious transgressions like refusing jizya, killing a Muslim, insulting the Prophet, or adultery with a Muslim woman do not nullify the covenant. We will trace the Hanafi argument and the Shafi'i objection, dissecting the legal reasoning and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position and the listed acts
Read the passage and note the four acts: refusal of jizya, killing a Muslim, insulting the Prophet, adultery with a Muslim woman. The Hanafi ruling is that none of these break the covenant.
The ruling is explicit: 'lam yantaqid ahduh' – his covenant is not broken.
2
Understand the Hanafi reasoning for jizya refusal
The text explains that the goal of fighting is the acceptance (iltizam) of jizya, not its actual payment. Since the acceptance remains, the contract continues.
This reasoning is specific to jizya but sets the pattern: the contract is based on ongoing commitment, not mere performance.
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection regarding insulting the Prophet
Imam al-Shafi'i argues by analogy: if a Muslim insults the Prophet, his faith (iman) is broken. Since the dhimma contract is a substitute (khalaf) for iman, insulting the Prophet should break the security (aman) as well.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) from the case of a Muslim to the case of a dhimmi.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response distinguishing aman from iman
The Hanafi reply: insulting the Prophet is an act of disbelief (kufr). But the dhimmi was already a disbeliever at the time of the contract. Since his prior disbelief did not prevent the covenant, this new disbelief also does not remove it. The dhimma is a contract of aman (security), not iman (faith).
The key distinction is between aman and iman. The contract is based on worldly security, not religious faith.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
Practical consequence: Hanafis say the dhimmi remains under protection; Shafi'is say he becomes an enemy subject to execution or expulsion. The Hanafi school relies on this position as mufta bihi, supported by the Zahir al-Riwayah.
This difference has significant legal and policy implications for non-Muslim residents in Muslim lands.

The acts listed are: refusing to pay jizya, killing a Muslim, insulting the Prophet, and fornicating with a Muslim woman. Breach of Dhimmi Covenant: Conditions and Legal Consequences
Matn from al-Hidayah on conditions for breaking dhimmi covenant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that breach the dhimmi covenant and explain the legal consequences, including the difference from apostasy.

Core Idea
The dhimmi covenant is only broken by physical defection to enemy territory or active combat; breach leads to legal death and expropriation, but enslavement not execution.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a critical question: what actions terminate the dhimmi covenant? The answer is narrow: only joining Dar al-Harb or actively fighting Muslims. The rationale is that the covenant's purpose is to prevent war; when the dhimmi becomes a combatant, the covenant becomes purposeless. The consequences are severe but distinct from apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the dhimmi's breach of covenant. The ruling is that the covenant is not broken except by two specific actions.
2
List the two conditions for breach
(a) joining Dar al-Harb – physically relocating to enemy territory; (b) actively fighting Muslims by forming a group and waging war.
These are the only causes that invalidate the covenant.
3
Explain the rationale (illah)
Such actions make the dhimmi a harbi (enemy), rendering the covenant futile since its purpose was to prevent harm from war.
The contract of dhimmah becomes devoid of benefit.
4
Describe the consequences of breach
The dhimmi is legally considered dead (inheritance dissolved, marriage ended) and his wealth becomes booty (ghanimah) if captured by Muslims.
He is treated like an apostate in property and legal death.
5
Note the key difference from apostasy
If captured alive, the dhimmi can be enslaved, unlike an apostate who is given a chance to repent or must be executed.
The dhimmi never became a Muslim; his status changes to harbi.

Ruling on a Dhimmi Who Breaches Covenant
Declaratory ruling on a dhimmi's breach of covenant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dhimmi who breaches the covenant and identify the key exception regarding capture.

Core Idea
A dhimmi who joins the enemy is treated like an apostate, but if captured alive, he is enslaved, not executed.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar addresses the legal status of a dhimmi (a non-Muslim subject under Muslim protection) who violates his covenant by joining the enemy. The ruling draws a careful analogy to apostasy while introducing a critical exception regarding punishment. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to the law of war and the treatment of protected persons.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a dhimmi breaking his covenant (by joining dar al-harb). The general ruling is that he becomes like a murtad (apostate).
This is a declaratory ruling establishing legal status, not a taklifi obligation.
2
Understand the analogy to murtad
Being 'like a murtad' means in death rulings: his marriage is dissolved and he is considered dead by joining the enemy. Also in property: whatever wealth he carries is treated as fay'/ghanimah if Muslims conquer that territory.
The analogy covers dissolution of marital ties and property disposition.
3
Identify the exception on capture
If the dhimmi is captured alive, he is enslaved (mustaraqq). This differs from a murtad, who is given the choice to repent or be executed.
The exception stems from the dhimmi's original status: his kufr was tolerated, so he is not liable for execution purely for apostasy.
4
Trace the legal basis
The dhimmi's treachery makes him a harbi (enemy combatant) but his underlying unbelief is not punishable by death, unlike a Muslim apostate. Hence enslavement is possible.
This ruling reflects the Hanafi principle that the dhimmi's covenant is binding on both sides; breach allows the state to treat him as an enemy.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'ata (stipend) for recipients who die mid-year and explain the legal reasoning based on the gift/debt distinction and the condition of possession (qabd).

Core Idea
'Ata is a gift, not a debt; ownership requires possession, so death before receipt extinguishes the right.

This passage addresses a practical treasury question: if an official receiving an annual stipend dies before the year ends, does the stipend go to his heirs? The Hanafi ruling, grounded in the legal nature of 'ata as a discretionary grant rather than a contractual obligation, clarifies that the stipend is forfeited. The reason lies in the condition of possession (qabd) required for ownership of gifts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that if a recipient of 'ata dies mid-year, he has no claim to the stipend. The ruling is absolute: nothing is owed to him or his heirs.
2
Analyze the Legal Nature of 'Ata
The author classifies 'ata as a type of gift (silah), not a debt (dayn). This classification is crucial because debts survive death and must be paid to heirs, while gifts do not transfer ownership until possession occurs.
The term 'ata linguistically means 'gift'.
3
Apply the Hanafi Principle on Gifts
In Hanafi fiqh, a gift is not owned until physical possession (qabd) is taken. Without qabd, ownership is incomplete. Therefore, if the recipient dies before receiving the stipend, ownership never transferred, and the right lapses.
Qabd is a condition (shart) for ownership in gifts.
4
Identify Recipients and Context
The passage notes that in the author's time, recipients included the judge (qadi), teacher (mudarris), and mufti. This helps to modernize the ruling: similar discretionary grants today are treated the same way.
5
Understand the Practical Implication
The ruling prevents confusion in estate distribution: heirs cannot claim unpaid 'ata unless it was already possessed by the deceased. Contractual salaries, however, are debts and must be paid.

Core Idea
Execution of the male apostate is wajib; the three-day respite is mustahabb, and killing before inviting is makruh tanzihi, not sinful.

In this passage, the author presents the Hanafi position on how a male apostate should be dealt with: imprisonment, invitation to Islam, and execution if unrepentant. A key dispute arises with Imam al-Shafi'i over whether the three-day respite is obligatory or merely recommended. Additionally, the ruling on killing the apostate before inviting him is clarified: it is disliked (makruh tanzihi) but carries no civil liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the punishment of a male apostate (murtadd). The ruling: execution is wajib if he does not repent; three-day imprisonment is recommended (mustahabb).
The text distinguishes between obligatory execution and recommended respite.
2
Locate the evidence from Quran and Sunnah
The Hanafi proof: Quran 9:5 'kill the polytheists' and hadith 'whoever changes his religion, kill him' are cited as absolute commands without condition of respite.

3
Analyze the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i
Shafi'i holds three-day respite is wajib based on the need for reflection and narrations from Umar. Hanafi responds that the apostate is a harbi and the da'wah has already reached him, so immediate execution is allowed; the respite is mustahabb.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Hanafi, execution may be immediate if no request; under Shafi'i, it must be delayed three days.

The meaning of karahah here is not prohibition but merely leaving a mustahabb.
5

The practical difference: in Hanafi, the leader may execute immediately after verifying apostasy and presenting Islam once; in Shafi'i, a three-day wait is obligatory. Also, if killed early, Hanafi considers it makruh but not punishable, while Shafi'i would view it as impermissible.
This clarifies the scope of liability and the weight of recommended acts.

What is the punishment of the female apostate?

'As for the female apostate, she is not killed. And al-Shafi'i said: She is killed, because of what we narrated (the hadith) and because the apostasy of a man makes his blood permissible, since it is a grave crime, so a severe punishment (death) is imposed. And the woman's apostasy shares in that severity, so she shares in its requirement. Our proof is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade killing women, and because the original principle regarding punishments is to postpone them to the Hereafter, since immediate punishment would disrupt the meaning of trial. This principle is departed from to repel immediate evil, which is warfare. But that does not apply to women because they lack the capacity for fighting, unlike men. So the female apostate becomes like the original disbeliever (who is not killed for disbelief alone). He (al-Quduri) said: But she is imprisoned until she becomes Muslim, because she has refrained from fulfilling the right of Allah after having affirmed it, so she is compelled to fulfill it through imprisonment, as in rights of servants. And in al-Jami' al-Saghir: The woman is compelled to Islam, whether free or slave.'

Translation: And the female apostate will not be killed. But Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him) said that she will also be killed, because of the same hadith that we narrated ('Whoever changes his religion, kill him') and because a man's apostasy makes his blood permissible, so his punishment should be severe, i.e., execution, and woman also shares in this severe crime, so she should also share in the same punishment. Our proof is that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) forbade killing women (as mentioned in the authentic collections). And also because the original principle regarding punishments is that they should be delayed to the Hereafter as much as possible, because immediate punishment disrupts the testing of the criminal (to see how audacious he can be). Despite this principle, immediate punishment is applied for repelling immediate evil and warfare. But from women, such fighting is not expected, because naturally they do not have the capacity for war, unlike men. Hence, the female apostate (who becomes disbeliever after having brought faith) comes under the ruling of a original disbelieving woman. (Just as an original disbelieving woman is not killed even in the battlefield, similarly an apostate woman will not be killed). However, she will be put in prison and will remain there until she accepts Islam again, because she affirmed the right of Allah and its performance, but now she is refusing to perform it. So she is compelled to perform it by imprisonment, as is done in cases of rights of servants. And in al-Jami' al-Saghir: The woman is compelled to (re-)embrace Islam, whether she is free or a slave.

Explanation: If a woman becomes an apostate, according to the Hanafis she will not be executed; rather she will be imprisoned and given encouragement and invitation. If she still does not accept Islam, she will be left in prison until death decides her fate. Imam al-Shafi'i says that if a woman becomes apostate, she is executed like a man. The proof is the Prophet's statement: 'Whoever changes his religion, kill him.' This prophetic command is unqualified, so the ruling is general. The second proof: the cause for making blood permissible is disbelief, which is found in the woman. The third proof: post-Islam disbelief is more severe than original disbelief. The Hanafis say: The hadith states: 'Do not kill women and children.'

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the punishment of a female apostate. The Hanafi position is that a female apostate is not executed but rather imprisoned until she repents. The text presents the opposing view of Imam al-Shafi'i, who argues that she should be killed like a male apostate, based on the general hadith 'Whoever changes his religion, kill him' and the severity of the crime. The Hanafi response provides two proofs: (1) The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade killing women in general, and this prohibition remains unless there is specific evidence (like warfare) to override it. Since an apostate woman does not engage in combat, she is not subject to execution. (2) The original principle in punishments is to delay them to the Hereafter, as immediate punishment disrupts the divine test. This principle is set aside only to repel immediate worldly harm, namely warfare. Women, by nature, do not have the capacity for warfare, so the exception does not apply to them. Therefore, the female apostate is treated like an original disbelieving woman who is not killed for disbelief alone. Instead, she is imprisoned to compel her to fulfill the right of Allah that she had accepted and then neglected. This is analogous to compelling a debtor to pay his debt. The text further notes that in al-Jami' al-Saghir, the woman is to be compelled to Islam, whether free or slave. The explanation (tashrih) summarizes the Hanafi view: the female apostate is imprisoned, given da'wah, and if she persists, she remains imprisoned until death. The Shafi'i position is again presented with its proofs: the unqualified hadith, disbelief as a cause, and the severity of apostasy. The Hanafi rebuttal is based on the prophetic prohibition against killing women and children.

Ownership of an apostate: Suspension or retention?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on apostate's property ownership cessation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on apostate's ownership cessation, explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Core Idea
An apostate's ownership is suspended, not severed — hope of return keeps it alive until fate is sealed.

This lesson delves into a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does an apostate lose ownership of his property immediately upon apostasy, or does ownership persist until execution or death? The ruling has significant implications for inheritance and the treatment of earnings during apostasy. We will examine the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling attributed to Abu Hanifa in the text: ownership ceases upon apostasy, but if he returns to Islam, it revives. If he dies or is killed in apostasy or joins Dar al-Harb, his ownership permanently ends.
This is the mufta bihi (relied upon) position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that ownership does not cease until execution or death because the apostate remains a mukallaf (legally responsible) and in need of property.
They analogize to a person sentenced to death by stoning (rajm) or retaliation (qisas) who retains ownership until executed.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa compares the apostate to a harbi (enemy combatant) under Muslim control. A harbi's property becomes fay' (booty for the community). He also notes that the apostate is invited to Islam and his return is hoped for, so the ruling is suspended (tawaqquf) until his fate is known.
The suspension is the key mechanism: if he returns, apostasy is treated as if it never happened; if he dies in apostasy, the cause operates and ownership ceases.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that the apostate is a mukallaf in need of property for his livelihood, just like a condemned prisoner. He cannot be deprived of ownership solely for being a potential threat.
Their analogy focuses on the apostate's ongoing legal capacity and practical needs.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference appears when the apostate dies in apostasy: under Abu Hanifa, property acquired before apostasy goes to his Muslim heirs, and property acquired during apostasy becomes fay'. Under the Sahibayn, all his property (pre- and post-apostasy) goes to his Muslim heirs.
Also affects transactions: under Abu Hanifa, sales by the apostate during apostasy are invalid (since ownership is suspended); under the Sahibayn, they are valid until execution.

Commentary
This passage addresses the effect of apostasy on property. It begins with the ruling from al-Quduri: the apostate's ownership ceases upon apostasy, but this cessation is temporary—if he returns to Islam, his ownership is restored as if it never ceased. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, arguing that ownership does not cease because the apostate remains a legally responsible person in need of property, analogous to someone sentenced to death by stoning or retaliation—they retain ownership until execution. Abu Hanifa's proof is that an apostate is like a harbi (enemy combatant) under our control, whose property is considered fay' (booty), thus his ownership should end. However, because he is invited back to Islam and his return is expected, the ruling is suspended until his fate is determined. If he returns, the apostasy is treated as if it never happened. If he dies, is killed, or joins Dar al-Harb, his apostasy is confirmed, and his ownership ceases retroactively. This is a key intra-Hanafi dispute based on different analogies: Sahibayn compare to a condemned prisoner, Abu Hanifa compares to a harbi.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An apostate's property becomes ghanimah or returns to heirs depending on when the qadi ruled him laḥiq.

This passage from Al-Hidayah's Kitab al-Siyar presents two scenarios concerning the property of an apostate who flees to dar al-harb. Note that in the second scenario, the heirs recover the property before ghanimah distribution.
The first scenario involves immediate capture; the second involves a prior return.

The ruling differs based on the ownership status at the time of capture.

This is a Hanafi principle: a murtadd's property is suspended until the qadi's ruling.

Validity of Kitabah by Heir After Apostate's Return
Apostate's slave given to son by qadi, son makes mukatab, then apostate returns Muslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a kitabah made by an heir after the owner's apostasy and explain why the rights of the contract revert to the original owner upon his return as Muslim.

Core Idea
A qadi's judgment creates binding agency, so a kitabah by the heir is valid and rights revert to the returned apostate owner.

This lesson examines a case from Kitab al-Siyar where an apostate flees to dar al-harb, his slave is awarded to his son by the qadi, and the son makes the slave a mukatab. Later the apostate returns as Muslim. The Hanafi ruling demonstrates how a valid judicial ruling creates ongoing legal effects even after circumstances change.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Statuses
List the apostate (father), his slave, the son, and the qadi. Note that the apostate is legally considered dead for certain purposes, but his return as Muslim revives his ownership rights.
The apostate's status changes from 'dead' to 'alive' upon repentance.
2
Understand the Qadi's Judgment
The qadi awards the slave to the son. This judgment is binding and gives the son lawful possession and authority over the slave, even though the real ownership is suspended.
A qadi's valid judgment creates legal effects ex nunc.
3
Analyze the Kitabah Contract
The son makes a kitabah with the slave. The contract is valid because the son had authority from the qadi. The contract is binding and creates rights and obligations.
Kitabah is a binding contract in the Hanafi school.
4
Consider the Apostate's Return and its Effect
When the apostate returns as Muslim, his ownership over the slave is revived. The question is whether the kitabah made by the son remains valid.
The return does not retroactively invalidate prior valid acts.
5
Apply the Agency Principle (Wakalah)
The son is considered an agent (wakil) for the father by virtue of the qadi's judgment. Therefore, the rights of the kitabah (payments and wala) belong to the principal (father), not the agent (son).
The wala belongs to the one from whom emancipation proceeds – the original owner.

Diyah of a Murtadd: Which Property Applies?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah from murtadd's earnings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the payment of diyah from a murtadd's property and state the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A murtadd's diyah is only from his Islam-earned property; riddah earnings are not his property.

This lesson examines a classical intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the payment of diyah (blood money) when an apostate (murtadd) kills someone by mistake. The central question is whether property earned during riddah can be used for diyah. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on the legal capacity of the apostate's transactions during riddah. Understanding this dispute deepens your grasp of how legal status (ahliyyah) affects property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the passage and note the factual situation: a murtadd kills a man by mistake (khata') and then either joins dar al-harb or is killed on his riddah.
The diyah is not borne by the 'aqila because they cease to support the apostate.
2
State the agreed rule
Both parties agree that diyah must be paid from the murtadd's own property since the 'aqila do not bear it.
3
Locate the ikhtilaf
Identify the point of disagreement: What constitutes 'his property'? Abu Hanifa restricts it to property earned during Islam; the Sahibayn include property earned during riddah.
4

Abu Hanifa's dalil: transactions during riddah are suspended (mawqūf), so riddah earnings are not valid property. Sahibayn's dalil: transactions are effective (nāfidh) in both states, so all earnings are property.
5
Determine the thamarah and tarjih
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, diyah is only from Islam earnings; under Sahibayn, from all earnings. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
Note that inheritance also differs: Islam earnings are inherited, riddah earnings become fay'.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the payment of diyah (blood money) when an apostate (murtadd) kills someone by mistake (khata') and then either joins dar al-harb or is killed on his riddah. The issue: from which portion of the apostate's property should the diyah be paid? The text first states the general ruling: the apostate's 'aqila (tribal supporters) do not bear responsibility because they cease to support a person who has left the faith. Therefore, the diyah falls entirely on his own property. The disagreement is over what counts as 'his property.' Imam Abu Hanifa's position: only property acquired during his state of Islam (while he was a Muslim) is considered his property. Property earned during riddah is not his; his transactions during riddah are suspended (mawqūf). So diyah is paid only from the Islam-acquired wealth. According to Abu Hanifa, upon his death (or being killed on riddah), the Islam-earned property is inherited (mīrāth) by his Muslim heirs, while the riddah-earned property becomes fay' (spoils) for the Muslim treasury. The Sahibayn's position: Both earnings, whether in Islam or riddah, are his property because his transactions are effective (nāfidh) in both states. Therefore, diyah is paid from all his wealth, and inheritance applies to both types of property. This difference stems from a fundamental point: Imam Abu Hanifa considers the apostate's legal capacity (ahliyyah) for transactions to be suspended during riddah, while the Sahibayn consider it intact. The muftā bihī (relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, as stated in al-Hidayah and other reliable works. This illustrates a key usul al-fiqh principle: the effect of riddah on property ownership and transactions.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money paid for unintentional homicide or bodily injury, here specifically due to khata' (mistake) killing.
عاقله (ʿāqila): The group of male agnates (or supporters) who are responsible for paying diyah for accidental killings by a member.
خطأ (khaṭa'): Unintentional; here used for accidental killing (qatl al-khata').

Diyah for Cutting a Muslim's Hand After Apostasy
Ruling on diyah when victim apostatizes after hand cut and dies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah when the victim apostatizes after injury and explain the principles of sarāyah and hadar that reduce liability.

Core Idea
Apostasy after injury nullifies half the diyah because progression occurred in a non-protected state.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar in Al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced scenario: a Muslim's hand is cut intentionally, then he becomes an apostate. If he dies from the wound, how much diyah is due? The text explains that only half is wajib, because the progression (sarāyah) of the wound occurred in a body that lost its inviolability. The reasoning is based on analogy with remission (ibra'), where the victim waives his right. This lesson unpacks the two specific cases covered and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and parties
The act is intentionally cutting a Muslim's hand. The victim is initially a protected Muslim. The aggressor would normally be liable for full diyah or qisas if the wound progresses to death.
2
Note the change in victim's status
After the injury, the victim apostatizes (leaves Islam) and then dies from the wound. Alternatively, he joins dar al-harb (with qadi ruling) and later returns as a Muslim before dying.
Apostasy removes inviolability (isma) from the moment of apostasy.
3
Apply the principle of sarāyah and hadar
The progression of the wound into a non-protected body (ghayr ma'sum) causes partial nullification (hadar) of the diyah. Only half remains wajib.
Death cuts off progression; here progression occurred in a non-protected state.
4
Examine the analogy with ibra' (remission)
The text analogizes apostasy to remission: just as a victim who remits (ibra') nullifies progression, so does apostasy. Both involve the victim's own action that changes the status of the injury.
This qiyas strengthens the ruling.
5
Consider the second scenario (lāḥiq and return)
If the victim joins dar al-harb and the qadi rules him lāḥiq, he is deemed dead. Death cuts off progression. His later Islam is a new life, so the original liability does not revive. Half diyah remains.
If qadi did not rule, there is an ikhtilaf deferred.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Siyar addresses a specific scenario involving qisas and diyah. A Muslim's hand is intentionally cut off (manifesting qisas liability). But because the victim became an apostate, the inviolability (isma) of his life was lost at the moment of apostasy. The progression (sarāya) of the wound, which would normally be attributed to the initial aggression, occurred in a body that was no longer protected (ghayr ma‘ṣūm). Therefore, half the diyah is waived (hadar). The text contrasts this with the opposite scenario: if an apostate's hand is cut and then he embraces Islam and dies from it, the progression is not nullified, because apostasy itself does not automatically nullify liability; nullification only occurs if the victim remits (ibra’) or if the wound progressed in a non-protected state. Additionally, if the victim joins dar al-harb and the qadi rules him laḥiq (deemed dead), then later he returns as Muslim and dies, the same ruling applies: half diyah. The reasoning is that once the qadi ruled him laḥiq, he was estimably dead; death cuts off progression. His subsequent Islam is a new life, so the original injury's effect (full diyah) does not revive. If the qadi did not rule, there is a difference of opinion (ikhtilāf) within the school which is deferred for later discussion. This passage illustrates the Hanafi principle that apostasy can reduce liability for injuries because it removes the inviolability of life.

لاحق (lāḥiq): An apostate who has joined dar al-harb, considered by qadi's ruling as legally dead for purposes of inheritance and marital status.

Diya for the Murtadd Who Returns to Islam: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Full diya if isma existed at injury and death; apostasy in between does not nullify.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute. The core question: if a Muslim has his hand cut, then apostatizes, later repents, and dies from the wound, is the cutter liable for full diya, half, or nothing? The answer hinges on whether the intervening apostasy breaks the causal link (sirāyah). Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that protection at the time of injury and death suffices; Muhammad and Zufar argue the apostasy nullifies the wound's effect. We will unpack their proofs and a key analogy from divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenerios and rulings
The passage discusses multiple cases: (a) murtadd dies in apostasy (no diya), (b) murtadd reaches Dar al-Harb then returns and dies (no diya), (c) murtadd does not go to Dar al-Harb, repents, dies (dispute: full diya vs half). Focus on (c).
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf: full diya; Muhammad & Zufar: half diya in all such cases.
3
Analyze the opposing proof
Muhammad and Zufar argue that intervening apostasy nullifies sirāyah, so no liability revives upon Islam, analogous to cutting a murtadd's hand who later becomes Muslim.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that isma is considered at the time of the cause (injury) and at the time of the ruling (death), not during the continuation. The qiyas from oath of divorce shows this principle.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (full diya), as supported by the qiyas and the majority of the school.

If [the murtadd] died in that state or went to Dar al-Harb but returned as Muslim and died from that wound, then the one who cut his hand must give half the diya from his own wealth to the heirs of the deceased. Similarly, in the first case when he died in apostasy, this ruling is based on the fact that the cutting of the hand affected his person and caused his death, but it penetrated a place that is not inviolable. Therefore his blood was forfeited, i.e., blood-money was not obligatory. Contrary to that, if the hand of a murtadd was cut, then he became Muslim, then he died from that wound, nothing is obligatory because at the time of cutting, due to his apostasy, his blood was worthless. His subsequent Islam does not change that. And any [right of] retaliation that was previously valid becomes ineffective and forfeited, just as if he had been forgiven. Similarly, due to apostasy it is also forfeited. Now the second case: when he had already reached Dar al-Harb, meaning the qadi had announced his arrival there, then he became Muslim and died in Dar al-Islam from that wound. The reason for his blood-money being nullified is that although he was alive, he had already been legally declared dead, and a wound does not affect a dead person. According to this explanation, his Islam is a new life for him, so the previous crime is not revived. This entire detail applies when he had reached Dar al-Harb. If the qadi had not yet made a ruling about his arrival there, then there is disagreement, which we will mention later, inshaAllah. If he did not go to Dar al-Harb, then became Muslim and died, then the cutter owes full diya. He said: 'If he did not go [to Dar al-Harb] and became Muslim then died, then upon him is the full diya.' This is according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Muhammad and Zufar said: In all these cases, half the diya is due, because the intervening apostasy nullifies the effect (sirāyah), so it does not turn into liability upon Islam, just as if he cut the hand of a murtadd and then he became Muslim. And for them (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) [their proof is] that the injury occurred upon a place that was inviolable (ma‘ṣūm) and was completed in it, so liability for the life is obligatory, as if apostasy had not intervened. And [they said] that regard is not given to the existence of inviolability during the state of the injury's continuation; rather regard is given to it at the time of the cause's inception and at the time of the ruling's establishment. The state of continuation is remote from all that. It becomes like the existence of ownership during the state of the oath's continuation. [Then the Urdu translation and elaboration follow. A detailed example is given of a man who says to his wife: 'If you enter this house, you are divorced,' then gives her a ba’in divorce, later remarries, and she enters the house—the divorce occurs because the condition was fulfilled when marriage existed, even though ownership did not exist during the continuation of the oath.] Similarly, in our issue, at the time of the hand-cutting he was Muslim, then he apostatized in between, then when the wound caused death he was Muslim again.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the diya (blood-money) owed when a murtadd (apostate) who had his hand cut later returns to Islam and dies from that wound. The author first explains that if the murtadd died in apostasy, no diya is due because his blood is not protected. If he did not go to Dar al-Harb and died after returning to Islam, full diya is due according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, but Muhammad and Zufar hold that only half diya is due. The reasoning revolves around whether the inviolability of life at the time of the injury and at death controls, or whether the intervening apostasy breaks the causal connection (sirāyah). The author provides a qiyas: just as in an oath of divorce, the validity of the condition depends on the existence of marriage at the time of the oath and at the time of fulfillment, not during the intermediate period, similarly, the protection of life at the time of the injury and at death governs, not the apostasy in between. The passage then discusses the case where the murtadd had already reached Dar al-Harb before returning and dying; here, no diya is due because he was legally considered dead. The text clarifies that if the qadi had not yet adjudicated his arrival, there is disagreement to be covered later.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A Muslim who has renounced Islam; his blood is not protected during apostasy, but may become protected again upon repentance.
دیت (diya): Blood-money, the financial compensation for homicide or bodily injury short of death.
عصمت (isma): Legal inviolability or protection of life and property; a Muslim's life is ma‘ṣūm.
سرایت (sirāyah): The effect of a wound extending from the initial injury to cause death.

The Legal Status of Apostates' Children in Dar al-Harb: Forced Conversion and the Grandchild's Exception
Ruling on children of apostates as spoils of war and forced conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on children of apostates captured in Dar al-Harb, distinguish the forced conversion of the first child from the grandchild, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding taba'iyyah.

Core Idea
A child follows his mother into slavery, but only the immediate child is forced to Islam — the grandchild's compulsion is disputed.

This passage from the Kitab al-Siyar addresses the fate of apostates' descendants when they are captured in enemy territory. It presents the default ruling and a known intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf about the extent of forced conversion and the principle of legal followership (taba'iyyah). The resolution of this dispute has practical implications for three other fiqh issues, forming a system of two narrations across multiple chapters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the parties
A married couple apostatizes and flees to Dar al-Harb. The woman becomes pregnant and gives birth to a child (first generation). That child grows up and has his own child (second generation). All are captured by Muslims.
2
Apply the ruling on the mother and first child
The apostate woman is considered enslaved under spoils of war. Her child follows her legal status (taba'iyyah). The first child is compelled to embrace Islam (jabr).
The child follows the mother in slavery and in being compelled to Islam.
3
Note the dispute on the grandchild
According to the zahir al-riwayah, the grandchild is NOT compelled to Islam. Hasan ibn Ziyad narrates from Abu Hanifa that the grandchild IS compelled, following the grandfather's religion.
The grandchild's legal link is through his father (the first child), not directly to the apostate grandparents.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The difference hinges on whether the grandchild legally follows the grandfather in religion (taba'iyyah li al-jadd) or not. This principle repeats in four issues.
The same two-narration pattern applies to Sadaqat al-Fitr, transfer of wala', and bequests.
5
Recognize the system of narrations (riwayatayn)
The two positions form a consistent pattern: in one narration, the grandfather is treated like a father; in the other, he is not. The mufta bihi is the zahir al-riwayah position.
This is an example of how ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school is systematized across multiple rulings.

The Book of Siyar: When a man and his wife apostatize—we seek refuge with Allah from that—and both reach Dar al-Harb, and the woman becomes pregnant in Dar al-Harb and gives birth to a child, and that child of theirs then has a child, and then [the Muslims] gain dominance over all of them, then the two children are part of the spoils of war because the apostate woman is enslaved, so her child follows her. The first child is compelled to embrace Islam, but the child's child is not compelled. Al-Hasan reported from Abu Hanifa that he [the grandchild] is compelled in following the grandfather. The foundation of this is the principle of followership in Islam. This is the fourth of four issues, all of which have two narrations: the second is Sadaqat al-Fitr, the third is the transfer of wala', and the other is the bequest to relatives.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding children of apostates. It addresses a scenario where a married couple apostatizes and flees to Dar al-Harb (the abode of war). The woman becomes pregnant and gives birth to a child (the first child), who later grows up and has a child of his own (the second child). When Muslims defeat the area and capture them, the ruling classifies both children as part of the spoils of war (ghanimah). This is because the apostate woman is treated as a slave (since apostates who are captured are enslaved), and her child follows her legal status. The first child, who is a direct descendant of the apostate, is compelled to embrace Islam. The key dispute is about the second child (the grandchild). According to the zahir al-riwayah (the established authoritative transmission from Imam Muhammad), this grandchild is not compelled to become Muslim. However, Hasan ibn Ziyad narrates from Abu Hanifa that the grandchild is compelled to Islam, following the principle of taba'iyyah (legal followership) to the grandfather. The passage then notes that this difference of opinion (ruwayatayn) extends to three other issues: (1) Sadaqat al-Fitr—whether a grandfather must pay fitrana for his grandson if the father is poor (Hasan's narration: yes; zahir al-riwayah: no); (2) Transfer of wala' (jar al-wala')—if a grandfather frees his grandson and the father is a slave of someone else, whether the grandfather can draw the wala' to himself (Hasan's narration: yes; zahir al-riwayah: no); (3) Bequest to relatives (wasiyyah li al-qarabah)—whether the grandfather is included in the term 'relatives' in a bequest (Hasan's narration: not included; zahir al-riwayah: included). This highlights how the principle of followership (taba'iyyah) is applied differently across narrations.

Key Terms
دار الحرب (Dar al-Harb): Territory under non-Muslim rule where Islamic law is not enforced and with which Muslim states may be in a state of war.
التبعية (al-taba'iyyah): Legal followership: a person's status follows that of another due to relationship, such as a child following the mother in slavery or freedom, or in religion under certain conditions.

Treatment of Wounded and Fleeing Rebels
Inter-madhhab dispute on post-battle treatment of rebels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing wounded and pursuing fleeing rebels, explain the condition of a supporting faction (fiah), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A wounded rebel may be killed if a supporting faction exists; the act repels future harm, not vengeance.

The treatment of wounded and fleeing rebels after a battle raises a critical question: is the fighting truly over? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the rebels have a supporting faction (fiah) that could revive the rebellion. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that once the immediate fighting ceases, killing is no longer defensive. This lesson unpacks the dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two scenarios: when rebels have a fiah, killing wounded and pursuing fleeing is wajib; when they do not, it is prohibited.
The ruling is contingent on the existence of a fiah.
2
Understand the rationale of daf' al-sharr
The Hanafi reasoning is that killing and pursuing are acts of repelling evil, not punishment. The fiah indicates the rebellion is ongoing even if the immediate skirmish has stopped.
The act is preventive, not retaliatory.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i forbids killing and pursuing in both cases, arguing that once fighting ceases, there is no ongoing harm to repel.
Shafi'i holds that killing after the fact is excessive.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal is that what matters is the evidence (dalil) of continued fighting, not the literal reality of the current ceasefire. The fiah serves as that evidence.
The principle: al-mu'tabaru daliluhu la haqiqatuhu.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, Hanafis allow killing wounded and pursuing fleeing rebels when a fiah exists; Shafi'is never do. This affects military tactics and post-battle protocols.
The ikhtilaf has real-world consequences for how rebellions are handled.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the treatment of wounded and fleeing rebels after the battle. The Hanafi ruling distinguishes based on whether the rebels have a supporting faction (fiah). If they have a supporting faction, the wounded may be killed and the fleeing pursued to prevent them from rejoining and resuming hostilities. If they have no supporting faction, they are left alone because the threat is already neutralized. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that in both cases it is impermissible, arguing that once the fighting has stopped, killing them does not serve as a repulsion of harm. The Hanafi response is that what matters is the evidence (daliil) of continued fighting—the existence of a supporting faction indicates that the fighting is not truly over, even if the immediate engagement has ceased. Thus, killing and pursuing are acts of repelling evil, not mere revenge. This reasoning is based on the principle of daf' al-sharr (repelling harm) and the need to prevent the rebellion from reigniting.

Key Terms
فِئَة (fiah): A supporting faction or group of rebels that could provide reinforcement and continue the rebellion.
جَرِيح (jarih): A wounded rebel who cannot flee but may recover and rejoin fighting.
مُوَلِّي (muwalli): A fleeing rebel, one who turns his back and runs away from the battlefield.

Rights of Rebels: Children, Property, and Using Their Weapons
Rulings with proofs on rebels' children, property, and weapon use, plus a madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the children, property, and weapons of rebels, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument on using their weapons.

Core Idea
Rebels remain Muslims — children not enslaved, property not taken; weapons may be used in need.

This passage addresses three critical aspects of dealing with rebels (bughat) in Islamic law: the status of their offspring, their property, and the use of their weapons and mounts. The Hanafi school, relying on the precedent of Ali ibn Abi Talib, holds that rebels' children are not to be enslaved and their property is not to be divided as booty. However, the Imam may permit using their weapons and mounts in battle if needed, a point disputed by Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify each ruling and its scope
Separate the three rulings: (1) offspring not enslaved, (2) property not divided as booty, (3) weapons/mounts may be used if needed. Note the different hukm levels: first two are haram, third is mubah when necessary.
These rulings apply specifically to rebels (bughat), not to non-Muslim combatants (kuffar harbi).
2
Trace the proof from Ali's precedent
The primary proof is the action and statement of Ali at the Battle of the Camel. He forbade killing prisoners, uncovering private parts, and taking property. This establishes a general principle that rebels' life, honor, and property are inviolable.
Ali's statement is considered a qawl sahabi and is authoritative in this context.
3
Distinguish the condition for prisoners
The text clarifies that Ali's prohibition on killing prisoners applies only when the rebels have no supporting faction (fiah). If they have a fiah, the Imam may execute or imprison prisoners. This shows that the ruling is contextual.
This distinction prevents the enemy from regrouping.
4
Analyze the dispute on weapons
Compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on using rebels' weapons. Hanafi permits when needed, citing Ali's distribution and qiyas from property of just Muslims. Shafi'i prohibits because a Muslim's property cannot be used without consent.
The underlying principle for Hanafi is inflicting lesser harm to prevent greater harm.
5
Derive the underlying principle
The Hanafi argument rests on the principle of daf' al-sharr (warding off evil). Using property without consent is a lesser harm compared to the greater harm of the rebellion succeeding. This principle justifies the permissibility.
This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference).

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof and an inter-madhhab dispute. It begins with the ruling that the offspring (dhurriyyah) of rebels are not to be enslaved, and their property is not to be taken as booty. The proof is the precedent of Ali ibn Abi Talib at the Battle of the Camel, where he declared that no prisoner would be killed, no woman's honor violated, and no property taken. Ali is the model (qudwah) for the ummah in dealing with rebels. However, the text clarifies that Ali's statement about not killing prisoners applies only when the rebels have no supporting faction; if they do, the Imam may execute or imprison prisoners as needed, based on the same reasoning as in the previous lesson (to prevent them from rejoining the fight). The passage then affirms that rebels remain Muslims, and the Imam is responsible for protecting life and property. The underlying principle is that the lesser harm (taking property without consent) is inflicted to repel the greater harm (enabling the rebellion to continue).

Reclaiming Kharaj and Ushr from Regions Overcome by Rebels
Ruling on Imam recollecting taxes after rebel occupation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether the Imam can recollect kharaj and ushr from regions previously under rebel control, and distinguish when recollection is not required.

Core Idea
If rebels spent kharaj on fighters or ushr on the poor, no recollection – payment reached lawful recipients.

When rebels temporarily control a region, they may collect taxes like kharaj and ushr. Upon regaining authority, the Imam might consider collecting these taxes again from the people. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the key is whether the money reached its legitimate recipients. If it did, the Imam cannot demand it again. Otherwise, the people must re-pay as a religious duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of levy
Determine whether the tax in question is kharaj (land tax for general state expenses including fighters) or ushr (tithe as zakat for the poor). The ruling differs based on the category of recipients.
Kharaj is for all fighters and state needs; ushr is specifically for the poor.
2
Check where the money went
If the rebels spent the collected money on the legitimate recipients of that levy, then the payment is considered fulfilled. For kharaj, the rebels themselves are fighters, so spending on themselves is valid. For ushr, spending on poor rebels fulfills the obligation.
The rebels' status as fighters or poor matters.

Core Idea
Without the just Imam's authority over the kill site, no qisas or diya is due — even among rebels.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hidayah: when one rebel kills another within the rebel camp, and later the just Imam regains control, no qisas (retaliation) or diya (blood money) is required. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A rebel's corrupt interpretation plus power may preserve his inheritance, but only if he remains convinced of his right.

This passage addresses a sensitive intersection of faraid and jihad: can a rebel (bāghī) who kills a just relative inherit from him? The Hanafi imams differ, reflecting deeper principles about liability, authority, and the effect of sincere but erroneous interpretation. We will unpack their arguments and the operative fatwa position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note that the passage discusses two situations: (1) a just man kills a rebel – the just man inherits; (2) a rebel kills a just man – dispute arises.
The first scenario is uncontroversial and establishes the baseline.
2

According to them, the rebel inherits only if he says he was and remains upon the truth; if he admits falsehood, he does not inherit.
The key condition is the rebel's belief in the validity of his action.
3

Abu Yusuf holds that the rebel never inherits, regardless of his belief. This aligns with al-Shafi'i's view.

They cite the consensus of the Companions (narrated by al-Zuhri) that rebels with power (man'ah) are not liable for damages. Their corrupt interpretation (ta'wīl fāsid) combined with power makes the killing analogous to a legitimate killing for defense, so it does not bar inheritance.
The principle: 'al-ahkām lā budda fīhā min al-ilzām aw al-iltizām' – rulings require either enforcement or acceptance.

Abu Yusuf argues that ta'wīl fāsid is only relevant for defense (daf'), not for acquiring a right like inheritance. The majority responds that the need to prevent deprivation (ḥirmān) is also strong, so the interpretation is considered—provided the rebel remains steadfast.
The condition of remaining on that belief is crucial; if he admits falsehood, the justification vanishes.

The dispute is about the reverse scenario: if the rebel kills the just man, does the rebel inherit from his victim? According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the rebel inherits—but only if he held the sincere belief (even if mistaken) that he was fighting for truth. If he admits he knew he was wrong, he does not inherit. Abu Yusuf holds that the rebel never inherits in either case, aligning with Imam al-Shafiʿi's position. The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) argue based on the principle that rebels are treated like warriors in Dar al-Harb regarding liability: their corrupt interpretation (taʾwīl fāsid) combined with their power (manʿah) exempts them from worldly liability in this context, similar to how their lives and property are protected during battle. However, inheritance is different: it is based on kinship, and the barrier (ḥirmān) of killing is lifted if the killing was based on a valid interpretation. For the rebel, his belief that he is right (even if mistaken) prevents the killing from being considered an act of rebellion against Islamic law in a way that would bar inheritance. Abu Yusuf counters that the corrupt interpretation is only relevant for the defense (dafʿ) during battle, not for establishing entitlement to inheritance. The reply is that the need to prevent deprivation (of inheritance) is also strong, so the corrupt interpretation is considered—provided the rebel remains steadfast in his belief. If he later admits he was wrong, that shows the killing was intentional wrongdoing, and inheritance is barred.

Key Terms
باغي (bāghī): Rebel who fights against the just Imam based on a fallible interpretation; distinct from apostates or bandits.
تأويل فاسد (ta'wīl fāsid): An interpretation of Islamic law that is legally incorrect (not in line with the majority or established rulings) but sincerely held.
منعة (man'ah): Power, strength, or the ability to resist the just Imam; a key factor in treating rebels as a de facto authority.
حرمان (ḥirmān): Deprivation of inheritance due to a legal barrier, such as killing the testator.
إلزام (ilzām): Enforcement; the imposition of a legal obligation by a court or authority.
التزام (iltizām): Acceptance; a person's own acknowledgment or assumption of a legal obligation, as in belief or conscience.

Inheritance of a Rebel Under Corrupt Interpretation
Detailed dispute on rebel's inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a rebel (baghi) inherits from a just (adil) relative he killed, and explain the intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab dispute based on the consensus of the Companions.

Core Idea
A rebel who kills under corrupt interpretation inherits if he believed he was on the truth; his belief, not just the act, determines deprivation.

This passage delves into a nuanced inheritance dispute when a rebel kills his just relative during battle. The Hanafi school splits: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad allow inheritance if the rebel believed he was on the truth, while Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam al-Shafi'i deny it entirely. The key lies in the effect of corrupt interpretation (ta'wil fasid) on liability and inheritance. Understanding this requires analyzing the consensus of the Companions regarding the Khawarij and the analogy to warring disbelievers embracing Islam.

Guided Path
1
State the Two Scenarios
Identify the two scenarios presented by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the rebel who says he was on the truth inherits; if he admits falsehood, he does not.
This is the default Hanafi position from the majority.
2
Identify the Opposing Positions
Note that Abu Yusuf (within Hanafi) and al-Shafi'i (outside) hold that the rebel never inherits, regardless of his belief.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within and across schools.
3
Examine the Basis: Liability (Daman)
The root dispute is whether corrupt interpretation removes liability for damages during battle. The Hanafis (Abu Hanifa/Muhammad) say it does; Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i say it does not.
This foundational difference drives the inheritance ruling.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Evidence: Consensus of Sahaba
Read the narration of Imam al-Zuhri: the Sahaba agreed that Khawarij who killed under their interpretation face no qisas, hudud, or property liability. This consensus is the Hanafi proof.
The consensus is cited as zanni in transmission but qati in meaning.
5
Apply to a Father-Son Case
Consider a just son fighting with the imam killing his rebel father: by consensus the son inherits. Reverse: rebel father kills just son. Apply the Hanafi principle: father inherits if he believed his interpretation; otherwise not.
The text confirms this application with no difference except based on belief.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed inter-madhhab and intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the inheritance of a rebel (baghi) who kills his just (adil) relative during battle. The central question is whether the rebel can inherit from the slain, and the answer depends on his own belief at the time of the killing. The passage begins by stating two scenarios: (1) if the rebel asserts that he considered himself on the truth both before and after the killing, he inherits; (2) if he admits he was on falsehood at the time, he does not inherit. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that the rebel never inherits in either case, and Imam al-Shafi'i agrees with him. The basis (asl) of this difference is a prior dispute about liability (daman) for damages caused during battle. According to the Hanafis, a just person who kills or destroys property of a rebel is neither liable nor sinful because he is commanded to fight them. Conversely, if a rebel kills a just person, he is sinful but not liable, because his corrupt interpretation (fasid ta'wil) removes civil liability. Imam al-Shafi'i (in his qawl qadim) and Imam Malik hold that he is both sinful and liable. The Hanafis rely on the consensus (ijma) of the Companions, as narrated by Imam al-Zuhri, regarding the Khawarij: no hudud or qisas for acts done under their interpretation, and property returned if found intact. This consensus establishes that corrupt interpretation, when accompanied by power, has the same effect as correct interpretation in removing liability. The text then draws an analogy to warring disbelievers who become Muslims after battle—no retribution is taken. The same principle applies to rebels by primary degree (bi-darjah awla). The passage then addresses a potential objection regarding inheritance: the general rule is that a killer is deprived of inheritance, but here the rebel may not be deprived if he believed his action was justified. The difference between the two Hanafi positions turns on whether corrupt interpretation can also remove deprivation of inheritance. Abu Yusuf restricts it to liability only, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad extend it to inheritance, conditional on the rebel's continued belief in his interpretation. If the rebel later admits he was on falsehood, he becomes liable and is deprived of inheritance. The passage concludes by applying this to a specific case of father and son on opposite sides, confirming the same distinction.

عادل (adil): A just person; in this context, one who is on the side of truth against the rebels, i.e., the people of justice (ahl al-adl).
تاویل فاسد (ta'wil fasid): A corrupt interpretation of Quran or Sunnah that leads to a wrong ruling, but which is sincerely believed by the one who makes it.
