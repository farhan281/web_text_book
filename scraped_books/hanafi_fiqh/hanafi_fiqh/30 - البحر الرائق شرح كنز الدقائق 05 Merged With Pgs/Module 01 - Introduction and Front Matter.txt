The Three Layers of a Hanafi Fiqh Commentary
Title page identifying matn, sharh, and hashiya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three textual layers (matn, sharh, hashiya) of a classical Hanafi fiqh work and explain the role of each layer in the transmission of rulings.

Core Idea
A Hanafi fiqh text has three layers: matn, sharh, hashiya – each building on the previous.

This lesson introduces the structure of a major Hanafi fiqh reference: Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The title page reveals a three-tiered textual tradition: the concise matn, the expansive sharh, and the critical hashiya. Understanding these layers is essential for navigating Hanafi source texts and knowing where to find rulings, proofs, and later refinements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (base text)
Locate the name of the matn and its author. The matn is Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Imam al-Nasafi (d. 710 AH). This text contains concise rulings without proof, intended for memorization.
The matn is the foundation; rulings are stated directly.
2
Identify the sharh (commentary)
Find the commentary on the matn: Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq by Ibn Nujaym (d. 970 AH). The sharh expands on the matn with proofs, explanations, and often resolves intra-school disputes.
Ibn Nujaym's sharh is a standard reference for Hanafi fiqh.
3
Identify the hashiya (marginal notes)
Note the supercommentary: Minhah al-Khaliq by Ibn 'Abidin (d. 1252 AH). The hashiya critiques and refines the sharh, often providing the later preferred position (mufta bihi).
Ibn 'Abidin's hashiya is highly authoritative in the later Hanafi school.
4
Understand the page layout
Read the editor's note: the matn is at the top, the sharh directly below, and the hashiya in the margins. This layout allows simultaneous study of all three layers.
This format is common in printed editions of Hanafi fiqh texts.

Commentary
This is the title page of the fifth volume of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The passage identifies the three scholarly layers of this edition: the base matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Imam al-Nasafi), the main sharh (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq by Ibn Nujaym), and the hashiya (Minhah al-Khaliq by Ibn 'Abidin). This format is common in many Hanafi fiqh publications to allow simultaneous study of all three textual layers. Each of these authors is a major figure in the Hanafi school: al-Nasafi (d. 710 AH) wrote the concise matn, Ibn Nujaym (d. 970 AH) wrote the expansive commentary, and Ibn 'Abidin (d. 1252 AH) wrote critical footnotes refining the commentary.

Key Terms
متن (matn): The base legal text being commented upon; here Kanz al-Daqa'iq by al-Nasafi.
شرح (sharh): A commentary that expands on the matn, providing proofs, explanations, and often resolving disputes.
حاشية (hashiyah): Marginal notes or supercommentary on the sharh, critically refining or explaining it.

Why Kitab al-Ibaq Follows Jihad: The Principle of Chapter Arrangement
Explains why Kitab al-Ibaq follows jihad, and discussions on using 'kitab' vs 'bab'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rationale behind the placement of Kitab al-Ibaq after Jihad in Hanafi texts and distinguish it from other chapters on lost property and foundlings.

Core Idea
Chapter arrangement in Hanafi fiqh follows purposeful logic: intentional human agency links Ibaq to Jihad.

Have you ever wondered why the book on runaway slaves is placed right after the book on holy struggle (jihad)? This lesson unpacks the reasoning from al-Bahr al-Raiq, showing that the ordering of legal topics is not arbitrary but reflects deep juridical insight. You will learn how the commentator uses the nature of the act—deliberate vs. passive—to justify the sequence, and why some chapters are called 'kitab' while others are 'bab'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The commentator mentions ibaq (runaway slave), laqit (foundling), and luqata (lost property). All are subject to loss, but the cause differs.
Ibaq involves deliberate action by the slave; laqit and luqata are passive losses.
2
Understand the analogy with Jihad
Jihad also involves deliberate human action (fighting). The commentator argues that since Ibaq involves a free agent choosing to flee, it is more similar to Jihad than passive losses are.
The phrase 'التعرض له بفعل فاعل مختار' is key.
3
Consider the counter-argument
Some might argue that laqit (foundling) has a higher risk of death and thus should follow Jihad. The commentator responds that the risk in Ibaq is to the master's benefit, not to the slave’s life, while laqit is at risk of death if uncared for.
The commentator affirms the arrangement of the earlier scholars (mashayikh) as more appropriate.
4
Analyze the naming: 'kitab' vs 'bab'
The commentator states that using 'kitab' for all three is more appropriate than 'bab' because the rulings of each are independent and not subsumed under another topic.
In Hanafi texts, luqata is sometimes treated as a bab, but the commentator prefers kitab for consistency.
5
Note the linguistic definition
The word 'abaqa' is defined as going away without fear or with hiding, and it inherently implies intention. Hence, the Hanafi legal definition of ibaq includes intent, unlike the 'dall' (lost) who has no intent.
This clarifies why ibaq is distinct from other loss categories.

Commentary
This passage is a front-matter discussion from al-Bahr al-Raiq explaining why the Book of Runaway Slaves (Kitab al-Ibaq) is placed after the Book of Jihad in Hanafi legal texts. The commentator addresses a potential confusion: why is ibaq treated as a separate kitab while luqata (lost property) is often treated as a bab (chapter)? He argues that ibaq, like laqit (foundling) and luqata, involves risk of loss, but ibaq differs because the slave's disappearance is caused by his own deliberate action (flight), whereas luqata is a thing that gets lost without intention. Thus, ibaq is more akin to jihad (where human action is involved) than to passive loss. He also notes that some scholars found it more appropriate to place laqit after jihad due to the higher risk of death for a foundling. The commentator then provides a linguistic definition of 'abaqa' from dictionaries, clarifying that flight (harab) inherently implies intention, so no further qualification is needed. He distinguishes the 'abaq' (runaway) from the 'dall' (lost animal or person) who has no intent to hide but is simply ignorant of the way.

لقيط (laqit): A foundling; an abandoned infant or child.
لقطة (luqata): Lost property that has no owner or whose owner is unknown.

Defining Partnership (Shirkah): Linguistic, Legal, and Types
Defines partnership linguistically, classifies types, and cites proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define partnership linguistically and legally, distinguish between partnership of ownership and partnership of contract, and cite the Quranic and Prophetic proofs for its legitimacy.

Core Idea
Partnership exists without contract through co-ownership; the Quran and Sunnah validate it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq opens the Book of Partnership by defining the term شركة both linguistically and in legal usage. It establishes the foundational division between ownership partnerships (inheritance/purchase) and contractual partnerships, and then proves the validity of partnership from the Quran, Sunnah, and reason. Understanding this classification is essential because the rulings differ based on the type.

Guided Path
1
Parse linguistic definition
Identify the key components: 'mixing of two shares' and distinguish between the action (the partners' mixing) and the resulting state of property.
The author emphasizes that partnership is the action, not the state.
2
Classify by type
Separate partnership of ownership (ملك) from partnership of contract (عقد). Note the pillar of each: mixing of property vs verbal expression.
This distinction determines the legal effect.
3
Sub-categorize ownership partnership
Identify compulsory (جبرية) and voluntary (اختيارية) based on cause (inheritance vs purchase).
Inheritance creates partnership automatically.
4
Analyze the proofs
Examine the Quranic verse (Nisa 4:12) as a direct proof for ownership partnership, and the hadith as supportive proof.
The Quran is qat'i al-thubut, the hadith is zanni, both validate.
5
Apply the ruling
State the permissibility of both types and the condition that each party must be capable of partnership (ahl al-shirkah).
This condition is mentioned in al-Muhit.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of partnership (شركة) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by defining the linguistic meaning as the mixing of two shares such that they become indistinguishable. The author distinguishes between the action of mixing (done by the partners) and the resulting state of the property (being mixed), emphasizing that the term 'partnership' applies to the human act, not the property itself. The passage then outlines two main types: partnership of ownership (شركة الملك) and partnership of contract (شركة العقد). For partnership of ownership, the pillar is physical mixing of the properties; for partnership of contract, the pillar is the verbal offer and acceptance. The legitimacy of partnership is established from three sources: the Quran (verse 4:12 implying co-ownership in inheritance), the Sunnah (the hadith of al-Sa'ib where the Prophet acknowledged a pre-Islamic partnership), and rational evidence (the general permissibility of cooperation). The matn line 'شركة الملك أن يملك اثنان عيناً إرثاً أو شراء' is introduced as the definition of partnership of ownership, with 'inheritance or purchase' given as examples, not restrictions. The passage also alludes to two sub-types of partnership of ownership: compulsory (جبرية, as in inheritance) and voluntary (اختيارية, as in purchase).

Key Terms
الشركة (al-shirkah): Partnership: linguistically, mixing of two shares; legally, a joint ownership or contract with shared benefits.
شركة الملك (shirkat al-milk): Partnership of ownership: joint possession of a specific asset without a partnership agreement, arising from inheritance, purchase, or other means.
شركة العقد (shirkat al-'aqd): Partnership by contract: a contractual agreement to pool capital and share profits, considered valid in Hanafi law.
جبرية (jabriyyah): Compulsory: created by law without choice, e.g., inheritance.
اختيارية (ikhtiyariyyah): Voluntary: created by choice, e.g., purchase or gift.
