Joint Liability in Shirkat al-A'mal: Istihsan and Its Limits
Explains istihsan basis for joint liability and its limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joint liability in shirkat al-a'mal and explain the istihsan reasoning behind it, including its limitations regarding acknowledgment of debts.

Core Idea
In shirkat al-a'mal, work accepted by one partner binds both due to istihsan, but this does not extend to acknowledgment of debts.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the legal reasoning behind the Hanafi position that in a partnership of work (shirkat al-a'mal), each partner is liable for work accepted by the other. The author contrasts the apparent qiyas (which would require a full Mufawadha partnership for such joint liability) with the Hanafi adoption of istihsan (juristic preference), which treats the partnership as akin to Mufawadha in two key aspects: guarantee of work and entitlement to wages. However, this treatment is limited; it does not extend to matters like acknowledgment of debts for consumed goods, which require explicit Mufawadha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text begins with the ruling: 'Everything one of them accepts binds both.' Understand that this is the default ruling for shirkat al-a'mal.
2
Locate the proof type
The text contrasts qiyas (which would require Mufawadha) with istihsan. The proof for istihsan is the inherent guarantee: 'this partnership necessitates guarantee.'
Istihsan overrides qiyas here.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Because accepting work creates a guarantee on both partners, the partnership runs in the manner of Mufawadha in two respects: guarantee of work and claim of compensation.
4
Note the limitation
The text clarifies that this treatment does not extend to acknowledgments of debts for consumed goods or past services; that would require explicit Mufawadha terms.
The example of soap or ash illustrates this.
5
Apply the distinction
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: if one partner acknowledges a debt for soap, the other is not liable unless they are Mufawadha. This shows the practical boundary of joint liability.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into the legal reasoning behind the ruling that each partner is bound by the work accepted by the other. The author presents two views: the literal (qiyas) view would require a Mufawadha partnership for such joint liability, but the Hanafi school adopts istihsan (juristic preference) to treat it like Mufawadha in two aspects: guarantee of work and entitlement to wages. This is explained by the fact that accepting work creates a guarantee, so the other partner is also liable. However, this treatment does not extend to other matters, such as acknowledgment of debts for consumed goods or past services: in such cases, the acknowledgment binds only the acknowledging partner, not the other, unless the partnership is explicitly Mufawadha. The text then discusses the difference between 'Inan and Mufawadha partnerships in this context, noting that if the partnership is absolute, it is 'Inan, and only the partner who directly caused the obligation is liable. The views of the Hanafi masters are recorded: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the earnings are shared even if one partner is incapacitated (istihsan), while Muhammad al-Shaybani follows qiyas in requiring personal causal connection. The practical takeaway is that such a partnership is valid, but the extent of joint liability depends on whether it is 'Inan (default) or Mufawadha (explicitly stipulated with conditions).

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas (analogy) due to equity or custom.
مفاوضة (mufawadha): A full partnership with mutual agency and surety, where each partner is liable for the other's actions.
عنان (inan): A limited partnership where each partner is an agent but not a surety for the other beyond the partnership.
تقبل العمل (taqabbul al-'amal): Accepting a job or contract on behalf of the partnership, creating liability for both partners.

Craft Partnership: Earnings When One Partner Works Alone
Intra-Hanafi dispute on profit sharing in craft partnerships.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on profit distribution in a craft partnership when only one partner works and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Core Idea
In a craft partnership, earnings are shared between partners as stipulated, even if only one works — because acceptance binds both.

This lesson examines the rulings of craft partnership (shirkat al-sana'i'), focusing on a key intra-Hanafi dispute: when one partner works alone, is the non-worker entitled to a share? The text from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the default rule and the differing views of the three Hanafi imams.

Guided Path
1
State the default rule
In both 'inan and mufawada partnerships, the wage is divided between the partners according to their stipulation, even if only one performs the work. This is because the non-worker is a guarantor for the work by virtue of acceptance.
The default applies whether the non-worker has an excuse or not.
2
Identify the dispute
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that both partners are liable for earnings and the wage is shared. Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees on the issue of acknowledgment, applying qiyas to restrict liability.
The dispute is specifically about the binding effect of one partner's acknowledgment.
3
Examine Muhammad's qiyas
Muhammad analogizes to a case where three persons accept work without partnership; if one does all, he only gets his share. He argues that only the worker deserves the wage.
This is qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) applied to the partnership context.
4
Understand the majority's istihsan
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf prefer istihsan: acceptance of work by one is considered like acceptance by both, due to mutual guarantee (kafala) in partnership. Thus both are entitled to the wage.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here because of the binding nature of the partnership contract.
5
Note the practical difference
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is the majority view: both partners share earnings even if only one works. Muhammad's view is not adopted for fatwa.
This ruling applies regardless of whether the non-worker had an excuse.

No partnership contract means each is responsible for their share; doing another’s share is volunteer work, not paid.

The key principle is that without a partnership contract, each person is liable for their proportional share of the work. The commentator begins by noting that this type is like the partnership of crafts in that it can be both مگاوضة (full delegation) and عنان (limited). This distinguishes شركة الوجوه from عنان, where profit can be unequal despite equal capital because it is analogous to مضاربة.

Key Terms
شركة الوجوه (shirkat al-wujuh): A partnership without capital where partners buy on credit using their social standing (وجاهة). Profit is based on the proportion of ownership in purchased goods, which is determined by each partner's guarantee.
مفاوضة (mufawada): A full-delegation partnership where partners are equal in capital, profit, and liability, and are mutual agents and sureties.
عنان ('inan): A limited partnership where partners contribute capital but may have unequal profit-sharing; no mutual suretyship.

Invalid Partnerships and Profit Distribution in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on invalid partnership with proof and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a partnership in mubahat is invalid, apply the rule that earnings belong to the worker with an equivalent wage owed to the other, and derive profit distribution in an invalid partnership according to capital contribution.

Core Idea
Profit in partnership follows capital; in invalid contracts, the worker gets the earnings and pays an equivalent wage.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq (commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq) establishes the Hanafi position on sharika fasida (invalid partnership). The commentator begins with the fundamental principle that profit is only legitimately earned through capital, work, or guarantee (daman). Using examples like collecting firewood and hunting, he demonstrates why partnerships in mubahat are invalid and how earnings and compensation are determined. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying contract law in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of invalidity
The partnership is invalid because it involves agency (wakala) in acquiring mubah property, but agency in mubah is not valid since the agent can own the property without an order.
Mubah refers to things that are free for anyone to take, like firewood in a public forest.
2
State the distribution rule
Earnings belong to the one who performs the work (taking and acquisition). If only one works, he keeps all; if both work, it is shared equally. The worker owes the other an equivalent wage (ujrat al-mithl) for any asset or assistance provided.
The wage is the fair market value for the work or use of property.
3
Apply the profit entitlement principle
Profit (or earnings) is only entitled through capital, work, or guarantee. The worker earns through work; the asset owner is entitled to an equivalent wage for the use of his property (equivalent to 'capital' in this context).
This principle is stated as: لَا يُسْتَحَقُّ الرِّبْحُ إِلَّا بِالْمَالِ أَوْ بِالْعَمَلِ أَوْ بِالضَّمَانِ
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Muhammad holds that the worker must pay the full equivalent wage (ujrat al-mithl), while Abu Yusuf limits it to half the value of the produce. This affects the final distribution.
The mufta bihi (operative view) is not explicitly settled here; later practice may favor Muhammad's view.
5
Infer the general rule for profit distribution in invalid partnerships
In any invalid partnership (even beyond mubahat), profit is distributed according to the proportion of capital contributed, regardless of any stipulation to the contrary.
This is analogous to yield in sharecropping (muzara'a) where the produce follows the seed.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqaiq, addressing the chapter on invalid partnership (sharika fasida). Profit in a fasid partnership follows capital, not agreement.

Unilateral Revocation of Partnership with Commodity Capital
Intra-Hanafi dispute on faskh of shirkah with urud, critique of al-Khulasa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on unilateral revocation of partnership when capital is commodities, compare al-Tahawi's opposing view, and apply the ruling to scenarios involving partner silence.

Core Idea
A partner can unilaterally revoke even with commodity capital, unlike mudarabah; silence does not imply consent to private purchase.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: can one partner unilaterally dissolve the partnership when the capital consists of commodities (urud) rather than cash? The preferred view (al-mukhtar) distinguishes shirkah from mudarabah, allowing revocation with commodities. We will work through the different authorities, the case of a partner's silence when another declares a private purchase, and the critique of al-Khulasa's contrary statement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Recognize that the dispute focuses on whether a partner can unilaterally revoke (faskh) a shirkah when the capital is commodities (urud). This is absent from the base text and arises only in later fatawa.
The ruling for cash capital is clear; commodities introduce uncertainty.
2
Examine the conflicting views
Al-Tahawi equates shirkah to mudarabah, denying revocation with commodities. The majority (including al-Sadr) distinguish, allowing it. Also, al-Khulasa claims no revocation without the other's consent.
Note the authority level: al-Tahawi vs. later authorities like al-Sadr.
3
Analyse the chosen view and its evidence
The preferred view (al-mukhtar) holds that one partner can revoke even with commodities. The example: after goods are bought, one partner says 'I will not work with you' and the other sells; the proceeds belong to the seller but he owes the value, as the statement is a revocation.
This shows that revocation is effective but the selling partner must account for the other's share.
4
Understand the critique and reconciliation
Fath al-Qadir corrects al-Khulasa's claim: a partner can unilaterally revoke; al-Khulasa's statement must be interpreted as referring to changing the subject matter (e.g., making a private purchase), not terminating the contract.
This subtle distinction preserves the authority of both texts.
5
Apply the distinction to the silence scenario
When a partner says 'I want to buy this slave girl for myself' and the other is silent, the purchase belongs to the partnership unless the speaker says 'yes' (explicitly making it private). Silence of the other does not imply consent because the inherent agency (wakalah hukmiyyah) of the partnership remains.
This differs from a pure agency where the principal's silence after the agent's self-appointment terminates the agency.

Silence in Partnership vs. Agency: When Does Silence Imply Consent?
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi reconciliation on the effect of silence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a partner's silence and an agent's silence in determining exclusive ownership of a purchase and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
In a partnership, silence never implies consent to exclude the other partner; the purchase remains joint property.

This lesson examines a critical distinction between two types of legal relationships: partnership (shirkah) and agency (wakalah). When one partner announces an intention to buy an item for himself, the other's silence does not operate as consent. But when an agent makes the same statement, the principal's silence does allow the agent to buy for himself. Understanding why hinges on the nature of the implicit agency that exists within a partnership. We also explore an intra-Hanafi dispute and its reconciliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize that the passage presents two separate cases: (1) one partner tells his partner he wants to buy a specific item for himself, and the other is silent; (2) an agent tells his principal he wants to buy a specific item for himself, and the principal is silent.
The ruling in each case is different.
2
Analyze the partnership scenario
In the partnership case, the purchase is on the partnership unless the silent partner explicitly says something indicating consent to exclusive ownership. The reason: the partnership contract creates an implicit agency (wakāla ḥukmiyya) that makes each partner an agent of the other for purchases. This implicit agency remains in effect as long as the partnership exists. The speaking partner's declaration alone does not dissolve this implicit agency for that particular purchase; the silent partner's silence does not amount to consent because he might be relying on the common ownership principle.
The key is that in partnership, one cannot unilaterally change the status of a purchase from joint to exclusive.
3
Analyze the agency scenario
In the pure agency case, the agent's statement 'I want to buy it for myself' is effectively a dismissal of himself from the agency regarding that purchase. The principal's silence is considered consent because if he had an objection, he would speak up. Since the agent can dismiss himself at any time (whether the principal consents or not), and the principal's silence indicates acceptance, the purchase is exclusively for the agent.
The agent's declaration terminates his agency for that specific transaction.
4
Compare the legal effects and identify the subtle difference
The difference lies in the nature of the relationship. In a partnership, there is an ongoing implicit agency for all purchases; one partner cannot selectively remove that agency for a single item without dissolving the entire partnership. The silent partner's silence is ambiguous — it could mean he relies on the partnership's default rule of shared ownership. In contrast, in a standalone agency, the agent's declaration about a specific item is clear; the principal's silence is a meaningful response because the agency is limited to that specific task.
This distinction is 'لطيف' (subtle) as the text says.
5
Understand the intra-Hanafi dispute and its reconciliation
The text reports a conflict: al-Khulāṣa says 'one partner cannot dissolve without the other's consent,' while Fatḥ al-Qadīr calls this a mistake and says a partner can dissolve alone even with goods (goods being the partnership capital). The commentator al-Maqdisī and Ibn Nujaym reconcile: al-Khulāṣa meant changing the required effect of the partnership (i.e., making a specific purchase exclusive) requires consent, not dissolving the entire contract. Thus, complete dissolution of the partnership can be done unilaterally, but altering the joint-ownership nature of a particular purchase needs explicit approval.
This reconciliation preserves the validity of both statements.

Rulings on Three Partners and Denial of Partnership
Rulings on three partners and consequences of denying partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the dissolution of muqawada partnership when one partner denies it, and explain the buyer's options after separation.

Core Idea
Denial of partnership by a single partner dissolves the entire partnership and makes the denier a guarantor.

Partnership in Hanafi law comes with strict rules about dissolution. This lesson covers two important scenarios: the inability to partially dissolve a three-person muqawada partnership without the absent partner, and the automatic dissolution that occurs when a partner denies the partnership. Understanding these rules protects both partners and third-party buyers.

The passage presents two distinct rulings: first, about three partners where one is absent; second, about denial of partnership by one partner.
2

When three muqawada partners want to dissolve, they cannot do so partially or without the absent partner. The partnership must be dissolved by all or not at all.
This shows the mutual dependency in muqawada.

If one partner denies the partnership, the partnership dissolves immediately, and that partner becomes liable for half the property in his hand.

Burden of Proof in Mufawada Claims
Rulings on proof and evidence when one claims mufawada partnership and the other denies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the burden of proof in mufawada claims, identify when testimony is sufficient, and contrast the positions of Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Denier's word stands; claimant must prove partnership and shared property.

When one party claims a mufawada partnership and the other denies, the default procedural rule places the burden on the claimant. But what exactly must the claimant prove? This lesson parses the requirements for valid testimony and the consequences of different forms of evidence, including an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a simple declaration of mufawada suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default procedural rule
Note that the property is in the denier's hand; the denier's word is taken, and the claimant must bring evidence.
2
Distinguish types of testimony
Examine the three forms of testimony: (1) mufawada + property shared, (2) mufawada only (with timing dispute), (3) mufawada + property in denier's hand.
Each has different acceptance conditions.
3
Analyze post-judgment claims
After judgment, the possessor may claim property as his own (inheritance, gift, etc.) but the admissibility depends on the original testimony.
If testimony covered property sharing, such claims are barred; if only mufawada, they are accepted per Muhammad.
4
Understand the intra-Hanafi dispute
Muhammad accepts limited testimony (mufawada only) for subsequent claims; Abu Yusuf rejects it.
This affects the outcome when the possessor later claims ownership.
5
Apply the rule for share disagreement
If both agree on mufawada but differ on shares, the property is divided equally (half each).
This is a default rule when evidence is lacking.

And in al-Zahiriyya: If someone claims that he had a mufawada partnership with another and the property is in the hand of the denier, then the word is that of the denier, and the burden of proof is on the claimant. If he produces evidence, then if the witnesses testify that it was a mufawada partnership and that the property in his hand is between them or is from their partnership, it is accepted and judgment is given accordingly. If they testify only to the mufawada, al-Sarakhsi mentioned its acceptance, and Khwahar Zadeh mentioned its acceptance if they testify in the session of the claim. Commentary
This passage from al-Zahiriyya details the rules of evidence when one party claims a mufawada partnership and the other denies it. The default is that the denier's statement is taken, and the claimant must bring proof. The proof must be specific: witnesses must testify to both the existence of the mufawada and that the property is shared or from the partnership. There is a difference of opinion between al-Sarakhsi and Khwahar Zadeh regarding the timing of testimony. After judgment, if the possessor of property claims it as his own through inheritance, gift, or charity, his claim is barred if the earlier testimony covered both the mufawada and the property being half; but if the testimony was limited to the mufawada, Muhammad accepts his claim while Abu Yusuf does not. If the possessor claims to have received the property from the claimant, it is accepted. Finally, if both agree on the existence of mufawada but disagree on the shares, they are divided equally (half each).

Key Terms
الجاحد (al-jāḥid): The one who denies the partnership claim.
مدعي المفاوضة (muddaʿī al-mufāwaḍa): The claimant who asserts the existence of a mufawada partnership.

Key Takeaway
In mufawada disputes, the burden is on the claimant to bring evidence that covers both the partnership and the shared nature of the property; mere testimony of mufawada may not suffice, and post-judgment claims depend on the scope of that testimony.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership by Permission: Gift or Debt?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership and compensation when a mufawada partner authorizes purchase for intercourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership of a slave girl purchased by a mufawada partner with permission for intercourse, and state the legal reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Permission for intercourse in a mufawada partnership triggers a gift, not a sale, of the partner's share.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands a matn ruling on mufawada partnership. When one partner authorizes the other to buy a slave girl specifically for intercourse, Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer owns her exclusively without compensating the authorizer, while the two Sahibayn require compensation. The dispute turns on whether the permission implies a gift or a debt repayment. We will dissect the legal reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two positions
Recognize the factual situation: one mufawada partner authorizes the other to buy a slave girl for intercourse. The buyer then purchases her. Under Abu Hanifa, the buyer owns her for free. Under the Sahibayn, the buyer must pay half the price to the authorizer.
Mufawada partnership means full agency and guarantee.
2
Trace Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that because intercourse requires full ownership, the only way to reconcile the purchase with partnership principles is to treat the permission as a gift (hibah) of the authorizer's share. The sale cannot give partial ownership, so the permission implicitly transfers the share as a gift.
Gift is established in the permission itself.
3
Trace the Sahibayn's reasoning
The Sahibayn analogize to buying food or clothing for personal use: the buyer used partnership funds to pay a debt owed specifically by the authorizer (since the benefit is for him). Therefore, the authorizer must reimburse half the price. They see the permission as a license to buy, not a gift.
They distinguish: food and clothing are necessities excepted by custom; the slave girl is not a necessity.
4
Compare the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the authorizer gets nothing. Under the Sahibayn, he gets half the price back. The seller can claim the full price from either partner due to mutual guarantee.
The difference is 50% of the price.
5
Note the restriction to explicit permission
The ruling only applies when explicit permission is given. Silence or simply buying without authorization results in shared ownership. Silence does not imply permission.
This highlights the importance of explicit consent in partnership transactions.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding a matn ruling regarding a mufawada (full) partnership. The matn states that if one partner authorizes the other to buy a slave girl specifically for intercourse, and the other does so, the slave girl belongs exclusively to the buyer, and he does not owe the partner anything. The commentator clarifies that this is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa. The two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that the buyer must refund half the price to the authorizing partner, because the buyer used partnership funds to pay a debt owed specifically by the authorizer (since the intercourse is for the authorizer's benefit). They analogize this to buying food or clothing: if a partner buys food or clothing for himself with partnership money, he must compensate the partnership because ownership vests solely in him. Abu Hanifa's reasoning is different: by authorizing the purchase for intercourse, the partner implicitly gifts his share of the slave girl to the buyer because intercourse is only lawful with full ownership, and the sale cannot be structured to give partial ownership. Therefore, the slave girl becomes exclusively the buyer's as a gift, and no compensation is due. The passage also notes that the seller can demand the full price from either partner because the debt is connected to trade activities, and mufawada includes mutual guarantee. It ends by emphasizing that the ruling applies only when explicit permission is given; silence does not count as permission. If the slave girl were bought without any prior discussion, she would be jointly owned.

Key Terms
مفاوضة (mufawadah): A full partnership where partners are mutual agents and guarantors in all matters of trade.
أمة (amah): A female slave.
هبة (hibah): A gift; transfer of ownership without compensation.
