Mutual Qadhf Between Spouses: Hadd Takes Precedence Over Li'an
Ruling on husband-wife qadhf and li'an

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband and wife mutually accuse each other of zina, and explain why hadd is applied instead of li'an.

Core Idea
When spouses accuse each other of zina, both receive hadd; li'an is inapplicable.

This passage addresses a specific scenario where a husband calls his wife 'adulteress' and she responds in kind. The key question: does this trigger the special li'an procedure, or the standard hadd for qadhf? The Hanafi school rules that both receive hadd, because mutual accusations remove the possibility of li'an. Understanding the reasoning requires grasping the relationship between li'an and hadd. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply this rule and appreciate the logic behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
State the ruling: when a husband and wife call each other 'adulterer' or similar, both are guilty of qadhf and must receive the hadd punishment. No li'an is performed.
Based on the text starting with 'وله لا لعان'.
2
Analyze the Reasoning (Talil)
Why does hadd prevail? Because they are both 'qadhifan' (slanderers). Hadd is the right of Allah, and li'an is a substitute designed to avert hadd in cases of doubt. Here, there is no doubt. Also, applying hadd first makes the person ineligible for li'an.
The text says: 'لأنهما قاذفان الحد' and 'وفي البداية بالحد إبطال اللعان'.
3
Examine the Expanded Scenario
Consider the case where the husband insults the wife and her mother. The order of lawsuits matters: if the mother sues first, the husband receives hadd for slandering her, which disqualifies him from li'an with the wife. If the wife sues first, li'an is performed between the spouses, and then the mother's lawsuit leads to a separate hadd for the husband.
Shows the priority: hadd for a third party takes immediate effect and prevents li'an.
4
Extract the General Principle
Hadd and li'an cannot coexist for the same person. When both are potentially applicable, hadd takes precedence. Li'an is 'in the meaning' of hadd (al-li'an fi ma'na al-hadd), so they are mutually exclusive.
Aligns with the Hanafi maxim that hudud are averted by doubts, but here there is no doubt.

Commentary
This passage deals with mutual accusations of zina between husband and wife. The general rule is that if a husband calls his wife 'adulteress' (or similar) and she responds in kind, both are guilty of qadhf and receive the hadd punishment; li'an (the special procedure for spouses) does not apply because li'an is designed to avoid hadd when there is a possibility of truth. Since both have insulted each other, the hadd is enforced. The author then explains a more complex scenario: if the husband says to his wife 'you are an adulteress, daughter of an adulteress', and the wife's mother (as the victim of qadhf) sues the husband first, the husband receives hadd for slandering the mother, and that hadd disqualifies him from li'an (since he is now a convicted slanderer). If, however, the wife sues first (for being called adulteress), then li'an is performed between the spouses (the qadi prescribes li'an), and afterwards if the mother sues, the husband is flogged for qadhf against the mother. This shows the priority: hadd and li'an cannot both apply to the same person; li'an is a substitute for hadd in marital accusations.

Key Terms
لعان (li'an): A special oath-swearing procedure between spouses when a husband accuses his wife of adultery without witnesses.
الدرء (al-daf'): Repelling or averting punishment; here, the idea that li'an is used to avert hadd in appropriate cases.

A Legal Trick (Hila) to Avoid Oath Violation in Meat Purchase
A legal trick (hila) to avoid violating an oath about meat weight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi hila for conflicting oaths about meat weight and explain why it is permissible.

Core Idea
Cooking meat before weighing prevents oath violation when spouses swear conflictingly about its weight.

This passage from Khizanat al-Fatawa presents a clever legal stratagem (hila) to resolve a standoff between a husband and wife over the weight of meat. Both have sworn conflicting oaths, and without intervention, one would inevitably break an oath or trigger a divorce. The Hanafi solution is simple yet profound: cook the meat before it is weighed. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this permissible hila and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting oaths
The wife swears the meat is less than a mann. The husband swears a conditional divorce: if it is not exactly a mann, his wife is divorced. These oaths are mutually contradictory if the actual weight is known.
A mann is a unit of weight, approximately 1 kg.
2
Recognize the dilemma
If the meat is weighed and found to be less, the wife's oath is true but the husband's condition triggers divorce. If it is exactly a mann, the wife breaks her oath. Either way, someone would sin or suffer.
The goal is to avoid both sin and harm.
3
State the hila
The trick is to cook the meat before any weighing. Cooking causes water loss, reducing weight and making the original weight indeterminable.
The hila does not involve lying or deception.
4
Explain the legal reasoning
Once cooked, the subject of the oath (the raw meat's weight) cannot be verified. Therefore, neither oath can be proven false. The condition for divorce becomes unverifiable, so it is not triggered. Both oaths are considered fulfilled.
This relies on the principle that impossible conditions are ignored.
5
Note the source and authority
This hila is recorded in Khizanat al-Fatawa, a Hanafi fatwa compilation. It is a permissible stratagem, reflecting the Hanafi school's flexibility in using legal devices to prevent hardship.
Not all schools permit such hilal; the Hanafi school is known for this approach.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal trick (hila) from the Hanafi tradition to resolve a conflict of oaths regarding the weight of meat. A husband buys a mann (a unit of weight) of meat. His wife swears that the meat is less than a mann, and the husband swears (conditional divorce) that if it is not a mann, his wife is divorced. The apparent dilemma is that one of them must be breaking their oath. The trick lies in cooking the meat before weighing it. Once cooked, meat loses weight due to water evaporation, so the original weight cannot be verified. Thus, neither oath can be proven false, and both oaths are considered valid or avoided. This hila is based on the principle that once the subject of the oath is altered (by cooking), the condition becomes impossible to verify, so both parties are considered to have fulfilled their oaths. This demonstrates the Hanafi approach to using legal stratagems to avoid sin and conflict. Note that this is a specific fatwa from Khizanat al-Fatawa, not necessarily from Zahir al-Riwayah, and it applies only when the weight is uncertain after cooking.

Key Terms
حيلة (hila): A legal stratagem or trick that is permissible under shari'ah to achieve a legitimate aim or avoid a sin, used within the bounds of the law.
من (mann): A unit of weight; in the context, a mann is approximately 2 ratls (around 1 kg) but varies regionally. Here it is used as a standard for the oath.
