Testimony on Stale Hadd Crimes: Pure Hudud vs. Qadhf
Ruling on witnesses testifying to a delayed hadd crime except qadhf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony regarding a stale hadd crime, explain the exception for qadhf, and identify the talil (legal reasoning) for the rule.

Core Idea
Testimony on a stale hadd is rejected due to suspicion; qadhf is excepted because it includes a personal right.

After dealing with general rules of testimony, the Hanafi jurists address the effect of delay. This lesson examines the critical distinction between pure hudud (rights of Allah) and those involving personal rights, and how staleness affects the acceptance of testimony. The text from Bahr al-Raiq provides the foundational ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Locate the statement: 'شهدوا بحد متقادم سوى حد القذف لم يحد'. This is the core ruling: testimony on a stale hadd (except qadhf) results in no hadd punishment.
Note that the witnesses are also liable for financial penalty (يضمن المال) if they caused loss.
2
Understand the Talil (Reason)
The text explains: 'والأصل أن الحدود الخالصة حقاً لله تعالى تبطل بالتقادم'. The rationale is that a witness who delays had an opportunity to conceal; his later testimony is suspected of being motivated by enmity or grudge.
If the delay was not for concealment, the witness becomes a sinner (fasiq), which also invalidates his testimony.
3
Recognize the Exception for Qadhf
The exception is stated: 'بخلاف التقادم في حد القذف'. Qadhf is treated differently because it involves the right of the accused to clear his name (حق العباد).
This distinction is crucial: pure hudud are rights of Allah; qadhf is a mixed right.
4
Compare with Other Schools
Imam al-Shafi'i may accept testimony even with delay if witnesses are reliable. The Hanafi school prioritises the suspicion caused by delay to avoid fitna.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different considerations of public interest.

Commentary
This passage from Bahr al-Raiq deals with the chapter on testimony concerning zina and retraction. The author clarifies that if witnesses come forward to testify about a hadd crime that occurred long ago (muqaddam), their testimony is not accepted for hudud that are solely the right of Allah (like zina, theft, drinking), except for the hadd of qadhf (false accusation of zina) because that also involves the personal right of the accused. The reasoning is that a witness who delays had an opportunity to conceal, so his later testimony is suspect. The text then discusses a separate issue regarding the caliph and the execution of hudud: the caliph cannot be subjected to hadd by himself, but retaliation and financial penalties are possible. Then it returns to the commentary on the matn, explaining that 'testifying to a stale hadd' means testifying about the cause of the hadd, not the hadd itself. The principle that pure hudud are invalidated by staleness is based on the witness's choice between two good deeds. For qadhf, staleness does not invalidate because it involves the right of the individual to clear his name. The passage ends with a note on retraction after confession, but the text cuts off.

Key Terms
متقادم (mutaqadim): Stale or old; refers to a hadd crime that occurred long before the testimony.
حد القذف (hadd al-qadhf): The prescribed punishment for false accusation of zina, which is 80 lashes. It is treated differently because it involves the right of the accused.

The Caliph's Immunity from Hadd and Subjection to Qisas
Ruling that the caliph is subject to qisas but not hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the caliph is subject to qisas and financial penalties but not to hadd punishments, and derive the implications for Islamic governance.

Core Idea
The caliph cannot enforce hadd on himself, but qisas and financial claims proceed because they are rights of individuals.

This section of Bahr al-Raiq addresses the unique legal status of the caliph. As the executor of hudud, he cannot be subjected to them himself due to lack of authority over himself. However, in matters of personal rights (qisas and financial penalties), he is like any other person because the victim's right supersedes. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Categories
The text distinguishes between 'rights of people' (qisas and financial penalties) and 'rights of Allah' (hudud). The caliph is treated differently in each.
Qisas includes retaliation for murder or injury; financial penalties include compensation.
2
Understand Subjection to Qisas
The caliph is subject to qisas because it is a right of the victim or his heirs. The Imam acts as a representative for execution, but the right belongs to the individual. Since the victim or his guardian can demand execution, the Imam is like any other person.
The text notes that qisas can be carried out even without a judge's ruling; the judge's role is to enable the guardian, not a condition.
3
Understand Immunity from Hadd
The caliph is not subject to hadd because the hadd is a right of Allah, and the Imam is the one tasked with its execution. He cannot execute it on himself, and no one has authority over him to execute it. Therefore, the obligation is impossible, so it does not apply.
The act of his deputy (na'ib) is considered like his own act, but that does not resolve the impossibility of self-execution.
4
Note the Exception for Local Governors
The exemption is specifically for the caliph (the supreme Imam). A local governor (amir al-balda) is subject to hudud by order of the higher Imam.
This distinguishes between those with ultimate authority and those subordinate.
