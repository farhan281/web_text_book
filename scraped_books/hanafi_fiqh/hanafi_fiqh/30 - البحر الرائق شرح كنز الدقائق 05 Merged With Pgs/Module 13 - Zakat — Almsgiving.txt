Rulings on Accepting Islam After the Call
Explains consequences when enemies accept Islam after da'wa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the status of land and property when a disbeliever accepts Islam after the call, and identify the exceptions for Arab polytheists and apostates.

Core Idea
When an enemy accepts Islam, his land becomes 'ushri and he keeps his property; jizya is not an option for Arab polytheists or apostates.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the consequences when disbelievers, after receiving the Islamic call (da'wa), choose to accept Islam. It clarifies the Hanafi position that jizya is only offered to certain groups, and that conversion transforms land status from potential kharaj to 'ushr. The commentary also addresses recommendations regarding moving to Dar al-Islam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage concerns the scenario where disbelievers are called to Islam; if they accept, certain legal consequences follow. The ruling is that their land becomes 'ushri and they retain their property.
The base text says 'if they accept Islam, otherwise to jizya'.
2
Locate the dalil
The ruling is based on the well-known hadith about jizya, which implies the obligation to call to jizya if Islam is not accepted. The imperative indicates wujub.
The hadith is not quoted; it is referenced as 'al-hadith al-ma'ruf'.
3
Note the exception
The commentator restricts the general statement: jizya is not accepted from Arab polytheists or apostates. For these groups, only Islam or the sword applies.
This exception appears later in the matn according to the author.
4
Understand the consequences of acceptance
If they accept Islam, their wealth is left to them, their lands become 'ushriyya, and they are commanded to move to Dar al-Islam unless their land is contiguous. If they refuse to move, they have no share in public funds.
The rule about moving is based on dislike (karahah) of residing in Dar al-Harb.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The commentator explains the consequences when disbelievers, after being called to Islam, actually accept Islam. The matn states that if they accept Islam, fine; otherwise, they are called to jizya. The commentator clarifies that this general statement is restricted: for Arab polytheists and apostates, jizya is not accepted; only Islam or the sword. When they accept Islam, their property is left to them and their land becomes 'ushri (subject to 'ushr tax) instead of kharaj. They are recommended to move to Dar al-Islam because living in Dar al-Harb as a Muslim is makruh. If they refuse to move, they are told they will have no share in public funds. However, if their land is contiguous to Dar al-Islam, they are not required to move. The commentary also quotes al-Tatarkhaniyya that the Imam should explain the amount and timing of jizya if they are called to it, specifying rates for rich, poor, and middle.

Key Terms
الجزية (al-jizyah): Poll tax imposed on dhimmis in exchange for protection and exemption from military service.
أرض عشرية (ard 'ushriyyah): Land that is subject to 'ushr (tithe) tax, typically land owned by Muslims or acquired peacefully.

Hanafi Ruling on Khums: Three Shares and the Cancellation of the Prophet's Share
Explaining the five shares of khums, the status of dhawi al-qurba, and the cancellation of the Prophet's share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi division of khums into three shares, identify the correct recipients, and explain why the Prophet's share is canceled, using the evidence from the Caliphs' practice and hadith.

Core Idea
Khums is divided into three shares (orphans, poor, wayfarer); the Prophet's share is canceled after his death, not transferred to the caliph.

The distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils) is a key fiqh issue with differences between madhhabs. This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi position: khums is divided into three shares for orphans, the poor, and wayfarers, and the poor among the Prophet's relatives are included in these shares. The passage also proves that the Prophet's share is canceled after his death, contrary to the Shafi'i view. Let's study the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on khums distribution
Read the first sentences: 'Radkh... khums is for orphans, the poor, and the wayfarer.' Note that radkh is a small reward, not a full share. The khums itself is divided into three categories.
2
Analyze the proof from the Caliphs' practice
The text says the four Rightly-Guided Caliphs divided khums into three shares. This is a key proof (dalil) for the Hanafi position. Why is this considered authoritative? Because it reflects the practice after the Prophet.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith 'O Banu Hashim...' is cited. Identify the reasoning: the Prophet (PBUH) compensated Banu Hashim with khums of khums because they are prohibited from receiving charity. This compensation is for the poor among them, not the rich.
The hadith is used to show that the khums is given to support the poor, not as a right of kinship.
4
Understand the inclusion of poor relatives
The text clarifies that only poor relatives are included in the three shares. Rich relatives have no right. This is deduced from the hadith and the Caliphs' practice.
The orphan's share is based on poverty, not orphanhood; mentioning 'orphan' removes the misunderstanding that minors cannot earn spoils.
5
Learn the cancellation of the Prophet's share
The Prophet's share of khums was canceled after his death, just like his special selection (safi). The proof is that the Caliphs did not take that share; they divided it into three. The Shafi'i view that it goes to the caliph is refuted.
Also note that the mention 'Allah's share' is for blessing, not a separate recipient.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the distribution of khums (one-fifth of spoils). The author states that radkh (a token reward) is given to some participants, not a full share. The khums itself is divided into three shares: for orphans, the poor, and the wayfarer (ibn al-sabil). Among the relatives (dhawi al-qurba), only the poor are entitled; rich relatives get nothing. The proof is the practice of the four Rightly-Guided Caliphs who divided it into three shares, and a hadith where the Prophet (PBUH) tells Banu Hashim that they have been compensated with khums of khums for not receiving charity. The compensation is for the poor among them. The Prophet's reason for giving them is their nearness in support (nusra), not merely blood relation. The orphan's entitlement is based on poverty, not orphanhood, but mentioning 'orphan' removes the assumption that a minor cannot earn spoils through jihad. The author also discusses the status of a slave fighting: if unauthorized, he gets radkh by istihsan; if authorized by the imam, he gets a full share? Actually the text says there is an explicit statement in al-Fath that it's the same whether permitted by master or not. Then the passage clarifies that the fatwa is to give to poor relatives, not rich. The author then specifies that 'relatives' here means Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib only. The mention of 'Allah's share' in the Quranic verse is for blessing, not a separate recipient. The Prophet's share is canceled after his death, analogous to safi (the Prophet's special selection). Imam al-Shafi'i's view that it goes to the caliph is refuted by the Caliphs' practice of dividing it into three shares only.

Ruling on 'Ushri Lands and Their Boundaries
Defines 'ushri lands and their boundaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on which lands are 'ushri and identify the boundaries of Arab lands as defined by the early Hanafi masters.

Core Idea
Arab lands and lands conquered by force and distributed are 'ushri—never kharaji.

This lesson opens the chapter on 'Ushr, Kharaj, and Jizya by establishing a fundamental distinction: some lands are subject to 'ushr (tithe) while others are subject to kharaj (land tax). The author clarifies that Arab lands, lands whose inhabitants accepted Islam, and lands conquered by force and then distributed among the fighters are all 'ushri. This ruling is based on the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the Rightly Guided Caliphs, who never imposed kharaj on Arab soil. We will explore the proof, the rationale, and the differing definitions of Arab lands' boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Find the categorical statement that defines which lands are 'ushri. The author says: 'The land of the Arabs and what its people accepted Islam or was conquered by force and distributed among the fighters is 'ushri.'
This is the core ruling of the passage.
2
Locate the Dalil
The evidence is the practice of the Prophet and the Caliphs: they never took kharaj from Arab lands. This is a dalil naqli based on absence of transmission.
The proof is negative: if kharaj had been taken, it would have been reported.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
Understand why the absence of kharaj proves 'ushri status. The author argues that kharaj can only be imposed when the inhabitants are permitted to remain in disbelief, but Arab polytheists were only offered Islam or the sword. Therefore, their lands cannot be kharaji.
This links the ruling to the principle that kharaj requires continued disbelief.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf on Boundaries
The passage provides varying definitions of Arab lands from Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and al-Karkhi. These are not disputes over the ruling but over the precise geographical limits.
The boundaries are confirmations (tawkid) and there is flexibility (manduha).
5
Apply the Classification
Using the given boundaries, identify whether a specific region (e.g., Hijaz, Yemen) is 'ushri or kharaji. Also apply the categories: land whose people accepted Islam, or conquered and distributed, is 'ushri.
This trains the student to classify lands in practice.

Kharaj vs 'Ushr on Conquered Lands: The Irrigation Condition Dispute
Determining kharaj or 'ushr on lands conquered by force with intra-Hanafi dispute on irrigation condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on lands conquered by force (عنوة) and left with inhabitants, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the irrigation condition, and explain why the unconditional kharaj position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Conquered land with inhabitants is always kharaji — the condition of river irrigation is rejected by the majority.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq delves into a critical intra-Hanafi disagreement: is the classification of conquered lands as kharaji dependent on the source of irrigation? The author, Ibn Nujaym, clarifies the relied-upon position and refutes a qualification attributed to Imam Muhammad in Al-Jami' al-Saghir. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi scholars weigh textual traditions against foundational principles (usul).

Guided Path
1
Scope the ruling
Identify the types of land discussed: Sawad (conquered by force, inhabitants left), lands conquered by treaty, and lands divided among Muslims. Note the base rule: kharaj for the first two, 'ushr for divided lands.
The passage begins with 'Umar's imposition on Sawad.
2
Find the qualification
Locate the condition in Al-Jami' al-Saghir (via Al-Hidayah) that restricts kharaj to lands irrigated by river water; spring/rain-irrigated lands would be 'ushr.
The qualification is attributed to Imam Muhammad in some sources.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Understand the positions: Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf hold that even transfer of 'ushr land to a disbeliever makes it kharaji, so initially it cannot be 'ushr. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that transferred 'ushr land remains 'ushr, but even he would not impose 'ushr initially.
The author uses this ikhtilaf to argue against the qualification.
4
Evaluate the author's critique
Follow the author's reasoning: the qualification contradicts the established principle that kharaj is more appropriate for disbelievers, and even Muhammad's view does not support initial 'ushr. The author rejects the condition.
The author calls the wording in Al-Hidayah 'problematic' (مشكل).

This summary clarifies the scope of the irrigation condition.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq discusses the classification of conquered lands into kharaj or 'ushr. The author first explains the term Sawad, referring to the Sawad of Iraq, its boundaries and area, as set by Umar (رضي الله عنه) with consensus of the Companions. Classification of Agricultural Land: Kharaji vs. 'Ushri in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on land tax based on water source and reviver's religion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for classifying agricultural land as kharaji or 'ushri based on the irrigation source and the religion of the reviver, and apply it to a hypothetical scenario.

Core Idea
Land revived by a Muslim is 'ushri if spring-irrigated, kharaji if river-irrigated; for a non-Muslim, it is always kharaji — 'ushr is exclusive to Muslims.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a key classification in Hanafi land tax law: whether agricultural land is subject to kharaj (a land tax) or 'ushr (a religious tithe on produce). The classification depends on two factors: the irrigation source (river vs. spring) and the religion of the person reviving the land. The rule is derived from the practice of the Companions, particularly 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, and is supported by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being classified? The act of reviving dead (unowned) land. The ruling is that if a Muslim revives it, the land becomes either kharaji or 'ushri depending on irrigation; if a non-Muslim revives it, it is always kharaji.
The ruling on the land type determines whether kharaj or 'ushr is wajib.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites the action of 'Umar (RA) imposing kharaj on Egypt and the consensus (ijma) of the Companions on Syria. This is the proof for the legitimacy of kharaj on conquered lands.
The dalil is the practice of the Rashidun caliphs and ijma.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this practice prove the rule? The Companions' consensus establishes that kharaj is an obligatory levy on land that is not subject to 'ushr. The distinction by irrigation source is then derived from the analogy (qiyas) that river water requires no effort, so the land remains as conquered (kharaji); spring water requires effort, so the land becomes 'ushri (like Muslim-revived land).
This is a form of istidlal (inference) from the general principle of kharaj being a tax on land.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Hanafi position is that the irrigation source matters for a Muslim reviver. The Shafi'i school generally does not make this distinction; they consider any conquered land as kharaji regardless of irrigation.
This ikhtilaf is noted in the pre-populated analytical toolbox.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is the one stated in the passage: the detail applies only to Muslims; for non-Muslims, kharaj is always due. This is supported by the texts of Al-Hidayah and Al-Tabyin.
The authority weight is Zahir al-Riwayah.

Determining Tax on Revived Land: Proximity vs Water
Commentary on criteria for 'ushr/kharaj on revived land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi criteria for classifying revived dead land as 'ushriyya or kharajiyya and apply them to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Revived land's tax status follows its proximity or its water source — two valid Hanafi views.

When a Muslim revives barren land, a key question arises: is it subject to 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax)? The Hanafi school has two answers: Imam Abu Yusuf looks at the land's proximity to already cultivated land, while Imam Muhammad looks at the water source used for irrigation. This lesson unpacks their reasoning, including Abu Yusuf's analogy of the courtyard, and highlights the exception for non-Muslims.

Guided Path

2

Abu Yusuf determines the tax status by the nature of adjacent land: if near kharaj land, it is kharajiyya; if near 'ushr land, it is 'ushriyya. He supports this with the analogy of the courtyard (fina' al-dar): the owner of a house benefits from the adjacent courtyard even though it is not owned, because of proximity.
He also argues that reviving land close to already cultivated land is prohibited, reinforcing the principle.

Imam Muhammad considers the water source: if the land is irrigated with water that is subject to kharaj, the land becomes kharajiyya; otherwise it is 'ushriyya.
This view focuses on the source of irrigation rather than location.

The entire discussion is restricted to a Muslim reviver. A non-Muslim who revives dead land must pay kharaj absolutely, regardless of proximity or water source, because non-Muslims are not eligible for 'ushr (a purely Islamic obligation).
This is a clear rule across both views.

1
Memorise the rates
For land suitable for cultivation (crops): 1 sa' and 1 dirham per jarib. For ratbah (moist fodder): 5 dirhams per jarib. For continuous vineyards and palm trees: 10 dirhams per jarib.
2
Understand the rationale
The rates are based on the expenses (mu'an) of cultivation. Vineyards have the lightest expenses, so the tax is highest (10 dirhams). Farms have high expenses, so tax is lowest (1 sa'+1 dirham). Ratbah is in between (5 dirhams).
3
Define key terms
Jarib: unit of area (60x60 royal cubits). Sa': volume measure (8 ratls Iraqi). Dirham: coin (7/10 mithqal). Ratbah: moist fodder like alfalfa, cucumber, etc.
4
Note conditions for applicability
Land must be suitable for cultivation; if not suitable, no tax. For ratbah, continuity (ittisal) is required: if the ratbah is scattered and the land is planted with other crops in between, no tax on the ratbah. Vineyards must be continuous.
5
Recognise the authority
These rates are from the action of 'Umar (ra) with the consent of the Companions, thus forming an ijma'. They are firmly established in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn statement concerning two distinct but related issues: the exceptional status of Basra as 'ushriyya land, and the prescribed rates of kharaj per jarib for different types of crops. The commentator begins by addressing why Basra is classified as 'ushriyya despite being located within the territory of kharaj land. The matn explicitly states it as such, and the commentator explains that this is an exception to the general rule (qiyas) made by the consensus (ijma') of the Companions. They set the 'ushr upon Basra even though it was conquered by force (anwatan), which normally makes it kharaj land. The commentator compares this to the exception of Makka, which was also conquered by force but exempted from kharaj by the Prophet out of honor. Just as Arabs are not subject to slavery (riqq), so their lands are not subject to kharaj. This is supported by a citation from al-Binaya. The passage then moves to the second issue: the kharaj rates per jarib for land suitable for cultivation, moist fodder (ratbah), and continuous vineyards/palm trees. The rates are one sa' and one dirham for crops, five dirhams for ratbah, and ten dirhams for vineyards/palm trees. The commentator traces this to the action of Caliph 'Umar, who sent 'Uthman ibn Hunayf to survey the Sawad of Iraq, which measured 36,000,000 jaribs, and imposed these rates with the consensus of the Companions. The rationale is that expenses (mu'an) vary: vineyards have the lowest expenses, farms the highest, and fodder in between; thus the tax varies accordingly. The commentator then defines a jarib as land 60 by 60 cubits, but notes differences in cubit length: some say five handfuls (qabdhat), some say six. The cubit of Kisra (Persian royal cubit) exceeds the common cubit by a handful. The measurement can vary by country, so in Egypt the feddan is used. The land must be suitable for cultivation; if not, no tax. The ruling is general for whatever is planted, whether once or multiple times a year, or even if not planted. The sa' is defined as eight ratls (Iraqi), which equals four manns, and this is the view of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and according to one narration, Abu Yusuf. The dirham is seven tenths of a mithqal. The commentator also defines ratbah as fresh alfalfa or fodder crops like cucumber, watermelon, eggplant, etc. The author notes that from ratbah, something is taken from the external produce, and the condition of continuity (ittisal) is required: if the ratbah is scattered and the land in between is planted with other crops, then nothing is due on the ratbah. Similarly, non-fruit-bearing trees incur no kharaj; if trees are dense so that the land cannot be cultivated, it is treated as a vineyard. A detailed ruling from Sharh al-Tahawi is given: if one plants a vineyard, he pays kharaj until it fruits; after fruiting, if the yield is double the vineyard tax, he pays the full vineyard tax; if less, half the rate until it reduces to a qafiz and a dirham. Finally, the qafiz is equated to the Prophetic sa' of eight ratls.

Key Terms
خراج الجريب (kharaj al-jarib): The land tax per jarib, fixed by 'Umar for the Sawad of Iraq: one sa' and one dirham for crops, five dirhams for ratbah, ten dirhams for vineyards/palm trees.
الجريب (al-jarib): A unit of land area, originally 60x60 cubits, but varying by region; in Hanafi fiqh it is typically defined as 60x60 of the royal cubit.
الرطبة (al-ratbah): Moist fodder, including crops like alfalfa (barseem), cucumber, watermelon, eggplant, etc. The kharaj for a jarib of ratbah is five dirhams.

Mutual Exclusivity of 'Ushr and Kharaj: Proof and Analogies
Proof and expansion of mutual exclusivity of 'ushr and kharaj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that 'ushr and kharaj are mutually exclusive, identify the three proofs (hadith, consensus, and 'illa), and apply the same principle to analogous pairs such as zakat and kharaj.

Core Idea
‘Ushr and kharaj never combine on one land—hadith, consensus, and legal cause confirm this.

This passage establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi fiscal law: no land can be subject to both the tithe ('ushr) and the land tax (kharaj) simultaneously. The author cites direct proof from a hadith in Imam Abu Hanifa's Musnad, historical consensus of just and unjust rulers, and a rational explanation that the two obligations arise from incompatible descriptions of the land. The commentary then expands this principle to other legal pairs, showing a broader maxim of mutual exclusivity in Islamic rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
First, read the matn statement: 'There is no tithe on the produce of kharaj land.' This is the core ruling. Note that it prohibits combining both taxes on one piece of land.
The ruling is negative: 'no tithe' on kharaj land.
2
Examine the First Proof: Hadith
Locate the hadith cited: 'Tithe and kharaj do not combine in the land of a Muslim.' It is narrated by Abu Hanifa in his Musnad. Understand that this is a direct textual evidence (dalil naqli).
The hadith is a single narration (ahad) but accepted as proof.
3
Examine the Second Proof: Consensus
The passage says 'the imams of justice and injustice did not combine them.' This indicates a practical consensus (ijma') across rulers, which serves as evidence. Even unjust rulers followed this practice.
Consensus of rulers, even unjust ones, is considered a strong proof.
4
Understand the Rationale ('Illa)
The passage explains: kharaj is for land conquered by force; 'ushr is for land entered peacefully. These two descriptions cannot coexist in one land. The underlying cause (sabab) is the productive land, but the basis differs: 'ushr is actual produce, kharaj is estimated. Therefore, they are mutually exclusive.
This is a legal cause (مقتضى) that prevents combination.
5
Apply the Principle to Analogous Pairs
The commentator extends this mutual exclusivity to other pairs: zakat with kharaj/'ushr, hadd with ta'zir, flogging with stoning, etc. For each, identify why the two rulings cannot apply together, based on incompatible causes or conditions.
This illustrates the broader maxim: certain rulings exclude each other.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the hadith and consensus. The matn states that no 'ushr (tithe) is due on the produce of kharaj land. The commentator provides the evidence: a hadith narrated by Imam Abu Hanifa in his Musnad stating that 'ushr and kharaj do not combine in the land of a Muslim. Additionally, the historical practice of just and unjust rulers (imams) never collecting both, which constitutes a practical consensus. The rationale is that kharaj applies to land conquered by force, while 'ushr applies to land whose inhabitants accepted Islam peacefully; these two descriptions cannot coexist in one land. The underlying cause ('illa) is the same – the productive land – but the basis differs: 'ushr is based on actual produce, while kharaj is an estimated obligation. For this reason, both taxes are attributed to the land. The commentator then extends the principle of mutual exclusivity to other pairs of rulings in Islamic law, such as: zakat and kharaj/'ushr (i.e., no zakat on land subject to kharaj or 'ushr), hadd and ta'zir penalties, flogging and stoning, zakat of trade and sadaqat al-fitr, amputation and civil liability. Also, tayammum and wudu, pregnancy and menstruation, menstruation and postnatal bleeding. This illustrates the broader legal maxim that certain rulings exclude each other due to incompatibility of their causes or conditions.

Kharaj on Land Purchased from a Dhimmi and Its Incompatibility with 'Ushr
Ruling with proof on kharaj and 'ushr incompatibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that kharaj remains due on kharaj land when owned by a Muslim, and explain why 'ushr cannot combine with kharaj, citing the primary hadith proof and the rationale.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a charge on the land itself; a Muslim who owns kharaj land pays kharaj, never 'ushr.

When a Muslim acquires land that is subject to kharaj (land tax from conquest), does the obligation change? The Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the Companions and a prophetic hadith, rules that kharaj remains and 'ushr cannot be imposed. This lesson unpacks the proof and the reasoning behind this key ruling in land taxation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage addresses the status of kharaj when a Muslim buys kharaj land from a dhimmi, and the general rule that 'ushr and kharaj cannot combine on the same land. Understand that the core question is whether the tax obligation changes with ownership.
This is a ruling about ownership transfer, not about personal status.
2
Examine the precedent of the Companions
The author states that the Companions (Sahaba) bought kharaj lands and continued to pay kharaj. This acts as a practical proof (amal al-sahaba) that the tax remains despite the owner being Muslim.
Hanafi school considers actions of the Companions as a source of law.
3
Analyze the primary textual proof
The hadith narrated by Abu Hanifa in his Musnad: 'لا يجتمع عشر وخراج في أرض مسلم' (There is no combining of 'ushr and kharaj on the land of a Muslim). The negation indicates mutual exclusivity — if one is present, the other is absent.
This hadith is ahad (single-transmitter) but accepted in Hanafi usul.
4
Understand the rational argument
Kharaj is imposed on land conquered by force ('anwatan), while 'ushr is on land whose people submitted voluntarily (sulhan). These two descriptions cannot coexist in one land. Although both are based on land productivity, the basis differs.
The cause (sabab) of both is the productive land, but the legal classification differs.
5
Apply the ruling
Given a scenario (e.g., a Muslim inherits kharaj land, or a dhimmi sells kharaj land to a Muslim), determine that kharaj is due on the land, and 'ushr is not. Classify land types based on their origin.
The ruling does not change even if the land is left uncultivated.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is an expansion probe on the rulings of kharaj (land tax) when a Muslim acquires kharaj land, and the fundamental principle that 'ushr and kharaj never combine. The author, Ibn Nujaym, first clarifies a current reality: in his time, kharaj lands had largely been converted to rental arrangements, so a farmer who leaves land uncultivated incurs no penalty—contra the practice of oppressors, which is condemned. He then addresses three scenarios: (1) A Muslim buys kharaj land from a dhimmi: the ruling is that kharaj is on the buyer if he can cultivate, otherwise on the seller. This is based on the practice of the Companions who bought such lands and paid kharaj. (2) A non-Muslim landowner converts to Islam: kharaj continues to be taken from him because the tax is a charge on the land's productivity, not on the person's religion. The two descriptions cannot coexist. By analogy, other pairs of contradictory obligations are mentioned: zakat and hadd, diyah and lashing, etc. The lesson concludes with a comparative list of mutually exclusive obligations.

العشر (al-'ushr): A tithe (10% of produce) on land whose inhabitants submitted voluntarily (sulhan) and became Muslim; it is a zakat on agricultural produce.

Fixed vs Proportional Kharaj and the Vacant Land Rule
Ruling on fixed vs proportional kharaj and vacant land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between muwazzaf and muqasama kharaj and determine who is liable for kharaj when purchasing vacant kharaj land.

Core Idea
Kharaj muwazzaf is annual; muqasama repeats with each harvest — timing of purchase determines liability.

This passage explains two types of kharaj: fixed (muwazzaf) and proportional (muqasama). For vacant land, the key factor is whether the buyer can cultivate before the year ends. Let's break down the conditions and the scholarly differences on what constitutes sufficient time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the kharaj type
Determine if the kharaj is muwazzaf (fixed) or muqasama (proportional). Muqasama is tied to each harvest, like 'ushr, so it repeats; muwazzaf does not.
Muqasama is based on actual produce, while muwazzaf is a fixed annual sum.
2
Apply the vacant land rule
If the land is vacant at purchase, check if the buyer has enough time to cultivate before the next year. If the remaining time is sufficient (e.g., three months per the fatwa), kharaj is on the buyer; otherwise on the seller.
The standard from Wāqi‘āt al-Nāṭifī is three months, considering crops like millet that ripen in that period.
3
Note the scholarly difference on crop
Some mashāyikh (e.g., Abu al-Qasim) only consider wheat or barley; Abu Nasr considers any crop. The fatwa uses three months as a general measure, avoiding the crop dispute.
The three-month standard is practical and used in fatwa.

Cultivated Land, Multiple Transfers, and Remission of Kharaj
Rulings on cultivated land, multiple transfers, and remission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply kharaj rules to cultivated land, multiple sales within a year, and understand the intra-Hanafi difference on remission of kharaj.

Core Idea
If land is cultivated and crop ripe, treat as vacant; if multiple buyers each hold less than three months, no kharaj is due.

This lesson covers more complex scenarios: when the purchased land is already cultivated, when it changes hands multiple times, and whether the sultan can remit kharaj. We also see an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Assess if land is cultivated
Determine the crop's maturity. If unripe, buyer pays kharaj regardless. If grain has formed (ripe), treat as vacant land — the time rule applies.

Apply the multiple-sale narration

3
Handle two harvests
If the land produces autumnal and spring harvests, and seller gets one and buyer the other, both are liable for kharaj proportionately.
From al-Muhīt: each pays based on the harvest they can obtain.
4
Understand the fatwa on remission
Abu Yusuf allows the sultan to remit (tark) kharaj to a landowner who is a recipient (masrif). Muhammad disagrees. The fatwa follows Abu Yusuf.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with practical implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on jizya amounts when set by consent vs. by force, and explain the evidence from the caliphs' practice and consensus.

Core Idea
Jizya amounts are graded: poor 12 dirhams, middle 24, wealthy 48, based on caliphal practice and ijma.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses two scenarios for jizya: when set by mutual consent (cannot be changed) and when imposed by force (amounts are fixed by wealth class). It provides the specific amounts and cites the practice of the caliphs as proof, along with a later fatwa on remission of the tax. Understanding this clarifies the basis of jizya as a graded obligation, not a flat punishment.

1

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Bayt al-Mal has four distinct funds; mixing is prohibited, but borrowing with repayment is allowed under specific conditions.

In this passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author outlines the four types of wealth that constitute the Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) and the rules governing their management. Understanding these categories is essential for grasping the Islamic fiscal system and the Imam's responsibilities. We will explore each category, the requirement of separate treasuries, and the permissibility of borrowing between funds when necessary.

Guided Path
1

The fourth category is added by the commentators; the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq omitted it.

The exception is based on the principle that if the recipient is already eligible for the borrowed fund's category, the transfer is valid without return.

Liability of a Partner Who Pays Zakat After the Other Has Paid: Dispute Between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Commentary expanding an intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of a second payer in zakat agency and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The second payer's liability hinges on whether his duty is to discharge the obligation or merely transfer ownership.

When two partners mutually authorize each other to pay their zakat, complications arise if both pay without coordination. The matn in Kanz al-Daqaiq states that if they pay consecutively, the second is liable. But is that absolute? Imam Abu Hanifa says yes; the Sahibayn disagree. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq unpacks the reasoning behind each view and reveals which position became the relied-upon one in the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two partners mutually authorize zakat payment. They pay consecutively. What does the matn rule regarding the second payer?
The matn says the second is liable absolutely, but this is Imam Abu Hanifa's view.
2
Note the qualification by the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad condition liability on the second payer's knowledge of the first payment. If he did not know, he is not liable.
Some narrations extend this to no liability even if he knew.
3
Trace the dalil behind each view
The Sahibayn analogize to the agent ordered to slaughter the blood of ihsar who slaughters after the ihsar is lifted: no liability. Abu Hanifa distinguishes because zakat is wajib whereas the blood sacrifice is not, so the purpose of agency is discharge, not just tamlik.
The analogy is a qiyas jali, but the Hanafi response argues the difference in obligation changes the ruling.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Later authorities (Fath al-Qadir, al-Hidaya) adopt the Sahibayn's view as the correct one. Thus the operative ruling is: no liability if the second payer did not know.
This is a good example of tarjih within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding a ruling from the matn regarding one partner paying zakat on behalf of the other. The commentator first clarifies that for a partner to pay zakat on the other's wealth, explicit permission is required because zakat payment is not a commercial act covered by the general agency of partnership. If they pay consecutively, the second payer is liable to the first (i.e., must reimburse the first). The commentator notes that this ruling from the matn is absolute, applying whether the second payer knew of the first payment or not—this is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that if the second payer did not know of the first payment, he is not liable. Virtue and Types of Waqf
Explanation of the best acts and the three legal categories of waqf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three legal categories of waqf (mubah, qurba, fard) and state the rulings for each, including the effect of intention and the validity from a dhimmi.

Core Idea
Waqf can be permissible, devotional, or obligatory; only intention determines reward and liability.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the virtue of various charitable acts and then defines the three types of waqf based on the intention of the endower. Understanding these categories is crucial because they determine the legal validity, reward, and obligation associated with the endowment. The text also clarifies that even a waqf without devotional intent is legally valid if done by a dhimmi, though no reward is earned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
The text states that waqf can be described as mubah (permissible), qurba (devotional), or fard (obligatory). Locate these terms in the passage.
Mubah is when done without intending closeness to Allah; qurba is with such intention; fard is when it is vowed.
2
Analyze the mubah type
For mubah, note that it is valid even from a dhimmi (non-Muslim) but carries no reward. This shows that basic validity does not require religious intent.
The proof: 'فالأول بلا قصد القربة ولذا يصح من الذمي ولا ثواب له'.
3
Analyze the qurba type
Qurba requires the intention of worship (qasd al-qurba) and is performed by a Muslim. It earns reward.
The text contrasts this with the first type: 'والثاني مع قصدها من المسلم'.
4
Analyze the fard type
The vowed waqf (nadhr) becomes obligatory upon fulfillment of the condition. Study the example of the vow regarding a son's arrival. Note the conditions about whom the endowment is made upon.
If endowed on those to whom zakah cannot be given (like relatives), the vow remains; if on others, it is cancelled.

This passage discusses the relative virtue of different acts of worship and the three categories of waqf.
