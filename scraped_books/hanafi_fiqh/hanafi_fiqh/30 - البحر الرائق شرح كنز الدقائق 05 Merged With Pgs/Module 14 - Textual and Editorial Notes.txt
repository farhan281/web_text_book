Clarifying the Scope of the Verse on Kindness to Non-Muslim Parents
Clarification of proof for not killing parents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school restricts the verse 'accompany them with kindness' to dhimmi parents and evaluate the proof for not killing polytheist parents.

Core Idea
The command of kindness to parents does not protect a harbi polytheist parent from being killed in jihad.

This lesson addresses a common question: if Islam commands kindness to parents, how can a Muslim soldier kill his own polytheist parent in battle? The Hanafi scholars refine the proof by showing that the Qur'anic verse applies only to dhimmi parents—those who have a peace agreement—not to those actively fighting against the Muslim state. This critical refinement solves an apparent contradiction between general commands and specific rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
What is the apparent conflict between the verse on kindness and the permissibility of killing polytheist parents?
2
Locate the Hanafi Refinement
How do the commentators restrict the verse? What is the basis for restriction?
The verse is specified to dhimmi parents to avoid contradiction with jihad.
3
Understand the Dalil
What proof do they rely on? The verse is considered 'amm but becomes khass by qarina from other texts.
4
Note the Practical Consequence
When is it prohibited to kill a polytheist parent? (When they are dhimmi; otherwise, it is permissible).
5
Address the Secondary Point
The obligation of support (nafaqah) does not apply to harbi parents, and the son's obligation is not like the father's.

Commentary
This passage is a critical_refinement addressing an objection regarding the proof for not killing parents. The matn had used Qur'an 31:15 ('accompany them in the world with kindness') as evidence that one should not kill his parents even if they are polytheists. The commentator from al-Hawashi al-Sa'diyyah clarifies that this verse is specifically about dhimmi parents (protected non-Muslims), not all polytheists, to avoid contradiction with the rule that no kindness is due to those fighting Islam. He also notes that the obligation of support (nafaqah) does not apply to polytheist parents who are harbi. Another comment: the proof based on obligation to support (i'hya') does not apply to the son, because the son is not like the father in terms of fiqh rulings; the father is superior.

مستأمن (musta'min): A non-Muslim given temporary safe passage.

Critical Refinement on Jizya Wording
A super-commentator's critique on jizya terminology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the linguistic precision in the Hanafi jizya classification regarding the term 'the poor' and identify the two opposing views.

Core Idea
Omitting 'poor' from the jizya formula is suggested, but al-Nahr objects.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq contains a critical refinement from the super-commentary al-Nahr on the wording used in jizya classification. The author suggests that dropping the term 'the poor' (al-faqir) would be more precise, but the response from al-Nahr challenges this. To understand the issue, we need to recall that in Hanafi fiqh, jizya is taken from non-Muslims who are able to pay, so including 'poor' might cause confusion about liability. However, the full argument is missing due to textual fragmentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Suggestion
First, note the claim: 'If he had omitted 'the poor' it would have been better.' This implies that the author believes the term 'poor' is problematic in the jizya formula.
The suggestion is about textual precision, not a substantive ruling.
2
Identify the Objection
The response from al-Nahr states: 'It is disallowed, for if he confined his statement and relied [on something], then what?' This indicates that omitting 'poor' is not permissible, but the reasoning is cut off.
The word 'mamnu' (disallowed) suggests a strong objection.
3
Analyze the Legal Context
In Hanafi fiqh, jizya is wajib on every male, free, adult dhimmi who is wealthy or has earning capacity. The poor (who cannot pay) are exempt. Including 'poor' might inadvertently exempt some who are actually able, or create ambiguity.
The term 'faqir' in Hanafi law can mean a person without any property, but sometimes includes those with minimal assets.
4
Note the Fragmentation
The passage ends midsentence. The full argument from al-Nahr is not preserved. This limits our ability to reach a definitive conclusion.
This is a common issue in older manuscripts.

The Authority of the Two Narrations in the Differences Between Grandfather and Father
Critical refinement on the scope of differences between grandfather and father

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why only the differences found in the two narrations (zahir al-riwayah and riwayat al-Hasan) are authoritative and identify the methodological flaw in adding unverified differences.

Core Idea
A valid legal difference requires transmission in the two narrations; unverified additions are irrelevant.

In earlier lessons, we encountered eight specific differences between the legal treatment of a grandfather and a father. Some commentators suggested adding more issues, such as from nafaqah. However, the great Hanafi jurist al-Nahr corrects this by reminding us that the original list is based solely on the two principal narrations (al-riwayatayn). Any proposed addition that lacks support in these transmissions has no place in the established madhhab. This lesson clarifies the proper methodology for determining the scope of ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim Under Refinement
The phrase 'yambaghi an yuzād' (it is appropriate to add) indicates a proposal to expand the list of differences. Recognize that this is the target of al-Nahr's critique.
The proposal likely came from a later commentator who noticed additional practical differences.
2
Recognize the Basis of the Original List
Al-Nahr states that the original discussion is specifically about what is found in the two narrations (zahir al-riwayah and riwayat al-Hasan). These two transmissions are the core of Hanafi fiqh.
The two narrations are the most authoritative sources after the Qur'an and Sunnah.
3
Understand the Critic's Objection
Al-Nahr points out that the proposed additional issues are not present in these two narrations. Therefore, they cannot be added to the original list of differences.
The absence from the two narrations makes the addition methodologically unsound.
4
Accept the Verified List
Since the additions are unsupported, the correct position is the one in al-Hidayah, which lists exactly eight differences.
Al-Hidayah is a central work of the Hanafi school and its position is relied upon.

Does Partnership of Ownership Include Debt?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether debt is included in partnership of ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over whether debt is included in partnership of ownership, and apply the preferred view to determine the partner's right when one partner collects a shared debt.

Core Idea
In Hanafi partnership of ownership, debt is owned property—the partner who collects must share with the other.

The definition of partnership of ownership (shirkat al-milk) states that two people jointly own a thing ('ayn). But what about debts (dayn)? Some Hanafi scholars argued that debt is merely a legal description and cannot be owned, making partnership in debt metaphorical. The dominant view, however, affirms that debt is fully owned and can be the subject of partnership. This lesson unpacks the arguments and the practical consequences of the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and its examples
The matn defines partnership of ownership as 'two owning a thing by inheritance or purchase'. Note that 'inheritance' and 'purchase' are examples, not restrictions; partnership can arise through gift, charity, seizure of war booty, or mixing of properties.
This classification of compulsory (jabriyya) and voluntary (ikhtiyariyya) partnerships sets the context.
2
Recognize the dispute: Does 'property' include debt?
The apparent meaning of 'ayn (property) excludes debt (dayn). Some Hanafis argue that partnership in debt is metaphorical (majaz) because debt is a legal description, not a physical asset, and thus cannot be owned. They claim that gifting a debt is actually a discharge (isqat), not a transfer of ownership.
This view relies on the distinction between haqiqa and majaz in Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the preferred Hanafi response
The preferred view, as stated in Fath al-Qadir, holds that debt is owned (milk). Evidence: if the debtor pays one partner, the other partner can reclaim half of what was taken, proving joint ownership. Also, the gift of a debt (hibat al-dayn) is a genuine transfer, not merely a discharge.
This view is adopted by the majority of later Hanafi authorities.
4
Apply the practical consequence
If debt is included in partnership, the partner who receives payment from the debtor must share it proportionally with the other partner. The receiving partner cannot claim the payment as his exclusive share, nor can the debtor settle with one partner and ignore the other.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf—the practical difference that matters in financial dealings.
5
Note the author's editorial suggestion
The commentator of al-Bahr al-Raiq suggests that the matn would be clearer if it explicitly added 'or a debt' after 'property'. This reflects the preferred view and highlights the importance of precise terminology in legal texts.
This demonstrates the interplay between matn and sharh in Hanafi legal literature.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn phrase 'شركة الملك أن يملك اثنان عيناً إرثاً أو شراء'. The commentator first explains that 'inheritance' and 'purchase' are merely examples, not exhaustive; partnership of ownership can also arise through gift, charity, seizure of war booty, or spontaneous mixing of properties. He then classifies these into compulsory (inheritance) and voluntary (purchase, gift, etc.). The main legal issue discussed is whether the word 'عين' (property) in the matn includes debt (دين). Some scholars argue that partnership in debt is metaphorical because debt is not a physical property and cannot be owned in the full sense; they claim the assignment of debt is actually a discharge. The preferred Hanafi view, as reported from Fath al-Qadir, is that debt is owned and can be the subject of partnership. Evidence: if the debtor pays one partner, the other partner can reclaim half of what was taken; also, a partner cannot unilaterally claim that what he received from the debtor is exclusively his share. Additionally, a trick (hila) is mentioned in the chapter on sulh: the debtor can gift one partner his share and the other partner can discharge him, allowing one partner to become owner exclusively. The commentator concludes that it would have been better if the author had explicitly included 'debt' alongside 'property'.

Key Terms
عين ('ayn): Property: corporeal asset, as distinct from debt (dayn).
دين (dayn): Debt: a financial obligation owed by a person, which may be the subject of ownership and partnership.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical: a term used in a non-literal sense, as some claim for partnership in debt.
إسقاط (isqat): Discharge: the act of canceling a debt, which some argue is the true nature of gifting a debt.
