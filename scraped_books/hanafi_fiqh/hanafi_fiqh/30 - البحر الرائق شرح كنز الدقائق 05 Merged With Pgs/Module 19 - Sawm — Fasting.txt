The Mawla of a Taghlibi: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Tax Liability
Commentary on the status of Taghlibi's mawla regarding jizyah and the refutation of Zufar's analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the jizyah liability of a Taghlibi's mawla and evaluate Zufar's qiyas to the Hashimi mawla.

Core Idea
The mawla of a Taghlibi does not inherit tax exemption—lightening versus prohibition distinguishes the cases.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq comments on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, clarifying the tax liability of a freed slave (mawla) of a Taghlibi. The core issue is whether the mawla follows the master's status for exemption from jizyah and kharaj. The Hanafi school affirms that the manumitter remains liable, rejecting an analogy to the Hashimi mawla made by Zufar. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the distinction between a tax that is a lightening (takhfif) and a prohibition (tahrim).

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine what ruling is being contested: whether the mawla of a Taghlibi is exempt from jizyah/kharaj like the mawla of a Hashimi, or whether the tax stays on the manumitter.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and his student Zufar.
2
Analyze the Hanafi position
State the Hanafi ruling: the manumitter (mu'tiq) is liable for the tax on behalf of his Taghlibi mawla. The mawla does not become exempt because the tax is a lightening, not a prohibition.
The hadith 'Mawla of a people is one of them' is restricted to specific rulings like zakat prohibition.
3
Examine Zufar's qiyas
Zufar analogizes the Taghlibi mawla to the Hashimi mawla: since the latter is exempt due to association with the master, the former should also be exempt.
Zufar's reasoning is based on apparent similarity in exemption status.
4
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal
Abu Hanifa distinguishes between lightening and prohibition. The Taghlibi's tax is a lightening (the mawla does not follow), while the Hashimi's exemption is a prohibition based on honor (sharaf), which extends to the mawla.
The example of a rich person's poor mawla (who is not exempt) further supports the distinction.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Under the authoritative view, the manumitter pays. Under Zufar's view, the mawla would be free of tax. The practical difference is clear.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's position.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It opens with the matn: the wealth from various sources (Qurashi tax, jizyah, kharaj, wealth of Taghlib, gifts, etc.) is spent on public welfare. Then a quotation from al-Siyar about the jizyah of Taghlib and Najran when one dies first. The author then clarifies the term 'Taghlibi' and mentions a condition from Umar. The core commentary is on the phrase 'wa mawlahu ka-mawla al-Qurashi' from the matn. The ruling: the freed slave of a Taghlibi is like the freed slave of a Qurashi — neither is tax-exempt due to being a mawla; rather, the tax falls on the manumitter. This is because the intensified charity (jizyah/kharaj) is a form of lightening, and the mawla does not follow the original's status. The author specifies Taghlibi and Qurashi because the mawla of a Hashimi is treated differently: charity is prohibited on him and on his mawla due to honor, as prohibitions are established by doubt (shubha). This invalidates Zufar's qiyas, who analogized the mawla of Taghlibi to the mawla of Hashimi. The objection that a rich person's mawla (poor) is not given charity is refuted: the rich is eligible in principle, but the preventer is richness, not applied to the mawla. The hadith 'Mawla of a people is one of them' is restricted to the specific ruling of not giving zakat, by consensus that a Hashimi's mawla does not attain his status in marriage competence or imamate.

Key Terms
مولى (mawlā): Freed slave; a client who has been manumitted and has a bond of patronage (walā') with the former master.
تغلبي (taghlibī): A member of the Christian Arab tribe Banu Taghlib, who paid a special jizyah (double charity) under a treaty with Umar.
قرشي (qurashī): A member of the Quraysh tribe; in this context, referring to the rule that their freed slaves are not exempt from jizyah.
هاشمي (hāshimī): A descendant of Hashim, the Prophet's clan; their honor prohibits receiving zakat, and this extends to their mawla.

Key Takeaway
The mawla of a Taghlibi does not become exempt from jizyah; the manumitter remains liable, distinguishing this case from the Hashimi mawla's exemption based on honor and prohibition.
