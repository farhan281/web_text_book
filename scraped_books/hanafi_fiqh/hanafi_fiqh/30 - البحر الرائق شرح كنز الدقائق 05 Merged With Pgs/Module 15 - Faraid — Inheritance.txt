Inheritance of a Soldier's Share After Iḥrāz
Ruling on inheritance of spoils share relative to iḥrāz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for inheriting a soldier's share of spoils and apply the analogy to waqf salary.

Core Idea
Inheritance requires ownership; spoils are owned only after iḥrāz.

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi inheritance law regarding spoils of war. The core principle is that inheritance depends on established ownership. Since a soldier does not own his prospective share until the spoils are secured in Dar al-Islam (iḥrāz), his share is not inherited if he dies before that point. The text also draws an analogy with endowment salaries, where the timing of the beneficiary's death relative to collection determines inheritance.

Guided Path
1
State the rule and its condition
Identify the ruling: the share is inherited only after iḥrāz. Note that before iḥrāz, there is no ownership, so no inheritance.
The text explicitly says 'ولا من مات فيها وبعد الإحراز بدارنا يورث نصيبه' implying the opposite for before iḥrāz.
2
Understand the rationale (ta'līl)
The reason given: 'لأن الإرث يجري في الملك ولا ملك قبل الإحراز'. Inheritance depends on ownership, which only arises after iḥrāz.
3
Apply the analogy to waqf salaries
The text extends the same logic to waqf: if the beneficiary dies after the supervisor has taken possession (iḥrāz), his salary is inherited; otherwise not. Two opinions exist, but the author prefers a detailed view (tafṣīl).
The analogy is qiyās: ownership established after securing the asset.
4
Distinguish the soldier from the ahl al-dīwān
The text mentions that a soldier from the register (dīwān) who dies before the stipend is given does not inherit it, consistent with the lack of ownership before actual disbursement.
This reinforces the general principle: no inheritance without ownership.
5
Formulate the muftā bihī position
The relied-upon position is the tafṣīl: death after iḥrāz results in inheritance; death before iḥrāz does not. This applies to both spoils and analogous situations.
The author says 'ينبغي أن يفصل' indicating this is the preferred approach.

Commentary
This lesson explains the ruling on whether the share of a soldier who dies after the spoils have been brought to Dar al-Islam (iḥrāz) is inheritable. The commentator states that inheritance requires ownership, and ownership of spoils only occurs after iḥrāz. Therefore, if a soldier dies before iḥrāz, his prospective share is not owned, so it is not inherited. If he dies after iḥrāz but before division, his right is established (ḥaqq mu'akkad), so his share is inherited. The commentator draws an analogy with waqf: the salary of a beneficiary is inherited if he dies after the supervisor has taken possession of the revenue, but not if he dies before. Similarly, for regular soldiers (ahl al-dīwān), if they die before the stipend is given, it is not inherited. The text leaves some divergence unresolved.

Key Terms
الإحراز (al-iḥrāz): The act of securing spoils in Dar al-Islam, establishing ownership rights.

Fixed Kharaj Rates Per Jarib: The Hanafi Position and Its Proof
Explains fixed kharaj rates and their ijma basis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fixed kharaj rates per jarib as established by Caliph Umar (ra) and explain the Hanafi proof of their binding nature via ijma.

Core Idea
The fixed kharaj rates of Umar are binding as ijma—every Hanafi jurist must uphold the three-tiered assessment per jarib.

In this passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author expands on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq to clarify the precise rates of kharaj on different types of land. These rates, fixed by Caliph Umar with the consensus of the Companions, form a definitive proof in Hanafi law. You will learn not only the rates but the legal reasoning behind their variation and the conditions under which they can be adjusted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three fixed rates and rationale
Read the passage to find the explicit rates: 1 sa' + 1 dirham for crop land, 5 dirhams for pasture, 10 dirhams for vineyard/date palms. Note the rationale: expenses differ—vineyards lightest, farms heaviest.
The rate for pasture applies to 'ratbah' (fresh fodder crops like clover, cucumbers, etc.).
2
Locate the dalil: ijma from Umar's practice
The text states that Umar's assessment was done in the presence of the Companions without objection, forming ijma. This makes the ruling qat'i (definitive).
Ijma is a primary source; the Hanafi school upholds that the Companions' silent consensus is binding.
3
Examine subsidiary rulings on measurement
The jarib is 60×60 cubits, but the cubit varies; also, the sa' is 8 Iraqi ratls per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the mufta bihi), though Abu Yusuf later retracted to 5⅓ ratls. The dirham is from the best coinage.
In Egypt, the feddan is used as the local unit, but the standard estimation (60×60) is preferred by the author.
4
Apply the principles of reduction and prohibition of increase
If the land cannot bear the fixed rate, it is reduced to what it can bear. However, even if the land can bear more, the rate cannot be increased. This is based on Umar's instruction to his agents.
This applies to lands assessed by Umar or an Imam following his model; for new lands, the Imam may assess based on capacity.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is an expansion of the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, explaining the fixed rates of kharaj (land tax) on different types of land as established by Caliph Umar (ra) with the consensus of the Companions. The author begins by stating the rates: for land suitable for cultivation, the kharaj is one sa' (a measure of grain) and one dirham per jarib; for pasture (ratbah), five dirhams; and for vineyards and continuously planted date palms, ten dirhams. This was the assessment of Umar, implemented by his surveyors, and accepted by all Companions, thus forming an ijma (consensus). The rationale is that expenses differ: vineyards require the least maintenance, farms the most, and pastures are intermediate, so the tax varies accordingly. The jarib is defined as a plot 60 cubits by 60 cubits, but the exact cubit is disputed: most Hanafi works say it is the 'cubit of Kisra' (Persian royal cubit) of four handfuls, while al-Mughrib says six handfuls. The author notes that in Egypt, the local unit (feddan) should be used, though the standard estimation is preferred. The land must be suitable for cultivation; otherwise no tax is due. The sa' is defined as eight ratls by Iraqi measure (four manns), which is the opinion of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, and also the earlier view of Abu Yusuf (who later retracted to the Medinan sa' of 5⅓ ratls). The dirham is taken from the best coinage, and the tax on pasture is taken from its produce. The condition of 'connectedness' for vineyards and date palms means the trees are so dense that they occupy the entire land; if scattered, the cultivated parts are taxed separately. Non-fruit-bearing trees incur no kharaj. If the land cannot bear the fixed tax, it is reduced to what it can bear, but it cannot be increased even if the land could bear more, following Umar's policy. In lands conquered later, the Imam may impose kharaj based on capacity, but cannot exceed Umar's rates when initially assessing a new land according to Abu Hanifa. The passage also discusses kharaj al-muqasamah (proportional tax), which is like 'ushr in that it is a share of the produce, recurring each harvest, but its expenditure is like that of fixed kharaj. The Imam can set a proportion from half down to a fifth, but cannot convert from fixed to proportional or vice versa without breaking the covenant.

Key Terms
خراج (kharāj): Land tax imposed on conquered lands that remain in the hands of the original owners, as opposed to 'ushr on Muslim-owned lands.
جريب (jarīb): A unit of land area, defined as 60 × 60 cubits, but varies by region; in Egypt the feddan is used.
رطبة (raṭbah): Fresh pasture or fodder crops, such as clover, alfalfa, cucumbers, melons, etc., taxed at 5 dirhams per jarib.
صاع (ṣāʿ): A measure of capacity used for grain taxation; in Hanafi kharaj, it is 8 Iraqi ratls (4 manns).

Nullification of Good Deeds upon Apostasy (Habt al-Amal)
Rulings on nullification of good deeds by apostasy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on habt al-amal, explain the intra-madhhab dispute on return of deeds, and determine which acts must be repeated after repentance.

Core Idea
Apostasy nullifies all good deeds; only Hajj must be repeated upon return, not prayers, zakat, or fasts.

This lesson addresses the concept of habt al-amal — the nullification of good deeds through apostasy. According to the Hanafi school, apostasy itself invalidates all previous acts of worship. Upon returning to Islam, one must repeat the Hajj if previously performed, but not other obligatory acts like prayer, fasting, or zakat. The lesson also explores a dispute among Hanafi scholars on whether the reward for good deeds can return after repentance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on habt al-amal
Apostasy nullifies all good deeds automatically, without any condition. This is the position of the Hanafi school, as opposed to al-Shafi'i who conditions it on dying in apostasy.
The dalil is qiyas to original disbelief.
2
Understand the practical consequences: which acts are repeated?
After returning to Islam, the former apostate must repeat Hajj if he had previously performed it and is now financially able. However, he is not required to repeat prayers, fasts, or zakat for the period of apostasy.
Al-Khulasa explicitly states this distinction.
3
Navigate the intra-madhhab dispute on return of reward
There is disagreement among Hanafi scholars: some say that the reward for good deeds can return after repentance (Abu Ali, Abu Hashim, and many Hanafis), while others say it does not return (Abu al-Hasan? and some). The adopted view is that the lost reward does not return, but the deeds themselves can become effective for new reward after repentance.
The author of Bahr al-Raiq favors the view that deeds 'return effective for reward' meaning Allah may grant fresh reward, not that the original reward is restored.

Conditions for Inheriting from an Apostate: Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on heir conditions for apostate inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Hanafi narrations regarding the timing of heirship for an apostate and apply the most correct view (Muhammad's narration) to determine who inherits.

Core Idea
For inheritance from an apostate, the heir must exist at death, not at apostasy — Muhammad's narration is the most correct.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the inheritance of an apostate's property earned during Islam. While the Hanafi school uniquely allows Muslim heirs to inherit such property (unlike other schools), there is an internal dispute regarding the precise moment the heir must exist. Three narrations from Abu Hanifa are recorded, and the passage explains their differences and the preferred position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core Hanafi position
Recognise that property earned during Islam is inherited by Muslim heirs, while property earned after apostasy becomes fay' (booty) for the treasury.
This distinguishes the Hanafi school from the three other Imams who consider all apostate property as fay'.
2
Understand the inter-madhhab dispute
Note the opposing school's argument: the apostate dies a disbeliever, so a Muslim cannot inherit. The Hanafi response: ownership remains after apostasy, and the hadith refers to an original disbeliever.
Consensus from Ali's division of the apostate's property is cited as proof.
3
Analyse the three intra-Hanafi narrations
Al-Hasan: heir must exist at both apostasy and death. Abu Yusuf: only at apostasy. Muhammad: only at death. The most correct is Muhammad's.
These are three transmissions from Abu Hanifa himself.
4
Apply Muhammad's narration to examples
A slave who was an heir at apostasy but freed before the apostate's death inherits. A child born after apostasy from a Muslim slave-woman inherits. A wife who is cohabited and in 'iddah inherits; on Abu Yusuf's narration, she inherits even after 'iddah.
The wife's inheritance is conditioned on being in 'iddah because the apostate is treated like a fugitive (far) who divorced in terminal illness.

Commentary
Passage type: intra-Hanafi dispute with proof. This passage explains the inheritance of an apostate. The matn states that if an apostate dies or is killed, his property earned during Islam is inherited by his Muslim heirs after paying his debts incurred during Islam, while property earned during apostasy becomes fay' (booty) after paying debts incurred during apostasy. The commentator clarifies that there is consensus that property from Islam goes to Muslim heirs, but the Hanafis uniquely argue it is not fay', unlike the three other Imams who consider it booty because the apostate died a disbeliever. The Hanafi proof: the apostate's ownership remains after apostasy, so upon death it passes to heirs based on the time before apostasy; otherwise, if the state takes it when there are no heirs, that would also be inheritance based on the pre-apostasy state. The interpretation of the hadith about a Muslim not inheriting a disbeliever refers to an original disbeliever. Ali's division of the property of the apostate al-Mustawrid among Muslims is cited as consensus. The passage then details conditions for being an heir: according to the reliable narration (Muhammad's from Abu Hanifa), the heir must exist at the time of death or execution, not necessarily at apostasy. Three transmissions are given: al-Hasan requires the heir be an heir at both apostasy and death; Abu Yusuf requires only at apostasy; Muhammad requires only at death. The second is considered most correct. Examples follow: a slave who was an heir at apostasy becomes free before death — he inherits; a child born after apostasy from a Muslim slave-woman inherits. A case from al-Khaniyah shows the importance of timing. The wife inherits if in 'iddah, because the apostate becomes like a fugitive (far). The condition of 'iddah applies except on Abu Yusuf's narration where she inherits even after 'iddah. Only the cohabited wife (madkhula) inherits; the non-cohabited is separated immediately, so no inheritance. The passage ends with a comparison to a child from a sale before delivery, illustrating the analogy for the second condition (heir at death).

Key Terms
كسب إسلامه (kasb Islamihi): Property acquired by the apostate during his time as a Muslim. This is inherited by his Muslim heirs.
كسب ردته (kasb riddatihi): Property acquired by the apostate after apostasy. This becomes fay' (booty) for the Muslim treasury after debts.
الوصفان (al-wasfan): The two conditions for an heir: being an heir at the time of apostasy and being an heir at death.
فار (far): A fugitive; in this context, an apostate who dies or is killed while intending to join enemy territory, making him like one who divorces in terminal illness.

When Does an Apostate Become an Heir?
Intra-Hanafi dispute with practical application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the time an apostate is considered an heir and apply the ruling to classify different types of property.

Core Idea
An apostate's inheritance turns on the moment of legal death—ruling or cause.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute: when does an apostate legally die? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani differ on whether the judge's ruling or the act of joining enemy territory determines heirship. The answer affects how the apostate's property is divided among his heirs.

The dispute is over the moment of legal death, not physical death.

Both are based on causal reasoning (ta'lil).

The thamarah is the actual distribution outcome.

Four Inheritance Differences: Grandfather vs Father
Lists four inheritance differences between grandfather and father.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four inheritance rulings where the grandfather differs from the father, including the reduction of the mother's share, prevention of the grandmother, treatment of siblings, and the effect of the manumitter's son.

Core Idea
In inheritance, the grandfather is less powerful than the father: the mother's share is reduced, the grandmother is not prevented, siblings are not excluded, and the manumitter's son does not prevent him.

In the Hanafi school, the father and grandfather have different effects on inheritance shares. This lesson covers four specific areas where the grandfather's presence leads to different results than the father's presence, as outlined in the apparent narration. These differences affect the shares of the mother, grandmother, siblings, and the son of the manumitter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Inheritance Issues
Locate the section beginning with 'وأما الأربعة التي في الفرائض' and list the four: radd of mother's share, hajb of grandmother, siblings not dropped by grandfather, and the son of the manumitter.
The text numbers them implicitly.
2
Understand the Mother's Share (رد الأم إلى ثلث ما بقي)
When there are children, the mother's share is reduced to one-third of the remainder after the spouse's share. With the father present, she takes one-third of the whole; with the grandfather, she takes one-third of the remainder.
This is a reduction.
3
Prevention of the Grandmother (حجب أم الأب)
The grandmother (mother of the father) is prevented from inheriting when the father is alive, but not when the grandfather is alive. The grandfather does not prevent her.
This differs from the father's effect.
4
Siblings and the Grandfather (الإخوة لا تسقط بالجد)
When a father dies, siblings are excluded by the father. But when a grandfather dies, siblings are not excluded; they share with the grandfather according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
By consensus, siblings are dropped by the father.
5
Son of the Manumitter (ابن المعتق)
If a manumitter dies leaving a son and a grandfather, the son prevents the grandfather from inheriting from the freed slave (by consensus). But the son does not prevent the father (according to Abu Yusuf); the father takes one-sixth.
This is the fourth inheritance difference.

Additional Differences and Rulings Involving Grandfather
Adds two maintenance differences, orphan status, and an apostasy case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the additional differences between grandfather and father in maintenance (nafaqat), orphan status, and the case of apostasy affecting inheritance.

Core Idea
The grandfather differs from the father in maintenance for minors, orphan status, and in the effect of apostasy on a child's inheritance.

Beyond the eight classical differences, later Hanafi scholars identified additional rulings where the grandfather is not like the father. These involve maintenance obligations (nafaqat), the definition of orphanhood, and a special case in inheritance when the mother apostatizes. This lesson covers these supplementary issues.

Guided Path
1
Learn the Maintenance Differences
Read the two issues from the nafaqat chapter: (1) The mother shares maintenance of a minor with the grandfather in thirds, but with the father she does not share (it is solely his duty). (2) Maintenance is not imposed on the grandfather if he is poor, whereas it is imposed on the father even if poor.
These are from the chapter on maintenance.
2
Understand the Orphan Status Difference
A child is considered an orphan (yatim) when the father dies, even if the grandfather is alive. The grandfather's life does not remove orphan status.
This is based on the hadith definition: yatim is one who has lost his father.
3
Analyze the Apostasy Case from Al-Bada'i'
If a Muslim dies leaving a pregnant wife who apostatizes and goes to dar al-harb, then gives birth there, the child is Muslim and inherits because the father's Islam gives him that status. If the child is born after capture in dar al-Islam, he is Muslim but enslaved (following mother's slavery) and does not inherit.
This case shows the father's Islam is determinative, not the grandfather's.
4
Synthesize: Why These Differences Exist
The grandfather is a secondary authority; the father holds primary responsibility and authority over children.
This underlies all differences.

Inheritance and Liability of the Foundling
Ruling on foundling's inheritance and liability with proof and scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the treasury inherits from and bears liability for an abandoned child (foundling) and distinguish the Hanafi and Abu Yusuf's positions on intentional homicide.

Core Idea
The treasury inherits from and is liable for the foundling because profit accompanies liability.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq deals with the legal status of a foundling (laqīṭ) regarding inheritance and homicide liability. Since the foundling has no known relatives, the treasury (bayt al-māl) steps in as both heir and guarantor. The text explains the treasury's role in various homicide scenarios—unintentional, intentional, and unknown perpetrator—and introduces an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether the killer's own wealth is liable for intentional killing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its basis
State the rule: a foundling's inheritance goes to the treasury, and his liability (e.g., blood-money for homicide) is also on the treasury. The basis is the maxim 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' (profit accompanies liability).
This is a key Hanafi principle extending beyond commerce to state responsibility.
2
Analyze the homicide scenarios
Examine each sub-case: (a) foundling found dead in a district → qasamah and diyah on district residents paid to treasury; (b) killed by mistake by finder or another → diyah on the killer's ʿāqilah but eventually for the treasury; (c) killed intentionally → imam's choice between qiṣāṣ or diyah, but no pardon.
Note the emphasis on the treasury as ultimate recipient in all cases.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Identify Abu Yusuf's opposing view: for intentional killing, the blood-money is from the killer's own wealth, not on the ʿāqilah or treasury. Understand why this differs (intentional acts are not covered by ʿāqilah guarantees).
This reveals how ijtihad can diverge on a single issue within the same school.
4
Examine the wala' and guardianship implications
Understand that wala' (patronage) of the foundling belongs to the treasury, similar to ʿaql (blood-money liability). The sultan is the guardian (ḥadīth). Upon maturity, the foundling may choose a patron unless the treasury already paid damages. The imam may assign wala' to the finder as an ijtihādi matter.
This reinforces the treasury's overarching role and the possibility of delegation.
5
Synthesize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference between the main Hanafi view and Abu Yusuf's view is who pays the diyah for intentional killing: the treasury (or indirectly the community) versus the killer personally. Both are acceptable within the school.
In fatwa practice, the mufta bihi often follows the main view for unintentional but may adopt Abu Yusuf's for intentional to emphasize personal responsibility.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the foundling's inheritance (irth) and liability (jinaayah). The author states that the foundling's inheritance goes to the treasury because he has no known heirs, and his liability (e.g., blood-money for homicide) is also on the treasury, based on the principle 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' (profit accompanies liability). If the foundling is killed, his blood-money goes to the treasury. For unintentional killing, the blood-money is on the killer's 'aqilah but ultimately for the treasury. For intentional killing, the imam can choose between retaliation or accepting blood-money, but cannot pardon. Abu Yusuf holds the blood-money is on the killer's own wealth (not the 'aqilah). The foundling's wala' (patronage) belongs to the treasury, similar to his 'aql. He may choose his patron upon maturity unless the treasury has paid damages on his behalf. The sultan is his guardian for wealth and marriage, not the finder. The finder may be assigned wala' by the imam under permissible ijtihad.

Time Limit for Declaring a Missing Person Dead: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Sharh of Kanz al-Daqaiq on the missing person's death ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi school's apparent narration on declaring a missing person's death and evaluate the various intra-Hanafi opinions on the time limit.

Core Idea
The school's default is extinction of contemporaries, but practical necessity led to a fixed term of 90 years for fatwa.

The missing person (mafqud) presents a unique legal challenge: how long must we wait before declaring death? The Hanafi school's apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) uses the death of all contemporaries as the benchmark. However, later scholars found this impractical and proposed fixed terms ranging from 60 to 120 years. This lesson unpacks the evidence and the tarjih that settled on 90 years as the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its School Basis
State that the missing person is judged dead after extinction of contemporaries according to the apparent narration. Understand that this is based on rarity of living beyond one's peers.
2
List the Main Intra-Hanafi Opinions
Enumerate the fixed-term opinions: Abu Yusuf (100 years), Muhammad? Actually, Muhammad's view is extinction; later scholars: 60, 70, 80, 90, 120. Note the author of Kanz chose 90.
3
Understand the Reasoning Behind Each
Each view is based on assessing the prevalent maximum age or most common age. The hadith 'Ages of my ummah are between sixty and seventy' supports 70. Some argued no fixed term is more in line with fiqh.
4
Identify the Mufta Bihi and Tarjih Mechanism
Recognize that the fatwa is on 90 years per al-Dhakhira and the author, but the commentator prefers leaving it to the judge. Note the practical reason: investigating contemporaries is impossible.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq comments on the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq regarding the missing person (mafqud). The commentator first explains the matn's ruling: a missing person is judged dead after ninety years. He then discusses the extensive intra-Hanafi disagreement on the precise time limit. The apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) from Imam Muhammad uses the extinction of contemporaries—i.e., the missing person is not declared dead as long as any of his contemporaries (peers in age) are alive, because it is rare for someone to outlive all his contemporaries. But the author of Kanz, following Ibn al-Fadl, prefers ninety years, and this is said to be the fatwa. Other opinions include one hundred years from Abu Yusuf, one hundred twenty from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, sixty chosen by later scholars, seventy by Ibn al-Humam, and even a view that no fixed limit should be set, leaving it to the judge's discretion. The commentator notes that many mashayikh chose a fixed term because investigating the death of contemporaries is impractical. The matn then states that upon the judgment of death, the missing person's wife must observe 'iddah and inherits from him, but she does not inherit before that. Similarly, the missing person does not inherit from relatives who died before the judgment, because he is presumed alive via istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). The commentator adds that the same applies to the freedom of his mudabbars and ummahat awlad at that time. The passage then addresses a case where the missing person is a legatee: Muhammad al-Shaybani says the bequest is neither granted nor invalidated until his fate is known; his share is stopped. If at any point his life is confirmed, he inherits from relatives who died before that confirmation, analogous to a fetus (haml). The text then gives a detailed inheritance scenario: a man dies leaving two daughters, a missing son, and a grandson/granddaughter. The estate is in the hands of a third party. The daughters are given half (the certain portion), the other half is stopped for the missing son. The grandchildren get nothing because they are excluded by the missing son (if alive). If the holder of the property claims the missing son died before the father, he is compelled to pay two-thirds to the daughters due to his admission. Finally, the passage compares the missing person's case to that of a fetus (haml) in inheritance: a fetus's share is stopped; heirs who would be eliminated by a live birth (e.g., brother if fetus is a son) get nothing; heirs whose share changes (e.g., mother, wife) get the lesser of the two possible shares. The example of a pregnant woman, mother, and wife illustrates the principle: the mother gets one-sixth (the minimum, because if the fetus is alive she gets one-sixth, if dead she would get one-third), and the wife gets one-eighth (if fetus dead she would get one-fourth, if alive one-eighth). The entire passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of handling uncertainty in inheritance through reservation (waqf) and giving the minimum certain share.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq comments on the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq regarding the missing person (mafqud). The commentator first explains the matn's ruling: a missing person is judged dead after ninety years. He then discusses the extensive intra-Hanafi disagreement on the precise time limit. The apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) from Imam Muhammad uses the extinction of contemporaries—i.e., the missing person is not declared dead as long as any of his contemporaries (peers in age) are alive, because it is rare for someone to outlive all his contemporaries. But the author of Kanz, following Ibn al-Fadl, prefers ninety years, and this is said to be the fatwa. Other opinions include one hundred years from Abu Yusuf, one hundred twenty from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, sixty chosen by later scholars, seventy by Ibn al-Humam, and even a view that no fixed limit should be set, leaving it to the judge's discretion. The commentator notes that many mashayikh chose a fixed term because investigating the death of contemporaries is impractical. The matn then states that upon the judgment of death, the missing person's wife must observe 'iddah and inherits from him, but she does not inherit before that. Similarly, the missing person does not inherit from relatives who died before the judgment, because he is presumed alive via istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). The commentator adds that the same applies to the freedom of his mudabbars and ummahat awlad at that time. The passage then addresses a case where the missing person is a legatee: Muhammad al-Shaybani says the bequest is neither granted nor invalidated until his fate is known; his share is stopped. If at any point his life is confirmed, he inherits from relatives who died before that confirmation, analogous to a fetus (haml). The text then gives a detailed inheritance scenario: a man dies leaving two daughters, a missing son, and a grandson/granddaughter. The estate is in the hands of a third party. The daughters are given half (the certain portion), the other half is stopped for the missing son. The grandchildren get nothing because they are excluded by the missing son (if alive). If the holder of the property claims the missing son died before the father, he is compelled to pay two-thirds to the daughters due to his admission. Finally, the passage compares the missing person's case to that of a fetus (haml) in inheritance: a fetus's share is stopped; heirs who would be eliminated by a live birth (e.g., brother if fetus is a son) get nothing; heirs whose share changes (e.g., mother, wife) get the lesser of the two possible shares. The example of a pregnant woman, mother, and wife illustrates the principle: the mother gets one-sixth (the minimum, because if the fetus is alive she gets one-sixth, if dead she would get one-third), and the wife gets one-eighth (if fetus dead she would get one-fourth, if alive one-eighth). The entire passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of handling uncertainty in inheritance through reservation (waqf) and giving the minimum certain share.

Inheritance of the Missing Person (Mafqud): Stopping Shares and Exclusion
Ruling statement on missing person inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for inheritance when an heir is missing and apply the principle of the lesser of two shares to excluded or diminished heirs.

Core Idea
A missing person's share is stopped; an heir excluded by him gets nothing, and an heir with diminished share gets the lesser certain share.

When a person goes missing, his status is uncertain—he may be alive or dead. This creates complications in inheritance because shares depend on who is alive at the time of death. The Hanafi school resolves this by stopping the share of the missing person until his life or death is established, and protecting other heirs' interests by giving them the certain minimum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Determine if the case involves a bequest to a missing person or inheritance from a missing person's relative. The rules differ slightly.
The passage begins with a bequest, then moves to inheritance.
2
Apply the rule for excluded heirs
If a missing person's existence would exclude another heir (e.g., a grandson from the missing son), that heir receives nothing due to uncertainty.
Exclusion (hajb) here is total (hirman).
3
Apply the rule for diminished heirs
If the missing person only reduces another heir's share (e.g., a mother's share is 1/3 without child but 1/6 with child), the heir receives the lesser certain amount (aqall al-nasibayn).
This is a precautionary principle.
4
Consider the property holder's acknowledgment
If a third party holds the property, his acknowledgment of the missing person's death or life affects distribution. He may be compelled to pay certain shares, while the doubtful part is stopped.
The text gives an example with a stranger holding the estate.
5
Understand the suspension of the bequest
A bequest to a missing person is neither enforced nor nullified but held in abeyance until his status is known. If declared dead, it reverts; if alive, it is given.
Imam Muhammad's view is cited: 'I do not enforce it nor nullify it.'

Therefore, if someone bequeaths to a missing person and the testator dies, the bequest is not valid. But Muhammad said: I do not enforce it nor nullify it until the situation of the missing person becomes clear. Meaning, the share of the missing beneficiary is suspended until his death is declared. When his death is declared, it is as if he died now. The conclusion is that he is alive regarding his own property, so he is not inherited from, but dead regarding others, so he does not inherit. This is if his life is not known until judgment of his death. If his life is known at some time, he inherits from relatives who died before that time, like the fetus due to the possibility of being alive. If it becomes clear that he was alive at the time of his relative's death, [he inherits]; otherwise, the suspended share is returned to the heir of the deceased whose property was suspended. His statement: 'and if there is an heir with the missing person who is excluded by him, he is not given anything, and if his right is diminished by him, he is given the lesser of the two shares.' Explanation: A man dies leaving two daughters, a missing son, and a son's son or a daughter's son. The property is in the hands of a stranger, and they agree on the loss of the son, and the two daughters claim their inheritance. They are given half because it is certain, and the other half is stopped. The children of the son are not given because they are excluded by the missing person if he were alive, so they do not deserve the inheritance due to doubt. The property is not taken from the stranger's hand unless treachery appears, e.g., he denied that the deceased had property with him. The two daughters establish proof against him, and judgment is given for them because one heir can represent the rest. Then the remaining amount is taken and placed with a trustworthy person due to his treachery. If he is among those whose share changes, he is given the lesser for certainty. If someone leaves a pregnant woman and a brother or paternal uncle, nothing is given [to them] because the brother is removed by the son, and it is possible the fetus is a son. Since it is between being removed or not, the original right of entitlement is doubtful, so nothing is given. Commentary
This passage from the sharh explains inheritance rules when an heir is missing or a fetus is involved. It is a ruling_statement elaborating on principles from the matn. The key principle is that when shares are uncertain due to a missing person or unborn child, the certain minimum is given to other heirs, and the questionable portion is delayed until clarity. For the missing person (mafqud), Imam Muhammad holds that a bequest to him is neither enforced nor invalidated but suspended. If a missing person may exclude another heir, that excluded heir gets nothing. If his right is merely diminished, he gets the lesser of the two possible shares. The text gives examples: a missing son with two daughters and a grandson. The daughters get their certain half, the rest is stopped; the grandson gets nothing because his right depends on the missing son being dead, which is uncertain. When a stranger holds the property, his acknowledgment of the missing person's death before the father is accepted regarding the daughters' share, but the leftover is stopped. The fetus (haml) is analogized: one son's share is stopped for the fetus. An heir whose share is unaffected (like a grandmother) gets his full share. An heir who could be excluded (like a brother) gets nothing because of uncertainty. An heir whose share changes (like a mother) gets the lesser share (e.g., one-sixth instead of one-third). The passage concludes with the normative presumption of death based on the lifespan of peers, though scholars differ on its definition.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): A person whose whereabouts and life status are unknown.
أقل النصيبين (aqall al-nasibayn): The lesser of two possible shares for an heir when his share depends on an uncertain factor.
حجب (hajb): Exclusion from inheritance due to the presence of a closer heir.

Inheritance of the Fetus (Haml): The Lesser of Two Shares Principle
Ruling statement on fetus inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school handles inheritance when a pregnant widow is an heir, applying the principle of aqall al-nasibayn to the fetus's share.

Core Idea
A fetus's share is stopped as if it were a son; heirs whose shares may change get the lesser certain amount.

A fetus is a potential heir, but its existence (live birth) determines shares of other heirs. To resolve uncertainty, the Hanafi school stops a share equivalent to one son for the fetus and gives other heirs the minimum share they are certain to receive, regardless of the fetus's status. This lesson explores the detailed application of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and the fetus
List all surviving heirs and note the pregnant woman. The fetus is considered a potential son (or daughter) until birth.
Hanafis stop the share of one son for the fetus.
2
Classify other heirs
Heirs fall into three categories: those unaffected by the fetus (e.g., grandmother), those totally excluded if the fetus is a son (e.g., brother), and those whose share changes (e.g., mother).
Unaltered heirs get full share; excluded heirs get nothing; altered heirs get the lesser share.
3
Apply the 'lesser of two shares' principle
For heirs whose shares depend on the sex and viability of the fetus, calculate what they would receive if the fetus is alive and if it is dead. Give them the smaller amount.
This is a precautionary measure.
4
Stop the fetus's share
The share equivalent to one son is withheld until the child is born. If born alive, it receives that share; if stillborn, the share is redistributed.
If multiple fetuses, adjust accordingly.
5
Consider the presumption of death of peers
The passage concludes with a note that the death of the mafqud can be presumed based on the general lifespan of his peers, though scholars differ on the exact timeframe.
This is more relevant to mafqud but mentioned in context of fetus as analogy.
