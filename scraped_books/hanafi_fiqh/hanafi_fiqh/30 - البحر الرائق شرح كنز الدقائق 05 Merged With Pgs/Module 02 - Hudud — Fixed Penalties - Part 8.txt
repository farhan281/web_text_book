Apostasy of an Intelligent Minor: Valid, Compelled, Not Killed
Establishes ruling with proofs and disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on apostasy of an intelligent minor, explain the proofs, and identify the three positions (Shafi'i/Zufar, Abu Yusuf, Hanafi majority) with their practical consequences.

Core Idea
A minor's apostasy is valid in Hanafi law, but he is not executed—scholarly disagreement creates a doubt that spares his life.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, we examine a unique ruling: an intelligent minor who apostatizes is considered a true apostate in worldly and afterlife rulings, yet he is not executed if he refuses to return. Why? Because the validity of his original Islam is debated among scholars, creating a legal doubt (shubha) that precludes capital punishment. This lesson unpacks the proofs, the disagreements, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the three parts of the ruling: (a) apostasy of an intelligent minor is valid, (b) he is compelled to return to Islam, (c) he is not killed if he refuses.
This is the mufta bihi position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
For the validity of the minor's Islam, Zufar and al-Shafi'i disagree. For the validity of his apostasy, Abu Yusuf disagrees. State each opponent's position and their core reasoning.
Shafi'i/Zufar: minor's Islam invalid because he follows parents and cannot incur harmful rulings. Abu Yusuf: apostasy is pure harm, so invalid.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Summarize the opponents' arguments as presented in the text: the minor is a follower, not an originator, and should not be burdened with harmful rulings (Shafi'i) or that harm alone invalidates the act (Abu Yusuf).
Note the different focus: Shafi'i on the nature of Islam, Abu Yusuf on the nature of apostasy.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds with two proofs: (a) 'Ali became Muslim as a child and the Prophet confirmed it; (b) the reality of Islam (affirmation and confession) can be genuinely realized by a minor, and eternal benefit outweighs any potential harm. For Abu Yusuf, they argue that apostasy is a change of belief, a reality that cannot be denied.
The Hanafi response relies on the principle that realities are not rejected.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical differences: Hanafi compels the minor to return and does not kill; Shafi'i does not consider him an apostate at all; Abu Yusuf does not consider the apostasy valid, so the minor remains Muslim.
All three positions lead to different treatments of the minor.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling on the apostasy (riddah) of an intelligent minor (sabi 'aqil), drawn from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The matn states: 'The apostasy of an intelligent minor is valid like his Islam, he is compelled to return, and is not killed.' The commentator first clarifies the status of the minor's Islam, which triggers the apostasy ruling. He presents the disagreement of Zufar and al-Shafi'i, who argue that a minor's Islam is not valid because he follows his parents and cannot incur harmful rulings. The Hanafi response (lana) provides two proofs: first, the historical fact that 'Ali (ra) embraced Islam as a child and the Prophet (saw) affirmed it; second, a rational argument that the essence of Islam is affirmation (tasdiq) and voluntary confession (iqrar), which a minor can genuinely possess, and since it brings eternal benefit, any potential harm is overlooked. The commentator then discusses whether after puberty one needs a new intention for faith, citing Fath al-Qadir and the views of Fakhr al-Islam and Shams al-A'imma, concluding that there is consensus that no new intention is required for an already valid Islam. He notes a third opinion from al-Maturidi that the minor is addressed with faith itself, but this is not elaborated. For apostasy, there is an intra-Hanafi disagreement: Abu Yusuf held that a minor's apostasy is not valid because it is pure harm, while the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) considered it valid as a reality. Even if valid in worldly rulings, it is agreed that the minor is considered an apostate for afterlife punishment. The ruling that he is not killed despite refusal is due to the existing disagreement among scholars about the validity of minor's Islam, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents capital punishment. However, he is compelled (yujabbar) to return to Islam because the benefit is certain. The commentator lists five cases where an apostate is not killed: (1) the intelligent minor, (2) one whose Islam was by following parents, (3) one who became Muslim as a child and apostatizes after puberty, (4) one coerced into Islam, (5) a foundling in dar al-Islam who reaches puberty as a disbeliever. In all these cases, the apostate is compelled to Islam, but if someone kills him before he returns, no liability (diya or qisas) is incurred. Finally, the ruling is conditioned on the minor being intelligent ('aqil); the apostasy of a non-intelligent minor, an insane person, or a drunkard is not valid as it does not indicate a change of creed.

Key Terms
ارتداد الصبي العاقل (irtidad al-sabi al-'aqil): Apostasy of a minor who has reached the age of discernment (tamyiz) and possesses intellect.
يجير عليه (yujabbar 'alayh): He is compelled and forced to return to Islam, not left as an apostate.
شبهة (shubha): A legal doubt that prevents the application of a prescribed punishment (hadd), in this case, the disagreement among scholars on the minor's Islam.

Apostasy of a Minor and Exceptions to Execution
Ruling on apostasy of minor and list of exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the apostasy of a minor who reached puberty and identify the five cases where an apostate is not executed due to doubt.

Core Idea
A minor who reaches puberty as an apostate is validly apostate but not executed because of doubt about his original Islam.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq examines the complex case of a minor who reaches puberty as an apostate. It provides the Hanafi ruling and lists five scenarios where the apostate is spared execution due to the presence of doubt (shubha). The passage also highlights an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Yusuf and the other two imams.

Guided Path
1
Understand the key dispute
Note that Abu Yusuf considers the apostasy of a minor who reached puberty invalid, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider it valid. Both agree on the Hereafter punishment, but differ on worldly rulings.
This ikhtilaf is central to understanding why execution is suspended.
2
Grasp the principle of shubha
The doubt (shubha) about the validity of the minor's original Islam prevents the application of the hadd punishment for apostasy, which is execution.
Shubha is a key principle in Hanafi criminal law to avoid capital punishment in ambiguous cases.
3
Identify the five exceptional cases
List the five cases given in the text where the apostate is not killed: (1) minor who reached puberty as apostate, (2) one who embraced Islam by following parents and then apostatizes at puberty, (3) one who embraced Islam in childhood and then apostatizes at puberty, (4) one coerced into Islam who later apostatizes, (5) a foundling in Dar al-Islam who becomes a disbeliever at puberty.
In each case, the apostate is compelled to return to Islam.
4
Note who cannot validly apostatize
The insane, a minor without reason, and a drunken person cannot validly apostatize because their declaration does not indicate a genuine change of belief.
Their apostasy is not counted, so no ruling of execution applies.
5
Apply the ruling: consequence of killing before return
If someone kills such an apostate before he returns to Islam, no penalty is imposed on the killer. This is stated in Al-Mabsut.
This reinforces the idea that the apostate's life is not considered fully forfeited due to doubt.

Commentary
This passage deals with the apostasy of a minor who has reached puberty. There is a disagreement between Abu Yusuf and the other two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). Abu Yusuf considers the apostasy of such a minor to be invalid because it is pure harm, while according to the two, apostasy is a reality and therefore valid in this world, though both agree that in the Hereafter the apostate is condemned. The text then lists five cases where an apostate is not killed but is compelled to return to Islam: (1) the minor who reached puberty as an apostate (this case), (2) one whose Islam was by following his parents and then becomes an apostate at puberty, (3) one who embraced Islam in childhood and then apostatizes at puberty, (4) one who was coerced into Islam and then apostatizes, (5) a foundling in Dar al-Islam who reaches puberty as a disbeliever. In all these cases, execution is avoided due to some doubt (shubha) in the validity of his original Islam or in the apostasy. The passage also notes that the insane, the minor without reason, and the drunken person cannot validly apostatize.

Key Terms
ردة (riddah): Apostasy from Islam by word or action or belief.
شبهة (shubhah): A doubt or uncertainty that prevents the application of a hadd punishment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term bughah, distinguish it from khawarij, and state the Hanafi ruling regarding khawarij.

Core Idea
Rebels (bughah) are Muslims; khawarij are a subset treated as rebels, not disbelievers, by the Hanafi school.

This passage opens the chapter on rebels (bughah) in a Hanafi fiqh manual. It first clarifies the lexical meaning of 'baghi' and then provides the technical fiqh definition used by jurists. The key legal point is the classification of those who rebel against the imam: the text distinguishes highway robbers, khawarij, and bughah, and explains that the majority of Hanafi jurists treat khawarij as bughah, not as disbelievers. This distinction has significant consequences for how these groups are handled in Islamic law.

Technical Fiqh Definition
Find the definition cited from Fath al-Qadir: 'the one who leaves the obedience of the true imam'. Understand that this is a lenient definition encompassing all rebels.

3
Three Categories of Rebels
List the three types of people who leave the imam's obedience: highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq), khawarij, and bughah. Note that highway robbers have a separate ruling already discussed.
This categorization helps in applying the correct legal treatment.
4
Distinguishing Khawarij
Identify the specific characteristics of khawarij as mentioned: they rebel with an interpretation, consider the imam a disbeliever or sinner, declare Muslim blood and property lawful, capture women, and revile the Companions.
These extreme beliefs set them apart from ordinary rebels.
5
Hanafi Ruling on Khawarij
State that according to the majority of Hanafi jurists and hadith scholars, khawarij are treated as bughah (rebels) – not as disbelievers. Note the minority view that some hadith scholars consider them disbelievers.
The practical effect: khawarij are fought but their lives and property are protected as Muslims unless they commit apostasy.

Commentary
This passage begins the chapter on rebels (Bughah). The author explains the lexical meaning of 'baghi' as one who oppresses or seeks corruption, and notes that the term is used for both men and women but with different connotations. The technical definition in fiqh, as in Fath al-Qadir, is one who leaves the obedience of the rightful imam. The passage then categorizes those who leave the imam's obedience into three types: highway robbers, Khawarij, and Bughah. The Khawarij are distinguished by their extreme beliefs: they declare Muslims as disbelievers, permit bloodshed and plunder, and revile the Companions. The majority of jurists and hadith scholars treat Khawarij as Bughah (rebels) with the same legal rulings, but some hadith scholars consider them disbelievers. This distinction affects the rulings on fighting them and the status of their lives and property.

Key Terms
بغاة (bughāh): Plural of baghi; a group that rebels against the legitimate imam with force and an interpretation.
خوارج (khawārij): An extreme group that rebels against the imam, declares Muslims disbelievers, and justifies killing them.
