Disciplinary Beatings in Hanafi Law: Husband, Parent, Teacher
Explains when disciplinary beating is permissible and when it incurs liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four situations where a Hanafi husband may discipline his wife with light beating, explain the condition of non-liability, and apply the rule to analogous cases.

Core Idea
Husband's disciplinary beating is a permission, not an obligation; liability arises when the beating is excessive or without right.

The Hanafi school grants a husband limited authority to discipline his wife for specific transgressions. However, this authority is tightly regulated: it is not an obligation, and if the beating causes death, liability depends on whether the beating was commanded by the Lawgiver or merely permitted. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Bahr al-Raiq, clarifying the four recognized grounds, the condition of non-liability, and the broader principle of ta'zir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
Determine that disciplinary beating is permissible (mubah), not obligatory (wajib). The source explicitly states: 'the husband is not obligated to beat his wife at all'.
2
List the four specific causes
The wife abandons obligatory prayer, refuses legitimate adornment, refuses intimacy without excuse, or leaves home without permission. Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on the first cause.
Al-Walwalji adds 'and what is similar', expanding the list to analogous sins.
3
Analyze the liability rule
If the beating is commanded by the Lawgiver (e.g., for a clear sin), no liability for death. If merely permitted (like passing through a road), liability attaches if death occurs because it is conditioned on safety.
This mirrors the rule for permissible acts that risk harm.
4
Examine the conditions and limits
The beating must be light (non-injurious), intended for correction, and not excessive. Custom (urf) may also restrict it. If beaten without right, the husband himself faces ta'zir.
Conditions from the source: wife must be capable, pure from hayd/nifas, and the act must be without valid excuse.
5
Apply the principle of ta'zir to other relations
The same principle applies to a master disciplining his slave, and to parents/teachers disciplining minors, but with rules on liability for excessive force.
The source explicitly links this to the Quranic verse 'And strike them' (4:34).

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is an expansion on disciplinary beatings within the Hanafi school. It begins by establishing the principle that if a husband beats his wife for a purpose commanded by the Lawgiver (like disciplining her for abandoning prayer or refusing intimacy), and she dies, he is not liable; but if the beating is merely permitted (like hitting her to stop her from harming someone), he is liable because it is conditioned on safety. The text clarifies that the husband is never obligated to beat his wife; it is a permission. The core ruling is that a husband may beat his wife in four situations: leaving prayer, refusing adornment, refusing intimacy, and going out without permission. However, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute on beating for leaving prayer: many later scholars permit it, but some (like al-Hakim) disallow it because the benefit returns only to the wife. Al-Walwalji expands this to four things 'and what is similar', meaning the permission is not exhaustive. Similar cases include: if she beats her husband's slave out of jealousy, if she beats a crying child, if she curses or insults him, if she talks to a non-mahram, if she gives away food without custom, etc. The common thread is that she commits a sin with no fixed hadd; the husband may administer ta'zir, just as a master may discipline his slave. If the husband beats her without right, he himself is subject to ta'zir. The second part discusses ta'zir for minors. The Hanafi position is that ta'zir is valid for minors near puberty for discipline; the view that ta'zir drops upon reaching puberty (attributed to Shafi'is) is rejected in the preferred Hanafi view, especially if the right is for a human. The father and teacher are generally not liable for beating a child moderately for discipline, but excessive beating incurs liability. The text concludes by citing the Quranic verse 'and strike them' (4:34) as the proof for the husband's right to discipline.

Key Terms
التعزير (al-ta'zir): A discretionary punishment determined by the judge (or the husband/master) for sins that have no fixed hadd (e.g., cursing, minor assault, disobedience).
الضرب (al-darb): Beating as a form of discipline, not punishment; it must be light, non-injurious, and not excessive.

How a Musta'min Becomes a Dhimmi through Marriage and Kharaj
Explains when a musta'min becomes a dhimmi by marrying a dhimmi or through kharaj obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a musta'min woman becomes a dhimmi by marriage, and explain why a musta'min man does not become dhimmi by marrying a dhimmi woman, citing the underlying rationale of permanent residence.

Core Idea
Marriage to a dhimmi obligates a musta'min woman to permanent residence; kharaj obligation similarly establishes dhimmi status.

In Hanafi siyar, the status of a musta'min (temporary resident) can change to that of a dhimmi (permanent subject) through actions that indicate a commitment to residing in Dar al-Islam. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains two key triggers: marriage to a dhimmi and the obligation to pay kharaj. Understanding these rules is essential for determining the legal obligations (jizya, kharaj) and rights (ability to return to Dar al-Harb) of non-Muslims in the Islamic state.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actors and their initial statuses
Determine who is a musta'min (temporary resident with aman), who is a dhimmi (permanent subject), and who is a harbi (enemy non-Muslim). Each has different legal capacities.
The musta'min woman is the focus; she enters with aman.
2
Analyze the marriage scenario: woman vs man
Examine why a musta'min woman becomes dhimmi by marrying a dhimmi (or Muslim a fortiori) while the opposite does not hold. The key is commitment to residence: the woman follows her husband, implying permanence; the man can divorce and return.
The text explicitly says 'لا عكسه' (not the opposite).
3
Examine the kharaj scenario
When a harbi owns land subject to kharaj, he does not become dhimmi merely by cultivation; rather, when the kharaj becomes due, he is considered dhimmi and jizya becomes obligatory the following year.
Kharaj obligation is a trigger, not cultivation itself.
4
Note the evidentiary dispute
There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: al-Sarakhsi holds that evidence of marriage confession from Dar al-Harb is not accepted; al-Hinduwani accepts it absolutely. The mufta bihi is al-Sarakhsi's view.
This affects whether a wife can be prevented from returning.
5
Synthesize the underlying principle
All these rules revolve around the concept of iltizam al-iqama (commitment to residence). Marriage for the woman and kharaj liability both indicate such commitment, triggering dhimmi status.
This principle can be applied to other scenarios.

The Five Acts That Depend on Religion and the Five That Do Not
Enumeration of acts valid or invalid from an apostate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five acts that are invalid from an apostate because they depend on religion, and the five acts that are valid by consensus, and explain the underlying principle of dependence on millah.

Core Idea
An apostate's acts are either dependent on religion (invalid) or not (valid); five in each category by consensus.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a crucial classification in Hanafi fiqh: the acts of an apostate are divided into two categories. Understanding which acts depend on the very basis of religion (millah) and which do not is fundamental to predicting the validity of an apostate's transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Look for the phrases 'ما يعتمد الملة' (what depends on religion) and 'ما لا يعتمد الملة' (what does not depend on religion).
The second category is also defined as not depending on guardianship (wilayah) or true ownership (haqiqat milk).
2

Memorize these lists as they are agreed upon in the Hanafi school.
3

Acts dependent on religion require Islamic faith (iman) for validity. Acts not dependent on religion rely only on legal capacity (guardianship or ownership), which the apostate still possesses.
This explains why divorce is valid—it is a right of ownership/guardianship, not religious belief.
4
Check for consensus
The passage uses 'اتفاقاً' (by agreement) for both sets, indicating no intra-Hanafi dispute on the classification itself.
Disputes arise only in the details, not in the core classification.
5
Distinguish 'millah' from 'wilayah' and 'haqiqat milk'
The second category is explicitly not dependent on these three, meaning acts that require religious faith (millah) are invalid, while acts that only require legal capacity (guardianship or ownership) are valid.

Inheritance of Child Born to Apostate's Slave Woman
Commentary on child born to apostate's slave woman six months after apostasy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the child born to an apostate's slave woman and apply the conditions that determine inheritance.

Core Idea
The child's religion follows the mother, determining inheritance from an apostate father.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a man apostatizes and his Christian slave woman gives birth six months later. The commentary clarifies the child's status, inheritance rights, and the role of the mother's religion. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to timing, parentage, and the religion of the parties involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their statuses
Determine the father's status (apostate), the mother's religion (Christian or Muslim), and the child's birth timing relative to apostasy.
The father's apostasy triggers special rules for marriage and children.
2
Evaluate the validity of istilad (claiming paternity)
Check whether the father's claim makes her umm walad and the child free. The key point: istilad does not require ownership at conception; it suffices that the child is born to his slave.
This is a distinctive Hanafi principle.
3
Determine the child's religion
The child's religion follows the mother if she is Christian (due to the father's apostasy) or if she is Muslim (because she is the better religion).
This contrasts with the general rule that children follow the father's religion.
4
Apply the inheritance rule
If the child is ruled apostate (because mother is Christian), they cannot inherit. If the child is Muslim (because mother is Muslim), they do inherit from the apostate father.
Apostates do not inherit from anyone, but Muslims inherit from apostates.
5

Limits of the Finder's Authority Over a Foundling's Services
Continuing foundling chapter: finder's authority over benefits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the limits of the finder's authority over a foundling's benefits and distinguish permissible from impermissible actions.

Core Idea
The finder may use the foundling's services only for the foundling's training and benefit, not for personal profit.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the boundaries of the finder's (multaqit) authority over the foundling (laqit). While the finder can manage the foundling's property and even train him in a craft, he cannot exploit his services for personal gain. The text also addresses the issue of circumcision and resolves a seeming contradiction about hiring.

Guided Path
1
Identify permitted actions
List the actions the finder may perform: selling the foundling's property, hiring him out (for the foundling's benefit), handing him over for a craft, and accepting his gift.

2
Identify prohibited actions

The reasoning: wasting the foundling's benefits is impermissible, and circumcision is not part of tathqif.

Note the contrast with the mother (who may use the child's services for herself) and the paternal uncle (who may not, like the finder).
This comparison indicates that the finder's authority is closer to that of a non-relative caretaker.

Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The justification for teaching a craft or hiring for the foundling's benefit is tathqif (training, discipline) and preservation of the child's wealth. Prohibited actions do not serve these ends.
The term tathqif is derived from straightening a spear, metaphorically for upbringing.
5
Note the role of the ruler
Ultimate authority over the foundling's person and property rests with the ruler (sultan). The finder's authority is a limited delegation; if the ruler formally assigns guardianship, the finder's powers expand.

No Separation Between Missing Person and His Wife
Proves that the wife of a missing person remains married until death or divorce is certain

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding the wife of a missing person and explain the evidence from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
A missing person's wife remains married until definite proof of death or divorce arrives — absence alone does not dissolve the marriage.

This lesson examines the ruling on a missing person's marital bond. The Hanafi school holds that the wife must wait indefinitely until clear evidence (bayān) of her husband's death or divorce is established. This is based on a prophetic hadith and the practice of the companions, and it distinguishes this case from other forms of separation like ilā' or impotence.

The act under discussion is separating a missing person from his wife. The ruling is that it is not permitted (haram) — the wife remains his wife.

The hadith is the core evidence.

The phrase 'حتى' (until) indicates continuation of the marriage until a specified event (bayān). Absence alone does not trigger dissolution.

Umar initially disagreed but retracted to Ali's view, establishing a consensus. The passage also distinguishes from the case of ilā' and impotence.
Other cases have different rulings.
