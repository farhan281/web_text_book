Definition and Conditions of Actual Possession (Qabd Haqiqatan)
Defining actual possession and the three conditions for valid delivery in sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for valid delivery (taslim) in a sale and identify which acts constitute actual possession (qabd haqiqi) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Possession in sale is established either by physical taking or by the seller's declaration of vacating (takhliya) with the buyer's ability to take, provided three conditions are met.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the Hanafi concept of possession (qabd) in sales. It distinguishes actual possession (by hand) from legal possession (by vacating), and specifies three conditions for valid delivery (taslim). Understanding these conditions is crucial for determining when a sale is complete and liability transfers.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the main distinction: actual vs. legal possession
The commentator suggests replacing 'in reality' with 'legally' because physical taking by hand is the true reality, while vacating only enables it. Note that takhliya constitutes legal possession (qabd hukmi) in the Hanafi school.
This distinction affects when risk transfers to the buyer.
2
Identify the three conditions for valid delivery (taslim)
From the sources cited (Jami' al-Fusulayn, al-Ajnas), the conditions are: (a) the seller declares 'I have vacated between you and the sold item', (b) the buyer is present and able to take without hindrance, (c) the item is separated and not encumbered by another's right.
The first condition emphasizes verbal declaration, which is a key Hanafi requirement.
3
Examine the examples of valid and invalid possession
Physical acts like leading an animal, having a slave walk with you, taking a garment, or sending a slave on an errand count as possession. Giving a key without verbal declaration does not. Fruits on trees are validly delivered by vacating even if attached.
These examples illustrate the application of the three conditions.
4
Note the special case of void sales (bay' fasid)
In a void sale, possession also occurs by takhliya, which transfers liability to the buyer. This shows that takhliya is a recognized legal mode of possession even in irregular transactions.
This aligns with the general principle that takhliya suffices for legal possession.
5
Understand the practical consequence of the verbal declaration requirement
The requirement of verbal declaration means that physical acts alone (like handing over keys) without spoken words do not transfer possession. This protects both parties by making the transfer of possession explicit.
Contrast with schools that consider physical delivery alone sufficient.

Key Terms

تسليم (taslim): Delivery of the sold item by the seller, valid with three conditions as listed.

Possession by Takhliya for Distant Properties: The Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on possession by takhliya for distant properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on possession by takhliya for distant properties and explain why the mufta bihi position follows al-Halwani.

Core Idea
Vacating a distant property does not transfer possession; only physical control suffices in the Hanafi school.

In a sale contract, valid possession is required for transfer of liability. Hanafi law recognizes 'takhliya' (vacating) as a means of possession, but only when the buyer can physically take control. This lesson explores an intra-madhhab dispute: does takhliya work for distant properties? By examining the positions of Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn, and al-Halwani, we will see why the relied-upon view requires actual physical control.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify that for a 'near' property (one the buyer can immediately close or access), takhliya by the seller and verbal acceptance by the buyer constitute valid possession.
Near property defined: buyer can close a door without difficulty.
2
Identify the Dispute
Note the disagreement: Abu Hanifa (reported in Jami' Shams al-A'immah) holds that takhliya is valid even for distant properties, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Halwani hold it is not.
Distant property: buyer cannot physically take control at the time of vacating.
3
Trace the Authority Chain
Recognize that al-Halwani's view is identified as Zahir al-Riwayah (the apparent transmission from the founding imams) in sources like al-Khaniyyah and al-Zahiriyyah, giving it greater weight.
Zahir al-Riwayah carries the highest authority in the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the Examples
Analyze the illustrative cases: handing over a key is valid only if immediate opening is possible; for animals, possession requires ability to catch without help; riding together without a saddle does not transfer possession.
All examples reinforce the condition of physical control.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Conclude that the relied-upon position is al-Halwani's: takhliya of a distant property does not effect valid possession, and this applies to sales, leases, gifts, and acknowledgments.
Contrary views in al-Muhit and Jami' Shams al-A'immah are considered weak.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key dispute within the Hanafi school regarding possession by takhliya (vacating) when the sold property is distant from the parties. The starting point is a distinction: if a house is 'near' (i.e., the buyer can physically close it immediately), then verbal delivery and acceptance constitute possession; if 'distant', they do not. Al-Halwani reports from al-Nawadir that vacating is only effective for near properties, and criticizes common practice. In Jami' Shams al-A'immah, Abu Hanifa holds that possession is valid even for distant real estate, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree. The commentator then sides with al-Halwani, stating that this view is the Zahir al-Riwaya (the apparent transmission from the founding Imams) as confirmed in al-Khaniyyah and al-Zahiriyyah, and that the contrary views in al-Muhit and Jami' Shams al-A'immah are weak. Practical examples follow: handing over a key is delivery only if immediate opening is possible; birds and horses require ability to catch; riding together does not transfer possession without a saddle. The passage concludes that the rule also applies to lease and acknowledgment.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The apparent or clear transmission from the founding Imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) as recorded in the six core books.
قريب/بعيد (qarib/ba'id): Near: the buyer can physically take control of the property immediately (e.g., able to close a door). Distant: cannot.

Ten Actions That Constitute Possession with Seller's Permission
Listing and explaining ten actions that constitute possession with seller's permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten actions that legally transfer possession and explain the condition of the seller's permission.

Core Idea
Legal possession can be established by the seller's specific actions with the buyer's permission, even without physical handling.

In Hanafi fiqh, possession is not always physical. The Bazzaziyyah collection lists ten actions that, when performed by the seller with the buyer's permission, transfer legal possession to the buyer. This lesson explains these actions, their conditions, and the implications for liability and defect rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ten actions
Read the list of actions from circumcision to renting the house. Note that each action requires the buyer's permission.
The actions vary: they include acts on slaves, animals, garments, food, and real estate.
2
Understand the role of permission
Permission is essential. Without it, the buyer does not become a possessor. The seller acts as the buyer's agent.
3
Distinguish scenarios where possession is not achieved
For example, marrying a slave girl without consummation does not constitute possession. Similarly, giving goods to the buyer's wife does not transfer possession.
4
Note the intra-madhhab dispute on freeing a slave
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider freeing as valid possession, while Abu Yusuf disagrees. The majority view is relied upon.
This dispute is an example of legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from the Bazzaziyyah collection enumerates ten specific actions that, when performed by the seller with the buyer's permission, legally constitute possession (qabd) by the buyer. These include: circumcising a slave, cupping, cutting a horse's mane, laundering, washing, making soles for shoes, cooking food, renting the house back from the seller, and marrying off a slave girl (with consummation). The common principle is that the seller's act with permission transforms the buyer into a possessor, transferring liability. Importantly, knowledge of a defect before such acts bars the right to return the item. The passage also discusses related scenarios: hiring the seller to wash or cut (if it decreases value, possession occurs), freeing a slave before possession (valid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad but not Abu Yusuf), and throwing goods into water (valid for possession but not for debt repayment). Later paragraphs address possession without seller's permission, tadbir, kitaba, lease conflicts, and sale of absent goods.

Key Terms
قبض (qabd): Legal possession established by specific actions of the seller with the buyer's permission, even without physical handling.
إذن المشتري (idhn al-mushtari): Permission of the buyer for the seller to perform an act that results in possession.

Checkpoint
Which of the following actions, when done by the seller with the buyer's permission, makes the buyer a possessor?
A) The seller gives the item to the buyer's friend.
B) The seller launders a garment.
C) The seller throws the item into water without permission.
D) The seller rents the house to a third party.

Key Takeaway
The ten actions are a unique Hanafi device to transfer possession through seller's permitted acts, with significant implications for liability and defect rights.
