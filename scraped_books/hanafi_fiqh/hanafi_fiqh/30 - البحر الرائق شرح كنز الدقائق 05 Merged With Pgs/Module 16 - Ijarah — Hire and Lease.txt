Rights of Lessee, Depositor, Borrower in Captured Property
Ruling on possessors' right to reclaim captured property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the difference in the right of mukhasamah before and after division for a lessee versus a borrower/depositor in captured property.

Core Idea
Before division, lessee, depositor, borrower may litigate; after division, only the lessee retains the right to reclaim by paying value.

The passage begins with a question about whether a lessee, depositor, or borrower who had the captured item in hand before capture can litigate to reclaim it. The Hanafi scholars clarify that before division of the spoils, all three may litigate and take the property without payment. However, after division, only the lessee retains the right to reclaim by paying the value; the borrower and depositor are like strangers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The text mentions three types of possessors: musta'jir (lessee), mudi' (depositor), musta'ir (borrower). Note their relationship to the captured property: they had it on lease, deposit, or loan before capture.
These are not owners but have a possessory interest.
2
Distinguish before vs. after division
Before division of the spoils, all three may litigate (mukhasamah) and take the property without payment. After division, only the lessee may reclaim by paying the value; the borrower and depositor lose the right to litigate.
Why? Because the lessee has a stronger right due to the lease contract.
3
Understand the effect on lease
If the lessee reclaims before division, the slave returns to the lease and the rent is waived for the period of captivity. This shows the lease resumes.
The lessee does not pay rent for the time the slave was in enemy hands.
4
Note the evidentiary rule
If the person who received the slave in his share denies the lease, the lessee can bring evidence to prove it, and it will be accepted.

Dispositions by Captor: Tadbir, Emancipation, Marriage, and Claims on Uqr/Arsh
Ruling on the effect of dispositions by the captor on captured slaves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the original master cannot annul a captor's tadbir or emancipation, but can take children born from marriage, and why uqr and arsh are separate.

Core Idea
The captor's disposition in a slave he owns is valid; original master cannot undo it, but can take offspring because they are part of the slave, while uqr/arsh are separate property.

This lesson examines the rulings when a person who receives a captured slave in his share performs tadbir (promise of freedom upon death), emancipation, or marriage. The Hanafi school affirms the validity of these acts by the new owner. The original master cannot annul them. However, the original master may take the slave and her children born after marriage, but the uqr (dower) and arsh (compensation for injury) belong to the captor, not the original master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts
The passage mentions tadbir, emancipation (itq), and marriage (tazwij) by the captor. Note that tadbir is a promise of freedom after the owner's death, and emancipation is immediate freedom.
These are dispositions of ownership.
2
Rule on tadbir and emancipation
If the captor declares tadbir or emancipates the captured slave, it is valid and the original master has no claim. The captor's ownership gives him full authority.
The text says 'لا يبقى للمولى عليه سبيل'.
3
Rule on marriage and offspring
If the captor marries off the female slave, the original master may take her and her child because the child is from her parts (ajza'), so it follows her. Marriage does not prevent taking (nifl).
The original master can take both mother and child.
4
Rule on uqr and arsh
If the captor takes uqr (dower from marriage) or arsh (compensation for injury) from someone, the original master cannot claim them because they originated in the captor's ownership and are not part of the slave's body.
Uqr and arsh are fungibles and redemption does not apply.

Guardian's Right and Pawn vs. Lease of Captured Property
Rulings on guardian's right and pawn/lease scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the guardian's right to reclaim captured property for the orphan and for himself, and distinguish the effect of pawn vs. lease on the original master's right.

Core Idea
A guardian may reclaim captured property for the orphan but not for himself; a pawn blocks the original master's claim until redeemed, but a lease can be nullified because it dissolves with excuse.

This lesson covers two distinct rulings: first, the guardian of an orphan may reclaim a captured slave from the buyer by paying the price, but only for the orphan, provided the price equals the value. Second, if the buyer pawns the slave, the original master cannot take it until the pawn is redeemed, and he is not forced to redeem. However, if the buyer leases the slave, the original master may take it and nullify the lease, because the lease dissolves due to excuses (like capture).

Guided Path
1
Understand the guardian's role
The guardian (wasi) has authority to manage the orphan's property. He may reclaim a captured slave from the buyer by paying the price, but only for the orphan, not for himself. The price must equal the slave's value.
The guardian cannot benefit personally from the transaction.
2
Pawn vs. Lease distinction
If the buyer pawned the slave, the original master cannot reclaim him until the pawn is redeemed. He is not forced to redeem unless he wishes. In contrast, if the buyer leased the slave, the original master can take him and cancel the lease, because lease contracts are subject to dissolution due to valid excuses.
The difference hinges on the nature of the contract: pawn is a security that persists, lease is a service contract that can be terminated.
3
Rationale behind lease nullification
The text states that the lease 'dissolves due to excuses' (تنفسح بالأعذار). Captivity is considered an excuse that allows the original master to reclaim and nullify the lease.
This is unlike pawn, which is a security right that cannot be easily dissolved.

Slave with Crime or Debt Liability: Effect of Capture
Ruling on liabilities of a captured slave with prior crime or debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the treatment of intentional crime, debt, and accidental crime when a captured slave returns to the original master or not.

Core Idea
When a captured slave returns to the original master's ownership, all liabilities remain on his neck; if he does not return or returns under new ownership, intentional crime and debt persist but accidental crime is voided.

The final ruling in this passage concerns a slave who had a crime (jinaayah) or debt (dayn) on his neck before capture. If he returns to his old master's ownership, the liabilities remain. If he does not return (e.g., remains with the captor) or returns under a new ownership (e.g., purchased by another), then intentional crime and debt remain because they are attached to his life or liability, but accidental crime (jinaayatul khata') is voided because it is attached to his material value, which changes ownership that does not replace the original.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two variables
First variable: whether the slave returns to the original master's ownership or not. Second variable: type of liability: intentional crime, debt, accidental crime.
2
Rule when returns to original master
If the slave returns to the original master (by reclaim), then all liabilities (intentional crime, debt, accidental crime) remain on his neck, i.e., the master is responsible.
The text says 'فالكل في رقبته'.
3
Rule when does not return or returns under new ownership
If the slave does not return to the original master (stays with captor) or returns under a new ownership (e.g., sold to someone else), then the intentional crime and debt remain (they are attached to his life/dhimma), but the accidental crime is voided because it is attached to his material value, which changed to a new ownership that does not replace the old one.
The text says 'جناية العمد والدين بحاله وسقطت جناية الخطأ'.
4
Understand the rationale
Intentional crime is linked to the slave's life (if he is executed, it ends), debt is linked to his dhimma (legal capacity), so they survive ownership change. Accidental crime is linked to his monetary value, so when ownership changes to a non-compensating ownership, it drops.
Reference: 'كما في المحيط'.

The Hired Money Changer's Liability for Counterfeit Coins
Commentary on the ruling of a hired money changer who returns counterfeit coins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a hired money changer's liability when counterfeit coins are discovered and explain why the fee must be returned proportionally.

Core Idea
A hired expert is not liable for loss but must return the fee if the service is defective.

When you hire a professional to examine coins and they turn out to be counterfeit, who bears the loss? This lesson dives into a classic Hanafi ruling from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, where the commentator Ibn Nujaym clarifies that the money changer must return his fee because his contracted work was not fulfilled. We'll explore the main position, a conflicting opinion, and the basis for the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main scenario and ruling
Understand the core case: a money changer (صيرفي) is hired to examine dirhams (نقد) for a fee. After examination, counterfeit coins (زيوف) are discovered. The ruling from al-Bazzaziyyah is that the changer must return the entire fee if all are counterfeit, or a proportional amount if only some are counterfeit.
The fee is returned because the purpose of the contract—proper examination—was not achieved.
2
Examine the supporting evidence from other sources
Review the additional citations: Imam al-Turi's fatwa, al-Muhit al-Muntaqa, and al-Khaniyyah. All confirm the same ruling: the fee is returned because the lessor (the money changer) did not fulfill the work (لم يوف عمله).
Note that in al-Muhit, it explicitly states there is no liability for the value of the coins (لا ضمان عليه).
3
Analyze the conflicting opinion from Jannat al-Ahkam
Abu Bakr in Jannat al-Ahkam holds that the examiner is not liable for damages and is not entitled to any fee, even if he botched the examination. The commentator points out this contradicts the majority view.
The substitution (بدل) falls on the person who gave the money, not the examiner.
4
Determine the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The commentator (Ibn Nujaym) favors the position of al-Bazzaziyyah and al-Khaniyyah, which is also found in al-Bahr. This is the mufta bihi: the fee must be returned.
This illustrates the Hanafi method of tarjih: preferring the view supported by multiple authoritative sources.

And he said in Jannat al-Ahkam: Abu Bakr was asked about a man who examined another man's dirhams but did not examine properly; is he required to guarantee? And is he entitled to a fee? He said: No guarantee on him, and the substitute is on the one from whom the money was taken, and no fee for the examiner. And you are aware that this contradicts what was transmitted in al-Bahr from al-Bazzaziyyah where he said in Ijarat al-Bazzaziyyah etc. I said: And I saw in al-Khaniyyah a mention similar to what is in al-Bazzaziyyah, mentioned before the chapter on invalid sale.

Commentary
This passage discusses the liability of a money changer (صيرفي) who is hired to examine coins (نقد) and later counterfeit coins are discovered. The commentator quotes several rulings. According to al-Bazzaziyyah, if the money changer is hired to examine dirhams and all turn out to be counterfeit, he must return the entire fee; if only some are counterfeit, he returns proportionally. Imam al-Turi rules similarly: if all are fake, the client may reclaim the fee. Al-Muhit al-Muntaqa adds that if the examiner finds the coins but they are defective, there is no liability on the changer, but he must return the fee because he did not perform the work properly. Then a contradictory opinion from Jannat al-Ahkam is cited: if the examiner did not examine properly, he is not liable for damages and is not entitled to a fee; the substitute (بدل) is on the person who gave the money (the owner). The commentator notes the contradiction and states that he saw in al-Khaniyyah a similar ruling to al-Bazzaziyyah, which is the relied-upon view. The lesson highlights the principle that a hired service provider is not liable for loss but must return fees if the service is not properly performed.

Key Terms
صيرفي (ṣayrafī): A money changer or coin examiner hired to check the quality and authenticity of coins.
نقد (naqd): The act of examining or checking coins for genuineness and weight.
زيوف (zuyūf): Counterfeit or defective coins (singular: زائف).
أجرة (ujrah): Fee or hire paid for the service of the money changer.
