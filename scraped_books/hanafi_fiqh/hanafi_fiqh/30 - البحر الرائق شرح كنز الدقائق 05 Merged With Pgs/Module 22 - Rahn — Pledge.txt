Witnessing Requirement for Taking a Runaway Pawned Slave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witnessing requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi requirement of witnessing when taking a runaway pawned slave and explain the intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Yusuf and the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).

Core Idea
Witnessing your intent to return a runaway slave is required to avoid liability and claim compensation.

When a pawned slave runs away, the person who retrieves him must fulfill certain conditions to avoid being held liable and to claim compensation. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on whether witnessing (ishhad) is necessary for these purposes. This lesson examines the positions of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) versus Abu Yusuf alone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
Recognize that the ruling concerns a person (taker) who finds and returns a runaway pawned slave. The key issue is whether he must witness his intention to return.
The taker is not the owner or pledgee.
2
State the requirement according to the two
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that witnessing (ishhad) is a condition for both non-liability (if the slave runs away again or dies) and entitlement to compensation (ja'ala). Without witnessing, the taker is treated as taking for himself.
They analogize to a buyer or heir who gets no compensation.
3
Explain Abu Yusuf's exception
Abu Yusuf does not require witnessing for non-liability; the taker is a trustee (amin) regardless, as in the case of found property (luqata). However, he agrees that witnessing is needed for compensation.
The text clarifies that the two disagree with Abu Yusuf on non-liability.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the taker does not witness, according to the two he is liable if the slave runs away and gets no compensation. According to Abu Yusuf, he is not liable but still gets no compensation (since all agree compensation requires witnessing). The mufta bihi is the view of the two.
The practical difference is liability for loss.
5
Note the exception of inability
If the taker cannot find witnesses, witnessing is not required by agreement. His word is accepted regarding inability.
This parallels the rules of luqata.

Commentary
This lesson covers two main points from the matn. First, the requirement of witnessing (ishhad) when taking a runaway slave. The commentator explains that witnessing serves two purposes: it negates liability if the slave runs away or dies, and it entitles the retriever to compensation. The majority (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider witnessing a condition for both, while Abu Yusuf does not require it for non-liability. If the retriever fails to witness, he is treated as having taken the slave for himself, akin to a purchaser or heir, and gets no compensation unless he explicitly witnesses his intention to return. However, if the retriever was unable to find witnesses, then witnessing is not required, and his statement is accepted. This is analogous to the rules for found property (luqata). Second, the matn states that compensation for a pawned slave is on the pledgee (murtahin), not the pledgor (rahin), because the benefit of returning the slave accrues to the pledgee's security interest. The commentator clarifies that this applies even after the pledgor's death, as the pledge remains valid. However, if the slave's value exceeds the debt, the pledgee only pays compensation proportional to his debt, and the pledgor pays the remainder. The discussion extends to other cases: if a slave is bequeathed with usufruct to one person and corpus to another, the compensation is on the owner of the usufruct. For a slave authorized to trade with debt, compensation is on the person who ultimately owns him (the master if he pays the debt, or from the sale price if sold). The longest section deals with a runaway slave who committed unlawful homicide (jinaaya). If the homicide is unintentional (khata'), the compensation (diya) is on the person who chooses to keep the slave: the master if he ransoms him, or the victim's heirs if the master hands him over. If the master initially pays the compensation and then is forced to hand over the slave, he can recover from the heirs. If the homicide is intentional ('amd), no compensation for returning the slave. If the injury occurred while the slave is in the retriever's possession, no compensation. If it occurred after the slave ran away but before recapture, and the slave is killed, no compensation for the retriever; if handed over to the guardian, compensation is on the guardian.

Key Terms
الإشهاد (al-ishhad): The act of taking witnesses to testify that the retriever took the slave to return it to the owner.
الجعل (al-ja'ala): Compensation or finder's fee for returning a runaway slave.

Compensation for Returning a Pawned Runaway Slave
Ruling on compensation liability for returning a pawned runaway slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that compensation for returning a pawned runaway slave is on the pledgee and apply the qualification when the slave's value exceeds the debt.

Core Idea
The pledgee pays compensation proportional to his debt; the pledgor pays the surplus value.

When a pawned slave is returned, who should pay the finder's compensation? The Hanafi school places the obligation on the pledgee because the benefit of the return strengthens his security. However, this rule is not absolute; the pledgee only pays to the extent of his debt, and additional value is the pledgor's responsibility. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its extensions to cases involving bequests, authorized slaves, and criminal liability.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling
The matn says: 'The compensation is upon the pledgee.' The commentator explains the rationale: the pledgee revived the financial value of the slave by returning it, and since the slave secures his debt, the compensation is on him.
The pledgee is the murtahin, the creditor holding the pawn.
2
Understand the qualification when value exceeds debt
The commentator qualifies the absolute statement: if the slave's value is more than the debt, the pledgee pays only proportional to his debt, and the pledgor pays the remainder. Reason: the pledgee's right is only in the amount secured.
Analogy to medicine and ransom: the cost is proportional to the benefit.
3
Apply to other situations: bequests
If a slave is bequeathed with usufruct to one person and corpus to another, compensation is on the owner of the usufruct (the one who benefits from the return). After the usufruct period ends, he may recover from the corpus owner.
Benefit determines who pays.
4
Apply to authorized slaves (ma'dhun) with debt
If the master pays the slave's debt, compensation is on the master; if he sells the slave, compensation is taken from the sale price. Nothing on the buyer.
The compensation follows the ultimate owner.
5
Handle cases of criminal injury (jinaya)
If the slave committed unintentional homicide (khata') after running away but before recapture, compensation is on the one who chooses to keep him: master if he ransoms, heirs if he hands over. If intentional (amd), no compensation. If injury occurs while in taker's possession, no compensation.
Only unintentional injuries outside the taker's possession trigger compensation.

Pawning Partnership Property: 'Inan vs. Mufawada
Rules for a partner pledging or taking pawn on behalf of the partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a partner pawning partnership property in 'inan and mufawada partnerships, and identify the condition of permission.

Core Idea
In partnership, authority to pawn property depends on the type: 'inan requires permission, mufawada grants full authority.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a key question in partnership law: can one partner pledge partnership property as collateral? The answer depends on the nature of the partnership. The distinction between 'inan (limited) and mufawada (unlimited) partnerships is fundamental in Hanafi fiqh. Let us examine the rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify partnership types
The passage mentions two types: shirkat al-'inan and shirkat al-mufawada. Note that 'inan is a partnership of capital where each partner contributes and profits are shared proportionally; mufawada is a full agency partnership where each partner has unlimited authority.
2
Ruling for 'inan partnership
For 'inan, a partner cannot pawn partnership goods for a debt (whether personal or joint) without the other partner's permission. If he does, the pawn is invalid (lam yajuz). The text cites Muhammad from the Book of Rahn.
The phrase 'lam yajuz' indicates the contract is not valid
3
Ruling for mufawada partnership
For mufawada, a partner may pawn and receive pawns on behalf of his partner without explicit permission. This is because mufawada grants each partner full agency (wakalah mutlaqah).
Source: Ghayat al-Bayan
4
Consequences of invalid pawn
If the pawn perishes and its value equals the debt, the pawning partner loses his share of the pawned property, and his partner recovers from the debtor. The debtor can then recover half the value from the pledgee.
This shows the legal effect of an invalid transaction
5
Permissions and exceptions
The text from al-Muhit adds that neither partner may pawn partnership property for a personal debt without permission, and that the one who conducted the sale may pawn for the price.
This emphasizes that authority is tied to the nature of the transaction

Among them is rahn (pawn). If it is an 'inan partnership, he does not have that right. Al-Karkhi said in his Mukhtasar: Muhammad said in the Book of Rahn: If one of the two 'inan partners pawns partnership goods for a debt owed by both, it is not valid as a guarantee for the pawn. And if he receives a pawn for a debt owed to both, and they accept it and take possession, it is not valid for his partner because he did not receive the pawn. If the pawn perishes and its value equals the debt, he loses his share, and his partner recovers his share from the debtor, and the debtor recovers half the value of the pawn from the pledgee (the partner). Or if the other partner wishes, he can make his partner liable for his share of the debt, because the destruction of the pawn in his hand is like satisfaction. Then he said afterwards: It is permissible for one of the two mufawada partners to pawn and to receive a pawn on behalf of his partner; thus in Ghayat al-Bayan. In al-Muhit: Neither of them may pawn anything from the partnership for a debt owed by himself unless with his partner's permission. Similarly, he may not receive a pawn for a debt of the partnership in his partner's share unless he is the one who conducted the contract or he appoints someone to do so. In al-Khaniya: The one who conducts the sale may pawn for the price.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of rulings on rahn (pawn) in partnership. It distinguishes between 'inan and mufawada partnerships. For 'inan, a partner cannot pawn partnership property for a debt unless authorized. If a partner pawns partnership goods for a joint debt, it is invalid. If a partner receives a pawn for a joint debt, it is only valid for his own share. The text then details the consequences if the pawn perishes. For mufawada, a partner can pawn and receive pawns on behalf of the partnership. The passage cites multiple sources: Mukhtasar al-Karkhi, Ghayat al-Bayan, al-Muhit, and al-Khaniya, indicating it is a well-established ruling in the school.

Key Terms
الرهن (ar-rahn): A contract in which property is pledged as security for a debt.
شريك عنان (sharik al-'inan): A partner in a limited partnership ('inan) where each contributes capital and shares profits proportionally.
شريك مفاوض (sharik al-mufawada): A partner in an unlimited partnership (mufawada) where each has full agency to act on behalf of the other in business.
