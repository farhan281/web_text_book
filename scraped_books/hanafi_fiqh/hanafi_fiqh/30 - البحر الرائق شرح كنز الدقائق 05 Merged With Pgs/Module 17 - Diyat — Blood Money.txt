Blood Money for Musta'min Muslims and Captives
Commentary on matn rulings for musta'min Muslims and captives who kill each other.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for diyah when a musta'min Muslim kills another musta'min Muslim, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding captives.

Core Idea
Inviolability survives aman, not captivity: musta'min pays diyah, captive pays only kaffarah.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses the complex question of blood money when Muslims kill each other outside dar al-Islam. The author distinguishes two scenarios: Muslims who entered enemy territory with a safe conduct (musta'min) and those taken captive. While both retain their Muslim inviolability for some purposes, the absence of Islamic authority and the practicalities of enforcement lead to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two distinct cases: musta'min Muslims (entered with aman) and captives (asiran).
2
State the ruling for musta'min Muslims
When one musta'min kills another, diyah is wajib from his wealth in both intentional and accidental killing; kaffarah is added for accidental.
Qisas is not possible due to lack of enforceability.
3
Explain why qisas is not applicable
Retaliation requires organized force under the Imam and Muslim community, which is absent in dar al-harb.
4
Discuss the liability of 'aqila
The 'aqila is not liable: for intentional killing they never are, and for accidental killing they cannot protect due to different abodes.
5
Describe the intra-Hanafi dispute on captives
Abu Hanifa holds only kaffarah in accidental killing; the Sahibayn require diyah in both intentional and accidental.
The dispute hinges on whether captivity nullifies inviolability.

The gist is that whoever restricted idana to selling on credit is strict, and whoever included loan and similar is lenient, and it is more appropriate.

Commentary
This passage contains two rulings. First, regarding two Muslims who are musta'min (having entered dar al-harb with aman) and one kills the other: the killer must pay diyah (blood money) from his own wealth, whether the killing was intentional or accidental, and if accidental, also kaffarah (expiation). Retaliation (qisas) is not applicable because in dar al-harb, there is no Muslim authority to enforce it. The 'aqila (tribal insurers) are not liable because for intentional killing they are never liable, and for accidental killing they cannot protect the killer due to different abodes. Second, regarding two captives (asiran) in dar al-harb: According to Abu Hanifa, only kaffarah is due in accidental killing, and nothing for intentional. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), diyah is due in both intentional and accidental killing (though qisas is still not possible). Abu Hanifa's argument is that captivity makes them followers of the enemy, thus their inviolability (isma) is effectively lost. The text then diverges into a lexical discussion about idana, which seems to be a sub-commentary on the earlier ruling, possibly misplaced here.

Key Terms
المستأمن (al-musta'min): A Muslim who enters dar al-harb with aman (safe conduct).
العاقلة (al-'aqila): The paternal relatives who bear the blood money for accidental killing.
الدية (al-diyah): The blood money paid to the heirs of the victim in cases where qisas is not applicable.

Liability for Accidental Killing by an Apostate and the Source of Diyah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah source for apostate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the source of diyah for an apostate's accidental killing and evaluate the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
For an apostate's accidental killing, the Sahibayn hold both Islam and riddah wealth liable for diyah; Abu Hanifa restricts it to Islam wealth alone.

In this passage, we delve into an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the liability of an apostate who accidentally kills. While all agree that diyah is wajib and that the apostate has no 'aqila, they differ over which of his assets the diyah should be drawn from. The matn states Abu Hanifa's view that only wealth acquired during Islam is liable, but the commentator challenges this, aligning with the Sahibayn's broader liability.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's view: the diyah is taken specifically from the apostate's wealth acquired during Islam (kasp al-Islam). If he has only riddah wealth, the killing is hadr (no compensation).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) hold that diyah is taken from both kasp al-Islam and kasp al-riddah. If only one exists, it is taken from that.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's reasoning: the apostate has valid ownership over both types of wealth because his transactions in both are effective; thus inheritance applies to both, and diyah is a debt-like obligation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Examine the commentator's critique: Abu Hanifa's view is inconsistent because for debts he has a narration that riddah wealth is liable. The commentator considers Abu Hanifa's position an error (sahw) and supports the Sahibayn.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the apostate has only riddah wealth, under Abu Hanifa the victim's family gets nothing; under the Sahibayn they receive diyah. The mufta bihi follows the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the source of diyah (blood money) when an apostate accidentally kills someone and then joins dar al-harb or is killed (while on apostasy). The matn states that the diyah is taken specifically from the apostate's wealth acquired during Islam (kasp al-Islam). The commentary explains that this is Abu Hanifa's view. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) hold that diyah is taken from both kasp al-Islam and kasp al-riddah (wealth acquired during apostasy) because the apostate has valid ownership over both, as his transactions in both are effective. However, Abu Hanifa distinguishes: during apostasy, the apostate's transactions are suspended, so only kasp al-Islam is inherited; kasp al-riddah becomes fay' (spoils). The Sahibayn argue both are inherited. The passage also addresses the case where the apostate has only kasp al-riddah: Abu Hanifa says the wrong act is of no consequence (hadr), but the Sahibayn say diyah is taken. The commentator challenges Abu Hanifa's view on this point, citing stronger evidence that diyah should be like debt, and presents three narrations from Abu Hanifa about debt, concluding that the correct view is that riddah wealth is also liable. The passage extends the same ruling to damages for wrongful seizure and destruction of property. It also notes that the ruling applies when the wrong is established by eyewitness; if by confession, the Sahibayn still take from both, but Abu Hanifa takes only from kasp al-riddah (due to suspicion). For wrong acts committed before joining dar al-harb, the apostate is liable after returning for rights of people, but hudud fall away due to the doubt posed by joining.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money or compensation paid to the heirs of a victim of accidental or intentional killing, as an alternative to qisas.
كسب الإسلام (kasb al-Islām): Property acquired by a person while he was Muslim (i.e., before apostasy or after returning to Islam).
كسب الردة (kasb al-riddah): Property acquired by a person during the state of apostasy (while he is a murtadd).
عاقلة (ʿāqilah): The collective of male relatives (asabah) who are liable to pay diyah on behalf of one who kills accidentally.

Liability for Cutting the Limb of an Apostate
Ruling on liability for cutting an apostate's limb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when someone cuts an apostate's limb and later dies, and explain why the cutter's acknowledgement (iqrar) is the basis of liability.

Core Idea
An acknowledgement of causing injury creates liability even if the victim's status would normally prevent it.

This ruling may seem counterintuitive: cutting an apostate's limb might be considered without penalty because an apostate's blood is not protected. However, the Hanafi school holds that the cutter's own acknowledgement of the act makes him liable, by analogy with a person who acknowledges a slave belonging to another and later buys him. This lesson explains the ruling and its rationale, emphasizing the power of iqrar in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is intentionally cutting off the limb of an apostate. The apostate later dies from the wound, either while still an apostate, after returning to Islam, or after joining dar al-harb and then returning.
The cutting is described as 'amdan' (intentional).
2
State the ruling
The cutter is liable (daman) for the death, meaning he must pay diyah or compensation.
Liability is wajib.
3
Identify the basis of liability
The liability is based on the cutter's own acknowledgement (iqrar) of the act. Even if the cutting did not legally correspond to ownership (i.e., the apostate's limb is not considered owned in a way that normally triggers liability), the acknowledgement suffices.
Iqrar is a binding legal admission in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the analogy
The text draws an analogy: just as if a person acknowledges a slave belonging to another and then buys that slave, the acknowledgement creates liability, so too here the cutter's admission makes him liable.
The analogy demonstrates that iqrar establishes a debt-like obligation regardless of the underlying property status.
5
Apply the ruling to variations
The same ruling applies whether the apostate dies while still an apostate, after returning to Islam, or after coming from dar al-harb as a Muslim. The key is the cutter's admission.
The apostate's status at death does not affect the liability once iqrar is made.

Commentary
This passage presents a succinct ruling about liability when someone cuts off the limb of an apostate. The scenario: a person cuts the limb of an individual who is an apostate (possibly in retaliation for a crime, or as a wrong act). The apostate then either dies from the wound while still an apostate, or dies after returning to Islam, or joins dar al-harb and then returns as a Muslim and then dies from the wound. The ruling: the cutter is liable (i.e., must pay diyah or compensation) based on his own acknowledgement (iqrar). Even if the cutting did not actually result in ownership (i.e., the limb was not owned by the apostate in a way that would normally require compensation), the cutter is still liable by analogy with a case where someone acknowledges a slave belonging to another and then buys him: the acknowledgement creates liability. The commentary then references al-Tatarkhaniyya for a related ruling about the apostate's wrongful seizure and damage: compensation is taken from both his Islam and riddah wealth without ranking (i.e., both are equally liable). This appears to be an additional note, not directly about the limb-cutting ruling itself.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): An acknowledgement by a person that results in liability; here, the cutter's admission of the act makes him liable for diyah.
قطع (qaṭʿ): The act of cutting off a limb; in this context, it is an aggressive act for which diyah may be due.
مقر (muqirr): The person who makes an acknowledgement (iqrar); in this ruling, the cutter is the muqirr.
