Evidence Requirement for Finder's Permission to Spend
Ruling on evidence requirement for finder's spending permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the evidence requirement for a finder to obtain the qadi's permission to spend on found property and identify the exception for perishable items.

Core Idea
A finder must provide evidence before spending on found property, unless the item is perishable.

In Hanafi fiqh, a finder of lost property (luqatah) does not have an automatic right to spend on it. The qadi's permission is required, and that permission typically hinges on the finder presenting evidence (bayyinah) to prove that the item is not usurped. This lesson examines the ruling from al-Bahr al-Raiq, including the exception for perishable items and the rule's application to foundlings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the passage to find the condition that the finder must establish evidence (bayyinah) before the qadi permits spending.
Look for the phrase 'لم يذكر المصنف إقامة البينة من الملتقط قبل إذن القاضي وشرطه في الأصل'.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason for requiring evidence is that without it, the found item might be considered a usurpation in the finder's hand. The qadi cannot order spending on a potentially usurped item.
The text says: 'لأنه يحتمل أن يكون غصباً في يده'.
3
Note the exception for perishable items
If the found property is feared to perish before evidence can be produced, the qadi may authorize spending immediately, without evidence.
Look for: 'وكذا لو كانت اللقطة شيئاً يخاف عليه الهلاك متى لم ينفق عليه إلى إقامة البينة'.
4
Apply the rule to foundlings
The same evidence requirement applies to foundlings (laqit). However, the debtor for maintenance differs depending on whether the father appears.
The passage mentions that the maintenance of a foundling is from the public treasury if no father appears.

Hiring Out Found Animals and the Dispute on Runaway Slaves
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on hiring lost property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring out found animals and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on hiring runaway slaves, including the reconciliation.

Core Idea
A found animal can be hired out to preserve it; a runaway slave's hire is disputed based on escape risk.

The qadi is tasked with preserving lost property. For a found animal with benefit, hiring it out covers costs without debt. But what about a runaway slave? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement, with some works permitting and others forbidding. This lesson unpacks that dispute and the reconciliation offered by later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
State the ruling on hiring out a found animal (the stray animal with benefit). Note the reasoning: preservation without debt.
This is the uncontested starting point.
2
Locate the disputed case
Identify the runaway slave (al-ābiq) as the disputed case. Note that some sources (al-Hidaya, al-Kafi) equate it with the stray animal, while others (al-Muhit, al-Bada'i') forbid it.
The disagreement is intra-Hanafi, not with another madhhab.
3
Analyze the two positions
Contrast the permissive view (hiring allowed because the slave has value) with the restrictive view (hiring not allowed due to risk of escape).
Each position has its own dalil (evidence) and reasoning.
4
Apply the reconciliation
Understand the reconciliation: the permissive view applies when the lessor is strong or informed; the restrictive view applies otherwise.
This resolves the apparent contradiction by contextualizing the rulings.
5
Extend to the foundling case
Consider the foundling (abandoned child) – the text raises the question but does not resolve it. Note that the finder may hire the child out for the child's benefit, but not for himself.
The qadi's authority likely follows the same reasoning.

The Finder's Right to Detain and the Effect of Detention on the Maintenance Debt
Explains the qadi's authority to sell and the effect of detention on the maintenance debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the survival of the maintenance debt after detention of lost property and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Zufar and the majority.

Core Idea
Detention turns the found item into a mortgage: if it perishes after detention, the debt is extinguished.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into a subtle point of fiqh al-luqatah: when a finder spends maintenance on a lost item with the qadi's permission, may he detain the owner's property until reimbursed? If the property is destroyed after detention, does the debt survive? The Hanafi masters differ, and the text argues that the correct position is that the debt drops.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is the finder's detention (habs) of the lost property to secure reimbursement. The dispute is whether the maintenance debt (dain al-nafaqah) is extinguished if the property perishes after detention.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold that after detention, the debt drops upon destruction. The property becomes like a mortgage (shabih al-rahn).
This is stated in al-Hidaya and al-Kafi and described as 'al-madhhab'.
3
State Zufar's opposing position
Zufar (and reportedly al-Quduri) hold that the debt does not drop because it is a personal debt not tied to the property by a guarantee contract.
The passage refutes this view as not being the madhhab.
4
Analyze the proof for each side
The majority analogize to rahn: detention creates a security interest. Zufar argues that the debt is not a substitute for the property nor wages for work; it is not secured by a formal contract of guarantee.
The Hanafi response: the security arises from the qadi's authorization and detention itself.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and why
The relied-upon position is that the debt drops after detention, as supported by the three imams and the majority of later texts.
The text explicitly says 'wa huwa al-madhhab' and dismisses Zufar's view.

However, if spending is more beneficial (e.g., as for a foundling), the qadi may permit spending and count it as a debt. The qadi may order sale after a short waiting period. The finder's spending with permission is treated like the qadi's own spending. A significant discussion concerns the validity of claims against a purchased slave: if someone later claims the slave is mudabbir or mukatib, it is not accepted to overturn the qadi's sale. The passage then addresses the finder's right to detain the owner (or the item) until reimbursed. After detention, the item is like a mortgage; the debt drops if the item perishes after detention. This is a matter of dispute: al-Quduri says the debt does not drop, but the correct Hanafi position is that it drops after detention, as per al-Hidaya and al-Kafi. The reasoning is that detention makes the finder akin to a secured creditor. The passage concludes with a discussion on the finder's right to assign the debt to the owner without the owner's consent, analogizing to a wife's maintenance debt.

Key Terms
الحبس (al-ḥabs): The finder's physical detention of the found item to secure reimbursement of maintenance expenses.
الرهن (al-rahn): A pledge or mortgage; the finder's detention makes the property like a pledge.
المدبر والمكاتب وأم الولد (al-mudabbir, al-mukātib, umm al-walad): Categories of slaves: mudabbir (slave promised freedom upon owner's death), mukatib (slave contracted to purchase freedom), umm walad (slave mother of her owner's child).
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): The legal transfer of a debt from one debtor to another; here, the finder's right to refer the creditor to the owner without consent.

Finder's Duties: Spending, Sale, and Detention
شرح أحكام الإنفاق والبيع والمنع للملتقط

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on spending on found property, the effect of sale by the qadhi, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the debt of expenses falls if the item perishes after detention.

Core Idea
The finder must preserve the property; spending requires qadhi's permission, and the debt of expenses does not fall if the item perishes after detention.

When you find lost property, Islamic law imposes duties to protect the owner's rights. The finder must try to locate the owner, and if unsuccessful, may need to spend on the item or sell it with judicial oversight. A key question arises: if the finder detains the item to recover expenses, and it is destroyed, does he lose his right? The Hanafi school has a clear position, which this lesson unpacks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial duty
The text states that the finder may spend on the found property for a few days hoping to find the owner, otherwise must sell it. Understand that this is not a choice but a duty to preserve the property.
The finder is a trustee (amin) and cannot simply keep the item.
2
Understand the role of the qadhi
The sale of the found property is only binding if done by the qadhi or with his permission. The finder's sale without permission is suspended pending ratification.
This ensures the owner's rights are protected.
3
Analyze the right of detention
The finder can prevent the owner from taking the item until expenses are reimbursed. This right arises because the finder's expenditure created a claim similar to a seller's right to withhold goods.
Analogous to the returner of a runaway slave who can detain for reward.
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
If the item perishes after detention, does the debt of expenses fall? The three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) say no; Zufar says yes, by analogy with a pledge.
The text refutes Zufar's analogy because the debt is not a substitute for the corpus or work.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that the debt remains due. This is supported by al-Hidaya and al-Kafi, and al-Quduri's attribution of the opposite view to Zufar alone is rejected.
Thus the finder's right to reimbursement survives even after destruction.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, discusses multiple rulings related to found property (luqatah). The text begins by stating the finder's duties: he may spend on the found item for a few days hoping to find the owner; if not found, he sells it with qadhi's permission. The text then discusses a separate issue: if the claimant describes the markings (number, weight, pouch, etc.) of the found item, it is permissible to hand it over without compulsion, based on the hadith. The finder may use the item if poor, otherwise must give it in charity. The passage also covers evidentiary issues: testimony of Muslims vs. non-Muslims, and the finder's right to refuse delivery except before a qadhi.

Handing Over Found Property: Evidence vs. Markings
شرح دفع اللقطة بالبينة أو العلامة

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a claimant of found property must provide legal evidence (bayyinah) and when description of markings suffices, and apply the relevant hadith.

Core Idea
A claimant usually needs evidence, but accurate description of unique markings makes delivery permissible without compulsion.

When someone claims a found item, the finder must be cautious to avoid giving it to the wrong person. The default rule is that the claimant must provide evidence (two witnesses). However, a special exception from the Prophet ﷺ allows delivery if the claimant describes distinctive markings of the item. This lesson explores the interplay between strict evidence and circumstantial identification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
The text states that the finder should not hand over the found property to a claimant without evidence (bayyinah). This is based on the hadith 'Al-bayyinatu 'ala al-mudda'i' and the principle that possession is a protected right.
Evidence typically means two male Muslim witnesses.
2
Analyze the exception for markings
The hadith 'If its owner comes and recognizes its pouch and number, give it to him' establishes that accurate description of distinctive markings suffices. The finder may hand it over without witnesses.
Markings include weight, number, pouch ('iffasah), tie (wikaa'), etc.
3
Distinguish between compulsion and permissibility
When markings are described, handing over is permissible (halal) but not compulsory. The finder is not forced to give it; he may still require evidence if in doubt. If evidence is provided, he must hand over.
The text says 'حل الدفع بلا جبر' (permissible without compulsion).
4
Apply evidentiary nuances
If the claimant brings evidence, the finder must deliver; if he refuses, the qadhi compels delivery. The text also discusses scenarios of conflicting claims and testimony of non-Muslims, where istihsan may apply.
Testimony of non-Muslims is generally not accepted against a Muslim, but istihsan allows it in certain cases.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, discusses multiple rulings related to found property (luqatah). The text begins by stating the finder's duties: he may spend on the found item for a few days hoping to find the owner; if not found, he sells it with qadhi's permission. The finder can prevent the owner from taking the item until his expenses are reimbursed. The finder should not hand over the item to a claimant without evidence (bayyinah). The commentary then expands on each point. It explains that the qadhi's sale is binding, while the finder's sale without qadhi is suspended pending ratification. If the owner claims after sale that the item was a mudabbir (slave freed upon death) or mukatab (slave under contract of manumission), such claim is not accepted to invalidate the sale. The text addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether the debt of expenses falls if the found item perishes after the finder detains it. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the debt does not fall; it remains a liability of the owner. Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl, another Hanafi scholar, held that it falls because detention makes it like a pledge. The commentary argues that the view of the three imams is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The text then discusses a separate issue: if the claimant describes the markings (number, weight, pouch, etc.) of the found item, it is permissible to hand it over without compulsion, based on the hadith. The finder may use the item if poor, otherwise must give it in charity. The passage also covers evidentiary issues: testimony of Muslims vs. non-Muslims, and the finder's right to refuse delivery except before a qadhi.

Handing Over a Found Item: Evidence vs. Accurate Description
Rules for handing over a found item based on evidence vs. accurate description.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings when a claimant provides evidence (bayyinah) versus accurate description (alamah) and state whether the finder is compelled or merely permitted to hand over the item.

Core Idea
A finder is compelled to hand over only with proof; accurate description makes it permissible without compulsion.

The finder of a lost item (luqatah) must protect it and return it to its owner. But how does he verify the claimant? The default rule requires formal evidence (bayyinah). The Prophet (saw) also permitted delivery upon accurate description of the item's distinguishing marks. This lesson unpacks the legal distinction between compulsory and permissive handover, and the evidence behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
The finder must not hand over the item except to a claimant who provides evidence (bayyinah). This is based on the hadith 'al-bayyinatu ala al-mudda'i' and the principle that possession is a right akin to ownership.
2
Understand the exception: accurate description
If the claimant accurately describes the item's marks (e.g., weight, number, tie, container), it becomes permissible to hand over without compulsion (halla al-daf' bi la jabr). This is from the hadith: 'If its owner comes and recognizes its container and number, give it to him.'
3
Distinguish compulsion and permission
With evidence, the finder is compelled (jabr) to hand over; with description, he is not compelled, but he may choose to do so if satisfied. If the finder himself confirms the claimant, he may hand over even without description.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on liability
If the finder hands over by court order and later another proves ownership, Abu Yusuf holds the finder not liable, while Muhammad holds him liable. This affects whether the finder can be sued after judicial delivery.
5
Apply the precaution of taking a surety
When handing over based on description, the finder should take a surety (kafil) from the claimant as a precaution. With evidence, no surety is needed.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Lost and Found (Kitab al-Luqatah) in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses two interrelated rulings: first, how a finder should hand over a found item to a claimant, and second, what the finder may do with the item if the owner does not appear. The author, Ibn Nujaym, expands upon the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq.

The text begins by establishing a fundamental principle: the finder must not hand over the lost item except to its rightful claimant, and this must be proven either by clear evidence (bayyinah) or by accurate description of the item's distinguishing marks (alama). This is based on the hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant' (البينة على المدعي). The reasoning is that the finder's hand on the item is a legitimate right, similar to ownership; therefore, it cannot be removed without solid proof. If the claimant brings witnesses, the finder is compelled to hand it over (jabr). If he merely describes the item accurately, it becomes permissible to hand it over without compulsion (halla al-daf' bi la jabr), but he is not forced to do so unless he is satisfied. However, if the finder himself confirms the claimant's ownership, he may hand it over even without description.

The text then discusses scenarios of conflicting claims: if one person admits the item belongs to another but a third party brings evidence, judgment is given to the one with evidence. If the finder hands it over to someone (by admission) and that person consumes it, then another proves ownership, the true owner may hold the recipient liable (like a usurper of a usurper) and may also hold the finder liable if the delivery was without a judicial order. There is a disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: if the delivery was by judicial order, Abu Yusuf says the finder cannot be held liable, while Muhammad says he can.

The next part clarifies the concept of mark (alama): it includes the weight, number, tie, and container of the item. The condition for permissibility is that the description must match exactly; partial accuracy is insufficient. If two claimants both accurately describe the item, the finder may hand it over to both? The text suggests permissibility.

Regarding taking a surety (kafil): when handing over based on description (not evidence), the finder should take a surety as a precaution, according to al-Hidayah. However, if the claimant brings evidence, no surety is needed.

The second major ruling: if the owner does not appear after the required period of announcement (ta'rif), the finder may benefit from the item only if he is poor (faqir). A rich finder may not use it for himself; he must give it in charity to the poor, including his own poor parents, wife, and children. The poor finder may own it by taking possession. The Hanafi school restricts this to the poor, based on the hadith 'let him give it in charity' and the principle that another's property cannot be used without consent. The Shafi'i school allows the rich to benefit as well, citing the hadith of Abu (Radiyallahu Anhu) who was rich and was told to benefit. The Hanafi response explains that Abu's permission was from the Imam, and it was on the basis of a loan, not outright ownership. For the poor, the permissibility does not require the judge's permission.

The text ends with a note: the poor finder's 'benefit' means he may spend the item on himself, but technically it remains the owner's property until he actually disposes of it (e.g., by consuming or selling). If it reaches the nisab threshold for zakat, and a year passes, zakat may be due on it under certain conditions.

Key Terms
اللقطة (al-luqatah): A lost item found by someone; it has specific rules for announcement, handling, and disposal.
البينة (al-bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically two witnesses or a confession, used to prove ownership of a found item.
العلامة (al-'alamah): Distinguishing mark or description of the item (e.g., weight, number, container, tie) that suffices for handing over without formal evidence.
الكفيل (al-kafil): Surety or guarantor taken from the claimant when handing over the item based on description, as a precaution.

Benefiting from a Found Item: Poor Finder vs. Rich Finder
Rules for the finder's benefit from a found item after announcement: poor vs rich.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a finder benefiting from a lost item after announcement, distinguish between a poor and rich finder, and explain the Shafi'i opposition and its rebuttal.

Core Idea
Only a poor finder may benefit from a lost item; a rich finder must give it in charity.

After announcing a lost item and the owner does not appear, what may the finder do? The Hanafi school draws a clear line between a poor and a rich finder. The poor may own the item and use it, while the rich must give it as charity. This lesson explores the proofs and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school, which permits the rich to benefit as well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position on finder's benefit
After the required announcement period (ta'rif), a poor finder may benefit from the item (by owning it), but a rich finder must give it in charity to strangers, including his own poor relatives (parents, wife, children).
2
Understand the Hanafi proof
Based on the hadith: 'If its owner does not come, let him give it in charity.' Charity is for the poor. The item is another's property and cannot be used without consent. The hadith of Abu (who was rich) is interpreted as specific permission from the Imam (on loan basis) and not a general rule.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i allows the rich finder to benefit, citing the same hadith of Abu (RA) and arguing that both rich and poor are encouraged to pick up lost items for preservation.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds that Abu's case was a special permission (by the Imam) or as a loan. The general textual evidence (charity hadith) and the principle that another's property is inviolable support the restriction to the poor. The consensus (ijma) of the Companions is also cited.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
A rich finder in the Hanafi school must give the item to the poor; in the Shafi'i school, he may keep it. This affects the finder's financial gain and liability.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Lost and Found (Kitab al-Luqatah) in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses two interrelated rulings: first, how a finder should hand over a found item to a claimant, and second, what the finder may do with the item if the owner does not appear. The author, Ibn Nujaym, expands upon the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq.

The text begins by establishing a fundamental principle: the finder must not hand over the lost item except to its rightful claimant, and this must be proven either by clear evidence (bayyinah) or by accurate description of the item's distinguishing marks (alama). This is based on the hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant' (البينة على المدعي). The reasoning is that the finder's hand on the item is a legitimate right, similar to ownership; therefore, it cannot be removed without solid proof. If the claimant brings witnesses, the finder is compelled to hand it over (jabr). If he merely describes the item accurately, it becomes permissible to hand it over without compulsion (halla al-daf' bi la jabr), but he is not forced to do so unless he is satisfied. However, if the finder himself confirms the claimant's ownership, he may hand it over even without description.

The text then discusses scenarios of conflicting claims: if one person admits the item belongs to another but a third party brings evidence, judgment is given to the one with evidence. If the finder hands it over to someone (by admission) and that person consumes it, then another proves ownership, the true owner may hold the recipient liable (like a usurper of a usurper) and may also hold the finder liable if the delivery was without a judicial order. There is a disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: if the delivery was by judicial order, Abu Yusuf says the finder cannot be held liable, while Muhammad says he can.

The next part clarifies the concept of mark (alama): it includes the weight, number, tie, and container of the item. The condition for permissibility is that the description must match exactly; partial accuracy is insufficient. If two claimants both accurately describe the item, the finder may hand it over to both? The text suggests permissibility.

Regarding taking a surety (kafil): when handing over based on description (not evidence), the finder should take a surety as a precaution, according to al-Hidayah. However, if the claimant brings evidence, no surety is needed.

The second major ruling: if the owner does not appear after the required period of announcement (ta'rif), the finder may benefit from the item only if he is poor (faqir). A rich finder may not use it for himself; he must give it in charity to the poor, including his own poor parents, wife, and children. The poor finder may own it by taking possession. The Hanafi school restricts this to the poor, based on the hadith 'let him give it in charity' and the principle that another's property cannot be used without consent. The Shafi'i school allows the rich to benefit as well, citing the hadith of Abu (Radiyallahu Anhu) who was rich and was told to benefit. The Hanafi response explains that Abu's permission was from the Imam, and it was on the basis of a loan, not outright ownership. For the poor, the permissibility does not require the judge's permission.

The text ends with a note: the poor finder's 'benefit' means he may spend the item on himself, but technically it remains the owner's property until he actually disposes of it (e.g., by consuming or selling). If it reaches the nisab threshold for zakat, and a year passes, zakat may be due on it under certain conditions.

Key Terms
اللقطة (al-luqatah): A lost item found by someone; the rules for benefit differ based on the finder's financial status.
الفقير (al-faqir): A person in need, who does not possess enough wealth to meet basic needs for a year.

Finder's Use of Lost Item After Definition
Ruling on rich vs poor finder using lost item with or without judge's permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a finder's use of a lost item after the definition period, distinguishing between a rich and poor finder, and identify the condition of judge's permission for the poor finder.

Core Idea
A rich finder cannot use the lost item at all; a poor finder can only use it with the judge's permission — and 'use' means ownership.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the crucial distinction between a rich and poor finder of a lost item (luqatah) after the mandatory period of definition (ta'rif). The ruling determines when and how the finder may benefit from the item, interpreting 'benefit' as actual ownership (tamalluk). The text also notes an intra-Hanafi difference of opinion from Bishr al-Marwazi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of finder
The passage divides finders into rich and poor based on their financial status. The ruling differs for each.
Wealth is determined relative to the finder's need; a rich finder is one who does not need the item's value.
2
Determine the ruling for a rich finder
For a rich finder, use (intifa') is absolutely prohibited, whether the judge permits it or not. This is the majority Hanafi view.
The prohibition extends to loans; the rich finder cannot even take the item as a loan without permission.
3
Determine the ruling for a poor finder
For a poor finder, use is permissible only if the judge grants permission. Without that, it remains prohibited.
The permission is a condition (shart) for the permissibility of use.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Bishr al-Marwazi holds that a poor finder may use the item even without the judge's order. This is a legitimate difference within the school.
The majority position (with judge's permission) is the mufta bihi.
5
Interpret 'intifa'' as ownership
The commentator explains that 'benefit' (intifa') in this context means tamalluk (ownership), not mere permissive use. Hence, the finder may sell the item and use the proceeds.
This interpretation has practical consequences: the finder can treat the item as his own property after proper procedure.

A runaway slave is treated like a foundling (luqata) for expenses, but the judge cannot rent him out and imprisons him as ta'zir.

It covers the reward for returning a runaway, the expenses of keeping him, his imprisonment, sale by the judge, the validity of freeing him, the cutting of his hand for theft, and the wages if hired. The key principle is that a runaway is analogous to a foundling (luqata) for most purposes, but with important distinctions, such as the judge not being allowed to rent him out.

Commentary
This lesson presents a series of rulings on runaway slaves (Kitab al-Ibaq) from later fatwa sources. The first ruling concerns the reward (ja'l) for returning a runaway slave who fled from a usurper (ghasib). The reward is on the usurper because he wrongfully held the slave. If the slave was given as a gift and the recipient returned him, the reward is on the recipient, since he was the owner at the time of return. However, if the gift was made to the finder himself, the ruling depends on whether the master had taken possession before the gift: if not, no reward; if yes, reward on master. But in a sale transaction, the reward always goes to the seller. The second topic is the expenses of the runaway. The slave is treated like a foundling (luqata): if the finder spends without judge's permission, he is a volunteer and cannot reclaim; with permission, he can reclaim only if he explicitly states his intent to reclaim. Notably, the judge cannot rent out the slave, unlike a foundling. The slave is imprisoned as ta'zir (discretionary punishment), unlike a stray animal. The seventh ruling states that freeing the slave is valid, even for kaffarat al-zihar (expiation of zihar). A key intra-Hanafi dispute is mentioned: the hand of a runaway slave is not cut for theft until the master appears, contrary to Abu Yusuf's view. Finally, if someone hires the runaway, the wages are to be given in charity; but if given to the master, it becomes halal by istihsan.
