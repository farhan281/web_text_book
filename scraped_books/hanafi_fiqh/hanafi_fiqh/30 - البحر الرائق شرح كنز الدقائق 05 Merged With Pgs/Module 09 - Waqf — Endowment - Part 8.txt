Makruh Acts in Mosques and the Effect of a Barrier
Listing of makruh acts with a refinement on the mihrab ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state five actions that are makruh tanzihi in a mosque and explain when the dislike for the mihrab facing a graveyard is removed due to a barrier.

Core Idea
A barrier (ha'il) removes the dislike of the mihrab facing impure places—context matters in Hanafi rulings.

Mosques are sacred spaces where reverence must be maintained. The Hanafi school identifies several actions as makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) because they diminish respect, even if not sinful. However, not all situations are absolute; the presence of a physical barrier can nullify the dislike, as the commentator emphasizes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disliked actions
List the actions and states mentioned: mihrab facing graveyard/place of wudu/bathhouse; performing wudu in the mosque; spitting; taking it as a thoroughfare; engaging in worldly talk; brandishing weapons; entering without ritual purity; removing valuable grass without purchase; decorating walls with pictures or writing.
These are all considered makruh tanzihi due to disrespect to the mosque.
2
Analyze the condition for the mihrab ruling
Note that the dislike for the mihrab facing a graveyard, etc., applies only if there is no barrier (ha'il) like a wall between them. The commentary explicitly states this condition.
The condition is based on textual analysis from Sharh Munyat al-Musalli.
3
Apply the barrier condition to other cases
Understand that the barrier principle is explicitly mentioned only for the mihrab ruling. However, it shows the Hanafi attention to contextual nuance. For other disliked acts, no such condition is mentioned, so they apply absolutely.
Do not analogize the barrier condition to other acts unless supported by texts.

Mosque with Cellar or Upper Room: Ruling and Proof
Ruling with proof and exception for a mosque with cellar/upper room

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mosque with a cellar or upper room, explain the proof from Quran 72:18, and identify the exception for spaces used for the mosque's interests.

Core Idea
For a mosque to be valid, the entire structure must be exclusively for Allah; any retained private right invalidates it.

This lesson delves into a nuanced issue in mosque endowment (waqf): what happens when a mosque includes a cellar or an upper room? The matn rules that such a mosque is not valid because a private right remains attached. The commentator clarifies the underlying condition — complete dedication to Allah (takhlees) — and provides the Quranic proof. You will also learn about permissible exceptions when such spaces serve the mosque's own interests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Read the matn: a person makes a mosque with a cellar or upper room, or converts part of his house into a mosque. The ruling is that he may sell it and it will be inherited — meaning it is not a valid, irrevocable mosque.
The mosque is not established because a private right remains.
2
Determine the condition for a valid mosque
The commentator states that for a place to be a true mosque, its entire structure (lower and upper) must be exclusively for Allah, cutting off any human right.
This condition is called takhlees (making exclusively for Allah).
3
Locate the proof
The proof is Quran 72:18: 'And that the mosques are for Allah.' This indicates that mosques belong to Allah alone and cannot be mixed with private ownership.
The dalil is from the Quran, with qati' thubut but zanni dalala in this context.
4
Understand the exception
If the cellar or upper room is itself endowed for the mosque's interests (e.g., storage for the mosque's needs), it is permissible because no one owns it; it is part of the mosque's endowment.
Example: the cellar of Masjid Bayt al-Maqdis.
5
Apply the principle to related cases
Building a room on the mosque roof for the imam is allowed if it serves the mosque. However, if the mosque has been handed over to the public, the founder cannot later build on it. Building on the mosque wall by others is forbidden and must be demolished.
The administrator also cannot use mosque property for income or dwelling.

Fate of a Ruined Mosque: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the fate of a ruined mosque and its fixtures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding a ruined mosque and identify the mufta bihi position regarding the land and movable fixtures.

Core Idea
The land of a ruined mosque remains waqf forever, but its movable items can be transferred or sold for benefit.

When a mosque falls into ruin and is no longer used, a critical question arises: does the land revert to private ownership or remain dedicated to Allah perpetually? The Hanafi school has a nuanced intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute clarifies the legal status of abandoned mosques and their fixtures, and shows how later scholars adjusted rulings based on practical circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
Carefully read the passage to distinguish the views of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf regarding the land of a ruined mosque. Summarize each in one sentence.
Pay attention to the conditions Muhammad sets (e.g., cannot be repaired, community independent).
2
Understand the Proof Behind Each
Consider the underlying rationale: Muhammad focuses on the purpose of the mosque (prayer), while Abu Yusuf emphasizes the perpetuity of dedication to Allah. Note which principle each relies on.
The text does not explicitly state proofs, but the logical basis is implied. Relate to the concept of waqf.
3
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Work out the practical consequences of each view. Under Muhammad, the land and movables return to the waqif or heirs; under Abu Yusuf, the land remains a mosque forever, and movables are handled separately.
The thamarah al-khilaf is significant for property rights and communal benefit.
4
Apply the Tarjih to Land and Movables
Identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position for the land and for the movable items (mats, lamps). Note how later scholars refined the ruling for movables, including the issue of judge's permission and corrupt judges.

Ruling on a Mosque with a Private Basement or Upper Room
States ruling on mosque with private basement or upper room.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mosque with attached private property and identify the condition that makes the waqf incomplete.

Core Idea
A mosque is not fully dedicated to Allah if any human right remains attached; it can then be sold and inherited.

This lesson explores a nuanced waqf ruling: when a mosque has a private basement or upper room that is not exclusively for the mosque's needs, the dedication is incomplete. We examine the condition of full dedication (takhlish), the Quranic proof, and the practical implications for the waqif and the community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making a mosque with a private basement or upper room. The ruling is that such a mosque is not fully dedicated (ghayr mukhallas), so the waqif may sell it and it is inherited.
2
Locate the condition for a valid mosque waqf
The condition for a mosque to be fully dedicated is that both its lower and upper parts must be exclusively for mosque use, cutting off any human right (haqq al-'abd).
The passage uses 'أن يكون سفله وعلوه مسجداً'.
3
Understand the Quranic dalil
The verse 'And that the mosques belong to Allah' (Quran 72:18) is cited as proof that mosques must be exclusively for Allah. Any remaining human right contradicts this exclusivity.
The dalil is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala; the imperatives indicate obligation of exclusivity.
4
Distinguish the exception
If the basement or upper room is itself endowed for the mosque's needs (e.g., storage or imam residence), it is permissible and does not break the dedication. This is like the basement of the Mosque of Jerusalem.
The exception applies because such use is a completion of the mosque's needs (min tamim masalih al-masjid).
5
Apply the ruling to scenarios
Consider two cases: (1) Private apartment above mosque with separate entrance → mosque incomplete, can be sold. (2) Rooftop room built for imam after mosque is handed over → not allowed (must have been built before).
The distinction is based on timing and purpose.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It addresses a ruling regarding a mosque that has a basement (sardab) or an upper room, and the door is to the road, or if someone designates the middle of his house as a mosque and allows people to enter. The ruling is that such a mosque is not considered fully dedicated to Allah because the human right (haqq al-'abd) remains attached—either through private access or usage. Therefore, the waqif can sell it, and it can be inherited. The key evidence is the verse 'And that the mosques belong to Allah' (Quran 72:18), implying that a mosque must be exclusively for Allah's sake. If any human right remains, the dedication is incomplete. The exception is when the basement or upper room is itself endowed for the mosque's needs (like storage or imam residence), then it is part of the mosque's utility and permissible. The commentary notes that building a house on the roof for the imam is a need and does not affect the mosque's status. It distinguishes between actions before and after the mosque is handed over to the public: before handover, the waqif can build; after, not. Also, building on the mosque wall by others is prohibited and must be demolished. The custodian cannot use any part of the mosque for income or residence.

Key Terms
سرداب (sardāb): A basement or underground chamber built for cooling water or storage, as part of a mosque structure.
مسجد (masjid): A place dedicated to Allah for prayer; here, its dedication requires that no human right remains attached.
وقف (waqf): Endowment; the act of dedicating property for religious or charitable purposes, with the corpus remaining inalienable.

The Ruined Mosque: Intra-Hanafi Dispute Between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ruined mosque and analogous waqf items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on a ruined mosque, explain the mufta bihi, and apply the ruling to analogous waqf cases.

Core Idea
A ruined mosque remains a waqf forever, never reverting to private ownership.

The author of al-Bahr al-Rā'iq delves into a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: when a mosque falls into ruin and is no longer needed, does it revert to the founder or remain perpetually dedicated? This question triggers a cascade of rulings for similar waqf assets like mats, lamps, horses, shops, and water tanks. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi commitment to the perpetuity of waqf.

Guided Path
1
State the ikhtilaf
Identify the two positions: Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybanī holds that if a mosque is ruined and there is no hope of repair or use, the waqf terminates and the property returns to the waqif or his heirs. Abū Yūsuf insists the mosque remains a mosque forever until the Day of Judgment, and its assets must be transferred to another mosque.
The disagreement is about the legal effect of the waqf's purpose becoming impossible.
2
Identify the tarjīḥ
Determine which position is considered muftā bihī. The text states that most mashāyikh and Fatḥ al-Qadīr prefer Abū Yūsuf's view. The fatwā is given accordingly for mosques.
The tarjīḥ is based on the principle that a waqf is a permanent dedication to Allah.
3
Apply to movable items
Examine the ruling for mosque furnishings like mats (ḥuṣur) and lamps (qanādīl). For Abū Yūsuf, they are transferred to another mosque or sold by the custodian; for Muhammad, they revert to the waqif. Note that some later scholars permit sale without a judge due to corrupt judges.
This shows the dispute extends beyond the real estate itself.
4
Extend to analogous cases
The text analogizes the mosque ruling to other waqf properties: a war horse (ḥabīs) that becomes unusable, funeral equipment in a deserted neighborhood, a burnt shop, a ruined water tank, and a ribāṭ. For each, the same two positions apply: reversion (Muhammad) vs. transfer to similar waqf (Abū Yūsuf).
The analogy is based on the shared ʿillah of impossibility of the original purpose.
5
Resolve apparent contradiction in Muhammad's narrations
The author addresses a critique: Muhammad is reported to allow sale (istibdāl) in one narration (from Hishām) but also to rule for reversion. The commentary clarifies that the permissibility of sale is not contradictory because it is a separate issue—istibdāl is allowed when the waqf cannot be used, while reversion is about the termination of waqf. The muftā bihī for sale is the Hishām narration.
This resolution shows the importance of distinguishing between different riwāyāt.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hāshiya-style) from Al-Bahr al-Rā'iq, focusing on the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad about the status of a mosque after it falls into ruin and is no longer needed. The author first presents the ikhtilāf: Muhammad holds that if the mosque is ruined and cannot be repaired, or if people have moved away and no longer need it (e.g., due to another mosque or the village's decay), the waqf reverts to the ownership of the founder or his heirs. Abu Yusuf, however, insists that a mosque remains a mosque forever until the end of time, cannot be inherited, and its belongings (like mats, lamps) cannot be transferred to another mosque; they must be used for mosque purposes. The text notes that the majority of later scholars and Fath al-Qadir prefer Abu Yusuf's view. Regarding movable items like mats and lamps, there is further nuance: Muhammad says they revert to the waqif, while the correct position from Abu Yusuf is that they are transferred to another mosque or sold by the custodian for the mosque's benefit. The commentary then discusses the practical problem of corrupt judges: some later scholars permit sale without judge's permission due to widespread corruption. The passage also extends the ruling to similar cases: a horse dedicated for jihad that becomes unusable, funeral equipment in a deserted neighborhood, shops, water tanks, and ribats – all follow the same principle according to Muhammad (reversion to heirs) or Abu Yusuf (transfer to similar waqf). The author responds to a criticism that there is contradiction in Muhammad's statements: the permissibility of sale (from Hisham's narration) and the impermissibility of reversion are from different sources; the operative fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view for mosques and derivatively for other cases.

Disposition of Waqf When Unusable: Hanafi Rulings
Presents Muhammad's rulings on specific waqf items when unusable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for waqf items that become unusable and explain the underlying principle of transferring the benefit to another location without reverting to heirs.

Core Idea
A waqf never reverts to private ownership; when unusable, it is transferred to preserve its charitable benefit.

A waqf (endowment) is established for perpetual benefit. But what happens when the endowed property becomes completely unusable? This passage from al-Khulasa, quoting Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, provides clear rulings for several cases: a warhorse too weak to ride, funeral equipment in a depopulated neighborhood, and mosque furnishings. The common principle is that the waqf does not return to the owner's heirs but is redirected to continue its purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cases and their rulings
The passage lists four distinct situations: (1) a habis horse that cannot be ridden, (2) funeral items (bier, cloth, washing place) in a neighborhood where all residents have died, (3) mats and baskets used in a mosque, and (4) a mosque and water tank that are ruined and no longer needed. For each, note what should be done with the item.
2
Determine the common principle
In every case, the waqf property does not revert to the original owner or heirs. Instead, it is either sold with proceeds going to the owner/heirs (horse) or physically moved to another location (funeral items, mosque furnishings). The underlying principle is that waqf is perpetual and cannot return to private ownership.
The horse is an exception: its price goes to the owner because the horse itself was a 'habis', which has a different nature.
3
Note the procedural differences
For the horse, if the owner is known, the sale price is given to him or his heirs. If unknown, the price is used to buy another horse for jihad without need for a judge. For other items, they are simply transferred to another place where they are needed.
4
Understand the expansion by later scholars
Al-Halwani extends the same principle to a mosque and water tank that become ruined and abandoned: their endowments (e.g., income) are transferred to another mosque or water tank. This shows the application of the principle to buildings and ongoing revenues.
5
Relate to the core idea of perpetual charity
All these rulings reflect the Hanafi view that waqf is a perpetual appropriation (tahbis al-asl wa tasbil al-manfa'ah). The corpus cannot revert to private ownership; only the benefit is redirected.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on ruined waqf and apply the mufta bihi distinction between immovable waqf (transferred) and movable accessories (reverted).

Core Idea
A ruined waqf does not always revert to heirs — immovable properties are transferred, movable accessories revert.

What happens to a mosque that collapses, a shop that burns down, or a water tank in a deserted village? The Hanafi school has two major views on this, and the correct answer depends on the type of property. This lesson unpacks the nuanced dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, showing how later Hanafi authorities reconciled their positions to produce a workable fatwa.

Guided Path
1

Read the first part of the Arabic text to understand the two opposing views: Muhammad holds that a completely ruined waqf becomes void and the property reverts to the waqif's heirs; Abu Yusuf holds that it remains waqf and must be transferred to another similar purpose.
Note the examples: mosque, shop, water tank, ribat.
2
Analyze the evidence for each view
Muhammad's view relies on the principle that when the purpose of the waqf is permanently defeated, the waqf ends. Abu Yusuf relies on the permanence (ta'bid) of waqf. Look for the supporting narrations mentioned in the text, especially Muhammad's permissibility of istibdal (substitution) as narrated by Hisham.
The istibdal narration shows Muhammad did not always insist on reversion.
3

The author of al-Bahr al-Raiq defends Abu Yusuf for mosques but notes that later authorities adopted a combined fatwa: for movable accessories (mats, lanterns, biers) follow Muhammad's view (reversion to owner/heirs); for the mosque itself and similar immovable waqf, follow Abu Yusuf's view (transfer).
This is stated explicitly toward the end of the passage.
4

Using the mufta bihi rule, determine the ruling for: a mat donated to a mosque that becomes ruined, a ribat that collapses, and a shop that burns down. Consider the purpose of the waqf and whether the land retains value.

When Does Ownership Cease in a Non-Mosque Waqf?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on cessation of ownership in non-mosque waqf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on when ownership of a non-mosque waqf building ceases and explain the practical consequence (thamarah) of each.

Core Idea
For a waqf other than a mosque, ownership may leave by judge's ruling, by utterance, or by actual use—depending on which imam you follow.

Building a sadaqa (water station), khan, ribat, or cemetery as a waqf is recommended, but there is a key difference of opinion on exactly when the builder's ownership ends. This matters because it determines whether the builder can revoke the waqf or change its use before it is considered fully transferred. In this passage, al-Marghinani's commentator presents the views of the three Hanafi imams and then identifies the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the waqf type
First, note that the ruling applies to non-mosque waqf buildings: sadaqa (water station), khan, ribat, and cemetery. Mosques have a different rule because the builder loses all right to benefit immediately.
Distinguish between mosque and non-mosque waqf.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says ownership does not leave until a judge rules it so. Reason: the builder retains a right to benefit (e.g., drink, reside), so ownership continues until a judge declares it transferred.
The right to benefit is key.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that ownership leaves by the mere utterance (sigha) of the waqf. Delivery (tasleem) is not a condition; the waqf is binding from the moment the words are spoken.
This is the mufta bihi according to Fatawa Qadikhan.
4
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad al-Shaybani says ownership leaves when people actually use the waqf (e.g., drink from the saqaya, stay in the khan). Delivery of the type is a condition, and one instance suffices.
Use triggers transfer, not the mere saying.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the builder can revoke until judge's ruling; under Abu Yusuf, the waqf is binding immediately; under Muhammad, it becomes binding only after use, so before that the builder can change his mind.
This affects the waqf's irrevocability.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq is an expansion probe that explains and comments on Ibn Nujaym's matn in Kanz al-Daqa’iq regarding waqf buildings other than mosques. The author first clarifies the matn: when someone builds a sadaqa (water station), khan, ribat, or cemetery, his ownership does not cease until a judge rules it so, according to Abu Hanifa. The reason is that the builder retains a right to benefit (e.g., reside, drink, be buried), unlike a mosque where he has no such right. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership ceases by the mere utterance of the waqf (since delivery is not a condition for him), and this is adopted in Fatawa Qadikhan. Muhammad holds that ownership ceases when people actually use the structure (e.g., drink or reside), because delivery of the type is a condition, and one instance suffices due to the impossibility of all. The author then discusses delivery to a trustee, noting that if the builder delivers to a trustee (mutawalli), the delivery is valid in all cases because the trustee is a deputy for the beneficiaries. For a mosque, the ruling differs due to earlier discussion. The cemetery is sometimes treated like a mosque (no trustee customarily) and sometimes like a sadaqa (trustee appointment is possible). Next, the author expands on cases of making a house in Makka for pilgrims, etc., extracting the principle that income (ghalla) is for the poor only, while in other things like residence in a khan or drinking, rich and poor are equal—based on custom and need. The author criticizes the matn for restricting to the judge's ruling, noting that adding the condition 'after death' (like a bequest) is also sufficient and allows revocation during life. Then al-Qunyah is quoted: if a reservoir or mosque becomes ruined and people leave, the judge can transfer its endowments to another mosque. If two mosques in a village become ruined, the judge can use one's wood for the other if the builder and heir are unknown; if known, the builder spends it himself. If a public reservoir is ruined and someone builds shops on the land, the judge takes market rent and spends it on another reservoir. In al-Khaniyyah, a far-away ribat no longer needed: its income can be used for a nearby ribat. Some say ruined ribat or mosque becomes inheritance, but al-Shurunbulali refutes this as contrary to the mufta bihi, except he permits transferring a reservoir or well like a mat (hasir). The passage thus covers intra-Hanafi disputes and the correct fatwa on transferring waqf assets.

Transfer of Ruined Waqf Assets: The Judge's Authority
Application of waqf transfer rules: judge's authority and distinction between mosque and other waqf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the transfer of a ruined waqf building to another similar purpose and distinguish between a mosque and a non-mosque in this context.

Core Idea
When a waqf building becomes unusable, the judge may transfer its assets to another waqf of similar benefit, but a mosque's building cannot be transferred according to the mufta bihi.

What happens to a waqf water station or mosque that falls into ruin and is no longer used? Can the land and materials be repurposed? The Hanafi school addresses this through the judge's authority (qadi). This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents practical cases: a ruined reservoir's land may be rented and its income used for another reservoir; a mosque's wood may be used for another mosque if the original builder is unknown. There is also a dispute: some say ruined waqf becomes inheritance, but this is rejected as against the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of waqf
First, distinguish between mosque and non-mosque waqf (reservoir, ribat, etc.). The ruling on transfer differs. Mosques have special inviolability.
The passage later makes this distinction explicit.
2
Recall the general principle for non-mosque waqf
For a ruined non-mosque waqf (e.g., reservoir, ribat, well), the judge may transfer its benefit (e.g., rent income) to another similar waqf. The original purpose is preserved as much as possible.
Example: ruined public reservoir – judge takes rent of the land and spends on another reservoir.
3
Apply the ruling to specific cases
Apply the principle: ruined ribat no longer needed – its income goes to a nearby ribat. Ruined mosque – if the builder/heir is unknown, judge may use its wood for another mosque.
The judge's knowledge of the builder matters.
4
Note the contravening opinion
Some scholars held that ruined waqf becomes inheritance. The author, citing al-Shurunbulali, rejects this as contrary to the mufta bihi, except for mosques regarding the building itself.
This opinion is not operative for fatwa.
5
Special rule for mosques
According to the mufta bihi, the building of a mosque (its structure) cannot be transferred even if ruined; only its movable assets (like mats) can be transferred. Non-mosque buildings (e.g., reservoir, well) can be transferred.
This is al-Shurunbulali's final view.

Revocation of Cemetery Waqf and the Role of Idafa
Critical refinement of cemetery waqf revocation rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revoking a cemetery waqf made by idafa, and distinguish between areas with and without graves.

Core Idea
A cemetery waqf made contingent on death can be revoked during life, but after burial the grave sites become irrevocable.

The author of Kanz al-Daqaiq held that a court judgment is necessary for a cemetery waqf. However, this passage refines that view, showing that making the waqf contingent upon death (idafa) is sufficient and allows revocation during the donor's life. More importantly, once burial takes place, the graves themselves cannot be disturbed, even if the waqf is revoked. This lesson traces the legal reasoning and the intra-Hanafi narration that protects the sanctity of graves.

Guided Path
1

Recognize that al-Musannif restricts valid cemetery waqf to those established by court judgment. This lesson critiques that restriction.
The critique is based on the equivalence of idafa to a court order.
2
Understand idafa as an alternative
Idafa ila ma ba'd al-mawt (attachment to death) makes the waqf effective only after death, like a bequest. It does not require a court judgment.
During the donor's life, the waqf is not binding; revocation is permitted.
3
Apply revocation rules before death
Before the donor dies, he can revoke the entire cemetery waqf because it is not yet binding.
This is consistent with the nature of a bequest.
4
Examine the post-burial scenario
After burials have taken place, the specific grave sites are exempt from revocation. The narration from al-Hasan confirms this. The rest of the land may be reclaimed.
The sanctity of graves (hurmat al-nubush) prohibits exhuming bodies.
5
Apply the prohibition of exhumation
Even after revocation, the graves cannot be dug up; the land around them can be leveled and used for other purposes.
This balances the donor's right to revoke with the inviolability of the deceased.

Rulings on Trees and Buildings in Cemetery Waqf
Rulings on trees, buildings, and graves in cemetery waqf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on trees, buildings, and grave usage in a cemetery waqf and identify when custom overrides the original ruling.

Core Idea
Trees in a cemetery at waqf time are not part of the waqf; heirs may cut them.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq details several unique rules for cemetery waqf, distinguishing them from other waqf properties. We focus on trees, buildings, and grave usage, noting that the operative fatwa often follows custom rather than Abu Hanifa's original view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Note that the fatwa in these matters is contrary to Abu Hanifa due to inherited custom. This sets the stage: later Hanafi practice may override the early view.
This is a key example of custom ('urf) influencing fatwa.
2
Parse the ruling on trees at waqf time
If trees existed in the cemetery at the moment of endowment, the heirs may cut them because the spot they occupy did not enter the waqf. This is unlike other properties where trees follow the waqf.
Compare with the general rule of 'trees follow land' in other waqf.
3
Understand the rule for trees after waqf
If trees grow after the waqf, they belong to the planter if known; otherwise the judge may sell them and use proceeds for cemetery upkeep. The proceeds are treated like waqf.
The judge's discretion is guided by maslahah.
4
Review building and grave usage rules
Temporary structures for storage are allowed if space is available, but can be removed for burials. A grave dug for oneself can be used by others, but the first digger should not be disturbed if space permits; if forced, he is compensated for his labor.
These rules balance individual rights with community need.
5
Note the rule on abandoned cemetery
Grass from an abandoned cemetery may be cut and fed to animals, but animals cannot graze there directly.
This maintains reverence for the site while allowing limited benefit.

Fatwas on Cemetery Waqf: Kharaj Exemption and Spending 'Ushr
Fatwas on problematic waqf lands, kharaj exemption, and 'ushr spending.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on kharaj exemption for waqf land and evaluate the dispute between Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding spending 'ushr on a muezzin.

Core Idea
Land made into a cemetery waqf is exempt from kharaj; spending 'ushr on a needy muezzin is permissible according to Muhammad but not Abu Yusuf.

This passage presents fatwas from al-Khaniyah and other sources on specific issues: whether a woman can sell land unsuitable for graves, whether waqf land is exempt from kharaj, and a controversy over using sultan-granted 'ushr for a muezzin. The resolution depends on following Muhammad's opinion.

Guided Path

Understand the case of unsuitable land
A woman made a land a cemetery but it became waterlogged. Al-Faqih Abu Ja'far ruled that if people avoid burying there due to corruption, it does not become a cemetery; the woman may sell it, and the buyer may remove the deceased.
This shows the importance of the land's actual suitability for the intended purpose.

Learn the ruling on kharaj exemption
If a person makes his land a cemetery, a khan (caravanserai), or a dwelling, the kharaj (land tax) is dropped if the land was kharaji. There is a difference of opinion, but the correct view is that it is dropped.
This is the mufta bihi according to the author.
3
Examine the ribat lifetime tenure
If someone builds a ribat on condition that it remains in his hand as long as he lives, it stays with him unless he does something to deserve eviction. This is a valid conditional waqf.
This illustrates the Hanafi permissibility of conditional endowments.
4
Analyze the 'ushr fatwa: the scenario
A group cultivated dead land on the Jihun riverbank, subject to 'ushr (tithe) because the water is not considered kharaj water according to Muhammad. The sultan granted that 'ushr to a nearby ribat. Can the trustee spend it on a needy muezzin?
The question tests the limits of transferring usufruct of waqf funds.
5
Identify the two positions and the mufta bihi
Abu Ja'far says: if the muezzin is needy, it is permissible; but the trustee should not spend it on repair of the ribat. This is based on Muhammad's view. Abu Yusuf held that transferring the 'ushr to another is not allowed. The fatwa is on Muhammad's opinion.
The tarjih is by later authority (as with the earlier custom-based rulings).

Disposal of Unused Ribat: Transfer to Nearby Ribat or Reversion
Fatwa on disposal of unused ribat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling for a ribat that is no longer used and determine when its revenue should be transferred to another ribat or when the waqf reverts to heirs.

Core Idea
When a ribat becomes obsolete, its revenue goes to a nearby similar waqf; if none exists, the property reverts to the founder's heirs.

This lesson addresses a pragmatic scenario: a ribat (waqf hospice) that travellers no longer use. Al-Faqih Abu Ja'far provides guidance on how to redirect its resources. This reflects the Hanafi principle that a waqf should continue to serve its charitable purpose, and if that becomes impossible, the property returns to the founder's estate.

Identify the Situation

Understand the Principle
Waqf is perpetual but conditional on benefiting the stated purpose. When that purpose permanently fails, the property is not left idle but returns to the original owners.
This is known as 'ruju' al-waqf' or reversion.

In this lesson, we examine a passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq that defines the term 'sabil' (public water fountain) and explains the legal rulings on its use. Rulings on Ribat Endowments: Bridge Repair and Revenue Disposition
A collection of fatwas on practical ribat endowment issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which ribat revenue may be used for bridge repair, identify the ruling on trustee loans, and explain the disposal of surplus revenue when a ribat becomes disused.

Core Idea
Waqf revenue must follow the founder's conditions; deviation requires explicit permission or legal necessity.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq records fatwas of Imam Abu Ja'far al-Hindwani on practical challenges facing trustees of ribat (frontier hospice) endowments. The core question is whether endowment revenue can repair an access bridge, and the answer hinges entirely on the founder's stated conditions, illustrating the Hanafi principle: 'shurut al-waqif ka-nuss al-shar'i' (the founder's conditions are like a legislated text). The passage then addresses trustee personal loans, diversion of surplus to another ribat, reversion of defunct endowments, and interpretation of bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary issue and conditional ruling
The first question: can bridge repair be paid from ribat revenue? Abu Ja'far makes it conditional on the founder's stipulation for 'masalih' (interests) of the ribat. Recognize that this is a condition (shart) for permissibility.
The condition is not about the act of repair itself, but about the founder's expressed intent.
2
Derive the underlying legal principle
The bridge is necessary for access, yet Abu Ja'far does not deem repair automatically permissible. This shows that necessity does not override the founder's conditions in waqf. The revenue is tied to the purpose specified by the founder.
Compare with the rule that waqf property cannot be sold except in extreme necessity.
3
Analyze the ruling on trustee loans
The trustee using surplus as a personal loan is deemed 'la yanbaghi' (improper). However, if he later spends his own money on the ribat, he hopes to be cleared. This illustrates that even intend-to-repay loans from waqf revenue are not permitted, but post-facto compensation may bring exoneration.
The term 'rajawtu' (I hope) indicates a non-binding opinion, typical of fatwa discourse.
4
Examine the disposal of surplus from a disused ribat
When a ribat is no longer needed, its revenue should be spent on a neighboring ribat (closest purpose). If none exists, the endowment reverts to the founder's heirs. This follows the principle of 'qurb al-maslahah' (closest benefit) and 'awdat al-waqf ila al-waqif' (reversion when purpose fails).
Some Hanafi later sources require a judge's order for reversion.
5
Interpret bequests to an unnamed ribat
If someone bequeaths one-third to 'the ribat', the executor must look for indications of intent. If ambiguous, the bequest goes to maintenance of the ribat building, not to residents. This shows the Hanafi method of interpreting ambiguous endowment language.
The term 'ribat' can refer to the building or its inhabitants; context clarifies.

Waqf of Roads and Cemeteries: An Intra-Hanafi Analysis
Various rulings on conversion of pagan cemeteries, waqf of cemeteries and roads, and related issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on converting a pagan cemetery to a Muslim one, explain why a mosque cannot become a cemetery, and evaluate the authority of the Nawadir ruling permitting waqf of roads and cemeteries within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A mosque cannot become a cemetery, but a pagan cemetery can become Muslim if traces are gone; Abu Hanifa's rare transmission allows waqf of roads and cemeteries.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq compiles several rulings related to consecrated spaces. It opens with a practical question: can a polytheist cemetery be reused for Muslims? The answer comes with an important condition. Then the text pivots to endowments, presenting a less common view from Imam Abu Hanifa on the waqf of roads and cemeteries. This lesson unpacks these rulings, highlighting the intra-Hanafi dimension of the Nawadir report.
