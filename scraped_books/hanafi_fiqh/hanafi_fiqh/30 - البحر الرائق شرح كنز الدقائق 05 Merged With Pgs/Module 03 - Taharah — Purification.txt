Shubha al-Mahal vs Shubha al-Fi'l: The General Rule on Lineage
Expansion on the rule of lineage in shubha cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shubha al-mahal and shubha al-fi'l and state the general Hanafi rule for establishing lineage by claim.

Core Idea
Lineage is established by claim only in shubha al-mahal, not shubha al-fi'l, because the latter is pure fornication.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on the matn's statement that lineage is established only in the first type of doubt. The commentator explains the key distinction between doubt in the subject (shubha al-mahal) and doubt in the act (shubha al-fi'l), and how that determines whether lineage can be established through the man's claim (da'wa).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of shubha
Understand shubha al-mahal as doubt concerning the lawfulness of the woman (e.g., mistaken belief in a valid marriage contract) and shubha al-fi'l as doubt concerning the permissibility of the act itself.
Shubha al-mahal involves a potential contractual basis; shubha al-fi'l does not.
2
State the ruling for each type
Lineage is established by claim in shubha al-mahal; it is not established in shubha al-fi'l even if claimed.
The ruling applies regardless of hadd dropping.
3
Understand the reasoning
In shubha al-fi'l, the act is still considered fornication (though hadd is dropped due to confusion), so the child is not linked to the man. In shubha al-mahal, the doubt taints the subject, making the intercourse resemble a valid one.
The lack of contractual doubt in shubha al-fi'l is key.

قوله : (والنسب يثبت في الأول فقط) أي يثبت النسب في شبهة المحل بالدعوة ولا يثبت في شبهة الفعل وإن ادعاه لأن الفعل تمحض زنا في الثانية وإن سقط الحد لأمر راجع إليه وهو اشتباه الأمر عليه ولم يتمحض في الأولى للشبهة في المحل، وقد قدم المصنف أن نسب ولد المعتدة البت يثبت إذا جاءت به لأقل من سنتين بغير دعوة، ولسنتين فأكثر لا يثبت إلا بالدعوة، وهو بعمومه يتناول المعتدة عن ثلاث طلقات فكان مخصصاً لقوله هنا .«فقط». والحاصل أنه لا يثبت النسب في شبهة الفعل عند الدعوة إلا في المطلقة ثلاثاً. والفرق أن الشبهة فيها شبهة في العقد بخلاف باقي محال شبهة الاشتباه فإنه لا شبهة عقد فيها فلا يثبت النسب بالدعوة وسيأتي أن من شبهة الاشتباه وطء امرأة زفت وقالت النساء هي زوجتك ولم تكن زوجته معتمداً خبرهن. وصرح الزيلعي بأن النسب يثبت فيه بالدعوة كما سيأتي فتحرر أن النسب لا يثبت في شبهة الفعل إلا في موضعين
His statement: (And lineage is established in the first only) meaning: lineage is established in shubha al-mahal (doubt in the subject) by a claim, and it is not established in shubha al-fi'l (doubt in the act) even if he claims it, because the act is purely fornication in the second [case] even if the hadd is dropped due to a matter returning to him, namely the confusion upon him, and it is not pure in the first due to the doubt in the subject. The author has previously stated that the lineage of the child of a woman in a irrevocable divorce is established if she gives birth with less than two years without a claim, and if two years or more it is not established except by a claim. And this generality includes the woman divorced by triple divorce, so it is a specification of his statement here 'only'. The summary is that lineage is not established in shubha al-fi'l upon a claim except in the case of the triple divorcée. The difference is that the doubt there is a doubt in the contract, unlike the remaining places of doubt of confusion (shubha al-ishtibah) because there is no doubt in the contract, so lineage is not established by a claim. And it will come that among shubha al-ishtibah is the intercourse of a woman who is brought [to a man] and women say she is his wife, and she is not his wife, relying on their news. And al-Zayla'i explicitly stated that lineage is established in it by a claim, as will come. So it is established that lineage is not established in shubha al-fi'l except in two places.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the matn statement 'and lineage is established in the first only'. The commentator explains that the 'first' refers to shubha al-mahal (doubt in the legal subject, e.g., the woman herself) where lineage can be established by the man's claim. In shubha al-fi'l (doubt in the act, e.g., the man thinks the act is permissible due to confusion), lineage is not established by claim because the act is still considered fornication, even if the hadd punishment is dropped due to the man's confusion. The exception to this is the case of a woman divorced by triple talaq who is in her 'idda: if she gives birth after two years, lineage is established only if the man claims the child. The commentator notes that this exception is a specification of the general rule. The difference between shubha al-fi'l in the triple divorce case and other cases is that the triple divorce case involves a doubt in the original contract (the nikah was valid but later dissolved), whereas other shubha al-fi'l cases (like a woman brought to a man and told she is his wife) have no contractual doubt. Al-Zaylai holds that lineage is established in the latter case by claim. The conclusion: lineage is not established in shubha al-fi'l by claim except in two specific places (the triple divorcée and the woman brought to a man).

Key Terms
شبهة المحل (shubha al-mahal): Doubt concerning the lawful subject of intercourse, e.g., mistakenly believing a woman is one's wife due to a valid contract.
شبهة الفعل (shubha al-fi'l): Doubt concerning the act itself, e.g., mistakenly thinking intercourse is permissible due to a misunderstanding, but the woman is not lawful.
الدعوة (al-da'wa): The man's claim or acknowledgment that the child is his offspring.

Exceptions: Lineage in Shubha al-Fi'l for Triple Divorcée and Woman Brought to a Man
Expansion on the exceptions to the rule of lineage in shubha al-fi'l.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two exceptions where lineage is established by claim in shubha al-fi'l and explain the basis of contractual doubt.

Core Idea
Two exceptions: triple divorcée's child after two years and woman brought to a man, both have contractual doubt.

This part of the passage specifies the only two cases where lineage is established in shubha al-fi'l by claim. The commentator traces the reasoning to a lingering doubt in the original marriage contract, distinguishing these from general shubha al-ishtibah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first exception
A woman divorced by triple talaq who gives birth to a child two years or more after the divorce. Lineage is established only if the man claims the child.
This is because the 'idda is over and the child could be from a later intercourse, so claim is needed.
2
Identify the second exception
A woman is brought to a man and women say she is his wife; he relies on their testimony and has intercourse. She is not his wife. Lineage is established by his claim per al-Zaylai.
This is considered a form of shubha al-ishtibah with a semblance of a contract.
3
Understand the common basis
Both exceptions involve a doubt that relates to the original marriage contract (or its appearance), unlike other shubha al-fi'l cases where the doubt is purely about the act.
The triple divorcée had a valid contract; the woman brought to a man has a strong indication of a contract (witnesses).

The Victim's Demand as a Condition for the Hadd of Theft
Expansion on condition for theft punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of the victim's demand for the hadd of theft, explain the Hanafi proof, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The victim's demand for his property is a condition for cutting the thief to remove doubt of ownership.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on a ruling in Kanz al-Daqaiq: the victim's demand is a condition for the hadd of theft. The commentator explains why this condition is necessary even if the thief confesses, and highlights an important internal disagreement within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the condition: the victim's demand (for the property) is a prerequisite for cutting the thief. Without it, no cutting.
2
Understand the wisdom
Why is the condition required? Because of the possibility that the victim might acknowledge the thief's ownership, creating a doubt (shubha) that prevents the hadd. The victim's presence removes this doubt.
3
Compare the views on confession
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the condition applies even if the thief confesses; the victim must be present. Abu Yusuf: by istihsan, confession suffices and the thief is cut immediately.
4
Determine the operative position
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the condition is required), as supported by the matn and Ibn Nujaym.
5
Identify valid litigants
Anyone with a valid hand (yad sahihah) over the stolen property, such as owner, depositary, borrower, usurper, etc., can demand the cutting. A finder of lost property (multaqit) does not have this right.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the rule that the victim's demand (طلب المسروق منه) is a condition for the hadd of theft. The commentator explains that 'demand' here refers to the demand for the property, not necessarily the demand for the cutting itself. The reason is that the cutting is a right of Allah, but it is linked to the absence of doubt (shubha) about the victim's ownership. If the victim is absent or unknown, there is a possibility that he may acknowledge the thief's ownership, which would negate the theft. Therefore, the victim must be present or his claim must be established. The text then discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, even when the thief confesses, the victim's claim is a condition; Abu Yusuf says it is not. The preferred ruling (mufta bihi) is that the condition is necessary. The commentary also explains who can be considered a valid litigant: anyone with a valid hand over the property, such as the owner, depositary, borrower, usurper, etc. A special case is the usurer who has taken the property in an invalid sale — he is considered a litigant because the property in his hand is like usurped property. The finder of a lost item (multaqit) does not have the right to demand cutting because his hand is not as strong as a depositary's. The overall conclusion is that the victim's demand for the property is a condition, and the demand for cutting is not required.

Key Terms
طلب المسروق منه (talab al-masruq minhu): The demand by the victim (the one from whom property was stolen) either for his property or for the hadd punishment, but here interpreted as demand for the property.
خصومة (khusumah): Litigation or legal claim by the victim to establish his right and demand the property.
يد صحيحة (yad sahihah): A valid hand over property, meaning lawful possession that gives authority to litigate for theft.

A ruling on the recommendation to keep nails long in jihad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on nail length for a mujahid in dar al-harb and explain the rational proof that overrides the general sunnah of clipping.

Core Idea
For the mujahid in dar al-harb, long nails are recommended (mandub) for self-defense, even though clipping them is normally sunnah.

You may know that trimming nails is part of the fitrah and a confirmed sunnah. But there is an exception for the mujahid in enemy territory. In this lesson, we examine a succinct ruling from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq that explains why the mujahid is encouraged to grow his nails—and how a rational benefit (maslahah) can override a general sunnah within Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? What is its hukm level? The act is keeping nails long for a mujahid in dar al-harb. The ruling is mandub (recommended, equivalent to sunnah ghayr muakkadah).
Mandub is a category between mubah and wajib.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does the author cite? The proof is rational (ta'lil): the benefit of self-defense if the weapon is lost. An analogy is drawn to the mustache, which is normally sunnah to trim but in jihad may be kept for intimidation.
No textual (naqli) proof is cited; it is a qiyas based on maslahah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the proof establish the ruling? The phrase 'because if the weapon falls from his hand and the enemy approaches him, he might be able to defend himself with his nails' provides the effective cause (illah): the need for a last-resort defense.
This illah is specific to the mujahid in dar al-harb.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there any disagreement? The passage indicates no dispute; cross-madhhab status is 'muttafaq alayh' (agreed upon). Other schools may not have this specific exception but do not object.
The ruling is straightforward within the Hanafi school.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon? The relied-upon position is the recommendation (mandub) for the mujahid. There is no conflicting view within the school, so no tarjih is needed.
The ruling is from zahir al-riwayah.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on revived land criteria and Basra's exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Yusuf's proximity criterion and Muhammad's water-source criterion for classifying revived dead land, and explain why Basra is 'ushri despite being conquered by force.

Core Idea
Proximity vs water source: two Hanafi criteria for revived land; Basra's 'ushri status overrides analogy by ijma'.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explores the classification of revived dead land as either kharaji or 'ushri. Two intra-Hanafi views are presented: Abu Yusuf looks to proximity to existing land, while Muhammad al-Shaybani considers the water source. The text then addresses Basra's exceptional treatment as 'ushri by consensus of the Companions, analogous to Mecca. This lesson unpacks the dispute and the use of ijma' to override qiyas.

Basra was conquered by force, so by analogy it should be kharaji. But the Companions unanimously made it 'ushri. Understand the role of ijma' al-sahaba in overriding qiyas.

Commentary
This passage discusses two main issues: the criteria for determining whether revived dead land becomes kharaji or 'ushri, and the exceptional status of Basra as 'ushriyya. Regarding revived land, the author explains the matn's statement 'consideration is given to its proximity' and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf considers proximity to existing kharaji or 'ushri land, while Muhammad considers the water source used for irrigation. The ruling is restricted to Muslims because for a non-Muslim, the land is kharaji unconditionally. The text then addresses Basra: although logically it should be kharaji because it is in the kharaj zone and was conquered by force, the Companions unanimously agreed to impose tithe (ushr) instead, analogous to the exception of Mecca. The author also includes a discussion on the obligation of ushr on waqf lands, noting that the later Hanafi authorities uphold its obligation, but he hesitates regarding Egyptian waqf lands purchased from bayt al-mal due to lack of explicit transmission. The passage demonstrates how qiyas (analogy) can be overridden by ijma' (consensus) or established practice.

أرض موات (ard mawat): Dead land, uncultivated and unowned, which can be revived by a Muslim.

Core Idea
New non-Muslim places of worship are absolutely prohibited—even if a treaty allows it, the Imam must not honor the condition.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on erecting new churches and synagogues in Islamic lands. The text from Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that while peace treaties may include permissions, the school's definitive view prioritizes Islamic dignity and prohibits such construction. Conditions for Valid Apostasy and the Meaning of Iman

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid apostasy according to the Hanafi school and distinguish between the definitions of iman as tasdiq only versus tasdiq plus iqrar.

Core Idea
Apostasy is valid only from a sane, voluntarily acting person; iman is inner affirmation, while verbal confession is merely for worldly rulings.

In this passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author clarifies who can legally commit apostasy and what iman truly means. The passage also highlights an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether puberty is necessary for a child's apostasy.
