Payment Priority and Seller's Right to Withhold Goods
Order of delivery: buyer pays first, seller may withhold goods until payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of delivery in a sale contract, identify conditions for the buyer's obligation to pay first, and apply the seller's right to retain goods until full payment.

Core Idea
The buyer must pay first in a cash sale; the seller may keep the goods until the last dirham is paid.

This passage from Baḥr al-Rāʾiq explains the Hanafi position on who performs first in a sale: the buyer delivers the price before receiving the goods, unless the price is deferred or an option is present. The seller has a robust right to retain (ḥabs) the sold item until full payment, even if the buyer offers a pledge or guarantor. Understanding this principle is essential for structuring valid sales and protecting the seller's interest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? (Buyer paying the price first). What is the hukm level? (Wajib).
The ruling is based on the contract's requirement of equality.
2
State the conditions for the obligation
List the conditions that must be present for the buyer to be obligated to pay first: immediate price, no active option, goods determinable.
If any condition is absent, the buyer is not required to pay first.
3
Note the exceptions
Identify when the buyer is not required to pay first: if the price is deferred or if the buyer has an option (khiyar).
The text gives specific scenarios: deferred price, khiyar al-ru'yah, khiyar al-shart.
4
Identify the seller's right of retention
The seller may retain the sold item until the entire price is received, even if only a dirham remains.
This right applies even if the buyer offers a pledge or guarantor.
5
List what waives the retention right
The retention right is waived only by a transfer of debt (hawalah) onto the buyer.
Pledge, guarantor, and partial payment do not waive it.

Commentary
This passage discusses the order of performance in a sale contract. The author comments on the statement 'Whoever sells a commodity for a price, he delivers it first' meaning the buyer must pay the price before receiving the item, because the contract implies equality and reciprocity: the buyer's right to the item is fixed, so he pays first to fix the seller's right. If the seller stipulates delivery of the item before payment, the sale is void (fāsid) because it contradicts the contract's requirement. Muhammad adds that if no time is specified, it is also invalid due to jahālah (uncertainty of term). The seller must present the goods to prove their existence; then the buyer is ordered to pay. If the goods are absent, the buyer may withhold payment. The text contrasts sale with pledge (rahn): for a pledge, the pledgee is not forced to bring it if costly because it is a trust. In sale, the buyer may take a guarantor if the seller is unable to deliver. The price must be immediate; if deferred, the buyer is not required to pay first. The buyer's option (khiyār) must also be absent; if present, the seller cannot demand payment until the option period ends. The seller has the right to retain (ḥabs) the sold item until full payment, even if only a dirham remains. This right applies even if part of the price is deferred—he can retain until the immediate part is paid. If two items are sold together, he can retain both until the full price for both is paid. Even if the buyer releases the seller from part of the price, retention continues for the remaining amount. The right to retention is not lost by taking a pledge or guarantor, but it is lost by a transfer (ḥawālah) of the debt onto the buyer. The passage ends with a sub-ruling about a money-changer (ṣayrafī): if someone pays dirhams to a money-changer for inspection and debased coins are found, if the money-changer charged a fee, the client may reclaim the fee.

Key Terms
تسليم الثمن (taslīm al-thaman): Delivery of the price by the buyer to the seller, which must precede receipt of the sold item when the price is immediate.
حبس المبيع (ḥabs al-mabī‘): The seller's right to retain the sold item until the price is fully paid.
صيرفي (ṣayrafī): A money-changer or banked, who examines and exchanges coins.

The Buyer's Obligation to Pay First and the Seller's Right to Withhold
Commentary on the obligation to pay first

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the buyer's obligation to pay the price first and identify the conditions under which the seller may withhold the sold item, as well as the exceptions to this right.

Core Idea
The buyer must pay the price first when goods are present; the seller may withhold the goods until full payment.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the ruling behind the matn statement 'من باع سلعة بثمن سلمه أولاً' (Whoever sells a commodity for a price, he delivers it first). It clarifies that the buyer is obliged to pay the price before taking delivery because the contract requires equality. However, this obligation is conditional on the seller producing the goods and the price being prompt. The passage also details the seller's right to withhold the sold item until full payment and the exceptions to this right, such as when an assignment (hawala) occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that the buyer must pay the price first. This is wajib (obligatory) because it is a necessary consequence of the contract to establish equality between the parties.
The ruling is derived from the phrase 'يُسلم الثمن أولاً' – he delivers the price first.
2
Locate the conditions for the obligation
The buyer's obligation to pay depends on three conditions: (1) the seller must produce the goods so the buyer knows they exist, (2) the price must be prompt (hal), and (3) the buyer must have no option (khiyar) that would allow him to cancel the sale.
The text says 'ولا بد من إحضار السلعة ليعلم قيامها' and 'ولا بد من كون الثمن حالاً' and 'ولا بد أن لا يكون في البيع خيار للمشتري'.
3
Understand the seller's right to withhold
The seller has the right to retain possession of the sold item (habs) until he receives the full price, even if only a part remains unpaid. This right applies even if the buyer has paid part of the price or has been released from part of it.
The text explicitly states 'أن للبائع حق حبس المبيع حتى يستوفي الثمن كله'.
4
Note exceptions and limits
The right to withhold is not dropped by giving a pledge (rahn) or a guarantor (kafil), but it is dropped by an assignment (hawala) of the debt by the seller to the buyer. Additionally, if part of the price is deferred, the seller withholds only until the prompt portion is paid.
The passage contrasts sale with pledge (رهن) where the pledgee is not obliged to produce the item at his own expense.
5
Consider the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Muhammad ibn al-Hasan holds that if the seller conditions delivery of the goods before payment, the sale is invalid (fasid) due to uncertainty of the term, unless a specific time is set. The relied-upon view (from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is that the sale is simply invalid (fasid) because it contradicts the contract's requirement.
The commentator quotes al-Bazzaziyyah: 'باع بشرط أن يدفع المبيع قبل نقد الثمن فسد البيع'.

Hawala and the Seller's Right of Retention: Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hawala and habs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the effect of hawala on the seller's right to retain goods and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Assignment of debt (hawala) is not always like payment; the seller's right to retain depends on who is assignor and who is assignee.

When a buyer assigns the price to a third party, does the seller lose the right to hold the goods until payment? Imam Abu Yusuf says yes — assignment is equivalent to payment. But Imam Muhammad disagrees: we must ask who is the assignee. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind both positions and extends the analysis to a similar issue in rahn (pledge).

Guided Path
1
State the shared background
Identify the basic scenario: a buyer has not paid the price; the seller has a right to retain the goods until payment. Then the buyer performs hawala (assignment) of the debt to a third party.
2
Extract Abu Yusuf's position
Notice that Abu Yusuf treats hawala like payment (ka al-ifa'). Therefore, the seller's right to retain falls immediately.
3
Extract Muhammad's position and distinction
Muhammad differentiates: if the seller is the assignee (the buyer assigns the debt to him), the seller still has a claim against the buyer, so his right to retain remains. If the seller is the assignor (he assigns his own right to someone else), his claim falls, so the right to retain is lost.
4
Analyze the analogy to rahn
Muhammad applies the same logic to a mortgagee (murtahin). If the mortgagee assigns his debt to the mortgagor (rahn), he loses the right to retain the pledge. If he assigns to a third party, the right remains.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In a case where the buyer assigns the price to the seller, under Abu Yusuf the seller cannot retain goods; under Muhammad he can. This matters for immediate possession and security.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Takhliya alone does not guarantee possession for distant property—the Sahibayn require physical access.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq's commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq lays out the conditions that make a buyer a true possessor. While takhliya (vacating) is central, a key dispute arises when the sold item is far away: does verbal vacation transfer possession, or must the buyer be able to physically take control? Understanding this disagreement is essential for correctly applying Hanafi rules of sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Qabd haqiqi (actual possession) is the act by which a buyer takes physical or constructive control of the sold item. The passage distinguishes it from legal possession (qabd hukmi).

Both positions are legitimate within the school, but the more practical view is recommended.

Slave Buying Himself: Constructive Possession in Sale
Ruling on a slave buying himself and the master's right to withhold

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave who buys himself gains possession by the contract itself, just as a resident who buys the house he lives in—the master cannot withhold.

This lesson examines a unique sale scenario: a slave purchasing his own person from his master. The Hanafi school rules that the sale is valid and, crucially, the master cannot detain the slave for the price. The reasoning rests on an analogy to a person who buys a house they already occupy—possession is achieved instantly by the contract. This principle of constructive possession (qabd hukmi) is key to understanding when a seller’s right to withhold (haqs al-habs) is extinguished.

Read the two cases presented: (1) the slave buys himself from the master directly; (2) a third party appoints the slave as agent to buy himself from the master. Note that in both, the sale is valid and the master cannot withhold the slave for the price.
The ruling is the same in both scenarios.

The author draws an analogy (qiyas) to a person who buys a house while already living in it. Just as that buyer becomes possessor by the contract alone, so does the slave. Identify the asl (original case) and far‘ (new case).
The illa (effective cause) is that the buyer already has de facto control over the item at the time of contract.
3

No objections are raised in the text.

The agency does not change the possession analysis.

Conclude that whenever the buyer already controls the subject matter of the sale (e.g., residing in a house, being the slave), possession is transferred by the contract itself. This eliminates the seller’s right to withhold for the price.
