Diyah and Qisas for Killing a Person with No Wali: The Laqit Dispute
Ruling on diyah and qisas where wali is absent, with laqit dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah and qisas for killing a person with no wali, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding qisas for a laqit.

Core Idea
The imam acts as wali for those with no guardian, but a possibility of an heir creates a doubt that may drop qisas.

When a person with no known heirs is killed, who claims compensation? The Hanafi school answers that the imam steps in as the guardian, but a dispute arises among the founders over whether the imam can execute qisas for a foundling (laqit) because of the potential existence of unknown heirs. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the reasoning behind the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State that for accidental killing of a person with no wali, diyah is on the 'aqilah for the imam. For intentional killing, the imam has the choice between qisas and diyah (with consent), but cannot pardon.
The imam does not own the diyah; it is placed in the public treasury.
2
Trace the dalil
The hadith 'السلطان ولي من لا ولي له' establishes the imam's authority. Analyse its application: the imam is the wali, so he can claim rights on behalf of the deceased.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but used in this context.
3
Apply the ruling to the laqit
The text explicitly includes the laqit in these rulings. For accidental killing, diyah goes to the imam. For intentional killing, there is a dispute.
The laqit has no known lineage, so he has no wali.
4
Analyse the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf argues that a laqit may have an heir (e.g., biological parents), creating a doubt (shubhah) that prevents qisas. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that an unknown heir is legally non-existent, so qisas is allowed.
The text states 'وهو قولهما' referring to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the dominant view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Although the text presents both, the context and later practice prefer the majority view allowing the choice.
Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq does not explicitly tarjih, but the structure implies the majority view is operative.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the diyah and qisas for killing a person who has no wali (legal heir/guardian) — specifically a mu'min (believer) with no wali, or a harbi who became Muslim after coming with aman. For accidental killing, the diyah is paid by the 'aqilah (tribe/agnates) to the imam, who places it in the public treasury (bayt al-mal), because there is no individual heir. For intentional killing, the imam has the option of qisas (execution) or taking diyah by agreement with the killer; he is not allowed to pardon without compensation because the right belongs to the Muslim community. The text then extends this to the case of a laqit (foundling) killed by mistake or intentionally. An intra-Hanafi dispute is recorded: Abu Yusuf holds that for a laqit killed intentionally, the imam cannot execute qisas because there is a possibility of an heir (e.g., unknown parents), which creates a doubt (shubhah) that drops qisas. Key Terms
عاقلة ('aqilah): The agnate relatives (tribe) who are liable for paying diyah in cases of accidental killing.
لقيط (laqit): A foundling (abandoned child of unknown parentage).
شبهة (shubhah): A doubt or ambiguity that prevents the application of a harsh penalty (like qisas).
السلطان ولي من لا ولي له (al-sultan wali man la wali lahu): The sultan (imam/ruler) is the guardian of whoever has no guardian – a key hadith used to justify the imam's authority in these cases.

Key Takeaway
When a person with no wali is killed, the imam acts as his guardian and may choose qisas or diyah (but not pardon); for a laqit, the majority allows qisas despite the possibility of an unknown heir.
