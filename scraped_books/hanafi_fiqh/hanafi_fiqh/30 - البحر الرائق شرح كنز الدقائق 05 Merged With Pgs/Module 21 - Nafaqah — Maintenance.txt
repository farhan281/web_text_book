Maintenance of the Foundling from the Treasury
Ruling on foundling's maintenance from treasury with finder's details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the foundling's maintenance, identify the evidence from sahaba and qiyas, and explain the finder's right to reimbursement under judicial authorization.

Core Idea
The foundling's maintenance is an obligation of the treasury, not the finder, by sahaba practice and analogy to the disabled.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on the ruling that a foundling's maintenance is from the public treasury. The author provides the evidence from the Companions Umar and Ali, along with a qiyas with the disabled. It also clarifies the finder's role: initially charitable, but with a judge's order, any expenditure becomes a recoverable debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage rules upon and what the hukm is. The act is maintenance (nafaqah) of a foundling (laqit), and the ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) from the public treasury (bayt al-mal).
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Find the proofs cited: (1) Reports from Umar and Ali, and (2) Qiyas with a disabled person (maq'ud) who has no wealth or relatives.
The reports are from sahaba, not directly from Quran or Sunnah, yet they are authoritative in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof proves)
For the sahaba practice: the fact that they placed maintenance on the treasury shows it is the state's duty. For qiyas: the foundling shares the illa (effective cause) of the disabled—inability to earn, no wealth, no relatives—so both deserve treasury support.
4
Analyze the finder's expenditure
Note the default: the finder is mutabarri' (volunteer) with no right to reimbursement. However, if the judge orders the finder to spend and stipulates recourse, it becomes a debt recoverable from the foundling's future estate, subject to evidence.
This protects both the foundling and the finder.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools hold the finder primarily responsible for maintenance. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa). Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence): in Hanafi, the treasury bears the cost; in other schools, the finder must pay.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the maintenance of a foundling (laqit). The author opens by stating the rule from the matn: his maintenance is from the public treasury (bayt al-mal). This is supported by reports from Sayyiduna Umar and Sayyiduna Ali (radi Allahu anhuma). The reasoning is that the foundling is a Muslim who cannot earn, has no wealth, and no relatives, thus he resembles a disabled person who is supported by the treasury. The finder (multaqit) is initially a volunteer caring for the foundling; but if the judge orders the finder to spend and stipulates reimbursement, the finder's expenditure becomes a debt he can recover from the foundling after he reaches majority, but only with evidence if denied. The term 'nafaqah' includes clothing. The foundling's marriage expenses (mahr) are also from the treasury if he has no wealth. If the finder refuses to spend, the judge may remove the foundling and give him to someone else willing to spend; the judge has discretion.

Key Terms
اللقيط (al-laqīṭ): A foundling; an abandoned infant whose lineage is unknown.
بيت المال (bayt al-māl): The public treasury of the Islamic state, responsible for welfare.

Maintenance from a Missing Person's Wealth: Who is Entitled?
Establishes the principle of maintenance from a missing person's wealth and lists qualifying relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle for maintenance from a missing person's wealth and identify which relatives qualify.

Core Idea
Only those entitled to maintenance without a court order in the missing person's presence receive it in his absence.

When a person is missing, their wealth does not become ownerless. The Hanafi school establishes a clear principle for who may be maintained from that wealth. The key distinction is whether the relative would have been entitled to maintenance in the missing person's presence without needing a qadi's order. This lesson unpacks that principle and lists the qualifying relatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the passage that begins 'والأصل فيه أن كل من يستحق النفقة في ماله حال حضرته بغير قضاء القاضي ينفق عليه من ماله في غيبته'. Extract the two categories: those entitled without qadi order (first category) and those only entitled with qadi order (second category).
The rationale is that qadi order in the first case is mere assistance, but in the second case it is the source of obligation, which is impossible against an absentee.
2
List the first category
Identify from the text: young children, adult females (unmarried daughters), and disabled adult males (zamanā). Also, the wife is included even if wealthy.
The wife is an exception to the poverty condition – she gets maintenance even if rich.
3
List the second category
Identify from text: brother, sister, maternal uncle, maternal aunt, and every mahram relative. These do not receive maintenance from the missing person's wealth because they would need a qadi order in his presence.
This includes siblings, uncles, aunts, and other non-mahram relatives? The text says 'كل محرم' meaning all mahram relatives.
4
Understand the payment method
The passage states that maintenance is to be paid in dirhams and dinars (silver and gold coins), not in kind (clothing or food directly). If such goods are not available, the qadi values them in currency.
Bullion (tibr) is treated like struck coins because it can be valued.
5
Note the exception of the father
The father is exempted from the restriction: he may sell goods (ʿurūḍ) to provide for maintenance, as mentioned earlier in the nafaqah chapter.
This is a special facility for the father due to his higher authority.

Commentary
This passage continues the chapter on the missing person, focusing on practical rulings for managing his wealth. First, it states that no claim or evidence is accepted against the missing person for debts, deposits, etc., because there is no opponent (khasm). The qadi's appointee is not an opponent, and heirs cannot act since death is unproven. The author then mentions that if property is feared to spoil, the qadi may sell it. The core of the passage is the principle for maintenance (nafaqah) from the missing person's wealth: those who would receive maintenance in his presence without a court order (e.g., young children, adult females, disabled males) continue to receive it in his absence. Those who need a court order in his presence (e.g., siblings, mahrams) do not receive it in his absence because judgment against an absentee is not allowed. The payment must be in dirhams/dinars, not in kind, unless the qadi values it. The text also discusses cases where the wealth is a deposit or debt: if the depositary/debtor acknowledges, they pay; if they deny, the maintainees cannot claim until the qadi appoints a litigant. If the depositary pays without qadi's order, he becomes liable, and the debtor is not discharged. The passage ends by noting that the author omitted mentioning taking a guarantor because it is covered earlier.

Key Terms
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance (food, clothing, shelter) owed from the missing person's wealth to eligible relatives.
الزمنى (al-zamanā): Disabled adult males (chronically ill or incapacitated) who are entitled to maintenance without a court order.
المحرم (al-mahram): A relative with whom marriage is permanently forbidden (e.g., brother, uncle).

Managing a Missing Person's Wealth: Sale of Property and Handling Deposits/Debts
Procedural rules for selling property at risk of spoilage and for valid collection of deposits and debts from the missing person's wealth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for selling a missing person's property to prevent spoilage and explain the conditions for valid payment by a depositary or debtor without a qadi order.

Core Idea
Property that may spoil can be sold; deposits and debts require acknowledgment or qadi order to be validly collected.

Beyond maintenance, the missing person's wealth includes property, deposits, and debts. This lesson covers the Hanafi rules for selling property that may spoil and for collecting debts and deposits. The key issues are preventing loss and ensuring that payments are legally valid so that the depositary or debtor is discharged.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the restriction on claims
The passage begins by stating that no claim or evidence is accepted against the missing person for debts, deposits, etc., due to the lack of an opponent (khasm). The qadi's appointee is not a proper opponent, and heirs cannot act because death is unproven.
This is a procedural hurdle: the missing person cannot be sued while absent.
2
Identify the exception for spoiling property
The author then quotes al-Hidayah: 'ما كان يخاف عليه الفساد يبيعه الموفق' – what is feared to spoil, the prudent (qadi's appointee) sells it.
This is a necessary measure to preserve value. The authority to sell lies with the qadi or his appointee.
3
Understand the rules for deposits and debts
When the missing person's wealth consists of a deposit (wadīʿah) or a debt (dayn) owed to him, payment can be made to maintainees only if the depositary/debtor acknowledges the deposit/debt and the family relationship (marriage or lineage).
Acknowledgment is necessary because these are not apparent to all. If they are apparent to the qadi (e.g., registered), acknowledgment is not needed.
4
Learn the consequences of unauthorized payment
If the depositary pays without a qadi order, he becomes liable (damān). If the debtor pays without a qadi order, he is not discharged from the debt.
The qadi is the deputy of the missing person; only his order validates payment.
5
Note the case of denial and the role of the qadi
If the depositary/debtor denies the obligation entirely, the maintainees cannot claim because the basis of the right is not established. However, if the qadi appoints a litigator, the case can proceed.
This protects the missing person from false claims.

The first category includes minor children, adult females, and chronically ill adult males. The second includes siblings, uncles, aunts, and other mahrams. The payment is to be made in dirhams and dinars (cash), not in goods, because the right of these dependents is in food and clothing, which require cash to purchase. However, the father is an exception: he may sell goods to obtain cash. The commentator then discusses cases where the missing person's wealth is a deposit or debt: if the depositor/debtor acknowledges the claim, the dependents are supported from it; if they deny, the dependents cannot enforce their right without a court-appointed litigant. The passage then moves to the second ruling: the wife of the missing person is not to be separated from him (i.e., the marriage remains valid). The proof is the hadith 'She is his wife until clear evidence comes to her' and the report of Ali (RA) that she must wait until death or divorce is confirmed. The marriage is not dissolved by the absence, as death is only a possibility. Umar (RA) is reported to have adopted Ali's view. The commentator also notes that divorce by Ila' (vow of abstinence) is different because it was an immediate divorce in Jahiliyyah but is treated as postponed in Islam.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): A person whose whereabouts are unknown and whose life or death is uncertain.
بيان (bayan): Clear evidence or clarification; in this context, proof of death or divorce.
إيلاء (ila'): A vow by a husband to abstain from sexual relations with his wife for a period, which in Jahiliyyah was considered an immediate divorce, but in Islam becomes a postponed divorce after four months.
