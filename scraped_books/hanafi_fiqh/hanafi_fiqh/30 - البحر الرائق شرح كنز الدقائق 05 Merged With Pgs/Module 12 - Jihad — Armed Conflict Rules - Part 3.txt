Insulting the Prophet: Breach of Covenant or Severe Ta'zir?
Inter-madhhab dispute with reconciliation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on insulting the Prophet by a dhimmi and distinguish between breach of covenant and severe ta'zir punishment.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds that insulting the Prophet does not break the covenant, but the offender may be executed as a ta'zir penalty.

The ruling on a dhimmi who insults the Prophet triggers a major disagreement. Does this act nullify the covenant of protection, or is it a crime punishable by ta'zir that can include death? In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi stance, the opposing view, and the critical distinction between 'breaking the covenant' and 'allowing execution'.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The matn and majority of Hanafi scholars hold that insulting the Prophet does not break the dhimma. The offender is disciplined by ta'zir, which may escalate to capital punishment.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opposing view
Al-ʿAynī quotes al-Shāfiʿī, Mālik, and Aḥmad as holding that insulting the Prophet breaks the covenant. Al-ʿAynī himself chooses this view in his commentary.
However, Ibn al-Subkī notes that the relied-upon Shāfiʿī position actually agrees with the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The covenant is not broken because the dhimmi already holds disbelief; a new act of disbelief does not remove what is already present. The purpose of the covenant (repelling harm) can still be achieved through punishment.
This is based on the principle that the covenant is a contract of protection, not a contract on belief.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
If the covenant is broken, the dhimmi loses protected status and becomes a combatant; his family also loses protection. If the covenant is not broken, he remains a dhimmi, and his family is protected, but he may be executed by ta'zir.
The practical difference is the scope of the punishment's effect on the family.
5
Note the reconciliation by Ibn al-Subkī
Ibn al-Subkī clarifies that not breaking the covenant does not prevent execution. The ruling on breach is separate from the ruling on punishment. This resolves the apparent conflict between schools.
This is a key analytical point: the two issues are distinct.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Bahr al-Raiq on the chapter of Siyar, specifically on the conditions that breach the dhimma covenant. It begins by stating that four acts do not break the covenant: refusal to pay jizya, adultery with a Muslim woman, killing a Muslim, and insulting the Prophet. The author explains that the purpose of fighting is to secure commitment to pay jizya, not actual payment; thus refusal does not break the covenant—the Imam compels payment. For adultery and killing, the legal punishments (hadd and qisas) are applied without breaking the covenant. Insulting the Prophet is an act of disbelief, but since the dhimmi already holds disbelief, a subsequent act of disbelief does not nullify the covenantal relationship. The covenant is only broken by physically joining the enemy forces or dominating a territory for war against Muslims, as these actions render the dhimmi effectively hostile. The author also discusses related issues: Muslims must not honor dhimmis in ways that disrespect Islam, but serving for material gain or with the hope of their embracing Islam is permitted. The permissibility of dhimmis living among Muslims and buying property is conditioned on their being few in number; if they become numerous or cause harm, they may be expelled. A significant part of the passage addresses the disagreement on whether insulting the Prophet should break the covenant. Some scholars like al-Ayni and Ibn al-Humam argued for breach, but the relied-upon Hanafi position maintains that it does not break the covenant, although the offender may still be severely punished, even to the point of death, as a discretionary ta'zir penalty. This is because tas'ir can escalate to capital punishment if the offense warrants it. The author supports this view with citations from al-Ramli, Ibn al-Subki, and al-Maqdisi, emphasizing that not breaking the covenant does not mean the offender is immune to execution.

Key Terms
التعزير (al-taʿzīr): A discretionary punishment set by the judge for offenses that do not have a fixed hadd penalty. It can range from a reprimand to death.
الانتقاض (al-intiqad): The legal breach or nullification of the dhimma covenant, rendering the dhimmi as a combatant.
الأمان (al-amān): The guarantee of safety and protection granted to a non-Muslim entering the Islamic state, either through a covenant (dhimma) or a temporary safe conduct (amān muʾaqqat).

Dhimmi Joining the Enemy: Comparison with Apostates
Commentary on comparison of dhimmi to apostate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dhimmi who joins the enemy (ilhaq), distinguish it from the ruling on an apostate, and apply the exceptions regarding enslavement and compulsion.

Core Idea
A dhimmi who joins the enemy is like an apostate in death and property, but is enslaved and not forced to re-accept dhimma.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the Hanafi position on a dhimmi who physically joins the enemy (ilhaq). The matn compares such a person to an apostate, but the commentary explains that the comparison is limited to specific consequences: the dhimmi may be killed and his wealth treated as fay'. However, there are crucial differences: the dhimmi is enslaved (both male and female) and is not compelled to renew his dhimma contract upon return, whereas an apostate is not enslaved and must be forced back to Islam. The proof is drawn from the Quranic verse (60:1) and the incident of Hatib ibn Abi Balta'a, illustrating that mere verbal communication does not break the covenant—only the physical act of ilhaq does.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act (ilhaq of a dhimmi) and what is the baseline ruling? The matn says the dhimmi becomes 'like an apostate'.
The comparison is not absolute; it is qualified by exceptions.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? Quran 60:1 and the story of Hatib, which show that verbal communication alone does not constitute breach of covenant.
The dalil is used by the commentator to argue that only physical joining breaks the dhimma.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse support the ruling? The verse forbids taking enemies as allies, but Hatib's action (sending a letter) was not considered a breach because his intention was not to aid the enemy. Thus, the prohibition applies to physical joining, not mere words.
The commentator distinguishes between the scout (tali'a) who is sent to gather information and a dhimmi who joins the enemy.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Where is the disagreement? The comparison with apostates is limited because the dhimmi is enslaved and not forced to return to dhimma, unlike an apostate.
There is also an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether an apostate woman is enslaved after joining.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
What is the mufta bihi position? The author prefers the general comparison but excludes enslavement and lack of compulsion, citing al-Muhit.
The authority weight is zahir riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn phrase 'wa saraw kal-murtaddin' (and they become like apostates). The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains that when a dhimmi joins the enemy (ilhaq) or is overpowered by them, he is treated like an apostate in two respects: he may be killed and his wealth is given to his heirs. However, the comparison is not absolute; there are significant differences. For instance, the dhimmi who joins may be enslaved (both male and female) and is not forced to re-accept dhimma, whereas an apostate is not enslaved and is forced to return to Islam. The commentator draws on the incident of Hatib ibn Abi Balta'a to illustrate that merely communicating with the enemy in words does not break the covenant of Islam or dhimma; only the physical act of joining (ilhaq) does. He discusses the definition of a scout (tali'a) and reconciles conflicting reports. The passage also clarifies that if a dhimmi joins and then repents and returns, his dhimma is restored. Various rulings from al-Muhit, Fath al-Qadir, and others are cited to detail the consequences. The overall ruling is that the comparison with apostates applies in seven specific matters, with the exceptions noted.

المرتد (al-murtadd): A Muslim who leaves Islam; in this context, compared to a dhimmi who joins the enemy but with different rules for enslavement and compulsion.

Spending Public Funds from Non-Muslim Sources and Rules for Gifts
Commentary on spending jizyah, kharaj, etc. on public welfare and rules for accepting gifts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that jizyah, kharaj, and wealth taken without battle are spent on public welfare, and explain the condition for accepting gifts from war people.

Core Idea
Wealth from non-Muslims without battle is state property for Muslim welfare, not booty.

How does the Islamic state handle wealth received from non-Muslims without a battle? Jizyah, kharaj, and gifts from war people are not divided among the troops like war booty. Instead, they are allocated to public works such as repairing forts, building bridges, and supporting judges, scholars, and fighters—including their families. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the rule and also discusses a nuanced condition for accepting gifts: the imam must be confident that the giver believes Muslims fight for the sake of Allah, not for worldly gain. Additionally, an intra-Hanafi dispute is presented on whether family stipends continue after the recipient's death.

Guided Path
1

List the five categories mentioned: jizyah, kharaj, Taghlibi wealth, gifts from war people, and money taken without battle.
2
State the general ruling
These funds are not subject to khums nor divided among soldiers; they must be spent on public interests like forts, bridges, and salaries of judges, scholars, workers, and fighters.
Reason: it is Bayt al-Mal property coming without battle.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is implicit ijma: the universal practice of the early community shows these funds are for public welfare.
The dalil is zanni in both thubut and dalala but accepted.
4
Examine the condition for gifts
The imam only accepts a gift if he is predominantly sure that the giver believes Muslims fight for Allah's word and honor, not for worldly gain.
Additionally, gifts from those whose faith is hoped for by rejection are not accepted.
5

The apparent meaning of the matn implies families are given after the father's death, but some scholars limit it to his life. The mufta bihi is that stipends continue.
Abu Yusuf and al-Hawi support continuation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Kharaj and jizya are spent on those serving the Muslim community, including their dependents, with sufficiency as the standard.

The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies who receives from the treasury funds collected from kharaj and jizya. Beyond military and public works, it includes salaries for judges, muftis, teachers, and even students who dedicate themselves to learning. The key principle is that anyone who devotes their time to the service of Muslims is entitled to a sufficient stipend, which also extends to their dependents after death.

The passage mentions: salaries of governors, judges, muftis, muhtasibs, soldiers (mujahideen), teachers, students, and public works like mosques and canals.

This standard is applied to all recipients with discretion left to the imam.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Lahaq severs liability like death: the cutter may owe only half diya.

In Hanafi fiqh, the liability for cutting an apostate's limb depends on the victim's status at the time of the injury and any subsequent events. This lesson examines a nuanced scenario: a Muslim's hand is cut, then he apostatizes, joins dar al-harb (lahaq), later returns to Islam, and dies from the wound. Two conflicting positions within the Hanafi school arise, each grounded in a different view of how lahaq affects the chain of causation (siraayah).

Guided Path

Identify the scenario
The key scenario: the cut was made while the victim was a protected person (Muslim), then he apostatized, joined dar al-harb (lahaq), returned to Islam, and died from the cut.
Contrast with the case where the cut itself was made on an apostate (no liability at all).
2
State the two positions
According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, the cutter pays half diya. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two), the cutter pays full diya.
The text says 'عندهما الدية' and 'محمد: النصف'.

Analyze the proofs
Muhammad's view relies on the principle that lahaq is like death, which severs the causal chain (siraayah). The two argue that the original jinaya was on a protected person, and subsequent apostasy does not nullify full liability once the victim returns.
The qiyas: lahaq is analogous to death (اللحاق كالموت).
4
Discuss the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference in the amount of diya: half vs. full. A judge must choose which view to follow.
The text does not give a definitive tarjih.

A rebel who kills a just person is exempt from compensation only if he had both power (man'ah) and interpretation (ta'wil); inheritance depends on his belief.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and also an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the liability (daman) and inheritance when a rebel (baghi) kills a just person. The author first presents the scenario of a rebel entering with aman and being killed by a just person, where blood money (diya) is obligatory like any Muslim killing. Then the focus shifts to when the rebel kills the just person. Abu Yusuf prevents the rebel from inheriting from the victim because the rebel killed without right. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) distinguish based on the rebel's belief: if he claims to have been on the truth (i.e., his interpretation was valid at the time), he inherits; if he admits wrongdoing, he does not. The underlying principle is that liability (daman) is negated only when two conditions exist together: power (man'ah) and interpretation (ta'wil). If only one exists, liability applies. The passage then cites al-Hidayah and al-Bada'i for additional rulings: a rebel who kills a just person is not liable for financial compensation but commits a sin; however, if the act occurs before the rebellion gains strength or after defeat, liability is imposed. The issue is summarized in a four-case classification: both just, both rebels, a just killing a rebel, a rebel killing a just. The commentary then expands on the Hidayah reference, including the Shafi'i difference: the old view of Shafi'i (followed by Malik) imposes liability, while the new view agrees with the Hanafi position. The matn at the end restates the inheritance rule: if the rebel says he was on the truth, he inherits; if he says he was wrong, he does not.

Key Terms
باغ (baghi): A group of Muslims who rebel against the legitimate ruler with a valid interpretation (ta'wil) and possess power (man'ah).
منعة (man'ah): Power or strength that enables the rebel group to resist the state; one of the two conditions for exempting liability.
تأويل (ta'wil): A legitimate interpretation or justification that the rebel believes makes his actions permissible.
ضمان (daman): Financial liability or compensation for damage or injury.

Seeking Help from Polytheists Against Rebels: Condition of Dominance
Commentary on the prohibition of seeking polytheist help against rebels when polytheists are ascendant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on seeking help from polytheists against rebels and identify the key condition (dominance of polytheists' rule) that triggers the prohibition.

Core Idea
Seeking help from polytheists against rebels is prohibited when the polytheists' rule is dominant, to avoid humiliation and loss of Muslim authority.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling: it is not outright forbidden to cooperate with polytheists in all circumstances. The critical factor is whose authority prevails. We will explore how the dominance of polytheists' rule (hukm al-zahir) makes such assistance impermissible, a point clarified by later Hanafi authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is 'seeking help from polytheists against rebels (ahl al-baghi)'. The ruling is 'la yajuz' — not permissible, which implies prohibition (haram).
2
Locate the proof and condition
The proof is not a specific Qur'an or hadith but a rational reasoning (talil): the condition of dominance. The text states that when the polytheists' rule is apparent (hukmuhum al-zahir), seeking their help is prohibited.
The commentator notes variant manuscript readings on whether the condition refers to 'people of justice' or 'polytheists'.
3
Understand the rationale (talil)
The underlying cause (illa) is that involving polytheists when they are dominant subjects Muslims to their authority, leading to humiliation and weakening of Islamic sovereignty.
4
Analyze the expansion from al-Fath
The commentator quotes al-Fath al-Qadir: even if the just Muslims are dominant but have driven rebels into polytheist territory, fighting alongside polytheists is not allowed because the polytheists' rule is still apparent over those Muslims.
This shows the dominance condition is not about territory but about whose authority effectively governs the Muslims in that situation.
5
Apply the condition
Test scenarios: if polytheists are clearly ascendant → prohibition applies. If Muslims are ascendant and polytheists are merely auxiliary → ruling may differ (but the text emphasizes caution).
The mufta bihi position is that prohibition attaches when polytheists' rule is the apparent one.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn ruling that it is not permissible for Muslims to seek help from polytheists against rebels (baghi). The commentator notes that in most copies of the matn, the condition is 'if the rule of the people of justice is apparent', but in some copies 'the polytheists' is mentioned. He then quotes the full text from al-Fath: When the people of justice (the just Muslim faction) are dominant and they drive the rebels to the abode of polytheism, it is not permissible for them to fight the rebels alongside the polytheists, because doing so would subject the Muslims to the rule of the polytheists, which is humiliating. Similarly, seeking their help is prohibited when the polytheists' rule is apparent. The underlying reason is that if the polytheists are ascendant, the Muslims would be in a position of weakness, and engaging with them even against rebels could lead to Islamic interests being compromised. The ruling emphasizes that Muslims should not rely on non-Muslims in internal conflicts when it gives the latter dominance.

Key Terms
الاستعانة بأهل الشرك (al-isti'ana bi-ahl al-shirk): Seeking help or military assistance from polytheists against internal rebels.
أهل البغي (ahl al-baghi): Rebels against a legitimate Muslim ruler.
حكم الظاهر (hukm al-zahir): The apparent or dominant rule; here referring to which faction's authority is predominant.
