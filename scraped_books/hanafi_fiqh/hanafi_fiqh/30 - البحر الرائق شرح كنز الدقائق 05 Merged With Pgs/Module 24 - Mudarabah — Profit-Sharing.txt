Profit Distribution When a Partner Takes Mudaraba Capital
Detailed ruling on profit when a partner receives mudaraba capital

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios for profit distribution when a partner takes mudaraba capital and apply the rule to a given case.

Core Idea
When a partner takes mudaraba money, profit belongs to him alone unless the trade is within the partnership’s scope and the partner is absent.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the absolute statement in Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding a partner engaging in mudaraba. It explains that the ruling is detailed: the profit distribution depends on whether the trade is within the partnership's line of business and whether the partner is present or absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
A partner (in an 'inan partnership) takes money as mudaraba from a third party.
2
Recognize two key factors
Determine if the trade is within the partnership's line of business, and whether the partner is present or absent.
3
Apply scenario 1: Outside trade
If the trade is outside the partnership's line of trade, the profit belongs exclusively to the partner who took the mudaraba.
Because it did not fall under the partnership contract.
4
Apply scenario 2: Inside trade, partner present
If the trade is within the partnership's line and the partner is present, the profit also belongs exclusively to the partner who took the mudaraba.
5
Apply scenario 3: Inside trade, partner absent
If the trade is within the partnership's line (or absolute) and the partner is absent, the profit is shared: half goes to the absent partner, and half is divided between the mudarib and the capital provider.
This is from al-Muhit.

Among them is the case when one of them takes money as mudaraba; the profit is exclusively his. The answer in the book (matn) is absolute, but it is detailed: if he takes money as mudaraba to transact in something that is not within their trade, the profit is exclusively his because it did not fall under the partnership contract. Similarly, if he takes the money as mudaraba in the presence of his partner to transact in something that is within their trade, it is also exclusively his. But if he takes the money as mudaraba to transact in something that is within their trade or absolutely (mutlaqan) during the absence of his partner, then the profit is shared between them: half goes to his partner, and half is divided between the mudarib (the partner who took the money) and the owner of the mudaraba capital. Thus in al-Muhit. So his statement in the book 'yudarrib' means he gives money as mudaraba. As for his taking money as mudaraba, it is detailed as you have learned.

Commentary
This passage clarifies a point of potential confusion: the matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) says a partner may engage in mudaraba. The sharh distinguishes between the partner giving mudaraba (which is allowed) and the partner taking mudaraba (receiving capital as a mudarib). The ruling for taking mudaraba is detailed. If the partner takes mudaraba capital to trade in something outside the partnership's line of trade, the profit belongs solely to him. Similarly, if he takes it in the presence of his partner to trade in the partnership's line of trade, it is also his profit. However, if he takes it to trade in the partnership's line of trade or absolutely during the partner's absence, the profit is shared: half goes to the absent partner, and the remaining half is divided between the partner-turned-mudarib and the capital provider. This detailed ruling comes from al-Muhit.

Key Terms
مضاربة (muḍāraba): A contract where one party provides capital and another provides labor, with profit shared as agreed; loss is on the capital provider.
شريك (sharīk): Partner in a partnership (e.g., 'inan partnership).
رب المال (rabb al-māl): Owner of capital in a mudaraba; the investor.
محاذير (muḥādhīr): Precautions or restrictions.
