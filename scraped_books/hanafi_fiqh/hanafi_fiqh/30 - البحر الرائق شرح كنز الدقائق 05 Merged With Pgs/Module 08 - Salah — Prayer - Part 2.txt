Mosque Etiquette: Entry, Spots, Dust, and Crowding Rules
Compilation of mosque etiquette rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on entering mosques from endowed houses, claiming specific spots, handling dust, dealing with belongings, crowding, and dividing mosques.

Core Idea
Mosque etiquette preserves sanctity and community harmony.

This passage from Al‑Bahr al‑Raiq compiles several rulings related to mosque use and etiquette. Following the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) is emphasized, and the Hanafi school balances individual devotion (khushu') with communal needs. You will learn when it is permissible to enter through an endowed house, why claiming a permanent spot is disliked, how to handle mosque dust, and what to do when the mosque is crowded.

Guided Path
1
Identify rulings on entry and specific spots
Read the first part of the passage: the imam may enter from an endowed house because the Prophet did. Then note that a regular spot should not be claimed as exclusive; displacing someone else is not permitted.
The dispute with al‑Awza'i illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on khushu'.
2
Understand rulings on dust and belongings
Mosque dust has no sanctity when collected (e.g., swept into a pile) but retains sanctity when spread (e.g., on the floor). If one fears for belongings, tayammum is allowed before prayer.
This shows pragmatism within sacred space.
3
Examine rulings on crowding and mosque division
When crowded, a standing person may ask a sitting person to move, even if the latter is engaged in worship. Neighbourhood residents may divide the mosque with a wall and have separate imams; having one mu'azzin is permissible but a separate one is better.
These rules facilitate orderly worship.

Commentary
This passage covers etiquette and rulings regarding mosques. First, entering a mosque from an endowed house is permissible for the imam, following the Prophet's practice. Second, claiming a specific spot is disliked, and displacing someone from their regular spot is not permitted in the Hanafi school even if al-Awza'i allowed it. Third, mosque dust has no sanctity when collected but does when spread. Fourth, if someone has belongings in the mosque and fears for them, they may perform tayammum (even if water is available? Actually the text says he does tayammum and enters prayer, indicating a concession). Fifth, in crowded conditions, a standing worshipper may ask a sitting person to move to pray, even if that person is engaged in worship. Sixth, neighborhood residents may divide a mosque and have separate imams, which is permissible, though having a separate mu'azzin is preferable.

Rulings on Trade, Teaching, and Children in Mosques
Rulings on combining mosques, trade, teaching, and children in mosques

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on teaching children the Quran for a fee in a mosque and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two disciples regarding teaching in a shop in the mosque courtyard.

Core Idea
Mosques are for worship; paid teaching and disruptive trade are prohibited, but studying and unpaid teaching are allowed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a range of activities that may occur in mosques. While mosques are primarily for salah and dhikr, the Hanafi school provides detailed rulings on what is permitted and what is prohibited. Key issues include teaching children (with or without fee), disciplining children, using endowed mats for study, and even the validity of combining mosques. The hadith 'Keep your mosques away from your children and your insane' serves as a primary proof. Additionally, an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) arises regarding teaching in a shop within the mosque courtyard.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What specific activities are mentioned? State whether each is permissible, disliked, or prohibited.
Teaching children the Quran for a fee is prohibited; without fee is permissible. Disciplining for a fee is not permissible. Selling and poetry are not disliked unless excessive.
2
Locate the dalil
What hadith is cited? Note its transmission (ahad, zanni) and meaning. The imperative 'جنبوا' indicates a prohibition, but here it is interpreted as disapproval.
The hadith is about children and insane people, not directly about teaching, but it supports the sanctity of the mosque.
3
Understand the basis of prohibition
Why is teaching for a fee not allowed? Link to the purpose of the mosque: it is built for worship, not worldly transactions.
The text states 'لأنه ما بنى له' – it was not built for that.
4
Distinguish the ikhtilaf
Compare Abu Hanifa's view (not permissible) with that of the two disciples (permissible if no harm). What is the practical difference?
The two disciples focus on the absence of harm; Abu Hanifa emphasizes the original purpose.
5
Apply the rule
Given a scenario (e.g., a teacher wants to teach Quran in a mosque for free; a merchant wants to sell shoes in a mosque), determine the ruling using the principles from this lesson.
Use the hierarchy: purpose of mosque → hadith → ikhtilaf analysis.

Commentary
This section discusses activities in mosques. Changing the number of mosques (combining or splitting) is permissible for congregation but not for teaching or commemoration. Trade activities like selling, mending shoes, and poetry recitation are not inherently disliked unless they dominate. Studying in the mosque is permissible. However, teaching Quran to children for a fee is prohibited and sinful, though teaching without fee is allowed. A hadith instructs keeping children and insane away from mosques. Teaching in a shop within the mosque courtyard is prohibited according to Imam Abu Hanifa, but the two disciples permit it if there is no harm to the public. This illustrates a well-known intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Ruling on making part of a road into a mosque
Commentary on permissibility of using road for mosque expansion and vice versa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on converting part of a public road into a mosque and explain the condition of no harm and the proof from the Companions.

Core Idea
A road can be partially turned into a mosque if no harm is caused; custom permits a passage through a mosque.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq expands on a ruling from Kanz al-Daqā'iq: it is permissible to take part of a public road to expand a mosque, provided it does not harm road users. The same applies in reverse—making a passage through a mosque—based on the customary practice of the people of the cities. The commentator invokes the precedent of the Companions' expansion of the Masjid al-Ḥarām by acquiring adjacent land with compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two directions of the ruling
The matn states two scenarios: (1) taking part of a road to make a mosque, and (2) 'the opposite' which is making a passage (marr) through a mosque. Understand that 'opposite' does not mean converting a mosque into a road but allowing a thoroughfare through it.
2
State the condition for taking road space
The key condition is that the taking must not harm the road users (لا يضر بأصحاب الطريق). If harm occurs, it is not permissible.
3
Locate the dalīl from the Companions
The author cites the expansion of Masjid al-Ḥarām when the Companions took land under compulsion with payment. This serves as a precedent for expropriation for communal religious benefit.
The dalīl is a historical report with zannī thubūt and dalālah.
4
Note the custom-based exception for passages through mosques
For the reverse case, the permissibility is based on the customary practice (ta'āruf ahl al-amṣār) in congregational mosques. This allows even non-Muslims to pass through, except those in major ritual impurity.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) explaining the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq: 'If part of a road is made a mosque, it is valid; and the opposite likewise.' The commentator expands on both directions. First, taking part of a public road to expand a mosque is permissible if it does not harm the road users. This is based on the precedent of the Companions expanding the Masjid al-Haram by taking adjacent land with compensation, albeit under compulsion (a unique case). The opposite scenario: making a passage through a mosque (for public thoroughfare) is permissible due to customary practice in congregational mosques, even for non-Muslims, except those in a state of ritual impurity requiring ghusl (janabah, hayd, nifas). However, animals are not allowed inside. The text then cites al-Khaniyya: Building a mosque in a wide public road is allowed if it doesn't harm the road, based on the principle that both road and mosque belong to the Muslim community. But individuals cannot take road space for their private homes, even without harm. The people of the quarter may relocate the mosque door. Crucially, if expansion harms the road, it is not allowed. For adjacent private land, it can be expropriated with compensation, as in the Haram case, but this seems specific to the Haram. For waqf land dedicated to the mosque, addition requires the judge's permission. This commentary harmonizes the principles of public benefit, non-harm, and adherence to prophetic precedent.

Key Terms
تعارف أهل الأمصار (ta'āruf ahl al-amṣār): Customary practice of the people of the cities; used as a basis for allowing passages through mosques.
المسجد الحرام (al-masjid al-ḥarām): The Sacred Mosque in Mecca; its expansion by the Companions is cited as precedent for compulsory purchase of adjacent land.
كرهاً (karhan): Under compulsion; the land was taken against the owner's will but with compensation.
