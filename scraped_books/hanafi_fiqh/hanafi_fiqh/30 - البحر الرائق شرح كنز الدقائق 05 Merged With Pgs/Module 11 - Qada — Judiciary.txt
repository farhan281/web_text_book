Liability for Indirect Causation (Tasbibb): The Escaping Prisoner
Fatawa on liability when a thief's victim dies while escaping prison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on liability for diya when a prisoner dies escaping due to fear, and evaluate the two positions within the school.

Core Idea
Indirect causation with transgression (tasbibb ma'a ta'addi) can establish liability for both fine and blood money.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a fatawa concerning a thief whose transgression led to the death of an innocent prisoner. The prisoner, fearing torture, climbed the roof to escape and fell. The question is whether the thief is liable for both the fine (gharama) and blood money (diya). The Hanafi scholars disagreed, reflecting a nuanced understanding of causation and coercion.

Guided Path
1
State the facts of the case
Identify the key actors: thief, ruler, deceased prisoner. Note the chain: thief's theft → ruler's punishment → prisoner's fear → escape attempt → death.
The thief's transgression (ta'addi) is the starting point.
2
Identify the legal issue
The core question is whether the thief is liable for diya in addition to the gharama (fine) that the heirs already paid to the ruler.
This is about the scope of liability for indirect causation.
3
Follow the first opinion (no diya)
Some scholars argue that the prisoner climbed the roof by his own choice (ikhtiyar), breaking the causal link for diya. Only the fine remains.
The key concept here is free choice (ikhtiyar) severing causation.
4
Follow the second opinion (diya included)
Other scholars argue that the prisoner was effectively compelled (mukrah) due to fear of torture. Thus, the causal link remains intact, and the thief is liable for both.
This relies on a broader understanding of coercion (ikrah) – even without direct force.
5
Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference directly affects the heirs: they receive only the gharama under the first opinion, but both gharama and diya under the second.
This shows how ijtihad within a school leads to different outcomes.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling (fatawa) from the Hanafi school regarding a complex case of highway robbery. A thief stole, and the ruler punished him by beating and imprisonment. Another prisoner, fearing torture, climbed the roof to escape and died. Later, the theft was discovered to have been committed by a different man. The question: are the heirs of the deceased entitled to claim blood money (diya) and the fine (gharama) from the actual thief? The answer given is yes, because the thief's transgression (ta'addi) in causing the imprisonment and beating led to the death indirectly (tasbibb). However, there is a disagreement: one opinion says this ruling is correct only for the fine, not for blood money, because the deceased chose to climb. The other opinion says it applies to both, because he was effectively coerced due to fear. This ikhtilaf reflects the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to causation in liability.

Key Terms
تسبيب (tasbibb): Causation: indirect involvement where the act leads to harm through a chain of events.
سعاية (sa'ayah): Originally a reference to the issue of an informer (sa'i) who causes loss; here analogized for the fine.
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion; compelling someone to act against their will under threat.

Definition and Initial Ruling on the Missing Person (Mafqud)
Defining the mafqud and the judge's duty to appoint a nazir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the mafqud according to Hanafi law, distinguish its immediate and eventual rulings, and explain the judge's duty to appoint a guardian for his property.

Core Idea
A missing person is one whose life and death are unknown — the judge must protect his property like a minor's.

This lesson opens the chapter on the missing person (mafqud). We begin with the precise legal definition — not simply someone whose location is unknown, but one whose status of life or death is uncertain. This distinction has profound consequences: for himself, he is treated as alive; for others, as dead. The judge is obligated to appoint a guardian (nazir) to preserve his wealth, akin to the guardianship for a minor or insane person. We will also explore the nuances of an existing agent's authority and the limits of the guardian's powers.

Guided Path
1
Define the mafqud
Identify the linguistic root (fa-qaf-dal) and the legal criterion: ignorance of life and death, not just location. Note the example of the Muslim captive whose location is known but status is unknown.
The text emphasizes the criterion is 'al-jahl bi-hayatihi wa mawtihi' not 'al-jahl bi-makanihi'.
2
Distinguish the two categories of rulings
Separate the immediate ruling (hukm fi al-hal) from the eventual ruling (hukm fi al-ma'al). For himself: alive (property not inherited, wives not remarried). For others: dead (no inheritance from him, property not divided).
The dual status is unique to the mafqud.
3
Understand the judge's obligation
Explain why the judge must appoint a nazir: the mafqud is unable to manage his affairs, similar to a minor or insane person.
The text says 'فينصب القاضي من يأخذ حقه ويحفظ ماله ويقوم عليه'.
4
Examine the role of an existing agent
If the mafqud had a wakil for preservation, he is not dismissed. However, he cannot perform acts beyond preservation (e.g., renovating a house) without the judge's permission.
Cited from al-Walwaljiyyah: the wakil preserves but cannot contract new obligations.
5
Define the nazir's powers
The nazir can collect revenues and debts acknowledged by debtors, and litigate for obligations arising from contracts. This falls under preservation.
The text says 'يقبض غلاته والديون المقر بها لأنه من باب الحفظ'.

Commentary
This lesson begins the Book of the Missing Person (Kitab al-Mafqud). It first provides a linguistic definition: the root fa-qaf-dal indicates absence, so a mafqud is a person whose location is unknown. The text immediately clarifies a crucial point: the legal definition is not simply unknown location, but unknown life or death. Thus, a captured Muslim in enemy territory whose location is known but whose life or death is uncertain is still a mafqud. The author then summarizes two categories of rulings: immediate (hukm fi al-hal) and eventual (hukm fi al-ma'al). In the immediate sense, the missing person is considered alive for himself (his property is not inherited from him, his wives remain married) but dead for others (no one inherits from him, his property is not divided). The eventual ruling is that after a certain period, the judge may declare him dead. The text then discusses the judge's duty to appoint a guardian (nazir) to take his rights, preserve his wealth, and manage his affairs, analogous to the case of a minor or an insane person. An important nuance: if the missing person had already appointed an agent (wakil) before disappearing, that agent is not automatically dismissed and can continue to preserve the property, but he cannot enter new contracts like renovating a house without judge's permission. The text cites al-Walwaljiyyah and al-Tajnis for this point. Finally, it notes that the appointed guardian can collect revenues and debts acknowledged by debtors, as this falls under preservation. The guardian may also litigate regarding obligations arising from contracts. The passage from al-Nahr clarifies that if the existing agent lacks the power to collect debts, the judge must appoint a new agent. This establishes the principle that the mafqud's property must be protected by a court-appointed guardian when existing arrangements are insufficient.

Key Terms
مفقود (mafqud): A person who is missing: one whose death or life is unknown, regardless of whether his location is known or unknown.
وكيل (wakil): An agent appointed to manage someone's affairs; in the context of a missing person, the wakil remains in place but his powers may be limited to preservation without judicial permission.
ناظر (nazir): A guardian or supervisor appointed by the judge to take care of the missing person's property and rights.
