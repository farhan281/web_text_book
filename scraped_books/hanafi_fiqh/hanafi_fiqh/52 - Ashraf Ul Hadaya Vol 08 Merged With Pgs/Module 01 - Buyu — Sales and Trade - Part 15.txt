Ratification After Subject Matter is Destroyed in a Fasid Sale
Ruling on owner's ratification after slave's death in a fasid sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid ratification (ijazah) in a fasid sale and distinguish it from a valid sale by identifying the role of ownership and substitute.

Core Idea
Ratification requires the subject matter to exist; destruction before ratification prevents cure.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school regarding a fasid (defective) sale where a usurper sells someone else's property. If the buyer from the usurper does not resell, but the item is destroyed before the owner ratifies, does the owner's later approval validate the sale? The answer is no, and the reason reveals a key principle about the function of ratification and the nature of ownership in fasid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the owner's ratification (ijazah) of a fasid sale after the subject matter (slave) has died or been killed. The ruling is that such ratification does not validate the sale.
The sale itself is fasid because it involved a usurper selling without permission.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a rational inference (qiyas): ratification requires the existence of the subject matter (qiyam al-ma'qud 'alayh). Since the slave is dead, the condition is unfulfilled.
The dalil is aqli (rational) and considered zanni in transmission but sound in derivation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The principle that ijazah is a form of consent that can only apply to an existing object. Consent to something that no longer exists is meaningless. Therefore, the condition is essential.
This is contrasted with the possibility of a substitute (badal) in a valid sale, where ownership is firmly established.
4
Distinguish from a valid sale
In a valid sale (bay' sahih), the buyer's ownership is firmly established (milk thabit). If the slave dies before delivery, the buyer can claim the price (qimah) from the seller, and the sale remains valid because the substitute stands in place of the subject matter.
In a fasid sale, the buyer's ownership is suspended (milk mawquf), so no substitute can be imposed to preserve the contract.
5
Practical implication
If the subject matter is destroyed before ratification, the fasid sale cannot become valid. The buyer is liable to return the value (qimah) to the owner as compensation for usurpation, not as a price of a valid sale.
The owner's ratification cannot cure the defect after destruction.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling within the chapter on sales (buyu'), specifically addressing a fasid (defective) sale where a usurper (ghasib) sells someone else's slave without permission. The ruling states that if the buyer from the usurper does not resell the slave, but the slave dies or is killed while in the buyer's possession, and later the owner ratifies the sale, the ratification is ineffective and the sale does not become valid. The proof provided is that ratification (ijazah) requires the subject matter (al-ma'qud 'alayh) to exist at the time of ratification. Since the slave has perished, he no longer exists. In a fasid sale, the buyer's ownership is suspended (mawkuf) and cannot be compensated by a substitute (badal) because the buyer does not have a firm ownership claim against which a substitute could be imposed. In contrast, in a valid sale (bay' sahih), the buyer's ownership is established, so if the slave dies before delivery, the buyer can claim the price (qimah) from the seller, and the sale remains valid because the substitute stands in place of the subject matter. The commentary emphasizes this distinction: in the fasid sale, since ownership is suspended, no substitute can be compelled, so the subject matter is completely lost, invalidating ratification. The author then contrasts with a valid sale where the buyer may choose to take the substitute or not, but the sale remains intact.

Key Terms
اجازة (ijazah): Ratification or permission given after the contract; here specifically the owner's subsequent approval of a sale made without his permission.
معقود عليه (al-ma'qud 'alayh): The subject matter of the contract, here the slave that was sold.
ملك موقوف (milk mawquf): Suspended ownership; the buyer's ownership in a fasid sale is not fully established until the owner ratifies and the contract becomes valid.

Contradiction in Claim: Rejection of Buyer's Evidence
Ruling that buyer's witness about seller's admission is rejected due to inconsistency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a buyer's own action can create legal contradiction (tanaqud) and apply it to determine when his witnesses are rejected.

Core Idea
A buyer who purchases an item cannot later claim the sale was invalid without contradicting his own act.

This passage from the Hidayah deals with a fundamental principle of legal evidence: a party cannot simultaneously affirm and deny the validity of a transaction. When a buyer knowingly purchases a slave from an unauthorized seller, his act of buying is considered an implicit admission that the sale was valid. If he later brings witnesses to prove the seller lacked authority, his claim is contradictory and his witnesses are rejected. This lesson breaks down the scenario and the reasoning behind the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
You are given a case: A person sells another's slave without permission. The buyer wants to return the sale and brings witnesses that either the seller or the owner admitted the seller had no command. First, confirm the parties: seller (bai'), buyer (mushtari), and owner (rabb al-'abd).
The buyer's goal is to rescind (radd) the sale.
2
Recognize the Buyer's Contradictory Position
The buyer's claim that the sale is invalid contradicts his own prior action of purchasing. In Hanafi law, 'proceeding to purchase is an admission of validity' (al-iqdam 'ala al-shira' iqrarun minhu bi-sihhatihi). This implicit admission (iqrar) creates a legal inconsistency (tanaqud) when the buyer later alleges invalidity.
The contradiction is between the buyer's conduct and his later statement.
3
Understand the Ruling on Evidence
Because the buyer's claim is self-contradictory, his da'wa (legal claim) is invalid. Since bayyinah (witnesses) are meant to support a valid claim, they cannot stand when the claim itself is flawed. Therefore the buyer's witnesses are not accepted (la tuqbal bayyinah).
The rejection is procedural, not based on witness credibility.
4
Apply the Principle to Similar Cases
The same principle applies in other contexts: any party who acts inconsistently with their later claim will have their evidence rejected. For example, a seller who delivers goods cannot later claim the contract was void for lack of capacity, if he himself performed.
This ensures legal certainty and prevents abuse of process.

Seller's Judicial Admission and Buyer's Demand: Voidal Sale Conditions
Ruling on seller's judicial admission and buyer's demand voiding the sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a seller's judicial admission voids a sale and distinguish the variant ruling from al-Ziyadat.

Core Idea
A seller's admission alone does not void the sale; the buyer's demand creates the binding agreement.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial nuance in Hanafi sale law: while a seller's admission that he sold without permission is binding against himself, it does not automatically annul the contract. The school requires the buyer's demand to establish mutual consent for voiding. We also explore a variant ruling where the buyer's witness is accepted if the slave is in the owner's possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the seller's judicial admission of selling without permission. The ruling is that the sale becomes batil (void) only when the buyer demands it.
The buyer's demand is a condition, not automatic.
2
Locate the dalil
The reasoning is based on the principle that contradiction (tanaqud) does not invalidate an admission (iqrar). The buyer's cooperation establishes agreement (ittifaq) between parties.
Iqrar is hujjah qasirah; it binds only the one who admits.
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Note the variant in al-Ziyadat: if the buyer confirms the owner's claim and brings witnesses to the seller's admission, the witness is accepted. The distinction (farq) is that in the main case, the slave is in the buyer's hand; in the variant, it is in the owner's hand.
Possession affects procedural acceptance of evidence.
4
Apply the condition for returning the price
The buyer can reclaim the price only if the goods ('ayn) are not intact (salim) – meaning he has not consumed or disposed of them.
This protects the seller from double loss.

Contradiction in Claim Invalidates Buyer's Evidence
Ruling on buyer's contradictory claim nullifying his evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tanāquḍ (contradiction) to determine when a claimant's evidence is rejected due to inconsistency between their actions and claims.

Core Idea
A buyer's act of purchasing is an implicit acknowledgment that the sale is valid, so later denying its validity creates a contradiction that bars his evidence.

In Hanafi fiqh, a person's actions can carry legal weight as an implicit acknowledgment (iqrār bil-fi‘l). This lesson explores a scenario where a buyer purchases a slave sold without the owner's permission, then seeks to nullify the sale. The buyer's own act of buying contradicts his later claim, and the Hanafi school rules that his evidence (bayyinah) is not accepted due to this tanāquḍ. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly evaluating the validity of claims in sales disputes.

2
2. Recognize the Implicit Acknowledgment
The buyer's action of voluntarily purchasing the slave is considered an implicit acknowledgment (iqrār bil-fi‘l) that the sale was valid. By buying, he effectively accepted the contract.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of a person who sells another's house without permission when the buyer incorporates it into his own building. The main issue is whether the unauthorized seller is liable to the owner for the value of the house. Abu Hanifa and the latter opinion of Abu Yusuf hold that the seller is not liable because ghasb (usurpation) of immovable property (al-‘aqār) is not recognized in the sense that it can be taken into possession by the usurper in a way that makes him liable for its value; rather, the property is considered to remain in the owner's possession legally. Muhammad and the earlier view of Abu Yusuf hold that ghasb of immovable property is possible, so the seller (as a usurper) is liable. The commentary notes that this topic is covered in detail in the chapter on ghasb. The ruling here is specific to the case where the buyer has taken physical possession and altered the property.

When Can the Buyer Reclaim the Price?
Distinguishing two textual reports on buyer's right of price recovery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two scenarios from Jami' al-Saghir and al-Ziyadat and state the ruling on the buyer's right to reclaim the price based on possession of the sold item.

Core Idea
Possession determines safety: the buyer can reclaim the price only if the sold item is not in his possession.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hadaya examines a subtle difference between two reports from Imam Muhammad's works. In both, a third party claims ownership of a sold slave, and the buyer confirms. The critical variable is where the slave is at the time of the claim. Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to apply classical Hanafi principles to similar real-world disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two texts
Recognize Jami' al-Saghir and al-Ziyadat as source texts from Zahir al-Riwayah.
Both are authored by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
State the common scenario
Both involve a buyer who accepted a third-party claim of ownership and proved the seller's prior confession.
The buyer's confirmation is not contradictory in the case.
3
Locate the differentiating factor
The slave is in the buyer's possession in Jami' al-Saghir, but in the claimant's possession in al-Ziyadat.
Possession is the key variable.
4
Apply the safety principle
If the slave is with the buyer, the sale is safe (salāmat); if with the claimant, the sale is not safe.
Safety means the buyer has effective control.
5
Derive the ruling
Safe sale: no reclaim. Unsafe sale: buyer can reclaim the price from the seller.
The condition for reclaim (rujūʿ bi al-thaman) is the absence of safety.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with a nuanced distinction between two similar cases from classic Hanafi texts. It addresses a scenario where a slave is sold, and a third party claims ownership. The buyer confirms the claimant, proving the seller had previously confessed that the slave belonged to the claimant. The buyer then demands his price back. This distinction hinges on the principle that the right to return the price (ruju' bi al-thaman) requires that the sold item not remain with the buyer safely.
