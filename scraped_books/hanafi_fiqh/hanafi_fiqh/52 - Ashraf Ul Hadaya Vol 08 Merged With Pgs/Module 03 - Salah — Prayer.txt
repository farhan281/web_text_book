Deferred Payment Sales: Ruling and Condition of Known Term
Establishes permissibility of deferred payment sales and condition of known term

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on deferred payment sales and explain why the term must be known.

Core Idea
Deferred payment sales are permissible, but the term must be known or the sale is invalid.

In this passage, the author establishes the basic permissibility of both spot and credit sales in the Hanafi school. The proof is drawn from the Quranic verse that permits trade absolutely and from the Prophet's own practice of buying on credit with a specified term. However, a crucial condition is highlighted: if the deferment period is unknown, the sale becomes invalid because it leads to dispute. Let's unpack the reasoning behind this condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
The author states that both spot sale (naqd) and deferred payment sale (nasi'ah) are permissible. First, pinpoint this general ruling.
2
Locate the primary proof (Quran)
The Quranic verse 'Allah has permitted trade' is cited. Understand how this absolute permission covers both types of sale.
The Hanafi principle: an absolute command implies generality unless specified otherwise.
3
Locate the secondary proof (Sunnah)
The hadith of the Prophet buying grain on credit with a known term and pawning his armor is used as a practical example.
This hadith shows that deferred payment sales were practiced and approved.
4
Identify the condition for validity
The passage emphasizes that the term must be known. If it is unknown, the sale is fasid (invalid).
Why? Because ignorance of the term leads to dispute, which invalidates the contract.
5
Explain the rationale
The author explains that an unknown term causes conflict over when payment is due, leading to dispute. Since dispute hinders the purpose of the contract, the sale is invalid.
This is based on the principle that gharar (uncertainty) that leads to dispute invalidates a sale.

Now the ruling is that spot sale and deferred payment sale are both permissible, provided that the term of deferred payment is known. If the term of deferred payment is unknown, the sale is invalid. The proof is that Allah Almighty said: 'Allah has permitted trade' absolutely, meaning trade is absolutely lawful whether spot or deferred. Both are included under this verse. The second proof is the hadith of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him. The hadith is that the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, bought some grain on credit with a specified term from a Jew named Abu Hham, and he pawned his armor with him. In Bukhari's hadith, the wording is 'thirty sa' of barley,' meaning he bought thirty sa' of barley. From the mentioned verse and narration, it is understood that just as spot sale is permissible, similarly deferred payment is also permissible. However, it is necessary that the period and term of deferred payment be known, because ignorance of the term prevents delivery of the price, whereas delivery of the price is obligatory due to the contract of sale. It prevents delivery because the seller will demand the price in a short period, and the buyer will try to pay after a long period, so a dispute will arise. Since ignorance that leads to dispute is a hindrance to the validity of the sale, therefore ignorance of the term will prevent the sale from being valid.

Commentary
This is a ruling passage with proof. The author states the Hanafi position that both spot and deferred payment sales are fundamentally permissible. The proof is twofold: first, the Quranic verse (2:275) which permits trade absolutely, and second, the practice of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wasallam) who bought grain on credit from a Jew with a known term, even pawning his armor. However, a crucial condition is that the term of deferment must be specified and known. If the term is unknown, the sale becomes invalid (fasid) because ignorance of the term leads to potential dispute between buyer and seller regarding the timing of payment. Dispute prevents the validity of the contract. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, as found in al-Hidayah and its commentaries.

Key Terms
بیع نقد (bay' naqd): A sale where the price is paid immediately at the time of contract.
بیع ادھار (bay' nasi'ah): A sale where payment of the price is deferred to a future date.
میعاد (mi'ad): The specified term or period for deferred payment.

Ruling on Selling After Friday Adhan
States ruling and proof for selling after Friday adhan being makruh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling after the Friday adhan and explain why it is makruh tanzihi using the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Selling after Friday adhan is makruh, not haram, because it may disrupt the wajib act of proceeding to prayer.

In this passage, Imam al-Quduri states that selling at the time of the Friday adhan is makruh. The proof is the Quranic command to leave off trade when the call is made. But the ruling is nuanced: the sale itself is valid, but the action is disliked because it can interfere with the obligatory duty of sa'y (hastening) to the mosque. This lesson will unpack the evidence and the reasoning behind the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What specifically is being ruled upon? The act of selling after the Friday adhan. The ruling: makruh (disliked).
The sale itself is valid, but the act is disliked.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the Quranic verse 62:9, which commands believers to leave off selling when the call is made for Friday prayer.
The verse uses the imperative 'wa dharu al-bay' (and leave off selling).
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative indicates prohibition, but the Hanafi school understands it as a prohibition for the sake of avoiding disruption of the wajib sa'y, not as a nullification of the contract.
Thus, the sale is not invalid; the act is makruh tanzihi.
4
Note the exception
If a person conducts a transaction while walking to the mosque, it is not makruh because it does not disrupt the sa'y.
The key is whether the act interferes with proceeding to prayer.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi relied-upon position is that the sale is valid but makruh. Some Shafi'i scholars consider the sale invalid based on a stricter interpretation of the prohibition.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf with practical consequences.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the legal status of sale transactions after the Friday adhan. The author, Quduri, states that such a sale is makruh (disliked). The primary proof is the Quranic verse in Surah al-Jumu'ah: 'O you who believe, when the call is made for prayer on Friday, hasten to the remembrance of Allah and leave off buying and selling.' The commentator explains that the reason for the karahah is not that the sale itself is inherently defective, but rather that engaging in it may disrupt the obligatory act of sa'y (proceeding) to the Friday prayer. If the transaction is conducted while already en route to the mosque, it does not cause disruption and is thus not problematic. The passage also clarifies that the adhan referred to is the first adhan (the one before the khutbah) after the time of zawal (post-noon). This ruling is based on the Quranic command to abandon trade at the time of adhan, interpreted as a prohibition that makes the act makruh tanzihi (disliked but not invalid) because the sale itself remains valid.

Key Terms
اذان (adhan): The call to prayer. Here it specifically refers to the first adhan for Friday prayer after zawal.
سعی الی الجمعہ (sa'y ila al-jumu'ah): The act of proceeding to the Friday prayer, considered wajib in the Hanafi school.
مکروہ (makruh): Disliked but not prohibited; the act is valid but sinful if done without need.
