Testimony of Buyer Rejected Due to Contradiction
Ruling on buyer's contradictory testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling regarding the acceptance of a buyer's testimony when he claims the sale was unauthorized, and explain the legal principle of tanāquḍ (contradiction).

Core Idea
A buyer who purchases an item cannot later claim it was sold without authorization—his purchase is an implicit admission of validity, contradicting his testimony.

In this passage, Imam al-Marghinani explains a key evidentiary rule: when a buyer tries to rescind a sale by proving the seller had no authority, his own act of purchasing works against him. The Hanafi school holds that the buyer's testimony is inadmissible because his action and his claim are contradictory. Understanding this principle clarifies how consistency is required for legal claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case involves a sale of another's property without permission. The buyer later wants to reject the sale based on testimony from the seller or owner that no authorization was given.
2
Locate the ruling
The buyer's testimony (bayyinah) is not accepted (ghayr maqbul).
3
Follow the reasoning
The reason is tanāquḍ (contradiction): by entering into the purchase, the buyer implicitly admitted the sale's validity. Now he wants to prove it was invalid, which contradicts his earlier action.
The principle: al-iqdām 'alā al-shirā' iqrār minhu bi-ṣiḥḥatih (proceeding with the purchase is an admission of its validity)
4
Understand the legal effect
The buyer's evidence is rendered inadmissible. He cannot use his own contradictory statements or actions to undo the contract.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on evidence in sales. The scenario: A person sells a slave belonging to someone else without the owner's permission. The buyer (who purchased the slave) later wants to rescind the sale based on testimony from the seller or owner that no authorization was given. The ruling is that the buyer's evidence (bayyinah) is not accepted. The reason is a legal contradiction (tanāquḍ): by entering into the purchase, the buyer implicitly affirmed the validity of the sale. Now he is trying to prove that the sale was unauthorized, which contradicts his earlier action. In Hanafi law, a person cannot both act as if a contract is valid and then claim it is invalid based on the same facts. The principle is that 'al-iqdām 'alā al-shirā' iqrār minhu bi-ṣiḥḥatih' (proceeding with the purchase is an admission of its validity). Therefore, the testimony (which is meant to clarify facts) cannot override the buyer's own prior admission. This ruling highlights the importance of consistency in legal claims.

Key Terms
بینہ (bayyinah): Legal proof or testimony presented in court; here specifically, witnesses that the buyer brings.
تناقض (tanāquḍ): Contradiction in the claimant's own statement or action; here, the buyer's act of purchasing contradicts his claim that the sale was unauthorized.
اقرار (iqrār): Acknowledgment or admission; here, the buyer's purchase is considered an implicit admission of the sale's validity.
