Inheritance of Khiyar al-Ta'yin
Explains that khiyar al-ta'yin is not inherited but granted anew to heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why khiyar al-ta'yin is not inherited and identify the difference between the deceased's and the heir's right.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-ta'yin does not pass by inheritance; it is voided upon death and a fresh, unlimited right arises for the heir.

You learned previously that options can be inherited in some cases. This passage addresses the specific case of khiyar al-ta'yin (the option to specify). It clarifies that this option is not transferable by inheritance; instead, a new right is created due to the mixing of ownerships. This lesson will walk you through the reasoning and the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
The passage states that khiyar al-ta'yin does NOT transfer by inheritance. This is the key distinction from other options.
2
Understand the legal reason
When the buyer dies without specifying, the heir's ownership of the unsold item mixes with the seller's ownership. This necessitates a fresh right to specify.
3
Compare the periods
The deceased's khiyar had a fixed period (three days per Abu Hanifa, known period per Sahibayn). The heir's new right has no fixed period. Why? Because it is a new right, not an inheritance.
4
Note the intra-madhhab difference
Abu Hanifa holds three days; Sahibayn say a known period. This difference pertains to the original right, not the heir's.

Ruling on Selling Hide of a Dead Animal Before and After Tanning
Clarifies prohibition before tanning and permissibility after tanning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling the hide of a dead animal before and after tanning and explain the proof from hadith.

Core Idea
Selling untanned dead animal hide is haram; after tanning it becomes pure and permissible to sell.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common practical question: Is it permissible to sell the hide of an animal that died without slaughter? The author clarifies that the answer depends on whether the hide has been tanned. Before tanning, the hide is impure and legally unusable, so its sale is prohibited. After tanning, the hide becomes pure, and both selling and benefiting from it become permissible. The ruling is based on a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the legal principle that impurity prevents beneficial use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Two States
Identify the act being ruled upon: selling the hide of a dead animal (maytah). Consider two distinct states: the hide before tanning and after tanning.
The term 'ihab' in the hadith refers specifically to untanned hide.
2
State the Ruling for Each State
Before tanning: sale is haram (impermissible). After tanning: sale is jaiz (permissible).
Impermissibility is due to impurity and lack of lawful benefit.
3
Locate the Dalil
The proof is the Prophet's statement: 'La tanta'u min al-maytati bi-ihab' (Do not benefit from the dead animal's untanned hide). This is a hadith ahad.
The hadith is found in various collections; it is zanni in both thubut and dalala.
4
Apply the Wajh al-Dalalah
The command 'do not benefit' indicates prohibition of all forms of benefit, including sale. The term 'ihab' limits the prohibition to untanned hide. Thus, after tanning, the prohibition is lifted.
Tanning removes the impurity (najasah), making the hide usable.

Selling Bones and Ivory: The Status of Dead Animal Parts and the Elephant
Fatwa on maytah parts and elephant dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling solid parts of a dead animal and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the elephant's legal status, applying the hadith of ivory bangles as evidence.

Core Idea
Solid parts of a dead animal are pure and saleable; the elephant is not najis al-ayn, so its tusks may be sold.

This passage tackles two related fiqh issues: the permissibility of selling parts of a dead animal (maytah) and the legal classification of the elephant. The Hanafi school generally permits selling bones, wool, horns, and hair of a dead animal because these parts lack life and remain pure. However, a dispute arises regarding the elephant: is it like a pig (najis al-ayn) or like a predatory animal? The majority view, supported by a hadith, treats it as a predator, making its tusks pure and tradeable. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school weighs textual evidence versus analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Find the explicit ruling: 'There is no objection to selling bones, sinews, wool, horns, hair...' Note the reason given: 'because they are pure; death does not affect them due to lack of life.' Recognize this as a permissive ruling (mubah).
The ruling applies to any dead animal that is not najis al-ayn.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Read the text: 'And the elephant, according to Muhammad, is like the pig, najis al-ayn, while according to the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), it is in the category of predatory animals.' Identify the two positions and note that this is a dispute within the Hanafi school itself.
Imam Muhammad's view is the minority; the two shaykhs' view is the relied-upon position.
3
Analyze the evidence for the majority view
The text cites a hadith: 'The Prophet purchased ivory bangles for Fatimah.' Explain how this serves as evidence that elephant tusks are pure. If the elephant were najis al-ayn, its ivory would be impure and could not be used or sold.
The hadith is found in Fath al-Qadir; it is a strong naqli dalil.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
Based on the hadith and the fact that riding and loading an elephant is permissible, the majority view (elephant like predator) is preferred. Consequently, selling elephant tusks (ivory) and bones is permissible, and they are considered pure.
According to Imam Muhammad, nothing from the elephant is pure, so even its bones cannot be sold.
5
Apply the rule to other cases
Extend the general principle: any solid part from a dead animal that is not najis al-ayn (pig, dog) is permissible to sell. This includes not only bones and wool but also other similar parts not explicitly mentioned, as long as they are not liquid or flowing blood.
The illa is that these parts lack life and are therefore not rendered impure by death.
