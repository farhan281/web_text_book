The Validity of Excluding the Foetus in a Will (Wasiyya)
Ruling on excluding foetus from a will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why excluding the foetus from a will is valid in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish between valid and invalid exclusions based on the nature of the excluded item.

Core Idea
A will is not commutative, so a corrupt condition does not invalidate it; excluding the foetus is valid because inheritance applies to specific entities.

We have seen that in commutative contracts like sale, a condition that excludes a part of the subject matter invalidates the contract. However, what about a will? The Hanafi school treats a will as a non-commutative transfer akin to inheritance. This lesson explores a key ruling: when a testator wills a slave-girl but excludes her unborn foetus, both the will and the exclusion are valid. Why? Because the will is not a commutative contract, and the foetus is a specific entity (ayn) to which inheritance attaches.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that a will (wasiyya) is a non-commutative (tabarru') contract, unlike sale (ba'y). This classification determines how conditions are treated.
Commutative contracts are invalidated by corrupt conditions; non-commutative are not.
2
Apply the rule on corrupt conditions
Since wasiyya is not commutative, a condition that would corrupt a sale (e.g., excluding part of the subject matter) does not invalidate the will. The will remains valid.
3
Analyze the exclusion's effect
The exclusion of the foetus is a condition. Because the will is non-commutative, the condition does not invalidate it. The exclusion itself is also valid, meaning the foetus does not pass to the legatee.
4
Justify the exclusion's validity
The will is akin to inheritance (both take effect after death). Inheritance applies to specific entities (a'yan), and the foetus is a specific entity. Therefore, the foetus goes to the heirs by inheritance, making the exclusion valid.
Service (khadimah) is not a specific entity, so inheritance does not apply; thus, excluding service is invalid.
5
Distinguish invalid exclusions
Contrast with excluding the slave-girl's service. Service is not a specific entity (it is a usufruct), so inheritance does not apply. Therefore, the exclusion of service is invalid, and the legatee gets the service rights.
This distinction hinges on whether the excluded item is an 'ayn (specific entity) or a manfa'ah (usufruct).

And the third category: where both the contract and the exclusion are valid is the will (wasiyya). For example, a person in his terminal illness makes a will of his slave-girl to someone, and he excludes her foetus, i.e., he does not will the foetus. Then excluding the foetus will not invalidate the will, and the exclusion will also be valid. Consequently, as a result of the will, the slave-girl will belong to the legatee, and the foetus, i.e., the child to be born, will be inheritance for the heirs. The will is not invalid because it is not a commutative contract, and when it is not a commutative contract, it will not be invalidated by a corrupt condition. The exclusion is valid because the will is the sister or counterpart of inheritance, as both take effect after death, and inheritance applies to what is in the womb, i.e., the foetus, because the foetus is a specific entity (ʿayn), and inheritance applies to specific entities. Therefore, inheritance will apply to the foetus. In contrast, if he makes a will of the slave-girl to someone and excludes her service, this exclusion is not valid because service is not a specific entity, so inheritance does not apply to it. And when inheritance does not apply, the right to the slave-girl's service will belong to the legatee, not the heirs.

Commentary
This passage presents the third category, specifically regarding wills (wasiyya). Here, both the contract (the will) and the exclusion (of the foetus) are valid. The key distinction is that a will is not a commutative contract (muʿāwaḍah) but a gratuitous transfer that takes effect after death. Therefore, it is not invalidated by a corrupt condition; rather, the condition stands because will is akin to inheritance, which applies to specific entities (aʿyān), including the foetus. Consequently, excluding the foetus from a will is valid, and the foetus goes to the heirs by inheritance. However, if one excludes the service (khadimah) of the slave-girl, that exclusion is invalid because service is not a specific entity and inheritance does not apply to it; thus, the legatee gets the service rights.

Key Terms
وصیت (wasiyya): A will or bequest that takes effect after death, up to one-third of the estate without heirs' consent.
عین شئی (ʿayn shayʾ): A specific, tangible entity that can be subject to ownership and inheritance.

The Validity of Excepting Pregnancy in a Bequest
Ruling on exception in bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on excepting a corporeal thing (like an embryo) from a bequest and distinguish it from excepting a non-corporeal thing (like service).

Core Idea
Excepting an embryo from a bequest is valid because bequest resembles inheritance, which applies to corporeal things.

This lesson examines a ruling from the Hidayah on the effect of an exception (istithna') in a bequest (wasiyyah). The author categorizes bequest as the third type where both the contract and the exception are valid. Using the example of a slave-girl, we learn that excluding her pregnancy does not invalidate the bequest; the child goes to the heirs. Why? Because bequest is non-commutative and akin to inheritance, which applies to tangible assets. In contrast, excluding a service is invalid, as service is not a corporeal thing subject to inheritance. By the end, you will be able to apply this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the exception
The act is a bequest of a slave-girl with an exception of her pregnancy. Note that the exception is a corporeal thing (the embryo).
2
State the ruling on the bequest
The bequest remains valid; the exception does not invalidate it. Confirm the reasoning: bequest is not a commutative contract, so a void condition does not void it.
3
State the ruling on the exception
The exception is valid. The embryo is excluded from the bequest and goes to the heirs as inheritance.
4
Trace the analogy (talil)
Why is the exception valid? Because bequest resembles inheritance: both take effect after death. Inheritance applies to the embryo because it is a corporeal thing ('ayn). Thus, excluding the embryo is like giving it as inheritance.
5
Distinguish the invalid case
Contrast with excluding service: service is not corporeal, so inheritance does not apply. Hence the exception is invalid, and the legatee gets the right to service.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hidayah commentary explaining the effect of an exception (istithna') in a bequest. The author categorizes three types of contracts with exceptions; the third type is wasiyyah where both the contract and the exception are valid. The example: a dying person bequeaths his slave-girl but excludes the pregnancy. The bequest is valid and the exception is valid: the slave-girl goes to the legatee, the child (when born) goes to the heirs as inheritance. The reasoning: wasiyyah is not a commutative contract ('aqd mu'awadah), so it is not invalidated by a void condition. Moreover, the exception is valid because wasiyyah resembles inheritance (both take effect after death), and inheritance applies to the embryo because it is a corporeal entity ('ayn). Thus the exception of the embryo is like specifying a share of inheritance. In contrast, if he excluded the service of the slave-girl, that exception is invalid because service is not a corporeal thing; therefore the right to service goes to the legatee as part of the bequest.

Key Terms
وصیت (wasiyyah): A bequest or will that takes effect after death.
استثناء (istithna'): An exception or exclusion of something from a bequest or contract.
حمل (haml): The embryo or unborn child.
عین شئی ('ayn shay): A corporeal thing or tangible object.
میراث (mirath): Inheritance; the transfer of ownership of the deceased's estate to heirs.
