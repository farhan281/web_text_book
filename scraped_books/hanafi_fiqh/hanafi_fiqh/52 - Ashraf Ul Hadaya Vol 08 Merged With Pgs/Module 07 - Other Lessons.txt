Excusing Contradiction in Claims of Freedom
Commentary on accepting witnesses for freedom after acknowledging slavery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on contradiction in claims of freedom and explain why a prior acknowledgment of slavery does not invalidate a later claim of freedom when the matter is hidden.

Core Idea
Contradiction in claims is excused when the hidden circumstances of lineage or manumission are later discovered.

This passage addresses a common legal dilemma: a person first acknowledges being a slave, then later claims freedom with witnesses. The Hanafi school accepts this despite the apparent contradiction. The key is that original freedom or prior manumission can be hidden, and once revealed, the earlier acknowledgment is no longer decisive. We will explore the reasoning behind this position and the intra-Hanafi dispute over whether a formal claim (da‘wā) is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The core issue: Does a person's earlier acknowledgment of slavery prevent him from later proving his freedom? What is the role of contradiction (tanāquḍ)?
2
State the preferred Hanafi position
According to the majority including Abu Hanifa, original freedom is implied by the prohibition of the mother's private part, so no claim (da‘wā) is needed. Contradiction does not arise because the earlier acknowledgment was made in ignorance.
The text says: 'اصل آزادي اس کی ماں کے فرج کے حرام ہونے کو متضمن ہے'
3
Examine the opposing view (mashā’ikh)
Some later Hanafi scholars argue that a claim is required for original freedom. However, they agree that contradiction is excused because the lineage is hidden (khafī). The claim is still accepted.
The text says: 'بعض مشائخ کا قول یہ ہے کہ اصلی آزادی ثابت کرنے کیلئے بھی دعویٰ شرط ہے'
4
Analyze the reasoning for excusing contradiction
The passage gives analogies: a woman who took khula later proves prior triple divorce; a slave who made kitāba later proves prior manumission. In all cases, the earlier act was done in ignorance of a hidden fact.
The text says: 'نطفہ کا قرار پانا ایک مخفی چیز ہے'
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Consider a person who acknowledges slavery, then later learns his parents were free. His witnesses are accepted. The contradiction is excused. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that the person is declared free without penalty for inconsistency.

339. Book of Sales. Ashraf al-Hidayah commentary Urdu Hidayah volume eight: ...the testimony of witnesses is accepted. The conclusion is that original freedom implies the prohibition of the mother's private part, and for the prohibition of the private part, since a claim is not required, therefore for original freedom which includes prohibition of the private part, a claim will also not be required. And when for original freedom a claim is not required, then for the person who said 'You bought me, I am a slave' there will be no contradiction in his speech. And some scholars say that a claim is also required to prove original freedom, but then a contradiction will occur because he first acknowledged being a slave and now claims freedom. The answer to this is that here contradiction is not an obstacle for the validity of the claim, rather it is excused because the settling of the sperm is a hidden matter, such that he was brought from Dar al-Harb as a child and did not know the state of his father's or mother's freedom, so he acknowledges being a slave; then later he learns that his parents were free, so he claims his freedom. In any case, the settling of the sperm and offspring is a hidden thing, and in a hidden thing contradiction is excused, so here too contradiction will be excused. And if by the author's saying 'then he is free' he means the freedom obtained from the master's manumission, then in that case too contradiction will not prevent the validity of the claim because the master is independent in manumitting his slave. So it is possible that when the master freed him, some people knew but the slave himself did not know, until when the master sold him, he unknowingly acknowledged that he is his slave. Then when he inquired from people, he established witnesses to his freedom and claimed that he is a freedman. This is like a woman who took khula from her husband and later established witnesses that her husband had given her three divorces before the khula; her witnesses are accepted and there is no contradiction because the husband is independent in effecting divorce, and it is possible that the woman did not know of the divorce before the khula and later came to know. Similarly, a slave made a kitaba with his master and then established witnesses that his master had freed him before the kitaba, so he established witnesses to his being freed. Likewise, in the issue of the text, when a man acknowledged to the buyer that he is a slave, he can say: I did not know of the master's manumission at that time, so I acknowledged being a slave; but when I later learned, I claimed freedom. Thus there is no contradiction of any kind in this.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and commentary (sharh) on the issue of contradiction (tanāquḍ) when a person first acknowledges being a slave and then claims freedom with witnesses. The author explains that the preferred position (Abu Hanifa's) is that original freedom (aslī āzādī) is implied by the prohibition of the mother's private part (ḥurmat al-farj), which is established without a claim (da‘wā). Therefore, a claim is not required to prove original freedom, and no contradiction arises when the person later claims freedom. Some later mashā’ikh hold that a claim is required, but contradiction is excused because the circumstances (like lineage) are hidden (khafī). The commentary supports this by analogies: a woman who takes khula and later proves prior triple divorce, and a slave who makes kitāba and later proves prior manumission. In all cases, the contradiction is excused due to the hidden nature of the fact and the later acquisition of knowledge. The Hanafi position is that da‘wā is not a condition for proving original freedom, and tanāquḍ does not invalidate the claim when the matter is hidden.

Key Terms
تناقض (tanāquḍ): Contradiction between earlier acknowledgment and later claim.
دعوی (da‘wā): A formal claim or assertion of a right in court.
اصلی آزادی (aslī āzādī): Original freedom (born free) as opposed to freedom acquired by manumission.
