Inheritance Eligibility and Apostasy after Bain Divorce
Rulings on continuous eligibility and apostasy in deathbed divorce inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for a bain divorcee's inheritance and apply it to apostasy scenarios to determine whether she inherits.

Core Idea
For a bain divorce in deathbed illness, the wife must be eligible to inherit at both divorce and death.

In Hanafi law, a woman divorced irrevocably (bain) during her husband's fatal illness may still inherit from him if she meets certain conditions. This passage clarifies that her eligibility must be present continuously from the moment of divorce until the husband's death. It then explores how apostasy by either spouse affects this right, introducing nuances rooted in the concept of right attachment (ta'liq al-haqq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general condition for bain inheritance
The wife must be eligible (ahl) for inheritance from the time of divorce to death. If she was a Kitabiyya or slave at divorce and later becomes Muslim or free, she does not inherit because eligibility was lacking at divorce.
For raj'i divorce, only eligibility at death matters.
2
Understand apostasy scenarios
Analyze the text's three apostasy cases: (a) both apostatize, husband returns to Islam and dies – she does not inherit; (b) wife returns to Islam, husband dies as apostate – she inherits; (c) wife apostatizes and dies – husband does not inherit, unless she was sick and died, then by istihsan he inherits.
The distinction hinges on whose act caused the separation and when rights attached.
3
Distinguish firar (fraudulent divorce)
If the husband conditions divorce on the wife's future conversion to Islam, she inherits because divorce occurs when she is eligible. If he simply divorces her tomorrow and she converts in between, she does not inherit.
Firar is avoided when the condition maintains eligibility.
4
Note the slave and conversion cases
A slave who divorces in illness then becomes free: she does not inherit. A dhimmi wife converts after her Muslim husband's deathbed divorce: she inherits only if the divorce was conditioned on her conversion.
Eligibility is assessed at the moment of divorce, not later.

Effect of Wife's Consent to Mahram and Li'an on Inheritance after Bain
Rulings on consent to mahram and li'an in deathbed divorce inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a wife’s consent to a mahram or her act in li'an does not negate her inheritance after a deathbed bain divorce.

Core Idea
Consent to a mahram after bain does not forfeit inheritance; li'an is treated as a compelled act.

The second part of the passage addresses two specific scenarios where the wife acts after receiving a deathbed bain divorce: consenting to a kiss from her stepson, and initiating li'an. The Hanafi ruling is that she inherits in both cases. This lesson explores the rationales: consent does not affect eligibility because the act does not cause the separation, and li'an is an unavoidable defensive measure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on consent to stepson
If the wife consents to a kiss from her stepson (a mahram) after the bain divorce, she still inherits from her ex-husband because the act does not nullify her inheritance eligibility.
The restriction is that the consent happens after divorce; if it caused the separation (e.g., by her initiating), she would not inherit.
2
Understand the distinction between bain and raj'i
The ruling applies only when the consent is after bain divorce. If she consents during marriage or after a raj'i divorce, she does not inherit.
In raj'i, the marriage is still intact, so such an act would invalidate inheritance.
3
Analyze the li'an case
If the husband falsely accuses his wife and li'an occurs, the wife inherits even though she participates in the li'an because she is compelled to defend herself. The separation is treated as a ta'liq on an unavoidable act.
This is distinguished from a voluntary act like consent to a mahram during marriage.
4
Note the exception for consent during sickness or after raj'i
If the wife consents after a raj'i divorce or while still married, she does not inherit because the act occurs while the marriage is potentially still valid.
In such cases, the consent nullifies her right because it shows willingness to end the marriage.

Inheritance of Twins: Li'an vs Zina
Faraid case comparing twin inheritance for child of li'an and child of zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance share of a twin sibling in cases of li'an and zina, and correctly distinguish between the two scenarios using Hanafi faraid rules.

Core Idea
A child of li'an inherits from a twin as a full sibling; a child of zina inherits as a uterine sibling.

This lesson explores a nuanced difference in Hanafi inheritance law: how twins inherit when their paternity is denied through li'an versus when they are born out of wedlock (zina). While both children have no paternal lineage, the status of their sibling relationship differs, leading to different inheritance shares.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of child
Determine if the deceased is a child of li'an (walad al-mula'anah) or zina (walad al-zina). This affects whether the surviving twin is a full or uterine sibling.
Li'an only severs the child's lineage from the father; siblings from the same mother and (denied) father are still full siblings.
2
List the surviving heirs
Identify all heirs present: mother, the surviving twin, and any other siblings (uterine or full). In the given scenario, there is also a uterine brother from the mother's side.
For children of zina, all siblings are uterine (maternal half-siblings). For children of li'an, twins are full siblings, but additional siblings may be uterine if from a different father.
3
Determine the sibling status
For zina, the twin is a uterine brother. For li'an, the twin is a full brother. This determines their share category: uterine siblings get a fixed fraction (1/6 each or 1/3 collectively), while full brothers take asabah (residuary).
Uterine siblings are those who share only the mother. Full siblings share both parents.
4
Assign Quranic shares
Give the mother her fard share of 1/6. If there are uterine siblings, give them their prescribed share: 1/3 total if more than one, or 1/6 if a single uterine sibling.
The mother's share is always 1/6 when there are children, including grandchildren.
5

Fara'id of Manumission and Bequest: Proportional Calculation
Ruling and calculation for conditional manumission linked to hajj after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the distribution of a slave's value when manumission is bequeathed from the one-third alongside another bequest, applying the proportional reduction method.

Core Idea
A bequest to free the slave and a separate bequest of one-third share the one-third proportionally; the slave works to pay the excess to heirs and legatee.

This passage from al-Muḥīṭ presents a fara'id-style calculation involving a conditional manumission linked to ḥajj after death. The master's statement creates a bequest of freedom for the slave, but since the slave has no other property, the manumission can only take effect from the one-third permissible for bequests. The passage also introduces a competing bequest to a third party, requiring proportional division within that one-third. Understanding this calculation is essential for correctly applying the rules of waṣiyyah and ʿitq when the estate is limited.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequests
There are two bequests: the manumission of the slave (entire value) and a bequest of one-third of the estate to a legatee.
The manumission is treated as a bequest from the one-third because it is without payment.
2
Determine the available fund
Only one-third of the estate is available for bequests. The slave is the only asset, so the one-third equals one-third of his value.
The heirs inherit the remaining two-thirds.
3
Calculate the ratio of claims
The slave claims his full value (1), the legatee claims one-third of the estate (1/3). The ratio is 1 : 1/3 = 3 : 1.
Convert to same denominator: 3/3 : 1/3, so claims are 3 and 1.
4
Divide the one-third proportionally
The one-third is divided into 4 shares: 3 for the slave, 1 for the legatee. The slave's share of the one-third is 3/4, the legatee's is 1/4.
The denominator 4 comes from the ratio 3+1=4.
5
Determine what the slave must work to pay
The slave is freed from the one-third, but must compensate for the remaining value. He works to pay the legatee's share (1/4 of one-third = 1/12 of his value) and the heirs' share (2/3 = 8/12). He retains 3/12 (his freed portion).
Total paid: 1/12 + 8/12 = 9/12 = 3/4 of his value; he keeps 1/4.

Dual Paternity Claim by Co-Owners of a Slave-Girl
Ruling on joint paternity claim by co-owners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when both co-owners claim paternity of the child from a jointly owned slave-girl, explain the evidence from the Companions, and evaluate the theoretical basis of divisible vs. indivisible legal effects.

Core Idea
Lineage is indivisible but its legal effects can be divided between co-claimants.

In this passage, Al-Haskafi explains the ruling when two co-owners jointly claim paternity of a child born to their jointly owned slave-girl. The Ḥanafī school uniquely establishes lineage to both, with each paying half the dower and inheriting mutually. The proof comes from the practice of Caliph Umar and Ali, which grounds the reasoning that divisible legal effects can be shared even if lineage itself is one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn: lineage proven to both, slave-girl becomes umm walad for both, each pays half dower and they offset, mutual inheritance. This is the Ḥanafī position.
The offset (taqāṣā) avoids actual payment since both owe the same amount.
2
Examine the Proof
The commentator cites the letter of Umar to judge Shuraih and a similar ruling from Ali. This is qawl ṣaḥābī, considered a proof in Ḥanafī uṣūl when not contradicted.
The incident was witnessed by other Companions, implying consensus (sukūtī).
3
Understand the Reasoning
Lineage (nasab) is indivisible in essence, but its legal consequences (ahkām) are divisible. Thus, inheritance shares and dower obligations can be split, while paternity itself is established for each fully.
This is a key Ḥanafī legal maxim: mā lā yatajazzā'u fī nafsihi yatajazzā'u fī aḥkāmihi.
4
Note the Practical Implementation
Each pays half the dower (ʿaqr) to the other for using the other's share of the slave-girl. Since they owe each other the same amount, they offset. The child inherits from each as a son and each inherits from the child as a father.
The child’s status as heir is wajib, and the dower is wajib compensation for intercourse.
5
Contrast with Other Schools
Other madhāhib generally require a child to have only one legal father. They might force the parents to agree on one, or resort to casting lots. The Ḥanafī view is based on equality of claim and the divisibility of rulings.
Cross-madhhab difference is legitimate ikhtilāf.

The Oath on Kasb: How Inheritance and Transfer Affect the Ruling
Ruling on oath from kasb with inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath not to eat from someone's kasb and apply it to inheritance and purchase scenarios.

Core Idea
Inheritance transfers the same 'kasb' identity; purchase or bequest creates new ownership and breaks the oath-link.

When someone swears not to eat from the acquisition (kasb) of a specific person, the key is whether the property in question still belongs to that person's own acquisition. Ibn Nujaym, in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, clarifies that inheritance does not change the kasb attribution, but a transfer by purchase or bequest does. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its subject
The swearer says 'I will not eat from the kasb of so-and-so.' The condition for breaking the oath (hanth) is eating something that is the kasb of that specific person.
Kasb means property acquired through personal effort, not inherited or gifted.
2
Apply to inheritance scenario
If the swearer inherits from the person, the inherited property is still considered the kasb of the deceased. So eating it breaks the oath.
The text says 'الثابت للوارث عين الثابت للمورث' – what is established for the heir is the same as what was established for the testator.
3
Contrast with purchase or bequest
If the property is transferred by purchase or bequest to a third party, it becomes the kasb of the new owner. The swearer eating from it does not break the oath because it is no longer the kasb of the original person.
This is based on al-Waqi'at: 'بخلاف ما لو انتقل إلى غيره بغير الميراث بشراء أو وصية'.
4
Examine gift and charity
If the original owner gives the property as a gift or charity to the swearer, and the swearer eats it, he breaks the oath. This is because the property remains linked to the original owner's kasb until it is consumed.
Gift and charity are similar to inheritance in that they do not sever the link for the purpose of the oath.
5
Summarize the principle
The ruling hinges on whether the property retains its identity as the kasb of the named person. Inheritance preserves it; purchase/bequest does not.
This principle guides many other cases in the passage.

Oaths on Inheritance, Property, and Food: Detailed Scenarios and Name Nuances
Detailed scenarios for oaths on inheritance, property, and food

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for oaths when the subject is inheritance, property, or specific food items, and distinguish when a change in ownership or shared ownership affects the ruling.

Core Idea
The wording and context of the oath determine its scope: the generality of 'food' and 'bread' vs 'loaf' matters, and second inheritance abrogates the first.

Ibn Nujaym, in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, extends the principles of kasb oaths to numerous specific cases. He addresses inheritance oaths that are abrogated by subsequent inheritance, the effect of buying and selling, a Hanafi internal dispute on grinding and baking, and the critical difference between general names like 'food' and specific ones like 'loaf'. This lesson walks through these examples to sharpen your ability to deduce the ruling from the oath's exact wording.

Guided Path
1
Understand inheritance oaths
If someone swears not to eat from the inheritance of X, and X dies, then X's heir dies and another inherits, the second inheritance abrogates the first. Eating from that property does not break the oath.
The text says: 'بالإرث الثاني ينتسخ حكم الأول'.
2
Analyze father's inheritance oath
If one swears not to eat from his father's inheritance, buying food directly with inherited money breaks the oath. But if he buys something else with the money and then buys food with that, he does not break the oath.
This shows the distinction between direct consumption and conversion into another asset.
3
Apply rules for property and purchase
If the oath is on property or what X owns, a transfer of property out of X's ownership ends the oath's application. If X buys something and then sells it, the sale abrogates the purchase, so eating does not break the oath.
The text says: 'الشراء الثاني فسخ للأول'.
4
Resolve the grinding/baking dispute
There are two transmissions in al-Muntaqa: one says grinding and baking break the oath, another says they do not. The author does not explicitly choose but the more followed view is that they do break.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the practice (mufta bihi) is hanth.
5
Differentiate names: food, bread, loaf
If the oath mentions 'food' or 'bread', it applies to small and large quantities, so shared food with another still breaks the oath. But 'loaf' is specific, so shared loaf does not break the oath.
The text says: 'اسم الخبز يطلق على القليل والكثير ولا كذلك اسم الرغيف'.
6
Consider the swearer's own share
If the swearer eats from food shared between him and the named person, he does not break the oath because he is eating his own share.
This is unless the oath is general and he did not specify.
