Oaths on Working with Partners and Slaves
Rulings on oaths and indirect work relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether working with the partner or authorized slave of the person named in an oath breaks the oath, and explain the reasoning based on liability recourse.

Core Idea
Working with a partner breaks an oath because of mutual recourse; working with an authorized slave does not.

This lesson focuses on the first two rulings from al-Zahiriyyah: if a person swears not to work with someone, does working with that person's partner or authorized slave break the oath? The distinction hinges on whether the person you work with has legal recourse against you for liabilities, which determines if you are effectively working with the oath-bound person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's target
Determine who the oath-taker swore not to work with—the specific person, their partner, or their slave.
The target is the person named; the ruling examines indirect connections.
2
Analyze liability recourse
Understand the Hanafi principle: each partner in a partnership can be held liable for the other, creating a direct link. An authorized slave, however, does not make his master liable for his actions.
Recourse (al-‘uhdah) is key: if you work with someone who can pass liability to the oath-bound person, you effectively work with him.
3
Apply the ruling
If the work is with the partner, mutual recourse exists → oath broken. If with the slave, no recourse to the master → oath not broken.

وفي الظهيرية : ولو حلف لا يعمل مع فلان في قصارة ففعل مع شريك فلان حنث، ولو عمل مع عبده المأذون لا يحنث لأن كل واحد من الشريكين يرجع بالعهدة على صاحبه ويصير الحالف عاملاً مع المحلوف عليه وإن كان عقد الشركة نفسه لا . يوجب الحقوق، أما العبد المأذون فلا يرجع بالعهدة على المولى فلا يصير الحالف شريكاً لمولاه . ولو حلف لا يشارك فلاناً في هذه البلدة ثم خرجا عنها وعقدا عقد الشركة ثم دخلاها وعملا فيها إن كان الحالف نوى في يمينه أن لا يعقد عقد الشركة في البلدة لا يحنث، وإن نوى أن لا يعمل بشركة فلان حنث . وإن دفع أحدهما إلى صاحبه مالاً مضاربة فهذا والأول سواء لأن المضاربة شركة في عرفنا . ولو حلف لا يشارك فلاناً فأخرج كل واحد منهما دراهمه واشتركا حنث الحالف خلطا أو لم يخلطا. ولو حلف لا يشارك فلاناً فشاركه بمال ابنه الصغير لا يحنث، ولو حلف لا يشارك فلاناً ثم إن الحالف دفع إلى رجل مالاً بضاعة وأمره أن يعمل فيه برأيه فشارك المدفوع إليه المال الرجل الذي حلف رب المال أن لا يشاركه يحنث لأن الحالف لأنه صار شريكاً للمحلوف عليه لأن المستبضع لا حق له في الربح فكان العامل شريكاً لرب المال، ولو كان مكان المستبضع مضارب والمسألة بحالها لا يحنث لأن المضارب له حق في الربح فكان المحلوف عليه شريكاً للمضارب، ولو كان المستبضع حلف أن لا يشارك أحداً فدفع المال شريكه بإذن المستبضع لا يحنث رجل . قال لأخيه إن شاركتك فحلال الله علي حرام ثم بدالهما أن يشتركا قالوا : إن كان للحالف ابن كبير ينبغي أن يدفع الحالف ماله إلى ابنه مصاربة ويجعل لابنه شيئاً يسيراً من الربح ويأذن لابنه أن يعمل فيه برأيه، ثم إن للابن أن يشارك عمه فإذا فعل الابن ذلك كان للابن ما شرط له الأب من الربح والفاضل على ذلك إلى النصف يكون للأب ولا يحنث، ولو كان مكان الأب أجنبي فالجواب كذلك اهـ.
And in al-Zahiriyyah: If he swore not to work with so-and-so in laundering, and he worked with the partner of so-and-so, he breaks his oath. And if he worked with his (so-and-so's) authorized slave, he does not break his oath, because each of the two partners (i.e., the oath-taker and the partner of so-and-so) has recourse against the other (for liability), so the oath-taker becomes a worker with the one against whom the oath was made, even though the contract of partnership itself does not create rights. As for the authorized slave, he has no recourse against his master, so the oath-taker does not become a partner of the master. And if he swore not to partner with so-and-so in this town, then they both left the town and made a partnership contract, then entered the town and worked in it: if the oath-taker intended by his oath not to make a partnership contract in the town, he does not break his oath; if he intended not to work as a partner with so-and-so, he breaks his oath. And if one of them gave to the other capital as mudarabah, it is the same as the first case, because mudarabah is a partnership in our usage. And if he swore not to partner with so-and-so, and each of them brought out his dirhams and they formed a partnership, the oath-taker breaks his oath, whether they mixed the wealth or not. And if he swore not to partner with so-and-so, and he partnered with him using his young son's capital, he does not break his oath. And if he swore not to partner with so-and-so, then the oath-taker gave capital as bidā'ah (commodity investment) to a man and commanded him to act according to his own opinion, and the recipient of the capital partnered with the man against whom the owner of the capital had sworn not to partner, the oath-taker breaks his oath, because he became a partner of the one against whom the oath was made. This is because the mustabdi' (investor in bidā'ah) has no right to profit, so the worker (the recipient) is a partner of the capital owner. And if the mustabdi' were a mudarib (capital manager in mudarabah), and the situation is the same, he does not break his oath, because the mudarib has a right to profit, so the one against whom the oath was made is a partner of the mudarib. And if the mustabdi' swore not to partner with anyone, and he gave the capital to his partner with the permission of the mustabdi', he does not break his oath. A man said to his brother, 'If I partner with you, then what is lawful from Allah to me is forbidden.' Then both decided to form a partnership. They said: If the oath-taker has an adult son, he should give his capital to his son as mudarabah, and assign a small share of profit to his son, and permit his son to act according to his opinion. Then the son may partner with his uncle. When the son has done that, the son gets what the father stipulated of profit, and whatever surplus above that up to half is for the father, and the father does not break his oath. And if instead of the father there were a stranger, the answer is the same. End.

Commentary
This extensive passage from al-Zahiriyyah provides detailed rulings on oaths related to partnership (shirkah) and its variants. The general principle is that any act that results in the oath-taker being a partner in fact with the person mentioned in the oath will break the oath. Key points: (1) Working with the partner of the person named is like working directly with that person, because the oath-taker and the partner have mutual recourse. (2) Working with the person's authorized slave does not break the oath because the slave has no recourse against his master. (3) The location and intention of the oath matters: if the oath is restricted to a specific town, partnership outside that town does not break the oath unless the intention was broader. (4) Mudarabah is treated as partnership in this context. (5) Mixing capital is not necessary for partnership in oath-breaking. (6) Using a child's capital to partner does not break the oath because the child is not a party. (7) If the oath-taker gives capital as bidā'ah (a type of investment where the investor takes all profit, not a share) and the recipient then partners with the prohibited person, the oath is broken because the bidā'ah investor has no profit share, making the worker the partner. But if it were mudarabah, the mudarib has a profit share, so the partnership is with the mudarib, not the capital owner, so no breach. (8) A clever legal stratagem (hila) is provided: if a man swears not to partner with his brother, he can give his capital to his adult son as mudarabah, the son partners with the brother, and the father avoids the oath while still benefiting.

Key Terms
العهدة (al-‘uhdah): Liability recourse: the right to hold one party responsible for the actions or debts of another in a partnership.
العبد المأذون (al-‘abd al-ma’dhūn): Authorized slave: a slave given permission by the master to engage in trade; his actions do not bind the master beyond the slave's own capital.

Oaths on Partnership: Location, Intention, and Mixing
Rulings on location and intention in partnership oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that an oath may be conditioned by location and intention, and that mixing capital is not required for breach in partnerships.

Core Idea
An oath can be limited to a specific place; intention determines breach; mixing capital is irrelevant.

This lesson continues with rulings from al-Zahiriyyah regarding oaths to partner with someone. It covers three scenarios: (1) an oath restricted to a specific town, (2) an oath without such restriction but where intention matters, and (3) the effect of mixing capital. The Hanafi approach focuses on the oath-taker's intention and the factual creation of a partnership, not on formalities like location or mixing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's scope
Determine if the oath includes words like 'in this town' or if it is absolute. Also consider the oath-taker's intention (niyyah).
If the oath is restricted to a town, partnership outside the town may not break it.
2
Analyze intention
If the oath-taker intended not to make a contract in the town, no breach if contract is made outside. If he intended not to work as a partner anywhere, then any partnership—even outside—breaks the oath.
Intention matters because oaths are interpreted by the oath-taker's intention in Hanafi law (al-umur bi-maqasidiha).
3
Apply the mixing rule
Even if the partners do not mix their capital (e.g., they keep separate piles), the mere act of forming a partnership contract breaks the oath.
Mixing is not a condition for partnership existence in this context.

Mudarabah, Bidā'ah, and Legal Stratagem for Oaths
Rulings on investment types and legal stratagem in oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mudarabah and bidā'ah in oath-breaking and explain the legal stratagem (hila) using a child's capital.

Core Idea
Bidā'ah makes the capital owner a partner; mudarabah makes the mudarib the partner; a child's capital can be used to avoid an oath.

This final lesson from the passage covers the most nuanced cases: the difference between bidā'ah (commodity investment without profit share) and mudarabah (profit-sharing). It also introduces a legal stratagem (hila) for a man who swore not to partner with his brother: he can give his capital to his son as mudarabah, and the son then partners with the brother, avoiding the oath. These rulings demonstrate the Hanafi mastery of legal effects based on the underlying reality of profit entitlement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of investment
Determine whether the capital owner gave the money as bidā'ah (no profit share for the worker) or mudarabah (worker gets profit share).
Bidā'ah: the worker is merely an agent; all profit goes to the owner. Mudarabah: the worker (mudarib) gets a share.
2
Apply the effect on partnership
In bidā'ah, the worker effectively becomes a partner of the capital owner because the owner gets all profit and bears all loss. In mudarabah, the worker is a partner in profit, so the partnership is between the worker and the person he deals with, not directly with the capital owner.
Thus, if the worker partners with the prohibited person, the capital owner breaches the oath only in bidā'ah, not in mudarabah.
3
Understand the stratagem
If a man swears not to partner with his brother, he can give his capital to his adult son as mudarabah with a small profit share for the son, then authorize the son to partner with the brother. The father avoids the oath because the partnership is between the son and the brother, not the father.
The father still benefits from the profit after the son's share, but he is not a direct partner.

And if the mustabdi' swore not to partner with anyone, and he gave the capital to his partner with the permission of the mustabdi', he does not break his oath. A man said to his brother, 'If I partner with you, then what is lawful from Allah to me is forbidden.' Then both decided to form a partnership. They said: If the oath-taker has an adult son, he should give his capital to his son as mudarabah, and assign a small share of profit to his son, and permit his son to act according to his opinion. Then the son may partner with his uncle. When the son has done that, the son gets what the father stipulated of profit, and whatever surplus above that up to half is for the father, and the father does not break his oath. And if instead of the father there were a stranger, the answer is the same. End.

Commentary
This extensive passage from al-Zahiriyyah provides detailed rulings on oaths related to partnership (shirkah) and its variants. The general principle is that any act that results in the oath-taker being a partner in fact with the person mentioned in the oath will break the oath. Key points: (1) Working with the partner of the person named is like working directly with that person, because the oath-taker and the partner have mutual recourse. (2) Working with the person's authorized slave does not break the oath because the slave has no recourse against his master. (3) The location and intention of the oath matters: if the oath is restricted to a specific town, partnership outside that town does not break the oath unless the intention was broader. (4) Mudarabah is treated as partnership in this context. (5) Mixing capital is not necessary for partnership in oath-breaking. (6) Using a child's capital to partner does not break the oath because the child is not a party. (7) If the oath-taker gives capital as bidā'ah (a type of investment where the investor takes all profit, not a share) and the recipient then partners with the prohibited person, the oath is broken because the bidā'ah investor has no profit share, making the worker the partner. But if it were mudarabah, the mudarib has a profit share, so the partnership is with the mudarib, not the capital owner, so no breach. (8) A clever legal stratagem (hila) is provided: if a man swears not to partner with his brother, he can give his capital to his adult son as mudarabah, the son partners with the brother, and the father avoids the oath while still benefiting.

Key Terms
المستبضع (al-mustabdi'): One who entrusts capital as bidā'ah (commodity investment) where the worker gets no profit; all profit goes to the owner.
المضارب (al-mudarib): The worker-manager in a mudarabah contract, who receives a share of the profit.
الحيلة (al-hilah): Legal stratagem: a permissible means to achieve a lawful objective while avoiding prohibited or oath-breaking consequences.
