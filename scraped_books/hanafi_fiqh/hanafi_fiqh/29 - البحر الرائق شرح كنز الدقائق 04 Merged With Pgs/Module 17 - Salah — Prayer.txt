Joint Ownership and Paternity Claims for Umm Walad
Expansion on joint ownership and paternity claims for Umm Walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on joint ownership and paternity claims for a female slave, distinguishing between istilad and da'wat 'itq based on timing of conception.

Core Idea
A jointly owned slave's child is free, but Umm Walad status depends on conception timing; otherwise it is a claim of emancipation.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on the ruling when two men jointly own a female slave and both claim paternity of her child. The core ruling is that each owner's share becomes Umm Walad, but this is conditional on conception occurring in joint ownership. Let's work through the details and exceptions.

Guided Path
1
State the Basic Ruling
When two men jointly own a female slave and she gives birth, the child's lineage is established from both, and each owner's share of the mother becomes an Umm Walad. The mother's service, earnings, and produce are divided according to their shares.
The child is free from conception, inheriting from both fathers.
2
Identify the Condition for Istilad
The mother becomes an Umm Walad only if she conceived while in the joint ownership. This is evidenced by birth occurring at least six months after the date of joint purchase.
If birth is earlier, conception predates joint ownership.
3
Apply the Exception: Bought Pregnant or After Birth
If the slave was pregnant at purchase (birth <6 months) or bought after childbirth, the claim is not istilad but da'wat 'itq (claim of emancipation). The child is free only from the time of the claim, and the mother does not become Umm Walad.
Da'wat 'itq frees the child but does not affect the mother's status.
4
Handle Special Scenarios
If the slave was married to one owner before joint purchase and gives birth <6 months later, that owner's share becomes Umm Walad, and since istilad does not admit division (per Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it extends to the partner's share. If one owner sells half after conception, the original owner's claim is stronger.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold indivisibility; Abu Hanifa views differ.
5
Note the Joint Umm Walad Distinctiveness
A jointly owned Umm Walad is not like a singly owned one: if she later bears another child, lineage must be established by claim because intercourse is forbidden.
This preserves the requirement of paternity acknowledgment.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on the ruling regarding joint ownership of a female slave when both owners claim paternity of her child. The author begins by stating the core ruling: when two men jointly own a female slave and she gives birth, the child's lineage is established from both of them, but each owner becomes an Umm Walad (mother of child) owner only for his own share of the mother. This means that the child is free and inherits from both, but the mother remains a slave except that each owner's share becomes an Umm Walad, which cannot extend to the other's share. Thus the mother's service, earnings, and produce are divided according to the owners' shares. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that istilad (becoming an Umm Walad) is established from each owner only in his share, so it cannot be established for the other's share. The author then notes that this is the ruling in al-Zahiriya. He then qualifies (taqyid) the author's absolute statement: she is an Umm Walad for both only if she conceived while in their joint ownership, i.e., she gave birth six months or more after the joint purchase. If they bought her already pregnant and she gives birth less than six months after purchase, or if they bought her after delivery and then claim the child, then she does not become an Umm Walad for them. Instead, this is a claim of emancipation (da'wat 'itq) not istilad, so the child is free only from the time of the claim, not from conception. The text then discusses various scenarios: if the pregnancy was from one owner's marriage before joint purchase, then she becomes Umm Walad for that owner (the husband), and since istilad does not admit division (according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it extends to the partner's share as well. Similarly, if one owner owned her alone and then sold half, and she gives birth after the sale, the first owner is more entitled because conception was in his ownership. Finally, the author clarifies that this joint Umm Walad is not like a single owner's Umm Walad because if she later has another child, lineage is not established without a claim (since intercourse is forbidden).

Key Terms
استيلاد (istilad): The act of a female slave becoming an Umm Walad (mother of her master's child) through bearing his child, resulting in her eventual freedom upon his death and the child's freedom from conception.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master, thereby gaining certain rights including eventual freedom upon the master's death.
دعوة عتق (da'wat 'itq): A claim of emancipation that frees the child only from the time of the claim, not from conception, and does not make the mother an Umm Walad.

Master's Claim of Paternity from Mukatab's Female Slave
Ruling on master's claim with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a master's claim of paternity from his mukatab's female slave is valid and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the need for the mukatab's confirmation.

Core Idea
A master's claim of paternity from a mukatab's slave requires the mukatab's confirmation; without it, lineage is not established.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when a master (mawla) claims a child born to a female slave owned by his mukatab (a slave contracting his freedom). The primary ruling hinges on whether the mukatab confirms the claim. The passage also presents a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of the master's authority over the mukatab's property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The master claims that the child of the mukatab's female slave is his. The mukatab's response determines the outcome.
Note the unique status of the mukatab: he owns property but is still a slave.
2
State the ruling when the mukatab confirms
If the mukatab confirms, the master incurs liability: he must pay the 'aqr (dower for the slave) and the value of the child. The child's lineage is established to him, but the female slave does not become his Umm Walad.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
3
State the ruling when the mukatab denies
If the mukatab denies, the lineage is not established, and the master owes nothing.
This is consistent with the principle that the master does not own the slave.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf holds that lineage is established even without the mukatab's confirmation, by analogy with a free father claiming a child from his son's slave girl.
This is an ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Understand the distinction that underpins the majority view
Unlike a father who can own his son's property, the master does not have a right to own the mukatab's earnings. Therefore, the mukatab's confirmation is necessary.
This distinction (talil) is key to understanding the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from the matn. It deals with a case where the master (mawla) claims paternity of a child born to a female slave owned by his mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom). The primary ruling: if the master claims and the mukatab confirms, then the master is required to pay the 'aqr (dower for the slave) and the value of the child, and the child's lineage is established to the master, but the female slave does not become an Umm Walad for the master. If the mukatab denies the claim, the lineage is not established. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf differs: he says lineage is established without the mukatab's confirmation, by analogy with a free father claiming a child from his son's slave girl. The commentator explains the distinction: a master does not own the earnings of his mukatab's property, while a father owns the right to own his son's property, so the son's confirmation is not needed. The master must pay the 'aqr because he has a right in the slave, but not full ownership. He must pay the child's value because he is like a 'maghrur' (deceived) who relied on the fact that the child was his property (since the slave is in his ownership) and thus the child is free in exchange for the value. However, unlike the maghrur case, the value is calculated from the day of birth, not the day of litigation. The mother does not become Umm Walad because the master does not have full ownership of her. The commentator then clarifies a previous point: the phrase 'she is not like the Umm Walad of one person' refers back to the original issue of joint ownership. He explains that in the original case (two men jointly claiming a child from a jointly owned slave), if there is no preponderant factor, both are equal and the child's lineage is established from both, and the slave becomes Umm Walad for both, and neither can have intercourse with her. But if there is a preponderant factor (e.g., one is the father, or one is Muslim), then lineage is established from the preponderant one only, and he alone becomes Umm Walad owner, so the slave is no longer jointly owned.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered into a contract of kitaba with his master to purchase his freedom by paying agreed installments.
عقر ('aqr): A compensatory payment (dower) made to the owner of a female slave when she is made pregnant by someone other than her owner, equivalent to the dower of a free woman of her status.
مغرور (maghrur): One who is deceived, e.g., a person who mistakenly marries a woman who is actually a slave, or here the master who mistakenly believes he owns the child.

Joint Claim on a Child from a Jointly Owned Slave
Expansion on joint claim rules for a jointly owned slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule when two co-owners both claim paternity of a child born to a jointly owned female slave and identify when a preponderance factor alters the outcome.

Core Idea
When two men jointly own a slave and both claim paternity, the child's lineage is established from both equally unless there is a preponderant factor (e.g., one is father or Muslim).

This lesson builds on the previous one, now addressing a scenario where a female slave is jointly owned and two men each claim the child born to her. The text explains the default rule of equality and the effect of preponderance factors, such as one claimant being the father of the other or being a Muslim versus a dhimmī. Understanding this clarifies how conflicting paternity claims are resolved in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two men jointly own a female slave. She gives birth to a child, and both men claim paternity.
Joint ownership means each has an undivided share.
2
State the default ruling
If there is no preponderant factor (tarjīḥ), the child's lineage is established from both equally.
This is based on equality in the cause of entitlement.
3
Understand the consequences of joint lineage
When lineage is established from both, the female slave becomes Umm Walad for both, meaning she will be free upon their death. Intercourse with her is prohibited for both because she is jointly owned.
This prevents any one owner from having exclusive rights.
4
Identify the preponderance factors
If one claimant is the father of the other (i.e., a father and son), or if one is Muslim and the other is a dhimmī, then lineage is established only for the preponderant one (the father or the Muslim).
These factors strengthen the claim.
5
Apply the ruling with preponderance
With preponderance, the child's lineage is from the preponderant claimant only. That claimant's share of the slave becomes Umm Walad, and the slave is no longer jointly owned (she becomes exclusively owned by him? Actually, the text says she becomes Umm Walad for him, so she is free upon his death, and the joint ownership dissolves in effect).
The other claimant's claim is dismissed.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from the matn. It deals with a case where the master (mawla) claims paternity of a child born to a female slave owned by his mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom). The primary ruling: if the master claims and the mukatab confirms, then the master is required to pay the 'aqr (dower for the slave) and the value of the child, and the child's lineage is established to the master, but the female slave does not become an Umm Walad for the master. If the mukatab denies the claim, the lineage is not established. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf differs: he says lineage is established without the mukatab's confirmation, by analogy with a free father claiming a child from his son's slave girl. The commentator explains the distinction: a master does not own the earnings of his mukatab's property, while a father owns the right to own his son's property, so the son's confirmation is not needed. The master must pay the 'aqr because he has a right in the slave, but not full ownership. He must pay the child's value because he is like a 'maghrur' (deceived) who relied on the fact that the child was his property (since the slave is in his ownership) and thus the child is free in exchange for the value. However, unlike the maghrur case, the value is calculated from the day of birth, not the day of litigation. The mother does not become Umm Walad because the master does not have full ownership of her. The commentator then clarifies a previous point: the phrase 'she is not like the Umm Walad of one person' refers back to the original issue of joint ownership. He explains that in the original case (two men jointly claiming a child from a jointly owned slave), if there is no preponderant factor, both are equal and the child's lineage is established from both, and the slave becomes Umm Walad for both, and neither can have intercourse with her. But if there is a preponderant factor (e.g., one is the father, or one is Muslim), then lineage is established from the preponderant one only, and he alone becomes Umm Walad owner, so the slave is no longer jointly owned.

Swearing to Do a Sin: Obligation to Break the Oath
Ruling with proof from hadith, categorizing objects of oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that breaking an oath involving sin is wajib, explain the five categories of actions sworn upon, and apply the rule that no expiation is required for breaking such an oath.

Core Idea
An oath to sin must be broken — preserving the prohibition overrides the oath.

What should a Muslim do if he swears to commit a sin, like 'I will not pray today'? Should he keep his oath or break it? The Hanafi school has a clear ruling: breaking such an oath is an obligation (wajib). This lesson examines the proof from the Sunnah and the detailed categorization of oaths that scholars like al-Marghinani laid out. By the end, you will understand not only the ruling but also the reasoning behind it and how it applies across different types of actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is swearing to do something that is a sin or to omit an obligatory act. The Hanafi ruling is that breaking such an oath (hanath) is wajib. This is stated explicitly in the matn: 'من حلف على معصية ينبغي أن يحنث' meaning 'he should break his oath' – and the commentator clarifies that 'should' means 'must'.
The ruling applies equally to oaths involving sins (e.g., committing zina) and omitting obligations (e.g., not praying).
2
Locate the dalil
The source cites two hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari. First: 'Whoever vows to obey Allah, let him obey Him; whoever vows to disobey Allah, let him not disobey Him.' Second: 'If you swear an oath and see something better, do what is better and expiate your oath.' These prove that obedience to Allah overrides any oath leading to sin.
The term 'yamin' (oath) in the hadith is interpreted as the object sworn upon (al-mahluf 'alayh), not the swearing itself.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The first hadith directly prohibits fulfilling a vow or oath that involves disobedience to Allah – it commands breaking it. The second hadith permits breaking an oath for something better, implying that for sinful actions, breaking is required (without needing permission) and also that expiation is not intended as a compensator for sin. The commentator states: 'there is no compensator for sin in its opposite.'
Thus, for a sinful oath, breaking is wajib without expiation; the only compensator is repentance.
4
Categorize the objects of oaths
The text divides the mahluf 'alayh into ten types based on the nature of the action (five actions and five omissions). Each type has a distinct ruling: (1) sin/obligation – break wajib; (2) better than alternative – break is better; (3) alternative better – keep is better; (4) equal – keep is better. For the fourth category (when the object is itself obligatory, like swearing to pray Zuhr), faithfulness is obligatory, creating two obligations: the act and the oath.
The commentator also notes that oaths limited to a time (e.g., 'today') are needed for the ruling to apply; absolute oaths (e.g., 'ever') are handled differently.
5
Apply to disbeliever's oath
The text then rules that a disbeliever's oath is invalid – no expiation is due even if he later breaks it as a Muslim. The proof is the Quranic verse: 'Indeed they are not people of oaths' (9:12). The Hanafi interpretation is that such oaths lack legal effect because they are not made with proper veneration of Allah.
This highlights the condition that the swearer must be Muslim for the oath to be valid.

Does Salam in Prayer Break an Oath? Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Analysis
Ruling on salam in prayer vis-à-vis oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred Hanafi ruling on whether the imam's salam during prayer breaks an oath not to speak, and identify the differing opinions within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The salam in prayer is not speech for oath purposes; the preferred view holds neither the first nor second salam breaks an oath.

When a person swears not to speak, and then performs prayer, does saying the salam (the closing greeting) count as speech that violates the oath? The Hanafi school has an internal debate on this issue, especially regarding the imam who says salam to the right and then to the left. This lesson unpacks the opinions, their proofs, and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Oath Context
The act is the imam saying 'assalamu alaykum wa rahmatullah' twice (to the right and left) during prayer. The oath is 'I will not speak.' The question: does salam count as speech?
Salam is wajib in prayer but its nature is dual: it ends the prayer and is a greeting.
2
Recognize the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Two opinions exist: (1) Only the second salam (to the left) breaks the oath because the first is still part of prayer; (2) Neither breaks because both are part of the prayer's completion.
The first opinion relies on a literal view: the first salam is within prayer, the second exits it.
3
Identify the Basis of Each Opinion
First opinion: The first salam is an integral of prayer, so it is not independent speech; the second salam is the actual exit and thus a new action. Second opinion: Both salams together constitute the exit; saying one without the other is incomplete, so both are part of the prayer's conclusion.
The second opinion is attributed to Imam Muhammad and is called 'sahih' (correct) in the text.
4
Apply the Preferred Opinion
According to the mufta bihi (Imam Muhammad's view), neither salam breaks the oath. The imam need not expiate his oath if he swears not to speak and then prays and says salam.
This ruling applies only when the salam is done in the proper prayer context.
5
Extend the Principle to Related Cases
The passage also covers other scenarios: saying 'Who?' to a knock, 'Labbaik' to a call, unintelligible speech, and indirect speech (addressing a wall). Each is judged separately: direct, understood speech breaks the oath; indirect or unintelligible may not.
These cases illustrate the general principle: not all utterances are 'speech' for oath purposes.

Rulings on Oaths Regarding Prayer: General vs. Specific
Distinguishing between general and specific prayer oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to pray is broken—distinguishing between a general oath (broken by one rak'ah) and an oath not to pray a specific prayer (requires a valid two-rak'ah prayer)—and explain the legal reasoning (istihsan) behind the distinction.

Core Idea
A general oath not to pray is broken by one rak'ah; an oath not to pray a specific prayer requires a valid two-rak'ah prayer.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the critical distinction between two types of oaths related to prayer. When Does an Oath Not to Pray Break? The Final Sitting and Leading
Explains when an oath not to pray is broken, focusing on sitting for tashahhud and leading prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to pray a specific prayer breaks, distinguishing between two-rak'ah and four-rak'ah prayers, and identify the conditions for breaking an oath regarding leading prayer.

Core Idea
An oath not to pray breaks after sitting for two-rak'ah prayers, but before sitting for four-rak'ah prayers; leading breaks only with intention except in Jumu'ah.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school: when exactly does an oath not to pray become broken? The placement of the final sitting (tashahhud) and the number of rak'ahs affect the outcome. Additionally, the issue of leading prayer (imamah) introduces subtleties about intention and declaration. Understanding these details is crucial for accurate fatwa and personal practice.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between two-rak'ah and four-rak'ah prayers
Identify the number of rak'ahs of the prayer in the oath. The oath breaks at different points: after sitting for two-rak'ah prayers, but before sitting for four-rak'ah prayers (for general oaths).
Two-rak'ah: Fajr; four-rak'ah: Zuhr, Asr, Isha.
2
Understand the nature of the final sitting
Analyze whether the final sitting (qa'dah) is an essential pillar (rukn) or an additional element. The Hanafi view considers it an additional closing element, not a rukn, which affects when the oath breaks.
Al-Zahiriyyah views it as a pillar; Fath al-Qadir considers it additional.
3
Apply the ruling to specific oaths
Differentiate between a general oath ('I will not pray a prayer') and a specific oath ('I will not pray Zuhr'). For general oaths on four-rak'ah prayers, the oath breaks before sitting; for specific oaths on Zuhr, it breaks only after sitting.
The wording of the oath matters: 'prayer' vs 'Zuhr'.
4
Analyze oaths related to leading prayer
Consider intention and public declaration. If a person swears not to lead, leading breaks the oath only if he intends to lead. A declaration (ishhad) protects him judicially, except in Jumu'ah where intention alone suffices.
Leading in funeral prayer or sajdah tilawah does not break an oath on absolute prayer.

Commentary
This passage addresses two major issues. First, if someone swears not to perform a prayer (e.g., Zuhr), does the oath break before completing the final sitting (qa'dah) for tashahhud? The author presents an ikhtilaf: if the oath was on the mere act of praying, the breaker does not break until sitting; if on the obligatory prayer of two rak'ahs, similarly; but for four-rak'ah prayers, he breaks even without sitting. There is a dispute between the view in al-Zahiriyyah and that of Fath al-Qadir. The author resolves by stating that the sitting is not a true pillar but an additional element for closing the prayer, so it should not affect oath-breaking. However, the relied-upon position may vary. Second, regarding oaths about leading prayer (imamah): if one swears not to pray behind someone, or swears that no one will pray behind him, various scenarios are discussed. Intentions matter: if he intends not to lead, and declares it publicly before prayer, he is not considered to have led even if people follow him. However, in Friday prayer, public declaration does not suffice; his intention alone matters. Leading in funeral prayer or prostration of recitation does not break the oath because these are not considered 'absolute prayer' (salat mutlaqah). Leading in supererogatory prayer does break the oath. The passage also clarifies that the desire may be different for various writings; the author sides with the view that the oath on a four-rak'ah prayer breaks before the sitting.

Oath Not to Pray Behind a Specific Person: Cases and Timing
Rulings on oath not to pray behind a specific imam in various situations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on when an oath not to pray behind a specific person is broken, including cases of hadath, sleep, and catching part of the prayer.

Core Idea
If you swear not to pray behind someone, breaking the oath depends on the extent of your participation in the prayer.

This passage details various scenarios where a person swears not to pray a specific prayer behind a particular imam. The rulings hinge on whether the person physically follows the imam's prayer, and if so, for how much of it. The text distinguishes between cases where the person completes the prayer with the imam versus when they miss part due to hadath or sleep.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between 'behind' and 'with'
The text uses two phrases: 'خلف فلان' (behind so-and-so) and 'مع فلان' (with so-and-so). Both indicate praying in congregation with that imam.
2
Analyze the hadath case
If the person had an event (hadath) and left, then returned after the imam finished and completed alone, he does not break the oath because he did not complete the prayer with the imam.
3
Analyze the sleep case
If the person slept and missed a rak'ah but then woke and followed the imam for the rest, he breaks the oath because he effectively prayed with him after the missed part.
4
Consider the Jumu'ah substitution
If the imam became invalid, and the person stepped forward and led Zuhr instead of Jumu'ah, he does not break the oath because he did not pray Jumu'ah with the original imam.
5
Consider the 'by the prayer of' (bilingual) oath
If the person swore not to pray Zuhr 'بصلاة فلان' (by the prayer of so-and-so), entering with the imam and catching even one rak'ah breaks the oath.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on rulings related to oaths concerning leading prayer or praying behind a specific person. It opens with a scenario: if a person swears not to lead anyone, then prays behind an imam, he breaks the oath unless he intends to pray with the imam without anyone else between them. The key principle is that the breaking of the oath depends on intention (niyya) and public declaration (ishhad). If he declares publicly before prayer that he is praying for himself, he does not break the oath either way. The passage also discusses specific cases: leading in funeral prayer or sajdat al-tilawa does not break the oath because the oath is directed to absolute prayer (regular salah), not these. Leading in nafl prayer breaks the oath even though leading in nafl is disliked. The author also addresses the timing of breaking the oath for specific prayers: if one swears not to pray a specific prayer like Zuhr, the oath is broken only after completing the tashahhud (sitting) of that prayer, according to al-Tatarakhaniyya. However, if the oath is tied to the mere act of praying (e.g., 'I will not pray any prayer'), then the oath is broken before the tashahhud. This is an intra-Hanafi difference: the author prefers the view that the knot (rak'ah) is an extra pillar, so the oath is broken earlier. Timing of Breaking an Oath: Before or After Tashahhud?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on timing of breaking an oath for a specific prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to pray a specific prayer is broken, distinguishing between general and specific oaths, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
If you swear not to pray any prayer, you break it before tashahhud; if you swear not to pray a specific prayer (e.g., Zuhr), you break it after tashahhud.

This passage tackles a key intra-Hanafi dispute: at what point is an oath not to pray a particular prayer considered broken? The author prefers that if the oath is on the mere act of praying (general), it is broken as soon as the person engages in the prayer, specifically before the final sitting. But for a specific prayer like Zuhr, the oath is broken only after completing tashahhud. This difference arises from whether the 'knot' (rak'ah) is considered an extra pillar or integral to the prayer.

Oaths on Prayer Timing: The Hila and the Delay
Rulings on oaths about prayer timing and congregation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a legal stratagem (hila) for an oath involving prayer and ghusl, and state the two opinions on whether a delayed but made-up prayer breaks an oath.

Core Idea
A carefully sequenced schedule can fulfill a restrictive oath without breaking it.

Oaths are binding, but Hanafi fiqh permits legal stratagems (hiyal) to fulfill an oath without violating its wording. This lesson examines a classic example from Imam Ibn al-Fadl: a man swears to pray five prayers in congregation, have intercourse, and not perform ghusl. How can he fulfill this? The solution lies in reordering actions across the day. We also explore rulings on oaths about delaying prayer and praying in mosques.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's components
The oath has three elements: (1) praying five prayers in congregation today, (2) having intercourse, (3) not performing ghusl. The challenge is that intercourse usually requires ghusl, which would break the oath.
Note the timing: 'today' includes the night? The text discusses this.
2
Determine the apparent impossibility
If the man has intercourse during the day and then prays, he would need ghusl, but he swore not to perform ghusl. How can he avoid breaking the oath?
The key is that ghusl is only required before prayer if he is in janabah. He can delay ghusl until after the day ends.
3
Apply the hila: reorder actions
The stratagem is: pray Fajr, Zuhr, and Asr in congregation (no ghusl needed yet), then have intercourse, then delay ghusl until after sunset (the day ends), then perform ghusl, then pray Maghrib and Isha. This way, he has prayed five prayers, had intercourse, and did not perform ghusl during the day.
The ghusl occurs after the day, which is not prohibited by the oath.
4
Verify the condition
The oath says 'today' – if we interpret 'today' as the day period, then ghusl after sunset is not inside the oath. The text mentions a nuance: if 'today' includes the night, the same sequence works as long as ghusl is after the day.
The author notes that this interpretation avoids breaking the oath on both possibilities.

Conditional Divorce Tied to Prayer: Intention and Credibility
Rulings on conditional divorce tied to prayer and credibility of prayer statements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on conditional divorce when the wife delays prayer, and explain when a person is believed in his oaths concerning prayer.

Core Idea
In conditional divorce, the occurrence of the condition depends on the husband's intention and the wife's actual act, and a person is believed in what is between him and Allah unless the matter is apparent.

This lesson explores two linked topics: conditional divorce (ta'liq) where the condition is prayer, and the credibility of a person's statement regarding prayer in oaths. The passage presents a clear ikhtilaf among Hanafi scholars on whether a wife who delays prayer and then makes it up triggers divorce. It also clarifies when a person is believed (yuṣaddiqu) regarding his prayer claims, distinguishing between inward sincerity and outward apparent matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional divorce scenario
Husband says: 'If you leave prayer, you are divorced.' The wife delays a prayer beyond its time but then makes it up. Does divorce occur?
The key term is 'leave' (taraka). Does it mean abandoning permanently or simply delaying?
2
Understand the ikhtilaf
Two opinions: (1) Al-Karmini says divorce occurs because delaying is a form of leaving. (2) Al-Sughdi says no divorce because making it up means she did not leave permanently. The text says al-Sughdi's view is 'al-ashbah wa al-azhar' (more similar and apparent).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Apply the tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative position) is the view of al-Sughdi: no divorce if she makes it up. However, some judges may rule otherwise.
The text does not explicitly say this is the final fatwa, but calls it 'more similar and apparent'.
4
Analyze the second conditional divorce scenario
Husband says: 'If you do not pray tomorrow morning, you are divorced.' The wife starts prayer but does not finish before sunrise. Al-Hulwani says no divorce (because she began), al-Sa'di says divorce occurs (because she did not complete). The text calls al-Sa'di's view 'al-azhar wa al-abyan' (most apparent and clearest).
Note the difference: here the condition is 'not pray' (i.e., failure to pray), while earlier it was 'leave prayer'.
5
Examine the credibility rulings (yuṣaddiqu)
When a person makes a statement about his prayer in an oath, he is believed in what is between him and Allah (i.e., inwardly) unless the matter is apparent and can be verified by others. For example, if he says 'I prayed today' meaning in congregation, he is believed. But if he says 'I prayed Zuhr' meaning in congregation, Imam Muhammad says he is not believed because prayer in congregation is an apparent matter.
This distinction affects the ruling on the oath: if he is believed, he does not have to pay kaffarah; if not, he must.

وعن محمد في رجل قال والله ما صليت اليوم يعني بجماعة قال : يصدق فيما بينه وبين الله تعالى وكذلك لو قال والله ما صليت اليوم ظهراً يعني ظهر أمس يصدق فيما بينه وبين الله تعالى. ولو قال والله ما صليت الظهر يعني بجماعة قال محمد لم يصدق عندي في هذا. ولو صلى الظهر في السفر ثم قال والله ما صليت ظهراً يعني ظهر وهو مقيم يصدق فيما بينه وبين الله تعالى اهـ.
And from Muḥammad regarding a man who said, 'By Allah, I did not pray today,' meaning in congregation: He said, 'He is believed between him and Allah Almighty.' Similarly, if he said, 'By Allah, I did not pray Zuhr today,' meaning yesterday's Zuhr, he is believed between him and Allah Almighty. And if he said, 'By Allah, I did not pray Zuhr,' meaning in congregation, Muḥammad said, 'He is not believed in my view in this.' And if he prayed Zuhr while traveling, then said, 'By Allah, I did not pray Zuhr,' meaning Zuhr while resident, he is believed between him and Allah Almighty.

Commentary
This passage from Imam Muḥammad al-Shaybānī discusses the role of intention in oaths. When a person swears an oath but has a specific intention that differs from the apparent meaning, his intention is accepted between him and Allah (meaning he is not sinful if the oath appears broken but his intention valid). However, in judicial matters, the apparent meaning might prevail. The examples: (1) Saying 'I did not pray today' but meaning 'in congregation' is accepted. (2) Saying 'I did not pray Zuhr today' but meaning yesterday's Zuhr is accepted. (3) But if he said 'I did not pray Zuhr' meaning 'in congregation', Muhammad says: He is not believed in this (because the default 'Zuhr' without qualification does not imply congregation). (4) If he prayed Zuhr while traveling (shortened) and swore he did not pray Zuhr, meaning while resident (full length), he is believed. The key principle: The intention must be plausible in the context; otherwise, it is not accepted even before Allah.

Key Terms
يصدق فيما بينه وبين الله تعالى (yuṣaddaq fīmā baynahu wa bayna Allāh ta‘ālā): His statement is accepted as true in his relationship with Allah, meaning no sin for breaking the apparent meaning if his intention is valid.
