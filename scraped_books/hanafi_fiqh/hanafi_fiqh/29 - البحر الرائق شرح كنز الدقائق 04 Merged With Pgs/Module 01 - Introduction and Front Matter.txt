Understanding the Three-Layer Structure of Hanafi Fiqh Texts
Front matter identification of text layers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three layers of a classical Hanafi fiqh text (matn, sharh, hashiya) and explain their relationship.

Core Idea
A Hanafi fiqh text often has three layers: matn (core text), sharh (commentary), and hashiya (gloss).

This lesson introduces you to the structure of a classical Hanafi fiqh work as seen in the front matter of Al-Bahr al-Raiq. You will learn to distinguish between the base text (matn), its commentary (sharh), and the super-commentary (hāshiya). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for navigating advanced Hanafi literature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text (matn)
Locate the name of the matn in the title: Kanz al-Daqa'iq. This is the concise text that forms the foundation. The author is al-Nasafi.
The matn is placed at the top of the page by the publisher for easy reference.
2
Identify the commentary (sharh)
Find Al-Bahr al-Raiq, written by Ibn Nujaym. This expands on the matn, explaining issues and providing evidence.
The sharh is printed directly below the matn.
3
Identify the gloss/ super-commentary (hāshiya)
Identify Nafhat al-Khaliq by Ibn Abidin. This provides further annotations, corrections, and clarifications on the sharh.
The hāshiya is placed in the margins or middle sections of the page.
4
Understand the page layout
The publisher's note explains the physical arrangement: matn at top, sharh immediately below, hāshiya in the middle/margins. This layout is typical for such editions.
Memorizing the layout helps you locate information quickly.
5
Recognise the scholarly lineage
Note the death dates: al-Nasafi (710 AH), Ibn Nujaym (970 AH), Ibn Abidin (1252 AH). This shows the chronological progression and the accumulation of commentary.
Each layer represents a later scholar building on earlier work.

Conditions for Becoming Umm Walad in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines conditions for umm walad status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for umm walad status and apply them to scenarios involving childbirth, ownership, and paternity.

Core Idea
Birth with fully formed limbs within six months + master's paternity = umm walad status

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi conditions under which a slave woman becomes an umm walad. The ruling is not merely about childbirth; it integrates paternity acknowledgment, timing, and ownership. Understanding these conditions is essential because the umm walad gains rights including automatic freedom upon the master's death—a status that overrides other arrangements like tadbir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is childbirth from the master's seed. The consequence is the slave woman becoming an umm walad, which grants her freedom upon the master's death and other rights.
The status is a legal consequence, not an act of worship.
2
Assess the child's formation condition
The child must be born with clearly formed limbs (مستبين الخلق) either in whole or in part. Partial formation suffices.
The Arabic phrase indicates visible human form, not necessarily a complete baby.
3
Apply the timing condition
Birth must occur within less than six months from the act that caused pregnancy (e.g., intercourse or insemination). If after six months, the status is not established.
This is based on a minimal pregnancy duration in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Confirm ownership and paternity
The master must own the slave girl wholly or partially at the time of birth (or later acquire her if the child is from him). Paternity must be established through acknowledgment or legal proof.
Partial ownership suffices; istilad is not divisible. Zina does not establish paternity.
5
Consider the scope of master and slave categories
The master can be Muslim or non-Muslim (dhimmi, apostate, musta'min). The slave includes both full-owned (qinn) and mudabbara. For a mudabbara, becoming umm walad voids the tadbir because umm walad status is more beneficial.
This shows the hierarchy of rights: umm walad > mudabbara in benefit.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the conditions under which a slave woman becomes an umm walad (mother of her master's child) in Hanafi fiqh. The core ruling: if she gives birth to a child with fully formed limbs (or even partial formation—meaning the limbs are discernible), she attains the status of umm walad. However, the timing of the birth relative to the act that caused pregnancy is crucial. If she gives birth within less than six months from that act (e.g., intercourse or even insemination by the master's fluid), the child is conclusively attributed to that act, and she becomes umm walad. If the birth occurs after six months or more, the child cannot be definitively linked to that act, so she does not gain the status. The author clarifies that 'birth from the master' is interpreted broadly: it includes not only direct intercourse but also any means by which the master's sperm reaches the woman's womb and she conceives. A key example from Abu Hanifa is given: if the master masturbates and the slave girl takes his semen and inserts it into her own vagina, and she conceives, then both paternity and umm walad status are established. Importantly, the text emphasizes that establishing paternity (thubut al-nasab) is a prerequisite for umm walad status. The master must acknowledge the child as his own, or it must otherwise be legally proven. The passage refutes an objection from Fath al-Qadir that the author omitted paternity, explaining that 'birth from him' can only be confirmed through acknowledgment. The master's status is also general: he need not own the slave girl at the time of conception; if he later acquires her, she becomes umm walad, as long as the child is from him. However, if the child is from zina (illegitimate), no paternity is established, so she cannot become umm walad even if he later owns her. Partial ownership also suffices because istilad is not divisible. The master can be a Muslim or a non-Muslim (including dhimmi, apostate, or musta'min). Finally, the category of 'slave girl' includes both an absolute slave (qinn) and a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death). Both are equal in terms of establishing paternity, but if a mudabbara becomes umm walad, the tadbir agreement becomes void because the rights of an umm walad—especially freedom upon the master's death from his entire estate—are more advantageous for her. She is freed automatically and does not need to work.

Key Terms
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has given birth to a child from her master, thereby gaining certain rights including freedom upon her master's death.
استيلاد (istilad): The act of causing a slave woman to give birth to his child, or the resulting status.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A slave who has been promised freedom upon the master's death (tadbir).

Placement and Definition of the Chapter on Oaths
Opening of Kitab al-Ayman explaining placement and definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the chapter on oaths follows emancipation and define yamin as a transferred term with its linguistic and technical meanings.

Core Idea
Oaths are unaffected by jest or duress; the term 'yamin' is a transferred term from the hand to the oath.

This lesson opens the chapter on oaths (Kitab al-Ayman). The commentator first justifies placing it after emancipation: both are immune to jest and duress, like divorce. Then he defines yamin linguistically and technically, showing it is not merely the physical hand but a term transferred for oath and strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the placement rationale
Find in the text why the chapter on oaths comes after emancipation. The text says both are unaffected by jest (hazl) and duress (ikrah), like divorce. Emancipation is placed first because it is closer to divorce (both involve annulment).
This establishes the fiqh principle that serious legal effects occur even if the speaker is joking or forced.
2
Understand the linguistic meaning of yamin
The word 'yamin' has multiple literal meanings: right hand, oath, strength. The text then explains why the oath is called yamin: for strength (the swearer strengthens himself) and because Arabs used to clasp hands when swearing. This shows it is a transferred term (manqul).
The transfer is from the physical hand or strength to the oath itself.
3
Define yamin technically
Technically, yamin is defined as a first emphatic declarative sentence (inventive) that confirms a subsequent sentence. It excludes cases of verbal emphasis (e.g., 'Zaid is standing, Zaid is standing') because there the first is not confirming the second; rather, the second is emphatically repeating the first.
This definition includes both verbal and nominal sentences, with specific structures.
4
Recognize the six nouns for oath
The text lists six words that carry this emphatic meaning: al-hilf, al-qasam, al-'ahd, and others (the passage cuts off but likely includes yamin, qasam, etc.). These are synonymous in the context of swearing.
Knowing these terms helps in identifying oaths in classical texts.

Commentary
This is the opening of Kitab al-Ayman (Chapter of Oaths) in the sharh. The commentator first explains the placement of this chapter after emancipation (Kitab al-Itq). The connection is that both emancipation and oaths, like divorce, are effective even if the speaker is joking or under duress; they are not negated by lack of serious intent. Emancipation was placed before oaths because it is closer to divorce (both involve annulment - isqat). Then the definition of yamin (oath) is given. Linguistically, yamin can mean the right hand, an oath, or strength. The oath is called yamin for two reasons: because the swearer strengthens himself by it, and because Arabs used to clasp right hands when swearing. From a fiqh perspective, yamin is a first emphasis sentence (inventive) that confirms a subsequent declarative sentence. The commentator gives an example to distinguish it from verbal emphasis (tawkid lafzi) like 'Zaid is standing, Zaid is standing'. The yamin can be in verbal or nominal forms. The nouns meaning 'oath' include six words: hilf, qasam, 'ahd, and others (the text is cut off, but likely yamin, qasam, hilf, 'ahd, etc.).

Key Terms
اليمين (al-yamin): Linguistically, can mean the right hand, oath, or strength. Technically, it is a first emphatic sentence that confirms a subsequent sentence, used to strengthen a statement by swearing by Allah or other revered things.
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or joking; statements made in jest that are not intended seriously.
