Emancipation Conditional on Hajj and Heirs' Rights
Rulings on slave's conditional emancipation through Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a slave freed conditionally on performing Hajj and identify the heirs' right to prevent the slave based on their ownership share.

Core Idea
A slave's conditional freedom upon Hajj creates competing rights: the slave's right to freedom and the heirs' right to service for the unfreed portion.

This passage from the commentary of Kanz al-Daqaiq presents a series of scenarios involving a slave who is promised emancipation if he performs Hajj on behalf of his deceased master. The central legal tension is between the slave's opportunity to earn freedom and the heirs' property rights over the slave's value and service. By working through these cases, you will learn how Hanafi fiqh balances these interests with precise conditions and limitations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Read the first case: the master gives the slave an amount equal to the cost of Hajj and says he is free if he performs Hajj. Note the heirs' role and the distribution of the amount used.
2
Determine the slave's status
Understand that the slave is partially free due to the will (one-third) and partially owned by the heirs (two-thirds). This fractional ownership is the key to heirs' rights.
3
Analyze the heirs' right to detain
In the second scenario where no other property exists, the heirs can prevent the slave from going for Hajj and keep him in service for a period equivalent to the value of two-thirds of the Hajj expenses.
This is because the unfreed portion gives them a right to the slave's service as compensation.
4
Examine the options and limitations
Study the sub-scenarios: slave's refusal to go, request to postpone, the judge's role if delay is impossible, and the effect of the master specifying a time.
5
Summarize the principles
Conclude that the heirs' right is limited to recouping the unfreed portion's value through service, and it ceases once the slave performs Hajj and becomes fully free.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary explains a set of rulings about a slave who is promised emancipation conditional on performing Hajj on behalf of his deceased master. The core issue is the conflict between the slave's right to freedom through performance of the condition and the heirs' right to the slave's value and service. The passage first considers a case where the master gives the slave an amount equal to the cost of Hajj and says he is free if he performs Hajj. If the heirs agree and the slave uses the entire amount for Hajj, two-thirds of that amount belongs to the heirs (because the slave's value is shared: one-third of the slave is already free due to the will, and two-thirds remains the heirs' property). The remaining one-third is used to perform Hajj on behalf of the deceased from wherever the funds reach. Then the passage discusses the scenario where the master simply says 'Perform Hajj after my death and you are free' without leaving any other property. The heirs can prevent the slave from going for Hajj and keep him in service for a period equivalent to the value of two-thirds of what is needed for the journey, because the slave's freedom is conditional on performing Hajj, but until then he is partly owned by the heirs. The heirs have a right to his service for that portion. If the slave insists on going in the current year, the heirs can detain him; but if a year passes without them demanding, he may go in the second year. However, if the master specified a particular year or a delay of five years, the heirs cannot prevent him from going at that specified time.

Oath to Walk to the Kaaba: Rulings and Conditions
Rulings on oaths involving walking to the Kaaba for Hajj or Umrah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person vows to walk to the Kaaba for Hajj/Umrah and then rides, and identify the conditions under which dam is required.

Core Idea
An oath to walk to the Kaaba obligates Hajj or Umrah on foot; riding incurs a dam.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a common type of vow: swearing to walk to the Kaaba in the context of Hajj or Umrah. The Hanafi school clarifies that such an oath creates a binding obligation to perform one of the two pilgrimages on foot, and if one rides instead, a penalty (dam) is due. The ruling is based on customary understanding ('urf) and the idea that riding introduces a deficiency into the ritual. The text also explores nuances such as the starting point of walking and the status of alternative phrasings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is vowing to walk to the Kaaba for Hajj or Umrah. The ruling is that it obligates one of the two rituals on foot; riding then necessitates a dam (shedding blood).
2
Locate the primary proof (dalil)
The proof is customary usage ('urf): people commonly understand such an oath as requiring Hajj or Umrah on foot. Additionally, riding introduces a deficiency in the ritual, hence the penalty.
The passage mentions that the obligation is not from literal meaning but from custom.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'walking to the Kaaba' has become a 'customary reality' (haqiqa 'urfiyya) indicating one of the two pilgrimages. Riding negates the walking requirement, which damages the perfection of the ritual, thus requiring compensation.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There is disagreement among Hanafis about whether walking is required during the journey to the Hill (for Umrah) or only after returning. The preferred view (al-azh) is that walking is required from the start.
The text states 'the correct view is that walking is required'.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The author relies on the view of Abu Hanifa as transmitted in al-Hidaya and Fath al-Qadir, and the mufta bihi is that the oath obligates one of the two rituals on foot, with dam upon riding.
The oath is considered immediate (yamīn al-fawr); delay may cause the obligation to lapse.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on an oath to perform Hajj or Umrah on foot to the Kaaba. The commentator explains that if someone vows 'upon walking to the House of Allah' or 'to the Kaaba', it obligates either Hajj or Umrah on foot. If the person rides instead, he must slaughter a sheep (dam) as a penalty, because riding diminishes the perfection of the ritual. The obligation is due to customary usage ('urf): people commonly understand such an oath as requiring one of the two pilgrimages on foot. The oath includes walking from the person's house from where he enters ihram. If the person is in Makkah, he may choose to make it Hajj (by going to Arafat on foot) or Umrah (by going to the Hill and entering ihram). There is a difference of opinion whether walking is required during the journey to the Hill, but the preferred view is that it is required. The reason for the dam upon riding is that riding introduces a deficiency. The commentary clarifies that this applies only to walking to the Kaaba itself; if the oath specifies walking to its curtains, door, etc., no specific obligation follows. The last part mentions that such an oath is considered immediate (fawr), and if the person delays, the obligation may lapse, using an analogy of a pigeon that returns after flying.

The Obligation of Walking to the Kaaba: Custom as Legal Proof
Commentary on the matn concerning vowing to walk to the Kaaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a vow to walk to the Kaaba, identify the proof based on custom ('urf), and apply the distinction between binding and non-binding destinations.

Core Idea
A vow to walk to the House of Allah obligates Hajj or Umrah walking by custom, not literal meaning; riding requires a sacrifice.

This lesson unpacks a ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq: if someone vows to walk to the Kaaba or the House of Allah, they must perform Hajj or Umrah walking; if they ride, they owe a sacrifice (dam). The commentary explains that this obligation is not based on the literal words, but on a customary transformation ('urf) that turns a metaphor into a binding legal reality. You will explore how custom functions as a proof in Hanafi law and how it determines the scope of the vow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Determine what the person vowed to do (walk to the Kaaba/House of Allah) and what the law requires: walking plus performing Hajj or Umrah, and a dam if they ride.
The vow is a nadhr, making the act wajib by commitment.
2
Locate the proof for the ruling
The passage states the obligation is not from literal meaning, metaphor, or frequency, but because it 'became customary' (ta'awwafa) to obligate one of the two rites by such a phrase. Identify this as a proof based on 'urf.
Custom ('urf) is a valid source of law in the Hanafi madhhab.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Understand how custom transforms the phrase: 'walking to the House of Allah' has come to signify the obligation of Hajj or Umrah, not just literal walking. This is a haqiqah 'urfiyyah (customary reality).
The key Arabic phrase: 'بل لأنه تعورف إيجاب أحد النسكين به فصار مجازاً لغوياً حقيقة عرفية'.
4
Note the scope and exceptions
The ruling applies only when the vow mentions 'House of Allah' or 'Kaaba' generally. Vows to walk to specific parts (curtains, door, gutter) or to Arafat/Muzdalifah do not obligate anything. Also, walking to the Haram or Safa/Marwa is excluded.
This precision comes from the matn and commentary: 'بخلاف الخروج أو الذهاب إلى بيت الله أو المشي إلى الحرم أو الصفا والمروة'.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text mentions that the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) agree on the obligation but differ on whether walking from the start of the journey is required. Abu Hanifah's view is that walking is required from the point of ihram, but the preferred view (as in al-Hidayah) requires walking from the beginning.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school; the mufta bihi is the more stringent view.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn from Kanz al-Daqaiq concerning a vow to walk to the House of Allah or the Kaaba. The matn states that if someone vows to walk, they must perform Hajj or Umrah walking; if they ride, they must offer a sacrifice (dam). The commentary explains that this ruling is not based on the literal meaning of the words but on customary usage ('urf), which has transformed the phrase into a customary reality (haqiqah 'urfiyyah). The commentator clarifies that the obligation applies whether the person is in the Kaaba or elsewhere, and the rider must sacrifice because riding introduces a deficiency into the vow. The commentary further explains that if the one who vowed is in Mecca, they must walk from the Haram to Arafat for Hajj or to the Hill for Umrah. There is a dispute about whether walking is required only from the point of ihram or from the start of the journey; the preferred view is that walking is required from the beginning, as per a ruling of Abu Hanifah. The commentary also notes that vows to walk to specific parts of the Kaaba or other places do not obligate anything; only the general walking to the House of Allah triggers the obligation. The word 'at the end discusses that the two imams (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that one of the two rites is incumbent, and that this oath is one of immediate effect (yamin al-fawr), not a delayed one.

حقيقة عرفية (haqiqah 'urfiyyah): Customary reality: a term that has acquired a new legal meaning through widespread custom, becoming the primary meaning in legal contexts.
