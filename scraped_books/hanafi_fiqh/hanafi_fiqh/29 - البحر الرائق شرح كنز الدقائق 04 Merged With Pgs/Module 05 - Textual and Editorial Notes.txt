Learning Objective

Core Idea
A pregnant woman's 'idda ends with delivery, so counting months applies only to the menopausal woman.

In this brief passage, the commentator of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines a textual discrepancy in the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The printed copies read 'if he divorces the pregnant woman' but the logic of 'idda rules shows that the intended word is 'menopausal' (al-ha'il). This lesson walks through the reasoning behind this correction, which hinges on a fundamental Hanafi principle: a pregnant woman's 'idda is always by delivery, not by months.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent error
The matn says 'al-hamil' (pregnant). The commentator flags it as suspicious because the context of counting months does not apply to her.
The error is detected by a substantive legal inconsistency, not mere philology.
2
Understand the proposed correction
The commentator suggests 'al-ha'il' (menopausal) instead. This word is often written with a hamza and refers to a woman who has passed childbearing age.
In some scripts, 'al-hamil' and 'al-ha'il' can look similar, especially without diacritical marks.
3
Connect to the 'idda rule
Recall that in Hanafi fiqh, a pregnant woman's 'idda (waiting period) ends at delivery, regardless of divorce or death of husband. A menopausal woman's 'idda is three months (for divorce) or four months and ten days (for death). The matn's discussion of 'middle of the month' only makes sense for a menstrual or monthly-based 'idda.
This is a key substantive proof: the matn must refer to a woman whose 'idda is counted in months.
4
Appreciate the commentator's advice
He ends with 'tammal' (reflect), inviting the reader to critically examine the text and understand why the correction is necessary.
This is a typical method in Hanafi commentaries: using legal reasoning to verify textual transmission.

The commentator notes that specification (takhsis) is better than claiming abrogation (naskh). This is a Hanafi preference: reconciling texts by applying each to a different context rather than nullifying one.

Definition of 'Itq: Linguistic and Legal Dimensions
Definition and linguistic analysis of emancipation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of 'itq and explain the linguistic debate over whether it means 'exit from ownership' or 'strength'.

Core Idea
Emancipation ('itq) in Hanafi law is the exit from slavery, not merely granting strength; the verbal expression is its pillar.

In this passage, the author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks the definition of 'itq as presented in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He clarifies why 'itq is placed after divorce, categorizes it as one of four types of isqat (annulment), and then dives into a philological debate: does 'itq linguistically mean 'strength' or 'exit from ownership'? Understanding this definition is crucial because the legal effects of emancipation hinge on it being a complete removal of the master's property right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's definition and the author's expansion
The matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) defines 'itq as 'establishment of legal strength'. The author of Al-Bahr expands by noting this is an annulment of a right (isqat al-haqq). Identify the four types of isqat mentioned.
The categories: 'itq (slavery), talaq (marriage), bara'ah (debt), 'afw (retaliation).
2
Analyze the linguistic evidence for 'exit from ownership'
The author cites philological usages: a slave 'ataqa (became free), a mare 'ataqat (outran), a bird chick 'ataqa (flew). These all imply exit, not strength.
The masdar is 'itq and 'itaq, not to be confused with 'ataqah (old age) or 'itq (beauty).
3
Address the opposing view: 'itq as strength
Some Hanafi scholars (Al-Nahr, Al-Mabsut) argue that since riqq (slavery) means weakness, 'itq must mean strength because it removes weakness. Evaluate the author's rebuttal.
The author insists philologists never said a'taqa for 'became strong'; strength is only metaphorical in bird flight.
4
Identify the rukn (pillar) of emancipation
The rukn is the verbal expression that clearly indicates the intention to free, as stated in Al-Bada'i'. The passage also notes the cause (sabab) may be lineage, ownership of a relative, or acknowledgment.
Conditions: the owner must be free and legally responsible, and the slave must be his property.

Rulings on Comparative Expressions of Emancipation
Ruling on using comparative form for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the judicial and religious effects of using comparative forms in declarations of emancipation and apply the Hanafi rule regarding intention.

Core Idea
Comparative expressions do not emancipate in court but are valid religiously if intended.

This passage from Kitab al-Itq addresses the legal weight of utterances that are not the standard explicit wording for emancipation. In the Hanafi school, the court requires explicit wording; ambiguous expressions require proof of intention to have any legal effect. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of emancipation in both judicial and private contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wording and its classification
Determine what type of utterance is being considered: here, the comparative form (أفعل التفضيل) like 'you are more free'. This is not explicit (sarih) for emancipation.
Explicit wording for emancipation is 'you are free' (أنت عتيق/حر).
2
Determine the judicial ruling (qada')
From the sources al-Khaniyya and al-Zahiriya: such a statement does not effect emancipation in court. The court requires clear, unambiguous wording.
The court will not enforce emancipation based on ambiguous wording.
3
Determine the religious ruling (diyanatan)
If the speaker actually intended emancipation, it is valid before Allah (diyanatan). The speaker is believed in private conscience, but the court does not intervene.
The distinction between qada' and diyanatan is fundamental in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars (as in al-Mujtaba) hold that emancipation occurs without intention, but the relied-upon position requires intention.
The mufta bihi is the requirement of intention for judicial effect.
5
Apply the distinction to similar formulations
Contrast 'you are freed by so-and-so' (emancipates) with 'I freed you by so-and-so' (does not emancipate, as it is mere information).
The ruling depends on whether the statement is an explicit declaration or a report.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Itq addresses the legal effect of using the comparative form (أفعل التفضيل) in declarations of emancipation. In the Hanafi school, such wording does not effect emancipation in a court of law (qada'an) because it is not the explicit wording for emancipation. However, if the utterer actually intended emancipation, it is valid religiously (diyanatan) — i.e., he is to be believed before Allah, but the court will not enforce it unless there is corroborating evidence. The text also mentions a variant from al-Mujtaba regarding saying to a slave 'you are more free than so-and-so', and if the utterer intends both emancipation and divorce, both take effect. There is also a ruling about saying 'you are freed by so-and-so': this constitutes emancipation of the slave by the speaker, because it is an acknowledgment that the speaker has emancipated him through that statement. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, explaining the ruling that calling a slave 'O my son' or 'O my brother' does not effect manumission. The author begins by discussing a related case: if a slave is sent with instructions to say 'I am free' when asked, and the master previously named him 'Free', then the slave is not freed either judicially or religiously; but if the master did not name him, the slave is freed judicially but not religiously. The call 'O my son' is mere notification because the master cannot make someone his son at that moment. The author notes a shadhdh (anomalous) narration from Abu Hanifa that manumission does occur, but the relied-upon position (al-mu'tamad) is the contrary, as stated in al-Hidayah. The ruling extends to all familial relationships (father, grandfather, uncle, aunt, etc.), and the default is that manumission does not occur without intention. The discussion then moves to declaring someone as a relative by report: saying 'this is my brother' does not free the slave because 'brother' is ambiguous (it could mean religious brotherhood). However, if one specifies 'from my father' or 'from my mother' (making it specific), it becomes a kinayah (allusive expression) and requires intention to effect manumission. The Ruling on 'La Sultana' and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the ruling on the phrase 'la sultana li alayk' and the dispute over its effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi ruling on the phrase 'la sultana li alayk', explain the reasoning for the majority view, and evaluate the opposing position within the school.

Core Idea
Negating authority (sultan) does not negate ownership; therefore 'la sultana' does not free a slave, even with intention.

The expression 'la sultana li alayk' (I have no authority over you) is one of those subtle phrases where intention alone may not be enough to effect manumission. In this lesson, we explore an intra-Hanafi dispute: does this statement free a slave if the master intends it? The majority, as recorded in al-Hidayah, holds that it does not, because authority (sultan) can exist without ownership. However, some later mashayikh and even the three Imams (according to one narration) hold the opposite. We will dissect the reasoning behind both positions and understand why the majority view is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Majority Hanafi Position
State the ruling from al-Hidayah: the phrase 'la sultana li alayk' does not effect manumission even if the master intends it. Understand the key reason: 'sultan' refers to the master's physical power (yad), and ownership can remain without yad, as in the case of a mukatab slave.
The mukatab is a slave who has contracted to earn his freedom; the master retains ownership but not physical control.
2
Recognize the Opposing View and Its Basis
Note that some Hanafi scholars, including the three Imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) according to one narration, hold that 'la sultana' does effect manumission with intention. Their argument: 'sultan' means dominance (istila'), so negating dominance implies negating ownership, making it equivalent to negating 'sabil' (path/ownership).
This view is recorded in Fath al-Qadir and attributed to al-Karkhi.
3
Analyze the Key Linguistic Distinction
Examine the difference between 'sultan' and 'sabil'. The majority holds that 'sultan' (authority/power) is not the same as 'sabil' (path/ownership). Negating 'sabil' clearly negates ownership because it is directly linked to it. Negating 'sultan', however, only negates physical control, which can be separated from ownership. The opposing view equates the two by using metaphorical interpretation.
Al-Karkhi himself admitted that the difference was not clear to him, highlighting the subtlety.
4
Understand the Tarjih and Mufta Bihi
Conclude that the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view from al-Hidayah: 'la sultana' does not effect manumission. This is because the linguistic basis for the opposing view is considered insufficient to override the literal meaning, and the analogy to the mukatab is strong.
The tarjih authority is al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Divorce words do not free a slave because talaq removes a restraint while i'taq grants capability—two distinct legal realities.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a key distinction between the legal institutions of marriage and slavery in Hanafi fiqh. Even if a master intends manumission, the word 'taliq' (divorce) is legally incapable of effecting it. Understanding the underlying qiyas will sharpen your ability to distinguish between acts that only differ in intention and those that are fundamentally different in nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the specific act? The master says 'anti taliq' (you are divorced) to his female slave, intending to free her. The Hanafi ruling (our madhhab) is that this does NOT effect manumission.

2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a qiyas based on linguistic analysis. The author contrasts the definitions: i'taq (manumission) means establishing power (qudra) in the slave, while talaq (divorce) means removing a restraint (qayd).
The slave is like an inanimate object; manumission gives him life and capability. A married woman is already capable, but the marriage contract restrains her; divorce removes that restraint.

The linguistic distinction shows that 'talaq' is inherently unsuited to effect manumission because it only removes a barrier, whereas i'taq requires granting a new capacity. The expression 'taliq' cannot bear the meaning of manumission even metaphorically (though the text hints at a metaphorical possibility, it is not relied upon).
The author states: 'the word may be suitable metaphorically' but the madhhab holds that it does not.

Abu Yusuf narrates a view that if the master intends manumission, the divorce expression does effect it. This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.

The majority view is preferred because it is based on a sound qiyas that respects the distinct legal realities of marriage and slavery. The Free Child of Two Slave Parents
Ruling from al-Zahiriyya on a free child from two slave parents without emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific scenario in which a child of two slave parents is born free without emancipation and explain the reasoning based on paternal lineage.

Core Idea
A child can be born free even when both parents are slaves, if the father is a slave of a free person and the mother is that free person's slave-girl.

This passage from al-Zahiriyya presents a fascinating exception to the general rule that a child's status follows the mother. In a specific arrangement, lineage to a free person grants freedom to the child without any emancipation. The commentator also critiques the wording of the matn, highlighting a key distinction between the unborn and the born child.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the General Presumption
The default ruling is that a child of two slaves is a slave. This case is an exception that requires specific conditions.
2
Outline the Specific Facts
A free man has a son who is a slave of another. The free man marries his own slave-girl to his slave son with the master's permission. The slave-girl gives birth.
The key actors: free man, his slave son, his slave-girl, and the slave son's master.
3
Determine Why the Child is Free
The child is free because he is the offspring of the free man's son (the slave father), making him the grandson of a free man. The master has no claim over the child, as the father is not the master's slave but belongs to the free man's line.
The child's lineage to the free grandfather overrides the mother's slave status.
4
Distinguish Original vs Acquired Freedom
The freedom here is 'original freedom' (hurriyah asliyyah), not acquired through emancipation. The passage clarifies that this is different from cases where emancipation of the mother frees the child.
Original freedom is inherent; acquired freedom results from an act of emancipation.
5
Understand the Textual Critique
The commentator notes that the author of Kanz al-Daqaiq should have used 'haml' (fetus) instead of 'walad' (child) because the principle of following the mother applies only during pregnancy. After birth, the child does not automatically follow the mother's status.
This nuance affects rulings on emancipation and inheritance.

Dispute Between Master and Mudabbara Over the Child's Status
Intra-Hanafi dispute over testimony acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for whose testimony is accepted when a master and his mudabbara (or freed slave) disagree about the birth timing of her child, distinguishing between tadbir, itq, and kitabah.

Core Idea
The master's word is taken in tadbir; in itq, possession decides; and if the child can speak, his word prevails.

This passage from al-Bada'i and other Hanafi works lays out the nuanced rules for resolving disputes between a master and his mudabbara or freed slave regarding a child's birth. The ruling varies based on the type of emancipation contract and on who physically holds the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Determine whether the case involves tadbir (promise of freedom after death), itq (immediate emancipation), or kitabah (contract of manumission).
The ruling changes with each contract.
2
Apply the default rule for tadbir
In tadbir, the master's word is taken because both agree the child is a slave by origin. The mudabbara's evidence is preferred if she can produce witnesses.
The mudabbara remains in the master's possession.
3
Apply the rule for itq (emancipation)
In itq, judgment is based on possession: if the child is with the mother, her word is taken; if with the master, his word is taken. If in both hands, her word is taken because she claims the nearest time of birth.
The apparent situation supports the possessor.
4
Consider the child's testimony
According to a narration from Muhammad, if the child can speak for himself, his word is taken as the authoritative statement.
This overrides possession.
5
Apply the rule for kitabah
In kitabah, the mother's word is taken, similar to itq when the child is in both hands.
Kitabah gives the mother more autonomy.

Distinguishing Milk and Riqq: Ownership vs. Legal Incapacity
Definitional distinction between milk and riqq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between milk (ownership) and riqq (slavery) and list the legal effects of riqq on a slave's capacity.

Core Idea
Milk is the power to dispose; riqq is a legal incapacity that strips certain rights.

The author of al-Kanz attaches riqq to milk to highlight their contrast. Milk gives the owner the ability to transact initially, while riqq imposes a legal disability (ahliyyat al-ada') that prevents the slave from acting as a guardian, witness, judge, or owning property.

Guided Path
1
Define milk
Milk is the capacity to initiate transactions (al-qudra 'ala al-tasarruf ibtida'). This excludes guardians, executors, and agents who act on behalf of others.
This definition is unique to Hanafis.
2
Define riqq
Riqq is a legal incapacity (ajz hukmi) that prevents the slave from exercising guardianship, giving testimony, serving as a judge, and owning property. It is a disability imposed by the Law.
Riqq is not just physical subjugation but a legal status.
3
Identify the basis of riqq
Riqq arises because the Law has made the slave 'subject to ownership and exploitation' (al-'urda li al-tamalluk wa al-ibtidhal).
This is a positive legal construction, not a natural condition.
4
Note the dispute about riqq's nature
Scholars differed: is riqq a right of Allah or a right of the public? The passage suggests the first view, citing that disbelievers refused submission.
The passage cuts off, but this indicates theological underpinnings.

Definition of Yamin (Oath) Linguistically and Technically
Defines yamin linguistically and technically with conditions and synonyms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definitions of yamin and distinguish it from verbal emphasis and conditional suspension.

Core Idea
An oath is a declarative sentence invoking Allah's name to emphasize a second sentence; conditional suspensions are not oaths.

This lesson clarifies what constitutes an oath (yamin) in Hanafi fiqh. We begin with the linguistic origins of the term and its synonyms, then move to the precise technical definition that governs legal rulings. Understanding this definition is crucial because certain conditional phrases (ta'liq) are often mistaken for oaths but are treated differently under the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
The word yamin linguistically can mean limb, strength, or oath. The oath meaning is derived from the practice of clasping hands when swearing. Note the six synonyms: hilf, qasm, ahd, mithaq, ila', and yamin itself.
2
Understand the technical definition
Technically, a yamin is a first declarative sentence (insha'iyyah) in which the name of Allah or His attribute is mentioned, used to emphasize the content of a second sentence in the hearer's mind or to compel the speaker.
3
Distinguish from verbal emphasis (tawkid lafzi)
In verbal emphasis, the first sentence is emphasized by repeating it (e.g., 'Zayd is standing, Zayd is standing'). In an oath, the first sentence (the oath) emphasizes the second. This is a reversal of roles.
4
Recognize the exclusion of conditional suspensions (ta'liq)
Conditional suspensions like 'If you enter, then you are divorced' have a first sentence that is not declarative but conditional. Therefore, they are not true oaths in the technical sense, even though they share some emphatic function.
This distinction has practical consequences: breaking a conditional suspension does not require kaffarah (expiation), while breaking a real oath does.

Multiple Oaths and Their Expiations
Intra-Hanafi dispute on multiplicity of oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on multiple oath expressions and identify when multiple expiations are required according to Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
Multiple oath expressions with conjunction generally count as separate oaths requiring individual expiations.

This lesson explores a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether uttering multiple oath expressions in one sentence (e.g., 'Wallahi wa al-Rahman') creates one or multiple oaths. The discussion revolves around the interpretation of the conjunction 'waw' and its impact on the legal consequences of oath-breaking. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between different narrations within the Hanafi school and identify the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Zahir al-Riwayah)
According to Zahir al-Riwayah, if a person says 'Wallahi wa al-Rahman' (with conjunction), it counts as two oaths requiring two expiations.
This is the position adopted by most Hanafi scholars ('akthar al-mashayikh').
2
Identify the opposing view
Hasan ibn Ziyad narrated from Abu Hanifa that only one expiation is required when conjunction is used; the scholars of Samarqand followed this view.
Thus, there is a valid ikhtilaf within the school.
3
Follow the opposing proof
The opposing view argues that the waw could be a conjunction (waw al-‘atf), and an oath is not established by doubt or probability.
They prioritize the apparent meaning of the waw as conjunctive.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Zahir al-Riwayah responds that the waw may also be an oath waw (waw al-qasm), and since it is possible, the oath is established; certainty is not required to affirm an oath.
When the oath expression is repeated separately (with conjunction), the probability of it being an oath waw remains, so both are oaths.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: on Zahir al-Riwayah, two expiations are due; on the opposing view, one expiation.
The mufta bihi is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.

Oaths on Entering and Exiting a House: Detailed Rulings
Commentary expanding on Hanafi rulings for oaths of entering/exiting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a person breaks an oath of entering or exiting a house, and apply the Hanafi definition of 'entering' based on custom and physical separation.

Core Idea
Entering a house for an oath requires full bodily separation into the interior; partial presence on roof, threshold, or door does not break the oath without custom's recognition.

This lesson explores the detailed Hanafi rulings on oaths concerning entering and exiting a house. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies what actions constitute breaking such oaths, distinguishing between entering the house itself and merely being on its structure. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correctly determining when an oath is violated and expiation becomes due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Legal Question
What does it mean to 'enter' a house for an oath? The passage defines entry as complete separation from outside to inside. Any action that falls short of that, such as standing on the roof or outer threshold, requires careful analysis based on custom.
The definition is not purely physical; custom (urf) plays a central role.
2
Analyze the Threshold (Taq al-Bab) Ruling
Understand the distinction between the inner and outer parts of the threshold. Standing with both feet on the outer part does not constitute entry, but with one foot inside and weight on it may break the oath. This shows the Hanafi reliance on physical separation and weight distribution.
Al-Sarakhsi held that entry requires both feet inside; others consider the lower foot decisive.
3
Explore Roof/Wall/Tree Scenarios
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute: early scholars considered the roof, wall, or tree inside the house as part of it, so presence there breaks the oath. Later scholars (al-muta'akhkhirun) argued that custom does not label such actions as entry; therefore, the oath is not broken unless one enters the interior.
The text reconciles apparent contradictions by noting barriers that change custom.
4
Examine Special Structures (Dihliz, Courtyard, Tunnel, etc.)
Apply the same principles to other parts: entering the dihliz (entrance hall) is considered entering the house, but entering a tunnel under the house is not. The courtyard ruling depends on custom—in the author's time, fatwa considered it part of the house.
The garden inside the house breaks the oath if it is in the middle; a garden connected to the house does not.
5
Compare Oaths of Exiting
For oaths of exiting, the definition reverses: climbing the roof does not break the oath because one is still inside. The same threshold analysis applies in reverse for exiting.
The tree with branches outside the house is like a building; climbing it does not count as exiting.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, dealing with the legal rulings of oaths concerning entering and exiting a house. The text opens by discussing the scenario where someone swears not to enter a particular house. The author, Ibn Nujaym, explains the detailed rules and the differences among Hanafi scholars. The first issue is whether climbing onto the roof or standing on a wall inside the courtyard constitutes entering. According to the later Hanafi scholars (al-muta'akhkhirun), it does not break the oath because customarily one is not considered to have entered unless they enter the interior. However, the text indicates that the author (al-Marghinani or al-Quduri) holds that such actions do break the oath, reconciling the apparent contradiction by noting that if the roof has a barrier, it is like entering. The passage then covers various scenarios: entering the courtyard (sahn) with a specific intention, entering through a different door, entering via a tunnel (sardab) or canal (qanat), and exiting the house. Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq, explains the crucial distinction between oaths involving 'khurūj' (exiting) and 'ityān' (arriving). The passage contrasts going out to a distant city (Baghdad) with going out to a nearby funeral. The key point is that the legal evaluation depends on whether the act is considered a journey (safar) or a local movement. How Idafa and Ishara Define the Object of an Oath of Abandonment
Rule on idafa and ishara in oaths of hijr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath of abandonment with idafa alone versus idafa with ishara, and identify the exceptions for slaves and future relatives.

Core Idea
Without ishara, the oath binds while the idafa lasts; with ishara, it binds only the specified entity.

When someone swears to abandon a person or thing, how the object is defined determines when the oath is broken. This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the effect of using an addition (idafa) like 'so-and-so's slave' and whether the speaker adds a pointing word (ishara) like 'this'. The rule hinges on the motive: hatred of the owner (mudaf ilayh) or hatred of the object itself. We will walk through the consequences for existing and future entities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of idafa
Determine if the addition is of ownership (e.g., slave, house, animal) or of relationship (e.g., wife, friend). Both are considered idafa and define the entity through connection to the mudaf ilayh.
The author emphasizes that both types are idafa; there is no categorical distinction as some commentaries suggest.
2
Check for the presence of ishara
See if the oath includes a pointing word like 'this' (e.g., 'I will not speak to this slave of so-and-so'). If ishara is present, the entity is specifically identified at the time of the oath.
Without ishara, the entity is defined only by the idafa, which may change over time.
3
Apply the rule for idafa without ishara
The oath is presumed to be based on hatred of the mudaf ilayh (owner). Therefore, the oath is broken if the entity exists at the time of action and the idafa still holds. If the idafa ends (e.g., slave sold), the same entity is no longer covered. But if a new similar entity appears (e.g., new slave), the oath is broken.
Exception: if the object is intended for enmity itself (like a wife or friend), the reasoning may differ, but the default is hatred of the owner.
4

With ishara, the entity is fixed to what was pointed at. The oath does not break for a new similar entity unless the original entity was of a type that is typically intended for enmity (e.g., a friend). For a slave, the majority view is that it is not intended for enmity, so a new slave is not included.
There is a dispute: Ibn Sama'ah holds a slave is like a friend, so a new slave would break the oath.
5

The consensus is that future daughters are not included because the idafa at the time of marriage is to the daughter then existing? Oaths on Additive Plurals: The Two Types Distinguished
Distinguishing oaths on additive plurals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of additive plurals in Hanafi oath jurisprudence and apply the correct ruling for each type.

Core Idea
An oath on possessions is broken by three; an oath on persons requires all to be broken.

Oaths often reference groups of items or people using additive plurals (al-jam' al-mudaf), such as 'the mounts of Zayd' or 'the wives of Zayd'. Hanafi fiqh draws a critical distinction based on whether the oath's prohibition is directed at the attribution to a person or at the individuals themselves. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of additive plurals
Recognize that additive plurals in oaths fall into two categories: (1) those concerning possessions or attendants (e.g., mounts, clothes, slaves) where the prohibition is tied to the attribution to the owner, and (2) those concerning specific persons (e.g., wives, friends, siblings) where the prohibition is tied to the individuals themselves.
The type is determined by the reason for the prohibition, not the mere wording.
2
Apply the rule for type 1 (possessions)
For oaths like 'I will not ride the mounts of Zayd', breaking occurs if the oath-taker engages with any three of the named items, because the minimum for a plural is three and the oath is attached to the attribution, not the individuals.
This applies even if the owner has more than three items.
3
Apply the rule for type 2 (persons)
For oaths like 'I will not speak to the wives of Zayd', breaking occurs only if the oath-taker speaks to all of the named individuals, because the oath is attached to their persons.
The oath is interpreted as 'I will not speak to these specific people'.
4
Consider the role of intention
If the oath-taker intended the entire set in type 1 (e.g., all the mounts), his intention is accepted between him and Allah and in court, as per al-Ziyadat.
This allows for a more restrictive interpretation based on internal intent.
5
Note the fatwa hierarchy
The passage concludes with a procedural rule: when the Imam's (Abu Hanifa's) view is not explicitly recorded on a matter, fatwa must be given according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
This ensures consistency in issuing legal opinions.

Abu Yusuf's Distinction: Humans vs. Non-Humans in Plural Oaths
First two sub-rulings from Nawadir and al-Waqi'at on human vs. non-human plurals and knowledge condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a single act breaks a plural oath, applying Abu Yusuf's distinction between humans and non-humans and the role of the swearer's knowledge.

Core Idea
An oath on a plural of humans is not broken by one; for non-humans, one suffices — and knowledge changes the ruling.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths involving plurals have nuanced rulings depending on the subject and the swearer's awareness. This lesson unpacks two key principles from Abu Yusuf and al-Waqi'at: first, whether the plural refers to humans or property, and second, whether the swearer knew the true number of the referents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject category
Determine if the object of the oath is a group of humans (e.g., slaves, brothers) or non-humans (e.g., mounts, clothes). This determines the default rule.
Human plurals are treated collectively; non-human plurals are distributive.
2
Apply Abu Yusuf's human/non-human rule
If the subject is human, speaking to one individual does not break the oath. If non-human, performing the action on one item breaks it.
Example: 'slaves of Zayd' vs. 'mounts of Zayd'.
3
Consider the swearer's knowledge
If the swearer knew the plural was actually singular (e.g., 'brothers' but only one brother), speaking to that one breaks the oath. If unaware, it does not.
Analogous to swearing not to eat three loaves when only one exists.
4
Synthesize the ruling
Combine the subject category and knowledge condition to determine if the oath is broken and expiation is wajib.
For humans: collective rule applies regardless of knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents several additional rulings on oaths involving plurals. First, from Abu Yusuf via Nawadir: In oaths on human beings (e.g., 'slaves of Zayd'), one does not break the oath by speaking to a single individual; the oath applies to all. This is analogous to swearing not to eat three loaves from a pile that actually contains only one loaf, where the swearer does not break if unaware. Third, the text distinguishes between definite plurals (with alif lam) like 'al-fuqara' (the poor) and indefinite plurals like 'rijalan' (men). For definite plurals, if there is no specific customary referent, the oath applies to the genus (jins), and speaking to one breaks the oath. Finally, from al-Zahiriyyah: detailed distinctions based on wording, e.g., 'every day of this week' vs. 'in every day', and rulings from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Oath of Negation: Eternal Abstention Required
Commentary on rulings of oaths with eternal negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath of negation (yamin al-nafi) and explain why a single act constitutes breaking (hanth).

Core Idea
An oath of negation requires eternal abstention; doing the act once breaks the oath.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a fundamental distinction in oaths. If someone swears 'I will not do X' (e.g., 'I will not eat dates'), they must abstain forever. Performing the act even once triggers the obligation of expiation (kaffarah). The following guided steps will help you unpack this ruling and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath type
Determine whether the oath is a negation (e.g., 'لا أفعل كذا') or affirmation. Here we focus on negation.
The text explicitly marks negation with the phrase 'لا يفعل كذا'.
2
State the temporal scope
For a negation oath without a time limit, the abstention is eternal ('تركه أبدا'). The author derives this from the absolute nature of the negation.
If the oath is temporally restricted (e.g., 'today'), it expires after that time.
3
Define breaking the oath (hanth)
Hanth occurs when the proscribed act is performed at any time. Since the oath demands eternal omission, a single act violates it.
Contrast with an affirmation oath, where one fulfilment suffices.
4
Note the consequence
Upon hanth, the swearer must perform kaffarah (expiation) according to Hanafi rules.
The oath itself becomes void after the first breach; no further hanth for subsequent acts.
5
Consider exceptions
If the oath includes an exception (istithna') like 'إن شاء الله', or if fulfilment becomes impossible (e.g., death), the ruling changes.
These are detailed in other sections.

If he does it once, he breaks the oath (yahnath). Second, the ruling on an oath of affirmation (ليفعلنه): if someone swears to do something, he fulfills the oath by doing it once (bira bi-marrah). Even if he later omits it, no further oath is broken because only one action was pledged. The passage also discusses conditions like temporal restrictions (e.g., swearing for a specific day) and exceptions (istithna'). It includes a sub-discussion on oaths regarding debts, with legal stratagems (hiyal) to avoid breaking the oath. The text cites authorities like al-Zahiriyyah, al-Muhit, and mentions a difference with Abu Yusuf regarding the lapse of the object of the oath.

Key Terms
يمين (yamin): An oath, a solemn pledge to do or refrain from an act, often invoking Allah's name.
حنث (hanth): Breaking an oath by committing the prohibited act or omitting the required act.
بر (birr): Fulfilling the oath by doing the required act or refraining from the prohibited act.

The Duration of Unconditional Negation Oaths
Explains duration and dissolution of unconditional negation oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for an unconditional oath of negation, including its duration, dissolution upon first breach, and the linguistic rule about implied negation in affirmative oath phrases.

Core Idea
An unconditional oath not to do something binds forever until broken once; then it dissolves permanently.

When someone takes an oath not to perform an act without specifying a time period, how long does the obligation last? And what happens if the oath is broken? The Hanafi school provides clear guidance: the oath remains in force indefinitely until the first act of violation (hanth), at which point it dissolves entirely. This lesson unpacks the reasoning from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and corrects a common misunderstanding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath type
Read the passage and note the distinction between unconditional oaths (e.g., 'I will never do X') and time-bound oaths (e.g., 'I will not do X today'). Unconditional oaths imply perpetual negation.
The phrase 'la yaf'alu kadha' indicates absolute negation.
2
Understand the duration rule
For an unconditional oath, the obligation to abstain lasts forever until the act is actually performed. If the oath is time-bound, the obligation expires when the time passes without the act.
Death of either party also releases the oath because the possibility of action ceases.
3
Dissolution upon first breach
The text corrects a misconception: some thought the oath is not dissolved by action. The correct Hanafi position is that after the first act of hanth, the oath is dissolved (yanhall). Subsequent acts do not constitute a new breach.
This means expiation is required only once for the first hanth.
4
Analyze the linguistic point
The author notes that a phrase like 'By Allah, I will do such-and-such' is actually an oath of negation with an implied 'la' (not), because in affirmative oaths the emphasis particle cannot be dropped. This is a unique Hanafi view.
This affects the legal classification of the utterance.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the commentary clarifying the nature of unconditional oaths of negation. The author explains that when someone swears an absolute oath not to do something (e.g., 'By Allah, I will never do X'), the commitment is perpetual because the negation is unqualified. If the oath is time-bound, like 'I will not do it today,' then if the day passes without the act, the oath is considered fulfilled (birr). Similarly, death of either party ends the possibility of action, so the oath is discharged. The text also corrects a misconception: some thought that if the oath is dissolved by one act of breaking (hanth), subsequent acts would not break it again. But the correct Hanafi position is that once broken (by doing the act once), the oath is dissolved entirely; subsequent acts do not constitute a new breach because the obligation is gone. The passage further reviews a subtle point: a phrase like 'By Allah, I will do such-and-such' is actually an oath of negation with an implied 'la' (not), because in affirmative oaths the particle 'la' is not dropped; this is a unique Hanafi view.

Key Terms
يمين النفي (yamin al-nafy): An oath that negates an action, e.g., 'I will not do X.'
حنث (hanth): Breaking an oath by performing the act sworn not to do.
انحلال اليمين (inhilal al-yamin): Dissolution of the oath; it ceases to be binding.
لا مقدرة (la muqaddarah): An implied 'la' (not) in an apparently affirmative oath phrase.
