Oaths Regarding Hiring (Ijara) and Legal Stratagems (Makharij)
Rulings on oaths about hiring property and a legal strategy to avoid breaking an oath by selling and then hiring as an agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths related to hiring and apply the legal stratagem (makhraj) to avoid breaking such an oath.

Core Idea
A legal stratagem can avoid an oath on hiring by transferring ownership before the rental contract.

In this passage, we examine a situation where someone swears that if he rents out his house, it becomes charity. Later, he needs to rent it out. How can he avoid both breaking his oath and losing the property? The Hanafi scholars propose a legal stratagem (hila) involving a temporary sale and agency. This lesson unpacks that stratagem and the reasoning behind it, including a difference of opinion on its necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the problem
The swearer has made a conditional oath: if I rent out my house, it becomes charity. He needs to rent it out. He faces two undesirable outcomes: breaking the oath (requiring kaffarah) or fulfilling the charity condition (losing the property).
Note the condition: renting out triggers charity.
2
Understand the proposed stratagem (hila)
The text describes a multi-step process: sell the house to a third party, have that third party appoint the original owner as agent to rent it out, then after the rental, the original owner buys it back. This way, the rental occurs on the buyer's property, not the swearer's, thus avoiding the condition.
The key is that the rental is done on behalf of the new owner.
3
Analyze the alternative view
Some commentators argue that the stratagem is unnecessary because if the swearer appoints an agent to rent, he does not break his oath anyway. They suggest that the oath is only broken if the swearer himself performs the act.
This difference hinges on whether agency (wakalah) is considered the action of the principal or the agent in oath contexts.
4
Examine the broader list of actions
The passage lists many actions (writing, reconciliation, gift, etc.) and states that if one swears not to do them and orders another to do them, the oath is broken. This indicates that for these actions, agency does not shield from the oath. However, for hiring, the text seems to allow agency without breaking the oath.
This inconsistency suggests a special ruling for hiring contracts.
5
Apply the rule to a modern scenario
Consider a person who swears 'if I rent out my apartment, I will donate $1000 to charity.' Now he needs rental income. Applying the Hanafi stratagem, he can sell the apartment to a relative, have that relative appoint him as agent to lease it, collect rent, then buy it back. He avoids both the oath and the charity condition.
Ensure the sale is genuine and not a mere formality; Hanafi hilas require substantive transactions.

Commentary
This passage addresses oaths regarding hiring (ijara). The first part presents a hypothetical: someone swears that if he rents out his house, it becomes charity. To avoid both breaking the oath and losing the property, a legal stratagem (hila) is suggested: sell the house to a third party, then have that third party appoint the original owner as agent to rent it out. After the rental, the original owner buys it back. This way, the rental occurs on the buyer's property, not the swearer's. Some commentators argue this is unnecessary because if the swearer appoints an agent to rent, he does not break the oath anyway; but the distinction between a vow (nadhr) and an oath (yamin) is relevant. The text then cites al-Dhakhirah regarding a man who swears not to rent and his wife rents out his properties. The commentary from al-Ramli and others clarifies that the ruling depends on whether the oath was about a specific right or a general matter.

On page 583, the passage lists several actions (like writing, reconciliation for murder, gift, charity, etc.) and states that if one swears not to do them and then does them directly or orders someone else, the oath is broken. For actions like receiving rent and spending it, it does not break the oath if done by an agent. The passage also clarifies what constitutes a rental: leaving money in the hands of residents is not a rental; telling them to stay in the houses is a rental. Collecting rent for an unoccupied period is a rental, but paying rent for a period already occupied is not.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijarah): A contract for the use of a property or services in exchange for rent.
المخرج (al-makhraj): A legal stratagem or way out (hila) to avoid breaking an oath while still achieving the desired outcome.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath, swearing by Allah to do or not do something.
