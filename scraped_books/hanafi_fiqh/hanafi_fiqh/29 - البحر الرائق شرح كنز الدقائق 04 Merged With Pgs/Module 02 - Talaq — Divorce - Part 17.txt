Oath about Praying Fajr and Menses: Does Menstruation Break the Oath?
Ruling from al-Mubtagha on a conditional oath involving prayer and menses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a woman's oath to pray Fajr when she experiences menses and explain why she does not break the oath.

Core Idea
A woman does not break an oath to pray Fajr if she begins menstruating because a legal impediment makes performance impossible.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from al-Mubtagha concerning a conditional oath: a husband says to his wife, 'If you do not pray Fajr tomorrow, you are divorced.' What happens if she starts menstruating at the time of Fajr? Does she break the oath? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer based on the principle of legal capacity. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the oath
Who made the oath? What was the condition? What is the outcome? The husband makes a conditional oath: if the wife does not pray Fajr tomorrow, then something (implied divorce) occurs. The condition is her failure to perform a specific prayer at a specific time. She begins menstruating at dawn.
The oath is suspended on a future action.
2
Determine legal capacity
Is she capable of fulfilling the condition? A menstruating woman is legally prohibited from praying (based on the hadith: 'When she is menstruating, she does not pray'). Therefore, she is physically and legally incapable of performing the act at the appointed time.
The condition is impossible due to a shari'i excuse.
3
Apply the rule of impossibility
Does the impossibility excuse the breach? Since the condition is unfulfillable due to a legal impediment, the oath is not considered broken. This follows the Hanafi principle that oaths are not broken when the condition becomes impossible by a legitimate excuse.
This is the most correct view (al-ashahh); there is a weaker view that breaks the oath.

Oath to Have Intercourse Tonight After Dawn
Ruling on an oath to have intercourse 'tonight' made after dawn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default interpretation of 'tonight' in an oath made after dawn and apply the jar case analogy when the intention specifies the past night.

Core Idea
An oath sworn during the day referring to 'tonight' defaults to the future night unless the speaker intended the past night, in which case it is void.

This ruling clarifies how ambiguous time phrases in oaths are interpreted. When a husband swears after dawn to have intercourse 'tonight', does he mean the night just past or the coming one? The Hanafi school sets a clear default rule based on the time of the oath. Let's examine the text from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and its underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the key phrase
Note the condition: the husband says after dawn, "If I do not have intercourse with you tonight, you are divorced." He knows it is dawn and has no explicit intention.
The phrase 'this night' (هذه الليلة) is ambiguous – it could refer to the past night or the coming night.
2
Apply the default interpretation rule
Because the oath was made during the daytime, 'tonight' is interpreted as the coming future night. The condition must be fulfilled by the end of that night.
This is a linguistic default: when a time phrase is ambiguous, the future meaning is preferred for oaths of future action.
3
Consider the effect of explicit intention
If the husband intended the night that has already passed, the condition becomes impossible (the night is over). In that case, the oath does not take effect.
Intention overrides the default but leads to voidness, not a different time reference.
4
Recognize the juristic analogy (the jar case)
This scenario is a branch of the well-known 'jar case' (mas'alat al-kūz): if one swears to drink from an empty jar, the oath is void because the condition is impossible. Similarly, intending the past night makes the condition impossible, so the oath is void.
The common effective cause (illah) is the impossibility of fulfilling the condition.
5
Note the Hanafi authority and potential variant
The ruling is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf may have a different view (not mentioned in this text), but the default interpretation is relied upon in fatwa.
This consistency with the jar case shows the systematic use of qiyas in Hanafi law.

Oath to Bring Wife Home When She Was Already There
Ruling on an oath to bring wife home when she was already at home.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath that is impossible to fulfill and distinguish when a husband's confession renders the oath effective.

Core Idea
An oath to do something already done is void ab initio; only the husband's confession can revive it.

This passage from Bahru al-Ra’iq presents a classic case where a husband swears to bring his wife home, unaware she is already there. The Hanafi school gives a nuanced ruling: the oath is fundamentally void because the condition is impossible. However, if the husband later confirms her absence, his confession can trigger the divorce. This lesson examines the interplay between objective reality and subjective belief in oath enforcement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A man returns home after a brief absence. He mistakenly thinks his wife is not at home and swears: 'If I do not bring my wife to my house tonight, she is thrice divorced.'
Pay attention to the husband's belief versus objective reality.
2
Determine the objective fact
The wife was at home at the time of the oath. This means the condition 'if I do not bring her' is already satisfied—he cannot 'bring' someone who is already present.
The oath is based on an impossible condition.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule that the oath does not take effect (lam tan'aqid) because the condition is already fulfilled. An oath on a past or present event is void.
Contrast with the view that the husband's belief might matter.
4
Consider the second scenario
If the wife claims she was absent and the husband confirms, she is divorced. Why? Because the husband's confession constitutes an acknowledgment of the condition (being absent) and thus an admission that the oath was broken.
Objective reality is overridden by judicial confession.

An oath conditioned on an impossible past event is void — dawn's arrival makes sleeping 'tonight' impossible.

This passage addresses a scenario where a man swears an oath to sleep in a specific house 'tonight,' but dawn arrives without his knowledge. Since the night has passed, the condition of sleeping in that night can no longer be fulfilled. The ruling examines whether his ignorance of dawn affects the oath. The key principle is the impossibility of the condition due to the passage of time.

The author explicitly analogizes to an oath conditioned on a past event (e.g., fasting yesterday). If the condition is impossible from its inception or becomes impossible, the oath is not broken.

Note the consensus of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)

Oath to Perform Customarily Impossible Acts: Validity and Immediate Breach
Ruling on an oath to do customarily impossible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds an oath to perform a customarily impossible act as valid and immediately broken, and distinguish this from oaths that are impossible in reality.

Core Idea
An oath is valid if the condition is conceivable in reality, even if humanly impossible — breakage is immediate.

What happens when someone swears to climb to heaven or turn a stone into gold? These acts are impossible by custom, but are they impossible in reality? The Hanafi school draws a crucial distinction between conceptual impossibility and customary impossibility. This passage explains why such oaths are valid and broken at once, and how a time-bound oath or an oath on a truly impossible condition differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the legal act being ruled upon? (An oath to perform an action that is impossible by custom but not impossible in reality). What is the ruling? (The oath takes effect and the swearer breaks it immediately).
2
Examine the Hanafi proof
The text uses qiyas (analogy): The action is conceivable because angels ascend to heaven and Allah can transform matter. Just as a dying person's oath is broken despite the possibility of life, this oath is broken due to customary inability.
Key term: متصور (conceivable in reality).
3
Consider the opposing view (Zufar)
Zufar argues the oath is void because the action is impossible by custom, analogous to truly impossible acts like drinking from an empty jar.
The jar case: the water is absent at the moment of oath, making the condition inconceivable from the start.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes: customary impossibility does not negate real possibility. The condition is still conceivable, so the oath is valid. The jar case is different because the object (water) is missing at the oath, so the condition is inconceivable from the outset.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi says expiation is required; Zufar says none.
5
Apply the distinction to time-bound oaths
If the oath is time-bound (e.g., 'by tomorrow'), the breakage occurs after the time expires, not immediately. If the swearer dies before the time, no expiation because the condition never became due.
This shows the ruling is limited to absolute oaths (مطلقة) without a time limit.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a group swears to pay a tax, and one person pays it entirely, the others' oath becomes void because the condition is impossible to fulfill.

This passage from al-Waqi'at addresses a scenario where the sultan imposes an oath on a community to pay a land tax by a deadline. If one person, whether from the group or an outsider, pays the entire amount without the others' authorization, what happens to the others' oaths? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule that they do not break their oaths because the debt is extinguished, making fulfillment impossible. This ruling illustrates a key Hanafi principle: when the subject of an oath becomes impossible due to an external act, the oath is void.

Analyze why: Because the tax debt is fully satisfied, the condition for the oath (paying the tax) can no longer be fulfilled by the others. Hence, the oath is void due to impossibility.
The key term 'مؤقتة' (time-bound) indicates the oath has a deadline, but the void arises from impossibility, not from expiry.
4
Consider the practical implication
If the payment had been partial or the oaths were not time-bound, the ruling might differ. Here, full payment by any person without authorization voids the oath for all others.
This ruling applies to any communal financial obligation where one person discharges the debt.

The Particle 'Aw' in Conditional Oaths: Affirmation vs Negation
Expansion (sharh) on the particle 'aw' in oaths and acknowledgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for 'aw' (or) in conditional oaths to determine the legal effect in divorce, manumission, and acknowledgment cases, distinguishing between affirmative and negative contexts.

Core Idea
In affirmative oaths, 'aw' selects one; in negation, it generalizes – the third conjunct binds accordingly.

This lesson unpacks a dense passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq that explains how the particle 'aw' (or) functions when used in conditional statements involving divorce, manumission, and financial acknowledgment. The key is the context: when the statement is affirmative (e.g., 'This one is divorced, or this one and this one'), the last item is immediately affected, and the speaker has a choice regarding the first two. When the statement is negative (e.g., 'I will not speak to A or B and C'), the meaning generalizes: speaking to A alone breaks the oath, but speaking to either B or C alone does not; the oath is broken only after speaking to both B and C. We will explore the linguistic principle, the Quranic proof, and the subtle exceptions when dual predicates are used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that we are analyzing a conditional statement (oath or declaration) that includes the particle 'aw'. The ruling is about which person is immediately affected (divorced/freed) and which are subject to the speaker's choice.
The act is the utterance itself, not a physical action.
2
Locate the dalil
The commentator cites the Quranic verse 'Wa la tuti' athiman aw kafuran' (Do not obey any sinful or ungrateful person) to illustrate that 'aw' in a negative context (imperative with 'la') generalizes to all individuals.
The verse is from Surah al-Insan (76:24).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The proof lies in the negation: 'aw' here means 'any', so the command prohibits obedience to any sinful or ungrateful person, implying that in negative oaths, 'aw' also generalizes. The Hanafi rule: 'aw' in negation indicates a general prohibition.
The derivation is that 'aw' in negation takes on the meaning of 'any', not 'or'.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is an expansion (sharh) on the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq. It deals with the application of the particle 'aw' (or) in conditional oaths involving divorce, manumission, and financial acknowledgments. The commentary begins by explaining the first part of the matn: 'Every slave I own is free' includes all full-ownership slaves except the mukatab (slave with a manumission contract), because the master does not have complete control and ownership over the mukatab. The mukatab is only included by explicit intention. The same applies to a partially freed slave. The commentator then moves to the core ruling: when someone says 'This one is divorced, or this one and this one,' the last one is divorced, and he has a choice regarding the first two. The same rule applies to manumission and debt acknowledgment. The underlying principle is that 'aw' signifies one of the mentioned items, and the third is conjuncted (by 'and') to the one that actually receives the ruling. Hence, the last one receives the ruling, while the first two are subject to the speaker's choice. The commentary then points out a mistake in al-Mughni regarding an acknowledgment case, affirming the correct view that the third is a partner of the one who has the right. The correct phrase 'upon it is the fatwa' is noted, correcting a scribal error. Next, the passage distinguishes the behavior of 'aw' in affirmative vs. negative contexts. In negation, 'aw' takes on a generalizing function, as seen in the Quranic verse 'Do not obey any sinful or ungrateful person.' In negative oaths, if one says 'I will not speak to A or B and C,' speaking to A alone breaks the oath, but speaking to B or C alone does not; the oath is only broken by speaking to both B and C. This contrasts with affirmative contexts where 'aw' selects. The commentator then tackles a special case: if the second and third have a dual predicate (e.g., 'these two are divorcées'), then no immediate effect occurs; instead, the speaker has a choice. Finally, a series of objections and responses are presented, drawing from al-Tabyin, al-Fath, al-Talwih, and the glosses of Hasan Chalabi, exploring the nuances of conjunction and the implications for the ruling.

Key Terms
أو (aw): The particle 'or' – in affirmative contexts it affirms one of the mentioned items; in negative contexts it generalizes.
عطف (ataf): Conjunction in grammar; here specifically the conjunction of the third item to the one that receives the ruling.
إثبات (ithbat): Affirmation – the context where the speaker is declaring something, as opposed to negating.
نفي (nafi): Negation – the context where the speaker is denying or forbidding.

The Effect of 'Or' in Divorce and Manumission Oaths: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on 'aw' in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for the particle 'aw' in affirmative vs. negative contexts and explain the preferred view when a predicate is mentioned for the last two individuals in a divorce or manumission oath.

Core Idea
'Or' specifies in affirmation, generalizes in negation; but when a predicate is given, the speaker has a choice.

The particle 'aw' (أو) plays a crucial role in oaths involving multiple subjects. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines how 'aw' interacts with 'and' (واو) in divorce (affirmative) and oath (negative) contexts. We will trace the debate among Hanafi scholars and identify the relied-upon ruling, which gives the utterer a choice when a predicate is mentioned for the second and third individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Contexts
Distinguish between affirmative contexts (e.g., 'You are divorced or you') and negative contexts (e.g., 'I will not speak to you or you'). In affirmative, 'aw' specifies one; in negative, it generalizes to all.
Quran 76:24 is cited as evidence for generalization in negative commands.
2
Analyze the Attachment of the Third Person
When a third person is added with 'and', determine whom they attach to. In divorce (affirmative), the third attaches to the one for whom the ruling occurs (so the disjunction is between first and the last two). In oaths (negative), the third attaches to the second, so disjunction is between first and second alone or third alone.
3
Examine the Exception: Predicate Mentioned
If a predicate is mentioned for the second and third (e.g., 'This one is divorced or this one and this one are two divorced'), the ruling changes: none is divorced immediately, and the speaker has a choice.
Reason: singular predicate cannot be used for dual, so the structure implies a choice.
4
Trace the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Scholars from al-Fath and al-Talwih proposed alternatives, such as the third being conjoined to the second making it dual, or an immediate choice without effect until clarification. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq responds, affirming the preferred view that the speaker has a choice when a predicate is mentioned.
The debate hinges on whether the presupposed wording matches the uttered wording.
5
Apply the Mufta Bihi Ruling
In practice, if someone says 'You are divorced or you and you are divorced', none is divorced until he chooses which declaration he intended. If he chooses the first, only the first is divorced; if the second, the last two are divorced.
This is the tarjih of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, following al-Tabyin.

A defect does not remove a coin from its genus; paying with defective silver dirhams fulfills a debt oath.

When a person swears to pay a debt by a specific day, what happens if he pays with coins of lesser quality? Does the oath still count as fulfilled? The Hanafi school draws a clear line: as long as the coins belong to the same genus (silver), even if defective, the oath is discharged. Five Applications: Zuyuf Treated as Good Dirhams
Expansion: five specific applications of the genus rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five instances where zuyuf (defective dirhams) are treated like good dirhams and apply the genus principle to these contexts.

Core Idea
In five key transactions, zuyuf are legally equivalent to good dirhams because genus trumps quality.

Al-Walwalji, a Hanafi jurist, identified five specific cases where the rule 'defective coins are still dirhams' produces practical effects. These range from pre-emption to murabahah to debt recovery. This lesson examines each case to see how the genus principle operates across different fiqh chapters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five cases
Read the list from al-Walwalji in the passage: pre-emption, guarantee, murabahah, oaths, and debt recovery.
Each case involves a transaction originally agreed with good dirhams but settled with zuyuf.
2
Understand the common element
In each case, the zuyuf are considered as if they were good dirhams for the purpose of the original obligation (e.g., pre-emption price, guarantee capital, murabahah cost).
This is because the genus is the same; quality differences are secondary.
3
Apply the genus principle to each
For each case, trace how the ruling follows from 'al-ayb la yu'dim al-jins'. For example, in pre-emption, the pre-emptor pays the price the buyer paid, which was good dirhams. Since the buyer paid zuyuf, the pre-emptor still pays good dirhams.
The buyer's payment with zuyuf is considered payment in dirhams, so the original price (good dirhams) remains the benchmark.
4
Contrast with cases where zuyuf are not equivalent
Think about situations where zuyuf might not be equivalent, e.g., if the contract explicitly required good dirhams, the payer might need to compensate the difference. The passage does not discuss this, but it's implied.
The five cases are specific contexts; general rules may differ.

Taking Substitute and Definition of Parting in Oaths
Rulings on oath-breaking by substitute and parting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when taking a substitute breaks an oath and define 'parting' in the context of an oath to remain with a creditor.

Core Idea
Taking a substitute for the item sworn about counts as taking the original; parting means loss of direct sight and proximity.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies two key issues in oaths: the effect of taking a substitute for the thing sworn about, and what constitutes 'parting' from a creditor. Understanding these definitions is crucial to determining whether an oath is broken, which can have serious consequences like triggering a divorce.

Analyze the action taken

The text says taking the substitute is like taking the original.

Since the substitute ('iwad) stands in for the original, the condition is met. The divorce occurs.
This principle extends to other contexts, e.g., partner's right to recourse.
4

Parting occurs when the oath-taker loses direct sight of the creditor. Identify elements: visibility, proximity, barriers.
Barriers like walls, closed doors (unless key held) cause parting.
5
Consider exceptions and excuses
Sleep, heedlessness, or external interference do not break the oath because they are involuntary.
These are from al-Hiyal and Majmu' al-Nawazil.

A man has the price of a sale due from another man, and he said, 'If I take the price of that thing, my wife is divorced,' so he took wheat instead of it; the divorce occurs because he took the compensation for the price, and taking the compensation takes the place of taking the compensated thing. Similarly if there is a closed door between them, except if the key is with the oath-taker: if he entered him into a house and locked the door on him and sat at the door, he has not parted. And if the imprisoned one is the oath-taker and the released one is the one sworn about, and he closed the door on him and took the key, the oath-taker breaks his oath. And in al-Hiyal: If the claimant slept or was heedless of the demanded one, or a person distracted him with talk until the demanded one fled, he does not break his oath; similarly if a person prevented him from staying close until the demanded one fled, he does not break his oath. And in Majmu' al-Nawazil: A man swore by divorce of his wife that he would give her a dirham every day; sometimes he gives it at sunset, sometimes at evening prayer. And al-Uzjandi was asked about one who said to the debt-owner, 'If I do not pay your right by Eid, [then...]' and that day the judge of this town did not consider it Eid nor prayed Eid prayer due to evidence that appeared to him, while the judge of another town considered it Eid. He said: If a judge of a town rules that it is Eid, it is binding on the people of the other town if the moon-sightings do not differ, like the ruling on Ramadan. And Abu Nasr al-Dabusi was asked about one who swore that his creditor would come to his house tomorrow and show him, and he did not find him and he was absent; he does not break his oath.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents several distinct rulings. The passage type is a ruling statement with multiple sub-issues. The first ruling: if a man swears not to take the price of a sold item, but takes wheat instead, the oath is broken because taking the substitute is like taking the original. This is because the substitute is considered a compensation ('iwad) for the price. The second set of rulings deals with an oath not to part from a creditor until the debt is paid. 'Parting' is defined by direct sight and proximity; if a barrier prevents seeing, it is considered parting, except if the oath-taker controls the situation (e.g., holds the key). The third part from al-Hiyal discusses excuses: if the oath-taker is prevented by sleep, heedlessness, or external interference, the oath is not broken because he did not voluntarily part. The fourth from Majmu' al-Nawazil addresses daily payment: if the oath is to give a dirham every day, giving at any time during the day-night cycle fulfills it. The fifth is a fatwa about Eid: if there is disagreement between judges on the date, the ruling of each judge binds his jurisdiction if moon-sightings are the same. The sixth: if the debtor swears that the creditor will come to his house, and the creditor is absent, the oath is not broken because the condition is not met.

Key Terms
عود ('iwad): Compensation or substitute: in this context, taking wheat in place of the price of a sale.
مفارقة (mufaraqah): Parting: physical separation defined by loss of direct sight and proximity in the context of oaths.
حيل (hiyal): Legal stratagems: permissible ways to avoid breaking an oath without lying.

Oath on Fuduli Marriage: Action vs Speech and the Shafi'i Trick
Explaining the ruling on an oath conditional upon marriage by a fuduli and the trick of going to a Shafi'i judge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on an oath conditioned on marriage by a fuduli and identify the legal trick (hila) of going to a Shafi'i judge.

Core Idea
A conditional oath about marriage is only triggered by speech, not action, but if the oath explicitly includes acceptance by action, only a Shafi'i judge can nullify it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a common scenario in Egypt: a man makes an oath that if he marries a woman by himself, his agent, or a fuduli, then his wife is divorced. The commentator explains the Hanafi interpretation and provides a legal trick to avoid the divorce. The key is understanding that 'tazawwaja' (to marry) is a speech act, so acceptance of a fuduli's contract by action does not trigger the oath. However, if the oath is drafted more broadly, the only way out is to resort to a Shafi'i judge who can annul the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its conditions
Read the oath: 'If I marry a woman by myself, or by my agent, or by a fuduli, then you are divorced.' Note the three modes: by self, by agent, by fuduli.
The fuduli acts without authorization.
2
Distinguish acceptance by speech vs. action
The oath uses 'tazawwaja' which is a speech act. If the fuduli marries the man to a woman, the man may accept by speech (saying 'I accept') or by action (e.g., consummation). The Hanafi ruling hinges on this distinction.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle that marriage is a speech act
The commentator states that 'tazawwaja' is literal in speech. Therefore, the phrase 'or by a fuduli' refers only to acceptance by speech. Acceptance by action does not count as marriage under the oath.
This is based on the linguistic rule that marriage contracts require verbal offer and acceptance.
4
Examine the extended oath that closes the loophole
If the man adds 'or I accept the marriage of a fuduli, even by action,' then acceptance by action also triggers the oath. In this case, there is no escape within Hanafi law.
The extension explicitly covers action, overriding the default speech-only interpretation.
5
Understand the inter-madhhab solution
Since Hanafis do not allow nullifying the oath, the only way out is to go to a Shafi'i judge. Shafi'is permit annulment of oaths, so the judge can dissolve the conditional divorce.
This is a practical hila using madhhab difference.

From al-Qunyah: If [someone says] 'If I marry [a woman] over you, then her command is in your hand,' then a fuduli marries her, and he accepts by action, the command does not come into her hand, unlike if he said 'If a woman enters my marriage, then her command is in your hand,' then the command comes into her hand. Here there is a conditional [oath] frequently occurring in Egypt, that he says: 'If I marry a woman by myself, or by my agent, or by a fuduli, then you are divorced, or she is divorced.' Is there any way out? I say: If he accepts the fuduli's contract by action, then divorce does not occur, because his saying 'or by a fuduli' is conjoined to 'by myself' and the operative factor is 'I married,' and they have stated that it is literal in speech, so his saying 'or by a fuduli' only refers to his acceptance by speech only. If he adds 'or enters into my marriage' or 'into my protection,' the ruling is likewise, because we have stated that entering into it has only one cause, namely marriage, which only occurs by speech. If he adds 'or I accept the marriage of a fuduli, even by action,' then there is no way out unless the divorce is suspended for the married woman, then the matter should be raised to a Shafi'i [judge] to nullify the added oath, as we have presented in the chapter on conditional [oaths].

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on a ruling from al-Qunyah. It deals with a common scenario where a man makes a conditional oath regarding marriage. The key issue is the interpretation of the phrase 'or by a fuduli' in the oath. The commentator explains that if a fuduli marries the woman and the man accepts the marriage by action (such as consummation), then the divorce does not take effect because the oath refers to marriage by speech, not by action. However, if he accepts by speech, divorce occurs. The passage also discusses the trick (hila) of adding the phrase 'or I accept the marriage of a fuduli' which closes all loopholes. In that case, the only way out is to go to a Shafi'i judge to have the oath annulled, as Shafi'is allow nullification of oaths while Hanafis do not. This shows an inter-madhhab solution.

مخلص (makhlaṣ): A legal loophole or way out of a binding oath.
يمين (yamīn): An oath; a binding commitment with legal consequences.
