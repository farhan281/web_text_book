Forgetting to Wash a Limb During Ghusl
Ruling on forgetting part of ghusl and effect on raj'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on termination of raj'ah when a woman forgets to wash part of her body during ghusl after menstruation, and explain the distinction between a whole limb and less than a limb.

Core Idea
Forgetting less than a limb terminates raj'ah; forgetting a whole limb does not.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: after a woman performs ghusl from menstruation, if she forgets to wash a part of her body, does that affect the termination of raj'ah (the right to take back a spouse after revocable divorce)? The ruling hinges on whether the forgotten area is a full limb or less, with a precautionary approach for small areas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ghusl after menstruation. The ruling concerns whether the omission affects the termination of raj'ah. Note that the ghusl itself remains valid but the right of return may be affected.
Raj'ah termination is a separate legal consequence from the validity of ghusl.
2
Distinguish the size of the forgotten part
Classify whether the forgotten area is a whole limb (like hand or foot) or less than a limb (finger, part of limb, forearm, one nostril). The passage explicitly lists examples.
A limb is defined as something like a hand or foot; less than that includes the forearm, finger(s), and part of a limb.
3
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
Less than a limb dries quickly, creating uncertainty (shakk) about whether water reached it. This doubt triggers the precautionary termination of raj'ah. A whole limb does not dry quickly, so certainty remains that it was not washed, thus no termination.
The reasoning is based on the principle of overcome doubt (taghlib al-shakk).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement on rinsing mouth/nose
Leaving rinsing mouth or nose is analogous to leaving a limb according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (no termination), but al-Karhi argues it terminates because it relates to purity.
The passage presents multiple riwayat; the mufta bihi follows the precautionary view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is: less than a limb terminates raj'ah, a whole limb does not. For mouth/nose, the preferred view is that it terminates (al-Karhi's opinion).
This tarjih is supported by later Hanafi masters who prioritize caution in matters of marital rights.

Forgetting a Small Part in Ghusl and Its Effect on Raj'a
Ruling on ghusl oversight affecting raj'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on raj'a effectiveness when a woman forgets to wash a part of her body during ghusl and apply the distinction between forgetting less than a limb vs. a whole limb.

Core Idea
Forgetting less than a limb leaves doubt; forgetting a whole limb leaves certainty — thus raj'a is only effective in the latter case.

When a divorced woman performs ghusl after menstruation, she may forget to wash a small area. Does this affect the validity of her raj'a (revocation of divorce)? The Hanafi school makes a careful distinction based on whether the forgotten part is less than a limb or a full limb, and whether the omission was intentional or forgetful. This lesson unpacks that ruling, including an intra-Hanafi difference regarding the obligatory acts of rinsing the mouth and nose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the act being ruled upon: a woman forgetting to wash a part of her body during ghusl after menstruation. The ruling concerns whether this omission makes the raj'a effective immediately or not.
The act is forgetting, not intentional omission.
2
Distinguish between less than a limb and a whole limb
Note that the passage contrasts 'less than a limb' (e.g., finger, part of limb, nostril) with 'a limb' (e.g., hand, foot). The ruling differs based on this classification.
Madmadah and istinshaq are treated differently by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
3
Apply the rationale of doubt vs certainty
Less than a limb dries quickly, creating doubt whether water reached it. A whole limb remains wet longer, so missing it is certain. This rational (ta'lil) determines the rule.
The rule is based on qiyas: the presence or absence of certainty.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree on whether leaving madmadah/istinshaq is like leaving a limb or less than a limb. This affects the raj'a ruling in those cases.
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi.
5
Differentiate between forgetfulness and intentionality
The ruling is restricted to forgetfulness. If she intentionally leaves a small part, the raj'a is effective immediately because deliberate omission is treated as a complete omission.
This shows the school's sensitivity to intention.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a line from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding a woman who performs ghusl but forgets to wash a small part of her body, such as a finger or part of a limb. The ruling is that the raj'a does not become effective—meaning the husband cannot resume marital relations or remarry her—until she washes that spot or if she is unable, after the time of one prayer passes while she has the ability to perform ghusl. Tayammum and Raj'a: When Does the Revocability End?
Ruling on when a woman's tayammum affects raj'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a woman's tayammum terminates her raj'a and explain the majority's proof versus al-Karkhi's view.

Core Idea
Tayammum ends raj'a only after she prays — not before and not by other acts.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in the overlap of taharah and family law: the effect of a divorced woman's tayammum on the revocability of her divorce (raj'a). The passage clarifies that despite common misunderstanding, her husband may approach her before she prays, but the raj'a itself — her eligibility to remarry her former husband — is not finalized until she completes a prayer with that tayammum. We will also explore an intra-Hanafi disagreement with al-Karkhi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states that a woman's tayammum does not terminate her raj'a until she performs a prayer with that tayammum. Understand that 'termination' here means the end of the restriction that prevents her remarrying her ex-husband.
Her husband may approach her before prayer; the restriction is only regarding the legal effect on raj'a.
2
Understand the condition
The text restricts termination to prayer, not any other act. The reason given is the possibility of finding water during prayer, which would invalidate tayammum.
If water is found during prayer, she must start over. Hence raj'a is not confirmed until prayer ends.
3
Differentiate other acts
Acts like reciting Quran, touching mushaf, or entering masjid after tayammum do NOT terminate raj'a according to the majority. They are 'following prayer' but not given the same ruling.
Al-Karkhi disagreed, considering these acts as nullifying purification and thus terminating raj'a.
4
Examine the dispute
Al-Karkhi held that any nullifier of wudu nullifies tayammum, so any such act ends raj'a. The majority (including al-Asbijabi) restrict termination to prayer, due to the possibility of finding water.
The text favors the majority view as 'asahh' (more sound).
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The operative ruling for fatwa is that raj'a terminates only upon completing a prayer. Other acts do not suffice.
This is based on the consensus (ijma) mentioned in the passage.

Manumission for Consideration: Definition and Legal Ruling
Defines the term جعل and states the ruling of manumission in exchange for property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term 'جعل' in the context of manumission, state the ruling that the slave's acceptance is required for validity, and explain the analogy to sale that supports this ruling.

Core Idea
Manumission for a consideration is an exchange: the slave's acceptance makes him free immediately, like a sale.

In this passage, the Hanafi jurist defines the term 'جعل' (consideration) and applies it to manumission. The key ruling is that when a master offers freedom in exchange for property, the slave must accept. This is because the transaction is an exchange (mu'awada) of property for non-property, and by analogy to sale, acceptance is essential. Understanding this clarifies the nature of the contract and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Define the term 'جعل' in this context
Identify the linguistic meaning from the passage and distinguish it from other uses (e.g., in jihad). Note that here it specifically means the property exchanged for manumission.
The term is derived from what is given to a fighter.
2
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is 'عتق على جعل' – manumission for a consideration. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) and the slave becomes free immediately upon acceptance.
The text says: 'فإذا قبل صار حراً' – when he accepts, he becomes free.
3
Explain why acceptance is required
This is an exchange contract (mu'awada) where the slave gives up his freedom for property. Since the slave does not own himself, the transaction is akin to a sale, which requires acceptance from both parties.
The passage explicitly states: 'لأنه معاوضة المال بغير المال إذ العبد لا يملك نفسه'.
4
Analyze the analogy to sale (qiyas)
The author draws an analogy to sale: in a sale, the ruling (ownership transfer) is established immediately upon acceptance. Similarly, here the slave becomes free immediately upon acceptance, because it is an exchange.
The phrase 'كما في البيع' indicates the qiyas.
5
Note the practical consequences
The consideration can be a debt or a specific item, and it can be subject to suretyship. It can be exchanged hand-to-hand like currency but not on credit due to the prohibition of debt-for-debt exchange.
This distinguishes it from kitaba (contract of manumission for deferred payment).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partial acceptance of consideration is valid only if manumission is indivisible—split views within Hanafi school.

Acceptance (qabul) is a pivotal moment in manumission for a consideration (jual). The master says 'You are free for a thousand,' and the slave must accept. But does the acceptance need a formal session? Can the slave accept only half? This lesson unpacks the conditions for acceptance and a key internal dispute over partial acceptance, grounding the reasoning in the divisibility or indivisibility of manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for acceptance
Note that acceptance requires a session (majlis): if the slave is present, the session of the offer; if absent, the session when he learns of it. Rejection or turning away (by standing or another act) voids the offer.
This is standard Hanafi contract law applied to manumission.
2
Analyze the dispute over partial acceptance
The slave accepts only half the consideration. Abu Hanifa rules it invalid because manumission is divisible; partial acceptance would free half via sa'aya without master's full consent. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule it valid because manumission is indivisible; accepting half is accepting the whole, and the full sum is owed.
The dispute hinges on whether 'itq (manumission) can be split.
3
Understand the basis for each view
Abu Hanifa analogizes manumission to sale or property division—divisible. The Sahibayn analogize to divorce (talaq), which is indivisible by consensus, and also blood money (diyah).
The analogy to divorce is explicit in the text: 'if that were in divorce, acceptance of half is acceptance of the whole by agreement.'
4
Apply the analogy to other indivisible acts
The passage extends the same logic to blood money and other indivisible obligations. The Sahibayn's qiyas strengthens their position.
This shows the method of talil: identifying the illah (indivisibility) and extending it.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa: slave remains enslaved, no freedom. Under Sahibayn: slave becomes free and must pay the full amount by earning.
The later fatwa often follows the Sahibayn due to stronger analogy and maslahah.

Partial ownership means partial freedom: Abu Hanifa divides manumission, so one master frees only his share.

Valid Types of Property for Consideration in Kitaba

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In kitaba, any known-genus property suffices; if genus is unknown, the slave owes his own value.

The master and slave may agree on any property of value as the consideration for freeing the slave. The Hanafi school expands the categories to include cash, goods, animals, and measured items, as long as the genus is known. If the description within a known genus is unknown, the contract is still valid and the slave must provide the average quality (wasat) of that genus, e.g., a medium horse.

If the master says 'for a garment' without specifying type (e.g., silk, cotton), the genus is unknown. In that case, the slave is freed upon acceptance and must pay the value of himself (qimat nafsih).

Manumission with Invalid Consideration: Slave's Liability and Intra-Hanafi Disputes
Rulings when the consideration is owned by another, and disputes on value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on manumission when the consideration is claimed by a third party, and explain the three intra-Hanafi disputes regarding the slave's compensation, defect return, and burden of proof.

Core Idea
Manumission stands even if the consideration is stolen; the slave compensates, but how much differs among Hanafi imams.

When a master frees his slave for a specified consideration (e.g., another slave or a horse) that later turns out to be owned by a third party, a critical question arises: does the manumission hold? The Hanafi school affirms it does, protecting the freed slave's status. However, the slave must compensate for the loss. The amount and conditions of compensation sparked well-known disputes among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. This lesson unpacks those disputes and the related rules on defect returns and evidentiary burdens.

When the consideration is specified (mu'ayyan), Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say the slave pays his own value; Muhammad says he pays the value of the claimed item. For unspecificed consideration (ghayr mu'ayyan), the slave pays the fungible equivalent or average value.
The dispute extends to cases of perishing before delivery and defect returns.

Abu Hanifa permits return only for a major (fahish) defect – like a defect in a dowry; the Two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow return even for a minor (yasir) defect.
This mirrors the dispute in marriage (mahr) law.

If the master and slave dispute the genus or amount of the consideration, the slave's statement with oath is credited. If both present evidence, the master's evidence wins. However, if manumission was conditional on payment, the roles reverse.

The Dual Nature of Manumission for Consideration

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Master's offer is a binding suspension; slave's proposal is an exchange with option to retract.

The final sentences appear to contrast two expressions: 'You are free on condition you perform Hajj for me' (likely slave becomes free immediately without requiring performance) vs. 'You are free, perform Hajj for me and you are free' (perhaps a different structure? The text cuts off).

Key Terms
تعليق (taʿlīq): Suspending a legal effect (manumission) on a future condition.
معاوضة (muʿāwaḍa): Exchange contract; from slave's perspective, he exchanges property for freedom.
وكالة (wakāla): Agency: slave appoints master to free him for a consideration.
