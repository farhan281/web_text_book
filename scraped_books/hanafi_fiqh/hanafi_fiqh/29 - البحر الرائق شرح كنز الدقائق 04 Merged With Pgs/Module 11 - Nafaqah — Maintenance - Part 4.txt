The Master's Obligation to Provide for His Slave
Ruling on master's obligation to provide for his slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three components of a master's obligation to his slave and explain how the hadith cited proves this obligation using Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Providing food, clothing, and shelter to a slave is wajib on the master, proven by the Prophet's direct command.

This lesson examines a foundational ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding the master-slave relationship. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq succinctly states the obligation of maintenance (nafaqah) and then cites a hadith as the proof. We will unpack how this single hadith, through Hanafi usul, makes food, clothing, and housing wajib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three obligations
The text explicitly states that maintenance (nafaqah), clothing (kiswah), and shelter (suknā) are obligatory upon the master for his slave.
These three are considered the minimum necessities.
2
Recognize the legal category
The obligation is wajib (binding) not merely recommended. In Hanafi usul, an imperative verb indicates obligation unless there is evidence to the contrary.
The proof uses the imperative 'at'imuhum' (feed them).
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The hadith: 'Feed them from what you eat, and clothe them from what you wear.' The imperative form establishes wujub.
This hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear and binding.
4
Confirm the scope
The obligation covers food, clothing, and shelter. The word 'nafaqah' in the text encompasses maintenance including shelter, as clarified by the commentators.
Clothing and shelter are explicitly appended to food.
5
Note the consensus
This ruling is agreed upon across all four Sunni schools (muttafaq alayh). There is no valid dispute on the basic obligation.
Practical details may vary, but the core ruling is uniform.

The proof is the Prophet's ﷺ command: 'Feed them from what you eat, and clothe them from what you wear.' This hadith is a clear imperative (amr) indicating obligation (wujub) per Hanafi usul. The obligation covers daily sustenance, seasonal clothing, and a place to live. This is a fundamental ruling in Hanafi law regarding the treatment of slaves, emphasizing humane care.

Key Terms

Recovering Borrowed Child Maintenance from the Father's Estate
Intra-Hanafi dispute on recovery of borrowed maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on whether a mother can recover borrowed child maintenance from the father's estate after his death, apply the qiyas to the husband's own borrowing, and evaluate the tarjih based on Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
A mother's debt for child maintenance, incurred by judge's order, survives the father's death — her claim stands against his estate.

Maintenance of the child is the father's obligation. When the mother borrows to meet this need with the judge's permission, does her claim survive if the father dies before repayment? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents two Hanafi views: al-Khassaf denies recovery, but al-Asl affirms it, and the relied-upon position follows the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from al-Asl (Zahir al-Riwayah): the mother can recover from the father's estate.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
Al-Khassaf holds she cannot recover. Note that his work is not from Zahir al-Riwayah.
His reasoning is not explicitly given but likely based on the mother being a volunteer.
3
Follow the opponent's argument (implicit)
Al-Khassaf's view implies the debt is dropped at death, perhaps because the borrowing was not directly from the father.
No explicit dalil is provided for this view.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The response from al-Asl uses qiyas: the mother's borrowing by judge's order is like the father borrowing himself. If the father dies, his debt remains; similarly here.
The key phrase: 'بمنزلة استدانة الزوج بنفسه'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under al-Khassaf's view, the widow cannot recover; under the mufta bihi view, she can.
The tarjih is based on authority: al-Asl is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Punishment for Refusing Maintenance: Beating or Imprisonment?
Correction of a ruling in al-Bada'i' regarding punishment for refusing maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct Hanafi ruling on the punishment for a person who refuses maintenance of a close relative, and explain the reasoning (ta'lil) based on the necessity of preserving the right.

Core Idea
Imprisonment, not beating, is the correct punishment for refusing maintenance of close relatives because maintenance lapses with time.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a correction by al-Maqdisi. The initial quote from al-Bada'i' suggests beating, but al-Maqdisi shows that the original text actually says imprisonment. We will examine the reasoning behind imprisonment and why it applies specifically to maintenance obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The author first quotes al-Bada'i' stating that the one who refuses maintenance of a close relative is beaten, not imprisoned, because the right cannot be recovered after time passes.
This is presented as a false claim.
2
Recognize the commentator's correction
Al-Maqdisi points out that this statement is an oversight; the author's copy of al-Bada'i' was missing text. The actual ruling in al-Bada'i' is imprisonment.
This step involves identifying the source of the error: a corrupted manuscript.
3
Extract the correct ruling
From the corrected text of al-Bada'i': the refuser is imprisoned for maintenance of relatives, just as for maintenance of wives. There is a special ruling for the father: he is imprisoned for maintenance of his child but not for other debts.
Imprisonment is the operative punishment.
4
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Imprisonment is effective because maintenance obligations lapse daily; imprisonment compels immediate payment, preventing the right from being lost. Other debts do not lapse with time, so imprisonment is not necessary for them.
The key distinction is the perishable nature of maintenance rights.
5
Apply the ruling to the father's case
The father is imprisoned for child maintenance to prevent the child's destruction, even though harming the father is normally prohibited. This shows that necessity (darurah) overrides the general prohibition.
This is an exception: the necessity of preserving the child's life outweighs the harm of imprisoning the father.

Slave Maintenance: Same Type, Not Same Quality
Ruling with proof and dispute on equality in slave maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slave maintenance and explain why the preposition 'min' in the hadith indicates providing from the same type (jins) rather than identical quality.

Core Idea
A master must provide from the same type (jins) but not the exact quality of his own food and clothing.

This lesson examines the obligation of a master to maintain his slave—food, clothing, and housing—based on the Prophet's command. A key dispute arises over whether the master must give exactly the same quality as he consumes. The Hanafi school resolves this through a linguistic analysis of the word 'min' in the hadith, showing that only the same genus is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its evidence
Locate the core ruling: maintenance (nafaqah), clothing (kiswah), and housing (suknah) are wajib upon the master for his slave. The primary dalil is the hadith: 'Feed them from what you eat and clothe them from what you wear.'
The hadith is quoted in the text.
2
Understand the linguistic point
Focus on the word 'min' (مما) in the hadith. The Hanafis argue that 'min' is partitive (li-l-tab'id), meaning 'from some of' or 'from the type of'. If equality were intended, the hadith would have used 'mithl' (like).
This is the crux of the Hanafi position.
3
Examine the opposing view
Al-Tahawi reports that a group of scholars held that the master must make his slave equal to himself in food and clothing. Their proof is the same hadith but interpreted differently.
The opposing view is mentioned but not attributed to a specific madhhab.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis provide two answers: (1) The word 'min' indicates partiality, so feeding from some of what you eat suffices. (2) The intent is the type (jins), not the exact item. For example, if the master eats wheat bread, the slave must get wheat bread, but it can be of lower quality.
The second answer from Fath al-Qadir is more practical.
5
Apply to a concrete case
Consider: The master wears fine cotton shirts. He may give his slave cotton shirts of coarser weave, but he cannot give him sackcloth (jute) because that is a different jins. The ruling ensures dignifying the slave without imposing undue burden.
This clarifies the practical limits.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that a master must provide maintenance, clothing, and housing for his slave. The evidence is the Prophet's command: 'Feed them from what you eat and clothe them from what you wear.' There is a dispute (ikhtilaf) among scholars about whether the master must give his slave exactly the same quality of food and clothing as he consumes himself. One group says yes, interpreting the hadith to mean equality. The majority (including the Hanafi school) say no, because the hadith uses the word 'min' meaning 'from some of' or 'from the type of,' not the exact same. It is sufficient for the master to provide from the same general type (jins) as he uses, even if his own is better quality. For example, if the master wears fine linen, he can give his slave coarser linen, but not sackcloth. Another hadith (from Abu Huraira) allows the master to keep his food to himself and only give a morsel to the servant. The correct Hanafi position is that equality in quality is not required; only the same genus.

Key Terms
مما (mimmā): Preposition 'min' + relative pronoun meaning 'from what'; interpreted by Hanafis as partitive (giving from the same type, not identical).
الجنس (al-jins): The genus or category of the item; e.g., food type or fabric type. Hanafis require providing from the same jins as the master uses.

Rulings on who bears maintenance when slave's benefits are split

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'mamluk' for nafaqah and apply the rule that maintenance is on the owner of the benefits in cases of bequest, illness, and young age.

Core Idea
Maintenance follows benefit: the one who enjoys the slave's service pays for upkeep.

This lesson unpacks the foundational principle from Bahr al-Ra'iq that, for the purpose of maintenance (nafaqah), a 'slave' is anyone whose benefits are owned by another. We will see how this definition includes the mudabbar and umm walad but excludes the mukatab. Then we examine the general rule when a slave's ownership is split: the person who receives the service bears the maintenance, with modifications when the slave is ill or too young to serve.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi definition of 'mamluk' in this passage
Note that the text defines 'mamluk' not by ownership of the neck (رقبة) but by ownership of benefits (منافع). So a slave whose benefits are owned by another falls under the maintenance obligation even if the neck is owned by someone else. This includes mudabbar and umm walad, but excludes mukatab because he owns his own benefits.
This definition is key to understanding who must pay nafaqah.
2
State the general rule for split ownership
If a slave is bequeathed to one person (owner of the neck) and his service to another, the maintenance is on the one who receives the service (owner of the benefit). This is the basic application of the principle.
The ruling comes from a bequest scenario, but the principle applies more broadly.
3
Apply the modifications for illness and young age
If the slave becomes ill: if the illness prevents service, maintenance shifts to the neck owner. If the illness is not preventing, it stays on the service owner. If the slave is a child too young to serve, maintenance is on the neck owner until he reaches service age, then on the service owner.
These nuances show that the duty is tied to actual benefit received.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed rulings on who is responsible for maintenance when ownership of a slave or property is split. The definition: a 'mamluk' for these purposes is anyone whose benefits (manafi') are owned by another, regardless of who owns the 'neck' (ownership). So a mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death) and umm walad (slave mother of the master's child) are included, but a mukatab (slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) is excluded because he owns his own benefits. The general rule: maintenance follows the benefit (manfa'ah). If the service of a slave is owned by one person and the slave himself by another, the one who benefits from the service pays for maintenance during the period he enjoys the service. If the slave is too ill to work, maintenance shifts to the owner of the neck. If a small child slave cannot yet serve, the neck owner pays until he can. For borrowed slaves, the borrower pays maintenance but the lender pays clothing. For a house: if one owns the house and another has the right to dwell, the dweller pays for maintenance because he gets the benefit. If the house collapses, the dweller may rebuild at his own expense and prevent the owner from using it until reimbursed, because he is compelled to preserve his right. This is analogous to a dispute between owners of upper and lower floors.

Key Terms
منافع (manāfi'): Usable benefits or services of a slave or property; the right to utilize its use.
المدبر (al-mudabbar): A slave whose master has declared that he will be free upon the master's death. He is still a slave during the master's life, and his benefits are owned by the master.
المكاتب (al-mukatab): A slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom. He owns his own benefits and is thus not a 'mamluk' for nafaqah purposes.

Maintenance in Loan, Pledge, Deposit, and Property with Split Use
Rulings on maintenance in various contracts and real property splits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the person liable for maintenance in cases of borrowed slaves, pledged slaves, deposited property, and bequests splitting ownership and use of real estate.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the beneficiary bears maintenance, except in borrowing where clothing remains on the lender.

This lesson continues the application of the principle that maintenance follows benefit, now to contractual arrangements: 'ariyah (loan), rahn (pledge), and wadi'ah (deposit). The passage also applies the same logic to real property: when a house is bequeathed to one and its dwelling to another, the dweller pays for repairs. Interestingly, if the house collapses, the dweller may rebuild and recoup costs, reflecting the same compulsion principle found in shared buildings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maintenance rule for borrowed slaves
For a borrowed slave ('abd al-ariyah), the borrower (musta'ir) pays for maintenance (nafaqah), but the lender (mu'ir) pays for clothing (kiswah). This is a distinct rule: the benefit of service is with the borrower, so he shoulders the daily upkeep; the lender retains ownership and pays for the slave's clothing.
This nuance shows that the principle is not absolute; ownership of the 'neck' still entails some responsibility.
2
Extend to pledge and deposit
For a pledged slave (rahn) and a deposited slave (wadi'ah), the maintenance is on the pledgor (rahin) and depositor (mudi'), respectively, not on the pledgee or depositary. This is because the benefit remains with the original owner, not with the person holding the slave.
This contrasts with borrowing where the benefit transfers.
3
Apply to real property with split ownership
If a house is bequeathed to one and its dwelling to another, the one with the dwelling right pays maintenance because he enjoys the benefit. If the house collapses, the dweller may rebuild at his own expense and prevent the owner from using it until reimbursed, just as in the case of upper and lower floors.
This reinforces that benefit triggers the duty, and the holder of the benefit can take necessary steps to preserve it.

Maintenance of the Slave and Forced Sale in Hanafi Fiqh
Commentary on the ruling when a master refuses maintenance for his slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance of a slave when the master refuses, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the ruling to similar cases involving animals and partnerships.

Core Idea
The judge can compel the sale of a non-earning slave if the master refuses maintenance, according to the Sahibayn; Abu Hanifa holds imprisonment.

When a master owns a slave who cannot earn a living, the master is obligated to provide food, clothing, and shelter. But what happens if the master refuses? The Hanafi school provides a remedy that balances the rights of both parties. This commentary from al-Bahr al-Rai'q explains the ruling, the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), and how the principle extends to animals and co-owned property. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the Hanafi approach to enforcing obligations and the role of the judge in protecting the vulnerable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master's obligation to provide maintenance (nafaqah) for his slave. The ruling is wajib. Note that the passage distinguishes between a slave who can earn (maintenance from his earnings) and one who cannot (master must provide).
If the slave has earnings, the master is not forced to spend from his own pocket.
2
Apply the exception for mudabbar and umm walad
A mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death) and umm walad (slave mother of master's child) cannot be sold. The master must provide maintenance and cannot be forced to sell them.
These categories are protected from sale.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on enforcement
Abu Hanifa holds that the master is imprisoned until he voluntarily sells the slave. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the judge can directly compel the sale. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view.
Al-Tahawi and the majority prefer the Sahibayn.
4
Extend the ruling to animals
The passage notes that animals are not 'entitled' to maintenance in the same way as slaves, so the master is not legally compelled. However, it is religiously recommended due to the prohibition of cruelty and waste.
Some narrations from Abu Yusuf compel maintenance for animals, but the relied-upon view is no legal compulsion.
5
Apply to co-owned property
If an animal is co-owned and one partner refuses to spend, the judge compels him to either sell his share or spend. This is to prevent harm to the other partner.
Al-Khassaf mentions the judge's option to order sale or spending.

This passage is an expansion on the ruling from the matn regarding a master's refusal to provide maintenance for his slave. The commentator explains that if the slave can earn, his maintenance is taken from his earnings (like a wage slave). If he cannot earn (disabled, old, or a young girl), the master is compelled to sell the slave to fulfill the right of maintenance. This is distinguished from the wife's maintenance, which becomes a debt and cannot be enforced by selling the husband's property without his consent. The passage then notes that for a mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death) or umm walad (slave mother of master's child), the master cannot be forced to sell because they cannot be sold; he must provide maintenance. The commentator discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): Abu Hanifa holds that the master is imprisoned until he sells, while the Sahibayn hold that the judge can forcibly sell the slave. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of the Sahibayn, as stated by al-Tahawi and supported by the majority. The ruling extends to animals: the master is not legally compelled to spend on animals (since they are not 'entitled'), but it is advised religiously due to the prohibition of cruelty and waste. If an animal is co-owned, the refusing partner is compelled to either spend or sell his share. Finally, the passage discusses cases of bequests (wasiyya) involving trees and fruit, concluding that maintenance is according to benefit, and the judge should act based on what is most beneficial (aslah). The overarching principle: the ruling revolves around benefit (maslaha).
