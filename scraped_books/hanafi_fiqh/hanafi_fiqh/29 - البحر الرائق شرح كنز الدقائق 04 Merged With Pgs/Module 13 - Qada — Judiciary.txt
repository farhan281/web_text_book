Ruling on Runaway Slave Maintenance
Ruling on maintenance of a runaway slave by a finder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on spending on a runaway slave and identify when the finder can claim expenses.

Core Idea
Spending on a runaway slave without qadi's order makes one a volunteer; only with court order can expenses be recovered.

This lesson focuses on the first scenario from the passage: a runaway slave found by someone. The Hanafi school distinguishes between spending with and without the qadi's permission. Understanding this distinction is essential for anyone who might encounter lost property, as the same principle extends to stray animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the finder's action
Determine whether the finder spent on the slave with or without the qadi's order. The ruling hinges on this condition.
The finder is the person who takes the slave intending to return him to the master.
2
Understand the consequence without order
If the finder spends without qadi's order, he is a volunteer (mutatawwi') and cannot claim reimbursement from the master.
This is based on the principle that without authority, the spender acts out of generosity, not obligation.
3
Understand the qadi's discretion with order
When the finder approaches the qadi, the qadi evaluates the situation: if spending is beneficial (aslah), he orders it; if it consumes the slave's value, he orders sale.
The qadi's decision is based on public interest (maslaha) and preserving wealth.
4
Apply to stray animals
The same ruling applies to a stray animal found in the city or desert, showing a unified principle for lost property.

وأما العبد الآبق إذا أخذه رجل ليرده على مولاه وأنفق عليه إن أنفق بغير أمر القاضي كان متطوعاً لا يرجع وإن رفع الأمر إلى القاضي فسأل من القاضي أن يأمره بالإنفاق عليه نظر القاضي في ذلك، فإن رأى الإنفاق أصلح أمره بالإنفاق، وإن خاف أن تأكله النفقة أمره القاضي بالبيع وإمساك الثمن، وكذا إذا وجد دابة ضالة في المصر أو في غير المصر. وأما العبد المغصوب فإن نفقته على الغاصب إلى أن يرده إلى المولى فإن طلب من القاضي أن يأمره بالنفقة أو بالبيع لا يجيبه لأن المغصوب مضمون على الغاصب إلا أن يكون الغاصب مخوفاً منه على العبد فحينئذ يأخذه القاضي ويبيعه ويمسك الثمن، وأما العبد الوديعة إذا غاب صاحبه فجاء المودع إلى القاضي وطلب منه أن يأمره بالنفقة أو بالبيع فإن القاضي يأمره بأن يؤاجر العبد وينفق عليه من أجره، وإن رأى أن يبيعه فعل. وأما العبد إذا كان بين رجلين فغاب أحدهما وتركه عند الشريك فرفع الشريك الأمر إلى القاضي وأقام البينة على ذلك كان القاضي بالخيار إن شاء قبل هذه البينة، وإن شاء لم يقبل وإن قبل يأمره بالنفقة ويكون الحكم ما هو الحكم في الوديعة والكل من الخانية.
As for the runaway slave: if a man takes him to return him to his master and spends on him, if he spends without the order of the qadi, he is a volunteer and cannot claim reimbursement. If he raises the matter to the qadi and asks the qadi to order him to spend, the qadi considers that: if he sees that spending is more beneficial, he orders him to spend, and if he fears that the spending may consume him (the slave), he orders the qadi to sell and retain the price. Similarly, if he finds a stray animal in the city or outside it. As for the usurped slave: his maintenance is upon the usurper until he returns him to the master. If he asks the qadi to order him to spend or to sell, he does not answer him because the usurped slave is guaranteed by the usurper, unless the usurper is feared for the slave, then the qadi takes him and sells him and retains the price. As for the deposited slave: if his owner is absent, and the depositary comes to the qadi and asks him to order him to spend or to sell, then the qadi orders him to hire out the slave and spend from his hire, and if he sees fit to sell, he does so. As for the slave owned jointly between two men: one is absent and leaves him with the partner, the partner raises the matter to the qadi and provides evidence, then the qadi has the option: if he wishes, he accepts this evidence, and if he wishes, he does not accept. If he accepts, he orders him to spend, and the ruling is the same as the ruling for the deposit – all from al-Khaniyya.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) on the topic of maintenance (nafaqa) in the context of slaves. The commentator, Ibn Nujaym, presents four distinct scenarios regarding the maintenance of slaves, each with specific rules involving the qadi's discretion. The first scenario concerns a runaway slave (عبد آبق) found by someone. If the finder spends on the slave without a court order, he is considered a volunteer (متطوع) and cannot recover his expenses. However, if he approaches the qadi, the qadi will decide: if spending is more beneficial for the slave (e.g., to preserve his life), the qadi orders spending; if the cost of maintenance would exceed the slave's value, the qadi orders the sale. This ruling also applies to a stray animal (دابة ضالة). The second scenario is a usurped slave (عبد مغصوب): the usurper (غاصب) is responsible for maintenance until return to the owner, and the qadi will not order sale unless the usurper is dangerous to the slave, in which case the qadi can sell. The third scenario is a deposited slave (عبد وديعة): the depositary (مودع) can request the qadi to order hiring out the slave or selling, whichever is more beneficial. The fourth scenario is a jointly owned slave with one partner absent: the partner can approach the qadi with evidence; the qadi may accept and order spending, applying the same rules as for a deposit. All these rulings are from the Hanafi text al-Khaniyya.

Key Terms
العبد الآبق (al-'abd al-abiq): A slave who has run away from his master
متطوع (mutatawwi'): A volunteer who performs an act without legal obligation and cannot claim reimbursement
مغصوب (maghsub): A slave unlawfully taken by force (usurped)
وديعة (wadi'a): A slave placed under someone's care as a trust (deposit)

Ruling on Usurped Slave Maintenance
Ruling on maintenance of a usurped slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the usurper bears full maintenance of a usurped slave and identify the exception when the qadi may sell.

Core Idea
The usurper is liable for the slave's maintenance as part of his guarantee, and the qadi cannot order sale unless the usurper is dangerous to the slave.

This lesson covers the second scenario: a slave taken by force (maghsub). The Hanafi school holds the usurper fully responsible for maintenance because he is liable for any loss or damage. The qadi's role is limited to protecting the slave when the usurper poses a threat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the liable party
The usurper (ghasib) is responsible for the slave's maintenance from the moment of usurpation until return to the owner.
This is because the usurper's possession is unlawful and he guarantees the slave (daman).
2
Understand the qadi's refusal to intervene
If the usurper asks the qadi to order him to spend or to sell the slave, the qadi does not respond positively except in one case.
The qadi does not interfere because the usurper is already obligated by law; seeking qadi's order would suggest he could avoid responsibility.
3
Identify the exception
If the usurper is feared for the slave (i.e., he may harm or destroy the slave), the qadi may take the slave, sell him, and keep the price.
This protects the owner's property from the usurper's potential abuse.

Ruling on Deposited Slave Maintenance
Ruling on maintenance of a deposited slave when owner is absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the qadi orders maintenance for a deposited slave when the owner is absent, including hiring out the slave.

Core Idea
For a deposited slave, the qadi first orders hiring out the slave to cover maintenance; sale is a secondary option.

This lesson addresses the third scenario: a slave left as a deposit (wadi'a) whose owner is absent. The depositary is not liable for maintenance, but if the slave needs care, the depositary can approach the qadi. The qadi prioritizes self-sufficiency through the slave's labor before considering sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The depositary (mudi') holds the slave as a trust. The owner is absent, so maintenance cannot be obtained from him directly.
The depositary is not obligated to maintain the slave from his own pocket.
2
Understand the depositary's request
The depositary goes to the qadi and asks for an order to spend or to sell the slave.
He needs qadi's permission because otherwise he would be a volunteer.
3
Qadi's primary order: hire out
The qadi orders the depositary to hire out the slave and use the wages for maintenance. This preserves the slave as a productive asset.
The slave's labor covers his own expenses, avoiding depletion of the principal.
4
Qadi's secondary option: sale
If the qadi sees fit, he may order the sale of the slave. This is likely when hiring is not feasible or the slave is too expensive to maintain.

Ruling on Jointly Owned Slave with Absent Partner
Ruling on a jointly owned slave when one partner is absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the qadi's discretion when a partner seeks permission to spend on a jointly owned slave left by an absent partner.

Core Idea
The qadi may choose to accept or reject evidence from a partner about an absent partner, and if accepted, treats the case like a deposit.

The final scenario involves a slave co-owned by two men. One partner is absent, leaving the slave with the other. The present partner seeks the qadi's intervention. The qadi has full discretion whether to accept the partner's evidence about the absence. If accepted, the ruling mirrors that of a deposited slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and situation
Two partners own a slave. One partner is absent, and the slave is left with the present partner. The present partner cannot unilaterally decide maintenance.
The absent partner's share is analogous to a deposit with the present partner.
2
Understand the qadi's discretion on evidence
The partner raises the matter and provides evidence of the absence. The qadi may either accept or reject this evidence.
The qadi is not obliged to accept; he has discretion based on reliability.
3
Consequence of acceptance
If the qadi accepts the evidence, he orders the partner to spend, and the ruling becomes identical to that for a deposited slave (i.e., hire out or sell).

The fourth scenario is a jointly owned slave with one partner absent: the partner can approach the qadi with evidence; the qadi may accept and order spending, applying the same rules as for a deposit. All these rulings are from the Hanafi text al-Khaniyya.

Key Terms
بينة (bayyina): Evidence, proof (e.g., testimony or document)
خيار (khiyar): Option, discretion (here, the qadi's freedom to decide)

Key Takeaway
The qadi has discretion to accept or reject a partner's evidence of absence; if accepted, the ruling follows the deposit model.
