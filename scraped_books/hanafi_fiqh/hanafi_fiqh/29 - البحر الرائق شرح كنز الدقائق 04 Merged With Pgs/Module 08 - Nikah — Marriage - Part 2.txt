Oath Not to Marry: Third Party Marriage and Acceptance
Ruling on whether acceptance by word or act of a fuduli marriage breaks an oath not to marry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on whether acceptance by word or act of a marriage by a third party breaks an oath not to marry, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute and the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Acceptance by word breaks an oath not to marry; acceptance by act does not.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq examines a nuanced scenario where a man swears not to marry, and a third party (fuduli) contracts a marriage on his behalf without authorization. The key question is whether his subsequent acceptance (ijazah) — whether verbal or by action — breaks his oath. The Hanafi school distinguishes clearly between the two, and this lesson unpacks that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling branches
Recognize the basic case: a man swears not to marry, a fuduli marries him, and he later accepts. The ruling has two branches: acceptance by word and acceptance by act.
2
Distinguish between acceptance by word and by act
Acceptance by word (e.g., saying 'I accept') breaks the oath. Acceptance by act (e.g., sending the mahr) does not break the oath according to the relied-upon opinion.
The rationale: marriage is a verbal contract, so only verbal acceptance effects it.
3
Understand the opposing intra-Hanafi view
Some Hanafis (as in Jami' al-Fusulayn) argue that even verbal acceptance does not break the oath, because the fuduli's contract is like that of an agent and acceptance is like ratification, not a new marriage.
This view is considered weak (marjuh) by the majority of mashayikh.
4
Analyze the majority's rationale
The majority holds that the oath is on the act of marrying (tazawwuj), which is a verbal contract. The principal's verbal acceptance is the actual marriage act, thus breaking the oath.
The analogy: prior agency (wakalah) is not perfectly analogous because the fuduli acts without authorization.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its practical consequences
The chosen opinion (mufta bihi) is that verbal acceptance breaks the oath; acceptance by act does not. Also, what constitutes acceptance by act: sending the mahr suffices, but sending a gift does not.
This ruling is used in fatwa and is supported by most later Hanafi authorities.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq deals with a specific scenario: a man swears not to marry, and a third party (al-fuduli) marries him to a woman without his prior permission. Later, he accepts the marriage. The ruling distinguishes between acceptance by word (e.g., saying 'I accept') and acceptance by act (e.g., sending the dowry). According to the relied-upon opinion, acceptance by word breaks the oath because the object of the oath (tazawwuj) is the marriage contract, which is concluded by words. Acceptance by act does not break the oath because marriage is primarily a verbal contract. There is a contrary view from Jami' al-Fusulayn claiming that even verbal acceptance does not break the oath, but the majority of scholars reject this. The text also discusses what constitutes acceptance by act: sending the dowry is sufficient, but sending a gift is not. This ruling reflects the Hanafi emphasis on the verbal nature of marriage.

Key Terms
فضولي (fuduli): A third party who acts without prior authorization; in this context, he marries the man to a woman without his permission.
إجازة (ijazah): Acceptance or ratification of a contract made by a fuduli.
محلوف عليه (mahfuf alayh): The object of the oath, in this case the act of marrying (tazawwuj).

Acceptance by Speech vs Action in an Oath Not to Marry
Explaining when acceptance of a third-party marriage breaks an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether verbal or actual acceptance of a third-party marriage breaks an oath, and identify scenarios where acceptance by action does not break the oath.

Core Idea
Only verbal acceptance breaks the oath – action like sending mahr does not, because marriage is a contract based on speech.

When a person swears not to marry, can a third party's marriage contract on his behalf, followed by his acceptance, count as a breach? The Hanafi school distinguishes clearly: acceptance must be verbal to break the oath; mere actions (sending dowry, gifts) do not suffice. This ruling is grounded in the nature of marriage as a verbal contract and is the relied-upon position, despite an intra-madhhab disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the third-party action
The oath is 'not to marry'. A فُضولِيّ (unauthorized third party) marries the swearer to a woman. The swearer then either accepts verbally (says 'I accept') or by action (e.g., sends the mahr).
The fudūlī's contract is void until ratified.
2
Apply the general rule
Verbal acceptance (قول) breaks the oath; acceptance by action (فعل) does not. This is the muftā bihī position, supported by al-Tabyīn and most mashāyikh.
The opposing view (Jāmiʿ al-Fuṣūlayn) says even verbal acceptance does not break – but it is rejected.
3
Understand the ʿillah (legal cause)
The object of the oath is 'marriage', which is a contract effected by speech. Verbal acceptance directly engages the contract; action merely confirms it and is not part of the essential contract.
Marriage is not based on action; thus, action cannot break an oath tied to the contract itself.
4
Examine special cases
Acceptance by writing is disputed (some say it breaks, some not). Kissing with desire or intercourse is considered acceptance by action but is disliked (makrūh taḥrīmī). For those under guardianship (slave, minor child), verbal acceptance breaks the oath; for adults, only direct action breaks.
These details are vital for correct fatwa application.
5
Analyze the legal trick (ḥīlah)
If a free man swears 'Any woman I marry or accept from a third party is thrice divorced', he can avoid divorce by having a third party marry him without his prior command, then he accepts before the woman accepts (since she is not yet his wife). Thus, the oath is not triggered.
This trick is only needed when the oath includes 'and I accept'; otherwise, the divorce occurs immediately upon third-party marriage.

Oath on Marriage: Ratification by Action vs Speech
Ruling on oath-breaking via ratification by action and writing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether ratification of a fuduli marriage by action (e.g., sending dowry) breaks an oath, and explain the different ruling for writing according to the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Ratification by action breaks an oath to marry; writing does not break an oath of silence.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores how oaths interact with marriage ratified by a third party. The key distinction is between ratification by speech and by action; the latter includes physical acts like sending dowry or intercourse. A noteworthy intra-Hanafi dispute concerns whether writing a letter breaks an oath of silence. Understanding this helps navigate oath formulation and permissible tricks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario
The oath-taker swears not to marry or not to speak to someone. A third party (fuduli) contracts the marriage or the oath-taker writes a letter. Determine the exact wording of the oath.
Focus on the verb used: 'marry' vs 'speak'.
2
Classify the ratification act
Ratification (ijaza) can be by speech (qawl) or by action (fi‘l). Action includes kissing with desire, intercourse, sending dowry. Writing is considered speech or not depending on the view.
The passage mentions both types; note that action-based ratification is makruh tahriman.
3
Apply the ruling on breaking the oath
If the oath is 'I will not marry,' ratification by action (e.g., sending dowry) breaks the oath. For 'I will not speak,' writing does not break the oath according to the mufta bihi view.
The fatwa from al-Jami‘ is that writing does not break an oath of silence.
4
Evaluate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Ibn Sama‘a in Nawadir holds that writing does break the oath because writing conveys speech. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the contrary, as indicated by the tarjih of the earlier fatawa.
This affects practical fatwa: writing a letter to the person one swore not to speak to is safe.
5
Consider special cases and complex formulations
The passage also discusses oaths regarding slaves, minor children, and phrases like 'every woman who enters my nikah.' Ensure the precise wording of the oath is examined.
Tricks (hiyal) are permitted to avoid sin, but must be applied within the framework of the mufta bihi.

Book of Oaths, Chapter of Oath on Striking and Killing. Page 621: ... (It is) an authorization (ijaza) because it is not specific to marriage. And if he kisses her with desire or has intercourse with her, it becomes an authorization by action, but it is disliked with prohibition (makruh tahriman) because of the proximity of the contract to the prohibited. And if he authorizes the marriage of a third party (fuduli) by writing: is it an authorization by speech or by action? It is mentioned in Iman al-Jami' in the Fatawa: if he swears not to speak to so-and-so or says 'By Allah, I will not say anything to so-and-so,' and then writes a letter to him, he does not break his oath. And Ibn Sama'a mentioned in his Nawadir that he does break it. The condition is that the marriage occurs after the oath because if a third party marries him, then he swears not to marry, and then he authorizes, he does not break the oath by speech either because it goes back to the time of the contract. And there, he does not break it by direct action, so through authorization it is more so. And the author indicated that if he swears not to marry his slave or slave-girl, and then authorizes by speech, he breaks the oath just as he would by appointing an agent, because it is attributed to what depends on his permission for his ownership and guardianship. Similarly for his young son and daughter due to his guardianship over them. If they are adult, he does not break the oath except by direct action because he has no guardianship over them; rather he is like a stranger to them, so the oath attaches to the actual contract, which is his direct action. And if the one swearing is the slave or son, and his master marries him against his will, or his father marries him while he is insane, then they do not break the oath by that, unlike one who is coerced (mukrah) because action exists from him in reality, not from them. And in Jami' al-Fusulain: He said, 'Every woman I marry or whom someone else marries for my sake and I authorize (it), she is thrice divorced' - there is no ground for its permissibility. And in [unclear]: his trick (hila) is that a third party marries him without his order, then he authorizes it, so he breaks the oath before the woman's authorization? No, because of lack of ownership. Then she authorizes, but her authorization does not work, so they renew the oath and it becomes permissible. This trick is only needed if he said 'and I authorize it.' If he did not say it, al-Nasafi said: the third party marries for him, then she is thrice divorced because the condition is marriage by another, since it is absolutely for him, but she does not become forbidden to him because the divorce occurred before entering the husband's ownership. I say: there is looseness, as divorce before ownership is impossible. And in al-Khulasa: If he says 'Every woman who enters into my marriage (nikah) is divorced,' this is like saying 'Every woman I marry.' Likewise if he says 'Every woman who becomes lawful for me.' And if he says 'Every slave who enters my ownership is free,' then a third party buys a slave and he authorizes by action, he breaks the oath according to all because ownership has many causes. And it is reasoned in 'Umdat al-Fatawa for the first that entering into marriage does not have only one cause which is marriage, so there is no difference whether he mentions it or not. Based on this, if he says 'Every woman who enters into my protection (ismati) is divorced,' then a third party marries him and he authorizes by action, and he does not break the oath, as is not hidden. In al-Qasimiyya: His statement 'Pay the dirham to her' is an authorization by action, and it has occurred. Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the legal effect of oaths regarding marriage, particularly when a third party (fuduli) conducts the marriage contract and the oath-taker subsequently ratifies it. The commentary opens by explaining that ratification by action (such as kissing with desire, intercourse, or sending dowry) counts as fulfillment of the marriage, but it is makruh tahriman (prohibitedly disliked) because it is close to the prohibited act of marriage without a proper contract. Ratification by writing is debated: according to al-Jami' al-Fatawa, writing a letter does not break an oath of silence, but Ibn Sama'a holds that it does. The ruling hinges on whether the marriage occurs before or after the oath; if the fuduli married him before the oath, then ratification by speech does not break the oath because it relates back to the time of the contract. The author then addresses oaths regarding marrying one's slaves or minor children: if the guardian ratifies by speech, the oath is broken. For adult children, only direct action breaks the oath. If the one swearing is the slave or son, and the master/father marries him against his will, the oath is not broken. A detailed trick (hila) is presented: if a man says 'Every woman I marry or someone marries for me and I authorize, she is divorced,' the way to avoid the divorce is for a third party to marry him without his order; then he authorizes after the oath, but the authorization doesn't count because the woman hasn't yet authorized. They then renew the oath and the marriage becomes valid. The passage also discusses subtle differences in phrasing: 'enters my marriage' vs 'enters my protection' and their implications. The commentator al-Marghinani (or the author of the sharh) explains that the trick is only needed if the oath includes 'and I authorize'; without it, the third party's marriage triggers divorce. The text cautions that divorce before ownership is impossible. The lesson continues with specific rulings on sending dowry, seclusion as authorization, and concludes with the statement that if someone says 'If I marry a woman by myself, my agent, or a third party, then my wife is divorced,' there is no escape if he authorizes by action—unless he takes the case to a Shafi'i judge to annul the suspended oath.

Key Terms
فضولي (fudūlī): A third party who acts without authorization to contract a marriage for someone else.
إجازة (ijāza): Ratification or authorization of a prior contract, either by speech or by action.
يمين (yamīn): An oath (swearing by Allah or a equivalent expression) that creates a binding obligation to do or not do something.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, ratification of a fuduli marriage by action (like sending dowry) constitutes fulfillment of the oath to marry, while writing does not break an oath of silence unless intended as speech.
