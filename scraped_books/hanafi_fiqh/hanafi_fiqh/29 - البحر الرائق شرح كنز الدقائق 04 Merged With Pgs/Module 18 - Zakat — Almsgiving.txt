Expiation of the Oath: Three Options
Cites Quranic verse for expiation options.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three forms of expiation for a broken oath and explain the meaning of takhyir (choice) based on the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
The Quran gives a choice among three expiations: freeing a slave, feeding ten poor, or clothing them.

This lesson examines the ruling on expiation (kaffarah) for a broken oath. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq quotes the Quranic verse and explains that the word 'aw' (or) indicates choice, not combination. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly fulfilling the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The expiation (kaffarah) for a broken oath is wajib based on the Quranic command.
The act is the expiation itself, not the oath.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse (5:89) explicitly lists the three options.
The verse is the primary evidence.
3
Analyze the wording
The word 'aw' (أو) indicates takhyir (choice), meaning the person may choose any one of the three.
Hanafi usul holds that 'aw' in commands gives a choice unless otherwise indicated.
4
Note the conditions
Feeding must be from the average of what one feeds one's family; clothing must cover most of the body.
These are derived from the verse's wording.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, the expiator selects one of the three options and fulfills it.
All three are equally valid.

Commentary
This passage defines the expiation (kaffarah) for a broken oath. The author states that the expiation is one of three options: freeing a slave, feeding ten poor people, or clothing them. This is based on the explicit text of the Quran (5:89). The word 'aw' in Arabic indicates that the person has a choice among the three. The text also mentions that the ruling is similar to the expiation in zihar (a form of divorce). The phrase 'from the average of what you feed your families' indicates a standard of moderate feeding. The Hanafi position is that these are all valid forms of expiation, and the person may choose any one.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffarah): The expiatory act required for breaking a valid oath under Shariah.
تخيير (takhyir): The option given to the person to choose among the three forms of expiation.

Oaths with Istithna': When an Exception Protects You from Breaking Your Vow
Commentary on the matn regarding istithna' in property oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that triggers an oath breach in a statement like 'I have only a hundred dirhams' and identify which property types are considered in the breach.

Core Idea
An exception in an oath limits the property considered; only owning more than the excepted amount in zakatable property breaks the oath.

Have you ever heard someone say, 'I swear I only have a hundred dirhams'? This common oath involves an exception (istithna'), which creates a legal boundary. The Hanafi school teaches that such an oath is not broken simply by owning property—it is only broken if you own more than the excepted amount, and that property must be of a zakatable type. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its application to various assets, helping you avoid accidental vow-breaking in financial matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its exception
The oath uses an exception phrase like 'except a hundred' (illa mi'ah) or 'only a hundred' (ma siwa al-mi'ah). This indicates the person claims to possess exactly that amount, negating anything more.
The exception tools are 'illa', 'ghayr', and 'siwa'.
2
Determine the condition for breaking
The condition for breaking the oath (hanth) is that the person must own MORE than the excepted amount. Owning exactly the excepted amount or less does not break the oath, even if the property is of the same type.
The exception includes all parts of the excepted amount; owning part of it (e.g., ten dirhams when excepting fifty) is not a breach.
3
Assess the type of property owned
Only property classified as 'mal al-zakah' (zakatable wealth) or its equivalent (trade goods, grazing livestock subject to zakat) triggers the breach. Non-zakatable assets like personal residence, service slaves, or goods not for trade are excluded.
Dirhams and dinars both fall under zakatable wealth; owning dinars when the oath mentioned dirhams does break the oath.
4
Apply to subsidiary cases
If the person owns a slave for trade or livestock for zakat, it counts as zakatable wealth and breaks the oath. But a slave for service or real estate not for trade does not.
The text also cites fatwas from Abu al-Layth about oaths to stay with a debtor until payment, where intention and time frame affect the breach.
5
Distinguish from simple property oaths
The key difference: a simple oath 'I own no more than a hundred' is not broken by owning a hundred or less; only exceeding triggers it. This ruling prevents unintended violations when the person honestly has only the excepted amount.
Always consider the type and quantity relative to the exception.

Commentary
This passage discusses oaths involving exceptions, such as 'I have only a hundred dirhams' or 'I have except a hundred.' The ruling is that such an oath is only broken if the person owns more than the excepted amount. If he owns exactly the excepted amount or less, the oath is not broken. The key is that the exception applies to the whole amount, so owning part of the excepted amount (like ten dirhams when excepting fifty) does not break the oath. However, the type of property matters: if the oath is about dirhams but he owns dinars, it breaks because both are zakatable wealth. Similarly, if he owns trade goods or livestock subject to zakat, it breaks. But if he owns a non-trade slave or real estate not for trade, it does not break because they are not of the same category. The passage also includes a fatwa from Abu al-Layth about an oath of not parting until paid, emphasizing that the intention and time frame affect whether the oath is broken.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna'): Exception: a statement that excludes a specific amount from a general claim, e.g., 'I have except a hundred' means 'I have only a hundred.'
مال الزكاة (mal al-zakah): Zakatable wealth: property on which zakat is obligatory, including gold, silver, trade goods, livestock.
