What Counts as a Borrowed Item? The Requirement of Delivery
Definition of borrowable items and the condition of delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define what items are covered by an oath not to borrow and explain why delivery (taslim) is essential for a loan to be valid.

Core Idea
An oath not to borrow applies only to tangible items that can be lent with delivery; mere access without possession is not borrowing.

This lesson from al-Dhakhirah provides a general definition for items covered by an oath not to borrow: any tangible object whose benefit can be enjoyed while its substance remains. It then presents a dispute among early Hanafi scholars about borrowing a rope and bucket from a well, which clarifies that a loan requires physical delivery (taslim) into the borrower's hand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Find the definition: items that are 'mawjuda tasihhu i'aratuhu' – existing things whose lending is valid, meaning tangible, non-consumable items.
2
Understand the specific case
Read the example of borrowing a rope and bucket from a well in the oath-taker's property. Note the difference of opinion among mashayikh.
3
Analyze the reasoning
The scholars who say the oath is not broken argue that the borrower's hand was not established over the items because they remained in the owner's hand. This shows that i'arah requires delivery (taslim).
The text says 'li'anna yadahu lam tathbut 'alayhima'.
4
Apply the principle to the ride case
The author connects this to the earlier ride ruling: just as the rope and bucket were not delivered, the mount was not delivered in the ride case.

As for lending (i'arah) and borrowing (isti'arah), in al-Zahiriyyah: If he swore not to [lend?] and then so-and-so sent a proxy to the oath-taker and borrowed, and the oath-taker lent, he breaks his oath. And if he swore not to borrow from so-and-so, and then so-and-so accommodated him on his mount, and he accommodated him, he does not break his oath. End. And in al-Dhakhirah: If he swore not to borrow anything from so-and-so, it applies to every existing thing whose lending is valid, and it is a tangible object that can be benefited from while its substance remains. This indicates that lending is not complete except with delivery. And this is the way in the case when he accommodated him on his mount. By analogy to this reasoning, if he borrowed the rope and bucket from a well that is not in the ownership of the one against whom the oath was made, he breaks his oath. End. Imam al-Isbijabi added that from this category is maintenance (nafaqah): if he swore not to spend, and he appointed a proxy, he breaks his oath. Oaths and Legal Acts: Shuf'ah, Idhn, and Nafaqah
Rulings extending agency principle to shuf'ah, idhn, and nafaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that legal acts performed via proxy break an oath against them, while silence does not.

Core Idea
Appointing a proxy to surrender shuf'ah, permit a slave, or spend maintenance breaks the oath; silence does not.

This lesson extends the agency principle from i'arah to other legal acts: shuf'ah (pre-emption), idhn (permission), and nafaqah (maintenance). It also clarifies that silence – even if interpreted as consent – does not break an oath not to perform these acts. The rulings are drawn from al-Khaniyah, al-Zahiriyyah, and Imam al-Isbijabi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general extension
From al-Khaniyah: the same rule that lending via proxy breaks an oath applies to surrendering shuf'ah and giving permission (idhn). Note: 'yahnuthu fihima bi al-amr aydan.'
2
Examine the shuf'ah rulings
Read two scenarios from al-Zahiriyyah: (1) silence causing the loss of shuf'ah does not break the oath; (2) appointing a proxy to surrender does break it.
3
Examine the idhn rulings
Silently permitting a slave to trade does not break an oath; similarly, a virgin's silent consent to marriage does not break her oath. But a verbal or proxy permission would break it.
4
Understand the nafaqah extension
Al-Isbijabi adds: if a person swears not to spend (nafaqah) and appoints a proxy to spend, the oath is broken.

The Two Types of Lām in Oaths: Action vs. Object Allocation
Explaining the two types of ikhtiṣāṣ in oaths involving sale, purchase, hire, etc.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of lām in oaths (action allocation vs. object allocation) and state the condition required for each to break the oath.

Core Idea
The lām's position determines whether it requires the addressee's order or merely his ownership.

In Hanafi fiqh, the letter lām in oaths of sale, hire, and similar transactions carries a specific legal effect that depends on its placement relative to the verb and object. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, showing how a seemingly small linguistic difference—whether the lām precedes or follows the object—changes the condition for the oath to be broken. You will learn to identify these two patterns and apply them to real cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the lām's position
Look at the oath phrase. If the lām appears between the verb and its object (e.g., 'bictu laka thawban'), it is the first type. If it appears after the object (e.g., 'bictu thawban laka'), it is the second type.
In the first type, the lām attaches to the verb; in the second, it attaches to the object.
2
Determine whether the action admits substitution
For the first type, check if the verb (e.g., sale, hire, tailoring) can be performed by an agent. If yes, allocation of the action requires the addressee's order or knowledge. If not (e.g., eating, beating), this type does not apply; only the second type applies.
Actions like sale, purchase, and hire admit substitution (niyabah). Actions like eating, beating do not.
3
Apply the condition for breaking the oath (first type)
If the action admits substitution and the lām is before the object, the oath is broken only if the addressee ordered or knew about the action. Without order or knowledge, the oath remains intact, even if the action benefits the addressee.
The example from al-Farisi illustrates this: the swearer sold a garment belonging to Zayd without knowing, and the oath was not broken.
4
Apply the condition for breaking the oath (second type)
If the lām is after the object, the oath is broken whenever the object is owned by the addressee, regardless of his order or knowledge. The condition is purely ownership of the object.
In this case, the lām indicates 'ikhtiṣāṣ al-'ayn' (allocation of the object).

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is an expansion on the rule concerning the entry of the letter lām in oaths related to sale, purchase, hire, and similar transactions. The author explains that the lām can indicate either allocation of the action (fi'l) to the person addressed or allocation of the object ('ayn) to him. If the lām appears between the verb and its object, as in 'bictu laka thawban' (I sold to you a garment), it signifies that the action is performed specifically for the benefit of the addressee and by his order. Thus, if the person swears not to do such an action, he only breaks the oath if he performs it with the order or knowledge of the addressee. If the addressee's garment is sold without his knowledge, the oath is not broken because the action was not allocated to him by his command. On the other hand, if the lām appears after the object, as in 'bictu thawban laka' (I sold a garment to you), it indicates ownership of the object by the addressee. In that case, selling any garment owned by the addressee breaks the oath, regardless of whether the addressee ordered or knew. The passage then provides a detailed example from al-Farisi to illustrate the first case: a man swears, 'If I sold to you a garment, my slave is free.' He sells a garment that belongs to Zayd without knowing it, acting on an ambiguous order. The ruling is that he does not break his oath because the lām requires allocation by Zayd's order or knowledge, which is absent. Oaths to Buy for Another and Defining Ghulam in Beating Oaths
Expansion of oaths rulings on purchase and ghulam definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on oaths not to buy for someone by identifying when the order is for oneself, and explain the preferred interpretation of 'ghulam' in oaths of beating using Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
The oath not to buy for X is broken only if X orders for himself; 'ghulam' means child, not slave.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks intricate rulings within the chapter of oaths. It addresses two main areas: first, the conditions under which an oath not to purchase for someone is breached, including the effect of ordering for family members and subsequent ratification; second, an intra-madhhab dispute over the meaning of 'ghulam' in oaths of beating. Understanding these distinctions ensures correct fatwa and judicial application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath's subject
The oath 'I will not buy a garment for Zaid' is breached only if Zaid orders the purchase for himself. If Zaid orders it for his son or slave, the oath is not broken because the order is not for Zaid directly.
This applies even if the son is a minor; the key is that the beneficiary is not the one sworn against.
2
Understand the effect of agency and subsequent ratification
If the swearer sells a garment to a third party without Zaid's order but Zaid later ratifies the sale, it breaks the oath according to Muhammad al-Shaybani. Ratification (ijazah lahiqah) is treated like prior agency (wakalah).
This is based on a narration from Ibn Samaca; some sources conflict but the author clarifies it applies when the swearer intends ownership.
3
Recognize the intra-madhhab dispute on 'ghulam'
In oaths of beating ('daraba al-ghulam'), the term 'ghulam' is disputed. Zahir al-Din holds it means child (walad) based on Quranic usage (Surah al-Dhariyat 51:28). Qadikhan holds it means slave (abd) based on custom. The author sides with Zahir al-Din.
The Quranic evidence takes precedence over custom in determining linguistic meaning.
4
Distinguish between diyanatan and qada'an regarding intention
If the swearer intends a meaning that imposes hardship (such as intending ownership in a sale), his intention is accepted both religiously (diyanatan) and judicially (qada'an). If he intends leniency, it is accepted only religiously. This distinction does not apply to oaths by Allah, as there is no claimant for expiation.
The key is that the intended meaning must be plausible (muhtamal) in the oath's wording.

Conditional Emancipation with Option (Khiyar)
Analysis of oaths making emancipation conditional on sale or purchase when the contract includes a stipulation of option.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional emancipation when the sale or purchase includes an option (khiyar) and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding ownership.

Core Idea
A conditional emancipation is triggered even if the contract includes an option—the condition of sale/purchase is considered fulfilled.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific issue in oaths: when someone swears that a slave will be free upon a sale or purchase that includes a stipulation of option (khiyar). The key question is whether the condition is met. The Hanafi school rules that the emancipation takes effect, but the reasoning differs between Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, based on their views of ownership during the option period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
State the key scenario: a person says 'If I sell this slave, he is free' or 'If I buy this slave, he is free' and then executes the contract with a stipulation of option for himself. What is the hukm?
The ruling: the slave becomes free (the oath is broken).
2
Understand the effect of khiyar on ownership
Recognize that the ruling depends on who owns the slave at the moment the condition is fulfilled. For a sale with option for the seller, ownership remains with the seller. For a purchase with option for the buyer, the Sahibayn hold that ownership transfers to the buyer, while Abu Hanifa holds that ownership does not transfer.
Consensus: seller's option retains ownership. Dispute: buyer's option.
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Note that despite the disagreement on ownership, both positions lead to the same outcome: the slave becomes free. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that the condition (purchase) is met because the buyer owns the slave. Abu Hanifa argues that conditional emancipation is like immediate (munajjaz), so the condition triggers emancipation regardless of ownership.
The difference appears in other contexts, e.g., increase or decrease during the option period.
4
Apply to different khiyar configurations
Examine cases where the option is for the other party: if a seller sells with option for the buyer, the slave does not become free because ownership has left the seller. Similarly, if a buyer buys with option for the seller, the slave does not become free because ownership remains with the seller.
The passage also discusses purchase for another person, where the oath is not fulfilled.

Oath with Fasid and Mawquf Sale: Possession Determines Ownership
Detailed ruling on oaths with fasid and mawquf sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a fasid or mawquf sale breaks an oath and apply those conditions to different factual scenarios regarding possession and ownership.

Core Idea
A fasid sale breaks an oath only if it transfers ownership; possession determines the outcome.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq unpacks the concise rule that a fasid or mawquf sale, unlike a batil sale, can break an oath. The key determinant is whether ownership of the subject matter changes hands at the time of the contract, which hinges on the presence and possession of the item. We will trace the reasoning for both oaths concerning sale and purchase.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Oath Scenarios
The passage addresses two distinct oaths: 'If I sell you, you are free' and 'If I purchase him, he is free'. Note the different rulings for each.
The first scenario involves the seller's oath; the second involves the buyer's oath.
2
Determine Ownership after a Fasid Sale (Seller's Oath)
For the sale oath, check whether the seller's ownership is displaced. If the item remains in the seller's possession or is in the buyer's possession as a trust/pledge, ownership stays with the seller, so the oath is broken. If the buyer has possession and the item is guaranteed by itself, ownership transfers to the buyer, so the oath is not broken.
Guaranteed by itself (mamun bi-nafsihi) means the item's value is in the buyer's liability, e.g., a usurped item.
3
Determine Ownership after a Fasid Purchase (Buyer's Oath)
For the purchase oath, the key is whether the buyer takes possession (qabd) immediately after the contract. If the item is present with the buyer at contract time, he becomes qabid and owns it, triggering the oath. If absent, it depends on whether the item is guaranteed by itself (constructive ownership) or by something else (no ownership).
If absent and an amana or rahn, the buyer does not become qabid, so the oath is not broken.
4

Abu Yusuf's ruling: a fasid purchase dissolves the oath, so a later sound purchase does not revive it. Contrast with marriage: a fasid marriage does not dissolve the oath, so a subsequent sound marriage triggers the condition.
The difference arises because marriage is not purely a property transaction; a fasid marriage lacks full legal effect for oath purposes.

Oaths and Sale with Khayar: The Abu Yusuf–Muhammad Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oaths and option contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on whether a sale with khayar breaks an oath, and apply the dispute to predict the thamarah al-khilaf in a given scenario.

Core Idea
A sale with khayar does not break an oath until ownership transfers, according to Abu Yusuf; Muhammad disagrees.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute over the effect of an option condition (khayar) on oaths involving sale or purchase. Abu Yusuf argues that a contract with khayar is not a complete sale until the option lapses, so an oath conditional on the sale is not broken until ownership is confirmed. Muhammad, his student, considers the contract itself a sale, so the oath breaks immediately. This difference has practical consequences for when a conditional divorce, manumission, or expiation becomes due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its condition
Note the structure: a person swears or makes a conditional statement (e.g., 'If I buy this slave, he is free') and then enters a sale with an option clause (khayar). The question is whether the condition is fulfilled at the moment of the contract or only when the option expires.
All examples involve sale or purchase as the trigger.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and his dalil
Abu Yusuf holds that the oath is not broken by the contract itself. His reasoning: the term 'sale' (bay') in common usage does not include a contract with khayar because it lacks immediate transfer of ownership. The buyer becomes owner only after the option lapses, so the condition is met then.
He also cites the case where a slave is freed after the option period, confirming ownership transfer.
3
State Muhammad's position and his dalil
Muhammad holds that the oath is broken immediately upon the contract. His reasoning: the contract is still a valid sale in essence; the option only delays the effect. The reality of buying or selling is present, so the oath is violated as soon as the contract is concluded.
Muhammad also reports Abu Yusuf's own ruling that the slave becomes free after the option, but he uses that to argue that the sale does cause freedom eventually.
4

If a man swears 'If I buy this slave, he is free' and buys with a three-day option, according to Abu Yusuf the slave is not freed until after the option period if the sale is upheld. According to Muhammad, the slave is freed immediately, even if the option is later exercised to cancel the sale (though the text does not discuss the implications of cancellation). This affects when the man must act and whether expiation is due.
The difference applies to divorce, manumission, and vows.
5
Note the later tarjih (preferred view)
The author mentions that al-Qadi al-Isbijabi ruled that the oath is broken without noting the disagreement, and establishes a principle: any sale that causes ownership or is subject to ratification breaks the oath. This indicates that the later relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Muhammad's view.

Commentary
This passage expands on the principle from the matn: an oath is broken by a facid (defective) or mawquf (suspended) contract, but not by a batil (void) contract. The author first explains two scenarios: In the first, the seller swears to a buyer 'If I sell you, you are free' and sells with a facid contract. The ruling depends on possession: if the item is still with the seller or with the buyer but as a trust or pawn, the buyer is not freed because ownership did not transfer. But if the buyer has actual or constructive possession (as in a guarantee), ownership transfers and the buyer is freed. In the second scenario, the buyer swears 'If I buy it, it is free' and buys with a facid contract. Again, if the item is with the seller, the buyer does not own it and it is not freed. If the buyer takes possession immediately, he owns it and it is freed. If the item is absent but guaranteed (like a usurped item), ownership transfers and it is freed. If it is a trust or pawn, no immediate possession, so no ownership and no freeing. Then the text relates a fatwa from Abu Yusuf: if someone buys with a facid contract then cancels and buys correctly, the slave is not freed because the first purchase was a real purchase that dissolved the oath. This differs from marriage: a facid marriage does not dissolve an oath about marriage. The al-Dhakhira confirms that selling with a facid contract breaks the oath because the contract itself is valid in essence. Next, the mawquf contract: if a seller swears to sell and a fuduli (unauthorized agent) sells to an absent person, and the owner ratifies, the oath is broken from the time of ratification. For a buyer, if he buys from a fuduli, the oath is broken upon ratification. However, al-Tabyin contradicts this. The passage then explains the ruling for batil contracts: they do not break the oath because they are not sales at all. Examples: buying with wine or pig is facid (since wine and pig are property according to Hanafi), so it breaks the oath. But buying with blood or carrion is batil because those are not property. Buying a mukatab, mudabbar, or umm walad does not break the oath initially because their right to freedom prevents the sale. However, if a judge ratifies for a mukatab/mudabbar, or the mukatab himself ratifies by dissolving his kitabah, then the sale is valid and the oath is broken. The text ends with a discussion of the contradiction in al-Tabyin and a principle that the oath is broken whenever the essence of the sale is present, even if ownership is temporarily blocked.

Key Terms
بيع فاسد (bay' fāsid): A sale contract that is valid in its pillars but has a defect in its conditions (like riba or uncertainty), lacking full effect immediately.
بيع موقوف (bay' mawqūf): A suspended sale made by an unauthorized person (fuduli) pending ratification by the owner.
بيع باطل (bay' bāṭil): A void sale that lacks an essential element (e.g., selling what is not owned) and never effects ownership.

When Does a Defective Sale Break an Oath?
Clarifying when void and disputed contracts trigger oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which types of sales trigger an oath (fasid, mawquf, batil) and explain the Hanafi reasoning distinguishing fasid from batil.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a fasid sale is still a 'sale' for oath purposes; a batil sale is not.

When someone swears not to sell or buy, what counts as a 'sale'? The answer is not as straightforward as it seems. A fasid (voidable) sale, despite its defect, still triggers the oath, while a batil (void) sale does not. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rationale, the treatment of suspended sales, and the specific cases of slaves, free persons, and marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three contract types
First, note the three categories discussed: fasid (voidable), mawquf (suspended), and batil (void). The passage gives the general rule for each.
Fasid is a contract valid in formation but defective in condition; mawquf depends on ratification; batil is legally null.
2
Trace the reasoning for fasid
The passage argues that a fasid sale is a 'complete sale' in essence; the defect is only in the legal effect (ownership delay), which does not negate the act. This is why the oath is broken.
Contrast with batil: batil lacks both form and effect.
3
Analyze the mawquf sale
A suspended sale (e.g., by a fuduli) triggers the oath at the time of contract, before ratification, because the contract itself is validly formed.
The passage notes a disagreement in al-Tabyin, but the relied-upon view is that the oath is broken immediately.
4
Examine the special cases: slaves and free persons
Selling a mukatab, mudabbar, or umm walad does not initially break the oath due to the impediment of freedom rights. However, if a judge or the mukatab ratifies, the impediment is removed and the oath is broken. Selling a free person triggers the oath according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, following the literal meaning.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa applies the literal meaning to free women and umm walad; Abu Yusuf restricts it to free Muslim men.
5
Compare with marriage
The passage concludes that a fasid marriage does not trigger an oath not to marry because its purpose (halal intercourse) is not achieved, unlike sale where ownership is achieved despite the defect.
This highlights the ta'lil (causal reasoning) based on the primary objective of each contract.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raʼiq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqaʼiq, addresses the critical distinction between valid, void, and suspended contracts in relation to oaths. The author begins by establishing the general rule: if someone swears not to sell and then sells a fasid (voidable but not void ab initio) sale, he breaks his oath. The reasoning is that such a sale is 'complete' in its formation; the only deficiency is that ownership (the legal effect) is delayed, but this does not make it not a sale. The same applies to oaths about past actions (e.g., 'If I bought today'). The passage then distinguishes between three types of sale: mawquf (suspended, pending ratification), fasid (voidable), and batil (void). A suspended sale triggers the oath at the time of contract, not only upon ratification (contrary to views in al-Tabyin). A void sale (batil) does not trigger the oath because it lacks the legal meaning and effect of sale. The author provides examples: buying with wine or pig triggers the oath because they are considered property (mal) in Hanafi law, whereas buying with blood or carrion does not because they are not property. Significant detail is given about buying a mukatab, mudabbar, or umm walad: initially the oath is not broken because the right to freedom prevents full ownership transfer. However, if a judge ratifies or the mukatab himself rescinds his contract, the impediment is removed and the oath is broken. There is a dispute among later scholars about the converse case (buying with such slaves). Learning Objective

Core Idea
Defect does not negate the genus; paying with defective dirhams fulfills an oath, but paying with lead or copper does not.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on oaths to pay debts when the debtor pays with coins of varying quality. The key principle is that payment fulfills the oath only if the item paid is of the same genus as the debt. Defective dirhams remain dirhams, so they satisfy the oath, whereas lead or copper coins are not dirhams and do not. Oath Violation via Purchase or Consumption: Conditions for Breaking the Oath
Expansion on oath-breaking by purchase or consumption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three scenarios from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq to determine whether an oath not to take a debt today is broken when the creditor buys goods or consumes property of the debtor.

Core Idea
Taking possession of equivalent value today breaks the oath; fungible consumption does not.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on a matn concerning oaths and the taking of debts. It presents three detailed cases: purchase with immediate possession, defective purchase, and consumption of the debtor's property after usurpation. Each case turns on whether the creditor effectively 'takes' the debt by receiving equivalent value. The key distinction between fungible and valuable goods plays a central role.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the prohibited act
The oath is 'I will not take my debt from my debtor today.' The prohibited act is taking the debt on that day. The passage examines alternative ways the creditor might receive value that could count as taking the debt.
2
Analyze the purchase scenario
If the creditor buys something from the debtor and takes possession the same day, the oath is broken. If possession is delayed, it is not broken. Note that the contract is assumed valid unless specified.
The timing of possession is decisive.
3
Analyze the defective purchase scenario
If the purchase is fasid (defective) but possession occurs, the oath is broken only if the value of the goods equals or exceeds the debt. If less, partial payment does not break the oath.
Value, not price, is the measure.
4
Analyze the consumption scenario
If the creditor consumes the debtor's property, the result depends on the type of goods. For fungibles (mithli), the obligation is to return the like, not value, so the oath is not broken. For valuables (qimi), the obligation is value; if the value equals the debt, the oath is broken, but only if the creditor first usurped (ghasb) the property, because usurpation establishes possession (qabd).
Without ghasb, no possession, so no breach.
5
Synthesize the conditions
Summarize: Oath is broken when the creditor receives possession of property worth at least the debt on the same day, either by valid or defective purchase, or by consuming a valuable after usurpation. Fungible consumption never breaks the oath because it does not result in taking the debt.
Muqassa (set-off) only applies when possession is established.

Commentary on a matn regarding an oath about debt collection, with conditions and exemptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which an oath not to take debt piecemeal is broken, identify permissible exceptions (necessary separation, defective coins), and apply the distinction between 'his debt' and 'from his debt'.

Core Idea
An oath not to take debt piecemeal is only broken when the entire debt is taken separately; lump sum or necessary separation does not break it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a common oath in debt collection. The precise wording of the oath determines when it is broken. Understanding the nuances—such as necessary separation (tafriq daruri) and the status of defective or counterfeit coins—is essential for correct legal application. The commentary also provides practical legal tricks to avoid unintended oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath wording
Look at the precise phrase: 'I will not take his debt dirham by dirham.' The key is the possessive 'his debt' (dīnahu), which implies the entire debt must be taken piecemeal for the oath to break.
If the wording were 'from his debt' (min dīnahu), the ruling would differ.
2
Determine the condition for breaking
The condition (shart) for breaking the oath is taking the whole debt separately (piecemeal). Taking it all at once does not break the oath.
The author explains that the definite and possessive form directs the action to the whole.
3
Recognize exceptions
Necessary separation (tafriq daruri), such as weighing in two batches with no delay other than the weighing process, does not break the oath. Also, if after taking the whole in one go, the creditor later returns a defective coin (ustuqqa), the oath is not broken unless he replaces it. Finding counterfeit coins (zuyuf) after taking the whole means the oath is already fulfilled.
These exceptions prevent the oath from being broken due to practical necessity or post-fulfillment issues.
4
Distinguish between oath wordings
If the oath uses 'from his debt' (min dīnahu), taking any portion separately breaks the oath, because the condition is taking some of the debt separately, not the whole.
This distinction is crucial; the same act can have different rulings based on wording.
5
Apply legal tricks if needed
For someone who swore not to take in installments but wants to do so, a trick (hīla) is to leave one dirham unpaid and take the rest in one lump sum. Alternatively, have a third party collect on your behalf.
These tricks are legitimate ways to avoid breaking the oath without violating its intent.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a detailed commentary on the oath 'I will not take my debt dirham by dirham.' The key point is that the condition for breaking the oath is taking the entire debt in a piecemeal manner. Taking the whole in one go does not break it. Necessary separation, such as weighing in two installments without unnecessary delay, is excused. If the oath is time-bound, the condition is taking the whole within the day in a piecemeal fashion. If one takes the whole but later returns a defective coin, it does not break the oath unless he replaces it; but returning counterfeit coins does not break because the oath was already fulfilled. There is a crucial distinction between swearing 'not to take his debt dirham by dirham' (focus on taking the whole) versus 'not to take from his debt dirham by dirham' (focus on taking any part). The passage also provides legal tricks (hiyal) for avoiding breaking the oath, such as leaving a small amount and taking the rest, or having a third party collect.

Key Terms
تفريق ضروري (tafriq daruri): Necessary separation: taking debt in separate portions due to practical necessity, such as weighing in two batches without delay.
ستوقة (ustuqqa): A defective coin that is not accepted as valid currency; if received, the receiver may return it without breaking the oath.
زيوف (zuyuf): Counterfeit coins; returning them does not nullify the fulfillment of the oath.

Oath to Give: Gift vs Sale – The Role of Acceptance
Ruling with proof on gift vs sale oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an oath to give a gift is fulfilled by the donor's offer alone, while an oath to sell requires both offer and acceptance, and distinguish the Hanafi position from Zufar's view.

Core Idea
A gift completes by offer alone; a sale needs acceptance—because gifts are gratuitous, sales are exchange.

When someone takes an oath to give something or to sell something, the fulfillment of the oath depends on the completion of the underlying contract. But do these contracts complete differently? In this passage, Imam al-Haskafi explains a key distinction: a gift, being a gratuitous contract, is completed by the donor's action alone, whereas a sale, being an exchange, requires both offer and acceptance. We will explore the rationale, the implications for oaths, and the scholarly differences that arise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Find where the passage states that an oath to give a gift is fulfilled without acceptance, but an oath to sell is not. Note the two scenarios: positive and negative oaths.
The ruling is given in the first line: 'He is absolved by giving a gift without acceptance, unlike a sale.'
2
Understand the underlying distinction
The passage explains the difference: a gift is a 'gratuitous contract' (عقد تبرع) completed by the donor, while a sale is an 'exchange contract' (عقد معاوضة) requiring action from both sides. Identify this key principle.
The phrase 'الهبة عقد تبرع فيتم بالمتبرع' is the core textual proof.
3
Trace the general principle for contract types
The passage extends the rule to all exchange contracts (sale, lease, currency exchange, etc.) and all gratuitous contracts (gift, charity, loan, etc.). Note that Zufar contradicts for gift, but the Hanafi position is relied upon.
Zufar's view is mentioned but not accepted; the consensus is that gift does not require acceptance.
4
Examine the dispute on loan (qarḍ) and release (ibrā’)
The passage reports Abu Yusuf's requirement of acceptance for loan, Abu Hanifa's two narrations, and the ruling that loan is like gift. Similarly, ibrā’ is compared to both sale and gift, but the favoured view attaches it to gift.
The text mentions 'لا يعلم خلاف أن الاستقراض كالهبة' to settle the loan issue.
5
Apply the subtle point about the donee's presence
The passage ends with a nuance: for an oath to give a gift, the presence of the donee is a condition for the oath to be broken. If the donee is absent, the oath is not broken by agreement.
This is from Sharḥ al-Majma‘; the case from al-Khāniyya illustrates the principle.

Oath Denying Wealth: Is a Debt Considered Wealth?
Explaining that a debt is not considered wealth for the purpose of an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath denying wealth when the oath-taker has a debt, and explain the legal reasoning (qiyas) that a debt is not considered wealth for oath purposes.

Core Idea
A debt is not wealth — it is a description in the dhimmah that cannot be grasped, so an oath denying wealth is not broken.

When someone swears 'I have no wealth' but has a debt owed to them, does the debt qualify as wealth? The Hanafi school says no, because a debt is not tangible property. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the qiyas argument behind it, including the concept that debts are repaid by equivalents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The person does not break the oath when swearing 'no wealth' if he has a debt. This is the explicit ruling in the passage.
2
Understand the definition of wealth
In Hanafi fiqh, 'wealth' (mal) refers to tangible property that can be physically possessed. A debt is not tangible.
The passage states: 'because a debt is not wealth; rather it is a description in the dhimmah'.
3
Trace the qiyas argument
The asl (original case) is tangible physical wealth, which is considered mal. The far' (new case) is a debt. The illa (effective cause) is physical possession and actual grasping. Since a debt cannot be grasped, it is not mal.
This is a qiyas that a debt is not like physical property.
4
Consider the repayment by equivalents
The passage notes that debts are repaid by equivalents, meaning the debtor gives a substitute, not the exact thing. This further shows a debt is not the same as actual wealth.
The phrase 'الديون تقضى بأمثالها' supports the reasoning.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may consider a debt as wealth, leading to a different ruling. However, the Hanafi position is that the oath is not broken.
This ikhtilaf is noted in the hanafi_position_box as mukhtalaf fih.
