Prohibited Days and Menstruation's Effect on Expiation Fast Continuity
Ruling on days invalid for expiation fast and effect of hayd/nifas on continuity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which days invalidate expiatory fasting and explain why menstruation breaks continuity for oath expiation but not for two-month expiations in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Menstruation does not break continuity for two-month expiations due to impossibility; nifas always breaks.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq addresses two interconnected rulings: first, which days render expiatory fasting invalid, and second, how female bleeding (hayd and nifas) affects the required continuity (tatabu‘) of these fasts. The author emphasizes that menstruation does not break continuity for two-month expiations because a woman cannot find two consecutive months without menses, but it does break continuity for the three-day oath expiation. A subtle intra-Hanafi disagreement between Muhammad and Abu Yusuf further illustrates the role of possibility in determining the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited days and their effect
List the five prohibited days (two Eids and three days of Tashriq). Understand that fasting on these days is prohibited and therefore cannot fulfill an expiation; if these days occur during a two-month fast, continuity is broken even if one fasts them, because one could choose two months free of these days.
The logic is based on possibility: if an unobstructed two-month period exists, the fast must be performed in that period.
2
Understand the rule of tatabu‘ (continuity) and its exceptions
Tatabu‘ is required for two-month expiations (for zihar, killing, and breaking Ramadan fast). Illness and nifas break continuity. Hayd does not break continuity for these two-month expiations because it is impossible for a woman to find two months without menstruation. However, for the three-day oath expiation, hayd does break continuity because three consecutive days free of menses are possible.
The principle: what is impossible is excused; what is possible is required.
3
Distinguish hayd from nifas
Nifas (postnatal bleeding) breaks continuity in all expiation fasts without exception. Hayd only breaks continuity in the three-day oath expiation; it does not break continuity in two-month expiations for killing or breaking fast.
This distinction is explicitly cited from al-Muhit and al-Bada’i‘.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Muhammad holds that if a woman fasts one month, then menstruates, and then reaches menopause (i.e., becomes permanently pure), she must restart because she now has the ability to maintain continuity. Abu Yusuf holds that if she becomes pregnant in the second month, she continues without restarting. The relied-upon position is that hayd does not break continuity unless she later becomes able to observe it.
The dispute hinges on when ‘impossibility’ ceases to apply.

Commentary
This passage contains rulings on which days expiatory fasting is invalid and how menstruation affects continuity. First, the five prohibited days: the two Eids and the three days of Tashriq (11-13 Dhul Hijjah). Fasting on these days is prohibited and thus the full expiation cannot be fulfilled. Similarly, Ramadan cannot be used for expiatory fasting because it is already obligatory for the healthy resident. The author notes that these days break the continuity of a two-month fast even if one fasts them, because one could choose two months free of such days. Then the effect of menstruation: unlike postnatal bleeding (nifas) which breaks continuity in all expiation fasts, menstruation does not break continuity in expiation for killing or breaking fast (in Ramadan) because women cannot find two months completely free of menses. However, for expiation of an oath (three days), menstruation does break continuity because she can find three consecutive days free. The difference between hayd and nifas is emphasized. Within Hanafi school, Muhammad holds that if she fasts one month then menstruates and then reaches menopause (i.e., becomes pure indefinitely), she must restart because she now can maintain continuity. Abu Yusuf holds that if she becomes pregnant in the second month, she continues without restarting. The relied-upon position is that hayd does not break continuity for the two-month expiation unless she becomes able to observe continuity (e.g., menopause).

The Hanafi Ruling on Vows: Intention Determines the Obligation
Commentary on the ruling of vows and the distinction in conditional vows

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between vows conditioned on intended vs unintended events and apply the Hanafi takhyeer (choice) ruling for unintended conditions.

Core Idea
A vow on an unintended condition becomes like an oath: you may fulfill the vow or pay kaffarah.

This lesson dives into a nuanced ruling within the Hanafi school regarding vows (nadhr). While the default is that a vow must be fulfilled, the great imam Abu Hanifa later retracted his position and introduced a distinction based on the intent behind the condition. The source passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on this by reconciling two conflicting hadiths. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the ruling in everyday situations, especially for vows made out of prohibition or deterrence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for vows
The base text states that whoever makes a vow, whether unconditional or conditional, must fulfill it when the condition occurs. This is supported by the hadith: 'Whoever makes a vow and specifies it must fulfill what he specified.'
This is the apparent position of the school.
2
Discover the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Hanifa initially held that all vows must be fulfilled, but later retracted and said that if the condition is not intended to occur, the vow is like an oath and can be expiated by kaffarat al-yamin. Muhammad al-Shaybani agreed with the retraction.
This retraction appears in the Nawadir narrations, not in Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Understand the detailed distinction
The key is the intention behind the condition. If the condition is intended (e.g., 'If Allah heals my sick person'), the vow must be fulfilled because it is like an unconditional vow upon fulfillment. If the condition is not intended (e.g., 'If I enter this house,' said to prohibit oneself from entering), the vow contains the meaning of an oath (prohibition) and the person has a choice: fulfill the vow or pay kaffarah.
This distinction is preferred by the scholars of Balkh and Bukhara and is the basis for fatwa.
4
Reconcile the two hadiths
The hadith 'Whoever makes a vow and specifies it must fulfill it' applies to unconditional vows and those conditioned on intended events. The hadith 'The expiation of a vow is the expiation of an oath' applies only to vows conditioned on unintended events. This resolves the apparent conflict.
The reconciliation is based on the presence or absence of the 'meaning of an oath' in the vow.
5
Apply the distinction to a scenario
Given a new case, identify whether the condition is intended or not. If intended, the vow must be fulfilled. If not intended, the person may choose between fulfillment and kaffarah.
This takhyeer is a distinctive feature of the later Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the ruling of vows from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It begins by stating the general principle: whoever makes a vow, whether unconditional or conditional, and the condition is fulfilled, must fulfill the vowed act. This is based on the hadith: 'Whoever makes a vow and specifies it must fulfill what he specified.' The commentary then explains that the unconditional and conditional are both included. However, Abu Hanifa is reported to have retracted and said that if the condition is something the person does not intend to occur (e.g., 'if I enter this house, I owe a Hajj' when he does not want to enter), then it is like an oath and can be expiated by kaffarat al-yamin. This is also the view of Muhammad al-Shaybani. The Hidayah prefers this detailed distinction, and several scholars gave fatwa accordingly. The proof for the correct position is a hadith from Muslim that the expiation of a vow is the expiation of an oath. The commentary reconciles this hadith with the earlier hadith by applying the expiation-only hadith to the case where the condition is not intended to occur, and the fulfillment hadith to unconditional vows or vows conditioned on an intended event. Thus, the detailed distinction is established.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform a voluntary act of worship when a condition is fulfilled or unconditionally.
منجز (munajjaz): An unconditional vow that takes effect immediately.
معلق (mu'allaq): A vow conditioned on a future event.
كفارة اليمين (kaffarat al-yamin): The expiation for breaking an oath: feeding/clothing ten poor people or freeing a slave, or fasting three days.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The established transmitted positions in the six books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Oath on Fasting: Phrasing and Conceptual Possibility
Ruling on oaths with different phrasings and conceptual possibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the threshold for breaking an oath based on phrasing and explain the Hanafi principle of conceptual possibility (tasawwur shar'an) for oath validity.

Core Idea
Oath validity depends on linguistic precision and conceptual possibility, not actual feasibility.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between different phrasings in oaths about fasting. When the verb alone is used, any act of fasting suffices; when the object or time is specified, a complete fast is required. But what happens when the sworn act is impossible at the time of the oath? This passage introduces the key principle of conceptual possibility (tasawwur shar'an) to resolve this, along with an internal Hanafi dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three phrasings and their rulings
Find in the text the three oath phrases: 'la asumu' (I will not fast), 'la asumu sawman' (I will not fast a fast), and 'la asumu yawman' (I will not fast a day). Note the different thresholds for breaking the oath: any fasting suffices for the first, but a full day is required for the latter two.
The key is whether the phrasing is general (verb only) or specified (object/time).
2
Analyze the linguistic reasoning
The text explains that when the verb is used alone (la asumu), the act of fasting is understood in its essential meaning—refraining from nullifiers with intention—so any amount counts. When the word 'sawm' (fast) or 'yawm' (day) is added, it implies the complete Shari'a fast (dawn to sunset). Understand the distinction between implicit (daruri) and explicit (ikhtiyari) expression.
The verbal noun (masdar) with the verb makes the act explicit, requiring full attainment.
3
Examine the problem of timing
Consider the scenario: someone swears 'I will certainly fast this day' after midday or after eating. The full day is impossible because fasting cannot begin after midday. Yet the oath is ruled valid. Why? Because the oath depends on conceptual possibility, not actual feasibility.
The passage uses the example of a forgetful person who eats and then continues fasting—this shows the act is conceptually possible in Shari'a.
4
Understand conceptual possibility (tasawwur shar'an)
The principle: an act need only be conceptually possible in Shari'a (i.e., it could be legislated), not actually permissible at the moment. The text contrasts this with the water jug case where the act is impossible in any form. Identify how this applies to fasting after eating and to prayer for a woman in istihadah.
The author's comparison to the forgetful person is an analogy (tanzir), not an exact example.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the oath is valid because conceptual possibility suffices. Abu Yusuf argues that if the act is legally impossible at the time, the oath cannot be valid. Determine which view is mufta bihi and why.
The dispute hinges on whether the oath requires actual feasibility or only conceptual possibility. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on oaths concerning fasting with different phrasings. The rule: if someone swears 'I will not fast' (la asumu), then fasting for even a short time (e.g., one hour) with intention breaks the oath because the act of fasting is defined as refraining from nullifiers with intention, and any amount of such refraining constitutes fasting. However, if the oath is phrased as 'I will not fast a fast' (la asumu sawman) or 'I will not fast a day' (la asumu yawman), then breaking the oath requires a complete fast from dawn to sunset, because 'fast' (sawm) in these contexts means the full Shari'a fast, and 'day' (yawm) explicitly indicates the full duration. The commentary explains the linguistic reasoning: when the verb alone is used (la asumu), the act of fasting is invoked in its essential meaning, so any amount suffices. When the masdar or time is specified, it requires the complete act. The passage then addresses a problem: if someone swears 'I will certainly fast this day' and it is after midday (zawal) or after he has already eaten, the oath is still valid, even though a Shari'a fast cannot be started after midday or after eating. This seems contradictory because the full day is not possible. The answer is that the oath hinges on conceptual possibility (tasawwur), not actual feasibility. Fasting after midday is conceptually possible, as in the case of a forgetful person who eats mistakenly and then realizes and continues fasting. Similarly, if he swore on his wife's prayer, the oath is valid even if she is in istihadah (chronic bleeding), because prayer is conceptually possible for her, unlike a woman in menstruation (hayd) where prayer is prohibited. The commentary discusses a dispute within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the oath is valid because conceptual possibility suffices; Abu Yusuf holds that such oaths are not valid because the act is legally impossible at the time of the oath. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as clarified by later authorities like al-Dhakhira. The passage ends by noting that the author's comparison to the forgetful person is an analogy (tanzir) to illustrate conceptual possibility, not an exact example.

Key Terms
صوم ساعة (sawm sa'ah): Refraining from nullifiers for a short period (an hour) with intention; counts as fasting in the general sense, sufficient to break an oath phrased as 'I will not fast'.
تصور شرعاً (tasawwur shar'an): The conceptual possibility of an act in Shari'a, even if not actually permissible in the given situation; it is sufficient for an oath to be valid.

Oath Formulations for Fasting: General vs Specific
Ruling on oaths for fasting with different wordings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the oath formulations 'I will not fast' and 'I will not fast a fast/a day' and state the Hanafi ruling for each.

Core Idea
The explicit mention of 'fast' or 'day' requires a complete legal fast; the mere verb implies any restraint.

Oaths related to fasting are common in daily life, and their validity and consequences depend on the precise wording used. This lesson examines the Hanafi distinction between a general oath not to fast and a more specific oath that mentions 'a fast' or 'a day.' Understanding this difference is crucial for determining when an oath is broken and expiation becomes obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath wording
Examine the precise Arabic phrase used in the oath: is it 'la asumu' (I will not fast) or 'la asumu sawman/yawman' (I will not fast a fast/a day)? The addition of the object changes the ruling.
The verb 'sama' alone implies the act of fasting; adding the verbal noun or a time specifies the intended scope.
2
Classify the formulation as general or specific
According to Hanafi usul, an unqualified verb (la asumu) refers to the minimum instantiation of the act. A qualified expression (la asumu sawman) refers to the complete legally valid act.
The commentary explains that the explicit object is 'volitional' and entails a complete act.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule for breaking the oath
For the general phrase, hanth (breaking the oath) occurs with any amount of fasting, even a moment with intention. For the specific phrase, hanth requires a full day of fasting from dawn to sunset.
The key is what is intended by the oath: the essence (imsak) or the legally complete form.
4
Check the condition for hanth
If the person performs an act that matches the wording, the oath is broken. Ensure the fasting is valid (with intention, avoiding nullifiers).
The ruling does not depend on the duration of the fast; intention is necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn from Kanz al-Daqa'iq concerning oaths (al-yamin) related to fasting and prayer. The matn states three rulings: (1) If someone swears 'I will not fast' (la asumu), he breaks the oath by fasting even for a moment with intention. (2) If he swears 'I will not fast a fast' (la asumu sawman) or 'I will not fast a day' (la asumu yawman), he only breaks the oath by fasting a complete day. (3) For prayer, swearing 'I will not pray' (la usalli) is broken by one rak'ah, while swearing 'I will not pray a prayer' (la usalli salatan) requires a two-rak'ah prayer. The commentary explains the reasoning: for fasting, the essence is restraint (imsak) from nullifiers with intention. In 'la asumu,' the verb implies the act itself, so any period of restraint counts. The Principle of Conceptualization (Tasawwur) in Oaths
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oaths to fast after noon

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that oaths are valid based on conceptual possibility (tasawwur) and evaluate the intra-madhhab dispute regarding oaths to fast after noon.

Core Idea
An oath is valid if the action is conceivable in the mind, even if practically impossible at the moment.

What happens if someone swears to fast a specific day after having already eaten or after noon? Is the oath valid? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement rooted in the principle of tasawwur. This lesson unpacks the dispute between Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf, and explains how the concept of mental possibility governs oath validity.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the dispute
Consider the oath: 'I will surely fast this day,' said after eating or after noon. The dispute: is the oath valid? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say yes; Abu Yusuf says it is valid but broken immediately.
The key is whether the action is possible at the moment of the oath.
2
Identify the Hanafi principle: tasawwur vs wujud
The oath depends on conceptual possibility (tasawwur) — can the mind conceive of the action? — not on actual existence (wujud) at the moment of swearing.
This principle is derived from the school's usul.
3
Analyze the evidence for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
Fasting after eating/noon is conceivable because it is legally recognized in cases of forgetfulness (nasi). Similarly, prayer during menstruation is conceivable even if prohibited. The existence of a similar feasible scenario proves tasawwur.
The analogy with the forgetful person and the menstruating woman shows that the act is not inherently unimaginable.
4
Understand Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf considers the act practically impossible at the moment of the oath (because fasting after eating is not legally valid at that time), so the oath is either invalid or broken immediately.
He focuses on the current impossibility rather than conceptual possibility.
5
Determine the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The preferred view is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the oath is valid and broken only if the person completes the fast. This is based on the strength of the principle of tasawwur.
This is the position taken in the Zahir al-Riwayah and by later authorities.

Oath Not to Fast Applies Only to Valid Fasting
Ruling on oath not to fast: valid vs invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an oath not to fast applies only to valid fasting and explain the exception for past oaths.

Core Idea
An oath not to fast only breaks if the fast is valid, because invalid fast does not glorify Allah.

When someone swears not to fast, what counts as breaking the oath? The Hanafi school holds that only a valid fast (sawm ja'iz) fulfills the condition and thus breaks the oath. An invalid fast, such as one without proper intention, does not achieve the purpose of glorifying Allah and therefore does not break the oath. However, if the oath is about a past action, the ruling differs. This lesson explores the proof and an apparent conflict with an earlier ruling in Kanz al-Daqaiq.

The term ja'iz here means valid/sound (sahih).
2
Note the exception for past oaths
If the oath refers to the past (e.g., 'I did not fast'), it applies to both valid and invalid fasting.
Because past actions are evaluated as they occurred, irrespective of validity.

Address the apparent conflict with the matn
The text from Kanz al-Daqaiq seems to break the oath in a case where the man said 'then I broke my fast that day'—implying the fast was invalid. But the author explains that the matn is more correct because it is a direct text (nass) from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir.
This shows the weight of zahir al-riwayah over later analogies.
4
Understand the proof structure
The ruling is based on qiyas khafi: The purpose of the oath (glorification) is not achieved by invalid fasting, so it cannot break the oath, unless the oath explicitly covers past acts where the intention is to affirm the occurrence of any fast.
This is a case of istihsan or implicit analogy.

The Issue of the Jug: Oaths and Impossible Conditions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oaths and impossible conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that oaths depend on possibility (imkan) rather than validity (sihhah), and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability in cases of natural impossibility.

Core Idea
If an oath condition becomes impossible due to a natural event, the oath is not broken because oaths rely on possibility, not validity.

A classic Hanafi case: a man says to his wife, 'If you do not pray Fajr tomorrow, you are divorced.' She menstruates at dawn, making prayer impossible. Does she break the oath? This 'issue of the jug' (mas'alah al-kooz) triggers a rich intra-Hanafi debate. The more correct view is she does not break the oath. This lesson draws the parallel to fasting and clarifies the underlying principle: oaths depend on the possibility of performing the action, not on the validity of the act.

Guided Path
1
Understand the original case (mas'alah al-kooz)
A man says to his wife: 'If you do not pray Fajr tomorrow, you are divorced.' At dawn, she menstruates, which prevents valid prayer. Does the divorce occur? The debate centers on whether the oath considers the possibility of valid performance.
The name 'jug' is incidental; the case is about impossible conditions.
2
State the more correct view (al-asahh)
According to al-Muntaqa and supported by Ibn al-Humam, the wife does not break the oath because menstruation removed the possibility of valid prayer. The oath depends on possibility, not on the act's validity.
This is the position preferred by the author of al-Bahr al-Raiq.
3
Apply the analogy to fasting
Similarly, if someone swears not to fast, and then the fasting becomes impossible (e.g., due to illness), or if the fast is invalid, the oath is not broken unless the condition was possible. The text extends the principle: 'the oath does not depend on validity but on possibility and conception.'
This is qiyas khafi connecting the jug case to fasting.
4
Analyze the detail from Abu al-Fadl
Abu al-Fadl adds a nuance: if the woman could have prayed within the time but delayed purposely (lengthened the prayer), she is liable. Otherwise, not. But the correct view is that she is liable in any case if the act was possible in absolute terms.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school, there is further refinement.

Interpreting 'Month' in Oaths and Legal Stratagems for Ramadan
Rules for interpreting 'month' in oaths and a legal trick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for interpreting 'month' in oaths and explain the legal stratagem for avoiding fasting Ramadan when sworn not to fast.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'month' refers to any month unless the action is location-based; a legal trick for Ramadan is to travel.

When someone swears 'I will not fast a month' or 'I will not travel a month,' the interpretation differs. The Hanafi school has detailed rules based on the nature of the action. This lesson unpacks these distinctions and also presents a practical legal stratagem (hilah) for someone who swore not to fast in Ramadan: travel and break the fast, thereby avoiding sin without breaking the oath.

Guided Path
1
Differentiate between types of actions in oaths
Oaths about fasting or other acts that are measured in time (like fasting) treat 'month' as referring to any month in one's lifetime, not necessarily the next month. Oaths about locative actions (like traveling to Basra) refer to the following month.
The principle: time is used to measure the action (for fasting) vs. to measure the oath (for travel).
2
Apply the rule to negation vs. affirmation
The text explains that negation (e.g., 'I will not fast a month') is judged by analogy to affirmation (e.g., 'I will fast a month'). In affirmation, it refers to any month; similarly in negation.
This is based on the rule 'al-ashya' tu'rafu bi-addadiha' (things are known by their opposites).
3
Understand the exception for 'if I abandon fasting a month'
If someone says 'If I abandon fasting a month,' it refers to the following month, unlike 'if I do not fast a month.' The text gives this as a distinction.
The phrasing 'abandon' (taraka) implies a specific month, while 'do not fast' (lam asum) is general.
4
Learn the legal stratagem for Ramadan
If a person swears not to fast Ramadan, he can avoid breaking the oath by traveling, because the obligation of fasting Ramadan is suspended during travel, and the oath only applies to obligatory fasting.
This is a hilah from al-Walwaljiyya.

The author notes a potential conflict with a ruling in the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq where a man was deemed to have broken his oath after saying 'then I broke my fast that day,' but explains that the matn's ruling is more authoritative because it is a direct text from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir. Next, the text revisits the well-known 'issue of the jug' (مسألة الكوز): if a man says to his wife 'If you do not pray Fajr tomorrow, you are divorced' and she menstruates at dawn, the more correct view is that she does not break the oath, as supported by al-Muntaqa and Ibn al-Humam. The author clarifies that 'dawn' (بكرة) means the time from fajr to sunrise. Applying this to fasting, the more correct view is that one does not break the oath by fasting invalidly, but al-Muhit affirms liability in both cases. In al-Dhahiriyya, there is a difference of opinion: some say the answer (no liability) is consistent only with Abu Yusuf's view, while others say it is consistent with all. Abu al-Fadl provides a detail: if the woman could have performed the prayer within the time but lengthened it, she is liable; otherwise not, but the correct view is that she is liable in any case because oaths depend on possibility, not validity. The text then discusses oaths involving a month: 'If I do not fast a month, my slave is free' does not refer to the immediate next month but to any month in his lifetime, unlike oaths about cohabitation or travel which refer to the next month. The reasoning is based on the principle that negation mirrors affirmation. Finally, a legal trick from al-Walwaljiyya: if someone swears not to fast Ramadan, he can travel and not fast, thus avoiding sin without breaking the oath.

Key Terms
شهر (shahr): A month; in oaths, whether it refers to the immediate next month or any month depends on the phrasing and context.
الحيلة (al-hilah): A legal stratagem to avoid breaking an oath without sin, here traveling to avoid fasting Ramadan.
