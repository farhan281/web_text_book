Cost Allocation in Bequests of Shared Items
Ruling on processing cost allocation for bequests of divisible items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for allocating processing costs when a bequeathed item has two components given to different beneficiaries, and apply it to new cases.

Core Idea
The burden of processing cost follows the benefit: the recipient of greater value bears the cost, but if both portions have significant value, cost is shared proportionally.

When a person bequeaths a divisible item—like sesame oil and its residue, or a slaughtered sheep's meat and skin—to two different beneficiaries, a natural question arises: who should pay for the processing (pressing, slaughtering, skinning)? The Hanafi school does not leave this to chance. It applies a clear principle: the cost is borne by the one who receives the primary benefit, unless the secondary portion holds substantial market value, in which case costs are shared in proportion to value received. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind these rules and how local custom ('urf) can shift the default allocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components
Determine the two separate items bequeathed from a single source (e.g., oil and thujar from sesame; meat and skin from sheep).
Each component must be capable of independent ownership.
2
Assess relative value
Evaluate which part has greater value. In the default ruling, the more valuable part bears the processing cost entirely.
Value is determined by the market at the time of bequest.
3
Check for custom or inherent value of the lesser part
If the lesser part has significant market value (e.g., thujar sold for fodder, skin always saleable), then cost becomes proportional.
Custom ('urf) can elevate a seemingly negligible item to one with legal weight.
4
Allocate costs accordingly
Apply the ruling: either full cost on the primary recipient, or proportional cost based on the value each receives.
The timing of processing (pre‑ or post‑division) matters: pre‑division cost falls on the owner of the primary asset.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author discusses two cases of bequests where a shared item has a processing cost. First: sesame oil and its residue (thujar). The general rule from earlier scholars is that the one who receives the oil bears the processing cost because the oil is the valuable part; the thujar, though saleable, is considered negligible. However, the author notes that in their lands (Central Asia), thujar is commonly sold for fodder, so the cost should be shared proportionally, like the case of wheat and straw. Second: a narrated case from Imam Muhammad: a sheep's meat and skin bequeathed separately. The slaughter cost before division is borne by the meat recipient because the meat is the primary value, and the skin is secondary. After slaughter, the skinning cost is shared proportionally. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi principle that the burden of cost follows the benefit: the one who receives the more valuable portion bears the cost of obtaining it.

Division of the One-Third Bequest in Ambiguous Deathbed Emancipation
Explaining the arithmetic division of the one-third bequest among three slaves freed ambiguously during marad al-mawt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the shares of freedom for each slave when a master emancipates three slaves ambiguously during deathbed illness, applying both the Sahibayn and Muhammad al-Shaybani's methods, and state the conditions under which the bequest is valid.

Core Idea
Deathbed emancipation is a bequest limited to one-third; the freed fractions depend on ambiguous statements and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf in calculation.

When a master on his deathbed says 'one-third of my slaves are free' among three slaves without specification, the one-third bequest is divided proportionally to the slaves' 'shares of freedom' derived from the ambiguous statement. The Hanafi school offers two calculation methods: the Sahibayn's (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Muhammad al-Shaybani's. This lesson unpacks the arithmetic and the juristic reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and underlying principle
Recognize that the master's ambiguous emancipation during marad al-mawt is treated as a bequest (wasiyya), valid only up to one-third of the estate unless heirs permit more.
The one-third limit is a fixed maximum; the calculation distributes that amount proportionally.
2
Determine the 'shares of freedom' (siham al-‘itq)
Based on the ambiguous wording, assign conceptual shares: according to Sahibayn, the fixed slave (al-thabit) gets 3 shares, the external (al-kharij) and internal (al-dakhil) get 2 shares each (total 7). According to Muhammad, the shares are 3, 2, 1 (total 6).
These shares are not actual values but theoretical units for proportional division.
3
Set the total estate value to make shares of freedom equal to one-third
Scale the estate so that the total shares of freedom (7 for Sahibayn, 6 for Muhammad) equal one-third of the estate. For Sahibayn, total estate = 21 units; for Muhammad, total = 18 units. Then the one-third bequest equals the total shares.
This ensures the heirs get two-thirds of the estate value.
4
Calculate each slave's freed fraction and work obligation
Freed fraction = (Slave's shares) / (Total shares). The remainder of his value must be earned through work (sa‘y). For Sahibayn: fixed freed 3/7, works 4/7; others freed 2/7, work 5/7. For Muhammad: fixed freed 1/2, works 1/2; external freed 1/3, works 2/3; internal freed 1/6, works 5/6.
The actual monetary values depend on the assigned unit (e.g., 21 units for Sahibayn).
5
Apply supplementary rules for debt and heir consent
If the estate is fully encumbered by debt, the bequest is void and each slave works for his entire value for creditors. If heirs permit the bequest beyond one-third, the calculation proceeds as in health. If the estate is sufficient but heirs do not permit, only the one-third is executed.
These rules affect the final outcome and must be considered in practice.

The Dual Nature of the Oath 'Free After My Death': Tadbir and Bequest
Explaining the double nature of the oath as both manumission and bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-school dispute regarding the oath 'every slave I own is free after my death' and apply the resolution involving implied wording.

Core Idea
An oath of freedom after death is both tadbir (immediate) and bequest (future) – thus includes after-acquired slaves.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq delves into a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute. When a master declares all his slaves – current and future – free after his death, does the oath cover only those owned at the time of utterance, or also those acquired later? Abu Yusuf restricts it; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad expand it by analyzing the oath's dual nature. We'll unpack their arguments and the critical refinement from Fath al-Qadīr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its legal classification
The oath 'every slave I own or I own is free after my death' appears to be tadbir (free upon death) but also has bequest (wasiyyah) implications because it takes effect from the third of the estate. Recognise that the passage treats it as having two aspects.
The same words create both an immediate tadbir and a future bequest.
2
State the positions: majority vs Abu Yusuf
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold it frees both current and future slaves; Abu Yusuf restricts to current slaves only.
This is the core intra-Hanafi dispute.
3
Analyse the majority's reasoning
The majority splits the oath into two acts: tadbir for present (immediate mudabbar status) and bequest for future (treated like a bequest of property that includes after-acquired property). The dalil is analogy (qiyas) to bequest of wealth.
They consider the master's purpose of reward and goodness.
4
Examine the critique from Fath al-Qadīr
Fath al-Qadīr critiques the majority for combining present and future in one expression, which is methodologically problematic. The refinement: the bequest aspect involves an implied separate phrase at death, avoiding the combination.
This resolves the issue without violating usul principles.
5
Apply the practical ruling
Manumission is from the third of the estate; if insufficient, slaves share proportionally. If there is debt, the freed slaves must work to pay, as with any mudabbar.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.

When a master says 'you are free one month before my death', the ruling is disputed among Hanafi scholars. Does it create a restricted tadbir that becomes absolute after the month? Or is it not tadbir at all, allowing sale? This lesson unpacks the arguments and the relied-upon position.

Clarification of non-tadbir phrasings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the phrases 'after my death by a day', 'after the death of so-and-so', and 'if so-and-so dies, you are free' as either wasiyyah of emancipation or conditional emancipation, and distinguish them from tadbir.

Core Idea
'After my death by a day' is a bequest; 'after death of so-and-so' is conditional, not tadbir.

Not every mention of death creates tadbir. This lesson examines phrasings that postpone freedom beyond the master's death or tie it to a third party's death. The passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies that these are bequests or conditional emancipations, with different rules on sale, sources of payment, and invalidity upon master's death.

Oaths on Wills and Trust: Literal vs. Legal Meaning
Two rulings on oaths from Dhakhirah and Waqi'at

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that oath-breaking is determined by the literal name of an action, not its legal effect, using the examples of wasiyyah and i'timan.

Core Idea
An oath is not broken by an act that has a different literal name, even if it shares the same legal effect.

This lesson examines two rulings from al-Dhakhirah and al-Waqi'at about oaths not to make a will (wasiyyah) and not to entrust property (i'timan). The key principle is that oath-breaking is determined by the literal meaning of the action, not the legal status the Shariah may assign to it. Understanding this distinction is crucial for accurate fiqh application and fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the action
First, clearly state what the person swore to do or not do. Then, identify the specific action they performed that may or may not break the oath.
The oath is about the literal act, not its legal consequences.
2
Determine the literal name of the action
Ask: what is this action called in everyday language? Is it a 'will' (wasiyyah) or a 'gift' (hibah)? Is it 'entrusting' (i'timan) or simply 'showing'?
The literal name is decisive for oath-breaking.
3
Compare with the legal effect
Note that the Shariah may treat the action as equivalent to another for certain rulings (e.g., death-illness gift treated like a will). But for oaths, only the literal meaning matters.
Legal effects do not change the literal name.
4
Apply the principle to the case
If the literal name does not match the oath's subject, the oath is not broken. If it matches, the oath is broken.
In the first case: giving a gift is not a will, so no oath broken. In the second: handing over a mount and saying 'hold it' is literal entrustment, so oath broken.
5
Derive the ruling and its scope
State the ruling clearly and note that this principle applies to all oaths regarding actions, not just these two cases.
The principle prevents unintended oath-breaking due to legal fiction.

وفي الذخيرة: حلف لا يوصي بوصية فوهب في مرض موته شيئاً لا يحنث لأن ذلك ليس بوصية لكن أعطى الشرع حكم الوصية فلا يظهر في حق حكم الحنث .اهـ. وفي الوقعات: حلف لا يأتمن فلاناً على شيء فأراه درهماً وقال انظر إلى هذا ولم يفارقه لا يحنث لأنه لم يأتمنه، ولو دفع إليه دابته وقال امسكها حتى أصلي فهو حانث لأنه أئتمنة عليها. ولم يذكر المصنف التولية وقد
And in al-Dhakhirah: If he swore not to make a will (wasiyyah), and he gave something as a gift in his death illness (marad al-mawt), he does not break his oath, because that is not a will, but the law gave it the ruling of a will, so it does not appear with respect to the ruling of breaking the oath. End. And in al-Waqi'at: If he swore not to entrust (i'timan) so-and-so with anything, and he showed him a dirham and said, 'Look at this,' and did not part from him, he does not break his oath, because he did not entrust him. And if he handed over his mount to him and said, 'Hold it until I pray,' he breaks his oath because he entrusted it to him. Commentary
This final section contains two rulings. The first, from al-Dhakhirah, deals with an oath not to make a will (wasiyyah). If the person gives a gift during his death illness (marad al-mawt), this does not break the oath, because a death-illness gift is not a will in name, even though legally it is treated like a will in some respects (e.g., limited to one-third of estate). The breach of an oath is determined by the literal meaning, not the legal effect. The second, from al-Waqi'at, concerns an oath not to entrust (i'timan) someone. Simply showing an item and asking to look at it without giving possession does not constitute entrustment. However, handing over property with an explicit request to hold it is entrustment, breaking the oath. The passage ends with a note that the author of Kanz did not mention tawliyah.

Key Terms
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A will: a disposition of property to take effect after death.
الإئتمان (al-i'timan): The act of entrusting someone with property, making them a trustee (amin).
الحنث (al-hanth): The breaking of an oath, requiring kaffarah (expiation).
