Master's Refusal and the Sale of Slaves: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on forced sale and spending when a master refuses maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on selling a slave for maintenance and apply the ruling to animals and shared property.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prohibits selling a slave directly; the Sahibayn allow it — the qadi's power depends on the master's property status.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq critically examines the author's wording and reveals a significant intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, over whether the qadi can sell a master's slave to secure maintenance. The dispute extends to animals and shared property, where later authorities lean toward the majority view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The passage deals with a master who refuses to maintain his slave. First, check if the slave has earnings; if so, maintenance is from earnings. If not, the master is forced to sell the slave. But 'forced to sell' is disputed.
The slave's ability to earn (e.g., being able-bodied or senile) affects the ruling.
2
Distinguish Slave Types
Some slaves cannot be sold at all: mudabbar (promised freedom after master's death) and umm walad (mother of master's child). For these, the master is only forced to spend; no sale is possible.
This is an exception to the general rule.
3
Understand the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Abu Hanifa holds that the qadi cannot sell a free person's property (the slave) without consent; instead, he imprisons the master until he sells. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) argue that the qadi can sell directly, analogizing to sale for debt.
The author's phrasing 'ordered to sell' (أمر ببيعه) avoids stating the qadi's sale, leaning toward Abu Hanifa's view.
4
Apply to Animals and Shared Property
Animals are not legally 'deserving' of maintenance, so the master is not forced to spend. However, it is sinful to neglect them. Abu Yusuf holds that the qadi can force spending, but the correct view is otherwise. For a shared animal, a partner can force the other to either sell or share expenses.
The later fatwa (al-Tahawi) follows Abu Yusuf, but the apparent madhhab opposes.

Commentary
This passage offers a critical refinement of the matn and earlier commentaries. It begins by explaining the author's words: if the master refuses maintenance, the slave's earnings are used first; if none, and the slave is not leasable, the master is forced to sell. The commentator notes that the author's phrasing is less precise: the order to sell implies the qadi sells, not the master. A major intra-Hanafi dispute follows: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow the qadi to sell the master's property to cover maintenance, while Abu Hanifa does not allow selling a free person's property for this purpose but will imprison him until he sells himself. The commentator notes the author used 'command to sell' (أمر ببيعه) to avoid stating the qadi's sale directly, aligning with Abu Hanifa's view. Then the discussion turns to animals: generally, a master is not forced to spend on animals because they are not 'deserving' (أهل الاستحقاق); but a fatwa is given that it is sinful to neglect them. Abu Yusuf holds that the qadi can force spending, but the more correct view is that he cannot, though it is recommended. The majority (three imams) agree with Abu Yusuf. For non-animal property like houses, forced spending is not ordered unless there is wasting of wealth, which is makruh. For shared animals, a partner can force the other to either sell or share expenses. Finally, the passage addresses a bequest: if a palm tree and its fruit are given to different people, the fruit owner bears maintenance. For grain crops, maintenance comes from the crop itself; if insufficient, both beneficiaries share proportionally.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose freedom is promised after the master's death
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who bears a child to her master
حسبة (hisbah): A plea based on public interest or religious duty

Rulings on Mudabbar: Debts, Crime, and Offspring
Rulings on debts, crime, and offspring of a mudabbar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings concerning a mudabbar's debts, criminal liability, and the status of his offspring, and apply them to scenarios.

Core Idea
A mudabbar cannot be sold for debts; his criminal liability is limited; his offspring's status mirrors his mother's tadbir.

Tadbir grants the slave a protected status: his person is not subject to sale, even for debts. This lesson unpacks three key consequences of tadbir: how debts are handled, what happens if the mudabbar commits a crime against his master, and whether his children inherit his status. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi approach to balancing the master's rights with the mudabbar's protected autonomy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mudabbar's Protected Status
Note that the mudabbar's person is immune from sale. This is the basis for the first ruling: debts cannot attach to his person.
Compare to a regular slave who can be sold to pay debts.
2
Analyze Debt Attachment
Read the rule: debts attach only to the mudabbar's earnings. He must work to pay them off, but he cannot be sold. Understand that 'attaches to earnings' means the master cannot be forced to pay from his own assets, and the mudabbar's person remains free from sale.
The phrase 'بالغة ما بلغت' indicates no cap on the debt amount.
3
Examine Criminal Compensation
If the mudabbar commits a crime (jinayah) against his master, the liability is the lesser of the slave's market value and the arsh (blood money) for the injury. Multiple crimes do not expose the master to more than one full value.
This limits the master's financial risk.
4
Determine Offspring Status
A child born to a mudabbara (female mudabbar) after the tadbir is mudabbar only if the tadbir was absolute (unconditional on death). If the tadbir was conditional (e.g., tied to an event), the child is not mudabbar.
The child is freed upon the master's death together with the mother.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement listing additional legal consequences of tadbir. First, if the mudabbar has debts, these debts are not attached to his person (i.e., he cannot be sold to pay them) because the person of a mudabbar is not subject to sale. Instead, the debts are to be paid from his earnings, and he must work to pay them off. Second, if the mudabbar commits a crime against his master, the liability is the lesser of the slave's value and the blood money (arsh) due for the injury. If he commits multiple crimes, the master is not liable beyond one full value. Third, a child born to a mudabbara shares her status: if her tadbir was absolute (unconditional upon death), the child is also mudabbar and will be freed when the master dies. If the tadbir was conditional (e.g., upon a specific event), the child does not become mudabbar. These rulings are part of the Hanafi doctrine on tadbir.

Key Terms
جناية (jinayah): Criminal act by the mudabbar against his master.
تدبير مطلق (tadbir mutlaq): An unconditional declaration that the slave will be free at the master's death.
أرش (arsh): Compensation (blood money) for a specific injury, not full diyah.
