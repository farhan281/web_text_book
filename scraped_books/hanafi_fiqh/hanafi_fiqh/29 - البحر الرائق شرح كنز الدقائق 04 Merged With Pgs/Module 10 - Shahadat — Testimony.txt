Testimony on Divorce and Death: Immediate Action vs Judicial Oversight
Rulings on accepting testimony about divorce and death for a woman to remarry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting testimony for a woman to remarry and apply the distinction between divorce and death cases using istihsan.

Core Idea
Two just witnesses on divorce make immediate abstinence obligatory; a single witness on death suffices.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a critical question: when can a woman act upon news of her husband's divorce or death? The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between the two, and even more so between a woman's private right and a son's public right to qisas. Understanding these distinctions reveals the role of istihsan in shaping evidentiary rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
Note the two situations: testimony of divorce (two witnesses) and testimony of death (one witness). Also the qisas comparison with the son.
The passage contrasts the woman's case with the son's.
2
Extract the ruling for divorce
If two just men inform the woman of divorce (or testify before her), she must immediately abstain from marital relations. No court judgment is needed for her to act.
The ruling is based on istihsan, not strict qiyas.
3
Extract the ruling for death
Even one just witness suffices for the woman to consider herself a widow and remarry.
The difference in number is due to the nature of the information and public awareness.
4
Compare with the qisas case
A son informed by two witnesses that someone killed his father cannot kill the killer until a judge rules. The woman's right is personal, while qisas is a public legal matter.
The distinction is based on the type of right: private vs public.
5
Understand the istihsan basis
Strict qiyas would require a court judgment for the woman too, but istihsan allows immediate action due to necessity and the private nature of marriage.
Istihsan overrides qiyas to prevent hardship.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the evidentiary requirements for a woman to consider herself free to remarry after divorce or death. The ruling distinguishes between divorce and death: for death, even one just witness is sufficient for her to act; for divorce, two witnesses are required, and even then, if the husband is present and denies, a court judgment is needed. Furthermore, if two witnesses testify to a divorce, the woman must refrain from marital relations with her husband immediately, without needing a court order. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference). The passage also draws an analogy with the case of a son seeking revenge for his father's murder: the son cannot execute the killer based solely on testimony unless the judge has ruled, whereas a woman can act on the testimony of two witnesses regarding divorce. The difference is that divorce affects marriage, which is a private right, while qisas is a public right requiring judicial oversight.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahadah): Testimony or witness evidence in legal matters.
طلاق (talaq): Divorce, dissolution of marriage by husband's pronouncement.
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference where a ruling is made based on equity or public interest, overriding a strict analogy.

Testimony of Two Witnesses on Divorce and Remarriage
Rulings on testimony and remarriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a woman may remarry based on the testimony of two witnesses regarding divorce, and distinguish the ruling when the husband is present vs absent.

Core Idea
Testimony of two witnesses allows remarriage only if husband is absent; otherwise court judgment is needed.

This passage from al-Bazzaziyya addresses a practical scenario: when two witnesses testify that a husband divorced his wife three times. The ruling depends on whether the husband is present or absent. It also compares with the law of qisas using istihsan, highlighting the unique treatment of marital status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two witnesses testify that the husband divorced his wife three times (triple divorce). Consider whether the husband is present or absent.
2
Apply the ruling based on husband's presence
If the husband is absent, the woman may remarry. If present, and he denies, a court judgment of separation is required, which cannot be issued without the husband's presence.
Presence vs absence is decisive.
3
Consider testimony before the woman herself
If two witnesses inform the woman directly (not in court), she cannot remarry. If only one informant tells her, she does not have the right to refuse the husband (i.e., must continue marital relations).
Testimony outside court has limited effect.
4
Understand the distinction from death testimony
A single reliable witness is accepted for declaring a husband dead (allowing remarriage), but not for divorce. This shows divorce requires higher certainty.
Death and divorce are treated differently in evidentiary standards.
5
Compare with qisas via istihsan
In qisas, two witnesses informing a son about his father's killer do not permit him to kill without court judgment. For divorce, two informants make it haram for the woman to live with her husband even without court judgment. The istihsan reasoning weighs the harm of remaining in a forbidden marriage.
Istihsan departs from strict analogy to prevent greater harm.

Ambiguous Testimony on Emancipation: The Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on validity of testimony regarding ambiguous emancipation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ambiguous testimony regarding emancipation and explain why Imam Abu Hanifa distinguishes it from ambiguous divorce.

Core Idea
Uncertainty in testimony invalidates, unless the ambiguity is inherent in the event itself.

In this lesson, we examine a classic Hanafi dispute about testimony concerning an ambiguous emancipation. The core issue: can witnesses testify that someone 'emancipated one of his two slaves' without specifying which? Imam Abu Hanifa rejects such testimony unless the ambiguity arises from a will or an ambiguous divorce. The two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, accept it by analogy. Understanding this ruling sharpens our appreciation of the Hanafi emphasis on certainty in shahada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is testimony that someone emancipated one of two slaves without specifying which. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, this testimony is invalid (lughat).
The ruling is stated at the beginning of the passage.
2
Note the exceptions
The testimony is accepted only if the ambiguity occurs in a will (wasīyya) or ambiguous divorce (talāq mubham).
These exceptions are part of Imam Abu Hanifa's position.
3
Recognize the dispute with the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the testimony is accepted and the master is ordered to emancipate one slave by qiyas.
Their argument is based on analogy to other cases where ambiguous testimony is accepted.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's qiyas
They analogize to cases in inheritance where ambiguous testimony is accepted; the illa (effective cause) is that the act is known to have occurred, even if the subject is unspecified.
The text cuts off but the qiyas is clear from context.
5
Understand the Hanafi response
Imam Abu Hanifa distinguishes because emancipation is a transfer of ownership requiring certainty, unlike divorce where the ambiguity is inherent (a man may divorce one wife without specifying).
This is the likely rationale behind the exception for ambiguous divorce.

Testimony on Freeing One of Two Slaves: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Exceptions
Ruling on testimony for ambiguous emancipation with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and exception for will/divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the validity of testimony for ambiguous emancipation and explain the exception for wills and ambiguous divorce.

Core Idea
Testimony on freeing an unknown slave is invalid without a claim, except in wills and ambiguous divorce where opponents are known.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school handles testimony when a master is said to have freed one of his slaves without specifying which. Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on whether such testimony is accepted, with Imam requiring a valid claim from the slave. An important exception is made for wills and ambiguous divorce, where the testimony is accepted. We will walk through the reasoning and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two Hanafi positions
The passage discusses the validity of two witnesses testifying that a master freed one of his two slaves or umm walads. State Imam's view (invalid unless in will/divorce) and Sahibayn's view (accepted, master ordered to free one).
2
Understand Imam's dalil
Imam argues that testimony on emancipation requires a prior claim (da'wa) from the specific slave. Since the identity is unknown, no valid claim exists, so testimony is invalid. The exception for will (wasiyya) or ambiguous divorce (talaq mubham) is because in those cases a claim is either not needed (divorce) or the opponent (heirs) is known (will).
3
Examine Sahibayn's analogy and Imam's response
Sahibayn analogize to other cases where claim is not required. Imam distinguishes: for divorce, claim is not needed because it involves prohibition of sexual relations; for umm walad, claim is omitted for similar reason, but ambiguous emancipation does not cause such prohibition, so the analogy fails.
4
Apply the exception for wills
When the testimony is about an emancipation in the master's fatal illness (a will), istihsan allows acceptance because the opponent (heirs) is known, so a claim can be made. This also applies to tadbir (will after death).
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam, the testimony is ignored; under Sahibayn, the master is forced to free one slave. The mufta bihi is not explicitly given, but Imam's view is default.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the validity of testimony that a master freed one of his two slaves or umm walads (female slaves who bore his child). Imam Abu Hanifa holds that such testimony is invalid (laghat) because a claim (da'wa) from the specific slave is a condition for accepting testimony on emancipation, and here the identity of the freed slave is unknown, so no valid claim can be made. The exception is when the testimony is about a will (wasiyya) or an ambiguous divorce (talaq mubham), where the claim condition is not required or is fulfilled by heirs. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that the testimony is accepted, and the master is compelled to free one of them, by analogy to other cases where claim is not a condition. The text then elaborates Imam's reasoning for the distinction between male and female slaves and divorces. It also discusses the exception for wills, where istihsan allows testimony because the opponent (heirs) is known. The passage further critiques the view that the exception is disconnected (munqati'), arguing that it is rather connected (muttasil). Finally, it addresses a subtle point about testimony after the master's death, concluding that acceptance is correct due to istihsan. The later part of the passage includes numerous hypothetical scenarios of conditional emancipation based on the order of a child's birth, detailing the shares of freedom for each party. These are applications of the principle that ambiguity in the condition leads to proportional freedom. The commentator from al-Bada'i' provides the rulings without detailed reasoning, and the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq defends the soundness of the hanafi position.

Key Terms
عتق (itq): Emancipation of a slave, making the slave free.
شهادة (shahada): Testimony given by witnesses in legal matters.
مبهم (mubham): Ambiguous, not specifying which person or action.
وصية (wasiyya): A will regarding disposition of property or emancipation after death.
طلاق مبهم (talaq mubham): An ambiguous divorce where the husband says 'I divorce one of you' without specifying.

Testimony on Ambiguous Manumission and Divorce: Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on testimony for ambiguous acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the requirement of a claim for testimony on ambiguous manumission, and state the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Testimony on an ambiguous manumission requires a claim according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn accept it without a claim.

In this lesson, we examine a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of testimony when witnesses testify that a master has freed one of his two slaves or divorced one of his two wives, without specifying which. The disagreement centers on whether a prior claim from the beneficiary is necessary. We will explore the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the preferred view.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the legal problem: witnesses testify to an ambiguous act (freeing one of two slaves or divorcing one of two wives) without designating the specific individual.
2
Present Abu Hanifa's position
Recognize that Abu Hanifa requires a prior claim from the slave for testimony on manumission; without it, the testimony is not accepted because the claimant is unknown.
For divorce, Abu Hanifa agrees with the consensus that testimony is accepted without a claim.
3
Present the Sahibayn's position
Understand that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad accept the testimony even without a claim, as they do not consider a claim a condition for testimony in this context.
4
Analyze the distinction between manumission and divorce
Examine why divorce is treated differently: the absence of a claim does not impair testimony because the validity of divorce does not depend on a claim, unlike manumission in Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Determine that the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn, acceptance of testimony, as it facilitates the freeing of slaves and is supported by istihsan.
Later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani prefer this view.

The teacher should note that the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is generally the view of the Sahibayn for acceptance of testimony in these ambiguous cases, though the text presents both sides.

Testimony on an Implied Negative: Sacrifice in Kufa and Non-Performance of Hajj
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on accepting testimony of inferred negative fact

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on testimony that implies a negative fact and evaluate Muhammad's opposing view using the principle that testimony on pure negation is not accepted.

Core Idea
Testimony on a negative fact is not accepted, even if implied, unless the positive fact necessarily negates it.

This lesson explores a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of testimony that implies a negative fact. The case involves a man who swears his slave is free if he does not perform Hajj, and witnesses testify that they saw him sacrificing in Kufa. Does this testimony prove he did not perform Hajj? The answer hinges on a key principle: testimony on pure negation is not accepted unless the negative is necessarily entailed by a directly observed positive fact. We will examine the reasoning of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani to understand how this principle is applied.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the slave is not freed because the testimony is on a negative fact (not performing Hajj) and such testimony is not accepted.
This is the majority and mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the slave is freed because the testimony is on a known positive act (sacrifice) which normally implies the negation of Hajj.
Muhammad views the testimony as inherently positive, not negative.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Muhammad argues that the sacrifice is a positive, observable act, and from its occurrence, the non-performance of Hajj necessarily follows, fulfilling the condition.
He analogizes to cases where testimony on a positive act that implies a negative is accepted, such as being outside the house implying not entering.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that the purpose of the testimony is to prove the negative (no Hajj), not the sacrifice. Since there is no claim regarding the sacrifice, it is like pure negation. Moreover, the sacrifice does not necessarily negate Hajj because one could have performed Hajj and then sacrificed later by a miracle.
The principle is that testimony on negation is not accepted, and the positive fact must be such that it admits no alternative explanation.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if Muhammad's view is accepted, the slave is freed; if Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's view is accepted, the slave remains enslaved. The mufta bihi is the latter.
This difference underscores the importance of certainty in legal testimony.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of testimony that implies a negative fact. The case: a man swears that his slave is free if he does not perform Hajj this year. Two witnesses testify that they saw the man sacrificing an animal in Kufa during the Hajj season. They did not directly say he did not perform Hajj, but since sacrifice is usually done on Eid al-Adha, their testimony implies he was not in Mecca for Hajj. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the slave is not freed because the testimony is on a negative fact (not performing Hajj) and testimony on pure negation is not accepted in Hanafi law, even if the witness has direct knowledge. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the slave is freed because the testimony is on a positive fact (the act of sacrificing) which necessarily implies the negation of Hajj. The commentary in al-Hidayah and Fath al-Qadir elaborates: the rule is that testimony on negation is not accepted unless the negation is implicit in a positive fact that is directly observable. Examples: if someone says 'I heard him say the Messiah is the son of God', that is testimony on a positive utterance. For inheritance, witnesses say 'we know of no other heir' is testimony on the positive fact of the claimed heir, with negation implicit. For oaths conditional on not entering a house, witnesses can testify that the person was outside the house all day (a positive observation) which implies not entering. But in the sacrifice case, the sacrifice does not necessarily negate Hajj because one could still have performed Hajj and then sacrificed in Kufa later (a miracle or by going quickly). Therefore, the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is relied upon: the slave is not freed. The passage then concludes with a general rule: testimony on a negative fact, whether direct or implied, is not accepted. This is the mufta bihi position.
