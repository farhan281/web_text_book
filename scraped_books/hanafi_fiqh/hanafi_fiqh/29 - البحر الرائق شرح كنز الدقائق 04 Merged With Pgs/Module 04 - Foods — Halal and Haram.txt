Original vs. Accidental Descriptions in Claims of Exception
Legal maxim on original vs. accidental descriptions in claims of exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between original and accidental descriptions and apply the Hanafi rule that a claim based on an accidental description is not accepted in cases of general emancipation.

Core Idea
An accidental description cannot negate a general emancipation; only an original description can.

This passage from the end of Kitab al-Ayman establishes a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: when someone makes a general declaration of emancipation but excludes a specific category, the validity of the claim of exception depends on the nature of the description. If the description is accidental (like being an umm walad), the master's claim is not believed because it would undermine the originally established freedom. This lesson examines the rationale and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and general rule
The master says 'Every female slave I own is free except the mothers of my children.' The general rule: the claim of exception is treated based on the description type.
2
Distinguish original vs. accidental descriptions
Original descriptions are intrinsic (e.g., being a virgin). Accidental descriptions are acquired (e.g., being a cook, being an umm walad). The Hanafi principle: the master's claim is accepted for original, not for accidental.
Being an umm walad is accidental because it arises from childbearing.
3
Apply to the case of ummahat al-awlād
Since being an umm walad is accidental, the master's claim is not accepted. The general emancipation prevails, so the slave remains free.
The presence of a child does not change this ruling.
4
Consider the child's presence
The child could be from another man, so it does not prove the master's paternity. Therefore, the child's existence does not support the claim of ummiyyat al-walad.
The child is free, but the mother is not deemed an umm walad.
5
Examine the sub-case on timing of claim
If master and slave differ on whether the claim of lineage was made before or after an oath, the master's statement is believed because present ummiyyat indicates prior claim.
This is a practical nuance to resolve evidentiary conflicts.

And from the end of the Book of Oaths: If he said, 'Every female slave I own is free except the mothers of my children,' then he claims ummiyyat al-walad for them or some of them, he is not believed, whether there is a child with them or not. The principle is that when a master obligates emancipation with a general phrase and makes an exception with a specific description, then claims the existence of that description, if the description is accidental, his statement is not accepted; if it is original, his statement is accepted because the statement is that of one who clings to the original. And if he obligated emancipation with a specific phrase and then denied the existence of that description, his statement is accepted because he denies the emancipation entirely. Here, he obligated emancipation with a general phrase and made an exception with an accidental specific description, so he is claiming to negate the emancipation that is originally established, so he is not believed. The presence of a child does not indicate the truth of his claim because it is possible it is from someone else, but the lineage of the child is established from him based on the claim occurring in his ownership, and the child is free. The female slave does not become umm walad because she was freed by the general obligation. And if the claim of lineage from the master was known before the litigation and they differed—the master saying, 'I claimed before the oath and the slave was not freed,' and the slave saying, 'You claimed after the oath and I have been freed'—then the statement is the master's because ummiyyat al-walad is established in the present, and the present indicates what came before, as is known.

Commentary
This passage discusses a principle in oaths and emancipation from the end of Kitab al-Ayman. The passage type is a ruling with a legal maxim (talil). The principle: When someone makes a general declaration of emancipation (e.g., 'every slave is free') but excludes a specific category, the claim of that exception is treated differently based on whether the description is original (asli) or accidental (aridi). Original descriptions are intrinsic (like being a virgin), while accidental are acquired (like being a cook). If the description is accidental, the master's claim that it exists is not believed because he is trying to reverse the established general freedom. If original, his claim is believed because the default is the original state. The text applies this to 'ummahat al-awladi' (mothers of children) — being a mother is an accidental description (it arises after birth). So the master's claim is not accepted. Even if a child is present, it does not prove the claim because the child could be from someone else. However, lineage is established for the child, and the child becomes free, but the mother does not become umm walad because she is already freed by the general declaration. There is also a sub-case about when the claim of lineage (from the master) was made before or after an oath; the master's statement is believed because the present status (being umm walad) indicates a prior claim before the oath.

Key Terms
أمية الولد (ummiyyat al-walad): The status of a female slave who has borne a child to her master.
وصف عارض (wasf 'arid): An accidental, acquired description (not intrinsic to the person).

When Does 'I Am Free From' Constitute an Oath?
Ruling statements on bara'ah oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which statements of bara'ah constitute a binding yamin and apply the condition of intent to determine the ruling.

Core Idea
Not every 'I am free from' statement is an oath; it is an oath only when it implies denial of foundational Islamic truths or sacred matters.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings on when an expression of disavowal (al-bara'a) becomes a legally binding oath (yamin). The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, drawing from earlier Hanafi authorities, provides numerous examples and clarifies the role of intention. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correct fatwa and proper application of kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of the disavowed item
Determine whether the disavowed item is a sacred matter (Islam, Quran, qibla, Ramadan obligation) or a personal action (hajj, prayer).
Sacred matters include anything that implies disbelief when disavowed.
2
Apply the rule for sacred matters
If the disavowal targets a sacred matter, it is an oath; if it targets a personal action, it is not an oath.
Example: 'I am free from the Quran I learned' is an oath because the Quran is sacred.
3
Consider the speaker's intent
For statements like 'free from Ramadan', if the speaker intends the obligation, it is an oath; if they intend the reward, it is not; if no intention, it is not an oath in the legal ruling.
Intent is crucial because it determines the meaning of the statement.
4
Recognize explicit disavowals
Explicit disavowal of Allah, His Messenger, or worshipping other than Allah is always an oath.
These are considered clear expressions of disbelief.

Commentary
This passage from 'al-Bahr al-Ra’iq' presents multiple rulings on when expressions of disavowal (al-bara’a) constitute valid oaths. The author, Ibn Nujaym, draws from earlier Hanafi works: al-Mujtaba, al-Muhit, al-Zahiriyya, al-Walwalijiyya. The key principle: a statement is an oath if it implies denial of fundamental Islamic beliefs or sacred matters. The number of expiations depends on the number of distinct oath phrases: one phrase = one kaffarah, multiple independent phrases = multiple. This lesson collects concrete examples to clarify the doctrine.

Key Terms
يمين (yamin): A legally binding oath that incurs expiation (kaffarah) upon being broken.
براءة (bara'ah): A declaration of disavowal or freedom from someone or something, often used in oath formulations.
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation required for breaking a valid oath; typically feeding or clothing ten poor persons, or fasting three days.

Counting Multiple Oaths and Kaffarahs in Bara'ah Statements
Ruling statements on counting oaths and kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether multiple bara'ah statements constitute a single or multiple oaths, and calculate the required expiations.

Core Idea
The number of kaffarahs equals the number of distinct oath phrases, not the number of items disavowed if combined in one phrase.

This lesson focuses on how to count expiations when a person utters multiple bara'ah statements. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq provides key examples: 'I am free from the four books' is one oath, but listing each book separately with 'I am free from' constitutes four oaths. Similarly, 'I am free from Allah and His Messenger' is one oath, but repeating 'from' separately makes two. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly applying kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrasing
Look at the wording of the bara'ah statement. Determine if the items are connected by a single phrase (e.g., 'from A and B') or are separate independent clauses (e.g., 'from A, from B').
Independent clauses are separated by wa (and) but if the wa connects two nouns within one phrase, it is one.
2
Apply the rule for single vs multiple
If the disavowals are in a single phrase (e.g., 'I am free from A and B'), it is one oath. If they are separate phrases (e.g., 'I am free from A, and I am free from B'), it is multiple oaths.
Example from passage: 'from Allah and His Messenger' is one; 'from Allah, and from His Messenger' is two.
3
Count the phrases
Count the number of distinct oath phrases used. Each independent bara'ah statement (with its own 'I am free from') counts as one.
Even if the items are many, if they are wrapped in one phrase, it's one.
4
Determine the number of kaffarahs
If the oath is broken, the speaker must pay one kaffarah for each oath phrase. So if there are four phrases, four kaffarahs.
Kaffarah can be sequential; if multiple, each must be performed.

Dispute on Number of Kaffarat for a Complex Oath
Intra-Hanafi dispute on counting expiations in a composite oath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the number of expiations depends on the multiplicity of the disavowal formula, and evaluate the different views within the school for a given oath wording.

Core Idea
The number of expiations equals the number of distinct disavowal phrases; a single phrase repeated counts as one.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā’iq explores an intra-Hanafi dispute about how many expiations are owed for a complex oath wording: 'I am free from Allah and His Messenger, and Allah and His Messenger are free from me.' The analysis turns on whether the formula of disavowal (ṣīghat al-barā’ah) is repeated or not. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze oath wordings in practice and appreciate the role of textual transmission in ikhtilāf.

Guided Path
1
Parse the oath wording
Identify the Arabic phrase and break it into its constituent parts. Note each occurrence of the disavowal formula 'bari'' (or 'bari'an').
The original wording: 'إِنْ فَعَلْتُ كَذَا فَأَنَا بَرِيءٌ مِنَ اللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ وَاللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُ بَرِيئَانِ مِنْهُ'.
2
Count the disavowal formulas
Count each distinct 'bari'' phrase. The first part has one formula: 'ana bari' min Allah wa rasulihi'. The second part has one formula: 'Allah wa rasuluhu bari'an minhu' (dual verb but one disavowal statement).
The dual in the second part might be interpreted as two formulas by some scholars.
3
Apply the principle: multiplicity of formula = multiplicity of expiation
According to al-Ẓāhiriyya, when the formula of disavowal is multiplied, the expiation is multiplied; when unified, it is unified.
This principle is the cornerstone of the analysis.
4
Consider the counterarguments and textual variants
Some sources (Fatawa Samarqand, al-Walwalijiyya) rule four expiations, but the author finds that their wording includes repetition (e.g., 'bari' min Allah wa bari' min rasulihi'). The dual in the second part could also be read as two formulas, giving three.
The dispute hinges on the exact wording transmitted; the author argues the default is two unless repetition is explicit.
5
Determine the muftā bihi
The author relies on al-Mujtabā and al-Dhakhīra, which affirm two oaths. Thus the operative position for fatwa is two expiations for the standard wording.
If the wording is explicitly repeated, four expiations become applicable.

Rulings on Oath Expressions: The Role of Implied Disbelief
Rulings on various oath expressions and their validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether a given oath expression is valid by identifying its implied theological weight and applying the Hanafi criterion that only expressions implying disbelief constitute valid oaths.

Core Idea
An oath is valid only if its implied meaning entails disbelief; statements implying mere innovation or correct belief do not form a valid oath.

The Hanafi school judges oath expressions not by their literal wording alone, but by the implied consequence. An expression that implies disbelief (kufr) in a fundamental tenet is a valid oath; one that implies only innovation (bid'ah) or affirms correct belief (tanzih) is not. This lesson examines four distinct cases from al-Zahiriyya and al-Dhakhira that illustrate this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression and its literal meaning
Note the exact wording of the conditional phrase for each case: 'no god in heaven', 'free from believers', 'free from intercession', 'partner in expiation regarding unbefitting statements about Allah'.
The literal wording may seem similar, but the implied theological consequence differs.
2
Determine the implied theological consequence
For each expression, assess what denial or affirmation is implied. 'No god in heaven' implies denial of Allah's existence (kufr). 'Free from believers' implies denial of faith (kufr). 'Free from intercession' implies denial of a true doctrine but only to the level of bid'ah (innovation). 'Partner in expiation regarding unbefitting statements' implies attributing something unbefitting to Allah, which is kufr.
The speaker's intent matters: if one intends to negate space from Allah (tanzih), it is not kufr.
3
Apply the criterion: is the implication kufr or bid'ah?
If the expression implies kufr, it is a valid oath. If it implies only bid'ah or correct belief, it is not a valid oath. 'No god in heaven' and 'free from believers' imply kufr → valid oaths. 'Free from intercession' implies bid'ah → not an oath. Negating space from Allah is correct belief → not an oath. 'Partner in expiation' implies kufr → valid oath.
Denial of shafa'ah is bid'ah, not kufr, so the expression is not an oath.

Future vs Past Oaths and the Issue of Kufr
Commentary distinguishing future and past oaths and the kufr dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between oaths on future actions and false oaths on past events (yamin al-ghamus), and explain the Hanafi positions on when such oaths entail disbelief.

Core Idea
Yamin al-ghamus is a major sin requiring repentance; kufr depends on the swearer's belief about the consequences.

This passage from Ibn Nujaym's 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq' clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi oath jurisprudence. Future oaths (breaking the condition) are simply broken oaths requiring expiation (kaffarah). Past false oaths (yamin al-ghamus) are major sins with no kaffarah, but the question of kufr is disputed. The relied-upon position ties the ruling of kufr to the person's belief about whether the oath causes disbelief, applying equally to future and past oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of oaths
Distinguish between oaths conditioned on future actions (e.g., 'If I do X, I am a disbeliever') and oaths about past events (e.g., 'By Allah, I did not do X' while knowing it is false). Note that for future oaths, fulfilling the condition (doing the act) does not make one a kafir; it is simply a broken oath. For past oaths, false swearing is yamin al-ghamus.
The author uses 'qayyada' to indicate the restriction to future actions.
2
Understand the ruling of yamin al-ghamus
Yamin al-ghamus is a deliberate false oath about a past event. It is a major sin (kabirah) with no expiation (kaffarah) except sincere repentance (tawbah) and seeking forgiveness (istighfar).
This ruling is distinct from broken future oaths which require kaffarah.
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute on kufr
The passage presents multiple positions: (1) Some say it always causes kufr because it is like declaring disbelief now. (2) The correct view: if the swearer knows it is a false oath and does not believe it causes disbelief, he does not become a kafir. (3) If he is ignorant and believes it causes disbelief, then he becomes a kafir because he consented to kufr. (4) The mufta bihi (from al-Mujtaba and al-Dhakhira): it depends on his belief – if he believes the oath entails kufr, he becomes a kafir; otherwise not.
The dispute is internal to the Hanafi school; the later authorities settled on the belief-based criterion.
4
Apply to the special case of 'Allah knows'
The passage mentions a specific scenario: someone says 'Allah knows that I did such-and-such' while knowing it is false. The majority of mashayikh rule that this constitutes kufr because it involves using Allah's knowledge to support a lie, implying disrespect to divine omniscience.
This case has its own ikhtilaf, but the general rule of belief still applies.
5
Synthesize the practical ruling
When issuing a fatwa on a false oath and possible kufr, the mufti must ascertain the swearer's belief about the consequences. If the person believed the false oath would make him leave Islam, he must renew his faith (tajdid al-islam) and repent. Otherwise, he only repents from the sin.
This is the mufta bihi position as cited by Ibn Nujaym.

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, 'entering upon' depends on both physical presence and intention to visit that person, guided by custom.

The Hanafi school, relying on custom and the rulings of Imam Muhammad, provides a nuanced answer that balances literal meaning with practical reality. This lesson unpacks these rulings from al-Badā'i'.

Description in Oaths: Condition vs Definition
Ruling on description as condition vs definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a description used as a condition (sharṭ) and as a mere definition (taʿrīf) in an oath, and state the ruling when the condition is unmet.

Core Idea
A description in an oath only matters if it is intended as a condition.

When someone includes a description in an oath, the key is whether the description is the reason for the oath or just an identifier. The Hanafi school carefully analyzes the wording and intent. The passage from al-Dhakhīrah gives a clear example: a husband says to his wife, 'If you enter this house riding, you are divorced.' She enters walking. Is she divorced? The answer depends on whether 'riding' was a condition or a descriptive identifier.

Guided Path
1
Identify the description in the oath
Find the descriptive word or phrase that qualifies the action or person. In the example, 'riding' describes the manner of entry.
2
Determine its role: condition or definition
Assess whether the description is mentioned as a condition (sharṭ) that must be fulfilled for the oath to take effect, or as a definition (taʿrīf) that simply identifies.
The Hanafi rule: if the description is the motive (dāʿiyah) for the oath, it is a condition; otherwise it is a definition.
3
Apply the ruling
If it is a condition, the oath is not broken if the description is absent. If it is a definition, the oath is broken regardless of the description.
In the example, 'riding' is mentioned as a condition, so walking does not trigger divorce.

Commentary
This dense passage covers multiple rulings on conditional oaths and related issues. First, from al-Dhakhīrah: A description (ṣifah) in an oath is only considered if it is expressed as a condition (sharṭ), not just as a descriptive identifier. Example: 'If you enter this house riding, you are divorced.' Riding is a condition; if she enters walking, the divorce does not occur. Second, from al-Wāqi'āt: If two people each swear not to enter upon the other, and they both enter the house simultaneously, neither breaks his oath because neither is entering upon the other—they are entering together. Also, an oath about 'entering as usual' may be immediate or perpetual depending on context; if unrestricted, it applies perpetually to the customary entry. Third, from al-Muḥīṭ and others: The phrasing of the oath matters. 'I bring in' implies command; 'I allow' implies knowledge and failure to prevent; 'he enters' is unconditional. Fourth, from al-Muḥīṭ: The distinction between definite (ma'rifah) and indefinite (nakirah) in oaths: 'If any definite person enters my house' does not include oneself because 'any definite person' (as 'one' definite) does not cover the speaker; but 'if anyone enters' includes oneself. Also, 'your house' excludes the owner because 'your' makes it definite. Fifth, from al-Khāniyyah: A vow to prevent someone is fulfilled by preventing once; subsequent failure to prevent does not break the oath. Also, contradictory oaths: if someone first swears he did not enter, then says he was mistaken and swears he did enter, the first divorce is effective (because the first oath was false) and the second is not (because it is true and no divorce). On emancipation: making contradictory statements with successive oaths can free multiple slaves due to the retraction. This passage illustrates the complexity of oath jurisprudence and the reliance on linguistic precision and intention.

Definite vs Indefinite in Oaths and Contradictory Oaths
Rulings on definite/indefinite nouns and contradictory oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether an oath includes the speaker based on definite/indefinite wording, and apply the ruling on contradictory oaths.

Core Idea
A definite noun in an oath excludes the speaker; an indefinite noun includes everyone.

The wording of an oath determines who is included. If someone says, 'If any definite person enters my house, my slave is free,' and the house is shared, the speaker himself can enter without breaking the oath because 'one' is definite. But if he says 'anyone' (indefinite), he himself is included. Similarly, contradictory oaths have specific consequences based on truth and falsehood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the noun form in the oath
Is the noun definite (maʿrifah like 'one' or 'someone' known) or indefinite (nakirah like 'anyone' without specification)?
2
Apply the inclusion rule
If definite, the speaker is excluded; if indefinite, the speaker is included unless the house is attributed to another (e.g., 'your house' excludes the owner).
From al-Muḥīṭ: 'If any definite person enters my house' does not include the speaker.
3
Handle contradictory oaths
If someone swears something false then swears the truth, the first oath is broken; the second is not. If he makes contradictory statements with retraction, multiple slaves may be freed due to the retraction.
From al-Khāniyyah: first slave freed by second statement.

Default Scope of a Riding Oath
Ruling on default scope of riding oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which animals are covered by a generic oath not to ride and explain the application of istihsan in excluding camels and elephants.

Core Idea
A generic oath not to ride covers only animals commonly ridden in that society.

When someone swears not to ride, what animals does that include? The Hanafi jurists, relying on custom (urf), restrict the oath to animals conventionally used for riding—horses, mules, donkeys. Camels and elephants, though capable of being ridden, are excluded by istihsan (juristic preference) because people do not ordinarily think of them when saying 'ride'. This lesson unpacks that default and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default scope
From al-Waqi'at: the oath applies to what people ride, such as horses, mules, and the like. That means the determining factor is common custom (urf).
Custom is a recognized source of law in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Apply istihsan to exceptional animals
From al-Zahiriyya: riding a camel or elephant does not break the oath by istihsan unless the swearer intended those animals. Istihsan overrides qiyas, which would otherwise include them as riding animals.
Istihsan is a departure from strict analogy for a stronger reason.
3
Consider the role of intention
If the swearer intended (nawā) a specific animal, that intention is followed. The default only governs when there is no specific intention.
Intention is key in oaths; it can broaden or narrow the scope.

Conditional Charity upon Riding: Repetition of Obligation
Ruling on repetition of conditional charity vs. talaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the repetition of a conditional charity obligation upon each occurrence and the extinguishing of a conditional divorce upon the first occurrence.

Core Idea
Charity obligations repeated with each ride; talaq obligations do not repeat because marriage ends.

The passage concludes with a distinction between two types of conditional oaths. If someone says, 'Whenever I ride an animal, I must give it in charity,' each ride triggers a new charity obligation, even if he buys it back. In contrast, if a man says to a foreign woman, 'Whenever I marry you, you are divorced,' the first marriage triggers divorce, and subsequent remarriages do not trigger it again. This difference is due to the nature of the conditions: charity obligations can recur, while the condition of divorce affects a status that cannot be infinitely repeated.

2
Compare with conditional divorce
If a man says to a foreign woman, 'whenever I marry you, you are thrice divorced,' the first marriage triggers divorce. After she marries another and returns, a second marriage does not trigger divorce again. The condition is spent after the first occurrence.
Divorce changes the marital status, which cannot be repeated indefinitely.

The difference: in charity, the subject (the animal) can be re-acquired, so the condition can recur. In divorce, the condition is tied to a specific marriage, and once divorce occurs, the marriage ends; subsequent marriages are new contracts, but the condition is not triggered again because the talaq conditional on marriage is considered to extinguish with the first occurrence.
This is based on the concept of ta'liq (suspension) and its limits.

Definition of Fat (Shahm) in Oaths about Eating Meat
Explaining what counts as fat for oath violation

Core Idea
Not all fat is shahm: bone marrow is not fat, and back fat is actually fatty meat.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies what counts as fat (shahm) when a person swears an oath about eating fat or meat. Since oaths are interpreted according to custom and legal definitions, the Hanafi school precisely distinguishes between types of fat. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining when an oath is broken.

Identify the types of fat mentioned
The passage lists shahm al-batn (fat of the belly), the apparent fat on intestines, and back fat. Bone marrow is explicitly excluded from the definition of shahm.

There is consensus that eating shahm al-batn breaks an oath. The three main Hanafi jurists (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) differ on other details, but all agree on this point.
The phrase 'والثلاثة على الخلاف' is ambiguous but likely indicates disagreement among them on other matters.
3

Bone marrow does not cause hanth (breaking an oath) because it is not called shahm. The apparent fat on intestines unequivocally does cause hanth.

Interpreting 'House' in Oaths: Customary Residence vs. Ownership
Ruling on oaths about entering a particular person's house when the house is owned or rented by that person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for interpreting 'dar' in oaths and apply it to scenarios involving ownership and residence.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'house' means the person's customary residence, not merely their property.

When someone swears not to enter a particular person's house, what exactly counts as that person's house? Is it any property they own, or only where they actually live? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: custom ('urf) dictates that 'house' means the person's residence, whether owned or rented. This lesson unpacks that rule, including the condition of habitation and the role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main rule
From the passage: if a person swears not to enter so-and-so's house, they break the oath by entering what that person inhabits as a residence (maskin), whether by ownership, lease, or loan.
The key is custom: 'house' in oaths is understood as 'residence'.
2
Recognize the condition of habitation
The passage explicitly conditions that the house be the person's residence. If the person owns the house but does not live there, entering it does not break the oath.
Example from al-Waqi'at: a man swears not to enter Zayd's house, then enters a house Zayd owns but does not inhabit — no breach.
3
Apply to dual-ownership scenarios
If a house is owned by one person but inhabited by another (e.g., rental), entering that house can break oaths regarding both persons if the swearer named both, but intention is considered.
Al-Ramli's example: entering a house owned by Zayd but rented by Amr triggers both conditions if the swearer had no specific intention.
4
Consider the role of intention (niyya)
If the swearer had a specific intention (e.g., only meant the owned house), they are believed in fatwa regarding their own oath, provided they are truthful.
This shows the balance between custom and subjective intent.

Commentary
This passage addresses oaths regarding entering a specific person's 'house' (dar). The ruling is that the word 'dar' is interpreted according to customary usage ('urf): it refers to the person's residence, whether owned or rented. Therefore, if someone swears not to enter Zayd's house, he breaks his oath if he enters any place that Zayd inhabits, whether by ownership, lease, or even a loan (i'ara). The key condition is that the person actually resides there. If Zayd owns a house but does not live in it, entering that house does not break the oath. The commentator al-Ramli brings a useful example from al-Mujtaba: a man says, 'If I enter Zayd's house, my slave is free, and if I enter Amr's house, my wife is divorced.' Then he enters a house owned by Zayd but that is currently in Amr's possession by lease. The ruling is that both the slave is freed and the wife is divorced, because entering the house fulfills both conditions: it is Zayd's house (since he owns it) and it is Amr's house (since he rents it). However, if the man had a specific intention (e.g., he only meant Zayd's owned house where Amr is a tenant), his intention is accepted (in fatwa) but only if he is truthful. This shows the importance of custom and intent in interpreting oaths. The passage also clarifies that if the house is a garden or bathhouse, the ruling may differ; but for a house, the custom of residence governs.

مسكن (maskan): Residence; the place where a person actually lives.
إجارة (ijāra): Lease; renting a property.
