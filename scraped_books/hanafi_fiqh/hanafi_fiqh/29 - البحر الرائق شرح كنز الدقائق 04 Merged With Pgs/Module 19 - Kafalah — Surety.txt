When Purchase of a Slave Counts as Expiation
Explains validity of buying relative for expiation vs buying one vowed to free

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that purchase of a slave counts as expiation only when the purchase itself constitutes emancipation or when intention coincides with the cause of manumission, and distinguish cases where it does not.

Core Idea
Purchase of a relative counts as expiation; purchase of a slave vowed free does not.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school: when does buying a slave fulfill an expiation (kafāra) for an oath? The text clarifies that not every purchase with an intention of expiation works. The key is whether the purchase itself is considered an act of emancipation or merely a condition triggering a prior oath. We will walk through the reasoning and apply it to clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The text contrasts buying a relative (e.g., father) for expiation, which is valid, with buying a slave whom one had previously sworn to free (e.g., 'If I buy you, you are free'), which is not valid. Note the difference in the legal effect of the purchase.
The relative scenario is based on a prophetic ruling that the purchase itself is emancipation.
2
Understand the Prophetic Basis
The Prophet (ṣ) ruled that buying a relative is itself an emancipation, so no separate freeing act is needed. This means the purchase directly causes freedom. The text compares it to saying 'I watered him and quenched him' where the action achieves its effect directly.
This is the core proof (dalīl) for the first scenario.
3
Analyze the Role of Intention and Cause
In the vowed-free scenario, the purchase is only a condition (sharṭ) that triggers a prior oath; the cause of manumission is the oath itself. The intention to expiate must coincide with the cause, not just the condition. Since the purchase is not the cause, the expiation fails.
The text says: 'the condition accompanies the intention with the cause of manumission, which is the oath; as for the purchase, it is its condition.'
4
Apply to Umm Walad
An umm walad (slave mother of her master's child) already has an established right to freedom at the master's death. Therefore, freeing her cannot be attributed to a new oath; the manumission is not caused by the oath. Hence, buying her with expiation intention does not work.
This is contrasted with a non-umm walad slave, where no prior freedom exists, so the purchase can be linked to the oath.
5
Derive the General Principle
The overarching rule: expiation by purchase is valid only when the intention coincides with the direct cause of manumission and the slave is fully owned without any pre-existing claim to freedom. Otherwise, the purchase does not count.
This principle can be extended to analogous cases.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling explaining when purchasing a slave counts as a valid expiation (kafāra) for an oath. For a relative, the purchase itself is considered an emancipation by the Prophet's ruling, so intention to expiate suffices. Interpreting 'A or B and C' in Legal Statements
Ruling on disjunctive expressions in divorce, manumission, and acknowledgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi interpretation of the disjunctive expression 'X or Y and Z' to determine the legal effect in divorce, manumission, and acknowledgment.

Core Idea
In disjunctive expressions, the last item after 'and' is definite; the first two are subject to choice.

When someone says 'This one is divorced or this one and this one,' it seems confusing—who is actually affected? The Hanafi school has a clear rule: the last named (after 'and') is definitely divorced, while the first two are left to the speaker's choice. The same applies to freeing slaves and acknowledging debts. This lesson breaks down the principle and its applications.

Guided Path
1
Parse the expression
Identify the three items: the first two are separated by 'or' (aw), and the third is attached by 'and' (wa). The phrase is: 'This one is divorced OR this one AND this one.'
The third item is not independent; it is conjoined to whichever of the first two is eventually chosen.
2
Apply the principle of 'aw'
In Hanafi law, 'aw' gives the speaker a choice among the items immediately following it. So the speaker can decide which of the first two is the intended subject.
The choice is between the first two only, not the third.
3
Understand the effect of 'and' on the third
The third item is joined by 'and' to the second item. Since the second item is part of the optional pair, the third shares the ruling of whichever one is chosen. If the speaker chooses the first, the third is not affected; if he chooses the second, the third also receives the same ruling.
Thus the third is always affected if the second is chosen, but never affected if the first is chosen.
4
Derive the outcome for divorce/manumission
For divorce: the last wife (the third) is definitely divorced because the second wife is one of the options, and if the speaker chooses the second, the third is also divorced. But since the speaker has a choice, the third is always divorced regardless? Actually the text says 'the last one is divorced' unconditionally. This is because the speaker's choice must be exercised; if he does not choose, the law presumes the last is affected. So the definite outcome is: the third is divorced, and the speaker may choose which of the first two is also divorced (or none, if the choice is not exercised? Wait, the text says 'the last one is divorced, and he has choice regarding the first two'. That implies the last is definitely divorced, and the first two are optional. So if he chooses one of the first two, that one is also divorced; if he chooses none, only the last is divorced.
This is consistent across the passage: the last (third) is always bound, the first two are contingent on the speaker's choice.
5
Apply to acknowledgment of debt
For a debt acknowledgment like 'I owe 1000 dirhams to Zayd or to Amr and Bakr', the last (Bakr, the third?) Actually the phrasing is 'to so-and-so or to so-and-so and so-and-so'. The last named is the one after 'and', so he is due 500 dirhams. The speaker may assign the other 500 dirhams to whichever of the first two he wishes. This is analogous: the third gets a fixed share (half), the first two are subject to choice.
There is a divergent view from al-Mughni that divides half to the first and half to the last two; the author rejects this.

Oaths and Payment: The Effect of Hawalah and Kafalah
Rulings on oaths, hawalah, and kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether an oath not to pay a debt is broken when hawalah or kafalah is performed with or without the debtor's command, and distinguish the ruling for oaths to pay.

Core Idea
An oath not to pay is broken only if payment occurs through the debtor's command, whether directly or via hawalah/kafalah.

When a person swears not to pay a debt, they might think that using a third party—like a guarantor or a transfer of debt—absolves them of the oath. This lesson from al-Muhit clarifies when the oath is broken and when it is not, based on whether the debtor commanded the action. The same principle applies to oaths to pay, but with opposite effect: command fulfills the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the debtor's subsequent action
Read the scenario: the debtor swore not to pay a specific person. Then he either commanded another to become a guarantor and pay, or he arranged a hawalah (transfer of debt). Determine if the debtor gave a command or not.
The command is key: 'bi-amrihi' (by his command) vs 'bi-ghayri amrihi' (without his command).
2
Classify the action as with or without command
If the debtor explicitly instructed someone to pay or to become a guarantor, it is 'with command'. If someone else did it voluntarily or the debtor was unaware, it is 'without command'.
Hawalah and kafalah can be initiated by the debtor or by the creditor/third party.
3
Apply the ruling from al-Muhit for the oath not to pay
If the payment occurred with the debtor's command (even via hawalah/kafalah), the oath is broken. Without command, the oath is not broken. A voluntary payment by a third party also does not break the oath.
The reason: if the debtor commanded, the payment is attributed to him; the guarantor has recourse against the debtor, making it as if the debtor paid.
4
Apply the same principle to oaths to pay
If the debtor swore to pay, commanding another to pay or making a hawalah with his command fulfills the oath. Without command, the oath is not fulfilled.
Symmetry: command triggers the opposite result for oaths to pay vs oaths not to pay.
5
Consider custom in the conditional divorce case
The passage from al-Nawazil shows that custom can affect whether the wife's command to her maid is considered the wife's own action. If it is customary for her to wash herself, her command is not a substitute; if she customarily uses the maid, the command suffices.
This illustrates the role of 'urf (custom) in interpreting the oath's condition.

Oaths Concerning Kafalah: Distinguishing Property and Person Suretyship
Rulings from Muhit on oaths not to become a surety, distinguishing kafalah bi-mal and kafalah bi-nafs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between kafalah bi-mal and kafalah bi-nafs in the context of oaths, and state the ruling when an oath uses the particle ‘anhu’ versus other particles.

Core Idea
An oath not to be a surety 'for' (anhu) someone only covers property suretyship, not personal suretyship.

This passage from al-Muhit refines the rulings on oaths related to suretyship (kafalah). The key distinction is between kafalah 'anhu (suretyship for property) and kafalah bihi (suretyship for the person). The word 'anhu restricts the oath to property suretyship, so being a surety for the person (bihi) does not break the oath. However, different oath formulations (using 'li' or 'an' in a general sense) yield different rulings, and hawalah (debt transfer) can also break the oath under certain conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath formulation
Look at the particle used in the oath: 'anhu' (for him, property), 'bihi' (for his person), 'li' (for so-and-so), or a general 'an'.
The particle determines the scope of the oath.
2
Classify the suretyship action
Determine whether the surety is for property (kafalah bi-mal) or for the person (kafalah bi-nafs).
Use the linguistic cues: 'kafala 'anhu' = property; 'kafala bihi' = person.
3
Apply the linguistic rule
If the oath uses 'anhu', it is restricted to kafalah bi-mal. If the action is kafalah bi-nafs, the oath is not broken. If the oath uses 'li' or a general 'an', both types may break the oath.
The passage explicitly states: 'كفل عنه أي بماله وكفل به أي بنفسه'.
4
Consider hawalah
If the person mentioned in the oath transfers a debt (hawalah) to the oath-taker, the oath is broken only if the transferor actually owed a debt to the creditor. Otherwise, it is not broken.
Because a valid hawalah includes both obligation and guarantee, which is more than suretyship.

He said in al-Muhit: If he swore not to be a surety 'for' (anhu) anything for so-and-so, and he became surety 'for' (bi) his person, he does not break his oath, because he became surety 'for' him (kafala bihi) not 'for' him (anhu), because the word 'anhu' is used only in suretyship for property (kafalah bi-mal), not in suretyship for person (kafalah bi-nafs). It is said 'kafala 'anhu' meaning for his property, and 'kafala bihi' meaning for his person. And if he became surety for his guarantor by his command, he does not break his oath, because he did not become surety for him (the original), but rather for another. If he swore not to be a surety for so-and-so 'of' (li) so-and-so, and he became surety for his person, he breaks his oath. And if he became surety for him for property, he does not break his oath. If he swore not to be a surety for, and then so-and-so made a hawalah on the oath-taker in favour of his creditor, if the creditor had a debt from the transferor, he breaks his oath; otherwise not, because in hawalah there is what is in kafalah and more, because it involves obligation and guarantee. End.

Commentary
This passage from al-Muhit refines the rulings on oaths related to suretyship (kafalah). The key distinction is between kafalah 'anhu (suretyship for property) and kafalah bihi (suretyship for the person). The word 'anhu restricts the oath to property suretyship, so being a surety for the person (bihi) does not break the oath. However, if the oath uses the word 'li' (for) or 'an' in a general sense, further details apply. The passage also discusses hawalah: if the person named in the oath transfers his debt to the oath-taker (so the oath-taker becomes liable), this breaks the oath if the transferor actually owed the debt, because hawalah includes suretyship and more. If the transferor did not owe the debt, the hawalah is invalid and does not break the oath.

Key Terms
الكفالة بالمال (al-kafalah bi-l-mal): Suretyship for a debt or property obligation.
الكفالة بالنفس (al-kafalah bi-l-nafs): Suretyship for the person of the debtor (i.e., guaranteeing his presence).
الحوالة (al-hawalah): Transfer of debt from one person to another with the consent of all parties.

Oaths and Debt Collection: Agent, Volunteer, and Hawalah
Rulings on oaths when debt is received from agent, transferee, or before oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi rulings on oath-breaking when debt is taken through agents, volunteers, or hawalah, and evaluate the analogy to marriage/divorce agency.

Core Idea
An agent's act in debt collection does not break the principal's oath, unlike in marriage and divorce.

This lesson examines nuanced rulings about oaths concerning debt collection. It clarifies when an oath is broken if the debt is taken or paid through an intermediary such as an agent, a volunteer, or a hawalah transferee. Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying oaths correctly in financial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario
Determine who swore and what they swore about: e.g., 'not to take my money from the debtor' or 'to pay a right'.
The oath is about the action of taking or paying, not about the intermediary.
2
Identify who takes/pays the debt
Is the debt taken by the oath-taker, his agent, the debtor's agent, a volunteer, or a hawalah transferee?
The relationship of the actor to the oath-taker is key.
3
Apply the rule
If the oath-taker himself takes from the debtor's agent → oath broken. If a volunteer or his own agent takes → oath not broken. If hawalah after oath → oath broken; if before → not broken.
The rule distinguishes between direct and indirect receipt.
4
Analyze the dispute on agency analogy
Some analogize to marriage/divorce agency where the agent's act breaks the oath. The Hanafi position rejects this analogy for debt collection because the taking is attributed to the agent, not the principal.
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether kaffarah is required.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, quoting from two earlier works: al-'Uyun and al-Quduri. The passage type is a ruling statement with an intra-Hanafi dispute. The text presents nuanced rulings about oaths concerning debt collection through agents or transferees. Key point: if a person swears not to take his money from the debtor directly but takes it from the debtor's agent, he breaks his oath because the agent represents the debtor. However, taking from a volunteer (mutatawwi') who is not an agent does not break the oath. Taking from one's own agent or from a transferee (muhtal 'alayh) also does not break the oath because these are considered different persons. Al-Quduri adds that if the debtor swears to pay, and someone else orders him to pay or arranges a hawalah, he fulfills the oath; but if a volunteer pays on his behalf, he does not. There is a difference of opinion: some analogize the agent's action to the principal's in marriage and divorce, concluding the oath is broken. The Hanafi relied-upon position is that the oath is not broken by the agent's action in debt collection, as stated in al-'Uyun.

Key Terms
متطوع (mutatawwi'): A volunteer who pays the debt without being ordered or appointed by the debtor.
وكيل (wakil): Agent: a person appointed to act on behalf of another in collecting debt.
حوالة (hawalah): Transfer of debt from one person to another.
