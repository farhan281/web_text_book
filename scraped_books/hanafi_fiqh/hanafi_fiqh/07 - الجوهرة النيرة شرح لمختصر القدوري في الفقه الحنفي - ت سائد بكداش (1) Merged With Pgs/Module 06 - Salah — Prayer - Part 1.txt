Qawma (Standing After Ruku) Is Not Fard According to Sahibayn
Ruling on the standing after bowing (qawma) in prayer: is it fard?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi majority (Sahibayn) consider qawma not fard and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Qawma is wajib, not fard: omission does not invalidate prayer but requires sajdah al-sahw.

In the Hanafi school, the brief standing after bowing (qawma) is a subject of internal dispute. While Abu Yusuf and Imam al-Shafi'i consider it fard (invalidating if omitted), the majority of Hanafis—including Muhammad al-Shaybani and the later relied-upon position—hold that it is merely wajib. This distinction has direct practical consequences for the validity of your prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the qawma (standing after ruku). Determine its legal classification according to each camp.
Is it fard, wajib, or sunnah?
2
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
State the view of Abu Yusuf and the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Note that Abu Yusuf himself had two positions here.
The text says 'His saying: This standing is not fard according to the two' meaning Sahibayn.
3
Analyze the qiyas argument
Understand how Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i analogize qawma to jalsah (sitting between prostrations) to argue it is fard.
The illa: both are transitional pauses.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The majority rejects the analogy because qawma lacks the same textual emphasis as jalsah.
The ruling relies on zahir al-riwayah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? If qawma is omitted, does the prayer need to be repeated or just compensated?
The majority: sajdah al-sahw suffices.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the legal status of the brief standing (qawma) after bowing (ruku') in prayer. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the qawma is not a fard (obligatory) pillar; it is wajib or sunnah. However, Abu Yusuf alone considered it fard, analogizing it to the sitting between the two prostrations (jalsah), which is a pillar. Imam al-Shafi'i shares Abu Yusuf's view. The text from 'Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah' also explains the phrase 'sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' as an acceptance of praise. The 'Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj' clarifies that the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is that qawma is not fard, aligning with the two companions. This difference has practical implications: if one intentionally omits the qawma, the prayer is valid according to the majority Hanafi view, but would require repetition according to Abu Yusuf and Shafi'i.

Key Terms
القومة (al-qawma): The brief standing after bowing (ruku') before going into prostration.
الفرض (al-fard): An obligatory act that invalidates the prayer if omitted intentionally or unintentionally.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The most authoritative and widely transmitted narrations within the Hanafi school (the six major works of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani).

The Qawma Dispute: Is Standing After Ruku' Obligatory?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qawma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that qawma is not fard and evaluate Abu Yusuf's and al-Shafi'i's opposing argument.

Core Idea
The qawma is not fard; omitting it makes prayer valid but severely disliked.

This passage addresses a significant intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the standing after ruku' (qawma). While Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani consider it not fard, Abu Yusuf argues it is fard, aligning with al-Shafi'i. Unpacking the proofs for each side reveals important usul principles about interpreting the Quran and hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the positions and authority levels
State the two Hanafi positions: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (zahir al-riwayah) say qawma is not fard; Abu Yusuf says it is fard. Note that the opposing school (al-Shafi'i) agrees with Abu Yusuf.
The 'two' (هما) refer to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
2
Examine the Hanafi majority proof
The majority relies on the Quranic verse 'ارْكَعُوا وَاسْجُدُوا' which only mentions ruku' and sujud, implying qawma is not required. They also argue that standing before ruku' is a rukn and is not repeated in one rak'ah (qiyas with qira'ah).
The principle that a hadith ahad cannot add to the Quran is invoked.
3
Examine the opposing proof
Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i cite the hadith to the desert Arab 'then raise your head until you stand upright' and the hadith 'لا صلاة لمن لم يقم صلبه'. They also analogize qawma to the sitting between prostrations (jalsah), which is fard.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The majority interprets the hadith as negating perfection, not validity (like 'لا صلاة لجار المسجد'). They also distinguish qawma from jalsah because after ruku' one can prostrate without standing, whereas after the first sajdah standing is impossible without jalsah.
The key distinction: jalsah is necessary for transition, qawma is not.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If someone omits the qawma, according to the majority the prayer is valid but makruh tahrimi; according to Abu Yusuf it is invalid. The mufta bihi is the majority view.
Al-Khujandi confirms the zahir al-riwayah position.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling of the qawma (standing after rising from ruku') in prayer. According to the majority Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the qawma is not fard (obligatory) in the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah). Also, standing is already a rukn before ruku', so it is not repeated in the same rak'ah, similar to recitation. Regarding the hadith 'No prayer for the one who does not straighten his back,' they interpret it as negating perfection, not validity, analogous to the hadith 'No prayer for the neighbor of the mosque except in the mosque.' They also argue that a hadith ahad cannot add to the Quran. The response to the analogy with jalsah is that the jalsah is necessary for the transition to the second prostration, whereas after ruku' one can go directly to sujud without standing. Thus, the two positions differ. The text concludes with a ruling from al-Khujandi: the qawma is not fard in the apparent narration; if one omits it, the prayer is valid but it is severely disliked (makruh tahrimi). Abu Yusuf's view that it is fard is also transmitted but not the preferred position.

The Meaning of 'Sami'a Allah' in Prayer
Defining 'Sami'a Allah' as acceptance in prayer context

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi interpretation of the phrase 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' in prayer and distinguish between literal hearing and legal acceptance.

Core Idea
The phrase 'Sami'a Allah' in prayer signifies divine acceptance and response, not auditory hearing.

In the prayer, Muslims recite 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' (Allah hears the one who praises Him). But what does 'hears' mean in this context? The Hanafi school, as explained by classical commentators, clarifies that this hearing is not physical but denotes acceptance and reward. This lesson unpacks the linguistic and legal meaning of this key phrase.

Guided Path
1
Examine the literal meaning of 'sami'a'
The passage shows that 'sami'a' in this context does not mean physical hearing but rather acceptance and response, as illustrated by examples of an amir and a judge.
The amir 'hearing' means responding; the judge 'hearing' evidence means accepting it.
2
Analyze the tafsir of Abd al-Samad
The commentator quotes a tafsir that explicitly states: 'Allah accepts the praise of the one who praises Him.' This confirms the interpretation.
This direct statement leaves no room for ambiguity.
3
Consider the poetic evidence
The poet's line demonstrates the same usage: 'hear' meaning 'respond'.
Classical Arabic poetry is used as evidence for linguistic meaning.
4
Appreciate the difference between commentaries
The passage notes that al-Jawhara provides more evidence and discussions than al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, indicating a deeper scholarly treatment.
This shows the development of the commentary tradition.

Siwak Benefit and the Sunnah of Madmadah and Istinshaq
Inter-madhhab disputes on siwak and on madmadah/istinshaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on madmadah and istinshaq, explain the siwak benefit across multiple prayers, and identify the ikhtilaf with Malik and Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Siwak suffices for multiple prayers on one wudu; madmadah and istinshaq are emphatic sunnah, not fard.

This lesson from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah clarifies two important sunan of wudu: the use of siwak and the acts of rinsing the mouth and sniffing water. The commentator explains the Hanafi position that one siwak before Zuhr prayer covers subsequent prayers as long as wudu remains, contrary to al-Shafi'i who recommends siwak for each prayer. He then addresses the dispute with Imam Malik over the obligation of madmadah and istinshaq, and details their proper performance, including the intra-Hanafi difference on using fresh water each time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal issues
The passage covers (a) the benefit of siwak across multiple prayers and (b) the ruling and method of madmadah and istinshaq.
Both involve inter-madhhab disputes: Hanafi vs Shafi'i on siwak, Hanafi vs Maliki on madmadah/istinshaq.
2
Analyze the siwak ruling
Note that Hanafi holds: one siwak used for Zuhr suffices for subsequent prayers as long as wudu remains. The Shafi'i view requires siwak for each prayer. Also note the consensus on recommending siwak if forgotten.
The Hanafi position is based on the principle that the sunnah of siwak is attached to the act of prayer, not each instance.
3
Examine the madmadah/istinshaq dispute
Hanafi: sunnah mu'akkadah; Maliki: fard. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: omission does not invalidate wudu in Hanafi, but does in Maliki.
The text does not cite explicit dalil, but the Hanafi school relies on ahad hadith and interpretation of the Prophet's consistency.
4
Assess the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on method
Some scholars say three rinses from one handful does not fulfill the sunnah; al-Sayrafi says it does. For istinshaq, the return of used water to the palm makes it problematic.
The preferred method is with fresh water each time, though the text does not give a definitive tarjih.
5
Understand mubalaghah (exaggeration)
Mubalaghah in rinsing and sniffing is sunnah for a non-fasting person. Two opinions on its description: circulating water or gargling.
If one swallows the rinsing water, it suffices but it is better to expel it as it becomes used water.

The Time of Zuhr: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Its End
Ruling on Zuhr time and dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on its end

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the beginning and end times of Zuhr according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, explain the practical consequence of the disagreement, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school agrees on when Zuhr begins but not on when it ends—Abu Hanifa says double shadow, the Sahibayn say equal shadow.

The time of Zuhr prayer is a foundational topic in prayer (salah) with agreed-upon beginning but an important internal Hanafi dispute on its end. In this lesson, we examine the views of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on when Zuhr time expires. Understanding this dispute not only clarifies the practice of the prayer but also introduces the method of tarjih (preference) within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed-upon beginning time
The text states that Zuhr begins at zawal (the sun's decline from its zenith). This is unanimous.
Zawal is the moment when the sun starts to move westward after reaching its highest point.
2
Extract the two positions on the end time
Abu Hanifa: end when the shadow of every object becomes twice its length (excluding the shadow at zawal). Sahibayn: end when the shadow equals the length of the object (without exclusion).
The shadow at zawal (sawi az-zawal) is the minimal shadow at noon.
3
Understand the linguistic distinction: fay' vs. zill
The commentary explains that 'fay'' is the shadow after zawal (from west to east), while 'zill' is general. This helps clarify which shadow is measured.
Abu Hanifa's phrase 'sawi fi al-zawal' (excluding the zawal shadow) means we measure from the minimum shadow.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Hanifa, Zuhr time lasts longer; according to the Sahibayn, it ends earlier. This affects when Asr prayer can be started.
Example: if an object is 1 m and its zawal shadow is 0.2 m, Abu Hanifa's end is when total shadow = 2 m (i.e., 1.8 m after zawal); Sahibayn's end is when total shadow = 1 m (i.e., 0.8 m after zawal).
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative position)
The commentary notes that the Sahibayn's view is also a narration from Abu Hanifa. Later Hanafi scholars prefer it for ease and to avoid delay. Thus the mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view.
This is a classic example of tarjih within the madhhab.

The Shafaq Dispute: Determining Maghrib and Isha Times
Explains prayer times with intra-Hanafi dispute on shafaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi definitions of shafaq, explain the evidence for each, and identify the mufta bihi position in the school.

Core Idea
Shafaq defines the end of Maghrib and start of Isha — the companions' view (redness) is relied upon for ease.

Prayer times are conditions for the obligation of salah, but the exact boundaries can be subject to ijtihad. The most notable internal Hanafi dispute is over the definition of al-shafaq — the afterglow that signals the end of Maghrib and the beginning of Isha. This lesson unpacks the linguistic and analogical arguments from the two sides and shows how the school ultimately resolved the difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
What exactly is being disputed? It is the definition of al-shafaq: Is it the white glow after redness (Abu Hanifa) or the redness itself (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)? This affects when Maghrib ends and Isha begins.
The times for Zuhr and Asr also have a minor dispute (mithl vs mithlayn), but the shafaq dispute is the focus here.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues linguistically: shafaq derives from riqqah (softness/thinness), and white is softer than red. He also adopts a precautionary approach — choosing the later time for Maghrib (until whiteness disappears) ensures the prayer is offered within its time with certainty.
He attributes this view to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq and the linguist al-Mubarrad.
3
Analyze the two companions' proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue by analogy (qiyas): just as there are three risings (false dawn, true dawn, sun) and the middle one (true dawn) determines the times for Fajr and Isha, so there are three settings (sun, red shafaq, white shafaq); the middle (red shafaq) should determine the end of Maghrib. They also cite linguistic usage (shafaq is commonly used for redness) and attribute their view to Ali ibn Abi Talib and linguists al-Asma'i and al-Khalil.
There is also a narration from Abu Hanifa supporting redness.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, Maghrib time extends until whiteness disappears — Isha begins later. Under the companions, Maghrib ends when redness fades — Isha begins earlier. This can shift the prayer times by about 15-20 minutes depending on location.
The author notes that the companions' view is 'more spacious for the people' (awsa' li al-nas), while Abu Hanifa's is more precautionary (ihtiyat).
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi scholars, including the mutakhkhirin, have adopted the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (shafaq = redness) as the operative ruling for fatwa, because it brings ease and aligns with the Shafi'i school and common practice.
This is the position used in most Hanafi prayer timetables today.

Imam Abu Hanifa holds that Witr is wajib (obligatory), while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, consider it sunnah mu'akkadah (stressed recommended). By studying their analogical arguments, you will understand how qiyas operates differently depending on the chosen asl (original case).

Repeating the Sunnah of Isha When the Fard is Invalid

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The sunnah of Isha is built upon the fard; if the fard is invalid, the sunnah must be repeated.

In Hanafi fiqh, the relationship between sunnah and fard prayers is not merely sequential but structural. This lesson examines a specific case: when the Isha prayer is performed along with its two rak'ahs of sunnah, but later the fard is found to be invalid. The scholars of the school debate whether the sunnah must be repeated, and the text resolves it with a consensus in one scenario.

Guided Path
1

A person prays Isha and its sunnah, then discovers the Isha prayer was invalid (e.g., missed a condition). Identify the two possible sub-cases: only the fard is invalid, or both the fard and sunnah are invalid.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's position
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (عندهما), the sunnah must be repeated in both cases because it is a sunnah of Isha, just like the two rak'ahs.

When the Isha prayer alone is invalid (but the sunnah was valid), there is consensus (إجماعاً) that both the fard and the sunnah must be repeated. This establishes the principle that the sunnah is built upon the fard.
The text states: 'أعادها وأعاد الركعتين إجماعاً'.
4

The reason given is 'لأنها تُبنى عليها' — the sunnah is built upon the fard. Thus its validity depends on the fard's validity.
This is the key legal principle (qā'ida) underlying the ruling.

The ikhtilāf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn concerns the case where both the fard and the sunnah are invalid. Abu Hanifa reportedly did not require repeating the sunnah; the Sahibayn required it. The relied-upon view (muftā bihī) is to repeat the sunnah in all cases of fard invalidity.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended times for Maghrib, Isha, and Witr according to the Hanafi school and apply the seasonal and personal exceptions.

Core Idea
Expedite Maghrib, delay Isha in winter, delay Witr for the vigilant — all to follow the Sunnah.

Prayer times have optimal windows. The Hanafi school, following the Prophet ﷺ, emphasizes expediting Maghrib immediately after sunset and delaying Isha in winter to the first third of the night. For Witr, the recommendation depends on one's ability to wake at night. This lesson breaks down these rulings and their exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three prayers discussed
Note that the passage addresses Maghrib, Isha, and Witr prayers separately. Understand each recommendation in context.
2
State the recommended time for Maghrib and its exception
Maghrib is always to be expedited immediately after sunset. The only exception is a cloudy day, where one should delay until certain of sunset by dominant probability.
Certainty is based on ghalabat al-zann (dominant probability), not absolute certainty.
3
State the recommended time for Isha in winter and summer
In winter, it is recommended to delay Isha until before one-third of the night. Delaying up to half is permissible; beyond half is disliked. In summer, due to short nights, it is recommended to expedite Isha.
The night is measured from sunset to dawn. One-third and half are approximate.
4
State the recommendation for Witr and the condition
For one who is accustomed to night prayer (tahajjud), it is recommended to delay Witr to the end of the night based on the hadith. If one fears not waking, Witr should be performed before sleep.
Witr is wajib in Hanafi school, but the timing recommendation does not affect its obligation.

Prayer Timings on Cloudy Days (Hanafi Rule)
Clarification of recommended timings on cloudy days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended timings for each prayer on a cloudy day and explain the reasoning behind delay or haste.

Core Idea
Cloudy days: delay Fajr, Zuhr, Maghrib; hasten Asr, Isha to avoid doubt and disliked times.

When the sky is overcast, determining exact prayer times becomes difficult. The Hanafi school provides a practical guideline: delay prayers whose start is uncertain (Fajr, Zuhr, Maghrib) and hasten prayers whose end is uncertain (Asr, Isha). This lesson unpacks the rule and its rationale from the classical commentary Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five daily prayers
List the five obligatory prayers: Fajr, Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha.
These are the same every day.
2
Classify each prayer into delay or haste group
From the text, note which prayers are delayed and which are hastened on a cloudy day.
Fajr, Zuhr, Maghrib: delay. Asr, Isha: hasten.
3
Understand the reason for delaying Fajr, Zuhr, Maghrib
On a cloudy day, the start of these prayers may be unclear. Delaying ensures they are performed after the time has certainly begun.
Fajr: true dawn may not be visible. Zuhr: declining sun (zawal) may be hidden. Maghrib: sunset may not be seen.
4
Understand the reason for hastening Asr and Isha
Hastening Asr avoids the disliked time when the sun turns yellow. Hastening Isha avoids reducing the congregation due to darkness.
Asr delay risks entering makruh time; Isha delay loses congregation.
5
Apply the mnemonic 'eye for eye'
The phrase 'you match eye with eye' means: hasten two prayers (Asr, Isha) and delay the other three. This is a memory aid.
Opposites: haste ↔ delay; two prayers vs three.

Witr is valid all night but best at dawn (sahar).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Some scholars argue imamah excels adhan, but the Hanafi school does not enforce a hierarchy between these two virtuous acts.

In this brief passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the commentator presents a view held by 'some' scholars within the Hanafi tradition: that leading the prayer (imamah) is better than calling to it (adhan). The proof is drawn from the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the Rightly Guided Caliphs, who served as imams but never as mu'adhdhins. This lesson unpacks that argument, situates it within the school's broader outlook, and clarifies the operative position for fatwa.

The passage states that 'some scholars' hold imamah to be superior (afdal) to adhan. Pinpoint the exact claim and note that it is a comparative ruling (tafdil).

The proof is two-part: (1) the Prophet ﷺ and the caliphs were imams, not mu'adhdhins; (2) they only choose the best of affairs. This is a dalil naqli from prophetic practice (sunnah fi'liyyah).
