Fasting and Vows on Prohibited Days: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fasting and vows in prohibited days, with reasoning based on the nature of the first moment of the act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Abu Hanifa's ruling on qada' for a broken fast on prohibited days, distinguish his reasoning from that of the Sahibayn, and apply the Hanafi ruling on vows to such days.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: No qada' for breaking a prohibited fast because the first moment is itself prohibited.

Fasting on Eid and Tashreeq days is haram, but what if someone starts such a fast and then breaks it? According to Abu Hanifa, no make-up (qada') is required, while his two main students disagree. The key lies in how each school views the first moment of fasting versus prayer. This lesson unpacks the istidlal behind the dispute and extends it to vows made for these days.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues
The passage covers two disputes: (a) obligation of qada' if a fast on a prohibited day is broken, (b) validity of a vow to fast/pray on prohibited days.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
He says no qada' because the first part of fasting is itself fasting, done in a prohibited manner, so no obligation attaches.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
They analogize to prayer: if one starts a prayer in prohibited time and breaks it, qada' is required, so the same for fasting.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's differentiation
He differentiates because prayer's first act (tahrimah) is not part of prayer itself, whereas fasting's first act is already fasting.
This distinction relies on the Hanafi view that tahrimah is not a rukn of prayer.
5
Apply the reasoning to vows
The passage also covers vows: Hanafi says vow is valid but better fulfilled elsewhere; Zufar and Shafi'i say invalid because it's a vow of sin.

Commentary
This passage discusses two related issues: fasting on prohibited days (Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of Tashreeq) and vows to perform prayers or fasts during these disliked times. Abu Hanifa holds that if one begins a fast on a prohibited day and then breaks it, there is no obligation to make it up (qada'), because the very first moment of the fast is prohibited, so the act is invalid from the start and does not create an obligation. His two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, analogize fasting to prayer: since starting a prayer in a disliked time obligates its completion and qada' if broken, likewise for fasting. Abu Hanifa distinguishes: prayer's first moment (the takbir) is not itself prayer, so it is not directly prohibited, whereas fasting's first moment is already an act of fasting, which is directly prohibited. For vows: if someone vows to pray or fast during these prohibited times, it is valid (the vow itself is a valid commitment), but it is better to perform the act in a permissible time. If they do perform it during the prohibited time, they have fulfilled their vow. Zufar and al-Shafi'i disagree, considering the vow invalid because it involves sin. The Hanafi position, supported by al-Hidayah, is that the vow is valid, but the person should break the fast and make it up later.

Key Terms
الأيام المنهية (al-ayyam al-manhiyyah): The days on which fasting is prohibited: Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the three days of Tashreeq (11th, 12th, 13th of Dhul Hijjah).
التحريمة (al-tahrimah): The opening takbir (Allahu Akbar) that initiates prayer and makes certain things haram (forbidden) during prayer.

A) Yes, because they started a voluntary act and then broke it, so qada' is required.
B) No, because the first part of the fast is itself prohibited, so no obligation arises.

D) No, because fasting on Eid is not actually prohibited.

Key Takeaway
Abu Hanifa rules that no qada' is required for breaking a fast on prohibited days, and a vow to fast on such days is valid but should be fulfilled on a permissible day.
