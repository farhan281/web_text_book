Giving Zakat to Your Spouse: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat to spouse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat to spouse, identify the dalil for each position, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Giving zakat to your spouse is prohibited by Abu Hanifa but allowed by the Sahibayn for wife to husband — later fatwa follows the Sahibayn.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the permissibility of paying zakat to one's spouse. Imam Abu Hanifa prohibits both directions based on the concept of shared benefits (ishtirak fi al-manafi') and intermixing of wealth (ikhtilat fi al-amwal). In contrast, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) permit a wife to give her zakat to her husband, citing the prophetic hadith of Zaynab bint Jahsh. The passage highlights how the Hanafi school internally resolves such differences in favor of the more practical, hadith-based ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its scope
Identify the exact issue: giving zakat to one's spouse (both directions: husband to wife and wife to husband).
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa prohibits both directions. His evidence is the principle of shared benefits (ishtirak fi al-manafi') and intermixing of wealth, supported by a Qur'anic allusion (Surah al-Duha 93:8) suggesting spouses benefit from each other's wealth.
The Qur'anic verse is not an explicit text on zakat; it is used as a basis for the principle.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad permit a wife to give her zakat to her husband. Their evidence is the hadith of Zaynab bint Jahsh, who asked the Prophet ﷺ about giving charity to her husband, and he allowed it.
The hadith is an explicit textual proof for permissibility.
4
Compare the strength of dalils
Note that the Sahibayn rely on an explicit hadith (zanni al-thubut but explicit dalala), while Abu Hanifa uses a general principle (Qur'anic allusion with zanni dalala). In Hanafi usul, a specific hadith can override a general principle.
This comparison explains the later preference for the Sahibayn's view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its rationale
Later Hanafi scholars (like Ibn 'Abidin) adopt the Sahibayn's view for wife to husband due to direct textual evidence and practical ease. The husband-to-wife prohibition remains mufta bihi from Abu Hanifa.
The fatwa distinguishes direction: wife to husband permissible, husband to wife prohibited.

Ruling on Paying Zakat to One's Spouse: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat to spouse with two 'illas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying zakat to a spouse, explain the two 'illas for the prohibition, and evaluate the Sahibayn's counterargument based on the hadith of Zaynab.

Core Idea
Spouses cannot give zakat to each other due to intermixing of benefits — unless the receiver is only the breadwinner.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute: may a woman give her obligatory zakat to her husband? The matn (Mukhtasar al-Quduri) records Abu Hanifa's prohibition and the Sahibayn's permission. The commentaries in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira and al-Siraj al-Wahhaj unpack the reasoning, revealing two competing 'illas: the sharing of benefits (ikhtilat) and the husband's maintenance obligation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply usul al-fiqh to fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the undisputed ruling
Both sides agree that a husband cannot give his zakat to his wife. Why? Because of the sharing of benefits and mixing of wealth between spouses.
This is the foundation; the dispute only concerns the reverse case.
2
Locate the two 'illas
From al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, two underlying causes are given: (1) the husband's obligation to maintain his wife (like a child), (2) the intermixing of wealth between spouses. Abu Hanifa uses the second; the Sahibayn use the first.
The 'illa chosen determines the ruling for the wife-to-husband case.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
They rely on the hadith of Zaynab bint Jahsh, where the Prophet permitted her to give charity to her husband and promised two rewards. This appears to allow giving zakat to one's spouse.
The hadith uses the word 'sadaqah', which can be general.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa counters that the hadith refers to voluntary charity (sadaqat al-tatawwu'), not obligatory zakat. Evidence: Zaynab was a craftswoman, not wealthy, and obligatory zakat cannot be given to one's dependents (e.g., children).
This is a classic example of interpreting a general text (amm) in light of qarinah (context).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, a woman must not give her zakat to her husband; under the Sahibayn, she may. The mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view.
The practical impact is significant for zakat distribution.

Core Idea
Giving obligatory zakat to one's spouse is invalid in the Hanafi school; the hadith of Zainab is interpreted as voluntary charity.

The obligation of zakat is one of the pillars of Islam, but its distribution is governed by specific rules. One question that arises is whether a woman may give her zakat to her husband. A well-known hadith appears to permit giving charity to a spouse with extra reward, but the Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between obligatory zakat and voluntary charity. In this lesson, we examine the hadith and Abu Hanifa's interpretation, which restricts its application to voluntary charity only.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What act is being ruled upon? Giving zakat (obligatory charity) to one's husband. What is the Hanafi ruling? It is invalid (batil).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is the hadith of Zainab bint Mas'ud, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) said she would receive two rewards for giving charity to her husband.
The hadith is ahad (singular transmission) and thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does Abu Hanifa understand this hadith? He interprets it as referring to voluntary charity (sadaqat al-tatawwu'), not obligatory zakat, based on the context that Zainab was not wealthy enough to be obligated to pay zakat.
The phrase 'you have two rewards' does not specify the type of charity; Abu Hanifa uses contextual evidence (qarinah) to limit it.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) permit giving obligatory zakat to a poor spouse based on the general implication of the hadith. The difference arises from whether the hadith is taken as 'amm (general) or interpreted with a qarinah.
This ikhtilaf reflects different usul principles regarding the scope of a text.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school relies on the contextual evidence that Zainab was not a zakat-payer, thus the hadith cannot establish permissibility for obligatory zakat. This is the mufta bihi position.
The authority is from al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, a later Hanafi work, but it represents the established madhhab view.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Hanafi school regarding giving zakat to one's husband. The hadith of Zainab bint Mas'ud is cited, in which the Prophet (peace be upon him) told her she would receive two rewards for giving charity to her husband: the reward of charity and the reward of maintaining family ties. Imam Abu Hanifa interprets this hadith as referring to voluntary charity (sadaqat al-tatawwu'), not obligatory zakat. The reasoning is that Zainab earned income through her own labor and was not wealthy enough to be obligated to pay zakat. Thus, the hadith does not prove that it is permissible to give obligatory zakat to one's husband. This is the position of the Hanafi school: giving obligatory zakat to one's spouse (husband or wife) is not valid, because the spouse is within one's own household and zakat is meant for those outside one's immediate dependents. However, giving voluntary charity to one's spouse is recommended and carries great reward.

Key Terms
صدقة التطوع (sadaqat al-tatawwu'): Voluntary charity given freely, not obligated by zakat.
الزكاة (al-zakat): The obligatory charity that is one of the five pillars of Islam, with specific rules and recipients.
