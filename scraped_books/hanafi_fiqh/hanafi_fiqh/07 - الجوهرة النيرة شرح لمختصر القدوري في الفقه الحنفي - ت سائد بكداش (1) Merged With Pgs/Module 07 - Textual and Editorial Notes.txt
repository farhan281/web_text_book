Sources of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah: Understanding the Hanafi Textual Network
List of primary and secondary sources cited by Imam al-Haddad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the primary and secondary sources cited by Imam al-Haddad and explain their significance in the Hanafi textual tradition.

Core Idea
The authority of a Hanafi commentary relies on the chain of sources linking it to the founding Imams.

In this lesson, we examine the list of works that Imam al-Haddad drew upon in his commentary al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah. Understanding this network of sources is essential for navigating Hanafi fiqh literature and evaluating the strength of transmitted opinions. This passage provides a glimpse into the scholarly apparatus of a major Hanafi commentator.

Guided Path
1
Identify the most frequently quoted works
List the works named in the first part of the passage: al-Hidayah, al-Nihayah, al-Khujandi, Fatawa Qadi Khan, al-Asl and its commentary, al-Fatawa, al-Dhakhirah, al-Karkhi, al-Wajiz, Manzumah al-Nasafi, al-Mustasfa, al-Musaffa, and al-Mabsut.
These are the core references al-Haddad relied upon.
2
Categorize the sources by type
Distinguish between early foundational texts (e.g., al-Asl, al-Mabsut) and later commentaries and manuals (e.g., al-Hidayah, al-Ikhtiyar).
Early texts are part of Zahir al-Riwayah; later works build on them.
3
Note the chronological range
Observe that the sources span from the 2nd/8th century (al-Asl by Muhammad al-Shaybani, d. 189 AH) to the 7th/13th century (al-Ikhtiyar by al-Mawsili, d. 683 AH).
This range shows the cumulative nature of Hanafi scholarship.
4
Recognize the significance of each source
For each major source, recall its author and its role: al-Asl (foundation), al-Mabsut (comprehensive commentary), al-Hidayah (authoritative manual), etc.
This helps in assessing the weight of a transmitted ruling.
5
Understand the concept of Zahir al-Riwayah
Note that al-Asl and other early works by Muhammad al-Shaybani are part of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, the most authoritative layer of Hanafi fiqh.
Later commentaries like al-Hidayah are secondary but widely used.

Understanding Manuscript Colophons in Hanafi Fiqh
Manuscript colophon and note

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a manuscript colophon and explain its importance for establishing the provenance and reliability of a Hanafi fiqh text.

Core Idea
A manuscript colophon is the birth certificate of a copy—it tells us when, by whom, and with what authority the text was transmitted.

This lesson examines a colophon from a manuscript of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, a classical Hanafi commentary. The colophon records the completion date and scribe details, serving as a crucial tool for scholars to verify the accuracy and lineage of the text. Understanding colophons helps us appreciate the chain of transmission that preserves our fiqh heritage.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Locate the colophon
Find the phrase indicating completion (الفراغ من تشاء) or similar. This marks the end of the copyist's work.
In this passage, the colophon begins with 'وكان الفراغ من تشاء'.
2
Step 2: Identify the date
Extract the date of completion. It is usually given in the Islamic calendar. Here it is the 29th of Dhu al-Hijjah 973 AH.
The year is partially legible as 973 AH.
3
Step 3: Note the time of day
Colophons often mention the time (e.g., after Asr). This indicates the care taken in recording details.
The passage mentions 'وقت العصر' (Asr time).
4
Step 4: Compare with other copies
Scholars compare multiple copies to establish the most reliable text. This passage references a copy from 950 AH and another from around 974 AH.
The differences in dates help trace the transmission history.

The Correct Pronunciation of Maytah: Takhfif vs Tashdid
Linguistic clarification of the term 'maytah'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct pronunciation of 'maytah' in the Hanafi matn and explain the linguistic evidence for takhfif over tashdid.

Core Idea
Maytah is pronounced with takhfif (no shaddah) because tashdid refers to the living who will die.

In the Hanafi fiqh texts, precise reading of key terms preserves legal accuracy. One such term is 'maytah' (carrion). This lesson clarifies its correct pronunciation, distinguishing it from a similar word, and grounds the distinction in Quranic usage and classical poetry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Term
The passage defines the word 'maytah' – carrion. Recognize it as a technical fiqh term.
2
Note the Two Pronunciations
The word can be read with takhfif (maytah, no shaddah) or tashdid (mayyitah, with shaddah on the ya). The author states that the correct form in the matn is with takhfif.
Takhfif means lightening, tashdid means doubling.
3
Identify the Quranic Evidence
The author quotes Quran 39:30: 'Indeed, you are to die' – the word there is 'mayyit' (with tashdid), meaning a living person who will die. This proves tashdid is for the living, not the dead.
4
Analyze the Poetic Evidence
A line of poetry distinguishes 'mayit' (alive/soul) from 'mayt' (dead body). The dead body takes takhfif.
5
Conclude the Correct Pronunciation
Based on the evidence, for dead carrion ('maytah'), the correct pronunciation is with takhfif. This ensures accurate reading of the matn.

Commentary
This passage is a linguistic clarification (definition of a term). The author explains that the word 'maytah' (carrion) in the text of al-Quduri is pronounced with takhfif (lightening, without shaddah), because with tashdid (shaddah on the ya) it refers to a living being who will die, as in the Quranic verse. For something already dead, the correct pronunciation is with takhfif. The author supports this with a poetic verse. This is a standard Hanafi linguistic refinement to ensure the correct recitation of the matn.
