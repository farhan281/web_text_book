Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Takbirah al-Ihram: Condition or Fard?
Explains the status of takbirah and its counting among fard.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between shart (condition) and fard (integral) for takbirah al-ihram in the Hanafi school, and state the practical consequences of this intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
Takbirah al-ihram is a condition according to the majority, but a fard according to Imam Muhammad — with real effects on prayer validity.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah delves into a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: is the opening takbir (tahrimah) a condition (shart) for prayer or an integral part (fard)? The author presents the views of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf against Imam Muhammad, then draws out two practical consequences that hinge on this classification. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how the school categorizes the elements of prayer and why terminology matters in legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Locate the exact point of disagreement: is tahrimah a shart or a fard? Note the specific imams associated with each position.
The text says: 'وهي شرط عندهما، وفرض عند محمد' — it is a condition according to the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), and a fard according to Muhammad.
2
Understand the analogy
The author likens the tahrimah to a door of a house. A door is not part of the house but is counted as part of it due to connection. Similarly, tahrimah is counted among the obligations (fara'id) because of its close connection to prayer, even though it is outside the prayer's essence.
This analogy explains why the author first says 'عدها من الفروض' (they counted it among the obligations).
3
Trace the practical consequences
Two scenarios illustrate the thamarah al-khilaf: (1) if the obligatory prayer becomes invalid after tahrimah, it becomes nafl according to the two but not according to Muhammad; (2) if tahrimah is performed before the time of Zuhr and then time enters, the prayer is valid according to the two but not according to Muhammad.
The difference hinges on whether tahrimah is a condition (so the prayer started correctly) or a fard (so the prayer was invalid from the start if time was not yet in).
4
Resolve the count issue
The passage addresses a question about counts: conditions become seven and fard become six. The answer: taharah in its types counts as one condition, and tahrimah is the sixth condition. For fard, the five well-known ones (qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, final sitting) plus a sixth: khuruj bil-san' according to Abu Hanifa, tuma'ninah according to Abu Yusuf, and intiqal according to both.
This shows how the school harmonizes the enumeration of pillars and conditions.

Commentary
This passage discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the status of takbirah al-ihram (the opening takbir), known as tahrimah. The author states that it is considered among the fara'id (obligations) due to its close connection to the prayer, likening it to the door of a house. It is called tahrimah because it prohibits previously permissible actions. The core dispute: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, takbirah is a condition (shart) for the validity of prayer; according to Imam Muhammad, it is a fard (integral part). The practical consequence (thamarah) of this difference is twofold: (1) if the obligatory prayer becomes invalid (e.g., due to a major nullifier), the prayer, having begun with takbirah, transforms into a nafl (supererogatory) prayer according to the first two, but not according to Muhammad; (2) if someone begins Zuhr prayer before the time (before zawal) and completes the takbirah, then the time enters, the prayer is valid according to the two, but invalid according to Muhammad. The passage then addresses a question about the count of conditions and fard: it explains that the conditions are six (including taharah in its types as one, and tahrimah as the sixth) and the fard are six (the five mentioned—qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, final sitting—plus the sixth, which differs: khuruj bil-san' (exiting by action) according to Abu Hanifa, tuma'ninah (tranquility) according to Abu Yusuf, and intiqal (transition between pillars) according to both of them.

Key Terms
تحريمة (tahrimah): The opening takbir (takbirat al-ihram) that initiates the prayer and prohibits worldly actions.
شرط (shart): A condition for the validity of an act, not part of the act itself.
فرض (fard): An obligatory element (integral) of the prayer, omission of which invalidates it.

The Five Fard Elements of Prayer: Standing, Recitation, Bowing, Prostration, and Final Sitting
Defines the five fard acts of prayer with proofs and rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five fard elements of prayer, identify their Quranic proofs, and distinguish between fard, wajib, and sunnah in the context of salah.

Core Idea
Five fard acts define the minimum valid prayer; omission invalidates it completely.

This lesson from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira defines the five essential (fard) components of the prayer: standing (qiyam), recitation (qira'ah), bowing (ruku'), prostration (sujud), and the final sitting (qa'dah) for tashahhud. The author cites Quranic evidence for each and explains that additional elements are sunnah but contain wajibat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five fard elements
List each: qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, qa'dah.
2
Locate the dalil for each
For qira'ah: Quran 73:20; for ruku' and sujud: Quran 22:77. Qiyam and qa'dah are established by practice and necessity.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative form (amr) indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. Since recitation is not obligatory outside prayer by consensus, it becomes specific to salah.
4
Distinguish fard from wajib and sunnah
The text clarifies that within what is called sunnah, there are wajibat (e.g., reciting al-Fatiha) that require sajdat al-sahw if omitted forgetfully.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If a follower finishes tashahhud before the imam and then speaks, his prayer is valid because the fard of sitting is fulfilled.

The Five Fard Pillars of Prayer and the Wajibat within 'Sunnah'
Commentary on Al-Quduri's matn: identifying fard pillars and reclassifying 'sunnah' as wajib.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five fard acts of prayer according to the Hanafi school, explain their Quranic proofs, and classify additional acts as wajib based on the technical Hanafi distinction between fard and sunnah.

Core Idea
Five pillars are fard from the Quran; 'sunnah' in the matn means wajibat proven by ahad hadith.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah unpacks the fundamental pillars (fard) of prayer in the Hanafi school, citing explicit Quranic evidence. It then tackles a critical terminological point: when the matn says 'whatever is added beyond that is sunnah', it does not mean merely recommended — it includes many acts that are actually wajib (obligatory), such as reciting Fatiha and adding a surah. Understanding this distinction is essential for proper practice and for knowing when sajdah sahw is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five fard acts
Locate the five acts explicitly listed: recitation, bowing, prostration, and the final sitting to the extent of tashahhud. Note the wording 'and whatever is added beyond that is sunnah' indicates these five are the fard.
The final sitting is fard only to the extent of tashahhud; longer is sunnah.
2
Analyze the Quranic proofs for each
For recitation: Al-Muzzammil 73:20 ('So recite what is easy from the Quran') – the imperative indicates wujub, and since recitation is not obligatory outside prayer by consensus, it becomes fard in prayer. For bowing and prostration: Al-Hajj 22:77 ('Bow and prostrate') – direct command.
The dalal (indication) is zanni because the imperative can be interpreted for nadb or farḍiyyah, but the context and consensus restrict it.
3
Understand the special case of the follower finishing tashahhud early
The commentator includes a ruling from al-Muhit: if a follower finishes the tashahhud before the imam and then speaks or eats, his prayer is still valid. This shows the final sitting is complete once the tashahhud is recited.
This is an exception – normally talking breaks the prayer.
4
Recognize the technical meaning of 'sunnah' in this context
The author explains that the term 'sunnah' here includes actions whose obligation is established by the sunnah (ahad hadith) rather than the Quran. These are called wajibat in the Hanafi school. Omission deliberately is sinful; forgetfully requires sajdah sahw.
This Hanafi distinction between fard (Quran/mutawatir) and wajib (ahad) is not used by other schools.
5
List the wajibat mentioned in the commentary
The commentary explicitly lists: reciting al-Fatiha, adding a surah, maintaining order in repeated acts (e.g., two prostrations), the first sitting, reciting tashahhud in the final sitting, qunut, Eid takbirs, and proper volume of recitation. These are all wajib.
The list is not exhaustive; other Hanafi texts add more.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Quduri regarding the essential acts (fard) of prayer. It first identifies five fard acts: recitation of Quran, bowing, prostration, and the final sitting for the tashahhud. Each is supported by Quranic evidence. Recitation is obligatory in prayer because the general command in the Quran is restricted by consensus to prayer only. Bowing and prostration are commanded directly. The final sitting must be the duration of the tashahhud, and the correct wording is from 'at-tahiyyat' to 'wa rasuluh'. The commentary then addresses an unexpected case: if a follower finishes the tashahhud before the imam, he may speak or eat without invalidating his prayer, according to al-Muhit. Then the author explains that what the matn calls 'sunnah' includes actions that are actually wajib (obligatory) in the Hanafi school, such as reciting Fatiha, adding a surah, maintaining order in repeated acts like prostration, first sitting, qunut, Eid takbirs, and proper volume of recitation. These are called 'sunnah' because their obligation is established by the sunnah (ahad hadith) rather than the Quran, making them a separate category from fard. Neglecting these wajib acts intentionally is sinful, and omitting them forgetfully requires sajdah sahw.

Key Terms
القراءة (al-qira'ah): Recitation of Quran in prayer, which is one of the fard acts.
الركوع (al-ruku'): Bowing in prayer, defined as bending (الانحناء).
السجود (al-sujud): Prostration, defined as lowering oneself to the ground (الانخفاض).
التشهد (al-tashahhud): The testimony recited in the sitting, beginning with 'At-tahiyyat' up to 'wa rasuluh'.
السنة (al-sunnah): In this context, it means actions proven by the sunnah (ahad hadith) as opposed to the Quran. The author uses it to include wajibat.
الواجب (al-wajib): Obligatory actions that are not fard; their obligation is established by ahad hadith. Omission deliberately is sinful, forgetfully requires sajdah sahw.

In this lesson, we examine the opening supplication (thana') recited at the start of prayer. We learn its wording, its legal status, and the proof from the Quran. Learning Objective

Core Idea
The qawma is not fard; the imam says tasmi' only, the follower says tahmid — a division of roles.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra clarifies the rulings for the standing after ruku' (qawma) and the specific supplications said by the imam and follower. While the Sahibayn consider qawma fard and hold that the imam should recite tahmid quietly, Abu Hanifa maintains that qawma is not fard and the imam should not recite tahmid, relying on the division implied in the hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues
The passage addresses two separate but related issues: (1) whether the standing after ruku' (qawma) is fard (obligatory), and (2) whether the imam should recite the tahmid phrase 'Rabbana lakal hamd' after the tasmi'.
These are distinct disputes within the Hanafi school.
2
State the majority Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad)
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the qawma is not fard. Regarding the tasmi' and tahmid, the imam says 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' aloud, but does not say 'Rabbana lakal hamd'. The follower says 'Rabbana lakal hamd' but does not repeat the tasmi'.
This is considered the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
3
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi view (Abu Yusuf and Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf holds that the qawma is fard. Both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the imam should say the tahmid quietly after the tasmi', because the imam urged others to praise and should not neglect it himself.
Note that Muhammad agrees with Abu Yusuf on tahmid but with Abu Hanifa on qawma.
4
Examine the proofs
Abu Hanifa's proof is the hadith: 'When the imam says Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah, say (plural) Rabbana lakal hamd.' The imperative is directed to the followers, not the imam, implying a division of roles. The Sahibayn's reasoning is based on analogy (qiyas) with the individual pray-er, who combines both, and on the idea that the imam's urging entails his own compliance.
The hadith is a dalil naqli; the Sahibayn's argument is talil (analogical extension).
5
Determine the mufta bihi and apply to the munfarid
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: qawma is not fard, and the imam does not say tahmid. For the individual praying alone, the most sound view (al-ashah) is that he combines both tasmi' and tahmid, as there is no division of roles.
This is consistent with the principle that the munfarid acts as both imam and follower in one sense.

The commentator (al-Halwani or another) explains why al-Quduri's matn says 'on his nose and forehead' instead of 'forehead and nose'. The reason is practical: when a person prostrates, the nose is the first part to touch the ground because it is closer to the ground than the forehead. This is a subtle point about the sequence of placement, not a legal ruling.

الجبهة (al-jabhah): Forehead; the part of the face that, along with the nose, must touch the ground in sajdah.

Condition of Not Lifting Feet and Prostration on a Dais
States that lifting both feet invalidates prostration; lifting one is makruh; prostration on dais invalid if feet dangling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for lifting one or both feet during prostration and for praying on a raised surface with dangling feet, and identify which condition is violated.

Core Idea
Lifting both feet invalidates prostration; lifting one is makruh. Dangling feet from a dais invalidates prayer.

Prostration (sajdah) has specific postural conditions in the Hanafi school. This passage from a classical text clarifies two practical issues: what happens if a worshipper lifts one or both feet during sujood, and whether it is valid to prostrate on a raised platform like a dukkan or bed with feet unsupported. These rulings teach us the importance of body parts being firmly placed during worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is sujood (prostration). The condition here is that the feet must be placed on the ground during sujood.
This condition is specific to the Hanafi school.
2
State the ruling for lifting both feet
If a person lifts both feet off the ground during sujood, the prostration is not valid (la tujzi'uhu al-sajdah). The prayer must be repeated or the sajdah redone.
3
State the ruling for lifting one foot
If only one foot is lifted, the prostration is valid (yujzi'uhu) but it is disliked (makruh).
The source cites 'al-Murtaba' for this view.
4
Apply the condition to the dais case
If a person prays on a raised platform (dukkan) or a bed (sarir) and his feet dangle off the edge without touching the ground during sujood, the prayer is not permissible (la yajuz).
This is because the condition of feet being on the ground is not satisfied.

Among the conditions for the permissibility of prostration is that he not lift his feet during it. If he lifts both in his prostration: the prostration is not valid. And if he lifts one, it is said in al-Murtaba: it suffices him, with dislike. And if he prays on a platform and dangles his legs from the platform at prostration: it is not permissible. Similarly, on a bed if he dangles his legs from it: it is not permissible.

Commentary
This passage covers two related rulings. First, lifting both feet during sajdah invalidates it. Lifting only one foot is permissible (sufficing) but disliked (makruh), according to the work 'al-Murtaba'. Second, if a person prays on a raised surface (dakkan or sarir) and his feet hang off the edge without touching the ground during prostration, the prayer is not permissible. This is because the feet must be placed on the ground as part of the prostration posture.

Key Terms
الدكان (al-dukkān): A platform or dais; a raised structure used for sitting or praying.
الكراهة (al-karāhah): Dislike; here makruh tanzih for lifting one foot.
