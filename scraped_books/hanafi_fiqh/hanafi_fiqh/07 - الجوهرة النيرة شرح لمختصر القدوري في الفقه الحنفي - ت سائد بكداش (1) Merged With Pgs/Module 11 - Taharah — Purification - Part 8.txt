Purification from Physical Impurity is Wajib, Not Fard
A ruling that purification from physical impurity (najasah) is wajib, explained through evidence hierarchy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purification from physical impurity and explain why it is wajib rather than fard, distinguishing the evidentiary basis.

Core Idea
Removing physical impurity for prayer is wajib, because its evidence is zanni, not qat'i like the evidence for wudu.

In this passage, the commentator explains why Imam al-Quduri used the term 'wajib' for purifying from physical impurity, rather than 'fard'. This distinction is fundamental to Hanafi legal theory, as it reflects the strength of the evidence. Understanding this distinction will sharpen your ability to assess the binding force of Islamic obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its legal category
The passage states that purification from najasah is wajib on body and clothes of the one praying. Note that 'najasah' here means real, physical impurity.
The commentator clarifies that 'purification' means removal from the place, not cleansing the substance itself.
2
Distinguish fard and wajib in Hanafi usul
Fard is proven by qat'i evidence (e.g., explicit Quranic text for wudu); wajib is proven by zanni evidence (e.g., hadith, ijma). The passage explicitly contrasts wudu (fard) with najasah purification (wajib).
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school; other schools treat both as fard.
3
Examine the linguistic analogy
The commentator uses the Quranic phrase 'ask the town' (Surah Yusuf 82) to show that 'purification' can mean 'removal' from the place, not cleaning the substance itself. This addresses a potential confusion.
The verse is used as a linguistic figure of speech analogy, not as direct evidence for the ruling.
4
Note the cross-madhhab implication
Other schools (Shafi'i) treat purification from najasah as fard, giving it the same weight as wudu. The Hanafi classification as wajib affects the legal consequence (sin vs. disbelief).
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in differing usul principles.

Core Idea
Impurity under hands or knees during prostration does not invalidate prayer; purification can be done with any pure liquid, not only water.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two key issues of taharah: the ruling on impurity under the limbs during prostration, and the scope of permissible cleansing agents. The Impurity of Semen: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that semen is impure, identify the Shafi'i counter-proof, and apply the Hanafi response using the hadith of the five impurities.

Core Idea
Semen is impure because it is listed with consensus impurities; the command to wipe indicates obligation, not purity.

This lesson presents one of the classic inter-madhhab disputes in taharah: whether semen is pure or impure. The Hanafi school holds that it is najis (impure), while Imam al-Shafi'i considers it tahir (pure). Both schools rely on hadith and analogy, but their interpretations diverge. By studying this dispute, you will sharpen your ability to evaluate conflicting proofs within the Usul al-Fiqh framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
Note the core question: Is semen impure or pure? The Hanafi position is stated first: 'al-mani najis'. The Shafi'i position follows. Understand the practical difference: washing vs. wiping.
The dispute is not about whether semen requires removal, but about its intrinsic legal status.
2
Examine the Shafi'i proofs
The Shafi'i school uses two proofs: (1) the hadith to Ibn 'Abbas comparing semen to mucus and ordering it to be wiped away with a grass, and (2) an analogy: semen is the origin of human creation, so it should be pure like earth.
Both proofs are based on apparent meanings and analogical reasoning.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school cites a hadith listing five things from which a garment is washed: urine, feces, blood, semen, and vomit. Since the other four are unanimously impure, semen is included by association (qarina).
The hadith is reported in Sunan al-Daraqutni and Musnad al-Bazzar. The word ‘min’ (from) indicates the cause of washing.
4
Apply the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i hadith
The Hanafis argue that the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas actually supports their position because the command to remove (imata) indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. An obligation to remove implies that the substance is impure.
The principle ‘al-amr yufīdu al-wujūb’ is key here; no qarina (indication) shifts the command from obligation to recommendation.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For Hanafis, any garment soiled with semen must be washed; wiping alone is insufficient. For Shafi'is, wiping suffices because the substance is not impure. The difference affects daily practice.
The mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school is that semen is impure and requires washing.

Earth is purified by dryness (jafaf) according to the hadith 'The purification of earth is its dryness.'

This passage addresses a practical concern: if impurity dries on earth and its traces disappear, can one pray on that spot? The Hanafi school permits it, relying on a prophetic tradition. However, Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) and al-Shafi'i object, arguing that no active removal occurred. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafis treat earth as a distinct purifying medium and clarifies the limits of the ruling.

Identify the act and ruling
The passage states: if impurity hits earth, dries by the sun, and its trace disappears, then prayer on that place is permissible. This means the earth becomes pure (tahir).
The ruling applies to earth; other surfaces are treated differently.

The Hanafi proof is the hadith: 'The purification of earth is its dryness.' This is a textual evidence (dalil naqli) of ahad transmission.

The term 'dhakāt' (purification) in the hadith indicates that dryness itself is the purifying agent for earth, much like slaughter (dhakāt) purifies an animal. The imperative implies sufficiency.

Zufar and al-Shafi'i argue that no active remover (muzil) is present; thus the impurity remains. They analogize to tayammum, which requires earth that is itself pure (not previously impure).

The earth is purified by dryness alone — a hadith permits what qiyas would reject.

Tahir vs Tahur: Tayammum from Dried Impure Earth
Ruling with proof: tayammum from dried impure earth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between tahir and tahur to determine the validity of tayammum from earth that has dried after being contaminated by najasah.

Core Idea
Earth dried from najasah is tahir, not tahur — cannot be used for tayammum.

The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between tahir (pure) and tahur (purifying). This lesson unpacks a ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: if earth is wetted by a liquid impurity and then dries, it becomes tahir but loses its purifying quality. Tayammum, which substitutes for wudu, requires a tahur medium. Understanding this distinction prevents invalid purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing tayammum from earth that has been wetted by najasah and then dried. The ruling is that it is not permissible (batil).
This applies whether the earth dried by sun or shade.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites two proofs: Quran 5:6 requiring 'صعيداً طيباً' (pure sa'eed) and the hadith 'ذكاة الأرض يبسها' (purification of earth is its dryness).
The Quran establishes a condition of purity (tayyib); the hadith only confirms that drying purifies (tahir), not that it becomes purifying (tahur).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Quran's 'tayyib' is interpreted as tahur (purifying), not just tahir. The hadith's 'yubs' (dryness) only removes the najasah, making the earth tahir. Since tayammum substitutes for wudu, which requires tahur water, the substitute must also be tahur.
Analogous to vinegar which is tahir but not tahur and cannot be used for wudu.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There are two narrations from Abu Hanifa on whether the earth returns to najis upon rewetting. One says it becomes najis again (al-Quduri, al-Sarakhsi); the other says it does not (al-Isbijabi). Al-Mabsut prefers the latter.
Regardless of the dispute, tayammum from such earth is invalid in both views.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the earth does not return to najis upon rewetting, as per al-Mabsut. However, the earth remains non-tahur, so tayammum is still impermissible.
The authority weight is nawadir (rare narrations) for the rewetting dispute.

Heavy vs. Light Impurities: Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Defines heavy vs. light impurity with intra-Hanafi dispute on dung

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's criterion for heavy impurity, apply it to animal dung, and explain the Sahibayn's counter-argument and its practical consequence for prayer.

Core Idea
A heavy impurity is proven by a definitive text with no opposing text; disputed rulings become light.

In the Hanafi school, impurities are not all equal. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira distinguishes heavy (mughallazah) from light (mukhaffafah) impurities based on the strength of textual evidence. This classification directly affects the amount excusable in prayer—a critical practical matter. We will examine the criterion, apply it to the disputed case of animal dung, and clarify the nuance regarding blood.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the two definitions
Abu Hanifa defines heavy impurity as one for which a text (nass) establishes impurity and no text establishes purity. The Sahibayn define light impurity as one where ijtihad is possible regarding its purity—meaning scholars have differed.
The key difference is whether the existence of ikhtilaf lightens the ruling.
2
Apply to animal dung
Abu Hanifa cites the hadith 'إِنَّهُ رِجْسٌ' (it is filth) for dung, with no opposing text, so it is heavy. The Sahibayn argue that since Malik and Ibn Abi Layla consider it pure, the difference of opinion makes it light.
The hadith is the only direct evidence; no text declares dung pure.
3
Identify the practical consequence
Heavy impurity is excused in prayer only if less than a dirham (approx. 3.36g). Light impurity is excused up to one-quarter of the garment. Thus the classification changes how much impurity is tolerated during worship.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf—the fruit of the disagreement.
4
Note the blood distinction
The passage clarifies that 'blood' in the list means spilled blood (al-dam al-masfuḥ). Blood that remains in meat after slaughter is pure—a separate ruling.
This is not a dispute; all Hanafi imams agree on the purity of blood in slaughtered meat.
5
Understand Abu Yusuf's added nuance
Abu Yusuf adds that blood in meat is excused (ma'fuww) in eating but not in clothes or on the body, because avoidance is possible in clothes but not in food.
This is an intra-Hanafi refinement on the scope of excusability.

Core Idea
Horse urine is excused unless excessive—but the reason why reveals the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning.

In this short but rich passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, we encounter a rare intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: both Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf agree on the practical ruling that horse urine does not affect the validity of prayer unless it becomes excessive, yet they disagree on the underlying legal cause (ta'lil). Guided Path
1
State the Agreed Ruling

Exception for Difficult Traces and Washing Heavy with Light Impurity
Explaining the exception for remaining traces that are difficult to remove

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on difficult-to-remove traces and explain the dispute regarding washing a heavy impurity with a light impurity.

Core Idea
Traces requiring extra effort (soap, boiling water) are excused; washing heavy impurity with light does not transfer the ruling.

Purification from impurity generally requires complete removal of the substance and its trace. However, the Hanafi school grants a concession: if removing the trace demands something beyond plain water—like soap, alkali, or heated water—that effort is not obligatory. This lesson unpacks that exception and then tackles a related intra-Hanafi debate: if a heavy impurity (e.g., dog saliva) is washed with a light impurity (e.g., urine of a halal animal), does the ruling of the heavy impurity transfer? The source text presents two views and indicates the chosen position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and the exception
The base rule is that after removing the substance of an impurity, any remaining trace (color, smell) must also be eliminated. The exception is when removing that trace would require something other than plain water—such as soap, wood ash, or boiling water—causing hardship. In such cases, the trace is excused.
Hardship (mashaqqah) is defined as needing a cleaning agent beyond water.
2
Define 'mashaqqah' as per the text
The text explicitly states: 'Interpretation of hardship: that one needs something other than water, like soap, alkali, and water boiled by fire; then it is not obligatory upon him.' So the ruling is that the remaining trace is excused; the item is considered pure.
This is a clear definition of mashaqqah in this context.
3
Understand the sub-issue: washing heavy impurity with light impurity
If a heavy impurity (mughallazah) is washed with a light impurity (mukhaffafah, e.g., urine of a halal animal) and the heavy impurity is visible, some scholars said the heavy impurity's ruling is removed and replaced by the light impurity's ruling. Al-Sayrafi's chosen view is that the heavy impurity's ruling does not transfer; it remains. The Fatawa also state that washing with urine does not purify.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Note the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
According to the source, the relied-upon position (al-mukhtār) is that the heavy impurity's ruling does not transfer, and washing with urine does not purify. Thus, one must still follow the heavy impurity's purification rules.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin via al-Sayrafi.

Purifying Non-Visible Impurities by Predominant Belief
Setting the criterion for purifying non-visible impurities based on predominant belief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi criterion for purifying non-visible impurities and apply it to determine whether mere drying suffices.

Core Idea
For non-visible impurities, wash until predominant belief of purity; drying alone is not purification.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies a critical point in taharah: how do we know when a non-visible impurity has been removed? The answer is not by sight or certainty, but by predominant belief. The author also addresses a common misconception: drying does not purify even if the trace vanishes. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the impurity type
Recognize that the ruling applies to impurities with no visible substance (e.g., dried urine stain with no color or smell).
Visible impurities follow different rules.
2
State the purification method
The passage states: 'its purification is that it is washed until the washer's predominant belief becomes that it has become pure.' The standard is ghalabat al-zann.
3
Understand the rational proof
Why ghalabat al-zann? Because repetition is needed to extract the impurity, and certainty of removal is impossible. Thus, predominant belief is the best achievable standard.
This is an aqli (rational) proof.
4
Address the objection
The passage raises: if the impurity dries and its trace goes away, the substance is gone, yet it is not pure. The answer: the author's phrase 'its purification' implies the need for a purifier (mutahhir), i.e., an act of washing. Mere drying is not a purifier.
The purifier must be applied; absence of trace is irrelevant.
5
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, the mufta bihi is: non-visible impurities require washing with water until the washer has predominant belief of purity. Drying alone is insufficient.

The Ruling on Istinja: Sunnah, Stones, and Water
Ruling with commentary on istinja and its materials

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istinja as a sunnah and apply the conditions under which stone suffices versus water for different types of impurities.

Core Idea
Istinja is a sunnah that removes physical impurity; stone suffices for normal stool/urine, but only water for blood/pus.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the legal status of istinja (cleansing after using the toilet) and the materials that suffice for it. The author first establishes that istinja is a sunnah, not a fard, and then clarifies when dry substances like stones are acceptable versus when water is necessary. The discussion includes a reported opinion that imposes additional conditions, illustrating the Hanafi method of weighing multiple transmissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hukm of istinja
The text states 'والاستنجاء سنة' meaning it is a sunnah. Note that it is not mentioned with other sunan of wudu because it deals with physical impurity (najasa haqiqiyya) rather than ritual impurity (najasa hukmiyya).
This classification impacts whether the act is obligatory or recommended; sunnah means there is reward but no sin for leaving it.
2
Understand the default ruling for dry substances
The text says 'يجزئ فيه الحجر وما قام مقامه' — stone or any similar dry substance (like earth) suffices for normal stool and urine. This is the default ruling.
The phrase 'ما قام مقامه' extends to any clean solid that can remove impurity.
3
Recognize exceptions where water is required
If the exit is pus or blood, only water suffices for istinja. For madhy (pre-ejaculate) and wady (prostatic fluid), stone still suffices like for normal stool.
The distinction is based on the consistency and severity of the impurity.
4
Analyze the reported opinion (وقيل)
Some scholars report that stone only suffices if the stool has not dried and the person has not moved from his place. The reasoning: moving causes the impurity to spread beyond the exit, and drying makes it hard to remove with a solid.
This opinion is presented as 'وقيل' (it is said), indicating it is a weaker transmission.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The default ruling (stone suffices unconditionally for normal cases) is the relied-upon position, as the condition is from a weaker opinion. Water is only required if the impurity has actually spread or if it is blood/pus.
The tarjih authority is the textual presentation: the unqualified statement is given priority over the later qualified one.

Chapter of Impurities

He wipes it until he cleans it.

His statement: (He wipes it until he cleans it). Its form: He sits turning away from the qibla, and from the sun and moon, with three stones. He begins with the first stone from the front of the right side, and rotates it until it returns to the point he started from. Then with the second from the front of the left side, and rotates it similarly. Then he passes the third over both sides. Some said: He wipes forward with the first, wipes backward with the second, and rotates the third. Abu Ja'far said: In winter, he wipes forward with the first, backward with the second, and rotates the third. In summer, he wipes backward with the first, forward with the second, and rotates the third, because his testicles hang in summer and are drawn up in winter. Al-Sarakhsi said: There is no specific method; the aim is cleanliness. The woman does as the man does in winter at all times. It is recommended that the clean stones be on his right, and he places what he used for istinja on his left, and puts the used side downwards.

Commentary
This passage begins with the matn statement: 'He wipes until he cleans it.' The commentator then explains the method: the person sits facing away from the qibla, sun, and moon, with three stones. He starts with the first stone on the right side, wiping from front to back in a circular motion back to the start. Then similarly on the left with the second stone, and finally passes the third over both sides. Some scholars differ: they prescribe wiping forward with the first, backward with the second, and rotating the third. Abu Ja'far further differentiates between winter (when testicles are contracted) and summer (when they hang), altering the order accordingly. However, al-Sarakhsi states that there is no fixed method; the only goal is cleanliness (inqa'). For women, they follow the winter method at all times. It is also recommended to keep clean stones on the right and used ones on the left, with the used side facing down. The overall lesson is that while various methods are reported, the emphasis is on achieving purification rather than adhering to a specific ritual.
