Commentary

Tahara is of two types: real with water and legal with earth; the Quran commands wudu with clear imperatives.

This lesson unpacks the foundational concept of tahara in Hanafi fiqh. We begin with the linguistic and legal definitions, explore the classification into types, and then examine the Quranic proof that establishes the obligation of wudu. Understanding this sets the stage for all purification rulings.

The Implied Condition in the Verse of Wudu and Janabah
Explaining the Quranic phrases for wuḍū' and janābah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the linguistic distinction between 'idhā' and 'in' in the Quranic verse on wuḍū' and janābah and identify the implied condition (ḥadath) that establishes the legal obligation.

Core Idea
The Quran uses 'idhā' for wuḍū' because it is constant; 'in' for janābah shows it is conditional.

This lesson delves into the precise wording of the Quranic verse on purification (Āyat al-Wuḍū'). The author of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah highlights an implied condition in the verse that is crucial for understanding the obligation of wuḍū' and ghusl. By examining the Arabic particles 'idhā' and 'in', we uncover a subtle yet powerful argument from the text itself about the nature of these purifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the implied condition
Read the verse carefully. The author states that the word 'ḥadath' (ritual impurity) is implied. That is, the verse means: 'When you rise to pray while you are in a state of ḥadath.' Recognizing this implicit element is the first step in understanding the legal import.
2
Contrast 'idhā' and 'in'
The Quran uses 'idhā' (when) for wuḍū' and 'in' (if) for janābah. 'Idhā' implies a matter that is certain or frequently occurring; 'in' implies a matter that is possible but not constant. This linguistic choice is deliberate.
3
Understand the rational basis
Rising to prayer is a constant act (it occurs five times daily). Therefore, the need for wuḍū' is constant. Janābah, however, is not constant—it may or may not exist. Hence, 'in' is appropriate. This highlights the legal implication: wuḍū' is regularly required, while ghusl is conditional.
4
Apply to the ruling
From this linguistic analysis, the Hanafi position is derived: wuḍū' is obligatory every time one intends to pray while in a state of ḥadath, whereas ghusl from janābah is obligatory only when one is in that state. This proof is textual (Quran) and rational.

Wiping or Washing the Feet? The Hanafi Refutation of the Rawafid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school insists on washing the feet in wudu, using the Quranic qira'at and the principle of al-mujāwara.

Core Idea
The feet must be washed; the khafd qira'a is due to adjacency, not a command to wipe.

The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a well-known dispute between the Hanafis and the Imami Shia over the meaning of وَأَرْجُلَكُمْ in the wudu verse. While the Shia argue that the feet are to be wiped based on a variant reading, the Hanafis counter with the principle of al-mujāwara, showing that the reading does not change the ruling.

Detailed Rulings on Wiping the Head in Wudu
Expansion of the Quranic command to wipe the head with specific rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mash (wiping) and identify which actions validly fulfill the wiping of the head based on the given scenarios.

Core Idea
Mash is simply touching; wiping below the ear or with used water does not count.

The Quran commands 'And wipe your heads' (5:6), but what exactly constitutes valid wiping? The Hanafi school defines mash as 'isabah' (touching) and provides specific rulings for common cases like long hair, shaved patches, rainwater, and used water. This lesson unpacks these details so you can apply them correctly.

Guided Path
1
Define mash according to the Hanafi school
The passage states that 'al-mash is al-isabah' – wiping means touching. This sets the minimum requirement: a wet hand must come into contact with the head. No rubbing or flow is needed.
This definition distinguishes mash from ghusl (washing).
2
Identify the valid area for wiping long hair
If hair extends below the ear, wiping only the hanging part is invalid because that part is not considered part of the head. The roots above the ear are considered the 'head' for wiping.
The ruling is based on customary understanding: hair below the ear is not part of the head.
3
Apply the ruling to a shaved head
If part of the head is shaved, wiping the remaining hair (above the ear) is sufficient. The bald area does not require wiping.
This shows partial coverage is acceptable.
4
Evaluate cases of rainwater and post-wipe shaving
Rainwater touching the head counts as wiping even without intention. Shaving after wiping does not require repeating the wipe, as the obligation was already fulfilled.
Intention is not required for validity in the case of rainwater.
5
Explain the issue of used water (musta'mal)
Water taken from the beard (already used for wudu) is musta'mal – pure but not purifying. Wiping with it invalidates the wipe. However, moisture that has not been used (e.g., residual water on the beard from rinsing) is acceptable.
Musta'mal water cannot be used for any part of wudu.

Washing the Feet in Wudu: The Hanafi Refutation of the Rafidah
Refutation of the Shia position using linguistic analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument that the feet must be washed (not wiped) in wudu, using the linguistic principle of mujawarah to refute the Shia position.

Core Idea
The genitive reading of 'arjulikum' is due to adjacency, not a command to wipe — the feet must be washed.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses a long-standing dispute between the Hanafi school and the Shia (Rafidah) regarding the obligation in wudu: do we wash the feet or merely wipe them? The Shia cite the variant Quranic reading with genitive case as evidence for wiping, but Hanafi scholars employ a well-known linguistic principle — mujawarah (adjacency) — to show that the genitive does not change the ruling. This lesson explores the grammatical and legal reasoning behind the Hanafi position, demonstrating how variant readings must be interpreted within their grammatical context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two qira'at and their implications
The verse has two readings: 'arjulakum' (accusative) and 'arjulikum' (genitive). The accusative indicates washing (object of 'faghsilu'), while the genitive could imply wiping (as a follower of 'ru'usikum'). The Shia argue from the genitive alone.
2
Understand the Shia argument from the genitive
The Rafidah hold that the genitive reading shows that the feet are to be wiped like the head, since grammatically they appear to be an adverb on 'heads'.
3
Learn the Hanafi response: mujawarah
The Hanafis explain that the genitive is due to adjacency (mujawarah) — a word takes the case of a nearby word without being linked in meaning. Thus the ruling remains washing, as the majority reading and context indicate.
Compare with the parallel example of 'wa hurun 'in' in Surat al-Waqi'ah.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis wash the feet completely; Shia wipe them. This difference affects the validity of wudu and thus prayer. The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi).

This linguistic analysis supports that washing the feet is the intended action, and the genitive reading does not indicate wiping.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of Al-Quduri explaining the obligatory (fard) acts of wudu. The author begins by defining the term 'fard' both linguistically and technically. Linguistically, fard means to cut or determine, as in the Quranic verse where Allah says 'We have ordained it' meaning determined it. In sacred law, fard is a ruling established by decisive evidence (such as the Quran or mutawatir hadith) that is fixed and not subject to increase or decrease; rejecting it constitutes disbelief. The strict definition underscores the absolute necessity of the acts prescribed in wudu. The author then states the fard of wudu: washing the three limbs (face, hands, feet) and wiping the head. He clarifies that 'three limbs' refers to five body parts because each pair (hands and feet) is treated as a single limb for legal purposes, analogous to blood-money rules. The elbows and ankles are included in the washing because the verse says 'to the elbows' and 'to the ankles', and the text explains that the limit here is a 'ghayat isqat' (limit of removal), meaning the purpose is to define the included part and exclude what is beyond; thus the limit itself is part of the wash. A dispute arises with Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) who argues that the limit does not include the limited thing, using the analogy of fasting where the night is the limit but not part of the fast. The author counters that the limits in wudu are different: they are 'ghayat isqat' where the limit is included, whereas in fasting it is 'ghayat wujub' where the limit (night) is excluded. This distinction is based on the general meaning of the verse: 'and your hands to the elbows' indicates the hands include the elbows, and similarly for the feet. The passage thus maintains the Hanafi position that washing must include the elbows and ankles, and this is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the school.

Ruling on Beginning with the Right Side in Wudu
Explaining the virtue of starting with the right side in ablution and its exception for the ears.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on beginning with the right side in wudu according to the Hanafi school and apply the exception when simultaneous wiping is not possible.

Core Idea
Beginning with the right side in wudu is a virtue, not an obligation, based on the Prophet's love for the right side.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on starting wudu with the right hand and foot. The text from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies that it is a recommended virtue (fadilah), not a binding obligation. It also addresses a potential question about wiping the ears, explaining why they are wiped together and when order should be observed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is beginning wudu by washing the right hand before the left and the right foot before the left. The ruling is that it is a virtue (fadilah), meaning recommended (mustahabb), not wajib or fard.
The text explicitly says 'وهو فضيلة' (and it is a virtue).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a hadith: 'The Prophet (ﷺ) loved to begin with the right side in everything, even in putting on his sandals.' This shows the source of the recommendation.
This hadith is reported by al-Bukhari.
3
Analyze how the proof indicates the ruling
The word 'يُحب' (loves) indicates preference, not a command. In Hanafi usul, an act of the Prophet expressed as love or habit yields istihbab (recommendation), not wujub (obligation).
The dakil is ahad and zanni in both transmission and meaning, further confirming a non-obligatory ruling.
4
Apply the ruling to the ears: exception and normal case
Normally, both ears are wiped simultaneously because it is easier, so no order is observed. However, if one cannot wipe both together (e.g., only one hand usable), then the right ear is wiped first, then the left, following the pattern of hands and feet.
This shows the general principle of preferring the right is maintained when simultaneous action is not possible.
5
Understand the consequence of omission
If someone begins with the left hand or foot, their wudu is valid but they miss the virtue. Similarly, if they wipe the left ear first when they could have done both together, they miss the virtue, but if they cannot do both, order is recommended.
No sin or invalidation results from not following this order.
