Rulings on amah's 'awrah, freedom during prayer, and related conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'awrah of an amah and apply the ruling when she is freed during prayer, distinguishing it from the case of a naked person who finds clothes.

Core Idea
An amah's head is not 'awrah; if freed during prayer, she covers and continues—the obligation begins at that moment.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies the Hanafi position on what parts of an amah's body must be covered in prayer. Unlike a free woman, an amah's head is not 'awrah, but the ruling changes if she is freed during the prayer. The author also addresses the limits of movement and the case of a khuntha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the amah's 'awrah
From the passage, the amah's 'awrah is the same as a man's plus the belly and back; everything else, including her head, is not 'awrah. This is because she is property, so her covering requirement is lighter.
This is specific to the Hanafi madhhab; other schools differ.
2
Ruling on prayer with uncovered head
If an amah prays with her head uncovered, her prayer is valid because covering her head is not a condition for validity.
This changes if she is freed during prayer.
3
Effect of being freed during prayer
If she is freed mid-prayer, she must immediately cover her head (take the qina') and continue without breaking the prayer. The obligation to cover only becomes binding at that moment.
Contrast with the naked person who finds clothes: he must restart because the obligation was binding from the start.
4
Walking during prayer
If the amah walks three steps or fewer, her prayer remains valid; more than three steps invalidates it.
This is a general rule for the amah's prayer.
5
Covering after a rukn
If she does not cover her head when required (after freedom) or covers it after having performed a rukn without it, her prayer becomes invalid.
This applies after the obligation to cover is in effect.
6
Ruling on the khuntha
The khuntha (hermaphrodite) is treated as a woman in 'awrah rulings. If a slave, he is like an amah; if free, like a free woman.
Hanafi position is consistent.

Praying with Impurity When Unable to Remove It
Expands a ruling from Mukhtasar al-Quduri on unavoidable impurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a person cannot remove impurity before prayer and identify the two scenarios based on the amount of pure garment, applying the qiyas jaliy proof.

Core Idea
If you cannot remove impurity, pray with it and do not repeat—but wear a garment if at least a quarter is pure.

This lesson examines a concession in the Hanafi school: when a person is unable to remove impurity from his garment or body before prayer, he may still pray and his prayer is valid without repetition. The passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah breaks down the ruling based on how much of the garment remains pure, and provides a qiyas-based proof for the quarter threshold. By understanding these conditions, you will know exactly what to do in such circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
From the matn, the base ruling is that one prays with impurity and does not repeat. Note the concession given to someone who cannot find means to remove impurity.
The prayer is valid but it is a concession, not the norm.
2

The commentary divides cases based on the amount of pure portion: a quarter or more, or less than a quarter. If at least a quarter is pure, one must wear that garment; praying naked invalidates. The quarter threshold is specific to the Hanafi school.

The proof is qiyas jaliy using the legal principle that a quarter stands in place of the whole. Just as a quarter of a garment is enough for covering the 'awrah, a quarter pure is enough to make the garment suitable for prayer despite impurity on the rest.
This is analogy (qiyas), not direct text from Quran or Hadith.
4
Identify the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
When the pure part is less than a quarter, Muhammad requires wearing it; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf may permit praying naked (their view is not stated here). The text only gives Muhammad's view, showing there is variation within the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for praying with najasah when unable to remove it and distinguish between the positions of Imam Muhammad and the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) regarding the minimum pure portion required in the garment.

Core Idea
When unable to remove najasah, pray with it; but the Sahibayn grant a choice if pure portion is less than a quarter, while Imam Muhammad requires covering.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a common practical scenario: a person has najasah on his garment or body and cannot find water or a pure cloth to remove it. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that the prayer is valid and need not be repeated. However, within the school, there is a nuanced dispute between Imam Muhammad and the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) on whether one may pray naked when the pure portion of the garment is extremely small. This lesson unpacks that ikhtilaf and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling and its condition
The passage begins with the general rule: whoever cannot find anything to remove najasah prays with it and does not repeat the prayer. Understand that this ruling is based on necessity (darurah) and that the prayer is valid as long as some covering is possible.
The condition for this ruling is that the person is truly unable to remove the impurity – no water or pure cloth is available.

Distinguish the two scenarios based on the amount of pure cloth
The text presents two scenarios: (1) if a quarter or more of the garment is pure, the person must pray in it and cannot pray naked; (2) if the pure portion is less than a quarter, a difference of opinion arises.
A quarter of the garment is considered equivalent to the whole for covering the 'awrah in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Muhammad holds that even if the pure portion is less than a quarter, one must pray in the impure garment – praying naked is not permissible. The Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) give a choice between praying naked or in the garment, but state that praying in it is better.
The rationale: Muhammad prioritizes covering the 'awrah absolutely; the Sahibayn consider that a very small pure portion does not truly cover, so impurity avoidance takes precedence.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its implication
The text indicates that the known view (al-ma'ruf) is that of Muhammad, meaning it is the relied-upon position for fatwa. Therefore, in practice, if the pure portion is less than a quarter, the person must pray in the impure garment.
This is an example of tarjih within the Hanafi school based on later scholarly preference.

The Fara'id of Prayer: Tahrimah and Qiyam

Core Idea
The tahrimah is the door to prayer: some Hanafis call it a condition, others a fard — and this changes what happens when prayer invalidates.

Understanding this difference is crucial for knowing the fate of a prayer that becomes invalid and the validity of prayers begun before their proper time.
