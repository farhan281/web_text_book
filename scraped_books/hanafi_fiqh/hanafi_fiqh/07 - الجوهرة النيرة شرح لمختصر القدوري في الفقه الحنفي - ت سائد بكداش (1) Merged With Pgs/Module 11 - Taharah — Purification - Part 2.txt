Definition of Nullifiers of Wudu: Exit from the Two Passages
Commentary defining the first nullifier of wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of nullifiers of wudu according to the Hanafi school and identify which types of exits nullify wudu based on the generality of 'kull'.

Core Idea
Everything that exits from the front or rear private parts nullifies wudu, agreed upon by all scholars.

After discussing the obligations, sunan, and mustahabbat of wudu, the commentator now turns to its nullifiers. He begins with the foundational ruling that any exit from the two passages breaks wudu. This lesson explores the meaning of 'naqd', the agreement on this nullifier, and the extensive scope of 'kull' that covers all possible exits.

Guided Path
1
Define 'naqd' in this context
The commentator explains that 'naqd' for abstract states means removing the person from the desired state. Here, wudu's nullification means losing the ability to perform acts requiring purity (prayer, touching Qur'an).
This distinguishes physical invalidation (breaking objects) from legal invalidation (removing a state).
2
Identify the agreed-upon nullifier
The author starts with the exit from the two passages because it is unanimously agreed upon among scholars (muttafaq 'alayh). This establishes the core ruling before discussing disputed nullifiers like blood or vomit.
The commentator explicitly states this order: 'يبدأ بالمتفق عليه، ثم بالمختلف فيه'.
3
Analyze the word 'kull'
The word 'kull' (كل) indicates generality of individuals. It encompasses all types of exit, whether usual (urine, stool) or unusual (blood, worms, pebbles, madhiy, etc.).
In Hanafi usul, 'kull' is a tool of 'umum' that is binding unless specified otherwise.
4
Apply to examples
Apply the ruling to specific cases: istihadah blood, madhiy, worms, pebbles – all nullify wudu because they exit from the two passages, regardless of quantity or nature.
This counters the misconception that only urine and stool nullify wudu.

The Exception of the Ears in Starting with the Right during Wudu
Stating a sunan ruling on order of washing for ears and cheeks

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which limbs in wudu are exceptions to the sunnah of starting with the right, and explain the basis for the analogy regarding the cheeks.

Core Idea
The ears are the only limb where starting with the right is not recommended.

In wudu, it is generally recommended (mustahab) to start washing with the right side for limbs like hands and feet. However, the ears are an exception: there is no preference for right over left. Some Hanafi scholars extend this exception to the cheeks, but the relied-upon position maintains the general recommendation for cheeks. This lesson clarifies the rule and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the general sunnah
Start by acknowledging that for most limbs washed in wudu (hands, feet, etc.), it is mustahab to begin with the right. This is a well-established sunan practice.
2
Identify the exception
From the passage, note that the ears are explicitly mentioned as the only limbs among the limbs of purification for which it is NOT recommended to start with the right.
The Arabic phrase: 'ليس في أعضاء الطهارة عضوان لا يُستحب تقديم الأيمن منهما إلا الأذنين'
3
Understand the basis
The reasoning is that the ears are washed simultaneously by inserting wet fingers, so there is no sequential order. Hence, no preference for right over left.
4
Consider the analogy to cheeks
Some Hanafi scholars analogize the cheeks to the ears, arguing that cheeks are also washed all at once (as part of the face). However, this analogy is not universally accepted.
The mufta bihi position is that cheeks follow the general rule (mustahab to start with right).
5
Apply the ruling in practice
When performing wudu, you may wash the ears in any order or simultaneously. For cheeks, it remains mustahab to start with the right cheek.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Everything from the two passages nullifies wudu except wind from the front – a dispute resolved by the correct view.

The commentary now turns to the nullifiers of wudu. The author first defines 'naqd' and then states the agreed-upon rule: anything exiting from the two passages (front and back) invalidates wudu. However, there is a key exception regarding wind from the front, and a special case for a woman with merged passages. The passage also presents a consensus on the worm as a nullifier.

Guided Path

Identify the general rule and its scope

Blood and Pus as Nullifiers of Wudu: The Flowing Condition
Explaining the Hanafi condition of flowing for blood and pus from the body

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for blood and pus nullifying wudu, distinguish it from the positions of Zufar and al-Shafi'i, and apply it to a concrete scenario.

Core Idea
Blood and pus nullify wudu only if they flow – a middle ground between always and never.

This lesson explains a key nullifier of wudu: blood, pus, and similar fluids exiting from the body (not the two passages). The Hanafi school introduces a condition of flowing (tajawuz) that sets it apart from the views of Zufar and al-Shafi'i. Understanding this condition is essential for correctly applying purity rulings in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core Hanafi ruling
Read the source text and locate the Hanafi position: blood and pus from the body nullify wudu only if they flow beyond the exit point (tajawuz). Note that this condition distinguishes body exits from the two passages, where any amount nullifies.
The phrase 'qayyada bil-badan' indicates the restriction to the body.
2
Understand the definition of sadid
Sadid is the thin, yellowish fluid from a wound before it thickens into pus. The text explicitly includes it in the ruling, so it is treated like blood and pus.
Sadid is sometimes confused with blood but is a distinct substance.
3
Contrast with Zufar's opinion
Zufar holds that blood and pus nullify wudu even without flowing. He analogizes to the two passages. The Hanafi response distinguishes the body from the passages, which are the primary source of hadath.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Contrast with al-Shafi'i's opinion
Al-Shafi'i holds that blood and pus never nullify wudu, even if they flow. He argues that only hadath from the passages nullifies. The Hanafi school responds by treating flowing blood and pus as substantial impurities that break purity.
This is an inter-madhhab dispute.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete case
Imagine a person with a small cut that produces a drop of blood that does not flow. According to the Hanafi school, wudu remains valid. If the blood flows, wudu is invalidated. Compare with Zufar and al-Shafi'i.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf – the practical difference.

Blood invalidates wudu only if it flows beyond the wound—mere exit is insufficient in the Hanafi school.

The Hanafi Ruling on Blood and Pus: When Does It Nullify Wudu?
Details conditions for blood/pus nullifying wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for blood/pus to nullify wudu (tajawuz) and apply it to common scenarios.

Core Idea
Blood or pus nullifies wudu only if it flows beyond the wound opening and does so naturally.

Many believers assume that any blood exiting the body breaks wudu, but the Hanafi school requires a specific condition: the blood must flow (tajawuz) beyond the wound opening. This lesson unpacks the precise ruling, including exceptions for medical treatment, and clarifies common scenarios based on the classical text of al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core condition for nullification
The passage emphasizes that blood/pus must 'exceed' (tajawuz) the wound opening by descending (inhidar). Mere swelling or rising does not nullify.
This is the key distinction from other schools that consider any exit as nullifying.
2
Distinguish natural exit from treatment
The matn qualifies 'khuruj' (exit) to exclude cases where the substance exits due to medical treatment (mu'alajah). This is the choice of al-Hidayah.
Al-Sarakhsi disagreed, but the relied-upon fatwa follows al-Hidayah.

Apply the condition to specific examples
The passage gives multiple cases: nosebleed, toothpick, bandage, etc. For each, determine if tajawuz occurred based on whether the substance flowed or was prevented.
For mixed cases (e.g., blood in spittle), the ruling depends on which dominates.

Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on swelling
Imam Muhammad held that swelling beyond the wound nullifies, but the author declares the first opinion (Abu Hanifa's) as correct—swelling alone does not nullify.
The preferred view is that only descent (inhidar) counts as tajawuz.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If blood would have flowed beyond the wound without intervention, wudu breaks even if you wipe or apply ash.

Imagine you have a bleeding wound, and you constantly wipe the blood away to prevent it from dripping. Does your wudu remain valid? The Hanafi school rules that if the blood would have flowed down had you left it alone, your wudu is nullified regardless of your intervention. This lesson unpacks that ruling and the critical condition of potential flow.

Identify the Act
The passage addresses two actions: applying dust/ash to a bleeding wound and wiping blood away (e.g., with cotton). Both are attempts to stop blood from flowing.

Identify the Key Condition
The ruling hinges on whether the blood would have exceeded the wound's opening (i.e., flowed beyond) if the person had not intervened. This potential flow is the decisive factor.
Actual flow is irrelevant; potential flow is what matters.

If the potential to exceed exists, then wudu is nullified (naqada) despite the intervention. If the blood is insufficient to flow even without intervention, wudu remains valid.
Nullification occurs because the cause (blood flow) is present in potential.

Consider a deep cut where blood would drip down the arm if uncared for. Wiping it repeatedly does not save wudu. Conversely, a scratch that only oozes a tiny drop that would not flow nullifies only if that drop would have flowed.
The principle is about the blood's quantity and tendency to flow.

Sleep is not a hadath; it is a legal nullifier because it presumes the likely escape of wind.

The Shari'ah classes certain actions as nullifiers of wudu (nawāqiḍ). Some are true nullifiers, like the passage of wind, while others are legal nullifiers (naqiḍ ḥukmī) – situations that make nullification legally effective even if the actual impurity was not perceived. Rulings on Water Reaching the Body in Ghusl: Immersion, Foreskin, Barriers
Rulings on validity of ghusl with immersion, foreskin, nails, and barriers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which substances on the body act as barriers (mawani') requiring removal for valid ghusl, and state the Hanafi ruling on the foreskin of an uncircumcised man.

Core Idea
In ghusl, water must reach every external part; natural attachments are not barriers, but artificial barriers must be removed.

Ghusl is invalid if any part of the body remains dry. Not all substances prevent water from reaching the skin; the text distinguishes between natural features like the foreskin, ordinary dirt, and substance that create a solid barrier. Understanding this distinction ensures your ghusl is valid and your subsequent acts of worship are accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle

Immersion in sea, large pond, or flowing water with one plunge and water reaching all body, plus mouth and nose rinsing, is sufficient. Similarly, rain can suffice.
No rubbing required.

If an uncircumcised man performs ghusl and water does not reach the skin under the foreskin, his ghusl is still valid because the foreskin is a natural part of his creation.

The key is whether the substance allows water to pass through or forms a waterproof layer.

Any substance that forms a solid barrier—like fish scales, chewed bread matted on the body, or dried henna (khidab mutajassid)—must be removed for ghusl to be valid.
The ruling applies equally to men and women.

Shurut vs Asbab: What Really Obligates Ghusl?
Commentary on conditions for ghusl and correction of matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a shart (condition) and a sabab (cause) for the obligation of ghusl and explain the Hanafi view on what triggers janabah.

Core Idea
Emission of semen with desire causes janabah, not ghusl; ghusl is only obliged by the intention to pray.

The matn of al-Quduri lists the 'meanings that obligate ghusl,' but the commentator corrects this: these are conditions for janabah, not causes for ghusl. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to Hanafi taharah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's claim
The matn states: 'The meanings that obligate ghusl: emission of semen with force and desire from man and woman.' Note the use of 'obligate ghusl.'
This is the base claim being corrected.
2
Recognize the commentator's correction
The commentator immediately refutes: 'These meanings obligate janabah, not ghusl, according to the correct view; because they nullify it, so how could they obligate it?'
He points out a logical inconsistency: janabah nullifies purity, but ghusl restores it; the same act cannot do both.
3
Define shart vs sabab
A shart (condition) is something that must exist for a ruling to apply but does not directly cause it. A sabab (cause) is the direct trigger for the obligation. Here, emission is a condition for janabah, while the cause for ghusl is the intention to pray or do an act forbidden in janabah.
For example, being in janabah is a condition for requiring ghusl before prayer, but the prayer intention is the cause.
4
Apply the distinction to this case
The emission of semen with desire causes janabah (a state of impurity). This state then becomes a condition for the obligation of ghusl when one intends to perform an act that requires purity, such as prayer.
Thus, the matn's wording is technically imprecise; the commentator refines it.
5
Derive the consequence
If a person is in janabah but does not intend to pray or do any act that requires purity, ghusl is not immediately wajib. However, the state of janabah itself is a condition that will trigger wajib when intention arises.
This is the correct Hanafi position.

Cause vs. Condition for Ghusl
Clarifying that the Shaykh's listed causes are actually conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal cause (sabab) and condition (shart) for ghusl in the Hanafi school, and identify the specific cause stated in the passage.

Core Idea
The cause of ghusl is intention to worship; the physical events are only conditions.

In Hanafi fiqh, a clear distinction is made between the cause (sabab) that directly triggers a legal obligation and the condition (shart) that enables it. This lesson examines a short but crucial passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah that clarifies this distinction for ghusl. Understanding this difference prevents confusion about when purification becomes obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being discussed? It is ghusl (ritual bath). In the Hanafi school, ghusl is obligatory (wajib) for a person in a state of janabah (major impurity).
The ruling level is wajib, not fard, because its evidence is from ahad hadith.
2
Locate the cause (sabab)
The passage explicitly states: 'سبب وجوب الغسل: إرادة الصلاة، أو إرادة ما لا يَحِلُّ فعله مع الجنابة.' The cause is the intention to pray or to perform any act that is forbidden in janabah (e.g., reciting Quran, staying in a mosque). This intention makes ghusl obligatory.
Intention is a mental act; it is the direct trigger.
3
Distinguish the conditions (shurut)
The 'things mentioned by the Shaykh' (likely sexual intercourse, emission of semen, etc.) are conditions, not causes. They are prerequisites that must exist for the intention to become relevant, but they do not themselves obligate ghusl without intention.
For example, if intercourse occurs but one does not intend to pray and simply remains in janabah, ghusl is not yet obligatory—though it becomes recommended to purify.
4
Apply the distinction
The practical consequence: ghusl becomes wajib when you intend to perform an act that is prohibited during janabah, provided you are aware of the state. The physical events only make the state of janabah certain, but the obligation is tied to intention.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that acts of worship require intention (niyyah).

The Legal Criterion for Ghusl due to Intercourse
Commentary on the definition of ghusl criterion from the matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi criterion for ghusl becoming wajib due to intercourse and explain why the matn's wording 'meeting of circumcised parts' is imprecise.

Core Idea
Ghusl is wajib upon disappearance of the glans, not merely the meeting of circumcised parts.

The matn of al-Quduri uses the phrase 'meeting of the two circumcised parts' to describe the trigger for ghusl without ejaculation. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira unpacks that phrase, revealing that the actual legal criterion is the concealment of the glans. The commentator critiques the matn's wording for being incomplete and offers a more precise alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The matn defines 'meeting of circumcised parts' as the legal trigger for ghusl. Note that this refers to the alignment and insertion of the entire glans.
The term is taken literally from the matn, but the commentator will critique it.
2
Understand the commentator's critique
The commentator points out that the phrase 'meeting of circumcised parts' is inadequate: it fails to cover anal intercourse (where there is no female circumcised part) and cases where the glans is amputated.
This is an example of a commentator refining the wording of a matn for greater precision and generality.
3

The correct criterion is 'disappearance of the glans' (ghaybubat al-hashafah). This applies to both vaginal and anal penetration, and if the glans is amputated, the equivalent length from the remaining shaft triggers ghusl.
This criterion is broader and more accurate, as it focuses on the actual physical event that necessitates ghusl.
4

Consider anal intercourse: only the male is circumcised, so the original phrase fails; the new criterion covers it. Consider an amputated glans: insertion of the equivalent length is sufficient.
These applications show why the commentator prefers the wording of Kanz al-Daqa'iq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two intra-Hanafi positions on when ghusl becomes obligatory after hayd/nifas and explain the practical consequence (thamarah) of each.

Core Idea
Ghusl after menstruation is wajib; the chosen view allows delay until the next prayer time, not immediately.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses two related disputes among Hanafi scholars. The first dispute is about when ghusl becomes obligatory after menstruation or postpartum bleeding stops: immediately or only when the next required prayer time enters? The second dispute, between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, concerns a woman who was in janaba and then menstruated: does her subsequent ghusl remove both impurities or only one? These disputes have real-world consequences for sin and oath-breaking, and the text explicitly chooses one view as al-mukhtar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main act and its ruling
The act is ghusl (ritual bath) after the cessation of menstrual (hayd) or postpartum (nifas) bleeding. The ruling is that ghusl is wajib (obligatory) after the bleeding stops, but there is disagreement about the precise moment of obligation.
While bleeding continues, ghusl is not obligatory because it would serve no purpose.

According to al-Karkhi and the Iraqi scholars, ghusl becomes wajib immediately upon cessation of bleeding (inqita'). If her bleeding stops after sunrise, and she delays ghusl until zuhr, according to al-Karkhi she sins (because she delayed the wajib), but according to al-Bukhari she does not sin (because ghusl was not yet obligatory).
This illustrates that the difference is not merely theoretical but affects the ruling of sin.
4

If a woman was in janaba (major impurity), then menstruated, and after menstruation performs ghusl: Abu Yusuf says the ghusl addresses only the janaba (the first impurity), while Muhammad says it addresses both janaba and menstruation simultaneously.
This is a dispute about the intention and scope of a single ghusl.

If she had sworn not to perform ghusl from that janaba, then after menstruation she performs ghusl: according to Abu Yusuf, she breaks her oath because she intended to avoid ghusl for janaba; according to Muhammad, she does not break her oath because the ghusl was for both impurities. The consensus case shows that the oath specifically refers to janaba, not menstruation.
