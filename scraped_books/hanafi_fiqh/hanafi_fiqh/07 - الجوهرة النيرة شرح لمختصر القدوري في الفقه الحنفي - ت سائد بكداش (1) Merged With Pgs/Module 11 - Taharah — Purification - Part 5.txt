Equality of Tayammum for Janabah and Hadath, and Permissible Materials
Two intra-Hanafi rulings on tayammum: intention and materials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the intention required for tayammum and identify the permissible materials for tayammum according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Core Idea
In tayammum, a general intention to purify suffices; the earth-like substance—not only pure soil—is valid.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah addresses two important issues in tayammum: the necessity of specifying the type of impurity in one's intention, and the range of acceptable materials. You will learn the relied-upon Hanafi positions that simplify the practice.

Guided Path
1
State the two issues
The passage covers two distinct questions: (a) does tayammum require a specific intention for janabah vs. hadath? (b) what substances are valid for tayammum?
2
Identify the differing Hanafi opinions
For intention: Abu Bakr al-Razi requires niyyat al-tamyeez; the correct view (الصحيح) does not. For materials: Abu Yusuf restricts to pure soil; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad permit anything from the genus of earth.
3
Analyze the definition of earth's genus
The passage defines jins al-ard as substances that do not hold shape when pressed, do not soften, and do not become ash when burned.
4
Derive the mufta bihi
The relied-upon positions: general intention suffices; any earth-like substance is valid.
5
Apply to practice
A person needing tayammum can intend simply to purify or to pray; they can use dust, sand, or pebbles, not only pure soil.

Commentary
This passage covers two distinct but related rulings. First, tayammum for major impurity (janabah) and minor impurity (hadath) are identical in both the physical act and the intention. There is a disagreement: Abu Bakr al-Razi (a Hanafi scholar) required a specific intention distinguishing between the two types of impurity. However, the correct Hanafi position (as the text states) is that a general intention of purification or allowing prayer is sufficient; specification is not needed. This also applies to tayammum performed for menstruation or postnatal bleeding. Second, the passage discusses the type of substance on which tayammum is valid. According to Imams Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, tayammum is permissible with anything that falls under the genus of earth (jins al-ard). The text defines this genus as substances that when shaped do not hold shape and do not soften, and when burned do not become ash (i.e., they are not clay or gypsum, but rather dust, sand, pebbles, etc.). This implies a disagreement with Abu Yusuf, who reportedly limited tayammum to pure soil (turab).

Key Terms
نية التمييز (niyyat al-tamyeez): An intention to distinguish between janabah and hadath when performing tayammum.
جنس الأرض (jins al-ard): The genus of earth; defined as substances that when pressed do not retain shape, do not soften, and when burned do not become ash.

Permissible Substances for Tayammum: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Explains substances valid for tayammum and intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define jins al-ard according to the Hanafi school, identify the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on permissible substances for tayammum, and apply these positions to new cases such as smooth stone, wall, moist ground, and salt.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school allows tayammum with any earth-genus substance, but Abu Yusuf restricts to soil and sand.

In the chapter of tayammum, the jurists discuss which substances may be used for dry ablution. While the Qur'an grants permission with 'clean earth' (sa'id tayyib), the exact scope of 'earth' is debated. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition of jins al-ard (genus of earth) and the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf between the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) and Abu Yusuf. It also covers edge cases like smooth rocks, walls, moist ground, and different types of salt, showing how the principle is applied in practice.

Guided Path
1
Define the key term
Identify the Hanafi criterion for 'jins al-ard': a substance that when pressed does not become soft (i.e., does not take an impression) and when burned does not turn to ash. This definition excludes things like gold, wood, or cotton.
The underlying basis is the Qur'anic 'sa'id tayyib' interpreted broadly.
2
State the majority position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that any substance meeting the criterion is permissible. Examples given: soil, sand, stone, gypsum, quicklime, antimony, orpiment, pebbles, crushed brick/pottery (if from pure clay).
The term 'turab' (soil) is mentioned first because it is agreed upon by all.
3
State the minority position
Abu Yusuf restricts valid substances to soil and sand only. For sand, he has two narrations; the more correct one in the madhhab is non-permissibility. Note that this ikhtilaf exists only when soil is available; if soil is absent, Abu Yusuf agrees with the majority.
4
Analyze mixed substances
If a substance is mixed with something not from the genus of earth, and the non-earth element is predominant, tayammum with it is invalid. If the earth-element predominates, it remains valid.
This follows the principle of majority (ghalaba) in mixtures.
5
Apply to edge cases
Consider smooth stone (no dust), a wall, moist ground, and salt. According to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, smooth stone, wall, and moist ground are valid. Muhammad has two narrations on some of these. For salt: sea salt (māʼī) is invalid because it dissolves and is not from jins al-ard; mountain salt (jabalī) is valid per some, but Shams al-A'imma preferred non-permissibility.
These rulings show the operation of the definition in real scenarios.

It begins by stating that tayammum from major impurity (janabah) and minor impurity (hadath) is the same in terms of action and intention. This passage systematically presents the Hanafi ikhtilaf on this issue, with the majority position being the broader permissibility.

Permissible Substances for Tayammum and Abu Yusuf's Restriction
Intra-Hanafi dispute on valid tayammum substances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the substances valid for tayammum according to Abu Hanifa, apply the mixture condition, and compare Abu Yusuf's view to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Tayammum is valid with any earth-based substance; Abu Yusuf restricts to dust and sand only when dust exists.

Tayammum is a dry purification method when water is unavailable. The Hanafi school permits a wide range of earthen substances, but there is an intra-madhhab difference with Abu Yusuf. This lesson examines the ruling and the disagreement, focusing on the condition that the substance must be from the earth's genus and not predominantly mixed with non-earth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general Hanafi ruling
From the passage, list the substances mentioned as permissible: dust, sand, stone, gypsum, quicklime, antimony, arsenic, pebbles, crushed brick, crushed pottery.
Dust is agreed upon; others are by analogy.
2
Note the condition for validity
The substance must be from the earth's genus and not mixed predominantly with non-earth materials. If mixed and the non-earth part is more, tayammum is invalid.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf restricts tayammum to dust and sand. For sand, there are two narrations; the preferred one forbids sand when dust is present. But if dust is absent, he agrees with the majority that sand is permissible.
4
Compare the two views
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa allows stone, gypsum, etc., while Abu Yusuf only allows dust (and sand in absence of dust). The disagreement only matters when dust is present.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is Abu Hanifa's view, allowing a wide range of earthen substances. This gives greater ease to the people.

Tayammum on Smooth Stone, Moist Ground, and Salt
Rulings on tayammum with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for tayammum on smooth stone, moist ground, wall, and salt, and explain the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf along with the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Tayammum on any earth-based surface, even smooth, is valid; salt is valid only if rock salt, and later scholars rule it impermissible.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses three specific cases of tayammum: smooth stone, wall, moist ground, and salt. While Abu Hanifa and Zufar permit all three earth-based surfaces, Muhammad has two narrations on the first set. For salt, the school distinguishes between sea salt (invalid) and rock salt (valid), though Shams al-A'immah later preferred total impermissibility. Understanding these nuances is essential for correct fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and initial ruling
Read the passage to determine what acts are being ruled upon: tayammum on a smooth stone without dust, on a wall, on moist ground, and with salt. Note the basic ruling: it suffices (ajza) according to Abu Hanifa and Zufar for the first three, and for salt it is differentiated by type.
2
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Muhammad has two narrations on the first three cases. Understand that this is a dispute among the three major Hanafi imams. Note that the source does not detail the reasons, but the disagreement likely hinges on whether dust transfer is essential or whether mere earth contact suffices.
The two narrations from Muhammad may be found in other sources.
3
Differentiate between sea salt and rock salt
The passage explicitly declares sea salt (al-milh al-māʼī) invalid and rock salt (jabalī) valid. Grasp the rationale: sea salt is not considered from the earth's genus (it dissolves in water), while rock salt is a mineral from the earth. Then note Shams al-A'immah's objection, who considers all salt impermissible.
Shams al-A'immah's view represents a later tarjih.
4
Determine the mufta bīhī
For the first three cases, the relied-upon fatwa is that of Abu Hanifa and Zufar (validity). For salt, the majority of later scholars, following Shams al-A'immah, adopt impermissibility for all types. This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.
The passage cites al-Khujandi and al-Fatawa as sources for the initial differentiation, but Shams al-A'immah's opinion overrides in later practice.

Tayammum with Wet Mud: Hanafi Ruling and Dispute
Ruling on tayammum when only wet mud is available

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tayammum with wet mud and identify the relied-upon position among the differing views.

Core Idea
Wet mud is a valid material for tayammum according to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, even if it does not stick to the hands.

When water and dry earth are both unavailable, a person may be faced with only wet mud. The Hanafi school addresses this scenario with a clear ruling, though early scholars differed on the method. This lesson unpacks the passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, explaining the recommended precaution and the authoritative position that wet mud is directly permissible for tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the ruling applies when only wet mud (al-ṭīn) is available and no water or dry dust is found. This is the condition triggering the discussion.
2
Examine the recommended method
Note the first solution: smear the mud on the edge of a garment or similar object to dry it, then use the dried earth for tayammum. This is a cautious approach.
3
Locate the scholarly dispute
If drying is impossible, al-Khujandi advises waiting for water or dry earth. But al-Karhīī rules that wet mud is directly permissible. Identify the two opposing positions.
4
Identify the correct (ṣaḥīḥ) view
The commentator states that the correct view, according to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, is the permissibility of tayammum with wet mud even without drying. This is the muftā bihī (operative) position.
The reliance on Abu Hanifa and Zufar indicates that this view is from the ṣāḥibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) may have differed; but the text affirms it as saḥīḥ.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, if a person has only wet mud, they may perform tayammum directly with it. The mud does not need to stick to the hands; it is sufficient to dust the hands with the mud, even if wet.
This is a concession (rukhṣa) for the one in need.

Mixing Impermissible Substances with Dust for Tayammum
Ruling when impermissible substances like flour or ash are mixed with dust

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid tayammum when impermissible substances are mixed with dust and apply the majority rule to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
Tayammum is valid with mixed dust only if the dust is the majority.

In this passage, we encounter a practical rule for tayammum when the dust medium is contaminated with non-earth substances like flour or ash. The key is proportion: the permissible element (dust) must predominate. This simple yet precise criterion ensures that tayammum substitutes for wudu only when the essential element is dominant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of the mixture
Recognize which component is permissible (dust from earth) and which is impermissible (e.g., flour, ash).
The impermissible substances are those not from the earth's genus.
2
Determine which component is more abundant
Compare the quantity of dust to the impermissible substance by weight or volume.
The majority is determined by the prevalent component.
3
Apply the majority rule
If dust is more than half the mixture, tayammum is permissible; if less, it is not.
If dust is exactly half, it is not majority, so tayammum is invalid.

Core Idea
Without water or earth, purification is impossible — Abu Hanifa says no prayer; Abu Yusuf says pray and repeat later.

This lesson examines an extreme case: a prisoner who has absolutely no access to water or pure earth. The obligation of prayer is clear, but purification is a condition. Can prayer be valid without it? Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf differ sharply, and their disagreement reveals how the Hanafi school balances proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
State the exact conditions: the prisoner finds neither water nor pure earth (tayammum also impossible).
This is the extreme end of the taharah spectrum.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa says: no prayer. His proof is the hadith 'لا صلاة إلا بطهور' – the negation defeats the prayer without purification.
He takes the hadith as absolute: no purification, no prayer.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position and his reasoning
Abu Yusuf says: pray anyway, then repeat when purification is possible. He argues the obligation of prayer remains even when a condition is absent; the act is not valid but must be performed to fulfill the duty.
He prioritises the fard obligation over the condition when the condition is impossible.

If water is absent but pure earth exists: all three (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) agree on tayammum and prayer. Zufar opposes and holds that tayammum alone is insufficient.
This shows a separate khilaf on the validity of tayammum in prison.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa leaves the prisoner without prayer; Abu Yusuf imposes two prayers (one now, one later).
The source text does not resolve the issue, but Abu Hanifa's view is generally stronger in the school.

Learning Objective

Wet clay is a valid medium for tayammum according to Abu Hanifa and Zufar—necessity overrides preference for dryness.

In this passage, we encounter an intra-Hanafi disagreement about the validity of tayammum when only wet clay (tin) is available. While some Hanafi scholars, like al-Khujandi, require the clay to be dried first, the relied-upon position permits direct use. The practical difference: if only wet clay is available, one view delays prayer indefinitely, the other allows immediate tayammum and prayer. The mufta bihi is permissibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tayammum when soil is mixed with non-permissible substances and apply the majority criterion to determine validity.

Core Idea
Tayammum is invalid if non-earth substances outweigh the soil in the mixture.

You may encounter situations where the available soil is mixed with flour, ash, or other substances. The Hanafi school provides a clear guideline based on the proportion of pure earth. This lesson explains the ruling and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its validity condition
The act under consideration is tayammum (dry ablution) using a mixture of soil (turab) and other substances not valid for tayammum, like flour or ash. The ruling depends on which component is predominant.
The source text explicitly mentions flour and ash as examples of non-permissible substances.
2
Apply the majority criterion
If the soil is the majority (akthar) of the mixture, then tayammum is permissible (ja'iz). If the soil is the minority (aqall), then tayammum is not permissible.
Majority is determined by quantity, not quality. Equal parts are treated as minority (invalid).
3
Understand the rationale
The mixture must be predominantly earth to be considered valid sa'id (pure earth). Non-permissible substances alter the medium so that it no longer qualifies.
This ruling applies to all Hanafi imams as per the text.
4
Determine the ruling for borderline cases
If the soil and other substances are equal in quantity, the ruling follows the minority case (invalid) because the majority condition is not met.
The text does not explicitly mention equal proportions, but by inference from the rule.

A minority Hanafi view from Zufar argues for consistency between the two, but the majority Hanafi response relies on both linguistic definition and the inherent nature of the purification mediums.

This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on language.

Water (maa') is inherently purifying (mutahhir) — it cleanses by its nature, so no intention is needed to make it purifying. Earth (turab) is inherently soiling (mulawwith) — it only becomes purifying through the intention of the user. Hence, intention is essential in tayammum.
This distinguishes the two acts on the basis of the element used.

Based on the linguistic and rational proofs, the Hanafi school holds that intention is obligatory in tayammum but not in wudu. Zufar's analogy (qiyas) of substitution is overridden by the direct evidence from language and the nature of the purification media.

Core Idea
A disbeliever's tayammum for acts requiring taharah is invalid if he converts, because he lacked valid intention; wudu is valid regardless.

Nullifiers of Tayammum: General and Specific
Expansion on general and specific nullifiers of tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two categories of nullifiers of tayammum and apply the condition of ability to see water to determine if tayammum is nullified.

Core Idea
Seeing water nullifies tayammum only if it is sufficient and accessible – otherwise, tayammum remains valid.

Tayammum, as a substitute for wudu, is nullified by anything that nullifies wudu. However, it has an additional specific nullifier: seeing water when one is able to use it. This lesson unpacks the ruling from Al-Quduri's matn, clarifying the conditions and the metaphorical attribution of nullification to seeing water. Understanding these nuances ensures correct practice in states of water absence or restriction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of nullifiers
The passage states that tayammum is nullified by everything that nullifies wudu (general category) and additionally by seeing water when able (specific category). Recognize that the general category includes all hadath and other nullifiers of wudu.
General nullifiers: breaking wind, urination, etc. Specific nullifier: seeing sufficient water.
2
Understand the metaphorical attribution
The author explains that seeing water is not a direct nullifier; it merely makes the previous hadath effective again. The nullification is attributed to seeing water metaphorically because the effect of the prior hadath becomes manifest at that moment.
This clarifies why the matn says 'nullified by seeing water' – it is a figure of speech.
3
Apply the condition of sufficiency
The water seen must be sufficient for performing wudu. If the water is insufficient (e.g., a small puddle), tayammum is not nullified. This prevents unnecessary invalidation of a valid purification.
Sufficiency is measured by what is needed to remove hadath, not for other uses.
4
Apply the condition of ability and absence of urgent need
Even if water is sufficient, the person must be able to use it. Ability includes physical access and safety. If the water is needed for drinking or kneading dough (urgent needs), or if accessing it would expose one to an enemy or wild animal, tayammum remains valid.
Legal inability (e.g., fear of harm) is treated as actual inability.

Core Idea
The sleeper who passes water is considered capable by estimation, so tayammum is nullified – unlike the one who fears a predator.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) regarding whether passing water while asleep nullifies tayammum. The key distinction is between the sleeper, who is treated as capable because sleep during travel is rare, and the one who fears an enemy or animal, who is genuinely incapable. The majority view: sleep during travel in a way that one is unaware of water is rare, especially without intervening wakefulness. Therefore, the sleeper is treated as awake (capable by estimation).

The one fearing an enemy or predator is genuinely incapable (ajiz), so tayammum is not nullified. The sleeper is not a real excuse.

5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the sleeper's tayammum is nullified (majority view), he must repeat tayammum upon waking before prayer. If not nullified (Abu Yusuf), his tayammum remains valid.

Ruling and Conditions of Wiping Over Khuff
Definition, ruling, conditions, and duration of wiping over khuff

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wiping over khuff, explain why it is ja'iz (permissible) and not wajib or mustahabb, identify the condition of complete tahara at the time of hadath, and apply the correct duration for resident and traveler.

Core Idea
Wiping over khuff is ja'iz (permissible) by Sunnah – not obligatory, not recommended – and requires complete wudu at the moment of hadath.

In this lesson, we examine the chapter on wiping over khuff from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah. You will learn the legal definition of mas-h, its ruling (ja'iz), the crucial condition of purification at the time of hadath, and the different durations for resident and traveler. Understanding this will clarify common misconceptions about when and how to wipe.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wiping over khuff. The matn states it is 'ja'iz bi-sunnah' – permissible by Sunnah. The sharh explains that 'ja'iz' was chosen to indicate it is not obligatory (wajib) nor recommended (mustahabb). The servant is free to do it or leave it, and one who leaves it is better than one who does it (i.e., no extra reward).
Ja'iz in Hanafi terminology is mubah – neutral, allowed.
2
Locate the dalil and its basis
The proof is the Sunnah (Prophet's sayings and actions), not the Quran. The sharh refutes those who claim a Quranic reading (bi-rijlika in the genitive) as evidence, calling it invalid. The term 'Sunnah' is used rather than 'hadith' because Sunnah includes both qawl and fi'l.
The hadith quoted is cut off in the source: 'The resident wipes for one day...'
3
Trace the condition of purification
The sharh clarifies a common misunderstanding: the condition is NOT complete tahara at the time of wearing the khuff, but at the time of hadath. Example: washing feet, putting on khuff, then completing wudu, then having hadath – wiping is valid. The phrase 'on complete purification' in some copies refers to the moment of hadath.
This is a key Hanafi position. Many students mistakenly think the full wudu must be complete before wearing.
4
Determine the duration for resident and traveler
A resident wipes for one day and one night (24 hours). A traveler wipes for three days and three nights (72 hours). The basis is the prophetic hadith mentioned. Travel conditions (distance, intention) are defined elsewhere in Hanafi fiqh.
The source text ends mid-hadith; the full hadith is well-known.
5
Identify the scope of the concession
Wiping over khuff is only for minor hadath (requiring wudu), not for major impurity (janaba) which requires ghusl. The matn says 'from every hadath that necessitates wudu' – this excludes janaba.
This is explicitly stated in the passage.
