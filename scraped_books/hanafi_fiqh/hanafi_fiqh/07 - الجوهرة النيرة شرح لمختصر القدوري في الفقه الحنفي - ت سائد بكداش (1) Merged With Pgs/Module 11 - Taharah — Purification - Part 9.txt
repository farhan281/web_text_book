This lesson examines a classic inter-madhhab dispute over the number of stones required for dry cleansing (istinja). The Shafi'i school mandates three stones or a tri-edged stone, while the Hanafi school holds that no specific number is prescribed—odd is recommended but not required. Definition and Types of Shart (Conditions) in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition of shart and its three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shart in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish its three types with examples.

Core Idea
A condition (shart) is a necessary prerequisite for an act's validity, and its continuity may be required throughout the act.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi definition of shart (condition) and its classification into three types. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly applying conditions in prayer and other acts of worship. The passage first gives the linguistic and legal meanings, then details each type with concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and legal meanings of shart
Note that linguistically 'shart' means a sign, and legally it refers to something that must precede an act, without which the act is invalid, and its continuity is generally required.
The legal definition emphasizes both precedence and continuity.
2
Identify the first type: shart al-in'iqad
These conditions are required only at the inception of the act. Examples: intention (niyyah), opening takbir (takbir tahrima), time (waqt), and the sermon (khutbah) for Jumu'ah.
They do not need to persist after the start.
3
Identify the second type: shart al-dawam
These conditions must persist throughout the entire act. Examples: purity (taharah), covering the 'awrah, and facing the qibla.
If any of these breaks during prayer, the prayer becomes invalid.
4
Identify the third type
These conditions must exist during the continuance of the act (halat al-baqa'), but neither precedence nor concurrence at the start is required. Example: recitation (qira'ah) in prayer.
Recitation must occur while standing, but it need not begin with the takbir.
5
Memorize the classification with examples
Practice recalling the three types and their examples from the passage. This classification is foundational for understanding the validity of prayer in Hanafi fiqh.
Create a mental chart: Type 1 (inception), Type 2 (continuity), Type 3 (during act).

Facing the exact Ka'bah is required for Makkah and Madinah; others need only face its direction.

Five rare events nullify both purity and prayer because they break the state of intimacy with Allah.

The author then discusses preference: for funeral prayer, immediate performance is better because of the hadith urging haste; for prostration of recitation, delay to a permissible time is better because its obligation is non-urgent (ala al-tarakhi). The answer is that the hadith about laughter uses 'prayer' to mean the full prayer with takbir, ruku', and sujud, not a mere prostration without takbir. Here, the prohibition is to avoid resemblance to sun-worshippers, and prostration alone also creates that resemblance, so it is included. Core Idea
A vow in a disliked time is valid because the prohibition is external, not inherent in the act.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah addresses a key Hanafi principle: the distinction between internal and external prohibition. Understanding why reveals the school's method of weighing intention and external factors.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sajdah is wajib only if you recite the prostration word plus one word before and one after.

This lesson clarifies a nuanced ruling within the Hanafi school: when does reciting part of a prostration verse obligate the sajdah? The correct view sets a precise threshold, while another opinion requires more. Understanding this helps you avoid invalidating your recitation or missing an obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sajdah al-tilawah (prostration of recitation). The ruling is that it is wajib under certain conditions. The question is whether reciting a partial verse triggers it.
Sajdah al-tilawah is wajib for the reciter in the Hanafi school when a prostration verse is recited (with conditions).
2
State the sahih condition
According to the sound (sahih) position, if you recite the exact word of prostration (harf al-sajdah) plus one word before and one word after, then sajdah becomes wajib. Less than that, it is not wajib.
This is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school.
3
Present the alternative opinion
A second opinion (qil) requires reciting the majority (akthar) of the prostration verse. If you recite more than half of the verse, sajdah becomes wajib.
This is a weaker opinion within the school.
4
Consider the edge case
If someone recites the entire prostration verse except the final word (which is the prostration word itself), sajdah is not wajib. This confirms that the prostration word is central.
Also, if one recites the prostration word without the surrounding words, sajdah is not wajib according to the sahih view.
5
Apply to practice
Now you can determine, for any partial recitation of a prostration verse, whether sajdah is obligatory. For example, if you recite only 'yassumuna' (from Surat Fussilat) without surrounding words, sajdah is not wajib.
This ruling is specific to the reciter; the listener's obligation may differ.

This lesson examines the detailed rulings of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira on how the obligation of sajdat al-tilawa multiplies when the same prostration verse is repeated in the same general location. The key is the concept of 'majlis al-tilawah' – the recitation session. The rulings distinguish between the reciter and the hearer, and between the one who changes position and the one who stays.

If a reciter recites a prostration verse, leaves the room, returns and recites it again, how many prostrations does he owe?
A) One prostration, because it is the same verse.
B) Two prostrations, because each recitation in a new sitting requires a separate prostration.
C) None, because he already prostrated the first time.
D) One prostration, but it must be performed twice.

Key Takeaway
The obligation of multiple prostrations for the same prostration verse depends on the continuity of the sitting session (majlis) for the reciter and the hearer separately.
