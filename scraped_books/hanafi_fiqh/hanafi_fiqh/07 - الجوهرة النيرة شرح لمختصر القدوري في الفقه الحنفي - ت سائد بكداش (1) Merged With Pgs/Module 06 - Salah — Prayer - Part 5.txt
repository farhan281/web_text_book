Ruling of Qunut in Witr: Obligation and Details
Commentary on the matn regarding qunut in witr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the proof that qunut in witr is wajib, differentiate between silent and aloud recitation with evidence, and identify the preferred hand posture according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Qunut in witr is wajib; omission requires sajdah al-sahw, and silent recitation is preferred.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah clarifies the ruling on qunut in the witr prayer. It lays out the primary evidence from hadith, explains the wajib nature of qunut, and then delves into the preferred method of recitation (silent) and hand posture (release). The commentary also addresses an inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school and internal Hanafi differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary proof for witr's obligation
Examine the hadith cited at the beginning: 'صلاة إلى صلاتكم ألا وهي الوتر، فصلوها ما بين العشاء إلى طلوع الفجر'. Note that the command 'فصلوها' is an imperative, which in Hanafi usul indicates wujub (obligation). The passage also mentions consensus on making it up, which strengthens the evidence.
The passage uses both textual proof (hadith) and rational argument (attributing the prayer to Allah, not the Prophet) to establish obligation.
2
Understand why qunut itself is wajib
Focus on the statement: 'القنوت واجب على الصحيح، حتى إنه يجب السهو بتركه ساهياً'. The author explicitly declares qunut as wajib and explains that forgetting it necessitates sajdah al-sahw. This is the core ruling.
The word 'واجب' is key; it is less than fard but omission requires compensation.
3
Resolve the recitation method: silent or aloud?
The passage presents a difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars. It quotes al-Nihayah and al-Mabsut preferring silence, citing the hadith 'خيرُ الذكر: الخفي'. Weigh the arguments: silence is preferred because qunut is a supplication; aloud is argued because it resembles Quran. The chosen view is silence.
The hadith 'خيرُ الذكر: الخفي' is the decisive evidence for the preferred view.
4
Determine the hand posture during qunut
There is a difference between al-Karkhi/al-Tahawi (release hands) and Abu Bakr al-Isfaf (support), with the latter attributed to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad. Note which view is considered the relied-upon (mufta bihi) and why.
The text does not explicitly state the mufta bihi, but later commentators favor releasing hands.
5
Note the recommendation regarding blessings on the Prophet
Two opinions: Abu al-Layth says to include salawat in qunut; Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar restricts it to the final sitting. This shows how different jurists apply the principle of following the sunnah in details.
The commentary does not resolve this difference, leaving it as a matter of ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Quduri regarding the witr prayer. It begins by establishing the obligation of witr through the hadith 'A prayer among your prayers...' and the consensus on making it up. The reasoning that the Prophet attributed the prayer to Allah rather than himself indicates it is a fard or wajib, not a sunnah. The author then explains why witr has no separate adhan: it falls within the time of Isha. Next, the commentary addresses the matn's statement 'He does not separate them with a salutation,' which refutes the Shafi'i view that witr can be prayed as two rak'ahs plus one with a salutation. The core of the passage is the qunut in the third rak'ah before ruku'. The commentator affirms that qunut is wajib (according to the correct view), so forgetting it requires a forgetfulness prostration (sajdat al-sahw). Then follows a discussion of whether the imam recites qunut aloud or silently: the preferred view in al-Nihayah and al-Mabsut is to recite silently, as it is a supplication, and the hadith 'The best remembrance is the hidden one' supports this. However, some scholars (like Muhammad ibn al-Fadl) favored silence, while others allowed aloud because qunut resembles Quran recitation. On the issue of raising hands, al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi say to release them (i.e., not raise them), while Abu Bakr al-Isfaf reports that Abu Hanifah and Muhammad al-Shaybani support resting them (i.e., raising or supporting on the knees?). This indicates a difference even among the founders. Finally, the commentator discusses whether to send blessings on the Prophet during qunut: Abu al-Layth recommends it, but Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar restricts it to the final tashahhud. The commentary ends by referring to another work (al-Siraj al-Wahhaj) for the exact wording of qunut. The overall passage shows the richness of Hanafi legal reasoning, balancing textual proof (hadith) and principle (sunnah of supplication) with intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Key Terms
الوتر (al-witr): The odd-numbered prayer performed after Isha, consisting of three rak'ahs, considered wajib in the Hanafi school.
القنوت (al-qunut): A supplication recited in the third rak'ah of witr before ruku', considered wajib in this passage.
الإخفاء (al-ikhfa'): Reciting the qunut silently, which is the preferred method for the imam according to many Hanafi scholars.

Compilation of multiple subsidiary rulings in Witr prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on recitation volume, hand position, and sending salawat during the qunut of Witr, and identify the preferred position with its proof.

Core Idea
The best remembrance is hidden: quiet recitation in Witr is preferred, but hand position and salawat are matters of Hanafi internal ikhtilaf.

This lesson examines three subsidiary rulings of the Witr prayer: whether the qunut is recited loudly or quietly, what to do with the hands, and whether to send blessings on the Prophet. These issues are not fundamental pillars but are governed by sunnah practice and Hanafi legal preference (ikhtiyar). Understanding them helps the student appreciate how the madhhab navigates variant transmissions and ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three subsidiary rulings
Extract from the passage the three issues: (1) recitation volume (ikhfa/jahr), (2) hand position (irsal/i'timad), (3) sending salawat. State the category of each: they are sunnah practices, not obligatory.
These are all within the qunut of Witr.
2
Examine the proof for ikhfa
The author cites a hadith: 'The best remembrance is the hidden one.' Apply the Hanafi usul principle that the preference for ikhfa is derived from takhsis of the general command to recite in prayer. Note that the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) and thus yields preference, not obligation.

Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on hand position
Two positions: al-Karkhi and al-Tahawi say irsal (let hands hang); Abu Bakr al-Iskaaf, Abu Hanifa, and Muhammad say i'timad (lean). Determine the mufta bihi by weighing the authority of the narrators and the later fatwa tradition.

Core Idea
Hanafi: qunut every Witr all year, before ruku'. Shafi'i: only last half of Ramadan, after ruku'.

A brief passage in our source presents two points of difference between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i concerning qunut in the Witr prayer. Forgetting Qira'a in the Third Rak'ah of Witr
Detailed ruling on correcting forgotten qira'a in Witr's third rak'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of forgetting qira'a in Witr's third rak'ah and explain why not repeating ruku' still suffices per the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Missing the qira'a in Witr's third rak'ah requires repeating ruku' after reciting—but tartib within a rak'ah is not a condition.

This passage details the Hanafi remedy for a common error in the Witr prayer: forgetting the recitation in the third rak'ah after qunut. It covers three possibilities (forgot both Fatiha and surah, forgot only surah, forgot only Fatiha) and explains the required corrective actions. It also introduces an intra-madhhab disagreement with Imam Zufar regarding the necessity of repeating ruku'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the error and context
The person is in the third rak'ah of Witr, has already performed qunut, but forgot to recite the required qira'a (Fatiha and/or surah) and went into ruku'. He remembers while still in ruku'.
This is a specific case; the third rak'ah of Witr requires qira'a after qunut per the Hanafi school.
2
Recognize the three scenarios
The passage distinguishes: (1) forgot both Fatiha and surah, (2) forgot surah only, (3) forgot Fatiha only. Each has a similar but distinct remedy.
The remedy always includes rising from ruku', reciting the missed part(s), repeating qunut, repeating ruku', and performing sajda sahw.
3
Apply the remedy
For all scenarios: raise head from ruku', recite the missed qira'a (in the correct order: Fatiha then surah), repeat qunut, then repeat ruku' (go back into ruku' with proper dhikr), then complete the prayer and perform sajda sahw at the end.
The ruku' must be repeated after the recitation to restore proper order, but the Hanafi school deems the prayer valid even if ruku' is not repeated.
4
Analyze the exception
If the person does not repeat the ruku', the prayer still suffices (ajza'ahu) because the sequential order (tartib) of actions within a single rak'ah is not a condition for validity in the Hanafi school.
This is a key Hanafi principle: tartib within a rak'ah is not a shart, unlike tartib between rak'ahs.
5
Contrast with Zufar's view
Imam Zufar holds that if ruku' is not repeated, the prayer does not suffice. His view is based on considering tartib as a condition. The majority Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is the more lenient one.
This difference shows how varying usul principles (e.g., importance of tartib) affect fiqh rulings.

Core Idea
Congregational prayer is an emphasized sunnah, not an obligation; leaving it without excuse is akin to hypocrisy.

This lesson tackles a key fiqh question: Is congregational prayer obligatory or just strongly recommended? The Hanafi school holds it as an emphasized sunnah (sunnah mu'akkadah), close to wajib but not binding. The text presents the evidence from Quran and Sunnah, acknowledges a minority Hanafi view that it is wajib, and then clarifies the practical implication by listing the excuses that allow one to skip it. Learning Objective

Core Idea
The imam stands ahead; standing between is valid but disliked.

In congregational prayer, the imam's position relative to the followers is guided by established practice. When there are exactly two followers, the Hanafi school holds that the imam should stand ahead of them. Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: the imam's placement when there are two followers. The hukm level is sunnah muakkadah (recommended), meaning it is not obligatory but strongly encouraged.

Locate the dalil for the position
The Hanafi position relies on the general practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the interpretation that Ibn Mas'ud's standing between two followers was due to narrow space, not the norm.

The tarjih is supported by the majority of later Hanafi scholars.
