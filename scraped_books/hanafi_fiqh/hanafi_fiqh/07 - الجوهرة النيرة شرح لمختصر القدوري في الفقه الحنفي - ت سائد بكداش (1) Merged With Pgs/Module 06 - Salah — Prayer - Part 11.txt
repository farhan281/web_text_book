Conditions of Sajdat al-Tilawah and the Takbir Dispute
Explaining conditions of validity and the takbir dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of validity for sajdat al-tilawah and explain the Hanafi view on the takbir dispute with al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Sajdat al-tilawah has no ihram; omitting the takbir does not invalidate.

Sajdat al-tilawah is a wajib act upon reciting or hearing a verse of prostration. Its conditions resemble those of prayer, but it lacks certain elements like tashahhud and salam. The Hanafi school holds that the initial takbir is not a pillar, unlike the Shafi'i view. This lesson clarifies the conditions and addresses the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what sajdat al-tilawah is and its legal status (wajib) and basic requirements.
The ruling is wajib, not fard, because it is established by zanni evidence.
2
List the conditions of validity
From the text, extract the shurut (conditions) required for a valid sajdat al-tilawah: purification, covering awrah, facing qibla, no tayammum unless excused.
These conditions mirror those of salat.
3
State the Hanafi position on the takbir
According to the Hanafi school, omitting the takbir that is for ihram (tahrima) does not invalidate the sajdah; it suffices.
The takbir is sunnah, not a pillar.
4
Identify the Shafi'i objection and proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the takbir is necessary for prostration, similar to the takbir in prayer.
The Shafi'i view considers the takbir a pillar.
5
Explain the Hanafi response and thamarah al-khilaf
The Hanafi response distinguishes: the takbir is for transition, not ihram. Practical consequence: if one forgets the takbir, Hanafi says still valid, Shafi'i requires repetition.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage is a combination of a ruling statement and an inter-madhhab dispute. According to Abu Hanifa, sajdat al-shukr is not considered (la i'tibara biha) and is makruh.
This means it has no legal recognition and is disliked.

Consider the basis
The text does not provide a detailed proof, but the ruling implies that it is not established by strong evidence and may resemble innovation.

Sajdat al-Tilawah: Rulings, Conditions, and the Takbir Controversy
Explains rulings of prostration of recitation and takbir controversy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of sajdat al-tilawah, identify which acts invalidate it, and explain the Hanafi position on omitting the takbir along with its qiyas proof.

Core Idea
Sajdat al-tilawah has no tahrimah — the takbir is for transition, not consecration.

Sajdat al-tilawah is a wajib act upon reciting or hearing a sajdah verse. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies its unique nature — it is not a full prayer, so it lacks tashahhud and salam. A key point of discussion is whether the takbir is necessary for validity. The Hanafi school relies on qiyas to the prostration within prayer to argue that the takbir is merely transitional.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basic rulings
Sajdat al-tilawah has no tashahhud or salam because it has no ihram. It requires the same conditions as prayer (purity, covering awrah, facing qiblah).
Note that tayammum is only allowed if no water or illness.
2
Examine the takbir controversy
The text states omitting the opening takbir is valid according to Hanafis, contrary to al-Shafi'i. Understand the practical difference: Hanafis do not require it, Shafi'is do.
The takbir here is not called tahrimah; it is for transition.
3
Understand the proof: Qiyas to prayer sajdah
The Hanafi proof is qiyas: the takbir in prayer's sajdah is for transition, not for entering prayer. Similarly, the takbir in sajdat al-tilawah is for transition from recitation to prostration.
This is an example of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Apply the conditions and invalidators
List the shurut (purity, awrah, qiblah) and mufsidat (speaking, laughing, breaking wudu). Understand that omitting the sajdah itself invalidates, but omitting takbir does not.
The invalidators apply whether intentional or by mistake.
5
Note the special case of a woman prostrating beside a man
If a woman prostrates beside a man following him, it does not invalidate his prostration, even if she intended to be led by him.
This shows that the prostration is individual, not congregational.

Sajdat al-Shukr: Ruling and Its Effect on Purity – Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on Sajdat al-Shukr and its purity implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on Sajdat al-Shukr's ruling and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding its effect on wudu and tayammum.

Core Idea
Sajdat al-Shukr's ruling and purity effect hinge on whether it is considered a significant act of worship.

This lesson explores the internally contested ruling of Sajdat al-Shukr (Prostration of Thankfulness) within the Hanafi school. While Imam Abu Hanifa viewed it as a non-rewarded act, his disciples Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani considered it a rewarding act of worship. This difference leads to practical consequences for ritual purity—specifically, whether sleeping during this prostration invalidates wudu and whether tayammum performed for it suffices for prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on the ruling of Sajdat al-Shukr
Read the passage to determine what Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn say about the prostration's status—is it rewarded or not?
Abu Hanifa: لا يثاب عليها, leaving better. Sahibayn: قربة يثاب عليها.
2
Understand the basis for each position
For Abu Hanifa, the reason is that if it were obligatory, it would be perpetually obligatory due to continuous blessings, which is impossible. For the Sahibayn, they analogize it to sajdat al-tilawah, considering it a similar act of worship.
Abu Hanifa's argument is based on usul (avoiding perpetual obligation).
3
Explain the practical consequence (fa'idat al-khilaf) of this difference
The passage highlights that the ikhtilaf affects two purity issues: (1) Does sleeping during this prostration nullify wudu? (2) Is tayammum performed for this prostration valid for prayer?
Abu Hanifa says yes to nullification and no to tayammum; Sahibayn say opposite.
4
Trace the reasoning for the purity rulings
Abu Hanifa considers Sajdat al-Shukr ghayr mu'tabara (not significant), so rules of similar acts don't apply. The Sahibayn consider it mu'tabara, so they analogize to sajdat al-tilawah.
The key is the concept of mu'tabara.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position
According to later Hanafi authorities like al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Tahtawi, the ruling of Sajdat al-Shukr is mustahabb (recommended). However, the passage does not explicitly resolve the purity dispute, indicating it remains an area of ikhtilaf.
The footnote says the mustahabb view is the fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira discusses the ruling of Sajdat al-Shukr (Prostration of Thankfulness) and its effect on purity. The passage begins by presenting the positions of the major imams: According to Abu Hanifa, one is not rewarded for it and leaving it is better—meaning it is not a legislated act of worship in his view. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is a good deed (qurba) for which one is rewarded. Malik, Shafi'i, and Ahmad are aligned with the Sahibayn. The text then describes the recommended occasion and manner of performing this prostration: it is mustahabb when a manifest blessing occurs, and the form follows that of sajdat al-tilawah. The lengthy footnote clarifies Abu Hanifa's view: he denied its obligation because blessings are continuous, making perpetual obligation impossible. Interpretations of his statement range from its being merely permissible to being disliked, but the relied-upon (mu'tamad) position is that it is mustahabb and the difference of opinion is only about its being sunnah, not about permissibility. The passage then highlights the practical consequence (fawa'id al-khilaf) of this difference: whether sleeping during this prostration breaks wudu and whether tayammum performed for it suffices for prayer. According to Abu Hanifa, sleeping does break wudu and tayammum for it is not valid for prayer. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, sleeping does not break wudu and tayammum is valid, analogizing it to sajdat al-tilawah because they consider it a significant act of worship (mu'tabara). This is a standard intra-Hanafi dispute with clear practical implications.

Sleep During Prostration of Recitation: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nullification of wudu by sleep during prostration of recitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the nullification of wudu by sleep during the prostration of recitation and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) for the validity of subsequent salah.

Core Idea
Sleep during prostration of recitation: Abu Hanifa says it breaks wudu; Sahibayn say it doesn't.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on two connected issues: whether sleeping during the prostration of recitation nullifies wudu, and whether one can use the tayammum performed for that prostration for a later obligatory prayer. The practical consequence is direct: the validity of salah depends on which position one follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed points
The passage discusses two things: (1) the effect of sleep during the prostration of recitation on wudu, and (2) whether the tayammum performed for that prostration can be used for salah.
The context is likely a traveler riding an animal, prostration is performed by gesture.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that sleep nullifies wudu, making the tayammum invalid for subsequent salah. Therefore, one cannot pray with that tayammum.
The general Hanafi rule is that sleep breaks wudu; Abu Hanifa applies it here.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that sleep does not nullify wudu in this specific context, so the tayammum remains valid for salah.
They analogize to the regular prostration of recitation (sajdah al-tilawah) where sleep does not break wudu.
4
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if one sleeps during the prostration, Abu Hanifa requires a new wudu or tayammum for salah; the Sahibayn allow the existing tayammum to be used.
5
Consider the underlying reasoning
The Sahibayn use qiyas: the prostration of recitation is equivalent whether performed in travel or normally. Abu Hanifa may distinguish based on the context (riding/travel) where sleep is more likely to nullify wudu.
The passage explicitly mentions 'as in the prostration of recitation; because it is considered equivalent according to them.'
