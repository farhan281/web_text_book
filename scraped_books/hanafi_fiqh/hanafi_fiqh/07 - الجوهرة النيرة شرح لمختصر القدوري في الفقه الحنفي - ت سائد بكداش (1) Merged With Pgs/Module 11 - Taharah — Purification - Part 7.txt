Ghusl After Complete Cessation of Menses: Timing Rules
Ruling on timing of ghusl after complete cessation of menses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when ghusl becomes required after complete cessation of menses and distinguish between cessation during complete vs. incomplete prayer times.

Core Idea
Ghusl after menses is required to confirm purity, but its immediacy depends on the prayer time in which cessation occurs.

This lesson examines a key rule from the chapter of menstruation: the requirement of ghusl upon complete cessation of menstrual bleeding. The legal nuance lies in the timing—whether the cessation occurs during a 'complete' prayer time (obligating a full salat) or an 'incomplete' one (supererogatory prayer time). Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the rule correctly in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under consideration is the complete cessation of menstrual blood (الانقطاع التام). The basic ruling is that ghusl becomes necessary (fard) to give predominance to the side of purity and permit acts like prayer and intercourse.
2
Understand the caveat 'complete' (كاملة)
The base text adds the word 'complete' to specify that the ruling applies only when the cessation is definitive. If the blood stops but there is doubt (e.g., spotting), additional rules may apply.
The commentary explains that 'complete' serves to exclude cases of partial cessation or uncertainty.
3
Distinguish between complete and incomplete prayer times
A 'complete' prayer time is one that has a full obligatory prayer (e.g., Fajr, Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha). An 'incomplete' prayer time is a supererogatory time (e.g., Duha, Eid prayer time). If cessation occurs during a complete time, she may delay ghusl until that time ends. If it occurs during an incomplete time, she must perform ghusl before any permissible intercourse or she may wait until a complete prayer time enters.
The legal reason (illah) is that purity established by a complete prayer time is legally significant; an incomplete time lacks that weight.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If menses stops at dawn of Fajr (a complete time), she may delay ghusl until after Fajr time elapses. If it stops during Duha (incomplete), she must perform ghusl before any sexual contact or before the time of Zuhr enters.

Core Idea
If menses stops before the usual habit, precaution forbids intercourse until the full habit passes.

This lesson addresses a common situation: a woman's menstrual bleeding stops earlier than her normal pattern. The Hanafi school, emphasizing precaution (ihtiyat), imposes a waiting period to avoid the likelihood of renewed bleeding. Understanding this ruling is essential for both matters of purity and marital relations.

Identify the scenarios
Distinguish between stoppage at the usual habit and stoppage before it. The ruling differs.
The base text says 'if it stops before her habit' is the critical case.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason is that return of blood within the habit period is common (ghalib), so precaution (ihtiyat) dictates avoidance.
This is based on empirical likelihood, not mere doubt.
3
Apply the consequences
The woman must perform ghusl, pray, and fast, but intercourse is prohibited until the habit passes. If this is the last menstruation of 'idda, raj'ah is nullified.
These consequences are derived from the same precaution.

Delaying ghusl after the blood stops is recommended if stoppage is at the habit, but obligatory if before the habit.

The Effect of Tayammum on Purity and Intercourse
Intra-Hanafi dispute on purity and the right of revocation (raj'ah)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on when intercourse is permitted after tayammum for a traveling woman whose menses stopped, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the right of revocation (raj'ah).

Core Idea
Tayammum purifies but intercourse requires a prior prayer — a condition unique to the Hanafi school.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira examines a specific scenario: a traveling woman whose menstruation ends but has no water for ghusl. She performs tayammum. The text raises an important question: does tayammum fully restore her purity for marital relations and for cutting off the husband's right of revocation? The Hanafi school has an internal division on this issue, with Abū Ḥanīfah and Abū Yūsuf requiring a prayer after tayammum, while Muḥammad and Zufar consider tayammum sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A traveling woman's menses stops, no water is available. She performs tayammum instead of ghusl. The initial ruling states she is pure and intercourse is permissible.
This is the starting point of the discussion.
2
State the initial ruling and the issue of raj'ah
The text says her husband may have intercourse, but then narrows the discussion to raj'ah (right of revocation during 'idda). The key question: does tayammum alone cut off raj'ah, or is prayer required?
Raj'ah is relevant if the woman is in 'idda from a revocable divorce.
3
Map the intra-Hanafi dispute
According to the Two (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad al-Shaybānī in one transmission), raj'ah is not cut off until she prays with that tayammum. According to Muḥammad and Zufar, raj'ah is cut off by tayammum alone, like ghusl.
The text cites Al-Khujandī and the commentary (Sharh) for these positions.
4

The commentary then states that according to Abū Ḥanīfah and Abū Yūsuf, intercourse is not permissible until she performs a prayer (any prayer) with that tayammum. This is the muftā bihī position.
Thus, tayammum alone does not fully establish purity for intercourse; prayer is an additional condition.
5
Analyze the legal reasoning
The difference hinges on whether tayammum is a full substitute (badal kāmil) for ghusl. For Abū Ḥanīfah and Abū Yūsuf, tayammum removes major impurity (janābah) but does not fully restore the state of purity required for intercourse until a prayer is performed, perhaps as a confirmation of intention.
This shows a nuanced view within the school about the functional equivalence of tayammum.

Menstruation During Prayer Time and Qada
Ruling on qada for prayers missed due to menstruation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada for obligatory prayers missed due to menstruation during the time, identify Zufar's ikhtilaf, and apply the ruling to supererogatory acts.

Core Idea
No qada for obligatory prayers missed during menstruation, but nafl acts started must be made up.

This passage clarifies the effect of menstruation on the obligation to make up (qaḍā') missed prayers. It presents the majority Hanafi view, an intra-Hanafi disagreement from Zufar, and a consensus on the post-time case. It also covers supererogatory acts, which follow a different rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Determine what act is being discussed: obligatory prayer (fard) and supererogatory prayer/fasting (nafl). Note that the general principle for prayer is it is fard 'ayn.
Obligatory prayers are fard; supererogatory are nafl.
2
Examine the condition and the Hanafi position
The condition is menstruation occurring during the prayer time. The Hanafi position: no qada is required for obligatory prayers regardless of how much time remains or whether she started the prayer.
This is the majority view among the Sahibayn and later scholars.
3
Analyze Zufar's ikhtilaf
Zufar holds that if sufficient time remained to perform the prayer before menstruation, qada is obligatory; if not, it is not. Understand his reasoning: the obligation is established if the time was ample.
Note that Zufar's view is not the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
4
Apply the post-time consensus rule
If menstruation begins after the prayer time has ended without having prayed, qada is obligatory by consensus. This is because the obligation had already been established.
Ijma: all scholars agree.
5
Distinguish supererogatory acts
If she begins a nafl prayer or nafl fast and then menstruates, qada is wajib. This is because the act was voluntarily commenced and invalidated, so completion via qada is required to secure the reward.
This applies to both prayer and fasting.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling on a break of purity (tuhur) between two blood flows during the maximum menstrual period of ten days. The issue arises when a woman sees blood, then a period of no blood, then blood again, all within ten days. The question is whether the break (tuhur) separates the blood into two distinct menstrual periods or is considered as one continuous period. The text presents the views of four major Hanafi imams: Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, Zufar, and Hasan ibn Ziyad. Abu Yusuf holds that the break is like continuous blood, meaning it does not separate; the entire span is considered one menstrual period if there is blood at the beginning and end. His principle is that menstruation begins with purity and ends with purity, provided blood exists before and after. Muhammad differentiates based on the length of the break: if less than three full days (even by an hour), it does not separate; if three days or more, then further criteria apply: if the break is equal to or less than the combined blood flows, it still does not separate; only if the break exceeds the blood flows does it separate, and then each side may be evaluated separately for being valid menstruation. Zufar's view: if the woman sees blood in the majority of the maximum period equal to the minimum period (i.e., at least three days of blood in ten), the break is considered continuous; otherwise, none of it is menstruation. Hasan ibn Ziyad agrees with Muhammad on breaks less than three days, but for three days or more, he holds that it always separates, regardless of comparison with blood flows. The text then notes that in Al-Hidayah, Abu Yusuf's view is preferred for ease. Al-Wajiz declares Muhammad's view as the most correct and the basis for fatwa, while Al-Fatawa gives fatwa on Abu Yusuf's view to facilitate women. This shows that the mufta bihi is not unanimous among later authorities; some follow Muhammad's stricter approach, others follow Abu Yusuf's more lenient one. The common misunderstanding is that any break of purity automatically ends a menstrual period; in the Hanafi school, short breaks (less than three days) within the ten-day window are considered part of the same menstrual period, and even longer breaks may not separate if the blood is more dominant.

Maximum Nifas and Ruling When Blood Exceeds Forty Days
Statement of maximum nifas with proof and habit rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maximum of nifas (40 days), explain the rational qiyas proof, and apply the habit rule when blood exceeds 40 days.

Core Idea
Nifas maximum is 40 days; beyond it is istihadah. A woman with a known habit reverts to her habit days.

This passage establishes the definitive Hanafi position on the maximum duration of postpartum bleeding (nifas) as forty days, providing a rational proof based on the accumulation of blood during pregnancy. It then addresses the practical case where bleeding exceeds forty days but the woman has a customary duration from previous births, detailing how to determine her nifas period. Understanding this rule is essential for distinguishing between nifas, which exempts from prayer, and istihadah, which requires wudu for each prayer.

Guided Path
1
State the maximum nifas ruling
Identify the explicit Hanafi ruling: maximum nifas is 40 days. Any blood beyond that is istihadah (abnormal bleeding).
This is a fixed temporal limit; the hukm is wad'i (determinative).
2
Analyze the rational proof (dalil aqli)
Follow the author's reasoning: during pregnancy the uterus is blocked, blood accumulates for 4 months, then exits at birth. Since the maximum haid per month is 10 days, 4 months × 10 days = 40 days.
This is a qiyas based on customary knowledge, not transmitted dalil.
3
Compare with Shafi'i position
Note that al-Shafi'i holds the maximum is 60 days, based on their maximum haid of 15 days per month. The Hanafi response refutes this by insisting on the 10-day maximum for haid.
The ikhtilaf stems from a difference in the maximum duration of haid.
4
Apply the habit rule when blood exceeds 40 days
If a woman has a known habit (custom) from previous births, she is returned to her habit days even if bleeding continues beyond 40. The remaining bleeding is istihadah.
Abu Yusuf adds that this applies whether her habit ends with blood or with purity.
5
Derive practical consequences
Days within the habit or within 40 (if no habit) are nifas, exempting from prayer. Days beyond are istihadah, requiring qada of missed prayers and wudu for each prayer.
This affects obligations of prayer and fasting.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Nifas and Istihadah: When Does Postpartum Bleeding End?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on classifying bleeding after birth as nifas or istihadah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad/Zufar on classifying a second bleeding after a 40-day gap as nifas or istihadah and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
When bleeding stops and restarts after a gap, the classification of the first bleeding is disputed among Hanafi imams, affecting prayer and fasting duties.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about how to determine if a second round of bleeding after childbirth is still nifas or has become istihadah. The ruling determines whether the woman must perform prayers and fasts. Understanding this difference sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi principles of continuity and habit in purity rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The text presents a woman who experiences two separate bleedings after childbirth with a gap of 40 days between them. Determine the two bleedings and the gap.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the first bleeding is nifas (postpartum bleeding) and the second is istihadah (irregular bleeding). The woman does not pray/fast during the first, but must pray/fast during the second with wudu for each prayer.
They consider the first bleeding as the true nifas, and the gap indicates the second is not part of nifas.
3
State the position of Muhammad and Zufar
Muhammad al-Shaybani and Zufar hold the opposite view: the first bleeding is istihadah and the second is nifas. Thus the woman must pray/fast during the first bleeding and refrain during the second.
They interpret the gap as evidence that the first bleeding was not true nifas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the woman prays and fasts during the first bleeding period. Under Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf, she does not; under Muhammad/Zufar, she does. This directly affects her religious obligations.
This is a clear example of how ikhtilaf within a school still leads to different rulings for the individual.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the classification of postpartum bleeding (nifas) versus irregular bleeding (istihadah). The scholars agree that the maximum period of nifas is 40 days (according to the school), but they differ on how to count when a woman experiences two distinct bleedings with a gap. In the first scenario, if a woman bleeds for a period, then stops, then bleeds again after a gap of 40 days, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf consider the first bleeding as nifas (postpartum) and the second as istihadah (irregular). Muhammad and Zufar consider the first as istihadah, presumably because they view the gap as indicating the end of nifas. In the second scenario, if a woman has a habit (customary cycle) of 20 days, and she sees 20 days of bleeding after the first and 21 after the second, the same split occurs: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf count the first 20 as nifas, while Muhammad and Zufar count them as istihadah. In the third scenario, if both bleedings are 20 days and her habit is 20, then the second bleeding is unanimously nifas, but the first is nifas for Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, while for Muhammad and Zufar it is istihadah. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that if it is classified as nifas, the woman does not pray or fast; if istihadah, she must pray and fast as a menstruating woman would? Actually istihadah is similar to chronic illness—she prays and fasts, but performs wudu for each prayer. The commentary highlights the differences in ijtihad among the early Hanafi masters.

Key Terms
نفاس (nifas): Postpartum bleeding occurring after childbirth; in this passage, it is the ruling for bleeding that occurs within the first 40 days (according to the Hanafi school).
استحاضة (istihadah): Irregular (non-menstrual) bleeding that occurs outside the normal cycle or postpartum period; the woman must continue to pray and fast, performing wudu for each prayer as a person with chronic excuse.
عادة ('adah): The habitual length of a woman's menstrual or postpartum cycle; used here to determine the type of bleeding.
