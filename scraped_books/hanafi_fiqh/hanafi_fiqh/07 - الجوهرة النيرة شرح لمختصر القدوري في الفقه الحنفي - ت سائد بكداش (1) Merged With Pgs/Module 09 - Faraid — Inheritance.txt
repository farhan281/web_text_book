Faraid Base: Husband, Father, and Son
Faraid case with husband, father, and son

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the shares of a husband, father, and son in a Hanafi inheritance case by applying the base number (asl al-mas'alah) and residuary distribution.

Core Idea
The son inherits the residue after prescribed shares—this is the fixed Hanafi method.

In this faraid case, three heirs survive: the husband, the father, and the son. The husband and father receive fixed Quranic shares, while the son takes everything remaining as the agnatic residuary (asabah). Understanding how to set the base number and distribute the shares is essential for mastering Hanafi inheritance calculations.

Guided Path
1
List surviving heirs and identify categories
Begin by listing all heirs: husband (spouse), father (ascendant), and son (descendant). Categorize each as a Quranic sharer (fard) or residuary (asabah). The husband and father are fard heirs; the son is asabah.
In Hanafi law, the son as an agnatic heir blocks all other residuaries.
2
Determine prescribed Quranic shares
The husband receives 1/4 (Quran 4:12) because the deceased has a child. The father receives 1/6 (Quran 4:11) because the deceased has a son. Allocate these fractions.
If there were no child, the husband would get 1/2 and the father would get residuary or 1/6 depending on other heirs.
3
Set the base number (asl al-mas'alah)
Find the least common multiple of the denominators of the prescribed shares. Here, denominators are 4 and 6; LCM is 12. This becomes the base number.
The base number represents the total number of shares before distribution.
4
Distribute shares to fard heirs
Multiply each prescribed share fraction by the base: husband gets 1/4 of 12 = 3 shares; father gets 1/6 of 12 = 2 shares.
The shares must be whole numbers; if they are not, the base may need adjustment (tashih).
5
Assign residue to asabah
Subtract the total of fard shares from the base: 12 - (3+2) = 7 shares. The son receives all 7 shares as the asabah.
The son's share is the residue, not a fixed fraction. If there were multiple residuaries, the residue would be divided among them per Hanafi agnatic precedence.

The base number is the smallest number that gives whole‑number numerators for all shares.
4

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed worked example of faraid (inheritance calculation) according to the Hanafi school. The author begins by explaining the units of weight used in distributing the estate, specifically the dirham, dāniq, qīrāṭ, and habba (barley grain). The Hanafi and Iraqi definitions differ slightly: a qīrāṭ is half a sixth of a dirham (i.e., 1/12), equivalent to four barley grains. The main example is a deceased person survived by a husband, father, mother, and son. The base number is 12: the husband receives 1/4 (3 parts), the father 1/6 (2 parts), the mother 1/6 (2 parts), and the son receives the residue (5 parts). The text then scales this up to 72 to avoid fractions when converting to dirhams, and further adjusts to 48 dirhams for the actual distribution. The method involves multiplying each heir's share by the total estate and dividing by the base number. The author also introduces a calculation trick: subtract the scaled share from the scaled base to verify the correct portion.
