Prostration on Barriers: Turban, Cloth, and Other Materials
Ruling on prostration on barriers with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for prostration on various barriers and apply the condition of feeling the hardness of the ground.

Core Idea
Prostration on a barrier is valid if the ground's hardness is felt; grain is always permissible.

This lesson covers scenarios where a person prostrates on something that separates the forehead from the ground, such as the crown of a turban, excess cloth, cotton, grass, or grain. The key principle is whether the hardness of the ground is perceptible through the barrier. Al-Halwani adds a thickness limit for cloth barriers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and General Ruling
The act is prostration on an object between the forehead and the ground. The general ruling is permissibility (jawaz) for turban crown and excess cloth.
Both the turban crown (kawr al-'imāmah) and excess garment (fadil thawb) are permissible.
2
Apply the Condition: Feel Hardness of Ground
The permissibility is conditional upon feeling the hardness of the ground through the barrier. If the ground cannot be felt, prostration is not valid.
This applies to cloth, cotton, grass, and hay.
3
Apply the Thickness Limit (Al-Halwani)
Al-Halwani specifies that if the thickness of the barrier exceeds one or two bricks (standing on edge, each a quarter cubit), it is not permissible. This is an additional condition for cloth barriers.
The brick measurement is for the standing brick (mansubah), not laid flat (mafrushah).
4
Handle Solid Materials: Grain
Prostration on grain (wheat or barley) is always permissible without the condition of feeling hardness, because grain is solid and does not prevent contact with the ground.
This is an exception to the general condition.
5
Apply to Similar Materials
Carded cotton, placed grass, and hay follow the same rule: if the ground's hardness is felt, it is valid; otherwise not.
The test is the perceptibility of the ground's firmness.

Sujood on Turban, Garment, and Natural Materials
Lists conditions for valid sujood on different surfaces and materials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid sujood on a turban, garment, and grains, and apply the rule regarding feeling the hardness of the ground.

Core Idea
Prostration on an intermediary is valid only if the ground's hardness is felt and the height difference does not exceed one brick.

This lesson outlines the Hanafi ruling on prostrating upon an intermediary such as the coiled part of a turban or the excess of a garment. The passage sets key conditions: the height difference must be minimal and the hardness of the ground must be perceptible. It then extends the ruling to various materials like cotton, grass, and grains, establishing a clear distinction between permissible and impermissible surfaces based on their ability to transmit the ground's firmness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the core ruling
The matn states that prostrating on the turban's coil or the garment's excess is permissible. Note that the act is sujood, and the ruling is permissible (jā'iz) with conditions.
This is the base ruling from the matn.
2
Determine the conditions added by al-Halwani
Al-Halwani adds two conditions: the height difference between the ground and the prostration surface must not exceed one or two upright bricks (each brick = 1/4 cubit), and the hardness of the ground must be felt through the material.
The brick measure is a guideline for permissible thickness.
3
Apply the condition to various materials
For carded cotton, grass, and straw, the ruling depends solely on feeling the ground's hardness. For grains: loose wheat and barley are permissible; loose maize and millet are not. If all grains are in a sack, they become permissible.
The distinction is based on the natural softness of the material.
4
Understand the rationale behind the sack exception
When grains are in a sack, the container compresses them and allows the hardness to be felt through the sack, making the prostrating surface effectively firm. Hence all grains become permissible.
This shows the principle of focusing on the actual encounter with hardness.
5
Summarize the Hanafi conditions for valid sujood on intermediaries
Valid sujood requires: (1) the forehead must touch something that transmits the ground's hardness, (2) the height difference from the ground must be minimal (≤ 2 bricks), and (3) the material must not be naturally soft (unless contained).
These conditions ensure the sujood is performed on a firm base.

Core Idea
Du'a resembling human speech after tashahhud does not invalidate prayer – it is like actual speech, which also does not invalidate.

This lesson explains the Hanafi position on what supplications are allowed during prayer. The matn permits du'a using Quranic phrases or prophetic supplications, but prohibits du'a that resembles ordinary requests between people. However, a crucial nuance: if such 'human-like' du'a is recited after the tashahhud, it does not nullify the prayer. We will examine the textual definition, the proof by analogy, and the intra-Hanafi differences that fortify this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of permissible du'a
From the matn: permissible du'a includes that which resembles the words of the Quran and the prophetic supplications (ma'thur). The commentator clarifies that 'resembles' means using the same meanings or phrases, not literal similarity.

The matn prohibits du'a that resembles the speech of people (kalām al-nās). This is defined as requests that could normally be asked of humans, e.g., 'O Allah, clothe me' or 'O Allah, marry me to so-and-so'.

If such du'a is made after finishing the tashahhud (the final sitting), it does not invalidate the prayer. This is based on qiyas: actual human speech after tashahhud also does not invalidate.
The reasoning: 'since actual speech does not invalidate, then that which resembles it is even less likely to invalidate.'

The analogy: asl = actual human speech after tashahhud (ruling: does not invalidate); far' = du'a resembling human speech after tashahhud; illa = resemblance (shabah) to human speech. Therefore, the same ruling applies.
This is a qiyas of shabahah (resemblance), not a strict analogy based on effective cause.

Abu Hanifa holds that actual human speech after tashahhud completes the prayer and exits from it, so it does not invalidate. The Sahibayn hold that it requires sajdah al-sahw but does not invalidate. Both agree that du'a resembling speech after tashahhud does not invalidate.
The mufta bihi is that the prayer is valid and no sajdah al-sahw is required for du'a resembling speech.

Defining Jahr and Mukhafatah in Prayer: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on definitions of jahr and mukhafatah with rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi definitions of jahr and mukhafatah, identify the mufta bihi, and apply the rulings for the imam and the individual praying alone.

Core Idea
Jahr means making others hear; mukhafatah means making oneself hear – sound is essential for recitation.

This lesson unpacks a key intra-Hanafi dispute over the precise definitions of loud (jahr) and silent (mukhafatah) recitation in prayer. The commentary on al-Quduri presents two views: al-Karkhi’s, which equates jahr with hearing oneself, and al-Hindwani’s, which requires that others hear. The commentator sides with al-Hindwani, and this choice directly affects how we understand the obligations of the imam and the individual praying alone. By mastering this dispute, you will grasp why the Hanafi school insists that recitation must involve audible sound, not mere tongue movement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Definitions
From the commentary, extract al-Karkhi’s view (jahr = hearing oneself, mukhafatah = correcting letters) and al-Hindwani’s view (jahr = making others hear, mukhafatah = making oneself hear).
Note that both are valid within the Hanafi school, but only one is relied upon for fatwa.
2
Understand the Rationale
Al-Karkhi argues that recitation is an act of the tongue, so self-hearing suffices. Al-Hindwani counters that sound is essential; mere tongue movement is not recitation. The commentator accepts al-Hindwani’s reasoning.
This rationale applies to all verbal acts (divorce, manumission, etc.), not just prayer.
3
Note the Tarjih and Its Basis
The commentator declares al-Hindwani’s view as “as-sahih” (correct) and adopts it as the mufta bihi. The basis is that sound is necessary for the act to be called recitation.
This tarjih is consistent with the principle that actions are judged by their realities, not mere forms.
4
Apply to the Rulings for Imam and Munfarid
The imam must recite loudly (jahr) in Fajr, Maghrib, Isha, Jumu‘ah, and ‘Id prayers; silently (mukhafatah) in Zuhr and ‘Asr. The munfarid has a choice in loud prayers but must recite silently in silent prayers; raising his voice beyond self‑hearing is blameworthy.
The proof is the Prophet’s practice and the command “Pray as you see me praying.”

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on al-Quduri's matn regarding the rulings of loud (jahr) and silent (mukhafatah) recitation in prayer. The commentary begins by clarifying the definitions of jahr and mukhafatah according to two Hanafi authorities: Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi and al-Hindwani. Al-Karkhi defines the minimum of jahr as making oneself hear, while its maximum is making others hear; mukhafatah is merely correcting the letters (i.e., articulating correctly without audible sound). Al-Hindwani, however, maintains that jahr is making others hear, and mukhafatah is making oneself hear. The commentator declares al-Hindwani's view as the correct one because recitation requires sound; mere tongue movement does not constitute recitation. This distinction applies to all verbal acts like divorce and manumission. The text then explains the ruling for the individual praying alone (munfarid): in prayers that are normally recited silently (Zuhr and Asr), the munfarid has no choice; he must recite silently. If he raises his voice beyond what his own ears can hear, he has acted wrongly. For prayers that are normally recited loudly (Fajr, Maghrib, Isha), the munfarid has the option to recite loudly or silently. The imam, however, must recite loudly in loud prayers and silently in silent prayers, even if at Arafah during Hajj (where Zuhr is usually recited loudly in congregation). The proof for silent recitation in Zuhr and Asr is the hadith 'The daytime prayer is silent' (though the commentator notes this is not a marfu' hadith but a statement of Ibn Abbas). A stronger proof is the hadith of Khabbab indicating that the Prophet's recitation in Zuhr and Asr was known by the movement of his beard, implying he was not loud. The command 'Pray as you see me praying' makes this practice obligatory. The commentary also addresses the case of a person who misses Isha and prays it after sunrise: if he leads, he recites loudly; if alone, he must recite silently without choice, because the loudness is tied either to congregation (for Isha) or to time (for Fajr), neither of which applies here.
