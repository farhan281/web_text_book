Ruling on standing when unable to bow/prostrate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a person who can stand but cannot bow or prostrate, and identify the preferred method of nodding.

Core Idea
If you can stand but cannot bow or prostrate, standing is not required; sitting and nodding suffices.

This lesson examines a specific concession in salah for the sick. While standing (qiyam) is normally a pillar, Hanafi fiqh recognizes that if a person is able to stand yet unable to perform bowing and prostration, the obligation of standing is lifted. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents the basic ruling and then discusses the best manner of nodding (ima') to fulfill the prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The person is able to stand but unable to perform ruku and sujud due to illness.
This is a specific condition that triggers the concession.
2
State the default ruling
Standing is not obligatory; he may pray sitting and nod with his head for ruku and sujud.
The Arabic says 'lam yalzamhu al-qiyam' – standing is not required.
3
Note the permissible variation
If he nods while standing entirely, it is also permissible, as cited from al-Muhit.
This shows the ruling is flexible.
4
Distinguish the preferred method
According to al-Fatawa, the best method is to sit entirely and nod for both actions, to avoid confusion and maintain khushu'.
The preferred method is all sitting (al-afdal huwa al-ima' qa'idan bil-kull).

The Four Cases of Qada for the Sick Person: Consensus and Dispute
Ruling on when a sick person must make up missed prayers, with consensus and ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four cases of qada for a sick person, identify the two cases of consensus, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the mixed cases.

Core Idea
Qada depends on consciousness and duration; two cases are settled, two are disputed.

When a person misses prayers due to illness, the obligation to make them up is not always straightforward. Hanafi scholars classify the situation into four cases based on two factors: how long the illness lasted (more or less than a day and night) and whether the person was conscious during that time. This passage presents the cases where there is consensus, and those where later Hanafi scholars differed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two variables

The unit 'a day and a night' means 24 hours.
2

List all four combinations: (1) short illness + conscious, (2) short illness + unconscious, (3) long illness + conscious, (4) long illness + unconscious.
3
Recognize consensus cases
Identify which two cases have consensus: short + conscious (qada required) and long + unconscious (no qada). These are universally agreed upon among Hanafis.
The word 'ijmaʻan' indicates consensus.
4

The remaining two cases are disputed: short + unconscious, and long + conscious. Note that these are 'mixed' because the two factors point in opposite directions.

The passage mentions that some shuyukh (like al-Marghinani, author of al-Hidayah) say qada is required in both mixed cases, while others (like al-Bazdawi al-Saghir and Qadi Khan) say no qada. Understand the practical implication: a mufti may follow either view.

Signaling in the Sick Person's Prayer: Head Only
Matn ruling and inter-madhhab dispute on ima'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ima' (signaling) in the sick person's prayer and distinguish it from the views of Zufar, al-Hasan, and al-Shafi'i, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) regarding repetition of prayer.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the sick signal only with the head; other body parts invalidate the ima' but do not require repetition.

When a person is too ill to bow and prostrate normally, Islamic law permits signalling (ima') as a substitute. But which body part may be used? This lesson explores the Hanafi matn from Al-Quduri which strictly limits ima' to the head, and contrasts it with the views of other early scholars who allowed the eyes, heart, or eyebrows — though requiring repetition after recovery.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
From the matn, identify that the sick person must not signal with eyes, heart, or eyebrows. The only permissible form is with the head. Note that the matn does not mention repetition because the Hanafi view considers the prayer valid as performed.
The phrase 'لا يومى بعينيه' is a clear prohibition of eye-signaling.
2
Identify the opponents and their views
List the three differing opinions: Zufar allows heart (requires repetition), al-Hasan allows eyebrows and heart (requires repetition), and al-Shafi'i allows eyes (requires repetition). Recognize that all opponents require repetition upon recovery.
Opponents see signaling with non-head parts as an invalid substitute, thus necessitating a repeat.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand that the Hanafi school does not require repetition because the person performed the prayer to the best of ability (istihsan). The ima' with the head is the only valid substitute; using other parts does not render the original prayer invalid, only the ima' itself is deficient, but the prayer is complete as a concession.
The Hanafi view is lenient: validity of the prayer is preserved despite imperfect ima'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a Hanafi follower who signaled with eyes or heart does not repeat the prayer after recovery; a Shafi'i follower would have to repeat it. This difference affects real-life practice for the sick.
The ikhtilaf is about whether the prayer is valid and whether qada' is required.

Qada of Missed Prayers During Illness: The 24-Hour Threshold and Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qada for illness duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two consensus cases and the two disputed cases regarding qada of prayers during illness, and state the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the borderline scenarios.

Core Idea
Consciousness and duration together determine qada: consensus on clear cases, ikhtilaf on mixed ones.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about when a sick person must make up missed prayers. The early authorities agreed on two straightforward cases: if unconsciousness lasts more than a full day and night, no makeup; if consciousness lasts less than a day, makeup is required. But what happens when the illness lasts longer but the person is conscious, or shorter but the person is unconscious? Later Hanafi scholars debated this, with the author of al-Hidayah siding with obligation and others with exemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two consensus cases
Locate in the text the two scenarios where all scholars agree. The first: illness more than 24 hours with unconsciousness → no qada. The second: illness less than 24 hours with consciousness → qada required.
Note that the threshold is a full day and night (24 hours).
2
Identify the two disputed cases
The text mentions two borderline scenarios: (a) illness more than 24 hours but conscious, and (b) illness less than 24 hours but unconscious. These are not covered by the early consensus and became the subject of ikhtilaf among later mashayikh.
3
Understand the ikhtilaf among mashayikh
Some mashayikh (including the author of al-Hidayah) ruled that qada is obligatory in these cases; others (al-Bazdawi al-Saghir and Qadi Khan) ruled it is not obligatory. Both opinions have basis in Hanafi usul.
4
Trace the tarjih (preference)
The text records that the choice of the author of al-Hidayah is for obligation, while al-Bazdawi al-Saghir and Qadi Khan choose exemption. Later fatwa often relies on the Hidayah position as more widely accepted.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
In contemporary practice, the preferred view is that qada is obligatory in both disputed cases. However, a mufti may also accommodate the other view as a legitimate ikhtilaf, especially where hardship is involved.

Falling Ill Mid-Prayer: Continuation in Reduced Form
Ruling on completing prayer when falling ill mid-prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for when a healthy person falls ill during prayer and apply the principle of building the lesser upon the greater.

Core Idea
A prayer started in a complete form can be completed in a lesser form without invalidation.

When you begin prayer in health but are struck by illness mid-prayer, you do not need to restart. The Hanafi school provides a clear path: continue in whatever posture your condition allows. This ruling is based on a rational principle: the greater (standing) inherently includes the lesser (sitting). Let's unpack the progression and the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting point
The prayer began with the person in a healthy state, standing, capable of full movements (ruku and sujud).
The prayer is validly initiated with all conditions.
2
Recognize the impediment
An illness occurs that prevents continuation of standing. This is the trigger for the ruling.
The impediment must be genuine and not deliberately induced.
3
Apply the progression of postures
If he can sit, he sits and performs ruku and sujud normally. If he cannot bow/prostrate, he signals with his head while sitting. If he cannot sit, he lies on his back and signals.
This stepwise reduction maintains the prayer's validity.
4
Understand the principle: binā' al-adnā 'alā al-a'lā
The more complete form (standing) 'builds' the lesser form (sitting); hence transitioning is valid. This is analogous to a standing imam leading sitting followers.
This principle is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
5
Conclude: no new intention or restart required
The prayer continues with the same niyyah and takbirat al-ihram. No invalidation occurs.
Contrast with schools that require restart when ability changes.

The Obligation of Sajdat al-Tilawah: Wajib vs Sunnah and the Persian Recitation Dispute
Opening of the chapter: ruling, intra-Hanafi dispute on Persian, and consensus for Arabic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sajdat al-tilawah as wajib, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding Persian recitation, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position after Abu Hanifa's retraction.

Core Idea
Sajdat al-tilawah is wajib on reciter and listener; for Persian recitation, the listener must understand to be obliged.

This lesson opens the chapter on prostration of recitation from al-Jawhara al-Nayira. The author begins by establishing the default ruling—wajib—and immediately introduces a key intra-Hanafi dispute: what if the Quran is recited in Persian? The passage also touches on the cross-madhhab disagreement with Malik and Shafi'i, who consider it sunnah. We will walk through the proof structure and the important tarjih (weighing) that settled the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The passage begins by stating that sajdat al-tilawah is wajib in all the specified places. Note the distinction: it is wajib in practice (amalan), not as a matter of belief (i'tiqadan).
This is a standard Hanafi nuance: wajib is less than fard in epistemological certainty.
2
Contrast the cross-madhhab view
The text records that Malik and al-Shafi'i consider it sunnah. This is a legacy ikhtilaf that goes back to different interpretations of the evidence.
The Hanafi position is based on proofs they consider stronger; we'll see the ijma' reference later.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on Persian recitation
Abu Hanifa held that the listener must prostrate even if they do not understand Persian. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) required understanding. Explore the reasoning: the obligation stems from the act of recitation of Quran, but if the meaning is not conveyed in a non-Arabic tongue, understanding may be a condition.
4
Apply the tarjih: Abu Hanifa's retraction
The text states that Abu Hanifa reportedly retracted to the view of the Sahibayn, and reliance (i'timad) is upon that. Thus the mufta bihi is that the listener is obliged only if they understand the Persian recitation.
This retraction is a famous example of an Imam changing his position.
5

The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Sajdahs in Surah al-Hajj and Surah Sad
Inter-madhhab dispute on specific sajdah verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school recognizes only one sajdah in Surah al-Hajj and treats the sajdah in Surah Sad as a recitational prostration, contrasting with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Hanafis: one sajdah in Hajj (the first), and Sad's sajdah is tilawah; Shafi'is: two in Hajj, Sad's is shukr.

This lesson focuses on a well-known ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding specific verses of sajdah. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira directly addresses two points: the number of prostrations in Surah al-Hajj and the nature of the sajdah in Surah Sad. Understanding the reasoning behind each school's position sharpens one's ability to evaluate textual evidence (istidlal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed surahs and the Hanafi position
The passage states: 'In Surah al-Hajj, there is one sajdah according to us, and it is the first. And the sajdah in Surah Sad is according to us a sajdat al-tilawah.'
Note that the Hanafi position differs from Shafi'i on both suras.
2
Present the Shafi'i position
According to al-Shafi'i, there are two sajdahs in Surah al-Hajj (both verses 18 and 77 are considered sajdah verses), and the sajdah in Surah Sad is a sajdat shukr (prostration of thanks), not a recitational prostration.
This leads to a practical difference: Shafi'is do not perform sajdah for Sad in prayer.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof for al-Hajj
The passage explains: 'As for the second sajdah in al-Hajj, it is not according to us a sajdat al-tilawah because it is connected to ruku', and that is a command for prayer, not for prostration.' The second verse (ayat 77) says 'ya ayyuha alladhina amanu irka'u wa usjudu', which is a command to pray, not a prostration verse.
The Hanafi school distinguishes between general commands to pray and specific sajdah verses.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof for Surah Sad
The Hanafi position treats the verse in Sad (ayat 24: 'wa kharra raki'an wa anaba') as a sajdat al-tilawah because it describes a prostration and is thus included among the sajdah verses. The text does not elaborate the proof here but implies a different reading of the same verse.
The Shafi'i view is that this sajdah was one of gratitude (shukr) and not a general recitational prostration.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical impact)
The consequence is that in the Hanafi school, when reciting Surah al-Hajj in prayer, only one sajdah is performed (at verse 18), while Shafi'is perform two. For Surah Sad, Hanafis perform a sajdat al-tilawah (even in prayer), while Shafi'is do not consider that verse a sajdah at all for prayer.
This is a clear practical difference that affects the validity of the prayer if not followed correctly.

Conditions for the Listener's Obligation in Sajdat al-Tilawah
Conditions and exceptions for listener's obligation in sajdat al-tilawah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which sajdat al-tilawah becomes wajib upon the listener, including exceptions for legal capacity, state of the reciter, and multiple hearings.

Core Idea
The listener's obligation depends on their own legal capacity and understanding of non-Arabic recitation, not on the reciter's purity.

The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira continues with a detailed discussion of the scenarios that affect the listener's obligation. While the general rule is that the sajdah is wajib on the reciter and listener alike, the author enumerates exceptions based on the listener's eligibility (taklif) and some special cases like hearing from a sleeping person or an echo. This lesson unpacks those conditions.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The text says: 'Upon the reciter and the listener, whether he intends to hear the Quran or not.' This establishes the default: the listener is obliged even if not intentionally listening.
The rule is broad: any hearing triggers the obligation.
2
Identify exceptions based on the reciter's state
The reciter's state (junub, haid, kafir, etc.) does not affect the listener's obligation; the sajdah is still wajib on the listener. The text lists these cases explicitly to refute a possible counterargument.
This is important: the obligation attaches to the listener independently.
3
Identify exceptions based on the listener's legal capacity
If the listener is not legally responsible (saghira, majnun, kafir, etc.), the sajdah is not wajib upon them. The text uses the criterion of those 'upon whom prayer is not obligatory' to define non-responsibility.
Legal capacity (ahliyyah) is a condition for obligation.
4
Discuss special cases: hearing from a sleeping, unconscious, or insane reciter
There are two narrations (riwayatan) on whether the listener is obliged when the reciter is asleep, unconscious, or insane. The text mentions that the more correct (asahh) is that it is not obligatory, but fatwa literature sometimes says it is obligatory.
This shows the flexibility within the school on edge cases.
5
Apply the rules to multiple hearings and the imam
If one recites and then hears the same verse from another in the same gathering, only one sajdah is due. Hearing from an echo does not obligate. The imam's recitation obligates both the imam and the followers to prostrate.
These are practical scenarios that arise in congregational settings.

Core Idea
In congregational prayer, only the imam's recitation of a sajda ayah obligates prostration; a follower's recitation does not.

Sajdat al-tilawah (prostration of recitation) is an act of worship that becomes obligatory under specific conditions. When praying in congregation, the imam's recitation obligates both him and the followers to prostrate. However, if a follower recites an ayah of sajdah, the obligation does not transfer to others. Sajdat al-Tilawah When the Follower Recites: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on follower's recitation of sajda ayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on sajdat al-tilawah when the follower recites and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute with its practical consequence.

Core Idea
A follower's recitation of a sajda ayah does not obligate prostration, because the imam's recitation suffices.

Fajr prayer, you are following the imam. You recite an ayah of sajda under your breath. Should you prostrate? Should the imam? This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah tackles exactly that question, clarifying an intra-Hanafi dispute between the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad. The ruling hinges on the nature of the follower's recitation and the structure of imamate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act: a follower recites an ayah of sajda during congregational prayer. The dispute: does this obligate sajdat al-tilawah for anyone, and if so, when?
The text uses 'عندهما' to mark the majority view.
2
State the majority position (Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf)
According to the two shaykhs, no prostration is required—neither during the prayer nor after it. This applies to both the imam and the follower.
The ruling applies equally to loud and silent prayers, though it is recommended not to recite such ayahs in silent prayer.
3
State the dissenting position (Imam Muhammad)
Muhammad holds that the cause (recitation of the ayah) has been established and there is no preventer after prayer; therefore, prostration becomes wajib after the prayer.
During the prayer, Muhammad agrees that prostration is not required because it would conflict with the imam's role.
4
Analyse the majority's proof
The text cites two hadith: (1) The Prophet told a reciter 'You were our imam; if you had prostrated, we would have prostrated'—showing the reciter should lead, not the imam. (2) 'Whoever has an imam, the imam's recitation is his recitation'—proving the follower's recitation is legally ineffective.
The follower is legally restricted (mahjur) from independent recitation; the imam's recitation substitutes for it.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference is clear: under the majority, if a follower recites an ayah of sajda, no one prostrates ever. Under Muhammad, both imam and follower must prostrate after completing the prayer.
Most Hanafi fatwa works follow the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Sajdat al-Tilawah from an Outsider During Prayer
Ruling on sajdat al-tilawah during prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on performing sajdat al-tilawah during prayer for a verse heard from an outsider and identify the relied-upon opinion (mufta bihi).

Core Idea
A prohibited action does not invalidate prayer but also does not fulfill an obligation.

When someone in prayer hears a verse of prostration from a non-praying person, the ruling involves a subtle distinction between deficiency and invalidation. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, its reasoning, and an intra-school disagreement between Imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Initial Ruling
The passage addresses a group in prayer who hear a sajdah verse from someone not in their prayer. The immediate ruling: do not prostrate during prayer; prostrate after.
This is the default recommendation to avoid prohibition.
2

The prostration is not prayer-related (ghayr ṣalātiyyah), so inserting it is prohibited (manhi). The obligation of sajdah is complete (kāmilah), and a deficient act (due to prohibition) cannot fulfill it.
The distinction between deficiency (naqṣ) and invalidation (fasād) is key.
3

Despite being prohibited, the prostration is still an action of prayer (min afʿāl al-ṣalāh), so it does not invalidate. The text explicitly states 'lam tufsid ʿalayhim al-ṣalāh'.
Prohibition does not automatically imply invalidation in Hanafi fiqh.
4

Muhammad al-Shaybani (recorded in al-Nawadir) holds that it does invalidate. The relied-upon view (al-aṣaḥḥ) is that of the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) that it does not.
This ikhtilaf hinges on whether a prohibited act in prayer is treated as a mubtil.
5

The commentator declares the non-invalidating view as al-aṣaḥḥ, making it the operative position. This is from the ẓāhir al-riwāyah (core texts).
When giving fatwa, the prayer is valid; the sajdah must be made up later.

Core Idea
Repetitive physical work like treading grain or weaving breaks the sitting, requiring separate prostrations, while moving within one mosque does not.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the rulings on the repetition of sajda al-tilawah (prostration of recitation). The author begins by defining what constitutes a break in the 'majlis' (sitting) that would necessitate a new prostration. The majlis is only changed by eating to fullness, drinking to quenchedness, manual work, or excessive speech. Engaging in dhikr or Quran recitation does not break it, nor does changing posture, sleeping while sitting, or mounting and dismounting an animal before it walks. If one recites the verse twice in the same location while treading grain, weaving cloth, or turning a millstone, the obligation is repeated due to the change of place (or activity). Similarly, moving from branch to branch repeats the obligation. In a mosque, all corners are considered one place (like for prayer), so only one prostration is required. For swimming, the obligation is repeated unless in a small tank (some say not). Walking changes place with each step, so each recitation requires a prostration. In a house or stationary/moving ship, one prostration suffices. For a riding animal, if in prayer, one prostration suffices even if reciting multiple times while it moves; if not in prayer, each recitation requires a separate prostration because the animal's movement changes the place. If one recites before the animal walks, one prostration on ground suffices; after it walks, two prostrations. If one recites in prayer and the prayer becomes invalid, the prostration is not repeated because it was valid in that prayer. A menstruating woman who recites the verse in prayer and does not prostrate before menstruation: the obligation is dropped because she is not required to pray then.

Unity of Place and Verse in Sajdah al-Tilawah
Ruling on sajdah when verse and place are unified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why one sajdah suffices when the listener later recites the same verse in the same place.

Core Idea
Unity of verse and place merges multiple obligations into one sajdah.

When reciter and listener are in the same session, the obligation of sajdah is tied to both the verse and the place. If the listener later recites that same verse in that place, only one prostration is needed. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage describes a reciter reciting an ayat as-Sajdah and a listener hearing it in the same place. The listener then recites that verse himself.
Both the reciter and listener are in the same physical location.
2
State the ruling
One prostration suffices for the listener, despite having both heard and recited it.
3
Extract the proof (talil)
The ruling is justified by 'unity of the verse and the place' (ittiḥād al-āyah wa-l-makān).
The proof is a causal statement, not a textual source.
4
Apply the reasoning
The obligation to prostrate attaches to the specific verse and location; since both are identical, the listener's recitation does not create a new obligation.
This avoids unnecessary duplication.

Commentary
This section continues the rulings on sajdat al-tilawah, focusing on the effect of movement of reciter and listener. Sajdah Tilawah on a Riding Animal: Basic Ruling and City Exception
Ruling and exception for sajdah tilawah on a riding animal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on performing sajdah tilawah on a riding animal and identify the exception for riders inside the city.

Core Idea
Prostration on a riding animal is valid for a rider, except inside the city.

When travelling, a rider may recite a prostration verse while on his mount. The Hanafi school allows him to perform the sajdah tilawah on the animal itself, without dismounting. However, this leniency has a limitation: it applies only when the rider is outside the city. This lesson explains the basic ruling and the critical exception noted by al-Halwani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing sajdah tilawah (prostration of recitation) on a riding animal. The ruling is jā’iz (permissible/valid).
2
Locate the qualification
Al-Halwani adds that this ruling is restricted to a rider outside the city (khārij al-miṣr). Inside the city, it does not suffice according to Abu Hanifa.
The city (miṣr) refers to a settled area where the rider is no longer considered a traveller.
3
Understand the rationale
The inside city exception likely arises because the obligation to prostrate on the ground is stronger when one is not travelling; the rider can easily dismount and prostrate properly.
4
Apply the ruling
When presented with a scenario, first determine if the rider is inside or outside the city. If outside, sajdah on the animal is valid; if inside, it is not and must be done on the ground.
This distinction is specific to Abu Hanifa's view as reported by al-Halwani.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira discusses the validity of performing sajdah tilawah on a riding animal. The Hanafi school (as per Abu Hanifa and the two companions) holds that if a rider recites a prostration verse and prostrates on his animal, it is valid. Al-Halwani qualifies that this applies outside a city; inside a city, Abu Hanifa rules that it does not suffice because the obligation to prostrate on the ground is stronger when one is in a settled area. A more interesting scenario: if a rider recites a prostration verse, dismounts without prostrating, then remounts and prostrates on the animal, the Hanafi view (attributed to 'us' i.e., Abu Hanifa and the companions) holds that it is valid. Zafar ibn al-Hudhayl (a Hanafi jurist who sometimes differed) argues that once the person dismounts, the obligation becomes tied to a ground prostration (with sujood on the earth), so later prostrating on the animal is invalid. Zafar draws an analogy (qiyas) to a case where someone recites on the ground and then mounts: prostration on the animal would not suffice. The Rider Who Dismounts: Hanafi View vs. Zafar's Qiyas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sajdah tilawah after dismounting and remounting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on a rider who dismounts then remounts to prostrate, and critique Zafar's qiyas analogy.

Core Idea
A rider who dismounts after reciting may remount and prostrate; Zafar's qiyas fails because the initial obligation was with gesture.

A rider recites a prostration verse but dismounts without prostrating. Later, he remounts and prostrates on the animal. Is this valid? The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) says yes; Zafar ibn al-Hudhayl says no. This lesson dissects the dispute, the qiyas used, and the Hanafi rebuttal. It also covers a related case of delaying prostration from sunrise to sunset, and a separate ruling on fourteen prostrations for reciting the entire Quran in one sitting.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and Hanafi position
The scenario: rider recites, dismounts, later remounts and prostrates on animal. Hanafi: valid (yujzi'uhu).
2
Identify Zafar's objection and analogy
Zafar says invalid because after dismount, the obligation becomes without gesture (i.e., ground prostration). He analogizes to reciting on ground then mounting: prostration on animal would not suffice.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy).
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafi: the initial obligation was with gesture (īmā'), so it remains a gesture obligation. The analogy fails because the far' (rider who dismounts) has a different initial state (rider) than the asl (ground reciter).
The illa (cause) in asl is 'obligation without gesture', but in far' the obligation was with gesture initially.
4
Note the extension to sunrise/sunset
The same dispute applies if one delays prostration from sunrise to sunset: Hanafi allows, Zafar disallows based on same reasoning.
This shows the principle extends to delayed prostration within a day.
5
Identify the secondary ruling on full recitation
If one recites the entire Quran in one sitting, fourteen prostrations are obligatory due to different verses (i.e., fourteen distinct sajdah verses).
This is a separate ruling but contextually related to sajdah tilawah.
