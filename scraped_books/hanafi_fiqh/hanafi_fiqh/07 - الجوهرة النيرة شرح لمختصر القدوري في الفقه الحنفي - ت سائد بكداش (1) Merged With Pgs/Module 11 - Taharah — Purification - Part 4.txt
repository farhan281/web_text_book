Two Animals in a Well: Complete Removal of Water
A concise legal rule about well water purification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for two cats, two hens, or two pigeons dying in a well and distinguish it from the ruling for a single similar animal.

Core Idea
When two small animals die in a well, all water must be removed.

This lesson presents a concise ruling from Al-Quduri's matn regarding well water purification. The text specifies that if two cats, two hens, or two pigeons fall dead into a well, the entire water must be removed. This contrasts with cases involving a single animal, where a specific number of buckets suffices. Understanding this ruling helps you grasp how the Hanafi school scales impurity based on the number and size of animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the animals and count
Note that the ruling applies specifically to two cats, two hens, or two pigeons. The dual form in Arabic indicates exactly two.
The same ruling likely applies to pairs of similar small animals.
2

The text ordains: 'yenzahu kullu al-ma' – all water must be drawn out and removed from the well.
This is the complete purification: emptying the well entirely.
3
Contrast with a single animal
For a single mouse, the established ruling is to remove 40–60 buckets. For a single cat or hen, a lesser amount than full removal may apply. The doubling triggers full removal.
The exact amount for a single cat/hen is not given here, but it is less than all water.
4
Consider the rationale
Two animals increase the impurity load. The Hanafi school determines that the well water becomes substantially impure, necessitating complete removal.
This is based on the principle that impurity (najasa) spreads and accumulates.
5
Apply to other pairs
By analogy, any pair of small animals of similar size (e.g., two sparrows) would likely receive the same ruling.
Qiyas: the illah is the doubling of carcasses contaminating a confined water source.

Ruling for swelling/decay and severed parts

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Swelling or decay of a dead animal in a well requires removing all water, even from a small part, unless the cut is sealed against moisture.

This lesson clarifies a practical ruling in purification (taharah). You already know that if an animal dies in a well, the water becomes impure and must be removed in varying amounts depending on type and size. Here, the matn and commentary address two extreme conditions: swelling and decay, where the entire well must be emptied regardless of size. Additionally, a severed part like a mouse's tail is treated similarly due to inherent moisture, but the ruling changes if the cut is sealed.

Swelling (intifakh) means the animal's limbs disintegrate; decay (tafasukh) means the body breaks into pieces. These conditions indicate severe contamination.

When swelling or decay occurs, all water in the well must be removed, regardless of the animal's original size. The general rule of differentiated buckets does not apply.

A severed part (e.g., mouse tail) that falls into the well causes full removal if it contains moisture. However, if the cut is sealed (e.g., with a candle), only the water inside that part is considered impure and must be removed.
The presence or absence of moisture determines the extent of contamination.

Measuring Buckets for Well Water Removal
Explains how to measure bucket counts using average, specific, or substitute standards.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method for determining the number of buckets to remove from a well when using different bucket sizes.

Core Idea
The number of buckets is measured by volume, not count — the average bucket sets the standard.

When a well is impure, a specific number of buckets must be removed. But what if wells use different bucket sizes? This lesson clarifies how the Hanafi school measures the required removal: by the average bucket of the region, or by a standard one-sa' bucket, and how larger buckets are counted proportionally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard for measurement
The primary standard is the average bucket (al-dalw al-wasaṭ) commonly used for wells in that town. Consider what is typical, not any particular bucket.
Custom ('urf) determines the average — it may vary by region.
2
Apply the well-specific rule
For a specific well, its own bucket is the measure. If the well has a bucket, use that bucket to count the required number.
This is the default when the well's bucket is known.
3
Handle a well without a bucket
If the well has no bucket, fashion a standard bucket that holds exactly one sa' (approximately 2.6 liters). Then count the required number of bucketfuls using that standard bucket.
One sa' is the recognized substitute measure.
4
Account for a larger bucket
If a large bucket is used for removal, it is counted by its equivalent volume in average buckets. For example, if a large bucket holds twice the average, each large bucketful counts as two average bucketfuls.
The objective is removing the required volume, not the number of actions.

Use of a Large Bucket for Well Purification: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on bucket counting for well purification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on using a large bucket for well purification and evaluate the objection raised by Zufar and al-Hasan ibn Ziyad.

Core Idea
A large bucket counts proportionally because the goal is fewer drips; flow analogy fails since flow exists without drawing.

This lesson examines a specific dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the calculation of bucket counts when purifying a well. The matn of al-Quduri allows using a large bucket, counting its volume as equivalent to multiple middle buckets. However, two notable Hanafi scholars, Zufar and al-Hasan ibn Ziyad, objected based on the analogy of flowing water. We will unpack the reasoning on both sides and understand why the majority view prevailed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The matn states the number of buckets is measured by the middle bucket; if a large bucket is used, it is counted proportionally. The reason given: 'the purpose is achieved with less dripping.'
2
Analyze the objection
Zufar and al-Hasan argue that repeated drawing makes well water like flowing water (jaryan), and a single large draw does not achieve that flowing property.
The objectors consider the flowing nature essential to the purification process.
3
Examine the Hanafi response
The response: 'The meaning of flowing is negated because it occurs without drawing.' That is, the flowing nature of well water (replenishment) exists independently of drawing.
Thus the flowing analogy is irrelevant to the count of buckets.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, the majority view is mufta bihi: it is permissible to use a large bucket and count it proportionally. The objectors' view is considered weak.
The practical difference is that most Hanafis follow the matn position.
5
Understand the authority weight
The matn's position represents the relied-upon view (zahir al-riwayah), while the objection is a nawadir opinion.
This hierarchy is important for fatwa and daily practice.

Purification of a Flowing Well and Methods of Estimation
Ruling on removing water from a flowing well and methods to estimate the required amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purifying a flowing well and apply Abu Hanifa's preferred estimation method based on the testimony of two knowledgeable men.

Core Idea
Estimation by two knowledgeable men is preferred over a fixed bucket count for a flowing well.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses a practical problem: how to purify a well that never runs dry. The classical Hanafi texts recognize that a flowing well (bi'r ma'een) cannot be completely emptied, so when it becomes najis, an estimate of the original water must be removed. 1

2
Understand the problem
A flowing well cannot be completely emptied because water flows in continuously. So how much must be removed? The answer: an amount equal to what was originally in the well before contamination.
3

Imam Muhammad reports a specific number (200-300 buckets). Abu Hanifa provides two estimation methods: (1) rely on the well owners' statement after removal, (2) have two knowledgeable men descend and estimate. Note the difference in approach.

The commentator prefers the second method of Abu Hanifa and cites evidence: the Quranic verse requiring two just witnesses (ذَوَا عَدْلٍ مِنكُمْ). How does this verse apply? It is used by analogy (qiyas) to show that estimation by two upright men is reliable.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference between the two views? With Abu Hanifa's method, the amount varies depending on the well's size and the estimation. With Imam Muhammad's, a fixed range is used. The mufta bihi is the estimation method.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Estimating Well Contamination Time
Intra-Hanafi dispute on estimating time of impurity in a well

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding repetition of prayers when a dead mouse is found in a well, and apply the usul principle 'certainty is not removed by doubt' to a purification scenario.

Core Idea
Certainty of past purity is not overridden by doubt about when impurity occurred, but if impurity is certain, estimates may be made.

When a dead mouse is found in a well used for wudu, a classic intra-Hanafi dispute arises: must one repeat past prayers? Abu Hanifa uses signs like bloating to estimate the time of contamination, while the Sahibayn insist on certainty. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and its practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
A dead mouse is discovered in a well. The well was used for wudu. No one knows exactly when the mouse fell in. This triggers a dispute about whether past prayers remain valid.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and their usul principle
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that no prayers are repeated until the time of contamination is known with certainty. Their basis is the principle 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt). The well was certainly pure in the past; the time of impurity is doubtful, so the original certainty remains.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
State Abu Hanifa's counterargument and use of signs
Abu Hanifa argues that the presence of the dead animal provides certainty of impurity now, which overrides the doubt. He uses bloating (intifakh) as an indicator: no bloating means death within a day, bloating means up to three days. He analogizes this to the rule that prayer over a grave is only valid within three days due to decomposition.
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas (analogy) and external signs.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, one must repeat prayers for the estimated period (1 or 3 days). Under the Sahibayn, no repetition is required unless the exact time is known. The difference affects daily practice.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view)
The story of Abu Yusuf changing his opinion after seeing a bird drop a mouse into a well strongly supports the Sahibayn's view. Later Hanafi authorities generally adopt the Sahibayn's position as mufta bihi, though some acknowledge Abu Hanifa's reasoning as valid.
Tarjih is based on later consensus and the weight of the certainty principle.

The Five Categories of Su'ur (Leftover Water) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines five categories of su'ur with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the five types of su'ur according to Hanafi fiqh and state the ruling for each type.

Core Idea
Su'ur is pure from humans and halal meat animals, with two exceptions; impure from dog, pig, and beasts; includes disliked and disputed cases.

The ruling on leftover water (su'ur) is essential for proper purification (taharah). This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the Hanafi classification into five types: pure by consensus, impure by consensus, disputed, disliked, and doubtful. Understanding these categories helps you determine when water remains pure for wudu.

Guided Path

Understand that su'ur is the water left after drinking from a vessel. Its purity depends on the drinker's status and affects validity of wudu.

Ruling on Su'r of Free-Range Hen, Birds of Prey, House Animals, and Cats
Explaining the dislike of su'r of certain animals and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the su'r (leftover water) of a free-range hen, birds of prey, house animals, and cats, and explain the reasoning based on impurity contact, confinement, and the distinction between beak and tongue.

Core Idea
Su'r of these animals is makruh (disliked) due to impurity contact; confinement removes dislike for birds but not cats.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on leftover water (su'r) of certain animals: free-range hens, birds of prey, house-dwelling animals like snakes and mice, and cats. The matn states that their su'r is makruh (disliked), and the commentary elaborates on the reasons, exceptions, and a critical objection regarding birds of prey. We will walk through the legal reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the animals and the ruling
The text states: the su'r of a free-range hen, birds of prey, and house animals (snake, mouse) is disliked. Know that 'makruh' here means tanzihan (slightly disliked).
The cat is mentioned later separately.
2
Understand the cause (ʿilla) for the dislike
The dislike arises because these animals come into contact with impurities when free-ranging. If they are confined so that their beaks cannot reach filth, the original ruling of purity (based on edible meat) applies, and the su'r is not disliked.
This shows that the dislike is due to probable impurity, not inherent impurity.
3
Analyze the qiyas for birds of prey
Birds of prey are analogous to free-range hens because both eat carrion and contact impurities when free. Thus their su'r is disliked. If confined, the dislike vanishes because they drink with beaks (bone, pure), unlike cats.
This is a clear qiyas jaliy.
4
Address the objection about land predators
An objector argues: birds of prey should be like land predators (e.g., dogs) whose su'r is impure because their meat is not eaten. The response: birds drink with beaks (bone) not tongues (flesh), and necessity (they are ubiquitous) demands leniency.
The key distinction is the drinking organ: bone vs. flesh.
5
Apply the cat's unique ruling
The cat's su'r is always disliked, even if confined, because it drinks with its tongue (flesh). Additional rulings: if a cat licks a limb, prayer is disliked without washing; if it eats from food, the remainder is disliked. For a rich person, it is disliked; for a poor person, it is not due to necessity. If a cat eats a mouse and drinks immediately, the water becomes impure unless it delays long enough to wash its mouth.
The hadith 'innahā min aṭ-ṭawwāfīna' indicates permissibility of cat's su'r, but here it's used to justify not ruling impurity.

The Doubtful Su'r of Donkey and Mule
Ruling statement introducing the su'r of donkey and mule as doubtful.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the su'r of a donkey and mule, distinguish between the two opinions on the nature of the doubt, and apply the practical consequences regarding sweat, saliva, and milk.

Core Idea
The su'r of a donkey and mule is mashkuk—doubtful—neither definitively pure nor impure, with practical concessions.

In Hanafi fiqh, the category of 'mashkuk' (doubtful) is recognized for certain substances where the evidence is inconclusive. This passage addresses the su'r (leftover water) of donkeys and mules, placing it in this category. The discussion then explores whether the doubt concerns its purity (taharah) or its purifying capacity (tahuriyyah), leading to different practical rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act is the su'r (leftover water) of a donkey or mule after drinking. The text classifies it as 'mashkuk fīh' (doubtful). Recognize that this is an intermediate category between tahir (pure) and najis (impure) in Hanafi fiqh.
Other schools may not have this intermediate category.
2
Analyze the two opinions on the doubt
The text presents two views: (1) The doubt is about its purity (taharah) – if it were pure, it would be purifying, but since we doubt, it is treated as impure. (2) The doubt is about its purifying capacity (tahuriyyah) – it is definitely pure (tahir) but we doubt whether it can remove ritual impurity.
The first opinion is attributed to al-Sayrafi; the second is unnamed but also held by some Hanafi scholars.
3
Derive practical implications
According to the first opinion: sweat and saliva are excused on body and clothes in small amounts (due to necessity), but its milk is considered impure (najis) – if it soils more than a dirham's area, prayer is invalid, and drinking it is prohibited. The second opinion would treat the su'r as pure but not purifying, meaning it is permissible to use but not for wudu.
The text only gives rulings for the first opinion; the second is incomplete.
4
Note the mufta bihi
The text does not explicitly state which opinion is relied upon for fatwa. Later Hanafi sources often adopt the second view (pure but not purifying) for ease, but both are considered valid within the school.
This is a memorization anchor: remember the category and the two opinions.

The Su'ur of Donkey and Mule: A Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute on Doubt
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the su'ur of donkey and mule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two positions regarding the doubt in su'ur of donkey and mule, and explain the preferred Hanafi ruling that it is pure (tahir) but not purifying (tahur).

Core Idea
The su'ur of donkey and mule is pure but not purifying according to the relied-upon Hanafi view — the doubt is in tahuriyyah, not taharah.

This passage dives into a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about the residual water left by donkeys and mules. While the su'ur is classified as 'mashkook fihi' (subject to doubt), the key disagreement is about the nature of that doubt: does it concern the water's purity itself or only its purifying power? Understanding this distinction has real implications for daily practice, especially for wudu and dealing with milk and sweat. We will follow the reasoning of early authorities like al-Sayrafi and the later preference of al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of su'ur and its categories
Recognize that su'ur is the water left after an animal drinks. The Hanafi school classifies su'ur into five types; the su'ur of donkeys and mules is the fifth type, which is mashkook fihi.
Other types include pure/purifying (e.g., human, horse) and impure (e.g., pig).
2
Identify the two positions on the nature of the doubt
The passage presents two views: (1) the doubt is about purity itself — meaning the su'ur may be impure (supported by al-Sayrafi); (2) the doubt is about purifying power — the su'ur is definitely pure but not capable of ritual purification (preferred by al-Hidayah and al-Wajiz).
Note that both views agree the su'ur is mashkook fihi; the dispute is over what exactly is doubtful.
3
Analyze the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf) of each view
List the branching rulings: According to the first view, milk is impure, sweat is excused only if not excessive; wudu with that water is invalid. According to the second view, milk and sweat are pure, and wudu is valid if the water does not overwhelm.
The passage also mentions a further dispute on whether such water can purify other impurities.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position and its authority
The preferred view, stated as 'al-ashah' by al-Hidayah, is that the su'ur is pure but not purifying. The tarjih is based on the works of al-Hidayah, al-Wajiz, and later authorities like al-Bazdawi who consider excessive amount.
This is the position relied upon for fatwa today.

Core Idea
When only donkey's water is available, both wudu and tayammum are needed, but order does not matter.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a person has no pure water but only donkey's leftover water (su'r himar). The Hanafi school requires both wudu (using that water) and tayammum for one prayer. An intra-madhhab dispute arises over whether wudu must precede tayammum. Imam Zufar argued for a required order, while the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) held that any order is permissible. We will explore the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The person has no pure water, only donkey's su'r (and possibly another impure water). What does the matn rule?
The matn says: 'he performs wudu with both and performs tayammum, and whichever he begins with is permissible.'
2
State the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani agree: no specific order is required. Either wudu or tayammum can come first.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position and the mufta bihi.

Imam Zufar holds that wudu must precede tayammum. His proof: donkey's su'r is water whose use is obligatory, so it resembles absolute (pure) water; thus wudu is the primary purification.
Zufar's view is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

The majority reply: 'the purifier is one of them' (al-mutahhir ahaduhu) – meaning only one of the two acts is actually purifying (either the wudu or the tayammum), but we cannot be certain which. Therefore, both must be done for the prayer, but order is irrelevant.
They emphasize 'combination without order' (al-jam' duna al-tartib).

Under Zufar's view, one must do wudu first then tayammum; under the majority, either order is valid. The majority view is relied upon for fatwa.

Introduction to Tayammum: Definition and Proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of tayammum and identify its primary proofs from the Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Tayammum is the substitute for water purification, established by Quran and Sunnah, and is wajib when water is unavailable.

In this passage, the author explains why tayammum is placed after the rulings on wudu and ghusl: the substitute follows the original. We then learn the definition of tayammum, both linguistically (intention) and legally (using pure earth on the face and hands). Finally, the two primary proofs are cited: the Quranic verse '...perform tayammum with clean earth' and the hadith 'Tayammum is the purification of a Muslim as long as he does not find water.' Understanding this foundation is essential before delving into the detailed conditions and rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the reason for the chapter placement
Notice that the author, al-Quduri, discusses tayammum immediately after purification with water. The commentator explains that the substitute always follows the original. Understand this logical sequence in fiqh texts.
This reflects the principle that khilaf (substitute) comes after the asl (original).
2
Extract the definition of tayammum
The passage gives two definitions: linguistic (qasd/intention) and legal (using a portion of pure earth on the place of tayammum). Note that the legal definition focuses on the act and the material used.

3

The verse from An-Nisa (4:43) is cited: 'If you do not find water, then perform tayammum with clean earth.' The imperative verb 'fatayamamu' indicates obligation (wajib) according to Hanafi usul. The condition 'if you do not find water' defines the cause (sabab) for tayammum.

The hadith 'Tayammum is the purification of a Muslim as long as he does not find water' emphasizes that tayammum is a valid purification until water is found. It reinforces the Quranic ruling and clarifies that tayammum is a complete substitute.
The hadith is classified as hasan sahih by al-Tirmidhi, so it is a valid proof even though ahad.
5
Summarize the Hanafi position
Tayammum is wajib when water is absent or harmful. It substitutes for both wudu and ghusl. The definition and proofs are clear, and the ruling is agreed upon (muttafaq alayh).
Some schools limit tayammum to travel or specific conditions, but the Hanafi position is broad.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tayammum inside the city is impermissible except for fear of missing funeral prayer, Eid prayer, or cold for the junub.

The classical Hanafi position on tayammum sharply distinguishes between inside and outside a city. While traveling outside the city allows tayammum when water is absent, inside the city the default ruling is prohibition. This lesson examines the rare exceptions and the reasoning behind them, as explained in Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira.

The Two Strikes in Tayammum: Part of the Act or Preparatory?
Ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute and practical consequence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on whether the strikes in tayammum are integral, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to determine validity after a mid-act hadath.

Core Idea
The two strikes are wajib, but whether they are part of tayammum or preparatory determines the effect of a break between striking and wiping.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah addresses a subtle but legally significant dispute within the Hanafi school: are the two strikes of tayammum part of the act itself or merely preparatory? The answer changes the ruling when an interruption (hadath) occurs between striking and wiping. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to trace how a difference in conceptualizing an act's boundaries affects its validity.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling
The matn states that tayammum consists of two strikes. This is the foundation: the strikes are wajib (obligatory).
The obligation of two strikes is settled; the dispute is about their legal nature.
2
Identify the dispute
Two Hanafi scholars differ: Ibn Shuja' holds that the strikes are part of tayammum, while al-Isbijabi holds they are merely preparatory (not part of the act).
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf; both views are Hanafi.
3
Trace the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a person strikes, then breaks wudu (hadath) before wiping the face, or makes niyyah after striking, then: according to Ibn Shuja', the tayammum is invalid because part of the act was done and then nullified; according to al-Isbijabi, the tayammum is valid because the strikes are separate.
The same applies if niyyah is made after striking – intention must precede the act according to the view that strikes are part of the act.
4
Analyze the analogy
Al-Isbijabi analogizes to wudu: if one fills his hand with water for wudu, then breaks wudu, he may still use that water for wiping his face because the water-in-hand is not part of wudu. Similarly, the strikes are not part of tayammum.
This qiyas reveals the underlying principle: preparatory acts do not require purity.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on istiya'ab with practical consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on istiya'ab in tayammum and explain why removing a ring and interleaving fingers become obligatory when istiya'ab is adopted.

Core Idea
Tayammum stands in place of wudu; therefore, complete wiping of face and hands is wajib, just as complete washing is required in wudu.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: is complete wiping (isti'ab) of the face and hands required in tayammum, or does wiping the majority suffice? The text clarifies that the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that complete wiping is necessary, based on the analogy (qiyas) that tayammum substitutes for wudu. A minority narration from Abu Hanifa via Hasan ibn Ziyad holds that complete wiping is not a condition. Understanding this dispute helps you navigate conflicting transmissions within the Hanafi school and apply the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed condition
The act is tayammum. The disputed point is whether complete wiping (isti'ab) of the entire face and hands is a condition (shart) for its validity.
The text uses 'ليس بشرط' for the minority view, indicating that the majority considers it a condition.
2
State the two positions
The majority (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds that istiya'ab is wajib; the minority (Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa) holds that wiping the majority suffices and istiya'ab is not a condition.
The minority is transmitted via Hasan ibn Ziyad, which is considered a nawadir (rare) transmission.
3
Analyze the majority's proof
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to wudu: the hadith 'لا صلاة لمن لا وضوء له' (No salah for one without wudu) and the fact that tayammum is a substitute for wudu. Just as wudu requires complete washing of face and hands, tayammum requires complete wiping.
The text in Al-Hidayah states: 'because it stands in place of wudu' (لقيامه مقام الوضوء).
4
Apply the relied-upon position
If we adopt the position of istiya'ab, then removing a ring (خاتم) and interleaving fingers (تخليل) become obligatory to ensure the earth reaches the entire skin surface.
The footnote clarifies that this ruling also applies when wearing khuff, meaning the obligation to remove the ring remains even if one is wiping over leather socks.
5
Determine the authority weight
The mufta bihi is based on Zahir al-Riwayah, which carries more authority than the nawadir transmission of Hasan ibn Ziyad. Therefore, the position of istiya'ab is the one to act upon.
Al-Hidayah explicitly confirms this in the apparent transmission (ظاهر الرواية).

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah discusses the ruling of istiya'ab (complete wiping) in tayammum. It opens with the chapter heading on tayammum. The phrase 'جاز' likely concludes a previous ruling. Then it presents a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa that complete wiping is not a condition; it suffices to wipe the majority. However, the relied-upon position (stated after 'إذا قلنا بالاستيعاب') is that complete wiping is necessary, making it obligatory to remove a ring and interleave fingers to ensure full coverage of the skin. The author cites al-Hidayah, confirming that istiya'ab is obligatory in the apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah) because tayammum substitutes for wudu, which requires full washing. The footnote explains that the ruling about removing the ring applies when one is wearing a khuff (leather sock). This is a typical Hanafi legal reasoning: the ruling is derived via qiyas (analogy) to wudu. The narration from Hasan is a weak (gharib) transmission, but it represents a minority view within the school.

Key Terms
الاستيعاب (al-isti'ab): Complete wiping of the entire face and hands in tayammum.
الخاتم (al-khatam): Ring worn on finger; must be removed to allow earth to touch the skin underneath.
التخليل (al-takhlil): Interleaving fingers to let earth reach between them during tayammum.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The well-known and authoritative transmission from Imam Muhammad's six books.

Sunnah Acts of Tayammum and the Dispute on Dust Shakes
Listing sunnah acts and step-by-step method with an intra-Hanafi dispute on shakes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah acts of tayammum and distinguish the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the number of dust shakes, applying the principle of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
The sunnah of tayammum includes saying Bismillah, cupping the hands, and one dust shake according to the apparent transmission.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains the recommended (sunnah) elements of tayammum and provides the step-by-step method. A key intra-Hanafi disagreement is mentioned: Abu Yusuf holds that two dust shakes are sunnah, while the majority reliance is on one shake from Zahir al-Riwayah. Understanding this helps you apply the correct practice for tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah acts
List each sunnah act mentioned: tasmiyah, cupping/rotating hands, shaking once (or twice per Abu Yusuf). Note that soiling is not sunnah.
2
Compare the intra-Hanafi disagreement
State the Zahir al-Riwayah position (one shake) and Abu Yusuf's position (two shakes). Understand why the majority follows one.
The phrase 'fi zahir al-riwaya' indicates the authoritative transmission.
3
Follow the method narration
Walk through the two-strike process: first strike, shake, wipe face; second strike, shake, wipe right arm, then left.
4
Answer the why question
Reflect on why tayammum is only on face and hands as a substitute for washing.
The head and feet have different obligations in wudu.

Commentary
This passage details the sunnah acts and the physical method of performing tayammum. The sunnah acts include: saying 'Bismillah' before striking the ground, cupping and turning the hands, and shaking the dust once upon raising. There is a disagreement in the school: Abu Yusuf holds that two shakes should be performed, while the apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah) states one. Then it mentions that soiling oneself (talwith) is not required; the purpose is merely wiping, not becoming dirty. The method is then described: first strike the hands once on the earth, raise and shake them, wipe the face; second strike, then wipe the right arm from fingertips to elbow with the left hand's inner fingers, then the right forearm to the wrist with the left palm, and the right thumb with the left thumb. Repeat with the left hand. A question is then posed: why is tayammum restricted to face and hands? The answer is that tayammum is a substitute for ghusl (washing) which originally covers the entire body, but the head is wiped (in wudu) and the feet's obligation is between wiping and washing, so only face and hands are substituted.

Key Terms
التسمية (al-tasmiyah): Saying 'Bismillah' (in the name of Allah) before beginning tayammum.
النفضة (al-nafdah): Shaking off dust from the hands after striking the earth.
التلويث (al-talwith): Soiling the hands with mud or dirt; not required or intended.
