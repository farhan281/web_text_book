The Five Categories of Islamic Legal Subjects (Mashru'at)
Introduces a logical division of all fiqh topics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the five categories of mashru'at according to the Hanafi classification and identify the subcategories within each.

Core Idea
All Islamic legal rulings fall into five categories: beliefs, worship, transactions, penalties, and expiations.

The author of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah begins his work by providing a comprehensive yet concise classification of the entire scope of Islamic jurisprudence. This division helps the student of fiqh to conceptualize the different domains of Shari'ah rulings. Understanding this framework is the first step in organizing your study of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five main categories
The text states 'al-mashru'at khamsah: i'tiqadat, 'ibadat, mu'amalat, 'uqubat, kaffarat.' Memorize these five terms and their English equivalents: beliefs, acts of worship, transactions, penalties, expiations.
Each category represents a distinct area of legal rulings.
2
Examine the subcategories of each
For each main category, the text lists five sub-items. For beliefs: belief in Allah, angels, books, messengers, the Last Day. For worship: prayer, fasting, zakah, hajj, jihad. For transactions: exchanges, marriages, disputes, trusts, partnerships. For penalties: deterrents for killing, stealing, illicit sex, slander, apostasy. For expiations: killing, zihar, breaking fast, oath, hajj violations.
Note that jihad is included under worship (a communal obligation).
3
Understand the rationale behind penalties
Each penalty protects one of the five essential maqasid (objectives): life (qisas), property (amputation), lineage (flogging/stoning), honor (hadd for slander), religion (execution for apostasy). This shows the wisdom behind each punishment.
The text uses the term 'mazajir' (deterrents) to describe penalties.
4
Recite and recall
Practice reciting the full classification from memory: the five categories and their sub-items. Use the framework to organize any fiqh topic you encounter; it will help you locate where a ruling belongs.
This classification is a mental map for the entire fiqh syllabus.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the entire subject matter of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) as presented by the author of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah. The author begins by stating that all legislated matters (al-mashru'at) are divided into five major categories: beliefs (i'tiqadat), acts of worship ('ibadat), transactions (mu'amalat), penalties ('uqubat), and expiations (kaffarat). Each category is then further subdivided into five subcategories, providing a comprehensive framework that covers the core topics of the Shari'ah.

1. Beliefs (i'tiqadat) include the six articles of faith: belief in Allah, His angels, His books, His messengers, and the Last Day. (Note: The standard six articles also include belief in divine decree, but here only five are listed, possibly because qadr is subsumed under 'iman.)
2. Acts of worship ('ibadat) include the five pillars: prayer, fasting, zakah, hajj, and jihad. Jihad is considered a communal obligation here.
3. Transactions (mu'amalat) include five types: exchange contracts (like sale, ijara), marriage, disputes (judicial matters, litigation), trusts (like wadi'ah, 'ariyah), and partnerships (including mudarabah, shirkah).
4. Penalties ('uqubat) are deterrent punishments (zawajir) that protect five essential interests: life, property, lineage, honor, and religion. Each corresponds to a specific hadd punishment: retaliation (qisas) for murder, amputation for theft, flogging/stoning for unlawful sexual relations, hadd for false accusation, and execution for apostasy.
5. Expiations (kaffarat) cover five acts that require compensation: intentional killing, zihar (a pre-Islamic form of divorce), breaking a Ramadan fast, breaking an oath, and violations during hajj.

This classification is presented without proof or dispute; it is a logical structuring of fiqh topics intended for the student's conceptual understanding. It follows the Hanafi method of organizing legal subject matter.

Key Terms
مشروعات (mashru'at): Legislated matters; all legal rulings derived from the Shari'ah.
اعتكاد (i'tiqad): Belief; the inner convictions of faith.
عقوبات ('uqubat): Penalties; punitive measures for violations of Shari'ah.
كفارات (kaffarat): Expiations; compensatory acts required for specific sins or omissions.

Requirement of Imam's Permission for Reviving Dead Land
Comparing two commentaries on imam's permission for ownership via ihya al-mawat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the imam's permission is required for ownership of revived dead land, identify each side's dalil, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires the imam’s permission to own revived land; Sahibayn say revival alone suffices.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira and al-Siraj al-Wahhaj presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute. At stake is the ownership of dead land revived without the ruler's permission. Abu Hanifa insists on permission, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that the act of revival itself confers ownership. Understanding the proofs and the practical difference will sharpen your ability to analyze Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions
Identify the two opposing views within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa's requirement of imam's permission vs. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's (Sahibayn) view that revival alone grants ownership.
Both views are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the opponent and their proof
The opponent here is Sahibayn. Their dalil is the hadith: 'Whoever revives a dead land, it is his.' This appears to give ownership unconditionally.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa cites the hadith: 'A person only has what his Imam willingly gives him.' He also argues by analogy: the land is a right of all Muslims, like Bayt al-Mal, so no one may take it exclusively without the imam's permission.
The qiyas to Bayt al-Mal strengthens his argument.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa's supporters argue that his hadith is specific and qualifies the general hadith of Sahibayn. The imam's permission is a condition for ownership.
This is a classic case of takhsis (specification) of a general text by a specific text.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if someone revives without permission, according to Abu Hanifa he does not own it (though the imam may grant it later), while according to Sahibayn he owns it immediately.
Later Hanafi practice generally follows Sahibayn, making the mufta bihi the permissive view.

Ownership and Taxation in Reviving Dead Land: The Hanafi Internal Debate
Intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs and tax rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding ownership of revived land without the Imam's permission, and apply the tax implications based on irrigation source.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: revival requires Imam's permission; Sahibayn: revival alone grants ownership.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute over reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). Abu Hanifa holds that the reviver does not own the land without the Imam's permission, while his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, maintain that revival itself confers ownership. The passage also explores how the tax status ('ushr or kharaj) of the revived land is determined by the water source. Understanding this dispute is essential for applying Hanafi rulings on land ownership and taxation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The central issue is whether reviving dead land without the Imam's permission grants ownership. Abu Hanifa says no; the Sahibayn say yes.
2
Trace Abu Hanifa's proof
He cites the hadith 'ليس للمرء إلا ما طابت به نفس إمامه' (a person only has what his Imam is pleased with) and argues that land is a right of all Muslims, like public treasury funds, so exclusive possession requires the Imam's authorization.
The proof combines a textual evidence (hadith) with qiyas (analogy to bayt al-mal).
3
Trace the Sahibayn's proof
They cite the hadith 'من أحيا أرضاً فهي له' (whoever revives a land, it is his) and argue that dead land is permissible wealth (mubah) that becomes owned by prior possession, like gathering firewood.
The Sahibayn rely on a different hadith that implies immediate ownership.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Abu Hanifa, the reviver does not own the land until the Imam grants it, and the Imam may reclaim it if the reviver was negligent. According to the Sahibayn, the reviver owns it immediately and the Imam cannot take it back.
This difference affects land rights, taxation, and the Imam's authority.
5
Analyze the tax classification
Once ownership is resolved, the land's tax status depends on irrigation: if rain-irrigated, it is 'ushr land; if irrigated by a river, the ruling follows the land through which the river flows (kharaj or 'ushr).
The tax classification is independent of the ownership dispute but builds on it.

Commentary
This passage presents comparative texts from two Hanafi works—Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira and Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj—on the topic of reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). The central issue is whether ownership is acquired without the Imam's permission. The passage begins by stating Abu Hanifa's position: revival alone does not grant ownership; the Imam must grant it. If the reviver acted ignorantly, the Imam should leave the land to him and not reclaim it, but if the reviver was negligent, the Imam may take it back as a deterrent. The land is then subject to 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax). A quote from Al-Hidayah clarifies that 'ushr is obligatory because imposing kharaj on a Muslim initially is not allowed, unless the land is irrigated with water that itself is subject to kharaj. The second part, from Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, explicitly sets out the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says the reviver without permission does not own the land, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) say he does. Abu Hanifa's proof is the hadith 'A person only has what his Imam's soul is pleased with' and the analogy to public treasury funds—since the land is a right of all Muslims, no individual can take it without the Imam's authorization. The Sahibayn's proof is the hadith 'Whoever revives a land, it is his' and the analogy to firewood, which is permissible property taken by prior possession. After restating Abu Hanifa's position, the author adds that if the Imam does grant ownership, he may impose 'ushr or kharaj, except for those entitled to state stipends (ahl al-'ataya), for whom it is given as a stipend. Finally, Al-Khujandi's ruling addresses the tax classification based on the water source: if irrigated by rainwater, it is 'ushr land; if by a river, it follows the classification of the land through which the river flows—if that land is kharaj, the revived land is kharaj; if 'ushr, then 'ushr. The passage then describes how sajdat al-shukr is performed: upon receiving a manifest blessing (such as wealth, a child, recovery from illness, etc.), one faces the qiblah, praises Allah, prostrates, says another takbir, and rises, similar to sajdat al-tilawah. The dispute highlights a difference in principle: Abu Hanifa considers the continuous nature of Allah's blessings to make prostration impractical, while the Sahibayn see it as a sunnah act of gratitude. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in later Hanafi practice is the recommendation (istihbab), as mentioned in al-Durr al-Mukhtar.

Key Terms
سجدة الشكر (sajdat al-shukr): Prostration performed as an expression of gratitude to Allah upon receiving a blessing or deliverance from harm.
قربة (qurbah): An act that brings one closer to Allah; here used to indicate that the action is rewarded.

Key Takeaway
Sajdat al-shukr is recommended in the Hanafi school according to the Sahibayn, and the later fatwa follows this view, though Abu Hanifa considered it disliked.
