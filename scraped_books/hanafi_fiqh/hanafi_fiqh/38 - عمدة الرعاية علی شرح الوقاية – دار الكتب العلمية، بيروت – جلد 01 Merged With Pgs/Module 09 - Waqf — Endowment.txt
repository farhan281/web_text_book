The Mufti's Duty to Give the Most Beneficial Fatwa for Waqf
States the ruling on waqf fatwa and warns against misuse of maslaha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waqf fatwa and explain why overriding texts with rational maslaha is rejected.

Core Idea
The mufti must choose the most beneficial opinion for a waqf, but cannot use maslaha to override clear texts.

This passage from al-Ashbah and other Hanafi sources establishes a key principle for issuing fatwa on endowments: the mufti is duty-bound to select the most beneficial opinion for the waqf. However, the author immediately cautions against a dangerous misinterpretation—some claim that public interest (maslaha) can justify abandoning textual rulings. This lesson unpacks both the ruling and the warning.

Guided Path
1
Extract the ruling on waqf fatwa
Find the phrase 'يتعيّن الإفتاء في الوقف بالأنفع له'. This explicitly states that the mufti must (يجب) give the most beneficial fatwa for the waqf.
This ruling is quoted from al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, Sharh al-Majma', and al-Hawi al-Qudsi.
2
Distinguish between legitimate benefit and illegitimate maslaha
The passage then contrasts the above principle with a false claim: that if a text conflicts with maslaha, the text is abandoned. Identify the strong condemnation—'fitna', 'corrupt methodology'—from the author and al-Kawthari.
The al-Kawthari quote highlights the danger of making maslaha an independent source of legislation.
3
Understand the epistemological boundary
In Hanafi usul, maslaha is not an independent source if it contradicts a clear text (nass). The mufti's duty is to choose among valid opinions, not to create rulings based on pure reasoning.
Rational maslaha (al-maslaha al-'aqliyya) is rejected when it opposes revealed evidence.
4
Apply to a modern waqf scenario
Consider a mufti advising on a waqf that is losing value. He must find the most beneficial permissible opinion (e.g., renting, investing), but cannot allow a transaction prohibited by a clear text (e.g., selling a productive waqf) just because it seems more profitable.
True benefit always aligns with Sharia; contradiction indicates a mistaken understanding of benefit.

Commentary
This passage begins with a ruling: the mufti is required to give the most beneficial fatwa for a waqf. This is quoted from al-Ashbah. Then it warns against a dangerous word: some claim that maslaha (public interest) overrides texts. The author strongly condemns this, calling it a fitna. He references his other works for a detailed refutation. He then quotes al-Kawthari who denounces those who say that in transactions, if a text opposes maslaha, they take maslaha and leave the text. This is a corrupt methodology that undermines the Shari'ah.

Key Terms
الإفتاء في الوقف بالأنفع له (al-ifta' fi al-waqf bil-anfa' lahu): Issuing a legal ruling for an endowment based on what yields the greatest benefit for that endowment
المصلحة العقلية (al-maslaha al-'aqliyya): Rational public interest as understood by human intellect without textual basis

Checkpoint
What does the passage say about the claim that maslaha can override a text?
A) It is a valid scholarly difference.
B) It is a fitna and corrupt innovation.
C) It is acceptable in transactions.
D) It is the basis of Hanafi waqf rulings.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the mufti must give the most beneficial fatwa for a waqf, but this benefit must be derived from within the Sharia, never by overriding clear texts with rational maslaha.
