Critique of Misclassification: Wajib and Haram in Khulāṣat al-Kaydānī
Critical commentary on definitions in Khulāṣat al-Kaydānī

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and correct the misclassification of prayer acts as wājib or ḥarām in Khulāṣat al-Kaydānī, applying the standard Ḥanafī gradations of rulings.

Core Idea
Not every disliked act is ḥarām; not every recommended act is wājib—precision in legal categories preserves the integrity of fiqh.

The commentator of Al-Hidāyah dissects the definitions in a popular Ḥanafī primer, showing that even a respected author can err in applying the categories of wājib and ḥarām. We will see how the standard Ḥanafī sources reclassify the takbīr, reciting basmalah aloud, turning in prayer, and leaning on a pillar. This lesson sharpens your ability to spot inconsistencies between uṣūl definitions and furū‘ applications.

Guided Path
1
Review the definitions of wājib and ḥarām
Understand the uṣūlī definitions from Khulāṣah: wājib is established by a proof containing doubt; ḥarām is established by a prohibition without countervailing evidence, with the caveat 'agreed upon' for definitive cases.
These definitions are standard; the problem lies in their application.
2
Identify the acts mislabeled as wājib
The author lists takbīr taḥrīmah as a wājib of prayer. Compare with the majority view that it is sunnah, and note the internal Ḥanafī difference (Abū Yūsuf says sharṭ).
The commentator calls this a 'slip of the pen'.
3
Analyse the evidence for each ḥarām claim
For jahr bi-t-tasmiyah, iltifāt, and ittikā', examine the ḥadīth and rulings cited in the passage. Determine whether they reach the level of ḥarām or only makrūh.
Use the ḥadīth and fiqh quotes from al-Nābulsī, Ibn Nujaym, etc.
4
Apply the correct category
Reclassify each act according to the relied-upon Ḥanafī position: takbīr = sunnah, jahr = makrūh, iltifāt = makrūh taḥrīman, ittikā' = makrūh.
The prayer remains valid in all cases.
5
Evaluate the methodological slip
Understand why the author may have overstated: perhaps confusing uṣūl definition with furū‘ classification, or relying on weaker transmissions.
This highlights the need for precision in tarjīh.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hāshiyah) by the commentator of Al-Hidāyah, analyzing and correcting the definitions and applications found in the matn of Khulāsat al-Kaydānī. The commentator first addresses the definition of wājib (obligatory). The author of Khulāsah defined wājib as that which is established by a proof containing some doubt (shubhah), and stated that its ruling is like farḍ (absolute obligation) in practice but not in belief. This is the standard Ḥanafī uṣūlī definition. However, the commentator criticizes the author for then listing the takbīr taḥrīmah (the opening takbīr of prayer) among the wājibāt of prayer. Most authoritative Ḥanafī books state that the takbīr taḥrīmah is sunnah, not wājib, and certainly not a condition (sharṭ). The commentator notes that even the author himself admitted at the end of the chapter that the author of Al-Muḥīt counted these acts as makrūh (disliked). The commentator suggests this may be a slip. He also points out that the author incorrectly stated that leaving tuma’nīnah (tranquility) in rukū‘ and sujūd does not require sajdat al-sahw, which contradicts the relied-upon (mu‘tamad) position. Despite these criticisms, the commentator praises the book for its brevity and depth, recommending it for teaching once these errors are noted. 

The passage then moves to the definition of ḥarām (prohibited). The author of Khulāsah defined it as what is established by a prohibition without any countervailing evidence, with the ruling that leaving it brings reward, doing it brings punishment, and deeming it lawful causes disbelief (kufr) if it is something agreed upon (muttafaq ‘alayh). The commentator defends this definition, explaining that the phrase 'agreed upon' ensures it refers to definitive (qaṭ‘ī) prohibitions whose rejection constitutes kufr because they are known necessarily from religion. The author then lists three acts as ḥarām: (1) saying Bismillāh aloud in prayer (jahr bi-t-tasmiyah), (2) turning the face side to side (iltifāt), and (3) leaning on a pillar or hand without excuse (ittikā’). The commentator strongly objects to these classifications. For jahr bi-t-tasmiyah, he quotes al-Nābulsī who cites multiple ḥadīth showing the Prophet and the caliphs began with al-ḥamdu lillāh, not saying Bismillāh aloud, indicating it is merely contrary to sunnah (makrūh), not ḥarām. For iltifāt, he cites a ḥadīth from ‘Ā’ishah equating it to a snatching by the devil, and notes it is makrūh taḥrīman (prohibitively disliked), which still does not reach the level of ḥarām. For ittikā’, he quotes Ibn Nujaym and Al-Muḥīt al-Burhānī stating it is disliked (makrūh) for obligatory prayers without excuse, but the prayer remains valid; it is not ḥarām. Thus, the commentator demonstrates that the author of Khulāsah was overly strict in labeling these acts as ḥarām, while the school consensus is that they are either makrūh or less. This lesson illustrates a key principle in Ḥanafī fiqh: the careful gradation of rulings (ḥukm) from farḍ to ḥarām, and the need to distinguish between definitive and speculative proofs in establishing these categories.

Key Terms
الواجب (al-wājib): An act established by a proof containing some doubt (shubhah); its ruling is like farḍ in practice but not in belief; denying it does not cause disbelief.
الحرام (al-ḥarām): An act established by a prohibition without countervailing evidence; if agreed upon, deeming it lawful is kufr.
التكبير للتحريمة (al-takbīr li-l-taḥrīmah): The opening takbīr of prayer; according to most Ḥanafī authorities, it is sunnah (recommended), not wājib or sharṭ, though Abū Yūsuf considers it a sharṭ.
الجهر بالتسمية (al-jahr bi-l-tasmiyah): Saying “Bismillāh” aloud in prayer; considered contrary to sunnah (makrūh) according to the commentator, not ḥarām.
الالتفات (al-iltifāt): Turning the face away in prayer; considered makrūh taḥrīman (prohibitively disliked) based on ḥadīth.
الاتكاء على الإسطوانة (al-ittikā’ ‘alā al-usṭuwānah): Leaning on a pillar during prayer without excuse; considered makrūh for farḍ prayers, but prayer is valid.

Ruling on Loud Basmalah in Prayer
Clarifying the ruling on loud basmalah in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on loud basmalah in prayer and explain the dalil that supports it.

Core Idea
Loud basmalah is makruh tanzihi, not haram, because the sunnah is quiet recitation.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on reciting 'Bismillah' loudly during prayer. While some might consider it prohibited, the Hanafi school holds it to be merely disliked (makruh). We will analyze the key textual evidence from the hadith of Anas ibn Malik (ra) and understand how the school derives this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, identify the act in question: reciting the basmalah (Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim) loudly in prayer. According to al-Nabulsi, this act is 'makruh' (disliked), not haram. Note that the text initially lists it among prohibitions, but the footnote clarifies that it is actually makruh.
The ruling is makruh tanzihi, meaning slightly disliked and not sinful.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the hadith narrated by Anas (ra): 'I prayed behind the Messenger of Allah, Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman, and they would open the prayer with Al-hamdu lillahi rabbil alameen... and they would recite Bismillah quietly.' This hadith appears in multiple collections (Sahih Muslim, Sahih al-Bukhari, etc.) with slight variations.
The hadith is considered zanni al-thubut (plausible but not definitively authentic) and zanni al-dalala (the meaning is open to interpretation).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'they would recite quietly' indicates that the established practice of the Prophet and the caliphs was to recite the basmalah silently. This proves that loud recitation contradicts the sunnah, so it is makruh.
The different narrations are reconciled: some mention opening with 'Al-hamdu', others mention silent basmalah. Together they show that the sunnah is quiet recitation.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school differs, holding that the basmalah should be recited loudly in certain prayers (jahri prayers). However, the Hanafi school relies on the evidence of consistent silent practice by the early community.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf; each school has its proofs.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative position) is that loud basmalah is makruh. This is based on the weight of the hadith evidence and the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs, as emphasized by later Hanafi authorities like Imam al-Nabulsi.
The ruling is derived from the Sunnah, not from a clear Quranic text.

Terminological Fluidities of Wajib and Fard in Hanafi Fiqh
Clarifying terminological fluidity of wajib and fard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the technical and loose usages of 'wajib' and 'fard' in Hanafi fiqh texts and correctly identify the intended meaning from context.

Core Idea
In Hanafi texts, 'wajib' can include 'fard', and 'fard' can refer to any essential element, not just pillars—context determines meaning.

This passage tackles a common source of confusion for students of Hanafi fiqh: the apparent interchangeability of the terms 'wajib' and 'fard' in classical texts. While technically distinct in usul (fard from qat'i evidence, wajib from zanni), many fiqh works use them loosely. Understanding this terminological flexibility is crucial for accurate reading and fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Recall technical definitions
In Hanafi usul, fard is an obligation established by definitive (qat'i) evidence (Qur'an, mutawatir sunnah), while wajib is from probable (zanni) evidence. This is the strict distinction.
Other schools do not distinguish; for them, fard and wajib are synonyms.
2
Identify loose usage of 'wajib'
The passage states that 'wajib' is often used in a general sense that includes both fard and wajib. So when you see 'wajib' in a fiqh text, it may encompass all obligations regardless of evidentiary strength.
This is especially common in later works and discussions of specific acts like fasting.
3
Identify loose usage of 'fard'
'Fard' is sometimes used as the opposite of 'rukn' (pillar), meaning any element without which the act is invalid, even if it is a condition (shart) or something else. For example, takbirat al-ihram is called a 'fard' of prayer, though technically it is a condition.
This usage blurs the line between pillars, conditions, and essential components.
4
Apply to an example
Consider the statement 'From the farā'id of prayer is takbirat al-ihram.' Here, 'fard' is used loosely to mean an essential element. Recognize that takbirat al-ihram is actually a shart (condition) in Hanafi fiqh, but the text still calls it fard.
This shows the need for contextual interpretation rather than rigid adherence to technical definitions.
5
Read contextually
When encountering 'wajib' or 'fard' in a Hanafi text, consider the author's usual practice, the topic, and whether the term appears in a list of pillars (arkan) or conditions. This will help you determine the intended meaning.
Later commentaries like Radd al-Muhtar often note these terminological variations.

Zufar holds that the obligation on Friday is Jumu'ah itself, and Zuhr is a substitute (khalaf) for it. Thus, if Jumu'ah is missed, Zuhr is performed as a replacement.

Another narration from Muhammad says the obligation is one of them, unspecified.

According to al-'Ayni, the chosen position (mukhtar) is that Zuhr is the original. This means that Jumu'ah is a substitute for Zuhr when conditions permit. However, regardless of the dispute, all positions agree that tayammum is not permitted for fear of missing Jumu'ah because there is always a substitute (either Zuhr or Jumu'ah itself being the original with a substitute).

Prayer Obligation at the End of Time for a Menstruating Woman
Condition for prayer obligation at end of time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prayer obligation when a woman becomes pure at the end of the prayer time and apply the condition of sufficient time for ghusl and takbir.

Core Idea
A woman's prayer becomes wajib if she becomes pure at the end of the time WITH enough time for ghusl and takbir — the last moment is the trigger.

This lesson examines a precise fiqh issue: does a prayer become obligatory on a menstruating woman who becomes pure at the very end of its time? The Hanafi school bases the answer on a usul principle — the cause of obligation is the last part of the time. We will see how this principle applies to two distinct scenarios and what conditions must be met.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine whether the woman menstruates or becomes pure at the end of the time. If she menstruates at the end, the prayer drops. If she becomes pure, it becomes wajib.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle
The end of the time is considered for obligation. The cause of obligation transfers to the last part in expansive obligations.
This is based on the usul maxim that the part concurrent with the prayer is the cause.
3
Distinguish the duration of purity
If purity lasts a full ten days (maximum hayd), obligation is certain regardless of remaining time. If less than ten days, further conditions apply.
Ten days is the maximum hayd; beyond that is istihadah.
4
Check the condition of remaining time (for less than 10 days)
The remaining time must accommodate ghusl and at least the opening takbir (tahrimah). If only ghusl is possible, the prayer is not wajib because purity is not achieved until after ghusl.
Ghusl time counts as part of hayd duration.
5
Understand the usul basis
The cause of obligation is the part of time concurrent with the prayer. In expansive obligations, this cause extends to the end, so the end becomes the determining factor.
This is a characteristic of expansive (mawsu') obligations.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof addressing the consideration of the end of the prayer time for a menstruating woman. The commentator explains that the effective factor for determining the obligation of a prayer is the last moment of its time. If a woman menstruates at the very end of the prayer time, the prayer is not obligatory on her, even if she was pure earlier. Conversely, if she becomes pure from hayd at the end of the time, the prayer becomes obligatory. The passage distinguishes two scenarios: if her purity lasts a full ten days (the maximum hayd), then regardless of how little time remains in the prayer, the prayer is obligatory. If her purity lasts less than ten days, it is not certain that the hayd has ended (it might resume), so there is a condition: the remaining time must be sufficient to perform ghusl and at least the opening takbir (Tahrimah). If only ghusl can be done, the prayer is not obligatory because purity is not achieved until after ghusl. The commentator then cites the usul principle that the cause of obligation is the part of time concurrent with the prayer (the end part in expansive times). He also explains why ghusl time counts as part of hayd duration: because the woman is not considered pure until she performs ghusl. Therefore, if she only has time for ghusl, she hasn't achieved purity within the prayer time, so the prayer is not obligatory. But if she has time for both ghusl and takbir, she is considered to have caught the prayer time, making it obligatory. This ruling is based on the well-established Hanafi principle that catching even the opening takbir of the prayer time makes the entire prayer wajib.

الغسل (ghusl): The ritual bath required after menstruation.
التَّحريمة (tahrimah): The initial takbir (Takbirat al-Ihram) which begins the prayer.
