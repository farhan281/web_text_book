The Significance of the Basmala Opening in Hanafi Texts
Commentary on basmala opening and its legal basis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi justification for beginning texts with basmala and identify the preferred view on whether the basmala is part of the book.

Core Idea
The basmala opens every important act; its part-of-book status is resolved by the meaning of the preposition.

In Hanafi fiqh works, the opening with 'Bismillah' is not merely a convention but a practice rooted in the Quran, Sunnah, and consensus. The commentator in 'Umdat al-Ri'aya elaborates on the reasons and addresses a subtle linguistic point: is the basmala considered part of the book or a separate invocation? Understanding this distinction reveals the precision of Hanafi methodology in reconciling textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three bases for opening with basmala
The commentator states the author follows the best speech (the Prophet's practice), the practical consensus of the Companions, and a specific hadith. This shows the legal-evidential structure for the custom.
2
Examine the hadith as a proof
The hadith 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with basmala is cut off' is cited from multiple sources. It serves as a primary dalil for recommending the basmala.
Variants use 'aqṭa‘' (cut off) or 'abtar' (curtailed).
3
Analyze the two interpretations of the preposition 'ba'
If 'ba' indicates conjunction ('beginning with'), basmala is part of the book. If it indicates instrumentality or seeking blessing ('seeking help by'), basmala is not part of the book. This linguistic nuance changes the ruling.
4
Evaluate the preferred view and its reconciliatory effect
The commentator favors the instrumental sense (seeking blessing), making basmala not part of the book. This resolves the apparent contradiction with the hadith that says to begin with hamd (praise), as both can be done separately.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya on the basmala opening of Al-Wiqaya. The term 'aqṭa‘' means cut off from blessing, not invalid.
4
Reflect on practical application
The author's practice sets a precedent for scholars and students to begin any important endeavor with ḥamd, whether writing a book, giving a lecture, or starting a lesson.
This is not obligatory (fard) but a strongly recommended sunnah.

Introduction: The Importance of Beginning with Praise and Blessings
Author's introduction with hadith and grammatical analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on beginning an important matter with hamd and salawat, and explain the linguistic distinction between salat and du'a.

Core Idea
A weak hadith suffices for virtuous acts: beginning with praise and blessings ensures baraka.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is the author's introduction, where he lays down the sunnah of beginning any important work with praise of Allah and salawat upon the Prophet. He cites a weak hadith that is acted upon for virtues, addresses a potential linguistic confusion about the preposition 'ala with salat, and clarifies why he uses the word 'Rasul' rather than 'Nabi'.

Guided Path
1
Locate the hadith and assess its authority
Find the hadith cited: 'Every important matter that does not begin with praise of Allah and salawat upon me is severed...'. Note its chain is weak (zanni al-thubut) but Hanafis act upon weak hadiths for fada'il (virtuous acts).
The hadith is from al-Rahawi and al-Daylami.
2
Analyze the linguistic objection and response
Some might think 'salah' is synonymous with 'du'a' and that 'ala with 'salah' could imply evil. The author refutes this by citing Quran 33:56 and the hadith formulas 'Allahumma salli...' showing that 'salah' is a special blessing, not ordinary supplication.
Synonyms need not share all grammatical properties.
3
Understand the choice of 'Rasul' over 'Nabi'
The author explains that 'Rasul' is more specific and implies a new book and law, carrying greater honor. This is why he uses it in the formula.
This is a theological distinction common in Hanafi works.
4
Distinguish performative from declarative sentences
The sentence of praise (al-hamd) can be either declarative or performative, but the sentence of salawat is necessarily performative because reporting salawat does not constitute the act of sending blessings.
This shows Hanafi attention to the performative nature of worship phrases.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a super-commentary on the Hanafi fiqh text al-Wiqayah. The author states he compiled this work for his son Ubayd Allah. Linguistic and Legal Nuances of the Preface: Salat, Rasul, and Tawassul
Expansion probe on preface linguistic rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi linguistic justification for using 'ala' with 'salat', distinguish 'rasul' from 'nabi' in legal terms, and identify the evidence for tawassul through righteous persons.

Core Idea
Salat with 'ala' is not a curse; synonyms differ in usage, and 'rasul' implies a messenger with a new law.

This passage from 'Umadat al-Ri'aya' provides a detailed commentary on the opening phrases of the original text. It addresses a potential linguistic objection regarding the use of 'ala' with 'salat', clarifies the superiority of the term 'rasul' over 'nabi', and explains the performative nature of the salutation. Additionally, it touches on the concept of tawassul, supported by Qur'anic and hadith evidence, demonstrating the Hanafi commitment to precise language and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Linguistic Objection
Recognize that the word 'ala' typically indicates a curse when used with 'dua' (e.g., 'dua alayhi'). The objection is why 'salat' with 'ala' is permissible.
The objection is based on the assumption that synonyms are fully interchangeable.
2
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The commentator refutes by noting that synonyms are not identical in all contexts. He cites Qur'an 33:56 and hadith to show that 'salat' with 'ala' is established usage.
The response emphasizes that language is ruled by usage, not logical extension.
3
Distinguish 'Rasul' from 'Nabi'
The commentator explains that 'rasul' is chosen because it is more specific, implying a new book and law, giving it greater grandeur. Other opinions (synonymy, distinctness) are noted but the commentator prefers this view.
This distinction has theological implications regarding the status of prophets.
4
Understand the Performative vs. Declarative Nature
The sentence of praise (al-hamd) can be declarative or performative, but the salutation is purely performative because reporting it does not fulfill the command.
This shows careful analysis of speech acts in Islamic legal theory.
5
Examine the Concept of Tawassul
The author describes himself as 'al-mutawassil' (the one seeking means), which is derived from the Qur'anic command to seek means to Allah (5:35). The commentator cites hadith to show the permissibility of tawassul through righteous deeds and persons.
Tawassul is a practice endorsed by early Muslims and accepted in Hanafi fiqh.

Analyzing the Phrase ‘Bi Aqwa al-Dhari‘a’
Commentary on the author's phrase 'with the strongest means'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the multiple possible meanings of 'al-dhari'a' in the phrase 'bi aqwa al-dhari'a' and identify the grammatical conditions under which a superlative noun retains its comparative sense.

Core Idea
The phrase 'bi aqwa al-dhari'a' is not ornamental; it requires careful linguistic and legal analysis to preserve its superlative force.

The opening words of Al-Wiqaya, 'bi aqwa al-dhari'a', are more than a rhetorical flourish. The commentator, Sadr al-Shari‘a, unpacks its potential referents—ranging from the Prophet ﷺ to fiqh itself—and then engages in a grammatical debate about the superlative 'aqwa'. This lesson guides you through his analysis, showing how Hanafi scholars treat even introductory phrases with precision to avoid misinterpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Literal Meaning and Referents
Read the commentator's statement that 'al-dhari'a' means 'the means'. Then list the several possible referents he considers: the Prophet, the Qur'an, prayers upon him, knowledge of Shari‘a, fiqh, and others. Note that he considers fiqh the most appropriate since the text is about fiqh.
Multiple interpretations are discussed; the commentator does not force a single meaning.
2
Understand the Grammatical Rule for Superlative Nouns
The commentator analyzes whether 'aqwa' is for 'absolute increase' (superlative) or 'increase over the addendum' (comparative). If it is comparative, the addendum (al-dhari'a) must be a 'genus noun' (اسم جنس) that applies to many instances. 'Al-dhari'a' is not a genus noun, so either the 'al' is for collective universality or a different interpretation is needed.
This distinction shows the linguistic rigor expected in Hanafi usul.
3
Evaluate and Reject Alternative Readings
Consider the view that 'al' is for 'mental reference' (making it indefinite) and Abu al-Makaram's claim that 'aqwa' simply means 'strong'. The commentator rejects both: the first because the word is still definite in form, the second because stripping the superlative of its comparative sense requires the word to be without 'al' or idafa.
These rejections illustrate how Hanafi scholars defend textual precision.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the opening words of the matn 'Al-Wiqaya'. The commentator, known as Sadr al-Shari'a, analyzes the phrase 'bi aqwa al-dhari'a' (بأقوى الذريعة), meaning 'with the strongest means'. He begins by defining 'al-dhari'a' as 'the means' (al-wasila). Then he lists several possible interpretations: the Messenger of Allah (صلى الله عليه وسلم), the Qur'an, prayers upon him, the entire body of Islamic legal knowledge (shari'a), or specifically fiqh, which he considers most appropriate given the author's subject. He also includes the acknowledgment of human inability to comprehend Allah's essence. The commentator relies on his father's glosses (hawashi) and adds further meanings: everything mentioned before (basmala, hamdala, salat), Islam itself, the author's grandfather and teacher (the author of 'Al-Wiqaya'), the Hanafi madhhab, and the scholars of ijtihad, especially Imam Abu Hanifa. Then he delves into a grammatical and logical analysis of the superlative (ism tafdil) 'aqwa'. He considers whether it indicates an absolute increase (fa`ila mutlaqa) or a comparative increase over the addendum. If absolute, there is no issue. If comparative, the addendum must be a genus noun. 'Al-dhari'a' is not a genus noun, so he proposes interpretations: either the 'al' denotes collective universality (meaning 'the strongest of all means') or an implied addendum ('the strongest types of the means'). He rejects the view that 'al' is for mental reference making it indefinite, because the word is still definite in form. He also rejects Abu al-Makaram's claim that 'aqwa' simply means 'strong' (qawi), arguing that stripping the superlative of its comparative sense requires the word to be without 'al' or idafa, which is not the case here. This analysis shows the precision of Hanafi scholars in handling even minor linguistic issues in legal texts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-Wiqayah was composed portion by portion for gradual memorization, not as a standalone reference.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah reveals the pedagogical genius behind al-Wiqayah. The author didn't just write a summary; he designed a method where composition and memorization went hand in hand. Understanding this process helps you appreciate why Hanafi texts are often structured for memorization and how you can benefit from that tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) on the commentary of 'Sharh al-Wiqayah'. The commentator, Abd al-Rahman ibn Muhammad ibn Sulayman, explains the author's statement regarding revisions in the manuscript. He clarifies that the changes (taghyir, mahu, ithbat) are from the author himself, not from copyists, as some might assume. The author's later words 'fakatabtu' (I wrote) and reference to 'the text was settled upon' indicate that. The commentator expands on the natural process of authorial revision: after initial completion, authors often add and subtract from their drafts as long as they are in manuscript form. This is common and accounts for the scratched-out and erased sections found in drafts. A 'white' draft (musawwadah mubayyadah) is rare and considered praiseworthy, as illustrated by the quote from al-Suyuti. The commentator then turns to the phrase 'mahu wa ithbat' (erasing and writing), noting that the choice of this term suggests it is not a defect, drawing an analogy to Allah's attribute of erasing and establishing in the Quran (13:39). While Allah's erasure is due to wisdom, human erasure arises from limitation. Thus, the author's revisions are a normal part of scholarly work, not a flaw.

المسودة (al-musawwadah): A draft copy of a work that is not yet finalized, often containing revisions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A sharh physically carries the matn; it is not a separate work but a protective bearer of the text.

This lesson introduces a foundational distinction in Hanafi legal literature: the difference between a concise text (matn), a commentary that preserves it (sharh), and a collection of rulings (fatawa). The author of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains why his work is a sharh, not merely a gloss, and how he marks the text to guide readers.

The matn is concise and foundational; the sharh bears and explains a matn; the fatawa covers many branches and issues.

A sharh is distinguished by being a 'bearer of the text' (hāmil al-matan). This means it includes the original matn verbatim. The author states his commentary is a sharh because it incorporates the corrected text.
Contrast with a hashiyah or ta'liq, which do not bear the full matn.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah 'ala Sharh al-Wiqayah', a super-commentary (hashiyah) on the Sharh al-Wiqayah. Key Terms
متن (matn): A concise foundational text containing the core issues of a science, serving as the basis for commentaries and glosses.
شرح (sharh): A commentary that bears the original text (matn) and explains it, often including the matn verbatim.
فتاوى (fatawa): Collections of juristic rulings that cover many branches and issues, not limited to the content of a specific matn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the scholarly debate over whether the preface of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah was written originally or appended later, and cite the evidence used by each side.

Core Idea
The preface of a Hanafi commentary may be appended after the main work, reflecting scholarly care for textual authenticity.

This passage from the introduction of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah offers a window into how Hanafi scholars critically examined the composition of their own works. The author discusses whether the preface (الديباجة) was part of the original text or added later, especially after the death of his son Mahmud. Understanding this debate helps you appreciate the meticulous methodology Hanafi scholars used to preserve the integrity of their texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue
The author mentions that some reviewers of the commentary consider the preface to be appended (إلحاقية). Your first step is to recognize this as a textual authenticity debate about the preface's origin.
The term 'إلحاقية' (ilḥāqiyyah) is key: it means attached or added later.
2
Analyze the first argument for appending
The reviewers argue that the phrase 'in shā’ Allāh' (إن شاء الله) in the preface is problematic because it is normally used for future events, yet the preface describes past events (the son's memorization). This suggests the preface was written later.
The author points out a 'flaw' (خدشة) in this argument: the future-oriented phrase seems inconsistent with a past narrative.
3
Examine the author's two alternative possibilities
The author offers two possibilities: (1) The preface up to 'in shā’ Allāh' is original, and the mention of the son is appended later; (2) The entire preface was composed after a portion of the commentary was written and after the son's death. The author rejects the idea that the whole preface is original because 'wa qad kāna al-walad...' indicates a later addition.
These possibilities show the author's careful consideration of textual chronology.
4

The author then explains words like 'barrada' (cooling) and 'madja' (resting place), linking them to supplications for the deceased. He also defends that this commentary is on al-Wiqayah, not al-Niqayah, clarifying authorship.
Such clarifications are common in Hanafi commentaries to avoid confusion about the text's identity and meaning.
