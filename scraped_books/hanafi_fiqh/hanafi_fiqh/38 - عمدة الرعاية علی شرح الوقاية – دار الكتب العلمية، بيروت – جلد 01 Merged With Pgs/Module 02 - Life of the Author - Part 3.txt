A Hanafi scholar's legitimacy is traced through a chain of teachers – Abu Ali al-Daqqaq is linked to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

This lesson introduces you to Abu Ali al-Daqqaq, a Hanafi jurist known for his specialized work on menstruation (Kitab al-Haydh). Understanding his place in the Hanafi scholarly network helps you assess the reliability of his works and their transmission.

Al-Maturidi is the founder of Maturidi theology and a Hanafi faqih whose chain reaches Abu Hanifa.

Abu Mansur al-Maturidi is a towering figure in Hanafi tradition, known as the founder of one of the two main Sunni theological schools. This biographical entry from Sharh al-Wiqayah places him within the Hanafi chain of transmission and highlights his major works. By studying this lesson, you will appreciate his dual role as both a theologian and a jurist, and understand his connection to the Hanafi madhhab.

Note the chain: al-Maturidi studied under Abu Bakr al-Juzjani, from Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani, from Muhammad al-Shaybani, from Abu Hanifa. This links him directly to the founder of the Hanafi school.

His works show his engagement with theological debates, especially against the Mu'tazila.

Abu Yusuf: The First Chief Judge and Spreader of Hanafi Fiqh
Biographical entry on Abu Yusuf, highlighting his judicial career and role in spreading the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical facts of Abu Yusuf and explain his significance in the establishment and spread of the Hanafi madhhab.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf's appointment as Qadi al-Qudat elevated the Hanafi school to official state madhhab.

This lesson introduces Abu Yusuf, the foremost student of Abu Hanifa and the first Chief Judge in Islamic history. His biography is not merely a historical curiosity; it is essential for understanding how the Hanafi school gained official patronage and spread across the Muslim world. We will explore his life, his relationship with the Abbasid caliphs, and his unparalleled contribution to Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify his full name and background
Start with his name: Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim al-Kufi. Note his origin (Kufa) and his early education under Ibn Abi Layla before joining Abu Hanifa.

He served as judge under three caliphs: al-Mahdi, al-Hadi, and Harun al-Rashid. He was the first to hold the title 'Qadi al-Qudat' (Chief Judge). Understand the significance of this appointment.
This title became a standard institution in later Islamic states.
3
Assess his piety and scholarship
Note his daily devotion: 200 rak'ahs of prayer after becoming judge. He memorized tafsir, hadith, and Arab history. This establishes his credibility as a scholar.
4
Evaluate his role in spreading Hanafi fiqh
He was the first to disseminate Abu Hanifa's knowledge across regions. He compiled and transmitted the masai'l (legal rulings) systematically. This explains why the Hanafi school became widespread.
Abu Yusuf's judicial authority helped canonize Hanafi rulings.
5
Note his death and biographical sources
He died in 182 AH. The text cites Mir'at al-Jinan and Tarikh Ibn Khallikan. This anchors the historical record.

Core Idea
Al-Sarakhsi, the 'Sun of the Imams', authored foundational commentaries that shaped Hanafi fiqh and usul.

This biographical entry introduces Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, a towering figure in the Hanafi school. His commentaries on the Zahir al-Riwayah texts, especially Sharh al-Mabsut, became essential references for later Hanafi jurists. Understanding his life and works helps situate the chain of transmission and authority within the school.

Al-Halwani himself was a major Hanafi jurist.

He authored Sharh al-Siyar al-Kabir, Sharh al-Mabsut, a book on usul al-fiqh, and others. His death is placed around 490 AH or 500 AH. These works are key references in the school.

This lesson presents the biography of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib, a towering figure among the Tabi'un and one of the seven jurists of Medina. His life exemplifies the ideal of a jurist who combines deep knowledge with unwavering piety and independence from political influence.
