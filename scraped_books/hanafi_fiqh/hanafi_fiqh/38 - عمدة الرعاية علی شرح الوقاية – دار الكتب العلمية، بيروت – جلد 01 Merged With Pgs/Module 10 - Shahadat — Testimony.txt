Testimony of a Sinner: Valid for Judgment
States that a fasiq's testimony is valid for judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of a fasiq and explain its practical application in court.

Core Idea
A sinner's testimony is valid for judgment unless proven false.

Many assume that a person who commits major sins cannot testify in court. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the testimony of a fasiq is accepted and can form the basis of a valid judgment. The passage emphasizes the importance of memorizing this ruling, as ignorance of it has led to many errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that the testimony of a fasiq is valid for judgment (yusallam al-hukm biha). This is the core ruling.
The ruling is unconditional in this text, but other Hanafi sources note exceptions for proven dishonesty.
2
Understand the Ambiguous Condition
The phrase 'wa in lam yahill' is unclear; some interpret it as 'even if it is not permissible' or 'even if he does not make it lawful'. The exact meaning is debated, but the key point is that the ruling stands.
Scholars recommend memorizing this ruling despite the ambiguity.

Commentary
This passage states a legal ruling: the testimony of a person who commits major sins (al-fasiq) is accepted for adjudication. The condition 'wa in lam yahill' is unclear; it may refer to something like 'even if he does not make it lawful' or 'even if it is not permissible' in some technical sense. The author emphasizes that many have erred by not knowing this rule, so the student should commit it to memory. This ruling is from the Hanafi school, where the testimony of a righteous person is preferred, but a fasiq's testimony is still valid unless he is condemned as a liar or has a conflict of interest.

Key Terms
شهادة الفاسق (shahādat al-fāsiq): The testimony given by a person who commits major sins (fasiq) in a court of law.
