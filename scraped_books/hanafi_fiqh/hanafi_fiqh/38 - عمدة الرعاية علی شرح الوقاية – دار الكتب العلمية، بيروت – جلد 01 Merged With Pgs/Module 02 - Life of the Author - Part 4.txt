Detailed biography of the author of al-Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the scholarly lineage of al-Marghinani and explain the composition history of al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Al-Hidayah is an abridgment of a commentary on al-Marghinani's own matn, al-Bidaya.

This lesson introduces you to the author of one of the most authoritative Hanafi texts, al-Hidayah. Knowing the background of the author and the genesis of his work is essential for understanding the weight his rulings carry in the school. Al-Marghinani's scholarly credentials and his meticulous method of compilation established al-Hidayah as a pivotal text in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author
Recognize that the author is Ali ibn Abi Bakr al-Marghinani, known as Sahib al-Hidayah. Note his full name and epithets.
2
Trace his teachers
List the names of his prominent teachers, including al-Nasafi, al-Sadr al-Shahid, and al-Bikandi. This shows his connection to the authoritative Hanafi tradition.
These teachers link al-Marghinani to earlier giants like al-Sarakhsi and al-Samarqandi.
3
Examine his works
Note his major works: al-Muntaqa, Nashr al-Madhhab, but focus on al-Bidaya and al-Hidayah. Understand that al-Bidaya combines Mukhtasar al-Quduri and al-Jami' al-Saghir.
This synthesis of two core texts shows his aim to unify the school.
4
Understand the composition of al-Hidayah
Al-Hidayah is an abridgment of his own commentary Kifayat al-Muntahi, which itself commented on al-Bidaya. So al-Hidayah is not an original work but a distilled summary of his earlier commentary.
This is a common point of confusion.
5
List his students
His students include his own son and other notable scholars, ensuring transmission of his work.

Understanding the Author's Lineage and Prayer of Reward
Commentary on author lineage and prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the meaning of 'Sadr al-Shari'ah' and 'Jazahu Allahu 'anni', and identify the rationale for choosing 'Muslims' over 'believers' in the context of fiqh.

Core Idea
A prayer for reward acknowledges the author's benefit to all Muslims.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah' introduces the author Mahmud ibn Sadr al-Shari'ah and the traditional supplication for him. The commentator unpacks the title 'Sadr al-Shari'ah' and the phrase 'jazahu Allahu 'anni', explaining their meanings and the careful word choice of 'Muslims' over 'believers'. Understanding these elements gives insight into Hanafi scholarly etiquette and the conceptual link between fiqh and outward actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his lineage
Note that 'Ibn Sadr al-Shari'ah' refers to the commentator's grandfather, the author of 'Talqih al-'Uqul'. This establishes the scholarly chain.
The title is a name, not a description.
2
Analyze the title 'Sadr al-Shari'ah'
Break down 'sadr' as 'chief' or 'chest', implying the foremost or core of the Shari'ah. The commentator offers multiple interpretations.

3
Examine the supplication 'jazahu Allahu 'anni'

Commentary
This passage continues the introduction of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah', now focusing on the author's name and the traditional prayer for him. The commentator first explains the title 'Ibn Sadr al-Shari'ah' (son of the foremost of the Shari'ah). He notes that 'sadr' means the chief or best of a group, so the title signifies the greatest of the people of the Shari'ah. Alternatively, 'sadr' (chest) implies he was like the highest part of the human body due to his deep involvement in religious practice. He clarifies that this title applies to the grandfather of the commentator (the author of 'Sharh al-Wiqayah'), who wrote 'Talqih al-'Uqul fi al-Furuq', as mentioned by al-Kafawi. Then he discusses the supplication 'jazahu Allahu 'anni' (may Allah reward him on my behalf). He explains the verb 'jaza' means to give compensation, and the 'an' indicates the cause. The sentence is a prayer that Allah reward the author for his good work on behalf of the commentator and all Muslims, since the benefit of the book extends to them. He also notes that 'jaza' can mean 'qada' (fulfill) or 'kafa' (suffice). Finally, he comments on the choice of 'Muslims' over 'believers': because Islam is more associated with outward actions (a'maal zaahirah), it is more fitting for fiqh, which is a practical science. He also points out that the commentator in his 'Mukhtasar al-Wiqayah' replaced 'jami'' (all) with 'sa'ir' (the rest), which has the same meaning of 'the remainder.'

Key Terms
صدر الشريعة (sadr al-shari'ah): Title meaning 'foremost of the Shari'ah', used as a proper name for the grandfather of the commentator, who was the author of 'Talqih al-'Uqul'.
جزاه الله عني (jazahu Allahu 'anni): A prayer: 'May Allah reward him on my behalf,' meaning may Allah give him the reward due for his good work, with the intermediary of the speaker and others.
مسلمين (muslimin): Those who have submitted to Allah; specifically chosen here instead of 'mu'minin' because Islam relates to outward actions, fitting for fiqh.
