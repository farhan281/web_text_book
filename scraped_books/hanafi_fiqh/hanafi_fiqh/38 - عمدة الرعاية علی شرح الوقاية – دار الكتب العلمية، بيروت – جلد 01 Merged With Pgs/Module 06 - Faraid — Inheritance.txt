Explicit vs. Implicit Correction in Hanafi Fatwa
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the weight of textual corrections in faraid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasheeh sarih and tasheeh iltizami and apply the rule that explicit correction overrides implicit correction in Hanafi fatwa inheritance cases.

Core Idea
Explicit correction (tasheeh sarih) in a commentary outweighs the implicit correction (tasheeh iltizami) of a matn.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all corrections carry equal weight. While matn authors commit to presenting only the correct rulings (tasheeh iltizami), later commentators may explicitly correct a position (tasheeh sarih). This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies that when both are present, we follow the explicit correction. The specific inheritance case of a paternal uncle's daughter (bint 'amm) and a maternal uncle's son (ibn khal) illustrates the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of correction
Define tasheeh sarih (explicit correction) and tasheeh iltizami (implicit correction). The matn's content is implicitly correct because the author committed to mention only correct rulings, while a commentary may explicitly state that a particular opinion is the correct one.
This distinction is crucial for determining the operative ruling in Hanafi fatwa.
2
Recognize the inheritance scenario
The deceased leaves a paternal uncle's daughter (bint 'amm) and a maternal uncle's son (ibn khal). Understand the general faraid rules for these distant relatives.
These are not Quranic heirs; they inherit as residuaries or due to their relation to the deceased.
3
Note al-Ramli's explicit fatwa
Khayr al-Din al-Ramli gave a fatwa that all the estate goes to the paternal uncle's daughter. This fatwa is explicitly corrected in Jami' al-Mudmarat.
This explicit correction is a tasheeh sarih.
4
Understand the argument on correction weight
The matn's content is implicitly correct, but explicit correction is stronger. The matn's purpose is to transmit the madhhab, not to perform tarjih. Both opinions claim to be from Zahir al-Riwayah, so we need a further criterion.
Zahir al-Riwayah is the authoritative corpus; both sides cite it.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi position
Since the explicit correction exists, we follow it. The estate goes to the paternal uncle's daughter. This resolves the ambiguity.
This principle applies generally in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi methodological discussion on the weight of different corrections. The passage presents a specific inheritance case: a person dies leaving a paternal uncle's daughter (bint 'amm) and a maternal uncle's son (ibn khal). Khayr al-Din al-Ramli gave a fatwa that all the inheritance goes to the paternal uncle's daughter. The issue is that some matn (concise texts) may have implicitly suggested a different distribution, but the author argues that the matn's implicit correction (tasheeh iltizami) is not as strong as an explicit correction (tasheeh sarih) found in a commentary like Jami' al-Mudmarat. The rule is that the matn's purpose is to transmit the madhhab, not to weigh opinions. Since both positions claim to be from Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmitted corpus), we must follow the explicitly corrected opinion. Thus, the fatwa of al-Ramli is preferred. This shows the hanafi methodology of prioritizing explicit textual correction over implicit commitment in matn works.

Key Terms
تصحيح التزامي (tasheeh iltizami): A ruling being correct by virtue of the author's commitment to only mention the correct position, even if not explicitly stated.
تصحيح صريح (tasheeh sarih): An explicit statement by an author that a particular ruling is the correct or relied-upon position.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The transmitted corpus of Hanafi rulings in the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Key Takeaway
When matn and commentary conflict on a ruling, we follow the explicitly corrected opinion (tasheeh sarih) over the implicit correction (tasheeh iltizami).
