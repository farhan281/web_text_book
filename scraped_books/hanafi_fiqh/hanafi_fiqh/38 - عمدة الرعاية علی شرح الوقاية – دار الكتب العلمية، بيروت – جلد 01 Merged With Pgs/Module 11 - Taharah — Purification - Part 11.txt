Prayer on a Carpet with a Najis End
States ruling on impurity at one end of a carpet

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prayer on a carpet with a najis end and explain why the carpet is treated like the ground.

Core Idea
The entire carpet is like the ground; only the prayer spot must be pure.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: you have a carpet or rug with an impure patch, but you want to pray on a clean part. The Hanafi school provides a practical ruling: as long as the actual place of prostration is pure, the prayer is valid, even if the rest of the carpet is impure. This is based on the analogy of the carpet to the earth, which is a single unit. A mistaken view restricts this to cases where the impure part does not move during prayer; we will see why the preferred view rejects that restriction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is prayer (salat) on a clean end of a carpet that has a najis (impure) end. The ruling is that such prayer is sahih (valid) without any restriction.
The ruling is unconditional per the preferred view.
2
Determine the condition for validity
The only condition is that the actual place of prayer (where the forehead rests in sajdah) must be pure. The impurity on the other end does not affect validity.
The carpet is considered like earth (ard), which is a single unit for taharah rulings.
3
Understand the rationale and the disputed point
The analogy to the earth means the whole carpet is one surface; impurity on one part does not render the whole impure. Some earlier scholars held that if the ends move together (small carpet), prayer is invalid, but the author rejects this because the carpet's unity is not broken by movement.
The text explicitly says 'whether one moves with the movement of the other or not' to counter that objection.

Ash from a Pot: The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute on Purification by Fire
Footnote indicating a dispute with al-Shafi'i on ash purity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that ash from burning impure substances is pure (tahir) and contrast it with the Shafi'i view that najis al-ayn is not purified by fire except for wine and hide.

Core Idea
Fire transforms impurity: ash is pure, but Shafi'i only allows wine-to-vinegar and hide-tanning.

This lesson examines a brief but significant dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i over the purity of ash produced from burning inherently impure substances. The Hanafi school, relying on the principle of transformation (istihalah), holds that the fire completely changes the substance, rendering it pure. The Shafi'i school maintains that najis al-ayn (inherently impure things) cannot be purified by mere burning, with exceptions only for wine turning to vinegar and tanning of hides. Understanding this difference clarifies the broader usul principle regarding purification through transformation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed case
The case is ash (ramad) from a pot (qidr) that was used to cook impure substances or from burning najis fuel.
2
State the Hanafi position
The ash is pure (tahir) because fire transforms the substance (istihalah) and removes its impurity.
3
State the Shafi'i position
The ash remains impure because najis al-ayn is not purified by burning; only wine-to-vinegar and tanning purify.
The Shafi'i position is based on limiting purification of najis al-ayn to specific textual exceptions.
4
Identify the underlying usul principle
Hanafi relies on istihalah (transformation) as a general purifier; Shafi'i restricts purification to explicitly mentioned processes.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For Hanafi, prayer with ash on clothing is valid; for Shafi'i, it is invalid until the ash is removed.

When Dampness Transfers Impurity: The Nadwah Ruling
States ruling on moisture transferred from wet najis cloth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a pure garment becomes najis due to contact with a wet najis cloth, and apply the criterion of dripping upon wringing.

Core Idea
Dampness alone does not transfer impurity; only the ability to drip upon wringing makes a garment najis.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in purity jurisprudence: if a pure garment is wrapped in a wet najis cloth, when does it become impure? The Hanafi school uses a practical test – wringing the garment – to determine whether impurity has transferred. We will examine the primary ruling, its conditions, and an alternative view within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a pure garment coming into contact with a wet najis cloth. The ruling is that it becomes najis only if wringing produces dripping water.
2
Distinguish dampness (nadwah) from liquid flow
Understand the key technical term 'nadwah' — moisture that does not flow or drip. This distinction is central to the ruling.
Nadwah does not transfer impurity in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Apply the criterion
Determine what 'dripping upon wringing' means: the pure garment is wrung; if water drips (even small droplets with continuous flow), it is najis. If only dampness appears without dripping, it remains pure.
The test is on the pure garment, not the najis cloth.
4
Note the alternative view
Some Hanafi scholars (al-Burhan, al-Shurunbulali) consider the state of the najis cloth: if the najis cloth would drip when wrung, the pure garment becomes najis regardless of its own state.
This view is not the relied-upon position.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view is that the pure garment's ability to drip is the criterion, as declared the most correct (asahh) in al-Khulasah.
Fatwa is given based on this view.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Transfer of Najasah Through Moisture
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a dry pure cloth becomes impure when wrapped in a wet impure cloth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on impurity transfer through moisture and apply the wringing test to determine the purity of a cloth.

Core Idea
The wringing test determines impurity transfer: focus on the pure cloth's drip or the impure cloth's drip—a key intra-Hanafi dispute.

When a dry pure cloth is wrapped in a wet impure cloth, does it become impure? Hanafi scholars differ: some consider whether the pure cloth drips when wrung; others consider whether the impure cloth drips. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each view and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and the Disputed Ruling
Recognize the core scenario: a dry pure cloth wrapped in a wet impure cloth. The dispute is whether the pure cloth becomes impure and, if so, under what condition.
The ruling impacts the permissibility of prayer using that cloth.
2
Analyze the First View (al-Halwani)
Al-Halwani's position: the criterion is the pure cloth. If the pure cloth, when wrung, would drip water, it is impure; if only moisture appears without dripping, it remains pure.
This view is supported by many scholars and deemed 'most correct' in al-Khulasa.
3
Analyze the Second View (al-Burhan and Shurunbulali)
These scholars focus on the impure cloth: if the impure cloth drips when wrung, the pure cloth becomes impure regardless of its own state; otherwise it does not.
This view is considered more practical because the pure cloth may absorb impurity that does not emerge on wringing.
4
Examine the Reasoning (Talil) Behind Each View
Al-Halwani: impurity transfer requires actual flow from the pure cloth. Al-Burhan: impurity is certain if the source is capable of releasing impurity; the pure cloth's failure to drip does not negate absorption.
Both rely on observable moisture but differ on the point of reference.
5
Determine the Practical Difference and Mufta Bihi
In cases where the impure cloth is very wet but the pure cloth only appears damp, the first view rules pure, the second impure. The mufta bihi is debated; later texts often support the second view.
The passage notes that al-Burhan's view is approved by Shurunbulali and contradicted by some earlier texts.

Rulings on Impurity Transfer and Forgetting the Impure Spot
Two rulings on impurity transfer and obligatory searching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rulings on: (1) a wet cloth placed on dry impure clay, and (2) washing a garment when the exact impure spot is forgotten.

Core Idea
Impurity does not transfer from a dry substance; when the impure spot is forgotten, washing any part suffices.

This lesson addresses two practical purity scenarios from 'Umda al-Ri'aya. First, accidental contact with a dry impure surface—does the moisture transfer impurity? Second, forgetfulness about the exact location of impurity on a garment—must one search? The Hanafi school provides clear, hardship-relieving answers based on the state of the impurity and the principle of raf' al-haraj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario
Recognize the situation: a wet cloth placed on a surface plastered with impure dung (sarqīn) that has dried. The key condition is that the clay is dry at the time of contact.
The ruling hinges on the dryness of the impure substance.
2
Understand the consequence for the cloth
The wet cloth does not become impure; it only acquires a trace (athar) that is legally excused (ma'fu 'anhu). Contrast with when the clay is wet—then the cloth becomes impure.
This is a concession to avoid hardship.

It begins by stating the rule: istinja is prescribed (sunnah) for every hadath (impurity event) except sleep and wind (passing gas). Ruling of Istinja: Exceptions and Number of Stones
Defines istinja, its exceptions, and the sunnah ruling with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling of istinja as sunnah mu'akkadah, identify the exceptions (sleep and wind), and explain the evidence for not requiring a specific number of stones.

Core Idea
Istinja is sunnah, not obligatory, and its validity depends on cleanliness, not a fixed number of wipes.

This passage from 'Umda al-Riaya clarifies the Hanafi ruling of istinja (cleaning after relieving oneself). It addresses two key points: first, what qualifies as a cause for istinja (excluding sleep and wind), and second, the number of stones or wipes. The Hanafi school holds that istinja is a highly recommended sunnah without a set number, as long as cleaning is achieved. We'll examine the definition, the exceptions, and the evidence from hadith.

Guided Path
1
Define istinja and its conditions
Identify the legal definition of istinja and the materials that can be used (stone, earth, cloth, etc.). Note that it is performed after exiting matter from the front or back passage.
The term 'hadath' in the passage refers to anything that exits the two passages, including urine and stool.
2
Identify the exceptions
Understand why sleep and wind are except from the rule. Sleep is an estimative hadath (hadath taqdiri) but does not leave impurity, while wind does not leave any substance on the passage.
This distinction is crucial to avoid performing istinja unnecessarily.
3
Locate the Hanafi ruling and its proof
Find the hukm level (sunnah mu'akkadah) and the evidence: the hadith 'Whoever uses stones should use an odd number; whoever does so has done well, and whoever does not is not blamed.'
This hadith shows flexibility and that the number is not obligatory.
4
Compare with Shafi'i view
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that three stones are sunnah based on other hadith. The Hanafi response interprets those hadith as guidance for the minimum needed, not a requirement.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that a Hanafi may use one wipe if it cleans, while a Shafi'i must use three.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umda al-Riaya on Sharh al-Wiqaya, explaining the chapter on istinja. The Number of Stones for Istinja: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explains Hanafi ruling and refutes Shafi'i view on number of stones

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the number of stones for istinja and explain why the Hanafi school rejects a fixed number, using the hadith evidence from this passage.

Core Idea
Cleanliness is the goal; the number of stones is not fixed—one suffices if it cleans.

The act of cleaning oneself after relieving oneself (istinja) using solid substances like stones is a confirmed sunnah. However, the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ on whether a specific number of stones is required. This lesson examines the Hanafi position that no fixed number is needed, and explores the evidence and reasoning that supports this view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
Recognize that the dispute is about the number of stones for istinja (istijmar). The Hanafi school says there is no fixed number; the Shafi'i school says three stones is sunnah.
The act itself is sunnah mu'akkadah, but the point of contention is the number.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi position is that any number achieving cleanliness suffices; an odd number is recommended but not required. The hadith 'Whoever performs istijmar, let him do it with an odd number' implies a minimum of one.
The least odd number is one.
3
Examine the Hanafi evidence
Two key proofs: (1) The hadith about oddness, showing one is acceptable. (2) The incident of Ibn Mas'ud where the Prophet accepted two stones and did not ask for a third, proving number is not a condition.
The objective is cleanliness, not the number.
4
Examine the Shafi'i evidence
The Shafi'i school relies on hadith that seem to recommend three stones, e.g., 'When one of you cleans himself with stones, let him use three stones.'
These are understood as guidance for completeness, not obligation.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi rebuttal and thamarah
The Hanafi response: The command for oddness is for obtaining purity; if purity is achieved with two or one, the objective is met. The thamarah (practical difference): Hanafi allows one stone; Shafi'i recommends three even if one would clean.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position.

He said: I brought him two stones and some dung. He took the two stones and threw away the dung and said: 'It is impure.' Al-Tirmidhi and al-Nasa'i narrated it. So the Prophet took the two stones and discarded the dung, and did not ask for a third, showing that the number is not a condition for cleaning. And from Abu Hurayra (may Allah be pleased with him) who said: 'When one of you performs istijmar, let him do it with an odd number' in Sahih Muslim, because the least odd number is one. Indeed, the command for oddness is not for itself but for obtaining purity; so if it is obtained with less than three, the objective is achieved and the legal ruling ends. See: al-Mishkat (p. 16) and Nafahat al-Suluk (p. 50).

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the number of stones required for istinja (cleansing after relieving oneself). The Hanafi position, as stated, is that istinja with stones is a sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunnah) without any specified number. The goal is complete cleanliness, so if one stone achieves it, that is sufficient. The Shafi'i school holds that using three stones is sunnah, based on many hadith. The text refutes this by citing hadith: the Prophet did not require a third stone when two sufficed, and the hadith 'let him do it with an odd number' implies a minimum of one. Thus the relied-upon Hanafi view is that the number is not fixed; what matters is achieving cleanliness. The commentary also explains that 'by something like a stone' includes any solid, absorbent, cleansing material like earth, cloth, etc.

The Method of Istinja: Seasonal and Gender Variations in Wiping Direction
Explanation of the preferred istinja method with seasonal and gender considerations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi preferred method of istinja with three stones, distinguish the summer and winter approaches for men, and explain the reason for the gender difference.

Core Idea
The preferred direction of wiping in istinja changes with the season for men but not for women—all to maximize cleanliness while avoiding soiling.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi school's detailed ruling on the mustahabb (preferred) method of istinja using stones. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains that while any direction of wiping suffices, the prophetic practice (and the Hanafi recommendation) involves alternating backward and forward wipes. Importantly, the method adjusts for men based on whether it is summer or winter, due to the position of the testicles. Women, however, always begin with a backward wipe. Understanding this ruling helps you appreciate how the Hanafi school balances effectiveness of cleaning with the preservation of bodily purity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its hukm level
Read the passage to determine whether the described method is obligatory or recommended. The text states that wiping entirely forward or entirely backward would still be sufficient (ajza'ahu), indicating the specific directions are mustahabb (preferred), not fard.
The overall obligation (fard) is removing the impurity; the manner is sunnah.
2
Understand the two directions: Idbar and Iqbal
The passage defines idbar as moving the stone toward the anus (front to back) and iqbal as the opposite (back to front). Note that iqbal is said to be more effective for cleaning.
These terms are key: idbar = backward, iqbal = forward.
3
Analyze the summer method for men
In summer, the testicles hang low (mudlah), close to the orifice. The man wipes backward with the first stone to avoid soiling the testicles, then forward with the second, then backward with the third for thoroughness.
The first wipe is backward to avoid transferring impurity to the testicles.
4
Analyze the winter method for men
In winter, the testicles retract and are raised, so there is no risk of soiling them. The man wipes forward with the first stone (since iqbal is more effective for cleaning), then backward with the second, then forward with the third.
The more effective direction (iqbal) is used first when there is no risk of soiling.
5
Examine the woman's method and the reason
The woman always wipes backward with the first stone, regardless of season, because her vagina is directly adjacent to the anus and forward wiping would soil it. After the first backward wipe, she alternates: forward with the second, backward with the third.
Preservation of the private parts from impurity overrides the preference for forward wiping.

Order of Istinja with Stones for Men and Women
Explains the recommended sequence of wiping with stones based on season and gender.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended order of wiping with stones for men in summer and winter and for women, and explain the reasoning based on avoiding soiling and achieving thorough cleanliness.

Core Idea
The recommended order of istinja varies by gender and season to prevent soiling and maximize cleanliness.

Performing istinja (cleansing after relieving oneself) with stones is a recommended practice in the Hanafi school. While the basic requirement is to remove impurity, a specific sequence of wiping is preferred to achieve thorough cleanliness and avoid contaminating other body parts. This lesson explains the details of that recommended order and the reasoning behind it, as presented in the commentary on al-Wiqayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act under discussion is istinja using stones or similar solid, pure materials. Recognize that the detailed order is a recommended (mustahab) practice, not an obligation.
The obligation (fard) is only to remove impurity; the method is flexible.
2
Distinguish seasonal factors for men
Understand that in summer, a man's testicles hang down (mudlāh) and are close to the anus, so wiping from front to back first risks soiling them. In winter, the testicles retract upward, eliminating that risk.
The physical condition drives the difference in recommended order.
3
Apply the seasonal distinction to the sequence
For men in summer: start with idbār (back to front), then iqbāl (front to back), then idbār. In winter: start with iqbāl, then idbār, then iqbāl.
The three wipes are recommended; the first wipe handles the greatest impurity, so the most effective direction is chosen when possible.
4
Apply the gender distinction for women
Women always start with idbār first, regardless of season, because the vulva is adjacent to the anus without a barrier. Starting with iqbāl would contaminate the private parts.
The principle of avoiding harm (ḍarar) takes priority.
5
Summarize the ruling and its rationale
The recommended sequence is based on the legal maxims of avoiding harm and achieving thorough purification. The goal is to remove impurity while preventing its spread to other body parts.
Any sequence that achieves purification is valid, but this is the preferred method.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn concerning istinja (cleansing after relieving oneself). It begins by stating the method: one should wash the exit with the inner side of one, two, or three fingers, not with the tips, and then wash the hands a second time. The commentary clarifies that washing the hands after istinja is not obligatory but Sunnah according to the correct view, though some said it is obligatory because the hands become impure. It then discusses the amount of water to pour: the sound opinion is that no fixed number is required; one should wash until certain of purity. It also stresses removing odor if possible. The commentary then explains why one should use the inner fingers and avoid the back or tips: inner sides are more effective, and unnecessary actions are avoided. The index finger and palm are especially not to be used due to honor or lack of need. The text does not give tarjih, but later Hanafi practice favors the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, i.e., that the site of istinja is excluded from the measurement and only the excess area is counted.

5

The practical consequence: Hanafis must avoid facing the qibla even in buildings; Shafi'is consider it permissible there. This affects daily practice in mosque bathrooms, public toilets, etc.
This difference matters for fatwa and personal practice.

Commentary

The matn states that it is disliked (makruh) to face or turn one's back to the qibla in both buildings and open desert, and this is the established Hanafi position. The Hanafi response is based on the general hadith from Abu Dawood and others: 'When one of you goes to relieve himself, let him not face the qibla nor turn his back to it.' They argue that when there is a conflict between a general statement (qawl) and a specific action (fi'l), the statement takes precedence because it is a general ruling, while the action might have been specific to the Prophet or done for a particular reason. The Hanafi position is that it is makruh tahrimi to face the qibla, and this applies regardless of location. However, if one sits facing or with his back to the qibla for the purpose of istinja (cleansing after relieving), it is not prohibitively disliked but leaving it is better.

Ruling on Facing the Qibla While Relieving Oneself
Ruling and proof for prohibition of facing qibla during elimination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on facing or turning one's back to the qibla during elimination, identify the hadith proof, and explain why the Hanafi school gives precedence to the Prophet's verbal command over his action in this case.

Core Idea
Facing the qibla while relieving oneself is prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) even inside buildings, based on the Prophet's explicit command.

The Hanafi school rules that it is makruh tahrimi to face or turn one's back to the qibla when urinating or defecating, whether in an open desert or inside a building. This lesson examines the primary evidence from the hadith prohibiting this act and addresses the Shafi'i objection that the Prophet himself was seen with his back to the qibla while relieving himself. You will learn the Hanafi principle for resolving apparent conflicts between the Prophet's statements and his actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? It is the act of facing (istiqbal) or turning one's back (istidbar) to the qibla while relieving oneself. The ruling is 'yukrah' (disliked), which in this context means makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked).
Makruh tahrimi is closer to haram in degree of prohibition but is not absolute haram due to the existence of a counter-indication from the Prophet's action.
2
Locate the primary proof (dalil)
The main evidence is the hadith: 'When one of you goes to relieve himself, let him not face the qibla nor turn his back to it with excrement or urine.' This is a command (nahy) that indicates prohibition.
This hadith is narrated by the four Sunan compilers (Abu Dawud, al-Nasa'i, Ibn Majah, al-Tirmidhi).
3
Analyse the objection from the Shafi'i school
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that it is not disliked inside buildings, relying on the hadith of Ibn Umar that the Prophet relieved himself with his back to the qibla. This is an action (fi'l) of the Prophet.
The action appears to contradict the verbal command.
4
Apply the Hanafi rule for conflicting evidence
When a statement (qawl) and an action (fi'l) of the Prophet appear to conflict, the statement takes precedence. Actions may be specific to the Prophet (khususiyyah) or subject to interpretation, while general commands are binding on the ummah.
This is a well-established principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.
5
Note the exception for istinja (cleansing)
If a person sits facing or with his back to the qibla for the purpose of cleansing after relieving himself (istinja), it is not makruh tahrimi, though abandoning it is still preferable (adab).
The prohibition is specifically for urination and defecation, not for cleaning.

Commentary
