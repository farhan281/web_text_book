Classification of Hanafi Legal Issues into Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah and Ghayr Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah
Explaining the three layers of Hanafi legal issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two primary layers of Hanafi masāʾil and identify which sources belong to each layer.

Core Idea
Hanafi rulings are tiered; the most authoritative come from the six books of Imam Muhammad.

In the Hanafi tradition, not all rulings carry the same weight. This lesson introduces the foundational classification of legal issues into those of Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah – the core of the madhhab – and those that lie outside it. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correct fatwa and scholarly work.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two layers
Identify that the first layer is Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah and the second is Ghayr Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah as defined in the passage.
The third layer is not detailed here.
2
Identify the books of Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah
List the six core books by Imam Muhammad: al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar, al-Ziyadat, and note that al-Mabsut is also called al-Asl.
Also note the copies and commentaries mentioned.
3
Understand the transmission basis
Explain why these are called Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah: they are narrated from Imam Muhammad by reliable narrators, making them mutawatir or mashhur.
See the footnote in the passage.
4
Distinguish Ghayr Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah
Identify that this layer includes issues from Imam Muhammad but not in the six books, such as al-Kaysaniyyat, al-Raqiyyat, and al-Jurjaniyyat.
These are considered less authoritative.
5
Appreciate the importance of this classification
Understand that for fatwa and ijtihad, Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah is primary; knowing the hierarchy prevents misuse of weaker sources.
This classification is unique to the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is an introductory discussion on the classification of Hanafi legal issues (masa'il). The author, quoting al-Kafawi, states that the issues of the Hanafi madhhab are divided into three layers, though only two are elaborated here: issues of Ẓahir al-Riwayah and issues of Ghayr Ẓahir al-Riwayah. The first layer comprises the core texts of the madhhab: al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar, and al-Ziyadat, all authored by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. These are called Ẓahir al-Riwayah because they were transmitted from Imam Muhammad through reliable chains, making them either mutawatir or mashhur. The text notes various copies of al-Mabsut, highlighting the most famous ones, including the copy of Abu Sulayman al-Juzjani (called 'al-Asl') and the commentary of Khwaharzadeh (called 'al-Mabsut al-Kabir'). It also mentions al-Muntaqa and al-Kafi by al-Hakim al-Shahid as foundational works after Muhammad's books. The second layer consists of issues narrated from the imams but found outside the core books, such as al-Kaysaniyyat, al-Raqiyyat, and al-Jurjaniyyat, which are collections of Imam Muhammad's rulings compiled in specific locations. This classification is crucial for understanding the authority and hierarchy of Hanafi legal sources.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah): The primary, widely transmitted issues from Imam Muhammad via reliable narrators, forming the core of the madhhab.
غير ظاهر الرواية (Ghayr Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah): Issues transmitted from the imams but not included in the six core books, found in other collections like al-Kaysaniyyat.
المبسوط (al-Mabsūṭ): One of Imam Muhammad's six core works, a comprehensive collection of Hanafi rulings.

Fabricated Hadiths in Fiqh Books: A Warning
Author warns that fiqh books may contain fabricated hadiths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the authority of fiqh books for legal rulings and their authority for hadith authenticity, and identify why a hadith in a fiqh book cannot be accepted without verification from hadith scholars.

Core Idea
A fiqh book is reliable for rulings, not for hadith verification — always verify hadiths with hadith criticism.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah addresses a critical methodological point: fiqh books, even those authored by great jurists, are not sources for hadith authenticity. The author quotes Ali al-Qari's warning about a fabricated hadith cited in al-Hidayah commentaries, and expands on the principle that one must not rely solely on fiqh compilations for verifying prophetic traditions. Let's work through the author's argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Read Ali al-Qari's statement. What is he warning against regarding the hadith about the last Friday of Ramadan?
The hadith claims praying on that day substitutes for missed prayers — al-Qari says it is baseless.
2
Locate the reason
Why does al-Qari say that the commentators of al-Hidayah (like the author of al-Nihaya) should not be relied upon for this hadith?
Because they are not hadith scholars (muhaddithun) and did not attribute the hadith to any original collector.
3
Extend the principle
The author of our passage expands: 'one should not rely entirely on hadiths transmitted in fiqh books.' What examples does he give?
He mentions the hadith about the language of Paradise being Arabic and Dari Persian, and another cut-off hadith, both fabricated.
4
Recognize the consequence
What should a student or scholar do when encountering a hadith in a fiqh text?
Verify its authenticity by consulting hadith criticism works (takhrij) and scholars of hadith.

Commentary
This passage addresses the methodological point that fiqh books, even those considered reliable for legal rulings, are not reliable for hadith authenticity. The example given is a fabricated hadith about praying on the last Friday of Ramadan being a substitute for missed prayers. Ali al-Qari dismisses the authority of fiqh commentators on hadith matters. The author adds that many fabricated hadiths are found in reliable fiqh books, such as the hadith that the language of Paradise is Arabic and Dari Persian. The lesson cautions students and scholars not to assume a hadith's authenticity simply because it appears in a fiqh book; they must verify with hadith scholars and primary sources.

Key Terms
الحديث الموضوع (al-ḥadīth al-mawḍūʿ): A fabricated hadith, invented and falsely attributed to the Prophet, having no authentic chain.
الكتب الفقهية (al-kutub al-fiqhiyyah): Books of fiqh (Islamic jurisprudence) that compile rulings derived from primary sources.

The Principle of Verifying Hadith in Fiqh Works
Methodological benefit on hadith authenticity in fiqh works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that hadith cited in fiqh books cannot be assumed authentic without verification, and explain why this caution is necessary.

Core Idea
Fiqh books are reliable for rulings, but their hadith citations need verification by hadith specialists.

Students often assume that any hadith found in a respected fiqh work like al-Hidayah or al-Nihayah must be authentic. This lesson, drawn from a note by 'Ali al-Qari, corrects that misconception. It teaches that jurists (fuqahā') are experts in deriving rulings, not necessarily in hadith criticism; their citation of a hadith does not guarantee its authenticity. This is a crucial methodological insight for anyone engaging with Hanafi fiqh texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim of the passage
Read the passage and extract the main warning: that one cannot rely on hadith transmitted in fiqh works merely because they appear there.
The author quotes 'Ali al-Qari's criticism of the author of al-Nihayah and other commentators of al-Hidayah.
2
Understand the reason for the warning
The passage explains that fuqahā' are not hadith specialists (muhaddithūn). They may include fabricated hadith without knowing. The secret (sirr) is that each field has its own experts.
Examples given: fabricated hadith about the language of Paradise, praying behind a pious scholar, etc.
3
Note the exceptions
The passage says that if the author of the fiqh book is himself a muhaddith, or if he attributes the hadith to a specific hadith book from a trustworthy transmitter, then reliance is possible.
This provides a practical guideline for muftis and students.
4
Apply the lesson to a real scenario
Consider a mufti who finds a hadith in al-Hidayah supporting a ruling. Should he accept it automatically? According to this principle, no—he must verify its authenticity from hadith sources.
This prevents issuing fatwas based on weak or fabricated evidence.

Understanding Mafhum al-Mukhalafa in Hanafi Literature
Explains the validity and limitations of mafhum al-mukhalafa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on mafhum al-mukhalafa and explain why it is considered 'akthari' (majority-based) rather than absolute.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-mukhalafa is valid in Hanafi fiqh, but it is a majority rule, not universal.

When reading a Hanafi fiqh text, you may encounter conditional statements. The concept of mafhum al-mukhalafa (implication of opposition) helps interpret the opposite case. This passage explains its validity and limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term
Recognize that mafhum al-mukhalafa refers to the implication that if a ruling is stated for a condition, the opposite condition has the opposite ruling.
E.g., 'If water is pure, then it is not impure' – the opposite is also implied.
2
Locate the dispute
Note that while Sadr al-Shari'ah considers it valid without dispute, al-Zahidi's Ijarah states it is invalid.
This shows ikhtilaf among later scholars.
3
Analyze the resolution
The author of Jami' al-Rumuz concludes that it is valid but 'akthari' (majority-based), not 'kulli' (universal).
This means it applies in most cases but can be overridden.
4
Understand practical implication
When applying mafhum al-mukhalafa, check if stronger evidence exists to override it.
It is a strong but not conclusive proof.

Decoding the Phrase 'Qalu' in Hanafi Texts
Explains the technical meaning of 'qalu' in Hanafi literature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when 'qalu' is used and state its implication for the strength of the opinion.

Core Idea
When Hanafi texts say 'qalu', it signals disagreement among early authorities and often indicates weakness.

You may have noticed the word 'qalu' appearing in fiqh books. This passage explains its technical meaning. Understanding this will help you assess the reliability of opinions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the term
'Qalu' is a plural form meaning 'they said' and is used where there is ikhtilaf among the mashayikh.
It is not used for a single authority.
2
Identify the implication
Ibn al-Humam and al-Taftazani state that 'qalu' indicates that the opinion is weak (da'if).
This is a conventional signal to the reader.
3
Distinguish from other phrases
Compare with 'qala' (he said) for a single strong authority, and 'qalu' for a group with disagreement.
The presence of disagreement implies the opinion is not the relied-upon one.
4
Apply in reading
When you see 'qalu', consider that the opinion may be less authoritative and should be treated cautiously in fatwa.
It often points to a minority or weak view.

The Dual Meaning of 'Jāza' in Hanafi Fiqh

Learning Objective

Distinction between Sahih and Asahh
Technical clarification of the terms sahih and asahh in Hanafi legal literature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the terms 'sahih' and 'asahh' in Hanafi legal texts and state which to prefer when both appear.

Core Idea
When a ruling is both 'sahih' and 'asahh', prefer 'sahih' because both agree on it.

In Hanafi legal literature, terms like 'sahih' and 'asahh' have specific technical meanings that guide a mufti's decision-making. This lesson unpacks the distinction, showing that 'asahh' is more emphatic but 'sahih' carries broader agreement, and explains the practical rules for fatwa when these labels appear in different sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms and their relative emphasis
Note that 'asahh' (أصح) is a stronger emphasis than 'sahih' (صحيح), just as 'ahwat' is stronger than 'ihtiyat'. Recognize that both terms are used to indicate reliability, but with different nuances.
This is a general linguistic observation; the legal application may differ.
2
Apply the rule from Ghuniyat al-Mustamli
When two reliable imams disagree, one calling a ruling 'sahih' and the other 'asahh', the mufti should take the 'sahih' because both agree it is correct. Agreement is preferred over emphasis.
This rule prioritizes consensus over subjective strength.
3

If a book labels a narration with 'asahh' or similar (e.g., 'awla'), the mufti may choose either side. But if it says 'sahih' or 'bihi yufta', he must follow it, unless there is a conflict between equally reliable sources like al-Hidayah and al-Kafi.
The binding force of a label depends on the specific wording used.
4
Understand the nuance from Radd al-Muhtar
Although 'asahh' is often the opposite of 'sahih', al-Bayri cautions that the opposite of 'asahh' can sometimes be a 'shadhdh' (irregular) narration, not necessarily 'sahih'. This means 'asahh' may not always be the next step down from 'sahih'.
This nuance prevents a rigid hierarchy and accounts for exceptional cases.
