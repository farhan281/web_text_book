Mufti Defined as Mujtahid; Task of Non-Mujtahid
Definition of mufti and rules for non-mujtahid when asked

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of a mufti according to the Hanafis and explain the obligation of a non-mujtahid when asked for a fatwa, including the conditions for proper transmission.

Core Idea
The true mufti is a mujtahid; a non-mujtahid must narrate, not create, rulings.

This lesson draws from Ibn al-Humam's Fath al-Qadir to clarify who can issue fatwas. Many today claim the title of mufti without ijtihad, but the classical Hanafi position reserves the role for the mujtahid. For those who only memorize rulings, their task is careful transmission, not independent verdicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Find Ibn al-Humam's statement that the mufti is the mujtahid. This is the settled view of the usuliyun.
2
Define the non-mujtahid's role
Note that a non-mujtahid who memorizes rulings is not a mufti. When asked, he must narrate the mujtahid's opinion as a report (hikaya), not as his own fatwa.
3
Examine the transmission rules
The passage gives two valid ways to transmit: either a continuous chain or from a widely circulated book (like those of Muhammad al-Shaybani). Rare books like al-Nawadir are not reliable unless cited in a famous work.
This safeguards against weak or unknown sources.
4
Analyze the dispute on narration scope
Some say a non-mujtahid with multiple opinions must narrate them all, leaving choice to the mustafti. The author adds that it suffices to narrate one, since a muqallid may follow any mujtahid.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a student who knows only one Hanafi ruling on a matter but is not a mujtahid. According to this lesson, he may narrate that one ruling as a report, but should not present it as an independent fatwa.
Practice distinguishing between narration and ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling regarding who qualifies as a mufti. Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir states that according to the usuliyun, a mufti is a mujtahid. A non-mujtahid who merely memorizes rulings is not a mufti; his task is to narrate the mujtahid's opinion only as a report. The narration must come either through a direct chain or from a well-known, widely circulated book (like those of Muhammad ibn al-Shaybani). Rare books like al-Nawadir cannot be attributed to the imams because they are not popularly transmitted. However, if the rare opinion is quoted in a famous book like al-Hidayah, one may rely on that. For someone who knows multiple opinions but cannot weigh evidence (tarjih), he should not issue a definite fatwa but narrate them all, leaving the choice to the mustafti. The author adds his own view: it is not obligatory to narrate all opinions; one suffices, because a muqallid may follow any mujtahid.

Key Terms
المجتهد (al-mujtahid): One who possesses the qualifications to derive rulings from the primary sources independently; the true mufti.
المستفتي (al-mustafti): The person who seeks a fatwa (legal opinion).
الحكاية (al-hikaya): Narration or transmission of a mujtahid's ruling, not an independent fatwa.

The Mufti Must Be a Mujtahid: Conditions for Valid Fatwa and Transmission
Defining the mufti as a mujtahid and methods of transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that a mufti must be a mujtahid, identify the two valid methods of transmitting a mujtahid's ruling, and explain why rare books like al-Nawadir cannot be relied upon unless verified in well-known works.

Core Idea
Only a mujtahid can issue a fatwa; a non-mujtahid must merely narrate.

In the Hanafi tradition, issuing a fatwa is a serious act that requires independent juristic reasoning (ijtihad). This lesson explains why only a mujtahid can be a mufti, and how a non-mujtahid should properly transmit the rulings of the imams. Understanding these rules prevents confusion between personal opinion and authoritative transmission.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of mufti
Ibn al-Humam states that the mufti must be a mujtahid. Identify what makes someone a mujtahid: ability to derive rulings from evidence independently.
The proof is the consensus of the usuliyyun (al-asaliyyun).
2
Recognize the obligation of hikaya
A non-mujtahid who is asked about a ruling must only narrate the statement of the mujtahid (e.g., Abu Hanifa) as a report. This is called hikaya, not fatwa.
It is wajib (obligatory) upon him to do this; issuing his own fatwa would be invalid.
3
Identify the two methods of valid transmission
The non-mujtahid must have either a continuous chain (sanad) to the mujtahid or refer to a well-known, widely circulated book (like the works of Muhammad al-Shaybani), which is akin to mutawatir or mashhur narration.
Well-known books are considered reliable because they have been transmitted by many and are free from error.
4
Apply the rule to rare books and note the author's clarification
Rare books like al-Nawadir, even if found, cannot be used for attribution unless their content appears in a well-known book. However, the author adds that if the rare book is verified from multiple early manuscripts, attribution may be possible.
This shows the Hanafi emphasis on tawatur and istifada in transmission.

The Mufti's Duty to Narrate Opinions and the Authority of Rare Books
Author's view on narrating multiple opinions and ruling on rare books like al-Muhit al-Burhani

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the mufti's obligation to narrate all opinions and identify the conditions for using a rare book in fatwa.

Core Idea
A mufti need not cite all views; one suffices. Rare books like al-Muhit are unreliable for fatwa.

This passage clarifies two important points for muftis. First, it is not obligatory to exhaustively list all juristic opinions; a single, valid opinion is enough. Second, rare and lost books like al-Muhit al-Burhani cannot be cited in fatwa. The author supports these points with a detailed discussion on the common person's madhhab and the need for widespread acceptance of texts.

Principles for Issuing Fatwa from Written Sources
Compilation of fatwa-issuing conditions from later Hanafi sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for issuing fatwa from written sources and identify reliable vs. unreliable Hanafi texts.

Core Idea
Fatwa must be based on explicit transmission from authoritative works, not general principles or obscure texts.

This lesson compiles several key principles from later Hanafi authorities on the conditions for issuing fatwa from written sources. The mufti must rely on explicit naql sarih, prefer furu' works over usul when they conflict, and avoid non-famous or unreliable books. We also examine the controversy around al-Quhustani and Abu al-Makarim, whose works are rejected by the majority.

Guided Path
1
First Principle: No Fatwa from General Maxims
Identify the first principle: fatwa must be from explicit transmission (naql sarih), not derived from general qawa'id and dawabit.
This is quoted from Ibn Nujaym via al-Hamawi.
2
Second Principle: Furu' Over Usul
Understand that books of usul al-fiqh are disregarded if they contradict the established furu' rulings. The practical fiqh takes precedence.
This ensures consistency with the school's applied positions.
3
Third Principle: Avoid Non-Famous Works
Examine the rule that fatwa is not permissible from non-famous (ghayr mashhurah) books, as stated in al-Fawa'id al-Zainiyyah.
Only widely accepted and transmitted texts are authoritative.
4
Well-Known Books and Oral Chains
Assess the permissibility of quoting from well-known authored books like those of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan and Malik's Muwatta'. Such books achieve the status of tawatur, so no direct oral chain is needed.
This is from Abu Bakr al-Razi in al-Qunyah.
5
Caution on Obscure Works and Ibn 'Abidin's Refutation
Analyze the rejection of al-Quhustani's Jami' al-Rumuz and Abu al-Makarim's commentary. Ibn 'Abidin and 'Ali al-Qari deem them unreliable, thus not valid for fatwa.
This overrides any contrary opinions, such as those of al-Shurunbulali.

Fourth Study: Miscellaneous Benefits for the Mufti. In the commentaries of Sayyid Ahmad al-Hamawi on al-Ashbah wa al-Nazair, quoting from al-Fawa'id al-Zainiyyah by the author of al-Ashbah, Ibn Nujaym al-Misri: 'It is not permissible to issue fatwa from general principles and maxims; rather, the mufti must relate explicit transmission, as they have stated.' End. In it also in another place: 'No consideration is given to what is in the books of principles if it contradicts what is mentioned in the books of branches, as they have stated.' End. In it also in another place, quoting from some treatises of the author of al-Ashbah: 'Fatwa is not permissible from non-famous works.' End. In al-Qunyah, quoting from Usul al-Fiqh by Abu Bakr al-Razi: 'As for what is found of a man's statement and school in a book known with him, which copies have taken up, it is permissible for one who looks into it to say: So-and-so said thus and the school of So-and-so is thus, even if he did not hear it from anyone, like the books of al-Hasan, and Muwatta' Malik, and similar books compiled in the various sciences; because their existence in this description is like mutawatir and well-known transmission, such that it does not need a chain.' End. In Nawazil al-Faqih Abi al-Layth: 'It was said to Abu Nasr: There have reached us four books: the book of Ibrahim ibn Rustam, and Adab al-Qadi by al-Khassaf, and the book al-Mujarrad, and al-Nawadir from the direction of Hisham. Is it permissible for us to give fatwa from them? He said: What is authentic from our companions is knowledge that is chosen, desirable, and pleasing. As for fatwa, I do not think anyone should give fatwa on something he does not understand, nor bear the burdens of the people. If the issues are well-known and apparent from our companions, I hope that reliance on them is permitted.' End. Benefit: 1. Among the unreliable books: Sharh Mukhtasar al-Wiqayah by al-Quhustani Shams al-Din Muhammad, Mufti of Bukhara, who died in the year 950 or 962, known as Jami' al-Rumuz, and Sharh Mukhtasar al-Wiqayah by Abu al-Makarim. Ibn 'Abidin said in Tanqih al-Fatawa al-Hamidiyyah, in the chapter on dislike of wearing red garments, in response to al-Shurunbulali who permitted it based on the statements of Abu al-Makarim and al-Quhustani: 'Indeed, what is obligatory upon the muqallid is to follow the school of his Imam. It is apparent that what these Imams narrated is the school of the Imam, not what Abu al-Makarim narrated, for he is an unknown man, and his book is likewise, and al-Quhustani is like a torrent and a gatherer of firewood at night, especially since his reliance is on the books of al-Zahidi the Mu'tazili.' Commentary
This passage is a compilation (fawa'id) for the mufti, extracted from multiple Hanafi sources, addressing the conditions under which a scholar may issue fatwas from written works. It begins by quoting al-Hamawi's hashia, which itself quotes Ibn Nujaym, stating that fatwa cannot be derived from general qawa'id and dawabit alone; the mufti must rely on explicit textual transmission (naql sarih). A second principle follows: books of usul al-fiqh are disregarded if they contradict the furu' works—the practical fiqh rulings take precedence. Third, fatwa is not permitted from non-famous works (tasannif ghayr mashhurah), ensuring that only widely accepted texts are used. The passage then quotes Abu Bakr al-Razi from al-Qunyah, who explains that well-known authored books (like those of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, Malik's Muwatta') achieve the status of tawatur and istifada, so quoting from them without a direct oral chain is permissible. The Nawazil of Abu al-Layth reports a question to Abu Nasr about four specific books: Ibrahim ibn Rustam's work, Adab al-Qadi by al-Khassaf, al-Mujarrad, and al-Nawadir. Abu Nasr's cautious response emphasizes that fatwa requires understanding and authority; only well-known and authentically transmitted issues may be relied upon. Finally, the passage identifies two works as unreliable: Sharh Mukhtasar al-Wiqayah by al-Quhustani (known as Jami' al-Rumuz) and the same commentary by Abu al-Makarim. Ibn 'Abidin, in his refutation of al-Shurunbulali who had used these works to permit wearing red, declares that the muqallid must follow the Imam's madhhab, and these authors are unreliable—Abu al-Makarim is unknown, and al-Quhustani is likened to a 'torrent' and 'night gatherer,' relying on the Mu'tazili al-Zahidi. 'Ali al-Qari further disparages al-Quhustani as a mere book seller, untrained under Shaykh al-Islam. The overall thrust is that a mufti must use only authoritative, well-known texts transmitted through reliable lines within the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
المفتي (al-mufti): A qualified scholar who issues fatwas (legal opinions) based on the Hanafi school.
النقل الصريح (al-naql al-sarih): Explicit textual transmission from authoritative Hanafi sources, as opposed to deriving rulings from general principles.
الكتب الغير المعتبرة (al-kutub ghayr al-mu'tabarah): Books that are not considered reliable for issuing fatwa due to the author's lack of authority or the work's obscurity.

The Hierarchy for Fatwa Among Hanafi Scholars
Ruling on hierarchy for fatwa when Hanafi scholars disagree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy for fatwa when Hanafi scholars disagree and apply the rule that a single opinion must be followed unless it contradicts madhhab principles.

Core Idea
When Hanafi scholars differ, the mufti follows Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad, then others. A lone opinion binds unless it conflicts with principles.

Knowing which Hanafi opinion to follow is vital for any mufti. This passage establishes a clear hierarchy when the school disagrees. It also clarifies that even a single opinion from later sources must be taken unless it contradicts established principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
First, determine whether all Hanafi scholars agree or differ. If they agree, the mufti follows that agreed opinion. If they differ, a hierarchy applies.
Agreement is when only one ruling exists in the school.
2
Memorize the Hierarchy of Preference
When they differ, the order is: Abu Hanifa, then Abu Yusuf, then Muhammad al-Shaybani, then Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad. This is the standard order for preference in fatwa.
Some later scholars sometimes reversed the order of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, but the cited text gives this sequence.
3
Understand the Single Opinion Rule
If only one opinion exists (whether from Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, or later fatwas), it must be followed unless it is intuitively known to contradict the principles or rules of the madhhab.
This guards against dismissing later fatwas as less authoritative.
4
Apply to a Hypothetical Case
Consider a mufti faced with a question where Abu Hanifa ruled one way and Muhammad another. The mufti should adopt Abu Hanifa's view. If Abu Hanifa had no recorded opinion, then Abu Yusuf's, etc.
The mufti must not skip to a later opinion if an earlier imam's view exists.

The fourth study: miscellaneous benefits for the mufti. If our companions (Hanafi scholars) agree on a matter, the mufti gives fatwa accordingly. If they differ in it, then it is said: fatwa absolutely is on the statement of Abu Hanifa, then on the statement of Abu Yusuf, then on the statement of Muhammad, then on the statement of Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad. Our teacher, the scholar Muhammad Taqi al-'Uthmani, in 'Usul al-Ifta' (pp. 28-29) made this one of the principles of the faqih and mutafaqqih, saying: 'If the issue has only one statement from the Hanafi jurists, both early and later, it is obligatory to act upon it. For the division of issues into different categories only affects issues where different statements exist in the madhhab. As for if the issue has only one statement, it is necessary to act upon it, whether that issue is from Zahir al-Riwayah, from Nawadir, or from Waqi'at and Fatawa, unless it is known intuitively that the issue is defective due to a cause, like contradicting the principles and rules of the madhhab or similar issues.'

Commentary
This passage is a fatwa methodology ruling. It establishes two scenarios: 1) When Hanafi scholars (aṣḥābunā) agree on a ruling, the mufti must give fatwa accordingly. 2) When they differ, the hierarchy is: first Abu Hanifa's view, then Abu Yusuf's, then Muhammad's, then Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad. This hierarchy is based on the seniority and reliability of the imams. The footnote from Muhammad Taqi 'Uthmani clarifies that when there is only one opinion in the madhhab (regardless of whether it is from Zahir al-Riwayah or later fatwas), it must be followed unless it contradicts established principles. This guards against the misunderstanding that only Zahir al-Riwayah bind the mufti.

Key Terms
الفتوى على الإطلاق (al-fatwā 'alā al-iṭlāq): Fatwa absolutely or without restriction, meaning the default position to be followed in the school.
أصحابنا (aṣḥābunā): Our companions, referring to the Hanafi jurists.
