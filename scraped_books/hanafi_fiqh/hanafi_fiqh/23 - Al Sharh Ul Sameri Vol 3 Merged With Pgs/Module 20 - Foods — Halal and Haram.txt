Definition of Khamr and the Inter-Madhhab Dispute on Prohibited Drinks
Definition of khamr and other prohibited drinks with inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi definition of khamr, distinguish it from other intoxicants, and evaluate the opposing Shafi'i/Maliki position on the scope of khamr and its effect on hadd punishment.

Core Idea
Khamr is only grape wine; other intoxicants are haram but carry hadd only upon intoxication.

In this passage from the Book of Drinks, the Hanafi school establishes a precise definition of khamr — limited to fermented grape juice — and contrasts it with other intoxicating beverages. The legal classification determines not only prohibition but also the application of the hadd punishment. The text then presents the opposing view of the Shafi'i and Maliki schools, who consider all intoxicants as khamr, leading to a significant practical difference in hudud enforcement.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi definition of khamr
From the text, identify the specific criteria: grape juice that froths, becomes strong, and throws foam. This is the only true khamr according to Abu Hanifa.
Note the linguistic and legal precision.
2
List the four prohibited drinks
The text mentions khamr (grape wine) and three others: date juice, raisin juice, and cooked grape juice that foam. Only the first is true khamr.
All four are haram, but only grape wine has the full hadd ruling.
3
Understand the hadd rule distinction
For khamr, hadd applies even without intoxication; for the other three, hadd only if intoxication occurs. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
This is a key practical difference.
4
Examine the opposing school's position
Imam Shafi'i and Malik consider all intoxicating drinks as khamr, citing hadith from Umar and Nu'man ibn Bashir that list five sources of khamr.
The disagreement is about the definition of khamr itself.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response to the opposing evidence
The Hanafi school argues that the term khamr is linguistically specific to grape wine; the cited hadith refer to the prohibition of intoxicants, not the legal category of khamr for hadd.
This response relies on usul al-fiqh principles of linguistic interpretation.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the Book of Drinks (Kitab al-Ashribah) and presents a legal definition of prohibited beverages with an inter-madhhab dispute. The author first states that there are four types of prohibited drinks, but the focus is on khamr (wine). Khamr is defined specifically as grape juice that has undergone fermentation (foaming and becoming strong). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, this is the only true khamr; drinking even a drop of it incurs the hadd (prescribed punishment of 80 lashes) regardless of intoxication. The other three types (from dates, raisins, and cooked grapes) are also haram but are considered 'secondary' khamr. For these, the hadd is only applied if the drink actually causes intoxication. The author provides evidence from a hadith (from Ibn Abbas) and an effect to support that grape wine is the original. Then the text presents the opposing view of Imam Shafi'i and Imam Malik: they consider all intoxicating drinks as khamr, citing hadith from Umar and another from Abu Dawud. The footnote elaborates the sources. The Hanafi position is that only grape wine is khamr in the strict sense; other intoxicants are haram by analogy but do not carry the full hadd unless intoxication occurs. This distinction has practical implications for the application of punishments.

Key Terms
خمر (khamr): Specifically, the juice of grapes that has fermented (foamed, become strong, and thrown foam). In Hanafi: the only true khamr.
مسكر (musakkir): Any intoxicating substance; in this text, drinks that intoxicate but are not khamr.
