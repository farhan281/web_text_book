Core Idea
Only explicit false accusation of adultery triggers the fixed penalty of eighty lashes.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri introduces the chapter on the hadd penalty for false accusation of adultery (qadhf). It clearly states that a free person who commits qadhf against a chaste person (muhsan) is to be flogged eighty lashes by the judge. The primary proof is the clear Quranic verse (Surah an-Nur 24:4). The text further distinguishes between explicit accusation and indirect implication (ta'rid) through the hadith of the Bedouin, showing that only explicit slander warrants the hadd. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the hudud.

Core Idea
Lashes must be distributed over the limbs, avoiding the head, face, and private parts.

The Hadd punishment for certain crimes includes lashes. The Hanafi school specifies how these lashes should be administered: not all in one spot, but distributed over different limbs, while protecting sensitive areas. This is based on the instruction of 'Ali ibn Abi Talib, recorded in a well-known athar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who has standing to demand hadd for a deceased person accused of zina, and identify which relatives are affected.

Core Idea
Only a person whose own lineage is tainted by the accusation can demand hadd for the deceased.

This lesson addresses a specific rule in the law of qadhf (false accusation of zina). When the victim is deceased, not every relative can demand the hadd punishment. The ruling limits standing to those whose lineage is directly harmed by the accusation, typically the son or father of the deceased. Understanding this condition is essential to know who can properly initiate the hadd penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is demanding hadd (the fixed punishment) for a deceased person who was falsely accused of zina. The ruling is that only a specific category of people can do so.
2
Extract the condition
The condition is 'من يقع القدح في نسبه' (one whose lineage is affected). The accusation must create a defect (qadah) in the demandant's own lineage.
For example, if the deceased mother is accused, her son's lineage becomes tainted (he is called child of zina).
3
Apply the condition to examples
The typical eligible persons are the son or the father of the deceased. Other relatives like a brother or cousin are not affected in their lineage, so they cannot demand hadd.
4
Understand the rationale
Hadd is only imposed when the victim or their representative demands it. Representation is limited to those who suffer direct harm in their lineage.
5
Note the legal implication
If an ineligible person demands hadd, the demand is rejected. The judge does not proceed with the punishment.

Non-Muslim or Slave Son Can Demand Hadd for Deceased Muhsan Mother
Ruling that a kafir or slave son may demand hadd for his deceased muhsan mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a kafir or slave son may demand hadd for his deceased muhsan mother and identify the proof from the athar of Ibn Mas'ud.

Core Idea
A non-muhsan heir can demand hadd for a deceased muhsan mother because the accusation is against her muhsan status, not his.

In the law of qadhf, only muhsan persons can demand hadd for false accusations against themselves. But what if the victim is deceased and the claimant is a kafir or slave—who are not muhsan? The Hanafi school clarifies that such claimants still have standing because the right belongs to the deceased muhsan, and the child simply acts on her behalf. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the proof from Ibn Mas'ud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act is a kafir or slave son demanding hadd (fixed punishment) for qadhf (false accusation) against his deceased muhsan mother. The ruling is that it is permissible (jā'iz) for him to do so.
The demand itself is a right, not an obligation.
2
Identify the key condition
The mother must have been muhsan (chaste, free, Muslim, adult, married) at the time of the accusation. The son's own lack of ihsan (kufr or slavery) does not affect his standing because the harm is to her honor.
Ihsan is defined differently for men and women in qadhf, but the mother's ihsan is the decisive factor.
3
Locate and analyze the proof
The proof is a qawl sahabi (statement of a Companion) from Ibn Mas'ud (ra): 'There is no hadd except upon two men: one who accuses a muhsan or one who denies a man from his father, even if his mother is a slave.' The phrase 'even if his mother is a slave' analogously supports that the son's slave status does not prevent hadd when the victim is muhsan.
This athar is narrated by Ibn Abi Shayba and is considered zanni in transmission but qati in meaning for this ruling.

Who Can Demand Ḥadd for a Deceased Slander Victim?
States that only the person whose lineage is impugned can demand ḥadd for a deceased.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who may demand ḥadd al‑qadhf for a deceased person and identify the supporting proof.

Core Idea
Only the one whose lineage is impugned can demand ḥadd for a deceased slander victim.

In Hanafi law, the right to demand the ḥadd punishment for slandering a deceased person is not open to all relatives. It is restricted to those whose own lineage is damaged by the accusation. This lesson explains that restriction, the reasoning behind it, and the proof from the tradition of Abdullah ibn Mas‘ud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the Hanafi position: only the person whose lineage is directly impugned by the slander can demand ḥadd for the deceased. This right belongs specifically to the son or father.
The ruling is based on the principle that the ḥadd protects honor, but for a deceased person, the only living honor affected is that of the descendant or ancestor.
2
Examine the illustrative example
Consider the scenario: someone says to a man, 'Your deceased mother was a fornicator.' This implies the man is illegitimate, thus impugning his lineage. Therefore, the son can demand ḥadd on his mother's behalf.
The example shows that the claimant's lineage must be directly at stake.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The proof is the athar of Abdullah ibn Mas‘ud: 'There is no ḥadd except for two men: a man who accuses a chaste woman (muhsana) or a man who denies someone's paternity.' Slandering a deceased person that denies lineage falls under the second category.
The athar limits ḥadd to cases where paternity is denied, which directly ties to lineage.
4
Note the cross‑madhhab difference
The Shafi‘i school allows any heir of the deceased to demand ḥadd, while the Hanafi school restricts it to those with impugned lineage. Understand the thamarah al‑khilaf (practical consequence): in the Hanafi view, if the slander does not affect the claimant's lineage, the ḥadd is not applied.
This difference stems from how each school interprets the purpose of the ḥadd and the scope of the proof.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Test your understanding: If someone says, 'Your deceased uncle was a fornicator,' can you demand ḥadd? The answer is no, because the uncle's slander does not impugn your lineage. Only the uncle's own son (your cousin) could potentially demand it.
The ruling is specific to those directly affected by the paternity denial.

Non-Muslim or Slave Son May Demand Ḥadd for Muhsan Mother
Permits an unbeliever or slave son to demand ḥadd if his mother was muhsan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a non-Muslim or slave son's right to demand hadd al-qadhf for his muhsan mother, and explain the dalil that supports it.

Core Idea
A non-Muslim or slave son can demand hadd for his muhsan mother because the slander targets her chastity, not his status.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in hadd al-qadhf: who has standing to demand the punishment when the slandered party is deceased but was a muhsan woman. The Hanafi school takes a remarkably inclusive position, allowing even a non-Muslim or slave son to step forward as a claimant. We will examine the ruling, its conditions, and the supporting evidence from the practice of the early community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that if the slandered person (maqdhūf) is muhsan, it is permissible (jāʿiz) for his son, even if the son is a kāfir or a slave, to demand the ḥadd. The ruling is about the permissibility of the son's demand, not the obligation of the ḥadd itself.
The ḥadd itself is wajib on the slanderer, but the right to demand it is given to the son.
2
Understand the condition: muhsan mother
The key condition is that the mother (the actual victim) must be muhsan at the time of the slander. If she were not muhsan, no ḥadd is due and the son's demand would be irrelevant.
Muhsan means free, adult, Muslim, chaste, and not previously convicted of zinā.
3
Recognize the exception: son's personal status
The son being non-Muslim or a slave does not bar him from claiming. The slander is against the mother's chastity, not the son's. The son acts as a claimant, not as the victim.
If the slander were against the son directly, he could not demand because he is not muhsan.
4
Examine the dalil
The author cites an athar from al-Zuhri: a man denied another's paternity during a period of shirk, and al-Zuhri said the ḥadd is due. This shows that the claimant's own unbelief at the time does not negate the ḥadd when the slander affects lineage.
The athar is used analogically: unbelief of the claimant does not block the ḥadd.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafiʿi) typically require the claimant to be a free Muslim. The Hanafi school is unique in allowing kāfir or slave sons to demand ḥadd for a muhsan mother.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

Slave Cannot Demand Hadd Against Master for Slandering His Free Mother
Prohibits a slave from demanding ḥadd against his master for slandering his free mother.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a slave cannot demand hadd against his master for slandering his mother and explain the analogical proof from the father-son case.

Core Idea
Respect for the master prevents a slave from seeking hadd for slandering his free mother, by analogy to father-son.

This passage presents a specific ruling within the Hanafi school regarding hadd al-qadhf. A master slanders his slave's free mother, who is deceased. The slave wishes to have the master punished with the hadd punishment. However, the Hanafi school rules that the slave has no right to demand this, even though the mother was free. The reasoning is based on the master's status (iḥtirām al-mawlā) and is supported by an analogical argument from the case of a father slandering his son.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a master (mawlā) slandering (qadhf) his slave's free mother, accusing her of zinā. The ruling is that the slave cannot demand hadd (the 80 lashes) against the master.
The mother's freedom does not grant the slave the right to demand hadd; it is the slave's status that prevents it.
2
Identify the proof (dalīl)
The proof is an athar from ʿAṭāʾ: a father who slanders his son is not flogged (lā yujlad). This is used as the basis for analogy.
The athar is found in Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba and Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah (point of analogy)
The common factor (ʿilla) is the relationship of authority and respect (iḥtirām). Just as a son cannot demand hadd against his father out of respect, a slave cannot demand against his master.
The ʿilla is the special hierarchical relationship that precludes the application of ḥadd.
4
Consider the exception and thamarah
If the mother were alive, she could demand hadd herself. But the slave cannot, because his own right is subordinate to the master's dignity. The practical consequence is that a slave may not seek legal punishment against his master for slander, even if a free relative would have that right.
This ruling is specific to the master-slave relationship; other relationships may differ.

Retraction After Confessing Qadhf Is Not Accepted
States that retraction after confessing qadhf is not accepted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retraction after confessing qadhf and distinguish between huquq Allah and huquq al-'ibad in hudud.

Core Idea
Retraction after confessing qadhf is rejected because the victim's right (haqq al-'abd) dominates, unlike purely divine hudud.

In this lesson, we explore a unique aspect of the hadd for false accusation of zina (qadhf). Unlike some other hudud, retraction of a confession does not nullify the punishment. Why? Because this hadd is not purely a right of Allah; the victim's personal right is paramount. We'll see how the Hanafi school uses an athar from al-Zuhri to establish this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? A person confesses to qadhf (slander) and then retracts. The Hanafi ruling: the retraction is not accepted; the hadd remains wajib.
2
Understand the nature of hadd al-qadhf
Hadd al-qadhf is not purely a right of Allah (huquq Allah); it is a mixed right that includes the victim's right (huquq al-'ibad). This is why retraction does not nullify it.
Compare with hadd al-shurb (drinking) where retraction may be accepted because it is purely huquq Allah.
3
Examine the evidence (athar)
The author cites an athar from al-Zuhri: even if the victim forgives and takes witnesses, he can still demand the hadd later, even after thirty years. This shows the strength of the victim's right.
The athar is mursal (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in Hanafi fiqh for this issue.
4
Apply the ruling
Once the slanderer confesses, the victim's right is established. The retraction of the slanderer cannot undo that right. Therefore, the hadd must be enforced.
This is consistent with the principle that rights of people (huquq al-'ibad) are not easily nullified.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hadd for slander only applies when the victim is muhsan; a fornicator loses muhsan status.

The hadd punishment for slander (hadd al-qadhf) is prescribed to protect the honor of chaste Muslims. However, if the person accused of zina has themselves committed unlawful intercourse, they are no longer considered muhsan. In such a case, the slanderer is not liable for hadd, though they may be subject to ta'zir. This lesson examines the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act of slandering someone of zina when the accused has committed unlawful intercourse. The ruling is that no hadd is applied.
Distinguish between hadd (fixed punishment) and ta'zir (discretionary discipline).
2
Understand the key condition: muhsan
Hadd al-qadhf requires the victim to be muhsan: free, adult, Muslim, and chaste (not previously convicted of zina). If the victim is not muhsan, the hadd falls.
The text clarifies that a person who commits zina ceases to be muhsan.
3
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Take the given example: a man has intercourse with a woman who is neither his wife nor his slave. This makes him non-muhsan. Accusing him of zina does not trigger hadd.
Consider also if the fornicator repented — does that restore muhsan? The classical position is that muhsan is a status based on past actions, not repentance.
4
Note the consequence: possible ta'zir
Although hadd is not applied, the slanderer may still be punished with ta'zir at the judge's discretion, as slander remains a sin.
This shows that the absence of hadd does not mean the act is permissible.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a figurative expression of praise like 'O son of the water of the sky' does not constitute qadhf under Hanafi law, and identify the condition of explicitness required for the hadd punishment.

Core Idea
A phrase that is clearly praise cannot be qadhf, even if it uses the word 'son'.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school: when someone says to another 'O son of the water of the sky', it is not considered qadhf (false accusation of zina). The phrase is a metaphor for generosity, likening the person to rainwater that benefits all. The Hanafi position is that the hadd of qadhf is only applied when the accusation is explicit and unambiguous. This ruling is supported by the statement of al-Qasim ibn Muhammad and the practice of al-Sha'bi, as well as the Quranic principle that only clear slander merits punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its apparent meaning
The phrase 'O son of the water of the sky' is a metaphorical expression. In Arabic culture, it is understood as praise for generosity, comparing the person to rainwater that benefits everyone regardless of status.
The phrase does not literally claim the person is born from sky water, but rather highlights a trait.
2
Determine whether it constitutes an accusation of zina
Qadhf requires an explicit accusation of zina or illegitimate birth. This phrase carries no such imputation; it is purely laudatory. Even the word 'son' is used metaphorically.
Context and customary usage are key.

According to the Hanafi school, the hadd of qadhf is only incurred when the accusation is 'bayyin' (clear and explicit). This is derived from the athar of al-Qasim ibn Muhammad: 'We do not consider flogging except for clear slander and clear denial of lineage.'
Implicit or figurative expressions do not trigger the hadd.
4
Cite the supporting proof
The text provides an athar from al-Qasim ibn Muhammad and a statement from al-Sha'bi. Al-Sha'bi was asked about a man who said to an Arab something like 'O son of...' and he ruled no hadd, indicating that customary praise is not qadhf.
These evidences strengthen the Hanafi stance.

Imam al-Shafi'i may consider any imputation of zina, even indirect, as qadhf if it clearly implies zina. However, the Hanafi school restricts the hadd to explicit statements, so this expression remains free of punishment.
The difference lies in the definition of 'explicitness' required for hadd.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling regarding the punishment for false accusation of zina (qadhf). The hadd is reserved for accusing a muhsan person—one who is free and has consummated a valid marriage. If the accused has ever committed unlawful intercourse, even outside of marriage or proper ownership, he or she loses ihsan, and the accuser cannot be subjected to the hadd. We will explore the textual proof from the Quran and the reasoning behind this expansion of the exemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the matn: 'Whoever commits unlawful intercourse outside of his property, his slanderer is not to be punished with hadd.' Determine the act (accusation of zina) and the subject (the accuser). The ruling is that the accuser does not receive the hadd of qadhf.

The practical difference: Under the Hanafi view, if a man commits adultery with a woman who is not his wife or slave, accusing him of zina does not lead to hadd for the accuser. Under a stricter view, the accuser might still face hadd unless the zina is proven by four witnesses.
The Hanafi position is considered more lenient, protecting the accuser when the accused's reputation is already compromised.

No Hadd for Slandering Li'an Woman with a Living Child
Ruling with proof: no hadd for slandering li'an woman with child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slandering a li'an woman with a living child and explain the proof from Abu Hanifa's athar.

Core Idea
A child without established paternity makes the mother non-muhsana, so the slanderer escapes the hadd.

In this lesson, we examine a specific edge case in hudud: when a woman has undergone li'an (mutual imprecation) because her husband denied paternity of her child, and someone later slanders her by accusing her of zina. The Hanafi school holds that no hadd (flogging) applies to the slanderer. Why? Because the presence of a living child without established lineage is considered a visible sign of zina, which prevents the woman from being fully muhsana. The proof comes directly from a statement of Abu Hanifa preserved in Imam Muhammad's Kitab al-Athar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is qadhf (slandering) of a specific woman: a woman who has undergone li'an due to a child. The ruling is that the slanderer is not flogged (no hadd).
This is the specific ruling; the general rule for slandering a muhsana is flogging.
2
Determine the cause (illa) for the ruling
The woman is not fully muhsana because the presence of a living child without established paternity from the husband is a sign of zina. This lack of full ihsan removes the hadd.
The condition is that the child is alive and its lineage is not established from the husband.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The proof is a qawl sahabi from Abu Hanifa (recorded in Kitab al-Athar). He explicitly states: 'He is not flogged because she has a child with no established lineage.' This is a direct textual evidence from an Imam of the school.
The athar is considered a valid proof within the Hanafi usul. Its thubut and dalala are both zanni but authoritative within the school.
4
Consider the ikhtilaf and its practical impact
Other schools may hold that the li'an woman is still muhsana because li'an itself removes the suspicion. In those schools, the slanderer would be flogged. The practical difference (thamarah) is whether the slanderer receives 80 lashes or only ta'zir.
In Hanafi practice, the mufta bihi is that no hadd applies; ta'zir is at the judge's discretion.

Ta'zir for Slandering Non-Muhsan and Non-Zina Insults
Ruling on slandering slaves, kafirs, and non-zina insults

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slander of non-muhsan individuals and insulting a Muslim without accusing zina, and apply the distinction between hadd and ta'zir.

Core Idea
Hadd of qadhf is only for accusing a muhsan of zina; all other slander incurs ta'zir.

The hadd punishment for false accusation of zina (qadhf) is one of the most severe fixed penalties in Islamic law. But this severity is reserved for a specific scenario: accusing a chaste, free Muslim (muhsan) of zina. What happens when the victim is a slave, a non-Muslim, or when the insult does not involve zina? In this lesson, we explore how the Hanafi school draws a clear line: no hadd, only ta'zir (discretionary punishment). The proofs are sayings of the early authorities (athar), and the reasoning is based on the condition of ihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and rulings
List the three scenarios presented: (a) slandering a slave (male or female) by accusing of zina, (b) slandering a kafir by accusing of zina, (c) insulting a Muslim with words like 'O sinner' or 'O wicked' without zina. The ruling for each: no hadd, but ta'zir.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites three athar: from al-Zuhri on slandering a slave, from Ibrahim on slandering a Jew/Christian, and from Ali on calling someone 'wicked' or 'sinner'. Identify each as the proof that hadd is not applied.
These are qawl sahabi (sayings of companions or early tabi'un), considered zanni in thubut and dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit words in the athar—'there is no hadd on him' or 'he is given ta'zir'—show that hadd is absent. The underlying logic: hadd requires the victim to be muhsan (free, Muslim, chaste, married). Slaves and kafirs lack ihsan; thus hadd is inapplicable. For non-zina insults, the accusation is not qadhf, so no hadd.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Hanafi position is that hadd is restricted to muhsan victims of zina accusations. Some other schools may extend hadd to slander of any Muslim, even if not muhsan. This difference stems from how they interpret the condition of muhsan in the Quranic verse.
The Hanafi reliance on athar and strict reading of 'muhsan' leads to this distinction.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school holds that the explicit Quranic condition of muhsan (Quran 24:4) and the athar confirm the restriction. Thus, ta'zir is the appropriate punishment for all non-qadhf slander. The mufta bihi is ta'zir, no hadd.
Later Hanafi jurists affirm this as the correct position.

Ruling on ta'zir for specific insults, with custom-dependent caveat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on calling someone a donkey or pig and explain how 'urf (custom) affects ta'zir.

Core Idea
Ta'zir depends on custom: words not insults in a society incur no punishment.

This lesson examines a specific case: calling someone a donkey or pig. According to the matn, there is no ta'zir. However, the commentator clarifies that this ruling is context-dependent: if the words are considered insults in that society, ta'zir applies. This highlights the Hanafi principle that ta'zir is sensitive to 'urf (custom).

2

3
Understand the custom condition
The commentator explains that the ruling is based on the fact that these words were not insults in the author's society. If they are insults in another society, ta'zir is imposed. This introduces 'urf as a key factor.
This is a Hanafi principle: ta'zir varies with custom.
4
Apply to different societies
In a society where 'donkey' is a grave insult, the judge would impose ta'zir. In a society where it is a neutral term, no punishment. The ruling is not absolute.

Ta'zir for Qadhf of Non-Muhsan and Insults Other Than Zina
Ruling with proof from athar on ta'zir for non-muhsan qadhf and insults.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the punishment for qadhf of a non-muhsan and for insulting a Muslim without zina, and cite the supporting transmitted proofs (athar).

Core Idea
False accusation of zina only incurs hadd if the victim is muhsan; otherwise, the punishment is ta'zir.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies an important boundary in the law of qadhf (false accusation of zina). While the Quran prescribes a fixed hadd of 80 lashes for accusing a muhsan person, the same does not apply when the victim is a slave, a non-Muslim, or when the accusation is not zina but a lesser insult. In such cases, the judge imposes ta'zir—a discretionary punishment. The author supports this ruling with athar (reports) from early authorities. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when hadd applies and when ta'zir is the appropriate penalty, and identify the key condition of ihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The passage discusses two scenarios: (1) accusing a slave, slave-woman, or non-Muslim of zina, and (2) calling a Muslim a 'fasiq', 'kafir', or 'khabith' without accusing them of zina. In both cases, the ruling is ta'zir, not hadd.
The text uses the passive verb 'yu'azzar' (he is given ta'zir), indicating the obligation falls on the judge.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does the author cite? He mentions the Quranic verse (implied) that hadd is for muhsan victims. He then explicitly cites three athar: from al-Zuhri (concerning slaves), from Ibrahim (concerning Jews/Christians), and from 'Ali (concerning insults like 'khabith' and 'fasiq').
These athar are from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, indicating they are reliable transmitted reports.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does each proof substantiate the ruling? The athar from al-Zuhri says 'he is given ta'zir' for slandering a slave—so no hadd. Similarly, Ibrahim's statement says 'no hadd' for accusing a Jew or Christian. 'Ali's statement says such insults are punishable by ta'zir because they are foul speech, but not hadd because they are not qadhf of zina.
The key legal link: the condition of ihsan (being a free Muslim who has experienced valid intercourse) is absent in slaves and non-Muslims, so hadd drops.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Does any school disagree? The source indicates no intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf here, and the ruling is mutually agreed upon (muttafaq alayh) across schools.
The cross-madhhab status is 'muttafaq alayh', meaning all schools agree that ta'zir applies, though details may vary.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon? The Hanafi ruling is that tampering with any of these offenses is wajib ta'zir. The weight is from Zahir al-Riwayah, based on these athar.
The judge has discretion in the amount of ta'zir, but the punishment itself is obligatory.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A kafir's hadd for qadhf does not disqualify his testimony after Islam – Islam wipes out the past.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a non-Muslim commits qadhf (false accusation of zina), receives the hadd punishment (flogging), then converts to Islam. The Hanafi school holds that his testimony becomes valid again because Islam erases all previous sins, including the punishment's effect on his credibility. This is based on a legal maxim and an athar from Imam al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1

The testimony of a kafir who was subjected to hadd for qadhf and then embraces Islam is accepted. This is the explicit position of the Hanafi school.

The maxim 'al-Islam yahdimu ma kana qablahu' (Islam destroys what was before it) removes all legal consequences of sins committed before conversion. This includes the disqualification from testimony due to hadd.
The same maxim is applied in other areas, e.g., a Muslim cannot be executed for killing a kafir before Islam.

This highlights the unique effect of conversion on legal capacity and credibility.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Samari presents two rulings from the chapter on Hadd al-Qadhf (the punishment for false accusation of adultery). The first ruling (2521) states that when a Muslim is punished with the hadd penalty for qadhf, his testimony becomes invalid permanently, even if he repents from the act. The second ruling (2522) states that if a non-Muslim receives the same hadd penalty and then converts to Islam, his testimony is accepted after conversion.
The passage begins by stating the rulings in concise form, then elaborates. For the first ruling, the author cites Quran 24:4 which explicitly commands: 'Accept not their testimony ever.' This verse is decisive (qat'i al-dalala) and establishes that the testimony of a qadhif is permanently rejected. The author also cites a hadith from Aisha (ra) that includes 'one who has been flogged with a hadd' among those whose testimony is not accepted. The Hanafi position is that both the Quranic text and the hadith indicate permanence; repentance does not restore the legal capacity to testify because the disqualification is a punishment (hadd) that cannot be lifted by tawba, unlike sins that are merely sinful. Imam al-Shafi'i disagreed, arguing that repentance should restore the testimony, relying on an athar from Umar (ra) who flogged some people for qadhf and then said: 'Whoever repents, his testimony is accepted.' The Hanafi school rejects this athar as being in conflict with the clear Quranic verse, which overrides it, and interprets the verse as absolute ('abadan' = ever). Additionally, the hadith includes the phrase 'majlood hadd' which covers the qadhif.
For the second ruling, the Hanafi position holds that when a non-Muslim receives the hadd and then accepts Islam, Islam erases all previous sins and legal disqualifications. The author cites the statement of Sufyan al-Thawri: 'Islam destroys what was before it.' Therefore, the conversion wipes out the effect of the hadd, making the testimony acceptable. This is based on the principle that Islam purifies the individual and grants a fresh start.
The passage thus highlights a key intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab difference. The Hanafi school maintains the permanent disqualification for Muslims regardless of repentance, while Shafi'i allows restoration upon repentance. For disbelievers, the Hanafis apply the principle of jadid (renewal) through Islam. The authority of the Quranic verse and the hadith supports the Hanafi view on the first point.

محدود (mahdood): A person who has been subjected to a hadd punishment, specifically here one who has been flogged for qadhf.

No Amputation for Perishable Items and Fruit on Trees
Ruling on perishable items and fruit on trees exempt from amputation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which items are exempt from hadd amputation in the Hanafi school and state the conditions that lead to ta'zir instead.

Core Idea
Perishable items and fruit on trees are not in secure custody, so theft incurs ta'zir, not amputation.

In Hanafi fiqh, the severe punishment of hand amputation for theft is tied to strict conditions. The stolen item must be a valuable property kept in a secure place (hirz) and have durability. This lesson examines items that fail those conditions—perishable foods, unharvested crops, and fruit on trees—and explains why the punishment is reduced to discretionary ta'zir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items and the general ruling
List the items mentioned: unharvested crops, intoxicating drinks, musical instruments, tharid, meat, and fruit on trees. Note that for all of these, the ruling is no amputation but ta'zir.
The text also includes an athar from Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq supporting this.
2
Determine the underlying condition
These items share a common trait: they are perishable (quickly spoil) or not in secure custody (hirz). For amputation, the stolen property must be mal mutaqawwim (valuable) with baqa' (durability) and in hirz.
Perishability means the item does not have baqa'—it spoils within a day.
3
Apply the condition to fruit on trees
Fruit on the tree is not considered in hirz because it is still attached and accessible; it also may spoil. Thus, no amputation, only ta'zir.
The same logic applies to unharvested crops.
4
Distinguish from conditions that would require amputation
If the stolen item is non-perishable, such as gold or dry goods, and is kept in a secure location (e.g., a locked cabinet), then amputation applies if the nisab threshold is met and other conditions are fulfilled.
5
State the consequence
When the conditions for hadd are not met, the judge applies ta'zir—a discretionary punishment based on the severity of the offense and the judge's assessment.
Ta'zir can be lighter or heavier than hadd, depending on the case.

(Abu Dawud, Kitab al-Hudud, no. 4394). This hadith shows that if one gifts the stolen item to the thief, the hand is not cut. And since the Quran carries the meaning of gift, the hand is not cut. Imam al-Shafi'i says that the Quran is valuable property (mal mutaqawwim), so the hand is cut for stealing it. And one narration says that if it has gold ornamentation worth ten dirhams, it is cut because it is valuable property.
Source pages p. 419

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The Hanafi position is that stealing a mushaf (physical copy of the Quran) does not incur the hadd punishment of amputation, even if it has gold ornamentation exceeding the nisab (ten dirhams). The reason is that people commonly lend their Qurans to others, and the owner is usually pleased to let someone read it. This creates a resemblance to a gift (hiba), and it is established in hadith that if the owner gifts the stolen item to the thief after the theft, the hand is not cut. Since the mushaf is inherently associated with this permissive attitude, the hadd is not applied. The gold ornamentation is subordinate to the mushaf, so it does not change the ruling. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, considering the mushaf as valuable property (mal mutaqawwim). He argues that the gold alone, if it reaches the nisab, would make amputation necessary because it is property. However, the Hanafi school maintains the default ruling based on the hadith of Safwan ibn Umayyah and the principle of dar' al-hudud (averting prescribed punishments by doubts).

Key Terms
مصحف (mushaf): A physical copy of the Quran, including its binding and pages.
مال متقوم (mal mutaqawwim): Property that is legally valuable and protected; its destruction or theft entails liability.
هبة (hiba): A gift; transfer of ownership without compensation.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school does not amputate for stealing a mushaf due to the shubha of hiba, and this ruling applies even if the mushaf has gold ornamentation.
