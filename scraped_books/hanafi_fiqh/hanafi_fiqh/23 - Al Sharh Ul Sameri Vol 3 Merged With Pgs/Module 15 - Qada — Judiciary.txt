This passage addresses a narrow but important dispute within the Hanafi school: when a slave fulfills the condition for emancipation, can the master change his mind and refuse the payment? Exclusion from Qasamah: Children, the Insane, Women, and Slaves
Ruling on who is excluded from qasamah with proof from athar

Core Idea
Qasamah oaths are limited to those who can bear diyah: children, the insane, women, and slaves are excluded.

In cases of unsolved homicide, Hanafi law requires the inhabitants of a quarter to swear collective oaths (qasamah) to deny responsibility. However, not everyone is eligible to swear. This lesson identifies the four categories excluded and the reasoning behind their exclusion, anchored in an athar from Sufyan al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qasamah (collective oaths). The ruling is that certain individuals are excluded from swearing. Name the excluded categories: child, insane person, woman, slave.
The exclusion is a prohibition: these individuals do not participate.
2
Understand the rationale
The text gives reasons: children and insane lack reason; women cannot pay diyah; slaves have no property. Explain why each reason leads to exclusion.
The link between swearing and ability to bear diyah is key.
3
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar from Sufyan al-Thawri in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, stating that there is no qasamah for women, children, and slaves.
This is a qawl sahabi (companion statement) used as a proof.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit negation ('laysa 'ala... qasamah') directly indicates the exclusion. The ruling is derived from the text's clear wording.
The dalala is qati' (definitive in meaning).
5
Note the ikhtilaf and tarjih
Some other schools may allow women or slaves to swear under certain conditions. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi based on the athar and the link to diyah liability.
The Hanafi position is considered the relied-upon view.

No Qasamah or Diyah Without Injury Mark
Ruling on dead body without injury mark

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a dead body lacks an injury mark and identify the proof from the athar of Sufyan al-Thawri.

Core Idea
If no visible injury mark, no qasamah or diyah – the sign of homicide is a prerequisite.

In cases of unexplained death, Islamic law presumes natural death unless there is clear evidence of foul play. The Hanafi school requires a visible injury mark (athar) on the body to trigger qasamah and diyah obligations. This lesson examines the ruling and its proof from early authorities, protecting communities from liability when homicide cannot be established.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the obligation of qasamah (oath-swearing) and diyah (blood money) when a dead body is found with no mark of violence. The hukm is that these are not required.
The ruling is absolute: no mark means no liability from the aqilah (kinsmen) or inhabitants.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites an athar (statement) from Sufyan al-Thawri in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq. This is the proof.
The athar explicitly distinguishes between a body with a mark and without.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The conditional phrase 'اذا وجد القتيل في قوم به اثر' (if a slain person is found among a people and there is a mark) sets liability, while 'اذا لم يكن به اثر' (if there is no mark) removes it. The mark is the effective cause.
The reasoning is direct: the cause of liability is the visible sign of homicide.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools may consider other indicators (e.g., circumstances, missing persons) as sufficient. The Hanafi school restricts qasamah to cases with a visible injury mark.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; see the al-mukhtalaf fih section.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi and is based on this explicit athar, which is considered authoritative in zahir al-riwayah.
No intra-Hanafi disagreement is recorded.

Core Idea
Blood from nose, anus, or mouth is not a sign of homicide — the dominant presumption is natural death.

This passage extends the previous ruling on blood as a sign of homicide. It specifies that blood exiting from natural orifices—nose, anus, and mouth—does not indicate foul play because such bleeding commonly occurs in natural illness. Therefore, qasamah (oath-based procedure) is not triggered. The ruling relies on ghalib al-zann (predominant presumption) that the death was natural, not homicidal.

The text provides the reason: such bleeding can happen in common illnesses. Thus, it is not a reliable sign (athar) of homicide. The ruling is based on rational inference from common practice.
This is an example of ta'lil—customary occurrence determining legal presumption.
4
Apply Ghalib al-Zann
The dominant presumption (ghalib al-zann) is that the person died naturally. Legal consequences are built on this overwhelming probability, not on mere possibility.
Ghalib al-zann is a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh for establishing norms.

Body Found on Animal Driven by a Man: Liability on Him
Ruling when a dead body is found on an animal driven by a specific person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a dead body is found on an animal driven by a man and explain the proof from the athar of Shuraih.

Core Idea
If a body is found on an animal driven by a man, the driver's aqilah pays diyah — apparent signs shift liability.

This lesson deals with liability when a dead body is found on an animal that is being driven by a specific man. The Hanafi school uses the principle of apparent indication (ẓāhir al-ʿalāmah) to assign the diyah to the driver's aqilah rather than the whole quarter. An athar from Shuraih serves as the proof for this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the factual scenario? A dead body is found on an animal driven by a man. The ruling is that diyah (blood money) is wajib on the aqilah (tribe/insurance group) of that man, not on the inhabitants of the quarter.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author cites an athar from Shuraih, a judge from the Tabi'in. In that case, a body was found on a bench at a door with no marks. Shuraih made the household members swear (qasamah).
The athar is found in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
3
Understand the principle
The principle is that the apparent sign (ẓāhir al-ʿalāmah) points to the likely perpetrator. In the athar, the door is an apparent sign; here, the man driving the animal is the apparent sign.
4
Apply the analogy (qiyas)
The athar from Shuraih establishes that when a body is found at a door, the household is responsible for oath-taking. Analogously, when a body is found on an animal driven by a man, the driver bears the liability (diyah).
5

Because a specific suspect is identified, the qasamah (collective oath) from the quarter is dropped. The diyah is on the driver's aqilah.

Body Found in a House: Qasamah on Owner, Diyah on His Aqilah
Ruling when a dead body is found inside someone's house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a dead body is found in someone's house and explain the proof from al-Sha'bi's athar.

Core Idea
A dead body in your house presumes you killed him: qasamah on you, diyah on your aqilah.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Islamic judiciary (qaḍā) concerning liability when a corpse is discovered inside someone's home. The Hanafi school presumes that the house owner is the apparent killer, based on exclusive control over the premises. The homicide is treated as unintentional (qatl al-khaṭāʾ), so the owner must perform qasamah (oaths) and his aqilah must pay diyah. Let's explore the ruling and its evidential basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the ruling applies when the complete body of a slain person is found inside a person's house. Partial remains (e.g., head or leg) do not trigger the same ruling.
The athar from al-Sha'bi distinguishes between a full body and a limb.
2
State the two-part ruling
First, qasamah (a series of oaths denying guilt) is wajib upon the house owner personally. Second, diyah (blood money) is wajib upon the owner's aqilah (male agnates).
Qasamah is on the individual; diyah is on the clan.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The house owner's exclusive control over the property creates a strong apparent sign (ẓāhir) that he committed the killing. The absence of weapon or intent classifies it as unintentional homicide (khaṭāʾ), shifting financial liability to the aqilah.
Hanafis rely on circumstantial evidence (qarāʾin) in homicide when no eyewitnesses exist.
4
Analyze the proof
The ruling is supported by an athar (statement) of al-Sha'bi recorded in Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq. The athar explicitly ties the presence of the full body to the obligation of funeral prayer and diyah, indicating liability.
The athar is considered a qawl ṣaḥābī (Companion's statement), which is a source in Hanafi uṣūl.

Tenants Excluded from Qasamah: Abu Hanifa's Position
Dispute on including tenants in qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on qasamah for tenants and identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf from the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Qasamah falls only on original landowners, not tenants or buyers—ownership determines liability.

This lesson examines a specific ruling by Imam Abu Hanifa regarding who must swear the oaths of qasamah when a dead body is found in a quarter. According to Abu Hanifa, only the original landowners (ahl al-khattah) are obligated, not tenants or subsequent buyers. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold a different view, likely including all permanent residents. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how Hanafi jurists balance ownership and residency in criminal responsibility.

Abu Hanifa holds that qasamah is wajib solely upon the ahl al-khattah, even if only one remains. Tenants and buyers are excluded due to lack of permanent ownership.

Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) likely include all permanent residents, such as tenants, because they also benefit from the locality's protection.
The text does not detail their proof, but the practical consequence differs.

The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, fewer people may be obligated to swear, making it harder to reach the required 50 oaths. Under the Sahibayn, more residents participate, facilitating the process.
This affects whether the case proceeds or blood money is paid.
