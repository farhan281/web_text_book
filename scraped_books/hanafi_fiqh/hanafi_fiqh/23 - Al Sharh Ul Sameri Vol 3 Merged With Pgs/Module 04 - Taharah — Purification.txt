Mixing Milk with Water: The Rule of Predominance (al-Ghalib)
Ruling on tahrim when milk is mixed with water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of predominance (al-ghalib) to determine whether a mixed milk feeding establishes fosterage (rida').

Core Idea
For milk to establish prohibition, it must be the predominant component in the mixture.

In matters of fosterage (rida'), the presence of milk is essential. But what if milk is mixed with another liquid, like water? The Hanafi school provides a clear criterion: the ruling follows the majority. This lesson explains how predominance determines the legal effect of a mixture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mixture components
Recognise that the mixture consists of milk and water. The ruling depends on which component is greater in quantity.
2
Apply the rule: اعتبار للاکثر حکم الکل
The rule states that the ruling of the whole follows the majority component. Thus, the predominant substance determines the legal effect.
This is a core Hanafi principle in mixtures.
3
Determine the legal effect
If milk is predominant (even by a little), prohibition attaches and fosterage is established. If water is predominant, prohibition does not attach.
The mixture is treated as milk if milk is the majority.
4
Consider the equal case
When milk and water are equal, neither is predominant, so the rule does not favour either. Consequently, prohibition does not attach.
Equality is treated as non-predominance.

Commentary
This ruling addresses a scenario where breast milk is mixed with water and fed to an infant. The criterion is predominance: if the milk component is greater (even by a little), the milk retains its legal effect, and fosterage is established. If water is greater, the mixture does not count as milk for purposes of prohibition. The principle applied here is that the majority determines the ruling of the whole (اعتبار للاکثر حکم الکل).

Key Terms
الغالب (al-ghālib): The predominant component in a mixture; determines the legal ruling.

Maintenance of Minor Child and Mother's Priority in Breastfeeding
Ruling on father's maintenance obligation and mother's breastfeeding priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that maintenance of a minor child is wajib on the father regardless of religious difference, and explain the conditions under which the mother has priority in breastfeeding for wages.

Core Idea
A father must maintain his minor child even if different religion; mother has breastfeeding priority if she accepts the same wage as a stranger.

In this passage, we learn two important rulings from the Hanafi school regarding the rights of minor children and their mothers. First, the father's obligation to provide maintenance (nafaqah) for his minor child is established by Quranic verses and is not affected by religious differences. Second, after divorce, the mother has a priority to breastfeed her child for wages, provided she agrees to the same compensation as a stranger, to avoid harm to any party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
Find the statement that maintenance of a minor child is wajib upon the father.
The text says: 'نفقة الصغير واجبة على ابيه' (maintenance of a minor is obligatory upon his father).
2
Examine the scope of the ruling
Note that the obligation holds even if the father and child differ in religion.
The text explicitly says: 'وان خالفه في دينه' (even if he differs in religion).
3
Identify the secondary ruling
Locate the ruling on the mother's right to breastfeed for wages after divorce.
The text discusses the mother's priority if she agrees to the same wage as a stranger.
4
Understand the condition for breastfeeding priority
The mother's priority is conditional on accepting the same wages as a stranger; otherwise, the father is not compelled.
If she asks for more, the father is not forced because it would harm him.
5
Trace the proofs
Identify the Quranic verses and the principle of la darar used to support these rulings.
Verses from Surah al-Baqarah (2:233) and Surah al-Talaq (65:6) are cited.

Hierarchy of Custody Rights After Separation
Ruling on custody hierarchy with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi hierarchy of guardians entitled to custody after divorce and identify the condition that strips the mother of her right.

Core Idea
Mother is most entitled to custody unless she remarries; hierarchy follows maternal kin before paternal kin.

This passage establishes the sequence of custody rights after separation. The mother is given priority based on a prophetic hadith, but her right is conditional on not remarrying. When the mother is unavailable, the right passes to maternal grandmother, then paternal grandmother, then sisters, then aunts. The reasoning is that love and care are stronger from the maternal side. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for any family law practice in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary custodian and its condition
The mother is the first entitled to custody, but her right is conditional on not remarrying (to a non-mahram). Locate the hadith that states this condition.
The phrase 'mā lam tankaḥī' is the key condition.
2
List the secondary custodians in order
If the mother is unavailable, the hierarchy is: maternal grandmother, paternal grandmother, sisters, then aunts. Note the preference for maternal relatives over paternal ones.
The text explicitly says maternal grandmother is more entitled than paternal grandmother.
3

The passage cites the hadith of the woman who complained to the Prophet (ﷺ) and the precedent of Abu Bakr (ra) giving custody to the maternal grandmother. Understand how these proofs support the hierarchy.
The hadith 'anti aḥaqqu bihi mā lam tankaḥī' is the primary dalil for the mother's right.
4
Distinguish the maternal vs paternal preference
The text mentions that love is stronger from the mother's side, so her relatives take precedence. Recognise this reasoning as a principle behind the hierarchy.
This is a qiyas based on custom and natural affection.

Statement of Custody
[2161] (34) If separation occurs between husband and wife, then the mother is more entitled to the child. When separation occurs between husband and wife, during the period of minority, the wife is more entitled to raise the child. There is a hadith: 'From Abd Allah ibn Umar that a woman said, O Messenger of Allah, this son of mine, my womb was a container for him, my breast a source of drink for him, and my lap a refuge for him. And his father divorced me and wants to take him away from me.' The Messenger of Allah said to her, 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry.' (Abu Dawud, chapter on who is more entitled to the child, p. 317, no. 2276). From this hadith, it is understood that the mother is more entitled to custody. [2162] (35) So if the mother is not present, then the maternal grandmother is better than the paternal grandmother. And if there is no maternal grandmother, then the paternal grandmother is better than the sisters. And if there is no paternal grandmother, then the sisters are better than paternal aunts and maternal aunts. Non-Muslim Mother's Custody Rights Over Her Muslim Child
Ruling with proof on custody of Muslim child from non-Muslim mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dhimmi mother's custody of her Muslim child and apply the condition of religious discernment to determine when custody transfers.

Core Idea
A dhimmi mother keeps custody of her Muslim child until the child can discern religion and there is no fear of kufr.

This passage addresses a critical intersection of family law and religious identity: what happens to a Muslim child when the mother is non-Muslim? The Hanafi school balances the mother's natural right to custody with the obligation to protect the child's Islamic upbringing. The ruling is not absolute but conditional, and the proof comes from a prophetic hadith that shows the Prophet considering both the mother's bond and the child's religious welfare.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hadanah (custody) of a Muslim child by a non-Muslim mother. The ruling: the mother has the right (ahqaq) as long as two conditions are met: the child has not yet reached discernment (tamyiz) and there is no fear of kufr influence.
Custody here means physical care and upbringing, not legal guardianship (wilayah).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith of Rafi' ibn Sinan (Abu Dawud and Nasa'i). The child's mother was non-Muslim; the Prophet did not immediately give custody to the father but allowed the child to choose after supplication.
The hadith is ahad, so it gives zanni thubut and dalala, but it is sufficient for a ruling in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's initial action (letting the child choose) shows that the mother's kufr does not automatically disqualify her. However, after supplication, the child chose the Muslim father, indicating preference for the Muslim parent. The Hanafi interpretation balances these: the mother's right is not voided but is subject to the child's religious safety.
The Hanafi position adds the condition of 'fear of kufr' which is derived from the act of supplication and the eventual choice.
4
Note the ikhtilaf and evaluate the tarjih
The Shafi'i school holds that the mother's right continues until puberty regardless of fear of kufr. The Hanafi school relies on the hadith and the principle of protecting the child's deen. The mufta bihi position is the conditional right until discernment, with takhsis when fear of kufr arises.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on differing interpretations of the same hadith.

Divorced Woman Taking Child Outside the City
Ruling on divorced woman taking child out of city

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a divorced mother relocating with her child and apply the exception for her hometown, citing the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
A custodial mother cannot move the child to another city without father's consent, except to her own hometown where she was married.

This lesson addresses a common post-divorce conflict: can the mother who has custody of the child take the child to live in another city? The Hanafi school provides a balanced rule that protects the father's visitation rights while allowing the mother to return to her original home. The ruling is anchored in the Quranic principle that neither parent should be harmed because of the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is a divorced mother taking her child out of the city (al-misr) where the father resides. The default ruling is that she does not have the right to do so without the father's permission.
'Al-misr' in this context means the city where the father lives, not necessarily the place of marriage.
2
Recognize the exception
The exception is when she takes the child to her own 'watan' (hometown), specifically the place where the husband married her. In that case, she may relocate without permission.
The watan is the mother's original town, typically where the marriage contract was executed.
3
Understand the underlying rationale
The prohibition is based on preventing harm (darar) to the father. Taking the child far away would deprive him of regular visitation and cause hardship.
The Quranic verse (2:233) is cited: 'No mother shall be harmed because of her child, nor father because of his child.'
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The proof is from Quran 2:233, which prohibits harming either parent. The Hanafi interpretation holds that moving the child without consent harms the father, so it is not allowed.
This verse is zanni al-dalalah (speculative in meaning) as applied to this case, but the Hanafi school uses it as a primary evidence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Wala is a fixed right of inheritance that belongs to the emancipator and cannot be sold or gifted.

This lesson introduces the concept of wala, a unique form of inheritance in Islamic law that arises from emancipation or a pact of brotherhood. You will learn its definition, two categories, and the primary textual evidence from the Sunnah that establishes this right.

Grasp the definition of wala
Wala is the right of inheritance that transfers to the one who frees a slave (wala al-ataqah) or to the one who makes a muwalat pact (wala al-muwalat).
It is a fixed right, not a transferable property.
2
Distinguish the two types
Wala al-ataqah results from emancipation; wala al-muwalat results from a pact of brotherhood and mutual inheritance.
Both types are recognized in the Hanafi school.

Identify the proof for wala al-ataqah
The hadith of Aisha and Barirah: the Prophet said 'Indeed wala belongs to the one who frees.'
This is the primary evidence.
4
Identify the proof for wala al-muwalat
The athar of Tamim al-Dari, reported marfu'an: 'He is the most deserving of people in his life and death.'
This establishes the validity of the pact.
5
Note the legal consequence
Wala cannot be sold or gifted, as per another hadith: 'The Prophet forbade selling wala and gifting it.'
This reinforces its non-transferable nature.

And if the master dies, his mudabbir slaves and umm walad (mothers of his children) are freed, and their wala belongs to him. [2279] (5) If the master passed away and his mudabbah and umm walad are freed, their wala will be for the master. Although the mudabbah and umm walad will be freed after the master's death, since in reality the one who freed is the master himself, therefore the wala of both is for the master. And it will be distributed among the heirs of the master. (2) The proof is the same hadith: The Prophet said, 'Indeed wala belongs to the one who frees, even if they stipulate a condition.' (Bukhari) In this hadith, wala goes to the one who freed. And the mudabbah and umm walad are freed by the master, so wala belongs to him. (3) There is an athar from Ibrahim: 'Wala belongs to the one who initiated the emancipation first.' (Darimi) From this athar also it is known that whoever initiates the emancipation, wala will be for him.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It covers two special categories: mudabbir (a slave whose emancipation is conditional upon the master's death) and umm walad (a slave woman who bears a child to her master). Upon the master's death, both are automatically freed. Their wala belongs to the master, not to themselves or others, because the master is the one who effectively freed them (by tadbir or by impregnating). The commentary notes that the wala then passes to the master's heirs. The proofs are the general hadith of Barirah and an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbir): A slave whose master has declared that he will be free after the master's death.
ام ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who bears a child to her master, and thereby becomes free upon his death.
