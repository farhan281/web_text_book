Emancipation of Runaway Slaves from Dar al-Harb
Introduction to the Book of Emancipation with hadith on runaway slaves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on slaves fleeing from dar al-harb to Muslim territory and identify the hadith evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
A slave from enemy territory who escapes and comes as a Muslim is free.

This lesson presents a foundational ruling from the Hanafi school concerning the status of slaves belonging to non-Muslims who flee from dar al-harb to Muslim territory. The ruling is derived from a clear incident during the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, where the Prophet ﷺ declared such slaves free. We will examine the hadith evidence and the conditions that determine when emancipation occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the legal question? A slave belonging to polytheists flees from dar al-harb and comes to Muslim territory. Does he become free? The passage states the ruling: if he comes as a Muslim, he is free; if not, he remains a slave.
The ruling is specific to the slave's arrival as a Muslim.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith narrated by Ali ibn Abi Talib about the event of Hudaybiyyah. Two slaves came to the Prophet ﷺ before the treaty. The Prophet refused to return them and said, 'They are Allah's freed ones.'
The hadith is from Abu Dawud, a sound source.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The Prophet's statement 'هم عتقاء الله' directly indicates emancipation. His refusal to return them shows that the slaves are no longer owned by their former masters once they reach Muslim territory with faith.
The implication is that the act of coming as a Muslim severs the ownership of the polytheist master.
4
Note the condition
The commentary adds a condition: the slave must come as a Muslim. If he does not accept Islam, he is not freed. This is inferred from the context of the hadith where the slaves presumably embraced Islam.
The condition is based on the understanding that the Prophet's declaration was for those who came as believers.

At the time of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, some slaves of the polytheists came from noble Mecca to the presence of the Messenger (PBUH). He said: They are now free. A piece of the hadith is: from Ali ibn Abi Talib who said: Two slaves went out to the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, on the day of Hudaybiyyah before the treaty... and he refused to return them and said: They are the freed ones of Allah Almighty. In this hadith, it is that if a slave of polytheists flees from dar al-harb and comes, he will be free. And if he does not come as a Muslim, he will not be free.

Commentary
This passage is an introductory ruling from the Book of Emancipation, presenting a hadith as proof for the emancipation of runaway slaves from enemy territory. The text first narrates an incident from the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah where polytheist slaves came to the Prophet (PBUH) and he declared them free, refusing to return them. The commentary derives the legal ruling: a slave belonging to polytheists who escapes from dar al-harb and comes to Muslim territory becomes free if he accepts Islam. If he does not become Muslim, he is not freed. This is based on the hadith of Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA). The Hanafi school follows this ruling.

Key Terms
عتقاء (utaqa'): Those who have been freed; emancipated slaves.
دار الحرب (dar al-harb): Territory under non-Muslim rule where the laws of Islam are not enforced.
