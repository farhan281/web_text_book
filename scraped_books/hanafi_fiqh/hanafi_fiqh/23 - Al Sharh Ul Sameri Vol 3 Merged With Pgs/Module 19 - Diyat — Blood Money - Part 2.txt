Diyah for Cut Including Half Forearm
Ruling on diyah when cut includes the palm and part of the forearm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the diyah ruling for a cut that includes the palm and part of the forearm, and explain why the forearm portion requires a judge's assessment (hukuma).

Core Idea
The palm has a fixed half diyah; the forearm beyond it has no fixed amount and must be assessed by a judge.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in diyat (blood money) law: when an injury cuts through the palm and extends into the forearm. The Hanafi school distinguishes between parts of the hand that have fixed diyah (the palm) and those that do not (the forearm), requiring a judge's discretionary assessment (hukuma) for the latter. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly calculating compensation in such injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two anatomical parts
The injury involves the palm (kaf) and the forearm (sa'id). The palm includes the fingers and the hand up to the wrist joint. The forearm is the part from wrist to elbow.
Hanafi: only the palm is considered the complete hand for diyah purposes.
2
State the fixed compensation for the palm
The matn says 'fī al-kaff niṣf al-diyah' – half diyah is due for the palm. Full diyah is 100 camels or equivalent, so half is 50 camels.
This is based on the principle that loss of the hand (palm) incurs half diyah.
3
Recognize the compensation for the forearm
The matn says 'wa fī al-ziyādah ḥukūmah' – for the increase (the forearm) a government assessment. No fixed diyah exists for the forearm; a just judge determines the amount.
Hukuma is used for parts without a specified diyah, like bones or non-limb areas.
4
Understand the proof (dalīl)
The athar of Ibrāhīm al-Nakha'ī: 'If the palm is cut at the joint, full diyah is due. Then if something is cut from it after that, a just government assessment.' This supports the distinction.
The athar is narrated by Ibn Abī Shayba and is considered authoritative in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Apply the principle to partial cuts
If only part of the forearm is cut, the judge assesses compensation proportionally. The key is that any cut beyond the palm is not covered by a fixed diyah.
This prevents overcompensation by assuming fixed amounts for all body parts.

Diyah for Child's Eye, Tongue, Penis – Soundness Unknown
Ruling on diyah for child's eye, tongue, penis when soundness unknown

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for a child's eye, tongue, and penis when soundness is unknown, and explain the difference with the Shafi'i position using the supporting athaar.

Core Idea
When a child's organ's soundness is unknown, Hanafis treat it as defective – hukuma, not full diyah.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in diyat: injuries to a child's eye, tongue, or penis when we cannot verify if they were sound. The Hanafi school, based on reports from early authorities like Masruq and Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, holds that a judge's assessment (hukuma) is due, not the full diyah of a sound organ. We will explore the reasoning, the supporting evidence, and the contrasting Shafi'i view that assumes soundness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Read the matn: 'وفي عين الصبي ولسانه وذكره اذا لم يعلم صحته حكومة عدل'. This tells us that if a child's eye, tongue, or penis is injured and its soundness is unknown, the compensation is a just government assessment (hukuma).
The ruling applies only when soundness is not known. If soundness is known, full diyah applies.
2
Examine the underlying reasoning
The commentary explains that because soundness is unknown, the organ is treated like a defective organ (e.g., blind eye, paralyzed hand). The default is not soundness but defectiveness.
This is based on the principle of caution: one cannot claim full diyah without certainty of soundness.
3
Review the supporting evidence (athar)
The text cites athaar from Masruq and Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that for blind eyes, paralyzed hands, and mute tongues, only a judge's assessment is due. These are analogous cases where the organ is known to be defective.
These athaar are found in Musannaf Abdurrazzaq and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba.
4
Compare the Shafi'i opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that since soundness is unknown, we assume the organ is sound and award full diyah. The Hanafi response is that certainty is required for full diyah; uncertainty defaults to the lesser compensation.
This is a fundamental methodological difference over the role of presumption (istishab al-hal) in diyat.
5
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: Under Hanafi, if the organ was actually defective, the victim receives less; under Shafi'i, the victim always receives full diyah regardless. The Hanafi view protects the injurer from overpayment when the organ's value is uncertain.

Cutting a Finger Causing Another to Wither: Arsh, No Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas when consequential injury occurs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reason for the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding qisas when cutting one finger causes another to wither, and state the practical difference in rulings.

Core Idea
When one injury causes a secondary injury, exact equivalence for qisas is impossible, so arsh replaces it.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario in Hanafi penal law: a person intentionally cuts off one finger, and as a consequence, an adjacent finger withers (becomes paralyzed). Should the offender face qisas (retaliation in kind) for the first finger and pay arsh (compensation) for the second, or is only arsh due for both? The Hanafi school has a well-known difference of opinion. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of the conditions for qisas and the principle of equivalent retaliation.

Guided Path
1
State the case
Identify the fact pattern: a deliberate cut to one finger causes an adjacent finger to wither. Note that the first injury is direct and intentional, the second is consequential.
2
Identify the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Abu Hanifa holds that both injuries are one inseparable crime. Since qisas requires exact equivalence (like for like) and the second finger cannot be cut in the same manner (it withered, not cut), qisas is impossible. Therefore, arsh is due for both fingers.
Key principle: the two harms are treated as a single unit.
3
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn & Zufar)
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and Imam Zufar argue that the first cut is intentional, so qisas applies for that finger. The second withering is accidental (like a mistake in consequence), so arsh is due for it.
They treat the two injuries as separate: one intentional, one accidental.
4
Analyze the underlying principles
Abu Hanifa's view rests on the impossibility of exact equivalence (musawaa). The Sahibayn's view rests on classifying each injury independently. The disagreement is over whether to treat the overall act as one or two distinct harms.
Note: Both views are within the Hanafi school; no external madhhab is involved.
5
Note the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, the offender pays arsh for both fingers and avoids qisas. Under the Sahibayn, the offender undergoes qisas (cutting of the first finger) and pays arsh for the second. The difference affects both punishment and compensation.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the difference).

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute. The issue: a person intentionally cuts off one finger, and as a consequence, an adjacent finger withers (becomes paralyzed). The Hanafi school has a difference of opinion. Abu Hanifa holds that both injuries are considered as a single act; since qisas requires exact equivalence (like for like), and here the second finger cannot be 'cut' in retaliation because it withered not from a direct cut, qisas is not possible for either. Tooth pulled out and regrown: arsh dropped
Ruling on compensation when tooth regrows

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on arsh when a tooth regrows, distinguish the majority view from Abu Yusuf's view, and evaluate the evidence from the athar.

Core Idea
If a tooth regrows after injury, no compensation is due – the harm is undone.

This lesson examines a specific case in diyat: what happens when a tooth is pulled out but grows back? Does the victim still deserve compensation? The Hanafi school has a clear position based on an athar from Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, but there is an internal disagreement: Abu Yusuf argues for discretionary compensation. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply dalil and appreciate intra-madhhab nuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is pulling out a tooth that later regrows. The primary Hanafi ruling is that no arsh (fixed compensation) or diyah is due. Note that this is a ruling on the obligation of compensation, not on the act of pulling itself.
The ruling applies only if the tooth regrows naturally within a reasonable period (e.g., one year for a child).
2
Locate the dalil
The main dalil is an athar from Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz: 'If the tooth of a young boy who had not yet changed teeth is damaged, wait for a year; if it grows back, then there is no diyah and no qisas.' This is a Qawl Sahabi (saying of a Companion).
The athar is from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, indicating its transmission is zanni.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase is 'fala diya fiha wa la qawad' – 'no diyah and no qisas for it.' This directly negates any compensation. The reasoning: regrowth restores the body to its original state, eliminating the harm.
In Hanafi usul, a clear negation (لا) in a legal ruling indicates the absence of liability.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees. He argues that the initial act of cutting caused pain and was a crime, so some compensation (ghurm) should be left to the judge's discretion. He cites another athar from Ibn Shihab.
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani uphold the majority view. The mufta bihi is the majority view (no arsh).
5
Evaluate the tarjih and thamarah
The majority view is from zahir al-riwayah and is the relied-upon position. The practical difference: under the majority, the aggressor pays nothing if the tooth regrows; under Abu Yusuf, the judge may order a discretionary payment.
This ikhtilaf illustrates that even within a single school, different dalil can lead to different rulings.

Commentary

This lesson examines a ruling from the Hanafi school on liability when someone creates a hazard in a public road—such as digging a well or placing a stone—resulting in injury or death. The key distinction is between human and animal victims, with different rules of compensation. Understanding the category of 'killing by cause' (qatl bi-sabab) is essential to grasp why the 'aqila bears the diyah for a human life, while the individual pays for an animal.

Guided Path

For human death, diyah is wajib on the 'aqila because it is akin to accidental killing (qatl al-khata'). For animal death, it is a property matter (daman), so the perpetrator pays personally from his own wealth.
This distinction hinges on the nature of the victim: human life vs. property.

The relied-upon position is that for a human, diyah is on the 'aqila; for an animal, compensation is on the perpetrator. This is from the zahir al-riwayah and is considered muttafaq alayh across schools.

Core Idea
When a slave commits an accidental injury, the master may choose to pay the blood money or hand over the slave.

In Hanafi law, when a slave accidentally injures someone, the master is the responsible party because the slave has no 'aqila (tribal guarantors). However, the master is not forced to pay blood money; he has a choice: either pay the compensation (arsh) and keep the slave, or hand over the slave to the victim, who then becomes the owner of the slave in satisfaction of the debt. This ruling is based on the practice of the Companions, particularly 'Umar and 'Ali.

Master Frees Slave Unaware of Injury: Lesser of Value or Arsh
Ruling on master's liability when freeing an injured slave without knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a master's liability when he frees a slave unaware of the slave's accidental injury, and apply the rule of paying the lesser of value or blood money.

Core Idea
An ignorant master owes the lesser of the slave's value or the blood money, not full compensation.

This passage addresses a nuanced situation in diyat: a master frees his slave without knowing that the slave had caused an accidental injury requiring blood money. The Hanafi school limits the master's liability to the lesser of the slave's market value or the blood money amount, balancing the victim's right to compensation with the master's innocence. Let's unpack the ruling and its proof from the tradition of Sufyan al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the master freeing his slave while being unaware of a prior accidental injury committed by the slave. The ruling is that the master must pay the lesser of the slave's current market value or the blood money (arsh) due for that injury.
This is a wajib payment, not a penalty.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar (report) from Sufyan al-Thawri recorded in the Musannaf of Ibn Abi Shaybah. He states: if the master knew of the injury when freeing, he is liable for the full blood money; if he did not know, he is liable for the slave's value.
This is a hadith mawquf (companion-level) but accepted as evidence in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Sufyan's wording explicitly distinguishes between knowledge and ignorance. The phrase 'if he did not know, upon him is the value of the slave' indicates that ignorance limits liability to the slave's value, which is effectively the lesser of the two amounts when compared to the blood money.
The Hanafi understanding is that the master's ignorance excuses him from the full blood money, but he must still compensate the victim for the loss of the slave as a resource for payment.
4
Consider the opposite scenario
If the master knew of the injury at the time of freeing, he is liable for the full blood money. This contrast confirms that ignorance is a mitigating factor.
The same athar provides both rulings.

Commentary
This passage addresses the situation where a master frees his slave without being aware that the slave had previously committed an accidental injury for which blood money was due. The master's liability is limited to the lesser of the slave's market value or the blood money amount. The reasoning is that the master, by freeing the slave, deprives the victim of the ability to claim the slave (if he had been handed over) or to have the slave's value as security. Since the master was ignorant, he is considered excused but must still compensate for the loss caused by freeing the slave. The proof is from Sufyan al-Thawri who distinguishes between knowledge and ignorance: if the master knew, he is liable for the full blood money; if not, he pays the slave's value (i.e., the lesser). This ruling protects the victim while being fair to the ignorant master.

Master's Options for Slave's Two Crimes
Ruling on master's options, with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to a master when a slave commits two crimes and identify the proof from the athar of Salim ibn Abdullah.

Core Idea
A master may either surrender the slave to victims or pay the blood money for each crime.

This lesson from the Book of Blood Money examines the situation where a slave commits two crimes simultaneously. The master is given a binding choice: either hand over the slave to be divided among the victims, or pay the full blood money (arsh) for each injury and keep the slave. The ruling is supported by an athar from Salim ibn Abdullah, demonstrating that both options are permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave committing two crimes; the ruling is that the master has a choice (takhyir) between two options: surrender the slave or pay the blood money.
The text clearly states 'qila limawlahu imma an tadfa'ahu... wa imma an tafdiyah'.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar from Salim ibn Abdullah, recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba.
The athar explicitly mentions the two options.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrasing 'in sha'... wa in sha'' indicates an optional choice, confirming the permissibility of either surrendering or paying ransom.
This linguistic form shows takhyir.
4
Note the cross-madhhab status
The Hanafi school follows this view; other schools may differ, e.g., requiring the master to pay full diyah without the option to surrender.
The hanafi_position_box notes it as mukhtalaf fih.
5
Apply the ruling in practice
The master must choose one of the two options. If he fails to, the matter may be resolved by a judge based on maslahah.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Book of Blood Money (Kitab al-Diyat) regarding a slave who commits multiple crimes. The text first states a general principle: the ruling of the second crime is the same as the first. Then, the specific case: if a slave commits two crimes simultaneously, the master has two options. First, he may surrender the slave to both victims, who will divide the slave's value according to their respective shares. Second, he may pay the full blood money (arsh) for each crime and keep the slave. The passage illustrates with an example: a slave cuts one man's nose and another's ear, each requiring fifty camels as blood money. The master can either give up the slave (who will be sold to satisfy both victims) or pay 100 camels (50+50) and retain ownership. The proof is an effect (athar) from Salim ibn Abdullah, stating the same two options. The footnote clarifies the Arabic phrase. This ruling is based on the principle that the master's liability is limited: he can either bear the financial burden or forfeit the slave. The commentary emphasizes that once the master pays for the first crime, the slave is cleared of that liability; any subsequent crime is treated anew.

The text provides the rule and supports it with an athar from Sufyan al-Thawri.

The ikhtilaf is based on the weight given to the athar and the principle of excusing ignorance.

Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on a direct textual proof and the principle of excusing the ignorant. It is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.
The athar from a Companion (Sufyan al-Thawri) is considered strong evidence.

Core Idea
The master pays the lesser of value or blood money because the mudabbar/umm walad cannot be surrendered.

In previous lessons, we learned that when a regular slave commits a crime, the master can either pay the blood money or hand over the slave to the victim. But for mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) and umm walad (a slave mother of her master's child), the situation is different. Because these slaves have a 'semblance of freedom' due to their future freedom, they cannot be surrendered. This lesson explains how the master's liability is calculated and the proof for this ruling.

Guided Path
1

Recognize that the act is a crime (jinaayah) committed by a mudabbar or umm walad. The ruling is that the master (mawla) is liable for the lesser of the slave's value (qimah) or the blood money (arsh).
The example: value 800 dirhams, blood money 600 dirhams → master pays 600 dirhams.

Understand the reason behind the ruling
The mudabbar or umm walad cannot be handed over to the victim because they have a semblance of freedom (shaa'ibah al-hurriyah) from their future freedom. Since the master's act of making them mudabbar/umm walad prevents surrender, he must compensate, but only up to their value.
If the crime compensation is less than the value, that is paid. The master is not liable for more than the slave's worth.

The dalil is from the practice of the Salaf, considered a strong indication in Hanafi fiqh.
4

If a mudabbar commits a crime worth 1000 dirhams but his value is only 700 dirhams, what does the master pay? Answer: 700 dirhams, because the master's liability is capped at the value.
If the value is less than the blood money, the victim only receives the value. The remaining blood money is not recoverable from the master or the slave later.

If [the value was paid] by court decree, then nothing is upon him, and the guardian of the second crime follows the guardian of the first crime and shares with him in what he took. And if the master paid the value without a court decree, then the guardian has the choice: if he wishes he pursues the master, and if he wishes he pursues the guardian of the first crime.
Source pages p. 345

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi ruling on the liability of the master (mawla) of a mudabbir or umm walad who commits a second assault after the master has already paid the value (qimah) for the first. Two scenarios: (1) If the master paid by court decree (bi-qada), he is discharged; the second victim's guardian must share in the value already paid to the first victim. (2) If paid without a court decree (bi-ghayr qada), the second victim's guardian may choose to pursue the master (arguing the payment was not official) or the first victim's guardian (since the master paid once). The principle is that the master is liable only up to the slave's value once, as per the athar of Sufyan al-Thawri: 'The assault of a mudabbir is upon his master; he guarantees his value.'

Key Terms
مولى (mawla): Master of a slave; the owner of a mudabbir or umm walad.
مُدبَّر (mudabbir): A slave whose manumission is deferred until after the master's death.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne her master's child.
