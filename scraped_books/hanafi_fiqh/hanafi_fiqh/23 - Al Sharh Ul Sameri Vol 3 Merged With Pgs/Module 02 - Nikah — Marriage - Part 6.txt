Milk Mixed with Medicine: Prohibition Applies
Ruling on tahrim when milk is mixed with medicine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when milk is mixed with a foreign substance and apply the principle of predominance (ghalabah) to determine whether prohibition of rada' attaches.

Core Idea
If milk is predominant, prohibition attaches even if mixed with medicine.

In Hanafi fiqh, the prohibition of fosterage (rada') attaches only when the infant consumes milk. But what if the milk is mixed with something else, like medicine? The classical texts rule based on the principle that the majority determines the ruling of the whole. In this passage, the ruling is given for mixing milk with medicine: as long as milk is the predominant component, the prohibition stands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is mixing a woman's breast milk with medicine and feeding it to an infant. The ruling is that the prohibition of fosterage (tahrim) attaches.
This ruling applies only if the infant is within the rada' period (under two years).
2
Identify the condition
The condition is that the milk must be the predominant component (ghalabah) in the mixture. If medicine is predominant, prohibition does not attach.
Predominance is determined by quantity or effect.
3
Apply the principle of ghalabah
The principle is that the majority determines the ruling of the whole (al-ghalib mu'ayyin). Since milk is predominant, the mixture is considered milk for legal purposes.
This is a well-established Hanafi principle.
4
Note the rationale
The rationale is that milk satisfies hunger, while medicine does not. Since the mixture still satisfies hunger (because milk is predominant), the effect of rada' remains intact.
The prohibition is based on the nutritional effect, not merely the substance.
5
Summarize the ruling
If milk is predominant, prohibition attaches. If medicine is predominant or equal, prohibition does not attach.
Equality is treated as non-predominance; the mixture is then not considered milk.

Core Idea
Death of the nurse does not nullify the milk's legal effect; the milk's capacity to satisfy hunger establishes fosterage.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: milk taken from a woman after her death and fed to an infant. The ruling clarifies that the prohibition of fosterage (tahrim al-rada') is based on the milk's property of satisfying hunger, not on the woman being alive. This is a straightforward ruling from the Hanafi school.

Mixing Woman's Milk with Goat's Milk: Predominance Rule
Ruling on tahrim when woman's milk mixed with goat's milk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the predominance rule to determine whether fosterage prohibition (tahrim) is established when human milk is mixed with animal milk.

Core Idea
If human milk predominates, prohibition applies; if animal milk predominates, it does not.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: if a woman's breast milk is accidentally mixed with goat's milk and fed to an infant, does fosterage prohibition arise? The Hanafi school uses a predominance rule: the mixture is treated according to its majority. Let's see how this principle works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mixture components
The mixture contains two types of milk: human breast milk (woman's milk) and animal milk (goat's milk). One of these will be predominant in quantity or weight.
2
Determine predominance
Compare the volumes or weights. If the woman's milk is greater, it is predominant; if the goat's milk is greater, it is predominant. Equal amounts mean no predominance.
In case of equality, the ruling defaults to no prohibition, as predominance is not established.
3
Apply the legal rule
If woman's milk is predominant, prohibition (tahrim) of marriage due to fosterage applies. If goat's milk is predominant, prohibition does not apply.
4
Understand the rationale
The basis is the hadith 'al-rida'ah min al-maja'ah' — fosterage is from hunger. Only the predominant milk can satisfy hunger significantly enough to establish the fosterage relationship.
This ruling is specific to mixtures with animal milk; mixtures of two human milks follow a different rule.

Ruling on Virgin's Milk and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Two Women's Milk
States the ruling on virgin's milk and resolves an intra-Hanafi dispute on two women's milk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a virgin's milk establishes fosterage prohibition and explain the disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the case of two women's milk.

Core Idea
A virgin's milk creates fosterage; two women's milk: Abu Yusuf counts only the dominant, Muhammad counts both.

This passage addresses two related issues in Hanafi fosterage (rada'ah). First, it confirms that even a virgin woman can establish fosterage prohibition if she produces milk, regardless of the cause. Second, it presents a classical disagreement between two pillars of the Hanafi school—Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani—on the effect when two women breastfeed the same child. The dispute hinges on whether the milk that satisfies hunger is the 'original' and the other is 'subordinate,' or whether both are equal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on virgin's milk
Locate the statement that if a virgin woman's milk descends and she breastfeeds an infant, fosterage prohibition attaches.
The key phrase is 'albakr' and 'yataba' bihi al-tahrim'.
2
Trace the proof for the ruling
The author cites Quran 4:23 'your mothers who nursed you' as the basis. Understand that the general term 'ummahat' includes any nursing woman.
The dalala is qati, meaning the proof is definitive in establishing the prohibition.
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi issue
The issue: when two women breastfeed the same infant, does prohibition attach by both women or only by the one whose milk is more satisfying?
This is a dispute within the school itself, not between madhabs.
4
Compare the reasoning of the two imams
Abu Yusuf holds that the dominant milk is the asl (original) and the other is tabi' (subordinate); Muhammad holds that both are of the same jins (kind) and thus both are originals.
Their difference is in identifying the 'illa (effective cause) of the prohibition.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Yusuf's view, the child is only prohibited to marry the woman with more milk and her relatives; under Muhammad's view, the child is prohibited to both women and their relatives.
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi, so both remain valid opinions within the school.

Fosterage between Two Wives: Prohibition and Financial Effects
Presents a scenario of fosterage between two wives with consequences on marriage validity and mahr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the prohibition of marriage when fosterage occurs between two wives and determine the mahr obligations for each wife.

Core Idea
Breastfeeding a co-wife creates a mother-daughter foster bond, making both wives permanently haram.

This passage deals with a unique scenario where a man is married to an infant girl and an older woman. If the older woman breastfeeds the infant, a foster relationship is established, rendering both marriages invalid. The ruling is derived by analogy to the prohibition of simultaneously marrying a mother and her daughter. The passage also explains the financial implications, distinguishing between intentional and unintentional fosterage regarding mahr liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific act? The older wife breastfeeds the young wife. The ruling: both wives become permanently forbidden (haram) for the husband.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites an analogy (qiyas) to the prohibition of combining a biological mother and daughter in marriage, which is established by Quran 4:23.
The exact wording: "just as combining a biological mother and daughter is forbidden."
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illa) is the creation of a foster mother-daughter relationship through breastfeeding within the first two years. This triggers the same prohibition as blood relation.
Hanafi school equates fosterage with lineage for marriage prohibitions.
4
Analyze the financial consequences
For the older wife: no mahr if no consummation, because she caused the dissolution. For the young wife: half mahr. If the older acted intentionally, the husband can recover the half mahr from her.
The cause of separation matters for mahr entitlement.

Minor Boy as Muhallil: Ruling on When He Can Make Woman Permissible for First Husband
Hanafi ruling that adolescent boy's intercourse suffices for tahleel

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A minor boy's intercourse, even without ejaculation, suffices for tahleel because the 'illa is pleasure, not emission.

This lesson examines a specific ruling within the broader topic of tahleel—the process by which a thrice-divorced woman becomes permissible for her first husband through a valid intervening marriage. The Hanafi school holds a distinctive position that an adolescent boy (murahiq) can serve as a muhallil even without reaching full puberty or ejaculating. This ruling is supported by an athar (tradition) from the early scholar 'Ata ibn Abi Rabah and is contrasted with the position of Imam Malik.

3

Marriage with Condition of Tahlil: Makruh but Valid
Ruling with proof: marriage with condition of tahlil is makruh but valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a marriage contracted with the condition of tahlil and explain why it is makruh but valid, citing the dalil.

Core Idea
A marriage conditioned on tahlil is makruh but valid – the curse targets the condition, not the contract.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a woman divorced three times wishes to remarry her first husband, so she marries a second man with the explicit condition that he will divorce her after consummation. The Hanafi school rules that such a marriage is valid (sahih) but severely disliked (makruh tahrimi). The dislike stems from the prophetic curse on the muhallil, but the contract itself remains legally effective. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct fatwa on complex marital reconciliations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? It is a marriage (nikah) with an explicit condition of tahlil (temporary marriage to make the woman lawful for the first husband). The ruling is that it is makruh (disliked) but valid.
Makruh here is tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) due to the hadith.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites two hadith: (1) from Abdullah ibn Mas'ud: the Prophet cursed the muhallil and the muhallal lahu; (2) from Uqba ibn Amir: the Prophet called the muhallil a 'borrowed stud' and cursed him. These are the proofs for karahah.
Hadith are ahad (zanni thubut) but sufficient for prohibition.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The curse indicates the act is sinful, hence haram or makruh tahrimi. However, the prohibition targets the condition (tahlil), not the essence of the marriage contract. In Hanafi usul, a condition that contradicts the purpose of marriage does not invalidate the contract – it only makes the act of stipulating it disliked.
This is a key usul principle: prohibitions on conditions do not void the contract.
4
Note the practical consequence
Despite the karahah, if the second husband consummates and divorces, the woman becomes lawful for the first husband. The marriage achieves its intended effect. This shows the validity.
The passage also notes that if both intend tahlil without stating the condition, there is no karahah.
5
Consider the thamarah al-khilaf
Some other schools may consider such a marriage batil. The Hanafi position means that the woman does not need another marriage; she can return to her first husband after fulfillment. This has significant practical implications for family reconciliation.
The cross-madhhab dispute is noted but not detailed in this passage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on denial of paternity, distinguish between valid and invalid timing, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Denial of paternity must be immediate; silence after birth counts as acknowledgment.

This passage from the chapter on Li'an explains the critical timing requirement for a husband's denial of paternity. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the denial must occur immediately after birth or at the moment of congratulations or purchasing birth supplies. If the husband delays, his silence is treated as tacit acknowledgment, and he cannot later deny the child. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad extend the valid period to the entire postpartum period (nifas). The proofs include a hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari and rulings of Caliph Umar.

Identify the act and its ruling

The ruling is not about the validity of li'an itself, but about the validity of the denial as a precondition.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that denial is valid only immediately after birth or at the time of congratulations/purchase. The Sahibayn extend it to the entire postpartum period (nifas). Understand each view's reasoning.
This is an ikhtilaf within the school, not between madhabs.
3
Examine the proofs
The text cites a hadith that a child whose husband denied paternity was attributed to the mother (sunan nabawi). Also, athar of Umar shows that acknowledgment after denial establishes paternity and imposes hadd.
These proofs confirm that immediate denial severs lineage; delay equals acknowledgment.

Understand the legal basis
Silence after birth is considered tacit acknowledgment (iqrar 'amali). The basis is the maxim that silence in a context of immediate need (like receiving congratulations) counts as consent or acknowledgment.
This principle applies in other areas of fiqh as well.

If the husband denies within the immediate window, lineage is severed after li'an. If he delays, according to Abu Hanifa the denial is invalid in terms of severing lineage, but li'an is still performed because of the accusation. According to Sahibayn, if done in nifas, the denial is valid.

Acknowledging one twin from the same pregnancy establishes lineage for both; denying the second requires li'an, not hadd.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in family law: twin birth where the husband accepts paternity of the first child but rejects the second. The Hanafi ruling is grounded in the principle that twins originate from the same semen, so recognizing one necessarily recognizes both. Commentary
This ruling clarifies the status of a female slave (ama) who has been freed, and an umm walad (a slave who bore her master's child) after she is freed. Upon emancipation, they acquire the same rights as a free woman in matters of child custody (hadanah). The commentator emphasizes that once freed, they are no longer subject to the restrictions of slavery and can claim the same custody rights as any free mother.

Key Terms
ام الولد (umm al-walad): A female slave who has given birth to her master's child, gaining certain rights including eventual freedom upon the master's death.

No Custody for Slave Woman or Umm Walad Before Freedom
Ruling on slave woman and umm walad custody before freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on custody rights of a slave woman and an umm walad before emancipation.

Core Idea
A slave mother has no custody right until she is freed.

This brief ruling from Al-Sharh al-Saghir clarifies an important boundary: the right of hadanah (custody) belongs only to free women. For a woman who remains a slave (ama) or an umm walad (a slave mother of her master's child), that right does not exist until emancipation. Understanding this ruling ties custody rights to legal capacity, not just biological motherhood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subjects
The ruling mentions two types of women: the 'ama' (female slave) and the 'umm walad' (a slave mother who has borne her master's child). Both are still in a state of slavery.
The umm walad is a specific status: she cannot be sold, but she is still a slave.
2
Identify the condition
The phrase 'before freedom' (qabl al-'itq) indicates that the ruling is conditional on the woman not yet being emancipated. Once freed, her status changes.
3
Identify the ruling
The text states 'la haqq' (no right) meaning they have no legal claim to custody of the child. The child's custody belongs to the master or to other free relatives.
This is a definitive ruling; no exceptions for slave mothers.
