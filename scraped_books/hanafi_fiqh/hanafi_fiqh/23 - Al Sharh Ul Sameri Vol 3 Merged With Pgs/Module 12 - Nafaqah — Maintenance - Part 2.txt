Priority Order of Sisters and Aunts in Custody
Ruling on priority of sisters and aunts in hadanah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi priority order among sisters and aunts for custody and apply the hadith 'al-khalah bimanzilat al-umm' as a proof.

Core Idea
Full sisters first, then uterine, then consanguine; maternal aunts outrank paternal aunts.

Custody (hadanah) is a crucial right for the child. When multiple female relatives claim custody, Hanafi fiqh provides a clear hierarchy. This lesson unpacks the order of sisters and aunts, supported by the prophetic hadith that the maternal aunt holds the position of the mother.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The text states that sisters take priority over aunts when no grandmother is available. This is the first level of priority among female relatives after the mother and grandmother.
The grandmother herself is prioritized over sisters, as mentioned earlier in the source.
2
Order the sisters
Among sisters, the full sister (from both parents) is most entitled, followed by the uterine sister (from mother only), then the consanguine sister (from father only). The rationale given is closeness of relation and degree of affection.
This order mirrors inheritance priority in some ways but has its own logic.
3
Order the aunts
Maternal aunts (khalat) are preferred over paternal aunts (ammat). This is supported by the hadith 'al-khalah bimanzilat al-umm' – the maternal aunt is in the position of the mother.
The same gradation (full, uterine, consanguine) applies among maternal and paternal aunts.
4
Apply the hadith proof
The hadith from Bukhari (no. 2699) is cited: 'He said: the maternal aunt is in the position of the mother.' This establishes the maternal aunt's elevated status in custody matters, making her superior to the paternal aunt.
In Hanafi usul, this ahad hadith is zanni al-thubut but sufficient for establishing a ruling when no contradictory evidence exists.
5
Extend the same ordering to aunts
The text explicitly states that aunts 'descend' in the same order as sisters: full maternal aunt first, then consanguine maternal aunt, then uterine maternal aunt; similarly for paternal aunts.
This ensures a consistent hierarchy across all female relatives after the mother and grandmother.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling stating the priority order among female relatives entitled to custody of a child. The author first establishes that sisters (akhavat) take precedence over aunts (ammat and khalat) if no grandmother exists. Then within sisters, the full sister (from both parents) comes first, followed by the uterine sister (from mother only), then the consanguine sister (from father only). The rationale given in the Urdu sharh is that the full sister is related from both sides, and the uterine sister has more affection. Next, maternal aunts (khalat) are preferred over paternal aunts (ammat), supported by the hadith 'the maternal aunt is in the position of the mother.' Finally, the same gradation applies among aunts as among sisters: full, then consanguine, then uterine. This ruling is foundational for determining the female custodian when multiple relatives claim.

Forfeiture of Custody Upon Marriage and Male Custodian Priority
Stating that marriage of a female custodian forfeits her right, except grandmother, and ordering male relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a female custodian loses her right to custody, identify the exception for the grandmother, and apply the order of male custodians based on asabah priority.

Core Idea
Marriage to a stranger voids custody; grandmother's marriage to grandfather is the exception. Male custody follows inheritance order.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies two key principles of Hanafi custody law. First, a female custodian loses her right upon marriage to a non-mahram, with a critical exception for the grandmother. Second, when no female custodian is available, the right passes to male relatives in strict order of their asabah (agnatic) priority. The proofs include a hadith and the practice of the early caliphs.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Identify the two distinct rulings
The passage covers two separate issues: (a) when marriage terminates a woman's custody right, and (b) the order of male custodians if no woman is available. Separate these to avoid confusion.
2
Step 2: Locate the proofs for the first ruling
The hadith 'You have more right as long as you do not marry' is the primary proof. Also, the athar of Abu Bakr ruling against Umar. Note the condition 'as long as you do not marry' implies the right ends upon marriage.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud, classified as ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Step 3: Analyze the exception for the grandmother
The text explicitly says 'except the grandmother if her husband is the grandfather.' This means if she marries the child's grandfather (a mahram), her right remains. Understand the reasoning: marriage to a mahram does not create a conflict of interest.
4
Step 4: Apply the male custody order
When no female is available, the nearest asabah in the inheritance hierarchy takes precedence: son, father, grandfather, brother, uncle, etc. This mirrors the order of inheritance by ta'sib. The proof is the story of Hamzah's daughter.

Commentary
This passage contains two distinct rulings. First, any female custodian who marries a stranger (ajnabi) loses her right to custody, with the sole exception of the grandmother if she marries the child's grandfather (a mahram). The reason given is that marriage to a stranger places her under her husband's authority and may reduce her affection for the child. Proofs cited include the hadith 'You have more right as long as you do not marry' and the practice of the early caliphs. Second, if no female relative is available, the right passes to male agnates (asabah) in order of their priority in inheritance: closest in asabah first (son, father, grandfather, etc.). This is supported by the story of Hamzah's daughter, where the Prophet gave priority to the maternal aunt over the asabah, but the ruling here is that when neither mother nor maternal aunt exists, the nearest asabah takes custody. The commentary clarifies that the order among asabah follows the same as in inheritance law.

Duration of Maternal Custody for a Boy Until Independence
Matn ruling on custody duration with two hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a boy's maternal custody ends, explain the hadith proofs, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Maternal custody ends when the boy can independently eat, drink, and dress — not at a fixed age, but around seven.

This lesson examines the Matn's ruling that the mother and grandmother have the primary right to custody of a boy until he can care for his basic needs. The passage provides two hadith proofs: the command to order prayer at seven, and the incident of the Persian woman whose son chose to stay with his mother. We will analyse how these proofs support the Hanafi position that custody ends based on the child's capability, not a strict age.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the first line: 'The mother and grandmother are more entitled to the boy until he eats on his own, drinks on his own, and dresses on his own.' This sets the condition for the end of custody.
2
Understand the condition
The condition is independence in basic self-care, not a fixed age. This is unique to the Hanafi school.
Hanafi: custody is a right (haqq) of the mother, not a duty.
3
Examine the prayer hadith
The Prophet (ﷺ) said: 'Command the boy to pray when he reaches seven years, and when he reaches ten years, beat him for it.' This indicates that at seven, the child is accountable for basic duties, implying he is no longer in need of maternal custody.
The hadith comes from Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.
4
Examine the Persian woman hadith
A Persian woman came to the Prophet (ﷺ) because her husband wanted to take her son. The Prophet told the son to choose between them, and the son chose his mother. This shows that when a boy has discernment, he can choose, and custody ends.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud.
5
Derive the Hanafi position
Combining both hadiths, the Hanafi school holds that custody lasts until the boy can independently manage his basic needs, typically around age seven but depending on the child's ability.
The Shafi'i school sets a fixed age of seven or weaning; Hanafi uses capability.

Core Idea
Nursing expenses are first taken from the child's own wealth; if none, from his 'asabah (agnatic heirs).

This lesson focuses on the second part of the Matn: the financial responsibility for nursing (fosterage) a child. The ruling establishes a hierarchy: the child's own wealth is used first; only if the child has none do the 'asabah become liable. Guided Path

In practice, if the child owns property or money, it is used first. If he has nothing, the father (as primary 'asabah) pays. If the father is unable, the grandfather, and so on.
The order of 'asabah follows the inheritance hierarchy.

Ruling that maintenance of parents is solely on the child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a child's maintenance of his parents is an independent wajib obligation and explain the dalil from the hadith 'anta wa maluka li-abik'.

Core Idea
A child's maintenance for parents is independent; no one else shares this obligation.

This passage establishes a key principle in Hanafi family finance: when a child provides for his parents, he cannot consider the maintenance of his own children as part of that obligation. The child's duty to parents is separate and personal. The proof comes from a powerful hadith that affirms the father's right over the child's wealth, implying an independent duty of support.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first statement: 'ولا يشارك الولد في نفقة ابويه احد'. This is the core ruling: no one shares with the child in maintaining his parents. Understand that the child's obligation is individual, not collective with anyone else (including his own children).
The Urdu commentary clarifies that the child should not make his own children partners in the expenditure for his parents.
2
Understand the Legal Rationale
The commentary states that the child's maintenance for parents is an independent wajib obligation, separate from the child's obligation to his own children. This means each obligation stands on its own; they cannot be combined or shared.
This is a fundamental distinction: two separate wajib duties exist – one for parents, one for children.
3
Locate the Dalil
The passage cites the hadith of 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb: a man complained to the Prophet (ﷺ) that his father was taking all his wealth, and the Prophet said 'anta wa maluka li-abik' (you and your wealth belong to your father). Also 'inna awladakum min atyabi kasbikum fa-kulu min amwalikum'.
The hadith is from Ibn Majah and is a sunnah ahad with zanni thubut and dalala.
4
Extract the Wajh al-Dalalah
The phrase 'anta wa maluka li-abik' shows that the father has a right over the child's wealth. This right implies a corresponding duty on the child to provide maintenance. Since the father's right is direct and unrestricted, the child's obligation is independent and not shared with others.
The hadith does not explicitly mention maintenance, but the Hanafi school infers wujub from the general right.
5
Confirm the Hukm and Its Scope
The child's maintenance for parents is wajib, and it is an independent obligation. This means if the child has his own children, he must still fulfill the parents' maintenance fully; he cannot reduce it because he also spends on his children.
The practical outcome: the child must prioritize the parents' maintenance at its full amount, even if he has other dependents.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on maintenance (nafaqah) for mahram relatives—those marriage-prohibited kin. Conditions for Obligatory Maintenance on Relatives
States three conditions for nafaqah on relatives and proves with Quran, hadith, and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for obligatory nafaqah on relatives and explain how the amount of nafaqah is determined by inheritance shares.

Core Idea
Maintenance on poor mahram relatives is wajib on heirs in proportion to their inheritance shares.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on maintenance (nafaqah) for poor relatives. While many know that maintenance is due to parents and children, the school extends it to other mahram relatives under specific conditions. The amount is not arbitrary but tied to the inheritance shares, making it a proportional obligation. We will study the conditions, the proofs from Qur'an and Sunnah, and how to apply this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its nature
First, recognise that the passage establishes nafaqah on relatives as wajib (obligatory). Then note that the obligation is conditional and proportional.
2
List the three conditions
From the passage, extract the three requirements: (1) the relative is poor, (2) the relative is unable to earn (due to disability, minority, or being a woman without support), and (3) there is no closer person already providing maintenance.
The condition of inability to earn is key; if the relative can work despite poverty, nafaqah is not wajib.
3
Understand the amount mechanism
The amount of nafaqah is based on the inheritance share each heir would receive from that relative if he/she died. Example: two brothers each pay half.
This is derived from the Qur'anic phrase 'wa 'ala al-warith mithlu dhalik'.
4
Examine the proofs
The passage cites four types of evidence: Qur'an (2:233), hadith from Abu Dawud and Nasa'i, and athar from Zaid ibn Thabit, al-Dahhak, and Abu Bakr. Understand each proof and how it supports the ruling.
The athar from Abu Bakr limits the amount to 'sufficiency' (kifayah).
5
Apply to a scenario
Take the example given: two brothers and a poor blind sister. Calculate the nafaqah: each brother pays half of her basic needs, because each inherits half of her estate if she died.
This illustrates proportionality in practice.

Commentary
This passage is an expository commentary on the conditions for obligatory maintenance (nafaqah) on relatives in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by stating a ruling: maintenance on a relative is obligatory in proportion to the inheritance share. It then enumerates three conditions: (1) the relative is poor, (2) unable to earn due to disability (e.g., blindness, chronic illness, old age) or because they are a minor or adult woman without means, and (3) there is no one else closer to support them. The amount of maintenance is determined by the inheritance shares assigned by the Quran and Sunnah; for example, if two brothers are heirs, each pays half the maintenance. The passage then cites major proofs: the Quranic verse (2:233) 'And upon the heir is the like of that' establishing that maintenance is an obligation on heirs in proportion to their inheritance; a hadith from Abu Dawud where the Prophet orders honoring mother, father, sister, brother, and relatives as an obligatory right; a hadith from Nasa'i ordering to begin with those you support (mother, father, sister, brother, then nearest kin); an athar from Zaid ibn Thabit showing that if an uncle and mother are heirs, their maintenance is proportional to their inheritance; an athar from al-Dahhak that if a child has no wealth, nursing is on the 'asaba; and an athar from Abu Bakr that only what suffices may be taken from a son's wealth, not all of it. This last evidence establishes that the amount is 'sufficiency' (kifayah) and not more. The Hanafi position is that nafaqah on relatives is wajib based on need and inheritance proximity, with the amount matching the heir's share.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for maintenance of an adult daughter and disabled son on parents, explain the proportion (father 2/3, mother 1/3), and derive it from the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Maintenance of adult dependents is wajib on parents in proportion to their inheritance shares.

This lesson addresses a specific case of nafaqah: when an adult daughter is poor or an adult son is disabled and cannot work, who is responsible for their maintenance? The Hanafi school provides a precise ruling tied to inheritance proportions. We will explore the ruling, its ratio, and the evidence from the Quran and the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the dependent categories
The matn states that maintenance is wajib on the parents for an adult daughter (baligha) and a disabled son (zaman). The ruling only applies when they are poor or unable to earn.
The daughter is assumed to be without wealth; the son must be disabled (e.g., blind, chronically ill).
2
Understand the division ratio
The maintenance is divided 'athlathan' (in thirds): the father bears two-thirds, the mother one-third. This ratio mirrors the parents' inheritance shares from the child (father gets double the mother's share).
In Hanafi inheritance, when a child dies, the father inherits 2/3 and the mother 1/3 (if no spouse or offspring).
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The Quranic verse 2:233 says: 'And upon the heir (al-waarith) is the like of that' (wa 'ala al-waarithi mithlu dhaalik). This is interpreted to mean that maintenance is obligatory on each heir according to the amount they inherit.
The verse is from the context of breastfeeding, but the phrase 'heir' is general.
4
Consider the athar from Zaid ibn Thabit
The commentary cites an athar that when there are two heirs (uncle and mother), maintenance is proportional to inheritance. This supports the principle that nafaqah follows the inheritance share.
This athar is recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
5
See the practical reasoning
Since the father inherits double, he is obligated double in maintenance. The mother inherits half of the father's share, so she is obligated half. Thus the ratio is 2:1.
The ruling applies regardless of whether the child actually dies; it is a legal principle for living support.

No Maintenance with Difference of Religion
States that maintenance is not obligatory on relatives if they differ in religion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that maintenance of a mahram relative is not obligatory when they differ in religion, and explain the proof from Surah al-Baqarah 2:233.

Core Idea
Maintenance of a mahram relative is not obligatory if they differ in religion because the obligation is tied to inheritance.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that a Muslim is not required to provide maintenance to a mahram relative who is a non-Muslim. The source text clarifies that this applies to relatives other than parents, children, and spouse, who have separate rules. The ruling is based on a Quranic verse linking maintenance to inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is providing maintenance to a mahram relative. The ruling is that it is not obligatory when there is difference of religion (ikhtilaf al-din).
Note the exception: parents, children, and wife are not covered by this ruling.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is Quran 2:233, specifically the phrase 'and upon the heir is the like of that' (وَعَلَى الْوَارِثِ مِثْلُ ذَلِكَ).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'heir' (الوارث) is interpreted as a condition: only if the relative is an heir does the obligation exist. Non-Muslims cannot inherit from Muslims, so the obligation is lifted.
This is a clear example of a textual condition excluding a category.
4
Identify the scope and exceptions
The ruling applies to mahram relatives other than parents, children, and wife. These have separate obligations (e.g., parents are obligatory even if non-Muslim).
Understanding the exceptions prevents overgeneralization.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A poor person is not required to give maintenance to relatives because obligation requires capacity.

This short extract from a Hanafi fiqh text establishes a clear exception to the general duty of nafaqah: if the potential provider is themselves poor (faqir), the obligation falls away. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the principle that the obligation to support depends on the ability to give.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is providing nafaqah (maintenance) to relatives. The ruling stated in the text is that it is not obligatory (لا تجب).
The Arabic verb تجب indicates obligation (wujub). The negation lifts this obligation.
2
Identify the condition that triggers the ruling
The condition is that the person is poor (faqir). The Urdu commentary clarifies that maintenance is not necessary on a poor person.
In Hanafi fiqh, faqir is defined as someone who does not possess wealth sufficient for basic needs.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Obligations in nafaqah are contingent upon capacity. A poor person lacks the means to support others and is therefore exempt.
This aligns with the hadith 'The upper hand is better than the lower hand' – giving requires surplus.
4
Apply the exception correctly
If the person is not poor (i.e., has surplus wealth), the general obligation remains. This ruling only applies to those classified as faqir.
Poverty must be genuine; temporary lack of cash or assets that are not liquid may not qualify.

Maintenance of Adult Daughter and Disabled Son on Parents in Thirds
Ruling with proof: maintenance shares for parents of adult daughter or disabled son

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that maintenance of an adult daughter or disabled son is wajib on parents in proportion to their inheritance shares, using the dalil from Quran and athar.

Core Idea
Maintenance of adult children unable to earn is wajib on parents in the same proportion as their inheritance shares: father pays 2/3, mother 1/3.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri states a specific Hanafi ruling on maintenance (nafaqah) for an adult daughter or a disabled son who cannot support themselves. The ruling is not arbitrary but is derived from a key principle: maintenance follows inheritance shares. The commentator provides Quranic evidence (2:233) and an athar from Zaid ibn Thabit to show that the obligation is proportional to what each parent inherits from the child. Understanding this principle helps you see how the Hanafi school derives secondary rulings from primary texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Ratio
State the ruling from the matn: maintenance of the adult daughter and disabled son is wajib upon both parents, divided in thirds – father pays two-thirds, mother one-third. Note the conditions implied: the daughter is balighah and poor/unmarried; the son is disabled and poor.
2
Locate the Primary Dalil
Find the Quranic verse cited: 'Wa 'ala al-warithu mithlu dhalik' (2:233). This is the key proof that maintenance is obligatory on the heir.
Thubut is qati', dalala is zanni – the exact proportionality is inferred.
3
Analyze the Wajh al-Dalalah
The phrase 'mithlu dhalik' (like that) indicates that the maintenance obligation is 'like' or proportional to the inheritance share. Thus, if a father inherits twice what a mother inherits, his maintenance is double.
The proportionality is not explicit in the verse but derived through this linguistic inference.
4
Examine the Supporting Athar
The athar from Zaid ibn Thabit confirms that when an uncle and mother are heirs, maintenance is according to inheritance shares. This strengthens the Hanafi position that the same principle applies to parents.
Even though the athar is about uncle and mother, the qiyas extends it to parents.
5
Apply the Principle to Parents
Since a father inherits twice as much as a mother from a child's estate (e.g., father gets 1/3, mother 1/6 if there are children), his maintenance obligation is double, yielding the 2/3 to 1/3 split.
This is the operative rule for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

No Maintenance with Difference of Religion
Ruling that difference of religion negates maintenance for non-inheriting mahram relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance for non-Muslim mahram relatives and explain the Quranic evidence that ties maintenance to inheritance.

Core Idea
Maintenance of mahram relatives follows inheritance; difference of religion prevents inheritance, thus no obligation.

In previous lessons, we covered maintenance obligations for parents, children, and spouses regardless of religion. This lesson addresses the more restricted category of 'other mahram relatives' (siblings, uncles, aunts). The Hanafi school holds that for these relatives, maintenance is due only if they can inherit. Since difference of religion bars inheritance between Muslim and non-Muslim, the maintenance obligation falls away.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is providing maintenance (nafaqah) to a mahram relative (such as a sibling or uncle) when there is a difference of religion. The ruling is that it is not obligatory (lā tajib).
This ruling applies only to non-parent, non-child, non-spouse mahram relatives.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the Quranic verse: 'Wa 'ala al-wārithi mithlu dhālika' (And upon the heir is the like of that) from Surah al-Baqarah 2:233.
The verse is understood to mean that maintenance is incumbent upon those who are heirs of the relative.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'upon the heir' restricts the obligation to those who can inherit. Since difference of religion prevents inheritance between Muslim and non-Muslim, the relative is not an heir, and therefore maintenance is not obligatory.
The connection is that maintenance follows inheritance capacity.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Shafi'is hold that maintenance is due to non-Muslim parents and children regardless of religion, but for other mahram relatives they similarly tie it to inheritance. The Hanafi position consistently applies the principle to all non-heir relatives.
The practical consequence: Hanafis do not oblige maintenance for any non-Muslim mahram relative except parents, children, and spouse.

Liability of a Stranger Spending from a Minor's Property Without Qadi Permission
Ruling on a stranger being liable when spending from child's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a stranger spends from a child's property on the parents without qadi permission and explain why the stranger is liable.

Core Idea
A stranger holding a child's property is liable if he spends it without qadi permission, even for the parents' maintenance.

We have learned that parents may take maintenance from their child's property without liability. But what if the property is in the hands of a stranger? This lesson clarifies that a stranger is merely a custodian; spending without judicial authorization makes him personally liable for the amount spent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The act is a stranger (ajnabī) spending the child's property on the parents. The ruling: the stranger becomes liable (ḍāmin) for the amount spent.
Liability means the stranger must repay the minor's estate.
2
Understand the distinction from the parents' case
Parents have a direct right to maintenance from their child; they can take it from the child's property without liability. The stranger has no such right.
The right is attached to the parent-child relationship, not to possession.
3
Recognize the stranger's role as custodian
The stranger holding the property is a muḥāfiẓ (custodian). His duty is to preserve the property, not to disburse it. Any disbursement without authorization exceeds his authority.
Even if the spending is for wajib nafaqah, the custodian still oversteps.
4
Apply the exception: qadi permission
If the qadi orders the spending, the stranger is not liable because the qadi's permission is a general authorization (idhn ʿāmm) that grants him the right to disburse.
The qadi's authority covers all proper disbursements; the custodian is then merely executing the judge's order.

Master's Obligation to Maintain Slaves
Rulings on master's duty to maintain slaves and actions if he refuses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a master must provide nafaqa (food and clothing) to his slaves, and apply the graded remedies when the master refuses: the slave may use his earnings, and if no earnings, the master is compelled to sell.

Core Idea
A master must feed and clothe his slaves; if he refuses, the slave may take from his own earnings; if none, the master is forced to sell.

This passage from the Book of Nafaqat establishes the master's duty to maintain his slaves. It cites prophetic hadith as proof and then outlines two sequential remedies when the master fails: first, the slave may draw upon his own earnings to survive, and second, if no earnings exist, the court compels the master to sell the slave so that a new master can provide maintenance. The passage also warns of severe consequences for neglecting dependents, using the hadith of the woman punished for starving a cat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Obligation
The text begins by stating it is wajib upon the master (mawlā) to spend on his male slave (ʿabd) and female slave (amah). The reason given is that the slaves work for the master, so maintenance is a reciprocal obligation.
2
Locate the Dalil
Two hadiths are cited: (1) 'For the slave is his food and his clothing, and he should not be burdened beyond his capacity' (Muslim). (2) The report of Abu Dharr and the Prophet's instruction to feed and clothe slaves as oneself and not overburden them (Bukhari/Muslim). These prove the obligation.
The proofs are from Sunnah ahad, which in Hanafi usul indicates wujub, not fard.
3
Analyze the First Remedy
If the master refuses to provide nafaqa and the slaves have earnings (from previous work or current labor), the slaves are permitted to take their maintenance from that earnings. This preserves the slave's life and also protects the master's wealth (since the earnings belong to the master).
The passage cites the hadith of Abu Tayba, where the Prophet reduced his tax, indicating that slaves could use their wages for maintenance.
4
Analyze the Second Remedy
If the slaves have no earnings and cannot work, the court compels the master to sell them. This ensures the slave receives maintenance from a new master. The justification is that allowing the slave to die would incur punishment upon the master, analogized from the hadith of the woman punished for starving a cat.
The text also mentions that overburdening a slave is prohibited, from the earlier hadith.
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
While the passage does not discuss other schools, the Hanafi ruling is that the master's obligation is wajib (not fard) and the remedies are specific: the slave does not become free, nor is the master fined; rather, the slave uses his earnings or is sold.
The practical consequence: the master's property rights are preserved, but the slave's right to life is given priority through sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a master's obligation when a slave cannot earn and maintenance is neglected, and explain the qiyas (analogy) from the hadith of the cat used to derive the ruling.

Core Idea
Neglect of a slave's maintenance is akin to neglect of a cat; sale is compelled to prevent death.

This passage from the Kitab al-Nafaqat addresses a critical scenario: a master who fails to maintain a slave who cannot work. Using a hadith about a cat, the Hanafi jurists deduce that the master must sell the slave to ensure maintenance. Let's examine the ruling and the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the master is obligated to do and the consequence of neglect. The master must either provide maintenance (nafaqah) or sell the slave if the slave cannot earn. If he does neither, the judge compels the sale.
The ruling is wajib: the master is forced (yujbar) to sell.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The hadith about the woman punished for neglecting a cat (not feeding or watering it) is cited. This is a Sunnah ahad (singular narration) from Muslim.
The hadith is used as the basis for qiyas.
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah (how the dalil proves the ruling)
The analogy (qiyas) works as follows: the cat is a living being dependent on its owner; neglecting it caused punishment. A human slave is more significant, so neglecting maintenance is even graver. Thus, the master must sell to avoid sin.
The illa (effective cause) is the failure to provide sustenance to a dependent being under one's care.
4
Note the supporting hadith
The second hadith 'He is not burdened except with what he can bear' supports that the slave cannot be overburdened, reinforcing the duty to sell rather than force work beyond capacity.
This strengthens the ruling by showing the slave's incapacity is acknowledged.
5
State the practical outcome
The master is compelled to sell the slave to a new owner who will provide maintenance. This prevents the slave's death and the master's liability for punishment.
The ruling is specifically for slaves who cannot earn; if they can earn, the master is not forced to sell.

كتاب النفقات المولى على بيعهما . ار آقا غلام باندی کا نفقہ بھی ادا نہیں کرتا ہے اور غلام باندی کوئی کام بھی نہیں کر سکتے ہیں اور نہ ان کی کوئی کمائی ہے تو آقا کو مجبور کیا جائے گا کہ وہ اس کو بیچ دے تاکہ دوسرا آقا ان کا نفقہ ادا کر سکے اور ان کی جان جانے سے بیچا سکے۔ حدیث میں ہے کہ جانور کو کھانا نہیں دیا وہ مر گیا تو عذاب ہوگا۔ اس لئے اگر انسان کو نفقہ نہیں دیا اور بیچا بھی نہیں اور مر گیا تو آقا کو عذاب ہوگا۔ حدیث میں ہے۔ عن ابي هريرة ان رسول الله قال عذبت امرأة في هرة لم تطعمها ولم تسقها ولم تتركها تاكل من خشاش الارض (الف) (مسلم شریف، باب تحریم قتل الهر ة ص ۲۳۶ نمبر ۲۲۴۳، کتاب السلام ) جب جانور کو نفقہ نہ دے اور وہ مر جائے تو عذاب ہوتا ہے تو انسان کو نفقہ نہ دے اور وہ مرجائے تو آقا کو عذاب ہوگا ، اس لئے اس کو بیچنے پر مجبور کیا جائے گا (۲) اوپر کی حدیث ولا يكلف من العمل الا ما يطبق (مسلم شریف نمبر ۱۶۶۲) سے بھی اس کی تائید ہوتی ہے کہ طاقت سے زیادہ غلام باندی پر بوجھ ڈالنا جائز نہیں ہے۔ حاشیہ : (الف) حضور نے فرمایا ایک عورت کو ایک بلی کی وجہ سے عذاب ہوا وہ اس کو نہ کھلاتی تھی نہ پلاتی تھی اور نہ چوڑتی تھی کہ زمین کے کیڑے مکوڑے کھائے۔
Book of Maintenance: The master is compelled to sell them. If the master does not provide maintenance for the male and female slaves, and the male and female slaves cannot do any work nor have any earnings, then the master will be forced to sell him so that another master can provide their maintenance and save them from death. In the hadith it is said: When an animal is not fed and dies, there will be punishment. Therefore, if a human is not given maintenance and is not sold either, and dies, the master will be punished. In the hadith: From Abu Huraira that the Messenger of Allah said: 'A woman was punished because of a cat; she did not feed it nor give it drink nor let it eat from the insects of the earth.' (Muslim, Book of Salutations, Chapter on the Prohibition of Killing Cats, p. 236, no. 2243) When an animal is not given maintenance and it dies, punishment is incurred; so if a human is not given maintenance and dies, the master will be punished. Therefore he will be forced to sell it. (2) The above hadith 'he is not burdened except with what he can bear' (Muslim no. 1662) also supports that overburdening male and female slaves beyond their capacity is not permissible. Footnote (a): The Prophet said: A woman was punished because of a cat; she did not feed it nor give it drink nor let it eat the insects of the earth.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Nafaqat (Book of Maintenance) presents a ruling with proof by analogy (qiyas). The ruling: if a master owns a slave who cannot earn (due to disability or other reason) and the master fails to provide maintenance (food, clothing, etc.), the judge will compel the master to sell the slave. This ensures the slave receives maintenance from the new owner and avoids death. The proof is a hadith about a woman punished for neglecting a cat. Since the punishment is severe for neglecting an animal, it is even more so for a human slave. Therefore, the master must either provide maintenance or sell the slave to prevent his death. The passage also cites a supporting hadith: 'He is not burdened except with what he can bear,' indicating that one cannot burden the slave beyond his capacity.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance including food, clothing, and shelter that a master must provide for his slaves.
مولى (mawla): Master who owns a slave; here the one responsible for the slave's maintenance.
يُجْبَر (yujbar): He is forced/compelled; the judge compels the master to sell.
