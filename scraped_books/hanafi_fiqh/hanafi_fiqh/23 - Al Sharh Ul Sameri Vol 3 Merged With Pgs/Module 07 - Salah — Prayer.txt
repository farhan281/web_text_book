Kaffarah for Zihar: Sequence and Timing
Ruling with proof from Quran and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options for kaffarah in zihar in the correct sequence and explain the condition of performing it before intercourse based on the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for zihar must follow a strict sequence and be done before touching your wife.

This lesson covers the obligatory expiation (kaffarah) for zihar, a pre-Islamic divorce-like pronouncement. The Hanafi school derives the ruling directly from the Quranic verse 58:3-4 and the hadith, which prescribe a clear order: freeing a slave, fasting two consecutive months, or feeding sixty poor people. Understanding this sequence is essential for proper application, and a key condition is that the expiation must be performed before the husband resumes sexual relations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is zihar, and the ruling is that it requires expiation (kaffarah). Determine the hukm level: wajib.
Hanafi: wajib because the evidence is zanni (Ahad hadith); Shafi'i: fard.
2
Locate the dalil
The source text cites the Quranic verse (58:3-4) and a hadith (Abu Dawud). Identify the specific proof for the sequence and timing.
The verse uses the imperative 'tahriru raqabah' indicating obligation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative form indicates obligation. The phrase 'min qabli an yatamassa' explicitly conditions the expiation before intercourse.
The word 'yatamassa' (they touch) is interpreted as sexual intercourse.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Although the ruling is muttafaq alayh, there are minor differences: Shafi'i requires a Muslim slave; Hanafi allows any slave.
The text does not elaborate on ikhtilaf, but it exists.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school adheres to the literal sequence: freeing comes first, then fasting, then feeding. The mufta bihi is exactly as stated in the Quran.
No tarjih needed as the ruling is clear and consistent.

The Type of Slave for Zihar Expiation: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Ruling on the permissibility of freeing various types of slaves and the dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the type of slave valid for zihar expiation and explain why the Shafi'i school disagrees.

Core Idea
For zihar expiation, any slave suffices – the Quran says 'raqabah' without restriction.

In this lesson, we examine a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the expiation of zihar. The Hanafi school, based on the absolute wording of the Quran, permits freeing any slave, while the Shafi'i school restricts it to believing slaves through analogy. Understanding this dispute reveals how each school prioritizes different sources of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The expiation (kaffarah) for zihar, specifically the option of freeing a slave. What is the Hanafi ruling? Any slave, regardless of faith, gender, or age, is valid.
2
State the Hanafi position
The text explicitly states that it suffices to free a Muslim or non-Muslim, male or female, young or old slave. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position.
The ruling is unanimous within the Hanafi school.
3
Locate the Hanafi dalil
The evidence is the general command in Quran 58:3: 'tahriru raqabah' (freeing a neck). The word 'raqabah' is absolute and unqualified.
In Hanafi usul, an absolute command (mutlaq) applies to all instances unless restricted by a specific proof.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues by qiyas to the expiation for accidental killing (Quran 4:92), where a believing slave is explicitly required. He extends this restriction to zihar.
The Shafi'i school uses qiyas to qualify the absolute command.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if a person frees a non-Muslim slave, the Hanafi school considers the expiation valid, while the Shafi'i school would require another expiation.
This dispute affects the validity of the expiation in real cases.

The commentary notes that freeing a Muslim slave is better, but a non-Muslim slave is legally sufficient. The Urdu footnote (alif) reiterates the Quranic verse as evidence.

Defects in the Slave for Zihar Kaffarah
Detailed rulings on physical defects in the slave for zihar kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the physical defects that invalidate a slave for zihar expiation and identify which defects are permissible, based on the principle of complete benefit.

Core Idea
Blindness, amputation of both hands/feet, or both thumbs invalidate; deafness or amputation of opposite limbs is valid.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Saghir specifies the criteria for a slave's soundness when used for expiation (kaffarah) of zihar. The Hanafi school requires the slave to be free from severe defects that prevent full benefit. Using qiyas (analogy) to sacrificial animals, the scholars have delineated which defects render the slave invalid and which are considered minor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
The text mentions blindness, amputation of both hands and feet, deafness with amputation of one hand and one foot from opposite sides, and amputation of both thumbs.
Each category has a distinct ruling.
2
Classify as valid or invalid
Blindness, amputation of both hands and feet, and amputation of both thumbs are invalid. Deafness and amputation of one hand and one foot from opposite sides are valid.
The validity of deafness is explicitly stated.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is 'complete benefit' (raqabah tam). A slave must be able to perform full service. Severe defects that prevent work invalidate.
Minor defects like deafness do not prevent basic utility.
4
Apply the analogy (qiyas) to sacrificial animals
The illa (effective cause) is 'severe deficiency in benefit'. This is drawn from the conditions for udhiya animals, where similar defects forbid use.
The qiyas is jali (clear) and accepted by the Hanafi school.
5
Distinguish between major and minor defects
Major defects: blindness, loss of both hands/feet, loss of both thumbs. Minor: deafness, loss of one hand and opposite foot.
The text does not mention other combinations; by inference, single-limb loss may be valid.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed rulings on physical defects in the slave that affect validity in zihar expiation. The reasoning, as explained in the Urdu commentary, is based on the concept of 'complete' serviceability. A blind slave or one with both hands and feet amputated is severely disabled, lacking the ability to work, and thus is not considered a fully functional slave (not 'raqabah tam'). Similarly, the amputation of both thumbs renders the slave incapable of most work, as thumbs are essential for grip. These are analogous to sacrificial animals with severe defects. On the other hand, a deaf slave or one with amputation of one hand and one foot from opposite sides (e.g., right hand and left foot) retains some utility and can still be used; thus such a slave is valid. The ruling draws analogy (qiyas) to the conditions for sacrificial animals (udhiya), where minor defects are tolerated but major ones are not.

Key Terms
العمياء (al-'amya'): Blind female slave; invalid for expiation.
مقطوعة اليدين والرجلين (maqtu'at al-yadayn wa al-rijlayn): A slave with both hands and feet amputated; invalid.
الاصم (al-asamm): Deaf slave; valid for expiation.
مقطوع ابهامي اليدين (maqtu' ibhamay al-yadayn): A slave whose both thumbs are amputated; invalid due to severe loss of manual function.

Feeding Cooked Meals for Dhihar Kaffarah
Ruling that feeding twice daily fulfills kaffarah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Feeding cooked meals morning and evening fulfills kaffarah—the command to 'feed' is general.

When a person must expiate for dhihar, does the method of feeding matter? This lesson clarifies that offering cooked meals twice a day is valid, whether the recipients eat little or much. The Quranic and prophetic commands to 'feed' are general, encompassing any form of nourishing food.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is feeding sixty miskeen (poor persons) as kaffarah for dhihar. The ruling: it is sahih (valid) whether the food is raw or cooked, morning and evening.
The text explicitly states 'jaiz' (permissible) and 'ada ho jayega' (fulfilled).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the Quranic phrase 'it'aam sitteen miskeena' (feeding sixty poor) and a hadith from Abu Dawood: 'falyut'im sitteen miskeena'.
Both proofs are ahad (solitary) but sufficient for fatwa.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'it'aam' (feeding) is general—it includes any act of providing food, raw or cooked. The text argues that cooking is a means of feeding, so it fulfills the obligation.
The generality of the command is key.
4
Apply the ruling to quantity
The ruling does not depend on how much the miskeen eat—even if they eat less or more than half a saa', it is valid. The obligation is to provide food, not to ensure a specific intake.
This differs from some interpretations that require a minimum measure.

Intercourse During Feeding Kaffarah Does Not Require Restart
Ruling on validity of feeding kaffarah after intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that intercourse during feeding kaffarah does not invalidate it, and distinguish the condition for feeding from the conditions for freeing a slave and fasting.

Core Idea
Feeding kaffarah remains valid even if intercourse occurs — the verse lacks the 'before they touch' condition.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in kaffarah al-dhihar. While the first two options (freeing a slave and fasting) require completion before intercourse, the third option (feeding sixty miskin) does not. Why? The Quranic verse for feeding does not include the phrase 'before they touch' (qabl an yatamassa). This difference has practical implications for someone performing kaffarah.

1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is intercourse during the process of feeding sixty miskin as kaffarah for dhihar. The ruling is that the kaffarah remains valid; no restart is needed.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the Quranic verse: 'But whoever cannot afford, then feed sixty miskin' (Surah al-Mujadilah 58:4).
Verse reference: 58:4
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verse lacks the condition 'before they touch' (qabl an yatamassa) which is explicitly present in the earlier verses for freeing a slave and fasting. This absence indicates that feeding is not conditioned on completing it before intercourse.
Compare with verse 58:3-4 for context.
4

There is no significant disagreement within the Hanafi school or across madhahib on this point. The ruling is considered muttafaq alayh.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is clear: the feeding kaffarah is valid despite intercourse. This is based on the literal absence of the condition in the Quranic text.
The position is mufta bihi.

Restrictions on a Divorced Mother's Relocation with Her Child
Ruling on a divorced mother's right to take the child out of the city

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a divorced mother's right to take her child outside the city and identify the exception for her original hometown.

Core Idea
A divorced mother may not relocate the child except to her original hometown where she was married.

This lesson examines a specific custody ruling from the Hanafi school: when a divorced mother wishes to move the child away from the father's city, she is generally prohibited. The only exception is if she returns to her own hometown where the marriage was contracted. This balances the mother's desire to be near her family with the father's right to maintain contact with the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is 'taking the child out of the city' by a divorced mother. The general ruling is that she has no right to do so — it is prohibited (haram) without the father's consent or a valid exception.
2
Recognize the exception
The exception is when she takes the child to 'her city in which the husband married her' — i.e., the city where she originally lived at the time of marriage.
This city is her original hometown, not necessarily where the father currently resides.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The prohibition prevents harm to the father by making visitation difficult. The exception allows the mother to return to her family, as that was her pre-marriage home, and the father accepted that location when he married her.
This is based on the principle of preventing harm (ḍarar) on both sides.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a divorcee who wants to move to another city for work or remarriage. She cannot take the child without the father's permission. But if she wants to return to her parents' city (where she was married), she may do so.
If she moves to a different city, the father can seek a court order to return the child.

Co-owned Slave Emancipation: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on Partner's Options
Intra-Hanafi dispute on co-owner's options after partial manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the options of the co-owner when one partner frees his share, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the ruling to scenarios based on the freeing partner's wealth.

Core Idea
When one partner frees his share, the co-owner's options depend on the freeing partner's wealth and on whether emancipation is divisible.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute about partial manumission of a co-owned slave. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that emancipation is divisible, granting the co-owner three options when the freeing partner is wealthy and two when poor. His disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), disagree, arguing that freedom is indivisible, limiting the co-owner's options. We will explore the rulings, the hadith proofs, and the underlying usul principles that generate this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two partners co-own a slave (e.g., half each). One partner frees his share. The freeing partner may be wealthy (maysir) or poor (mu'sir).
Wealthy means having enough money to pay the value of the other partner's share.
2
List Abu Hanifa's options for the co-owner
If freeing partner is wealthy: (a) free his own share, (b) claim compensation (dhaman) from the freeing partner, or (c) make the slave work to earn the value (su'ayah). If poor: only (a) and (c) – no compensation.
Abu Hanifa's principle: freedom is divisible – only the freed portion becomes free.
3
List Sahibayn's options for the co-owner
If freeing partner is wealthy: only compensation (dhaman). If poor: only su'ayah. No option to free his own share in either case.
Sahibayn's principle: freedom is indivisible – the entire slave becomes free immediately.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
Both sides use the same hadith from Ibn Umar: 'Whoever frees a share in a slave… if he has wealth, he must free him completely; if not, a just value is assessed and he frees only what he freed.' The difference lies in interpreting whether 'free him completely' (عليه عتقه كله) is a command to the freeing partner or a description of the outcome.
Abu Hanifa reads the first part as conditional on wealth, the second as confirming partial freedom. Sahibayn read the first part as an absolute obligation to free the whole slave if wealthy.
5
Analyze the usul difference
The dispute hinges on whether ownership in a slave can be partially extinguished. Abu Hanifa allows tajzi'ah (divisibility) of freedom; Sahibayn reject it because freedom is a single inseparable quality.
Practical consequence (thamarah): under Abu Hanifa, the co-owner can choose su'ayah even if the freeing partner is wealthy; under Sahibayn, he cannot – he must take compensation.

Commentary
This passage deals with the emancipation of a slave co-owned by two partners, specifically the scenario where one partner frees his share. The text first presents the ruling in Arabic, then provides an Urdu commentary with reasoning and hadith proofs. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if the freeing partner is wealthy (maysir), the other partner has three options: (1) free his own share, (2) claim the value of his share from the freeing partner as compensation (dhaman), or (3) make the slave work to earn the value (su'ayah). If the freeing partner is poor (mu'sir), the second option of compensation is unavailable, leaving only two options: free his share or make the slave work. The basis for the three options is Abu Hanifa's principle that emancipation is divisible: only the freed share becomes free, the rest remains slave. However, since the freeing partner introduced an element of freedom (a defect) and is wealthy, the partner can claim compensation. The hadith of Ibn Umar is cited to support compensation from the wealthy freeing partner. The passage then presents the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn) hold that when the freeing partner is wealthy, the partner has only one option—to claim compensation. They argue that emancipation is indivisible; the moment one partner frees his share, the entire slave becomes free, and the freeing partner must pay the value of the other's share. Thus, su'ayah is only allowed when the freeing partner is poor. They cite the same hadith from Ibn Umar and also a hadith from Abu Hurairah about su'ayah. The commentary explains the difference in usul (principles): for Sahibayn, freedom does not admit part-ownership (no tajzi'ah), so the entire slave is freed immediately. Key Terms
العتاق (al-'itaq): Emancipation; freeing a slave from bondage.
شريكين (sharikayn): Two partners who co-own a slave, each having a share (usually half).
موسر (maysir): Wealthy; one who has sufficient funds to pay the value of the other partner's share.
معسر (mu'sir): Poor; one who does not have funds to pay compensation.
ضمان (dhaman): Liability; making the freeing partner liable to pay the value of the other partner's share.
سعاية (su'ayah): Working to earn money; the slave works for wages to pay the value to the other partner.

Key Takeaway
In a co-owned slave, when one partner frees his share, the options of the other partner depend on the freeing partner's wealth and on whether emancipation is considered divisible (Abu Hanifa) or indivisible (Sahibayn).
