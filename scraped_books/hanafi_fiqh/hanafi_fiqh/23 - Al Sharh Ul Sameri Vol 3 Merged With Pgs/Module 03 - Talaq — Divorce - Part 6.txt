Lineage of Child Born Two Years After Revocable Divorce
Ruling on lineage after revocable divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for establishing a child's lineage after a revocable divorce and apply the hadith 'al-walad li al-firash' to this case.

Core Idea
A child born to a revocably divorced woman within two years is legally linked to the husband unless she admits her iddah ended.

This lesson examines a specific ruling on lineage attribution when a child is born to a woman who has been given a revocable divorce. The Hanafi school uses the maximum pregnancy period of two years and the principle of firash to determine paternity. We'll explore the textual proof from hadith and the rationale behind this presumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the birth of a child to a revocably divorced woman. The ruling is that the child's lineage is established to the husband, provided the woman does not admit that her iddah has ended.
Note the key condition: lack of admission of iddah completion.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith 'al-walad li al-firash' (the child belongs to the bed). This is a sahih hadith narrated by Aisha (RA).
The hadith is a primary source for lineage attribution in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'al-walad li al-firash' directly indicates that the child is attributed to the owner of the marital bed (the husband). The wife in iddah is considered part of the husband's firash, so the child belongs to him.
The dalala (indication) is qati (definitive) in meaning.
4
Note the condition and rationale
The condition is that the woman does not admit that her iddah has ended. If she admits it, she is no longer in the husband's firash. The rationale is to protect the child from being considered illegitimate.
The two-year maximum pregnancy period is based on athar from Aisha (RA).

Mahr and Iddah for a Bain Divorcee Remarried in Iddah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mahr and iddah for remarriage during iddah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions regarding mahr and iddah when a bain divorcee is remarried in her iddah and divorced before consummation, identify the relied-upon position, and explain the legal reasoning behind each view.

Core Idea
Half mahr and completion of the first iddah is the relied-upon Hanafi view for a bain divorcee remarried and divorced before consummation.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a man divorces his wife irrevocably (talaq bain), then remarries her during her iddah, but then divorces her again before consummating the second marriage. The ruling on mahr and iddah is disputed within the Hanafi school, with two distinct positions attributed to the early scholars. Understanding this dispute illuminates the school's methodology in handling overlapping marital ties and the role of consummation in determining mahr.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the Hanafi position
Describe the factual scenario: bain divorce, remarriage in iddah, second divorce before consummation. State the relied-upon Hanafi position (Imam Muhammad): half mahr and completion of the first iddah.
This is the mufta bihi view.
2
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi view
Identify the alternative view attributed to Ibrahim al-Nakha'i (also associated with Abu Hanifa): full mahr and a new iddah.
This view is considered a weaker narration.
3
Follow the proof of each side
Examine the reasoning behind each view. The opposing view treats the second marriage as a new marital bond. Imam Muhammad's view relies on qiyas (analogy) to a standard divorce before consummation, where half mahr applies and no new iddah starts.
The key factor is whether consummation occurred.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Imam Muhammad responds that the second marriage was contracted during an invalid time, but it is still valid in Hanafi fiqh. However, since there was no consummation, the mahr is halved, and the first iddah continues.
This resolves the overlap of iddah periods.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: the amount of mahr (full vs half) and whether she must start a new iddah or complete the old one.
This affects the woman's waiting period and financial entitlement.

Commentary
This passage discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a specific scenario: a man gives his wife an irrevocable divorce (bain), then marries her again while she is still in her 'iddah from the first divorce, and then divorces her a second time before consummating the new marriage. The passage reports two positions. The text also cites narrations from Hasan al-Basri who gives a similar ruling of half mahr and no new iddah, with a variation that she completes the first 'iddah. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Imam Muhammad: half mahr and completion of the first 'iddah.

Lineage and Separation: Child Born Within Two Years of Revocable Divorce
Ruling on child born within two years: lineage and irretrievable separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the lineage of a child born within two years of a revocable divorce and explain the proof from the athar of Aisha RA.

Core Idea
A child born within two years of divorce is legitimate; the mother becomes bain automatically.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when a revocably divorced woman gives birth within two years, the child's lineage is established with the ex-husband and the woman becomes irrevocably separated. The key is the maximum pregnancy period of two years, derived from an athar of Aisha RA. We will analyse the ruling, its proof, and its practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states two consequences if a revocably divorced woman gives birth within two years: (1) the child's lineage (nasab) is established to the husband, and (2) the woman becomes bain (irrevocably separated).
Bain means she cannot remarry him without a new marriage contract.
2
Understand the legal reasoning
The ruling is based on the principle that the maximum pregnancy period is two years. Therefore, a birth within two years indicates that the woman was pregnant at the time of divorce. Delivery completes her iddah, ending the marriage irrevocably.
Iddah for a pregnant woman ends with delivery (Quran 65:4).
3
Examine the proof
The proof is an athar from Aisha RA: 'A woman's pregnancy does not exceed two years, nor the amount that the shadow of a spindle shifts.' This establishes the two-year maximum as a legal criterion.
The athar is narrated in Sunan al-Bayhaqi. It is a qawl sahābi, considered a valid proof in Hanafi usul.
4
Note the impact on iddah
The birth itself completes the iddah, making the woman bain. No further waiting period is required.
If birth occurs exactly at two years, it is still within the limit.
5
Compare briefly with other schools
Other madhhabs may have different maximum pregnancy periods (e.g., four years in some views). The Hanafi ruling is based on this specific athar.
Ikhtilaf is legitimate; the practical difference appears when birth occurs beyond two years.

Lineage and Revocable Return After Two Years
Ruling on child born after two years: lineage and revocable return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a child born after more than two years from a revocable divorce and explain the reasoning that infers intercourse during iddah.

Core Idea
Birth after two years implies intercourse, which automatically revokes the divorce and establishes lineage.

In the Hanafi school, the maximum duration of pregnancy is two years. If a revocably divorced woman gives birth after that period, the birth cannot be attributed to a pre-divorce pregnancy. This lesson examines the legal conclusion that such a birth proves the husband had intercourse during the iddah, which constitutes a revocable return (raj'ah), thereby making the child legitimate.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the two legal consequences: the child's lineage is established with the husband, and the birth itself is considered a revocable return (raj'ah).
The ruling is unconditional: it applies automatically upon birth after two years.
2
Understand the premise: two-year maximum pregnancy
Hanafi law holds that the maximum period of pregnancy is two years. Therefore, a child born after two years from the divorce cannot be from a pregnancy that existed at the time of divorce.
This premise is essential to the reasoning.
3
Infer the necessary intercourse
Since the child cannot be from a pre-divorce pregnancy, it must be from an intercourse that occurred after the divorce, during the iddah period.
The inference is based on necessity: the child must have a father.
4
Apply the rule that intercourse during iddah effects return
In Hanafi law, any intercourse with a revocably divorced woman during her iddah automatically constitutes a revocable return (raj'ah), without need for explicit words.
This rule is well-established; the husband cannot deny the return.
5
Conclude lineage
Since the child is from the husband's intercourse, the child is legitimate and his lineage is attributed to the husband.
The father must acknowledge the child; the child inherits from him.

This passage from Hanafi fiqh addresses a specific family law issue: how to prove the paternity of a child born to a woman in her waiting period ('iddah). Guided Path

Ruling on Saying 'O My Son' or 'O My Brother'
Statement of ruling and its legal reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a master says 'O my son' or 'O my brother' to his slave and explain why it does not effect emancipation according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Saying 'O my son' or 'O my brother' to a slave does not free him because such words are used as endearment, not for emancipation.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the chapter on emancipation: when a master addresses his slave as 'O my son' or 'O my brother', the slave is not emancipated. The Hanafi school distinguishes between explicit words of emancipation and expressions of endearment or respect. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling and understand why intention alone is insufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its classification
The act is a master saying 'O my son' or 'O my brother' to his male slave. This is an expression that could potentially be interpreted as a declaration of emancipation, but the ruling states it does not result in emancipation.
2
State the hukm (ruling)
The ruling is that the slave is not freed (lam yut'iq). The action is legally ineffective for emancipation.
3
Understand the rationale
These words are commonly used as terms of endearment or respect, not as technical terms for emancipation. Therefore, they do not clearly indicate the intent to free the slave.
The Hanafi school requires explicit words (sarih) or clear indication (kinayah) with intent for emancipation.
4
Compare with the ruling for 'mawla'
When the master says 'my mawla' or 'O my mawla', the slave is freed because 'mawla' has a specific meaning of 'freed slave' that can apply. In contrast, 'son' and 'brother' do not have such a technical meaning in emancipation.
5
Apply the principle
Other similar expressions of endearment or respect would also not effect emancipation unless they are from the established words of emancipation.

Using Divorce Words for Emancipation: A Hanafi–Shafi‘i Dispute
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute on using divorce words for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on using divorce terms for emancipation and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A weaker term (divorce) cannot imply a stronger meaning (emancipation) without customary usage.

How far can we stretch words? If a master says 'You are divorced' to his female slave, but intends to free her, does she become free? The Hanafi school says no, because divorce only severs partial ownership (of the vulva) while emancipation severs full ownership of the body. The Shafi'i school disagrees, arguing that both remove restrictions of ownership. Let's examine the reasoning and practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the legal act in question? A master says 'You are divorced' (anti tāliq) to his female slave, intending emancipation. Determine whether she becomes free.
This is not a divorce of a wife; the slave is owned, not married.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi ruling: she is not freed. The proof: divorce removes only partial ownership (milk al-bud‘ah), while emancipation removes total ownership. Using a weaker word for a stronger meaning is not a customary metaphor (majāz muta‘ārif).
Key principle: a weaker expression cannot carry a stronger meaning in ordinary usage.
3
State the opposing Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that she is freed. His argument: both divorce and emancipation remove the restriction of ownership, so the words can be interchanged. The difference in scope is irrelevant.
Shafi'i focuses on the common element: removal of ownership restriction.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the difference between partial and total ownership is legally significant. The custom of Arabic speakers does not allow using 'divorce' (a partial removal) to imply 'emancipation' (a total removal). Thus, such a usage is invalid.
The principle is based on linguistic custom (urfi 'āmm).
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical difference: according to the Hanafi school, the slave remains a slave; according to the Shafi'i school, she becomes free. This affects her status and rights.
This difference can have significant implications in inheritance, sale, etc.
