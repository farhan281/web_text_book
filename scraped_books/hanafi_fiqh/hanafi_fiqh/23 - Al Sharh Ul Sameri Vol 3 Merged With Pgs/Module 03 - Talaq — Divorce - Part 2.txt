Divorce of a Specific Limb Like Hand or Foot
Ruling that saying 'your hand' or 'your foot is divorced' does not effect divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband divorces a specific limb and explain the reason based on the distinction between explicit/kinayah and non-kinayah expressions.

Core Idea
Divorcing a limb is not talaq — only words referring to the whole or common kinayah effect divorce.

This lesson addresses a specific type of talaq (divorce) that often causes confusion. While some body parts like the neck can metaphorically refer to the entire person, others like the hand or foot cannot. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly identifying when a divorce has occurred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression
Determine exactly what words the husband used. Is it a specific limb (e.g., hand, foot) or a part that can conventionally refer to the whole (e.g., neck, head)?
In Hanafi fiqh, words are classified as sarih (explicit talaq words) or kinayah (allusive, requiring intention).
2
Check if it is a recognized kinayah
Consult the Hanafi list of kinayah expressions. Limbs like hand and foot are not considered kinayah for the whole wife, unlike neck (عنى) or face (وجه) which can be used metaphorically.
The criterion is customary usage (urf) in Arabic. If the word is not commonly used to mean the entire person, it does not effect talaq.
3
Apply the ruling
If the word is not a sarih talaq word and not a recognized kinayah, then no divorce occurs regardless of the husband's intention.
This is a definitive rule in the Hanafi school; there is no room for interpretation.
4
Contrast with kinayah cases
If the husband had said 'your neck is divorced,' talaq would occur only if he intended it. This highlights the importance of the word's category.
The intention is irrelevant for non-kinayah expressions because they cannot even allude to talaq.

This ruling is based on the principle that talaq is an indivisible legal act, and the husband's words are interpreted as a full pronouncement. The source text cites the practice of early authorities, confirming that talaq cannot be partially applied.

Coerced Divorce in Hanafi Fiqh
Inter-madhhab dispute on coerced divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced divorce, identify the primary Hanafi proof, and explain the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
Coerced divorce is valid in Hanafi law because the husband, facing a threat, chooses divorce over greater harm — he acts with intention.

This lesson examines the classical dispute over whether a divorce pronounced under coercion (ikrah) takes effect. The Hanafi school holds that it does, while Imam al-Shafi'i argues it does not. Understanding this ikhtilaf illuminates how each school weighs competing proofs: the Hanafis prioritize the coerced person's residual choice, whereas the Shafi'is emphasize that coercion negates true volition. We will walk through the evidence for both sides and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify that the Hanafi school rules coerced divorce as valid (sahih) and effective (waqi'). The husband's pronouncing talaq under duress dissolves the marriage.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Examine the athar of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: 'Divorce of the coerced is valid; it is something by which he ransoms himself.' Also note the rational argument: the husband chooses the lesser harm (divorce) over death, thus exercises his will.
3
Identify the opposing proof
Imam al-Shafi'i uses Qur'an 16:106 (coerced kufr not counted) by analogy, the hadith of pardoning coercion, the specific hadith 'no divorce in compulsion', and the athar of Ibn Abbas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi ruling, the marriage ends immediately; under Shafi'i, the marriage continues. This affects remarriage rights and issues of zina if one party remarries based on an invalid divorce.

Conditional Divorce: Ruling and Proof in the Hanafi School
Ruling and evidence for conditional divorce attached to a specific condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional divorce and identify the key evidence (dalil) that supports it.

Core Idea
A divorce suspended on a future condition takes effect when the condition is fulfilled, as long as the wife is owned at the time of suspension.

This lesson examines the ruling of conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq) in the Hanafi school. The passage clarifies that when a husband attaches divorce to a future event, the divorce occurs immediately upon fulfillment of that event, provided the wife is legally his at the time of susupension. The text supports this with prophetic hadith and an athar from Hasan al-Basri, establishing both the validity and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its condition
Read the first part of the passage: 'And if he adds it to a condition, it occurs after the condition.' Note that the divorce is suspended, not immediate. The condition must be a future possibility, and the wife must be in the husband's ownership (i.e., marriage valid) at the moment of suspension.
Ownership here means the marital bond, not literal property.
2
Identify the key evidence: hadith
The text cites the hadith: 'لا وفاء نذر إلا فيما تملك' (No fulfillment of a vow except in what you own). This is used analogically: just as a vow is only binding on what one owns, a conditional divorce is only valid if the husband owns the wife (i.e., is married) at the time of suspension.
The hadith is from Abu Dawood; it is ahad and has zanni thubut.
3
Apply the athar from Hasan al-Basri
The athar states that if the condition is uncertain (e.g., 'if such and such happens'), the divorce does not occur until it happens. In the interim, marital relations are permitted and inheritance rights between spouses remain intact. This shows that the marriage continues until the condition is fulfilled.
This athar is recorded in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah of the hadith
The hadith's operative phrase: 'فيما تملك' (in what you own). The Hanafi jurists derive that a condition (or vow) can only be effective if it pertains to something within one's ownership. Since a wife is owned by marriage, a conditional divorce is valid. The suspension itself is an act of ownership.

Divorce Conditional on a Complete Menstruation: Timing of Purification
Ruling that divorce occurs after purification from one full menstruation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two phrasings of conditional divorce involving menstruation and apply the Hanafi ruling on the timing of divorce based on the word 'haydah'.

Core Idea
A divorce conditioned on 'a menstruation' (haydah) occurs only after purification, not at the onset of blood.

In Hanafi fiqh, precise wording in conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq) is crucial. This lesson examines a specific case: if a husband says 'If you experience a menstruation (haydah), you are divorced.' Does the divorce occur at the beginning of bleeding or after purification? The answer depends on the linguistic and fiqh analysis of the term 'haydah'. We will explore the ruling and its proof from the statement of Sufyan al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional phrase
Look at the exact wording of the husband's statement. The key is whether he says 'idha hadat haydah' (if you have a menstruation) or 'mata hadat' (whenever you menstruate).
The word 'haydah' is a noun of instance meaning one complete cycle.
2
Understand the term 'haydah'
'Haydah' linguistically means a single occurrence of menstruation, which includes the entire period of blood flow and ends with purification (ghusl).
Contrast with 'hayd' which can mean menstruation in general.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
The condition is attached to a complete cycle, so it is fulfilled only at the end of that cycle, i.e., after the woman performs ghusl.
This is because the husband cannot take back his wife during menstruation, so the condition cannot be considered fulfilled until the end.
4
Note the thamarah (practical consequence)
If the husband says 'haydah', the divorce is postponed until after purification. If he uses 'mata hadat' (whenever you menstruate), divorce occurs at the start of blood.
The thamarah is the actual timing of divorce, affecting iddah and remarriage.
5
Verify with the athar
The passage cites Sufyan al-Thawri's statement, which explicitly distinguishes between the two phrasings and confirms the Hanafi position.
Al-Thawri's view is recorded in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
