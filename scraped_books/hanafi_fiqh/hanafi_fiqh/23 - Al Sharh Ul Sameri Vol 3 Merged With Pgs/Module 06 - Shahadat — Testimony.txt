Evidentiary Requirements for Proving Fosterage
States that fosterage requires two male witnesses or one male and two females.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi evidentiary requirement for proving fosterage and explain the legal basis from Quran 2:282.

Core Idea
Fosterage, being a right of people, requires two male witnesses or one male and two females — women alone cannot prove it.

In this passage, the author addresses a specific evidentiary rule: how to legally prove that fosterage (rada'ah) occurred. This is critical because establishing fosterage creates permanent marriage prohibitions. The Hanafi school classifies fosterage under the rights of people (huquq al-ibad), which demand stronger testimony than matters of worship. The Quranic verse on debts provides the standard.

Testimony for Fosterage: Hanafi vs Maliki
Ruling on evidence requirements for proving fosterage with inter-school dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on testimony required to prove fosterage and evaluate the Maliki counter-argument using the hadith of the black woman.

Core Idea
Fosterage testimony requires two men or one man and two women; women alone cannot prove it in Hanafi law.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Maliki schools over the minimum number and gender of witnesses needed to prove fosterage (rada). The Hanafi school, relying on the general principle of Quran 2:282 and the practice of Umar, requires at least one male witness. Imam Malik, citing the hadith of the black woman, accepts a single upright woman's testimony. Understanding the Hanafi response to this hadith is crucial for evaluating the strength of each position.

State that Imam Malik accepts a single just woman's testimony, citing the hadith of the black woman as evidence.
The hadith involves the Prophet ordering a man to separate from his wife based on a single woman's claim of fosterage.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response to the Maliki proof
Explain how Hanafi scholars reinterpret the hadith, arguing it was a specific case and does not establish a general rule overriding the Quranic principle.
The Hanafi response emphasizes that the husband had no counter-evidence, and the Quranic requirement remains authoritative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions under which the lineage of a child born to a woman in iddah is established according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
A child born during iddah is not automatically linked to the former husband — specific evidence is required.

A woman in iddah is no longer a full marital bed (firash), so the general presumption of lineage (al-walad li'l-firash) does not fully apply. Imam Abu Hanifa requires clear evidence to establish paternity. This lesson examines the required testimony and alternatives.

Identify the act and ruling
The act is giving birth during iddah. The default ruling according to Abu Hanifa is that lineage is not established without additional proof.
The woman in iddah is not a full firash.
2
List the three exceptions
There are three ways to establish lineage: (1) testimony of two men or one man and two women; (2) the pregnancy was visibly apparent before birth; (3) the husband acknowledges the child.
The testimony requirement mirrors financial transactions.
3
Understand the rationale
Because the woman is not a full firash, the child's lineage is not presumed. The requirement for testimony or visible evidence safeguards against false claims.
The hadith of 'Ali and Quran 2:282 are used as evidence.

The author cites the hadith: 'The testimony of women alone is not permissible even for a dirham unless a man is with them' and Quran 2:282 requiring two men or one man and two women for witnessing.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Witnesses for Lineage
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony of women for lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding testimony of women for lineage, identify the evidence used by each side, and apply the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
One woman's testimony suffices in hidden female matters, overriding general witness requirements.

This passage presents an internal Hanafi disagreement on the quantum of proof needed to establish lineage (nasab). While Imam Abu Hanifa adhered to the general rule of two men or one man and two women, his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argued for a special exception in matters that men cannot easily observe. Their view, supported by prophetic practice and precedents from the Companions, has become the relied-upon position in the school for cases like childbirth and breastfeeding. Understanding this dispute helps you see how the Hanafi school balances textual evidence with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default Hanafi rule
Recall the general principle for testimony in Hanafi fiqh: normally, two men or one man and two women are required. This is based on Quran 2:282 for financial transactions and extended by analogy to other claims.
This is Abu Hanifa's position for lineage when the pregnancy is not apparent and the husband does not confess.
2
State the Sahibayn's exception
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that in all hidden matters of women (like childbirth, lactation, and physical defects), one woman's testimony is enough. They argue because men cannot observe these events, requiring two witnesses would make proof impossible.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's evidence
They cite three proofs: (1) Hadith of Hudhayfa that the Prophet accepted the testimony of a midwife (al-qabila). (2) Athar from al-Sha'bi and al-Hasan that one woman's testimony is acceptable in matters men do not witness. (3) Hadith of 'Uqba ibn al-Harith where the Prophet advised separation based on one woman's testimony about breastfeeding.
These are dalil naqli (textual proofs) specific to hidden female matters.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is that in courts today, a single midwife's testimony is enough to establish childbirth and thus lineage. The general rule requiring two witnesses is overridden by istihsan (juristic preference) based on necessity.
This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.
5
Note the tarjih and madhhab status
Despite being a minority view against the Imam, the Sahibayn's position is preferred because it is easier and supported by specific evidence. Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and later scholars adopt this view. The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih, but within the school it is the relied-upon opinion.
Shafi'is have a similar exception but differ in the ratio of women replacing a man.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the testimony required to establish lineage (nasab). The author first states the ruling: if the pregnancy is apparent or the husband acknowledges paternity, lineage is established without testimony. Then the matn records the position of Imam Abu Hanifa (implicitly) that for establishing lineage in other cases, testimony of two men or one man and two women is required. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the testimony of one woman suffices in all cases concerning hidden matters of women, such as childbirth. The commentator explains that the Sahibayn base their view on the difficulty for men to witness such matters. They support it with a hadith from Hudhayfa that the Prophet ﷺ accepted the testimony of a midwife, and an athar from al-Sha'bi and al-Hasan allowing one woman's testimony in matters unseen by men. Additionally, they cite the hadith of 'Uqba ibn al-Harith where the Prophet ﷺ advised separation based on one woman's testimony about breastfeeding. Thus, the Sahibayn prioritize ease and necessity over the general rule of requiring two witnesses. The Hanafi position here is the mufta bihi view of the Sahibayn in these specific matters.

نسب (nasab): Lineage or paternal descent, legally established through valid marriage or acknowledgment.

Testimony of a Converted Non-Muslim
Ruling on acceptance of testimony after conversion to Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of a convert from kufr and identify the condition that removes disqualification.

Core Idea
Conversion to Islam removes all prior testimonial disqualification.

This lesson addresses a specific yet important question: can a person who was a non-Muslim, and possibly even a convicted slanderer (qadhif), ever be accepted as a witness in an Islamic court? The Ḥanafī school, citing an early effect (athar) from the Muṣannaf of ‘Abd al-Razzāq, holds that conversion alone clears the slate. Understanding this ruling clarifies the school’s emphasis on faith as the foundational requirement for witness credibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
What is being ruled upon? The acceptance of testimony (shahādah) from a person who was originally a non-Muslim and then converted to Islam.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that his testimony is accepted. Conversion removes the previous disqualification (whether due to kufr or a hadd punishment for qadhf).
3
Locate the evidence
The evidence is an athar (narrative) from Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, a major early ḥadīth collection. It is not a Quranic verse or prophetic sunnah but a companion/predecessor report, indicating that the ruling was established in early practice.
The authority weight is from a musannaf work, below the level of mutawātir or ḥadīth, but accepted in the Ḥanafī school.
4
Understand the condition
The key condition is the act of conversion (Islam). Without it, the non-Muslim's testimony remains unacceptable. Once converted, the testimony becomes legally valid.
5
Apply the principle
If you encounter a similar case in practice, state that a convert's testimony is accepted without further waiting period or additional conditions. The conversion is sufficient.
