Ownership of a Mahram Relative: Automatic Emancipation
Ruling with dalil from hadith about owning mahram relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a mahram relative is automatically emancipated upon ownership and explain the hadith basis for this ruling.

Core Idea
Owning a mahram relative triggers automatic emancipation – not master's choice.

This lesson covers a special ruling in Islamic law that prevents the degradation of family bonds: if a person comes to own a close blood relative (mahram), that relative is freed immediately. The ruling is based on a clear hadith of the Prophet ﷺ. You will learn the rule, its proof, and why it is automatically effective.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is 'owning a mahram relative'. The ruling is that the owned relative becomes free (emancipated) automatically.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith: 'Whoever owns a mahram relative, he is free.' It is narrated by Samurah ibn Jundub and recorded in Sunan Abi Dawood and Sunan al-Tirmidhi.
The hadith is a hadith ahad, but its ruling is accepted by consensus.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'فهو حر' (he is free) is a declarative statement indicating that emancipation is a direct legal consequence of ownership. No additional action by the master is required.
The meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalalah), even though the transmission is zanni.
4
Determine the scope and effect
The ruling applies to any mahram relative (e.g., mother, father, sibling, child, uncle, aunt). It is not limited to specific relatives. The effect is immediate freedom.
If the relative is not a mahram (e.g., a distant cousin permissible to marry), the ruling does not apply.

Status of Children: Slave Mothers and Free Mothers
Three rulings on child status from Kitab al-'Itaq, with companion athar as evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the status of children born from slave and free women, and identify the evidence (athar) used to support them.

Core Idea
A child’s legal status in Hanafi fiqh follows the mother, except when the father is the mother’s master.

This short passage from al-Quduri's matn presents three concise rulings that form the core of the Hanafi position on how a child's status is determined based on the mother's condition and the father's identity. Understanding these rulings reveals a fundamental principle: maternal status is determinative, except when the father is the mother's owner, in which case paternity elevates the child to freedom. The proofs from Companions Ibn Umar and Ali reinforce this principle through analogy.

Guided Path
1
List the three scenarios
Identify each of the three distinct cases described: (1) child of a slave woman from her master; (2) child of a slave woman from her husband (not the master); (3) child of a free woman from a slave husband.
Each case has a different combination of mother and father status.
2
Extract the ruling for each
For each scenario, state the child's legal status as given in the text: free, slave, or free.
Note that in scenario 2 the child is a slave of the mother's master.
3
Identify the general principle
From these rulings, derive the principle: the child's status follows the mother's status, except when the father is the mother's master (then the child is free).
This is the core legal maxim: al-walad yatba' ummahu fi al-riqq wa al-hurriyyah.
4
Analyze the proofs
Read the athar of Ibn Umar (on mudabbirah) and Ali (on mukatabah). Understand that they establish the general rule that the child shares the mother's status. Then see how the Hanafi school extends this by qiyas to all slave and free women.
The athar are used as evidence from the Companions, though they are not Quran or Sunnah directly.
5
Confirm consensus
Note that these rulings are accepted without intra-Hanafi dispute and are generally agreed upon across schools, though details differ in other madhahib.
This strengthens the reliability of the position.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-'Itaq presents three concise rulings on the status of children based on the mother's condition and the father's identity. First, if a slave woman (owned by a man) bears a child from her master, the child is free because the child is the master's son, and the mother becomes umm walad (a slave who becomes free upon her master's death). Second, if the same slave woman bears a child from her husband (not the master), the child is a slave belonging to the master, as the child follows the mother's legal status (al-walad li al-firash wa li al-malik? Actually the child is enslaved because the mother is a slave). Third, if a free woman bears a child from a slave father, the child is free, because the child follows the mother's free status. The proofs: Ibn Umar's statement that the child of a mudabbirah shares her status (free when she is freed, enslaved when she is enslaved), and Ali's statement that the child of a mukatabah follows her status. These analogically extend to all slave women and free women.

Key Terms
امه (amah): A female slave owned by a master. In these rulings, the child's status depends on whether the father is the master or another man.
مدبرة (mudabbirah): A slave woman whom her master has promised to free upon his death (tadbir). Her child follows her status.
مكاتبة (mukatabah): A slave woman who has made a contract (kitabah) with her master to purchase her freedom by installments. Her child's status follows hers until she is freed.

Lineage of Umm Walad's Children: First vs. Subsequent
Ruling on lineage of umm walad's children with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for establishing lineage of an umm walad's first child and explain the distinction for subsequent children using the provided proofs.

Core Idea
First child requires acknowledgment; later children are automatically linked.

This passage explains the Hanafi position on the lineage (nasab) of children born to an umm walad. It distinguishes between the first child, who requires the master's explicit acknowledgment, and subsequent children, who are automatically attributed. The ruling is supported by hadith and athar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and locate the two distinct rulings: one for the first child and one for children born after acknowledgment.
Key phrases: 'ولا يثبت نسب ولدها الا ان يعترف به المولى' (first child) and 'فان جائت بولد بعد ذلک ثبت نسبه منه بغیر اقرار' (subsequent children).
2
Understand the condition for the first child
The first child's lineage is conditional upon the master's explicit acknowledgment (i'tiraf). Without it, lineage is not established.
Rationale: the master-slave bond is weaker than marriage, and procreation may not be intended.
3
Grasp the automaticity for subsequent children
Once acknowledgment occurs for the first child, subsequent children are automatically linked. However, the master can still disown a child by denial.
Automaticity is based on the now-firm bond; denial (intafa) negates lineage.
4
Examine the proofs
The passage cites a hadith from Ibn Umar (status of umm walad), an athar from Ibn Abbas (denial), and a Bukhari hadith (acknowledgment establishes lineage). Evaluate how each supports the ruling.
These are dalil naqli (textual evidences) with zanni thubut and dalala.
5
Consider the practical implication
The ruling ensures that lineage aligns with the master's intention. Without acknowledgment, a child may remain unclaimed; with it, the child gains full nasab.
Contrast with the freedom of the umm walad upon master's death.

Status of a Child Born to an Umm Walad from Another Husband
Ruling on child born to umm walad from another husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the status of a child born to an umm walad from another husband and identify the proof from the athar of Ibn Umar.

Core Idea
A child of an umm walad follows the mother's status: slave until the master dies, then free.

This lesson clarifies the legal status of a child born to an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne her master's child) after she is married to another man. The key principle is that the child's status mirrors the mother's, which has practical implications for inheritance and ownership. We will examine the ruling and its proof from the Companion Abdullah ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act: an umm walad is married off by her master, and she gives birth from that husband. The ruling: the child is in the status of its mother (fi hukm ummihi). Determine the exact ruling: the child is a slave during the master's life and freed when the mother is freed upon the master's death.
Note that the child's status is directly tied to the mother's, not the father's.
2
Locate the Dalil
The author cites an athar from Abdullah ibn Umar. Find the text of the athar in the source: 'I heard Abdullah ibn Umar say: When a slave woman gives birth from her master, then marries after that and gives birth to children, her children are like her, slaves as long as her master lives; when he dies, they become free.'
3
Analyze the Wajh al-Dalalah
How does this athar prove the ruling? Ibn Umar explicitly states that the children are 'like her' (bimanzilatiha), meaning they share her legal status. The words 'slaves as long as her master lives' and 'when he dies, they become free' directly support the Hanafi position.
The dalil is a qawl sahabi, which is considered zanni in thubut and dalala, but it is relied upon in the absence of contrary evidence.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
The Hanafi position is that the child remains a slave. Some other schools may hold that the child is free immediately because the mother is an umm walad (and thus the child of the master is free, but here the child is from another father). The text does not elaborate on the opposing view, but the Hanafi position is based on the mother's slave status.
The practical difference: under the Hanafi view, the child is owned by the master and cannot inherit from the mother during the master's life.
5
Confirm the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is as stated: the child is a slave during the master's life and freed upon his death. This is the authoritative ruling for fatwa.
This ruling is part of the zahir al-riwayah.

Core Idea
A father's claim to his son's property makes him owner of the slave-girl ab initio, so the child is free and she becomes umm walad.

This passage addresses a nuanced ruling: a father has intercourse with his son's slave-girl, she bears a child, and he claims it. One Partner Claims Child from Shared Slave-Girl
Ruling when one of two partners claims a child from their jointly owned slave-girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one partner claims a child from a jointly owned slave-girl, and explain the compensation required.

Core Idea
Claiming a child from a shared slave-girl requires half her value and half her dower, but not the child's value.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the fiqh of slavery and lineage. When two partners co-own a slave-girl and she bears a child, only one partner may claim paternity. The Hanafi school has detailed rules to balance the rights of the co-owner, the recognition of lineage, and the status of the mother as Umm al-Walad. We will break down the ruling and its underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claim
The slave-girl is jointly owned by two partners. One partner claims the child is his. Note that only one claimant is considered here.
2
State the immediate consequences
The child's lineage is established to the claimant, and the slave-girl becomes his Umm al-Walad (mother of his child, with specific legal status).
3
Determine the compensation
The claimant must pay half the slave-girl's value and half her 'uqr (dower) to the other partner.
Half value compensates for buying out the partner's share; half dower compensates for intercourse that occurred while she was partly owned by the other.
4
Explain why no payment for the child
After paying the value, the claimant owns the entire slave-girl, so the child is considered born in his ownership. Hence no extra payment for the child is due.
5
Note the underlying reasoning
The half value compensates for the other partner's share in the slave-girl; the half 'uqr compensates for the other partner's right in the intercourse. The child's value is exempt because ownership is fully transferred.
Talil (reasoning) is central to Hanafi fiqh.

Joint Claim of Child from Shared Slave-Girl
Ruling when both partners claim the child and the dispute resolution method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when both partners claim a child from their jointly owned slave-girl and explain the proof from the athar of 'Umar and 'Ali.

Core Idea
When both partners equally claim a child, Hanafi ascribes the child to both with mutual inheritance.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling when two partners jointly own a slave-girl who gives birth, and both claim the child. The Hanafi school, based on the practice of the Companions 'Umar and 'Ali, holds that the child belongs to both partners, with each paying half the 'uqr (compensation for the slave-girl's depreciation) in offset. This contrasts with the Shafi'i view that uses physiognomy (qafa) to assign the child to one father.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the disputed claim
Understand the case: two partners jointly own a slave-girl (umm walad), she gives birth, and both partners claim the child as theirs. The question is how to resolve the conflicting claims.
The child's lineage is at stake, along with rights of inheritance and the status of the mother.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to the Hanafi school, the child is ascribed to both partners equally. The slave-girl becomes umm walad for both. Each partner owes half the 'uqr, but since each owes the other, the amounts are offset (muqassah). The child inherits from each as a full son, and each inherits from the child as a full father.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
3
Locate the dalil: the athar of 'Umar and 'Ali
The proof comes from two athar (narrations from Companions). In the first, two men came to 'Umar claiming a son; he declared both as fathers. In the second, 'Ali ruled that a child born from two men who had intercourse with the same woman in one purity period belongs to both men.
These athar are considered zanni in transmission and meaning, but are relied upon in the absence of stronger evidence.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The athar directly show that when two claimants have equal claims, the child is jointly attributed. 'Umar did not use physiognomy but made the child belong to both. This establishes the Hanafi rule that equal claims result in joint lineage.
The Hanafi school prefers this over the Shafi'i method of qafa.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that a physiognomist (qa'if) should be consulted to determine resemblance, and the child is assigned to the one he resembles. The Hanafi school rejects this when both claims are equally valid, relying on the athar.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in Hanafi it's joint inheritance; in Shafi'i it's single paternity.

When co-owners equally claim a slave woman's child, Hanafi school attributes the child to both, rejecting Shafi'i reliance on physiognomy.

This lesson examines a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding paternity when two co-owners of a slave woman both claim her newborn. The Hanafi school, based on the practice of the Companions, holds that the child is attributed to both owners equally, with legal consequences for inheritance and the woman's status. State the Hanafi position
What exactly does Imam Abu Hanifa rule when two co-owners both claim the child? The child is attributed to both, the slave woman becomes umm walad for both, and each pays half the 'aqr via set-off.

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the child should be attributed to one father based on qiyafah (physical resemblance) or, if that fails, by drawing lots.

Al-Shafi'i relies on hadith where the Prophet (S) approved of qiyafah (the case of Zayd and Usama) and on the hadith about Hilal's wife's child, as well as the hadith of Ali using lots.
Hanafi response: these proofs are not decisive when claims are equal and simultaneous.

What is the practical difference in inheritance? Hanafi: child inherits from both as a full son; each father inherits a full father's share (divided equally). Shafi'i: child inherits from one father only.

Commentary
This passage deals with the ruling when two co-owners of a slave woman both claim the child she bears. The Hanafi position, attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa, is that the child is attributed to both owners equally, because the claims are of equal strength and there is no preponderance. Consequently, the slave woman becomes umm walad for both, and each must pay half the 'aqr (dower) as compensation, but through mutual set-off (muqassah) actual payment is not needed. The child inherits a full son's share from each father, and each father inherits a full father's share from the child, but since the child is one, the total inheritance from the child is divided equally between the two fathers. The proof is based on athar from Umar and Ali. Umar, when two men claimed a child, made the child belong to both after examining physical resemblance; Ali similarly ruled that the child belongs to both men who had intercourse during the same tuhr (clean period). The Shafi'i position, in contrast, relies on qiyafah (physiognomy) to determine resemblance and thereby attribute the child to one father. Al-Shafi'i cites hadith where the Prophet approved of qiyafah in the case of Zayd and Usama, and where the Prophet described physical signs for Hilal's wife's child. If qiyafah fails, the Shafi'i school resorts to qur'ah (lot). The Hanafi response, implied in the text, is that the qiyafah evidence is not decisive in the case of equal claims; the athar from the Companions support attribution to both. The text also explains the inheritance consequences: each father gets a full son's share from the child, and the two fathers together get one father's share from the child, divided equally.

Key Terms
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who bears a child to her owner; upon the owner's death, she becomes free.
عقر ('aqr): The dower (mahr) due to a slave woman when a man has intercourse with her under a claim of ownership or marriage; here, it is half the 'aqr for each co-owner.
مقاصة (muqassah): Mutual set-off of debts; here, the two co-owners owe each other half the 'aqr, so they cancel out without actual payment.
قيافة (qiyafah): Physiognomy; the art of determining lineage or resemblance through physical features; used to attribute paternity.
قرعة (qur'ah): Lot; a method of decision by drawing lots when other methods fail; used by Shafi'i as a last resort for paternity determination.

Ruling on Owning a Mahram Relative
States the ruling for mahram ownership and wala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person owns a mahram relative and identify that the wala belongs to the owner.

Core Idea
Owning a mahram relative causes automatic freedom; wala follows the owner.

This lesson covers a unique Hanafi ruling: if a person comes to own a relative with whom marriage is prohibited (like a sister or aunt), that relative is freed immediately by virtue of the ownership. The master does not need to perform an act of emancipation. The wala – the right of inheritance and guardianship – then belongs to the owner.

Commentary
This passage contains two distinct rulings from Kitab al-Wala. The first ruling states that if a person owns a mahram relative (such as a sister or aunt), that relative becomes free automatically by virtue of the ownership, and the wala (rights of inheritance and guardianship) of the freed person belongs to the owner. This is because the freedom is caused by the owner's possession, even though he did not perform the emancipation. The second ruling presents a scenario: a slave belonging to one person marries the slave-girl of another. If the master of the slave-girl frees her while she is pregnant from the slave, both the slave-girl and the fetus are freed. The wala of the child goes to the master of the mother and never transfers to anyone else. This is because at the time of emancipation, the father was a slave, so the child's wala cannot go to the father or the father's master.

Key Terms
ذارحم محرم (dhu rahim muharram): A relative who is within the prohibited degrees of marriage, such as mother, sister, aunt, etc.
ولاء (wala): The right of inheritance and legal guardianship that a master has over his freed slave, and which the freed slave's children inherit.

Ruling on Wala of a Child Born to a Freed Pregnant Slave-Girl
States the ruling for wala of child born to freed pregnant slave-girl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling when a pregnant slave-girl is freed: the child is freed with her, and the wala belongs to the mother's master permanently.

Core Idea
A child born to a slave-girl freed while pregnant is free, and their wala never transfers from the mother's master.

This lesson presents a specific scenario from Kitab al-Wala: a slave of one man marries the slave-girl of another. If the slave-girl's master frees her while she is pregnant, both she and her unborn child become free. The wala of the child does not go to the father's master; it remains forever with the mother's master. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi principle that wala follows the mother's emancipation when the father is a slave at the time of conception.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial setup
Identify the parties: a male slave (owned by one master) marries a female slave (owned by another). The female slave becomes pregnant from the male slave.
Both are owned by different masters.
2
Identify the event
The master of the female slave frees her while she is pregnant.
Emancipation occurs during pregnancy, not before.
3
Determine the status of mother and child
Upon emancipation, the mother becomes free. Since the fetus is considered part of her, it also becomes free at birth (as a continuation of her freedom).
The child is free from birth, not conceived as a slave.
4
Assign the wala
The wala (right of inheritance/patronage) of the child goes to the mother's master. This wala is permanent and never transfers to another party, even if the father later becomes free.
The father's master gets no share in wala because at the time of emancipation the father was a slave.

20 (2) Whoever owns a mahram relative (forbidden in marriage), he becomes free by him, and his wala belongs to him. (7) And if a slave of a man marries the slave-girl of another, then the master of the slave-girl frees her while she is pregnant from the slave, she becomes free and her fetus becomes free, and the wala of the fetus belongs to the master of the mother, never transferring from him.

Commentary
This passage contains two distinct rulings from Kitab al-Wala. The first ruling states that if a person owns a mahram relative (such as a sister or aunt), that relative becomes free automatically by virtue of the ownership, and the wala (rights of inheritance and guardianship) of the freed person belongs to the owner. This is because the freedom is caused by the owner's possession, even though he did not perform the emancipation. The second ruling presents a scenario: a slave belonging to one person marries the slave-girl of another. If the master of the slave-girl frees her while she is pregnant from the slave, both the slave-girl and the fetus are freed. The wala of the child goes to the master of the mother and never transfers to anyone else. This is because at the time of emancipation, the father was a slave, so the child's wala cannot go to the father or the father's master.

Key Terms
ولاء (wala): The right of inheritance and legal guardianship that a master has over his freed slave, and which the freed slave's children inherit.
حمل (haml): Fetus or unborn child.
