Liability for a Leaning Wall on Public Road
Ruling on owner's liability when a leaning wall collapses after notice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the owner of a leaning wall becomes liable for damages and apply the ruling to cases involving Muslim and dhimmi claimants.

Core Idea
Negligence after due notice creates liability; malice is not required.

This lesson examines a specific liability scenario from Hanafi fiqh: a wall that leans into a public road. The ruling clarifies when the owner must pay compensation for harm caused by the wall's collapse. Understanding the precise conditions—notice, witness, and failure to act—is crucial for determining responsibility in property-related injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hazard and the demand
The text begins with a wall leaning toward the public road. The owner is asked to demolish it, and witnesses are taken. Identify these as the initial triggering conditions: the owner is put on notice, and the demand is formally witnessed.
The road is a public thoroughfare; the wall is private property causing public harm.
2
Determine the owner's failure
The owner fails to demolish the wall within a period in which he was able to do so. Focus on the element of feasibility: the owner had the capacity to act but did not.
The law does not require immediate demolition if not possible; only that the owner uses the opportunity.
3
Link the failure to the harm
The wall collapses and causes loss of life or property. The ruling establishes that the owner's negligence is the cause (sabab) of the harm, making him liable for compensation.
This is akin to qatl bi-sabab (causative homicide) in Hanafi law.
4
Apply the equality principle
The ruling explicitly states it makes no difference whether the demand came from a Muslim or a dhimmi. This underscores that the right to safety on public roads is universal for all residents under Muslim rule.
The dhimmi's right is equal; the owner's liability does not depend on the claimant's faith.
5
Extract the underlying maxim
The ruling reflects the maxim 'La darar wa la dirar' (no harm and no reciprocation of harm). The owner's inaction after notice transforms a private structure into a source of harm, which he is obligated to remove.
This maxim is foundational to Hanafi tort law.

Only the affected property owner can demand removal of a hazard on his exclusive domain.

This short but precise ruling clarifies standing in nuisance law. In the Hanafi school, the right to demand removal of a hazard is tied to the specificity of harm. If a wall leans toward a private house, only that house's owner may demand its demolition. This prevents interference by unrelated parties and respects property boundaries.

This distinguishes the private house case from the public road case.

Mutual Collision of Horsemen: Reciprocal Diyah
Ruling on liability in mutual accidental collision

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah when two horsemen collide and both die, and apply the condition of equal fault.

Core Idea
In a mutual accidental collision, each rider's 'aqila pays full diyah to the other's heirs.

Accidents involving riders can lead to death, and Islamic law provides a clear mechanism for compensating the victims. This lesson examines a classic case from Hanafi fiqh: two horsemen collide and both die. The ruling establishes that liability is reciprocal, with each rider's clan ('aqila) paying the blood money (diyah) for the other's death. This reflects the principle that in the absence of fault assignment, both parties bear equal responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a collision between two horsemen resulting in death. The ruling is that each rider's 'aqila must pay the diyah of the other rider.
This is a ruling of obligation (wajib) on the 'aqila.
2
Determine the type of homicide
Since there is no evidence of intent or clear fault, the deaths are classified as accidental homicide (qatl khata).
Accidental homicide triggers diyah on the 'aqila, not qisas.
3
Apply the rule of reciprocal liability
Both riders are considered equally at fault; therefore, each 'aqila compensates the heirs of the other.
The 'aqila of rider A pays diyah to rider B's heirs, and vice versa.
4
Confirm the absence of fault apportionment
In this scenario, no distinction is made between aggressor and victim. Both are treated as causing the other's death.
If one rider were clearly negligent, the ruling might differ.

Diyah for Accidental Killing of a Slave: The Cap at 9990 Dirhams
Determines maximum diyah for a slave at 9990 dirhams, with proof and dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for accidental killing of a slave and explain why it is capped at 9,990 dirhams, citing the athar of Ibrahim and distinguishing the position of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A slave's diyah is capped at 9,990 dirhams, ten less than a free man, to maintain a symbolic distinction.

In Hanafi law, the diyah for accidentally killing a slave is not simply his market value; it is subject to a maximum. This lesson examines the ruling that the payment cannot exceed 9,990 dirhams, even if the slave is valued higher. We will explore the proof from the athar of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and the opposing view of Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i, who allow full market value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is accidental killing of a slave (qatl khata'). The ruling is that the killer must pay the slave's market value, but not more than 10,000 dirhams. If the value is 10,000 or more, the payment is 9,990 dirhams—ten dirhams less than a free man's diyah.
The reduction of ten dirhams is symbolic, not proportional.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is an athar (statement) from the tabi'i Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, transmitted in Kitab al-Athar by Imam Muhammad and other early collections. The athar states that for accidental killing, the slave's value is paid but it is not made equal to the diyah of a free man, and is decreased by ten dirhams.
This athar is mursal (not directly from the Prophet) and thus zanni in thubut, but it is relied upon in the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase is 'لا يجعل مثل دية الحر وينقص عنه عشرة دراهم' (it is not made equal to the diyah of a free man, and is decreased by ten dirhams). This directly justifies capping at 9,990 when the value reaches or exceeds 10,000.
The decrease of ten ensures the slave's diyah never equals or exceeds that of a free man.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf (one of the Sahibayn) and Imam al-Shafi'i disagree. They cite an athar from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab that the diyah of a slave is his full market value, even if it exceeds the diyah of a free man. They hold that no cap exists.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf (Abu Yusuf) and an inter-madhhab ikhtilaf (Shafi'i).
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is that of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad: the cap at 9,990 holds. The athar of Ibrahim is given precedence to maintain a clear legal distinction between free and slave, even if only by ten dirhams.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is significant: a slave worth 15,000 would yield 9,990 under the Hanafi view, but 15,000 under the opposing view.

A slave's diyat in manslaughter is his value capped at 9,990 dirhams—ten dirhams less than a free man's—to preserve the distinction between free and slave.

While the diyat of a free man is fixed at 10,000 dirhams, the slave's diyat is based on his value, but with an important ceiling. The Hanafi school, following an athar of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, imposes a reduction of ten dirhams to ensure the free man's diyat remains higher. According to the matn, the diyat is the slave's market value, but not exceeding 10,000 dirhams. If the value is 10,000 or more, it is reduced by ten dirhams to 9,990.
The reduction ensures the slave's diyat is always less than the free man's 10,000 dirhams.

The primary evidence is an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i (a tabi'i) recorded in Kitab al-Athar: 'If a slave is killed unintentionally, his value is due, but it should not be made equal to the diyat of the free man; rather, it is reduced by ten dirhams.'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A female slave's diya is capped at 4990 dirhams, ten dirhams less than the free woman's diya.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in the law of diyat (blood money): when a female slave is accidentally killed and her market value exceeds the diya of a free woman. The Hanafi school sets a cap of 4990 dirhams, derived from the principle that a female slave's diya cannot equal or exceed that of a free woman. We will examine the ruling, its underlying reasoning, and the supporting evidence from hadith and athar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
The passage states: if a female slave is killed accidentally and her market value is more than 5000 dirhams (the free woman's diya), the killer must pay 4990 dirhams, not the full value.
The cap is exactly 'five thousand minus ten'.
2
Understand the rationale
The free woman's diya is half of a free man's diya (10,000 dirhams → 5,000 dirhams). To prevent the slave's diya from equaling or exceeding the free woman's, it is reduced by 10 dirhams.
This preserves the hierarchy: slave's diya < free woman's diya < free man's diya.
3
Locate the primary evidence
The author cites the hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal: 'The diya of a woman is half of the diya of a man' and the athar of 'Ali: 'The blood money for wounds of women is half of the diya of a man.' These support the half-diya principle.
A conflicting hadith (from 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb) suggests equality up to one-third, but the Hanafi school prefers the half-diya proof.
4
Note the tarjih and mufta bihi
The Hanafi position relies on the stronger dalil; the cap of 4990 dirhams is the operative ruling for fatwa.
Al-Samarqandi (the author) adopts this view as zahir al-riwayah.

A slave's limb diya is proportional to his value, but capped below the free person's equivalent to preserve status hierarchy.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on diya (blood money) for a male slave's hand. The rule is derived by analogy from the free man's diya: just as the free man's hand diya is half his full diya, the slave's hand diya is half his value. However, a crucial cap prevents the slave's diya from exceeding the free man's, thereby upholding the legal distinction between free and slave status.

1

Explicitly state the matn's ruling: Diya for a slave's hand is half his value, but it is never increased beyond 4995 dirhams (five thousand minus five).

The new case is the slave's hand. The effective cause (illa) is proportionality: the diya for a limb is half the full diya of that person (free or slave).

Even if half the slave's value exceeds 5,000 dirhams, the diya is capped at 4,995 dirhams. This ensures the slave's hand diya never equals or surpasses the free man's 5,000 dirhams, preserving the hierarchy that a slave is of lower rank.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave's diya for any limb is the same fraction of his value as the fraction of a free man's full diya.

This lesson introduces a key principle in Hanafi diyat: all fixed diya amounts for a free man's limbs apply proportionally to a slave's market value. The passage explains how this is derived from the practice of 'Umar and applied through qiyas. You will learn to calculate specific amounts and understand the underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
The text states that whatever is fixed from the diya of a free man is fixed from the value of the slave. This is the foundational rule.
2

The text gives examples: a free man's finger diya is 1/10 of full diya (1000 dirhams), so for a slave valued at 4000 dirhams, the finger diya is 1/10 of 4000 = 400 dirhams. Similarly, a tooth diya is 1/20, so 1/20 of 4000 = 200 dirhams.
3

The athar of 'Umar explicitly equates the 'aql (diya) of a slave to his price (value) just like the 'aql of a free man to his diya. This Qawl Sahabi is the primary evidence.
The athar is from Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq.
4
Understand the qiyas
The tooth example is derived by analogical reasoning: just as the finger's diya is a fraction of the full diya, the tooth's diya is also a fraction; similarly for the slave, the fraction is applied to his value.
The illa (effective cause) is the proportional relation per body part.
5
Note the cap
The slave's diya for a limb cannot exceed the corresponding free man's diya. If the calculated amount exceeds the free man's, it is reduced to the free man's amount.
This prevents the slave's compensation from being higher than a free man's for the same injury.

Calculating Diyah for a Slave's Injuries
Ruling on how to calculate diyah for a slave's injuries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the diyah for a slave's injury using the Hanafi proportional method and apply the cap that it cannot exceed the free person's diyah for the same injury.

Core Idea
A slave's diyah is proportional to his value, not a fixed sum — and it cannot exceed the free person's diyah.

In Hanafi fiqh, injuries to a slave are compensated based on his market value, in the same proportion as a free person's diyah. This lesson teaches the calculation method using concrete examples and the proof from Umar ibn al-Khattab. You will also learn the critical cap that prevents the slave's compensation from exceeding the free person's amount, ensuring fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the injury and its fraction of full diyah
For a free person, each injury has a known fraction of the full diyah (e.g., finger = 1/10, tooth = 1/20). For a slave, use the same fraction against his market value.
The full diyah for a free man is 1,000 dinars or 10,000 dirhams.
2
Apply the fraction to the slave's value
Multiply the slave's market value by the injury's fraction to get the base compensation.
Example: Slave worth 4,000 dirhams loses a finger: 1/10 of 4,000 = 400 dirhams.
3
Apply the cap: cannot exceed free person's equivalent
Calculate the free person's compensation for the same injury. If the slave's base amount is greater, reduce it to the free person's amount.
This ensures the slave's diyah does not surpass the free person's.
4
Cite the dalil
The athar from Umar ibn al-Khattab equates the slave's diyah (proportional to his value) with the free person's diyah (proportional to his diyah), establishing the proportional method.
Dalil: Qawl Sahabi (Umar), recorded in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq and Sunan al-Bayhaqi.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for a fetus when the mother dies first, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
If mother dies first, fetus's death is attributed to hers, not to the assault.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in jinayat: when a pregnant woman is assaulted and dies, and then the fetus is expelled dead. The question is whether the assailant must pay a separate ghurrah for the fetus in addition to the mother's diyah. The Hanafi school makes a crucial distinction based on the order of death, which this lesson unpacks through the classical text and the Shafi'i opposition.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from the text: only mother's diyah, nothing for fetus. Note the condition: mother died first, then fetus expelled dead.
The key phrase is 'la shay' fi al-janin'.
2
Understand the Hanafi rationale
Analyze the causal reasoning: the intervening death of the mother severs the direct link between the assault and the fetus's death. The fetus's death is attributable to the mother's death, not the blow.
This is based on the principle that death of the mother is an intervening cause.
3
Identify the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the assault initiated the chain of events; the fetus could not have survived the mother's death, so the blow is the cause of both deaths.
The Shafi'i ruling: diyah for mother + ghurrah for fetus.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, only diyah of mother; under Shafi'i, both diyah and ghurrah. This affects the total compensation amount.
The ghurrah is 1/20 of the mother's diyah (500 dinars or 5000 dirhams).
5
Confirm the mufta bihi
In the Hanafi school, the position that no ghurrah is due is the operative ruling for fatwa, as stated in the text.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah follows this view.

Diyah for a Slave Woman's Fetus
Ruling on diyah for slave woman's fetus based on value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the diyah for the fetus of a slave woman and distinguish it from the fixed ghurrah for a free woman's fetus.

Core Idea
For a slave woman's fetus, diyah is a fraction of value, not a fixed slave.

In Hanafi fiqh, the diyah for a free woman's fetus is a fixed ghurrah (a slave of medium value). However, for a slave woman's fetus, the diyah is proportional to the fetus's own market value. This lesson unpacks the fractions: 1/20 for a male, 1/10 for a female, and the proof from the statement of Sufyan al-Thawri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is causing the abortion of a slave woman's fetus. The ruling is that wajib diyah must be paid, calculated as a fraction of the fetus's value if it were alive.
This is a specific ruling for slave women; free women have a different rule.
2
Note the fractions by gender
The matn states: if male, half of one-tenth (1/20); if female, one-tenth (1/10) of the fetus's value.
The fractions are applied to the estimated value of the fetus as a slave.
3
Locate the proof
The author cites an athar from Sufyan al-Thawri in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, which directly states these fractions. This is a transmitted evidence (dalil naqli) but not from the primary Quran or Sunnah.
Hanafi school accepts this as a valid proof for the ruling.
4
Contrast with free woman's fetus
For a free woman's fetus, the diyah is a fixed ghurrah (a slave of medium value, approximately 1/20 of full diyah). For a slave woman, the diyah is a fraction of the fetus's own value, not a fixed amount.
This distinction avoids equating the two.
5
Apply the calculation
Given a value, multiply: male value × 1/20; female value × 1/10. Example: value 4000 dirhams → male: 200, female: 400.
The Urdu commentary provides this example.

When the mother dies first, fetal death is attributed to her death, not to the blow – so no separate ghurrah is due.

Compensation for a fetus is part of its estate and inherited by its heirs, just like an adult's diyah.

When a fetus is wrongfully killed, the perpetrator must pay a ghurrah (compensation). A key question is: who inherits this compensation? The Hanafi school rules that it is part of the fetus's estate and is distributed to its heirs according to the laws of inheritance. This lesson unpacks the proof from analogy and hadith.

No Kaffarah for Fetal Death: Distinguishing Qatal Sabab
Ruling that kaffarah is not required in fetal homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qatal sabab and qatal khata in the context of fetal homicide, and state the Hanafi ruling on kaffarah, citing the three proofs.

Core Idea
Kaffarah is not required for fetal death because it is indirect killing (qatal sabab) and certainty is lacking.

The question of expiation for causing a miscarriage reveals a key Hanafi principle: kaffarah is only wajib for direct accidental killing, not for indirect causation. This lesson unpacks the three proofs used by the Hanafi school and contrasts them with the Shafi'i view, helping you apply the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is causing a miscarriage by striking a pregnant woman. The Hanafi ruling is: the perpetrator pays a ghurrah (one-tenth of diyah) but does not owe kaffarah.
Kaffarah here refers to freeing a slave or fasting two months.
2
List the Three Hanafi Proofs
First, the act is classified as qatal sabab (indirect killing) not qatal khata (direct accidental). Second, there is no certainty (yaqeen) that the blow directly caused death; the fetus could have died from other causes. Third, the hadith that mention compensation for fetal death do not mention kaffarah.
These proofs are found in the source text.
3
Analyze the Distinction: Qatal Sabab vs. Qatal Khata
In Hanafi law, kaffarah is only obligated for direct killing where the killer's act is the immediate cause of death (qatal mubashir). Qatal sabab involves an indirect cause, like digging a well or causing a miscarriage, where another factor (e.g., the mother's condition) intervenes. Thus, kaffarah is not wajib.
Compare with direct accidental killing where kaffarah is wajib.
4

Imam al-Shafi'i considers fetal death as qatal khata because the blow is the apparent cause and prevailing assumption (ghalabat al-zann) suffices for obligation. The Hanafi response is that certainty is required for religious expiation, not mere assumption.
This highlights a usul al-fiqh difference: requirement of certainty vs. predominant probability.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is: Hanafi: only ghurrah (financial compensation); Shafi'i: ghurrah plus kaffarah. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school is that no kaffarah is required.
This affects the perpetrator's religious and financial obligations.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri deals with two related but distinct rulings within the Book of Diyat. First, the diyat (blood money) for causing a miscarriage (janeen) is discussed. Kaffarah for Quasi-Intentional and Accidental Killing
Ruling with proof from Quran for kaffarah in homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for kaffarah in qatl shibh al-amd and khata, and identify the sequential alternatives and the evidence from Quran 4:92.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for homicide: free a believing slave; if unable, fast two months; feeding is not an option.

This lesson covers the obligatory expiation for unintentional and quasi-intentional killing. The Quranic verse from Surah al-Nisa provides the primary evidence, and the Hanafi school emphasizes the strict sequential order: first freeing a slave, then fasting. Feeding is not accepted as an alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on kaffarah
The matn states: for shibh al-amd and khata, kaffarah is freeing a believing slave; if unable, fasting two consecutive months; feeding does not suffice.
This is a fixed sequence.
2
Locate the Quranic proof
The commentary cites Surah al-Nisa 4:92: 'And whoever kills a believer by mistake, then freeing a believing slave... and whoever does not find [the means] then fasting two consecutive months.'
The verse explicitly mentions freeing and fasting, not feeding.
3
Understand the Hanafi deduction
Since the verse does not mention feeding, it is not a valid alternative. The Hanafi school holds that the command is sequential: first attempt freeing, if impossible, then fast.
This is based on the principle that the mention of one alternative excludes others.
4
Apply the ruling to different scenarios
If a person can free a slave but fasts instead, the fast is not valid. If one cannot free a slave but can feed, feeding is not accepted; they must fast.
This is the practical consequence.
5
Compare with other schools (optional)
Imam al-Shafi'i allows feeding as an alternative to freeing a slave based on other sources. The Hanafi position restricts to the verse's explicit alternatives.
This is a point of ikhtilaf.

Qasamah: The Procedure of Oaths in Unresolved Homicide
Rulings on qasamah when a corpse is found in a locality with unknown killer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah and apply it to scenarios where a corpse is found in a locality versus a public place.

Core Idea
When a corpse is found in a locality and the killer is unknown, the community must swear fifty oaths to avoid paying diyat.

This lesson explains the unique Hanafi procedure of qasamah, which resolves liability when a homicide remains unsolved. You will examine the ruling, its conditions, and the evidence from the Prophet's practice. Understanding qasamah requires distinguishing when the procedure applies and when it does not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Qasamah is the oath-taking by fifty men from a locality where a corpse is found. The ruling is that it is wajib on the community when conditions are met; failure leads to imposition of diyat.
The hukm level is wajib, based on the hadith practice.
2
Locate the dalil
The evidence is the hadith of 'Abdullah ibn Sahl in Sahih Muslim and Sahih Bukhari. The Prophet offered fifty oaths to the Jews and later paid diyat.
This hadith establishes the principle of qasamah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's offer of fifty oaths and his eventual payment of diyat indicate that the community can be held liable through collective oaths. The Hanafi school uses this to require oaths from the locality before imposing diyat.
The dalala is zanni, but the Hanafi rely on this as a sunnah.
4
Distinguish scope of application
Qasamah applies only when the corpse is found in a specific locality where the killer can be presumed from that community. If found on a public road or city centre, no diyat is taken.
The distinction is crucial: the location determines liability.
5
Note implementation details
The victim's guardian selects fifty upright men from the locality. They must swear 'We did not kill him, nor do we know the killer.' After the oath, diyat is payable over three years.
The oath-takers are chosen by the wali, not by the community.

Chapter of Qasamah. If a corpse is found in a locality and the killer is not known, and there is no clue, the final measure is to take oaths from fifty chosen men of that locality: 'We did not kill him, nor do we know the killer.' When they swear, the diyat of accidental killing is imposed upon the people of the locality, to be collected from them over three years. But if the murder occurs on a public road or in the middle of the city, where it cannot be presumed that the people of the locality killed him, and it seems someone brought the body and dumped it, then no diyat is taken from the people of the locality, because they have no knowledge. 'Abdullah ibn Sahl ibn Zayd and Muhayyisa ibn Mas'ud ibn Zayd went to Khaybar, and 'Abdullah ibn Sahl ibn Mas'ud was killed by Jews. So Muhayyisa, Huwaysa, and 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Sahl came to the Prophet (peace be upon him). He asked, 'Do you have any witness to the killing?' They said, 'No.' He said, 'Then can you take fifty oaths from the Jews?' They said, 'They are disbelievers; they will swear false oaths, so what benefit is there?' Then the Prophet (peace be upon him) gave one hundred camels as diyat to 'Abd al-Rahman. This hadith establishes qasamah. The hadith fragment: 'Abdullah ibn Sahl went out... the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) mentioned the murder of 'Abdullah ibn Sahl and said to them, 'Will you swear fifty oaths and thereby become entitled to your companion (i.e., diyat) or your killer?' They said, 'How can we swear when we did not witness?' He said, 'Then the Jews will exonerate themselves by fifty oaths.' They said, 'How can we accept the oaths of a disbelieving people?' When the Messenger of Allah saw that, he gave his diyat.' (Muslim, Kitab al-Qasamah, no. 1669; Bukhari, Bab al-Qasamah, no. 6898). From this hadith, it is learned that from the locality where the murder occurred, fifty men should take oaths; this oath-taking is called qasamah. And according to the saying, if the killer is not known, then fifty men chosen by the victim's guardian should take oaths. The corpse is found in a locality and the killer is unknown, and it is presumed that someone from the locality killed and dumped it, then the fifty men of the locality swear: 'We did not kill him, nor do we know the killer.' After this oath, diyat is imposed on the people of the locality. Footnote: They mentioned the killing of 'Abdullah ibn Sahl to the Prophet, and he said, 'Can you swear fifty oaths to claim your companion's blood or your killer?' They said, 'How can we swear when we did not see the killing?' He said, 'Then the Jews will swear fifty oaths to exonerate themselves.' They said, 'How can we accept the oaths of a disbelieving people?' Then the Prophet paid the diyat himself.

Commentary
This passage under the chapter of Qasamah (oaths in homicide) outlines the Hanafi procedure when a dead body is found in a locality and the perpetrator is unknown. The ruling is that fifty upright men from the locality must swear that neither they nor anyone they know committed the murder. If they do so, the diyat for accidental murder is imposed on the community of that locality, to be paid within three years. However, if the body is found in a public place where it could have been left by outsiders, then no diyat is taken. The evidence comes from the famous incident of 'Abdullah ibn Sahl who was killed by Jews in Khaybar. When the Companions could not produce witnesses, the Prophet offered them the option of having fifty Jews swear to their innocence, but they refused to accept oaths from disbelievers. Eventually, the Prophet himself paid the diyat. This hadith establishes the principle of qasamah, though in practice the Hanafi school applies it by having the accused community swear and then pay diyat, rather than the victim's family proving guilt. The passage also clarifies that the fifty oath-takers are selected by the victim's guardian from the locality where the body was found.

Key Terms
قسامہ (qasamah): Oaths taken by fifty men from the locality where a dead body is found, swearing they did not kill the victim nor know the killer, after which diyat is imposed.
دیت (diyah): Blood money, here specifically the diyat for accidental killing (100 camels or equivalent) imposed on the locality.
ولی (wali): The guardian of the victim, who is responsible for claiming diyat and selecting the oath-takers.

Diyah for a Slain Person Found in Uninhabited Land
Ruling for a slain person in wilderness with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for a slain person found in uninhabited land and identify the proofs from the hadith and athar.

Core Idea
No Muslim blood is wasted: diyah from bayt al-mal when no locality can be suspected.

This lesson addresses a specific case in the Law of Blood Money (diyat): when a slain person is discovered in a remote wilderness with no habitation within earshot. The matn declares such blood 'wasted' (hader) meaning no specific party is liable. However, the commentary expands that the public treasury (bayt al-mal) pays diyah to prevent wastage of Muslim blood, based on the Prophet's action and Ali's practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario and initial ruling
The matn begins: 'If he is found in a wilderness with no habitation near it, it is wasted (hader).' Note that 'hader' means no liability on any individual or tribe.
The condition is 'barriyah' where a shout cannot be heard from any habitation.
2
Locate the two proofs narrated
First, the statement of Ali (marfu' to the Prophet): 'For any slain person found in a desolate land, his blood money is from the public treasury.' Second, the action of Ali himself in Kufa paying diyah from bayt al-mal when a slain person was discovered in the mosque.
The proof is from sunnah (action of Prophet) and practice of a Sahaba.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah from the proofs
The imperative 'fadiyatuhu min bayt al-mal' implies obligation of payment. The Prophet's dislike of wasting blood shows the underlying principle: Muslim blood must not go uncompensated.
The ruling is based on preventing bloodshed and ensuring justice.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require qasamah from the nearest inhabited village if there is suspicion. Hanafi holds that since there is no specific locality to suspect, no qasamah; diyah from treasury suffices.
Thamarah al-khilaf: Shafi'i would impose an oath on the nearest village; Hanafi exempts them.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
Al-Hidayah confirms: diyah from bayt al-mal, no qasamah. This is the mufta bihi relied upon for fatwa.
Authority: Zahir al-Riwayah; tarjih by Al-Marghinani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a slain person is found between two villages and explain the evidentiary basis from the hadith.

Core Idea
Even a handspan difference determines liability—precision prevents blood going unavenged.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in diyat law: a slain person discovered between two inhabited localities. The Hanafi school assigns liability for qasamah and diyah to the nearer village, based on a prophetic precedent. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proof, emphasizing the importance of precise measurement in assigning legal responsibility.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its legal context
Identify the act (slain person found between two villages) and the hukm (the closer village bears liability for qasamah and diyah). Place it within the broader rules of diyat and qasamah.
Qasamah is a collective oath and liability for homicide when the killer is unknown.
2
Identify the hadith and its key elements
Locate the hadith of Abu Sa'id. Note the Prophetic command to measure, the measurement by a handspan, and the final adjudication assigning diyah to the closer community.
The handspan (shibr) is the smallest measure mentioned, indicating that even minimal proximity suffices.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the Prophet's action proves the ruling: his command to measure and his ruling based on proximity demonstrate that distance determines liability. This is a direct precedent (dalil naqli).
The dalal is zanni but sufficient for a wajib ruling in the Hanafi school.
4
Consider practical implications
Reflect on the principle: even a marginal difference (a handspan) shifts full liability. This avoids disputes and ensures that blood does not go unavenged.
In practice, precise measurement is necessary; if exactly equidistant, the Hanafi view prioritizes whichever can be determined as closer.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some schools may require equal sharing if the body is exactly midway, but Hanafis follow the hadith: measurement resolves the doubt.
The ruling is mukhtalaf fih, but Hanafis adhere to the prophetic precedent.

Ruling When a Slain Person is Found in a Flowing River
Ruling for a slain person found in the middle of a flowing river

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a slain person is found in a flowing river and explain why no diyah is imposed due to uncertainty of origin.

Core Idea
If the source of a slain body cannot be traced due to flowing water, the blood is wasted (hader) and no diyah is due.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on a specific scenario in the chapter of diyat (blood money): a slain body found in the middle of a flowing river. The core ruling is that no liability falls on any party because the origin is unknown. Understanding this ruling illustrates how the Hanafi school prioritizes certainty (yaqin) in imposing financial penalties and avoids making a locality bear the cost when the connection is uncertain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage describes a slain person found in the middle of a flowing river (exemplified by the Euphrates). Note that the water is moving, so the body could have been carried from anywhere upstream.
The Euphrates is an example; the rule applies to any large flowing river where the source cannot be determined.
2
State the ruling
The text states 'فهو هدر' (it is wasted). This means the blood of the victim is considered 'hader' – no diyah (blood money) is due from any particular group or locality.
3
Understand the legal basis
The ruling is based on the principle that liability for homicide requires certainty. Since the flowing water makes it impossible to trace the body to a specific place, the condition for imposing diyah or qasamah (collective oath) on a particular locality is absent.
Compare to a body found on land in a village – there, the nearest village may be held liable under specific conditions.
4
Distinguish from related cases
Contrast with a body found in still water (e.g., a lake) or on the bank of a river: the ruling may differ if the origin can be linked to a specific locality. In still water, if the body is found near a village, liability may arise.
The key variable is whether the body's origin is traceable. Flowing water creates doubt (shubha) that removes liability.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may still assign diyah to the nearest villages on both banks because they consider the vicinity enough. Hanafis exempt due to the lack of certainty.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; the Hanafi position relies on the principle of 'al-asl bara'at al-dhimmah' and avoidance of imposing liability on doubtful grounds.

Liability for a Body Caught at the Riverbank
Ruling with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a slain person is found caught at the riverbank and identify the prophetic evidence for assigning liability to the nearest village.

Core Idea
Proximity determines liability: a body caught at the bank points to the nearest village.

In cases of found bodies near water, the location and state of the body are key to assigning responsibility. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a body carried by the current and one caught at the bank. In this lesson, we examine the latter scenario and the prophetic precedent that establishes liability on the nearest village.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the body is 'muhtabisan' (caught/stuck) at the riverbank, not flowing with the current. This implies the murder likely occurred nearby.
Contrast with a body found floating downstream, where liability is different.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The nearest village is obligated to perform qasamah (collective oath of innocence) and pay diyah (blood money) for the slain person.
The ruling is 'wajib' (obligatory) on the villagers.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith of Abu Sa'id reports that the Prophet measured distance between two communities for a slain body and adjudged diyah on the closer one, even by a handspan.
This demonstrates that proximity is the decisive factor.
4
Understand the rationale
The law presumes that if the body is stationary, the murder was local. This relieves the state from paying and places responsibility on the community most likely involved.
The principle is 'al-umur bi maqasidiha' — acts are judged by their evidence.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some schools may require direct witnesses or specific proof against an individual. Hanafis rely on the strong circumstantial presumption from the hadith.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within Islamic criminal law.

Therefore, the nearest village is obligated to perform qasamah and pay diyah. The same hadith from Abu Sa'id is cited as evidence: proximity determines liability even by a small margin. This shows that apparent location is key when the body is stationary.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Ma'aqil (Book of Blood Moneys) is a commentary on the rulings regarding the liability of the 'aqila (agnatic relatives) for paying diya (blood money) in cases of shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional killing) and khata' (accidental killing). The author opens by explaining the linguistic origin of 'ma'aqil' from 'aql' meaning to restrain, then defines the 'aqila as those family members who pay the diya, thereby restraining the killer from repeating the mistake. The primary ruling is that diya in cases of shibh 'amd and khata' is wajib (obligatory) upon the 'aqila, not upon the killer himself. This is supported by the hadith of Abu Huraira where the Prophet (peace be upon him) judged that the diya for a fetus was on the 'asaba (male agnates) of the woman who caused the miscarriage. The author clarifies that this ruling applies only to killings that are not purely intentional ('amd), which would require qisas (retaliation). If the killer settles with the victim's family on payment of diya in an intentional killing, the 'aqila are not liable. Similarly, injuries to limbs, whether intentional or accidental, are not borne by the 'aqila; the perpetrator pays compensation himself. The second ruling states that the 'aqila are specifically the ahl al-diwan (people of the military register) if the killer is registered. This is a key Hanafi doctrine: in organized states with military registers, the fellow soldiers registered in the same diwan are the 'aqila, not the blood relatives. The author provides evidence from the practice of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab who established the diwan. He then presents the view of Imam Shafi'i who holds that the 'aqila are always the 'asaba (blood relatives), rejecting the diwan distinction. The Hanafi response is that the hadith about 'every family (batin) is liable' is general, but it is qualified by the practice of the Companions who limited it to the diwan in organized contexts. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that if a registered soldier kills accidentally, Hanafis hold his fellow soldiers liable, while Shafi'is hold his blood relatives liable.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('aqila): The male agnatic relatives (or fellow soldiers in a diwan) who are obligated to pay diya on behalf of the killer in cases of shibh 'amd and khata'.
ديوان (diwan): The military register or office established by 'Umar ibn al-Khattab listing soldiers; its members are liable as 'aqila for registered killers.
عصبة ('asaba): Male agnatic blood relatives who inherit as residuaries; in this context, they are the default 'aqila when the killer is not registered in a diwan.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on the 'aqila—the collective responsible for paying blood money in accidental killings. The school integrates the state military register (diwan) into the definition, a distinctive feature. We will see how proofs from hadith and the practice of Caliph Umar support this ruling, and how the payment is structured.

Shafi'i school generally uses the tribe in all cases.

The diyah is taken from the 'aqila's stipends over three years. The maximum that can be taken from a single person over the three years is 4 dirhams, so per year about 1.33 dirhams. If the total is reached in less or more time, it adjusts accordingly.

This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both views have proofs.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on the 'aqila (agnatic relatives responsible for paying blood money) with proofs from hadith and the practice of Caliph Umar (may Allah be pleased with him). The author begins by citing the well-known hadith, 'al-aqlu 'ala 'asabatiha' (blood money is upon her agnates) from Bukhari and Muslim, establishing the general principle. Another hadith from Jabir ibn Abdullah states that the Prophet (peace be upon him) wrote that blood money is upon every family (batn). The author then quotes a longer document attributed to Umar, which outlines that the Muhajirun of Quraysh and other clans are responsible for paying blood money among themselves. This demonstrates that the obligation rests on the tribe or family. However, the Hanafi position refines this: if the killer is registered in the diwan (military register founded by Umar), then his 'aqila are the fellow registrants (ahl al-diwan), not his tribal relatives. This is supported by an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and the historical report that Umar was the first to establish the diwan. The passage then lists three specific rulings (numbered 2433-2435): (3) blood money is collected from the 'aqila's stipends over three years, adjusting if the total is reached sooner or later; (4) for those not in the diwan, the 'aqila is their tribe; (5) the amount is apportioned over three years, with a maximum of 4 dirhams per person per year (one dirham and two daniqs), though less may be taken. The commentary also references Imam al-Shafi'i's similar view that blood money is on the agnates, showing agreement on the principle but differing in the mechanism (diwan vs. tribe). The Hanafi school thus incorporates the state system (diwan) into the Sharī‘a ruling.

العاقلة (al-‘āqilah): The agnatic relatives (or in Hanafi law, the members of the diwan or tribe) who are collectively responsible for paying blood money (diyah) in cases of accidental killing.
الديوان (al-dīwān): The military register or office established by Caliph Umar, listing soldiers entitled to stipends. In this context, ahl al-diwan are those registered; they are the 'aqila for each other.
العطاء (al-‘aṭā’): The stipend or salary given to soldiers from the state treasury. The blood money is taken from these stipends over three years.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Diyah from the 'aqila is collected over three years, adjustable to the allowance schedule.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Saghir addresses the practical question: over what period should the 'aqila pay the blood money? The text establishes a principle based on the practice of the Prophet (ﷺ) and early scholars. By understanding this ruling, you will see how fiqh balances fixed legal principles with the financial capacity of the 'aqila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The matn states that diyah is taken from the allowances of the 'aqila over three years. This is the default rule.

The ruling allows flexibility: if the allowances are paid in more or less than three years, the collection period adjusts accordingly.
This shows the principle is not rigidly tied to three years but to the availability of allowances.
3
Examine the proof
The commentary cites narrations that the Prophet judged diyah on the 'aqila to be paid in three years. The phrase 'عاما فيهم' indicates widespread practice (amal), a proof in Hanafi usul.
The proof is a sunnah ahad but reinforced by 'amal.
4
Consider the 'aqila's composition
The 'aqila includes those who receive allowances (people of the register) or the tribe. The ruling applies to each group appropriately.
Flexibility ensures the 'aqila is not overburdened beyond their means.

Core Idea
A freed slave's 'aqila is his master's clan; a mawla al-muwalat's 'aqila is his patron's clan.

'Aqila Not Liable for Slave's Crime
Ruling with proof on 'aqila non-liability for slave's crime

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the 'aqila is not liable for a slave's crime and identify the textual evidence from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
The 'aqila does not pay diyah for a slave's crime; the master bears the loss as property.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling in criminal liability: the 'aqila (agnatic group responsible for blood money) is exempt from paying diyah when a slave commits a crime. Instead, the master bears the financial responsibility. Understanding this rule clarifies the boundaries of collective liability and the distinct legal status of slaves in Islamic law. We will explore the evidence from the Prophet's hadith and the practice of the early scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit ruling
The passage begins by stating that the 'aqila does not bear the crime of a slave. This is the core Hanafi position. Recognize that this is a ruling of non-liability (hukm wad'i).
This ruling is found in Kitab al-Athar of Imam Muhammad.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The evidence is a hadith from Sunan Abi Dawud where the Prophet (peace be upon him) fixes the value of a slave (ghurrah) at five hundred dirhams. This amount is one-twentieth of the full diyah for a free person (10,000 dirhams).
The hadith is: 'The ghurrah is five hundred dirhams.'
3
Understand the minimum threshold
An athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i states that the 'aqila does not pay for injuries less than a mudihah (wound exposing bone), which also equals one-twentieth of full diyah (five camels). This shows that the 'aqila only bears amounts of one-twentieth or more.
The minimum liability for 'aqila is mudihah.
4
Apply to the slave's case
Although the slave's diyah is also one-twentieth (500 dirhams), the 'aqila is still not liable because the slave is property, not a free person. The master is responsible for the slave's acts.
The text clarifies: 'the master has the option to hand over the slave or pay his value.'
5
Confirm with additional athar
An athar from Ibn Abbas explicitly states that the 'aqila does not bear intentional killing, settlement, confession, nor what a slave committed. This reinforces the rule.
Sunan al-Bayhaqi records this athar.

Aqila Not Liable for Confessed Crime
Ruling that 'aqila does not bear diyah for confessed crime

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'aqila liability for a confessed crime and explain the condition under which the 'aqila may voluntarily pay.

Core Idea
The 'aqila is never forced to pay diyah based on the killer's confession; only voluntary confirmation creates obligation.

This lesson addresses a key principle in Hanafi diyah law: the liability of the 'aqila (the killer's paternal relatives) for blood money. The ruling clarifies that a confession by the killer—whether admitting to the homicide or to a debt owed to the victim—does not automatically bind the 'aqila. Instead, their liability only arises if they independently confirm the confession and voluntarily agree to pay. The proof is a mursal athar from the Medinan jurists, emphasizing the voluntary nature of such payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the entity
Recognize that the passage concerns a confession (i'tiraf) by the killer (jani) and the 'aqila, the group responsible for paying diyah in homicide cases.
Confession here can be to the killing itself or to a related property debt.
2
State the general ruling
The 'aqila is not liable for diyah arising from the killer's confession. This is the default position.
Liability does not transfer from the confessor to the 'aqila.
3
Note the exception
The 'aqila may become liable if they voluntarily confirm (tasdiq) the confession and agree to pay. This is a voluntary act, not an obligation.
The exception is based on the athar: 'unless they desire to do so as a favor.'
4
Examine the dalil
The proof is a mursal hadith: 'The 'aqila does not bear... nor confession.' The exception shows voluntariness.
The dalil is zanni in both transmission and meaning, but establishes the principle.
5
Understand the wisdom
Confession binds only the confessor; the 'aqila is not obligated because they did not participate in the crime or admission.
Allowing voluntary payment respects the 'aqila's autonomy over their wealth.

Core Idea
A settlement payment is the killer's personal debt, never transferred to the 'aqila.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in diyat (blood money) law: when a killer settles with the victim's family to avoid qisas, who bears the payment? The Hanafi position, supported by the athar of Ibn Abbas, places the entire settlement burden on the killer himself, not on his 'aqila. Understanding this distinction clarifies the boundaries of collective responsibility in Hanafi fiqh.

1

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The 'aqila pays the value of a slave accidentally killed, just as it pays diyah for a free person, because the cause is the same.

This passage extends 'aqila liability from free persons to slaves in accidental killings. Imam Abu Hanifah uses qiyas, analogizing the value of a slave to the diyah of a free person, supported by an athar from 'Umar. Understanding this ruling clarifies that 'aqila liability depends on the nature of the act (khat') rather than the victim's legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a free person accidentally killing a slave. The ruling is that the 'aqila of the free person is liable for the slave's value.

Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar from 'Umar ibn al-Khattab: 'The diyah of a slave is its value, like the diyah of a free person.'
The athar is recorded in Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq.

The athar equates the 'aql (diyah) of a slave with its value, just as the 'aql of a free person is his diyah. This establishes an analogy between the two cases.

Asl (original case): accidental killing of a free person → 'aqila pays full diyah. Far (sub-case): accidental killing of a slave → ?. Illa (effective cause): accidental killing (khat') in both. Hukm far: 'aqila pays the value of the slave.

Non-Liability of 'Aqila for Sulh and Iqrar
Ruling on 'aqila's non-liability for sulh and iqrar with supporting athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the 'aqila is not liable for sulh (settlement) or iqrar (confession) and identify the proof from the Madinan jurists.

Core Idea
The 'aqila is not liable for sulh or iqrar; these obligations fall solely on the killer.

In this lesson, we examine two situations where a killer might try to shift financial liability to his clan ('aqila): through a settlement (sulh) to avoid qisas, or through a confession (iqrar). The Hanafi school, based on the practice of the Madinan jurists, holds that the 'aqila is not liable for either, unless they voluntarily choose to pay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two transactions
Recognize that 'sulh' is a settlement where the killer pays money to avoid qisas, and 'iqrar' is a confession of the crime by the killer.
2
State the default rule
The 'aqila does not bear the financial obligations arising from sulh or iqrar. These are binding only on the killer himself.
3
Note the exception
The 'aqila may voluntarily pay these amounts out of generosity, but they are not obligated to do so.
This exception is derived from the phrase 'illa an yuhibbu dhalika tawallan minhum'.
4
Examine the proof
The proof is an athar from the jurists of Madinah, transmitted by Ibn Abi al-Zinad, which explicitly excludes sulh and iqrar from 'aqila's liability.
The athar is recorded in Sunan al-Bayhaqi.
5
Understand the rationale
Sulh and iqrar are based on the killer's own action or admission, not on a claim established by independent evidence. Therefore, the 'aqila, who are liable only for diyah in unintentional killing (established by evidence), are not responsible.
This distinguishes these from diyah in qatl khatta'.

Liability of 'Aqila for Slave Killed Unintentionally
Ruling on 'aqila's liability for value of slave killed unintentionally, using qiyas and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi reasoning that the value of a slave killed unintentionally is treated like diyah and thus obligatory upon the 'aqila.

Core Idea
Unintentional killing of a slave triggers 'aqila liability for the slave's value, by analogy to the diyah of a free man.

This lesson extends the principle of 'aqila liability to the unintentional killing of a slave. While the diyah of a free man is a known obligation on the clan, the Hanafi school, led by Imam Abu Hanifa, uses qiyas (analogy) to include the value of a slave. The proof includes an athar from Umar ibn al-Khattab equating the two.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
The matn states that if a free man unintentionally kills a slave, the diyah (value) of the slave is upon the free man's 'aqila.
2
Identify the original case (asl)
The established rule is that for unintentional killing (qatl khatta') of a free man, the diyah is wajib upon the 'aqila.
This is based on ijma and sunnah.
3
Identify the new case (far')
The new case is the unintentional killing of a slave. The question is whether the value is also on the 'aqila.
4
Extract the effective cause (illa)
The illa is 'unintentional killing' (qatl khatta'), which is common to both cases. The cause is jali (clear) because the element of intention is absent in both.
The illa is extracted by tahqiq al-manat (verification of the cause).
5
Confirm with textual evidence
The athar of Umar ibn al-Khattab equates the 'aql of a slave (diyah) to his value, just as the 'aql of a free man is to his diyah. This strengthens the analogy.
The athar is from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
