Conditional Emancipation with Suspended Payment
Ruling on conditional emancipation with suspended payment and proof from Abu Hanifah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional emancipation where freedom is suspended on payment and identify when the slave becomes free.

Core Idea
Freedom is not immediate; it is suspended until payment, but the slave gains permission to trade.

In this passage, we examine a variation of conditional emancipation where the master suspends freedom on a future payment. The Hanafi ruling is that the condition is valid, but emancipation only occurs upon actual payment. The slave becomes a ma'dhun (authorized to trade) to enable him to earn and pay the master. Let's unpack the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the ruling
Identify the master's statement: 'If you pay me one thousand, you are free.' The ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
2
Identify the proof
The text cites an athar from Abu Hanifah where he directly rules on a similar case: 'If he pays it, he is free.' This is the dalil.
Dalil: Qawl al-Imam.
3
Understand the conditionality
Freedom is suspended on the condition of payment. The slave does not become free upon the statement alone.
This distinguishes it from immediate emancipation with a debt.
4
Derive the practical consequences
The slave becomes obligated to pay and is granted permission to trade (ma'dhun) so he can earn the money.
Without this permission, the slave might be unable to fulfill the condition.

And if he says: 'If you pay me one thousand, you are free,' it is valid, and the payment becomes obligatory on him, and he becomes authorized (ma'dhun). [2217] (28) If the master said: 'If you pay me one thousand, you are free,' it is valid, and this slave becomes liable for the payment, and he becomes authorized to trade (ma'dhun al-tijarah). In this issue, freedom is suspended on payment of the money, so as long as he does not pay, the slave will not be free. However, because the condition of payment is made, he will be permitted to trade so that he can earn the money and pay the master. There is an athar: I heard Abu Hanifah asked about a man who said to his slave: 'If you pay me one hundred dinars, you are free.' He said: 'If he pays it, he is free.' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, Bab al-Itq bil-Shart, vol. 9, p. 169, no. 16788) From this athar it is known that the slave becomes free upon payment.

Commentary
This passage presents a variation of conditional emancipation. Instead of immediate emancipation with a debt, here freedom is suspended on a future condition: payment of a fixed amount. The ruling: the statement is valid, the slave becomes obligated to pay, and he is given permission (idhn) to engage in trade to earn the money. However, freedom is not immediate; it only occurs once the payment is made. The text cites an athar from Abu Hanifah himself to support this. This differs from the previous ruling where freedom was immediate upon acceptance.

Key Terms
مأذون التجارة (ma'dhun al-tijarah): A slave who is permitted by his master to engage in trade; his business transactions are valid.
