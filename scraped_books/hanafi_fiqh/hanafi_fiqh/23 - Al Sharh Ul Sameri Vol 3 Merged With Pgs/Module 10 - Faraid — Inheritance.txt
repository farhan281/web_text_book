Negation of Lineage After Li'an
Ruling on denying paternity and attaching child to mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on negating lineage after li'an and explain the legal consequences and evidence.

Core Idea
After li'an, the child is legally attached to the mother and inherits only from her.

When a husband denies paternity of a child through li'an, the qadi must negate the child's lineage from the husband and attach the child to the mother. This lesson examines the ruling, its proof from the hadith of Ibn Umar, the legal effect on inheritance, and a key difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The husband denies paternity during li'an; the qadi must negate lineage and attach child to mother. Recognize that this is a wajib action for the qadi.
The child is then known by the mother's name.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the hadith of Ibn Umar where the Prophet performed li'an and attached the child to the mother.
Bukhari no. 5315; Abu Dawud no. 2259.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit action of the Prophet (attachment) serves as the proof. The imperative 'attach' indicates obligation.
Hanafi usul: the Prophet's action in a legal context indicates wujub.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that if the child was born before li'an, paternity cannot be negated, while Hanafi allows it for any child denied in li'an.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different interpretations of the hadith.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school follows the general application of the hadith; this is the mufta bihi position.
The qadi must implement this ruling in court.

Consequences of a Mukatab's Death with Wealth
Ruling on a mukatab's death with wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a mukatab who dies with sufficient wealth and explain the legal effects on his kitabat, estate, and children.

Core Idea
A mukatab who dies with enough wealth completes his kitabat from his estate, dies free, and his heirs inherit.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario within the kitabat contract: when the mukatab passes away while still owing payments but leaving behind an estate. The Hanafi ruling clarifies that the contract is not automatically rescinded; instead, the debt is settled from his assets. Understanding this ruling requires grasping how the mukatab's status transitions from slave to free at the moment of death, affecting inheritance and the status of his children.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering event
The event is the death of a mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission) who leaves behind wealth (tarkah).
The key variable is that the wealth is sufficient to cover the remaining kitabat payments.
2
Determine the effect on the kitabat contract
The kitabat is not rescinded (faskh). Instead, the outstanding debt is paid from the mukatab's earnings or estate.
This differs from the scenario where the mukatab becomes unable to pay while alive, which rescinds the contract.
3
Establish the mukatab's legal status at death
By paying off the debt from his estate, the mukatab is considered to have become free in the last part of his life.
This means he dies as a free man, which affects inheritance and the status of his children.
4
Trace the distribution of the estate
After paying the kitabat debt, the remaining wealth (tarkah) is distributed among his heirs according to inheritance law.
The mukatab's children, even if born during the kitabat period, are free because he is deemed free at death.

Wala of Emancipation: Inheritance of Patronage Rights
States the rule that wala goes to the master's agnatic relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that the wala of a freed slave belongs to the master's asabah (agnatic relatives) and apply it to inheritance scenarios.

Core Idea
Wala returns to the master's agnatic line, not the freed slave's kin.

This brief passage states a foundational rule in Hanafi faraid: the wala (patronage) of a freed slave is inherited by the agnatic relatives ('asabah) of the master who emancipated him. This rule ensures that the bond of emancipation stays within the master's family line, similar to lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The passage mentions 'wala' (patronage) and 'emancipation'. Recognize that wala is a legal relationship that creates inheritance rights between the freed slave and the master's family.
2
State the ruling
The rule is that the wala of a freed person goes to the 'asabah of the master. When a freed person dies without heirs, his inheritance (the wala) passes to the master's closest agnatic relatives.
3
Apply to a scenario
Consider a master who frees a slave and then dies. Later, the freed slave dies without any inheritors. According to this rule, the wala (and the property inherited through it) goes to the master's asabah, not to the state or to the freed slave's own relatives.

Wala' of Children from a Non-Arab Father and Arab Freedwoman (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wala' assignment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad versus Abu Yusuf on the wala' of children born to a non-Arab father and an Arab freedwoman, and explain the proof for each position.

Core Idea
Wala' belongs to the emancipator, not lineage; but a dispute arises when a non-Arab father marries a freed Arab woman.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the wala' of children when a non-Arab man marries an Arab freedwoman. The central question is whether the children's wala' follows the mother's patron (emancipator) or the father's lineage. We will explore the proofs from hadith and athar that each side relies upon.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the wala' of the children belongs to the mother's patrons (those who freed her).
This is based on the general rule that wala' is for the emancipator.
2
Identify the opposing position
Note that Abu Yusuf within the Hanafi school argues that the wala' belongs to the father's side—either to the father himself if alive, or to his patrons—because lineage is traced through the father.
Abu Yusuf considers wala' a branch of lineage.
3
Follow Abu Yusuf's proof
Examine the athar from Ali, Umar, and the hadith from Ibn Umar which state that wala' is like a bond of lineage, implying it follows the paternal line.
These reports are cited in the text to support the analogy to lineage.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand that the primary hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taq' (wala' is for the emancipator) takes precedence. The use of 'innama' (li l-hasr) restricts wala' to the emancipator, overriding the analogy to lineage.
This is a classic instance of a textual rule (nass) taking priority over reasoning (ra'y).
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf is: if the children die without blood heirs, who inherits their wala'? According to the mufta bihi view, it is the mother's patron; according to Abu Yusuf, it is the father's family.
This directly affects inheritance rights in the absence of blood relatives.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the wala' (patronage) of children born to a non-Arab father and an Arab freedwoman. The passage opens by stating the two positions: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that the wala' of such children belongs to the mother's patrons (those who freed her), because wala' is established by emancipation (al-wala' liman a'taq). Imam Abu Yusuf, however, argues that the wala' belongs to the father's lineage, because lineage (nasab) is traced through the father, and wala' is a branch of lineage. The text then provides evidence for Abu Yusuf's view through athar (reports from Companions): 'Ali ibn Abi Talib said that wala' is a branch of lineage, and whoever receives inheritance receives wala'; 'Umar ibn al-Khattab said that when a father is freed, he draws wala' to his own patrons; and a hadith from Ibn 'Umar stating that wala' is like a bond of lineage, cannot be sold or gifted. The Hanafi counter-argument, implicit in the passage, is based on the hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taq' (wala' belongs to the emancipator), which gives priority to the mother's patron since he is the one who freed the mother. The text does not explicitly resolve the dispute, but the dominant Hanafi position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, which is relied upon in fatwa. Thus, the student learns that while lineage is important, the source of freedom (emancipation) takes precedence in determining wala'.

عجم (‘ajam): Non-Arabs, especially Persians. Used here to describe the father's ethnic background.

Wala' of Emancipation Follows 'Asabah (Agnatic Heirs)
States the principle with evidence from Quran and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of entitlement in wala' al-'itaqah and apply the hadith 'alhiq al-fara'id bi-ahliha' to determine who inherits wala'.

Core Idea
Wala' of emancipation follows the same order as inheritance: fixed sharers, then agnates, then patron.

This lesson clarifies a common misconception: the patron of a freed slave does not automatically inherit everything. The Hanafi school holds that the freed person's blood relatives, especially agnatic heirs ('asabah), take precedence over the patron. We examine the Quranic verse and hadith that establish this order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the ruling being stated? The passage rules that wala' (inheritance from a freed slave) is treated as 'asabah, meaning it goes first to fixed-share heirs (ashab al-furud), then to agnates, and finally to the patron.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Two proofs are cited: Quran 4:11 which specifies fixed shares, and the hadith 'alhiq al-fara'id bi-ahliha' which commands giving fixed shares first and the residue to the nearest male relative.
The Quran verse establishes the principle of fixed shares; the hadith clearly orders the priority.
3
Trace wajh al-dalalah (how the evidence proves the ruling)
The Quran shows that some heirs receive prescribed fractions. The hadith explicitly states that after these fixed shares are given, the remainder goes to the nearest male relative. This is analogized to wala', where the patron is only the nearest male relative in the absence of blood relatives.
The analogy is based on the common illa of 'asabah.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
The passage indicates this is a unanimous position (muttafaq alayh) across the schools; no intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is mentioned.
This gives the ruling greater authority.
5
Apply to a scenario
If a freed man dies leaving a daughter and his former master, the daughter takes her fixed share (1/2 if only one daughter) and the master takes the residue as 'asabah. If the freed man also had a brother, the brother would take the residue instead of the master.
Practice using the order: fixed sharers -> blood agnates -> patron.

The text presents a clear rule with an underlying rationale and a prophetic tradition (athar) as evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its ruling
The act is the transfer of wala' by the client to another patron. The ruling is that it is permissible before diyah is paid and impermissible after.
2
Locate the condition stated in the matn
The condition is 'as long as his diyah has not been paid'. This is the decisive factor determining permissibility.
3
Understand the rationale behind the condition
The commentary explains that payment of diyah establishes a right for the patron; allowing transfer after payment would cause him loss as he cannot recover the diyah from the new patron.
4
Examine the athar as evidence
The athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i states: 'He has the right to transfer his wala' wherever he wishes as long as he (the patron) has not paid his diyah.' This is the proof for the ruling.
The athar is cited in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some schools might allow transfer after payment if the client reimburses the patron, but the Hanafi school forbids it absolutely to protect the patron's right.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the transfer of wala' by a mawla al-muwalat. The matn states that the client can transfer his wala' to another person as long as the patron has not paid his diyah. Once the patron has paid diyah, the client may not transfer. The commentary provides the rationale: the patron who paid diyah has a right over the client, and allowing transfer would cause him loss because he cannot recover his payment from the new patron. The athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i confirms that the client can transfer his wala' wherever he wishes as long as diyah has not been paid. This is the Hanafi position: the right to transfer is conditional upon the patron not having fulfilled his obligation of paying diyah.

Key Terms
جرمانہ / عقل (jurmanah / 'aql): Diyah (blood money) that the mawla al-muwalat is obliged to pay on behalf of his client if the client commits a homicide or incurs liability.

The Prohibition of Transferring Wala' al-Itaqa
Ruling with proof on the prohibition of transferring wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a freed slave transferring his wala' and explain the two hadith proofs that establish it.

Core Idea
Wala' of emancipation is a fixed right of the manumitter — the freed slave cannot transfer it.

This lesson addresses a fundamental ruling in Hanafi inheritance and patronage law: the freed slave (mawla al-itaqa) cannot make another person his patron. The bond created by emancipation is akin to lineage — immutable and exclusive. We will examine the two key hadith proofs that establish this prohibition and understand why any attempt to transfer wala' is invalid and sinful.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a freed slave attempting to make someone other than his manumitter his patron. The ruling is that it is not permissible (haram) and invalid.
2
Locate the primary hadith proof
The first hadith: 'إنما الولاء لمن اعتق' — 'Indeed, wala' is for the one who emancipates.' The word 'إنما' indicates exclusivity.
Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 6752
3
Analyse the second hadith (warning)
The second hadith (from 'Ali) curses anyone who enters into wala' with a people without the permission of his patrons. This shows the severity of transferring wala'.
Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 6755
4
Understand the legal analogy
The commentary compares the emancipation bond to lineage (nasab) — both are fixed and cannot be changed by the individual's choice.
5
Apply the ruling
Conclude that any attempt by a freed slave to transfer wala' is invalid and sinful; the manumitter remains the sole patron.
This is the mufta bihi position with no intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof that a freed slave (mawla al-itaqa) cannot transfer his wala' to another person; it is exclusively fixed to his manumitter. The commentary explains that emancipation creates a bond akin to lineage, hence it is inseparable. Two proofs are cited: first, the hadith 'Wala' is for the one who emancipates' establishes that the manumitter alone is entitled to the wala'. Second, a hadith of 'Ali warns of divine curse for those who transfer wala' without permission. The commentary concludes that the freed slave cannot validly make another person his patron. This is the Hanafi position.

Key Terms
مولی عتاقة (mawla al-itaqa): A freed slave who is in a patronage relationship with his manumitter; the wala' belongs to the manumitter and cannot be transferred.
ولاء العتاقة (wala' al-itaqa): Patronage by emancipation; the legal relationship that gives the manumitter the right to inherit from the freed slave and obligation to pay diyah.
