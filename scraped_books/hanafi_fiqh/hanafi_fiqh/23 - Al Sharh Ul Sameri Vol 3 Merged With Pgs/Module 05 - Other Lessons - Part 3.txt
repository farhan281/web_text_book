Option for a Female Mukataba Who Becomes Umm Walad
Ruling on choice for female mukataba who becomes umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to a female mukataba who bears her master's child and explain the legal effect of each choice.

Core Idea
A female mukataba who bears the master's child may choose to remain a mukataba or become an umm walad.

This passage presents a unique scenario where a slave woman who is both a mukataba (under a contract of manumission) and an umm walad (mother of her master's child) has a choice between two paths to freedom. The Hanafi school recognizes her dual status and grants her the option to either continue the kitabah for earlier freedom or abandon it for automatic freedom upon the master's death. Understanding this ruling clarifies the intersection of kitabah and umm walad status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mukataba's initial status
She is a female slave who has a kitabah contract: she pays installments to purchase her freedom.
Kitabah is a valid contract in Hanafi law.
2
Recognize the triggering event
She gives birth to a child from her master, which makes her an umm walad, but her kitabah remains in effect unless she chooses otherwise.
The birth does not automatically cancel the kitabah.
3
State the two options
Option 1: Continue the kitabah, pay the remaining amount, and become free during the master's lifetime. Option 2: Declare herself incapable of paying, thus terminating the kitabah, and become a pure umm walad, freeing upon the master's death.
The choice is hers (bil-khiyar).
4
Explain the legal effect of each choice
Option 1 leads to immediate freedom after full payment; Option 2 leads to freedom only after the master's death, but with no further obligation.
5
Understand the rationale
She has dual capacity (mukataba and umm walad), so the law grants her the option to choose the path that benefits her.
This is a specific Hanafi ruling.

This lesson addresses what happens if the master makes his mudabbara also a mukataba, and then dies without any other property. She may choose whichever option is easier for her. This shows the school's consideration of the slave's interest.

Wala' of a Second Mukatab When He Is Freed Before the First
Ruling on wala' in a chain of two mukatabs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that wala' attaches to the free person to determine the wala' of a second mukatab when the first is still enslaved.

Core Idea
Wala' belongs to the free person, not the slave — timing decides the master.

You encountered a chain of two mukatabs: the first mukatab makes his own slave a mukatab. The question is: if the second pays his kitaba and becomes free before the first does, who receives his wala'? The first mukatab? The original master? The answer hinges on a core principle: wala' only attaches to a free person. Since at the moment of the second's liberation the first is still a slave, the wala' cannot go to him — it goes to the original free master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chain of persons
List all parties: the original master, the first mukatab (who is both a slave and a master of the second), and the second mukatab.
The first mukatab is a master to the second but a slave to the original master.
2
Determine status at the moment of liberation
At the exact moment the second pays his kitaba and becomes free, the first mukatab is still a slave (he has not yet become free). The original master is free.
Timing is critical: wala' is determined at the instant of the freed slave's liberation.
3
Apply the principle: wala' is for the free person
The principle 'wala' is for the free person, not for the slave' means that a slave cannot hold wala'. Therefore, the first mukatab cannot receive wala' while he is a slave.
The original master is the only free person in the chain at that moment.
4
Conclude the wala' attribution
Since the wala' cannot go to the first mukatab, it must go to the original master, who is free and is the ultimate manumitter of the second (through his slave's action).
This ruling is consistent with the general Hanafi rule that wala' follows the original manumitter.

Wala of Child Born Within Six Months of Mother's Manumission
Ruling on wala for birth within six months

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wala for a child born within or after six months of the mother's manumission and distinguish it from the Maliki position.

Core Idea
If a child is born within six months of mother's manumission, wala stays with her master; if later, it may transfer to the father's master.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in the fiqh of wala (clientage). When a formerly enslaved woman gives birth after being freed, the timing of the birth determines which master gains clientage over the child. The Hanafi school uses a six-month presumption to establish the likely time of conception, a principle that also sets the minimum gestation period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mother's status and birth timeline
The mother is a freed slave-girl (mawlā). The ruling applies when she gives birth after manumission, specifically at less than six months.
The six-month period starts from the moment of emancipation.
2
State the ruling
If birth occurs within six months, the wala (clientage) of the child belongs to the mother's master (mawlā al-umm).
This is the default Hanafi position.
3
Understand the legal presumption
Six months is the minimum viable pregnancy in Hanafi law. Birth within this period indicates conception occurred while the mother was still a slave, so the child's wala follows her master.
The presumption is based on medical and legal custom (ʿurf).
4
Consider the exception
If birth occurs after six months, conception likely happened after freedom. In that case, if the father later becomes free, the wala may transfer to the father's master (as detailed in the next ruling).
This exception shows wala is not fixed solely by mother's status.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Maliki scholars may treat the child as free if the mother was free at conception, regardless of birth timing, removing wala entirely.
This difference stems from varying weight given to fixed time presumptions.

Transfer of Wala Upon Father's Emancipation
Explains that when the father is freed, he pulls the child's wala from mother's mawla to father's mawla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on transfer of wala upon father's emancipation and explain the proof from Umar's athar.

Core Idea
The father's freedom pulls wala: a unique Hanafi rule that transfers patronage from the mother's master to the father's.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi law of wala (patronage) where a child born to slave parents is initially freed with the mother's emancipation, placing wala with the mother's master. Upon the father's subsequent freedom, the wala transfers to the father's master. We will explore the legal reasoning and the proof from the Companion Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenario
Understand the family situation: a slave man married to a slave woman gives birth to a child. The mother is freed first, thereby freeing the child as well.
The child follows the mother's status upon her emancipation.
2
State the initial ruling on wala
Upon the mother's freedom, the wala (patronage) of the child goes to the mother's master, because the child's freedom is through her master's manumission.
This is the default rule: the one who frees the slave acquires wala.
3
Recognize the change upon father's freedom
When the father is later freed, he 'pulls' the wala from the mother's master to his own master. The child's wala now belongs to the father's master.
This transfer is unique to Hanafi law; other schools do not allow wala to move.
4
Locate the proof
The ruling is based on a report from Umar ibn al-Khattab, where he explicitly states this process. The athar is cited in the source.
Umar's statement is considered a qawl sahabi, carrying weight in Hanafi usul.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that wala is fixed and cannot transfer; it remains with the mother's master permanently.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf in the issue.

This is supported by the athar (report) of Umar ibn al-Khattab, which explicitly states this process. The Hanafi school follows this principle, treating wala as transferable through the father's freedom.

Wala of Children in Mixed Patronage: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute over wala of children in mixed-patronage marriages

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi position on wala of children in cross-patronage marriages and explain the basis of Abu Yusuf's disagreement.

Core Idea
The children's wala goes to the mother's masters because emancipation, not lineage, determines wala.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over the wala (patronage) of children born to a non-Arab (ajami) father and a woman who was freed by Arab masters. The key question: does the children's wala follow the mother's emancipators or the father's lineage? Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the foundational principle 'al-wala liman a'taqa' (wala belongs to the one who frees) and recognize when lineage claims may override it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A non-Arab man marries a woman who was a female slave freed by Arab masters. They have children. Determine to whom the wala of these children belongs.
The father is ajami (non-Arab), the mother is mu'taqatu al-'arab (freed by Arabs).
2
State the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani rule: the children's wala belongs to the mother's masters (the Arab emancipators).
Dalil: 'al-wala liman a'taqa' – the mother's masters directly freed her, so they have wala over her and her children.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds: the children's wala belongs to their father's masters (or to the father's lineage because the father is free).
His reasoning: lineage is through fathers; wala is like lineage, so it should follow the father.
4
Compare the two proofs
Majority rely on the explicit hadith text; Abu Yusuf uses a qiyas (analogy) comparing wala to lineage.
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad prioritize the direct text over analogy.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and why
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, because it is based on the clear text of the hadith.
This is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah and is the operative fatwa.

Women's Entitlement to Wala': Restricted Inheritance
Ruling on women's wala' inheritance with Companion proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on women's inheritance of wala' and apply it to identify which manumission chains entitle a woman to wala'.

Core Idea
Women inherit wala' only from their own manumitted slaves, not from relatives' freed slaves.

This passage from Kitab al-Wala' clarifies a critical exception in inheritance law: women's right to wala' is severely restricted. Unlike men, who inherit wala' from a wide range of agnatic relatives, women only acquire wala' through their own manumission acts or those of their own freed slaves. This rule is grounded in the practice of the Companions and shapes Hanafi inheritance practice.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify the baseline: women have no inheritance of wala' except in specified cases.
The default is exclusion; exceptions are listed.
2
List the Exceptions
Enumerate the specific cases: direct manumission, chain manumission, mukatib, mudabbar, and jarr al-wala'.
Each exception is tied to the woman's own action or her freed slave's action.
3
Understand the Rationale
Wala' follows 'asabah (male agnates); women are not part of that lineage, so their wala' is limited to their own manumissions.
4
Examine the Proof
Analyze the athar from Ali, Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, and Zaid ibn Thabit (Qawl Sahabi) that restricts women's wala' inheritance.
The Companions consistently gave wala' to the eldest agnate and excluded women except for their own manumissions.
5
Apply to a Scenario
A woman manumits a slave; that slave later manumits another. Does she inherit wala' from the second? Yes, because it is a chain from her manumission.
The ruling covers both direct and indirect manumission chains.

The Ruling and Conditions of Wala al-Muwalat
Ruling on wala al-muwalat with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wala al-muwalat and identify the conditions under which the mawla inherits.

Core Idea
Wala al-muwalat is valid but inheritance is conditional on absence of blood heirs.

This passage from the Book of Wala addresses a specific type of patronage: wala al-muwalat (patronage by contract). The Hanafi school affirms its validity, yet places important restrictions on inheritance. Understanding these conditions is crucial for correctly applying this ruling in estate cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states that wala al-muwalat is sahih (valid). Note the conditions: the client embraces Islam at the patron's hand, and they contract mutual inheritance and blood money liability.
The ruling is explicitly stated in the matn: 'فالولاء صحيح'.
2
Distinguish between wala al-muwalat and wala al-itaq
The commentary clarifies that this is not emancipation-based patronage (wala al-itaq) but a contractual clientage.
Wala al-itaq arises from freeing a slave; wala al-muwalat arises from a contract after embracing Islam.
3
Examine the inheritance hierarchy
The mawla inherits only when there is no blood relative, no former master from emancipation, and no clan (asabah). If any such heir exists, they take priority.
This order is derived from Quranic verses and hadith.
4
Understand the blood money liability
The mawla is responsible for paying 'aql (diyah) if the client commits a crime. This is part of the mutual agreement.
The liability is reciprocal; both parties agree to bear blood money for each other.

The Inter-Madhhab Dispute: Is Wala al-Muwalat Abrogated?
Inter-madhhab dispute on abrogation of wala al-muwalat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi and Shafi'i arguments regarding the abrogation of wala al-muwalat and state the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds wala al-muwalat is not abrogated; the Shafi'i school holds it is.

This passage presents a classical inter-madhhab dispute on the validity of wala al-muwalat. The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ on whether Quranic verses on blood relatives abrogate the earlier verses on patronage. Understanding the proofs and the practical consequence is essential for a deeper grasp of usul al-fiqh and inheritance law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that wala al-muwalat is valid and not abrogated. The mawla inherits only when no blood heir exists.
This is based on verse 4:33 and supporting hadith.
2
State the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that verse 8:75 ('وأولوا الأرحام بعضهم أولى ببعض') abrogates the earlier verse 4:33, so wala al-muwalat is nullified.
They also cite an athar from Hasan al-Basri that the inheritance goes to Muslims.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the earlier verse is not abrogated but merely qualified; the later verse applies to blood relatives, not to mawali. The hadith from Tamim al-Dari and Abu Umama support the continued validity of wala al-muwalat.
The Hadith: 'من أسلم على يدي رجل فله ولاؤه' and 'هو أولى الناس بمحياه ومماته'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Hanafi, if a convert dies with no blood relatives, his patron inherits. Under Shafi'i, the estate goes to the bayt al-mal (Muslim treasury).
This affects actual inheritance cases.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi view is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school, supported by strong hadith evidence and the principle that abrogation is not lightly assumed.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and later Hanafi masters prefer the Hanafi position.

Transfer of wala is free until 'aql is paid; payment binds the mawla's right, blocking further transfer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the two types of mistaken homicide (khata' fi al-qasd and khata' fi al-fi'l) according to the Hanafi school and explain why both are classified as qatl al-khata'.

Core Idea
Mistaken homicide includes both error in intention and error in action—both carry the same legal consequences.

This lesson focuses on two distinct forms of mistaken homicide recognised in Hanafi fiqh. Although the killer did not intend to take a human life, the law holds them accountable through diyah and kaffarah. Understanding these categories helps clarify when accidental killing incurs full liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common element
Both types involve a permissible aim (e.g., hunting or target practice) that results in a human death. The killer had no intention to kill a human.
This distinguishes qatl al-khata' from qatl al-amd (intentional homicide).
2
Distinguish the mistake
In khata' fi al-qasd, the mistake is in the object: the killer shoots a person thinking they are permissible game. In khata' fi al-fi'l, the mistake is in the action: the killer aims at a permissible target but misses and hits a human.
The key difference is whether the error lies in perception (qasd) or execution (fi'l).
3
Note the shared ruling
Both types fall under qatl al-khata', carrying the same obligations: diyah (blood money) upon the aaqilah and kaffarah (expiation) upon the killer, but no sin.
The Hanafi school treats both identically, unlike some other schools that may differentiate.

Commentary
This passage presents two types of homicide that are below intentional murder but above pure accident. The first is quasi-intentional (shibh al-amd), exemplified by a sleeping person who rolls over onto someone and kills them. Though the sleeper had no intent, the law treats this as equivalent to homicide by error (khata) because the sleeper could have taken precautions. Therefore, both expiation (kaffarah) and the heavier blood money (diyah) for error are obligatory. The second type is homicide by causation (qatl bi-sabab), such as digging a well or placing a stone in another person's property, leading to death. Unlike quasi-intentional, this does not require kaffarah because the actor did not directly cause the death but merely created a dangerous condition. However, the diyah is still due, paid by the aqila (the male paternal relatives) of the wrongdoer. The passage cites the well-known hadith on the diyah of error from Ibn Mas'ud, specifying the exact types of camels. For the causation type, it references the hadith 'al-ba'ru jubar' (the well is free) from Abu Huraira to highlight that if the well is dug on one's own property, no liability arises. The athar from Ibrahim supports that liability only applies when the act is done in another's property. This distinguishes between lawful and unlawful causation.

Key Terms
قتل بسبب (qatl bi-sabab): Homicide where the killer does not directly cause death but creates a situation likely to cause death, such as digging a well in another's property.
دية (diyah): Blood money payable to the heirs of the victim in cases of homicide that do not require qisas.

Indirect causation requires diyah but not kaffarah.

In this lesson, we examine a specific category of homicide: death caused indirectly (qatl bi-sabab). The Hanafi school distinguishes this from direct killing, including intentional homicide by error (khata). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rulings of blood money and expiation.

Liability for Digging a Well on Non-Owned Property
Ruling on liability for death caused by digging a well on another's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a person dies by falling into a well dug on another's property, and explain the proof from the athar of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i.

Core Idea
Digging a well on another's land makes the digger liable for diyah via 'aqilah, but no kaffarah.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Saghir addresses a specific scenario: a well dug on land that is not owned by the digger. The ruling distinguishes between property owned by the digger and property belonging to another. The key legal consequence is that the digger's 'aqilah (paternal relatives) must pay blood money (diyah), but no expiation (kaffarah) is required, because the death is considered a lesser degree of causation (sabab) rather than direct accidental killing (qatl khata'). The proof is an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that explicitly states liability for anyone who digs or builds outside their own property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Digging a well on non-owned property. What is the ruling? Liability for diyah on the 'aqilah, no kaffarah.
The passage also contrasts with digging on one's own property, where no liability exists.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: 'Whoever digs in other than his building or builds in other than his sky, then he is liable.' This is a reliable transmission (zanni al-thubut) used as a proof.
Also referenced: the hadith 'al-bi'ru jubbār' (the well is a 'pitfall') which supports no liability on one's own property.

The word 'daman' (liability) in the athar directly indicates that the digger must compensate. The phrase 'fi ghayr binā'ihi' explicitly restricts liability to non-owned property, implying no liability on owned property.
This is a straightforward text (zahir) establishing a rule.

Why no kaffarah? Because the death is not qatl khata' (accidental killing) but a lower degree—causation (sabab). The diyah is transferred to the 'aqilah by default, as in other cases of indirect killing.
The 'aqilah are the male agnatic relatives who traditionally share blood money payments.

While the Hanafi school holds this position, other schools may differ on liability for indirect causes. For example, the Shafi'i school might require direct liability on the digger himself, but this passage does not elaborate.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between injuries from the animal's front and those from its back, based on the rider's ability to control. You will learn the detailed ruling, its proofs from hadith and athar, and the underlying principle that liability follows negligence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the two parts: the rider is liable for trampling, foreleg strike, and bite; not liable for hind leg kick or tail strike.
2
Distinguish front vs back
Explain the reason: the rider controls the front via reins and can see ahead, so negligence is assumed if injury occurs. Hind leg and tail are not controllable.
3

4
Apply the principle of negligence
Understand the rule: where protection was possible and negligence occurred, liability arises; where impossible, no liability.
This is the usul of the ruling.
5

Consider athar from Hammad: if a person provokes the animal, liability applies even for hind kick.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Book of Diyat (blood-money) concerning the liability of a rider for harm caused by his mount. The ruling distinguishes between injuries from the animal's front (trampling, hitting with foreleg, biting) and those from its back (kicking with hind leg, tail). The rider is liable for the former because he can control the animal's front via the reins and can see what is ahead; if harm occurs, it is due to his negligence. He is not liable for the latter because he cannot control or anticipate kicks or tail strikes. The supporting proofs include hadith stating that injury by an animal is generally 'jubar' (no liability), but other narrations specify that this applies only to the hind leg, not the foreleg or mouth. The principle is that liability follows negligence: where prevention is possible, negligence entails liability; where prevention is impossible, there is no liability. The passage also cites athar from Ibrahim, Ibn Sirin, and Hammad reinforcing the distinction. The glossary at the end defines key Arabic terms for types of animal attacks.

No Liability for Animal Droppings and Urine on Road
Ruling on non-liability for animal excrement on roads

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for harm caused by animal excrement on public roads and explain the legal basis (no fault due to difficulty of control).

Core Idea
No liability for slipping on animal dung or urine because control is impossible.

A common scenario: an animal defecates or urinates on a public road, and a pedestrian slips and dies. Is the owner liable? Hanafi fiqh says no, because excreting is a natural function beyond human control. This lesson examines the ruling and its rationale, grounded in the principle that liability requires fault.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Harm
Note the act: an animal defecates or urinates on a public road. The consequence: a human slips and dies.
The animal is not under direct control at the moment.
2
State the Ruling
The Hanafi ruling: the owner (or rider) is not liable for compensation (damān). That is, no blood money or financial penalty is owed.
This is a declaratory ruling (hukm wadʿī) of non-liability.
3
Locate the Legal Basis (ʿIllah)
The reason given: controlling the animal's urine and dung is difficult (ḥaraja, mushkil). The owner cannot prevent it, so there is no fault (tafrīt) on his part.
Liability in Hanafi tort law generally requires fault (taʿaddī or tafrīt).
4
Apply the Principle
The general principle: no compensation for harm that cannot be prevented through ordinary means. This is consistent with the rule 'al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā' and the concept of istiḥsān in removing hardship.
Compare with the driver's liability for the animal's deliberate strikes (ruling 45–46).
5
Contrast with Other Animal Actions
Rulings 45 and 46 show that if the animal intentionally strikes with its hand or mouth, the driver/leader is liable. This distinction highlights that unpreventable natural acts (excretion) are exempt, while preventable aggressive acts trigger liability.
The animal's dung and urine are not considered 'acts' of the animal for liability purposes.

Liability of the Driver from Behind (Sa'iq)
Ruling with proof for liability of sa'iq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the driver from behind (sa'iq) is liable for injuries caused by the animal's hand or leg, explain the legal basis (control and negligence), and apply the ruling to analogous scenarios in transportation.

Core Idea
The driver from behind is liable because he has control and visibility; negligence makes him responsible.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Saghir addresses a specific case of liability: when an animal injures someone, who must pay compensation? The focus is on the person driving the animal from behind (sa'iq). The ruling establishes that the sa'iq is liable for injuries caused by the animal's front or hind limbs. The reasoning ties liability to control and negligence, and the ruling is supported by a transmitted statement (athar) from al-Hakam. Understanding this principle helps clarify broader rules of responsibility for those in charge of animals or vehicles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
Read the first sentence: the sa'iq (driver from behind) is liable for what the animal strikes with its hand (front leg) or foot (hind leg). Note that the ruling covers all types of limbs, not just one.
2
Understand the legal basis
The commentary explains that the driver is present, watching the animal's movement, and has control—yet harm occurred due to negligence. Thus, liability is based on the combination of control and failure to prevent harm.
This is a form of indirect causation (tasabbub) because the driver's negligence caused the injury, even though the animal directly caused it.
3
Examine the supporting evidence
The passage cites an athar from al-Hakam, recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, which states that the driver from behind, the leader from front (qa'id), and the rider all pay compensation for any strike or kick by the animal. This athar broadens the principle to other persons in control.
The athar serves as a precedent (dalil) establishing the rule in the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the conditions for liability
The ruling applies when: (1) the person is actually driving from behind (i.e., has control), (2) the injury is caused by the animal's limb (hand or foot or kick), and (3) the driver could have prevented it (negligence is implied by the fact of injury).
If the animal acts unpredictably despite due care, liability might be excused—but the default is liability because the driver is deemed to be in control.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Analogous cases: a driver of a car (sa'iq) is liable for accidents caused by the car's movement. The same principle of control and negligence applies. However, modern traffic laws also consider other factors (road conditions, etc.), but the fiqhi foundation remains the same.
This is an expansion (qiyas) based on the 'illa (effective cause) of control over a moving object.

Proof of Qasamah from the Hadith of Abdullah ibn Sahl
Cites hadith evidence for the ruling on qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi evidence for qasamah from the hadith of Abdullah ibn Sahl and explain why the oath is taken by the accused party.

Core Idea
The Prophet's offer of qasamah to the Jews shows the oath is on the accused, not the claimants.

Qasamah is a unique legal institution in Islamic criminal law for cases of murder where the killer is unknown. The Hanafi school derives its ruling primarily from the incident of Abdullah ibn Sahl, reported in both Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim. Understanding this hadith and its interpretation is key to grasping the Hanafi position that the oath is an obligation on the accused locality, not the claimants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
The hadith presents a scenario: a Muslim is killed among Jews, no witnesses, no known killer. What is the procedure to determine liability?
This is the exact situation qasamah addresses.
2
Analyze the Prophet's response
The Prophet offers two options: either the relatives swear fifty oaths to claim blood money, or the Jews swear fifty oaths to deny liability. The relatives decline both. The Prophet then pays diyah from the public treasury.
The key is that the Prophet first offered the oath to the claimants (relatives), but when they declined, he offered it to the accused (Jews).
3
Determine the Hanafi inference
Hanafi scholars focus on the fact that the Prophet offered the oath to the accused (Jews) as a means of clearing themselves. This indicates that in normal circumstances (without a capable claimant), the oath is on the accused party. The word 'qasamah' is derived from this procedure.
The Shafi'i school interprets the same hadith differently, arguing that the oath was originally for the claimants.
4

The Hanafi ruling: qasamah is wajib upon the fifty men of the locality where the body is found. They must swear that they did not kill the victim and do not know the killer. Upon their oath, the claim is dismissed, but diyah is paid from the state treasury (or the locality's wealth).
This ruling requires the murder to be found in a specific locality and the killer unknown.

Commentary
This passage presents the exact wording of the oath in qasamah according to the Hanafi school: the accused locals swear, 'By Allah, we did not kill him and we do not know his killer.' It then cites a hadith from Abu Dawud where the Jews swore this exact oath. The passage then introduces the Shafi'i view: if there is circumstantial evidence against the locality, the victim's guardian (wali) takes fifty oaths accusing the locals, after which diyah is imposed. The passage quotes the same hadith of Abdullah ibn Sahl that the Hanafis use, but the Shafi'is interpret it differently: they focus on the Prophet's offer to the relatives to swear. The Hanafi response is that the hadith shows the Prophet first asked the locals (the Jews) to swear, and only when they refused did he ask the relatives. The passage clarifies that the Hanafi position is that the oath is taken by the accused locality, not the claimants. The commentary explains that the diyah is decided after the oath.

Key Terms
ولى (walī): The legal guardian or heir of the victim who has the right to pursue blood money or retaliation.
قسامة (qasāmah): A collective oath taken in homicide cases when the killer is unknown, used to determine liability for blood money.
دية (diyah): Blood money or monetary compensation paid to the victim's family in cases of homicide or injury.

Imposing Diya on the Quarter in Qasama
Ruling with proof for diya on the quarter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diya in qasama, identify the hadith proof, and explain why the wali is not sworn.

Core Idea
Diya falls on the quarter, not on the wali — the quarter's oath clears them, the wali's oath does not.

The chapter of qasama deals with liability when a body is found in a populated area. This passage states that the people of the quarter must pay diya and that the victim's guardian is not sworn. The key proof is the hadith of Ibn Abbas, where the Prophet took fifty oaths from the accused and imposed diya. Let's break down the ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Main Ruling
Read the first sentence: 'He pronounces diya upon the people of the quarter, and the guardian is not sworn'. This is the core Hanafi position.
The ruling covers two points: diya on the quarter, and no oath on the wali.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The text quotes the hadith of Ibn Abbas. Identify it as a hadith ahad (single narration) from Sunan al-Bayhaqi.
The hadith describes the Prophet's action with the Jews.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah (How the Dalil Proves)
The Prophet took oaths from the people of the quarter (50 men) and imposed diya on them. He did not take oaths from the victim's family. Therefore, the wali is not sworn and diya is on the quarter.
The absence of swearing the wali is as significant as the positive action of swearing the quarter.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf (None)
The text mentions no difference among Hanafi imams on this ruling. All three (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) agree.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.
5
Analyse the Inter-Madhhab Dispute
The text contrasts with Imam al-Shafi'i, who requires oaths from the victim's heirs. The Hanafi response is that the explicit hadith overrides general qiyas (analogy).
The practical difference: who bears the burden of swearing? Quarter vs. heirs.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the chapter of Qasama. It states that when a body is found in a quarter, the judge imposes diya on the people of that quarter. The wali (heir) of the victim is not required to swear an oath, nor is a crime adjudged against him even if he does swear. The proof is the hadith of Ibn Abbas where the Prophet made the Jews swear and imposed diya. The commentary explains the rationale: the people of the quarter are responsible for the safety of their area; if diya were not imposed, they would neglect security. The text also distinguishes the Hanafi position from Imam al-Shafi'i: in the absence of clear signs, the Hanafis require oaths from the quarter's people, while al-Shafi'i requires oaths from the victim's heirs.

Key Terms
قسامة (qasama): A legal procedure where oaths are taken (repeated 50 times) to establish liability for a found dead body.
ولى (wali): The legal guardian or heir of the victim who has the right to seek blood money or retaliation.
دية (diya): Monetary compensation paid to the victim's heirs in cases of homicide or injury.
