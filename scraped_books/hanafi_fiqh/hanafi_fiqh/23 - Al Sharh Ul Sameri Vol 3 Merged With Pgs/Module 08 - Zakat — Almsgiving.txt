Invalid Slaves for Zihar Kaffarah: Blind and Double Amputee
Ruling with proof and qiyas on slave defects for kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using a blind or double amputee slave for zihar kaffarah and explain the basis from the Quranic interpretation of 'raqabah' and qiyas with udhiyah.

Core Idea
A severely defective slave is not a 'complete slave' (raqabah) and thus invalid for kaffarah.

Zihar expiation requires freeing a slave, but the Quranic term 'raqabah' is not any slave; the Hanafi school interprets it as a fully capable slave. This lesson explains why blindness and loss of both hands and feet render a slave insufficient for expiation, drawing an analogy to the rules of sacrifice (udhiyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states that freeing a blind slave or one missing both hands and feet does not fulfill zihar kaffarah. The ruling is that such an act is invalid (batil) for the purpose of expiation.
The Arabic phrase 'لا يجزئ' means 'does not suffice' — the expiation is not complete.
2
Locate the Quranic dalil
The ruling is based on the Quranic command 'tahriru raqabah' (freeing a slave) in Surah al-Mujadilah (58:3). The Hanafi school interprets 'raqabah' as a complete, sound slave capable of productive work.
The word 'raqabah' literally means 'neck' but idiomatically refers to the whole person. Soundness of limbs is implied.
3
Understand the interpretation of 'raqabah'
Hanafi usul holds that the unqualified command 'raqabah' requires a slave free from severe defects that destroy utility. Blindness and amputation of both hands and feet eliminate the slave's ability to perform most work, making the slave like an incomplete one.
Minor defects (e.g., lameness in one leg) do not invalidate, as they still leave the slave close to complete.
4
Apply the qiyas (analogy) to udhiyah
The author draws an analogy to sacrifice: just as a severely defective animal (blind, missing limbs) is not valid for udhiyah, a severely defective slave is not valid for kaffarah. The effective cause ('illah) is the absence of the required completeness.
This is a qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) because the link is the principle of full utility for the obligation.
5
Note the exception for minor defects
The passage adds that a slave with a minor defect (thoda bahut aib) is acceptable, just as a minor defect in an udhiyah animal is acceptable. This distinction is based on the extent of impairment.
The practical rule: only severe, utility-destroying defects invalidate; minor defects are overlooked.

Valid Slaves for Kaffarah: Deaf and Opposite-Side Amputees
Ruling on validity of deaf and one-hand/one-foot amputee slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of deaf and one-limb amputee slaves for kaffarah and identify the condition of opposite-side amputation.

Core Idea
Deafness or missing one hand and one foot from opposite sides does not destroy essential utility.

When expiation (kaffarah) requires freeing a slave, not every defect disqualifies the slave. The Hanafi school specifies that moderate defects—like deafness or amputation of one hand and one foot from opposite sides—still allow the slave to perform basic functions. This lesson explains the ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is freeing a slave for kaffarah. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) and valid (sahih). Note the hukm level: not obligatory but the kaffarah is fulfilled.
The ruling applies specifically to zihar kaffarah and similar expiations.
2
List the valid defects
Two types mentioned: (1) a deaf slave (aṣamm); (2) a slave with one hand and one foot amputated from opposite sides (min khilāf).
Deafness alone does not prevent work; amputation on opposite sides preserves mobility.
3
Understand 'min khilāf'
It means the missing hand and foot are on different sides of the body (e.g., right hand and left foot). This allows the slave to still balance and walk.
If amputations are on the same side, the slave is likely unable to walk, so invalid.
4
Analyze the proof
The reasoning is qiyas khafi: analogy to sacrificial animals (udhiyah), where minor defects are tolerated. The common illa is that the essential function (mobility, utility) remains.
The proof is not explicitly cited but implied by the analogy.
5
Note cross-madhhab status
The ruling is mukhtalaf fih: some schools may consider deafness a more serious defect and disallow it. Hanafis prioritize the ability to work.
Respectful scholarly difference; the mufta bihi is the Hanafi position.

Total insanity voids the slave's utility; occasional insanity preserves it for kaffarah.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on using an insane slave for kaffarah (expiation). The key distinction lies in the slave's capacity to work, which hinges on his rational understanding. A totally insane slave cannot benefit the owner, while one with lucid intervals retains partial utility and thus qualifies. This principle is derived through legal reasoning (qiyas) based on the purpose of manumission.

Slaves with Partial Freedom and Kaffarah: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute over hadith interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that freeing a mudabbar, umm walad, or mukatab who has paid part of his kitabah is invalid for kaffarah, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the two conflicting hadiths.

Core Idea
A slave with a share of freedom is not complete property; freeing him does not fulfill kaffarah.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute: can a slave who has a partial right to freedom be used to fulfill a kaffarah? The Hanafi school rules that mudabbar slaves, umm walad, and mukatab who have paid part of their kitabah are invalid because they are no longer complete property. The Shafi'i school disagrees, especially regarding the mukatab. We will explore the proofs from two hadiths and understand the practical consequence of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subjects
First, recognize the three categories of slaves ruled invalid: mudabbar (promised freedom upon master's death), umm walad (slave-girl who bore master's child), and mukatab who paid part of kitabah. The Hanafi position is that freeing them does not satisfy the kaffarah—the act is batil (invalid).
The common factor: each has a 'trace of freedom' (shā'iba al-ḥurriyya), making them less than complete property.
2
Understand the Hanafi dalil
The Hanafis cite the hadith of Umm Salama: the Prophet ordered hijab (veiling) from a mukatab who has the means to pay. This indicates he is partially free, not a full slave. The 'trace of freedom' disqualifies him from being a complete object for kaffarah.
The hadith shows a legal implication: partial freedom changes the ruling.
3
Identify the Shafi'i counter-dalil
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on the hadith of 'Amr bin Shu'ayb: 'The mukatab is a slave as long as one dirham of his kitabah remains.' This suggests that any unpaid balance keeps him as a full slave, so freeing him is valid.
Shafi'i interpretation: full ownership persists until the last payment.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and tarjih
The Hanafi response is that the hadith of Umm Salama takes precedence because it addresses partial payment and indicates a change in status. The other hadith is general and can be overridden by this specific evidence. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in this text.
Tarjih (preference) is based on the specific context of partial payment creating a share of freedom.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to Hanafis, a person who frees such a slave for zihar or other kaffarah must repeat with a complete slave. According to Shafi'is, the same act fulfills the kaffarah if the mukatab still owes any amount.
This affects how a mufti advises someone who has made a mistake.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling and an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the validity of freeing slaves who have partial freedom status—specifically the mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death), the umm walad (a slave-girl who has borne her master's child), and the mukatab (a slave who has contracted to buy his freedom) who has paid some of the kitabah. The Hanafi position is that none of these are valid for kaffarah because they are not complete property (milk kāmil); they have a share of freedom (shā'iba al-ḥurriyya). For the mukatab, even if only one dirham remains unpaid, he is still considered to have some freedom after paying part. The Hanafis cite the hadith of Umm Salama where the Prophet ordered hijab from a mukatab who has the means to pay, indicating that he is no longer fully a slave. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees: he argues that the mukatab is a slave as long as any amount of the kitabah remains unpaid, citing a different hadith. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf) is that in the Hanafi school, freeing such a slave does not fulfill the kaffarah, while in the Shafi'i school, it does, provided the kitabah has not been fully paid (i.e., he is still a slave in his view). The Hanafi position is relied upon in this commentary.

Invalid Slaves for Zihar Expiation: Defect and Partial Freedom
Ruling with proof on invalid expiatory slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which slaves are invalid for zihar expiation, explain the Hanafi proof using qiyas on defective animals, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument on the mukatab.

Core Idea
A hint of freedom renders a slave invalid for expiation; only a fully owned, defect-free slave can discharge the wajib.

This passage clarifies the Hanafi position on which slaves cannot be used for zihar expiation. Two categories are ruled invalid: (a) slaves with complete defect, like the insane person with no understanding, and (b) slaves with partial freedom, like the mudabbar, umm walad, and mukatab who has paid some of his kitaba. The proof is based on qiyas on the hadith of defective sacrificial animals and a hadith from Umm Salama indicating that a mukatab has acquired some freedom. The Shafi'i school disagrees on the mukatab, citing a different hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the text and list the slaves ruled invalid. Group them: those with complete defect (insane with no understanding) and those with partial freedom (mudabbar, umm walad, mukatab who paid some).

The hadith lists four defective animals invalid for sacrifice. By qiyas, the same defects in slaves make them invalid for expiation. The common illa is that the object of worship (sacrifice/expiation) must be complete and free from defect.
3
Analyse the mukatab dispute evidence
Hanafis cite the Umm Salama hadith: a mukatab with means must be treated as free for hijab, indicating partial freedom. Shafi'is cite the hadith 'the mukatab is a slave as long as a dirham remains.' Contrast the two proofs.
The Hanafi response: the hint of freedom (shā'ib al-ḥurriyya) overrides the remaining slavery for expiation.
4

Practically, Hanafis require a fully owned slave for expiation; Shafi'is allow a mukatab with remaining debt. This affects how expiation is performed in contemporary contexts where freeing slaves is not the norm.
Today, expiation is usually via fasting or feeding, but the principle of completeness remains.

Freeing Half of a Shared Slave for Expiation: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on expiation by freeing a half-share of a co-owned slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding freeing half of a shared slave for expiation and explain the underlying principle and evidence.

Core Idea
Freeing a portion of a co-owned slave for expiation: valid only if the freeder is wealthy, according to the Sahibayn; invalid altogether according to Abu Hanifa.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether freeing half of a jointly owned slave can fulfill an expiation obligation. The answer depends on the freeder's financial capacity, and the disagreement reveals different applications of a key hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is freeing half of a shared slave for expiation (kaffarah). The dispute is whether this fulfills the expiation or not.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that it is not valid at all (لم يجز), regardless of wealth. His reasoning likely emphasizes that expiation requires complete emancipation of a whole slave.
The text does not elaborate his specific proof here.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their distinction
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad distinguish based on wealth: if the freeder is wealthy (mowsir), it is valid and fulfills the expiation; if poor (mu'sir), it is not valid. They analogize to the general rule of freeing a share in a co-owned slave.
This distinction is key: wealth enables compensation to the partner, making full emancipation possible.
4
Understand the underlying principle (qa'idah)
The issue rests on two principles: (1) the rule about one partner freeing his share in a co-owned slave, and (2) the hadith of Abu Huraira. According to the first principle, if wealthy, the whole slave becomes free and the freeder pays the partner's share; if poor, only his share is freed and the slave must earn to buy out the other share.
This principle is applied by qiyas to expiation.
5
Recognize the evidence and its application
The evidence is the hadith: 'Whoever frees a portion or share in a slave, then his emancipation is upon him from his wealth if he has wealth, otherwise he is valued and the slave is made to earn...' This hadith is used by the Sahibayn to support their view. Abu Hanifa may restrict its application to non-expiation contexts.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), so differing interpretations are possible.

The commentator details conditions: the two months must be consecutive, and they must not include Ramadan (because that is a separate obligation and breaks the sequence), nor the days of Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of Tashriq (11th-13th of Dhul Hijjah) because fasting on those days is prohibited or disliked (makruh). The passage then discusses a subsidiary issue: if one frees part of a slave (e.g., half before intercourse and half after), according to Imam Abu Hanifa (as inferred from the hadith 'illa faqad utiqa minhu ma utiqa'), only the freed portion is freed, so the expiation is not complete. However, according to Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the entire slave becomes free because freeing part of a slave in a joint ownership triggers full freedom (based on the hadith of Abu Huraira). Therefore, they consider it sufficient. The text then cites an athar from Ata' stating that if Ramadan or the day of Nahr intervenes, the sequence is broken and one must start over.

Key Terms
ظهار (zihar): A form of divorce or separation by which a husband declares his wife to be like his mother, making marital relations prohibited until expiation is performed.
کفارة (kaffara): Expiation required for zihar: freeing a slave, fasting two consecutive months, or feeding sixty poor people.
متتابعین (mutatabi'ayn): Consecutive; the two months must be unbroken except for unavoidable breaks like menstruation or illness? (here the text specifies that if a permissible excuse occurs, the sequence may be considered broken and restart required—see lesson 3).
تجزی (tajzi): Partial emancipation or the concept of dividing the act of freeing; here used to argue whether freeing part is considered freeing the whole.

Quantity of Food in Dhihar Kaffarah: Hanafi Position vs. Minority View
Commentary on the amount of food per miskeen

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the quantity of food per miskeen in dhihar kaffarah and evaluate the counter-evidence from a hadith.

Core Idea
For dhihar kaffarah, one saa' per miskeen is wajib; a minority view based on a hadith argues for one mudd.

This passage builds on the matn ruling for dhihar kaffarah, specifying the amount of food each miskeen must receive. The commentator explains the Hanafi position of one saa' per miskeen, derived from total requirement of one wasq (60 saa') for 60 miskeen, and notes that half a saa' of wheat suffices as equivalent. He then presents a minority view citing a hadith that suggests one mudd per miskeen. Understanding this dispute deepens your grasp of how different evidences interact in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn ruling
The matn states: one gives one saa' of dates or barley or its value. The commentator deduces the total for 60 miskeen is one wasq (60 saa'), so each gets one saa'.
The matn is the starting point; the commentary expands it.
2
Understand the Hanafi calculation
The commentary explains that half a saa' of wheat can substitute for one saa' of dates because of value equivalence from the chapter on fitr.
This substitution is accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Examine the minority view
Some imams hold that one mudd suffices. Their proof is a hadith where the Prophet gave 15 saa' to 60 miskeen, implying 1 mudd per person (since 15/60 = 1/4 saa' = 1 mudd).
The hadith is from Abu Dawood and Tirmidhi.
4
Compare the two positions
The Hanafi position relies on the matn and general practice; the minority view relies on a specific hadith. The thamarah al-khilaf is the quantity required: 4 times more for Hanafi.
The commentator does not rebut the hadith, but the Hanafi position remains the mufta bihi.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The operative ruling (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is one saa' of dates or half saa' of wheat per miskeen.
This is the standard for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn that specifies the quantity of food for the kaffarah of dhihar. The original ruling states one gives one saa' of dates or barley or its value. The commentator explains that one wasq (sixty saa') is required total, so each miskeen gets one saa'. The Hanafi position remains that one saa' per miskeen is wajib, but the commentator does not rebut the hadith here.
