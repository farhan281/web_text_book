Mudabbar's Obligation to Pay Master's Debts
Ruling on mudabbar when master has debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mudabbar when the master has debts and identify the supporting evidence from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
A mudabbar is freed but must work to pay his full value to creditors, balancing tadbir rights with debt priority.

This passage addresses a key Hanafi ruling: what happens to a mudabbar when the master dies with unpaid debts? Unlike a simple bequest, tadbir creates a right to freedom that cannot be completely ignored. However, the Islamic principle that debts must be settled before any inheritance or bequest takes precedence. The Hanafi school resolves this tension by freeing the mudabbar while requiring him to work (sa'i) to pay his own value to the creditors. We will examine the proofs from the Sunnah and the reasoning behind this compromise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the competing claims
The mudabbar has a right to freedom via the tadbir contract, but the creditors have a right to their debt. The master has no other property to satisfy both.
This conflict triggers the need for a special ruling.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle of debt priority
Debts must be paid before any bequest or inheritance. Tadbir is treated as a bequest (wasiyya) because it takes effect after death. Thus, debts take precedence over the tadbir freedom.
This is based on the Quranic command to settle debts before distribution of estate (Surah al-Nisa 4:11-12).
3
Balance the rights
Since the tadbir has already created a 'taint of freedom' (shai'b al-hurriyya), it is not entirely nullified. The mudabbar is freed, but he must work (sa'i) to pay his full value to the creditors.
This compromise honors both the tadbir and the debt.
4
Examine the dalil
The hadith of Jabir (the mudabbar was sold due to debt) and the athar of Qatada (the mudabbar works for his price) are used as proofs. The hadith shows that debt overrides tadbir, but the Hanafi ruling modifies it to sa'i instead of sale.
The Hanafi interpretation emphasizes the 'taint of freedom' as the reason for not selling the mudabbar.
5
Formulate the rule
When a master dies with debts and no other property, the mudabbar is freed but must perform sa'i for his full value to satisfy the creditors.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Mudabbar's Work Obligation: Two-Thirds or Full Value
Ruling on mudabbar's work obligation based on master's financial situation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mudabbar's work obligation when the master has no other property, and distinguish between the cases with and without debt by applying the one-third bequest limit and debt priority principle.

Core Idea
A mudabbar must work for two-thirds of his value if no debt; for full value if debt exists.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a mudabbar (a slave freed upon the master's death) when the master's entire estate consists of that slave. Since tadbir is a bequest, it is limited to one-third of the estate. But when there is debt, creditors take priority. The source passage uses two key hadiths and an athar to derive the mudabbar's work (si'aya) obligation. Let's trace the legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mudabbar's legal status
A mudabbar is a slave who will become free upon the master's death. Because freeing is a bequest, it is limited to one-third of the master's estate unless heirs consent.
Tadbir is a form of bequest (wasiyyah).
2
Understand the master's financial condition
The passage specifically addresses when the master has no property other than the mudabbar. Two sub‑scenarios: no debt vs. debt.
If the master had other property, the rule would differ.
3
Apply the one-third bequest limit
From the hadith of 'Imran ibn Husain, the Prophet freed two out of six slaves (one-third). Thus a bequest cannot exceed one-third of the estate. The mudabbar is freed from one-third of his value; he must work for the remaining two-thirds to gain full freedom.
The hadith is the primary dalil.
4
Apply debt priority
Debts must be paid before bequests. Since the master died with debt and no other property, the mudabbar works for his full value to satisfy creditors. He is still freed but subject to the creditors' rights.
Hadith of Jabir and athar of Qatada support this.
5
Conclude the two rulings
No debt: works for two-thirds of value. Debt: works for full value. The mudabbar's freedom is conditional on completing the work.
Both rulings balance the bequest and creditors' rights.

Conditional Tadbir and the Sale of a Mudabbar
Ruling on conditional tadbir and its effect on sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an absolute mudabbar and a conditional mudabbar, and state the ruling on selling a conditional mudabbar.

Core Idea
A conditional mudabbar can be sold before the condition is met; absolute mudabbar cannot.

This passage clarifies the Hanafi position on tadbir when it is tied to a condition. If the master says 'you are free after my death if I die from this illness,' the slave is not a full mudabbar but a conditional one. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether the slave can be sold or not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of tadbir
Examine the declaration: if it includes a condition (e.g., 'if I die from this illness'), it is conditional (muqayyad); if unconditional, it is absolute (mutlaq).
2
Determine the effect on sale
If conditional, the slave can be sold because the condition may never occur. If absolute, sale is prohibited because freedom is certain.
3
Analyze the hadith evidence
The Prophet sold a mudabbar (Bukhari). Hanafis interpret this as a conditional mudabbar, proving that conditional tadbir does not prevent sale.
4
Apply the distinction
In practice, classify given tadbir declarations as mutlaq or muqayyad and apply the appropriate ruling.

آقا نے اپنی باندی سے محبت کی جس کی وجہ سے اس سے بچہ پیدا ہو تو یہ باندی ام ولد بن گی اب اس کا بیچنا جائز نہیں اور نہ ہبہ کر کے دوسرے کی ملکیت میں دینا جائز ہے۔ اوپر ابن ماجہ کی حدیث گزری فهى معتقة عن دبر منه (ابن ماجہ شریف نمبر ۲۵۱۵) (۱) عن ابن عمر ان النبي الله نهي عن بيع امهات الاولاد وقال لا يبعن ولا يوهبن ولا يورثن يستمتع بها سيدها مادام حيا فاذا مات فهي حرة (ج) (دارقطنی، کتاب المکاتب ج رابع ص ۷۵ نمبر ۴۲۰۳ سنن بیہقی ، باب الرجل يطاً امته بالملک فتلد لهج عاشر ص ۵۷۴، نمبر ۲۱۷۶۳) اس حدیث سے معلوم ہوا کہ ام ولد آقا کے مرنے کے بعد آزاد ہو جائے گی۔

حاشیہ : (الف) آپ نے فرمایا کسی بھی آدمی کی باندی اس سے بچہ دے تو وہ اس کے مرنے کے بعد آزاد ہو جائے گی (ب) آپ نے پوچھا حباب کی ذمہ داری کون لے گا؟ کیا گیا اس کے بھائی ابوالعیسیر بن عمر تو ان کے پاس پیغام بھیجا۔ آپ نے فرمایا اس کو آزاد کردو۔ پس جب خبر ملے کہ میرے پاس کوئی غلام آیا ہے تو میرے پاس آنا ۔ اس کا بدلہ دے دوں گا۔ وہ فرماتی ہیں کہ مجھ کو آزاد کر دیا۔ اور حضور کے پاس غلام آئے تو میرے بدلے میں اس کو غلام دیا (ج) آپ نے ام ولد کو بیچنے سے منع فرمایا اور فرمایا کہ نہ وہ نیچی جاسکتی ہیں نہ ہبہ کی جاسکتی ہیں اور نہ وارث بنائی جاسکتی ہیں۔ ان کا مالک ان سے استفادہ کرے گا جب تک زندہ ہے۔ پس جب مر گیا تو وہ آزاد ہو جائیں گی۔

Chapter on Umm al-Walad. [2230] (1) When a slave woman gives birth from her master, she becomes his Umm al-Walad; it is not permissible for him to sell her nor to give her away. [2231] (2) He may have intercourse with her, employ her, hire her out, and marry her off. (Urdu: Explanation: This essential matter: if the master has intercourse with his slave woman resulting in a child, and he acknowledges that the child is his, then she becomes Umm al-Walad due to being the mother of the child. She will become free after the master's death. There is proof in hadith: from Ibn Abbas, the Prophet said, 'Any man whose slave woman gives birth from him, she is free upon his death.' (Ibn Majah, Bab Ummahat al-Awlad, no. 2515) This hadith establishes Umm al-Walad and her freedom. (2) In Abu Dawud, a longer hadith about freeing Umm al-Walad includes: 'Free her; if you hear that slaves have come to me, bring me her replacement.' (Abu Dawud, Bab fi Haqq Ummahat al-Awlad, no. 3953) This also proves freeing Umm al-Walad. [2230] (1) When a slave woman gives birth to a child from her master, she becomes his Umm al-Walad. It is not permissible for him to sell her or give her away. (Urdu: The master has intercourse with his slave woman resulting in a child, so she becomes Umm al-Walad; selling her or giving her as a gift is not permissible. The above hadith from Ibn Majah states 'she is free upon his death.' (1) From Ibn Umar, the Prophet forbade selling Ummahat al-Awlad and said, 'They should not be sold, nor given as gifts, nor inherited; the master enjoys her while he lives, and when he dies she is free.' (Dar Qutni, no. 4203) This hadith shows Umm al-Walad becomes free after the master's death. [2231] (2) The master may have intercourse with her, take service from her, hire her out, and marry her off. [2232] (3) The lineage of her child is not established unless the master acknowledges it. [2233] (4) If she gives birth after that, the child's lineage is established from him without acknowledgment; if he denies it, it is denied by his statement.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Thamiri discusses the chapter on Umm al-Walad, a slave woman who bears her master's child. The ruling is that upon bearing a child acknowledged by the master, she attains the status of Umm al-Walad. The master cannot sell her or gift her to another; however, he retains the right to have intercourse with her, use her services, hire her out, and arrange her marriage. The proof is from multiple hadith: Ibn Abbas reports the Prophet saying such a woman is free upon the master's death, and Ibn Umar reports a prohibition on selling them. Regarding lineage, the child's lineage to the master is only established if the master explicitly acknowledges the child the first time. Once acknowledged, subsequent children are automatically attributed to him, though he can still deny them by saying they are not his. This distinction arises because the relationship with a slave woman is weaker than with a wife, and the master might not intend children. The passage includes the legal reasoning (ta'lil) that the initial acknowledgment solidifies the bond, so later births do not require renewal, but denial is still possible.

Key Terms
ام ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has given birth to a child acknowledged by her master.

Lineage of Umm al-Walad's Children: Acknowledgment and Automatic Attribution
Rules for establishing the child's lineage from the master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the first child's lineage requires the master's acknowledgment, while subsequent children are automatically attributed.

Core Idea
For the first child, lineage requires acknowledgment; later children are automatically linked unless denied.

The lineage (nasab) of a child born to an Umm al-Walad is not automatic. The master must acknowledge the first child as his own to establish paternal lineage. Once done, the bond is solidified, and any subsequent children are attributed without fresh acknowledgment, though the master may still deny them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is establishing the lineage of a child born to an Umm al-Walad. The ruling: for the first child, lineage is not established unless the master acknowledges it. For subsequent children, lineage is established automatically without acknowledgment.
But the master can still deny later children.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites the practice of Ibn Abbas (athar) and a hadith from Bukhari to show that acknowledgment is necessary.
The athar shows that Ibn Abbas denied a child from his slave woman because he did not intend offspring.
3
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The relationship with a slave woman is weaker than with a wife. The master may not intend children, so acknowledgment cements the bond. Once acknowledged, the relationship is strengthened, and subsequent children are presumed to be his.
This is based on istihsan or the established practice.
4
Note the possibility of denial
Even after acknowledgment, the master may deny a later child by explicitly saying it is not his. This results in the child's lineage not being attributed.
This shows the master's right to reject paternity.

Emancipation of Umm Walad Upon Master's Death
Ruling on umm walad's freedom and exemption from sa'yah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the emancipation of the umm walad upon the master's death and explain the proof that she is exempt from sa'yah for creditors.

Core Idea
The umm walad is freed entirely upon the master's death, with no obligation to work for his creditors.

This lesson examines a key ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the status of the umm walad (a slave woman who bore her master's child) after the master's death. The text establishes that she is freed from the entire estate, regardless of the master's debts, and is not required to perform sa'yah (earning money) to pay creditors. This ruling is supported by a prophetic hadith and the practice of Caliph Umar. Understanding this ruling clarifies the special legal status of the umm walad in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the legal event? The master dies. What happens to the umm walad? She is freed from the entire estate and not obligated to work for creditors.
Two-part ruling: freedom and exemption from sa'yah.
2
Extract the precise wording
From the Arabic: 'عتقت من جميع المال ولا تلزمها السعاية للغرماء'. Note the key terms: 'جميع المال' (entire estate) and 'لا تلزمها السعاية' (no obligation of sa'yah).
The wording is emphatic: 'all' and 'not obligated'.
3
Locate the first dalil (hadith)
The hadith of Ibn Abbas (Ibn Majah and Dar Qutni) states: 'Any man whose slave woman gives birth to a child from him, she is freed upon his death.' This directly supports the freedom upon death.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut but relied upon in this context.
4
Analyze the second dalil (athar of Umar)
The athar shows that Umar freed umm walad after masters' deaths and prohibited selling them for debt. Ali confirmed this. This establishes that she is not subject to sale or sa'yah.
This athar reinforces the exemption from creditors' claims.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi position is that she is unconditionally free. Some other schools may require sa'yah if the estate is insolvent. The Hanafi view is mufta bihi due to the clear textual proofs.
The dispute centers on whether the right of creditors overrides her freedom.

Grandfather's Intercourse with Grandson's Slave-Girl: Lineage and Umm Walad Status
Ruling on grandfather's intercourse with grandson's slave-girl: conditional on father's life.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a grandfather's intercourse with his grandson's slave-girl establishes lineage and umm walad status, and explain the role of the father's presence.

Core Idea
The grandfather's authority over a grandson's property only arises when the father is dead, determining lineage and umm walad status.

This lesson examines a specific Hanafi ruling regarding the grandfather's intercourse with his grandson's slave-girl. The outcome hinges entirely on whether the father (the intermediate) is alive or dead, reflecting a broader principle about the grandfather's authority over a grandson's property. Understanding this ruling clarifies the conditions under which lineage and umm walad status are established, and the financial consequences involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Two Scenarios
The act is the grandfather's intercourse with his grandson's slave-girl. The ruling distinguishes two scenarios: (1) the father is alive, and (2) the father is dead.
The father here is the son of the grandfather and the father of the grandson – the intermediate generation.
2
Apply the Authority Principle
In Hanafi fiqh, the grandfather has no authority over the grandson's property while the father is alive. If the father is dead, the grandfather steps into a position akin to a father regarding the grandson's property.
This principle is the core legal basis for the ruling.
3
Derive the Ruling on Lineage
When the father is alive, the grandfather's intercourse is not with his own property, so the child's lineage is not established. When the father is dead, the slave-girl is deemed to be owned by the grandfather at the moment of intercourse, so lineage is established.
Lineage here means the child is attributed to the grandfather.
4
Determine Umm Walad Status
If lineage is established, the slave-girl becomes umm walad (mother of the master's child), which entitles her to freedom upon the master's death. If lineage is not established, she does not become umm walad.
Umm walad status is consequential for inheritance and manumission.
5
Note the Financial Obligation
In the case where lineage is established (father dead), the grandfather must pay the price of the slave-girl to the grandson to compensate for the loss of property.
This ensures the grandson's property is not lost without compensation.

Compensation for Claiming Paternity in a Co-Owned Slave-Girl
Ruling when one co-owner of a slave-girl claims her child.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one co-owner claims a child from a jointly owned slave-girl and calculate the compensation owed to the other partner.

Core Idea
When one co-owner claims paternity of a slave-girl's child, he owes half the slave-girl's price and half her 'aqr, not the child's value.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: two partners own a slave-girl, she gives birth, and one partner claims the child as his own. The ruling establishes the child's lineage and the slave-girl's status as umm walad, but compensation is required to the other partner. We will explore why the claimant pays half the slave-girl's value and half her bride-money, and why the child's value is not owed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claim
Two co-owners share the slave-girl. One of them claims the child as his own. This is the initiating event.
The child is born during the period of co-ownership.
2
Determine the consequences for the child
The child's lineage is established from the claimant. The child is free and legitimate (nasab).
No compensation is owed for the child because he is the claimant's offspring.
3
Determine the consequences for the slave-girl
The slave-girl becomes umm walad for the claimant, meaning she cannot be sold and becomes free upon his death.
This status applies only to the claimant's share; the other partner's share remains as ordinary slave-girl.
4
Calculate the compensation to the other partner
The claimant must pay half the slave-girl's market price (the value of the other partner's share) and half her 'aqr (bride-money equivalent for the other partner's share).
The 'aqr compensates the partner for the loss of potential birthing revenue.
5
Explain why no child value is owed
The child is the claimant's own offspring, not a separate property. The partner has no claim on the child's value.
This distinguishes the case from one where a stranger claims the child.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid kitaba requires master's offer and slave's acceptance; payment may be immediate or by installments, both supported by Quran and Sunnah.

This lesson opens the Book of Mukatab in Hanafi fiqh. You will learn what makes a person a mukatab—a slave contracted for freedom—and the flexibility in structuring the payment, whether lump sum or installments. The rulings are rooted in the Quranic verse 24:33 and the practice of the Prophet's household as narrated by Aisha regarding Barira.

Guided Path
1

The passage states that the mal can be immediate (hal) or deferred (mu'ajjal) and in installments (munajjaman). Distinguish these three terms.

The commentator cites Quran 24:33. The imperative 'fakatibuhum' (so make kitaba) is qualified by the condition 'in ʿalimtum fīhim khayran' (if you know good in them). This indicates recommendation, not obligation.
Hanafi usul: a command with a condition is not absolute obligation.

Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith of Barira shows she owed five uqiyya over five years (installments), and Aisha offered to pay the full amount at once. This proves both installment and immediate payment are valid.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari.
5
Conclude the Hanafi ruling
Both payment options are permissible. The contract is valid upon offer and acceptance, regardless of payment structure.
No dispute within the Hanafi school on this point.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In kitabah, both immediate and installment payments are valid — proven by the Prophet's approval of both methods.

The contract of kitabah (manumission by payment) raises a practical question: can the master demand the entire sum at once, or must he allow installments? The Hanafi school, drawing on the well-known incident of Barira, rules that both options are equally permissible. This lesson unpacks the evidence and clarifies that neither method is exclusive.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is stipulating payment terms in a kitabah contract. The ruling is that both immediate payment (hālan) and deferred installment payment (munajjaman) are permissible (mubah).
No obligation to choose one over the other.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Barira (Bukhari no. 2560), where Barira had to pay five uqiyah over five years (installments), and Aisha offered to pay the full amount at once.
Both scenarios occurred with the Prophet's approval.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Barira's payment plan demonstrates deferred installments are valid; Aisha's offer shows immediate lump-sum payment is also valid. The Prophet's silence indicates approval of both.
The same incident thus proves both methods.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no known disagreement among the major schools – the ruling is muttafaq alayh.
No further tarjih needed.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Both payment structures are equally valid in practice; the master may choose either, and the contract is valid regardless.
This flexibility benefits both parties.

Scope of the Kitabat Contract: Which Relatives Are Included

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on which relatives are included in a slave's kitabat contract and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Only ascendants and descendants enter the kitabat according to Abu Hanifa, not other relatives.

The kitabat contract is a path to freedom for a slave. But which of his relatives are automatically included in the contract? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagreed on the scope. This lesson unpacks their arguments, rooted in the nature of familial maintenance obligations, and clarifies the practical impact of the difference.

When a mukatab misses a payment, the qadi first checks for hope of payment; Abu Hanifa allows return after one default, Abu Yusuf after two.

This passage details the qadi's role when a mukatab (a slave working to buy his freedom via kitabat) fails to pay an installment. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a mukatab becomes incapable and explain the proof from the hadith of Barirah and the athar of Jabir.

Core Idea
Upon incapability, the mukatab's earnings belong to the master because the slave can own property, which then passes to the master.

This lesson addresses a key consequence of the kitabat contract: what happens if the mukatab fails to pay. The Hanafi school rules that he reverts to full slavery, and all property he acquired during the kitabat period becomes the master's property. This includes installments already paid. The ruling is supported by clear textual evidence from the Sunnah and the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the opening sentence: the mukatab returns to slavery and his earnings go to the master. This is the legal consequence of his incapability (al-ajz).
2
Locate the proof from Sunnah
The hadith of Barirah shows that charity given to a slave becomes the slave's property; when the slave gifts it to the master, it becomes lawful for the master. This proves a slave can own property and transfer it to the master.
The hadith is cited to establish that a slave's ownership is recognized in Shariah.
3
Apply the analogy to the mukatab
Since a mukatab is still a slave (though with a promise of freedom), his earnings are his property. When he becomes incapable and returns to slavery, his property reverts to the master by the same principle as in the hadith.
The athar of Jabir explicitly states that whatever the master took from the mukatab (installments) is his when the mukatab returns to slavery.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may hold that the mukatab retains his earnings if he has paid some amount. The Hanafi position is that all earnings revert to the master because the condition of freedom was not fulfilled.
The practical difference affects distribution of the mukatab's estate if he dies after becoming incapable.

Commentary
This passage states the legal consequence when a mukatab is declared incapable of fulfilling his kitabat. He ceases to be a mukatab and returns to the full status of a slave (al-riqq). As a slave, all property he possesses, including earnings he accumulated during the kitabat period, automatically belongs to his master. This includes the amounts he already paid toward the kitabat; they are lawful for the master because they were paid in exchange for potential freedom, which did not materialize. The passage provides evidence from the well-known hadith of Barirah. Charity meat was given to Barirah (who was a slave being manumitted). The Prophet ﷺ initially refused to eat it because it was charity, but when informed that it became Barirah's property and she gave it as a gift, he ate it. This shows that a slave can own property and that property transferred from a slave to the master becomes lawful for the master. Another athar from Jabir explicitly states that whatever the master took from the mukatab (as installment payments) becomes his when the mukatab returns to slavery. Thus, all payments and earnings revert to the master.

Ruling on Mukatab Unable to Pay Kitaba
Ruling and proof for dissolution of kitaba upon inability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a mukatab becomes unable to pay, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the number of missed installments, and apply the relevant proofs (athar and hadith) to justify the ruling.

Core Idea
A mukatab who cannot pay returns to slavery, and his earnings become the master's — but the number of missed installments required is disputed.

This passage addresses a critical question in the law of kitaba: when does a mukatab's inability to pay trigger the dissolution of his contract? The Hanafi school agrees that the mukatab returns to slavery, but a dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf arises over the number of consecutive missed installments required. We will examine the proofs, including an athar from 'Ali and the hadith of Barira, to understand the relied-upon position and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the mukatab's inability to pay an installment when due. The ruling is that the kitaba is dissolved and he returns to slavery.
This is the general Hanafi position, regardless of the dispute over the number of installments.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites three proofs: an athar from Ali (two consecutive installments), the hadith of Barira (ownership of earnings), and an athar from Jabir (master's claim on remaining earnings).
The athar from Ali directly supports Abu Yusuf's view.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The athar from Ali explicitly states that if two installments are consecutive and the mukatab enters the year without paying, he is returned. This indicates that inability is only established after two missed installments.
The phrase 'tataaba'a alayhi najmān' is key.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that missing one installment suffices. Abu Yusuf requires two consecutive missed installments.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The text presents the athar from Ali as evidence for Abu Yusuf's view, indicating that this is the preferred (mufta bihi) position.
The ruling adopted for fatwa is that of Abu Yusuf.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on what happens when a mukatab (a slave under a kitaba contract) becomes unable to fulfill his payment installments. The core ruling is that the kitaba is dissolved (faskh al-kitaba) and he returns to the status of a regular slave (raj'a ila hukm al-riq). There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf conditions the inability on the passage of two consecutive unpaid installments, while the general position (attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) considers inability proven by failure to pay even a single installment when due. The passage supports Abu Yusuf's view with an athar from Ali (ra) stating the two-installment condition.

Upon return to slavery, all earnings the mukatab had in his hand become the property of the master. The passage provides two proofs: (1) The hadith of Barira shows that a slave can own property (including charity), and when that property is transferred to a free person (the master), it becomes permissible (halal) for the recipient, even if the master is wealthy. (2) An athar from Jabir explicitly states that whatever the master took from the mukatab before return remains his, and after return, any remaining wealth also becomes the master's.

The passage also includes a brief note that if the mukatab collected charity to pay the installments, those payments are lawful for the master, because the mukatab owned the charity upon receipt, and then transferred it to the master as valid payment.

Ruling on Mukatab Death with Sufficient Estate
Ruling that death with estate does not rescind kitaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a mukatab dies with sufficient estate and explain the legal consequences regarding his freedom, his children, and the distribution of his estate.

Core Idea
A mukatab who dies with sufficient estate is deemed free before death—his children are free and surplus is inherited.

This lesson examines the scenario where a mukatab dies while still owing installments but his estate can cover the debt. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling: the kitaba is not rescinded; rather, the mukatab is considered freed just before death. We will explore the ruling, its basis in hadith and athar, and its practical consequences for the mukatab's children and heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and legal question
The case: a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to buy his freedom) dies while still owing some payments. Does his death automatically cancel the kitaba? What happens to his estate?
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The text rules: 'If the mukatab dies and he has wealth, the kitaba is not rescinded.' Instead, the debt is paid from his earnings (estate). The contract stands.
3
Understand the retroactive freedom
The law deems the mukatab to have been freed in the last part of his life (akhir juz' min ajza' hayatihi). This is a legal fiction: he is considered to have paid the kitaba just before death, so he dies free.
Consequence: Children born from him are free.
4
Examine the consequences for children and heirs
Since the father died free, his children are free. After paying the kitaba, any surplus wealth is inherited by his heirs (warathah) as free persons.
The heirs are those of a free person, not of a slave.
5
Analyze the proofs
Two proofs are cited: (a) Hadith of Umm Salamah: the Prophet commanded hijab from a mukatab who has enough to pay, indicating he is treated as free. (b) Athar from Ali (ra) that the kitaba is paid and surplus goes to the children.
These show the ruling is rooted in strong evidence.

Commentary
This passage addresses the scenario where a mukatab dies while still owing installments, but his estate is sufficient to cover the entire remaining kitaba debt. The ruling is that the kitaba contract is not rescinded (lam tanfasikh al-kitaba). Instead, the debt is paid from his earnings (or estate), and he is legally deemed to have been freed just before death (fi akhir juz' min ajza' hayatihi). This has two important consequences: (1) his children become free because the father died free, and (2) any surplus after paying the kitaba is inherited by his heirs.

The passage supports this with two proofs: (a) The hadith of Umm Salamah where the Prophet instructs women to observe hijab from their mukatab if he has enough to pay, indicating that such a mukatab is treated as free. (b) An athar from Ata' reporting that Ali (ra) ruled that the kitaba is paid from the estate and the surplus goes to the children.

The commentary emphasizes that this ruling applies regardless of whether the mukatab had actually paid before death; the law presumes freedom retroactively if the estate can cover the debt.

Commentary
This ruling explains the situation after the master's death when multiple heirs inherit. Since the mukatab is jointly owned, one heir cannot unilaterally free him because that would prejudice the other heirs. All heirs must agree to free him. Upon collective manumission, the mukatab becomes free and the kitabah debt is cancelled.

Making an Umm Walad a Mukataba
Ruling on kitabah for an umm walad with consequence on master's death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on making an umm walad a mukataba and explain the legal consequence if the master dies before she completes payment.

Core Idea
An umm walad can be made a mukataba; if the master dies, the kitabah debt drops and she becomes free.

An umm walad is a unique category of slave who is guaranteed freedom upon her master's death. This lesson examines the interaction of that status with a kitabah contract, where the master agrees to free her in exchange for payments. The key question: if the master dies before she finishes paying, what happens to her debt and her freedom?

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the contract
The act is the master (mawla) making his umm walad a mukataba. Recognize the specific legal status of an umm walad: she becomes free upon the master's death automatically.
The kitabah contract is a promise of freedom in exchange for payment.
2
State the ruling on permissibility
The passage clearly states that such a contract is permissible (jaza). This is despite her existing route to freedom upon his death.
The ruling confirms no inherent conflict between the two statuses.
3
Analyze the consequence of master's death
If the master dies before the mukataba completes payment, the kitabah debt is dropped (sagata anha). She becomes free automatically as an umm walad, so there is no need to continue payments.
The debt does not pass to heirs; the contract ends upon his death.
4
Understand the rationale
The purpose of kitabah is to allow the slave to earn freedom during the master's lifetime. Since she gains freedom upon his death anyway, the unpaid balance becomes moot.
This prevents a double obligation or hardship.
