The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Partial Manumission
Intra-Hanafi dispute with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on partial manumission of a jointly-owned slave, cite the proof texts for each, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa says only the freed portion is free; the Sahibayn say the whole slave becomes free.

When one co-owner frees his share of a jointly-owned slave, does the entire slave become free or only the share? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples. We will analyze the key hadiths cited by each side and trace how each interprets the Prophetic statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
State the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: only the freed portion becomes free. Then state the ruling of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): the entire slave becomes free.
Note that this is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf; all three are Hanafi imams.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's Proof
Read the hadith of Isma'il ibn Umayya: the Prophet said to the man who freed half his slave, 'You are free in what you freed, and a slave in what remains.' Understand how this explicitly permits partial freedom.
Also the hadith from Bukhari and Muslim that only what was freed becomes free if the manumitter is poor.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's Proof
Read the hadith of Abu Huraira: 'Whoever frees a share... then his complete emancipation is upon him...' and the hadith of Abu al-Malih: 'He is entirely free.' These imply that freedom cannot be divided.
The Sahibayn rely on the apparent meaning of these texts.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
Understand how Abu Hanifa distinguishes the cases: the hadith of Abu Huraira applies when the manumitter is wealthy; otherwise the slave earns the remaining value, but is not automatically free. The hadith of Abu al-Malih may be a specific case or abrogated.
Abu Hanifa's interpretation preserves the possibility of partial freedom from the explicit text.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Identify the practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, if a poor man frees his share, only that share is free and the slave serves the co-owner for the rest. Under the Sahibayn, the slave becomes entirely free and must earn the co-owner's share value.
This affects the co-owner's options and the slave's status.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling when one co-owner of a slave frees his share. The base text states the positions: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, only the freed portion becomes free; according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the entire slave becomes free. The commentator then provides proof texts. For Abu Hanifa's position, two hadiths are cited: (1) the hadith narrated by Isma'il ibn Umayya where the Prophet told the man who freed half his slave that the slave is free in that portion and remains a slave in the rest, and (2) a hadith indicating that if the manumitter cannot pay the remaining value, only what was freed becomes free. For the Sahibayn's position, two hadiths are cited: (1) the hadith of Abu Huraira that whoever frees a share must free the whole if he has wealth, otherwise the slave earns the value, implying complete freedom; and (2) the hadith of Abu al-Malih where the Prophet declared the whole slave free. The commentator then explains the ruling in detail: If the manumitter is wealthy, the co-owner has three options: free his own share, hold the manumitter liable for the value, or make the slave earn the value. If the manumitter is poor, only two options remain: free or make the slave earn. This is based on Abu Hanifa's principle that only the freed portion is free, but the manumitter's wealth or poverty affects the co-owner's remedies. The Sahibayn's view that the whole slave becomes free would give the co-owner only the option to claim the slave's value from the manumitter or the slave's earnings, but here the text follows Abu Hanifa's framework.

Practical Options for the Co-Owner After Partial Manumission
Practical ruling from Abu Hanifa's perspective

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the options available to the co-owner when one partner frees his share of a jointly-owned slave, based on the manumitter's financial status.

Core Idea
If wealthy, the co-owner has three options; if poor, only two—this flows from Abu Hanifa's principle.

Now that we understand the dispute, we focus on the practical application according to Imam Abu Hanifa. The textual commentary provides a detailed ruling on the co-owner's options when one partner frees his share. The manumitter's wealth or poverty determines which options are available. This lesson walks through the scenarios and the rationale behind each option.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Underlying Principle
Remember Abu Hanifa's view: only the freed portion becomes free. The remaining share is still owned by the other co-owner.
This is the basis for the options.
2
Identify the Manumitter's Wealth Status
Check if the manumitter is wealthy (musir) or poor (mu'sir). Wealthy means he has enough to pay the value of the remaining share.
The hadith of Ibn Umar supports the wealthy case.
3
List the Three Options for the Wealthy Case
The co-owner may: (1) free his own share, (2) hold the manumitter liable for the value of his share, or (3) make the slave earn (istis'a') the value.
Option 2 is because the manumitter caused loss by freeing part and is able to compensate.
4
List the Two Options for the Poor Case
If the manumitter is poor, the co-owner cannot hold him liable. He may: (1) free his own share, or (2) make the slave earn the value.
The poor manumitter cannot be forced to pay.
5
Explain the Rationale for Each Option
Option to free: because the co-owner owns his share. Option to hold liable: because the manumitter's act diminished the slave's value. Option to make the slave earn: because the slave can work to buy his remaining freedom.
The slave is not forced into excessive hardship (ghayru mashfuq 'alayhi).

(He said): his partners' shares become free and the slave is obligated to him, otherwise only what was freed becomes free. (A) (Bukhari, chapter... hadith no. 2522; Muslim, hadith no. 150) In this hadith it is that if the manumitter does not have the remaining price of the slave, then only as much as was freed becomes free. From this it is understood that portioning and division can occur in the emancipation of a slave. Therefore, what the master freed becomes free, and the slave will earn the remaining portion for the master. (2) There is a hadith: Isma'il ibn Umayya narrated from his father from his grandfather: They had a slave called Tuhman or Dhekwan; his grandfather freed half of him. The slave came to the Prophet, peace be upon him, and informed him. The Prophet said: 'You are free in what you freed, and a slave in what remains as a slave.' He said: He served his master until he died. (B) (Sunan al-Bayhaqi, hadith no. 21319; Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, hadith no. 16705) From this hadith it is also known that only what was freed becomes free. According to the Sahibayn, the entire slave becomes free. This is the hadith: From Abu Huraira that the Prophet said: 'Whoever frees a share or a portion in a slave, then his complete emancipation is upon him from his wealth if he has wealth; otherwise, a fair value is assessed upon him and the slave is made to earn it without hardship.' (C) (Bukhari, hadith no. 2527; Muslim, hadith no. 1503) In this hadith it is that if the manumitter does not have the remaining price of the slave, even then the entire slave becomes free and the slave must earn and pay his price. From this it is understood that freeing half makes the entire slave free; division in emancipation is not allowed. (2) From Abu al-Malih that a man from his people freed one-third of his slave, and this was raised to the Prophet, who said: 'He is entirely free; Allah has no partner.' (D) (Sunan al-Bayhaqi, hadith no. 21317; Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, hadith no. 16708) From this hadith it is understood that the entire slave becomes free. According to the principle of Imam Abu Hanifa, only the portion that is freed becomes free; the rest remains a slave. According to the Sahibayn, there is no division in emancipation, so the entire slave becomes free. Commentary: (A) He said: If someone frees a jointly-owned slave and he has enough wealth to reach the slave's price, then a fair price is assessed upon him, and the partners are given their shares, and the slave becomes free on behalf of the manumitter. If he has no wealth, then only what was freed becomes free. (B) Isma'il ibn Umayya said: They had a slave named Tuhman or Dhekwan; his grandfather freed half of him. The slave came to the Prophet and informed him, and he said: 'What you freed is free, and what you kept is slave.' He said: He served his master until death. (C) He said: Whoever frees his share in a slave and he has enough wealth to liquidate it, then it is obligatory upon him to liquidate it; otherwise, a price is assessed upon him, and the slave earns it to avoid hardship. Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamiri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling when one co-owner of a slave frees his share. The base text states the positions: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, only the freed portion becomes free; according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the entire slave becomes free. The commentator then provides proof texts. For Abu Hanifa's position, two hadiths are cited: (1) the hadith narrated by Isma'il ibn Umayya where the Prophet told the man who freed half his slave that the slave is free in that portion and remains a slave in the rest, and (2) a hadith indicating that if the manumitter cannot pay the remaining value, only what was freed becomes free. For the Sahibayn's position, two hadiths are cited: (1) the hadith of Abu Huraira that whoever frees a share must free the whole if he has wealth, otherwise the slave earns the value, implying complete freedom; and (2) the hadith of Abu al-Malih where the Prophet declared the whole slave free. The commentator then explains the ruling in detail: If the manumitter is wealthy, the co-owner has three options: free his own share, hold the manumitter liable for the value, or make the slave earn the value. If the manumitter is poor, only two options remain: free or make the slave earn. This is based on Abu Hanifa's principle that only the freed portion is free, but the manumitter's wealth or poverty affects the co-owner's remedies. The Sahibayn's view that the whole slave becomes free would give the co-owner only the option to claim the slave's value from the manumitter or the slave's earnings, but here the text follows Abu Hanifa's framework.

Key Terms
موسر (Musir): Wealthy; having enough wealth to pay the value of the remaining share of the slave.
معسر (Mu'sir): Poor; lacking wealth to pay the value.
ضمان (Daman): Liability; holding someone responsible for a financial obligation.

Emancipation of Slave for Payment of Money
Ruling with proof for conditional emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of emancipation for payment and identify the proof used by the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Acceptance makes the slave free immediately; payment becomes a binding debt.

This passage addresses a specific transaction: a master offers freedom to his slave in exchange for a sum of money. When the slave accepts, two effects occur simultaneously. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing the offer from the condition, and recognizing that the slave's freedom is immediate while the payment becomes a debt. The Hanafi school supports this with a hadith of Safinah and the practice of Umar (RA).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Emancipation for payment. What is the ruling? Upon acceptance, the slave becomes free and owes the payment.
The ruling is sahih (valid).
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites two proofs: a hadith from Abu Dawud about Safinah freed on condition of service, and an athar from Umar freeing slaves on condition of serving the caliph.
Sunnah and Qawl Sahabi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet (PBUH) accepted Safinah's conditional emancipation, proving that conditions are valid. Umar's practice confirms this. By analogy (qiyas), emancipation for payment is also valid.
Payment is analogous to service as both are valuable considerations.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools may reject conditional emancipation, arguing that emancipation must be absolute. The Hanafi school maintains validity based on the proofs.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
5
State the mufta bihi
The reliable position is that the slave becomes free immediately upon acceptance and must pay the agreed sum.
Zahir al-Riwayah position.

Learning Objective

This passage from Sharh al-Samari opens the Book of Drinks by classifying prohibited drinks. The Hanafi position is considered the strongest due to the explicit permission in the hadith and the principle that things are permissible unless proven otherwise. The muftā bihī (operative ruling) is that nabidh from these sources is halal unless intoxicating.
This ruling is part of Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah and remains the relied-upon opinion.

Core Idea
Grape juice cooked until two-thirds evaporates is halal, even if strong, as long as it does not intoxicate.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on grape juice that has been cooked to reduce by two-thirds, known as tilā'. Core Idea
