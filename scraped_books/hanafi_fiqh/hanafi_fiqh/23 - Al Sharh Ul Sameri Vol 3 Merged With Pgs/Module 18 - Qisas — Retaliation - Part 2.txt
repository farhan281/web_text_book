One Arrow, Two Deaths: Distinguishing Qisas and Diyah
Ruling on combined 'amd and khata' killing by one arrow

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a single act causing two deaths yields separate liabilities: qisas for the intended victim and diyah on the 'aqilah for the unintended victim.

Core Idea
One intentional act can produce both intentional and accidental homicide rulings, each with its own liability.

Imagine an archer intentionally shoots a man standing in a line; the arrow passes through and kills a second person behind him. Both die. How does Islamic law treat the archer? The Hanafi rule distinguishes based on intent: the first killing is 'amd (intentional), requiring qisas; the second is khata' (accidental), requiring diyah paid by the 'aqilah (clan). This lesson unpacks the ruling and its prophetic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its dual consequence
Recognize that a single shot is one act but results in two deaths. The ruling treats each death separately based on the killer's intent toward each victim.
The arrow's penetration is a single physical cause, but the law looks at mental state for each victim.
2
Classify each killing
The first victim was intended → 'amd (intentional homicide). The second victim was unintended → khata' (accidental homicide). Confirm the legal definitions: 'amd requires an act ordinarily lethal; khata' occurs when there is no intent to kill that person.
Hanafi definition of 'amd: shooting a bow is considered a lethal act, so the first is 'amd even if the arrow would not always kill.
3
Determine the liability for the first victim
For 'amd, the killer must undergo qisas (retributive execution) unless pardoned or commuted to diyah. Here qisas is wajib (obligatory) on the killer himself.
Qisas is a fixed penalty; it cannot be replaced by financial compensation without the victim's family's consent.
4
Determine the liability for the second victim
For khata', diyah is wajib, but it is not paid by the killer alone; it is on his 'aqilah (male paternal relatives). The hadith proof: 'He (the Prophet) ruled that the diyah of a woman is upon her 'aqilah.'
The 'aqilah bears the financial burden to foster clan responsibility and prevent undue hardship on the killer.
5
Verify the proof and authority
The commentary cites a sahih hadith. The Hanafi position is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is unanimously held (muttafaq alayh). No intra-Hanafi dispute.
The hadith is general; its application here is through qiyas (analogy) to all accidental killings.

The Principle of Mumathala in Qisas: Linked Injuries
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas versus arsh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of exact equivalence (mumathala) to determine whether qisas or arsh applies in an injury causing secondary paralysis.

Core Idea
Qisas requires exact equivalence; if retaliation cannot mirror the injury, only arsh is due.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute about a specific injury scenario: cutting one finger causes a neighboring finger to become paralyzed. Abu Hanifa holds that both injuries constitute a single act, making exact equivalence for qisas impossible, so only arsh is due for both fingers. Sahibayn and Imam Zufar separate the injuries, applying qisas to the intentional cut and arsh to the paralysis. Understanding this difference sharpens your grasp of the crucial Hanafi condition of mumathala in qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Classification
The act is an intentional cutting of one finger that unintentionally paralyzes an adjacent finger. Classify the first injury as amad (intentional) and the second as khata (mistake) in terms of causation.
This initial classification is agreed upon by all parties.
2
Understand the Hanafi Principle of Mumathala
Qisas requires mumathala — exact equivalence between the injury inflicted and the retaliatory injury. If precise mirroring is impossible, qisas is not valid and arsh (financial compensation) becomes wajib.
This principle is uncontested in the Hanafi school but applied differently here.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Argument on Unity of the Injury
Abu Hanifa treats both injuries as one inseparable legal act. Since the retaliation cannot simultaneously cut one finger and cause paralysis in another, mumathala is impossible, and qisas is dropped entirely. Arsh is wajib for both fingers.
His conceptual basis: the two wounds are one in legal effect.
4
Contrast with Sahibayn's View of Separability
Sahibayn and Imam Zufar view the first wound as amad and the second as khata. Qisas applies to the first (the cut finger) because it can be mirrored exactly; arsh applies to the second (paralysis) since it is unintentional and cannot be retaliated upon.
They see the two wounds as two separate legal events.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the offender pays arsh for both fingers. Under Sahibayn, the offender loses his own finger by qisas and also pays arsh for the paralyzed finger. The ruling affects the amount and nature of liability.
The mufta bihi is not specified in this passage; later Hanafi sources prefer Abu Hanifa's view in such cases.

Tooth Pulled and Regrown: No Arsh
Ruling with proof from athar that arsh drops when tooth regrows

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a pulled tooth regrows and explain the proof from the athar of 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz.

Core Idea
When a pulled tooth regrows, no arsh is due because there is no permanent loss.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: if a person's tooth is pulled out and a new tooth grows in its place, the compensation (arsh) is dropped. The ruling is based on an athar from Caliph 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz and a clear rationale: no permanent harm means no liability. However, Imam Abu Yusuf dissents, allowing the judge to award something for pain and offense. We will walk through the proof and the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is pulling out someone's tooth. The ruling is that if the tooth regrows, arsh (compensation) is not obligatory.
arsh is the compensation for injuries without fixed diya.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is an athar (report) from 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz: wait a year; if the tooth grows back, no diya and no qisas.
The athar is from Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit phrase 'fala diya' (no blood money) shows that compensation is dropped when the tooth regrows.
The ruling applies to arsh by extension, as arsh is similar to diya.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that the hakim (judge) may award compensation for pain and offense, citing an athar from Ibn Shihab.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that arsh drops when the tooth regrows, as it is the majority view and supported by the athar.
Abu Yusuf's view is not adopted in fatwa for this specific case.

Blood from Natural Orifices Does Not Trigger Qasama
States that blood from nose, anus, or mouth does not trigger qasama

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In cases of suspected murder, qasama (a collective oath) is imposed when there is strong evidence of homicide. However, not all blood on a body indicates violence.
