Hadd in Banditry: Group Liability and Mahram Theft
Commentary on hadd rulings for group killing and mahram theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on group liability in banditry and the exception for mahram theft, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding children and insane participants.

Core Idea
All bandits share hadd liability even if only one kills; theft from a mahram drops the hand-cutting penalty.

This lesson examines two key rulings in the Hanafi law of theft and banditry (qat' al-tariq). First, when a group commits robbery and one kills, all are equally liable for the hadd punishment (death). Second, if the stolen property belongs to a mahram relative, the hadd of hand-cutting is not applied. We will also explore an intra-Hanafi difference where Imam Abu Yusuf distinguishes between participants who are legally liable and those who are not (children and the insane).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and extract the two main legal statements: (1) If one of a gang kills during robbery, hadd applies to the entire group. (2) If someone steals from a mahram relative, the hadd of hand-cutting is dropped.
The passage also includes a footnote from Sufyan al-Thawri supporting the mahram exception.
2
Analyze the proof for the mahram ruling
The text cites an effect (athar) from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq. This is a qawl sahabi (companion's statement) which serves as a proof. In Hanafi usul, such athar are considered strong evidence when not contradicted by stronger sources.
The dalil is zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala in this context.
3
Understand group liability in banditry
The reasoning is that all members of the gang are partners in the crime, whether they kill, assist, or watch. Therefore, the hadd (execution or amputation) is applied to all. This is a general rule in Hanafi jurisprudence.
The example of eight men illustrates that even passive participants (lookouts) are liable.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that if children or insane persons commit the actual killing and theft, no hadd applies to anyone because the principals are not liable. However, if sane adults are involved, only the sane receive hadd, while children/insane are exempt. This differs from the general position that all are liable.
The mufta bihi is the general position endorsed by the school.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The passage does not explicitly declare a mufta bihi, but the structure (presenting Abu Yusuf's view as an exception) suggests the standard Hanafi position is that all participants are liable. Later Hanafi works confirm this.
For fatwa, follow the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on legal rulings (ahkam) related to hadd in robbery (sariqa/muharaba). It addresses three issues. First, the ruling that if a member of a gang commits murder during robbery, the prescribed punishment (death penalty for banditry) is executed on all members of the gang, because they are all accomplices in the crime. The example of eight men makes this clear: even if only one kills, all share responsibility. Second, the ruling that the hadd of hand-cutting is dropped when the theft is from a mahram relative (e.g., a mother or uncle). This is based on an effect (athar) from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq. The Hanafi school generally holds that stealing from a mahram does not incur the hadd because of the special relationship. Third, Imam Abu Yusuf provides his view on the liability of children and insane persons in robbery. He argues that if the actual killing and theft were done by a child or an insane person, there is no hadd for anyone, because they are the principals and not liable. However, if a sane adult committed the crime, the hadd applies to them while the child and insane are exempt. This shows an intra-Hanafi discussion on the effect of participation by non-liable individuals.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): The prescribed corporal punishment for certain crimes such as robbery and theft.
ذو رحم محرم (dhu rahm mahram): A relative who is permanently forbidden in marriage (like mother, sister, etc.).
