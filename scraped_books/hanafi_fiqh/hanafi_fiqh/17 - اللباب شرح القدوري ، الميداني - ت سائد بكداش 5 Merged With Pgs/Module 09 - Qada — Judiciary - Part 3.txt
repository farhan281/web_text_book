Acceptance of a Judge's Letter to Another Judge
Ruling on the acceptance of a judge's letter in rights cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a judge's written communication is accepted in rights cases and explain why oral testimony is required.

Core Idea
A judge's letter is accepted only if witnessed before the recipient judge—the letter alone is insufficient.

How does the Hanafi tradition ensure the integrity of a judicial decision when it travels from one jurisdiction to another? This lesson examines a concise ruling: a judge's letter to another judge is accepted in rights cases only if it is witnessed before the recipient judge. This condition preserves the chain of testimony, as written documents are not treated as direct evidence without oral corroboration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the transmittal of a judicial decision via written letter from one judge to another. The ruling: it is accepted (yuqbal) in rights cases.
2
Determine the scope of application
The ruling is limited to 'rights' (al-ḥuqūq), i.e., financial and civil claims, not criminal punishments (ḥudūd) or retaliation (qiṣāṣ).
This scope is implied by the term 'al-ḥuqūq'.
3
Identify the condition
The condition is 'shuhida bihi ʿindahu'—the letter must be witnessed in the presence of the recipient judge. This means witnesses attest to the authenticity of the letter orally.
The witnesses must appear before the second judge.
4
Understand the rationale
A written document alone lacks the reliability of oral testimony. By requiring witnesses, the Hanafi school upholds the principle that testimony is the primary means of proof in court.

Core Idea
A judge's letter is accepted only in rights cases when attested by witnesses at the recipient court; a prior judgment requires the opponent's presence.

This passage from the Book of the Etiquette of the Judge lays down a key procedural rule: when can a judge enforce a judgment from another judge's letter? The Hanafi school sets clear conditions involving the nature of the case, witness attestation, and whether the opponent was present at the original hearing. Understanding these conditions is essential for grasping judicial cooperation in classical Islamic legal systems.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is the acceptance of a judge's written letter by another judge. The general ruling is that it is accepted (mubah) but only under specific conditions.
The ruling is permissive, not mandatory.
2
Locate the central condition: witness attestation
The letter must be witnessed (shuhud) at the court of the recipient judge. Without witnesses, the letter is not accepted.
Witnesses are required to authenticate the letter.
3

Scenario A: Witnesses testified in the presence of the opponent (خصم حاضر) → sending judge passes judgment and writes a sijill. Scenario B: Witnesses without opponent → sending judge does not pass judgment; only testimonial letter.

Acceptance is limited to rights (huquq), not hudud or qisas. Also note: the mufta bihi requires a travel distance of three days between judges, though Nawadir Hisham allows even in the same city.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Etiquette of the Judge (كتاب أدب القاضي) states the ruling on accepting a written judgment sent from one judge to another. The matn clearly lays down the rule: a judge's letter is accepted in matters of rights (الحقوق) – specifically those rights that are not nullified by ambiguity (شبهة), as the commentary clarifies – provided that the letter is witnessed (i.e., attested by two male witnesses or one man and two women) at the court of the recipient judge. The letter must be recognized as coming from the sending judge, bearing his seal.

The text then distinguishes two scenarios. First, if the witnesses testified before the sending judge in the presence of the opposing party (خصم حاضر), then the sending judge issues a judgment based on the testimony, writes it down, and sends it to the other judge for enforcement. This is akin to a recorded judgment (سجل). The commentary gives a practical example: a person buys an item that turns out to be owned by another (مستحق), and after the buyer is ordered to return it, he wants to sue the seller for the price. He asks the judge to write a judgment to the judge in the seller's city. The judge writes it, and it is called a sijill.

Second, if the witnesses testified without the opponent being present, the sending judge does not issue a judgment. Instead, he simply writes a letter containing the testimony, and the recipient judge acts on it as evidence, but no judgment is pre-decided.

The Hanafi position: The letter is accepted under these conditions. The authority weight of this ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah as found in the Mukhtasar al-Quduri and its commentary by al-Maydani. There is a condition that the distance between the two judges be at least three days' journey, as noted in the margin. Intra-madhhab differences exist: According to Nawadir Hisham, if both judges are in the same city (مصر واحد), the letter is also accepted. But the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the condition of travel distance, as mentioned in al-Jawharah.

حقوق (ḥuqūq): Legal rights that are not nullified by ambiguity; specifically financial rights and other claims, excluding hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation).

Core Idea
A written judgment or testimony between judges is valid only when authenticated by witnesses and sealed in their presence.

This passage from the Book of the Etiquette of the Judge addresses how a judge communicates a decision or testimony to another judge in a different locality. It distinguishes between writing a judgment (sijill) when the opponent is present and writing a testimonial letter (kitāb ḥukmī) when the opponent is absent. The conditions for accepting such written testimony are strict to prevent forgery and ensure authenticity.

1
Distinguish the two scenarios
Identify whether the opponent is present or absent when witnesses testify. If present, the judge issues a written judgment (sijill). If absent, he writes a testimonial letter (kitāb ḥukmī) for the other judge to judge upon.
The key is the presence of the opposing party at the time of testimony.

Extract the conditions for accepting the writing
The passage lists three conditions: (1) Authentication by two male witnesses or one man and two women; (2) The judge must read the letter to those witnesses so they know its contents; (3) The judge must seal the letter in their presence and hand it to them.

The commentary from al-Hidayah states that sealing in the presence of witnesses and preserving the contents are conditions according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, implying Abu Yusuf may not require it. The mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
This difference does not affect the other conditions.

Judge's Written Testimony: Conditions and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on written testimony between judges with conditions and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for accepting a judge's written testimony and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the requirements of sealing and witness knowledge.

Core Idea
A judge's written testimony requires witnesses, but sealing and knowledge of contents are disputed — Abu Yusuf's easier view is relied upon.

In Hanafi qada, a judge may send a written testimony to another judge to facilitate judgment. However, this writing is not accepted unconditionally. This lesson explores the mandatory conditions for validity and the scholarly disagreement within the Hanafi school on whether sealing in the witnesses' presence and their knowledge of the contents are necessary requirements.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling and conditions
Identify the ruling from the matn: a judge's written testimony requires two male witnesses or one man and two women. The writing must be read to the witnesses and sealed in their presence.
This is the foundational rule from Quduri's matn.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Note the difference: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require sealing and witness knowledge as conditions. Abu Yusuf later held that none of that is a condition; only witnesses' testimony that it is his writing and seal suffices.
Another narration from Abu Yusuf even drops the seal requirement.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's reasoning
Understand his talil: the purpose is the reliability of witnesses; news is not like direct observation. Therefore, the strict conditions are unnecessary.
This reflects practical ease (taysir) due to the difficulty of judging.
4
Examine the tarjih and mufta bihi
See how later authorities (Sarakhsi, Ibn al-Humam) prefer Abu Yusuf's easier view, but with a distinction: if the writing is with the plaintiff, sealing is recommended to prevent alteration; if with the witnesses, no sealing or knowledge needed.
The mufta bihi is this nuanced position.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider the practical difference: under the stricter view, a judge must go through the sealing ceremony; under the easier view, simple witness testimony suffices, making the process more efficient.
This affects the burden on judges and the speed of inter-judge communication.

Commentary
This passage from Lubab Sharh al-Quduri discusses the rules for a judge writing a testimony (kitab) to another judge regarding a case. It begins with the matn: the judge writes the testimony so that the recipient can judge by it. The ruling is that such a writing is accepted only with two male witnesses or one man and two women, due to the possibility of forgery. This applies when the opponent denies it is the judge's writing; if he admits, no evidence is needed. The writing judge must read the writing to the witnesses or inform them of its contents (since testimony requires knowledge), then seal it in their presence and hand it to them, to eliminate doubt.

The commentary then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute. According to al-Hidayah and Sharh al-Zahidi, sealing in the witnesses' presence and their knowledge of the contents are conditions according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf later held that none of that is a condition; the only condition is that the judge makes the witnesses testify that it is his writing and his seal. In another narration from Abu Yusuf, even sealing is not a condition—he made it easy due to the difficulty of judging. This easier view was preferred by Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi.

The author's teacher (Ibn al-Humam) in Sharh al-Hidayah supports this, reasoning that since the witnesses are reliable and carry the writing, the lack of a seal does not harm when they testify it is his writing. However, he distinguishes: if the writing is with the plaintiff, sealing may be required to prevent alteration; if with the witnesses, no sealing or knowledge of contents is needed—just their reliable testimony that it is his writing. The relied-upon fatwa is thus the easier view, as recorded in al-Tashih.

Core Idea
A ruling in a matter open to ijtihad is binding and cannot be overturned by another judge, because both ijtihads are equal in conjecture (zann).

This lesson addresses the enforcement of judgments across judges. The core principle is that rulings on ijtihad matters are final and binding. State the general rule: a judge must enforce the ruling of another judge in matters open to ijtihad. Explain why: because both ijtihads are equal in probability, and the first has been actualized by the ruling, so it cannot be overturned by an inferior second ijtihad.
The rationale is based on taswib (each mujtahid is correct) in matters of zann.

Identify that this rule applies only to matters where valid scholarly disagreement exists (mujtahad fihi). If the issue is qat'i (definitive), the ruling may be subject to different treatment.

The fatwa is upon the position of the Sahibayn.

Analyze the Sahibayn's reasoning: the judge's own belief that his ruling is wrong invalidates it, as the ruling is tainted by the judge's awareness of error. Abu Hanifa focuses on the objective nature of ijtihad: the ruling is still a valid ijtihad regardless of the judge's personal view.
This is a difference in emphasis: subjective error vs. objective validity.

State that the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn: the ruling is not enforced if the judge ruled contrary to his own madhhab, whether forgetfully or intentionally. This means that such rulings are considered void in practice.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A prior judge's judgment in a matter of ijtihad is binding unless it contradicts a clear text; but if the judge ruled against his own madhhab, the Sahibayn's view (not enforced) is the fatwa.

This passage from Adab al-Qadi addresses a critical practical question: when a new judge receives a case already decided by a previous judge, is he obligated to enforce that earlier judgment? The answer depends on several conditions, and there is an important disagreement within the Hanafi school about what happens when the first judge himself believed his ruling was wrong. We will analyze the general rule, the conditions, the intra-Hanafi dispute, and the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its justification
Read the opening sentence: the judge must enforce the prior judgment if it meets the conditions. The justification is that ijtihad is speculative, and the first judgment is strengthened by execution so cannot be overturned by an equal or weaker ijtihad.
This is the default rule for matters of ijtihad.
2

From the passage, identify the conditions: the prior judge must be muwallā (properly appointed), the lawsuit must have been valid (opposing parties present), the matter must be subject to ijtihad, and the judgment must not contradict a clear Quranic text where the salaf had no difference.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Conduct of the Judge (Adab al-Qadi) in al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri explains the rule of amḍāʾ (enforcement) when a previous judge's judgment is brought before another judge. The author first states the general ruling: if a properly appointed judge (muwallā) has issued a judgment after a valid lawsuit, the current judge must enforce it regardless of whether it agrees with his own opinion, provided the matter is one open to ijtihad (independent reasoning). The proof is based on the nature of ijtihad: all ijtihad leads to speculation (ẓann), and since both judges' ijtihad are equal in speculative strength, the first judgment is deemed stronger because it has already been executed; thus, it cannot be overturned by a later ijtihad that is merely equal.

Finally, an exception is mentioned: if the first judgment contradicts a clear, unequivocal text of the Qur'an regarding which the early scholars (salaf) had no difference of interpretation, then it is not enforced. Core Idea

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a fundamental principle of Islamic judicial procedure: certain categories of cases—those involving fixed punishments (hudud) and retaliation (qisas)—cannot be resolved through private arbitration. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A third party's claim on part of a share does not undo a valid division; compensation suffices.

After a division (qisma) of jointly owned property, it may emerge that one partner's share includes property rightfully belonging to a third party. Does this invalidate the entire division? Al-Quduri provides a clear Hanafi ruling: the division remains intact. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its rationale, distinguishing it from internal disputes that may rescind a division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario

2
State the ruling
Al-Quduri rules: 'the division is not rescinded' (لم تفسخ القسمة). The division remains sahih (valid).
3

The division was valid at its time. The defect only affects part of one share, so the other partner's portion is unaffected. Rescinding the entire division would be inequitable and unnecessary.
The remedy shifts to compensation: the affected partner must compensate the rightful owner.
4

If the partners themselves dispute the division's validity (e.g., mutual oath), the division may be rescinded. External claims do not trigger rescission.
This distinction protects the stability of completed transactions.
5

Conclude: the division stands; the partner whose share included the claimed property must compensate the third party with the property's value or return it if possible.
Compensation ensures justice without unravelling the entire division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three scenarios of reclamation (istihqaq) after partition and state the Hanafi ruling for each, including the intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
Reclaiming a specific part leaves partition intact; reclaiming an undivided share triggers dispute.

When a third party proves ownership of part of one partner's share after a partition, does the entire division collapse? The Hanafi school offers a nuanced answer, with disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and clarifies the three distinct cases that determine the outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base scenario
Understand the initial situation: two partners divide property, then a third party reclaims a portion of one partner's specific share.

2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the partition is not nullified; the affected partner gets compensation from the other partner. Abu Yusuf holds it is nullified because the third party was an undisclosed partner.
Focus on the practical difference: compensation vs. redoing partition.
3

Abu Hanifa's view relies on substitution (mithl) being sufficient; Abu Yusuf's view considers the presence of a third owner invalidates the original division.
The disagreement is about the nature of the reclaimed part.

This yields three distinct scenarios.

In practice, if the reclaimed part is specific (e.g., a particular plot), partition stands. If it is an undivided fraction of one partner's share (undivided within the share), dispute applies. If it is an undivided fraction of the whole property, partition is nullified.
This distinction is critical for judges and muftis.

Commentary
This passage is the index (fihris) of the book Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri. It lists the main topics covered in the section on judicial proceedings (qada') and the Book of Testimonies (Kitab al-Shahadat). The first part deals with the rules of litigation: what the judge does after a valid claim, the oath, refusal to take oath, and various disputes in sales, marriage, leasing, etc. The second part covers testimony: its ranks, conditions of witnesses, and specific rules for witnesses with special circumstances (blind, slave, relative, etc.). This index serves as a roadmap for the detailed fiqh rulings that follow in the book.

Key Terms
الشهادة (al-shahada): Testimony given in a legal case
اليمين (al-yamin): Oath taken by a party in litigation
القضاء (al-qada): Judicial proceedings; the function of a judge
