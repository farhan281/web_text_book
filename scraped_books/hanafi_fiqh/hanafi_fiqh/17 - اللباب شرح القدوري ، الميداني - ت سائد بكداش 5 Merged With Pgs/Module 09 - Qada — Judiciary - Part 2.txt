Oath Formulations in Claims of Usurpation, Marriage, and Divorce
Explains proper oath wording for three types of claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct oath formulation for claims of ghasb, nikah, and talaq, and explain the Hanafi rationale for swearing on the present legal status rather than the past act.

Core Idea
In judicial oaths, swear on the present legal status, not the past act – to avoid falsehood due to subsequent legal changes.

When a defendant is sworn in a legal dispute, the wording of the oath must be carefully chosen. The Hanafi school teaches that the oath should focus on the current legal reality rather than the past event. This lesson examines three classic cases – usurpation, marriage, and divorce – to understand how and why the oath formulation differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three claim types
The passage covers oaths in claims of ghasb (usurpation), nikah (marriage), and talaq (divorce). For each, note both the prohibited and the required oath formulation.
2
Distinguish oath on past act vs. present status
For each claim, the text contrasts an oath on the past act (e.g., 'By Allah, I did not usurp') with an oath on the present status (e.g., 'By Allah, you have no right to its return'). Identify the difference.
The past act oath is forbidden; the present status oath is required.
3
Understand the rationale (talil) for each
The passage gives reasons: in ghasb, the property might have been gifted or compensation paid; in nikah, an irrevocable divorce may have ended the marriage; in talaq, a revocable divorce may have been followed by renewal. These possibilities make the past act oath potentially false.
The rationale is to protect the defendant from committing a false oath due to legal changes after the act.
4
Generalize the Hanafi principle
From these cases, derive the principle: oaths in disputes should be administered on the present result (hasil) not the past cause (sabab). This prevents injustice and false testimony.
This principle applies whenever subsequent events could alter the legal status.

Oath on Result vs Cause: The Hanafi Doctrine in Property Disputes
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath form in property claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the oath on the result (al-hasil) and the oath on the cause (al-sabab), and apply the Hanafi rule to determine the proper oath form in a property dispute.

Core Idea
The oath focuses on the present right when the cause can change, not on the past act.

When two claimants present evidence against a defendant in possession of a house, a subtle but critical question arises: should the defendant swear about the past cause (e.g., 'I did not usurp') or about the present result ('You have no right now')? The Hanafi school's answer depends on whether the cause is removable. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, and the final relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Scenario
A defendant holds a house. Two claimants come forward: one claims all of it, the other claims half. Both provide evidence supporting their claims. The judge must decide what form of oath to administer to the defendant.
The defendant is in possession, so the burden of proof is on the claimants, but the oath is on the defendant once evidence is presented.
2
Identify the Dispute
The core dispute is whether the oath should be on the cause (e.g., 'By Allah, I did not usurp it') or on the result ('By Allah, you have no right to this house now'). Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue for the result; Abu Yusuf argues for the cause as the default.
The term 'cause' refers to the historical act that established the current state (e.g., usurpation, inheritance, purchase).
3
Analyze the Majority Reasoning
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad reason that the cause may have been superseded by a later legal event (e.g., a subsequent sale, gift, or divorce). Swearing on the cause could force the defendant into a false oath. Swearing on the result avoids this harm.
The text warns: 'because if he were sworn on the cause, the defendant would be harmed.'
4
Examine Abu Yusuf's Position
Abu Yusuf holds that the oath should generally be on the cause, because the cause is the root of the claim. However, if the defendant's denial specifically mentions the claim (e.g., 'You have no right as you claim'), then the oath shifts to the result.
This exception shows that Abu Yusuf acknowledges the practical need for result-based oaths in some cases.
5
Apply the Consensus Exception
Al-Hidayah clarifies that the result-based oath is the default when the cause is removable (e.g., a sale that can be rescinded). When the cause is permanent (e.g., slavery, lineage), the oath is on the cause by consensus.
Example: a Muslim slave claiming freedom from his master – the cause of slavery is permanent, so the oath is on the cause.

Core Idea
In defect claims, the oath is on the current state to avoid harming the defendant when the cause is removable.

When two parties claim a house in someone's possession, the judge must decide how to administer the oath. A key question arises: should the defendant swear on the cause of the claim or on the current state? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute, with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad preferring the current state to protect the defendant, while Abu Yusuf generally favors the cause. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and practical application.

The issue is on what basis the defendant should swear: on the cause (e.g., the original contract) or on the current state (the present situation)?

Dispute Over an Animal: Evidence and Dates in Hanafi Law
Rulings for conflicting evidence in animal ownership disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving competing evidence in an animal ownership dispute, distinguishing between cases where dates match, contradict, or are absent.

Core Idea
When both parties present evidence with dates, the animal's age determines which evidence is truthful — if it matches neither, both are false.

In Hanafi qada', when two claimants each produce evidence of ownership with a specific date, the judge must evaluate the apparent truth (zāhir). This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa wa al-Bayyinat guides the judge through several scenarios, using the animal's age as an objective check. Understanding these distinctions is essential for anyone studying Islamic judicial procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule of possession
Recall that in Hanafi law, possession (yad) gives a presumption of ownership. This is the baseline unless evidence overcomes it.
Possession is a key factor throughout the scenarios.
2
Analyze the effect of mentioning dates
If both parties present evidence with a specific date of birth, the judge checks the animal's age. A match with one date makes that evidence preferable because it aligns with external reality.
The date must be mentioned; otherwise, the default rule applies.
3
Handle ambiguous or contradictory age
If the age is ambiguous (cannot be determined) and the animal is in joint possession or with a third party, it is divided equally. If in one party's hand, that party wins because the date becomes irrelevant.
When age contradicts both dates, both evidences are invalid — falsehood is apparent.
4
Apply the condition of date mention
The entire ruling is conditioned on the parties having mentioned dates. If no dates are given, the simple rule applies: judgment is for the possessor.
The text explicitly restricts the ruling to cases with dates.

Preference for Rider over Rein-Holder in Animal Disputes
Ruling statement with brief reasons and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for priority when claimants have different physical control over an animal and apply it to analogous possession disputes.

Core Idea
The rider is preferred over the rein-holder because his control is more apparent and indicates stronger ownership.

When two people each claim an animal, the court looks at their physical possession (yad) and the degree of tasarruf (use). This passage from al-Quduri's matn, explained in al-Lubab, establishes a clear hierarchy: the rider is preferred over the one holding the reins, and the rider in the saddle is preferred over the rider behind. If both possess equally, the animal is shared.

Identify the parties and their actions

The ruling is based on the maxim that the party with more apparent tasarruf (use/control) is preferred. Determine why riding is more apparent than holding reins, and why being in the saddle is more apparent than riding behind.
The term 'أظهر' (more apparent) is key.
3
State the ruling for each scenario
From the passage: (a) rider > rein-holder, (b) rider in saddle > rider behind, (c) both riding => equal, so animal divided.

When both parties ride, their tasarruf is equal. The rule changes: the animal is not given to one but remains between them (i.e., each gets half or they share). This is the exception.
Equality of tasarruf leads to sharing.

Extend the reasoning to analogous cases
Consider other scenarios: e.g., one leads the animal by a halter vs. the other rides; or one sits in a cart pulled by the animal vs. the other holds the reins. Apply the same principle: the one with more apparent control is preferred.
The criterion is 'tasarruf aẓhar'.

Retraction of Testimony After Judgment: Liability Without Invalidating the Verdict
Second ruling on retraction after judgment from Kitab al-Ruju' with property example and analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school does not overturn a judgment based on retracted testimony and state the liability consequences for the witnesses.

Core Idea
After judgment, retraction does not invalidate the verdict but imposes liability on witnesses for the harm caused.

This lesson covers the second scenario: witnesses withdraw their testimony after the judge has ruled. The Hanafi view is that the judgment stands because it was based on the initial consistent testimony, but the witnesses must compensate the injured party to prevent injustice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Testimony has been given, the judge has issued a verdict based on it, and then the witnesses retract.
This is retraction after judgment (ba'd al-ḥukm).
2
State the ruling on the judgment
The judgment is not invalidated (lā yufsakh al-ḥukm). It remains valid and enforceable.
The judgment is not overturned because it was based on the original testimony which was consistent at the time of ruling.
3
Explain the reasoning for not invalidating
The retraction is a contradictory statement that comes after the judgment. The judgment was based on the first testimony, which was deemed credible. The later contradiction cannot retroactively undo the ruling.
Additionally, the first testimony has been strengthened by the fact that the judge relied on it and issued a ruling.
4
Determine liability for the witnesses
The witnesses become liable (ḍamān) for the damage they caused through their testimony. They must compensate the defendant (or the wronged party) for the loss.
Their retraction serves as an admission that they caused harm.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Kitab al-Ruju' an al-Shahadah (Book of Retraction of Testimony) from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with commentary from al-Maydani. The author first states the general rule: if witnesses retract their testimony before the judge issues a verdict, their testimony is dropped entirely. This is because a judge cannot build a ruling on contradictory statements; the retraction makes the earlier testimony unreliable. Since no verdict has been issued, no harm has been caused, so there is no liability on the witnesses. However, if the judge has already passed judgment based on the testimony, and then the witnesses retract, the judgment remains valid and is not overturned. The reason is that the later contradictory statement cannot undo a ruling that has already been executed. The judgment was based on the first consistent testimony, and the retraction is considered a mere claim that contradicts it. Nevertheless, the witnesses become liable for the damages they caused through their testimony, because by retracting they effectively admit that they caused harm to the defendant. The commentator clarifies that retraction is distinct from denial; denial (e.g., 'I never testified') is not considered retraction. Retraction is a positive statement withdrawing the earlier testimony. Furthermore, retraction must be made in the presence of a judge (not necessarily the same one) because it is a withdrawal of testimony, and testimony is given in the judge's court. The author then provides a specific application: if two witnesses testify about property and the judge rules in favor of the plaintiff, then the witnesses retract, they must pay the value of that property to the defendant. This is because their testimony caused the wrongful transfer of property. The analogy is made to a person who digs a well in a public path; they are liable for any resulting damage because their action caused harm. Similarly, the witnesses' testimony was a transgressive cause that led to loss, so they must compensate.

Key Terms
ضمان (ḍamān): Liability or compensation for harm caused; in this context, the financial obligation of witnesses to pay damages for the loss resulting from their testimony.
فسخ الحكم (faskh al-ḥukm): Invalidation or annulment of a judicial ruling. In this passage, the judgment is not invalidated despite retraction.

Condition for Valid Judgeship Appointment
Opening condition for valid judgeship appointment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a valid judgeship appointment in the Hanafi school and explain why the term 'al-muwalla' is used instead of 'al-mutawalli'.

Core Idea
A judge's appointment is valid only if conferred by a legitimate authority and the appointee has the required qualifications.

This passage opens the chapter on the etiquette of the judge by laying down a fundamental condition: the appointment of a judge is not valid until certain qualities are present in the appointed person. The author intentionally uses the passive participle 'al-muwalla' to emphasize that the appointment must come from another, not from oneself. This sets the stage for a discussion of the specific qualifications that a judge must possess.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Read the key sentence: 'لا تصح ولاية القاضي حتى تجتمع في المُوَلَّى'. The condition is that the appointment is not valid until qualities are present in the appointee.
2
Understand the term al-muwalla
Note that 'المولى' is a passive participle meaning 'the one appointed by someone else'. The author deliberately chose this over 'المتولي' (the one who takes the office himself) to stress that the appointment must be external.
This is a key grammatical point with legal effect.
3
Recognize the implication
The condition includes both the external appointment and the internal qualities of the appointee. Self-appointment is implicitly invalid.
4
Place in context
The chapter follows testimonies because judgment generally depends on testimony. This is mentioned to show the logical sequence.

Conditions for Valid Appointment of a Judge
Opening of Kitab Adab al-Qadi with ruling on conditions for judge's appointment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a judge's appointment in the Hanafi school and explain the ruling on a fasiq judge's appointment and dismissal.

Core Idea
A judge must meet the conditions of testimony and be a mujtahid; a fasiq can be appointed validly but is not recommended.

This lesson opens the chapter on the etiquette of the judge (Adab al-Qadi) with the fundamental requirement for a valid judicial appointment. The author explains that the judge must possess the qualifications of a witness and be capable of ijtihad. He then addresses the specific case of a fasiq (sinful) judge, clarifying the Hanafi position that such an appointment is legally valid though discouraged, and discusses whether a judge who becomes fasiq after appointment is automatically dismissed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions for valid appointment
The text states two requirements: possessing the conditions of testimony (shara'it al-shahada) and being a person of ijtihad (ahl al-ijtihad).
The conditions of testimony include Islam, sanity, maturity, freedom, and justice (adalah).
2
Understand the analogy between judiciary and testimony
The author links judiciary to testimony because both are forms of authority (wilaya). Whoever is qualified to testify is qualified to judge.
3
Apply the ruling on a fasiq judge
A fasiq can be appointed; the appointment is valid (sahih) but not recommended (la yanbaghi). This mirrors the rule for accepting a fasiq's testimony.
The ruling is based on the validity of the contract (taqlid) regardless of the person's justice.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi difference on automatic dismissal
If a just judge becomes fasiq (e.g., by taking a bribe), the relied-upon view (zahir al-madhhab) is that he does not automatically lose his position but deserves dismissal. Some mashayikh hold he is automatically dismissed because the appointing authority relied on his justice.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Distinguish between initial appointment and subsequent fasiq
The key difference is whether the appointing authority's consent was conditional on the judge's continued justice. In the first case (appointed already fasiq), no reliance on justice; in the second, reliance exists.

Commentary
This passage begins the book on the etiquette of the judge (Adab al-Qadi). The author first states a core ruling: the appointment (wilaya) of a judge is not valid unless the person being appointed (al-muwalla, the one given authority) possesses the conditions required for testimony (shahada) and is also a person of ijtihad. This means the judge must be upright, sane, Muslim, free, and able to derive rulings independently or follow a mujtahid if not fully qualified. The passage ends noting that 'be' (yakunu) is in the accusative, attached to 'are present', meaning the conditions include being a mujtahid.

Conditions for Judgeship Mirror Testimony Conditions
States that judgeship conditions are identical to testimony conditions due to shared authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that conditions for judgeship are derived from conditions for testimony and explain the basis of analogy (wilāyah).

Core Idea
Testimony and judgeship share the same legal qualification because both are forms of authority (wilāyah).

In this lesson, we examine a foundational principle from the chapter on the conduct of a judge (Adab al-Qāḍī). The author of Al-Lubāb establishes that the conditions required for a witness are identical to those for a judge. This is not an arbitrary rule but a logical consequence of the shared nature of the two roles: both involve exercising authority (wilāyah). Understanding this equivalence helps streamline the qualification criteria and avoids duplicating discussions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The passage asserts that the conditions for testimony apply to judgeship. Locate the explicit statement: 'What is required for the qualification of testimony is required for the qualification of judgeship.'
2
Locate the basis
The reasoning given is: 'because each of them falls under the category of authority (wilāyah).' Both roles involve making decisions that affect others' rights.
3
Recognize the type of proof
This is a logical analogy (qiyās) based on the common attribute of wilāyah, not a direct textual proof. Understand how analogy functions in Hanafi fiqh.
Qiyās is a valid source of law when based on a shared effective cause (ʿilla).
4
Recall the conditions for testimony
Standard conditions include Islam, sanity, adulthood, and justice (ʿadāla). These now apply to judgeship by extension.
Different schools may have additional conditions, but the principle of equivalence remains.
5
Note the practical consequence
A person who is unfit to testify (e.g., a fāsiq) is also unfit to be a judge. This prevents unqualified individuals from holding judicial authority.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A judge may only accept gifts from a mahram relative or from one who gave gifts before his appointment.

Judges are held to the highest standard of impartiality. This ruling protects the integrity of the judiciary by forbidding the acceptance of gifts that might create suspicion of bias, while allowing gifts from close relatives and pre-existing friends that are unlikely to influence judgment.

Guided Path
1

2
List the two exceptions
Exception 1: Gifts from a close relative (dhu rahim mahram). Exception 2: Gifts from someone who customarily gave gifts before the judge's appointment.
These exceptions are based on the assumption that such gifts are not intended to influence judgment.
3

The prohibition is to prevent corruption of the judiciary. The exceptions are permitted because the likelihood of improper influence is minimal: mahram ties are natural, and pre-existing gift habits indicate genuine friendship.
The Hanafi school emphasizes the intent (niyyah) and context in evaluating gifts.

Mahram: a relative with whom marriage is permanently forbidden (e.g., mother, sister, daughter). Muhadaat: the habit of exchanging gifts before the judge's appointment.
These terms determine the scope of the exceptions.

Judge's Acceptance of Gifts: Exceptions to the Prohibition
Ruling on judge's gift acceptance with exceptions and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two exceptions to the prohibition on a judge accepting gifts and apply the conditions that invalidate those exceptions.

Core Idea
A judge may only accept gifts from a mahram relative or a habitual giver; a lawsuit or increased amount reverts the gift to bribery.

This lesson examines the strict ethical boundaries placed on a judge regarding gifts. The Hanafi school generally prohibits judges from accepting gifts to avoid any appearance of bias. However, two specific exceptions allow acceptance: gifts from close family (mahram) and gifts from persons who habitually gave before the judge's appointment. Even these exceptions have limits—if the relative becomes a litigant or the giver increases the amount, the gift becomes unlawful. Understanding these nuances ensures that a judge's conduct remains above reproach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The text begins: 'ولا يَقبلُ هديَّةً' (He does not accept a gift). This establishes a blanket prohibition for a judge to accept any gift. Recognize that the default ruling is haram (prohibited) due to the potential for bribery.
The prohibition is from the matn of al-Quduri, a foundational Hanafi text.
2
Recognize the two exceptions
The exceptions are stated: 'إلا من ذي رحم محرم منه، أو ممن جرت عادته قبل القضاء بمهاداته' (except from a mahram relative or from one whose habit it was to gift him before assuming the judiciary). Note that the first exception is based on maintaining family ties (صلة الرحم), and the second is based on continuation of habit (جري على العادة).
Mahram relatives are those with whom marriage is permanently forbidden, e.g., parents, children, siblings.
3
Analyze the conditions limiting exceptions
Al-Hidayah commentary adds: 'حتى لو كانت للقريب خصومة: لا يقبل هديته' (even if the relative has a lawsuit, he does not accept his gift). Similarly, 'إذا زاد المهدي على المعتاد، أو كانت له خصومة' (if the giver increases beyond the usual amount, or if he has a lawsuit). These conditions show that the exceptions are not absolute—they are invalidated when there is a connection to a legal case or when the gift exceeds normal custom.
The underlying reason (talil) is that such gifts would be 'أكلاً بقضائه' (consuming due to his position).
4
Understand the ratio legis (talil)
The text explains: 'لأن الأول صلة الرحم، والثاني ليس للقضاء، بل جري على العادة' (the first is for maintaining family ties, the second is not for the judiciary but continuation of habit). The prohibition for all other gifts is because they constitute 'akl bi al-qada' (consumption via judgeship). This reasoning ties the ruling to the prevention of undue influence.
The same reasoning applies the other way: if the giver has a lawsuit, the gift is presumed to be for the case, not for kinship or habit.
5
Apply to hypothetical scenarios
Now practice: a judge receives a gift from his brother (mahram) who has no case—allowed. The same brother has a pending case—not allowed. A childhood friend who gave gifts before the judge's appointment gives a birthday gift of similar value—allowed. That friend gives a lavish gift worth ten times more—not allowed.
The operative rule: any gift that could be perceived as influencing the judge is haram.

Core Idea
A debtor is imprisoned immediately for debts arising from property or contracts, but for other debts, imprisonment only after the creditor proves wealth.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri explains the Hanafi rules on imprisoning a debtor who refuses to pay. The commentary expands on the matn by providing the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind the categories of debts and the procedure for investigating bankruptcy. Understanding this distinction is crucial for judges and legal practitioners in civil litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of debts
The passage distinguishes debts that arise from property that came into the debtor's hand (like price of sale, loan, hire) or from a voluntary contract (like dower, guarantee) from other debts (like khul' compensation, torts).
This classification determines whether imprisonment is immediate.
2
Understand the talil for immediate imprisonment
For the first category, the reasoning (ta'lil) is that possession of the property or voluntary undertaking of the obligation indicates solvency. The debtor is imprisoned without further investigation.

For the second category, if the debtor claims poverty, he is not imprisoned unless the creditor proves he has wealth. Then the judge may imprison him for a period (discretionary, not fixed) and investigate his financial situation.

The passage discusses whether evidence of bankruptcy (bayyinah al-iflas) can be accepted before imprisonment. The dominant view (Imam Abu Hanifa) says no; Qadikhan reports two narrations; Ibn al-Fadl favors delegation to the judge's discretion based on the debtor's character.
The practical difference: in the dominant view, the debtor must be imprisoned first; in the other view, he may be released immediately if evidence is credible.
5

The text states that the Imam's view (no acceptance before imprisonment) is al-mukhtar (preferred), but also notes that if poverty is apparent, evidence may be accepted quickly and the debtor released.
Thus, the operative ruling includes an exception for apparent poverty (al-Khaniyah).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A woman may judge all cases except hudud and qisas, but the appointing authority sins; correct judgments still stand.

This passage from al-Lubab presents the core Hanafi position on women's judgeship, along with later commentary that clarifies the scope and the underlying evidence. The ruling is that a woman can serve as a judge in all matters except hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The reasoning is based on analogy (qiyas) with testimony: since women's testimony is accepted in financial and family matters but not in hudud and qisas, similarly their judgment is accepted in those areas. However, some later Hanafi authorities, like al-Razi, expressed dislike (karahah) even for non-hudud cases. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) remains that it is permissible, though the ruler who appoints a woman incurs sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act being ruled upon is a woman serving as a judge (qadi). The Hanafi position is that it is permissible (ja'iz) in all cases except hudud and qisas.
Hudud are fixed punishments for crimes like theft and adultery; qisas is retaliation for homicide or bodily harm.
2
Locate the primary evidence (dalil)
The ruling is supported by analogy (qiyas) with the acceptance of women's testimony in non-hudud/non-qisas matters. Since a judge's function is akin to a witness's in establishing rights, the same limitations apply. Additionally, the hadith 'A people who entrust their affairs to a woman will never succeed' is addressed.
The hadith is interpreted as referring to general leadership (imarah), not specifically to judgeship.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the evidence proves the ruling)
The qiyas is based on the common illah: both testimony and judgment involve establishing legal rights. Since women's testimony is accepted in civil matters, their judgment is also accepted. The hadith does not invalidate a correct judgment; it only warns against appointing women as rulers.
Ibn al-Humam argues that if a woman judges correctly, there is no reason to overturn her decision merely due to her gender.

Note the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Some Hanafi authorities, such as al-Razi, consider women's judgeship in non-hudud cases to be disliked (makruh). The passage from al-Durr al-Mukhtar states that the appointing ruler sins, but the woman's judgment is valid.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school there is a range of opinions.
5
Determine the tarjih (preferred position)
The mufta bihi (operative ruling for fatwa) is that a woman may serve as a judge in non-hudud/non-qisas matters, as affirmed by al-Durr al-Mukhtar and other later Hanafi authorities.
The focus is on the validity of the judgment itself, not on the sin of the appointer.

(قال ابن عابدين ٢٩٨/٤): واختلفوا في المرأة فيما سوى الحدود والقصاص. اهـ، وقال في الدر المختار ٣٥٦/٤: «والمرأة تقضي في غير حد وقود، وإن أثم المولي لها، لخبر البخاري: لن يفلح قوم ولوا أمرهم امرأة». اهـ. ونص الرازي في خلاصة الدلائل ص: ٢٩١ على كراهية قضائها في غير الحدود. قال ابن الهمام في فتح القدير ٣٩١/٦: والكلام فيما لو وليت، وأثم المقلد لها بذلك، فقضت قضاء موافقاً لدين الله، أكان ينفذ أم لا؟ لم ينتهض الدليل على نفيه بعد موافقته ما أنزل الله إلا أن يثبت شرعاً سَلْب أهليتها، وليس في الشرع سوى نقصان عقلها، ... ثم هو النقصان منسوب إلى الجنس، فجاز في الفرد وخلافه، فقد يكون بعض أفراد النساء خيراً من بعض أفراد الرجال؛ ولذلك النقص الغريزي نَسَبَ صلى الله عليه وسلم لمن يوليهنَّ عدم الفلاح، ولهنَّ بنقص الحال، وهذا حقٌّ، لكن الكلام فيما لو وليت، فقضت بالحق: لماذا يبطل ذلك الحق؟». اهـ
It is permissible for a woman to judge in everything except hudud and qisas. (And a judge's letter to another judge is accepted in rights if witnessed before him.) (Ibn Abidin said: They differed concerning women in matters other than hudud and qisas.) And he said in al-Durr al-Mukhtar: 'A woman judges in other than hudud and qisas, even if the one who appoints her sins, due to the hadith of Bukhari: A people who entrust their affairs to a woman will never succeed.' Al-Razi stated in Khulasat al-Dala'il the dislike (karahah) of her judging in other than hudud. Ibn al-Humam said in Fath al-Qadir: The discussion concerns if she is appointed and the one who appointed her sins, then she judges in accordance with the religion of Allah, is it executed or not? The evidence does not preclude its execution after it agrees with what Allah revealed, unless it is established religiously that her capacity is negated, and in the Shari'ah there is only the deficiency of her intellect... then that deficiency is attributed to the gender, so it is possible in an individual contrary to that, for some women may be better than some men; that natural deficiency is why the Prophet (SAW) attributed lack of success to those who appoint them, and to them (women) a deficiency of condition, and that is true, but the discussion is if she is appointed and judges truthfully: why should that truth be invalidated?

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on women's judgeship along with a discussion of differing opinions. The core ruling from the matn is that a woman may serve as a judge in all matters except hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The reason given is evidentiary: women's testimony is not accepted in hudud and qisas, so by analogy, their judgment is also not accepted in those cases. The footnote from later Hanafi authorities (Ibn Abidin, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, Ibn al-Humam) shows that there is intra-madhhab disagreement on whether women can judge at all. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to al-Durr al-Mukhtar is that it is permissible in non-hudud/non-qisas cases, though the appointing ruler sins for appointing a woman due to the hadith. Ibn al-Humam argues that if a woman judges correctly, her judgment should not be overturned merely because of her gender, as her individual capacity may exceed that of some men. This reflects a nuanced Hanafi position that balances textual evidence and practical justice.

Key Terms
حدود (hudūd): Fixed punishments prescribed by Allah for specific crimes (e.g., theft, adultery, slander).
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation (eye for an eye) in cases of homicide and bodily harm.
