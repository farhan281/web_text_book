Dispute on Division Fee Distribution
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on fee division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the distribution of the divider's fee and identify the mufta bihi position with its evidence.

Core Idea
Fee for the divider is per head, not per share, because it rewards the uniform act of distinguishing shares.

When partners jointly own property and hire a divider to allocate shares, who pays what portion of the fee? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on this question. This lesson unpacks the rational arguments behind each view and explains which ruling is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the precise legal question: How should the fee for the divider (qassam) be distributed among the partners?
The fee is for the service of physically distinguishing the shares.
2
Present Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa rules that the fee is divided equally according to the number of partners (heads), regardless of their share sizes. Understand his reasoning: the fee is compensation for the act of distinguishing, which is a single uniform service.
His dalil: the act does not vary with share value, and calculating per share may cause hardship.
3
Present the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the fee is divided in proportion to each partner's share. Their reasoning: the fee is a burden of ownership, so it should follow the ownership ratio.
Their dalil: the greater the share, the more effort or benefit from the division.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical consequence. If partners have unequal shares (e.g., 1/2, 1/3, 1/6), under Abu Hanifa each pays an equal share of the fee; under the Sahibayn they pay 1/2, 1/3, 1/6.
The difference is significant when shares are unequal.
5
Determine the mufta bighi
Later Hanafi authorities (Al-Tashih, Al-Nasafi, Al-Mahbubi) adopt Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position due to its ease and avoidance of difficulty.
Tarjih authority: Al-Quduri presents both; later scholars prefer Abu Hanifa.

Partitioning Multiple Jointly Owned Houses: The Hanafi Intra-School Dispute
States Hanafi ruling with intra-school dispute on partitioning multiple shared houses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on partitioning multiple jointly owned houses in one city, explain the Sahibayn's alternative, and identify the relied-upon position with its supporting authorities.

Core Idea
Each jointly owned house must be partitioned separately; combining requires unanimous consent.

When co-owners share multiple houses in the same city, how should the partition be carried out? Can the judge combine them for convenience? This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by al-Maydani, presents a classical intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa mandates separate partition of each house, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) give the judge discretion to combine if beneficial. The commentary then traces how later Hanafi authorities resolved this difference, establishing the operative ruling for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Start by noting Abu Hanifa's position: each house is partitioned separately. Understand his reasoning: houses are different 'kinds' (ajnas) due to varying locations, neighbors, and amenities, making equalization in a combined partition impossible.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's alternative
Recognize that the two companions give the judge discretion to combine partitions if it is more beneficial for the co-owners, otherwise keep separate. Their basis is the judge's duty to act for the common good while preserving rights.
This introduces a maslaha-based exception.
3
Examine the later tarjih
See how later authorities like al-Isbijabi, al-Burhani, and al-Nasafi declare Abu Hanifa's view as correct (sahih) and that the judge's discretion is limited. This becomes the mufta bihi position.
Al-Hidayah adds that if houses are in different cities, even the Sahibayn do not combine them.
4
Apply to a scenario
Consider two partners owning two adjacent houses in the same city. Under the relied-upon view, each house must be divided separately, each owner receiving a half-share in each house. Under the Sahibayn, the judge might combine and then partition if doing so avoids fragmentation and benefits all.
The practical difference lies in the outcome: separate shares in each house vs. one owner gets one whole house and the other the other.
5
Confirm the authority weight
Note that the relied-upon view (Abu Hanifa) is from Zahir al-Riwayah and affirmed by later muta'akhkhirun, making it binding for fatwa in the Hanafi school.
The Sahibayn's view is a legitimate ijtihad but not the operative position.

Core Idea
Properties of different jins must be partitioned separately — mixing them is invalid.

When two people jointly own a house and a plot of land (or a shop), how should they partition? The general rule is that each property type is partitioned separately, regardless of convenience. This lesson explains the reasoning and notes a minority opinion within the Hanafi school.

State the general ruling

Classification of Dwellings in Hanafi Partition
Defines three dwelling types and their partition rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish among dar, bayt, and manzil and state the Hanafi partition rule for each type.

Core Idea
A house is its own jins; a room shares jins; an apartment sits between the two.

When co-owners inherit or share multiple dwellings, the method of partition depends on how the dwellings are classified. This passage from al-Durar al-Hukkam, a later Hanafi work, crystallizes the rule: houses (diyar) are separate property units; rooms (buyut) are of one kind; apartments (manazil) occupy a middle ground. Understanding this classification prevents invalid partitions and ensures each co-owner receives their rightful share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the passage and note the three terms: dar (house), bayt (room), manzil (apartment/dwelling unit larger than a room).
These are distinct in law because their partition treatment differs.
2
State the rule for houses (diyar)
Houses, whether adjacent or separated, are not combined into a single partition unless all co-owners mutually consent (taradi). Without consent, each house is partitioned separately.
Rationale: each dar is a complete, independent jins.
3
State the rule for rooms (buyut)
Rooms within a building are always partitioned together (combined into one partition) because they are all of the same kind (jins) and share the same purpose of dwelling.
Absolute combination: no need for consent.
4
State the rule for apartments (manazil)
Apartments are partitioned together only if they are within a single house (dar wahida) and are adjacent to each other. If they are in different houses or not adjacent, they are partitioned separately.
Manzil is above bayt in size; it is a more substantial unit, so its combination is conditional.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The classification is based on the degree of unity in the dwelling purpose. Rooms are closest in that unity; houses are most independent; apartments are intermediate. This is a form of qiyas on the meaning of dwelling (sukna).
This principle prevents arbitrariness in partition.

The basis is ikhtilāf al-jins (difference in genus). A house and a field are not the same kind of property, so mixing them in partition would cause injustice.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Lubab discusses the rules for partitioning properties of different types. It begins with the ruling: if a house (dar) and a field (day'a) or a house and a shop (hanut) are jointly owned, they are partitioned separately. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa, based on the difference in genus (ikhtilaf al-jins). A narration from Imam Muhammad holds that one may be partitioned within the other, but the relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's. The commentary then elaborates on the three categories of dwellings mentioned in al-Durar: dour (houses), buyut (rooms), and manazil (apartments). Houses, whether adjacent or separated, are not partitioned together unless by mutual consent. Rooms are always partitioned together because they are similar in being used for dwelling. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Partition Annulment Upon Third-Party Claim
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partition annulment upon discovery of a third partner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish and apply the three scenarios for annulment of partition (qisma) due to a third-party claim, and state the Hanafi position for each scenario.

Core Idea
A prior claim on a specific portion never annuls partition; on a common portion from the whole always annuls; on a common portion from one partner's share is disputed.

Imagine two partners divide a piece of land, and later a third person proves that a part of it actually belongs to them. Does the original division collapse? The Hanafi school gives a nuanced answer that depends on the nature of the claim. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, clarifying the three distinct scenarios that determine whether the partition stands or falls.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the trigger event
The act is a completed partition (qisma) of jointly owned property. The trigger is istihqaq: a third party successfully proves ownership of a portion of the divided property after the partition.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the partition stands; the affected partner recovers compensation from the other partner's share. Abu Yusuf holds that the partition is annulled because the presence of a third partner means consent was missing.
This ikhtilaf is limited to one specific scenario.
3
Distinguish the three scenarios
1) Claimed portion is mu'ayyan (specific, demarcated) → no annulment by consensus. 2) Claimed portion is shayi' (undivided) from the whole property → annulment by consensus. 3) Claimed portion is shayi' from the share of one partner only → the disputed case.
Understanding these scenarios is key to applying the correct ruling.
4
Apply the ruling to each scenario
Scenario 1: partition is valid (ijma'). Scenario 2: partition is annulled (ijma'). Scenario 3: the dispute is only here; mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (partition stands, compensation to the partner).
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether re-partition or compensation occurs.
5
Identify the underlying legal principle
The basis for Abu Hanifa: the right can be satisfied in kind (mithl) from the other's share, so no need to void the whole transaction. Abu Yusuf: the initial division lacked true consent because a partner was unknown.
This reflects a broader usul difference regarding contractual validity when a hidden interest appears.

Commentary
This passage from Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of istihqaq (establishing someone else's right) on a completed partition (qisma). The core issue: after two partners have divided property, a third party proves ownership of a portion—does the partition stand or is it annulled? Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that the partition remains valid; the affected partner recovers his loss from the other partner's share (ruju' bi hissah). Their reasoning: the rightful owner's claim can be satisfied in kind (mithl) from the other partner's portion without invalidating the entire division. Abu Yusuf argues that the partition is annulled because the discovery reveals that the original transaction involved a third partner whose consent was absent, rendering the division void. The commentator then clarifies that this ikhtilaf is limited to a specific scenario: when the claimed portion is a shayi' (undivided share) from the share of one partner only. If the claimed portion is mu'ayyan (specific and demarcated), the partition is not annulled by ijma'. Conversely, if a common share is claimed from the entire property (not just one partner's share), the partition is annulled by ittifaq (consensus). Thus there are three scenarios: specific part (no annulment by consensus), common part from whole (annulment by consensus), and the disputed case of common part from one partner's share (ikhtilaf). The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as indicated by the text's beginning.

استحقاق (istihqaq): The establishment of a prior right or ownership of a portion of property through proof, often by a third party after partition.
شائع (shā'i'): A common, undivided share; not specifically demarcated within the property.
معين (mu'ayyan): A specific, clearly defined portion of property that is demarcated from the rest.

Ruling on Muhaya'ah (Alternating Use of Common Property)
Ruling on the permissibility and conditions of muhaya'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on muhaya'ah and identify the conditions that affect its validity.

Core Idea
Muhaya'ah is a valid use-sharing agreement that survives death but ends when a co-owner demands partition.

Co-ownership often leads to disputes over usage. Islamic law provides flexible arrangements to manage common property. One such arrangement is muhaya'ah, where co-owners agree to alternate use without dividing ownership. This lesson unpacks the key rulings from the Hanafi school on this practice.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its basis
Identify that muhaya'ah is permissible (jā'iz) and note that it is allowed on the basis of istihsan (juristic preference).
Istihsan overrides any qiyas that might suggest otherwise.
2
Identify what does NOT invalidate
Confirm that the death of one or both co-owners does not invalidate the agreement.
3
Identify what DOES invalidate
Note that a demand for partition by any co-owner invalidates the muhaya'ah.
Partition is a stronger right and overrides the use agreement.
4
Apply to a physical division scenario
Understand that muhaya'ah can be implemented within a single house by dividing space (e.g., one takes upper floor, other lower).
This is a practical application of time-sharing.
5
Recognize rights to lease and rent
Each co-owner may lease his allocated share independently and collect the rent.
This preserves individual economic benefit.

Commentary
This passage from Lubab discusses muhaya'ah—an arrangement where co-owners of a property agree to use it in turns (time-sharing) or to divide its physical space for separate use while ownership remains undivided. The ruling: muhaya'ah is jā'iz (permissible) on the basis of istihsan (juristic preference), even though qiyas might suggest it is not allowed because it does not transfer ownership and could lead to disputes. However, the law accepts it for practicality. Importantly, muhaya'ah does not terminate upon the death of one or both partners; it remains in effect. However, if any co-owner demands a formal partition (qisma) of the property, the muhaya'ah agreement becomes invalid (batalat) because partition is a stronger right. The arrangement can be applied within a single house: for example, one co-owner occupies a specific room or floor (e.g., the upper floor 'ulw), and the other occupies a separate part (e.g., the lower floor 'sifl). Each co-owner retains the right to lease his designated share (ijārah) and collect its rental income (ghullah). This highlights that muhaya'ah is a division of use, not ownership; each retains ownership over the whole but agrees to specific use. The authority weight is not explicitly stated, but as part of the matn of al-Quduri, it carries weight of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
مهايأة (muhāya'ah): An agreement among co-owners to alternate use of a commonly owned property, either by time or physical division, without transferring ownership.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference: a method of reasoning that departs from strict qiyas for a more equitable result.
إجارة (ijārah): Leasing: a contract whereby one party transfers the usufruct of a specified asset to another for a known rental period and amount.
غلة (ghullah): Rental income or produce generated from property.
