Nukul in Qisas Claims: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Consequences
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nukul in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on nukul in qisas claims for limbs versus life, and explain the underlying reasoning (badhl ma'na vs. iqrar bi shubhah).

Core Idea
Nukul yields qisas for limbs but imprisonment for life (Abu Hanifa); Sahibayn impose arsh in both cases.

In Hanafi law, when a defendant refuses to take an oath (nukul) in a qisas claim, the consequence varies depending on whether the claim is for life or for a limb. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, focusing on their differing conceptual frameworks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the defendant's response
The claimant demands qisas; the defendant denies. The defendant is asked to swear by Allah. His refusal is nukul.
Nukul only occurs if the defendant has been offered the oath and refuses.
2
Distinguish between claims for limbs and claims for life
Abu Hanifa treats limbs differently from life because limbs are analogous to wealth, while life is inviolable.
The legal reasoning (talil) is key to understanding the difference.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's reasoning: badhl ma'na
Nukul is considered 'surrender of meaning' (badhl ma'na), which in wealth-related matters implies acceptance of the claim. Limbs are treated like wealth, so nukul leads to qisas.
Life is exempt because it cannot be made permissible by anyone's permission.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's reasoning: iqrar bi shubhah
They view nukul as an admission (iqrar) but with doubt (shubhah), insufficient for qisas but sufficient for arsh (financial compensation).
This applies uniformly to both limbs and life.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The correctors (musahihun) followed Abu Hanifa's view, making it the operative ruling for fatwa.
Thus, for life claims, imprisonment until confession or oath; for limbs, qisas.

Commentary
This passage deals with the legal consequences of nukul (refusal to take an oath) in claims of qisas. The matn states: if someone claims qisas against another for a non-lethal injury (e.g., amputation) and the defendant denies, the defendant is made to swear an oath. If he refuses (nukul), qisas becomes obligatory for that injury. If the claim is for life (qatl), the defendant is imprisoned until he either confesses or takes the oath. The commentator then reports that this is the position of Abu Hanifa. His reasoning: nukul is considered a form of 'badhl ma'na' (surrender of meaning, i.e., yielding to the claim) in matters related to wealth; limbs are given a monetary treatment and can be subjected to such surrender. Permissibility (ibaha) can operate on limbs, as seen in the examples of voluntary tooth extraction for pain or amputation for gangrene. Life, however, is too grave and cannot be made permissible by anyone's permission. If someone asks to be killed and the killer complies, diyah (blood money) is due, not qisas, because of doubt. Therefore, nukul in life-related claims does not lead to qisas but to imprisonment. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that in both cases, arsh (financial compensation) is due. Their reasoning: nukul is an admission (iqrar) but with doubt (shubhah), so it does not establish qisas (which requires full certainty) but does establish arsh, which is a lesser claim. The passage concludes that the correctors (musahihun) followed the saying of the Imam (Abu Hanifa), making his view the mufta bihi.

Key Terms
النكول (al-nukul): Refusal of the defendant to take the oath after it is offered to him.
بذل معنى (badhl ma'na): Surrender of meaning; a legal concept where nukul is treated as an implicit admission or yielding to the claim.
الأرش (al-arsh): Financial compensation for injuries, not including full diyah.

Witnesses Retracting Qisas Testimony: Liability for Diyah, Not Qisas
Ruling on witnesses retracting in qisas after execution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when witnesses retract after a qisas execution and explain why they are liable for diyah and not qisas.

Core Idea
False witnesses pay diyah from their wealth, not qisas, because they are indirect causes.

When witnesses testify in a qisas case causing execution, then retract, the Hanafi school holds them liable for diyah (blood money) but not qisas. This lesson examines the ruling, its rationale, and the practical implication that liability is personal, not tribal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The passage deals with two witnesses who testified to a crime warranting qisas (retributive execution). After the execution is carried out, they retract their testimony. The ruling: they must pay diyah (blood money) to the victim's heirs, and they are not subjected to qisas themselves.
The key point is that the retraction occurs after the execution, not before.
2
Understand the basis for diyah liability
The witnesses are liable for diyah because they have effectively confessed to causing the death through false testimony. The phrase 'ضمنا الدية' indicates they are responsible for the payment. The text explains it is 'في مالهما' (from their own wealth) because it is based on their own acknowledgment, and the 'aqilah (tribal insurance group) does not bear responsibility for a confession.
Confession-based liability is personal, not shared by the clan.
3
Explain why no qisas on the witnesses
The witnesses are not executed in turn because they did not directly commit the killing (لما يباشروا القتل) nor did they coerce the actual killer (ولم يحصل منهما إكراه عليه). Their role was indirect through testimony, which is not sufficient for qisas in Hanafi law.
Direct causation or coercion is required for qisas to apply.
4
Note the payment terms
The diyah is to be paid over three years (في ثلاث سنين). This is the standard payment period for diyah in Hanafi fiqh, providing ease for the liable party.
The three-year term is a concession to avoid undue hardship.
5
Recognize the proof structure
The ruling is supported by a rational proof (dalil aqli): the witnesses are not the direct killers nor did they coerce, so they cannot be subject to qisas. This is an analogy (qiyas) to other indirect causal agents who are not executed. The proof is based on the principle that qisas is only for direct culprits.
The proof is rationally derived, not from explicit text, but consistent with usul al-fiqh.

Compulsion to Kill: Liability for Qisas in Hanafi Law
Ruling on coerced killing and qisas liability with intra-madhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a coerced killer, explain why the act remains sinful despite duress, and identify who bears qisas liability according to the dominant Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Killing an innocent Muslim remains sinful even under threat of death; qisas falls on the coercer, not the coerced.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a severe moral and legal dilemma: a person forced under threat of death to kill an innocent Muslim. The Hanafi school confronts the tension between the absolute prohibition of murder and the reality of duress. We will explore the ruling, the rational proof, and the intra-madhhab dispute over qisas liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is being compelled to kill another under threat of death. The ruling: it is not permissible (لا يسعه) to proceed, and if the coerced does kill, he is sinful (آثم). This is the fundamental hukm.
Key phrase: 'لم يَسَعُه أَن يُقْدِم عليه'.
2
Locate the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The passage states: killing a Muslim is never permissible for any necessity (ضرورة), so this duress does not justify it. This is a rational derivation from the general sanctity of Muslim life.
The proof is based on the absolute prohibition of murder.
3
Analyze the cause (illa) for qisas liability
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that qisas is on the coercer because the coerced is like a tool (ālah); the effective cause of the killing is the coercion, not the coerced's free will. This is a mubashir (direct agent) vs. mutasabbib (indirect cause) distinction.
The coerced is the direct doer but not the true cause; the coercer is the cause.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Zufar holds qisas on the coerced (based on direct agency). Abu Yusuf holds no qisas on either (perhaps due to duress negating criminal intent). Identify the difference.
Intra-madhhab disagreement shows the range of ijtihad on causality.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text from al-Hidayah states: 'This is according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.' This is the relied-upon position for fatwa, as it is the dominant view and aligns with the principle that the coercer is the true cause.
Tarjih is based on the weight of the majority of the founders and the strength of the analogy.

Coercion to Kill: Prohibition and Liability for Qisas
States ruling on coercion to kill and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coercion to kill and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on who bears qisas, identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A coerced killer is like a tool; the coercer bears qisas in intentional murder.

This passage opens the Book of Coercion (Kitab al-Ikrah) with a clear principle: the coercer is treated as the true actor when the coerced is a mere tool. While the act of killing a Muslim remains absolutely forbidden, the question of who bears retaliatory punishment (qisas) divides the Hanafi school. We will explore the positions of the founding imams and identify the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage addresses a person coerced under threat of death to kill another Muslim. The ruling: the coerced must not comply even if killed; if he does, he is sinful.
2
Extract the underlying principle
The coerced is likened to a tool (ka-al-alah); this explains liability transfers to the coercer in property and homicide cases.
3
Locate the proof for the prohibition
Killing a Muslim is not permissible under any necessity; this is a rational (aqli) proof based on the absolute prohibition of murder.
4
State the main Hanafi position on qisas
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: qisas falls on the coercer if the killing was intentional.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Zafar: qisas on the coerced. Abu Yusuf: no qisas on either, but diyah on the coercer.
6
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as confirmed by later authorities like al-Ispijabi.

The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as confirmed by al-Ispijabi and subsequent authorities.
