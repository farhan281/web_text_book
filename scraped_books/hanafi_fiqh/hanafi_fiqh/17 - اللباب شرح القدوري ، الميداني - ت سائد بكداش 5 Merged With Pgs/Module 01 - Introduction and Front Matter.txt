Definition and Ruling of Wills (Wasaya) in Hanafi Fiqh
Opening the Book of Wills: definition and ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a will (wasiyyah), identify its default ruling (mustahabb), and explain when it becomes obligatory (wajib).

Core Idea
A will is a transfer of ownership attached to after death; it is recommended, not obligatory, unless needed to discharge religious duties.

This lesson opens the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasaya) from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri. We will explore the precise definition of a will as a deferred ownership transfer and its default ruling in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this foundation is essential before studying the detailed conditions and restrictions that follow.

Guided Path
1
Define the will (wasiyyah)
Locate the definition in the text: 'تمليك مضاف إلى ما بعد الموت' (ownership transfer attached to after death). Understand that it is a type of tamleek (transfer of ownership) that takes effect only after the testator's death.
This distinguishes it from a lifetime gift (hibah) which takes effect immediately.
2
Identify the default ruling
The text states 'الوصية غير واجبة، وهي مستحبة' (the will is not obligatory, it is recommended). Note the reasoning: it is a voluntary charity, like a gift.
The default hukm is sunnah ghayr muakkadah (recommended).
3
Recognize the exceptional obligation
The text adds: if the testator has unpaid zakat, fidya, or missed prayers, the will becomes wajib to discharge those duties. Locate the phrase 'وإلا فواجبة'.
This exception is based on the obligation to settle debts to Allah.
4
List the conditions
The passage lists conditions: testator must be legally capable (ahlan li al-tamleek), not fully indebted; legatee must be alive, not an heir, and not a killer; bequeathed property must be capable of ownership after death.
These conditions are essential for the will's validity.
5
Note the prohibition for heirs
The text concludes with the hadith: 'لا وصية لوارث' (no will for an heir). Understand that a bequest to an heir is invalid unless other heirs consent after death.
This prevents conflict with fixed inheritance shares.

The author then clarifies terminology: 'وصايا' (wills) is a general term including both the directive (وصية) and the appointment of an executor (إيصاء). 'أوصى إلى' means to appoint someone as executor, while 'أوصى ل' means to bequeath property. The core definition of a will (وصية) is: 'تمليك مضاف إلى ما بعد الموت' — a transfer of ownership that takes effect after death. Conditions for validity: The testator must be legally competent (e.g., sane, adult, not coerced) and not wholly indebted (debt does not prevent but must be paid first). The legatee must be alive at the time the will is made, not an heir (per the hadith), and not a killer of the testator (since killing prevents inheritance). The bequeathed property must be capable of ownership after the testator's death. The default ruling of a will is that it is recommended (مستحبة) but not obligatory (غير واجبة), because it is a voluntary transfer. However, if the testator has unpaid obligatory duties such as zakat, expiation for missed fasts, or missed prayers, then making a will becomes obligatory (واجبة) to discharge these liabilities. The commentary notes that according to al-Mujtaba, a will is permissible (مباحة) for a wealthy person but disliked (مكروهة) for a sinful person if it perpetuates their sin. The prohibition of a will for an heir is based on the well-known hadith, as it would disrupt the fixed shares of inheritance. This passage sets the foundational rulings for the entire chapter on wills.

Key Terms
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): An ownership transfer attached to after death (تمليك مضاف إلى ما بعد الموت).
الإيصاء (al-isa'): The act of appointing someone as an executor (جَعْلُهُ وَصِيّاً).
تمليك (tamleek): Transfer of ownership (making someone owner).
الموصى له (al-musa lahu): The legatee – the person to whom the bequest is made.
الموصى به (al-musa bihi): The bequeathed property or item.

Priority in Inheritance: Grandfather, Grandmothers, and Dhawi al-Arham
Inheritance priority rules and Dhawi al-Arham

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the inheritance priority of the grandfather over siblings, explain the exclusion of the maternal grandfather, and identify the categories of Dhawi al-Arham.

Core Idea
The grandfather inherits before siblings; maternal grandfather is excluded; Dhawi al-Arham inherit only when closer heirs are absent.

The index of Al-Lubab opens with key principles governing inheritance priority. Understanding who precedes whom and who is excluded is essential for solving any faraid case. This lesson introduces the rules for grandfather, grandmothers, and distant relatives (Dhawi al-Arham).

Guided Path
1
Understand the grandfather's priority over siblings
The index states 'الجد أولى بالميراث من الإخوة'. Note that in Hanafi fiqh, the paternal grandfather blocks siblings (both full and consanguine) from inheriting when present.
This applies to the true grandfather (paternal), not the maternal.
2
Recognize inheritance of multiple grandmothers
The phrase 'ميراث الجدات المجتمعات' indicates that when multiple grandmothers survive, they share a fixed share (1/6) equally.
Hanafi rules detail which grandmothers are eligible (maternal and paternal lines).
3
Identify the exclusion (hajb) of the maternal grandfather
'حجب الجد لأمه' means the maternal grandfather is excluded entirely by closer heirs (e.g., father, true grandfather). He is considered from Dhawi al-Arham.
Contrast with paternal grandfather who is a residuary heir.
4
Explore categories of Dhawi al-Arham
The index mentions 'باب ذوي الأرحام أصناف ذوي الأرحام'. Dhawi al-Arham includes relatives through females (e.g., maternal uncle, daughter's children). They inherit in the absence of all Quranic heirs and residuaries.
Hanafis give them priority over the Bayt al-Mal (treasury).
5
Determine the most entitled among Dhawi al-Arham
'أولى ذوي الأرحام بالميراث' indicates a ranking: children of daughters, then grandchildren, then others based on closeness.
A detailed ranking is covered later in the book.

Arithmetic Foundations: Makharij and Asl al-Mas'alah
Arithmetic section of index listing base denominators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the base denominator (asl al-mas'alah) for given sets of share fractions by identifying the least common multiple.

Core Idea
The base denominator is the least common multiple of all share denominators; it must be chosen from the standard origins (2,3,4,6,8,12,24).

The index lists a series of 'origins' (makharij) used as base denominators in inheritance calculations. Mastering the selection of the correct origin is the first step in solving any faraid problem. This lesson explains how to choose between 2,3,4,6,8,12, and 24.

Guided Path
1
Identify the denominators of the shares present
For each heir who takes a fixed share (fard), note the denominator of their fraction (e.g., 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8).
Denominators commonly found: 2,3,4,6,8,12,24.
2
Find the least common multiple (LCM) of the denominators
The LCM is the smallest number divisible by all denominators. This becomes the base number (asl al-mas'alah).
The index lists common LCM results: 2,3,4,6,8,12,24.
3
Check if the LCM is one of the standard origins
The index shows these origins are standard. If the LCM is not one of these, the case may involve 'awl (increase) or other adjustments.
For example, if denominators are 1/2 and 1/3, LCM is 6, which is in the list.
4
Set each share's portion in terms of the base number
Multiply the numerator by (base number / original denominator) to get the number of parts for each heir.
E.g., for 1/2 with base 6: 3 parts.
5
Sum the parts to verify total equals the base
If the sum of parts equals the base, no 'awl needed. If it exceeds, 'awl is applied (denominator increased).
The index separately lists 'awl of 6 and 12.

'Awl and Tashih: Handling Overflow and Uneven Shares
Adjustment methods: 'awl and tashih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when 'awl is required, apply 'awl to the base denominator, and determine when tashih (correction) is needed due to uneven division of shares.

Core Idea
'Awl increases the denominator when shares exceed the base; tashih corrects when the number of heirs does not divide evenly into the parts.

The index next addresses two critical adjustments: 'awl (increase of the denominator) and tashih (correction of problems). 'Awl occurs when the sum of fractional parts exceeds the base number. Tashih is needed when the number of heirs in a category does not divide evenly into their share of parts. This lesson covers both concepts and the four number relationships used in tashih: mutabayinan, mutamathilan, mutadakhilan, and mutawafiqan.

Guided Path
1
Recognize when 'awl is needed
If the sum of the parts (numerators) exceeds the base denominator, 'awl is applied by increasing the denominator to the sum of the numerators.
The index specifically mentions 'awl of 6 to 7,8,10 and 'awl of 12 to 13 or 15.
2
Apply 'awl: increase denominator to the sum
The new denominator becomes the sum of the parts. Each heir's share is recalculated as a fraction of the new denominator.
Example: parts 6, base 6, sum 7 → new denominator 7, shares become 6/7 of original.
3
Understand when tashih is required
After assigning parts, if a group of heirs (e.g., multiple sons) cannot divide their share evenly among themselves, tashih adjusts by multiplying the base by a correction factor.
The index lists 'إن وافقت سهامهم عددهم' (if shares agree with number) meaning if the share parts are divisible by the number of heirs, no tashih needed.
4
Classify the relationship between share parts and number of heirs
Four types: mutabayinan (coprime), mutamathilan (equal), mutadakhilan (one divides the other), mutawafiqan (common factor). Each determines the multiplication factor.
The index lists these four categories.
5
Apply the correct tashih factor
For mutabayinan: multiply base by the number of heirs. For mutamathilan: keep base same. For mutadakhilan: multiply base by the larger number divided by the smaller. For mutawafiqan: multiply base by the number of heirs divided by the common factor.
This is a complex step; further details are in the main text.

Commentary
This page is part of the index (fihris) at the end of the book, listing topics covered in the faraid (inheritance) section. The numbers are page references, though many are garbled in the OCR. The topics include technical terms of inheritance arithmetic: 'al-mutadakhilan' (the two overlapping numbers, a term in fractions), 'al-mutawafiqan' (the two congruent numbers), 'munasakhat' (inheritance transfers when one heir dies before distribution), and practical conversion methods between units (qirat to habbah). This index serves as a table of contents for the reader to locate specific rulings within the book.

Key Terms
اللباب (al-Lubab): Title of the book, meaning 'The Core' or 'The Quintessence', referring to Sharh al-Quduri.
المتداخلان (al-mutadakhilan): A term in inheritance arithmetic referring to two numbers where one is divisible by the other (e.g., 6 and 12).
المتوافقان (al-mutawafiqan): Two numbers that share a common divisor greater than 1 (e.g., 9 and 12 have a common factor 3).
المناسخات (al-munasakhat): Cases where an heir dies before distribution of the original estate, requiring a new distribution merging both deaths.
