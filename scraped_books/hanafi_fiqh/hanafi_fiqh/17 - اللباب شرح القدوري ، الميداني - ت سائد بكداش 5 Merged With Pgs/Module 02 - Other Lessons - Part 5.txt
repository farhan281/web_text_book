The Apostate's Right to Reclaim Property Upon Return
Ruling on apostate reclaiming property upon return after judicial declaration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which an apostate can reclaim his property upon returning to Islam and explain the legal reasoning (istighna') that supports the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
An apostate who returns after a judicial declaration can reclaim property still in kind because his legal need revives.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Hanafi fiqh: when an apostate (murtadd) has been judicially declared to have joined the enemy (Dar al-Harb) and his property has been distributed to heirs or treasury, his return as a Muslim revives his right to reclaim any property that still exists in its original form. The key condition is timing—the return must occur after the court's ruling. We will unpack the ruling, its condition, and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an apostate returning to Islam after having been judged to have joined Dar al-Harb. The ruling: he may take back any of his original property that remains in kind (ain) in the hands of his heirs or the treasury.
The ruling grants a right, not an obligation.
2
Identify the key condition
The condition is that the return occurs 'after the ruling of his joining' (ba'd al-hukm bi-lihaqihi). If he returns before the ruling, it is as if he never apostatized, and his property remains unaffected.
The phrase 'after the ruling' is explicitly emphasized.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (istighna')
The property was transferred to heirs because the apostate was considered legally dead—he no longer had a need (istighna'). Upon return and embracing Islam, his need revives, giving him a stronger claim than the gratuitous recipients.
This is analogous to revocation of a gift (hiba) when the donee is not a close relative.
4
Apply the condition to a hypothetical
If the apostate returns before the court ruling, the original distribution never occurs, so he retains full ownership. If he returns after, he reclaims only what remains in kind; if property has been consumed or sold, he has no claim.
The ruling only protects property still in its original form.

Ruling on the Apostate Who Returns to Islam and Finds His Property
Explains the ruling of an apostate's property upon return after judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an apostate can reclaim his property upon returning to Islam, and identify the conditions (after judgment, property in kind) and limitation (no claim if disposed).

Core Idea
An apostate returning after judgment may reclaim property only if it remains in kind; no compensation applies if sold.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with the delicate scenario of an apostate who returns to Islam after a judge has ruled his joining to dar al-harb. It clarifies that his property, now in the hands of heirs or the public treasury, may be reclaimed if it remains in its original form. The ruling balances the legal finality of the judgment with the apostate's renewed need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the apostate's reclaiming of his property after returning to Islam. The ruling is that he takes it (ya'khudhuhu) if found in kind.
This is a permissive ruling, not an obligation.
2
Note the critical condition: after judgment
The apostate must have returned after the judge's pronouncement (hukm bil-lihaq). If before, the apostasy is considered as if it never happened.
This shows the judgment has legal effect on property distribution.
3
Understand the limitation: property must be in kind
He can only take what remains physically (ayn) in the hands of heirs or public treasury. If the property has been sold or disposed, he cannot claim compensation.
The judgment validating the heir's ownership is not reversed.
4
Analyze the reasoning (dalil aqli)
The heir or treasury succeeded to the property because the apostate was considered 'dispensed with' (mustaghni). Upon return, the apostate needs it, so he takes it, analogized to a gift (hiba).
This is an istihsan reasoning based on need.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the property of an apostate who returns to Islam after the judge has pronounced the ruling of his apostasy (hukm bil-lihaq). The ruling states that if the apostate returns as a Muslim, he may reclaim any of his property that remains in the hands of his heirs or the public treasury, provided it is still in its original form (ayn). The reasoning is that the heir or treasury only took possession because the apostate was considered 'dispensed with' (mustaghni) – similar to a deceased person. Upon his return, he is in need, so he is given priority over them, as they acquired it without compensation, akin to a gift. The condition 'after judgment' is critical; if he returns before the judgment, it is as if he never apostatized, and his property is not inherited. The passage also clarifies that the property of his umm walad (mother of his child) and mudabbar (slave promised freedom after death) does not return to slavery. Additionally, if the heir has already sold or disposed of the property, the returning apostate cannot claim compensation from the heir, because the judgment confirming the heir's ownership was based on a valid proof and cannot be overturned. This follows the principle in al-Hidayah that a valid judgment is not reversed even if new circumstances arise.

بيت المال (bayt al-māl): The public treasury of the Muslims.

Validity of an Apostate Woman's Transactions
States that an apostate woman's transactions are valid during apostasy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of an apostate woman's financial transactions and explain the qiyas reasoning that supports it.

Core Idea
An apostate woman retains her inviolability in blood, so her property transactions remain valid by a fortiori analogy.

At first glance, one might assume that apostasy invalidates all acts—including financial transactions. However, Hanafi law distinguishes between the rulings for male and female apostates. This passage clarifies that a female apostate's transactions are valid because her apostasy does not extinguish her legal inviolability ('iṣmah) concerning her person; consequently, her property rights are even more protected. This ruling is derived through an explicit qiyās (analogy).

Guided Path
1
State the Ruling
Identify the subject (the female apostate) and the verdict. She transacts in her wealth during apostasy. The ruling is that her transaction is permissible (jā’iz) and valid.
The term jā’iz here means legally effective.
2
Locate the Rationale
The text provides the reason: 'because her apostasy does not remove her inviolability in respect to blood.' This means her life is protected; she cannot be killed without due process.
This distinguishes her from a male apostate who may be executed.
3
Understand the A Fortiori Argument (al-awla)
The author applies an argument of greater inclusion: if blood (a higher right) retains inviolability, then property (a lower right) is even more protected. Hence her transactions are valid.
This is a form of qiyās jaliy (obvious analogy).
4
Identify the Qiyās Structure
Original case (aṣl): protection of blood. New case (far‘): protection of property. Effective cause (‘illa): retention of legal inviolability despite apostasy.
The ‘illa is extracted by tanqīḥ al-manāṭ.
5
Note the Ikhtilāf
Other madhāhib (e.g., Shāfi‘ī) hold that apostasy voids all civil acts. The Hanafi position is the one presented here.
The dispute stems from differing views on the effect of apostasy on legal capacity.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the validity of an apostate woman's financial transactions. The commentator explains that although apostasy is a major sin, it does not strip the individual of legal capacity (hukm al-adamiyya) in matters of blood and property. The ruling is based on an argument from the obvious analogy (qiyas jaliy): since her apostasy does not justify killing her without due process (her blood remains inviolable), her property rights remain intact and her transactions are valid. This is the Hanafi position. Note that a male apostate's property is subject to different rules (confiscation after execution), but for a woman who is not executed, her transactions stand.

Key Terms

عصمة ('iṣmah): Legal inviolability that prevents harm to person or property.

States that public revenues are spent on general welfare of Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the categories of public revenue (kharaj, jizya, etc.) and state the Hanafi ruling that they are spent on the general welfare of Muslims.

Core Idea
Public revenues from kharaj, jizya, and similar sources are for the general welfare of Muslims, not the ruler's personal use.

This lesson covers a foundational ruling in Hanafi fiqh on the allocation of bayt al-mal funds. The passage lists the main sources of public revenue and explicitly states that they must be spent on the general welfare of the Muslim community (maṣāliḥ al-muslimīn al-‘āmmah). Understanding this principle is essential for grasping the Islamic fiscal system.

Guided Path

Identify Revenue Sources

State the Ruling
The text says 'يُصرف في مصالح المسلمين العامة' – it is spent on general welfare.
This is a clear, unqualified obligation.
3
Define General Welfare
Understand that 'maṣāliḥ al-muslimīn al-‘āmmah' includes defense, judiciary, infrastructure, salaries for public servants, support for the poor, etc.
From broader fiqh, this is a comprehensive term.
4

These funds are not the personal property of the Imam; they are a trust for the community.
Misallocation is a violation of sharia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sources of public funds in the Hanafi fiscal system and explain how these funds must be allocated to Muslim interests.

Core Idea
Public funds from kharaj, jizya, and similar sources are a trust for the Muslim community, spent on defense, infrastructure, and public servants.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on how revenues from land tax (kharaj), head tax (jizya), special payments from Banu Taghlib, and gifts from hostile parties must be spent. The passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies that these funds are not the personal property of the ruler but are designated for the general welfare of Muslims. Understanding this allocation principle is essential for grasping the Islamic fiscal system's ethical foundations.

Rulings on rebels without stronghold: prohibitions and permissions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five prohibitions regarding rebels without a stronghold and explain the underlying rationale (ta'lil) that distinguishes them from non-Muslim combatants.

Core Idea
Rebels without a stronghold are protected as Muslims; their lives, families, and property are inviolable.

This lesson examines rulings from the Book of Rebels (Kitab al-Bughat) in Hanafi fiqh. When a rebel group has no stronghold or fortress, the imam's army must observe strict limits: they may not kill the wounded, pursue the fleeing, or take captives or booty. These prohibitions stem from the fact that rebels remain Muslims, and the objective of fighting them is to suppress rebellion, not annihilate them. Understanding these rules prevents treating fellow Muslims as non-Muslim combatants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five prohibitions
List the five actions that are prohibited when dealing with rebels without a stronghold: finishing off wounded, pursuing fleeing, taking captives from children/women, taking property as booty, and (implicitly) executing captured rebels.
These are derived directly from the matn: 'do not finish off their wounded, do not pursue their fleeing, do not take captives from their children, do not take their property as booty.'
2
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
Analyze why these prohibitions exist. The commentary states: 'because the purpose is to disperse their assembly and scatter their group, which has been achieved, so there is no need to kill them.' Since rebels are Muslims, their lives and property are protected by the principle of ismah.
This is a rational (dalil aqli) argument based on the maqasid (objectives) of fighting rebels.
3
Examine the permissibility of using their weapons
Note that if Muslims need the rebels' weapons or horses, they may use them, because the imam can take a just person's property when necessary, so taking a rebel's property is even more justified.
This is a qiyas (analogy) from the property of a just person under necessity.
4
Determine the imam's handling of property
The imam holds the rebels' property (neither returns nor distributes) until they repent. Any taxes (kharaj or 'ushr) collected by rebels from territories they controlled are not collected again by the imam, since he did not protect them.
This preserves the rebels' ownership rights until they cease rebellion.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Rebels (Kitab al-Bughat) states rulings for dealing with rebels who do not have a stronghold (fiah) or fortress (hisn). The commentary expands on the matn of al-Quduri. The key principles: Rebels remain Muslims; their lives, families, and property are inviolable. Therefore, it is prohibited to kill their wounded, pursue their fleeing soldiers, take their women and children as captives, or seize their property as war booty (ghanimah). The underlying reason (ta'lil) is that the goal of fighting rebels is to suppress their rebellion, not to annihilate them. Once their assembly is dispersed, further killing is unnecessary. If a rebel is captured, he is not executed if his group lacks a stronghold; otherwise, execution is permitted to prevent resurgence. The imam is allowed to use the rebels' weapons and horses if Muslims need them, by analogy (qiyas) with the property of a just person under necessity. The imam holds the rebels' property (not distributing it as booty) until they repent, after which it is returned. Any taxes (kharaj or 'ushr) collected by rebels from territories they controlled are not re-collected by the imam because he did not provide protection during their rule. A textual variant is noted: 'yughnam' (taken as booty) vs. 'yuqsam' (distributed); the commentator prefers 'yughnam' to avoid repetition later.

Key Terms
البغاة (al-bughāt): Rebels who rise against the legitimate imam with a ta'wil (interpretation) and have a group.
الفئة (al-fi’ah): A stronghold, fortified position, or group that provides support and resistance.

Rebel Property and Collected Taxes in Hanafi Fiqh
Commentary on Hanafi rulings regarding rebel wealth and taxes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on rebel property and taxes collected by rebels, and explain the conditions under which repayment is required or not.

Core Idea
Rebel property is sequestered, not spoils; taxes paid to rebels are not recollected if the rebels used them properly.

This passage from al-Lubab, the commentary on al-Quduri, addresses a practical scenario of rebellion: what happens to the rebels' own wealth and to the taxes they collected from territories under their control. The Hanafi school distinguishes between rebels and other enemies, treating their property as held in trust rather than spoils of war. The rulings on taxes introduce a nuanced analysis of whether the taxpayers are liable to pay again to the Imam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on rebel property
Read the first part of the passage: the Imam 'imprisons' (yahbis) the rebels' wealth, sells the horses/weapons, and does not distribute as spoils. It is returned upon repentance.
This distinguishes bughat from other combatants.
2
Understand the ruling on taxes collected by rebels
The second part states that taxes (kharaj and 'ushr) collected by rebels from dominated lands are not collected again by the Imam, because his authority to collect depends on providing protection, which he did not provide.
The basis is the principle of himayah.
3
Analyze the exceptions for kharaj and 'ushr
If rebels spent the taxes in their proper channels (e.g., paying fighters), the taxpayers are discharged. For kharaj, even if rebels are rich, no re-collection because they are considered valid recipients (masarif). For 'ushr, re-collection is required only if rebels were rich and misused it.
The Hidayah commentary provides this detail.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider rebel property as spoils of war. The Hanafi position relies on the rebels having a valid interpretation (ta'wil), making them distinct.
This affects whether property is distributed or returned.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab (sharh of al-Quduri) covers two rulings concerning rebels (bughat). First, regarding the rebels' own property: the Imam confiscates their wealth to prevent them from using it for fighting, but he may sell their horses and weapons because keeping the price is more beneficial. Their wealth is not considered spoils of war (ghanimah), so it is not distributed among the Muslim army. Instead, it is held until they repent, then returned. This distinguishes rebels from other combatants. Second, regarding the taxes (kharaj and 'ushr) that rebels collected from territories they controlled: the Imam does not collect these taxes again from the people, because the Imam's right to collect is contingent on providing protection (himayah), which he did not provide to those territories under rebel control. If the rebels spent the collected taxes in their proper channels (e.g., paying salaries to fighters, etc.), then the taxpayers are discharged. If the rebels misused the funds, the taxpayers are advised (in matters between them and Allah) to repay the amounts, because the rightful recipients did not receive them. However, the commentary from al-Hidayah adds an important exception: for kharaj (land tax), there is no obligation to repay, because the rebels themselves are considered valid recipients (masarif) of kharaj, as they are fighters (muqatilah) engaged in the war effort, even if they are rich. For 'ushr (tithe on produce), if the rebels are poor, they are valid recipients, so no repayment; but if they are rich and misused the 'ushr, repayment is required. This nuanced ruling shows the Hanafi approach to balancing legal principles with practical realities of rebellion.

Key Terms
البغاة (al-bughāt): Rebels who have a valid interpretation (ta'wil) for their revolt against the legitimate Muslim ruler.
الكَرَاع (al-karāʿ): Horses and war equipment (weapons, armor).
يَحْبِس (yaḥbisu): The Imam takes possession of rebel property and holds it in trust; literally 'imprisons' or 'detains'.
الخراج (al-kharāj): Land tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis) or on agricultural land.
العشر (al-ʿushr): Tithe (one-tenth) on the produce of Muslim-owned land or on trade goods of Muslims.
المصارف (al-maṣārif): Categories of legitimate recipients or expenditure for state funds such as kharaj and 'ushr.

Rulings on Payments to Rebels (Bughat)
Ruling on sufficiency of payments to rebels with Hidayah commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which payments made to rebels (bughat) are sufficient and apply the Hanafi distinction between kharaj and 'ushr in such scenarios.

Core Idea
Payments to rebels suffice if funds reach rightful recipients; rebels are legitimate recipients of kharaj as fighters.

This passage from Kitab al-Bughat addresses a practical concern: if rebels take over a region and collect taxes, are the payers discharged? The ruling hinges on whether the rebels actually delivered the funds to the entitled beneficiaries. Al-Maydani, commenting on al-Quduri, clarifies the general rule and then quotes al-Hidayah on the special cases of kharaj and 'ushr, where the nature of the tax and the status of the rebels affect the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act of paying obligatory dues (zakat, kharaj, 'ushr) to rebels who have seized control. The ruling is on sufficiency (ijza): does the payment discharge the religious obligation? The text states that if the rebels spent the funds in their rightful place, the payment suffices; otherwise, repayment is required.
The 'rightful place' means the recipients entitled to that particular due (e.g., poor for zakat, state for kharaj).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the logical reasoning (talil): 'because the right reached its due owner' for sufficiency, and 'because it did not reach its due owner' for the need to repay. For kharaj, the dalil is that rebels are fighters (muqatilah), so they are among the legitimate recipients of kharaj even if wealthy. For 'ushr, if the rebels are poor, they are the rightful recipients as 'ushr is a right of the poor.
The Hidayah cites a general principle: kharaj is for fighters, 'ushr is for the poor.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
For the general rule: the legal cause (illa) is the delivery of the right to its entitled owner. For kharaj: the illa is that the rebels are muqatilah, which matches the purpose of kharaj (supporting the state's defense). For 'ushr: the illa is that the rebels are poor, matching the entitlement of the poor to 'ushr.
The Hidayah expansion shows that the rebels' status (muqatilah or poor) justifies their receipt of certain taxes.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Within the Hanafi school, there is no disagreement on these rulings; they are part of the Zahir al-Riwayah. Other madhhabs may differ on the legitimacy of rebels as recipients, but the passage does not address cross-madhhab disputes.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The position that no re-collection is required for kharaj and 'ushr under the stated conditions is mufta bihi, as affirmed by al-Hidayah and later scholars like al-Kasani and Ibn Abidin.
The tarjih authority is al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Permissible acts are not free from Allah's questioning; even a date will be accounted for.

You might think that as long as you avoid the haram and do the wajib, you are safe. The Hanafi tradition, through al-Ikhtiyar and other texts, proves this from the direct words of the Prophet (s) and the Quran. Understanding this deepens your sense of gratitude and mindfulness.

Core Idea
Silk is haram for men even with a barrier; but a small amount (4 fingers) is excused.

Silk (حرير) is a luxurious fabric that has specific rulings in the Hanafi school. Guided Path

The passage then provides an exception: if the silk is only the width of four fingers (أربع أصابع), it is permitted. This is based on the fingers of Umar (r), which are larger than average.

The proof for the permissibility for women and the prohibition for men is the hadith where the Prophet (s) pointed to gold and silk and declared them forbidden for males and permissible for females.

Using silk disrespectfully (as a pillow) removes the adornment, making it permissible per Abu Hanifa.

Trace the tarjih process
Later scholars like al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah chose Abu Hanifa's view. The mufta bihi is permissibility.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the analogy and the concept of istikhfaf.

This ruling is specific to silk use, not wearing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on employing eunuchs and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) based on sadd al-dhara'i'.

Core Idea
Employing eunuchs is makruh because it encourages the haram act of castration – a case of blocking the means.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from al-Quduri's matn on the employment of eunuchs. The ruling is not about the eunuch himself, but about the act of employing him. The underlying reason reveals a key principle: even permissible actions become disliked if they lead to something prohibited. We will break down the ruling, understand its proof, and see how it applies in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the act being ruled upon (employing eunuchs) and the hukm level (makruh).
The text explicitly says 'yukrah' (it is disliked).
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is not a revealed text but a rational argument: the act leads to haram mutilation.
This is a form of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means) in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The link: employing eunuchs creates demand for castration, which is prohibited. Thus, the employment is disliked to discourage the prohibited act.
The connection is indirect, hence makruh not haram.
4
Note the underlying principle
The principle is that means leading to haram become disliked (or even haram if direct).
Hanafi fiqh uses gradation: haram for direct causation, makruh for indirect.
5
Consider the ikhtilaf
Other schools may view it as haram if they see the causal link as stronger.
This is a legitimate difference in scholarly opinion.

Ruling on Castrating Animals
Ruling on animal castration with benefit justification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on castrating animals and explain the benefit-based proof (istidlal bil-maslaha) that justifies it.

Core Idea
Castrating animals is permissible because it brings clear benefit: better meat and fattening.

The Hanafi school permits the castration of animals based on clear benefit. While some might assume causing pain to animals is prohibited, this ruling shows that when a tangible benefit exists—such as improved meat quality and weight gain—the act becomes permissible. This lesson unpacks the short but decisive proof from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Locate the subject (castration of animals) and the phrase 'la ba'sa' (no harm) which signals permissibility. Note that the ruling level is mubah (permissible), not obligatory or recommended.
The absence of explicit prohibition in the primary texts is key; the author uses a common phrase for ibaha.
2
Extract the Proof (Dalil)
The text provides a rational proof: 'because it is done for benefit'. Identify that this is a maslaha (public interest) argument. The proof is aqli (rational), not naqli (textual).
Hanafi fiqh accepts maslaha as a valid basis for rulings when no direct nass exists, provided it does not contradict a clear text.
3
Understand the Benefit
The specific benefits mentioned are two: the animal becomes fat (tasmīn) and its meat improves (tayyib al-lahm). These are worldly benefits that serve human interests (eating better food).
The benefit is for humans, but it also indirectly benefits the animal by being well-fed; however, the primary intention is human utility.
4
Consider Limitations and Contrasts
Note that this permissibility is restricted to animals (al-bahā'im). Human castration remains haram (prohibited) by consensus, as it changes Allah's creation without overriding benefit.
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider animal castration makruh due to causing pain, but Hanafis outweigh the harm by the greater benefit (tahlil al-haram bil-maslaha).
5
Cross-Madhhab Awareness
Recognize that this is a mukhtalaf fih issue. While Hanafi permits it, other schools may restrict it. Understand the basis of ikhtilaf: weighing harm vs. benefit.
In practice, fatwa in the Hanafi school follows the permissibility view, as it is mufta bihi.

Accepting the Word of a Sinner in Transactions
Ruling on accepting the news of a sinner in transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting the word of a sinner and an unbeliever in transactions and explain the rationale of avoiding hardship (haraj).

Core Idea
To prevent hardship, Hanafis accept anyone's word in transactions—sinner or believer alike.

In everyday business dealings, it is often necessary to rely on information from various people, some of whom may be sinful or non-Muslim. This lesson explains why the Hanafi school permits accepting such reports, drawing on the principle of removing hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states: the word of a fasiq and a kafir is accepted in transactions (mu'amalat). Determine the hukm level: it is permissible (mubah) to rely on their word.
This does not apply to religious testimony or court testimony.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): because sinners and unbelievers are abundant among people, requiring a condition of integrity would cause hardship.
The key term is 'haraj' (hardship), which is a fundamental principle in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The link between the proof and ruling: the abundance (kathra) of such people makes it impractical to demand 'adala (integrity). The Shari'ah aims to remove hardship, so acceptance is permitted.
This is a clear application of the maxim 'al-mashaqqah tajlib al-taysir' (hardship brings ease).
4
Note the Ikhtilaf (Disagreement)
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) generally require a witness to be 'adil (just) even in transactions, but the Hanafi school dispenses with this to avoid hardship.
The difference stems from the weight given to 'adala vs. the principle of removing hardship.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi Tarjih
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) because it is based on a clear maslaha and is supported by the practice of people (urf). Al-Hidayah endorses it.
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is authoritative.

Core Idea
Looking at a non-mahram woman's face is permissible only if safe from desire; otherwise it is haram.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational Hanafi ruling on looking at a non-mahram woman's face. The passage from Quduri’s matn and its commentaries establishes that permissibility hinges on the absence of sexual desire. We will analyze the condition, the proof text, and the exceptions for judges and witnesses.

Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is looking (nazar) at the face of a non-mahram woman (ajnabiyya). The default ruling is not stated explicitly; instead it is conditional.

The condition is safety from desire (shahwa). The text says: if he does not feel safe from desire, he should not look. This implies that if he is safe, looking is permissible.
Shahwa is defined later in the passage as physical arousal or heart's craving.

Analyze the hadith proof
The proof is a hadith (though weak) threatening punishment for looking with desire. This indicates prohibition (haram) of such a look. The Hanafi school uses this dalil despite its weakness, supported by other authentic hadiths and the principle of sadd al-dhara'i.
The text also mentions many authentic hadiths prohibiting looking at non-mahram women.
4
Note the exception for judges and witnesses
A judge or witness may look at the woman's face even if they fear desire, because of necessity for justice and testimony.
This exception overrides the general prohibition when desire is present.
5
Understand the definition of shahwa
The passage provides multiple definitions: some say physical erection, others say heart's desire without physical change. The key is that the ruling depends on the presence of any form of sexual desire.
This definition is important for practical application.

Later Hanafi Fatwa: Prohibition of Looking at a Young Non-Mahram Woman's Face Due to Fitna
Presents the later Hanafi fatwa that prohibits looking at young non-mahram women's faces unconditionally, with reasons and Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why later Hanafi scholars changed the ruling on looking at a young non-mahram woman's face to unconditional prohibition, and cite the evidence from Quran and early Hanafi authority.

Core Idea
In later times, looking at a young non-mahram woman's face is prohibited even without desire due to prevalent fitna.

This lesson explores a significant development in Hanafi fatwa: the shift from conditional permissibility to an absolute prohibition for young women. The passage from al-Durr al-Mukhtar and other later texts shows that the ruling changed because of widespread corruption (fitna). We will examine the reasons, the evidence from the Quran (via al-Jassas), and how this reflects the Hanafi principle of fatwa evolving with time.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the later fatwa's statement
Al-Durr al-Mukhtar states: 'The permissibility of looking is conditioned by absence of desire; otherwise forbidden. This was in their time. As for our time, it is prohibited for a young woman.' This shows a clear temporal change.
The phrase 'في زماننا' indicates the fatwa is time-bound.
2
Identify the reason for the change
The reason is the prevalence of fitna (moral corruption and temptation). The text says 'لخوف الفتنة' and 'لغلبة الظن بوقوع الفتنة'.
Fitna here means the likelihood of sinful desire or social disorder.
3
Analyze the Quranic evidence cited
The passage quotes al-Jassas interpreting Quran 33:59, which commands women to draw their cloaks (jalabib) over themselves. Al-Jassas says this verse indicates that a young woman is commanded to cover her face from non-mahram men.
This is a Hanafi tafsir that supports the later prohibition.
4
Understand the application of sadd al-dhara'i
By closing the door to potential sin (looking at a young woman's face may lead to desire), the scholars deemed it prohibited even if the immediate look is free from desire.
This is a standard Hanafi principle.
5
Note the continuing exception for judge/witness
Even in the later fatwa, the exception for judges and witnesses remains. The text repeats the exception from al-Hidayah.
This shows that necessity still overrides the general prohibition.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Hazr wa al-Ibahah deals with the ruling on a man looking at the face of a non-mahram woman. The author begins with the conditional statement: if a man is not safe from desire (shahwa), he may not look at her face except for a need (such as marriage or medical treatment). The Hidayah clarifies that the permissibility of looking at her face is contingent upon being safe from desire. If he is not safe from desire, looking is prohibited except for a genuine necessity. The proof cited is a hadith (although weak) warning of punishment for looking with desire. The passage then defines shahwa: some say it is physical arousal, others say it is the heart's craving. There is an important exception: a judge and a witness may look at the woman's face, even if they fear desire, because of the necessity of justice and testimony. However, the later Hanafi position according to al-Durr al-Mukhtar and others is that this conditional permissibility applied in earlier times; in later times (i.e., when moral corruption is widespread), it is forbidden for a young woman to uncover her face and for a man to look at it, due to the likelihood of fitna. This is a clear example of fatwa changing with time and context within the Hanafi school. The passage concludes by noting that many authentic hadiths also prohibit looking at non-mahram women, and that the prohibition is especially emphasized for young women to prevent fitna.

Key Terms
فتنة (fitna): Moral temptation, social disorder, or the likelihood of sin. In this context, it refers to the strong presumption that looking at a young woman's face will lead to desire or illicit relationship.
شهوة (shahwa): Sexual desire; defined as physical arousal or the soul's craving for forbidden pleasure. In the later fatwa, the prohibition is not contingent on its presence.
أجنبية (ajnabiyya): A woman who is not a mahram to the man.

Ruling on Judge and Witness Looking at a Woman's Face
Permissibility and conditions of looking at a woman's face for judicial or testimonial purposes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on looking at a woman's face for judicial testimony and identify the conditions that change its permissibility.

Core Idea
Looking at a woman's face for testimony is permissible only without desire and when necessary; later fatwa forbids it for young women due to fitna.

A judge or witness may need to see a woman's face to establish justice or give testimony. The Hanafi school balances this necessity against the risk of fitna (temptation). This lesson unpacks the ruling: initially broad permissibility, then restricted by desire and time, and finally a fatwa change for young women. Understanding these nuances is essential for proper application in judicial settings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core act and initial ruling
Locate the statement from the matn: a judge or witness may look at a woman's face even if he fears desire. Note that this is an initial permissibility (mubah) for the sake of necessity.
The text says 'wa in khafa an yashtahi' (even if he fears desire), indicating that mere fear does not preclude permissibility.
2
Note the condition added by al-Durr
Read the commentary from al-Durr: permissibility is conditioned on the absence of desire (shahwah). If desire is present, looking becomes haram. This refines the initial blanket permission.
This distinction between 'fear of desire' and 'certain desire' is critical.
3
Understand the later fatwa shift
The text then states that in our time (later fatwa), young women (shabbah) are prevented from uncovering their face entirely due to the prevalence of fitna. This is not a change in principle but an application based on changed social circumstances.
Support comes from al-Jassas and al-Haskafi, and the Quranic verse on hijab (33:59).
4
Distinguish between tahammul (acquiring) and ada' (performing) testimony
The passage distinguishes looking to acquire testimony (tahammul) when desire is present: it is not permissible because there exist others who do not desire, so no necessity. For performing testimony (ada'), necessity may justify looking even with desire, but the majority view prohibits it.
The text says 'al-asahh annahu la yubahu' (the correct view is that it is not permissible) for tahammul when desiring.
5
Synthesize the mufta bihi position
Combine the elements: today, a young woman may not uncover her face in public; a judge/witness may only look at a woman's face without desire and for genuine necessity; if desire is certain, it is haram; if only fear, it is permitted but cautious.
The mufta bihi is from al-Haskafi: young women are prevented, and looking is only allowed without desire and for necessity.

Commentary

Moreover, in later times (zamanina), the fatwa has shifted to preventing young women from uncovering their faces altogether due to the prevalence of fitna. This is supported by the early Hanafi imam al-Jassas, who cited the verse of hijab (Quran 33:59) to mandate covering the face for young women. The commentator then adds that this ruling is a fortiori when fitna is certain or predominant. The passage also clarifies that the permissibility of looking for testimony is conditional on the absence of desire when acquiring testimony (tahammul al-shahadah), because there are others who can do it without desire, so necessity does not apply. However, when actually giving testimony (ada'), the necessity may justify looking even with desire, as there is no alternative.

The Awrah of a Man in Front of Another Man
Defines the awrah of a man relative to another man and the ruling on looking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the limits of a man's awrah relative to another man and apply the ruling on looking at a beardless youth.

Core Idea
A man's awrah before another man is only from the navel to the knee; all else may be looked at if free from desire.

In Hanafi fiqh, the rules of looking are tied to the concept of awrah. This passage from Al-Lubab provides a clear ruling on what a man may look at when seeing another man, including the special case of an attractive beardless youth. Understanding these boundaries is essential for practicing proper Islamic conduct in daily interactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is looking at another man. The default ruling is permissibility (mubah) — a man may look at another man's body.
2
Define the awrah exception
The exception is the awrah: the area from the navel to the knee. Looking at this area is not permissible.
The navel itself is not part of the awrah; the knee is included.
3
Apply the ruling to the amrad
Even if the other man is a beardless youth (amrad) with a beautiful face, looking is permissible as long as there is no fear of desire.
Caution is advised due to potential fitna, but the ruling remains permissibility.
4
State the condition for the exception
The permissibility of looking beyond the awrah is conditional on being safe from desire. If desire is present, even looking at the face may become prohibited.
5
Memorize the awrah boundary
The exact boundary: from the navel (surrah) to the end of the knee (muntaha al-rukbah). This is the awrah for a man with another man.
In Hanafi fiqh, the navel is excluded, the knee is included.

Muslim Bequeathing to Non-Muslim: Hanafi Ruling
Ruling with proof from qiyas jaliy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Muslim bequeathing to a dhimmi and explain the qiyas-based proof from lifetime gifts.

Core Idea
Equality in transactions under dhimma permits bequests between Muslim and non-Muslim.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hanafi school on bequests (wasaya) between Muslims and non-Muslims. The passage from Al-Hidayah uses qiyas (analogy) to lifetime gifts, establishing that the same transactional rights apply after death. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi principle of transactional equality under the dhimma contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the text: 'It is permissible for a Muslim to bequeath to a non-Muslim, and for a non-Muslim to bequeath to a Muslim.' Note that the ruling covers both directions.
The non-Muslim referred to is the dhimmi (protected citizen).
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof is given: 'because by the dhimma contract they are made equal to Muslims in transactions; for this reason donation is permitted from both sides during life, so likewise after death.'
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
3
Analyze the Qiyas
Identify the asl (original case): lifetime gifts between Muslim and dhimmi (permitted). The far' (new case): bequests after death. The illa (effective cause): equality in transactions under the dhimma contract.
The illa is the contractual equality, not mere friendship.
4
Note Cross-Madhhab Status
This ruling is mukhtalaf fih (disputed). Some schools restrict bequests to non-Muslims, but the Hanafi position is clear permissibility.
The Hanafi reliance on dhimma equality is a distinctive feature.
5
Summarize the Hanafi Position
The mufta bihi (operative) position: permissible. The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is recorded on this point.
