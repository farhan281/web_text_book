Testimony in Hudud: The Preference for Concealment
Ruling on testifying in hudud cases, with choice and preference for concealment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony in hudud and explain why concealment is preferred over disclosure.

Core Idea
In hudud, concealment is better than disclosure because of two conflicting goods: establishing punishment and avoiding disgrace.

This passage addresses a crucial aspect of Islamic criminal procedure: the role of witnesses in hudud cases. The Hanafi school teaches that a witness has a choice between concealing and disclosing an offense, but concealment is strongly recommended. The reasoning is rooted in balancing the societal duty to enforce punishments with the mercy of covering faults, supported by prophetic hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on testimony in hudud
The text states that the witness is given a choice between concealment and disclosure, but concealment is better.
The ruling applies specifically to hudud, not to other rights.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The choice exists because the witness is between two merits: establishing the hadd and avoiding disgrace.
This balancing indicates a permissive ruling with a preferred course.
3
Examine the dalil (evidence)
The Prophet's hadith about covering with a garment and the promise of covering in the Hereafter are cited as evidence for preference.
These ahadith are ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for preference.
4
Apply the principle of dar' al-hudud
The text alludes to teaching the warding off of punishments, which reinforces concealment.
Dar' al-hudud bi al-shubuhāt is a key Hanafi principle.
5
Contrast with other madhahib
Some schools may require testimony without choice, making disclosure obligatory.
The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih; this is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes testimony in hudud cases (fixed punishments for crimes like theft, zina, etc.) from testimony in other matters. The author explains that for hudud, the witness has a choice between disclosing and concealing the offense. This is because there are two conflicting goods: the duty to establish the punishment (to uphold public order) and the duty to avoid exposing people's faults (to preserve dignity). The preferred course is concealment, based on two hadiths: (1) The Prophet told someone who gave testimony about a crime: 'If you had covered him with your garment, it would have been better for you.' (2) The Prophet promised divine covering for those who cover a Muslim. The text also alludes to the principle of 'tadri' al-hudud' (warding off punishments by doubts), where the Prophet himself taught methods to avoid punishment, such as asking ambiguous questions. Thus, it is recommended to conceal sins that lead to hudud when possible, but if the right of another person is involved, disclosure may be necessary.

Testimony in Hudud: Concealment Preferred, Theft Exception
Ruling on testimony in hudud with proof and theft exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony in hudud, explain why concealment is preferred, and apply the exception for theft by requiring the witness to say 'akhadha' rather than 'saraqa'.

Core Idea
Concealment is better in hudud, but for theft wording must protect the hand.

This lesson addresses a nuanced ruling: when a witness observes a crime subject to a fixed punishment (hadd), is it better to conceal or disclose? The Hanafi school, based on hadith evidence, gives a general recommendation of concealment, but carves out a notable exception for theft. Understanding this balance between upholding Allah's rights and avoiding disgrace is essential for applying the law faithfully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is testifying in a hudud case. State the general ruling: the witness is given a choice (takhyir) between concealing and disclosing, and concealment is afdal (better).
This applies to all hudud except theft.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited: the hadith 'If you had concealed him with your garment, it would have been better for you' and the general hadith on concealment. Also note the practice of talqeen al-dar' (prompting withdrawal).
These hadiths are ahad, but their meaning is clear.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'khayr' (better) indicates preference (mandub), not obligation. The Prophet's action and companions' practice reinforce that concealment is recommended.
The ruling is mandub, not wajib.
4
Note the exception for theft
The text states it is 'wajib' in theft to say 'akhadha' (he took) and not 'saraqa' (he stole). Understand the reasoning: to combine the victim's right to recover property with protecting the thief's hand from amputation.
This exception makes the ruling specific and non-contradictory.
5
Evaluate cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require strict testimony without recommendation of concealment. Recognize the practical difference: in Hanafi, a witness may ethically choose silence; in Shafi'i, he must testify.
The thamarah al-khilaf is a different duty on the witness.

The First Rank of Testimony: Four Men for Zina
Statement of the requirement for zina testimony

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Zina testimony requires four men—no women, no fewer men.

In Hanafi law, testimony is ranked by the severity of the case. The highest rank is for zina, where the Quran explicitly demands four male witnesses. This lesson establishes that ruling and its proof from Surah al-Nisa.

State the requirement

The verse is a direct textual proof with qati' thubut and dalalah.

Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'fastashhidu' indicates obligation, and 'arba'atan minkum' specifies exactly four male witnesses.
This excludes women and lesser numbers.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi
The Hanafi school holds this position unanimously; no alternative is relied upon.
Cross-madhhab: all schools agree on four men for zina.

Coercion to Apostasy or Insulting the Prophet: Conditions and Ruling
Ruling with proof on coercion to kufr or sabb al-nabi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for valid coercion (ikrah) and apply the ruling on outward utterance of kufr or insulting the Prophet under duress.

Core Idea
Outward utterance of kufr under threat of death is permissible if the heart remains firm in faith.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on coercion to apostasy or insulting the Prophet. The passage establishes that minor threats do not constitute valid duress for such grave sins; only imminent danger to life or limb permits outward compliance with mental reservation. This ruling balances the preservation of life with the sanctity of faith, and contrasts with the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is being coerced? Uttering kufr or insulting the Prophet. The ruling is that outward utterance is permissible ONLY if valid ikrah exists.
2
Define valid ikrah
Valid duress (ikrah) requires fear of death or loss of a limb. Mere shackling, imprisonment, or beating does not constitute ikrah for kufr.
This is derived from an a fortiori qiyas with wine drinking.
3
Trace the dalil: qiyas jaliy
Since such threats do not permit drinking wine (a minor sin), they certainly do not permit kufr (the greatest sin). The proof is qiyas jaliy (a fortiori analogy).
The source text says 'ففي الكفر أولى' – a fortiori.
4
Apply taqiyyah (mental reservation)
When valid ikrah exists, one may utter the words outwardly while dissembling (yuwarri) – showing compliance but intending in the heart the opposite. No sin if the heart is firm in faith.
Taqiyyah is a key concept; it preserves life without violating inner conviction.
5
Note the reward for steadfastness
If one endures and is killed without uttering kufr, he receives reward. This shows that resisting is spiritually superior but not obligatory.
The Hanafi school recognizes the merit of patience but does not require it under duress.

Hadd for Coerced Zina and Ruling on Coerced Apostasy
Intra-Hanafi dispute on coercion and hudud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on hadd liability for coerced zina and explain why coerced apostasy does not dissolve marriage.

Core Idea
Hadd for coerced zina is dropped if coercion comes from any overpowering person; coerced apostasy does not dissolve marriage.

This passage from Lubab al-Quduri addresses two distinct scenarios under coercion: zina and apostasy. The first presents a known intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: does coercion from a non-sultan remove the hadd punishment? The second confirms a general principle: coerced apostasy is not genuine, so the marital bond remains intact. Understanding these rulings clarifies how the Hanafi school balances criminal liability with the mitigating factor of duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
Read the first sentence: the act is coerced zina. Abu Hanifa says hadd is obligatory unless coercion is by the sultan. The Sahibayn say hadd is not obligatory.
The dispute hinges on whether effective coercion can come from anyone else.
2
Analyze the basis of each position
Abu Hanifa argues that only the sultan's coercion is truly irresistible; others cannot fully compel. The Sahibayn argue that any overpowering person (mutaghallib) can coerce effectively.
The Hanafi term for such a coercer is 'mutaghallib'.
3
Identify the relied-upon fatwa
The text explicitly states 'wa 'alayhi al-fatwa' after the Sahibayn's view, and Qadi Khan confirms this, as do later authorities like al-Burhani and al-Nasafi.
Thus the operative ruling for fatwa is the Sahibayn's position.
4
Apply the same logic to coerced apostasy
The text then states that if a man is coerced into apostasy, his wife does not separate from him. The reason is that coerced apostasy is not genuine apostasy.
This is not a disputed point in this passage.
5

A sultan is recognized either by the pledge of the elite or by effective domination; an unjust sultan is deposed only if he lacks the power to resist.

This passage from al-Khaniya presents the Hanafi view on political legitimacy. Initiating Combat Against Rebels: The Hanafi Dispute on Timing
Intra-Hanafi dispute on initiating combat with rebels who have assembled.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on initiating fighting against rebels and evaluate the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Hanafis disagree: may the imam strike rebels who assemble but haven't attacked? The evidence (assembly) vs. the act (fighting) determines the ruling.

This passage from al-Maidani's commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a key question in the fiqh of rebellion (ahkam al-bughah). The imam is required to call rebels back and expose their doubts before any fighting. But what if they have already gathered and fortified themselves? Al-Quduri states that the imam must not initiate fighting until the rebels actually attack. However, the Hanafi scholar Khwaharzada disagrees, arguing that if the rebels have assembled and refused to return, the imam may attack preemptively because the effective cause (al-dalil) for fighting is their assembly and rebellion, not the act of fighting. This intra-Hanafi dispute has practical consequences for the conduct of the imam in maintaining order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The imam is recommended to call rebels back to the community and obedience, and to expose their doubts. The core ruling is that he does not initiate fighting until the rebels initiate.
This is based on the precedent of Imam Ali (RA) sending a delegate to the Khawarij at Harura before battle.
2
Understand the rationale for not initiating
The phrase 'إبلاء للعذر وإقامة الحجة عليهم' indicates the purpose: to provide an excuse and establish the proof against them, giving them every chance to return peacefully.
Ibla' al-'udhr is a recommended step in Hanafi law regarding rebels.
3
Note the variant position
Khwaharzada holds that it is permissible to initiate fighting if the rebels have already assembled and fortified themselves. His evidence is that the ruling follows the evidence (al-dalil), which is their assembly and refusal, not the actual act of fighting.
He argues that waiting may make it impossible to repel their evil, so necessity permits preemptive action.
4
Compare the proofs
Quduri relies on the precedent of Ali (RA) and the idea that fighting should only occur after they initiate. Khwaharzada focuses on the effective cause: the assembly itself constitutes rebellion.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Identify the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear: under Quduri's view, the imam must wait even if rebels are assembled; under Khwaharzada, he may strike preemptively if their assembly poses a threat.
The text does not give a tarjih, but the default position (Quduri) is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

ما يفعله الإمام مع البغاة : (دعاهم) : أي الإمام، أو نائبه استحباباً (إلى العود إلى الجماعة) والطاعة ، وكَشَفَ عن شبهتهم) إن أبدوا شبهةً، لعل الشرَّ يندفع به. ولا يبدؤهم بقتال حتى يبدؤوه، فإن بدؤوا : قاتلهم حتى يُفرق جمعهم. * (ولا يبدؤهم بقتال حتى يبدؤوه) ؛ إبلاء للعذر، وإقامة للحجة عليهم، ولذا بعث علي رضي الله عنه إلى أهل حَرُوراء مَنْ يُناظِرُهم قبل القتال (فإن بدؤوا) بالقتال: (قاتلهم حتى يُفرِّق جَمْعَهم). قال في «الهداية»: هكذا ذكر القدوري في «مختصره»، وذكر الإمام المعروف بخُوَاهَرْ زاده أن عندنا يجوز أن يبدأ بقتالهم إذا تعسكروا واجتمعوا؛ لأن الحكم يُدَارُ مع الدليل، وهو الاجتماع والامتناع، وهذا لأنه لو انتظر الإمامُ حقيقة قتالهم: ربَّما لا يُمكنه الدفع ؛ فيدار على الدليل؛ ضرورةً دَفْعِ شَرِّهم.
What the imam does with rebels: (He calls them) i.e., the imam or his deputy, as a recommendation, (to return to the community) and obedience, and (he exposes their doubts) if they present doubts, so that evil may be repelled. And he does not initiate fighting them until they initiate it, and if they initiate, he fights them until he disperses their assembly. *(And he does not initiate fighting them until they initiate it) [This is] to provide an excuse and to establish the proof against them, and for this reason Ali, may Allah be pleased with him, sent to the people of Harura someone to debate them before the fighting. (If they initiate) fighting: (he fights them until he disperses their assembly). He said in al-Hidayah: Thus al-Quduri mentioned in his Mukhtasar, and the imam known as Khwaharzada mentioned that according to us it is permissible to initiate fighting against them if they encamp and assemble; because the ruling follows the evidence, which is their assembling and refusal, and this is because if the imam waits for their actual fighting, perhaps he will not be able to repel [them]; so he acts on the evidence out of necessity to repel their evil.

Commentary
This passage from al-Maidani's commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the imam's conduct towards rebels (baghi). It begins with the recommended action: the imam or his deputy should call the rebels back to obedience and expose their doubts, hoping to avoid conflict. The core ruling is that the imam does not initiate fighting against the rebels until they start fighting; this is based on the precedent of Imam Ali (RA) with the Khawarij at Harura, where he sent a representative to argue with them before any battle. Once the rebels initiate combat, the imam may fight them until their assembly is broken. However, al-Maidani then cites a difference: al-Quduri's text states the above, but Khwaharzada (a Hanafi scholar) holds that it is permissible to initiate fighting if the rebels have already gathered and fortified themselves, because the effective cause (dalil) for fighting is their assembly and rebellion, not the actual act of fighting. Waiting could endanger the imam's forces. This is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Quduri's position (also the apparent position of the Hidayah) leans towards not initiating, while Khwaharzada allows preemptive strikes for necessity.

Key Terms
البغاة (al-bughah): Rebels who rise against a just imam with a ta'wil (interpretation).
شبهة (shubhah): A doubtful argument or misinterpretation that rebels use to justify their rebellion.
إبلاء العذر (ibla' al-'udhr): To present the excuse (i.e., to give the rebels a chance to return) before fighting.

Key Takeaway
