Expiation of an Oath: Three Options and the Minimum Garment Dispute
Ruling with proof on kaffarah options and a dispute on garment minimum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three permissible expiations for a broken oath according to the Hanafi school, identify the minimum required garment for the clothing option according to the preferred view, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on that minimum.

Core Idea
The expiation for a broken oath offers three options, and the dispute over the minimum garment illustrates how custom ('urf) determines legal sufficiency.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn presents the three alternatives for kaffarah al-yamin: freeing a slave, clothing ten poor persons, or feeding them. The commentary by al-Maydani elaborates on the details, especially the required quality of the garment, where an intra-Hanafi dispute arises. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the evidence, and the practical implications of the dispute, enabling you to apply these rules in real scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Kaffarah is wajib upon breaking a deliberate oath. Determine the hukm level (wajib) and note that it is an obligation with graded options.
The obligation is established by Quran 5:89 and consensus.
2
List the three options from the matn
Freeing a slave (any slave qualifies), clothing ten poor persons, or feeding ten poor persons. Each option is detailed in the sharh.
The three options are sequential; one must choose one of them.
3
Examine the details of each option
For clothing: each poor person receives a garment suitable for middle-class people, usable for more than three months. For feeding: each receives half a sa' of wheat/flour or a sa' of barley/dates or two filling meals.
The minimum garment is disputed; see next step.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on minimum garment
Muhammad al-Shaybani requires covering most of the body; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require covering the 'awrah (so that the wearer is not called naked in custom). The mukhtār (preferred) view is the latter.
The dispute is based on different interpretations of what constitutes 'covering nakedness'.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and thamarah al-khilaf
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: garments like trousers alone are insufficient. The practical consequence: if a person gives a garment that does not cover the 'awrah, it does not count for kaffarah clothing, but its value may be considered for feeding.
The thamarah is that the donor must ensure the garment meets the minimum to fulfill the obligation.

Commentary
This passage covers the three alternatives for expiating a broken oath (yamin), based on Quran 5:89. The matn states: freeing a slave, clothing ten poor persons, or feeding them. The sharh explains each option. First, freeing a slave: any slave qualifies (Muslim or not, adult or child), same as in zihar expiation. Second, clothing: each of the ten must receive a garment suitable for middle-class people, usable for more than three months. The minimum garment is debated: according to a narration from Muhammad (al-Shaybani) in al-Hidayah, it must cover most of the body. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the minimum is what covers the nakedness (e.g., a waist-wrap) such that trousers alone would not suffice because the wearer would still be considered naked in custom. The correct (sahih) view is the latter. However, if the garment does not meet the minimum for clothing, it can be counted toward feeding by value. Third, feeding: each poor person receives half a sa' of wheat/flour or a whole sa' of barley/dates, or two filling meals, identical to the feeding in zihar. The proof is the verse: 'its expiation is feeding ten poor persons' (Quran 5:89). The verse is the primary dalil, but the details come from the Sunnah and ijtihad of the Hanafi imams.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation required for breaking a valid oath or for certain sins; here it is a choice among freeing a slave, clothing ten poor, or feeding ten poor.
عتق رقبة (itq riqabah): Freeing a slave; any slave qualifies, as detailed under zihar expiation.
كسوة (kiswah): Clothing each of ten poor persons with a garment suitable for middle-class, usable for more than three months, covering the 'awrah minimally.
إطعام (it'am): Feeding ten poor persons each half sa' of wheat/flour or one sa' of barley/dates or two filling meals.

Khums: Recipients and Abrogation of Prophet's Share
Explains the recipients of khums and the dropping of the Prophet's share after his death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of khums recipients and explain why the Prophet's share and safiy dropped after his death.

Core Idea
The Prophet's share of khums and safiy ended with his death; relatives now receive only if poor.

Khums, the one-fifth of war booty, is distributed to specific groups. But not all shares are permanent: the Prophet's personal share and safiy ceased with his death. Moreover, the entitlement of relatives shifted from supporting the Prophet to need-based poverty. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position on these changes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five original recipients of khums
From Quran 8:41, five categories are listed: Allah, the Prophet, relatives, orphans, poor, wayfarers. The Hanafi school interprets 'Allah' as a blessing and 'Prophet' as a separate share.
The share of 'Allah' is not a separate distribution but an opening invocation.
2
Understand why the Prophet's share and safiy dropped
The Prophet's share (sahm al-nabi) and safiy (special item selection) were specific to his person and were abrogated by his death, as the text states: 'سقط بموته' (dropped with his death).
3
Analyze the change in relatives' entitlement
During the Prophet's life, relatives (Banu Hashim) deserved the share for their support (nusrah) of him. After his death, the basis becomes poverty; thus only the poor among them receive.
Wealthy relatives are excluded.
4
Note the Hanafi nuance on priority
Poor relatives from the Prophet's clan are included among the poor, but they are given precedence over other poor because accepting ordinary charity (sadaqah) is not permissible for them.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the distribution of khums (one-fifth of war booty). The base matn states the recipients: a share for the poor (masakin) and a share for wayfarers (ibn al-sabil). Poor relatives (of Banu Hashim) are included in these three categories (orphans, poor, wayfarers) and are given priority over others because accepting charity is not permissible for them, so they must receive from khums. Wealthy relatives, however, receive nothing because entitlement is based on need. The mention of Allah in the verse (Quran 8:41) is not a separate share but an opening blessing. The Prophet's share (a fifth of the khums) and the safiy (an item he would choose for himself from the booty) both dropped with his death. The share of relatives (dhawi al-qurba) was deserved during the Prophet's lifetime for their support of him; after his death, it is based on poverty, as the purpose of support ended.

Key Terms
خمس (khums): One-fifth of war booty (ghanimah) that is set aside for specific recipients as mentioned in Quran 8:41.
سهم النبي (sahm al-nabi): The Prophet's personal share from the khums, which he used for public welfare; it was dropped after his death.
صفِيّ (safiy): An item the Prophet would choose for himself from the booty before division, such as a sword or horse; also dropped after his death.

Arab Lands are 'Ushr Lands: The Hanafi Ruling and Its Rational Proof
Establishes that all Arab lands are 'ushr lands with a rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that all Arab lands are 'ushr lands and explain the rational proof based on the impossibility of dhimma for Arab polytheists.

Core Idea
No Arab land can be kharaj because no Arab polytheist can become a dhimmi.

In this passage, the author establishes a fundamental principle of Hanafi land taxation: all lands originally inhabited by Arab polytheists are 'ushr lands, not kharaj lands. This ruling is based on the rational proof that kharaj requires a dhimma contract, and such a contract is invalid for Arab polytheists. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the laws of agricultural taxation in Islamic finance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage explicitly states: 'All the land of the Arabs is 'ushr land.' This is the basic ruling.
2
Identify the proof
The author provides the proof: 'because kharaj is not initially obligatory except by the contract of dhimma, and the contract of dhimma for an Arab polytheist is not valid.'
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the impossibility of dhimma.
3
Understand the reasoning
The Prophet ﷺ did not accept jizya from Arab polytheists; they had to accept Islam or be fought. Hence, they cannot become dhimmis. Therefore, their land cannot be subject to kharaj.
This distinguishes Arab polytheists from other non-Muslims.
4
Distinguish 'ushr and kharaj
Ushr is a zakat obligation on Muslim-owned land; kharaj is a tax on the land of non-Muslims (dhimmis). The passage defines both.
This distinction is essential for correct classification.
