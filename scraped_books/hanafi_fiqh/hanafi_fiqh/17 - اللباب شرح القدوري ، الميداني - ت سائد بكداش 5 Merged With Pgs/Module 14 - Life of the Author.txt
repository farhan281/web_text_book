Paternity Claim When Child Born Between Six Months and Two Years
Sets conditions for paternity claims with timing between six months and two years

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a seller's paternity claim when a slave gives birth between six months and two years of sale, and identify the consequences if the buyer confirms.

Core Idea
A seller's paternity claim for a child born within two years is only accepted if the buyer confirms, voiding the sale and making the mother an umm walad.

The passage continues the discussion of paternity claims for a child born to a slave woman after her sale. Here, the child arrives more than six months but within two full years. The Hanafi ruling depends on whether the buyer confirms the seller's claim, and if both claim paternity, the buyer's claim takes precedence. Understanding this scenario clarifies the intersection of ownership, pregnancy timing, and lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the timing conditions
The child is born after more than six months but before the completion of two years from the sale. Recognize that this period allows for the possibility of conception after the sale.
Six months is the minimum pregnancy duration; two years is the Hanafi maximum.
2
Determine the default ruling for the seller's claim
Without the buyer's confirmation, the seller's claim is not accepted. The default is that the child belongs to the buyer because the mother was in his possession at the time of birth.
3
Analyze the effect of the buyer's confirmation
If the buyer confirms the seller's paternity, the lineage of the child is established to the seller, the sale is voided, the child is free, and the mother becomes the seller's umm walad.
The sale is voided because it turns out the child was conceived before the transfer of ownership.
4
Consider the scenario when both claim paternity
If both the seller and buyer claim the child, the buyer's claim is given priority because he currently owns the mother, making conception possible during his ownership.
This is from al-Qahistani's commentary, cited in the text.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents several detailed rulings regarding paternity claims for a child born to a slave woman. First, if the child is born more than six months after the sale but within two years, the seller's paternity claim is not accepted unless the buyer confirms it. The commentary notes that the seller's claim could be accepted based on the possibility that the child was conceived after the sale (i.e., while the mother was with the buyer), but the default is rejection without confirmation. If the buyer does confirm, the lineage is established, the sale is voided, the child is free, and the mother becomes an umm walad to the seller. Second, if the child is born after two years from the sale, the seller's claim is not valid even if the buyer confirms? No, the text says: if the buyer confirms, the lineage is established but it is considered that the child was conceived through marriage (not through being an umm walad), so the sale remains valid and the mother does not become an umm walad. Third, if the child dies and the seller claims him, but the birth occurred less than six months from the sale, the mother does not acquire umm walad status because the child's lineage was not established (since the claim is made after death when it is not needed for inheritance or other purposes). Fourth, the passage begins a ruling for when the mother dies and the child remains, but the text cuts off. Overall, these rules illustrate the Hanafi sensitivity to time periods and the interplay between paternity, slavery, and marriage.

Key Terms
حولين (hawlayn): Two years; the maximum period of pregnancy according to the Hanafi school.
استيلاد (istīlād): The status of being an umm walad; the legal recognition that a slave woman has borne her master's child.

Paternity Claims for Birth After Two Years or After Child's Death
Covers paternity claims when birth exceeds two years and when child dies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling for a seller's paternity claim when the child is born after two years of sale, and when the child dies before the claim is made.

Core Idea
A child born more than two years after sale is attributed to a marriage, not to the seller, so the sale stands; if the child dies, the mother does not become umm walad.

This lesson covers two additional scenarios from the same passage. First, when the child is born after the two-year maximum pregnancy period deemed possible from the seller's ownership. Second, when the child dies before the seller claims paternity and the birth occurred within less than six months from the sale. These rulings further illustrate how timing and death affect paternity and the status of the mother.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the birth after two years scenario
The child is born after two full years from the sale. Since two years is the Hanafi maximum pregnancy period, this birth cannot be attributed to the seller's ownership. Therefore, the seller's claim is not valid unless the buyer confirms.
Even with confirmation, the lineage is established but based on marriage, not ownership.
2
Determine the effect of buyer's confirmation for after-two-years birth
If the buyer confirms, the child's lineage is established to the seller, but it is considered that the child was conceived through a valid marriage (not while the mother was his slave). Hence the sale is not voided, and the mother does not become an umm walad.
The child is free because the father is free, but the mother remains a slave.
3
Examine the child death scenario
If the child dies before the seller makes a claim, and the birth occurred less than six months from the sale, the mother does not become an umm walad. This is because the child's lineage was not established after death (since no need), and the mother's status depends on the child's status.
The passage suggests that if the child had been alive, the lineage could be established; but after death, the claim is irrelevant for inheritance, etc.
