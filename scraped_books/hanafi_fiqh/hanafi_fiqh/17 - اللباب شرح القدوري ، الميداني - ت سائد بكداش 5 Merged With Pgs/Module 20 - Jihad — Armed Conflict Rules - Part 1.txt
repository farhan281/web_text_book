Defining Siyar and Jihad in Hanafi Fiqh
Terminology and chapter placement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'siyar' and 'jihad' as used in Hanafi legal works and explain why the Book of Siyar follows the Book of Ikrah.

Core Idea
Siyar is the Prophet's conduct in war; Jihad is the call to truth, fighting those who reject.

The Book of Siyar opens with a discussion of its placement after Ikrah and the definition of key terms. This lesson clarifies what Siyar and Jihad mean in the Hanafi tradition and how they relate to the previous chapter.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Placement
The author explains that Siyar (military expeditions) is placed after Ikrah (coercion) because both involve deterrence: Ikrah deters Muslims from disobedience, while Jihad deters disbelievers from kufr and tyranny. This is a progression from lower to higher.
This shows the thematic coherence of the Hanafi fiqh arrangement.
2
Define Siyar Linguistically and Technically
Linguistically, siyar (plural of sira) means 'way' or 'conduct'. Technically, in Sacred Law, it refers specifically to the Prophet's conduct in his military expeditions (maghanzi).
The Hidayah is cited for this technical definition.
3
Define Jihad Linguistically
Jihad linguistically is the masdar of jahada fi sabil Allah, meaning 'striving in the path of Allah'. Many jurists title the book 'Kitab al-Jihad'.
The link between siyar and jihad is highlighted.

The Hanafi Ruling on Jihad: A Communal Obligation
Ruling of Jihad with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that Jihad is fard kifayah, explain the proof through qiyas, and identify when it becomes fard 'ayn.

Core Idea
Jihad is communal because its purpose is collective exaltation of religion, achieved by some.

After defining the terms, the author turns to the ruling: Jihad is a communal obligation. This lesson unpacks the rational proof and analogy with funeral prayer, burial, and returning greetings. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying fiqh correctly.

Guided Path
1
State the Ruling
Jihad is fard kifayah (communal obligation). If a group of Muslims undertakes it, the obligation falls from the rest.
This is the established Hanafi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the Proof
The proof is qiyas (analogy) with other communal obligations: funeral prayer, burial, and returning greetings. The illa (effective cause) is that each is prescribed for a collective benefit that can be achieved by some Muslims.
The illa is jaliyyah (clear) and extracted by tahqiq al-manat.
3
Analyze the Illa
The purpose of Jihad is to exalt Allah's religion and repel corruption from people. This is a collective goal; if some achieve it, the rest are absolved.
The passage emphasizes Jihad is not for personal benefit but for the community.
4
Note Exceptions
If the objective is not achieved by some (e.g., the enemy attacks and the community is in danger), Jihad becomes fard 'ayn on every capable person.
This is explicitly stated: 'if not, then it is fard 'ayn.'

Jihad as a Collective Obligation: Ruling and Proof
Defines jihad as collective obligation and justifies with analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that jihad is fard kifayah and explain the proof by analogy to funeral prayer, burial, and responding to salam.

Core Idea
Jihad is a collective obligation; if some do it, all are absolved.

In the Hanafi school, jihad is not an open-ended personal duty. This passage from al-Lubab succinctly defines it as a communal obligation (fard kifayah) and justifies this ruling through a powerful analogy to other communal acts. You will see why the default is collective and when it becomes personal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the hukm of jihad: fard kifayah (collective obligation).
2
Understand the rationale
Jihad is a means to glorify religion and repel corruption, not an end in itself. Acts that are means are generally fard kifayah.
This distinguishes it from fard 'ayn.
3
Examine the analogy
Compare jihad to funeral prayer, burial, and responding to salam. In each, the purpose can be achieved by some, so the obligation is communal.
The illa (effective cause) is the achievability of the purpose by a group.
4
Apply the condition for switching to fard 'ayn
If the purpose cannot be achieved by some (e.g., insufficient defenders), jihad becomes fard 'ayn on those capable.
The text alludes to this when it says 'if the purpose is achieved by some'.
5
Note the exemptions
Jihad is not obligatory on minors or slaves due to lack of full legal capacity.
Other conditions like health and wealth may also apply but are not detailed here.

Commentary

The text also affirms that fighting disbelievers is obligatory even if they do not initiate, based on general proofs. Finally, it exempts minors and slaves from the obligation due to lack of full legal capacity.

Jihad as Fard Kifayah and the Obligation to Fight Even Without Enemy Initiation
Explains the obligation of jihad as a collective duty with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that jihad is fard kifayah and explain why fighting polytheists is wajib even without enemy initiation, citing the Quranic and prophetic proofs provided.

Core Idea
Jihad is a collective duty; fighting polytheists is wajib from absolute texts, not conditioned on enemy attack.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar clarifies the nature of jihad as a collective obligation (fard kifayah) and establishes that the obligation to fight polytheists is not conditional upon their initiation. The commentator uses general Quranic commands and a prophetic hadith to support this, and addresses an apparent contrary verse by stating it is abrogated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its primary ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (jihad) and its legal classification (fard kifayah). Understand that if a sufficient number perform it, the obligation is discharged; if all abandon it, all sin.
The base matn states: 'If no one undertakes it: all people sin by abandoning it.'
2
Extract the secondary ruling on fighting without enemy initiation
The text explicitly says 'Fighting polytheists is wajib even if they do not start.' This is a key point – not conditional on aggression.
This ruling is derived from general (mutlaq) texts.
3
Examine the proof (dalil) cited
Identify the Quranic verses (9:5, 8:39, 2:216) and the hadith (Bukhari and Muslim) and note that they are general commands, not restricted to defensive warfare.
The commentator explains that the obligation is unconditional due to the 'amm (general) nature of these proofs.
4
Address the counter-evidence and its abrogation
The verse 'If they fight you, then fight them' (2:191) might imply defensive-only. The commentator states it is abrogated (mansukh) by the unconditional verses.
This shows how Hanafi usul handles apparent conflict by nasikh and mansukh.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Fighting without prior call to Islam is haram; the sin is incurred, but no compensation is required.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a fundamental principle of Islamic warfare: the call to Islam (da'wah) must precede any fighting. The ruling is not merely recommended but obligatory; violation incurs sin, yet no blood money or indemnity is due because the victims lacked protected status (ʿāṣim). The reasoning ensures that warfare is understood as a religious duty, not a pretext for plunder.

The term 'lā yajūz' signals prohibition in Hanafi fiqh.
2

Find the proof cited: the hadith narrated from ʿAlī and Ibn ʿAbbās in which the Prophet said, 'Do not fight a people until you call them.' This is a clear textual evidence (naṣṣ̣).

Human Shields (Tatarruq) and Accidental Muslim Deaths
Commentary on Quduri's matn explaining the ruling on human shields and accidental Muslim deaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on continuing to shoot when the enemy uses Muslim human shields, and explain why no diyah or kaffarah is due for accidental Muslim deaths.

Core Idea
When the enemy shields with Muslims, shooting must continue but aiming at disbelievers; no diyah is due because jihad's obligation overrides incidental harm.

One of the most challenging scenarios in jihad is when the enemy uses Muslim children or captives as human shields. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that Muslims must continue fighting, aiming at disbelievers, and that accidental killings of Muslims incur no blood-money or expiation. The principle is based on the obligatory nature of jihad and the maxim that penalties cannot attach to obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The enemy shields themselves with Muslim children or captives. The Hanafi ruling: Muslims must not refrain from shooting; they must continue and aim at the disbelievers.
This is considered wajib (obligatory) to avoid giving the enemy a tactic to nullify jihad.
2
Understand the rationale
If Muslims stopped fighting whenever human shields were used, the enemy would always use this tactic, effectively nullifying jihad. So necessity (darurah) mandates continuation.
The rule 'al-darurat tubih al-mahzurat' applies here, but with the condition of aiming at kuffar.
3
Analyze the condition of intention
Muslims must intend to hit the disbelievers, not the Muslims. If it is impossible to distinguish in action, they must still intend in the heart. Obedience is according to capacity.
This is based on 'la yukallifu Allahu nafsan illa wus'aha'.
4
Apply the no-diyah principle
If a Muslim is accidentally killed, no diyah or kaffarah is due. The reason: jihad is fard (obligatory), and penalties do not attach to obligations. This is from al-Hidayah.
This is a unique Hanafi position; other schools may require compensation.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require diyah for accidental Muslim deaths, making commanders more hesitant. Hanafi removes this liability, enabling decisive action.

Taking Women and Mushafs to Battle: Conditions and Proof
Ruling with proof: permissibility of taking women and mushafs to battle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking women and mushafs to battle, identify the two scenarios (large army vs. small raiding party), and explain how the hadith 'Do not travel with the Quran to enemy land' is interpreted in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sacred items and women may accompany a large, secure army; with a small party it is disliked.

The duty of jihad requires Muslims to protect and respect sacred objects and vulnerable individuals. Yet the battlefield is a place of danger and potential disrespect. How does the Hanafi school balance these concerns? In this lesson, you will examine a precise ruling from the matn of al-Quduri, explained by al-Maydani, that distinguishes between large armies (where safety is assured) and small raiding parties (where disrespect is feared). The ruling is anchored in a prophetic hadith and demonstrates the Hanafi method of interpreting general prohibitions through contextual reasoning (istihsan).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between a 'large army' (askar 'azim) that is secure from danger, and a 'small raiding party' (sariyya) that is not secure. Note the opposite rulings for each.
Large = 'عسكر عظيم', small = 'سرية'.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For the large army: 'لا بأس' (no harm, i.e., permissible). For the small raiding party: 'يكره' (disliked, i.e., makruh tanzihi). Understand that makruh here means it is better to avoid, but not sinful.
The same ruling applies to women, mushafs, and books of fiqh/hadith.
3
Locate the dalil (prophetic hadith)
The text cites the hadith: 'Do not travel with the Quran to the enemy's land.' This is a general prohibition (nahy).
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Explain the Hanafi interpretation (ta'wil)
The Hanafi school interprets the prohibition as applying only when there is a reasonable fear of disrespect to the mushaf, i.e., in small parties. In a large army, the prohibition is lifted because the mushaf is safe. This is the 'correct interpretation' (al-ta'wil al-sahih) according to the text.
This is an example of takhsis (specification) of a general text by customary context (urf) and istihsan.
5
Apply the sub-ruling on aman
If a Muslim enters enemy territory with a safe conduct (aman) and the people are trustworthy, he may carry the mushaf because the default is safety unless proven otherwise.
This shows that the ruling depends on the actual likelihood of disrespect, not just the technical status of enemy land.

Core Idea
Non-combatants are protected unless they fight or have strategic military value.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a critical aspect of Islamic jus in bello: the protection of non-combatants. The Hanafi school distinguishes between combatants and non-combatants, with the general rule being that only those engaged in muharabah (fighting) may be killed. However, there are nuanced exceptions for those who contribute indirectly, such as providing strategic advice or rallying enemy forces.

Identify protected categories
List the persons the text says shall not be killed: women, frail old man, child, blind, cripple, and insane. Note that these are default categories.
The text also includes an insane person separately, but the ruling is the same: protected unless they fight.
2
Determine the legal cause (illa) for killing
The text states 'what permits killing with us is combat (muharabah).' Understand that the illa is being a combatant or having direct military utility.

For each protected category, if they do not fight and have no strategic role, killing is haram. The reason given: 'because they are not people of fighting.'

This aligns with the general rule: the intent is to neutralize threat, not punish.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure for breaking a peace treaty according to the Hanafi school and explain the rationale (talil) based on benefit and avoidance of treachery.

Core Idea
Break a treaty only after formal notice (nabdh) — otherwise it's treachery.

When a peace treaty no longer serves the benefit of the Muslims, the Imam may revoke it. But Islamic law does not allow sudden attack; it demands a formal process to avoid treachery. This passage from Al-Hidayah outlines the conditions and reasoning (talil) behind this ruling, emphasizing that even in wartime, trust and contractual obligations must be respected.

This reasoning shows that the treaty is a strategic tool, not an absolute obligation.

This condition is mentioned as from Al-Hidayah, emphasizing due process.

Core Idea
If the enemy breaks the treaty by collective agreement, no notice is needed — their action is the nullification.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a key question in Islamic international law: when does the enemy's hostile action nullify a peace treaty? The Hanafi school makes a precise distinction between treachery by the state and individual banditry. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the ruling on notice (نبذ) before war.

1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is that the enemy initiates treachery (خيانة). The ruling is that the Imam fights them without giving prior notice (نبذ).

Examine the condition of collective agreement
The ruling applies only 'if that (treachery) is by their agreement' (باتفاقهم). This means the action must be attributable to the entire enemy community or its leadership.
If a small group acts independently, the condition is not met.
3
Contrast with banditry without power
If a group enters Muslim lands, cuts the road, and has no power (منعة), their action is not a treaty breach for the whole nation. They are treated as individual criminals.
Power (منعة) indicates state backing or military capability.
4
Consider the role of power and open fighting
If a group has power and fights Muslims openly, it is a breach only for that group, not for others, because they acted without the king's permission.
The treaty remains intact for the rest of the nation.
5
Synthesize the attribution principle
If the king authorizes the action, it becomes collective treachery for the entire nation. The key is whether the act is attributable to the state or to individuals.
This prevents unjust escalation.

Ransoming Muslim Captives with Enemy Prisoners
Intra-Hanafi dispute on exchanging captives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on ransoming Muslim captives with enemy captives, explain the reasoning of both Abu Hanifah and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prohibits swapping captives because strengthening the enemy outweighs rescuing one Muslim.

This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifah and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding whether captured enemy combatants may be used to ransom Muslim prisoners of war. The disagreement turns on balancing the benefit of rescuing a Muslim against the harm of releasing a combatant who will fight again. Later Hanafi authorities settled on Abu Hanifah's prohibition as the operative position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Act
The act in question is exchanging captured enemy combtants for Muslim prisoners held by the enemy. Determine what exactly is being ruled upon: the permissibility of fida' in this specific form.
Fida' is a contract of ransom, but the dispute is not about monetary ransom; it is about exchanging persons.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifah's Prohibition
Examine the two arguments Abu Hanifah gives: (1) freeing the enemy combatant assists the enemy because he returns to fight; (2) the harm caused by that combatant is a greater evil than the continued captivity of one Muslim. Understand the principle of repelling harm (dar') over bringing benefit (jalb).
The reasoning is talil – juristic reasoning based on likely consequences.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's Permission
The two companions argue that rescuing a Muslim is a concrete benefit, and it outweighs the loss of killing or using the enemy captive. They prioritize the immediate good of saving a believer.
The Sahibayn's view is rooted in the maxim 'the welfare of Muslims is paramount'.
4
Examine the Tarjih (Preference)
Later Hanafi jurists like al-Isbijabi, al-Mahbubi, and al-Nasafi declared Abu Hanifah's view as the sound one (as-sahih). Understand why they gave weight to the public interest (maslahah) over individual rescue.
The tarjih is based on the principle that preventing harm to the Muslim community takes precedence over the benefit of a single individual.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The operative position for fatwa is the prohibition. Confirm that in practice, Muslim captives are not to be ransomed with enemy captives, though other means of ransom (money, goods) may be permissible.
This is a reliable position (zahir al-riwayah) according to later authorities.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hanafi school on spoils of war and the contract of protection (aman). It first addresses the participation of reinforcements (al-madad) who arrive after the main army. The ruling states that if reinforcements join the army in Dar al-Harb before the spoils are brought to Dar al-Islam, or before division or sale of the spoils, they share in the spoils. The reason given is that they participated in jihad before the ownership of the spoils was settled for the original army. Therefore, the right of participation ceases once the spoils are secured (ihraz) or divided by the Imam in Dar al-Harb, as ownership is then complete. Next, the passage discusses the people of the army market (ahl suq al-‘askar)—those who accompany the army for trade or services but do not fight. They have no right to spoils unless they actually engage in combat, because they lacked the intention to fight, which is the apparent cause for entitlement. The commentator clarifies that entitlement is based on one’s actual state (as a horseman or foot soldier) at the time of fighting. The second half of the passage covers the contract of protection (aman). According to Hanafi law, any free Muslim—whether man or woman—may grant aman to one or more disbelievers, or to an entire fort or city, and it is valid and binding on all Muslims. The reason is that such individuals are capable of fighting (ahl al-mana‘ah), so their aman is effective. The validity of aman is analogized to the guardianship of marriage: just as faith is indivisible, so is the ability to grant protection. Once aman is given, no Muslim may kill the protected persons or interfere with their property. The proof is the hadith: 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection on behalf of them all.' This indicates that even the least Muslim can bind the community with aman.
