Agency in Personal Status Contracts and Oath Violation
Ruling on oath not to marry/divorce/manumit when an agent acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why swearing not to marry, divorce, or manumit is violated when an agent acts, and distinguish this from agency in property contracts.

Core Idea
In personal status contracts, the agent is a mere messenger; the principal breaks the oath.

In the previous lesson, we saw that an oath concerning a property contract is not broken by an agent's action. Here, the rule flips for personal status contracts. The passage from al-Hidayah establishes that agency in marriage, divorce, and manumission does not shield the oath-taker from violation. The key is how the agent is characterized: as a safir (messenger) whose act is attributed entirely to the principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the passage and state what acts are covered. Note the three examples: marriage, divorce, manumission. The ruling is that the oath is violated (حنث) when an agent performs any of these acts.
The ruling applies to personal status contracts, not property contracts.
2
Examine the legal characterization of the agent
The passage describes the agent as 'سفير ومعبر' — a messenger and an expression of the principal's will. Understand that the agent does not act independently; he is a conduit for the principal's intent.
This characterization is specific to Hanafi fiqh for these contracts.
3
Analyze the legal consequence of this characterization
Because the agent is a messenger, the contract is attributed to the principal (الأمر), not to the agent. All rights and obligations revert to the principal. Therefore, the principal is considered the doer of the act, breaking his oath.
The proof is derived from the nature of agency in personal status: the agent cannot add the contract to himself, so it must be added to the principal.
4
Contrast with property contracts
Recall from the previous lesson that in property contracts, the agent is considered a true agent (وكيل) and the act is attributed to the agent, protecting the principal from breaking an oath. The distinction rests on whether the contract creates direct personal rights for the principal or primarily involves property transfer.
This contrast is essential for mastering the Hanafi rule on oaths and agency.
5
Conclude with the operative ruling
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is that the principal breaks his oath when the agent acts in personal status matters. This is the position of al-Hidayah and the majority of Hanafi authorities.
Some later scholars may discuss exceptions, but this is the standard ruling.

Cases Where No Oath Is Administered to the Defendant
List of non-property claims exempt from oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific claims where the Hanafi school does not administer an oath to the defendant and explain the underlying principle (badhl does not apply to non-property claims).

Core Idea
In non-property claims like marriage, no oath is administered because refusal to swear is not treated as payment.

In judicial proceedings, oaths can be a decisive tool. However, the Hanafi school carves out exceptions: in certain types of claims—such as marriage, lineage, and fixed punishments—the defendant is not made to swear even if they deny. This lesson unpacks Abu Hanifa's reasoning based on the nature of 'payment' (badhl) and refusals (nukul).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
Locate the main ruling: 'لا يستحلف المنكر في كذا...'. Note that the ruling is about procedural law (qada)—when a defendant should or should not be required to take an oath.
The ruling is attributed to Abu Hanifa.
2
List the excluded claims
From the passage, extract all types of claims where no oath is administered: marriage, revocation after iddah, return to intimacy in Ila' after expiry, slavery, istilad (claim of maternity), lineage, wala', and hudud.
The text also mentions 'if the claim is from him or her and the other denies', but the ruling still applies.
3
Understand the rationale (illah)
The reason given: 'because refusal (nukul) according to him is a payment (badhl), and payment does not apply in these matters.' In property claims, refusal to swear is equivalent to admitting the claim (badhl), so the defendant is made to swear to avoid that consequence. In non-property matters, refusal cannot be treated as payment, so no oath is needed.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on the concept of badhl.
4
Derive the general principle
Formulate the rule: The defendant is not required to take an oath in any claim that is not of a property nature (mal), because the legal effect of nukul (being treated as admission/payment) is confined to property matters.
This principle determines the mufta bihi position.

Two Claimants for Marriage with Evidence
Ruling on conflicting marriage claims with evidence and confession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving conflicting marriage claims when both claimants present evidence, including the role of the woman's affirmation and the hierarchy of evidence and confession.

Core Idea
When two men bring equal evidence, the woman's affirmation decides; timed evidence overrides untimed, and evidence overrides confession.

In this lesson, we examine a classic Hanafi ruling on conflicting marriage claims. When two men each claim to be married to the same woman and produce evidence, the judge faces a dilemma: neither evidence has preference, and the woman cannot have two husbands. The solution is a set of hierarchical rules that prioritize the woman's testimony, the timing of the contract, and the strength of evidence versus confession. These rules ensure that marital bonds are established justly, respecting both legal procedure and the consensual nature of marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
Both claimants produce evidence. Why can't both be accepted? Because there is no preference for one over the other, and a woman cannot be shared between two husbands.
The subject (the woman) does not accept sharing.
2
Default resolution: woman's affirmation
Refer to the woman's own statement; whichever man she confirms as her husband, that marriage is valid. This is because marriage is established by mutual consent.
The woman's affirmation is considered a form of confession (iqrar).
3
Apply time-stamping to evidence
If the evidence includes dates (e.g., witnesses specify the time of marriage), the earlier marriage takes precedence over the later one.
Time-stamped evidence creates preference and resolves the conflict.
4
Understand the hierarchy: evidence vs confession
If the woman confesses to one man before any evidence is produced, that marriage is valid. However, if the other claimant later produces evidence, his evidence prevails because 'al-bayyinah aqwa min al-iqrar' (evidence is stronger than confession).
Confession is binding but can be overridden by stronger proof.
5
Handle final judgments
Once a judge rules for one claimant based on evidence, that judgment is final. A later claimant with similar evidence cannot overturn the first ruling, because 'the first judgment is valid and cannot be voided by something equal to it.'
This ensures finality of court decisions.

Purchase and Marriage Claims: Equal Strength in Hanafi Law
Ruling: purchase and marriage claims over property are equal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a claim of purchase and a claim of marriage (dower) over the same property are treated as equal in Hanafi law, and apply this principle to distinguish between bilateral and gratuitous contracts.

Core Idea
A purchase claim and a marriage dower claim are equal because both are bilateral contracts that transfer ownership by contract alone.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi ruling on competing claims over property. When one person claims to have bought a slave and a woman claims the same slave was given as her dower, neither claim is inherently stronger. The key insight is that both purchase and marriage are bilateral exchange contracts (mu'awada). Without additional evidence, the law treats them as equal, which has implications for how such disputes are resolved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two competing claims
One claimant says he purchased the slave; the other says she was married with the slave as dower. Both assert ownership through a contract.
2
Classify each contract type
Both purchase and marriage are bilateral (mu'awada) contracts—they involve exchange of value. In purchase, price is given; in marriage, dower is given.
In Hanafi law, marriage is considered a contract of exchange (mu'awada) because dower is essential.
3
Apply the principle of equal strength
Since both are bilateral and transfer ownership by contract (not by possession), neither claim is legally stronger. The ruling states they are equal (sawa).
4
Consider the practical outcome
If both claims are equally proven, the qadi may divide the property or give priority to the party in possession. The ruling sets a baseline of equality.
This does not apply to gratuitous contracts like gift (hiba), which require delivery to complete transfer.

Dispute Over Mahr: Evidence, Oaths, and Validity of Marriage
Rules for resolving mahr disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for resolving a mahr dispute when both spouses disagree, including the conditions for preferring evidence and the effect of mutual oaths on the marriage contract.

Core Idea
When spouses dispute the mahr, mutual oaths do not invalidate the marriage — the mahr is subsidiary to the valid contract.

This passage from al-Quduri’s Matn presents the Hanafi rulings on how to resolve a mahr dispute between spouses. The law distinguishes three evidentiary scenarios and, critically, affirms that the marriage contract remains valid even after mutual oaths. Understanding these rules requires tracing the reasoning from the subsidiary nature of the dower and the analogy with sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The text presents three cases: only one spouse has evidence, both have evidence, or neither has evidence.
2
Understand the preference when both have evidence
When both spouses bring evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred, but only if her claim exceeds her proper dower (mahr al-mithl). If her claim equals or is less than her proper dower, the husband's evidence prevails.
Condition from al-Hidayah and al-Kifayah.
3
Analyze the effect of mutual oaths
When no evidence exists, they take mutual oaths (tahalf). The key point: the marriage remains valid because the specified amount is subsidiary to the marriage contract, unlike a sale where lack of specification invalidates the contract.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf with sale
The difference in outcome between marriage and sale highlights a core Hanafi principle: the dower is not a pillar of marriage, so its absence does not void the contract.

When spouses disagree on dower, the judge uses mahr al-mithl as the objective standard, not mere averages.

Imagine a marriage contract that did not specify a dower. Later, the husband and wife disagree on what was agreed. How does the Hanafi judge resolve this? This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri presents a clear three-scenario ruling based on the customary dower (mahr al-mithl). 1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the marriage contract had no specified dower (tasmia) and spouses later disagree on the amount.
2
Understand mahr al-mithl
Mahr al-mithl is the customary dower for women of similar standing in the family and locality, determined by the judge.
It is based on custom and not an arbitrary figure.
3
Compare each claim to mahr al-mithl
The judge compares the husband's acknowledgment and the wife's claim to the mahr al-mithl.
Three possibilities: equal/less, equal/more, or between.

The rationale is that after mutual oaths (tahalluf), only the amount supported by custom is established.

A child born less than six months after sale belongs to the seller; the slave girl becomes umm walad and the sale is rescinded.

This passage presents a critical Hanafi ruling on paternity claims when a slave girl gives birth after being sold. The passage distinguishes between birth less than six months from the day of sale and birth more than six months. This time-based division is the foundation of the ruling.

If less than six months, the child is attributed to the seller, the slave girl becomes umm walad, and the sale is rescinded. If more than six months, the seller's claim fails.

The ruling is based on istihsan: despite the apparent inconsistency that the seller sold the slave girl after she conceived, lineage matters are based on concealment (mabna al-nasab 'ala al-khafa'), so the internal contradiction is overlooked.

If the buyer also claims the child, the seller's claim takes precedence because it refers back to the time of conception, which is earlier. However, if the buyer claims first, the child's lineage is established for the buyer, treating the child as if from a marriage (not as umm walad).
Al-Qahistani notes that the buyer's prior claim would establish lineage from the buyer, and the ruling is then based on marriage (i.e., the child is free and the slave girl is not umm walad for the buyer).

Core Idea
Coerced mahr is valid, but excess over fair dower is void; fair dower becomes due and halves on pre-consummation divorce.

In Hanafi law, the dower (mahr) is a mandatory consideration in marriage. But what happens when a husband is forced to specify an excessive mahr? This passage from Al-Lubab explains the nuances: the coerced specification is not entirely invalid; rather, only the excess above the fair dower (mahr al-mithl) is void, and the fair dower is substituted. However, this ruling applies only before consummation and when the mahr was specified. After consummation, the mahr is established by the act itself, not the coerced agreement.

If the specified mahr is equal to or less than the fair dower, the agreed amount stands. If it exceeds the fair dower, the excess is void and the fair dower becomes due.

Ruling on 'Azl and Permission Requirement
States ruling on 'azl and permission requirement with Hanafi dispute on slave wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on 'azl with a free wife, identify the Hanafi dispute regarding a slave wife, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of the differing views.

Core Idea
Azl without permission is haram; for a slave wife, the master's permission is required (Abu Hanifa/Muhammad).

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a sensitive marital issue: coitus interruptus ('azl). The Hanafi school balances the husband's desire to prevent pregnancy with the wife's right to sexual fulfillment. The ruling hinges on permission, and a subtle dispute arises when the wife is a slave: whose consent is needed?

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Read the first sentence: 'ولا يعزل عن زوجته إلا بإذنها' — the husband may not practice 'azl with his free wife except with her permission. This establishes the default prohibition.
The reason given: 'because she has a right to intercourse' (لها حق في الوطء).
2
Recognize the restriction to free wives
The commentator explicitly restricts the ruling to free wives: 'قيّدنا بالحرة'. This signals that the rule changes when the wife is a slave.
Why? The slave wife does not possess full ownership of her person; her master has rights.
3
Pinpoint the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text states: 'فالإذن لمولاها عند أبي حنيفة ومحمد، خلافاً لأبي يوسف.' For a slave wife, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require the master's permission; Abu Yusuf holds the wife's permission suffices.
This is an ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school, not between schools.
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a scenario: a man performs 'azl with his slave wife with her consent but without her master's permission. According to Abu Hanifa/Muhammad, this is haram; according to Abu Yusuf, it is permissible.
The practical difference is whether the act is sinful or not.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the master's permission is required. This is the view adopted by the majority of Hanafi authorities.
Al-Quduri and al-Marghinani follow this view.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab explains the ruling on 'azl (coitus interruptus). It states that a husband may not practice 'azl with his free wife without her explicit permission. The reason is that the wife has a right to sexual fulfillment, and just as she has options in cases of impotence or physical deficiency, her permission is required for 'azl. The commentary then clarifies that this applies only to a free wife. If the wife is a slave, then the permission must be sought from her master, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Abu Yusuf dissents, holding that the wife's permission suffices even if she is a slave, because the act is a matter of her personal right. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is that the master's permission is required for a slave wife, as this is the view of the majority of the early Hanafi authorities, including the author of al-Quduri's matn.
