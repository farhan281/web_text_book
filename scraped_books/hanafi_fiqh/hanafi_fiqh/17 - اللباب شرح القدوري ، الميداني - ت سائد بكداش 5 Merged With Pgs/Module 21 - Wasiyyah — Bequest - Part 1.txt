Definition and Conditions of a Will (Wasiyyah)
Opening of the chapter on wills with definition and conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a will (wasiyyah) and identify the conditions for its validity.

Core Idea
A will is a transfer of ownership that takes effect only after death — its validity depends on the capacity of the testator and the eligibility of the legatee.

This lesson opens the chapter on wills (Kitab al-Wasaya) in al-Lubab. The author connects wills to the previous chapter on Permission and Prohibition because wills can be either permissible or prohibited depending on circumstances. He then provides the essential definition of a will as a deferred transfer of ownership and lists the conditions that must be met for a will to be valid. Understanding this definition and these conditions is the foundation for all subsequent rulings on wills.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context and terminology
Notice how the author links wills to the previous chapter on Permission and Prohibition. Also note that 'wasaya' includes both the testamentary gift (wasiyyah) and the appointment of an executor (īṣā’). This lesson focuses on wasiyyah.
The difference between wasiyyah and īṣā’ is clarified later in the text.
2
Parse the definition
Break down the definition: 'tamlik muḍāf ilā mā ba‘d al-mawt' — a transfer of ownership (tamlik) that is attached/contingent (muḍāf) to the time after death (mā ba‘d al-mawt). This means ownership does not transfer during the testator's life; it vests only upon death.
The term 'muḍāf' indicates the transfer is deferred to a future event.
3
List the conditions for the testator
From the text, the first condition is that the testator must be legally capable of ownership (ahliyyat al-tamlik) — i.e., a sane adult not under interdiction. The second condition is that the will must not consume the entire estate if there are debts; debts take priority.
The condition regarding debt is implied: the will is valid only to the extent of the estate remaining after debt payment.
4
List the conditions for the legatee
The legatee must be alive at the time the will is made, must not be an heir (unless all other heirs consent), and must not have killed the testator.
The prohibition on bequests to heirs is based on the hadith: 'There is no will for an heir.'
5
Apply the definition and conditions
Take a scenario and test each condition. For example, if a testator leaves property to a non-heir friend, the will is valid provided the testator was of sound mind, debts are settled, the friend is alive, and the friend did not kill the testator.
If any condition is violated, the will is invalid unless an exception applies (e.g., consent of other heirs).

Ruling on the Will: When It Becomes Obligatory
States the ruling and conditions for the will's obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the will and identify the condition that makes it wajib.

Core Idea
A will is not obligatory by default, but becomes wajib if the testator has unpaid religious duties.

The Book of Wills opens with a clear default ruling: the will is recommended, not obligatory. However, this is conditional. If the testator has neglected duties such as zakat, expiatory fasts, or missed prayers, the will becomes a binding obligation (wajib). The passage also sets two essential conditions for a valid will: the recipient must be a non-heir, and the bequeathed object must be capable of ownership after death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The text begins by stating that the will is not obligatory (ghair wajiba) and is recommended (mustahabb). This is the default hukm.
Note: 'mustahabb' is synonymous with 'mandub' in this context.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The author gives the reason: a will is a donation (tabarru'), like a gift (hiba). Donations are not obligatory because obligations require clear evidence.
This is a qiyas (analogy) comparing the will to a gift.
3
Recognize the exception that makes it wajib
If the testator is burdened with unpaid religious obligations (zakat, fidyah for fasting, missed prayers), then the will becomes wajib. The passage states 'otherwise it is obligatory' (wa illa fa wajiba).
This exception is based on the need to discharge debts after death.
4
Note the validity conditions
Two conditions are given: (1) The bequest must not be to an heir (la tajoozu li warith), (2) The bequeathed object must be capable of ownership after death (qabilan li al-tamlik ba'd al-mawt).
These ensure the will does not conflict with inheritance shares and is legally effective.

Commentary

If such obligations exist, then making a will becomes obligatory (wajib) because it is a means of discharging those debts after death. The recommended nature (istihbab) is due to it being a charitable act. No Will for an Heir and Consent of Other Heirs
Explains prohibition of will for an heir, its proof, and exception by consent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule regarding a will for an heir, explain its proof from the hadith, and identify when it becomes valid by consent of the heirs.

Core Idea
A will for an heir is invalid unless the other heirs consent after death.

This passage presents a foundational ruling in Hanafi inheritance law: a bequest to an heir is prohibited. The proof is a clear hadith. However, an important exception exists: if the other heirs voluntarily consent after the testator's death, the bequest becomes valid. Understanding the timing of heirship and the conditions for consent is crucial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making a will for an heir. The ruling is that it is invalid (batiil).
2
Locate the dalil
The prophetic hadith: 'إن الله قد أعطى كل ذي حق حقه فلا وصية لوارث' (Allah has given each entitled person his right, so no will for an heir).
The negation indicates prohibition and thus invalidity.
3
Understand the exception
The will becomes valid if the other heirs consent after the testator's death.
Consent must be from adult heirs; partial consent makes the will valid only to that share.
4
Apply the timing rule
Heirship is determined at the time of death, not at the time the will was made.
If a person ceases to be an heir at death, the will for them becomes valid; if they become an heir at death, the will is invalid unless consent.

Commentary
This passage presents the well-known ruling that a will in favor of an heir is invalid. The proof is the prophetic hadith: 'Allah has given each entitled person his right, so no will for an heir.' This indicates that the shares of inheritance are fixed by divine decree and cannot be altered by a will. The key point is that the heirship is determined at the time of death, not at the time the will was made. So if a person was an heir when the will was made but ceases to be an heir by the time of death (e.g., due to divorce), the will becomes valid for that non-heir. Conversely, if a person was not an heir when the will was made but becomes an heir by the time of death (e.g., by marriage), the will for that person is invalid unless the other heirs consent. The exception is the consent of the adult heirs after death. Since the prohibition exists for the sake of the heirs' rights, they can waive that right by consenting. If only some consent, the will is valid for the consenting heir's portion of the share. Also reiterated is that a will exceeding one-third of the estate is invalid unless the heirs consent, which is a separate but related rule.

الوارث (al-warith): A person who is entitled to inherit from the deceased at the time of death.
الإجازة (al-ijazah): Consent of the heirs after the testator's death authorizing the will for an heir or for more than one-third.

Bequest to an Heir and Exceeding One-Third: Ruling and Heirs' Approval
Ruling with proof from hadith on bequest to heir and exceeding one-third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to an heir and a bequest exceeding one-third, and explain how heirs' approval can validate such bequests.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir or exceeding one-third is void — but heirs' consent after death can make it valid.

This lesson examines two fundamental prohibitions in Islamic inheritance law: bequeathing to an heir, and bequeathing more than one-third of the estate. The basis is a prophetic hadith that reserves inheritance shares to Allah's decree. However, the Hanafi school teaches that these prohibitions exist to protect the rights of other heirs — and if those heirs willingly consent after the testator's death, the bequest becomes valid. Understanding this nuance is crucial for estate planning and avoiding invalid wills.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibited bequests
The testator cannot bequeath to an heir (anyone entitled to inherit from him at death) nor can he bequeath more than one-third of his estate.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'Allah has given every right-holder his right; so no bequest to an heir.' (Reported by Abu Dawud and al-Tirmidhi). The prohibition on exceeding one-third is derived from consensus and the principle of protecting heirs' shares.
3
Understand the timing of heir status
Heir status is determined at the moment of death, not at the time of the will. If a person bequeaths to someone who is an heir at the time of the will but ceases to be an heir at death (e.g., a divorced wife), the bequest is valid. Conversely, if a non-heir becomes an heir at death, the bequest is invalid.
Example in text: bequest to wife Fatima who later divorces.
4
Analyse the role of heirs' approval
The prohibition exists for the benefit of the heirs. Therefore, if all heirs (being adult and of sound mind) approve the bequest after the testator's death, it becomes valid. If only some approve, it is valid proportionally to their shares.
5
Note the inter-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that a bequest to an heir is absolutely prohibited and cannot be validated by heirs' approval, based on a literal reading of the hadith. The Hanafi school allows validation through consent because the prohibition is a right of the heirs that they can waive.

No Bequest to a Direct Killer
Ruling statement: bequest to a direct killer is invalid regardless of intent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a bequest to a direct killer is invalid and identify the condition of direct causation.

Core Idea
A killer cannot inherit or receive a bequest — direct causation, intentional or accidental, invalidates the gift.

In Islamic law, a person who directly causes another's death is barred from benefiting financially from the deceased through bequest. This ruling, stated succinctly in al-Lubab, applies equally whether the killing was intentional or accidental. Understanding this principle helps you apply the broader rule that a criminal should not profit from his crime.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that a bequest (wasiyyah) to a killer is invalid (batil).
2
Identify the killer's description
The killer must be a direct cause (mubashir) of death. Indirect causation (sabab) does not trigger the prohibition.
Example of indirect: digging a pit that someone later falls into.
3
Note the scope of intent
The ruling covers both intentional murder (amd) and accidental manslaughter (khata) equally.
Some later Hanafi scholars differentiated, but the relied-upon position is as stated.
4
Understand the rationale
The underlying principle is that a person should not benefit (through inheritance or bequest) from causing another's death.
This is akin to the rule in inheritance where a killer is barred from inheriting.

The condition 'direct' (mubashir) excludes an indirect cause (e.g., someone who dug a pit but did not push the victim). The wisdom behind this prohibition is to prevent the killer from benefiting from his crime. This ruling is part of the wider principle that a criminal should not benefit from his crime.

Key Terms

مباشر (mubāshir): Direct; one who directly commits the act (as opposed to causing death indirectly, e.g., by setting a trap).

Bequest Between Muslim and Non-Muslim

Division of the Bequeathable Third in Conflicting Bequests
Explains three cases and an intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing the third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for three cases of conflicting bequests when heirs do not approve, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute in the third case regarding the division of the third.

Core Idea
When bequests exceed the third, they are proportionally reduced; intent to favor may survive within the third (Sahibayn) or not (Abu Hanifa).

This lesson covers three scenarios from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar where a testator makes multiple bequests exceeding the bequeathable one-third, and the heirs refuse to approve the excess. In the first two cases, the Hanafi school agrees on proportional division. The third case, where one person is bequeathed all wealth and another one-third, reveals a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether the testator's intent to favor can be considered within the limited third.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases and the common condition
Read the passage and note that in all three cases, the testator has made two bequests that together exceed one-third, and the heirs do not approve. Understand that this makes the bequests effective only up to one-third of the estate.
The condition 'heirs do not approve' is the trigger for proportional reduction.
2
Analyze the first two cases (agreed rulings)
Case 1: two bequests of one-third each → divide the available third equally. Case 2: one bequest of one-third and one of one-sixth → divide the third in the ratio 2:1 (two-thirds to the one bequeathed third, one-third to the one bequeathed sixth). This proportional division is agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.
This is analogous to creditors' sharing of a bankrupt estate.
3
Focus on the third case and the dispute
Case 3: one bequest of all wealth, another of one-third. Abu Hanifa rules equal division (half each). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule division into four shares: three for the legatee of all, one for the legatee of one-third.
The disagreement is whether the testator's intent to favor the legatee of all can survive when the full bequest is invalid.
4
Trace the proofs of each position
Abu Hanifa: the bequest of all was invalid beyond the third, and the preference (tafdil) was part of that invalid bequest, so it falls entirely. Sahibayn: the preference is an independent intention that can still be realized within the permissible third, analogous to a gift where partial implementation still respects the donor's favoritism.
The disciples use qiyas (analogy) to hibah.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
Later Hanafi scholars (al-Maydani, etc.) adopt the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad as the relied-upon fatwa. The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, each gets half of the third; under the disciples, the legatee of all gets three-fourths of the third, and the other gets one-fourth.
This difference matters when the legatee of all is a favored person, e.g., a close friend or relative.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri, addresses the ruling on a bequest (wasiyya) made to unspecified relatives. The text first states the general rule: the bequest goes to the nearest mahram relative (unmarriageable kin), then the next nearest, and so on. Parents and children are explicitly excluded from this category because they are not considered 'relatives' in this technical sense. The reasoning given is that calling one's parent a relative would be disrespectful, as a relative is someone connected through an intermediary, while a parent is connected directly. The will is considered valid only if it covers at least two persons, as the phrase 'relatives' is plural and the minimum plural in wills is two, analogous to inheritance. The passage then presents specific cases and a intra-Hanafi dispute. According to Abu Hanifa, if a man has two paternal uncles and two maternal uncles, the entire bequest goes to the paternal uncles because they are closer in lineage (being from the father's side). The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) disagree; they hold that the bequest is distributed among all relatives traced back to the furthest ancestor who embraced Islam, giving shares equally to all such relatives regardless of closeness. The text then provides a case where a man has one paternal uncle and two maternal uncles: here, the paternal uncle gets half and the two maternal uncles share the other half. This ruling appears to be a compromise or a different scenario. Another case is given with a paternal uncle, paternal aunt, maternal uncle, and maternal aunt: the bequest goes equally to the paternal uncle and aunt, as they share the same degree of relation. The commentary concludes by citing authoritative later Hanafi works (Zād al-Fuqahā', al-Zāhidī, al-Maḥbūbī, al-Nasafī) affirming that the correct position is that of Abu Hanifa. The passage ends with the beginning of a new section on bequests of specific property (dirhams or sheep), but this section is incomplete on these pages.

Bequest of Specific Dirhams or Sheep: Effect of Partial Destruction
Ruling on bequest of a third of specific dirhams when two-thirds are destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a specific bequeathed property is partially destroyed and explain the qiyas reasoning.

Core Idea
When a bequest attaches to a specific item, the beneficiary gets all that remains of it, as long as it fits the one-third limit.

This lesson examines a case where someone bequeaths one-third of a specific set of dirhams or sheep, and two-thirds of that property is destroyed. The Hanafi school rules that the beneficiary receives the remaining one-third in full, because the bequest is tied to the specific property itself. The commentary expands on this by analogizing to a bequest of a specific item that is partly claimed by others.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Scenario
Consider a bequest of one-third of a specific container of dirhams or a specific flock of sheep. This is a bequest of a fraction of a specific property (ayn), not a fraction of the entire estate.
The specificity of the property is crucial for the ruling.
2
Identify the Key Principle
The bequest attaches to the specific 'ayn. Therefore, if part of that property perishes, the remaining part still belongs to the bequest, up to the limit of one-third of the total estate.
This principle distinguishes this case from a bequest of a share in the estate.
3
Recognize the Proof (Qiyas)
The author analogizes to a case where someone bequeaths one-third of a specific item (e.g., a slave) and two-thirds of that item are claimed by others (e.g., due to a debt). In both cases, the beneficiary gets the remaining third because the bequest is attached to the specific property.
The 'illa (effective cause) is that the bequest is tied to the 'ayn, not to a general share.
4
Apply the Condition
The remaining property must not exceed one-third of the total estate. If it does, the heirs may consent or the bequest is adjusted proportionally.
This is a general limitation on all bequests.

Bequest of a Third of Clothes: Effect of Partial Destruction and Type Distinction
Ruling on bequest of a third of clothes when two-thirds are destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a bequest of one-third of clothes suffers partial destruction and distinguish the outcome based on whether the clothes are of a single type or multiple types.

Core Idea
A bequest of a share in a genus (different types) gives only a proportion of what remains; same-type property gives all remaining.

This lesson builds on the previous ruling regarding bequest of a third of dirhams. Here, the property is clothes. The Hanafi masters draw a critical distinction: if the clothes are all of one kind, the bequest attaches to the specific items, just like dirhams. But if they are of different types (e.g., shirts, cloaks, turbans), the bequest attaches to the genus itself, and the beneficiary receives only a proportional share of whatever remains after destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The matn states: if a person bequeaths one-third of his clothes, and two-thirds of the clothes are destroyed, the beneficiary receives only one-third of what remains of the clothes.
Note: the ruling is not that he gets all the remaining clothes, but only a third of them.
2
Locate the distinguished exception
The sharh from al-Hidayah adds a condition: the above ruling applies only if the clothes are of different types (ajnas mukhtalifah). If they are all of one type (jins wahid), the rule changes.
Remember the contrast: same type → like dirhams → beneficiary gets all remaining (subject to estate third).
3
Understand the qiyas reasoning
The effective cause (illa) is whether the bequest attaches to a specific 'ayn or to a share in a genus. Different types make the bequest a share in a genus, so only a proportion is taken. Same type makes the bequest attach to the specific group, so the remainder goes to the beneficiary.
This is a qiyas jaliy with the illa being the specificity of the property.
4
Apply the rule to a concrete scenario
A testator leaves one-third of his wardrobe consisting of 2 shirts, 2 cloaks, 2 turbans (different types). Two-thirds are destroyed, leaving 1 shirt, 1 cloak, 1 turban (3 items). The beneficiary receives one-third of the remaining items, i.e., 1 item (any one, by agreement or lot). If all were shirts (same type), and 2 were destroyed leaving 1 shirt, the beneficiary would receive that shirt outright.
The practical difference is clear: proportion vs. full remainder.

Bequest of 1000 Dirhams When Assets Include Cash and Debts
Ruling on partial payment of bequest from third of cash and debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the amount a legatee receives when a testator leaves both ready cash and receivables, applying the Hanafi rule of paying from the third of each asset type proportionally.

Core Idea
A bequest is paid from the third of the estate; when assets include cash and debts, the legatee takes from each proportionally to ensure fairness among heirs.

When a testator bequeaths a specific sum, the maximum payable is one-third of the estate. But what if the estate includes both cash and debts? This passage from Al-Lubab explains the Hanafi method: the legatee first takes the entire third of the cash, and then collects one-third of each debt until the bequest is fulfilled. This ensures the heirs are not disadvantaged by the legatee taking only the more liquid assets.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest amount and the estate components
Determine the sum bequeathed and whether the testator left ready cash (ʿayn) and debts receivable (dayn). Note that the bequest is limited to one-third of the total estate.
The estate consists of both ʿayn (present assets) and dayn (debts owed to the testator).
2
Calculate the third of the cash
Compute one-third of the ready wealth (ʿayn). This is the maximum that can be taken from cash for the bequest without encroaching on the heirs' share.
For example, if cash is 3000 dirhams, its third is 1000.
3
Check if the bequest is covered by the third of cash
If the bequest amount is less than or equal to the third of cash, pay the full bequest from cash immediately. Otherwise, proceed to the next step.
This is the einfaches scenario: the bequest fits within the liquid third.
4
Pay the entire third of cash to the legatee
When the bequest exceeds the third of cash, the legatee first receives the entire third of the ready cash. This is because the legatee is a partner with the heirs in the estate, and giving him only cash would prejudice the heirs.
The legatee receives this amount as an initial payment.
5
Collect debts proportionally
As each debt is collected from the debtors, the legatee takes one-third of that collection. This continues until the legatee has received the full bequest amount.
The legatee shares proportionally in the collection of debts, ensuring fairness.

A bequest to or from a fetus is valid only if its existence at the will's time is proven by birth within Hanafi time limits.

This passage clarifies when a bequest involving an unborn child (ḥaml) is legally effective. Although the fetus is not yet a person, Hanafi law recognizes its potential to receive or be a subject of a will if existence at the will's time is verified. The key is timely delivery relative to the will.

Core Idea
An exception in a bequest is valid if the principal item is validly bequeathable.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from the Hanafi school on bequests involving an exception. The testator bequeaths a slave girl but excludes her fetus. The ruling confirms both the bequest and the exception are valid, based on a general principle of contract law. Understanding this rule clarifies when a testator can separate an apparently inseparable part from the bequeathed property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a bequest with an exception (istithnā'). The ruling states that both the bequest and the exception are valid (sahīh).

The testator says: 'I bequeath my slave girl except her fetus.' The exception excludes the fetus from the bequest.
The exception must be explicitly stated.

The rationale is: 'because whatever a contract may be validly entered upon, its exception is also valid.' This means if the principal item can be the subject of a valid contract/bequest, then excluding part of it is likewise valid.
Bequests are analogized to contracts in this principle.

The slave girl (principal) is validly ownable and transferable, so excepting the fetus (a separate potential property) is permissible.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Allocating One-Third Between Mother and Child
Intra-Hanafi dispute on bequest distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on distributing the one-third when mother and child are both legatees, explain the reasoning behind each, and identify the relied-upon view.

Core Idea
When two bequests exceed one-third, Sahibayn divide proportionally; Abu Hanifa gives priority to the original legatee.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: when a testator bequeaths something to both his mother and his child, but their combined bequests exceed one-third of the estate. How should the executor distribute the available one-third? The Hanafi school has two positions: one from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), and another from Abu Hanifa himself. Understanding the reasoning behind each view and which one is relied upon for fatwa is essential for correctly executing such bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
You are given a testator who makes bequests to his mother and his child, but together they exceed the one-third limit of the estate. The question is how to distribute that one-third among them.
Assume the bequests are equal or not; the excess is the trigger.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: distribute the one-third proportionally between both legatees. Their evidence is that when the child entered the bequest, it is as if the grant was made to both together, so neither is given precedence.
This is the view preferred by later scholars and is mufta bihi.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa: give the entire one-third to the mother first, and only give the child any leftover. His reasoning is that the mother is the origin in the contract (the bequest to her was made first or is primary), so priority in execution follows priority in the contract.
This view is not the relied-upon position.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical difference: under Sahibayn, if both bequests are equal, each gets half of the one-third. Under Abu Hanifa, the mother gets the entire one-third up to the amount of her bequest, and the child only gets what remains (if any).
For example, if each bequest is 40 and the third is 60: Sahibayn give each 30; Abu Hanifa gives mother 40, child 20.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon view)
The commentator explicitly states that al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, and others chose the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Thus, the proportional distribution is the operative ruling for fatwa.
The text ends with 'تصحيح' (correction), confirming this is the chosen view.
