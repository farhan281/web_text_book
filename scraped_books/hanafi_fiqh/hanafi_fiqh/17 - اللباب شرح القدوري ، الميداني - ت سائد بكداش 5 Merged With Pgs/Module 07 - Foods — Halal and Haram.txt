Conditional Vows and Abu Hanifa's Retraction on Nathr al-Lajaj
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditional vows with retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional vows and explain the exception for nathr al-lajaj, applying the detailed view based on the condition's intent.

Core Idea
A conditional vow used as a deterrent is treated like an oath; expiation suffices.

Vows (nathr) often involve a condition: 'If such happens, I will do such worship.' But what if the condition is meant to stop yourself from doing something? The Hanafi school has a nuanced position, including a reported retraction by Imam Abu Hanifa. This lesson unpacks the general rule and the exception for deterrent vows, known as nathr al-lajaj, and guides you through the intra-Hanafi dispute and the preferred ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule on conditional vows
When a vow is conditioned and the condition occurs, the vow must be fulfilled as specified. This is based on the generality of hadith and the principle that a conditional act is like an absolute one in requiring fulfillment.
The matn asserts this as the starting position.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's reported retraction
It is reported that Abu Hanifa retracted, agreeing with Imam Muhammad: if the condition is a deterrent (e.g., 'If I do X, then a Hajj is due'), then expiation of an oath suffices instead of fulfilling the vow.
This applies when the condition was not intended to occur.
3
Understand the basis for the exception
The exception treats the vow as having the meaning of an oath (aiming to prevent or motivate). The hadiths about fulfillment are general, but this reasoning (istihsan) recognizes the deterrent nature.
Al-Hidayah explains: if the condition is not desired, it is like an oath; if desired, it remains a vow.
4
Apply the detailed view (mufta bihi)
The preferred ruling distinguishes: if the condition is a genuine deterrent (not intended to occur), the vow-holder may choose to fulfill or give kaffarat yamin. If the condition is truly desired (e.g., 'If Allah cures my sick'), fulfillment is wajib.
This is the tarjih in al-Hidayah and Sharh al-Zahidi.

Oath Not to Enter a House: Defining 'Bayt'
Ruling on whether religious buildings are considered houses in oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on what counts as a 'house' (bayt) for oath purposes and apply the definition to religious buildings.

Core Idea
A 'house' in oaths means a dwelling; entering a mosque or temple does not break the oath.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in oaths: if someone swears not to enter a 'house', what buildings fall under that term? The Hanafi school rules based on the customary and literal meaning of 'bayt' as a place prepared for staying overnight. We will see why religious buildings are excluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is swearing not to enter a house, then entering a mosque, Ka'bah, church, or synagogue. The ruling: the oath is not broken (no hanth).
The ruling applies to all four types listed.
2
Understand the definition of 'bayt'
The passage defines a house as 'what is prepared for staying overnight' (ma u'idda li-baytutah). This is the key criterion.
Baytutah means spending the night.
3
Apply the definition to the listed buildings
Religious buildings are not built for dwelling; they are for worship. Therefore, they are not houses in the relevant sense.
Even the Ka'bah, despite its sanctity, is not a dwelling.
4
Note the basis of the ruling
The ruling is based on the literal and customary usage ('urf) of the word 'bayt'. It prevents undue hardship and sticks to common understanding.
No specific intention is needed; the default interpretation applies.

Coercion and the Permissibility of Prohibited Foods: Compelling vs. Non-Compelling Duress
Ruling on eating carrion or drinking wine under coercion, distinguishing compelling and non-compelling duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between compelling (mulji') and non-compelling (ghayr mulji') coercion, and state the Hanafi ruling on eating carrion or drinking wine under each type, including the obligation to comply under compelling duress.

Core Idea
Under threat of death or loss of limb, eating carrion becomes obligatory; under lesser coercion, it remains haram.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling on necessity (darurah) in the context of coercion. The classical text from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies that not all forms of duress lift the prohibition of carrion or wine. Only compelling coercion—where life or limb is genuinely at risk—permits one to consume the otherwise forbidden substance. Moreover, the Hanafi school holds that compliance in such cases is not merely permissible but obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Recognize that eating carrion and drinking wine are fundamentally haram. The discussion concerns an exceptional case where they may become permissible.
Default prohibition is based on clear Qur'anic and hadith evidence.
2
Distinguish between compelling (mulji') and non-compelling (ghayr mulji') coercion
Compelling coercion involves a credible threat to life or limb. Non-compelling coercion includes imprisonment, fetters, or beating that do not threaten life or limb. The ruling hinges on this distinction.
The text explicitly lists imprisonment, qayd (fetters), and darb (beating) as examples of non-compelling coercion.
3
Apply the principle of darurah (necessity)
Under compelling coercion, necessity lifts the prohibition, making the act permissible. The text emphasizes that it is even obligatory (wajib) to comply to preserve life. Under non-compelling coercion, necessity is absent, so the prohibition remains.
Darurah is a well-established principle in Islamic law: 'al-darurat tubih al-mahzurat' (necessities permit the prohibited).
4
Note the consequences of omission under compelling duress
The text states that it is not permissible to endure (sabr) the threat; one must comply. Refusing to eat under compelling duress would be sinful as it endangers one's life.
This is based on the preservation of life (hifz al-nafs), one of the Maqasid al-Shari'ah.

The text explicitly states that it is not permissible to passively endure the threat (i.e., to choose death) when the threat is compelling. The Sin of Refusing to Eat Under Coercion
Ruling on one who endures coercion to eat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on refusing to eat under coercion and explain the reasoning based on qiyas to extreme hunger.

Core Idea
When eating becomes permissible to save life, refusing it and enduring harm is sinful – you aid in your own destruction.

Coercion can make normally prohibited acts permissible. But what if a person refuses to take that permissible escape and instead endures the harm? This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling: such refusal is sinful. The reasoning is grounded in the principle that one must not assist in self-destruction when a lawful alternative is available, drawing an analogy to the case of extreme hunger (makhmassa).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon: a person coerced to eat (something haram) who endures beating and does not eat. The ruling: he is sinful (āthim).
The sin is not in the coercion but in the refusal after permissibility.
2
Understand the Basis of Permissibility
Recognize that under credible threat, eating the haram becomes permissible (mubāḥ) to preserve life. This is a general principle in necessity (ḍarūra).
The permissibility is conditional and limited to what is necessary.
3
Explain Why Refusal is Sinful
The commentary states that because eating was made permissible, refusing it makes the person an accomplice in his own destruction, which is prohibited.
Self-preservation is a maqṣad; rejecting a lawful means to achieve it is blameworthy.
4
Examine the Qiyas to Extreme Hunger
The analogy to makhmassa (famine) shows that just as refusing food in a famine when haram is permitted is sinful, so too is refusing under coercion.
In both cases, the person has a dispensation; ignoring it harms the self.
