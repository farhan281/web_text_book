Oaths on Changing Entities: Essence Over Description
Two rulings on oaths over changing entities with ta'līl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an oath attaches to the essence of an entity and apply it to cases where the entity undergoes natural change.

Core Idea
An oath on a specific entity is broken even if the entity's attributes change.

When you swear not to interact with a specific person or thing, what happens if that person ages or an animal grows? The natural world changes constantly, but does your oath remain binding? The Hanafi school provides a clear principle: the oath attaches to the essence ('ayn) of the entity, not to its temporal descriptions (awṣāf). In this lesson, we examine two illustrative cases from the Book of Oaths: a youth becoming old and a lamb becoming a ram. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly determining whether an oath has been broken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Oath and Its Subject
Read the two oaths in the passage: 'I will not speak to this youth' and 'I will not eat the meat of this lamb.' Note that the subject is a specific individual (this youth, this lamb) — the oath is tied to a particular entity.
The uniqueness of the subject is key: 'this' indicates a specific entity.
2
Identify the Change
In the first case, the youth becomes old (shaykh). In the second, the lamb becomes a ram (kabsh). Recognize that these are natural, gradual changes — the entity did not cease to exist or transform into a fundamentally different category (e.g., lamb to pig).
The change is in attributes (age, size), not in species or essence.
3
Determine the Ruling: Hanith
The author states clearly that in both cases, if the swearer speaks to the now-old man or eats the now-ram, he has broken his oath (ḥanith). This means the oath is invalid and kaffārah (expiation) becomes obligatory.
Ḥanith is a legal state — the oath is violated regardless of intent.
4
Analyze the Legal Reason (Ta'līl)
The passage provides the reason: the prohibition (al-man') was for their essences (li-'aynihimā), not for their being described with these attributes (li-inṣāfihimā bi-hādhā al-waṣf). The attribute (youth, lambhood) was not the motivator (dā'ī) for the oath — the oath was on the entity itself.
This ta'līl distinguishes Hanafi methodology: the oath's scope is determined by the swearer's intended subject, not the literal wording's current applicability.

Oath Not to Eat from a Palm Tree
Ruling statement specifying scope of an oath on a palm tree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath sworn on a palm tree and identify the condition that excludes industrially transformed products.

Core Idea
An oath on an inedible origin applies to its produce, but not after industrial transformation.

This passage addresses a common scenario: someone swears not to eat from a specific palm tree. Since the tree itself is not eaten, Hanafi fiqh interprets the oath as referring to the tree's fruit (dates). However, an important condition is added: if the fruit has been industrially transformed into a new substance (e.g., wine, vinegar, molasses), the oath is not broken. This ruling demonstrates how context and cause ('illah) shape legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of the oath
The person swears on a palm tree. Note that a palm tree is not edible.
2
Understand the default interpretation
Since the tree itself cannot be eaten, the oath is interpreted as applying to its fruit (dates). This is because the tree is the cause of the fruit, and the metaphor is valid.
3
Recognize the condition
The fruit must not have undergone a 'new manufacture' (sina'ah jadidah). If it has, the oath is not broken.
4
Apply the condition to examples
Examples of new manufacture: turning dates into wine, vinegar, or cooked molasses. In these cases, the transformed product is no longer considered the same fruit for oath purposes.

Oaths on Dates: The General Rule and the Mudhannab Dispute
Rulings on oaths about busr, rutab, and tamr with an intra-Hanafi dispute on mixed types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths involving dates at different stages and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding mudhannab dates.

Core Idea
An oath restricted to a description is not broken by a change; but a mixed date (mudhannab) breaks an oath on either stage.

This lesson examines two related rulings from the Book of Oaths. First, when someone swears not to eat a specific type of date (e.g., this busr, or indefinite busr), he does not break his oath by eating a later stage like rutab, because the description is the motivator. Second, an exception arises with 'mudhannab' dates that are mostly one type but have a little of the other attached. According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, eating such a date breaks an oath on either busr or rutab, while Abu Yusuf is reported to disagree. We will parse the reasoning and the practical consequence of this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct rulings
The passage first gives a general rule about oaths tied to a description (e.g., 'this busr' or indefinite busr) — they are not broken by a change in the fruit's stage. Then it gives a specific rule about mudhannab dates and an intra-Hanafi dispute. Separate these two rulings.
The general rule is unconditional; the mudhannab rule is an exception.
2
Understand the general rule: oath restricted by description
Why does eating rutab not break an oath on busr? Because the oath is motivated by the description (wajh al-yamin), which is the specific stage at the time of the oath. When the fruit changes stage, the original description no longer applies.
The description is the 'motivator' (da'i) – the reason the oath was taken.
3
Define mudhannab and its legal significance
Mudhannab is a date that is predominantly one stage but has a trace of the other attached at its tip (busr with a little rutab, or vice versa). This hybrid status raises the question: does it fall under an oath on either stage?
The term 'mudhannab' literally means 'having a tail'.
4
Trace the Hanafi position and the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani rule that eating mudhannab breaks an oath on either busr or rutab because it contains both. Abu Yusuf is implied to disagree (only two imams are mentioned). The reasoning (talil) is that the eater consumes both types, each of which is the subject of the oath.
This is a valid scholarly difference within the Hanafi school.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical consequence: under Abu Yusuf's view, eating mudhannab would not break the oath. The text sides with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, making that the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position. A person who ate mudhannab after swearing off busr must perform kaffarah.
The text's phrasing 'عند أبي حنيفة ومحمد' signals that this is the adopted view.

Oath Not to Eat Meat: Does Fish Break the Oath?
Ruling on eating fish after an oath to abstain from meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether fish breaks an oath not to eat meat and explain why custom (‘urf) governs oath interpretation over Quranic usage.

Core Idea
Oaths are interpreted by custom, not Quranic definitions—fish is not meat in ‘urf.

When a person swears not to eat meat and then eats fish, a common question arises: does this break the oath? The Hanafi school, relying on the principle that oaths are built on custom (‘urf) and common usage, rules that the oath is not broken. This lesson examines the legal reasoning, the proof from custom, and an alternative view based on qiyas (analogy) that reached a different conclusion. You will see how Hanafi jurists prioritize everyday understanding over literal or Quranic language in oath matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The issue is whether the act of eating fish violates an oath not to eat meat. The central question is how the word 'meat' (lahm) is interpreted in the context of an oath.
Oaths are a matter of verbal commitment; their scope depends on the speaker's intent and common understanding.
2
State the majority Hanafi ruling
The relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is that the oath is not broken (lam yahnath). This is the view of Abu Hanifa's students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
The ruling is from the apparent narrations, which carry authority in the school.
3
Analyze the proof: custom as the basis of oaths
The text states: 'because oaths are based on custom and habit (al-'urf wa al-'adah).' In custom, fish is not called 'meat' in the normal usage, so the word does not apply.
The proof is a general principle (dalil aqli) derived from the purpose of oaths and human interaction.
4
Address the counterargument from Quranic usage
The Quran calls fish 'meat' (lahman tariyyan). However, the text argues this is irrelevant because oaths are not built on Quranic definitions but on the custom of the people.
This shows the Hanafi commitment to 'urf in oath interpretation, overriding lexical or scriptural definitions.
5
Consider the opposing view and its refutation
Al-Ispijabi and a narration from Abu Yusuf argued by qiyas that since the Quran calls fish meat, the oath should be broken. The majority responds that qiyas does not override custom in oaths; the apparent narration is preferred.
The dispute is within the school; the mufta bihi is the position that the oath is not broken.

Oath Not to Drink from a River: Literal Meaning and Haqiqah
Ruling on breaking an oath by drinking from a river using a vessel

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking an oath by drinking from a source using a vessel, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the principle that haqiqah (literal meaning) prevents resort to majaz (figurative meaning).

Core Idea
An oath to not drink from a river is broken only by direct lapping, not by using a vessel, because the literal meaning governs.

This passage from al-Lubab al-Quduri clarifies a common oath scenario: swearing not to drink from a river, then drinking with a cup. The ruling hinges on whether the literal act of 'drinking from' the source has occurred. The author explains the Hanafi position, the dispute between Abu Hanifa and his students, and the crucial exception when lapping is impossible (like a well). This lesson unpacks the reasoning based on haqiqah and majaz, key principles in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the first part of the passage: a person swears not to drink from the Tigris and then drinks from it with a vessel. According to Abu Hanifa, he does not break his oath until he laps directly (kar'). Note that the action of drinking with a vessel does not trigger the oath.
This is the core ruling: vessel use = no breach; direct lapping = breach.
2
Understand the reasoning: haqiqah vs majaz
The passage explains that the literal meaning (haqiqah) of 'drinking from' a river is to take water directly into the mouth from its source. Since this is possible, it prevents resorting to the figurative meaning (majaz) of drinking via a vessel. The consensus that lapping breaks the oath confirms that the literal meaning is operative.
This is the key usul principle: when haqiqah is feasible, majaz is not used to interpret an oath.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
The commentary by Baha' al-Din al-'Ayni reports that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) held that drinking by any means breaks the oath. The correct view (sahih) is Abu Hanifa's, as followed by the later imams.
The Sahibayn considered customary usage (urf) as decisive, while Abu Hanifa prioritized the literal linguistic meaning.
4
Apply the exception for non-lappable sources
The passage adds an important condition: the ruling only applies when lapping (kar') is possible. For a well or narrow source, the literal meaning is abandoned because it is not customary to drink directly from it. Even if one forces a direct mouthful, the oath is not broken in the most correct view.

In oaths, custom determines meaning; 'shiwā'' is roasted meat unless intention expands it.

Commentary
This passage details the ruling on an oath restricting a wife's exit. The general rule is that permission is required for each separate exit. If the wife goes out again without new permission, the oath is broken. If the husband says 'Whenever you go out, I have permitted you,' this effectively cancels the effect of the oath because it becomes a standing permission. This shows the precision of Hanafi jurisprudence in balancing textual interpretation and intent.

Fulfilling a Positive Oath with a Single Act
Ruling that one performance suffices for an oath to do an act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that one performance of a sworn act fulfills a positive oath and explain the reasoning based on the objective of the oath.

Core Idea
One act fulfills the oath; breaking requires impossibility.

When you swear to do something, the key question is: what counts as fulfilling that oath? This lesson clarifies that performing the sworn act just once is sufficient, because the essence of the oath is simply to bring the act into existence. The only way to break the oath is if fulfilling it becomes impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states that if a person swears to do an act and performs it once, he has fulfilled his oath. Identify this as the central ruling.
The ruling applies to positive oaths (e.g., 'I will do X'), not negative oaths ('I will not do X').
2
Understand the reasoning
The reasoning (talil) is that the objective of the oath is simply the existence of the act. Once the act exists, the objective is met.
This is based on linguistic custom: swearing to do something aims at making it happen, not at repetition.
3
Determine when the oath is broken
The oath is only broken (hanath) when it becomes impossible to perform the act. This occurs upon the death of the oath-taker or the loss of the subject matter.
For example, if he swore to enter a house and the house burns down before he can enter, he breaks the oath and must expiate.
4
Apply to a new scenario
Consider: 'I swear I will give $10 to charity.' Giving $10 once fulfills it. Giving $10 multiple times is not required, but if he never gives and dies, he has broken the oath.
If he gave $10 but later rescinded it, that does not revive the oath; the oath was already fulfilled.

Permission for Wife's Exit: Need for Repeated Permission
Ruling on oath about wife's exit requiring repeated permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that in an oath restricting a wife's exit, each exit requires separate permission, and distinguish between diyāna and qaḍā' when the husband claims he intended only one permission.

Core Idea
One permission does not cover all exits—each exit requires its own permission or the oath is broken.

This lesson examines a specific ruling on oaths that restrict a wife's movement. The central point is that when a husband swears that his wife will not go out without his permission, each exit requires a separate permission; one permission does not cover subsequent exits. The passage also discusses the legal distinction between what is accepted in private conscience (diyāna) and what is proven in court (qaḍā'), as well as the invalidity of a phrase like 'whenever you go out I have permitted you.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and condition
The husband swears his wife will not go out except with his permission, command, or knowledge. This establishes a general prohibition on her exit unless an exception (permission) exists.
The wording may vary, but the effect is the same.
2
Analyze the consequence of one permission
If the husband gives permission once, she goes out and returns, then later goes out without permission, the oath is broken. One permission does not carry over to later exits.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The excepted case (exit with permission) is specific to that instance. All other exits remain under the general prohibition (al-ḥaẓr al-'āmm). Therefore, each exit requires a fresh exception.
This is based on the principle that an exception only applies to the immediate context unless specified otherwise.
4
Apply the nuance of intent and phrasing
If the husband claims he intended the permission to be only once, he is believed diyānatan (in conscience) but not qaḍā'an (in court). Also, if he says 'whenever you go out I have permitted you,' the permission is void because it is not a genuine grant of permission.
The distinction between diyāna and qaḍā' prevents false claims in court while acknowledging sincerity in private.

Effect of 'Except That I Permit You' Phrase
Ruling that an oath conditioned on permission ends upon one permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when an oath is conditioned on permission and explain the legal effect of one-time permission.

Core Idea
An oath conditioned on 'until I permit' ends upon first permission; subsequent acts do not break the oath.

In Hanafi law, the wording of an oath can determine its duration. This passage examines a specific phrase where the husband ties his wife's prohibition to his permission. Understanding the temporal condition (tawqit) is key to grasping why one permission suffices to end the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath phrasing
Recognize that the oath uses 'except that I permit' or 'until I permit' – words that indicate a temporal link between the prohibition and the permission.
Such phrasing is interpreted as a temporal condition (tawqit) in Hanafi law.
2
Understand the temporal condition
The oath is limited to the period before permission is granted. It does not cover actions after permission.
Tawqit here means the oath has a built-in time limit: the moment before permission.
3
Apply the effect of one permission
When the husband gives permission once, the time limit expires. The oath terminates permanently for that issue.
No further permission is needed; the oath is dead.
4
Conclude the ruling
If the wife goes out after the first permission without asking again, her husband does not break his oath. There is no sin and no expiation required.
The ruling is the same if he uses 'until I command you'.
