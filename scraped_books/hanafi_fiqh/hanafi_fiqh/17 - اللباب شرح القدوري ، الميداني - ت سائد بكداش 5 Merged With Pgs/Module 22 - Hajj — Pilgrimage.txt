Fulfilling a Bequest for Obligatory Hajj: Town and Mode
Ruling on fulfilling a bequest for obligatory Hajj from the testator's town, with adjustment for insufficient funds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on executing a bequest for Hajjat al-Islam and explain the rule regarding the substitute's starting point and mode of travel when funds are insufficient.

Core Idea
A bequest for obligatory Hajj must be fulfilled from the testator's town, riding, or from the nearest point the bequest can cover.

When a person bequeaths that the obligatory Hajj be performed on his behalf after his death, the executors must arrange for a substitute to perform Hajj for him. The Hanafi school specifies that the substitute should start from the testator's hometown and travel riding, not walking, because the testator was not obligated to walk. If the bequest amount is insufficient for the expenses from the hometown, the substitute may start from a nearer location that the funds can cover, as partial fulfillment is better than nothing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation
The testator was obligated to perform Hajjat al-Islam from his town. The bequest aims to fulfill that obligation.
2
Determine the mode of travel
Since the testator was not required to walk, the substitute rides. The bequest does not impose a higher burden than the original obligation.
The text clarifies: يَحجُّ عنه راكباً – he should perform Hajj for him riding.
3
Check sufficiency of funds
If the bequest covers expenses from the town, the substitute departs from the town. If not, he departs from the nearest point where the funds suffice.
Fulfillment to the extent possible: تنفيذاً لها بقدر الإمكان.
4
Apply the general principle
The bequest is executed as much as possible, respecting the location of the obligation.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Hajj for Testator Dying en Route
Intra-Hanafi dispute on location of substitute Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the location of performing Hajj for a testator who died en route, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: Hajj from hometown; Sahibayn: from where the deceased reached – the journey itself is an act of worship.

This lesson examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: if a person sets out for Hajj but dies on the road, and had bequeathed that Hajj be performed for him, where should the substitute perform the Hajj? The answer depends on whether the journey already begun is considered a fulfilled obligation of traveling the distance. Abu Hanifa and his two distinguished students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on this point. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how a principle (qurbah and saqat al-fard) leads to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa holds regarding the location of substituting Hajj for the deceased testator. Note that his view is based on the bequest being fulfilled from the place where the obligation originally fell due – the hometown.
2
Identify the opponents and their view
Now state the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). They hold that the substitute should start from where the deceased reached on his journey, not from his hometown.
3
Follow the opponents' proof
Analyse the dalil of the Sahibayn: the journey with the intention of Hajj is itself an act of worship (qurbah), and the obligation of covering that distance (fard qat' al-masafah) has already been fulfilled to the extent travelled. Therefore, the remaining obligation is to complete the rites from that point onward, as if the deceased were from that place.
Their reasoning hinges on the concept that the journey is part of the Hajj obligation.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's response
Examine why Abu Hanifa rejects this analogy. He argues that the bequest is for the original obligation, which was from the town. The journey does not change the place of duty; the obligation to travel from the town remains unfulfilled. Therefore, the substitute must start from the town.
This is a dispute about the nature of the bequest – whether it refers to the act of Hajj generally or specifically to the obligation as it was upon the testator.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Consider the real-world difference: if the estate's third is limited, performing Hajj from the hometown is more expensive than from the point reached. The choice affects the budget and feasibility of executing the bequest. Also note that both views agree a substitute can be sent – the disagreement is only about the starting point.
The commentary also mentions a related ruling about riding vs walking and insufficient funds, but the core dispute here is the location.

Hajj on Behalf of a Deceased Who Died en Route: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Ruling on performing hajj for a deceased who died on the journey

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on performing Hajj on behalf of a deceased who died en route and explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, including the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
The starting point of substitute Hajj depends on interpreting the will — Abu Hanifa insists on the hometown, the Sahibayn on the place of death.

When a person sets out for Hajj but dies en route, and had bequeathed that Hajj be performed on his behalf, a dispute arises within the Hanafi school: should the substitute Hajj begin from the deceased’s hometown or from the place where he died? This lesson unpacks the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and shows how later Hanafi authorities resolved the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the legal question
The act is performing Hajj on behalf of a deceased who died while travelling for Hajj after making a wasiyyah. The question: from where should the substitute Hajj start?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa: from the deceased's hometown. His evidence: the wasiyyah is interpreted as fulfilling the obligation that began at home; the will specifies the hometown.
The text says 'الوصية تنصرف إلى الحج من بلده' (the bequest refers to Hajj from his hometown).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: from where he died. Their evidence: the journey itself was an act of worship; the obligation to cover the full distance is reduced proportionally, so the substitute begins from that point as if he were starting from his own home.
They analogize: كأنه من أهله (as if he were from his family).
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: if the hometown is far and the place of death is closer, the substitute Hajj will be cheaper if starting from the place of death. The bequest's sufficiency is affected.
5
Determine the tarjih (mufta bihi)
According to later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi, Abu Hanifa's view is preferred (mufta bihi). The text states: 'والصحيح قوله واختاره المحبوبي والنسفي وغيرهما'.
The author also applies Abu Hanifa's ruling to the case of a pilgrim performing Hajj on behalf of another who dies en route.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab deals with two related issues regarding Hajj on behalf of a deceased person who had made a bequest (wasiyyah) for Hajj. First, if the bequest is insufficient for the full expenses from the deceased's hometown, the Hajj should be performed riding from wherever the funds suffice, to execute the will as much as possible. Second, the main dispute: if the deceased had already set out from his hometown with the intention of Hajj but died en route before performing the rituals, Abu Hanifa holds that the Hajj must be performed from his hometown, because the bequest is understood to be for Hajj from that starting point. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the Hajj should commence from the place where he died, because the journey itself is an act of worship, and the obligation of covering the full distance is waived proportionally; thus the substitute begins from that point, like someone who starts from his own home. Finally, the author notes that Jamal al-Islam applies Abu Hanifa’s ruling to the case of a pilgrim performing Hajj on behalf of another: if he dies, the Hajj is performed for the deceased from the pilgrim’s hometown. The preferred position (al-mukhtar) according to al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and others is Abu Hanifa’s view.

النسك (al-nusuk): The rituals of Hajj, including ihram, tawaf, sa'y, and wuquf.
الموصي (al-musi): The testator who makes the will.
