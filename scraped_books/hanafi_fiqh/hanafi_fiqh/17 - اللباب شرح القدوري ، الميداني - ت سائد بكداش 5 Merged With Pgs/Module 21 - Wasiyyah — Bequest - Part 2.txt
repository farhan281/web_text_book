In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on bequests of usufruct—specifically the services of a slave and dwelling in a house. The analogy is based on the nature of benefits as property.

This is a jaliy (clear) qiyas.

Core Idea
A bequest of usufruct is conditional on the legatee's life; his death extinguishes the right.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific gap: what happens when the legatee dies before benefiting from a bequest of a slave's service? The ruling is concise but carries a key principle of Islamic inheritance law: bequests of temporary benefits are tied to the beneficiary's existence. Understanding this helps you distinguish between bequests of corpus and usufruct.

1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a bequest (wasiyyah) of a slave's service (usufruct). The ruling states that if the legatee dies, the bequeathed slave returns to the testator's heirs.

The bequest of usufruct is contingent on the legatee being alive to benefit. Since the legatee dies, the benefit ceases, and the property (the slave and his service) reverts to the estate.
This reflects the Hanafi maxim that a transfer of usufruct is temporary and non-transmissible unless otherwise stipulated.

If the testator had bequeathed the slave's ownership (corpus) rather than just service, the slave would become part of the legatee's estate upon the legatee's death and pass to his heirs.
The ruling here only applies to bequests of manfa'ah (benefit).

Bequest of a Slave's Service and Temporal Division
Ruling on bequest of slave's service with temporal distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a bequest of a slave's service when the slave is the only asset and apply the temporal distribution (muhayah) rule.

Core Idea
When a slave is the only asset, his service is time-shared: heirs two days, legatee one day.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri explains how a bequest of a slave's service is treated when the slave is the testator's only asset. Since the slave cannot be physically divided, the benefit is shared proportionally by time. The passage also discusses a parallel case for a house and addresses the fate of the bequest if the legatee dies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The ruling distinguishes between: (a) when the slave's value is within one-third of the estate, and (b) when the slave is the only asset.
In (a), the slave is delivered entirely to the legatee. In (b), time-sharing is used.
2
Understand proportional division
The legatee's right is one-third, the heirs' right is two-thirds. Since the slave is indivisible, service is allocated by time: heirs get two days, legatee gets one day per rotation.
This mirrors the division of a tangible item if it were divisible.
3
Apply the principle to a house
For a house as the only asset, physical division into three parts is preferred, but temporal division is also allowed.
The text says the first (physical) is better, but temporal is permissible.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf allows the heirs to sell their share (two-thirds) of the house, but the relied-upon position (Abu Hanifa) prohibits sale.
The text sides with Abu Hanifa, citing al-Hidayah.
5
Analyze death of the legatee
If the legatee dies after the testator, the slave returns to the testator's heirs. If he dies before, the bequest is void because the right never vested.
The bequest is a commitment after death; the legatee's heir cannot take without the testator's consent.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri deals with bequests of a slave's service, known as al-wasiyyah bi-khidmat al-'abd. The main ruling states that if the slave's value (the corpus) exceeds one-third of the testator's estate, the slave is delivered to the legatee for service. However, if the testator has no other property besides this slave, the legatee cannot take exclusive service because the heirs have rights in the remaining two-thirds of the slave's value. Since the slave cannot be physically divided (he is a single person), the shares are allocated by time: the heirs take service for two days and the legatee for one day per rotation. This is based on analogy with a bequest of a tangible item (al-wasiyyah bi'l-'ayn) where a similar principle applies. The commentator also addresses the case of a house bequeathed similarly: if it is the only asset, it is divided physically into thirds, or if they prefer, they can use a temporal share. He then notes a disagreement from Abu Yusuf who allows the heirs to sell their share of the house (two-thirds), while the preferred position (from al-Hidayah) prohibits it. Finally, the passage deals with the death of the legatee: if he dies after the testator, the slave returns to the testator's heirs; if he dies before the testator, the bequest becomes void because the right had not yet become due. The reasoning is that the bequest is a commitment that takes effect after death, and the legatee's surviving heirs cannot take it without the testator's consent.

Key Terms
المهايأة (al-muhayah): Temporal division of the benefits of an indivisible property among co-owners, where each uses it for a designated period.
رقبة العبد (raqabat al-'abd): The corpus or entire value of the slave, as opposed to his service or usufruct.
الوصية بخدمة العبد (al-wasiyyah bi-khidmat al-'abd): A bequest of the slave's labor or service to a third party for a specified period or indefinitely, treated as a bequest of usufruct.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The will is void if the legatee predeceases the testator — the right never vested.

This lesson addresses a fundamental condition for a will to take effect: the legatee must survive the testator. If the legatee dies first, the will is automatically void. The reasoning is rooted in the nature of a will as a posthumous obligation — the right only arises at the testator's death. Understanding this principle prevents confusion about whether the legatee's heirs can claim under the will.

Guided Path

The passage states that if the legatee dies during the testator's lifetime, the will becomes void. This is the clear Hanafi position.

3
Analyze the rationale
The passage gives a reason: if the legatee's heirs took the property, they would acquire it from the testator without his consent, which is invalid. This highlights that the legatee's right never vested, so there is nothing to pass to heirs.

Core Idea
The word 'between' necessitates immediate division; if one legatee dies, the other gets only his share.

This lesson continues our exploration of precise wording in testaments. The Hanafi school attaches great weight to the exact words used by the testator. Here we examine the word 'between' (bayna) — it creates separate, immediate shares for each named beneficiary. If one of them dies before the testator, the survivor does not inherit the deceased's portion; instead, that portion lapses or returns to the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the testator's exact wording
Note the phrase 'between Zayd and Amr' (bayna Zaydin wa Amrin). The word 'bayna' is the operative term.
2
Recognize the legal effect of 'bayna'
According to the Hanafi school, 'bayna' necessitates sharing (ishtirak) and immediate division. Each legatee's share is fixed at the moment the will becomes effective (upon testator's death).
Contrast with 'li' (to), which creates a common share.
3
Apply the rule when one legatee is dead
If Zayd is dead before the testator, his share does not pass to Amr because the shares were separate from the outset. Amr only receives his own half of the third.
4
State the distribution
Amr receives half of the one-third (i.e., one-sixth of the estate). The other half of the third either reverts to the estate or is considered void (depending on whether the will is for one-third of the whole estate).
The text only states Amr gets half; the disposition of the other half is implied.
5
Contrast with the alternative wording
If the testator had said 'to Zayd and Amr' (li-), they would have a common share, and the survivor would take the entire third.
This contrast highlights the importance of linguistic precision in Islamic law.

The reasoning, as cited from al-Hidayah, is that a will is a contract of succession (عقد استخلاف) that is attached to after death (مضاف إلى ما بعد الموت). Therefore, the relevant time for the existence of wealth is at death, not at the time of the will. This distinguishes it from contracts like sale, where the subject matter must exist at the time of contract.

Key Terms
عقد استخلاف (aqd istikhlaf): A contract of succession, whereby the legatee takes the place of the testator in ownership after death.
مضاف إلى ما بعد الموت (mudaf ila ma ba'd al-mawt): Appended to after death, meaning the legal effect of the will is contingent upon and takes effect only after the testator's death.

Will Valid Despite No Property at Execution
Ruling on validity of will made without property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a will when the testator had no property at the time of execution, and explain why the condition for entitlement is wealth at death, not at the time of the will.

Core Idea
A will is valid even if the testator owns nothing when making it — the legatee only gets one-third of what exists at death.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a common scenario: someone writes a will bequeathing one-third of their wealth but owns nothing at that moment. Later, they acquire property before death. Is the will effective? The passage clarifies that under the Hanafi school, the will is perfectly valid and the legatee receives one-third of whatever the testator owned at death. The reasoning hinges on the nature of a will as a contract that only takes effect after death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The testator makes a will for one-third of his wealth but has no property at that time. Later, he acquires wealth before dying.
The will is not tied to the moment of execution.
2
State the Ruling
The legatee is entitled to one-third of what the testator owns at the time of death, not at the time of the will.
The will is valid (sahih) even if no property existed when it was made.
3
Understand the Legal Nature of a Will
A will (wasiyyah) is a contract of deputation (istikhlaf) attached to after death. Its legal effect occurs only after the testator's death.
Therefore, the subject matter (wealth) must exist at death, not at execution.
4
Derive the Condition
The existence of wealth at death is a condition for the legatee's entitlement. It is not a condition for the validity of the will itself.
If the testator never acquires wealth, the will becomes ineffective.
5
Recognize the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider the existence of wealth at the time of the will necessary for validity. The Hanafi school does not require this.
This difference affects practical cases where the testator had no property when making the will.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the Book of Wills in al-Lubab, explaining the effect of a will (wasiyyah) when the testator had no property at the time of making the will but later acquired wealth before death. The ruling: the legatee receives one-third of whatever the testator owns at the time of death. The reasoning (ta'lil) is provided: a will is a contract of deputation (istikhlaf) that is attached to after death, so its legal effect only occurs after death. Therefore, the condition for entitlement is the existence of wealth at death, not at the time of the will. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and the school, as confirmed in al-Hidayah. The ruling applies whether the testator had no wealth at all at the time of the will, or had wealth that later perished before he acquired new wealth.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyyah): A bequest or will, a contract of deputation (istikhlaf) attached to after death.
استخلاف (istikhlaf): Deputation; in the context of a will, it means appointing the legatee to take ownership of the bequeathed property after death.
