Two Buyers, One Slave: The Option of Split Sale
Ruling on two buyers claiming same slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when two buyers each prove purchase of the same slave and explain the consequence if one buyer declines his half after judgment.

Core Idea
Two equal buyers of one slave each get half with the option to exit; if one leaves, the other cannot take the whole.

When two buyers each present valid evidence that they bought the same slave from the same seller, the Hanafi school does not simply nullify both sales. Instead, it preserves fairness by giving each an option: accept half at half price or withdraw entirely. This passage from al-Quduri, as expanded by al-Maydani, explains the ruling and its consequence if one buyer later declines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Two buyers each claim they purchased the same slave from the same seller; both produce evidence. The judge must decide between them.
The seller is a third party in possession.
2
Understand the court's ruling
Both claims are accepted, but since two full ownerships cannot coexist, each buyer is granted ownership of half the slave at half the price.
This is the immediate effect after judgment.
3
Recognise the legal basis for the option
The option (khiyar) arises because the sale is split (tafriq al-safqah) against the buyer's will; only half the item is delivered, so the buyer may choose to accept or rescind.
Tafriq al-safqah is a recognised defect in Hanafi law.
4
Analyse the consequence after judgment
If one buyer after judgment says 'I do not choose' (declines his half), the other does not automatically get the full slave. Each contract is annulled (infisakh) as to its half, and a new contract would be needed for full ownership.
Infisakh is automatic dissolution by operation of law.
5
Apply to modern cases
This principle applies whenever a single item is sold to two claimants, e.g., inheritance disputes or double sales of real estate. The same logic of option and dissolution governs.

Purchase vs. Gift Claim Without Dates
Ruling with proof: purchase is stronger than gift when both claims lack dates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a purchase claim prevails over a gift claim when both lack dates, and explain the legal reasoning based on the nature of each contract.

Core Idea
Purchase is stronger than gift because it is a bilateral exchange that does not need possession.

When two claimants both present valid evidence—one for purchase, the other for gift with possession—but no dates, how does a qadi decide? This passage from al-Quduri establishes that the purchase claim takes priority. The decision rests on the inherent legal strength of a sale contract compared to a gift. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
Two claimants (one claiming purchase, the other claiming gift with possession) against a third party in possession. Both have evidence but no dates.
The defendant is a third party, not one of the claimants.
2
Note the equality of evidence
Since both have evidence and no dates, neither is superior in proof. The judge must rely on the legal strength of the underlying transactions.
3
Apply the legal principle
Purchase is a bilateral exchange (mu'awaḍah) that transfers ownership immediately upon contract, even without possession. It is self-establishing.
Gift (hibah) requires possession (qabḍ) to complete the transfer of ownership.
4
Contrast with gift
A gift claim is weaker because without a date, we cannot know if possession occurred before or after the purchase. Possession is essential for the gift to be valid.
If the gift claimant could prove possession with a date earlier than the purchase, the ruling might differ.
5
Conclude the ruling
Purchase is more entitled (awla). The judge awards the property to the purchase claimant.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Pledge Prevails Over Gift by Istihsan
Ruling with intra-madhhab dispute: pledge is preferred over gift by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school prefers a pledge over a gift by istihsan and contrast it with the qiyas position.

Core Idea
Guarantee (daman) makes a pledge stronger than a gift, even though gift confers ownership.

In a dispute over possession, both parties claim a valid contract—one claims pledge, the other gift. How does the Hanafi judge decide? This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi conflict between strict analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan), focusing on the role of guarantee (daman) as the decisive factor.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of the Hanafi school: when both claim possession with no dates, the pledge claim is preferred over the gift claim.
2
Identify the qiyas view
Understand the opposing view based on strict analogy: gift should win because it transfers ownership, while pledge does not.
This view is attributed to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) or an external qiyas.
3
Examine the istihsan proof
Analyze the reasoning behind istihsan: the pledged item is guaranteed (madmin), imposing liability on the pledgee, whereas a gift is not guaranteed. The contract of guarantee is stronger.
The proof is not a text but a juristic preference based on the principle of protecting the stronger right.
4
Analyze the concept of daman
Understand why guarantee (daman) is considered a stronger legal relationship than mere ownership: it creates responsibility and a right to compensation.
In Hanafi law, liability (daman) often translates to a higher level of legal protection.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Conclude that the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the istihsan view of Imam Abu Hanifa, as adopted in Al-Hidayah and the text.
The tarjih mechanism: Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah endorses the pledge preference.

Since a contract of guarantee (i.e., one that imposes liability) is stronger, the pledgee's claim is preferred. This is a classic example of istihsan overriding qiyas in Hanafi law.

Dividing a Disputed House: Two Claimants, Two Methods
Ruling on dividing a house between two claimants when it is in one man's hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi intra-madhhab methods (tariq al-munaza'a vs. al-'awl) for dividing property between two claimants and state the mufta bihi distribution.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa splits based on actual dispute; the Sahibayn use proportional reduction like inheritance.

This passage presents a classic property dispute where two claimants both present evidence: one claims the entire house, the other half. Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on how to divide the property. The disagreement illustrates two different legal reasoning approaches: one focusing on the scope of actual conflict, the other applying the inheritance principle of 'awl (proportional adjustment). Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to analyze Hanafi procedural law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the claims
The house is in one man's hand. Two claimants: one claims all, the other claims half. Both present evidence (bayyinah). Determine what each is actually contesting.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's method (tariq al-munaza'a)
Abu Hanifa looks at the extent of actual dispute. The half-claimant does not dispute the other half, so that half goes entirely to the all-claimant. They only dispute over the remaining half, so that is split equally. Result: all-claimant gets 3/4, half-claimant 1/4.
This method is based on the concrete conflict, not abstract fractions.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's method (al-'awl)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad treat the claims as fractions: 1 (whole) + 1/2 (half) = 1.5. The base number 2 is increased ('awl) to 3, so the all-claimant gets 2/3 and the half-claimant 1/3. This mirrors inheritance calculations when shares exceed the estate.
'Awl is usually used in inheritance; here it is applied analogously.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The all-claimant receives 3/4 under Abu Hanifa but only 2/3 under the Sahibayn. The difference is 1/12. This shows how a methodological choice changes real outcomes.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its authority
According to al-Tashih, later Hanafi authorities like al-Burhani and al-Nasafi chose the Sahibayn's view. Thus the operative ruling for fatwa is two-thirds for the all-claimant and one-third for the half-claimant.
This tarjih is based on the strength of the Sahibayn's analogy to 'awl.

Dispute Between Claimant of Whole and Claimant of Half
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of house claimed by two parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi method of dividing property when one claimant asserts full ownership and another claims half, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's view from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In dispute resolution, the non-contested portion is conceded, leaving only the contested half to be split equally—giving the whole claimant 3/4.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about dividing a house between two claimants: one claiming the entire property, the other claiming half. Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, offer different methods of division based on distinct legal principles. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi jurisprudence to overlapping claims in property law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim scenario
Recognize that there are two claimants: one claims the whole house, the other claims half. The dispute is over how to divide the house between them.
Note that both claimants assert ownership; the case is about resolving overlapping claims.
2
Apply Abu Hanifa's method of contest analysis
Abu Hanifa considers which portions are contested. The half claimant does not contest half of the house, so that half is conceded entirely to the whole claimant. The remaining half is contested equally, so it is split equally between them.
Result: whole claimant gets 1/2 (conceded) + 1/4 (half of contested) = 3/4; half claimant gets 1/4.
3
Apply the Sahibayn's method using 'awl
The Sahibayn treat the total claims as 1 (whole) + 1/2 (half) = 1.5. Using the inheritance principle of 'awl, they set the base at 2 and increase it to 3, giving the whole claimant 2/3 and the half claimant 1/3.
'Awl is normally used in inheritance when shares exceed the estate; here it is applied analogically.
4
Compare the outcomes
Under Abu Hanifa, the whole claimant receives 75% (3/4) and the half claimant 25% (1/4). Under the Sahibayn, the whole claimant receives about 66.7% (2/3) and the half claimant about 33.3% (1/3).
The practical difference is significant: the whole claimant gets 8.3% more under Abu Hanifa.
5
Note the tarjih (preferred view)
Later Hanafi authorities such as al-Burhani and al-Nasafi in al-Tashih chose Abu Hanifa's view. Thus, the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's ruling.
The tarjih is based on the principle that the half claimant's lack of contest over half is decisive.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a jointly possessed property when one party claims the whole and the other claims half, and identify which portion is awarded by judicial decree and why.

Core Idea
Judge decrees only the contested half; the uncontested half remains with its possessor.

In Hanafi property law, when both parties physically possess a property and one claims full ownership while the other claims half, the judge must carefully distinguish between what is actually contested. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Hidayah, showing how the decree is limited to the portion not already in the claimant's hand, preserving the principle that possession itself carries weight unless challenged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two parties each have possession of a house. One claims the entire house, the other claims only half. Note that each party's possession is over the whole house jointly, not divided.
Possession here means physical control, not ownership.
2
State the whole claimant's claim
The whole claimant demands the entire house. The opponent (half claimant) only claims half, implicitly conceding the other half to the whole claimant.
Because the half claimant's claim is limited to half, the other half is not in dispute.
3
Determine which portion is contested
The half that the half claimant claims is the contested portion. The half that was in the whole claimant's hand is not claimed by the opponent, so it is uncontested.
The whole claimant possessed the entire house along with the opponent; but the opponent's claim only targets half.
4
Apply the rule for judicial decree
A judge only decrees property that is genuinely contested. Since the half in the whole claimant's hand is not claimed by the opponent, it remains with the whole claimant without any judicial order. Only the contested half (claimed by the opponent) is subject to decree, and it is awarded to the whole claimant based on his evidence.
The judge's role is to resolve disputes; uncontested matters are left as they are.
5
Conclude the outcome
The whole claimant receives the entire house: half by judicial decree (the contested portion) and half by default (the uncontested portion stays in his hand).
This avoids unnecessary judicial interference and respects the principle that possession is not disturbed without a claim.

Core Idea
When buyer and seller disagree, the evidence proving an increase is preferred; if no evidence, the judge offers cancellation to encourage agreement.

Disputes in sales contracts are common. Hanafi fiqh provides clear rules: if only one party has evidence, that evidence prevails. If both have evidence, the evidence that proves an increase (ziyadah) takes priority. If neither has evidence, the judge offers the option to accept the other's claim or face cancellation. This lesson unpacks the reasoning from Al-Quduri's matn and its commentary.

Guided Path

Determine whether the dispute is over the price (thaman) or the quantity of the sold item (mabi') or both. The text covers both possibilities.

Check if one party, both parties, or neither party provides evidence (bayyinah). The ruling differs for each case.
Evidence here means two male witnesses or one male and two females.

If only one has evidence, that party wins. If both have evidence, the evidence that proves an increase (claiming more) is preferred. For price, seller's evidence wins; for quantity, buyer's evidence wins.

This threat of cancellation often leads to mutual agreement.

If they still do not agree and the goods exist, the judge administers an oath to each party. The text does not detail the oath's effect, but it confirms the claim under oath.
Oath-taking (istihlaf) is a final judicial tool in Hanafi procedure.

Mutual Oath in Sale: Sequence and Judicial Rescission
Ruling on procedure and effect when buyer and seller both swear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the oath sequence (buyer first) and explain why mutual oaths require judicial rescission rather than automatic voidance.

Core Idea
In sale disputes, the judge begins with the buyer's oath; if both swear, he rescinds the contract.

When buyer and seller disagree over the price or goods and neither has proof, the law prescribes a mutual oath procedure. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies not only who takes the oath first but also what happens after both swear. Understanding this ruling is essential for applying the Hanafi rules of dispute resolution in sale contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on oath sequence
Identify the dominant view that the judge begins with the buyer's oath. Note the supporting scholars: Muhammad al-Shaybani, Abu Yusuf (later), and a narration from Abu Hanifah.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There is an alternative narration that begins with the seller's oath. Recognize it as a legitimate minority view within the madhhab.
Mentioned by al-Isbijabi and Abu al-Hasan.
3
Analyze the preferred view's reasoning
Each party is both claimant and defendant. The buyer is claimant regarding the subject matter (goods), so he swears first. The seller is claimant regarding the price.
The later verifying imams (al-musahḥiḥūn) adopted buyer-first.
4
Determine the consequence after mutual oaths
If both swear, the contract becomes a sale without a specified consideration (badal), rendering it corrupt (fasid).
The oaths contradict each other; neither claim is established.
5
Understand the judicial role
The contract does not void automatically. The judge must actively rescind (faskh) to end the dispute, because the corrupt contract requires judicial dissolution.
Al-Hidayah explicitly states: 'it is not dissolved by the mere exchange of oaths'.

Disputes about term, option, or partial payment do not trigger mutual oath because they are ancillary, not central, to the contract.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the ruling of mutual oath-taking (تحالف) when part of the sold merchandise is destroyed before delivery and the parties then dispute the price. The chapter is Kitab al-Da'wa wa al-Bayyinat (Book of Claims and Evidence). The base text from al-Quduri states: 'The saying is made the saying of the buyer (with his oath) because he is denying an increase in price.' Then a view of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan is added: both parties take mutual oaths (يتحالفان) and the sale is annulled based on the value of the destroyed part. The commentary explains that this is because the dispute concerns the price of a subsisting contract, analogous to when the commodity still exists. The author, Jamal al-Islam (al-Zayla'i), states that the correct position according to the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) is that mutual oath-taking is required, and this was followed by later scholars like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.

The passage then considers the case where part of the sold item perishes (e.g., one of two slaves or garments). Abu Hanifa rules: no mutual oath-taking, because the rule of mutual oath-taking is an exception to analogy established only when the entire commodity exists; once part is destroyed, the basis for the exception is lost. However, there is an exception: if the seller agrees to forgo the share of the destroyed item (i.e., reduces the price proportionally), then mutual oath-taking occurs because the entire price then corresponds to the remaining part.

Abu Yusuf holds: mutual oath-taking does occur, and the sale is annulled for the remaining (living) part and the value of the destroyed part. This is also the view of Muhammad according to one narration. Al-Isbijabi notes a variant: in al-Jami' al-Saghir, according to Abu Yusuf, the buyer's word is taken for the destroyed share but mutual oaths are taken for the remainder; Muhammad says mutual oaths are taken for both parts. The final judgment (تصحيح) declares that the correct view is that of Abu Hanifa, and this is what al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and others followed. Thus the operative ruling is: when part of the merchandise is destroyed, no mutual oath-taking occurs, unless the seller agrees to waive his claim on the destroyed portion.

القول قول المشتري (al-qawl qawl al-mushtarī): The statement of the buyer is accepted (with oath) over the seller's claim when they dispute the price, because the buyer is denying an increase.
هالك (hālik): The portion of the sold item that has perished, e.g., one of two slaves or garments.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Sale with Perished Goods
States three Hanafi imams' positions on a sale where some goods perished.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on a sale where goods perish before delivery, and identify which position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: seller forfeits perished share; Sahibayn: mutual oaths and rescission.

When some goods in a sale perish before delivery and the parties disagree on the price, the Hanafi Imams differed on how to resolve the dispute. This lesson presents the three views and explains why Abu Hanifa's view is the operative one for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Recognize that the central question is the legal effect of partial perishment on the sale contract and the need for mutual oaths (tahalf).
The dispute only arises if the seller does not agree to forfeit the perished share.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
According to Abu Hanifa, no mutual oaths are required; the seller forfeits the price of the perished goods, and the buyer pays the full price for the remaining goods.
This is based on the idea that the refusal of oaths is due to the perishing, so the ruling is limited to that extent.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the parties take mutual oaths on the living goods, the sale is rescinded for them, and the buyer pays the value of the perished portion.
Al-Isbijabi notes a nuanced difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in how oaths are taken.
4
Analyze the tarjih
Later Hanafi authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi declared Abu Hanifa's view as the correct one (mufta bihi).
The tarjih is based on the principle that unnecessary oaths should be avoided when the perished portion is clearly a loss to the seller.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the buyer keeps the remaining goods at full price; under the Sahibayn, the buyer can rescind the sale for the living goods, leading to different practical outcomes.
This affects who bears the risk of loss and the seller's ability to enforce the contract.

Seller's Claim of Paternity: Returning the Price (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on price return when seller claims paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on the return of price when the seller claims paternity of a child born to a sold slave woman and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When the seller claims paternity of a child born to the sold slave, the mother becomes umm walad and the sale is void; the dispute is over how much price the seller must return.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). The common scenario: a seller of a female slave claims that a child born to her after sale is his own child, and the mother dies. The paternity claim is accepted, voiding the sale, but how much of the purchase price must the seller return? The answer depends on whether the mother's value is compensable or not, a key Hanafi distinction. We will explore the reasoning behind each position and see how later authorities resolved the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Understand the facts: a female slave is sold, then she gives birth to a child within six months of the sale. The seller claims the child is his. The child is alive, but the mother dies. The claim is accepted, establishing paternity and making the mother an umm walad. The sale is therefore void. The question: what must the seller return to the buyer – the entire price or only part?
The six-month period is significant because it indicates the child was conceived before the sale, strengthening the seller's claim.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its proof
Abu Hanifa rules that the seller must return the entire price. His reasoning: because the mother is an umm walad, her value is not compensable in contracts or usurpation (ghasb) according to his view. The price paid was for both mother and child, but since the mother's value is not guaranteed, the entire price is effectively for the child, which must be returned.
The principle: 'maaliyat al-umm walad ghayr mutaqawwimah' (the wealth of the umm walad is not compensable).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the seller returns only the portion of the price corresponding to the child's value. They argue that the price was in exchange for both mother and child, and the mother's value is compensable in their view. Therefore, the buyer is liable for the mother's value; only the child's share of the price is returned.
They would divide the price between the mother's value and the child's value, returning only the child's portion.
4
Analyze the underlying legal principle
The disagreement hinges on whether the umm walad's value is considered compensable (mutaqawwim) or not. Abu Hanifa: her value is not compensable because she is owned by her master in a special way (ownership of which is tied to the child). The Sahibayn: her value is compensable like any other slave. This affects liability for damages and contract prices.
This is a fundamental difference in Hanafi fiqh on the nature of umm walad ownership.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text cites al-Tashih, stating that later authorities (al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Sharia) followed Abu Hanifa's view. Therefore, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the seller returns the entire price.
Thus, in practice, the seller bears the full loss of the child's value (and the mother's) by returning the full price.

Division When Co-Owners Are Buyers or Property in Absent Party's Hands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state two conditions that prevent judicial division of property and explain the distinction between heir's ownership (milk khilafah) and buyer's ownership (milk mubtada') that underlies the rule.

Core Idea
Division requires representation: heirs represent one another; buyers do not.

In Hanafi law, property division (qisma) is a form of judgment that must ensure fair representation of all parties. This lesson examines two specific cases where the absence of a co-owner blocks division: when the co-owners are buyers and when the property is in the hands of an absent heir. The key distinction lies in the nature of ownership—whether it is derivative (inheritance) or original (purchase)—which determines if the present co-owner can legally represent the absent one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage states two scenarios where the judge does not divide: (1) when co-owners are buyers and one is absent, and (2) when the property or part of it is in the hand of an absent heir (or a minor). Note that division means judicial partition (qisma jabriyya), not mutual agreement.
2
Understand the distinction: milk khilafah vs milk mubtada'
The passage gives a rational distinction (farq): heir's ownership is milk khilafah (derivative, tied to the deceased), while buyer's ownership is milk mubtada' (original, independent). This affects the ability to represent an absent co-owner in litigation.
Key concept: In inheritance, the heir stands in the shoes of the deceased and can be represented by another heir regarding the deceased's share.
3
Apply the distinction to buyers' case
Because each buyer's ownership is original and independent, a present buyer cannot act as an adversary for an absent buyer. Thus, division would be a judgment against an unrepresented absent party, which is invalid.
4
Apply the distinction to property in absent heir's hands
If the property is physically in the possession of an absent heir (or his depositee, or a minor), there is no present adversary to represent that absent party regarding the possession. Therefore, division cannot proceed.
The same logic applies: division is a judgment affecting the absent party's right of possession without representation.

In Hanafi fiqh, the division of jointly owned property, especially real estate, is a specific transaction. A common question arises: can currency be used to balance shares? This lesson examines the ruling from al-Quduri's matn and its commentaries, clarifying when cash can enter a division.

Partition and Pre-existing Servitudes: Right of Way and Drainage
Ruling on partition with pre-existing servitudes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on partition when a co-owner has a pre-existing right of passage or drainage, determining whether the partition is valid or must be rescinded.

Core Idea
A partition that leaves a servitude intact is defective; it must be rerouted or rescinded.

Partition (qisma) among co-owners must result in complete separation of shares. If one co-owner already has a right of way or water drainage over another's portion, and this was not mentioned in the partition agreement, the Hanafi school provides a clear rule: if the servitude can be rerouted without harm, the right is lost; otherwise, the partition is rescinded. This lesson walks through the condition and its consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pre-existing servitude
Determine if one co-owner holds a right of passage (tariq) or water drainage (masil) over the other's land. Note that it was not stipulated in the partition contract.
This applies only to rights established before the partition.
2
Check feasibility of diversion
Ask: can the path or drainage be redirected away from the other's share without causing harm? If yes, the partition can proceed with the right rerouted.
The burden of proof lies on the party claiming impossibility.
3
Apply the consequence
If diversion is possible, the co-owner loses the right to use the other's land. If not, the partition is rescinded (faskh) because it remains defective—the shares are not fully separated.
Rescission means the judge annuls the contract; a new partition must account for the servitude.

Coercion in Contracts: The Right to Rescind
States Hanafi ruling on contracts under duress and defines actionable coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on contracts entered under coercion, identify what constitutes legal coercion (ikrah), and apply the option (khiyar) to rescind or confirm.

Core Idea
Coercion by credible threat gives the coerced party the option to confirm or cancel the contract.

A contract's validity depends on free consent. When a person is forced into a sale, lease, or acknowledgment by a serious threat, Islamic law does not automatically void the contract but grants the victim a choice. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling: the contract is suspended (mawquf), not void, and the coerced party may ratify or rescind it. We also examine what threats qualify as coercion and who is considered specially vulnerable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon—here, a contract (sale, purchase, lease, or acknowledgment) entered under coercion. The ruling is that the coerced party has an option (khiyar): he may either ratify the contract (imda') or rescind it (faskh). If he rescinds, he recovers the property (e.g., the sold item).
The contract is not void (batil) but suspended (mawquf), meaning its final effect depends on the victim's choice.
2
Identify Types of Coercion That Trigger the Ruling
The text specifies that only threats of death, severe beating, or prolonged imprisonment constitute ikrah. Mild threats such as a single whip, one day imprisonment, or shackles do not qualify according to custom. An exception exists for persons of high rank (sahib mansib) for whom even such mild threats cause significant harm and thus become coercion.
The standard of coercion is determined by custom ('urf), which may vary by time and place.
3
Understand the Legal Reasoning
Contracts require mutual consent (taradi). Coercion destroys consent (rida), so the contract is defective. However, Hanafis do not consider it void because the coerced person still exercises a form of choice (ikhtiyar) in performing the action. Therefore, the contract is suspended until the victim's subsequent ratification or rescission.
This distinguishes the Hanafi position from Imām al-Shāfiʿī, who holds that coercion renders the contract void ab initio.
4
Apply the Option
The coerced party should be given the opportunity to decide. If he ratifies, the contract becomes effective from that point. If he rescinds, the contract is annulled and the parties return to their pre-contract positions (e.g., the seller takes back the item).
The option does not expire until the victim explicitly confirms or a reasonable time passes indicating acceptance.

Voluntary vs. Coerced Receipt in Coerced Sales
Explains how voluntary receipt of price ratifies coerced sale; forced receipt does not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the legal effect of voluntary vs. coerced receipt of price or goods in a coerced sale and determine when the buyer is liable for the sold item.

Core Idea
Voluntary receipt of price or goods after a coerced sale ratifies the contract; coerced receipt does not, and if the sold item perishes with a willing buyer, he must pay its value.

When a sale is made under duress, the contract is not automatically void but is suspended. The coerced party's subsequent voluntary actions can indicate consent and thereby validate the sale. This lesson examines two key scenarios: receiving the price and delivering the goods. The critical factor is whether the act was done willingly or under further compulsion. We also explore the buyer's liability if the sold item is destroyed while in his possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core distinction
Recognize that the legal outcome hinges on whether the coerced seller's action (receiving price or delivering goods) is voluntary or coerced. This determines ratification or continued invalidity.
2
Apply to voluntary receipt of price
If the seller voluntarily accepts the price from the buyer, this is evidence of ratification (ijazah). The sale becomes valid from that moment, as if the initial coercion never occurred.
Compare with a suspended sale (bay' mawquf) where ratification by a non-coerced party cures the defect.
3
Apply to coerced receipt of price
If the seller receives the price under duress (e.g., the buyer forces him to take it), this does not count as ratification. The contract remains invalid, and the seller must return the price if it still exists.
The lack of consent (rida) prevents the act from indicating acceptance.
4
Extend to voluntary delivery of goods
The same rule applies if the seller voluntarily delivers the sold item to the buyer. As long as the coercion was on the original sale and not on the delivery itself, delivery signals ratification.
The Hidayah explicitly extends the ruling to this scenario.
5
Analyze liability for destruction
If the sold item perishes in the buyer's hands and the buyer was not coerced (i.e., he freely entered the transaction), he is liable for its value because he held another's property without a valid contract. The seller was coerced, so the buyer cannot rely on the contract.
This liability arises even if the buyer had not yet taken delivery? The text implies possession after a coerced but unratified sale.

Liability for a Sold Item Perishing in the Buyer's Hand Under Coercion
Ruling on liability when sold item perishes with a non-coerced buyer and the coerced party's option to sue.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the liability rules for a sold item that perishes in the buyer's hand under coercion scenarios and apply the coerced seller's option to recover value from either the coercer or the buyer.

Core Idea
When a sold item perishes in a coerced buyer's hands, the coercer pays—not the buyer.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies a nuanced scenario: a sale made under coercion, where the sold item subsequently perishes in the buyer's possession. The ruling hinges on whether the buyer himself was coerced. The Hanafi school provides a just solution by giving the coerced seller a choice to seek compensation from either the coercer or the buyer, ensuring that the real wrongdoer (the coercer) is ultimately held accountable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the sale under coercion, which is invalid (fasid) due to coercion. The item perishes in the buyer's hand. The ruling is that the buyer guarantees its value if he is not coerced; otherwise the coercer is liable.
The default state: a non-coerced buyer holds the item without valid title, so he is liable.
2
Differentiate based on buyer's coercion status
If the buyer is also coerced, the buyer is considered a mere tool of the coercer. Therefore, the guarantee shifts entirely to the coercer, and the buyer has no liability.
This is a key differentiator: the buyer's coercion status changes liability.
3
Analyze the coerced seller's option
The coerced seller (or his representative) may choose to demand compensation from either the coercer or the buyer. This is analogized to the case of a usurper and a sub-usurper (ghasib wa ghasib al-ghasib).
The analogy is based on the idea that the coercer is the effective cause (mutlif) of the loss.
4
Trace the consequences of choosing the buyer
If the seller chooses to sue the buyer, the buyer becomes the owner of the item by virtue of the guarantee (daman). Any subsequent sales made by the buyer after the guarantee are valid, but prior sales are not automatically validated unless the original owner ratifies them.
Ownership by guarantee is retroactive only to the time of possession (qabd).
5
Note the exception for ratification
If the coerced owner ratifies a particular contract (e.g., one of the sales made before the guarantee), that contract becomes valid from its inception. Otherwise, undisputed sales before the guarantee remain invalid.
Ratification (ijazah) is a separate act that can retroactively validate a contract.

Core Idea
A coerced seller may claim damages from whomever he chooses — the coercer or the buyer — just as an owner can choose between two usurpers.

In this passage from al-Lubab, we examine the legal position of a person forced to sell his property. The Hanafi school grants him a choice to hold either the coercer or the buyer liable. If the owner (the coerced) later ratifies a contract, both past and future contracts become valid automatically, because he has waived his right (the obstacle).
Ratification is a stronger act than mere claim of damages.

Core Idea
Necessity permits destroying another's property when life or limb is threatened, analogized to eating haram in famine.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a critical scenario: a person is coerced to destroy a Muslim's property under threat to his own life or limb. The Hanafi school rules that he may do so, invoking the principle of darurah – necessity makes the forbidden permissible. The proof is an analogical extension (qiyas) to the well-established case of extreme hunger (makhmassa) where consuming haram food is allowed. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the conditions of darurah and the school's method of istihsan (juristic preference) via qiyas khafi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
First, recognize that the act under discussion is destroying (itlaf) another person's property. By default, this is haram (prohibited) as it violates the sanctity of a Muslim's wealth.
The default ruling is important to appreciate the role of necessity.
2
State the exceptional ruling under coercion
When a person is coerced under a credible threat to his life or a limb, the ruling shifts from haram to permissible (mubah). Note the conditions: the threat must be direct ('by an order he fears') and grave (life or limb).

In Hanafi usul, necessity is recognized as a legitimate cause (sabab) that lifts prohibitions. The qiyas is valid because the illa (necessity) is present in both cases. The ruling (ibaha/permissibility) transfers from the asl to the far'.
This is a form of qiyas jaliy (evident analogy) or could be considered istihsan based on necessity.

The permission is strictly limited: it does not extend to killing or major sins, and some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require compensation or hold that property cannot be destroyed even under duress. The Hanafi position relies on the overriding weight of preserving life (hifz al-nafs).
Know the practical difference: under Hanafi law, the coerced person is not liable for compensation in this exact scenario (though later Hanafi fatwa may vary).

Buying Kharaj Land from a Dhimmi and Non-Combination of 'Ushr and Kharaj

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Buying kharaj land does not remove the kharaj; 'ushr and kharaj never combine on the same land.

Land taxation in Islamic law distinguishes between kharaj (a fixed tax on land conquered by force) and 'ushr (a tithe on produce from lands whose owners accepted Islam or are Muslims). This lesson examines two related rulings from al-Lubab: the permissibility of a Muslim purchasing kharaj land from a dhimmi while the kharaj remains due, and the principle that the two taxes never coexist on the same land. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Touching a slave woman for purchase is allowed only if no desire arises; looking is allowed even with desire due to necessity.

This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri addresses a practical scenario: inspecting a slave woman before purchase. While earlier Hanafi texts unconditionally permit touching and looking, later scholars refined the ruling by distinguishing the presence of sexual desire. This lesson unpacks that refinement and the underlying reasoning based on necessity versus enjoyment.
