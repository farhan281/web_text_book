Learning Objective

Core Idea
A parent's love may bias testimony; Hanafi law absolutely bars a parent from testifying for any descendant.

Testimony in Islamic law requires impartiality. The Hanafi school, based on the principle of avoiding bias, rules that a parent cannot testify for their own child — even if the parent is known to be honest. This lesson unpacks the ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri, focusing on its scope: the prohibition extends upward to grandparents and downward to grandchildren and beyond.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The text mentions 'al-walid' (parent) and 'al-walad' (child). The Hanafi school includes both father and mother in 'walid', and any direct descendant in 'walad'.
Later commentaries clarify that 'walid' also includes grandparents (ascendants) and 'walad' includes grandchildren (descendants).
2

The phrases 'wa in 'ala' (even if ascending) and 'wa in safal' (even if descending) extend the prohibition to all direct ascendants (e.g., grandfather) and all direct descendants (e.g., grandson).
These expansions are typical in Hanafi matn: they prevent any misinterpretation that the ruling only applies to the immediate parent-child pair.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason (illah) is the inherent emotional attachment (mashq al-nafs) that makes a parent inclined to favor their child, potentially compromising truthful testimony.
Hanafi law does not require proof of actual bias; the mere relationship is sufficient to bar testimony.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school disagrees: they accept a parent's testimony for a child if the parent is deemed trustworthy ('adl). The Hanafi position is stricter, rejecting it categorically.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different ta'wil of the same principles of testimony (shahada).

The prohibition applies regardless of the child's age, wealth, or independence.

Imam Abu Hanifa requires that their testimonies agree in both wording and meaning through direct indication (al-wad'). The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the later school is that of the Sahibayn, as it facilitates legal practice. The commentary also cites al-Hidayah, emphasizing that the prior claim is a necessary condition for accepting testimony in matters of private rights.

Hearsay Testimony: The Six Exceptions and Conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule prohibiting hearsay testimony, list the six exceptions with their conditions, and explain the Hanafi position on the disputes regarding wala' and waqf.

Core Idea
Hearsay testimony is invalid except for six matters known by necessity; its acceptance is conditional and the witness must not reveal it as hearsay.

This lesson unpacks a key principle in Hanafi testimony law: hearsay (tasamu') is generally rejected, but a concession (rukhsah) is made for six matters due to the difficulty of direct observation and the need to preserve legal rulings across generations. You will learn the exact conditions for this concession, the procedural rule that the witness must not qualify his testimony, and the nuanced positions on wala' and waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule and Its Basis
The text states that a witness may not testify to something he has not witnessed (lam yu'aayin). Understand that testimony (shahada) is derived from witnessing (mushahada), which requires knowledge. The general rule is based on this linguistic and legal connection.
The default requirement is direct observation.
2
List the Six Exceptions and the Rationale
The exception covers: (1) lineage (nasab), (2) death (mawt), (3) marriage (nikah), (4) entering (dukhul – consummation), (5) appointment of a judge (wilayat al-qadi). Note that some sources list six including something else; here the text mentions five clearly; the sixth is often 'possession' but it's disputed. The rationale is istihsan: hardship would result if hearsay were not accepted for these widely known but rarely directly witnessed matters.
The Hanafi school is unanimous on these exceptions.
3
Explain the Conditions for Hearsay Acceptance
The witness must be informed by two just men (rajulan 'adlan) or one man and two women. For death only, a single informant (male or female) may suffice. This provides a degree of certainty. The witness should not qualify his testimony as hearsay; if he explains to the judge that he is testifying by hearsay, his testimony is rejected (analogous to testifying about possession based on seeing a hand).
The condition of two male witnesses or one male + two females is standard for most testimony; death has a relaxation.
4
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Wala' and Waqf
The text discusses whether hearsay is accepted for wala' (clientage) and waqf (endowment). Abu Yusuf allowed it for wala' analogously to lineage; Muhammad allowed it for waqf. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that hearsay is not accepted for wala' because it depends on a specific transaction (removal of ownership) requiring direct witnessing, and for waqf it is accepted only for its establishment (asl), not its conditions (shurut).
This khilaf shows the application of qiyas and istihsan within the madhhab.

Testimony upon testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada) is permissible by istihsan for any right that is not invalidated by suspicion (shubha). This allows a secondary witness to report the testimony of the primary witness without having directly witnessed the original event.

However, an exception (istihsan) is made for six matters due to the difficulty of direct observation and the need to preserve legal rulings over time: lineage (nasab), death (mawt), marriage (nikah), entering (dukhul — meaning marital consummation), and the appointment of a judge (wilayat al-qadi). For these, hearsay testimony is accepted if the witness is informed by someone he trusts. Al-Hidayah clarifies that the condition for such testimony is that the informant be two just men or one man and two women, to give the witness a degree of certainty. For death specifically, a single informant (male or female) may suffice because it is rarely witnessed by many. The witness should state his testimony in absolute terms without specifying it is hearsay; if he qualifies it as hearsay, the testimony is rejected. This is analogous to testifying about possession of property based on seeing one's hand in it: if the witness says 'I saw his hand in it' without more, it is accepted; if he explains that his knowledge is based on that hand, it is not. Second, the ruling on testimony upon testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada): it is permissible for any right that is not invalidated by suspicion (shubha), by istihsan due to necessity. The passage also addresses two specific points: (a) hearsay is not accepted for wala' (clientage) because it requires direct witnessing of the transfer of ownership; Abu Yusuf allowed it analogously to lineage, but the preferred view is that it is not accepted. (b) For waqf (endowment), hearsay is accepted for its establishment (asl) because it becomes widely known, but not for its specific conditions (shurut). The text thus preserves the integrity of testimony while accommodating practical needs.

Commentary
This passage details the correct procedure for testimony upon testimony. The original witness (شاهد الأصل) must formally instruct the branch witness (شاهد الفرع) by saying 'اشهد على شهادتي' (bear witness upon my testimony) and then state the content of the testimony. It is recommended but not obligatory to include the phrase 'وأشهدني على نفسه' (and he made me a witness upon himself); omitting it is still valid because if one hears an acknowledgment directly, he is permitted to testify without being formally called as a witness. The branch witness, when testifying, must state that the original witness made him a witness upon his testimony, and then relay the content. The commentary explains the analogy: the branch witness is like a deputy (نائب) of the original, so there must be an authorization (توكيل). The footnote clarifies that the word 'إن' in the branch witness's phrase 'أنه يشهد أن فلاناً أقر' is a conjunctive (وصلية) linking the two statements.

Key Terms
شاهد الأصل (shāhid al-aṣl): The original witness who directly witnessed the event or received the acknowledgment.
شاهد الفرع (shāhid al-farʿ): The branch witness who receives the testimony from the original witness and transmits it to court.
تحميل (taḥmīl): The act of the original witness entrusting (burdening) the branch witness with the testimony.
توكيل (tawkīl): Authorization; the original witness appointing the branch witness as his deputy to testify.

Procedure and Conditions for Substitute Testimony
Practical procedure and conditions for substitute testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct wording for empowering a secondary witness and identify the conditions under which substitute testimony is accepted, including the intra-Hanafi difference on the required absence duration.

Core Idea
Substitute testimony requires explicit empowerment and is accepted only if the original is dead or absent for at least three days' journey.

This passage from al-Lubab explains the practical steps for substitute testimony, a procedure that avoids hardship when principal witnesses are unavailable. It covers both the formula for delegation and the conditions that trigger its validity, including an important disagreement within the Hanafi school on the required travel distance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two roles
Recognize the original witness (shāhid al-aṣl) who directly observed the event, and the secondary witness (shāhid al-farʿ) who is delegated to testify on his behalf.
The secondary is like a deputy (nā'ib) of the original.
2
Analyze the recommended empowerment wording
The original should say: 'Ishhad ʿalā shahādatī, annī ashhadu anna fulānan… aqarra ʿindī bi-kadhā, wa ashhadanī ʿalā nafsihī.' Note that the phrase 'ashhadanī ʿalā nafsihī' is recommended but not obligatory.
Omitting it still validates the empowerment.
3
Identify the secondary's court statement
When testifying, the secondary must say: 'Ashhadu anna fulānan ashhadanī ʿalā shahādatihī annahu yashhadu anna fulānan aqarra ʿindahu bi-kadhā, wa qāla lī: ishhad ʿalā shahādatī bi-dhālika.'
Shorter wordings are also acceptable.
4
Examine the acceptance conditions
Substitute testimony is only accepted if the original witnesses are dead or absent for a journey of three days or more (masīrat thalāthat ayyām).
This is the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
5
Compare with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf considered any absence that prevents the original from spending the night at home sufficient. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: under majority, a one-day absence does not permit substitute testimony; under Abu Yusuf, it might.
The mufta bihi is the three-day requirement.

Commentary
This passage from 'al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri' discusses the procedure and conditions for substitute testimony (al-ishhad 'ala al-shahada) in the Hanafi school. The text first explains the required wording for the original witness (shahid al-asl) when empowering the secondary witness (shahid al-far'). The original must say: 'Testify on my testimony, that I testify that so-and-so confessed before me for such-and-such, and he made me a witness upon himself.' However, if he omits the phrase 'he made me a witness upon himself', it is still valid because hearing the confession directly suffices for the secondary to testify. Then, the secondary witness, when presenting the testimony in court, must state: 'I testify that so-and-so made me a witness upon his testimony, that he testifies that so-and-so confessed before him for such-and-such, and he said to me: testify on my testimony to that.' This includes reference to the original testimony and the empowerment. The commentary notes that there are longer and shorter wordings; the shortest is for the original to say 'testify on my testimony to such-and-such' and the secondary 'I testify on his testimony to such-and-such', which is the preferred position according to al-Sarakhsi and others. Regarding conditions, substitute testimony is only accepted if the original witnesses are dead or absent for a journey of three days or more (i.e., travel distance). This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani. However, Abu Yusuf held that any absence that prevents the original from returning home overnight suffices. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the three-day travel requirement, though some later fatwa works reference Abu Yusuf's view.

Key Terms
شاهد الأصل (shāhid al-aṣl): The original witness who directly observed the event and commissions a secondary witness to testify on his behalf.
شاهد الفرع (shāhid al-farʿ): The secondary witness who has been delegated by the original witness to testify in court on his behalf.
التحميل والتوكيل (al-taḥmīl wa al-tawkīl): The empowerment and delegation of testimony from the original witness to the secondary witness, a necessary step for substitute testimony.
مسيرة ثلاثة أيام (masīrat thalāthat ayyām): A travel distance of three days (approximately 48-64 miles or 77-103 km in classical terms) used as a benchmark for the absence of the original witness.
