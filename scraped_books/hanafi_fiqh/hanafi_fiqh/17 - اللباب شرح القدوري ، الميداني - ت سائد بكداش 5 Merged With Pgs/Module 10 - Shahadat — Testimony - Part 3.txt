Definition and Wording of Shahada 'ala al-Shahada
Defines the legal conditions and prescribed wording for substitute testimony in Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of shahada 'ala al-shahada, identify its conditions, and specify the minimum required wording according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Substitute testimony is only valid when original witnesses are unavailable, and the wording must explicitly convey assignment.

This lesson introduces the concept of testimony upon testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada), a secondary form of evidence used when original witnesses cannot attend court. You will learn the precise conditions that make this testimony admissible and the exact phrasing required for both the original and substitute witnesses. The Hanafi school has settled on a concise formula that fulfills the essential elements.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of shahada 'ala al-shahada
Recognize that substitute witnesses (shuhud al-far') testify on behalf of original witnesses (shuhud al-asl) only when the originals are unavailable due to death, travel, or illness.
This is a concession to avoid loss of rights.
2
Identify the conditions for acceptance
The matn states three conditions: death of original witnesses, absence for three days travel or more, or severe illness. Note that Abu Yusuf expands 'travel' to any journey preventing overnight return (covered in next lesson).
These conditions ensure that substitute testimony is not used when the original is available.
3
Analyze the required wording
The original witness must say 'اشهد على شهادتي بكذا' (Bear witness to my testimony regarding such-and-such), and the substitute says 'أشهد على شهادته بكذا' (I bear witness to his testimony...). The wording must include three elements: the witness's own testimony, mention of the original testimony, and assignment (tahmil).
The assignment phrase 'على شهادتي' or 'على شهادته' is essential.
4
Compare longer and shorter formulations
The Hidayah notes there are longer and shorter wordings; the middle way is praised. Al-Durr al-Mukhtar cites the shortest acceptable form supported by al-Sarakhsi and Ibn Kamal Pasha.
The shorter wording is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
5
Recognize the mufta bihi wording
The relied-upon wording for the original is 'اشهد على شهادتي بكذا' and for the substitute 'أشهد على شهادته بكذا'. This is from the later fatwa tradition (mutakhkhirin).
This wording is concise yet fulfills all requirements.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Testimonies defines the concept of 'testimony upon testimony' (shahada 'ala al-shahada) and specifies the correct wording. The matn states that substitute testimony is generally not accepted unless the original witnesses are unavailable due to death, travel of three days or more, or illness. The sharh then explains the required wording: the original witness must say 'Bear witness to my testimony' (Ishhad 'ala shahadati bi-kadha), and the substitute must respond 'I bear witness to his testimony' (Ashhadu 'ala shahadatihi bi-kadha). This wording includes three elements: the witness's own testimony, mention of the original testimony, and mention of the assignment (tahmil). The Hidayah notes that there are longer and shorter formulations, and the middle way is best. Al-Durr al-Mukhtar cites the shortest acceptable wording, which is supported by al-Sarakhsi and Ibn Kamal Pasha, and is considered the more correct (asahh) position. This is from the later fatwa tradition (mutakhkhirin).

Key Terms
شهادة على شهادة (shahāda ʿalā al-shahāda): Testimony given based on another's testimony when the original witness cannot attend court.
شهود الأصل (shuhūd al-aṣl): The original witnesses who directly observed the event.
شهود الفرع (shuhūd al-farʿ): Substitute witnesses who testify on behalf of the original witnesses.
التحميل (al-taḥmīl): The act of the original witness assigning the testimony to the substitute.

Conditions for Substitute Testimony and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Travel Distance
Details conditions for substitute testimony and Abu Yusuf's differing view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for accepting substitute testimony in the Hanafi school and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the travel condition.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires three days of travel for substitute testimony; Abu Yusuf requires only prevention of overnight stay—two views, one need.

Substitute testimony (shahada al-far') is accepted only when the original witnesses are genuinely unable to attend court. This lesson examines the three conditions (death, travel, severe illness) that satisfy this inability, focusing on the well-known intra-Hanafi dispute over what constitutes sufficient travel. The passage from al-Lubab shows that while Abu Hanifa's three-day rule is the zahir al-riwayah, Abu Yusuf's more lenient view is often preferred in fatwa due to convenience (arfaq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions for accepting substitute testimony
Read the matn: death of the original witnesses at the time of performance, travel (masīrat thalāthat ayyām) or more, or severe illness preventing attendance at court. These represent the threshold for 'inability' (ʿajz).
The condition of death is absolute; travel and illness have degrees.
2
Understand the rationale (talil)
The commentary explains that permissibility is based on necessity (ḥāja). Substitute testimony is only relied upon when the original's inability is realized (taḥaqquq al-ʿajz). Without genuine inability, the original must testify.
This rationale underlies the entire institution.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute on travel
Abu Hanifa requires a travel distance of three days (or more). Abu Yusuf differs: any absence that prevents the witness from staying overnight with his family suffices. Note that this is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
Both are based on defining 'inability' differently.
4
Analyse the tarjih among later authorities
The commentary cites multiple sources: some (e.g., Tashih al-Quduri, al-Binayah) consider Abu Hanifa's view as better (aḥsan) and the apparent narration (ẓāhir al-riwāya); others (e.g., al-Qahistani, al-Sirajiyyah) give fatwa on Abu Yusuf's view because it is more convenient (arfaq) and better for reviving people's rights.
The muftā bihi is not uniform; muftis may adopt either depending on context.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, substitute testimony is not accepted if travel is less than three days, even if the witness cannot sleep at home. Under Abu Yusuf, it would be accepted. This has direct practical consequences in court procedure.
The difference affects over 2-day travel cases.

Condition of Illness for Testimony on Testimony
Condition for accepting testimony on testimony with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for accepting testimony on testimony and distinguish between the two opinions on the threshold of incapacity.

Core Idea
Testimony on testimony is permitted only when original witnesses are genuinely incapacitated from attending court.

Testimony on testimony (شهادة على الشهادة) allows substitute witnesses to convey the testimony of original witnesses who cannot appear. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi requirement that the original witnesses must be truly incapable of attending court due to illness or travel. We will explore the reasoning (need-based) and an intra-Hanafi difference on how to measure that incapacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The passage states that testimony on testimony is accepted if the original witnesses are ill or on a journey that prevents them from attending court. Note the qualifying phrase: 'لا يستطيعون معه حضور مجلس الحاكم' (they cannot attend the judge's council).
The condition is genuine inability, not mere inconvenience.
2
Understand the legal cause (ʿilla)
The permissibility is based on need (حاجة). The text says 'لأن جوازها للحاجة' and that it is resorted to when the original witness is incapacitated (عجز). Incapacitation is realized by the mentioned factors (illness, travel).
This is an istihsan based on necessity.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Two views are presented: (1) three days' travel distance as the threshold (zāhir al-riwāyah and relied upon for fatwa), (2) Abu Yusuf's view: inability to return home overnight. Compare the reasoning: the first is 'better', the second is 'more convenient'.
The dispute is about the measure, not the principle.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
According to al-Qahstani and others, the first opinion (three days) is the apparent narration and upon it is the fatwa. However, some later scholars (e.g., al-Faqih Abu al-Layth) adopted Abu Yusuf's view for practicality. The footnotes show the divergence even among later authorities.
Know both opinions but understand which is formally relied upon.

Acceptance of Testimony When Substitutes Remain Silent
Ruling on testimony on testimony when substitutes remain silent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding silence of substitute witnesses and apply the relied-upon position to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Silence of substitute witnesses does not invalidate testimony; the judge may investigate the original witnesses' uprightness.

The reliability of witnesses is central to Islamic judicial procedure. In testimony on testimony (shahādat ʿalā shahādah), substitutes may either declare the original witnesses upright or remain silent. This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi difference on whether silence is sufficient, and which view has been adopted for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage covers two cases: when substitute witnesses affirm the uprightness of originals, and when they remain silent. Focus on the silent case.
The dispute only concerns the case of silence.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that the testimony is accepted, and the judge must investigate the condition of the original witnesses.
This is the relied-upon position.
3
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that such testimony is not accepted because the substitutes did not confirm uprightness.
Muhammad demands explicit taʿdīl.
4
Identify the basis for each
Abu Yusuf relies on the principle that substitutes are qualified for tazkiya but silence does not imply deficiency; the judge can investigate. Muhammad requires explicit certification.
No explicit dalil is given in the passage.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The later correcting imams (al-muṣaḥḥiḥūn) adopted Abu Yusuf's view, making it the operative ruling for fatwa.
Refer to al-Tas-hih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules on witness retraction and apply the qiyas with the digger of a well to determine liability.

Core Idea
Retracting witnesses are liable for the property lost, analogized to a digger of a deadly well.

This lesson examines the legal consequences when witnesses withdraw their testimony after a verdict. The Hanafi school establishes that retraction does not nullify the judgment but creates a financial obligation to compensate the accused. The key principle is that the witnesses caused harm through transgression, and since the judge was compelled to act, liability falls on them. We will explore the specific rules for different numbers of witnesses and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule of liability
Identify that the text opens with the obligation (wujub) of witnesses to guarantee (damān) the loss caused by their testimony. This is the foundational principle for all subsequent cases.
The liability is financial, not criminal.
2
Identify the condition for valid retraction
Note that retraction is only accepted when made in the presence of a judge (ḥākim). This is because testimony is tied to the court, and retraction is an opening (fash) of testimony.
The judge need not be the same one who delivered the verdict.
3
Analyze the core case of two witnesses
When two witnesses both retract after the judge has ruled based on their testimony, they jointly guarantee the entire property to the accused (mashhūd ʿalayh).
The liability is joint and several, but the text implies each is liable for the whole if the other cannot pay? Actually, they are jointly liable.
4
Understand the qiyas analogy
The text analogizes the witnesses to a person who digs a well on a public road. Even though the judge is the direct cause of loss, he was compelled (muljaʾ) to rule, so the witnesses are liable because they created the transgressive cause.
This is an example of qiyās with a hidden cause (ʿilla khafiyya). The ʿilla is transgression (taʿaddī) combined with the compulsion of the direct agent.
5

The principle: liability is based on the testimony value of the remaining witnesses after retraction, not on the number of retractors. For three witnesses: one retracts → no liability (two remain to prove full right); two retract → they guarantee half (one remains to prove half); all three retract → liability split in thirds.
The underlying rule is that the right is divided according to what the remaining witnesses can independently prove.

The statement of a sinful person, a boy, and a slave is accepted in transactions, because custom involves sending gifts through these people and granting permission for trade, and they cannot bring witnesses with them when traveling or sitting in the market. If their statement were not accepted, it would lead to hardship. This applies if he believes them to be truthful; otherwise it is not permissible for him. In al-Jami' al-Saghir: If a slave girl says to a man, 'My master sent me to you as a gift,' it is permissible for him to take it, because there is no difference between her informing about the master gifting someone else or herself, due to what we said. The statement of a sinful person and a disbeliever is accepted in transactions because of their frequent occurrence among people. If we imposed an extra condition, it would lead to hardship. So the statement of a single person is accepted, whether just or sinful, disbeliever or Muslim, slave or free, male or female, to avoid hardship. In matters of religious reports, only the statement of a just person is accepted, because they do not occur as frequently as transactions, so it is permissible to impose an extra condition. Commentary
This passage explains the Hanafi position on accepting the report of a single person (khabar al-wahid) in two distinct domains. In transactions (mu'amalat), to avoid hardship (haraj), the report of any person—even a fasiq (sinner), a child, a slave, or a disbeliever—is accepted. The proof is based on necessity: people regularly send gifts or conduct trade through such individuals, and requiring witnesses would cause undue difficulty. An example from al-Jami' al-Saghir by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani illustrates this: a slave girl's claim that her master sent her as a gift is accepted on her word alone. In religious matters (diyanat), such as reporting the sighting of the Ramadan crescent or impurity of water, only the report of a just Muslim ('adl) is accepted. The reason is that these matters are less frequent, so imposing a condition of justice does not lead to hardship. The fasiq is untrustworthy, and the disbeliever is not bound by Islamic rulings, so his report cannot obligate a Muslim. This distinction is a key feature of Hanafi jurisprudence.

Key Terms
الفاسق (al-fāsiq): A person who commits major sins or persists on minor sins, and whose moral reliability is compromised.
الديانات (al-diyānāt): Matters of religious observance, such as sighting the crescent for Ramadan/Eid, or reports about purity and impurity.
المعاملات (al-muʿāmalāt): Financial transactions, contracts, and dealings between people.

Condition for Accepting Reports in Religious Matters
States the condition for accepting religious reports

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for accepting reports in religious matters and explain the rationale for requiring both 'adalah (justice) and Islam in the reporter.

Core Idea
Only the report of a just Muslim is accepted in religious matters.

In religious matters such as sighting the crescent moon or announcing impurity, the law requires stricter standards than in ordinary transactions. This lesson explains why Hanafis insist that the reporter must be both just and Muslim, and the rational basis for this condition.

Commentary

It establishes the condition for accepting reports in religious matters (akhbar al-diyanat), such as the sighting of the crescent moon for Ramadan or Eid, or news about the impurity of water or fosterage. The reason is that a sinful person (fasiq) is unreliable, and a disbeliever (kafir) does not abide by Islamic rulings, so he cannot enforce a ruling on a Muslim. This ruling is derived from Al-Hidayah and reflects the Hanafi position that religious reports require greater caution.
