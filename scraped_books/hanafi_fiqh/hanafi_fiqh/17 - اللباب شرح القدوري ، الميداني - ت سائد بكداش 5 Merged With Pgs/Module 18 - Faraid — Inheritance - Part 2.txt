One-Sixth for Paternal Half-Sisters with a Full Sister
Ruling stating the share of paternal half-sisters with one full sister

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the share of paternal half-sisters when inheriting together with one full sister and identify the condition that makes them residuary.

Core Idea
Paternal half-sisters share 1/6 in the presence of one full sister, but only if no male co-heir exists.

This brief passage codifies the share of paternal half-sisters (al-akhawāt li-ab) when they inherit alongside a single full sister. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly distributing the estate in this common family configuration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs
List the full sister (one) and the paternal half-sisters (one or more). Note that the deceased has no father, mother, spouse, or children in this scenario.
The full sister is called 'sister by father and mother' (ukht li-ab wa umm).
2
Check for a male co-heir
Determine whether there is a paternal brother (brother by father) who would make the paternal half-sisters residuary heirs (asabah). If present, they no longer take a fixed share.
The text says 'if there is none with them to make them residuary' (لم يكن معهن من يعصبهن).
3
Assign shares
If no male co-heir, the full sister receives 1/2 (fixed share), and the paternal half-sisters together share 1/6 as a group. One half-sister takes the full 1/6 alone; multiple share it equally.
This is analogous to the share of granddaughters with a daughter (as mentioned in previous lessons).

Calculate the base
The common denominator for 1/2 and 1/6 is 6. Full sister gets 3 shares (3/6), paternal half-sisters get 1 share (1/6). The remaining 2 shares go to other residuary heirs (e.g., distant relatives) or are subject to radd if none.
If there are no other heirs, the residue returns proportionally to those entitled to radd (e.g., full sister).

A single uterine sibling inherits one-sixth (1/6) of the estate.

The verse is the primary evidence for this share.

Fixed Shares of Granddaughters, Half-Sisters, and Maternal Siblings
Fiqh ruling statement with expansion on conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the shares of granddaughters (banāt al-ibn), half-sisters from father (akhawāt li-ab), and maternal half-siblings (walad al-umm) in the presence of a daughter, full sister, or alone, and identify when they become asaba.

Core Idea
Granddaughters and half-sisters get one-sixth when with a daughter/full sister — unless a male asaba converts them to residuary heirs.

In this lesson, we examine three specific inheritance rules from the Hanafi faraid system, as outlined in the matn of al-Quduri. Understanding these shares is critical because they are conditional: the presence or absence of a male agnate (asaba) changes whether these heirs take a fixed fraction or the residue. We will go through each category step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of heirs
The passage covers: (a) granddaughters through the son (banāt al-ibn) when inheriting together with one daughter, (b) half-sisters from the father (akhawāt li-ab) when with a full sister, and (c) maternal half-siblings (walad al-umm) inheriting alone.
Each category has its own share and conditions.
2
State the base share for each category
For (a) and (b): if only one such heir, she takes the entire one-sixth (1/6) fixed share; if there are two or more, they share that one-sixth equally. For (c): one maternal sibling gets 1/6, two or more share 1/3 equally.
These shares apply only when there is no male relative who makes them asaba.
3
Identify the condition that can change the share to asaba
If a male agnate at the same level exists (e.g., a brother for banāt al-ibn or akhawāt li-ab), those females become asaba bi-ghayrihi and take the residue after fixed shares, forfeiting their fixed share.
For maternal siblings, no such change occurs; they always take fixed shares.
4
Apply the rule to an example
Example: Heirs: daughter, one granddaughter (banāt al-ibn), no son. Daughter gets 1/2, granddaughter gets 1/6. If there were also a grandson, both grandchildren would be asaba and take the residue after the daughter's half.
Practice with variations to reinforce the conditions.

Three exclusion rules: mother excludes all grandmothers; father excludes grandfather and siblings; child/grandchild/father/grandfather exclude maternal half-siblings.

In Hanafi inheritance law, certain close relatives remove entire categories of heirs from the inheritance. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar outlines three fundamental rules of total exclusion (ḥajb ḥirmān). Exclusion and Asaba Transformation in Faraid
Explains exclusion and asaba transformation for granddaughters and half-sisters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for when granddaughters and half-sisters are excluded from fixed shares and when they become residuary heirs through a male agnate.

Core Idea
A female heir excluded from a fixed share can be brought back as a residuary heir by a lower male agnate, but not if she already holds a fixed share.

This lesson covers a key nuance in Hanafi faraid: the interplay between fixed-share heirs and residuary heirs when the maximum fixed share (two-thirds) is exhausted. We explore when granddaughters and half-sisters are excluded and how the presence of a male agnate can transform their inheritance status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the highest-tier female fixed-share heirs
Determine if daughters or full sisters are present and whether they take the full two-thirds. If so, the next-tier females (granddaughters or half-sisters) are excluded from fixed shares.
This exclusion applies only when the higher tier has taken the maximum possible fixed share (two-thirds).
2
Check for a male agnate in the same or lower degree
Look for a son's son (grandson) or a full brother for the respective females. If such a male exists, he can make the excluded females residuary heirs (asaba).
The male must be in the position of the females (equal degree) or below them.
3
Check if the excluded female already has a fixed share
If the granddaughter or half-sister already receives a fixed share (e.g., one-sixth when a daughter is present), she does not become residuary through the male; the male takes the residue.
A female who is 'dhat sahm' (fixed-share holder) is not transformed into a residuary by a lower male.
4
Apply the rule to the example given
In the scenario of a daughter, a granddaughter, and a great-grandson (son's son's son): the daughter takes half, the granddaughter takes one-sixth (fixed share), and the great-grandson takes the residue. The granddaughter does not become residuary because she already has a fixed share.
The great-grandson is below the granddaughter, but he does not make her residuary due to her fixed share.

Each full sister gets half of 2/3 = 1/3. Half-sister gets 1/9, half-brother gets 2/9. Total: 1/3+1/3+1/9+2/9 = 1.
No awl or radd needed; shares sum to unity.

Asabah inherit the residue; closer male agnates exclude more distant ones.

Guided Path

Define asabah
Identify the Hanafi definition: a male relative with no female in his lineage to the deceased. The text states: 'ذكر لم تدخل في نسبته إلى الميت أنثى' (a male in whose relation to the deceased no female enters).
This excludes relatives like maternal uncles or sons of daughters.
2
List the five categories in order
Memorize the sequence: sons → grandsons (sons' sons) → father → grandfather → brothers (full or consanguine) → sons of brothers → paternal uncles. Each category excludes those after it.

Understand the exclusion principle
A closer asabah excludes all more distant asabah from inheriting the residue. For example, a son excludes a grandson; a father excludes a grandfather.
If no asabah exists, the residue is distributed differently (e.g., radd).
4
Note the lineage condition
The relationship must pass only through males. For instance, a brother is connected through the father, not the mother. This is why uterine brothers are not asabah.
The phrase 'بمحض الذكور' (by pure males) appears in the text.
5
Cross-madhhab awareness
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) include females as asabah in certain circumstances (e.g., daughter with grandson). The Hanafi school strictly limits asabah to males except in specific cases like asabah bi-ghayrihi.

Grandfather's Precedence over Siblings: Abu Hanifa's Qiyas and Intra-Madhhab Dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on the grandfather's inheritance priority over siblings, explain the qiyas reasoning, and identify the Sahibayn's objection and the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
The grandfather inherits like the father: by qiyas, he excludes siblings.

This lesson unpacks a concise but significant Hanafi ruling: the true grandfather (al-jadd al-sahih) is more entitled to inheritance than siblings according to Abu Hanifa. The proof relies on qiyas (analogical reasoning): the grandfather stands in the place of the father (bi-manzilat al-ab). However, this position is contested within the Hanafi school by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). We will examine the reasoning and the practical consequences of this dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Basis
Locate the ruling: the grandfather is more entitled to inheritance than the siblings. Note that it is attributed to Abu Hanifa. The basis is the principle 'bi-manzilat al-ab' — the grandfather takes the father's position when the father is absent.
The Arabic text explicitly says 'عند أبي حنيفة' — according to Abu Hanifa.
2

Identify the four elements: asl (father), hukm al-asl (father excludes siblings), far' (grandfather), and illa (both are paternal ascendants). Confirm that the illa is jaliyyah (clear) and extracted by tahqiq al-manat.
The qiyas is explicit in the text: 'لأنه بمنزلة الأب عند فقده'.

The Sahibayn argue that the grandfather is not identical to the father; he is a more distant ancestor. Therefore, the analogy is not perfect. This leads to a different ruling: the grandfather shares with siblings rather than excluding them.
This objection is not in the source text but is known from other Hanafi works.
4

Under Abu Hanifa's view, siblings receive nothing when the grandfather is present. Under the Sahibayn's view, siblings share the estate with the grandfather. The difference is total exclusion vs. co-inheritance.
The thamarah is critical for fatwa and estate distribution.
5

Note that Abu Hanifa's view is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is considered the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in many Hanafi fatwa works. However, some later scholars prefer the Sahibayn's view in certain contexts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ten categories of dhawi al-arham and apply the precedence rule to determine the most deserving heir.

Core Idea
The child of the daughter is the foremost among dhawi al-arham, then the invalid grandfather, then children of parents.

In Hanafi inheritance law, when there are no residuary or Quranic heirs, the estate passes to distant relatives known as dhawi al-arham. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lists the ten categories of such relatives and establishes the first rule of priority: the closest in descent through a female line takes precedence. The commentary by al-Maydani refines the order based on the consensus of later Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
List the ten categories
Identify all ten categories of dhawi al-arham explicitly mentioned in the text. Note that they include both males and females connected through female lines.
The list begins with 'walad al-bint' (child of daughter) and ends with 'man adla bihim' (whoever is connected through them).
2
Understand the definition of dhawi al-arham
Recognize that dhawi al-arham are relatives who are neither Quranic heirs (ashab al-fara'id) nor residuary heirs (asabah). They inherit only when no such primary heirs exist.
This category is specific to Hanafi law; other madhhabs treat some of these as primary heirs.
3
Apply the first precedence rule
The text states: 'The most deserving among them is whoever is from the offspring of the deceased.' This means the child of the daughter (grandchild through a female line) has the highest priority among all dhawi al-arham.
This rule overrides other claims; for example, a daughter's son excludes a maternal uncle.
4
Note the correction from Zad al-Fuqaha
The commentary introduces a refinement: after the child of the daughter, the invalid grandfather (maternal grandfather) takes precedence over children of parents. This corrects a variant found in some copies of the Mukhtasar.
The invalid grandfather is defined as a grandfather not in the direct male line, e.g., mother's father.
5
Recognize the consensus among Hanafis
Al-Maydani affirms this order as correct, citing consensus (ijma') among the Hanafi companions. This establishes the mufta bihi (operative) ruling.
The tarjih authority is the consensus of the madhhab, not an individual scholar's preference.

Priority Order Among Dhawu al-Arham
Defines the order of priority among maternal relatives in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four-tier priority order of dhawu al-arham in Hanafi inheritance and apply the principle of closeness to identify the most entitled heir.

Core Idea
Among dhawu al-arham, the descendant of the deceased inherits first, then the maternal grandfather, then siblings' children, then grandparents' children.

This passage from al-Lubab explains how dhawu al-arham inherit when there are no 'asabah or quranic heirs. The Hanafi school gives them a specific priority order based on nearness to the deceased. Understanding this order is essential for correctly distributing inheritance in cases where only maternal relatives survive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of heirs
Determine that we are dealing with dhawu al-arham (maternal relatives), not 'asabah or ashab al-fara'id. Recognize that they inherit only in the absence of those primary heirs.
This passage assumes no 'asabah or quranic heirs exist.
2
Apply the principle of nearness of kinship
The first criterion is qurb al-qarabah: descendants of the deceased (e.g., grandchildren through daughters) are closest in kinship and thus have highest priority.
Descendants include all posterity through females, not just direct children.
3
Refine by nearness of degree
Among those of equal kinship, consider qurb al-darajah: closer generations (e.g., children of the deceased vs. grandchildren) take priority.
In this list, each tier is one step further from the deceased.
4
Consider strength of connection
Finally, apply qurb al-quwwah: if the connecting ancestor would have been an heir, that confers stronger claim. For example, the maternal grandfather's connection is through the mother, who would be an heir if alive.
This explains why the maternal grandfather comes before children of siblings.
5
Memorize the four-tier order
The established sequence: 1) descendants of the deceased (min walad al-mayyit), 2) maternal grandfather (abu al-umm), 3) children of siblings (daughters of brothers and children of sisters), 4) children of grandparents (maternal uncles, aunts, etc.).
This order is mufta bihi and confirmed by consensus.
