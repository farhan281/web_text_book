Core Idea
Bequeathing less than one-third preserves family ties; leaving nothing for poor heirs is even better.

While Islamic law permits a bequest of up to one-third of one's estate, the Hanafi school recommends a more generous approach: bequeath less than one-third to leave more for your heirs. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this recommendation and the exceptional case when heirs are poor.

Mustahab is a recommended act, not obligatory.
2

The reason (illah) is maintaining ties with relatives (silah al-qarib). By leaving more wealth to the heirs, the testator strengthens family bonds. The text says: 'because reducing [the bequest] is a means of maintaining ties with relatives by leaving one's wealth to them.'
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the value of family unity.

The distinction is between exercising a right and performing a recommended act of charity.

If the heirs are poor and their inheritance shares are insufficient, it is even more beloved (ahab) to leave no bequest at all. The text: 'And leaving the bequest altogether when the heirs are poor... is more beloved.'
This shows the Hanafi prioritization of the heirs' needs over the testator's desire to give to non-heirs.
5
Understand the Hanafi emphasis on family ties
The ruling is built on the principle that maintaining family ties is a highly recommended act (in some contexts even wajib). By reducing the bequest, the testator performs an act of silah al-qarib, which carries great reward.
This reflects the Hanafi value of family solidarity.

Excess bequests are not voided but proportionally reduced in three exceptional cases.

The general rule in Hanafi law is that a bequest exceeding one-third of the estate is not enforced without the heirs' consent. However, Abu Hanifa recognized three special cases where the excess is handled through proportional recalculation rather than outright rejection. Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab begins the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasaya) by addressing the fundamental rule that a bequest exceeding one-third of the testator's estate is not granted by Abu Hanifa unless the excess falls within one of three exceptional categories: al-muhabah (favor in a sale), al-sa'ayah (pursuit of freedom for a partially freed slave), and al-darahim al-mursalah (unspecified dirhams without a fractional share). In standard cases, if the heirs do not approve, the legatee receives only up to one-third, and the excess is invalid. However, in these three cases, the excess is not simply voided; instead, a proportional recalculation is made. The text then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the distribution when the heirs do approve. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), the one-third should be divided into fourths on the basis of 'awl (proportional reduction), while according to Abu Hanifa, it should be divided into thirds according to the proportion of the original bequests. Nevertheless, the one-third is divided between the two legatees in the proportion of their bequests, so Zayd gets ten and Amr gets twenty, even though Amr's bequest was forty. For sa'ayah, when a man bequeaths the freeing of two slaves valued at thirty and sixty without other assets, the estate is ninety, one-third is thirty. Each slave is partially freed (one-third of his value), and the remaining value is to be earned by the slave through work (sa'ayah). For unspecific dirhams, if the testator bequeaths twenty to Zayd and forty to Amr, and the total estate is ninety (so the bequests total two-thirds), it is agreed that the one-third is divided in thirds: ten to Zayd and twenty to Amr.

السعاية (al-sa'ayah): The effort/pursuit of a partially freed slave to earn his freedom by working for the estate.
الدراهم المرسلة (al-darahim al-mursalah): Specific amounts of dirhams bequeathed without being tied to a fractional share of the estate (e.g., not 'one-third' or 'half').

Debt is a prior obligation; bequest is void if debt covers all wealth.

This short ruling from the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasiyya) establishes a clear priority: debt must be satisfied before any bequest can be considered. If the debts equal or exceed the entire estate, any bequest is automatically invalid unless the creditors voluntarily forgive the debt. This rule reflects the principle that debts are a fard (obligation) that takes precedence over voluntary charity (tabarru').

The release must be voluntary; the creditors are not obligated to forgive.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Debt is a prior obligation (fard) that must be paid before any voluntary disposition. Since the bequest is a tabarru' (charitable gift), it yields to the prior right of creditors.
This aligns with the general Hanafi rule that debts are deducted from the estate before bequests and inheritance.

This short ruling from al-Quduri establishes a fundamental principle: you cannot bequeath what does not belong to you. Since an heir's share becomes fixed at the testator's death, bequeathing that share to another is an invalid disposition of another's property.

The bequest is void because it involves property that legally belongs to the son. A bequest is only valid over property that the testator owns or has the right to dispose of.

A bequest of a 'جزء' is valid; the heirs decide unless the school's custom defines it as one-sixth.

Dividing an Unspecified Bequest Among Relatives: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dividing an unspecified bequest among paternal and maternal relatives and explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's approaches, identifying which is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa divides an unspecified bequest by nearness of kin; the Sahibayn divide equally among all traceable relatives.

When a testator makes an unspecified bequest to 'relatives,' how should the executor distribute the shares among paternal and maternal kin? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute: Abu Hanifa gives the paternal uncle a larger share, while his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue for equal distribution among all relatives linked to the first Muslim ancestor. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and identifies the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exact scenario

The dispute only arises when the bequest does not specify distribution; if shares are stated, they are followed.
2
State Abu Hanifa's allocation
According to Abu Hanifa, the paternal uncle receives half of the bequest, and the two maternal uncles collectively receive the other half. This is based on considering proximity in kinship: the paternal uncle is closer to the testator than maternal uncles.
The paternal uncle (father's brother) is generally considered a nearer relative than maternal uncles (mother's brothers) in Hanafi inheritance principles.
3
State the Sahibayn's allocation
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the bequest should be divided equally among all relatives who can trace their lineage to the farthest ancestor who embraced Islam. This means all such relatives, near or far, male or female, share equally.
The 'farthest father in Islam' (أقصى أب له في الإسلام) is the first paternal ancestor who accepted Islam; all relatives descended from him are considered equally deserving.
4
Analyze the underlying principle
Abu Hanifa prioritizes closeness in blood relation to the testator, while the Sahibayn prioritize the shared lineage to a common Muslim ancestor, making all relatives equal regardless of degree of proximity.
The dispute reflects a deeper difference in how to interpret 'relatives' (الأقرباء) in an unspecified bequest: specific individuals versus a class of persons.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities such as al-Zahidi, al-Mahbubi, and al-Nasafi confirm that Abu Hanifa's view is the correct and relied-upon position for fatwa.
Thus, the operative ruling follows Abu Hanifa: the paternal uncle receives half, and the two maternal uncles receive the other half collectively.

Paying a Bequest from Mixed Assets: Cash and Debts
Ruling on paying a bequest of a fixed amount from liquid assets and outstanding debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi method for paying a fixed bequest when the estate contains both ready cash and outstanding debts, and apply the proportional rule to compute immediate and future payments.

Core Idea
A legatee shares proportionally in the one-third share of both cash and debts as they are collected, ensuring fairness between legatee and heirs.

This lesson addresses a practical problem: a testator bequeaths a fixed sum, but his estate consists partly of ready cash and partly of debts owed to him. The Hanafi school provides a careful method to ensure the legatee receives his due without unfairly burdening the heirs. We will unpack the ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage addresses a bequest of a fixed amount (e.g., 1000 dirhams) when the testator's estate includes both ready cash ('ayn) and debts (dayn). The ruling provides a method for distribution.
The bequest must be within the one-third limit of the total estate, assumed in the passage.
2
Determine the two scenarios
First, check if the bequest amount can be paid entirely from one-third of the ready cash. If yes, pay immediately from cash. If not, proceed to the second scenario.
This is the critical distinction that triggers the proportional rule.
3
Apply the proportional rule for the second scenario
If the bequest does not come out of one-third of cash, pay one-third of the cash immediately to the legatee. Then, as each debt is collected, give the legatee one-third of the amount recovered until he receives the full bequest.
The legatee shares in debt recoveries proportionally, not exclusively from cash.
4
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The legatee is considered a co-sharer with the heirs in the one-third share of the estate. Giving him only cash would harm the heirs because cash is more advantageous (immediately usable) than debts, which may be uncertain or delayed.
This reasoning explains why the legatee must take a proportionate share of debts as they come in.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may require the bequest to be paid entirely from available cash immediately. The Hanafi position prioritizes fairness by sharing the benefit of liquidity between the legatee and heirs.
Understanding this difference helps in evaluating the practical implications.

Core Idea

Consider a testator who bequeaths a slave-girl to someone. After his death but before the legatee accepts, the slave-girl gives birth. Does the child also go to the legatee? Ruling on bequeathing the usufruct for a specified time or forever.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequeathing usufruct (manfa'ah) and explain the qiyas argument that justifies it using ijarah and ariyah as the asl.

Core Idea
The usufruct of property can be bequeathed because it is ownable with or without compensation, like ijarah and ariyah.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, clarified by al-Maydani in al-Lubab. The text confirms that a testator may bequeath not only the physical property but also its benefit—such as a house's dwelling or a slave's service—for a fixed term or even perpetually. The reasoning is rooted in a qiyas (analogy) to well-known contracts, demonstrating the Hanafi method of extending rulings from established cases to new ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the first sentence: 'وتجوز الوصية بخدمة عبده، وسكنى داره سنين معلومةً، وتجوز بذلك أبداً'. Identify the act (bequeathing usufruct) and its ruling (jaiz). Note the two forms: specified years and perpetuity.
The ruling covers both service (khidmah) and dwelling (sukna).
2
Analyze the qiyas: asl, far', illa
The text provides the proof: 'لأن المنافع يجوز تمليكها بعوض، وبغير عوض، كالإجارة والعارية'. The asl (original case) is ijarah and ariyah, where usufruct ownership is valid. The far' (new case) is bequest. The illa (effective cause) is that usufructs can be owned with or without compensation.
The illa is the permissibility of owning usufruct in both compensated and uncompensated transfers.
3
Understand the legal effect: sequestration of usufruct
The final sentence: 'ويكون محبوساً على ملك الميت في حق المنفعة، كما في الوقف'. The usufruct is sequestered (mahbus), meaning the legatee enjoys the benefit while the corpus remains in the testator's estate, similar to waqf. However, unlike waqf, the corpus passes to heirs after the term (or perpetually in perpetual bequest?).
Perpetual bequest of usufruct is valid, but the corpus remains owned by the deceased's estate indefinitely? The text implies it functions like waqf in usufruct sequestration.

Commentary
This passage allows bequeathing the usufruct (manafi') of one's property, specifically the service of a slave or the dwelling of a house, for a specified number of years or even perpetually. The reasoning is that usufructs are legally ownable, both in exchange for compensation (as in ijarah) and without compensation (as in ariyah). Thus, bequest, being a form of gratuitous transfer, is valid. The property remains in the ownership of the testator's estate, but the usufruct is given to the legatee. It functions like a waqf in terms of sequestering the usufruct, though the corpus remains owned by the deceased's heirs after the period, except in perpetual bequest where the legatee enjoys usufruct forever (but the corpus remains in the estate).

Key Terms
منفعة (manfa'ah): Usufruct; the benefit or utility derived from a property, such as service or dwelling.

Overview of the Book of Oaths and Book of Claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the main topics covered in the Book of Oaths and the Book of Claims from the Hanafi manual al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri.

Core Idea
The table of contents reveals the systematic, scenario-based approach of Hanafi fiqh to oaths and judicial claims.

This lesson orients you to the structure of two major sections of al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri: Kitab al-Ayman (Book of Oaths) and Kitab al-Da'wa wa al-Bayyinat (Book of Claims and Evidence). By mapping the topics, you gain a bird's-eye view of the legal questions that Hanafi jurists address in these areas, from the classification of oaths to the conditions for accepting a legal claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two books
The TOC lists two books: the Book of Oaths and the Book of Claims and Evidence. They are separate but related in that both deal with legal disputes and proof.
2
Examine the Book of Oaths structure
Note the initial classification: al-yamin al-ghamus, al-yamin al-mun'aqida, al-yamin al-laghw. Then follow the sequence: expiation, rulings on expiation timing, oaths on sins, etc. Later, specific scenarios like oaths regarding eating, speaking, dwelling appear.
The pattern moves from general principles to particular cases.
3
Observe scenario-based methodology
The TOC lists numerous specific cases (e.g., swore not to eat meat, then ate fish; swore not to enter a house, then stood on its roof). This reflects the Hanafi method of addressing concrete real-life situations.

Transition to the Book of Claims
The TOC begins the Book of Claims with definitions of plaintiff and defendant, then conditions for accepting a claim. This shows a shift from substantive law (oaths) to procedural law (claims and evidence).

Commentary
This passage presents a table of contents from the Hanafi fiqh text 'al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri'. It lists the topics covered in two books: the Book of Oaths (Kitab al-Ayman) and the Book of Claims and Evidence (Kitab al-Da'wa wa al-Bayyinat). The Book of Oaths begins with the classification of oaths into al-yamin al-ghamus (the false oath sworn with knowledge), al-yamin al-mun'aqida (the deliberate oath on a future matter), and al-yamin al-laghw (the vain oath uttered inadvertently). It then covers expiation (kaffara), the ruling on expiating before breaking the oath, oaths regarding sinful acts, oaths of a disbeliever, the prohibition of lawful things, and vows (nudhur). Following that, it details specific scenarios: oaths concerning entering dwellings, speech, eating, drinking, and changes in the subject of the oath. It also addresses oaths suspended by the will of Allah (istithna), temporal clauses (hours, days, months), and oaths about financial matters like repaying debts. The Book of Claims and Evidence introduces the definitions of plaintiff (mudda'i) and defendant (mudda'a 'alayhi), and the conditions for accepting a claim. This index shows the thorough, scenario-based methodology of Hanafi fiqh in addressing real-life legal questions.

Key Terms
اليمين الغموس (al-yamin al-ghamus): The false oath sworn deliberately on a past event, known to be false; considered a major sin requiring repentance and expiation.
اليمين المنعقدة (al-yamin al-mun'aqida): A deliberate oath on a future action; if broken, expiation is obligatory; if fulfilled, no expiation.
اليمين اللغو (al-yamin al-laghw): An oath uttered without intent or based on a mistaken belief; no expiation or sin in the Hanafi school.
كفارة اليمين (kaffarat al-yamin): The expiation for breaking a deliberate oath: feeding/ clothing ten poor or freeing a slave; if unable, fasting three days.
