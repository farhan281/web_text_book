Definitions of Claim, Plaintiff, and Defendant in Hanafi Law
Defines key procedural terms for court claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a plaintiff and a defendant under Hanafi law and state the legal definition of a claim (da'wa).

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the defendant is the one compelled to respond, not necessarily the one who initiated the case.

The terms 'claim', 'plaintiff', and 'defendant' are foundational to Islamic procedural law. This lesson clarifies their precise Hanafi definitions as given by al-Quduri and expanded by al-Maydani, highlighting the key distinction of compulsion to litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions of da'wa
First, note that linguistically, da'wa is a statement by which a person intends to establish a right against another. Legally, it is an assertion of a right before a judge. This distinction shows that a claim is a formal act requiring a judicial setting.
The plural is دَعَاوَى (da'awa).
2
Learn the Hanafi definition of plaintiff (mudda'i)
The plaintiff is one who is not compelled to litigation if he abandons it. This is because he is the claimant; the burden of proof is on him, but he may withdraw.
Key: plaintiff's initiative does not compel him to continue.
3
Learn the Hanafi definition of defendant (mudda'a alayh)
The defendant is one who is compelled to litigation, because he is the one claimed against. The defendant must respond even if the plaintiff abandons the case (e.g., if the plaintiff dies, the defendant may still be required to answer before a judge).
This compulsion distinguishes the defendant from the plaintiff.
4
Contrast the two definitions from al-Hidayah: burden of proof based on possession
Al-Hidayah presents an alternative definition: the plaintiff is one who does not deserve (the thing) except by evidence (like an outsider), while the defendant is one who deserves by his statement without evidence (like the possessor). This aligns with the rule that the burden of proof is on the outsider claiming against a possessor.
The outsider vs. possessor distinction is practical for property disputes.
5
Understand the practical significance: who bears the burden of proof
The plaintiff must produce evidence to establish his claim; the defendant, if in possession, need only affirm his right. This allocation prevents injustice against those already in rightful possession.
This principle is a hallmark of Hanafi procedural law.

The plural is 'da'awa', similar to 'fatawa'. Then he introduces the definitions of plaintiff and defendant from the matn: the plaintiff is not compelled to litigate if he abandons the case, because he is the claimant; the defendant is compelled to litigate because he is the one being claimed against. Definitions of Plaintiff and Defendant in Hanafi Law
Defining the parties in legal proceedings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the plaintiff from the defendant in Hanafi law by applying the principle of considering underlying legal meaning over formal roles.

Core Idea
The plaintiff is not compelled to litigate; the defendant is compelled. The true test is burden of proof, not formal roles.

This lesson unpacks the foundational definitions of plaintiff (المدعي) and defendant (المدعى عليه) as laid out in the Hanafi school. While multiple wordings exist among the mashayikh, all converge on a single functional principle: who bears the burden of proof? We will explore the preferred definition from al-Quduri, the insightful test of form versus meaning, and a classic example involving a depository that crystallizes the Hanafi approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the formal definitions
Start by reading the primary definition from al-Quduri: the plaintiff is not compelled to litigate if he abandons the claim; the defendant is compelled. Write these down and note the rationale: the plaintiff is a claimant (طالب), the defendant is sought (مطلوب).
This is the 'comprehensive and sound' definition according to al-Hidayah.
2
Note alternative definitions from the mashayikh
Examine the two additional definitions: (a) plaintiff needs evidence to establish right (like an outsider), defendant's word suffices (like the possessor); (b) plaintiff seeks what contradicts the apparent, defendant clings to the apparent. Understand that these are not contradictory but different angles on the same distinction.
Imam Muhammad's definition: 'The defendant is the denier (المنكر).' This is foundational.
3
Analyze the depository example
Read the example: the depository (المودع) claims he returned the deposit. In form, he is a plaintiff (asserting return), but in meaning he is a defendant because he denies liability. Work through why his statement is accepted with an oath, not requiring evidence.
This example illustrates that the formal act of making a claim does not automatically make one a plaintiff.
4
Understand the principle of considering meanings over forms
Summarize: the Hanafi school gives weight to the underlying legal reality (المعنى) over the external form (الصورة). The decisive factor is who would be compelled to litigate if they remained silent. Apply this to the example and generalize.
This principle is key to correctly assigning procedural roles in complex cases.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Claims and Evidence (كتاب الدعوى والبينات) in al-Lubab, the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It begins by defining the plaintiff (المدعي) and defendant (المدعى عليه) according to the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school. The fundamental rule is: the plaintiff is not forced to pursue litigation if he drops it, because he is the claimant seeking something; the defendant is forced to participate because he is the one being claimed against. This definition is broad and sound, as it encompasses all the particular definitions given by the mashayikh (scholars). The author of al-Hidayah notes that the mashayikh expressed different wordings for the distinction. One definition: the plaintiff is one who cannot establish entitlement without proof (e.g., a stranger claiming ownership), while the defendant is one whose statement suffices without proof (e.g., the current possessor). Another: the plaintiff seeks what contradicts the apparent state of affairs, while the defendant clings to the apparent. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani said in his work al-Asl (one of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah) that the defendant is simply the denier (المنكر). This is correct, but the challenge lies in identifying who the denier is in practice. The preferred method among Hanafi experts (الحذاق من أصحابنا) is to rely on jurisprudential understanding (الفقه) by looking at the underlying legal meaning (المعنى) rather than the external form (الصورة). The passage provides a concrete example: a depository (المودع) who claims he returned the deposit. In form, he is a plaintiff (since he is asserting a claim of return), but in meaning, he is a defendant because he is denying liability (الضمان) for the deposit. Therefore, his statement is accepted with an oath, and he is not required to produce evidence. If he does produce evidence, it is accepted, but if he cannot, his oath suffices. This illustrates that the true distinction between plaintiff and defendant depends on who bears the burden of proof and who is compelled to litigate, not merely on who speaks first or initiates a claim.

Key Terms
المدعي (al-mudda'i): The plaintiff: the one who is not compelled to litigate if he abandons the claim; he is the claimant.
المدعى عليه (al-mudda'a 'alayhi): The defendant: the one who is compelled to litigate; he is the one being claimed against.
المنكر (al-munkir): The denier: the person who denies the claim; considered the defendant in legal proceedings.
المودع (al-mudi'): The depository: the person with whom a deposit (الوديعة) is left for safekeeping.
الضمان (al-daman): Liability: legal responsibility for loss or damage of property; here, the depository denies liability for the deposit.
الظاهر (al-zahir): The apparent: the external state of affairs that is considered default; the defendant clings to it.

Hanafi Critique of the Hadith 'al-Shahid wa al-Yamin'
Criticism of the hadith's authenticity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the authenticity of the hadith 'al-shahid wa al-yamin' and explain why it is not a valid basis for legal rulings.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school rejects the hadith 'al-shahid wa al-yamin' due to chain weakness; even the narrator denied it.

In Hanafi fiqh, evidence rules are strict and based on strong proofs. The hadith 'al-shahid wa al-yamin' is sometimes cited to allow judgment with one witness and an oath, but the Hanafi school critically examines its authenticity. This lesson explains why Hanafis consider this hadith weak and do not act upon it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith
Recognize the hadith 'al-shahid wa al-yamin' as a tradition that permits judgment based on one witness and an oath by the plaintiff.
This hadith is often quoted in discussions of evidence law.
2
Note the criticism
The author states the hadith is weak (ضعيف), citing Ibn Ma'in's rejection and the narrator's own denial.
Ibn Ma'in is a major hadith critic; the narrator's denial is a strong indication of fabrication.
3
Understand the basis
The weakness stems from chain issues; the hadith conflicts with Quranic verses requiring two witnesses (2:282) and the principle that the oath is on the defendant.
Hanafi usul prioritizes strong, consistent evidence.
4
Relate to the school's methodology
Hanafis apply rigorous hadith criticism and do not act upon weak hadith, especially when they contradict clear Quranic principles.
This reflects the school's commitment to textual and rational consistency.

Inadmissibility of Possessor's Evidence in Absolute Ownership
Ruling on the inadmissibility of the possessor's evidence in absolute ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule regarding the inadmissibility of the possessor's evidence in absolute ownership claims and identify the exceptions where it is accepted by consensus.

Core Idea
Possession is strong but not decisive: the possessor's evidence is fruitless in absolute ownership claims.

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi evidence law: when a dispute over ownership arises, the testimony of the person in possession is not accepted if the claim is absolute (no cause mentioned). The rationale is that possession already establishes apparent ownership, so the evidence adds nothing. However, the rule has important exceptions. We will explore the logic and the cases where the possessor's evidence is accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage states that the evidence of the possessor (sahib al-yad) is not accepted in claims of absolute ownership (al-mulk al-mutlaq).
2
Understand the reasoning
Because the possessor's evidence does not add more than the possession itself already establishes. Possession gives a presumption of ownership (zahir al-mulk).
The text says: 'لأنها لا تفيد أكثر مما تفيده اليد'.
3
Contrast with the outsider
If the outsider (non-possessor) brings evidence, it is preferred because it establishes ownership from scratch, making it more probative.

Core Idea
Nukul (refusal to swear) makes the claim binding; the judge must rule against the defendant after warning.

When a defendant in a civil claim refuses to take the oath after the plaintiff has presented evidence, the Hanafi school holds that this refusal legally binds the defendant to the claim. This lesson examines the rational proof, the difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the nature of nukul, and the recommended judicial procedure. Understanding nukul is essential for grasping how evidentiary rights shift when the oath is denied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is the defendant's refusal to take the oath after the plaintiff's evidence. The ruling is that the judge must pass judgment against the defendant, and the claimed amount becomes binding.

2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: if the defendant were truthful, he would swear the obligatory oath; his refusal indicates either evasion (buzul) according to Abu Hanifa or admission (iqrar) according to the Sahibayn.
This is a dalil aqli (rational inference), not a direct text.

Phrasing Testimony in Theft Cases
States the ruling for phrasing testimony in theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on phrasing testimony in theft and explain the rationale behind using 'took' instead of 'stole'.

Core Idea
Say 'he took' to recover the property; avoid 'he stole' to protect the hand.

Testimony in theft cases requires careful wording. The Hanafi school instructs witnesses to use the phrase 'he took' rather than 'he stole' when testifying about the stolen property. This rule balances two critical rights: the victim's right to recover the property and the accused's right to avoid the hadd penalty. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling, its justification, and why it reflects the Hanafi commitment to combining concealment and disclosure in judicial proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Locate the operative ruling in the text: the witness must testify about the property using the verb 'take' (أخذ) and must avoid using 'steal' (سرق).
The ruling is stated with the word 'يجب' (must), indicating a wajib level of obligation.
2
Understand the Rationale
Examine the two interests served: saying 'took' revives the victim's right to reclaim the property (إحياءً لحق المسروق عنه), while avoiding 'stole' protects the thief's hand from amputation (صوناً ليد السارق عن القطع).
The rationale combines both concealment (ستر) and disclosure (إظهار).
3
Analyze the Balance of Rights
Recognize that the testimony is not false: 'he took' is literally true if the property was taken, even if it was stolen. The wording is a permissible tawriya (indirection) to serve a greater legal purpose.
The balance preserves both the victim's property right and the accused's procedural right to have the hadd strictly proven.
4
Consider Cross-Madhhab Differences
Note that this ruling is unique to the Hanafi school. Other schools may require the witness to testify exactly as they saw, using the word 'stole', and would not permit this indirection.
The Hanafi position is based on the principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking means to evil) and istihsan.

First Rank of Testimony: Four Male Witnesses for Zina
Explains the first rank of testimony requiring four male witnesses for zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that proving zina requires four male witnesses and explain the Quranic and Sunnah proofs, as well as the exclusion of women's testimony in hudud and qisas.

Core Idea
Zina requires four male witnesses — women cannot testify in hudud or qisas.

This lesson examines one of the most distinctive procedural rules in Islamic criminal law: the requirement of four male witnesses for the crime of zina. Unlike most other cases where two male witnesses or a man and two women suffice, zina is treated with extraordinary caution. The Quranic command is explicit, and the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the early caliphs reinforces it. We will explore the textual proofs and the reasoning behind excluding women's testimony in these matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is testimony (shahadah) for the crime of zina. The ruling is that it requires four male witnesses; without them, the hadd penalty cannot be applied.
This is a condition for proof, not for the act itself.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The Quranic verses 4:15 and 24:4 explicitly require 'four from among you' ('arba'ah minkum'). This establishes the number and indicates gender via the masculine pronoun.
The verses are qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalalah regarding gender exclusivity.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The masculine form 'minkum' is interpreted in Hanafi usul as referring to males, thus excluding women. The command is a general instruction (amm) that is binding.
This interpretation is consistent with the principle that al-amm yufidu al-wujub.
4
Examine the secondary dalil
The hadith of al-Zuhri (mursal) states the sunnah has continued that women's testimony is not accepted in hudud and qisas. This reinforces the Quranic indication.
Hanafis accept mursal hadith when supported by other evidence, as here.
5
Note the consensus and practical effect
Ibn al-Mundhir reports consensus among scholars on the requirement of four witnesses for zina. The practical effect is that without four male witnesses, the accusation may lead to the hadd of qadhf (false accusation).
This is a matter of 'muttafaq alayh' across schools.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
No delegation, no testimony — the secondary witness must be expressly authorized to transmit the original testimony.

This passage explains when a person can testify based on hearing another witness testify. The Hanafi school requires formal delegation (ishhad) to avoid error and falsehood. We will examine the ruling, the proof by analogy to agency, and a critical exception for absolute certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is 'shahadat ala al-shahada' (testimony based on another's testimony). The ruling: it is not permissible (haram) unless the original witness formally delegates the listener.
The ruling classifies the act as invalid (ghayr maqbul) if delegation is absent.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): the secondary witness is analogous to an agent (wakil). Just as an agent cannot act without authorization (tawkeel), the secondary witness cannot transmit testimony without an explicit delegation (ishhad) and order (amr).
This is a qiyas jali, comparing shahadat ala al-shahada to wakalah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal cause (illa) is the need for certainty in transmission. Without delegation, the secondary witness only has hearsay, which is unreliable. The requirement of delegation ensures that the testimony is based on direct authorization, reducing the risk of error.
Hanafi usul: al-qiyas yuthbit al-hukm idha istawa al-asl wa al-far' fi al-illah.
4

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that mere hearing is sufficient without delegation. The Hanafi school responds by emphasizing the precaution against false testimony (sadd al-dhara'i').
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): under Shafi'i, a person can testify simply by hearing another's testimony; under Hanafi, he cannot unless delegated.
5

The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa) due to stronger caution. However, an exception exists when absolute certainty is attained (e.g., entering a house with only one occupant and hearing his confession).

Testimony of a Slave Is Not Accepted
Ruling that a slave's testimony is not accepted for anyone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's testimony and explain the underlying qiyas reasoning that invalidates it.

Core Idea
A slave's testimony is invalid because it is a form of guardianship, and he lacks guardianship over himself.

The passage before you presents a clear ruling from the Hanafi school: the testimony of a slave is not accepted in any case, whether for his master or for a stranger. The reasoning is based on a rational analogy: testimony is a form of legal authority (wilaya), and a slave, being under the ownership of another, does not even have authority over his own affairs. Therefore, he cannot have authority over others. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and explores its implications.

Guided Path

Identify the scope of the ruling

2
Examine the legal reasoning
The text states 'because testimony is from the category of guardianship'. Understand that in Hanafi jurisprudence, giving testimony is considered an act of wilaya (legal authority) over the rights of others.
This conceptualization is key: testimony is not merely a factual report but a form of legal agency.
3
Apply the a fortiori argument
The phrase 'so it is even more fitting' signals a qiyas al-awla (first-intent analogy). Since a slave lacks wilaya over himself, he necessarily lacks it over others.
This argument is logically compelling: if someone cannot manage his own affairs, how can he act on behalf of others?
4
Contrast with other schools
Recognize that this is a mukhtalaf fih issue. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i, Maliki) may allow a slave's testimony in limited contexts, such as for his master or in non-hudud cases.
The Hanafi school is the most restrictive; the condition of freedom (hurriyya) is considered a prerequisite for testimony.
