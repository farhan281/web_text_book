The Ruling on Saying 'علي حرام'
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the meaning of 'علي حرام'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the legal effect of saying 'علي حرام' and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Saying 'علي حرام' without intention: earlier view applies to food/drink; later fatwa treats it as divorce due to custom.

This lesson examines the phrase 'علي حرام' (upon me is forbidden), which often arises in oaths and divorce contexts. The passage from al-Quduri's matn with al-Maydani's commentary reveals an important internal Hanafi development: the school's ruling shifted from applying the phrase to food and drink (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) to treating it as a divorce declaration (talāq) based on common usage. Understanding this shift is crucial for issuing correct fatwas today.

Guided Path
1
State the two Hanafi positions
Begin by identifying the earlier position (Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) and the later position (mashāyikh). The earlier view holds that 'علي حرام' applies to food and drink unless the speaker intends otherwise. The later view holds that it constitutes divorce (talāq) without need for intention due to common usage.
The later view is explicitly stated as 'وعليه الفتوى' (upon it is the fatwa).
2
Identify the basis of each position
The earlier view relies on the literal meaning of the word 'ḥarām' and the default application in the corpus of Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah. The later view relies on the change in common usage (غلبة الاستعمال) where the term became predominantly associated with divorce.
Custom (ʿurf) can affect legal interpretation in the Hanafi school.
3
Apply the subsidiary ruling from al-Yanābiʿ
If the speaker has no wife, the phrase is considered an oath (yamīn) requiring expiation (kaffārah). If he has four wives, each receives one ṭalqah (divorce).
This shows the fatwa is contextual: the presence of a wife triggers divorce; otherwise, it remains an oath.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilāf
The practical difference: under the earlier view, saying 'علي حرام' without intention does not cause divorce; under the later fatwa, it does. The later fatwa is operative for fatwa.

However, he immediately presents the later position of the majority of Hanafi scholars (mashayikh) that due to the dominance of common usage (غلبة الاستعمال), the phrase constitutes divorce (talaq) without needing intention, and this is the relied-upon fatwa. Core Idea
An oath of divorce in terminal illness is treated like a real divorce to protect the wife's inheritance.

This passage from al-Yanabi' and al-Jawhara tackles a delicate scenario: a terminally ill man makes an oath of divorce. The ruling hinges on whether the marriage was consummated, and draws an analogy (qiyas) to the case of 'al-farr' – a husband who divorces his wife in his final illness to cut her out of inheritance. Understanding this analogy is key to grasping the Hanafi approach to protecting spousal rights.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between a marriage that was consummated and one that was not. The consequences of the oath differ accordingly.
2

If not consummated: wife has no inheritance and no iddah. If consummated: wife inherits and must observe the longer of the two iddah periods.
The longer iddah is the maximum of 'iddat al-talaq (3 cycles) and 'iddat al-wafat (4 months 10 days).

The ruling is likened to that of a husband who divorces in marad al-mawt to deprive his wife of inheritance. By analogy, the oath of divorce is treated the same way to prevent fraud.

If the wife dies before the husband, the oath does not effect a divorce, because death did not make it impossible for him to fulfill the oath (i.e., he could have divorced her while she was alive).
The condition of fulfillment (shart al-birr) remains possible.

The law nullifies legal tricks (hiyal) that subvert inheritance rights. The oath is treated as a substantive act to prevent the husband from circumventing his wife's share.

This ruling applies specifically to nafaqah (maintenance), not to other debts or obligations.

Ruling with proof on coerced dispositions.

Core Idea
Coercion does not nullify divorce, marriage, or manumission — they are effective because they are valid even in jest.

The Hanafi school addresses a critical question: if a person is forced to divorce, marry, or free a slave, does the act count? The answer is surprising: yes, it does. This passage from al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar provides the ruling and its proof. The key is that these verbal acts are independent of intent in Hanafi fiqh; they are valid even when done in jest (hazl), and coercion is even less of a barrier.

The proof is an analogy: 'because these acts are valid under coercion just as they are valid in jest.' This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
Jest (hazl) is a stronger test of validity than coercion because jest implies complete lack of intent.

Understand the illa
The effective cause is that these acts are verbal pronouncements whose legal effect is independent of internal will in Hanafi fiqh. Thus, both hazl and ikrah share the absence of serious intent, but the effect remains.
This is a specific Hanafi principle; other schools may differ.

Ruling on coercer's liability for half mahr in coerced divorce before consummation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the coercer must pay half the specified mahr in a coerced divorce before consummation, and explain the legal reasoning (daman itlaf) and the conditions for this liability.

Core Idea
A coercer is liable for half the mahr because his coercion caused the divorce and thus the mahr obligation – it is a guarantee of destruction.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: when a man is forced to divorce his wife before consummation, who bears the financial loss of the half mahr? The answer is not the coerced husband but the coercer himself. Al-Maydani's commentary expands on the reasoning, drawing from the concept of daman itlaf (guarantee for destruction) and the principle that the mahr was on the verge of falling, but the divorce confirms it, so the coercer is responsible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling from the Matn
Locate the core sentence: 'And for half the mahr of the woman if it was before consummation.' This states the coercer's liability for half the mahr.
The matn is a concise ruling; the commentary expands on its conditions.
2
Understand the Conditions
The liability applies only: (a) before consummation, and (b) when the mahr is specified in the contract. If after consummation, the mahr is confirmed by consummation, not divorce. If mahr is unspecified, the liability is for mut'ah instead.
These restrictions are explicit in the text.
3
Grasp the Legal Reasoning (Dalil)
The mahr was on the verge of falling due to potential separation from the wife's side (e.g., apostasy). The divorce confirmed the obligation, so the confirmation is attributed to the coercer, making him liable as if he destroyed property (daman itlaf).
This is a qiyas-based reasoning, treating the mahr as property at risk.
4
Differentiate Post-Consummation
After consummation, the mahr is fully confirmed by the act of consummation, not by the divorce. Therefore, the coercer is not liable for half mahr (the full mahr is owed, but the coercer's role is irrelevant).
This distinction prevents overlap of causes.
5
Consider Edge Cases: Unspecified Mahr or Excess
If mahr is not specified, the husband owes mut'ah, and the coercer is liable for that. If the specified mahr exceeds the bride's equivalent, the excess is void, and the equivalent is treated as the specified amount, halved by divorce before consummation.
These details ensure the ruling covers all scenarios.

The Effect of Coercion on Utterances of Disbelief: Istihsan over Qiyas
Ruling on accepting husband's claim of heartfelt faith despite uttered kufr under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on uttering kufr under coercion, explain why the husband's statement is accepted, and identify the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas.

Core Idea
Coercion does not change belief; the heart's sincerity controls apostasy, even if words of disbelief are uttered.

In Hanafi fiqh, the legal effect of an utterance depends on the speaker's intention and state of mind. When a person is coerced into uttering words of disbelief, the mere utterance does not establish apostasy because the heart may remain firm in faith. This lesson examines a passage from al-Hidayah that applies istihsan (juridical preference) to accept the husband's claim of sincere faith over the literal implication of his words. Understanding this ruling highlights the Hanafi emphasis on underlying realities over external forms, especially in matters of belief and marriage.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and apparent qiyas
The scenario: a husband utters words of disbelief under coercion. The wife claims bainunah (irrevocable separation). The apparent qiyas would equate coerced utterance with voluntary utterance, making the husband an apostate and dissolving the marriage. Recognize this initial analogy.
Qiyas here seems to look at the external act (utterance) alone.
2
Identify the principle: apostasy is about belief, not utterance
The passage states that apostasy (and thus bainunah) is tied to a change of belief (tabaddul al-i'tiqad). The uttered word is not intrinsically a cause of separation. Under coercion, the utterance does not indicate a change in the heart. Thus, doubt remains, and doubt cannot establish a legal consequence (al-bayyinah la tathbut bi al-shakk).
This principle is foundational: al-umur bi-maqasidiha (matters are judged by intentions).
3
Apply istihsan: why the husband's statement is credited
The ruling adopts istihsan, which prefers the underlying reality (heart's state) over the strict qiyas. The husband's assertion that his heart was firm in faith is given precedence. The word 'استحساناً' signals this departure from analogy. The husband's statement is accepted (al-qawlu qawluhu) because the utterance under coercion is not a reliable indicator of belief.
Istihsan is a distinctive Hanafi source; Shafi'is reject it as an independent proof.
4
Note the thamarah (practical consequence)
The practical result: the marriage remains valid; no bainunah occurs. The wife's claim is rejected. This protects the union from being dissolved by coerced speech and upholds the sanctity of the marriage contract.
This is a clear example of istihsan producing a just outcome that aligns with the spirit of the law.

تتعلق بالاعتقاد، ألا ترى أنه لو كان قلبه مطمئناً بالإيمان لا يكفر، وفي اعتقاده الكفر شك، فلا تثبت البينونة بالشك. فإن قالت المرأةُ : قد بنتُ منك، وقال هو : قد أظهرت ذلك، وقلبي مطمئن بالإيمان فالقول قوله؛ استحساناً؛ لأن اللفظ غير موضوع للفرقة، وهي بتبدل الاعتقاد، ومع الإكراه: لا يدل على التبدل، فكان القول قوله. «هداية».
It is related to belief. Do you not see that if his heart is at ease with faith, he does not become an unbeliever, and in his belief of disbelief there is doubt, so separation (bainunah) is not established by doubt. If the woman says, 'I have become separated from you,' and he says, 'I uttered that, but my heart is at ease with faith,' then his statement is accepted, by istihsan, because the utterance is not placed (as a cause) for separation, and separation occurs by change of belief, and under coercion, the utterance does not indicate change, so his statement is accepted. [From al-Hidayah]

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Kitab al-Ikrah in al-Hidayah. It addresses a scenario where a man utters words of disbelief under coercion, but his heart remains firm in faith. The ruling is that he does not become an apostate because the effective cause (illa) of apostasy is the actual change of belief, not the mere utterance under duress. The text uses the principle that doubt does not establish a legal effect (لا تثبت البينونة بالشك). Then it considers a dispute: the woman claims she is separated (bainunah) because the husband uttered kufr, while the husband asserts his heart was steadfast. The ruling is that the husband's statement is accepted (al-qawlu qawluhu) by way of istihsan. The reasoning is that the word (the utterance of kufr) is not intrinsically a cause of separation; separation only occurs through genuine change of belief. Under coercion, the utterance does not indicate such change, so the husband's claim of sincere faith is given credence. This is a clear application of istihsan over qiyas: qiyas might suggest that the utterance alone triggers the legal effect, but istihsan considers the underlying intent and reality.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Coercion: forcing someone to do or say something against their will under threat of harm.
البينونة (al-baynūnah): Separation: permanent dissolution of the marriage bond (bainunah kubra).
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juridical preference: departing from apparent analogy in favor of a more equitable ruling based on underlying rationale.
