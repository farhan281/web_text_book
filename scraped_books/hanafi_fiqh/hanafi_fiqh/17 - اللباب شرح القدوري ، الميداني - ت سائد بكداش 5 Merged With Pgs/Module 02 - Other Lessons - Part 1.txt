Definition and Two Major Types of Oaths
Definition and first two of three oath types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of an oath (yamin) and distinguish between the ghamus and mun'aqidah types by their definitions and consequences.

Core Idea
An oath legally strengthens resolve; ghamus immerses in sin without expiation, while mun'aqidah binds to fulfill or expiate.

This lesson opens the Hanafi chapter on oaths. We begin with the essential legal definition of an oath and then examine the first two types: the 'immersive' oath (ghamus) which is a major sin, and the 'binding' oath (mun'aqidah) which obligates expiation if broken. Understanding these categories is foundational for fiqh of oaths.

Guided Path
1
Define the term 'yamin'
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions of yamin as given in the text.
Linguistic: strength; legal: a contract that strengthens resolve.
2
Identify the three-type classification
Note that the author states oaths are of three types. The third type (laghw) is not discussed in this passage but should be remembered.
The passage only details the first two.
3
Analyze the meaning of 'ghamus'
Trace the etymology from 'ghams' (immersion) and understand why it is called 'ghamus'.
It immerses the swearer in sin and Hellfire.
4
Analyze the meaning of 'mun'aqidah'
Understand that 'mun'aqidah' comes from 'aqd' (contract/tie), referring to the intention and resolve that bind the swearer.
It is a valid future oath with full intention.
5
Differentiate the two types
Compare their consequences: ghamus requires repentance only; mun'aqidah requires fulfillment or kaffarah.
The time reference (past vs future) and intention are key.

Classification of Oaths and the Definition of the Ghammous Oath
Introduces the three types of oaths and details the first type with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three types of oaths in Hanafi fiqh, define the ghammous oath, explain its ruling, and identify its proof from the Sunnah.

Core Idea
The ghammous oath is a deliberate false oath about the past that plunges its swearer into sin; it is a major sin with no expiation.

Oaths are common in daily life, but not all oaths are treated equally in Islamic law. In this lesson, we begin the Book of Oaths (Kitab al-Ayman) by classifying oaths into three categories. The first and most serious is the ghammous oath—a false oath sworn with knowledge about a past event. The passage defines this oath, explains its sinfulness, and provides a prophetic proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of oaths
List the three types: ghammous, mun'aqidah, and laghw. Note their distinguishing features.
The ghammous is about the past, mun'aqidah about the future, and laghw is void except in specific cases.
2
Define the ghammous oath
Specify that it is a false oath about a past (or present) event sworn with deliberate knowledge of the lie.
The name comes from 'immersion' into sin and fire.
3
State its ruling
It is haram (major sin) and immerses the swearer in sin. No expiation is accepted; only repentance is required.
This is from the Hanafi position as summarized by al-Quduri.
4
Locate the proof
The hadith 'Whoever swears falsely, Allah will admit him to the Fire' is cited as evidence.
Though the exact wording is rare (gharib), its meaning is established.
5
Understand the proof's weight
The hadith is zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala—the threat of Hell clearly indicates the act is a major sin.
Imam al-Zayla'i and Ibn al-Humam note its authenticity in meaning.

Commentary
This passage from al-Maydani's Sharh al-Quduri begins the Book of Oaths (Kitab al-Ayman) by classifying oaths into three categories. The three types are: (1) al-yamin al-ghamus (the immersing oath), (2) al-yamin al-mun'aqidah (the contracted oath), and (3) al-yamin al-laghw (the vain oath). The term 'ghamus' is derived from immersion in water, indicating that this oath plunges its utterer into sin and fire. There is a variant reading with the definite article, but al-Mughrib considers it incorrect. The second type is named for the deliberate intent to fulfill. The third type is called laghw because it is essentially void and incurs no liability except in specific cases like divorce, manumission, or vows. The commentator notes that this exact wording is rare (gharib) but the meaning is well-established, citing a stronger hadith from Sahih Ibn Hibban about one who swears falsely to seize Muslim property.

يمين منعقدة (yamin mun'aqidah): An oath that is properly contracted with intent to fulfill a future action; it is binding and requires expiation if broken.
يمين لغو (yamin laghw): A vain oath that is void and incurs no liability, except in cases of divorce, manumission, or vows.

The False Oath (Yamin Ghamus) – Definition and Ruling
Defines the false oath, its sinfulness, and absence of expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the false oath (yamin ghamus) and state its ruling, distinguishing it from the vain oath (yamin laghw).

Core Idea
A false oath is a major sin that cannot be expiated; only sincere repentance removes it.

This lesson defines the false oath (yamin ghamus) – a deliberate lie under oath about a past event. The Hanafi school classifies it as a major sin (kabirah) and holds that no expiation (kaffarah) is valid; only sincere repentance (tawbah) and seeking forgiveness (istighfar) are accepted. Understanding this distinction is crucial for proper religious practice and giving correct legal advice.

Guided Path
1
Define the false oath
Identify the key elements: swearing about a past event, with intentional lying. Note the definition from the text.
Also called yamin fajirah (immoral oath).
2
Recognize its sinfulness
Understand that it is a major sin (kabirah), proved by the hadith threatening hellfire. This classifies it as haram.
Contrast with vain oath (yamin laghw) which is excused unless regarding divorce, manumission, or vows.
3
Understand the absence of expiation
The text explicitly states there is no kaffarah for the false oath. Reason: an oath is a legitimate contract, but this is a sin; calling it an oath is metaphorical.
Analogous to 'selling a free person' – the term 'sale' is used metaphorically, not legally valid.
4
Distinguish between false and vain oaths
The false oath is deliberate; the vain oath is unintentional. Vain oath carries no sin or expiation except in the three exceptional cases (divorce, manumission, vow).
This distinction is important for applying the correct ruling.

The Binding Oath (Yamin Mun'aqida) and Its Definition
Defines the binding oath as an oath about a future action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the binding oath as an oath on a future act and distinguish it from other types of oaths using the Hanafi definition.

Core Idea
A binding oath is swearing about a future action; only this type incurs expiation if broken.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are categorized. This lesson focuses on the binding oath (al-yamin al-mun'aqida), which concerns future acts and carries legal consequences. Understanding its precise definition prevents confusion with other oaths and ensures proper expiation when needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the opening line of the passage: the binding oath is swearing about a future matter that the swearer will do. Note that it must be a future act.
2

The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear.
3
Explain the term 'masburah'
The passage explains 'masburah' as an oath that binds its owner, making him patient under its obligation. This synonym reinforces the binding nature.
The term is metaphorical.
4
Note the legal implication
Since it is binding, breaking it requires expiation (kaffarah). The hadith shows that false swearing incurs punishment, affirming its legal effect.
Contrast with the false oath (yamin ghamus) on a past event.

The False Oath: No Expiation, Only Repentance
Explanation of the false oath and why it requires only repentance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on al-yamin al-ghamus and explain why it requires only repentance, not expiation.

The Ruling on Declaring Halal as Haram upon Oneself
Ruling and reasoning on declaring halal items as haram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when someone declares a halal thing as haram upon themselves, and explain the reasoning that treats it as an oath requiring expiation.

Core Idea
Declaring halal as haram upon oneself creates an oath, not a prohibition.

At times, a person might say 'this food is haram for me' as a form of self-pledge. Does this actually make the food forbidden in Islamic law? The Hanafi school, drawing on the implications of language and legal reasoning, provides a clear ruling: the item remains halal, but the speaker has bound themselves with an oath that requires expiation if violated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a person declaring a halal item they own as 'haram' upon themselves. The ruling is that the item does not become actually haram, but the person incurs an obligation to perform expiation (kaffarah) if they use the item.
The expiation is the same as for breaking an oath (kaffarah yamin).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The text provides a reasoning based on the linguistic implication of the word 'haram'. It states that the wording indicates establishing prohibition, but since the item is halal, the prohibition cannot apply to the item itself. Instead, it is implemented by treating the statement as an oath.
This reasoning is an example of qiyas khafi (subtle analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'haram' in the statement implies a prohibition. However, the prohibition cannot be actual because the item is inherently halal. Therefore, the prohibition is interpreted as an obligation to abstain, i.e., an oath. If the person violates this by using the item, they must perform kaffarah yamin.
This interpretation preserves the effect of the wording without contradicting the original halal status.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Maliki) treat such a statement as a vow (nazr) that makes the item actually forbidden for the person, requiring expiation for breaking a vow, not an oath.
The Hanafi position is based on the subtle analogy to oaths, while the opposing view relies on a literal interpretation.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school, as recorded in al-Hidayah and al-Khaneyah, adopts the view that the statement creates an oath. This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling). The reasoning is considered sound because it gives effect to the wording without making halal things haram, which is impossible.
The tarjih is supported by the principle that impossibility is avoided (la yumkinu).

A conditional vow becomes binding when the condition occurs, but Abu Hanifa later ruled that kaffarah suffices for excessive vows.

For conditional vows, the original Hanafi position is that upon fulfillment of the condition, the vow becomes binding. However, a significant intra-madhhab development is reported: Abu Hanifa retracted this strict view, holding that such a person may instead expiate with the kaffarah of an oath. This retraction, also adopted by Muhammad al-Shaybani, reflects the school's concern for avoiding excessive hardship. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for correct fatwa practice.

The retraction is likely based on istihsan and the resemblance to oaths of zeal (yamin al-lajaj).

Under the original view, one must perform the exact vowed act (e.g., Hajj, fasting a year). Under the retracted view, only kaffarah (feeding ten poor, clothing them, or freeing a slave; if unable, fasting three days) is required.
The difference is significant, especially when the vow involves great hardship.

Immediate Cessation in Oaths on Ongoing Actions
Rulings on oaths concerning ongoing actions: wearing, riding, residence, and the role of intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why immediate cessation of an ongoing action fulfills an oath and identify when delay causes violation.

Core Idea
Immediate discontinuation of an ongoing act fulfills an oath; any delay violates it.

Oaths often involve actions already in progress. The Hanafi school stipulates that if the swearer ceases the act immediately, the oath is fulfilled; delaying it constitutes violation. This lesson covers the rulings for wearing, riding, and residence, along with the role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The swearer is already engaged in the action (wearing a garment, riding an animal, residing in a house) at the moment of the oath.
The oath is about a specific item (e.g., 'this garment', 'this animal').
2
State the ruling for immediate cessation
If he removes the garment, dismounts, or begins moving out immediately (fi al-hal), he does not violate the oath.
Reason: He fulfilled the oath to the extent of his capacity; what is beyond his capacity (e.g., instantly disappearing) is excused by custom.
3
Identify the condition for violation
If he delays even for a short while (e.g., remains riding for an hour), he violates the oath.
Such actions are considered to have duration by custom (e.g., riding for a day).
4
Understand the rationale
Oaths are made for fulfillment (birr), not violation. Immediate cessation is within the swearer's ability, so it counts as fulfillment.
The principle: 'al-yamin tu'qadu lil-birr, la lil-hanth' (oaths are made for fulfillment, not violation).
5
Consider the role of intention
If the swearer intends a new beginning (i.e., from now on), his intention is accepted because his words can bear that meaning.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that intention is considered when the wording is ambiguous.

Core Idea
Oath broken only if you continue after swearing; stop at once, you're safe.

What happens if you swear not to do something you're already doing? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule based on custom and intention. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its proof, showing how immediate cessation preserves your oath while lingering triggers expiation.

This is a leniency from al-Hidayah.

Oath Not to Eat from a Palm Tree – Refers to Dates
Ruling on oath about a palm tree referring to its fruit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an oath about a palm tree applies to its fruit and apply the condition of no new manufacture to determine whether a transformed product breaks the oath.

Core Idea
An oath about a palm tree applies to its fruit; processed products do not breach it.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are interpreted according to customary usage (‘urf). When someone swears not to eat from a palm tree, the oath is understood to refer to the dates because the tree itself is not eaten. However, an important qualification arises: if the fruit is transformed into a new substance through manufacturing, the oath is not broken by consuming that new product. This lesson examines the ruling and its condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's object
Recognize that the person swore not to eat from a palm tree (nakhla). The tree is inedible, so the oath cannot be taken literally.
The tree is the cause (sabab) for the fruit.
2
Apply metaphorical extension
Since the oath is attached to something inedible, it turns to what comes out of it – the fruit (thamr). This is based on the principle that the cause stands for the effect metaphorically.
This is valid as a metaphorical usage (majaz).
3
State the condition of no new manufacture
The oath is broken only by eating the fruit in its original form. If the fruit is transformed by a new manufacture (sin‘a jadida), such as making nabidh (date drink), vinegar, or cooked molasses, the oath is not broken.
The transformation changes the substance entirely, so the oath no longer applies.
4
Apply the condition to scenarios
Practice: if one eats raw dates, the oath is broken. If one drinks date vinegar, the oath is not broken because it is a manufactured product.
The key is whether the fruit has undergone a change that removes it from its original category.
