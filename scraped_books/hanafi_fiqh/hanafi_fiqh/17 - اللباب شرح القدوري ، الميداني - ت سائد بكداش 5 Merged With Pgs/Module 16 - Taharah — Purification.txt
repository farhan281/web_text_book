Definition of 'Adalah: The Hanafi Standard
Defining judicial integrity and the standard of avoiding major sins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'adalah and apply the principle of considering the majority (aghlab) to determine witness integrity.

Core Idea
A witness's integrity is defined by avoiding major sins and not persisting on minor sins; rare minor sins are overlooked.

In this passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri, the author clarifies the Hanafi standard for witness integrity ('adalah). The text explains that complete sinlessness is not required; rather, the witness must avoid major sins and not persist on minor ones. This pragmatic standard ensures the door of testimony remains open for the protection of rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of definition
The passage defines 'adalah (legal integrity) as required for a witness. Identify what 'adalah means in the testimonial context.
The term 'adalah is specific to witness qualification.
2
List the positive requirements for 'adalah
The witness must avoid all major sins (kaba'ir) and must not persist (israr) on minor sins (sagha'ir). Note that persistence on a minor sin elevates it to the level of a major sin in effect.
Avoidance of major sins is absolute; persistence on minor sins is the additional disqualifier.
3
Explain the 'aghlab' principle
After avoiding major sins, the witness's conduct is judged by the majority of their actions. If major sins are rare, they do not disqualify; if they are frequent, they do. For minor sins, only persistence disqualifies, not occasional lapses.
The principle prevents the impossibility of finding witnesses.
4
Understand the rationale
The text states that requiring absolute sinlessness would close the door of testimony (sadd al-bab), which is meant to be open for reviving rights (ihya' al-huquq).
This is a key Hanafi maslaha-based reasoning.
5
Note the authoritative sources
The passage cites al-Jawhara, al-Hidaya, al-Mujtaba, and Mukhtarat al-Nawazil as confirming this definition. This shows the position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.
These are core Hanafi texts.

Classification of Ushr and Kharaj Land and Reviving Dead Land
Ruling with proof on land classification and ihya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ushr and kharaj lands, apply the rule that revived dead land takes the classification of its tract, and explain the role of ijma in overriding analogy.

Core Idea
Land classification depends on conquest and treatment of inhabitants; revived land follows its tract, but consensus can override analogy.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri defines the two main categories of land in Hanafi fiqh: 'ushr land (tithe for Muslims) and kharaj land (land tax for non-Muslims). It also addresses a practical question: when a Muslim revives dead land, how is it classified? The answer lies in the surrounding tract (hizz), with an important exception for Basra established by consensus of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two land categories
Classify land as 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax) based on how it was acquired and the status of its inhabitants at conquest. Use the criteria: inhabitants embraced Islam before conquest, or land was conquered by force and divided among fighters → 'ushr; conquered by force and left with its people, or peace treaty → kharaj.
Makkah is a special exception – conquered by force but no kharaj imposed.
2
Understand the rationale behind classifications
Note the Hanafi rationale: 'ushr land has a meaning of worship (for Muslims), kharaj land has a meaning of punishment (for disbelievers). This affects the spiritual dimension of land ownership.
3
Apply the rule for reviving dead land
When a Muslim revives dead land (ard al-mawat), its classification depends on the surrounding tract (hizz). If near kharaj land → kharaji; if near 'ushr land → 'ushri. This is based on qiyas with the courtyard of a house having the same ruling as the house.
This is Abu Yusuf's view, relied upon in the school.
4
Recognize the exception and its proof
Basra is classified as 'ushr land despite being in a kharaj tract. The proof is ijma (consensus) of the Companions, which overrides the qiyas. This illustrates the hierarchy of evidence: ijma > qiyas.
The dalil is from the passage: 'Basra... is tithe land by the consensus of the Companions'.
5
Evaluate the practical consequences
The classification determines the type of tax owed: 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax). Incorrect classification leads to wrongful obligation. The mufta bihi is the classification as per the tract, with known exceptions like Basra.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the commentary on Al-Quduri. It first defines the categories of land: Sawad land (Iraq) is owned by its inhabitants and they may sell it; the Imam's discretion upon conquest is explained. Then it defines 'ushr land: land whose inhabitants embraced Islam before conquest, or land conquered by force and divided among the fighters. The rationale is that such land is for Muslims, bearing the meaning of worship. Next, kharaj land is defined: land conquered by force and left in the hands of its original inhabitants, or land where the Imam made a peace treaty. The rationale is that it is for disbelievers, bearing the meaning of punishment. A special exception is noted from Al-Hidayah: Makkah was conquered by force but the Prophet left it to its people without imposing kharaj. The passage then discusses reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). According to Abu Yusuf, the revived land takes the ruling of its surrounding tract (hizz): if near kharaj land, it becomes kharaj land; if near 'ushr land, it becomes 'ushr land. This is based on the legal principle that what is near a thing follows its ruling, analogous to the courtyard of a house having the same ruling as the house. Finally, Basra is noted as an exception: according to Abu Yusuf, it is 'ushr land by consensus of the Companions, even though its tract would suggest kharaj. The consensus overrides the analogy.

Key Terms

أرض الخراج (ard al-kharāj): Land on which kharaj (land tax) is levied; it is land conquered by force and left in the hands of its original inhabitants.
أرض الموات (ard al-mawāt): Dead land; unused, unowned land that can be revived by a Muslim.
حيز (ḥayyiz): Tract or vicinity; the surrounding area of a piece of land that determines its ruling.

Property of an Apostate: Inheritance and Fay'
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute on apostate's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the fate of property earned during Islam and property earned during apostasy, and identify the mufta bihi position in the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
Earnings during apostasy become fay' because the apostate's blood is lawful — no ownership.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with the disposition of an apostate's property upon death or execution. The ruling distinguishes between wealth acquired while Muslim and wealth acquired while an apostate. The commentary then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that apostasy earnings are fay' for the state, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) give both categories to the heirs. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's, buttressed by later authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of property
Look at the text: 'what he earned in the state of Islam' and 'what he earned in the state of apostasy'. These are two distinct categories with different rulings.
The text uses 'kasabahu' (earned) – this includes all acquisitions, not just trade.
2
State the ruling for each category
For Islam-period earnings: transferred to Muslim heirs. For apostasy-period earnings: becomes fay' (booty) for the Muslim treasury.
The reasoning for inheritance: apostasy is like death, so the relationship pre-exists. For fay': analogy to harbi property.
3
Locate the proof (dalil) for fay' ruling
The text says: 'because his earnings in apostasy are the earnings of one whose blood is permissible (mubah al-dam), having no right for anyone, so it is fay', like the property of a harbi.' This is a qiyas (analogy).
The 'illa (effective cause) is the absence of protected ownership due to permissibility of blood.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree: they say both sets of earnings go to the heirs. The text states 'Wa qala: kalahuma li-warathatihi.'
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school, not a rejection of the school's fundamentals.
5
Apply tarjih to determine mufta bihi
The author declares: 'Wa al-sahih qawl al-Imam' – the correct view is Abu Hanifa's, and al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah chose his statement. This is the operative position for fatwa.
Tarjih relies on later consensus of the muta'akhkhirīn (later scholars).

Consequences of an Apostate Joining Dar al-Harb
Ruling on apostate joining enemy territory and its legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three main legal consequences when an apostate joins dar al-harb and the judge rules, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the timing of the apostate's inheritance status.

Core Idea
An apostate joining dar al-harb is like death in law, but only effective via a judge's ruling.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri deals with a specific case: a Muslim apostatizes and flees to enemy territory (dar al-harb). The Hanafi school treats this as constructive death once a judge confirms the joining. This has significant effects on his slaves, debts, and property. We will explore the ruling, the underlying reasoning, and a difference of opinion within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its trigger
The act is an apostate joining dar al-harb. The trigger is not just the act itself: a judge must rule on his joining, because the apostate might return.
Without the judge's ruling, the status is uncertain.
2
List the legal consequences
After the judge's ruling: mudabbars are freed from one-third of the estate; umm walad is freed from all wealth; mukatab pays kitabah to heirs; wala' belongs to apostate; debts become due; property earned in Islam goes to Muslim heirs.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The apostate is likened to a dead person because the authority to compel (wilayat al-ilzam) ceases, just as with death.
This is a qiyas: the underlying cause is cessation of authority.
4
Recognize the condition for implementation
The judge's ruling is necessary; the status is not automatic due to the possibility of the apostate returning to Islam and dar al-Islam.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the apostate's ability to inherit from others is evaluated at the time of joining, not at the judge's ruling. This affects inheritance if someone dies between joining and the ruling.
The majority view follows the judge's ruling as the cutoff.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct use and mere transfer in the context of prohibited vessels, state the ruling on silver-inlaid vessels according to Abu Hanifa, and list materials that are permissible for vessels.

Transferring content from a gold vessel without directly using it is permissible; silver-inlay is conditionally permissible; other materials are inherently allowed.

After establishing the core prohibition on gold and silver vessels, this lesson delves into the nuances. What exactly constitutes 'use' (istimal) of a prohibited vessel? 1
Define 'use' in the context of vessels
Use (istimal) means employing the vessel directly for its customary function (e.g., drinking from a gold cup). If you only transfer the content to another medium (e.g., pour into palm, then drink), that is not considered using the prohibited vessel.
This distinction is crucial for people who want to avoid sin while still benefiting from the vessel's content.

Based on the text and Ibn 'Abidin's elaboration: pouring oil from a gold bottle into your palm is permissible if done without the intention of using the bottle directly. Vessels made of glass, crystal (ballur), agate, ruby, chrysolite, and similar materials are permissible because they are not in the meaning (laysa fi ma‘na) of gold and silver.
The rationale: they lack the specific value and luxury that leads to the prohibition.
5

The rulings are tiered: (1) Gold/silver vessels: absolute prohibition. (2) Silver-inlaid: conditionally permissible per Abu Hanifa. (3) Other materials: inherently permissible.
The definition of 'use' applies only to the first category.

Ruling with proof using qiyas based on necessity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A female slave is like a mahram for looking; a young free woman's face and hands are forbidden to touch even without desire.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies the boundaries of looking at and touching women who are not one's own. The Hanafi school makes a careful distinction based on social role and necessity: the slave woman’s work needs allow broader permissibility, while the free woman’s modesty is preserved more strictly. Let’s unpack the ruling and its underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The text distinguishes between a female slave (mamlūkat ghayrihī) and a non-mahram free woman (ajnabiyya), further subdivided into young (shābba) and elderly (ʿajūz).
2
State the ruling for each category
A man may look at another's female slave as he looks at his mahram relatives (face, head, chest, shins, forearms). For a young non-mahram free woman, touching her face and palms is forbidden even if safe from desire. For an elderly free woman, touching is permissible if safe from desire.
3
Locate the proof for the slave ruling
The proof is based on necessity (qiyas): the slave goes out for her master's needs and serves guests in work clothes, so strict covering would be impractical. Therefore, looking is permitted as with mahrams.
This is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) based on ʿurf and necessity.
4
Note the condition of safety from desire
For both the slave and the elderly free woman, the permissibility is conditional on being safe from desire. If there is any desire or doubt, it becomes prohibited.
The text explicitly says: 'If he is not safe or doubts, touching and looking are not permissible.'
