Conditional Oath on Permission: One-Time Permission Ends the Oath
Ruling and rationale for one-time permission in conditional oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a conditional oath phrased as 'until I give permission' expires upon the first permission, and subsequent acts without permission do not break the oath.

Core Idea
One permission ends the oath: the condition is a time limit, not a per-act permit.

When a husband swears that his wife will not leave without his permission, the phrasing 'until I give permission' sets a time limit. The moment he gives her permission once, the condition is fulfilled and the oath ends. Even if she goes out again later without his permission, the oath remains intact. This lesson unpacks the rationale (tawqit) and its practical effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and condition
The husband swears: 'You will not go out unless I give you permission.' The condition for the wife's lawful exit is husband's permission.
Focus on the phrase 'unless' or 'until' which implies a future time.
2
Analyze the act of permission
The husband grants permission once, explicitly or implicitly. Consider whether the permission is general or specific.
The text assumes one explicit permission is given.
3
Determine the legal effect
After that one permission, the oath is considered fulfilled. The condition (permission) has occurred, so the oath ends. Subsequent exits do not break it.
The rationale is that the phrase sets a time limit (tawqit).
4
Derive the underlying principle
The principle of tawqit means that the condition serves as a temporal boundary. Once the boundary is reached, the oath is complete.
Contrast with an oath where permission is required for each exit; that would be a per-act condition.

Oaths to Perform Impossible Acts: Hanafi Validity Based on Divine Power
Ruling on oaths involving impossible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths involving impossible acts and explain the rational proof based on divine omnipotence.

Core Idea
An oath to do the impossible is valid because nothing is impossible for Allah – it is binding and requires expiation.

What happens when someone swears to do something normally impossible, like ascending to the sky or turning a stone into gold? The Hanafi school rules that such an oath is valid and binding. The reasoning is rooted in the absolute power of Allah: nothing is truly impossible for Him, so the oath is not on a categorically impossible act. This lesson unpacks the text from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri to understand this distinctive Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
First, locate the explicit ruling: 'his oath is valid' (انعقدت يمينه). Note that the Hanafi term for a valid, binding oath is 'mun'aqidah'.
The ruling is a clear statement of validity.
2
Identify the Acts Sworn
The passage gives two examples: ascending the sky and turning a stone into gold. Both are beyond normal human capability. Recognize that these are concrete examples of 'impossible' acts.
The acts are chosen to illustrate the principle.
3
Analyze the Proof (Ta'lil)
The proof is given: 'because it is possible to fulfill it in reality by the enabling of Allah Almighty.' This is a rational (aqli) argument that shifts the basis from human capacity to divine power. The oath is valid because Allah can enable it, so it is not categorically impossible.
Key phrase: 'بإقدار الله تعالى' – by the enabling of Allah.
4
Understand the Consequence
Since the oath is valid, if the swearer does not fulfill it (and they cannot by normal means), they must perform expiation (kaffarah) for breaking the oath, just like any other valid oath.
Expiation is the practical legal consequence.

Explains the oath to not inhabit a house and the dispute on required removal of belongings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on what constitutes breaking an oath not to inhabit a house and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
An oath not to inhabit is broken by custom; the three imams differ on how much removal suffices.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on oaths. The base ruling is clear, but the details reveal a practical difference rooted in custom (urf). Understanding this helps you see how the school resolves competing views by preferring ease and convenience.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling
Identify that if a person swears not to inhabit a house and leaves but leaves family or belongings, the oath is broken by custom ('urf).
Custom considers the presence of family and belongings as residence even if the person is physically absent.
2
Identify the three intra-Hanafi positions
List the views: Abu Hanifa requires removal of all belongings (down to a peg); Abu Yusuf requires removal of the majority; Muhammad requires removal only of what is essential for dwelling.
These positions reflect different interpretations of what constitutes 'leaving' the dwelling.
3
Analyze the reasoning behind each
Abu Hanifa argues that residence is established by the whole, so any remaining part counts. Abu Yusuf considers difficulty of total removal. Muhammad argues that only essentials matter for dwelling; beyond that is not part of residence.
The reasoning combines custom with practicality.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its practical effect
The later scholars prefer Muhammad's view as 'better and more convenient for the people'. The fatwa is upon it.
This means in practice, only essential dwelling items need to be removed to avoid breaking the oath.

Core Idea
An oath to do what is naturally impossible is valid because Allah can enable it, but it is broken immediately.

This lesson examines a unique Hanafi ruling on oaths: when someone swears to do something that is impossible by human means, such as ascending the sky or turning a stone into gold. The Hanafi school holds the oath valid because divine power makes it possible, yet immediately broken because human inability makes fulfillment impossible. This ruling is contrasted with an oath to do something absolutely inconceivable, like drinking water from an empty ewer. Understanding this distinction clarifies the condition of possibility for an oath's validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage addresses oaths to inherently impossible acts (e.g., ascending the sky). Determine the legal status: the oath is valid (in'aqadat) and the swearer breaks it immediately (hanitha 'uqaybaha).
Ruling: valid but broken at once.
2
Understand the rational evidence
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): fulfillment is truly possible by Allah's enabling. Even if human capacity is lacking, divine power makes it conceptually possible. This is why the oath is valid.
Divine omnipotence is the key premise.
3
Distinguish between types of impossibility
Compare: oath to ascend the sky (naturally impossible but conceivable) vs. oath to drink from an empty ewer (inconceivable). The former is valid and broken; the latter is not even an oath because the act is unimaginable.
The condition is conceptual possibility, not empirical feasibility.
4
Apply the principle to a new case
Given a scenario (e.g., swearing to fly), check if the act can be conceived as possible by Allah. If yes, the oath is valid and immediately broken; kaffarah becomes wajib. If the act is logically impossible (e.g., creating a married bachelor), the oath is invalid.
Always ask: 'Can Allah do this?' If yes, it's a valid oath.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an unconditional oath when the oath-taker dies before fulfillment, and explain the timing of the breaking (hanth) using the concept of the last moment of life.

Core Idea
An unconditional oath binds until death; the breaking occurs at the final moment when fulfillment becomes impossible.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a person swears an absolute oath to perform an act, such as going to Basra, but dies before doing it. The question is: when does the oath break? The Hanafi school provides a precise answer based on the nature of the oath and the moment of impossibility. Understanding this ruling clarifies the binding force of unconditional oaths and the obligation of expiation (kaffarah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's nature
Determine whether the oath is unconditional (muthlaq, no time limit) or conditional (mu'allaq). Here, the oath is absolute.
The text says 'انعقدت مطلقةً غير مؤقتة' — contracted as absolute, not time-bound.
2
Determine the ruling on breaking
If the person dies without fulfilling the oath, he breaks it (hanth). The ruling is that hanth occurs at the last moment of life.
Death itself does not nullify the oath; the binding continues until the very end.
3
Examine the reasoning (talil)
The reason: because the oath was absolute, it remains valid as long as fulfillment (birr) is possible. At death, fulfillment becomes impossible, so hope (ya's) is lost and the breaking is attributed to the last part of life.
The reasoning is based on the continuing possibility of fulfillment until the moment of death.
4
Understand the concept of 'akhir juz'
The phrase 'آخر جزء من أجزاء حياته' means the last instant of life. This precise timing determines when kaffarah becomes obligatory.
This avoids the absurdity of the oath being broken before or after death.
5
Note the practical consequence
Since the oath is broken in the last moment of life, kaffarah (expiation) becomes due. The heirs should pay it from the estate if the deceased did not.
Kaffarah for a broken oath is feeding/clothing ten poor people or fasting three days.

Core Idea
A claim is only accepted if the claimed item is known in type and quantity; unknown claims are non-compellable.

In Hanafi fiqh, a claim before a judge must meet strict conditions to be actionable. This passage from al-Lubab explains the key requirement that the claimed item be known in its type and quantity, and how this applies to movable property. Mastering these conditions is essential for understanding the adab al-qada.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is presenting a legal claim (da'wa) before a judge. The ruling is that the claim is accepted only if certain conditions are met; otherwise it is rejected.
The text begins with 'ولا تقبل الدعوى' – the claim is not accepted.
2
List the conditions for acceptance
The plaintiff must mention the claimed item with known type (jins) and known quantity (qadr). Examples of type: wheat, barley, gold, silver. Examples of quantity: qafiz, mithqal, dirham.
Both are required; one alone is insufficient.

The purpose of a claim is to compel the defendant through evidence. Compulsion over an unknown item is impossible, so knowledge of the item is a prerequisite.

If the claimed item is present in the defendant's hand, the defendant must bring it to court for pointing by plaintiff, witnesses, and for oath. If the item is absent (destroyed or burdensome to transport), the plaintiff must state its value to make it known.
Pointing is most effective for identification; value substitutes when item is not present.

Conditions for Claiming Immovable Property
Lists procedural requirements for claiming immovable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a valid claim of immovable property in Hanafi law and distinguish between omitting and erring in a boundary.

Core Idea
Three boundaries suffice; possession must be proven; demand is essential.

When claiming immovable property (عقار), you cannot simply point to it as with movables. Hanafi law requires precise identification through boundaries, proof that the defendant possesses it, and an explicit demand. This lesson explains these requirements and a subtle dispute over the number of boundaries needed.

Guided Path
1
List the three requirements
Identify the three mandatory elements the plaintiff must include: (1) define boundaries, (2) state the property is in the defendant's hand (possession), (3) demand it from the defendant.
These appear sequentially in the text: 'حدَّده، وذكر أنه في يد المدعى عليه، وأنه يطالبه به.'
2
Analyze the boundary rule
Understand why boundaries are required: immovable property cannot be pointed to or moved, so identification must be through its four boundaries. The plaintiff must name the owners of each boundary and their lineages (including grandfather unless the owner is well-known).
The text explains: 'لأنه تعذر التعريف بالإشارة؛ لتعذر النقل، فصار إلى التحديد.'
3
Distinguish omission from error
Note that mentioning only three boundaries suffices (Hanafi view), but erring in the fourth changes the claim and is not allowed. Omitting a boundary does not change the property claimed; erring in it mistakenly identifies a different property.
The text contrasts 'ترك' (omission) with 'غلط' (error) in the fourth boundary.
4
Examine the possession condition
The plaintiff must assert that the defendant is in possession. Mere acknowledgment by the defendant is insufficient; possession must be proved by evidence (بينة) or the judge's knowledge, to avoid collusion. This differs from movables where visible possession suffices.
The text says: 'لا تثبت اليد فيه إلا بالبينة، أو علم القاضي هو الصحيح؛ نفياً لتهمة المواضعة.'

Mutual Oath-Taking in Kitaba Disputes: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mutual oath-taking in kitaba contract disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on mutual oath-taking in kitaba disputes and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
In kitaba disputes, mutual oath-taking applies only if the contract is analogous to sale; Abu Hanifa denies it, the Sahibayn affirm it.

When the master and his mukatib slave disagree over the amount of the kitaba payment, a question arises: should they take oaths against each other as in sale disputes? This passage reveals an important intra-Hanafi difference on the analogy between kitaba and sale. Understanding the reasoning helps you see how Hanafi jurists treat contracts that are not fully binding on both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine the exact legal question: does mutual oath-taking (tahalf) apply when the master and mukatib dispute the amount of the kitaba?
The dispute is procedural, not about the validity of the kitaba itself.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that they do not take oaths. His reasoning: tahalf in sale is an exception to qiyas, and kitaba is not like sale because it is not binding on the mukatib (the slave can unilaterally cancel).
The key distinction is the lack of binding nature (luzum) on the slave's side.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that they do take oaths and the kitaba is rescinded. Their reasoning: kitaba is a commutative contract that can be rescinded, so it resembles sale in meaning (fi ma'na al-bay').
They emphasize the commutative (mu'awadah) nature of kitaba.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa's view, the case is decided by other means (e.g., the master's word with oath or evidence). Under the Sahibayn's view, they take oaths and the contract is rescinded, meaning the slave remains a slave.
This difference affects the outcome of the dispute.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi) prefer the Sahibayn's view. Therefore, the operative ruling for fatwa is that they take oaths and the kitaba is rescinded.
This illustrates how later tarjih can settle an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Priority of Seller's Claim in Paternity Disputes
Ruling on priority of paternity claims between seller and buyer of an umm walad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on priority of paternity claims between seller and buyer of an umm walad and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The seller's claim is prior because it relates to the time of conception, providing apparent evidence (shahada) under istihsan.

In this passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, the author explains a nuanced Hanafi ruling: when both the seller and buyer of a slave woman claim paternity of her child, the seller's claim takes precedence. This is based on the principle of istihsan (juristic preference), departing from strict analogy. Let's explore why and how this conclusion is reached.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
A seller sold a slave woman to a buyer. Later, she gives birth to a child. Both seller and buyer claim paternity of the child. Who gets the child?
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The seller's claim is given priority (أولى). The sale is annulled (يفسخ البيع) and the seller must return the price he received.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
Understand the dalil: istihsan
The reasoning is based on istihsan. The time of conception (علوق) occurred while the woman was still in the seller's ownership. This fact serves as apparent testimony (شهادة ظاهرة) that the child is from the seller, even though he later sold her. Because lineage is often hidden, shar'i considerations overlook the apparent contradiction.
Istihsan allows overriding strict qiyas to achieve a more equitable outcome.
4
Apply to buyer's claim
If the buyer also claims paternity, whether simultaneously or after the seller's claim, the seller's claim is still first because it traces back to the time of conception.
5
Note the exception
Al-Qahistani notes: if the buyer claimed paternity before the seller made any claim, then the buyer's claim is accepted and lineage is established from him, carried upon a valid marriage (since he owned the mother).
This shows the nuance: priority depends on who claimed first.

Partition (Qismah): Definition and Its Dual Nature
Defines partition, explains its dual nature as ifraz and mubadalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of partition and distinguish between ifraz and mubadalah, applying each to homogenous and heterogeneous property.

Core Idea
Partition is both separation (ifraz) and exchange (mubadalah); the dominant nature determines whether consent is needed.

Partition (qismah) is a common transaction encountered when co-owners wish to separate their shares. But is it a sale or a mere division? Hanafi jurists see it as partaking of both. This lesson unpacks the definition, the cause, the condition, and how the nature changes with the type of property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and legal elements
Start with the author's definition of qismah: 'gathering a common share in a specific place'. Note the cause (sabab) and condition (shart) that follow. These are the foundational elements.
The cause is the request of partners; the condition is that the benefit is not lost.
2
Analyze the dual nature: ifraz and mubadalah
The text argues partition is not free from exchange (mubadalah) because each partner gives up part of his right to the other. Yet it is also separation (ifraz) because shares are merely distinguished. Understand that both elements are present, but one dominates depending on the goods.
The phrase 'min wajh' (from one aspect) indicates co-existence.
3
Differentiate homogenous and heterogeneous goods
For homogenous goods (mikyalat, mawzunat), ifraz dominates because there is no significant disparity. Hence one partner can take his share in the absence of the other. For heterogeneous goods, mubadalah dominates, requiring both presence and consent.
This distinction is crucial for understanding compulsory partition later.

Labeling Shares and Drawing Lots
Explains the labeling and lottery method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Hanafi method for assigning shares through sequential labeling and lottery (qurʿa).

Core Idea
After equalization, shares are labeled in order and assigned by lottery to ensure impartiality.

Once the property is measured, valued, and separated into shares, the next step is to label each share and then assign them to co-owners through a lottery. This method prevents bias and is the standard Hanafi procedure for dividing jointly owned property when shares are equal.

Guided Path
1
Label the shares sequentially
The qāsim labels the shares as 'first', 'second', 'third', etc., in order of value or location.
This creates clear identification.
2
Prepare the lottery
The names of the co-owners are written on separate pieces of paper and placed in a bag.
Each name has equal chance.
3
Draw the lots
The qāsim draws one piece at a time; the first name drawn gets the first share, and so on.
This is the method of qurʿa.
4
Understand the purpose
Lottery removes any partiality and ensures that no co-owner can complain about an unfair assignment.
It is a sunnah method recommended by the Hanafis.

Division Invalidated if Easement Cannot Be Redirected
Ruling on division with an unstipulated easement, elaborating the condition of reroutability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a co-owner's unstipulated easement exists in a property division and apply the condition of reroutability to determine whether the division stands or is rescinded.

Core Idea
An unstipulated easement in a division is lost if reroutable, otherwise the division is rescinded.

Property division among co-owners must result in exclusive shares. When one co-owner has a right of way or water flow through another's share and this was not mentioned in the division, the law must balance the easement right with the integrity of the division. This passage from Al-Lubab explains the Hanafi rule: the easement holder loses the right if rerouting is possible; if impossible, the division is void and must be redone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
Recognise that the act is a division (qisma) of jointly owned property. The specific condition is the presence of a masīl or ṭarīq for one co‑owner in another's share, and that it was not stipulated in the division contract.
The condition 'not stipulated' is essential; if it had been stipulated, the easement continues by agreement.
2
Determine the possibility of rerouting
Check whether the easement can be physically redirected away from the co‑owner's share. This is the key factual issue that decides the outcome.
Rerouting must be feasible without rendering the easement useless.
3
Apply the outcome based on possibility
If rerouting is possible: the division stands, and the easement holder loses the right to use the path/water over the specific share. If rerouting is impossible: the division is rescinded (faskh).
Rescission means the division contract is annulled and a new division must be made, this time accounting for the easement.
4
Understand the legal reasoning
The division is rescinded because it remains 'mixed' (ikhtilāṭ) – the easement prevents full separation of the shares. A valid division requires complete severance of rights.
This is a rational proof (dalīl 'aqlī): division without mixing is a necessary condition for validity.

Division of Upper and Lower Stories: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on dividing mixed upper/lower property and identify the mufta bihi view according to later scholars.

Core Idea
Value is preferred over area when dividing properties with mixed upper and lower stories.

When partners own a building where some parts are lower floors only, others upper stories only, and some both, how should they divide it? Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani each offered a different method. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and explains why later Hanafi scholars eventually settled on one view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute context
The scenario is a property with three types of units: (1) lower floor only (no upper story above it), (2) upper story only (no lower floor below it), and (3) lower floors that have an upper story above them. The partners wish to divide the property fairly.
2
State the three positions
Abu Hanifa: divide by area (dhira') with a ratio of 1 cubit of lower floor = 2 cubits of upper story. Abu Yusuf: divide by area with equal ratio (1:1). Muhammad: ignore area entirely and divide by market value.
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf agree on the method (area) but differ on the ratio; Muhammad differs on the method itself.
3
Understand the reasoning
The text suggests two possible explanations: (a) each imam followed the building customs of his time or place, or (b) there is a substantive difference in principle. This affects how later scholars weigh the views.
Al-Isbījābī considered it a matter of custom and preferred Abu Hanifa among the area-based views.
4
Analyse the tarjih
Al-Isbījābī favoured Abu Hanifa over Abu Yusuf. However, later mashāyikh (as recorded in al-Tuḥfah and al-Badā'i') chose Muhammad's value-based division as the operative view. This is now the mufta bihi.
The tarjih is not a simple majority; it is based on perceived strength and practicality.
5
Apply the result
In practice, each unit is appraised at market value, and the total value is divided among partners according to their shares. Area is ignored.
This avoids unfairness when lower and upper floors have differing desirability or construction costs.

Claim of Mistake After Division: The Requirement of Evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a claim of mistake after property division and explain the role of evidence and oath in resolving such disputes.

Core Idea
A completed property division can be challenged for error only with clear evidence, not mere assertion.

Partition is meant to be final, but what if one party later discovers a mistake? This lesson examines a Hanafi ruling that balances finality with justice. The claimant must bring evidence; if not, oaths determine the outcome. Understanding this reinforces the principle that completed transactions are binding unless proven otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Two parties have divided property; each acknowledged receipt with witnesses. Later, one claims an error—that some asset in his portion actually belongs to the other.
2
Apply the general rule
The claim is to rescind a completed division. Hanafi law holds that such a claim is not accepted without clear evidence (bayyinah).
The prior admission of receipt does not automatically bar the claim if the error is proven.
3
Trace the proof
The author reasons: 'because he claims rescission of the division after its occurrence, so he is not believed except with a proof.' This is a corollary of the principle that completed transactions are binding.
This is a logical (qiyasi) proof based on the nature of finality.
4
Observe the intra-Hanafi dispute
Some later scholars argued the claim should be rejected outright due to contradiction. The majority (Abu Hanifa, al-Burhani, al-Nasafi) hold that if the error becomes clear upon re-examination, the admission does not bind.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.

Commentary

An explicit acknowledgment of receipt bars a later contradiction and preserves the division.

Commentary
This passage presents two practical rulings under the Book of Partition. The first case: someone initially says 'I received my right' (acknowledging full receipt), then later claims 'I only took part of it.' Here, the opponent's statement is taken with his oath, meaning the opponent is believed when he denies the partial receipt. This is because the first acknowledgment is an admission of full receipt, so the later claim is a contradiction; the opponent is considered a claimant of the rest (since the speaker now claims he did not receive full), and the original receiver is now a defendant denying that claim, so the defendant's word with oath is given weight. The second case: someone claims 'The share reached me to a certain place but you did not hand it over,' without having previously acknowledged receipt, and the partner denies. Since there is no prior acknowledgment, the mutual oath (taháluf) takes place, and the partition is rescinded. The commentary explains the underlying principle: prior acknowledgment of receipt removes the obligation of mutual oath, but does not affect the validity of the lawsuit itself. Core Idea
Muhaya'ah is valid only when usufructs are divisible or assets differ in use; produce and inseparable services are excluded.

Co-owners often need to share the use of a jointly owned asset. Muhaya'ah (alternating use) is a practical solution, but not every arrangement is valid. This lesson delineates the Hanafi rulings on which forms of muhaya'ah are permissible and which are invalid, based on the nature of the asset and the clarity of the division.

Guided Path

Apply the rule of ghallah and indivisible services
Assets whose benefits fluctuate or cannot be precisely measured—like fruit, milk, young animals—are not valid for muhaya'ah. Similarly, a single slave whose service is alternated daily is invalid because the service is indivisible and uncertain.
The key distinction: muhaya'ah in 'ayn (corpus) is not permitted if the benefit is not fixed.

Derive the underlying principle
The overarching principle: muhaya'ah is valid when each co-owner's share of the usufruct is clearly defined, measurable, and not subject to gharar (uncertainty). This is why different assets (slave + house) or same asset with alternating days for use (house) are valid.

Definition and Types of Duress (Ikrah)
Defines ikrah and its two types with effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ikrah in Hanafi legal terminology and distinguish between mulji' and ghayr mulji' duress, identifying their effects on the validity of coerced actions.

Core Idea
Only overwhelming duress (mulji') destroys free choice; lesser duress leaves the action valid but sinful.

This passage from the classical Hanafi text introduces the concept of duress (ikrah) and its two types. Understanding this distinction is crucial because the Hanafi school rules that only threats to life or limb render a coerced act invalid, while lesser pressures like imprisonment leave the act legally effective. This foundational taxonomy governs many later rules on contracts, divorces, and oaths made under duress.

Guided Path
1
Isolate the linguistic and legal definitions
Read the first lines: the linguistic definition is 'compelling someone to what he dislikes'; the legal definition adds 'removing consent and acting without choice, with the possibility of corrupting the act or not.'
Note that 'rida' (consent) is absent in all duress, but 'ikhtiyar' (choice) may remain or be destroyed.
2
Identify the two types and their criteria
From 'al-Tanqih': mulji' involves threat of loss of life or limb; ghayr mulji' involves imprisonment, shackling, or beating. Focus on the harm threshold.
Only the threat of death or amputation of a limb qualifies as mulji' in the Hanafi school.
3
Analyse the effect on consent and choice
Both types remove consent (rida). In mulji', choice (ikhtiyar) is also corrupted (ifsad al-ikhtiyar). In ghayr mulji', choice remains intact.
This distinction determines the legal outcome: corruption of choice invalidates the act; mere absence of consent does not.
4
Apply the conclusion from 'al-Durar'
The final summary: absence of consent is common to all ikrah, but choice is present in all; only in mulji' is choice corrupted.
This clarifies that a coerced action is not automatically void; the type of duress must be assessed.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a critical issue: when may a Muslim consume something normally forbidden (carrion, blood, pork, wine) if forced? Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the principle of necessity (darurah) correctly.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Duress does not invalidate these contracts, but shifts liability to the coercer.

This passage addresses a key question: when a person is forced to divorce, free a slave, or marry, does the act take effect? The Hanafi school answers with a clear rule — the act is valid, but the coerced party has financial recourse against the one who compelled them. In Hanafi law, coercion (ikrah) generally nullifies the legal effect of an act performed under duress. Non-Combatants in Islamic Warfare: Protected Categories and Exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which non-combatants are protected under Hanafi law and state the exceptions that permit killing them.

Core Idea
Only active combat permits killing; non-combatants are immune unless they contribute to war strategically.

This passage from al-Lubab lays down the rules of engagement in war: certain categories of people are not to be killed because they are not combatants. However, the ruling is not absolute – exceptions exist for those who have military opinion, harmful magic, or symbolic leadership. Understanding these nuances is crucial for applying the Hanafi position that the basis for killing is actual fighting (muharaba), not mere status.

The underlying rule is that only active combat (muharaba) permits killing. Non-combatants are protected because they do not fight.

For opinion: such a person is more valuable dead than alive because counsel aids the enemy. For queen: killing her splits enemy ranks. For sorceress: she harms through non-physical means. For child king: like the queen, his death disperses the assembly.
These exceptions show that the prohibition is lifted when the non-combatant's role is strategically significant.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A peace treaty is permissible if it benefits Muslims; it becomes jihad in meaning.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains the Hanafi ruling on making peace with the enemy. The key principle is that peace is not a sign of weakness but can be a strategic act of jihad when it serves the common good. The condition of benefit is essential; without it the treaty becomes impermissible.

This reasoning elevates peace from a concession to a commendable act.

Freedom of Disbelievers' Slaves Joining the Muslim Army
Ruling on escapee slaves becoming free and wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the freedom of slaves who join the Muslim army, explain the reasoning, and identify the absence of wala' in legal emancipation.

Core Idea
A slave who defects to the Muslim camp becomes free by law, but no wala' is created.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific scenario in the law of war (siyar): the fate of slaves belonging to enemy combatants. When such slaves escape to the Muslim army, Hanafi law holds that they are automatically free. The reasoning is that they have secured their own freedom by opposing their masters and choosing the Muslims. However, this freedom is a legal emancipation (itq hukmi) and does not establish a relationship of wala' (clientage) with any Muslim, unlike manumission by a master. This distinction has practical implications for inheritance and loyalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The slaves' act of going out to the Muslim camp triggers the ruling of freedom. The text states: 'If their slaves go out to the Muslim camp, they are free.'
2
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
The author explains: 'because they have secured themselves by coming out to us, opposing their masters.' This shows the effective cause (illa) is the slave's initiative to defect and renounce their masters.
This is a logical reasoning, not a direct textual proof.
3
Extend the ruling to a second scenario
The same ruling applies if the slaves convert to Islam in dar al-harb and the Muslims later conquer that territory. They become free even without escaping.
Conversion plus conquest achieves the same effect.
4
Note the consequence: no wala'
The text explicitly states that wala' is not established for anyone over these freed slaves because this is a legal (hukmi) emancipation, not a manumission by a master.
This means the former owner has no inheritance or clientage rights, and no Muslim gains such rights either.
