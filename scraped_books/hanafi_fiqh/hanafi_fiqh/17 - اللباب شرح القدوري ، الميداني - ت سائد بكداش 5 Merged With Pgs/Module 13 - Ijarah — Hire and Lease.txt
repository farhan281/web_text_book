Disputes in Hire Contracts Before and After Service
Shifting rulings for hire disputes before and after performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedural rules for hire disputes by distinguishing between disputes before and after service, and identifying the correct oath and evidence procedures.

Core Idea
Before service, hire disputes use mutual oaths and rescission; after service, the hirer's word prevails.

Hire disputes are common in daily life. Hanafi fiqh provides clear procedures depending on whether the service has been performed. Before performance, the contract is treated like a sale — parties swear and annul. After performance, the hirer's statement is trusted. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and details.

Guided Path
1
Identify the timing of the dispute
Determine whether the dispute arises before or after the full service (manfa'ah) has been rendered. This is the key factor.
The ruling changes completely based on this.
2
Apply the before-service procedure
If before service: both parties take mutual oaths (tahalluf) and the contract is rescinded (taradd). This is because ijarah before performance is a commutative contract like sale.
The oath order depends on what is disputed: hirer swears first on rent; lessor swears first on duration.
3
Handle evidence if presented
If one party brings evidence (bayyinah), it is accepted: lessor's evidence on rent, hirer's evidence on duration.
Evidence overrides the oath procedure.
4
Apply the after-service procedure
If after full performance: no mutual oaths. The hirer's statement (al-qawl qawl al-musta'jir) is taken without oath, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).

Dispute in Ijarah Before Full Use: Mutual Oath and Recission
States ruling for dispute before full use and provides analogy to sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the procedure (mutual oath and recission) for resolving disputes in ijarah before full use and explain the analogy to sale.

Core Idea
Before full use, an ijarah dispute is resolved by mutual oaths and recission, just like a sale.

In the Hanafi school, ijarah (hire) is treated as a commutative contract similar to sale (bay'). This passage addresses what happens when the parties disagree about the rent (badal) or the term (mubdal) before the hirer has taken full benefit (istifa'). The ruling prescribes a specific procedure: mutual oath-taking (tahlif) followed by recission of the contract, with the order of oaths determined by who is claiming which variation. The proof is qiyas to sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The passage applies only when the dispute arises before the hirer has fully taken delivery of the contracted benefit (istifa'). Determine whether the parties are arguing about the rent (badal) or the subject of hire (mubdal).
If the dispute occurs after full use, different rules apply.
2
State the procedure: mutual oath and recission
The default ruling is that both parties take mutual oaths (tahaluf) and then the contract is rescinded (taradd). This nullifies the ijarah, and the parties return to their original positions.
3
Determine the order of oaths
The passage specifies that the hirer (musta'jir) begins the oath if the dispute is about the rent (badal). If the dispute is about the term (mubdal), the lessor (mu'jir) begins. This shows the school's nuance in allocating procedural burdens.
This order is based on who is more likely to know the contested element.
4
Consider evidence over oaths
If either party brings evidence (bayyinah), evidence takes precedence. For the rent, the lessor's evidence is accepted; for the term, the hirer's evidence. This is consistent with the rule that external proof outweighs personal oaths.
The passage cites 'al-Durr' as a reference for this rule.
5
Understand the analogy to sale
The author states the reasoning: ijarah is a commutative contract (aqd mu'awadah) that is subject to annulment (qabil lil-faskh), just like sale. Therefore, the same dispute resolution procedure applies. Identify the illah: the nature of the contract as a commutative exchange.
This qiyas is considered jali (clear) because the commonality is strong.

Dispute After Full Use in Ijarah: No Oath, Hirer's Statement
Ruling for dispute after full use, with Hanafi internal disagreement on reason

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on disputes after full use of the leased benefit and distinguish the reasoning of Abu Hanifah/Abu Yusuf from that of Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
After full use, no mutual oath; the hirer's statement is accepted, but the reason differs among the Hanafi imams.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a case where the hirer and lessor dispute after the leased benefit has been fully consumed. All three Hanafi masters agree on the outcome: no mutual oath, and the hirer's statement is taken with his oath. However, they differ on the underlying rationale. Understanding this nuance reveals how legal reasoning can converge on the same ruling even when based on different principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
Read the passage and note: after full use (istifa') of the leased subject, a dispute arises (e.g., over the rent amount). The ruling is that the parties do not take mutual oaths (la yatahalafan) and the hirer's statement is accepted with his oath.
The context is a completed ijarah where the benefit has perished.
2
State the common outcome
All three imams (Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) agree: no mutual oath. The practical result is the same.
This agreement is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical effect) being identical.
3
Analyze the reasoning of Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf
They hold that the destruction (halak) of the contracted subject prevents mutual oath. Since the benefit is gone, rescission would be meaningless, so mutual oath is barred.
Their principle is general: huluk al-ma'qud 'alayhi yamna' al-tahalluf.
4
Analyze the reasoning of Imam Muhammad
Muhammad distinguishes between sale and ijarah. In sale, even if the item perishes, mutual oath occurs because the item has a replacement value. In ijarah, benefits have no inherent value (la tataqawwam bi nafsiha); they only acquire value through the contract. If mutual oath occurred and the contract was rescinded, there would be no value to return, so mutual oath is impossible.
His principle: benefits are like usufruct, not commodities.
5
Compare and note the thamarah al-khilaf
Both reasonings lead to the same ruling, so the practical consequence is zero. However, understanding the different i'llat (effective causes) is important for applying the rule to analogous cases.
This is a pure ihtilaf fi al-ta'lil (disagreement in reasoning) without effect on the fatwa.

Dispute After Partial Use: Mutual Oath and Rescission of Remainder
Ruling for dispute after partial use, with analogy to sale and distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for a dispute after partial use in ijarah and explain why it differs from sale.

Core Idea
Ijarah is renewed moment-by-moment; partial use does not bar partial rescission.

In ijarah, when a dispute arises after the hirer has used only part of the term, the law provides a unique remedy: mutual oaths and rescission only for the remaining period. This rule stems from the nature of ijarah as a continuing contract. Let's explore the reasoning and how it contrasts with the sale of goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage covers a dispute after partial use (istifa' ba'd) of the leased benefit. The hirer has enjoyed part of the term, then a disagreement arises (e.g., about rent).
2
State the ruling
The parties take mutual oaths (tahāluf), and the contract is rescinded only for the future portion (al-bāqī). The past usage is settled by the hirer's statement (with oath).
Unanimous among Hanafis (ittiqāqan).
3
Trace the reasoning (ta'lil)
Because ijarah 'is created moment by moment' (yan'aqid sā'atan fasā'atan), each time unit is like a separate contract. Rescission applies independently to future units.
The illa is the temporal divisibility of the contract.
4
Contrast with sale
In sale, the contract is a single occurrence (daf'atan wāḥidatan). If a dispute arises after partial delivery, rescission is not partial because the contract is indivisible.
This distinction clarifies the fiqh of mu'āmalāt.
5
Apply the procedure
In practice: court orders mutual oaths; then rescinds the contract for remaining time; past use is governed by hirer's statement.
The hirer is the defendant for past benefit.

Mutual Oath-Taking in Hire Disputes After Partial Performance
Ruling with proof on tahalf in hire after partial performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when lessor and lessee dispute after partial receipt of the benefit, and explain the reasoning including the intra-Hanafi difference on the effect of destruction.

Core Idea
In hire, the contract is continuous; partial dispute leads to tahalf and rescission of the remainder, with the lessee's word for the past.

In hire contracts, disputes can arise after the lessee has already used part of the benefit. The Hanafi school provides a specific procedure: mutual oath-taking (tahalf) for the remaining period and acceptance of the lessee's word for the past. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the nuanced reasoning behind it, including an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the effect of destruction of the subject matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario is a dispute between the lessor (mu'jir) and lessee (musta'jir) after the lessee has already received part of the leased benefit. The exact past performance is disputed—for example, the amount of rent paid or the condition of the property.
The dispute is about the past, not the future.
2
State the ruling
The Hanafi ruling has three parts: (1) The parties perform mutual oath-taking (tahalf). (2) The contract is rescinded (faskh) for the remaining future period. (3) For the past period, the lessee's word (with his oath) is accepted.
The lessee is considered the defendant (mudda'a 'alayh) for the past, so his word prevails.
3
Identify the proof
The proof is the nature of the hire contract: it is concluded moment by moment (yan'aqidu sa'atan fasa'atan), unlike a sale which is a single unit. Therefore, partial possession does not affect the continuity of the contract for the remainder; tahalf and rescission apply only to the future part.
This reasoning is from al-Hidayah and is key to distinguishing hire from sale.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on destruction
Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ on whether destruction (halak) of the leased property prevents tahalf. The Sahibayn hold that destruction prevents it because benefits have no independent value; Abu Hanifa holds it does not, because the contract is renewed each moment. However, in the case without destruction, they all agree on the ruling stated.
This ikhtilaf is only regarding the effect of destruction; the present case has no destruction.
5
Understand why the lessee's word is taken for the past
For the past, the lessee is the defendant (the one claimed against), and the default rule is that the defendant's statement with oath is accepted. The lessor is the claimant, so he must produce evidence.
This is based on the general principle of evidence (al-bayyinah 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala man ankar).

This passage presents a ruling on a dispute between a lessor and lessee after partial receipt of the leased benefit. The Hanafi ruling is that they perform mutual oath-taking (tahalf), the contract is rescinded for the remaining period, and the lessee's word is taken for the past. The commentary explains the reasoning: destruction of the subject matter prevents tahalf according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), and this is also consistent with Muhammad's principle. However, Abu Hanifa holds that in sale, destruction does not prevent tahalf because the sold item has a value that can be substituted. In hire, benefits have no independent value; therefore the Sahibayn argue that if destruction occurs, tahalf is prevented. But the Hidayah quote confirms that in the case of a dispute without destruction, they unanimously agree on the ruling of tahalf and rescission of the remaining contract. The past is decided by the lessee's word because he is the defendant.

Key Terms
التحالف (al-tahalf): Mutual oath-taking by both parties in a dispute when each claims against the other.
المستأجر (al-musta'jir): The lessee, the party who hires the benefit.

Key Takeaway
In hire disputes after partial performance, Hanafi law mandates mutual oath-taking, rescission for the future, and acceptance of the lessee's word for the past.
