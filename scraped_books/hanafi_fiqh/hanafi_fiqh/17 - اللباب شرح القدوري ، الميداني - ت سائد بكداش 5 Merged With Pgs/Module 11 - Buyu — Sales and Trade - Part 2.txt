Ruling on Hoarding and Its Exceptions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of hoarding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hoarding and apply the exceptions for one's own produce and imports, and identify the three positions of the Hanafi imams.

Core Idea
Hoarding is only disliked when it harms; one's own produce is never hoarding in the relied-upon view.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on ihtikar (hoarding), focusing on when it becomes disliked and the exceptions. The passage from al-Lubab presents the general rule, then introduces a key intra-madhhab disagreement among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding the treatment of one's own agricultural produce and imported goods. Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the correct fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its condition
The passage states that hoarding staple foods is makruh (disliked) if it harms the people of that town. If no harm, it is not disliked.
The condition of harm is decisive.
2
Locate the proof
The hadith 'al-jalibu marzuq, wa al-muhtakiru mal'un' (the importer is blessed, the hoarder is cursed) provides the basis for the ruling, though its chain is weak.
The curse indicates blameworthiness.
3
Understand the first exception
Hoarding the produce of one's own estate is not considered hoarding because it is one's exclusive property; the public has no right to force a sale.
Analogous to the right not to cultivate.
4
Analyze the second exception and the intra-Hanafi dispute
Hoarding goods brought from another country: Abu Hanifa says not hoarding; Abu Yusuf says makruh due to the general hadith; Muhammad says haram if it is commonly brought to the city.
The disagreement centers on the attachment of public right.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's, as the verifying imams follow him. Thus, hoarding one's own produce or imports is not disliked.
This is the operative fatwa.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Hoarding One's Own Produce and Imported Goods
Intra-Hanafi ruling on hoarding personal produce and imports

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on ihtikar and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Hoarding your own produce is not ihtikar; imported goods before entering the city are also exempt per Abu Hanifa, the relied-upon view.

In this passage from al-Labab fi Sharh al-Quduri, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute on the definition of ihtikar (hoarding). The author distinguishes between two scenarios: hoarding one's own estate produce and hoarding goods brought from another city. While Abu Hanifa considers both permissible, his two disciples differ, particularly on imported goods. Understanding this ikhtilaf and its resolution is essential for correctly applying the prohibition of ihtikar in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage discusses two distinct situations: (1) hoarding the produce of one's own estate, and (2) hoarding goods brought from another city. Note which case is unanimous and which is disputed.
Case 1 is agreed upon; case 2 has ikhtilaf.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds that hoarding one's own produce is not ihtikar because it is the owner's exclusive property; he may even choose not to cultivate. For imported goods, the public's right attaches only after goods enter the city, so before that the owner is free to withhold sale.
Proof is aqli (rational): right of public is limited to the city and its environs.
3
State the Sahibayn's positions and proofs
Abu Yusuf considers it makruh based on a general narration (itlaq ma rawayna). Muhammad considers it haram because such goods typically end up in the city, thus they are like the city's surroundings where hoarding is prohibited.
Both rely on different forms of evidence: Abu Yusuf on general textual evidence, Muhammad on logical extension.
4
Analyze the tarjih (preference)
The text states that the 'verifying imams' followed Abu Hanifa's view. This indicates that Abu Hanifa's position is the mufta bihi. The rationale is that the public's right is specifically tied to goods physically present in the city, not to potential future arrival.
Tarjih authority: al-Quduri and the verifying scholars.
5
State the mufta bihi
The relied-upon ruling is that hoarding one's own produce and imported goods before they enter the city is not considered ihtikar, hence legally permissible (mubah).
This is the operative position for fatwa.

Ruling on Price Fixing by the Ruler (Tas'ir)
Ruling on price fixing with exception for public harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling on price fixing and identify the exceptional condition under which it becomes permissible.

Core Idea
Price fixing is generally prohibited because the price belongs to the parties, but it is permitted when merchants cause public harm by excessive overcharging.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on tas'ir (price fixing) by the ruler. The text presents a clear principle: the price is a contractual right, so the ruler should not interfere. However, an exception is made for cases of public harm, where consultation is required. We will analyse the rational proof behind this ruling and understand its scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
State the default position on price fixing. The text says 'لا ينبغي للسلطان أن يسعر على الناس' – it is not proper for the ruler to fix prices.
The ruling is makruh or not recommended, not haram.
2
Identify the proof
The text provides the reason: 'لأن الثمن حق العاقد' – because the price is the right of the contracting parties. This is a rational proof (dalil aqli).
The proof is based on the principle of contractual freedom.
3
Identify the exception
Find the condition under which the ruling changes: 'إلا إذا تعلق به ضرر العامة' – unless harm to the general public is involved. Specifically, when merchants are tyrannical and exceed the fair price excessively.
The exception is narrow: only when there is gross overcharging causing public harm.
4
Note the procedural requirement
The text adds that fixing prices in such a case should be done 'بمشورة أهل الرأي والبصيرة' – after consultation with people of opinion and insight.
This ensures the decision is informed and just.
5
Determine the authority weight
This ruling is consistent with the Zahir al-Riwayah and is cited in Al-Hidayah. It represents the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school.
Cross-madhhab: other schools like the Maliki may permit price fixing more broadly.

Ruling on selling permissible juice to one known to make wine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling lawful juice to someone who will use it for wine, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
Selling grape juice to a winemaker is permissible because the sin is on the buyer, not the seller.

You might think it is forbidden to help someone commit a sin by selling them the raw material. Yet the Hanafi school, based on Abu Hanifa's reasoning, permits it. This lesson clarifies the ruling and the internal debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling grape juice (asir) to a person known to make wine. The ruling is 'la ba'sa' — no harm, i.e., permissible (mubah).
The Arabic phrase 'لا بأس' in classical fiqh texts indicates permissibility.
2
Understand the reasoning
The juice itself is lawful (halal). The sin of turning it into wine and drinking it belongs to the buyer alone. The seller is not responsible for the buyer's intention or action.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that permissibility of a transaction depends on the commodity's inherent lawfulness, not its potential misuse.
3
Note the ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school
Imam Abu Hanifa holds it permissible. His two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, consider it makruh (disliked) because the seller is assisting in sin.
The difference is based on whether focusing on the object (ayn) or the action (fi'l) of selling.
4
Identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon position)
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the permissibility view of Abu Hanifa. This is the majority view in the later Hanafi works.
Even though some later scholars preferred the companions' view for piety, the official fatwa is permissibility.

Selling Weapons During Fitnah: Blocking Means to Sin
Ruling on selling weapons during fitnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling weapons during fitnah and identify the condition that triggers the karahah, citing the principle of sadd al-dhara'i'.

Core Idea
Selling weapons to fitnah participants is makruh tahrimi because it facilitates sin.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school on commercial transactions during times of civil strife. The text provides a conditional ruling that balances the general permissibility of trade with the ethical obligation not to facilitate wrongdoing. Understanding this ruling requires attention to both the act and the context in which it occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act under consideration is selling weapons (bay' al-silah). In general, selling weapons is permissible (halal) like any other commodity. However, the text introduces an exception during fitnah.
Fitnah here means armed conflict among Muslims, not general chaos.
2
Determine the condition that changes the ruling
The ruling becomes makruh (disliked) when the sale occurs 'during days of fitnah' and specifically to a buyer 'who is known to be from the people of fitnah.' The condition is twofold: temporal and personal.
If the buyer is not known to be a participant, the sale remains permissible.
3
Analyze the legal reason (talil) provided
The text states: 'because it is a means to sin' (tasabbub ila al-ma'siyah). The sale facilitates the buyer's sinful act of fighting unlawfully. This transforms the ruling from permissible to makruh tahrimi.
The Hanafi principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means) is applied here.
4
Connect to broader Hanafi legal theory
This ruling exemplifies how Hanafi fiqh uses the concept of 'sabab' (means) to regulate actions that are otherwise permissible. The ruling is not based on the intrinsic nature of the sale but on its likely consequence.
Compare with other applications, e.g., selling grapes to a winemaker.

Selling Grape Juice to Non-Muslims: The Direct vs Indirect Sin Distinction
Ruling with proof: permissibility with comparative distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling grape juice to non-Muslims and explain the legal distinction (tafrīq) between selling grape juice and selling weapons during fitna.

Core Idea
Sin attaches to the substance only when direct; grape juice becomes wine only after transformation, so its sale is permissible.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling: selling grape juice to Magians and Dhimmis is permissible even though they may use it to make wine. The key lies in the principle that the prohibition of assisting sin (i‘ānat ʿalā al-maʿṣiya) attaches only when the sin inheres in the object itself. The author of al-Hidayah contrasts this with selling weapons during civil strife, where the sin is immediate and direct. Understanding this distinction helps us apply the maxim that means to sin are only prohibited when the sin is inevitable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling grape juice (عصير) to Magians and Dhimmis. The ruling is that it is permissible (لا بأس) — there is no harm in it.

2

The proof is an implicit qiyas (analogy) based on a distinction (farq) between two cases. The text contrasts selling grape juice with selling weapons during fitna.
The proof is not a direct text but a rational argument supported by the established ruling on weapons.

The 'illa (effective cause) for permissibility is that the act of disobedience (ma'siyah) does not occur in the substance of the grape juice itself (بعينه). It only occurs after the juice transforms into wine. Therefore, the prohibition does not attach to the juice.

5

Some scholars may prohibit such sales due to the likelihood of misuse, but the Hanafi school relies on the distinction that sin is not inherent in the juice. The mufta bihi (operative position) is permissibility.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is justified by the 'illa of direct vs. indirect sin.
