Implicit Oaths When the Preposition is Omitted
Ruling on implicit oath when preposition omitted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on implicit oaths when the preposition is omitted and explain the basis in Arab custom.

Core Idea
Omitting 'bi' in an oath does not invalidate it — Arab custom treats it as a valid oath.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies a key point in the Book of Oaths: when a person says 'Allah, I will not do such-and-such' without the preposition 'bi', it still counts as a valid oath in the Hanafi school. The ruling is grounded in the Arab practice of omitting the preposition for brevity, and the school honors that customary usage. Understanding this helps us correctly identify binding oaths in everyday speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is saying 'Allah, I will not do such-and-such' without the preposition 'bi'. The ruling is that this constitutes a valid oath (yamin).
The ruling is not about the entire book of oaths, only this specific form.
2
Recognize the grammatical basis
There is a grammatical debate: some say the noun is in the accusative because the preposition is removed, others say it remains in the genitive with a hidden kasra.
This grammatical discussion shows that scholars are aware of the omission but still consider it an oath.
3
Understand the customary basis
The validity rests on Arab custom ('urf) of omitting the preposition for brevity. The Hanafi school relies on this custom to uphold the oath.
Custom is a recognized source of law in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If someone says 'Allah, I will not travel tomorrow', this is a valid oath. Breaking it requires kaffarah (expiation).
The same principle applies to any similar utterance following Arab custom.

Is 'By the Right of Allah' a Valid Oath?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on whether 'wa haqq Allah' constitutes an oath and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding its interpretation.

Core Idea
'Haqq Allah' is obedience, not an attribute; thus it does not form a valid oath.

When a person intending to swear says 'By the right of Allah', does it count as a binding oath? The Hanafi school has a difference of opinion on this matter. This lesson examines the views of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Abu Yusuf, and identifies the relied-upon position that determines whether expiation (kaffarah) is required upon breaking such an utterance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and the legal context
The phrase in question is 'wa haqq Allah' uttered with the intent of swearing. Determine whether it meets the Hanafi definition of a valid oath (yamin).
Hanafi oaths require swearing by Allah or His names/attributes.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They hold that it is NOT an oath because 'haqq' here means obedience to Allah (acts of worship). Swearing by something other than Allah (i.e., by obedience) is not valid.
This is the majority view and later relied upon.
3
Identify the opposing narration from Abu Yusuf
One narration from Abu Yusuf holds that it IS an oath because 'haqq' is an attribute of Allah, and swearing by attributes is akin to swearing by Allah. He also cites customary usage.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the Hanafi counter-reasoning
The majority responds that in this context 'haqq' is primarily understood as obedience, not as a direct attribute. Thus it remains swearing by other than Allah, which is not recognized.
The linguistic analysis distinguishes the usage.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
Al-Isbijabi in Tashih declares Abu Hanifa's view correct, and the later imams follow it. Therefore, if one says 'wa haqq Allah' and breaks it, no kaffarah (expiation) is required.
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether kaffarah is obligatory or not.

Explicit Words That Constitute an Oath
Listing explicit phrases that legally constitute an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the explicit verbal and nominal phrases that form a valid oath in Hanafi law, and cite the Quranic and hadith evidence for those based on covenant and vow.

Core Idea
Uttering 'I swear,' 'covenant of Allah,' or an unspecified vow creates a binding oath requiring expiation if broken.

You may think only 'wallahi' counts as an oath. The Hanafi school teaches that many Arabic phrases — both verbal and nominal — constitute a legal oath when uttered with intent. This lesson presents the explicit forms as compiled by Imam al-Quduri and expanded by the authors of al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit verbal forms
The text lists 'uqsmu', 'ahlifu', 'ashhadu', 'a'zimu', and their variants with 'bi-llah'. Note that these are present tense verbs used for swearing.
These forms are explicit (sarih) and do not require a context to be understood as oaths.
2
Understand the linguistic basis
The present tense form is literally for the moment of speaking. Although it can indicate future, here it establishes immediate oath status. The 'Hidayah' commentary explains this.
The speaker is considered an oath-taker at the moment of utterance, even if the act referenced is future.
3
Recognize nominal phrases as oaths
'Ahd Allah' (covenant of Allah), 'mithaquhu' (His pledge), and 'nazr' (vow) are also valid oath phrases.
These nominal phrases do not contain a verb but are still legally binding.
4
Connect the nominal phrases to their proofs
'Ahd Allah' is supported by Quran 16:91, and unspecified 'nazr' is supported by a hadith equating it to an oath requiring kaffarah.
The proofs are textual (naqli), strengthening the authority of these phrases.

Two Types of Witness Bearing: Direct and on Testimony
Defining two types of witness bearing and their validity conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct testimony (shahada 'ala al-mushahada) and testimony on testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada), and state the Hanafi condition for each.

Core Idea
Direct testimony is valid without formal appointment; testimony on testimony requires explicit delegation from the original witness.

In Hanafi law, the validity of a witness's testimony depends on how he acquired his knowledge. This lesson examines the two fundamental types of bearing testimony (tahammul al-shahada) as outlined in al-Quduri's matn and its commentary al-Lubab. You will learn when a witness may testify directly and when he must be formally appointed by the primary witness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage to find the two types of witness bearing: what is established by itself (direct perception) and what is not (testimony on testimony). Note the examples given for each.
Direct perception includes hearing a sale or confession, seeing usurpation or homicide.
2
Understand the ruling for direct testimony
For direct testimony, the witness may testify even without being formally asked to bear witness (istishhad). The witness says 'I testify that he sold' rather than 'He made me testify.'
The witness has direct knowledge, which suffices for performance (ada').
3
Apply the condition for testimony on testimony
For testimony on testimony, the second witness cannot testify unless the first witness formally appoints him (yushhiduhu) and commands him to convey. This delegation (inaba) is essential; without it the testimony is invalid.
The second witness acts as an agent (wakil) of the first.
4
Analyze the exception about hearing from behind a curtain
The passage from al-Hidayah states that hearing from behind a curtain is generally insufficient for testimony because certainty of the speaker's identity is required. However, if the listener enters the house, confirms no one else is present, and hears from the only door, then knowledge is certain and testimony is valid.
This illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on certainty (yaqin) in testimony.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the two types of bearing testimony (tahammul al-shahada). The author explains that witness bearing is divided into two categories. The first category is what is established by itself, meaning the ruling of the testimony is based on the witness's direct perception. Examples include witnessing a sale (by hearing the offer and acceptance), a confession (by hearing), usurpation (by seeing), homicide (by seeing), and the judge's ruling (by hearing). When a witness directly hears or sees such an event, he may testify about it even if he was not formally requested to bear witness (istishhad). He should phrase his testimony as 'I testify that he sold' and not 'He made me testify' because the latter would be false if he was not actually appointed as a witness.

The second category is what is not established by itself, the prime example being testimony on testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada). This type requires a chain of transmission: the original witness testifies, and a second witness bears witness to that testimony. The second witness cannot testify unless the first witness formally appoints him (yushhiduhu) and commands him to convey the testimony. Without this delegation (inaba), the second witness's testimony is invalid because it lacks the necessary authorization, just as an agent requires authorization from the principal.

The passage also includes a ruling from al-Hidayah about hearing from behind a curtain: if a person hears a statement from behind a curtain without seeing the speaker, he cannot testify to it because he cannot identify the speaker with certainty. However, if he enters the house, confirms no one else is present, sits at the only door, and hears a confession from inside, then he may testify because knowledge of the speaker is certain. This illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on certainty in testimony.

إنابة (inaba): Delegation or authorization from the first witness to the second to convey testimony.

A hadith-based encouragement to study inheritance, followed by a chain critique

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school emphasizes the study of inheritance shares and evaluate the authenticity of the hadith cited for that purpose.

Core Idea
Learning inheritance law is a communal duty because disputes over fara'id will arise, and knowledgeable judges will be needed.

Kitab al-Fara'id opens with a powerful hadith urging Muslims to learn and teach the science of fixed inheritance shares. The author, al-Maydani, underscores the importance of this field by noting that it is a noble science due to people's constant need for it. However, he also provides a critical examination of the hadith's chain, demonstrating that despite its popularity, it is not without weakness. This lesson explores the hadith and the scholarly discussion around it.

Commentary
Passage type: Front matter / biographical discussion on the virtue of learning fara'id. The author opens Kitab al-Fara'id with a hadith encouraging the study and teaching of inheritance law. He explains that fara'id is a noble science because people are in constant need of it. The hadith predicts a time when knowledge will be taken away and disputes over inheritance will arise with no one to resolve them. The author then provides an extensive critical discussion of the hadith's chain, citing multiple scholars who assessed its authenticity. He notes that al-Hakim declared it sahih, but al-Haythami and others pointed out weaknesses and idtirab (confusion) in the text. Ibn Hajar also considered the narrators trustworthy but the hadith mudtarib. Importantly, the author notes that despite being attributed to the Musnad of Imam Ahmad, it is not actually found there. He then mentions that Ibn Taymiyyah (al-Jadd) traced it to a different source, suggesting the attribution to the Musnad may be an error. This discussion is typical of Hanafi works that begin a new book with a hadith to establish the importance of the subject.

Key Terms
الفرائض (al-farāʾiḍ): The science of fixed inheritance shares as determined by Quran and Sunnah.
مضطرب (muḍṭarib): A hadith that is narrated with inconsistencies in its chain or text, making it weak.
اضطراب (iḍṭirāb): The state of being mudtarib; confusion or inconsistency in a hadith.
