Preference Rules Among Dhawi al-Arham: Equal Degree and Proximity
Explains the two priority rules among Dhawi al-Arham when degrees are equal or different.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two Hanafi priority rules for Dhawi al-Arham: tracing through a legal heir when degrees are equal, and proximity when degrees differ.

Core Idea
When degrees are equal, tracing through a legal heir wins; when degrees differ, nearness wins even over tracing through a legal heir.

In Hanafi inheritance, when only Dhawi al-Arham survive, specific rules determine who inherits. This lesson covers two key criteria: tracing lineage through a legal heir (al-idla' bi al-warith) and proximity in degree (al-qurb). Understanding these allows you to correctly distribute among distant relatives.

Guided Path
1
Assess the Degrees
Determine whether the competing Dhawi al-Arham are equal in degree (same number of steps from the deceased) or different. For example, a daughter of a daughter's daughter and a daughter of a son's daughter are equal in degree (both are two generations down).
Degree is counted by the number of generations between the relative and the deceased.
2
Apply the Equal Degree Rule
If equal in degree, the one who traces lineage through a legal heir (idla' bi warith) is preferred. A legal heir is anyone who would have inherited if alive. The example: daughter of son's daughter vs. daughter of daughter's daughter. The son's daughter traces through the son (a legal heir), so she wins.
This rule is based on the principle that tracing through a stronger link confers a stronger claim.
3
Apply the Different Degree Rule
If the degrees differ, the nearer relative (shorter distance) is preferred, even if they trace through a non-heir, over a more distant relative who traces through a legal heir. Example: daughter of paternal aunt (nearer) vs. daughter of cousin (more distant). The daughter of paternal aunt takes everything.
Proximity in blood is given priority over the strength of the link.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab elaborates on priority rules among Dhawi al-Arham. First, when two relatives are equal in degree (e.g., both are grandchildren), the one who traces their lineage through a legal heir (i.e., someone who would have been an heir if alive) is preferred. For example, if there is a daughter of a daughter's daughter (through a female non-heir) and a daughter of a son's daughter (through a male legal heir), the latter takes the entire estate because the son is a legal heir. Second, if they differ in proximity (closeness to the deceased), the nearer relative is preferred even if they trace through a non-heir, while the more distant relative traces through a legal heir. Example: a daughter of a paternal aunt (nearer) vs. a daughter of a cousin (more distant but through a male legal heir). The nearer (daughter of paternal aunt) gets everything. Third, the passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that the maternal grandfather (even high) is preferred over children of a brother or sister (nephews/nieces), by analogy with residuaries (where closer asabah take precedence). However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that children of brother/sister (i.e., nephews/nieces) are preferred over the maternal grandfather. The text attributes this report to al-Zahidi and al-Isbijahabi. The footnote notes a variant in some copies of al-Quduri: 'if two legal heirs are equal in degree' instead of 'children of a father', which would change the scope.

Key Terms
الإدلاء بالوارث (al-idla' bi al-warith): Tracing one's lineage to the deceased through a person who would have been a legal heir if alive.
العصبات (al-Asabat): Residuary heirs; here, analogy is drawn to their priority order.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Maternal Grandfather vs. Children of Siblings
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on priority between maternal grandfather and children of siblings among Dhawi al-Arham.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding priority between the maternal grandfather and children of siblings among Dhawi al-Arham, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prefers the maternal grandfather; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad prefer children of siblings.

This lesson examines a well-known intra-Hanafi dispute in inheritance law. When only Dhawi al-Arham survive, the maternal grandfather and the children of a brother or sister (nephews/nieces) are potential heirs. Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree on who takes precedence. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to trace legal reasoning in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Case
The case is when the deceased leaves no Quranic heirs or residuaries, only Dhawi al-Arham, including the maternal grandfather (father of the mother) and children of a brother or sister (nephews/nieces). The question: who inherits?
This dispute occurs in the category of Dhawi al-Arham, which only inherit when no other heirs exist.
2
Abu Hanifa's Position and Dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that the maternal grandfather is preferred over children of siblings. His evidence: analogy (qiyas) with residuaries (asabah). In residuaries, the nearer in lineage (grandfather) is preferred over more distant relatives (nephews/nieces). Similarly, here the maternal grandfather is nearer to the deceased through the mother than a nephew/niece is through a sibling.
The maternal grandfather is two steps away (deceased -> mother -> maternal grandfather) while a nephew/niece is three steps away (deceased -> sibling -> nephew/niece).
3
Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that children of siblings (nephews/nieces) are preferred over the maternal grandfather. Their reasoning is not explicitly given in the text, but presumably because children of siblings are from the same paternal side, while the maternal grandfather is from the mother's side, and paternal relatives are generally given priority.
The text only states that they differ, not their proof.
4
Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The difference affects distribution of the estate among Dhawi al-Arham. According to Abu Hanifa, the maternal grandfather inherits everything; according to the Sahibayn, the children of siblings inherit everything.
No compromise or sharing; the winner takes all.

Priority Rules Among Uterine Relatives and an Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Establishes rules of priority and a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two Hanafi rules of priority among uterine relatives and explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the mother's father.

Core Idea
Among uterine relatives, proximity beats lineage — but when equal, descent through an heir wins.

The Hanafi school recognizes uterine relatives (dhawi al-arham) as inheritors in the absence of Quranic heirs and agnates. This passage from Al-Lubab establishes two key rules: when two uterine relatives are at the same generational level, priority goes to the one who connects to the deceased through a Quranic heir; when they differ in proximity, the closer relative wins even if they connect through a non-heir. Additionally, a well-known intra-Hanafi dispute arises: who is more deserving between the mother's father and the child of a brother or sister?

Guided Path
1
Identify the two bases of priority
Determine whether the uterine relatives are equal in degree (same generational level) or differ in proximity. The rule changes accordingly: for equal degree, look at descent through a warith; for different proximity, look at closeness regardless of descent.
These two rules are distinct and must be applied in the correct order.
2
Apply the 'idla' through a warith' rule
When uterine relatives are equal in degree, prefer the one who descends (idla') through a Quranic heir. Example: daughter of a daughter's daughter vs daughter of a son's daughter. The latter descends through a son's daughter (a warith) and takes all.
The strength of lineage is measured by passing through a warith, not through a non-heir.
3
Apply the 'proximity' rule
When uterine relatives differ in proximity (generational distance), the closer relative is preferred, even if the closer one descends through a non-heir. Example: daughter of a paternal aunt (closer) vs daughter of a son of a paternal uncle (more distant). The daughter of the aunt takes all.
Proximity overrides the strength of lineage from idla'.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Consider the case of a mother's father (abu al-umm) and a child of a brother or sister (e.g., nephew). Abu Hanifa holds that the mother's father is more deserving because he is considered akin to an agnate. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the child of the brother/sister is more deserving. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: different allocation of the entire surplus.
The text notes that al-Zahidi and al-Isbijabi report Abu Hanifa's view as the one stated, but later authorities may prefer the Sahibayn's view.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Dhawi al-Arham (uterine relatives who are not Quranic heirs or agnates) explains the rules of preference among them. The author first states a general rule: When uterine relatives are equal in degree (i.e., the same generational level), priority is given to the one who descends through a Quranic heir (warith), because such descent makes the connection stronger. The example given is between the daughter of a daughter's daughter (descended through a female non-heir) and the daughter of a son's daughter (descended through a male heir). The latter inherits all the wealth because her lineage passes through a Quranic heir (the son's daughter). Then, when there is a difference in proximity (generational closeness), the closer relative is preferred over a more distant one, even if the closer one descends through a non-heir. For instance, the daughter of a paternal aunt (closer) takes precedence over the daughter of a son of a paternal uncle (more distant), even though the latter descends through a male agnate. Finally, the passage addresses a dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that the mother's father (maternal grandfather) is preferred over the child of a brother or sister (nephew/niece), because the maternal grandfather is considered like an agnate (asabah). However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the child of a brother or sister is given priority. This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf, and we will later see the tarjih.

Key Terms
ذوي الأرحام (dhawi al-arham): Uterine relatives: those who are not Quranic heirs (ashab al-furud) nor agnates (asabat), such as maternal uncles, maternal aunts, daughters of cousins, etc.
إدلاء (idla'): The act of descending (or connecting) to the deceased through an intermediary relative; e.g., a grandchild 'idla' through their parent.
أبو الأم (abu al-umm): The mother's father (maternal grandfather). In this context, he is considered a uterine relative who may inherit as an 'asbah according to Abu Hanifa.

Inheritance of Manumitter and Mawla al-Muwalat Over Uterine Relatives
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheritance of manumitter's estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the manumitter takes precedence over uterine relatives for surplus inheritance, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the father and son of the mawla, identifying the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
In wala' al-'itaqah, the closer agnate (son) excludes the father, because clientage is ta'sib, not normal inheritance.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains the inheritance rights of the manumitter (mu'tiq) and the client by agreement (mawla al-muwalat). It affirms that the manumitter, like an agnate, takes any surplus after Quranic heirs, even over uterine relatives. Then it delves into a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: if the manumitter dies leaving both the father and the son of his freed slave, who inherits? The answer hinges on whether wala' is treated like ta'sib or like ordinary inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling about the manumitter
Read the first part: 'والمعتق أحق بالفاضل عن سهم ذوي السهام إذا لم تكن عَصَبَةٌ سواه'. Note that the manumitter is treated like an 'asbah (agnate) and takes the surplus after fixed shares, and he is preferred over uterine relatives (dhawi al-arham).
2
Understand mawla al-muwalat
The client by agreement (mola al-muwalat) inherits from the one with whom he contracted clientage, provided there is no other heir. This is a secondary rule.
3
State the specific case and the disagreement
The case: the manumitter dies leaving the father and son of his freed slave. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the son takes all (because wala' is ta'sib, and the son is closer than the father). Abu Yusuf says the father gets 1/6 and the son gets the rest (analogizing to normal inheritance where a father gets a share when there is a son).
Note: Abu Yusuf's initial individual view differed, but later he agreed with Abu Hanifa's proof.
4
Analyze the proof of each side
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad base their view on the principle 'ولاء العتاقة تعصيب' (clientage of manumission is ta'sib), meaning the rules of agnatic succession apply: the nearest agnate excludes the more distant. Abu Yusuf uses analogy (qiyas) with ordinary inheritance (الإرث) where a father gets a share.
The text says 'ورجحا دليل أبي حنيفة' meaning Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) later preferred the proof of Abu Hanifa. Thus they all agreed on the son taking all.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Al-Isbijabi states 'الصحيح قولهما', meaning the correct view is that of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), i.e., the son takes all. This is the relied-upon position. Therefore, in practice, the father of the mawla gets nothing.
This shows that within the school, a difference can be resolved by later scholars preferring the majority proof.

Awl of the Base Six: Increasing to Seven, Eight, Nine, Ten
Explains the 'awl of base six for combinations of half, third, and sixth shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the 'awl of base six for combinations of half, third, and sixth shares, reducing each heir's share proportionally.

Core Idea
When prescribed shares exceed the base, the base increases to the sum of numerators, reducing all shares proportionally.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains how to handle inheritance calculations where the total prescribed shares exceed the denominator of 6. This principle, called 'awl, ensures that the estate is distributed fairly among all heirs. Through concrete examples, you will learn to increase the base to 7, 8, 9, or 10, depending on the combination of heirs.

1
List the heirs and their prescribed shares
Identify each heir's fractional share from the Quranic categories (e.g., husband gets 1/2, two paternal sisters get 2/3, etc.).

If a share is 1/2, convert to 3/6; 1/3 becomes 2/6; 1/6 remains 1/6.
3
Convert shares to sixths and sum numerators
Express each share as a fraction with denominator 6, then add the numerators.

The new base is the sum of numerators.
5
Verify the total
Check that the new shares (each converted to the new base) add up to the new base number.
For example, 3/7 + 4/7 = 7/7, so the distribution is correct.

Correcting Inheritance Shares: The Rule of Muwafaqah
Explains the rule of muwafaqah between shares and heirs with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of muwafaqah to correct inheritance distributions when the shares of a group of heirs and their number share a common factor.

Core Idea
When shares agree with heads, multiply the factor (wafq) of the heads, not the full number.

This passage introduces a critical technique in Hanafi inheritance calculations: the rule of muwafaqah. When the shares of a group of heirs are not divisible among them but share a common factor with their number, you multiply the factor (wafq) of the heads by the base number. This lesson walks through three examples to illustrate the rule in action, including cases with and without 'awl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their shares
List all heirs and note the fractional share for each group (e.g., woman: 1/4, brothers: residue). Determine the base number (asl al-mas'alah) as the LCM of denominators.
If the estate is 'ailing ('awl), the base increases.
2
Check divisibility and find agreement
For each group, check if their share amount is divisible by the number of heads. If not, find the greatest common divisor (GCD) between the share amount and the number of heads. If GCD > 1, there is muwafaqah; if GCD = 1, there is mubayanah.
The factor (wafq) of heads = number of heads ÷ GCD.
3
Multiply by the correct number
If muwafaqah, multiply the base (or base with 'awl) by the wafq of the heads. If mubayanah, multiply by the full number of heads.
The product is the corrected base (tashih).
4
Recalculate shares from the new base
Multiply each group's original share amount by the multiplier used (wafq or full number). Divide among individuals if needed.
The corrected base ensures all shares become whole numbers.

Correcting Inheritance Shares with Tawafuq: Wife and Six Brothers
Exemplifies tawafuq correction in faraid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tawafuq rule to correct an inheritance distribution when a group of heirs' shares do not divide evenly among their heads.

Core Idea
When shares of a group do not divide evenly by their heads, use the concordant part (wafq) to scale the base.

In faraid, after assigning shares, you may find that the shares allocated to a group of heirs (e.g., brothers) cannot be split evenly among them. The Hanafi method corrects this by finding a common divisor between the share total and the headcount, then multiplying the original base by the concordant part (wafq). This lesson walks through the classic case of a wife and six brothers.

Guided Path
1
Identify heirs and initial shares
List the heirs: wife (1/4 fard), six full brothers (asabah for residue). Original base is 4. Wife gets 1, brothers get 3.
Residue is 3/4, divided among brothers equally in principle.
2
Check divisibility
The brothers' share (3) must be divided among 6 heads. Since 3 does not divide 6 evenly, we need tashih (correction).
If it divided evenly, no correction needed (tadakhul).
3
Find the greatest common divisor
Between the share total (3) and number of heads (6), the GCD is 3. This indicates tawafuq (agreement).
If GCD were 1, it would be tabayun (no agreement).
4
Compute the concordant part (wafq)
Divide the heads by the GCD: 6 ÷ 3 = 2. This is the wafq.
The wafq is the reduced headcount used for multiplication.
5
Apply correction to the base
Multiply the original base (4) by the wafq (2) to get the new base (8). Distribute: wife = 1×2 = 2, brothers = 3×2 = 6, each brother = 1.
Now each share is an integer.

General method for tabayun in tashih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the corrected inheritance distribution when the headcounts of two or more heir groups are mutually prime (tabayun).

Core Idea
When shares don't divide and headcounts are tabayun, multiply the headcounts together to get the juz' al-sahm.

In inheritance calculations, after determining the base number and assigning shares, we often face a problem: the shares do not evenly divide among the heads of the heirs. The classical Hanafi scholars developed a systematic method called tashih (correction). This lesson explains one of the four possible scenarios: tabayun, where the numbers are mutually prime. You will learn the general rule and apply it to a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heir groups and their shares
List each group with number of heads and the fractional share they receive. Note whether the shares divide evenly on the heads.

2
Determine the relationship between the headcounts
Compare the number of heads in each group. In this lesson, they are tabayun (no common divisor other than 1).
3 and 2 are mutually prime.
3
Apply the tabayun rule
Multiply the headcounts of the first two groups, then multiply the product by the headcount of the third group if present. The result is juz' al-sahm (correction factor).

4
Correct the base and shares
Multiply the original base (asl al-masalah) by juz' al-sahm to get the corrected base. Then multiply each group's share portion by juz' al-sahm.
Corrected base = base × juz' al-sahm.
5
Distribute among individuals
Divide each group's total corrected shares by the number of heads in that group to get each individual's share.
Final individual shares must be whole numbers.

Core Idea
When shares are indivisible, multiply heads based on their ratio: coprime → both; equal → one; nested → larger.

This passage explains the first three mathematical relationships between head counts in inheritance correction. Understanding these ratios is essential to avoid fractional shares when distributing estates. The author presents the general rule, then illustrates each case with examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify groups with indivisible shares
Find the groups of heirs whose allocated shares (from the original base) do not divide evenly among the number of heads in that group. For example, 1 share cannot be split among 3 wives.
The shares are fractions of the original base (aṣl al-mas'alah).
2
Compare the head counts of two groups
Take the number of heads in each group and determine their mathematical relationship: are they coprime (no common factor), equal, or nested (one is a divisor of the other)?
The fourth type (common factor) is covered later.
3
Apply the corresponding multiplication rule
For coprime: multiply the two head counts. For equal: use one of the head counts (since they are the same). For nested: use the larger head count (the smaller is already a part of it).

4

Take the juz' al-sahm obtained from the previous step and multiply it by the original base (aṣl al-mas'alah). The product is the corrected base (al-ṣaḥīḥ).
The corrected base ensures all shares become whole numbers.
5
Verify the distribution
Distribute the shares from the corrected base to each heir. Check that each group's total share is divisible by its number of heads without remainder.
If not, re-check the ratio classification.

This passage is a ruling statement detailing the first three of four possible relationships between the number of heads (ra's) in groups of heirs and their allocated shares when correcting (tashih) a faraid problem. The text explains the mathematical method for finding the corrected base number (al- asl) when shares do not divide evenly among heirs.

The author first states the general principle: if shares from a group do not divide among their heads, multiply the head counts of the groups sequentially, then multiply the result by the original base number. The heads are of four types: mutabayinan (coprime), mutamathilan (equal), mutadakhilan (one is a part of the other), and mutawafiqan (sharing a common factor). This window covers the first three.

1. Mutabayinan (Coprime): When the heads of two groups share no common factor other than 1, multiply the number of heads of one group by the other, then by any further groups, and finally by the original base. Example: three wives (3 heads) and two brothers (2 heads). Original base = 4 (from wives' 1/4 share). Wives have 1 share, not divisible by 3; brothers have 3 shares, not divisible by 2. Since 3 and 2 are coprime, multiply 2 × 3 = 6, then 6 × 4 = 24. Corrected base = 24. Wives get 1 × 6 = 6 (each wife 2), brothers get 3 × 6 = 18 (each brother 9).

2. Mutamathilan (Equal): When the number of heads in two groups is identical, simply multiply one of the head counts by the original base. Example: two wives and two brothers. Original base = 4. Wives have 1 share; brothers have 3 shares. Both groups have 2 heads. Multiply 2 × 4 = 8. Corrected base = 8. Wives get 1 × 2 = 2 (each 1), brothers get 3 × 2 = 6 (each 3).

3. Mutadakhilan (Nested): When one head count is a divisor (part) of the other, the larger number suffices for multiplication. The text only states the rule; the example is presumably on the following page. The method is to multiply the larger head count by the original base without using the smaller.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's text explains the rules for correcting the base number (asl al-mas'alah) in inheritance calculations when the shares of heirs in a category are not divisible by the number of persons in that category. The author discusses three of the four cases: al-tamathul (equality), al-tadakhul (overlap), and al-tawafuq (mutual sharing). 

First, when the numbers of heads in two categories are equal (al-tamathul), such as two wives and two brothers, the number of heads of one group (2) suffices to multiply by the original base number (4) to get a new base number (8) that makes shares divisible. For example, wives get 1/4 of 8 = 2, each gets 1; brothers get 3/4 of 8 = 6, each gets 3.

Second, when one number is a fraction of the other (al-tadakhul), like four wives and two brothers, the larger number (4) is multiplied directly by the original base number (4) to give 16, and this corrects the division. Multiplying the smaller number would be redundant because it is contained within the larger.

Third, when two numbers share a common factor (al-tawafuq), such as four wives and six uncles, the portion (wafq) of one number is multiplied by the whole of the other, then by the original base number. In this example, the numbers 4 and 6 share a common factor 2, so the portion of 4 is 2 (4/2) and the whole of 6 is multiplied: 2×6=12, then 12×4=48, which becomes the corrected base number.

The fourth case, al-tabayun (no common factor), is not included in this passage but is typically handled by multiplying the two numbers directly.

These rules ensure that every heir receives an integer number of shares without fractions.

تماثل (tamāthul): Equality of the numbers of heads in two groups of heirs, such that one number suffices to multiply by the base number to correct shares.
تداخل (tadākhul): One number is an exact divisor of the other, so multiplying by the larger suffices.
توافق (tawāfuq): Two numbers share a common divisor greater than 1, requiring multiplication of the portion (wafq) of one by the whole of the other.
وفق (wafq (also: wifq)): The portion of a number after removing its common factor with another number, used in tawafuq calculations.
أصل المسألة (aṣl al-mas'alah): The base denominator from which shares are assigned, typically the least common multiple of share denominators.

Applying Mudakhal and Mutawafiqan in Inheritance Tashih
Explains two proportionality rules for adjusting inheritance shares in faraid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of mudakhal and mutawafiqan to correct inheritance share division when a group's headcount does not divide evenly into their allocated share.

Core Idea
When shares don't divide evenly, use mudakhal (if one number is part of another) or mutawafiqan (if they share a common factor) to adjust the base.

In inheritance calculations, sometimes the shares allocated to a group of heirs do not divide evenly among the individual members. For example, four wives receive one share, but one share cannot be divided into four equal parts. The Hanafi school uses two arithmetic methods—mudakhal and mutawafiqan—to adjust the base number of the problem so that every heir receives a whole number. This lesson walks through these methods step by step using the examples from Al-Lubab.

Guided Path
1
Identify groups with broken shares
List all heir groups and their shares. A group has broken shares if the number of its members does not divide evenly into the share allocated to that group. In the examples, women (4) get 1 share (broken), brothers (2) get? Here, the brothers' share may also be broken, but the focus is on the relationship between group headcounts.
We only need to consider groups where shares are actually broken—those needing correction.
2
Determine relationship between headcounts
Compare the headcounts of two groups that both have broken shares (or even one broken and one unbroken). Check if one headcount is an exact multiple or part of the other (mudakhal) or if they share a common factor greater than 1 (mutawafiq). In the first example: 4 and 2 are mudakhal (2 is part of 4). In the second: 4 and 6 share factor 2, so mutawafiq.
If headcounts are co-prime (mutabayin), a different method is used, but that is not covered here.
3
Apply the appropriate rule
For mudakhal: multiply the larger headcount by the base number of the problem. For mutawafiq: compute the wafq (common factor) of one headcount, multiply it by the whole of the other headcount, then multiply that result by the base number.
In the mudakhal example: 4 × 3 = 12. In the mutawafiq example: wafq 2 × 6 = 12, then 12 × 4 = 48.
4
Multiply each group's share by the correction factor
The correction factor is the number obtained from step 3 before multiplying by the base (i.e., the factor that adjusts the shares). For mudakhal, the correction factor is the larger headcount (4). For mutawafiq, it is the product of wafq and the other headcount (12). Multiply each group's original share by this correction factor to get the final shares.
Then divide each group's final share by the number of its members to get per-heir amounts.
5
Verify all shares are integers
After correction, ensure that every group's total share is divisible by the number of its members. If not, re-check the steps or consider if additional groups need correction.
In both examples, the final shares are integers: women get 12 (3 each), brothers get 8 (4 each) in first; women get 12 (3 each), sister 24, uncles 12 (2 each) in second.

Commentary
This passage explains two rules for adjusting inheritance shares when the number of shares allocated to a group does not divide evenly among the number of members (ra's) in that group. The first rule is mudakhal (embedded): when the number of one group is an exact multiple or fraction of the other, you multiply the larger number by the base of the problem. Example: four women and two brothers: base problem is 3; women get 1 share, brothers get 2 shares. 4 (women) and 2 (brothers): 2 is part of 4, so multiply 4 into 3 to get 12. The second rule is mutawafiqan (compatible): when the two numbers share a common factor. Example: four women, a sister, and six paternal uncles. Base problem: 4 (women get 1 share, sister 2 shares, uncles 1 share). The number of women (4) and uncles (6) are compatible by half. Take half of one (2) and multiply by the whole of the other (6) to get 12, then multiply by base (4) to get 48. Then each group's share is multiplied by 12: women get 12 (3 each), sister gets 24, uncles get 12 (2 each). The passage ends by noting that once the problem is corrected (tashih), you multiply each heir's share by the correction factor to get their portion of the estate in dirhams or dinars.

متوافقان (mutawāfiqān): Two numbers that share a common factor (not 1).
وفق (wafq): The common factor between two numbers (e.g., for 4 and 6, wafq is 2).
تصحيح (taṣḥīḥ): Adjusting the base number so that shares divide evenly among all heirs.

Correcting Broken Shares and Distributing the Estate (Tashih and Qismat al-Tarikah)
Faraid case: tashih and estate distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi tashih method to a faraid case with two groups of broken shares and calculate each heir's inheritance from a given estate value using proportional division.

Core Idea
When heirs in a group cannot split their shares evenly, tashih (correction) using congruence (wafq) resolves the distribution, and then each heir's portion is computed by proportion.

Inheritance calculations often involve groups of heirs who must share a single fractional share among themselves. When the number of individuals does not divide the share evenly, the base number must be adjusted—this is tashih. This lesson walks through the standard Hanafi procedure for such cases, using the example of four wives, one full sister, and six paternal uncles. You will learn how to identify broken heads, apply the rule for congruent numbers, and then distribute an actual estate in dirhams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original asl al-mas'alah and shares
From the fard shares (1/4 for wives, 1/2 for sister, residue for uncles), find the least common denominator (4) and assign the number of shares: wives 1, sister 2, uncles 1. Note that the total is 4/4 = 1, so no awl or radd.
2
Detect broken heads (al-ru'us al-munakassarah)
Check each group: 4 wives for 1 share (broken), 1 sister for 2 shares (unbroken), 6 uncles for 1 share (broken). The broken groups are wives (4) and uncles (6).
A group is 'broken' when the number of individuals does not divide into the total shares assigned to that group.
3
Determine the relationship between the broken numbers
Compare 4 and 6: they share a common factor 2, so they are muwafiq bi al-nisf (congruent by half).
Hanafi faraid recognizes four relationships: tamathul (equal), tadakhul (one divides the other), wafq (common factor), and tabayun (no common factor).
4
Compute the multiplier (mudarib) and corrected base
For muwafiq numbers, take the half of one (6/2=3) and multiply by the whole of the other (4) to get 12. Multiply the original asl (4) by 12 to get the corrected base (tashih) = 48.
The rule: 'fadrib nisf ahadihim fi jami' al-akhar' (multiply half of one by the whole of the other).
5
Find corrected shares for each group and individual
Multiply each original share by the multiplier (12): wives 1→12, sister 2→24, uncles 1→12. Then divide each group total by the number of individuals: wives 12/4=3 each, uncles 12/6=2 each. The sister keeps 24.
6
Distribute an actual estate using the corrected base
For an estate of 96 dirhams: multiply each heir's corrected shares by the estate, then divide by 48. E.g., each wife: 3×96÷48=6; sister: 24×96÷48=48; each uncle: 2×96÷48=4. Verify sum equals 96.
Formula: (sahm al-warith min al-tashih) × (al-tarikah) ÷ (tashih).

Munasakhat: Recalculating Inheritance When an Heir Dies
Procedural guide for recalculating inheritance bases after an heir's death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of munasakha to adjust inheritance shares when an heir dies before estate division, distinguishing between even division and cases requiring multiplication or wafq.

Core Idea
When an heir dies before distribution, the inheritance base must be recalculated, not simply passed on.

This lesson explains the procedure for munasakha, a crucial step in faraid when an heir dies after the original deceased but before the estate is divided. The goal is to adjust the base number so that all shares become whole numbers. The author presents a clear method: if the second deceased's share divides evenly among his heirs, no further adjustment is needed; otherwise, multiplication or using a common factor (wafq) is required.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first scenario: even division
If the share of the second deceased from the first estate divides exactly among the second deceased's heirs, the base of the first problem remains unchanged. No new calculation is needed.
Example: First decease leaves son (2/3) and daughter (1/3); son dies leaving two sons. 2 divides by 2 = 1 each, so base stays 3.
2
Second scenario: uneven division without common factor
If the share does not divide evenly and the share and base have no common factor (i.e., they are coprime), multiply the two bases together to get a new common base (al-jami'a).
This ensures all shares from both problems are whole numbers.
3
Third scenario: uneven division with common factor
If the share and base share a common factor, multiply the whole first base by the fraction (wafq) of the second problem's base after removing the common factor.
The wafq is the second base divided by the common factor.
4
Extending to multiple deaths
If a third or fourth heir dies, treat the current jami'a as the new base for the first problem and repeat the process.
This iterative method handles any number of successive deaths.

The example given: a deceased leaves a son and a daughter (the son gets 2/3, the daughter 1/3, base = 3). Then the son dies leaving two sons. His share of 2 divides evenly into two shares of 1 each. So the two problems are valid from the same base of 3.

Second scenario: If the share does not divide evenly, then one must correct the second deceased's problem using the usual method (finding the base, etc.). Then multiply the two bases together if there is no common factor between the second deceased's shares and the base from which his problem was corrected. If there is a common factor (i.e., the numbers are not coprime), then multiply only the fraction (after simplifying by the common factor) of the second problem by the whole of the first problem. The resulting number is called al-jami'a (the comprehensive), and both problems are valid from that number.

Finally, if a third or fourth heir dies, the same process is repeated: make a new problem for each, and use the jami'a as the base for the next iteration.

Key Terms
مناسخة (munasakha): The process of recalculating inheritance shares when an heir dies before the estate is distributed.
جامعة (jami'a): The common base number resulting from multiplying or adjusting two inheritance problems; it serves as the new base for all shares.
تصحيح (tashih): The process of finding a common denominator so that shares become whole numbers.
وفق (wafq): The reduced part of a number after removing the common factor (i.e., the fraction after simplification).

Method of Munasakhat (Successive Deaths) and Combining Bases
Explains calculation of inheritance with successive deaths and allocation of shares from the combined base.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the combined inheritance base (جامعة) in a case of successive deaths (مناسخات) and distribute shares to each heir when the two bases are mubāyin (coprime).

Core Idea
In successive deaths, the combined base is the product of the two bases when the deceased heir's share and the second base are coprime.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains how to handle inheritance when two deaths occur in succession. The first estate is distributed, then the second heir dies, and his share from the first estate becomes part of his estate. The key is to find a combined base (jāmiʿa) that allows all heirs from both estates to receive their correct proportions. The commentator works through a concrete example involving a husband, parents, son, and grandson, showing exactly how the numbers are derived.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two death cases
First death: the original deceased. Second death: one of the heirs from the first estate. For both, list all heirs and determine the base number (asl al-mas'alah) for each.
The second base depends on who inherits from the second deceased.
2
Check the relationship between the second base and the deceased heir's share from the first estate
The deceased heir's share from the first case is called 'siham al-mayyit'. Determine if this number and the second base are mubāyin (coprime) or mutawāfiq (have a common factor).
If mubāyin, the combined base is the product of the two bases. If mutawāfiq, use the wafq (concord) to reduce.
3
Construct the combined base (jāmiʿa)
Multiply the first base by the second base (or by the wafq if applicable) to get the combined base. This is the common denominator for all shares.
The combined base ensures every heir's share is an integer.
4
Compute the shares for each heir
For heirs from the first estate (who do not die in the second death): multiply their share from the first base by the second base (or its wafq). For heirs from the second estate: multiply their share from the second base by the deceased heir's share (siham al-mayyit) from the first estate. For heirs who appear in both (e.g., the husband who becomes father), add the two products.
The sum of all shares should equal the combined base.
5
Reduce and convert if needed
If the combined base can be reduced by a common factor, do so. The final fractions represent each heir's portion of the total estate.
It is customary to convert to qirat or habbah for precision, but not necessary for the basic calculation.
