Using Enemy Provisions and Weapons Without Division in Dar al-Harb
Ruling on using found items in dar al-harb without distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using captured provisions and weapons in dar al-harb without division, and distinguish between permissible use and ghulul.

Core Idea
In dar al-harb, soldiers may use enemy supplies when needed but must return them; using without need is ghulul.

This lesson addresses the fiqh of the battlefield—specifically, how Muslim soldiers may lawfully use enemy provisions, firewood, oil, and weapons before the spoils are formally divided. The Hanafi school balances military necessity against the prohibition of ghulul (treacherous taking). You will learn the conditions for permissibility, the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether need is required, and the practical consequences for soldiers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
What are the specific actions being ruled upon? (e.g., eating food, feeding animals, using firewood, oil, weapons, riding horses). The ruling is mubah (permissible) under conditions.
The act is temporary use without formal division (bighayr qismah).
2
Locate the Dalil and Basis
What proof is cited? The commentary mentions 'due to the touching of need' (li masasi al-haja). This indicates the ruling is based on necessity (haja) as a concession. Also, two narrations within Hanafi school are considered.
The dalil is not a direct textual proof but derives from the principle of darura and the practice of the Companions.
3
Trace the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Identify the two narrations: one restricts permission to cases of need; the other allows absolutely. Al-Karkhi and al-Quduri chose the absolute view. This distinction affects when use becomes ghulul.
The restrictive view: need is a condition. The absolute view: no need required.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
According to al-Binaya, the preferred position (mufta bihi) is the absolute permissibility without need, chosen by al-Karkhi and al-Quduri. This is the operative ruling for fatwa.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin (later Hanafi fatwa position).
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
What practical difference does the ikhtilaf make? Under the absolute view, soldiers may take and use items even without immediate need, but they must still return them when no longer needed. Under the restrictive view, unauthorized use without need is ghulul and prohibited. The mufta bihi view simplifies battlefield conduct.
Both views agree that selling or keeping items without return is ghulul.

Commentary
This passage details the permissibility for Muslim soldiers in dar al-harb to use enemy provisions, firewood, oil, and weapons without prior division (ghayr qisma). The ruling is based on necessity: soldiers may feed their animals and eat whatever food they find (bread, meat, ghee, oil). Fay' Rulings for a Converted Enemy Combatant
Ruling with proof on fay' for a convert in Dar al-Harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on which property and family members of a Harbi who accepts Islam before conquest become fay', and explain the rationale based on effective possession and independent non-Muslim status.

Core Idea
Accepting Islam saves your person but not your property or family if they remain Harbi.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn, expanded by Al-Maydani, addresses a nuanced scenario in the law of war: when a resident of Dar al-Harb embraces Islam before the Muslims conquer the territory. The ruling distinguishes between what is under his actual control and what is not, and between independent and dependent persons. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of fay'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for real estate
The text states that the real estate (عقار) of the convert becomes fay'. The reason given is that it is in the hands of the people of Dar al-Harb, so it was not truly in his possession.
2
Identify the ruling for his wife
His wife becomes fay' because she is a disbelieving Harbiyya who does not follow him into Islam. Her status as a non-Muslim and her residence in Dar al-Harb make her part of the spoils.
3
Identify the ruling for the unborn child
The unborn child (حمل) becomes fay' because it is part of the mother, thus follows her in slavery and freedom. The text explicitly contrasts this with a separated child (born later), who is free.
The child follows the mother in status, not the father.
4
Identify the ruling for adult children
Adult children (أولاده الكبار) become fay' because they are independent non-Muslims (Harbis) with no legal dependency on the father; they are subject to their own judgment.
5
Note the exception for a separated child
A child born after the mother becomes spoils is free (حر) because the part–whole relationship no longer exists at that time.
This exception reinforces the rule: dependency is based on physical unity.

Abu Hanifa's Prohibition of Prisoner Exchange
Ruling with rational proof on prisoner exchange

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on exchanging Muslim prisoners with enemy prisoners and explain the rational proof (dalil 'aqli) based on preventing harm to the Muslim community.

Core Idea
Preventing harm to the community outweighs rescuing an individual captive.

This passage presents a clear ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa on a sensitive issue of war and captivity. While freeing a Muslim captive is a noble act, Abu Hanifa forbids exchanging them with enemy prisoners because it strengthens the enemy. This lesson unpacks the rational principle behind this ruling and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the exact ruling from the text: Abu Hanifa prohibits the exchange (fidā') of enemy prisoners for Muslim prisoners.
The phrase 'la yufadaw' indicates a prohibition (tahrim).
2

The text provides a rational argument: releasing enemy prisoners gives them assistance (ma'unah) to fight Muslims again.
The proof is not a textual source (Quran/Hadith) but a logical deduction.
3
Analyze the Underlying Principle
Abu Hanifa compares two outcomes and prioritizes repelling harm (dar' al-mafsadah) over securing a benefit (jalb al-maslahah).
This is a core Hanafi legal maxim: 'Preventing harm takes precedence over bringing benefit.'
4
Consider the Intra-Hanafi Disagreement
Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani may have permitted exchange under certain conditions, showing flexibility within the school.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position may vary; later scholars often allowed exchange if it served a greater benefit.
5
Apply the Principle to Modern Context
Think about modern exchanges or prisoner swaps: does the principle still apply? Consider if the enemy is likely to resume hostilities.
The core question remains: does releasing enemy combatants assist them in harming Muslims?

Imam's Options for Conquered Land: Division or Kharaj
Ruling on the Imam's two options and prohibition of mann

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to the Imam for land conquered by force and explain the prohibition of granting favor.

Core Idea
The Imam may either distribute conquered land to fighters or leave it to inhabitants with kharaj—both are valid, but granting it for free is forbidden.

When Muslim forces conquer a territory by force, the Imam faces a critical decision regarding the disposition of land. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar delineates the two permissible paths: distribution among the fighters or retention by the original inhabitants with a land tax. The text also emphasizes that simply granting the land as a favor is prohibited because it violates the fighters' rights. We will examine the ruling, its basis in Prophetic and Caliphal precedent, and the underlying legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal framework
The passage addresses land conquered anwatan (by force). Distinguish this from sulhan (by treaty), which has different rules. The Imam's discretion applies only to the former.
The term 'عنوة' is key—it conditions the entire ruling.
2
State the two permissible options
Option one: divide the real estate among the fighters (ghānimīn). Option two: leave the inhabitants on their land and impose kharaj (land tax). Both are lawful.
The text says 'فهو بالخيار'—he has a choice.
3
Explain the prohibition of mann
The matn explicitly forbids 'المن'—granting favor by releasing the land or prisoners without compensation. This would nullify the fighters' right to booty.
Mann is impermissible because it invalidates the rights of the ghazis.
4
Cite the proofs (dalil)
The first option is proven by the Prophet's action at Khaybar; the second by Caliph Umar's action in Sawad al-Iraq with the agreement of the Companions. Both are valid precedents.
These are sunnah ahad and ijma of the Sahaba.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and the qil opinion
Some later scholars said the first option is preferable when fighters are in need, the second when they are not. This is a non-binding preference.
The Hanafi position: both options are permissible; the choice is based on maslaha.

Ruling on Imam's options for war captives

Learning Objective

The Imam may kill, enslave, or release as dhimmis – but never return captives to enemy territory.

This passage from Lubab al-Mukhtar fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies the Imam's authority over prisoners of war. The matn gives three clear options, each with a rationale rooted in maslaha. The sharh adds a critical restriction: only prisoners eligible for dhimma can be released as free dhimmis. The absolute prohibition on returning captives to dar al-harb prevents strengthening the enemy.

The text explicitly conditions release as dhimmis on the prisoners being 'ahl al-dhimma' – eligible for dhimma. Apostates and Arab polytheists are excluded.
The exclusion is based on earlier rulings (as mentioned in 'as سبق').
4
Note the absolute prohibition
The Imam is forbidden from sending prisoners back to dar al-harb. The reasoning: it strengthens the enemy against the Muslims.
This prohibition is unconditional.
5
Link to the dalil (Umar's practice)
The sharh cites Umar's action in Sawad al-Iraq as proof for the dhimma option. This is a precedent (Sunnah of a Caliph with sahabi consensus).
The other options have similar prophetic precedents, though not cited here.

A Slave's Aman: Validity in Hanafi Fiqh
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a slave's aman is valid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on a slave's aman and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
A slave's aman is invalid unless permitted to fight—Abu Hanifa's view prevails despite Sahibayn's objection.

This passage addresses a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: can a slave grant safe conduct (aman) to an enemy combatant? The answer affects the binding nature of the contract on the Muslim community. Imam Abu Hanifa held that the slave's general legal incapacity (mahjur) prevents him from contracting aman, while his two students (Sahibayn) argued that the slave's belief and ability to cause fear make his aman effective. Understanding the reasoning and the later tarjih (preference) is essential for navigating conflicting reports within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling categories
The act is the granting of aman by a slave. Two rulings are presented: invalid (Abu Hanifa) unless permitted, and valid (Sahibayn). Note that 'invalid' here means the contract is not binding.
2
Trace the dalil for Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa reasons that aman is a contract, and a slave is legally interdicted (mahjur) in contracts. Therefore, the contract is void unless the master grants permission to fight, which removes the interdiction for that purpose.
The key principle: slaves cannot independently conclude contracts.
3
Trace the dalil for Sahibayn's position
Sahibayn argue that the slave is a believer with strength and the ability to inspire fear. Since aman is defined as a contract of protection rooted in fear, the slave's aman achieves its purpose regardless of his contractual capacity.
They focus on the underlying cause (illa) of aman: fear of the granter.
4
Understand the tarjih and mufta bihi
Imam al-Karkhi preferred Abu Hanifa's view, and later authorities like al-Burhani and al-Nasafi followed. Thus, the mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that a slave's aman is invalid unless the master permits him to fight.
Tarjih here is based on the general principle of interdiction prevailing over the specific purpose of aman.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If a slave without fighting permission gives aman to an enemy, according to the mufta bihi, the aman is invalid, and Muslims may still attack. If permission is given, the aman is binding.
This shows the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Rules on share and radhkh in spoils

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a fighter's share is determined at the time of crossing the border (mujawazah) and identify who receives a full share versus a radhkh grant.

Core Idea
A fighter's share is fixed at border crossing; non-combatants get only a discretionary grant.

This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri clarifies two critical issues in Hanafi spoils distribution. First, the share entitlement of a cavalryman or foot soldier is determined at the moment of crossing into enemy territory, regardless of later changes in status. Second, certain categories such as slaves, women, dhimmis, and children do not receive a full share but may be given a small grant (radhkh) at the imam's discretion. Understanding these rules ensures correct distribution of ghazima.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the two scenarios for status change
Read the first two examples: a cavalryman whose horse dies, and a foot soldier who later buys a horse. Note that in both cases the share is based on the status at the time of crossing (mujawazah), not at battle.
This is the core rule.
2
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The text explains that the ruling is based on the difficulty of ascertaining actual fighting. Crossing the border while intending to fight is taken as a reliable proxy for participation.
Note the use of 'because' (li-anna) indicating the proof.
3
List those excluded from full shares
Identify the categories mentioned: slave, mukatib, woman, dhimmi, child, insane, mentally deficient. Note that they do not get a sahm (share) but may receive radhkh.
Radhkh is a small grant at the imam's discretion.
4
Distinguish between sahm and radhkh
A sahm is a fixed obligatory share for combatants. Radhkh is a voluntary grant for non-combatants, whose amount is not fixed.
The difference is essential for correct distribution.
5
Apply the rule to a hypothetical scenario
Consider a case: a dhimmi fights alongside Muslims. According to the rule, he still does not receive a full share, but the imam may give him radhkh.
This illustrates the Hanafi position.

Abrogation of the Prophet's Share and Relatives' Share
Explaining abrogation of Prophet's share and relatives' share after his death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the abrogation of the Prophet's share and the share of his relatives from spoils after his death, and explain the proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
The Prophet's special share and his relatives' share lapsed at his death; the cause (support) ceased.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling regarding the distribution of booty: what happens to the special share of the Prophet (safiyy) and the share of his relatives (Dhawi al-Qurba) after his death. The Hanafi school holds that both are abrogated, based on the fact that the relatives' entitlement during his lifetime was due to their support and companionship, which ended with his death. We will walk through the reasoning and the hadith evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two shares
Recognize that there are two distinct entitlements discussed: the Prophet's personal selection (al-safiyy) and the share of his relatives (sahm dhawi al-qurba).
The safiyy was an item the Prophet could choose from spoils before division.
2
Understand the basis for each share
The relatives' share was based on their support (nusrah) during the Prophet's lifetime. After his death, the basis is no longer applicable.
The hadith of the interlaced fingers illustrates this support.
3
Note the abrogation
Both shares lapsed with the Prophet's death. The safiyy lapsed because it was a specific right of the Prophet; the relatives' share lapsed because the cause (support) ceased.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
4
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith states that the relatives will always be with the Prophet like fingers interlaced, indicating their support. This support was the reason for the share; when it ended, the share ended.
The hadith is transmitted as ahad, so it is zanni in transmission and meaning.
5
Derive the ruling
Based on the abrogation, today no one is entitled to the safiyy or the relatives' share. All booty is distributed according to the general rules of ghanimah.
The mufta bihi is that these shares are no longer distributed.

When is Booty Subject to Khums? Individuals vs Groups
Ruling on khums for booty taken by individuals vs groups

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khums for booty taken by one or two Muslims versus a group with strength, and explain the legal reasoning (qiyas khafi) based on the condition of overpowering and the Imam's duty to support.

Core Idea
Booty taken by one or two without permission is not ghanima; only a group with strength creates a valid ghanima subject to khums.

This lesson examines the conditions under which booty taken from enemy territory becomes subject to the khums (one-fifth) tax. The Hanafi school draws a crucial distinction between isolated individuals sneaking in and a group with military capability. The ruling hinges on whether the taking constitutes true ghanima – seizure by force and overpowering – or mere theft. Additionally, the Imam's obligation to support a group plays a role in the analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The passage presents three cases: (1) one or two Muslims enter without Imam's permission, (2) one or two with permission, (3) a group with strength (man'a) without permission. For each, note whether khums applies.
The third case is the key: group with strength, even without permission, triggers khums.
2
Define ghanima
Ghanima is booty taken 'by force and overpowering' (qahr wa ghalaba), not by stealth or theft. Determine which scenario fits this definition.
Individuals acting without public authority are like thieves, not soldiers.
3
Analyze the Imam's obligation
The Imam is obligated to support a group with strength; if he fails, the Muslims become weak. This makes the group's action equivalent to an authorized raid, justifying khums even without explicit permission.
Contrast with individuals: no duty to support them, so their action remains private.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
When permission is given to individuals, there are two narrations: one says khums applies, another says it does not. The well-known (mashhur) view, preferred in al-Hidayah, is that khums applies because permission implies the Imam undertakes their support.
This shows the principle that authorization changes the legal nature of the act.

A musta'min cannot stay more than one year without jizya; a short stay is allowed for trade.

The commentary explains the reason: long-term residence could make the musta'min a spy or helper against Muslims. However, short stays are allowed to facilitate trade and supplies. The one-year threshold aligns with the annual jizya obligation.

Presents Abu Yusuf's and Muhammad's views on land tax classification of revived dead land.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the tax classification of revived dead land and explain the difference in their criteria (vicinity vs. water source).

Core Idea
The tax status of revived dead land depends on either its vicinity (Abu Yusuf) or its water source (Muhammad) — a classic intra-Hanafi dispute.

When a Muslim revives dead land (ihya al-mawat), does it become 'ushriyya or kharajiyya? The Hanafi school contains two authoritative answers from the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa. This passage from al-Lubab presents the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, each relying on a distinct analogy and a notable exception for Basra.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and the two authorities
The issue is the tax classification of revived dead land. The two authorities are Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, both students of Abu Hanifa and transmitters of his madhhab.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf, meaning both views are part of the Hanafi school.
2
Understand Abu Yusuf's criterion: vicinity (ḥīz)
Abu Yusuf looks at the adjacent, inhabited land. If the revived land is near kharaj land, it becomes kharajiyya; if near 'ushr land, it becomes 'ushriyya. He analogizes this to a courtyard attached to a house.
The basis is qiyas: what is close to something takes its ruling.

Exemptions from jizya: Arab idolaters and apostates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why Arab idolaters and apostates are exempt from jizya according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Arab idolaters and apostates are not offered jizya; they must accept Islam or be fought.

While many non-Muslims may pay jizya and live under Islamic rule, some groups are given no such option. The Hanafi school excludes Arab idolaters because the Prophet's miracle was most manifest among them, and apostates because they rejected Islam after embracing it. For these, the only alternatives are Islam or war.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempt groups
The passage lists two groups: Arab idolaters (عبدة الأوثان من العرب) and apostates (المرتدين).
2
Explain the reason for Arab idolaters
The Prophet grew up among them and the Quran was revealed in their language, so the miracle of Islam was most manifest. Thus they are not excused in their kufr.
3
Explain the reason for apostates
Apostates rejected Islam after guidance, so their disbelief is considered more egregious. They are given no option but to return to Islam or face execution.
4
Distinguish from other groups
Non-Arab idolaters pay jizya because their enslavement is permissible; Arab idolaters do not because of the Prophet's unique mission among them. Apostates are distinct because they had already accepted Islam.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's covenant remains intact even after murder or insult; the state punishes but does not expel.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a critical aspect of dhimma: does a grave crime nullify the protected status? The Hanafi answer is no, and the reasoning draws on a powerful qiyas. Understanding this principle clarifies how the Islamic state balances justice with covenant fidelity.

Debts of an apostate are paid from apostasy earnings first, then Islamic earnings – but this is contested within the Hanafi school.

This lesson explores a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the estate of an apostate (murtadd). The central question: from which category of earnings—those acquired during Islam (kasb al-Islam) or during apostasy (kasb al-riddah)—should the apostate's debts be repaid first? The answer determines how the remaining estate is divided among heirs and the Muslim treasury. Suspended Transactions of the Apostate: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Ruling on apostate's transactions and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an apostate's suspended transactions and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An apostate's transactions are suspended; if he dies apostate, they become void per Abu Hanifa, but valid per Sahibayn.

This passage addresses a key question in Hanafi fiqh: what happens to the financial transactions of an apostate? The ruling is that they are suspended pending his final outcome. However, the two great disciples of Abu Hanifa disagree on the consequence if the apostate dies or joins the enemy. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your appreciation of how legal capacity (ahliyyah) and inviolability (ismah) interact.

The suspension is neither valid nor void.

If the apostate repents and becomes Muslim, the transactions become valid retrospectively.
This is agreed upon.
3
Determine the outcome if the apostate dies or joins the enemy
According to Abu Hanifa, the transactions become void because the loss of inviolability (ismah) causes a defect in legal capacity (ahliyyah).
This is the mufta bihi position.

Contrast with the Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the transactions remain valid in both cases because the apostate still has legal capacity (he is addressed by law) and ownership was established before apostasy.

Categories of an Apostate's Transactions
Categorization of apostate's transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an apostate's transactions into valid, void, suspended, or disputed categories and state the rationale for each classification.

Core Idea
Not all an apostate's dealings are invalid—some are valid, some void, some suspended, and a few disputed.

The Hanafi school provides a nuanced categorization of an apostate's transactions. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains that certain acts like divorce and istilad are valid by consensus, while marriage and slaughter are void. Others like mufawada partnership are suspended until the apostate returns to Islam. A final category is disputed among scholars. Understanding this framework is essential for correctly applying rulings in cases of apostasy within muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four categories mentioned: (1) Effective by agreement, (2) Void by agreement, (3) Suspended by agreement, (4) Disputed in suspension.
The first three are agreed upon; the fourth is subject to scholarly disagreement.
2
Analyze the underlying principle
For each category, identify why the transaction falls into that category. Effective acts do not require religion or ownership; void acts depend on religion; suspended acts require equality; disputed acts have varied opinions.
The rationale is based on the legal nature of each act.
3
Apply the classification to examples
Using the examples given (istilad, divorce, marriage, slaughter, mufawada), confirm which category each belongs to and why.
Refer to the key terms for specific definitions.
4
Recognize the significance of consensus
Note that for the first three categories, there is agreement among scholars, while the fourth category involves ikhtilaf. This affects the strength of the ruling.
Consensus-based rulings are more authoritative.
