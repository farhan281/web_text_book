Appointment of the Surveyor and His Salary
Rulings on appointment of surveyor, his salary, and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on appointing a surveyor and identify the conditions for his appointment and payment.

Core Idea
The surveyor should be paid from public funds; if not, he may take a fee from the shareholders.

In this passage, Al-Quduri and his commentator Al-Maydani explain the rules for appointing a surveyor (qasim) to divide shared property. The ruling covers the ideal method of payment from Bayt al-Mal, the permissibility of fee-based surveyors, and the necessary qualifications: justice, trustworthiness, and expertise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
The text begins with 'yambaghi lil-imam an yansiba qasiman yarzuquhu min bayt al-mal' meaning the imam should appoint a surveyor and provide his sustenance from the public treasury.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The commentator explains that division is akin to qada (judging) because it resolves disputes, so the surveyor's salary should be like a judge's salary.
This is an example of qiyas.
3
Recognize the alternative
If the imam does not do that, he appoints a surveyor who takes a fee from the co-owners; this is because the benefit is for them and it's not actual qada.
4
Note the preferred position
Al-Hidayah states it's better to pay from Bayt al-Mal to avoid suspicion.
5
List the conditions
The surveyor must be just, trustworthy, and knowledgeable in division.
These are shurut for validity of his work.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Qismah (Book of Division) discusses the appointment of a surveyor (qasim) to divide property among co-owners. It begins with a ruling that the imam (or judge) should appoint a surveyor and pay him from the public treasury (Bayt al-Mal) so that he divides for free. The reasoning is that division is akin to the work of a judge (qada) because it resolves disputes; therefore, his salary should be from public funds. If the imam does not do this, he appoints a surveyor who takes a fee from the co-owners, because the benefit is for them and it is not actually qada, so taking a fee is permissible unlike for a judge. Al-Hidayah states that providing from the treasury is preferable as it is kinder and avoids suspicion of bias.

The passage then discusses the nature of division: in homogeneous items (like grains or weights), the division is essentially separation (ifraz) because there is no disproportion, so one owner can take his share in the absence of the other. In heterogeneous items, it is essentially exchange (mubadala) because of disparity, so one cannot take without the other's presence. However, if the items are of the same genus, the judge can force division at the request of one party because it is essentially separation and exchange can be compelled, like in debt repayment. If they are of different genera, the judge cannot force division because equivalency is difficult due to great disparity in purposes. But if they mutually agree, it is valid because the right is theirs.

The second part lists conditions for the appointed surveyor: he must be just (because it is akin to qada), trustworthy (so his word is relied upon), and knowledgeable in division (to be capable). The judge does not force people to use a single surveyor; they are free to hire another or agree among themselves. If they mutually agree and divide, it is valid except when a minor is involved, then the judge's permission is needed because the minor is under their authority.

Key Terms
قاسم (qasim): The person appointed to divide shared property among co-owners.
إفراز (ifraz): Separation of shares when the property is homogeneous, without exchange.
مبادلة (mubadala): Exchange of shares when property is heterogeneous, requiring mutual agreement.

The surveyor should ideally be paid from public funds to avoid suspicion, but a fee-based surveyor is permissible.

Forced Division and Nature of Shares
Rules of division: ifraz and mubadala, forced division conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between ifraz and mubadala and determine when a judge can force division of shared property.

Core Idea
Division of homogeneous goods is separation (ifraz) and can be forced; heterogeneous goods require exchange (mubadala) and consent.

This passage from Kitab al-Qisma explains the legal nature of dividing property. The key distinction is whether the property is homogeneous (same genus) or heterogeneous (different genera). In homogeneous items, division is essentially separation (ifraz) and can be enforced by the judge; in heterogeneous items, it is exchange (mubadala) and requires mutual agreement. The analogy to debt repayment is used to justify forced division in the same genus.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two concepts
Ifraz: separating shares in homogeneous items (no exchange). Mubadala: exchanging shares in heterogeneous items.
2
Identify the practical consequence
In ifraz, one owner can take his share in the absence of the other. In mubadala, presence of both is required because it's a sale/exchange.
3
Determine forced division
If property is of one genus, the judge can force division at the request of one party because it is essentially separation and exchange can be compelled (like debt repayment). If different genera, judge cannot force.
Analogy to debt repayment is used as qiyas.
4
Note the exception
If co-owners mutually agree, division is valid even in heterogeneous.

Judge Refuses Division Without Proof of Inheritance
Explains judge's duty regarding estate division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a judge must not divide an estate based on mere claim, and explain the reasoning that division is a qada' against the deceased.

Core Idea
Division of an estate is a ruling against the deceased, so proof of inheritance is required.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn, expanded by Al-Maidani, addresses a critical procedural point in Hanafi inheritance law. When individuals appear before a judge claiming inherited property, the judge cannot simply divide it based on their word. Abu Hanifa holds that division is a judicial act that terminates the deceased's ownership rights, thus requiring formal evidence. This lesson explores the reasoning and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The matn states that the judge should not divide the property when claimants assert inheritance without proof. This is Abu Hanifa's ruling.
Other schools may differ, but Hanafi law is strict.
2
Understand the reasoning
The commentator explains that division is 'qada' 'ala al-mayyit' (a ruling against the deceased). The estate remains in the deceased's ownership until division, as evidenced by the fact that increases (offspring, profits) belong to the estate and are used for debts and bequests.
This analogy (qiyas) treats division like a verdict against an absent party.
3
Trace the dalil
The proof is aqli (rational): division cuts off the deceased's ownership rights, so it is like a judicial decision affecting a third party who is not present. Therefore, clear proof is necessary.
No explicit nass is cited; the proof is derived from the nature of the estate.
4
Note the authority
Al-Nasafi and Al-Mahbubi, as mentioned in Al-Tashih, adopt Abu Hanifa's view, making it the mufta bihi in the school.
Zahir al-Riwayah supports this ruling.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from Abu Hanifa concerning the division of property among individuals who claim to be heirs but lack formal proof. The matn from Al-Quduri states that if partners appear in court claiming they inherited property from a deceased person, the judge should not proceed to divide it. The commentator Al-Maidani explains the reasoning: division is considered a judicial ruling against the deceased (qada' ala al-mayyit) because before division, the estate is still technically owned by the deceased. Evidence of this is that any increase (like offspring of livestock or profits) is considered part of the estate and subject to debts and bequests. When division occurs, it cuts off the deceased's ownership rights, affecting those increases. Since the deceased is not present to defend his rights, the judge cannot make such a ruling without solid proof of inheritance (e.g., testimony or documentary evidence). The later authorities (Al-Nasafi, Al-Mahbubi) uphold Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position.

تركة (tarakah): Estate of the deceased, which remains in his ownership until division and payment of debts and bequests.
قسمة (qismah): Division of inherited property among heirs, which terminates the deceased's ownership.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Partition Based on Claimants' Admission
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partitioning inheritance based on claimants' admission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position and the Sahibayn's position on partitioning inheritance based solely on claimants' claim, explain the reasoning behind each, and identify which view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Partition of inherited property is a judgment against the deceased — Abu Hanifa requires proof; Sahibayn accept possession.

This passage from Kitab al-Qismah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the procedural requirements for dividing inherited property when the claimants are in possession. While both sides agree on the legitimacy of partition, they differ on what evidence the judge needs before ordering division. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi jurists balance procedural caution with practical expediency in inheritance matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The core question: May a judge partition inherited property based solely on the claimants' claim of inheritance and their possession, without external proof of the deceased's death and the number of heirs?
This is a procedural question about judicial authority, not about the validity of the division itself.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the judge must not partition until the claimants establish proof of death and the exact number of heirs. His proof: partition is a judgment against the deceased (qada' 'ala al-mayyit) because the estate remains in his ownership until division, evidenced by the fact that increases and profits belong to the estate.
The estate is treated as still owned by the deceased until division; thus, a judgment affecting it requires evidence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the judge can partition based on the claimants' admission because possession is evidence of ownership (al-yad dalil al-milk) and there is no disputant. They analogize to division of purchased property where possession alone suffices.
They require the judge to record that the division is based on the claimants' statement, to limit its effect to the known parties.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, claimants must bring witnesses to prove death and heir number before partition. Under the Sahibayn, the judge can proceed immediately based on their claim and possession. This affects the speed and cost of obtaining partition.
The mufta bihi follows Abu Hanifa, meaning the stricter requirement is operative for fatwa.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its authorities
Later Hanafi authorities such as al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, and Imam Jamal al-Islam preferred Abu Hanifa's view. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the serious nature of partition as a judgment against the deceased, which outweighs the convenience of accepting possession as sufficient.

The guardian is appointed only for answering (jawab), not for presence – the minor already satisfies presence.
3
Analyze the absent minor case
If the minor is absent, the judge does not appoint a guardian for presence. The reason is that a lawsuit requires a present adversary (khasm hadir). The minor's inability to answer (ajz 'an al-jawab) does not equate to inability to be present. Making someone else a representative for an absent person is a departure from the norm (khilaf al-haqiqah) and is only done when necessary (ind al-ajz). The minor is not incapable of being present, so no representative is appointed for presence.
The key distinction: the minor is capable of being present physically but incapable of answering legally. The law resolves the answering incapacity by allowing a guardian when present, but does not create a fictitious presence for an absent minor.
4
Contrast with the case of an adult heir and a legatee
The text also notes that if an adult heir and a legatee of one-third (musa lahu bi al-thulth) are present, partition proceeds because both are present and represent themselves. This reinforces the principle that presence is key.
The legatee is a separate party with a distinct interest.

Ruling on Embracing Islam in Dar al-Harb Before Capture
Ruling on effects of accepting Islam in dar al-harb before capture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on what a convert in dar al-harb secures and what becomes fay' upon conquest, and distinguish between movable and immovable property.

Core Idea
Islam secures the person, minors, and movable property in hand; but land, wife, and adult children become fay'.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from the Book of Siyar: a disbeliever who accepts Islam in enemy territory before being captured. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules about what the convert secures through his Islam and what remains subject to the laws of war. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between different types of property and dependents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Convert and the Context
The passage deals with a person who is originally a harbi (enemy combatant) and accepts Islam in dar al-harb before being taken captive. This is a unique situation where the conversion happens while the person is still legally an enemy.
Distinguish from a convert who comes to dar al-Islam; that case has different rules.
2
List What is Secured by Islam
According to the text, the convert secures: (1) his own person (cannot be enslaved), (2) his minor children (they follow him in Islam), and (3) every property in his valid hand (yad sahihah) or in the trust of a Muslim/dhimmi. These are protected because of the principle that Islam prevents enslavement and that prior possession is respected.
'Valid hand' (yad sahihah) excludes usurpers and enemies.
3
List What Becomes Fay' Upon Conquest
If Muslims later conquer the territory, the following become fay' (booty for the state): the convert's real estate (as part of dar al-harb), his wife (a harbiyyah who does not follow him), her pregnancy (part of her), and his adult children (independent harbis). These are not protected because they are not in his valid possession or not dependents.
Fay' is for the public treasury, not divided like ghanimah.
4
Understand the Reasoning
The ruling is based on rational proofs (dalil 'aqli): Islam precludes initial enslavement, thus the person and his dependents are free. Possession of movable property is respected because it is under his control. But immovable property is part of the enemy territory and not in his actual hand, so it is considered spoils. The wife remains a harbiyyah because marriage does not transfer her allegiance.
This reasoning is distinct from textual proofs; it's derived from the principles of siyar.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
The Shafi'i school holds that the convert's wife and real estate are also secured, not fay'. This difference arises from different views on whether the convert's property includes land and whether marriage follows the husband's allegiance. Understanding this helps appreciate the Hanafi position.
The Hanafi view is based on the distinction between movable and immovable, and dependence.

Inheritance of Spoils: Death Before and After Transfer
Ruling on inheritance of spoils, with expansion on the rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance of spoils when a fighter dies before or after the spoils are secured and explain the underlying principle of ownership.

Core Idea
No inheritance without ownership — death before spoils are secured leaves nothing for heirs.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position on when a fighter's share of spoils becomes inheritable. The key distinction is whether the fighter died before or after the spoils were brought to Islamic territory or divided. The ruling hinges on the concept that inheritance follows ownership, and ownership of spoils is only established after they are secured (ihraz) or divided. This passage from the Lubab commentary expands on the matn's concise rule.

Guided Path
1

The matn presents two cases: death in Dar al-Harb (before spoils are secured) and death after the spoils are removed to Dar al-Islam. Note the critical difference in the ruling.
The commentary clarifies that the second case includes death after division or sale, even if still in Dar al-Harb.
2

The commentary states 'inheritance applies to ownership, and there is no ownership before what was mentioned.' This is the core principle. Trace how the absence of ownership before ihraz prevents inheritance.
The phrase 'as previously' refers to earlier discussions on when spoils become owned.
3
Determine when ownership is established
The text mentions three acts: taking spoils to Dar al-Islam, division (qismah), and sale (even in Dar al-Harb). Any one of these establishes the fighter's right, making the share inheritable.
The right becomes 'established' (istiqrar) upon these acts, not before.
4

Given a scenario, decide whether the heir inherits. For example, a fighter dies after the spoils are brought to Dar al-Islam but before division: the heir inherits because transfer alone establishes ownership. Another fighter dies in Dar al-Harb before any such act: no inheritance.
Note that the location of death (Dar al-Harb vs Dar al-Islam) is not the decisive factor—it's the stage of the spoils.

Definition and Significance of Fara'id
Definition and significance of fara'id

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'fara'id' both linguistically and technically according to Hanafi fiqh, and explain why this science is named as such.

Core Idea
Fara'id are fixed inheritance shares proven by definitive Quranic and Prophetic evidence.

This lesson introduces the Book of Inheritances (Fara'id) by defining its core term. The author Al-Quduri begins by explaining that 'fara'id' refers to fixed shares in inheritance, established by qat'i (definitive) proof. Understanding this definition is essential before studying the detailed rules that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning of 'al-fard'
Reading the passage, note that 'al-fard' linguistically means 'estimation' (taqdir) and 'cutting' (qat'). This sets the foundation for the term's usage in inheritance.
These two meanings are distinct: estimation relates to determining shares, cutting relates to the severance of a portion.
2
Identify the technical (shar'i) meaning of 'fard'
In sacred law, 'fard' is defined as a ruling established by a definitive proof (dalil qat'i) with no doubt (shubhah). This is the core technical definition.
Hanafis differentiate fard from wajib; fard is from qat'i evidence, wajib from zanni evidence.
3
Understand why the science is called 'fara'id'
The passage states it is named fara'id because it consists of fixed shares (siham muqaddarah) established by definitive proof. This connects both the linguistic and legal meanings.
The plural 'fara'id' emphasizes the multiple fixed shares for different heirs.
4
Recognize the scriptural basis for the name
Allah Himself used the term 'faridah' in Surah an-Nisa (4:11) after detailing inheritance shares. The Prophet (peace be upon him) also commanded learning fara'id, highlighting its importance.
This scriptural sanction gives the science its religious authority.
5
Note the placement of this book at the end of fiqh works
Al-Zahidi explains that al-Quduri placed the Book of Fara'id at the end because its issues relate to the end of a person's obligations (i.e., after death). This suggests a deliberate pedagogical order.
Understanding this placement helps appreciate the structure of classical Hanafi fiqh texts.

The Ten Agreed-Upon Male Heirs in Hanafi Faraid
Listing of ten male heirs unanimously agreed upon

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list all ten categories of male heirs unanimously recognized in Hanafi inheritance law and explain the condition 'bi-mahd al-dhukur'.

Core Idea
Ten male heirs are agreed upon by consensus — each is either a fixed-share recipient, a residuary, or both.

In Hanafi inheritance, certain categories of male heirs are universally accepted without dispute. This lesson presents the foundational list from al-Quduri's matn, which every student of faraid must commit to memory. Understanding these ten categories is the first step before dealing with exclusions, shares, and complex distributions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle of agreement
Note that these ten are unanimously agreed upon (mujma' alayh), meaning no valid ikhtilaf among the Companions or early imams. This gives them strong authority.
2
Categorize by inheritance type
For each heir, determine whether they inherit by fard (fixed share), ta'sib (residuary), or both. For example, son is pure residuary; father can be both.
Refer to a later lesson for exact shares.
3
Apply the condition 'bi-mahd al-dhukur'
Understand that heirs like grandson and brother's son must trace through males only. For instance, son's son through a daughter does not qualify.
This condition ensures only agnatic (paternal) descendants are included.
4
Memorize the ten in order
Use the sequence from the matn: son, son's son, father, grandfather, brother, brother's son, paternal uncle, paternal uncle's son, husband, mawla al-ni'mah.
5
Distinguish from disputed heirs
Recognize that heirs like father's father's father (great-grandfather) or certain collaterals are not in this list because they are subject to ikhtilaf.
Disputed heirs are covered in advanced lessons.

Only ten specific male relatives are primary heirs; all others are kindred (dhawi al-arham).

Identify the categories of male heirs

Dhawi al-arham are relatives not in the list; they inherit only when no primary heir exists. For example, maternal uncle, maternal grandfather, etc.

Dhawu al-Arham and the Invalid Grandfather (al-Jadd al-Fasid)
Definition and exclusion clauses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Dhawu al-Arham and explain why the maternal grandfather is called 'al-jadd al-fasid' and how it affects inheritance.

Core Idea
Dhawu al-Arham are all male relatives not among the fifteen; the maternal grandfather is an 'invalid grandfather' because he does not inherit as a primary grandfather.

The passage introduces two important concepts: Dhawu al-Arham (uterine relatives) and al-jadd al-fasid (invalid grandfather). These are essential for determining who inherits when primary heirs are absent.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the exclusion clause for males
After listing the fifteen male heirs, the text says: 'And whoever besides these from the males are among Dhawu al-Arham.' This means any male relative not in that list—including maternal uncle, stepson, father's uncle, etc.—is a Dhawi arham.
Dhawu al-Arham are uterine relatives, meaning they are connected through females or distant lines.
2
Recognize the exclusion clause for females
Similarly, after listing the female heirs, the text says: 'And whoever besides these from the females are among Dhawu al-Arham.' So any female not in the ten is a Dhawi arham (e.g., paternal aunt, maternal aunt, niece).
This parallels the male exclusion.
3
Understand the 'invalid grandfather' condition for the grandmother
The grandmother is included as a primary heir unless she 'comes through an invalid grandfather' (jadd fasid). The invalid grandfather is the maternal grandfather. So if the grandmother is the mother's mother, she is valid? Actually, the grandmother's connection: if she is a paternal grandmother, she is valid; if she is a maternal grandmother, she is also valid as long as she does not come through an invalid grandfather. But the maternal grandmother's line goes through the mother, which involves the maternal grandfather. The text says 'unless she comes through an invalid grandfather' – this means if her lineage to the deceased passes through a male who is an invalid grandfather, she is excluded. That male is the maternal grandfather. So the maternal grandmother is valid because her connection is through the mother, not through the grandfather? This is tricky. Actually, the grandmother is defined as the mother's mother or father's mother. The condition 'as long as she does not come through an invalid grandfather' means that if her path to the deceased includes a maternal grandfather (i.e., she is the mother of the maternal grandfather), then she is a Dhawi arham. For example, the great-grandmother from the mother's side through a maternal grandfather is invalid. The text says: 'the grandmother (maternal or paternal) however high as long as she does not come through an invalid grandfather.' So both maternal and paternal grandmothers are primary, but if the grandmother is so high that her line goes through a maternal grandfather (e.g., the mother of the maternal grandfather), she is excluded. So the 'invalid grandfather' is a specific male relative who blocks the grandmother from being primary.
The invalid grandfather is the maternal grandfather, but also any male in the line that is not a valid father.
4
Link to the concept of Dhawu al-Arham
The maternal grandfather is not in the list of fifteen male heirs, and he is specifically called 'invalid grandfather'. He is a Dhawi arham. Similarly, any relative not in the primary lists is a Dhawi arham.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi inheritance rule regarding the mother's share.
