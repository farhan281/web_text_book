Intentional, Coerced, or Forgetful: All Break the Oath
Ruling that all three states equally constitute oath-breaking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that any action contrary to an oath—whether intentional, coerced, or forgetful—constitutes ḥanth and triggers wajib expiation, and explain the rational proof that the ruling is tied to the reality of the action, not to sin.

Core Idea
Oath-breaking is based on the real act, not on intent or culpability.

In Hanafi fiqh, the obligation to fulfill an oath is strict: once a valid oath is made, any occurrence of the sworn-against act—whether done deliberately, under duress, or even forgetfully—counts as a breach (ḥanth) and requires expiation (kaffārah). This lesson examines the ruling and its rational proof as stated in al-Quduri's matn and elaborated in al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is breaking an oath by doing the thing sworn against. The ruling: it is ḥanth, which makes kaffārah wajib.
The hukm level is wajib, not fard, because the proof is zanni (qiyas).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a rational (ʿaqlī) argument: the action is real; coercion and forgetfulness do not negate the reality of the action.
This is a qiyās based on the illah of actual occurrence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The legal cause (ʿillah) for ḥanth is the occurrence of the forbidden action, not the presence of sin. Since the action actually happened, the condition for ḥanth is fulfilled.
The ruling 'revolves around its evidence' (al-ḥukm yadūr maʿa ʿillatihi).
4
Note the ikhtilāf
Some other schools distinguish between intentional and unintentional acts, but Hanafis equate them all.
The practical difference: in non-Hanafi schools, forgetful or coerced acts may not require expiation.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjīḥ
The Hanafi position is muftā bihi; it is based on the principle that rulings revolve around their evidence, not around subjective culpability.
This is the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah position, with no intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Valid Forms of Oath in Hanafi Law
Definition of valid oath in Hanafi law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid and invalid forms of oath in Hanafi law by applying the criteria of swearing by Allah, His names, or essential attributes with customary usage.

Core Idea
An oath is only valid if it invokes Allah, His name, or an essential attribute customarily used.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by al-Hidayah, defines what constitutes a binding oath (yamīn) in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this definition is crucial because expressing an oath in an invalid form renders the expression non-binding, meaning no legal consequences follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three valid categories
The passage states that an oath is valid by Allah's name (the name of essence), by any of His names (e.g., al-Rahman, al-Rahim), or by an essential attribute (ṣifat al-dhāt) that has no opposite and is customary (e.g., might, majesty, greatness).
Attributes like 'knowledge' are excluded because they are not customarily used.
2
Understand the exception
The passage explicitly excludes 'by Allah's knowledge' (bi-ʿilm Allah) even though knowledge is an attribute. The reason is lack of customary usage (taʿāruf).
Knowledge is a ṣifa dhātiyya but not customary for swearing.
3
Explain the rationale for essential attributes
Essential attributes are those that cannot be described with their opposite (e.g., might has no opposite like weakness). Swearing by them is customary and evokes reverence, fulfilling the purpose of an oath.
Attributes of action (ṣifāt al-fiʿl) like 'creating' are not suitable.
4
Apply the rule to new phrases
Given any expression, check: Is it by Allah, a name of Allah, or an essential attribute? Is the attribute customary? If yes, it is a valid oath; otherwise not.
Example: 'By the mercy of Allah' – mercy has opposite (wrath), so not an essential attribute; not valid.

Oaths on Specific Date Types: Effect of Change in State
Ruling and reasoning for oaths on dates when the type changes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths when the described object changes state and explain the underlying principle of restriction by description.

Core Idea
An oath tied to a specific description is not broken when the description changes.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn, with commentary by al-Maydani, addresses a common scenario: a person swears an oath concerning a specific type or condition of dates (busr, rutab, tamr). If the dates change from one type to another, does eating them break the oath? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: the oath is restricted to the description at the time of swearing. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) swearing not to eat from a specific batch of busr that later becomes rutab or tamr, and (2) swearing absolutely not to eat busr and then eating rutab. Identify each scenario and its ruling.
Focus on the change in description, not the essence.
2
Extract the ruling for each
In both cases, the ruling is that the oath is not broken (لا حنث). State this clearly for each scenario.
The consequence is no kaffarah required.
3
Identify the common principle
The author gives the reason: 'because these descriptions are the cause of the oath, so the oath is restricted by them.' Understand that the descriptions (busr, rutab, tamr) are the motivating factor for the oath, so the oath applies only to the specific description that existed when the oath was taken.
This is a principle of interpretation based on 'urf and niyyah.
4
Understand the Hanafi reasoning (talil)
The reasoning is based on the idea that oaths are understood according to the specific attributes that prompted them. If the attribute no longer exists, the object of the oath has changed, and the oath does not apply. This is a form of qiyas: the 'illa is the description, and once it disappears, the hukm of the oath ceases.
Argument type: talil (reasoning by effective cause).
5
Note the inter-madhhab variance
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may hold that the oath is broken because the substance is essentially the same. The Hanafi position is different, and this step is important for understanding the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.

Oath on Mixed Dates: The Mudhannab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of mixed dates in oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether eating a mixed date (mudhannab) breaks an oath, identify the intra-madhhab disagreement, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
A date containing even a trace of the forbidden type breaks the oath — the Hanafi mufta bihi holds that the presence of both parts, not the predominant part, determines the ruling.

Oaths regarding specific types of dates can be tricky when the date is not purely one stage. The Hanafi school discusses a special category called 'mudhannab' — a date at the transition between busr (unripe) and rutab (ripe). This lesson examines whether eating such a mixed date violates an oath sworn against one or both types, and highlights a disagreement between the founding imams.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: eating a mudhannab date (whether busr with a little rutab or rutab with a little busr) breaks an oath sworn against either type or both.
The source explicitly says 'حنث عند أبي حنيفة ومحمد' (he breaks his oath according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that Abu Yusuf dissents: he holds that the oath is not broken because the predominant part determines the identity of the date.
Abu Yusuf's position is 'لا يحنث' (he does not break his oath).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Reconstruct Abu Yusuf's reasoning: a date is named by its majority; a busr mudhannab is still called busr, so it does not constitute eating rutab.
The source does not quote Abu Yusuf's dalil directly, but it is inferred from the disagreement.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Examine the majority's rationale: the busr mudhannab contains a portion of rutab, and vice versa. Since 'each one is intended in eating' (وكل واحد مقصود في الأكل), the act of eating encompasses both types, thus violating the oath.
This focus on intention and the physical presence of the forbidden substance is key.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, kaffarah is required; under Abu Yusuf, it is not. The mufta bihi is the majority view, relied upon by later Hanafi authorities.
The text ends with 'والصحيح قولهما' (the correct view is the view of the two).

Oath Not to Eat Meat: Does Not Include Fish
Ruling and proof that 'meat' in oaths does not include fish by custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether eating fish breaks an oath not to eat meat, explain the basis in 'urf, and distinguish the relied-upon position from the minority qiyas-based view.

Core Idea
Oaths are interpreted by common custom, not by Quranic usage or lexical definitions.

This passage addresses a classic Hanafi principle: when someone swears not to eat 'meat', does that include fish? The answer hinges on the definition of 'urf (custom). Even though the Quran calls fish 'fresh meat' (16:14), the Hanafi school holds that oaths are governed by what people customarily mean, not by Quranic terminology. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proof from custom, and the intra-madhhab difference with Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the act
The oath is 'I will not eat meat' (la aklu lahman). The act is eating fish (samak). Determine whether the act falls under the prohibition of the oath.
The key is the definition of 'meat' in the oath.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to the relied-upon position (mufta bihi), the oath is not broken (la yahinth). Fish is not considered 'meat' in common custom.
The hukm is sahih (valid oath, no violation).
3
Identify the dalil
The evidence is 'urf (custom). The term 'lahm' in everyday usage does not include fish. Oaths are built on custom, not on Quranic or lexical definitions.
The proof is not a text but a recognized principle in Hanafi usul.
4
Distinguish from Quranic usage
Even though the Quran calls fish 'lahman tariyyan' (fresh meat) in Surah al-Nahl (16:14), this does not affect the oath because oaths are based on 'urf, not Quranic wording.
This refutes a potential objection from a student relying on the Quranic verse.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Abu Yusuf (in one narration) and al-Isbijabi held by qiyas that eating fish would break the oath. However, the relied-upon view is the apparent ruling (zahir al-riwaya) adopted by al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and others.
The practical consequence affects kaffarah (expiation) for a broken oath.

Core Idea

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from al-Quduri's matn regarding the spoils taken by a small raiding party that enters enemy territory without the authority of the Imam. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between state-sanctioned warfare (ghazwah) and private raids, which affects the obligation of khums (one-fifth tax). This short passage from al-Hidayah clarifies the extent of permissible looking between women. Commentary
This short passage gives the ruling for a woman looking at another woman. The author states that a woman may look at another woman's body to the same extent that a man may look at a man's body. That is, she may see the parts that are not ‘awrah. The reason is that there is similarity in gender (mujanasah), and lust is generally absent between women. Additionally, there is often a practical need for women to uncover (e.g., during bathing or medical treatment) in front of each other. The source is al-Hidayah. Thus, the ‘awrah of a woman in front of another woman is the same as the ‘awrah of a man in front of a man: from navel to knee.

Key Terms
المجانسة (al-mujanasah): Shared gender or similarity between individuals, which reduces the likelihood of sexual attraction.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling equates woman-woman looking to man-man looking due to similarity and necessity, making the ‘awrah between women the same as between men: from navel to knee.
