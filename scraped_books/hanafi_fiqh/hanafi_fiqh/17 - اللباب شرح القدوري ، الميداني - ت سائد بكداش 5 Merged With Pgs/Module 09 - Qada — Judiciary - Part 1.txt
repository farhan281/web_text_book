Definition of Claim (Da'wa) in Hanafi Law
Defines the term 'da'wa' linguistically and legally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definitions of da'wa and distinguish between the claimant and defendant in a judicial context.

Core Idea
A da'wa is not just any assertion; it is a formal assertion of a right before a judge, with the claimant bearing the burden of proof.

Precise legal terminology is crucial in Islamic judicial proceedings. In the Book of Claims and Evidences, the Hanafi jurist al-Quduri begins by defining the central term 'da'wa' (claim). This lesson unpacks that definition, showing how it moves from a general linguistic meaning to a specific legal one that requires a judicial setting.

Guided Path
1
Morphological parsing
Identify the word 'da'wa' and note its morphological features: it is like 'fatwa', feminine, not nunated. Its plural is 'da'awa' or 'da'awi'.
This helps avoid common spelling mistakes.
2
Extract the linguistic definition
Find the sentence: 'قول يقصد به الإنسان إيجاب حق على غيره' – a statement by which a person intends to affirm a right over another.
This definition focuses on intention and claim of right, regardless of forum.
3
Extract the legal definition
Find the sentence: 'إخبار بحق له على غيره عند الحاكم' – an informing of a right he has over another before the judge.
The key addition is 'before the judge' (عند الحاكم), making the judicial context explicit.
4
Compare the two definitions
Note that the linguistic definition is broader (any statement asserting a right). The legal definition narrows it to assertions made before a judge, implying a formal legal process.
This distinction is essential for understanding later rules on evidence and burden of proof.
5
Introduce claimant and defendant
The passage explains that because the subject of claims requires knowledge of who is the claimant (mudda'i) and who is the defendant (mudda'a alayh), the author defines these terms next. The distinction is based on who would benefit if the statement is true.
The claimant bears the burden of proof; the defendant may take an oath.

Definition of Plaintiff and Defendant
Defines plaintiff and defendant with a key example from deposits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of plaintiff and defendant and apply the principle of prioritizing meaning over form to identify the true plaintiff and defendant in a given scenario.

Core Idea
In Hanafi judicial procedure, the defendant is the denier, determined by substantive meaning, not formal pleading.

This lesson defines two fundamental terms in Islamic court procedure: the plaintiff (al-mudda‘ī) and the defendant (al-mudda‘ā ‘alayhi). The Hanafi school uniquely identifies the defendant as the one who denies the claim, regardless of who initiates the lawsuit. Through a classic deposit example, the lesson shows how meaning overrides form in legal analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
The author first presents a common definition: plaintiff seeks what is contrary to the apparent, defendant holds to the apparent. Then he cites Imam Muhammad: defendant is simply the denier (al-munkir).
Note that the second definition is more operational.
2
Understand the principle of priority of meaning
The commentator stresses that Hanafi jurists prioritize meanings over forms. This principle guides the identification of plaintiff and defendant.
This is a key usul point in judicial procedure.
3
Analyze the deposit example
If the depositary says 'I returned the deposit,' he is formally making a claim (that he discharged his duty), but substantively he is denying liability for loss. Therefore, he is the denier (defendant) and benefits from the oath.
The ruling is that his statement is accepted on oath.
4
Derive the ruling
The practical outcome: the depositary, though plaintiff in form, is treated as defendant. The burden shifts; he takes an oath and the claim is dismissed if he swears.
This shows the real-world impact of the definition.

Conditions for Accepting a Legal Claim (Da‘wā) in the Hanafi Court
Ruling with rational proof for claim specificity in Hanafi qada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two essential requirements for a valid claim (genus and quantity), apply the rule to scenarios involving present or absent objects, and explain the rational basis for the specificity requirement.

Core Idea
A claim must specify genus and quantity — compulsion regarding the unknown is impossible.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, a plaintiff's claim must be precise for the court to act. This passage from Al-Hidayah sets out the fundamental conditions: the claimed item must be known in its genus (jins) and quantity (qadr). The author then addresses how to handle the claimed object when it is physically present or absent, providing a complete procedural rule grounded in rational necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fundamental requirement
Read the first sentence: the claim is not accepted until the plaintiff mentions something known in genus and quantity. Note the two elements: jins (category like wheat, gold) and qadr (measure like qafiz, dirham).
The requirement is wajib; without it the claim is dismissed.
2
Recall the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The text gives the reason: the benefit (fa'ida) of a claim is to compel (ilzam) through proof; compulsion regarding the unknown (majhul) cannot be realized. Understand that vagueness defeats the purpose of litigation.
This is a pure rational argument (dalil aqli), showing that fiqh is built on maqasid.
3
Distinguish present vs. absent object
If the claimed item is a tangible object in the defendant's hand, the defendant must produce it so the plaintiff can point to it, witnesses can testify, and the defendant can be sworn. If the item is not present (destroyed or too costly to transport), the plaintiff states its value, which is a known measure.
Pointing is the most effective identification for movable property; value substitutes when the object is unavailable.
4
Recognize the mufta bihi and authority
The ruling is from al-Hidayah, transmitting the established Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah). The mufta bihi is that the claim must specify both genus and quantity; otherwise it is rejected. Note that other schools may have different specificity requirements.
This rule is a procedural safeguard against frivolous or uncertain claims.

Conditions for Claiming Real Estate in Hanafi Law
Explaining the three conditions for a real estate claim and a dispute on boundaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a valid real estate claim in Hanafi law and apply the ruling that three boundaries suffice for specification.

Core Idea
A valid claim for real estate requires definition, proven possession, and explicit demand — and three boundaries are enough.

In Hanafi procedural law, a claim for real estate (ʿaqār) requires specific formalities because the property cannot be physically produced in court. This lesson unpacks the three essential conditions derived from al-Hidayah: taḥdīd (boundary definition), proof of possession (yad), and explicit demand (muṭālabah). We also examine the intra-Hanafi dispute over the number of boundaries required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
From the passage, extract the three conditions the plaintiff must satisfy: (1) define the property by boundaries, (2) state it is in defendant's possession, (3) demand it. These are cumulative.
Memorize these as the three pillars of a real estate claim.
2
Analyze the boundary requirement and the dispute
The plaintiff must mention boundaries (ḥudūd). The text says he should mention all four, but according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, three suffice. Understand the reasoning: error in the fourth changes the claim, omission does not. Contrast with Zufar's view requiring all four.
This is a key intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi position is three boundaries.
3
Understand the possession proof requirement
The plaintiff must assert that the defendant is in possession. However, mere acknowledgment by the defendant is insufficient. Possession must be proven by evidence (bayyinah) or the judge's knowledge (ʿilm al-qāḍī) to prevent collusion (muwāḍaʿa).
Compare with movable property where possession is observable.
4
Understand the demand requirement and its rationale
The plaintiff must explicitly demand the property. This is necessary because the property could be held as collateral (marhūn) or detained for the price (maḥbūs bi al-thaman). The demand removes these possibilities.
For movables, the plaintiff adds 'without right' to negate any lawful holding.

Judge's Response to Confession or Denial After a Valid Claim
The judge's procedure after a valid claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi judge's procedure after a valid claim and explain why confession is accepted without evidence while denial triggers the plaintiff's burden of proof.

Core Idea
Confession suffices; denial requires evidence.

This passage from Al-Lubab lays out the immediate steps a Hanafi judge must take once a plaintiff's claim is validly established. It first clarifies that for a debt claim (right in the dhimmah), the plaintiff must explicitly demand payment. Then the judge interrogates the defendant: if he confesses, judgment is entered; if he denies, the plaintiff must produce evidence. The rationale is that a person's confession against himself is trusted, but denial shifts the burden to the claimant.

Guided Path
1
Confirm the claim's validity regarding a debt
The text specifies that if the claim is a right in the dhimmah (e.g., a debt), the plaintiff must state that he demands payment. This is because the debtor is present and only the demand remains. Identify this condition before proceeding.
This step applies only to claims involving a debt or obligation in the dhimmah.
2
Identify the judge's first action
Once the claim is valid, the judge must ask the defendant about it. He does not immediately ask for evidence or dismiss the case. Recognize that the judge's role is to uncover the truth by first hearing the defendant's response.
3
Apply the ruling for confession
If the defendant confesses (iʿtirāf), the judge gives judgment against him. The reason is that a person is not considered suspect regarding his own right; his admission is trusted.
Confession must be clear and voluntary; the judge should verify it is not coerced.
4

The Sahibayn's view expands the scope of oath-administering, but still exempts hudud.

Under Abu Hanifa, fewer oaths are administered; under the Sahibayn, more oaths are administered. This affects the likelihood of the defendant being compelled to swear, which can impact the outcome of the case.

Core Idea
Earlier date or possession determines priority in conflicting purchase claims; otherwise each takes half with option.

This passage from the Book of Claims and Evidence addresses a common legal scenario: two parties each claiming to have bought the same item from the same vendor. The Hanafi school provides a structured approach to resolve such disputes, balancing evidence, timing, and physical possession. Identify the scenario and the basic ruling

A defendant may be ordered to provide a guarantor or accept surveillance to secure the claimant's witness — based on istihsan.

When a claimant declares he has a present witness, the judge may order the defendant to provide a guarantor of his person for up to three days or, failing that, to be accompanied by the claimant to ensure his presence. This ruling is unique to the Hanafi school and is justified through istihsan (juristic preference), balancing the claimant's right to secure his proof against the defendant's liberty. Core Idea
A traveler defendant need only be accompanied for the judge's session — protecting him from undue hardship.

The general rule requiring a guarantor or surveillance for a defendant who has a present witness against him is relaxed if the defendant is a traveler (gharib) on the road. To prevent excessive harm that would delay his journey, the Hanafi school limits the surveillance to the duration of the judge's session and does not require a guarantor beyond that. The reason (illah) for the exception is to avoid harm (darar) to the traveler by preventing him from continuing his journey. The limited surveillance does not cause significant harm, so it is allowed.
The text says 'لأن في أخذ الكفيل والملازمة زيادة على ذلك: إضراراً به بمنعه عن السفر'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a defendant's claim that property is from an absent person and explain the condition regarding witnesses' knowledge, including the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on knowing the absent person by face.

Core Idea
A defendant's claim of deposit from an absent person requires witnesses who know him; Imam Muhammad adds knowledge by face.

In property disputes, a defendant may respond to a claim of ownership by asserting that the property actually belongs to an absent third party. This lesson examines the Hanafi conditions for accepting such a defensive claim, focusing on the required knowledge of the absent person by witnesses. The source text presents the majority view and an additional condition from Imam Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defendant's claim scenario
The defendant asserts that the disputed property (movable or real estate) is a deposit, loan, lease, pledge, or usurped item from an absent person (gha'ib). The defendant must produce evidence to support this claim.
The claim transfers liability from the defendant to the absent person.
2
State the required evidence
The defendant must bring witnesses (bayyinah) who can testify that the property belongs to the absent person. The witnesses must know the absent person.
The witnesses are testifying to the fact of deposit, not merely the defendant's statement.
3
Analyze the condition for witnesses
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, it is sufficient for witnesses to know the absent person by name and lineage (or by face). Imam Muhammad additionally requires knowledge by face.
This difference affects the admissibility of testimony in practice.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad's stricter condition (knowledge by face) is a minority view within the Hanafi school. The majority holds that name/lineage or face is sufficient.
The mufta bihi (operative opinion) is the more lenient view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (name/lineage sufficient) is relied upon for fatwa, as it is easier to satisfy and avoids hardship.
The source text references al-Bazzaziyyah for further detail.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is administering an oath by divorce or manumission in a legal setting. The ruling is that it is prohibited (makruh tahrimi) according to the zahir al-riwaya.
The prohibition applies to the judge or official, not the defendant.
2
Recognize the intra-madhhab difference
Some later Hanafi scholars, like Qadi Khan (d. 592 AH), permitted such oaths in their time. Note that this was a minority view.
The correct and relied-upon position remains the prohibition.
3
Apply the ruling to the nukul scenario
If a defendant is forced to swear by divorce/manumission and refuses (nukul), the judge cannot rule against him because the oath itself is prohibited.
Ordinary nukul leads to an adverse judgment, but here the refusal is of something prohibited.

The reason is that the defendant is only declining to do something prohibited by the Shari'a—swearing by divorce or manumission. Thus, no adverse judgment follows. The ruling is based on the principle that such oaths are not legitimate means of asserting truth in legal proceedings.

Oaths for Non-Muslim Litigants: Formulas and Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Form of oath for non-Muslim litigants and intra-madhhab difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the oath formula for Jewish, Christian, Magian, and polytheist litigants, and identify the intra-madhhab difference regarding Magians.

Core Idea
Swearing a non-Muslim by Allah alone suffices, but the school recommends adding a reference to their scripture or belief for emphasis.

In Hanafi judicial practice, oaths are administered to non-Muslim litigants with formulas that reflect their beliefs, such as mentioning the Torah for Jews or the Injeel for Christians. However, a simple oath 'by Allah' is always valid, as even polytheists acknowledge Allah as creator. The passage also presents a difference of opinion within the school about whether Magians should be sworn by Allah and fire or by Allah alone.

1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage deals with the judicial act of administering an oath to a non-Muslim litigant. The ruling is that specific formulas are recommended (sunnah muakkadah) but a simple 'by Allah' is sufficient.

List the standard formulas for each group
Jew: 'By Allah who sent down the Torah to Moses.' Christian: 'By Allah who sent down the Injeel to Jesus.' Magian: 'By Allah who created fire.' Polytheist: 'By Allah alone' – citing Quran 43:87.

Oath Formulations in Sale and Usurpation Claims
Rule specifying the wording of the defendant's oath in sale and usurpation claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct Hanafi oath formulations for sale and usurpation claims and explain why the oath denies the current legal consequence rather than the past act.

Core Idea
Deny the current right, not the past act, to avoid false oaths.

In Hanafi judiciary, when a defendant denies a claim, the judge administers an oath with precise wording. This lesson focuses on sale and usurpation claims. The key insight: the oath must target the present legal effect (e.g., existence of a valid contract) rather than the historical action (e.g., 'I did not sell'), because the past act might have been nullified by subsequent events like rescission or compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim type
Determine whether the dispute concerns a sale (e.g., purchase of a slave) or usurpation (ghaṣb). This dictates the correct oath formulation.
Sale claims involve a contract; usurpation involves unlawful possession.
2
Identify the current legal consequence the claimant asserts
For sale: the claimant asserts an existing valid contract. For usurpation: the claimant asserts a current right to the return of the property.
The oath must deny this present right, not the past act.
3
Formulate the denial of the current consequence
For sale: 'By Allah, there is no existing sale between you two regarding this item.' For usurpation: 'By Allah, you are not entitled to its return from me.'
The wording directly negates the current legal claim.
4
Contrast with the prohibited formulation
The judge must NOT swear the defendant: 'By Allah, I did not sell' or 'By Allah, I did not usurp,' because those deny past actions, which could be true even if the current right no longer exists.
Prohibited formulations expose the defendant to potential perjury.
5
Explain the rationale (talīl)
The sale may have been rescinded (e.g., by option or mutual agreement), so the defendant can truthfully swear no sale exists even if a sale once occurred. Similarly, a usurped item may have been given as a gift or its value compensated, extinguishing the return duty.
This reasoning protects the oath's sanctity.

Commentary
This passage details the exact wording of the oath to be administered by the judge in four common types of claims: sale, usurpation (ghasb), marriage, and divorce. The key principle is that the oath must be phrased to deny the current legal consequence rather than the past act. For a claim of sale of a slave for 1,000, the defendant is sworn: 'There is no existing sale between you two regarding this slave.' He is not sworn: 'I did not sell,' because it is possible he sold but later the sale was rescinded (by mutual agreement or when the option of stipulation was exercised). Similarly, in usurpation, he swears: 'You are not entitled to its return from me' rather than 'I did not usurp,' because the usurped item might have been given as a gift later or damages paid, so the duty to return no longer exists. In marriage, he swears: 'There is no existing marriage between you two at present' rather than 'I did not marry her,' because there might have been a divorce (especially a irrevocable one) that ended the marriage. In divorce claims, he swears: 'She is not irrevocably divorced from you now as you claimed' rather than 'I did not divorce her,' because he might have divorced her but then revoked (in a revocable divorce) or the divorce might have been conditional. The aim is to avoid false oaths: the defendant can swear truthfully about the current nonexistence of the right, even if the past act did occur but was later voided.
