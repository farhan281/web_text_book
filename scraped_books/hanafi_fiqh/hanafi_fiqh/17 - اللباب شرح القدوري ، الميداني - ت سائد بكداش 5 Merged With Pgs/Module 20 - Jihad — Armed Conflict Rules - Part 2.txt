The Validity of Aman Given by Any Free Muslim
Ruling on the contract of aman and its proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that any free Muslim (man or woman) can grant valid aman, and explain the hadith proof and its implication.

Core Idea
Any free Muslim can grant protection to disbelievers, and it binds all Muslims.

This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling on the contract of protection (aman). According to the school, any free Muslim – whether male or female – can grant aman to one or more disbelievers, or even to an entire city. Once granted, no Muslim may harm the protected persons or their property. The proof is the hadith: 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection on behalf of them all.' This hadith establishes the binding nature of aman given by any free Muslim. The passage also provides a rationale: the granter is capable of fighting (ahl al-mana‘ah), so his/her aman is effective, and it extends to others because faith is indivisible.

Guided Path
1
Identify who can grant aman
Note that the text specifies: a free man or a free woman can grant aman. This is a broad category – any free Muslim, not just the ruler or commander.
2
Determine the scope of aman
Aman can be given to an individual, a group, or the people of an entire fort or city. It is not limited in number.
3
Analyze the legal effect
Once aman is validly given, it is impermissible for any Muslim to kill the protected persons or to interfere with their belongings. The aman is binding on the entire Muslim community.
4
Examine the dalil
The hadith 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection on behalf of them all' is the primary evidence. It shows that even the least ranking Muslim's aman is valid and must be honored by all.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hanafi school on spoils of war and the contract of protection (aman). It first addresses the participation of reinforcements (al-madad) who arrive after the main army. The ruling states that if reinforcements join the army in Dar al-Harb before the spoils are brought to Dar al-Islam, or before division or sale of the spoils, they share in the spoils. The reason given is that they participated in jihad before the ownership of the spoils was settled for the original army. Therefore, the right of participation ceases once the spoils are secured (ihraz) or divided by the Imam in Dar al-Harb, as ownership is then complete. Next, the passage discusses the people of the army market (ahl suq al-‘askar)—those who accompany the army for trade or services but do not fight. They have no right to spoils unless they actually engage in combat, because they lacked the intention to fight, which is the apparent cause for entitlement. The commentator clarifies that entitlement is based on one’s actual state (as a horseman or foot soldier) at the time of fighting. The second half of the passage covers the contract of protection (aman). According to Hanafi law, any free Muslim—whether man or woman—may grant aman to one or more disbelievers, or to an entire fort or city, and it is valid and binding on all Muslims. The reason is that such individuals are capable of fighting (ahl al-mana‘ah), so their aman is effective. The validity of aman is analogized to the guardianship of marriage: just as faith is indivisible, so is the ability to grant protection. Once aman is given, no Muslim may kill the protected persons or interfere with their property. The proof is the hadith: 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection on behalf of them all.' This indicates that even the least Muslim can bind the community with aman.

Validity of Aman Granted by Free Muslims
Ruling on the validity of aman and its binding effect on all Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on aman from free Muslims, explain the proof from hadith, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Any free Muslim can grant aman that binds all Muslims — the lowest can protect the highest.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a crucial ruling in the law of jihad: who can grant safety (aman) to a disbeliever? The Hanafi school takes an expansive view, holding that even the least-ranking free Muslim can bind the entire community. The proof is a famous hadith. We will examine the ruling, its evidence, and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
What exactly does the passage declare valid? Who can grant aman, and to whom? Note the categories: free man, free woman, group, fort, city.
The ruling applies to any free Muslim, male or female.
2
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The text gives two reasons: (1) the granter is among those who fight, so he has authority; (2) faith is indivisible, so the ability to grant aman is not partial. Analogize to wilayat al-nikah.
The indivisibility argument is unique to Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection on behalf of them all' is cited. Identify how it proves the ruling: 'the lowest' includes women and free men without official position.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qati' al-dalalah).
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school requires the ruler's permission for aman from non-rulers. The Hanafi response uses the same hadith to show that the Prophet (PBUH) did not restrict it.
The thamarah al-khilaf: binding effect differs.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Imagine a Muslim woman in a besieged city grants safety to an enemy soldier. According to the Hanafi view, all Muslims must honor that guarantee. What would be the consequence of violating it?
Violating aman is a major sin and nullifies the covenant.

The Validity and Binding Effect of Aman (Safe Conduct)
Ruling on aman granted by free Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state who may grant aman, explain its binding effect on all Muslims, and identify the exceptions based on the Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
Any free Muslim, man or woman, can grant aman; it binds all Muslims based on the hadith.

This lesson unpacks a core rule from the Hanafi fiqh of siyar (military expeditions): the granting of aman (safe conduct) to non-Muslims. The passage clarifies who is qualified to grant it, why it is binding on the entire Muslim community, and under what circumstances it may be revoked. We will examine the textual proof and the legal reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (aman granted by a free Muslim) and its legal status (sahih/valid).
The text explicitly states 'صح أمانهم'.
2
Examine the conditions for the grantor
Who can grant aman? The text specifies a free man or a free woman. Consider why other categories (dhimmi, prisoner, merchant) are excluded.
Conditions: free, Muslim, of sound mind (implied).
3
Analyze the dalil (proof)
Locate the hadith cited: 'المسلمون تتكافأ دماؤهم، ويسعى بذمتهم أدناهم'. Understand how this proves the ruling, focusing on the phrase 'adnāhum'.
The hadith is ahad, zanni in thubut and dalala, but accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the scope and binding effect
Once aman is valid, no Muslim may kill or harm the protected persons. The protection extends to their property and affects all Muslims.
The ruling is collective – the aman of one Muslim binds the whole community.
5
Note the exception: Imam's revocation
If granting aman leads to mafsada (harm), the Imam may revoke it (yanbidhu ilayhim). Understand the condition and procedure.
Revocation requires notification to the enemy; it is not arbitrary.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Siyar (military expeditions) addresses the ruling of granting aman (safe conduct) to non-Muslims. It is a ruling with proof. The text states that a free Muslim man or woman may grant aman to a single disbeliever, a group, or even the inhabitants of a fortress or city. Their aman is valid and binding on all Muslims—no Muslim may kill those protected or take their property. The reason given is that such a Muslim is capable of fighting (ahl al-qital) and thus has the authority to grant aman. Moreover, since the cause of aman (faith) is indivisible, the aman itself is complete, analogous to the guardianship (wilayah) in marriage which cannot be partial. The proof is the hadith: 'Muslims are equal in blood, and the lowest of them can grant protection' (al-muslimūn tatakāfa'u dimā'uhum, wa yas'ā bi-dhimmahum adnāhum). The phrase 'adnāhum' means the lowest or least significant, indicating that even a single Muslim's grant of aman is effective. An exception is made if granting aman leads to harm (mafsadah) for Muslims; in that case, the Imam may revoke the aman (yanbidhu ilayhim) by declaring it null. Additionally, the aman of a dhimmi (a protected non-Muslim under Islamic rule), a prisoner, or a merchant entering enemy lands (tājir yadkhulu 'alayhim) is not valid. The dhimmi is suspected of bias, and the prisoner and merchant are under constraint (maqhūrān) and thus lack the authority to grant protection.

Key Terms
الأمان (al-amān): Safe conduct or protection granted to a non-Muslim, guaranteeing security of life and property.
الذمة (al-dhimmah): Covenant of protection; the guarantee of safety and rights granted to non-Muslims under Islamic rule.
النَّبذ (al-nabdhu): Revocation of aman by the Imam when continuation would cause harm (mafsadah) to Muslims.
أهل القتال (ahl al-qital): People of fighting; those capable of warfare and defense.
المفسدة (al-mafsadah): Harm or detriment to the community; a reason for revoking aman.

Invalidity of Aman from Dhimmi, Captive, and Merchant
States ruling that aman is invalid from dhimmi, captive, and merchant, with reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid aman and identify why aman from a dhimmi, captive, or merchant is invalid.

Core Idea
Aman requires the granter to be feared—a dhimmi, captive, or merchant lacks this, so their aman is void.

Aman (safe conduct) is a vital instrument in jihad, allowing enemy combatants to enter Muslim territory safely. However, not every Muslim can grant valid aman. This lesson examines three categories of people whose aman is rejected by the Hanafi school: the dhimmi (protected non-Muslim), the captive, and the merchant entering enemy lands. Understanding their exclusion clarifies the fundamental condition that the granter must be capable of causing fear (shawka) in the enemy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the validity of aman when granted by a dhimmi, a captive, or a merchant. Determine the hukm level (batil/invalid) and the specific categories mentioned.
The ruling is explicit: 'لا يجوز' meaning it is not permissible/valid.
2
Locate the proof
The text provides two rational proofs: (1) the granter is under the enemy's control, so the enemy does not fear them; (2) if captives/merchants could grant aman, the enemy would exploit them to escape, preventing conquest. Identify these arguments.
The proof is aqli (rational), not naqli (textual), based on the purpose of aman.
3
Analyze the condition for aman
Aman is 'specific to the place of fear' (يختص بمحل الخوف). This means the granter must be someone the enemy fears, i.e., a free Muslim with military or political authority. A dhimmi, captive, or merchant lacks this station.
Even a single free Muslim can grant aman because he represents the Muslim community and is feared by the enemy.
4
Consider the exception
The passage begins with 'إلا أن تكون في ذلك مفسدة'—if granting aman leads to corruption, the imam may repudiate it. This applies even if the aman was initially valid. Note the difference between invalidity and repudiation.
The exception is about dissolving a valid aman, not about the initial invalidity discussed here.
5
Apply to new scenarios
Extend the ruling: Would aman from a Muslim woman with political influence be valid? According to the Hanafi school, yes, because she is a free Muslim capable of causing fear. Contrast with a slave or a child.
The key is the granter's ability to be feared by the enemy.

Core Idea
Whoever takes property of ahl al-harb owns it – even if the taker is also a non-Muslim.

This lesson examines a unique ruling: when non-Muslims (e.g., Turks) conquer other non-Muslims (e.g., Romans) and seize their property, do they legally own it? The Hanafi answer is yes. The passage from al-Lubab explains the reasoning: the property of ahl al-harb is mubah (permissible), so ownership is established by physical possession. Jizyah Falls with Conversion or Death
States that jizyah falls due to Islam or death of the dhimmi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of conversion or death on jizyah and explain the legal analogy (qiyas) used to justify it.

Core Idea
Jizyah, being a punishment for unbelief, is cancelled by Islam or death—just like a capital sentence.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a key question: what happens to the jizyah obligation when a dhimmi converts to Islam or dies? The Hanafi school holds that it drops entirely, even if the full year has passed. To understand why, we must grasp the nature of jizyah as a substitute for killing and the analogy drawn to capital punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The matn states: 'Whoever becomes Muslim while jizyah is due on him, it falls from him.' Note the unconditionality: it falls even after the year completes.
The sharh extends this to death: 'likewise if he dies.'
2
Understand the underlying principle
The sharh explains: 'Jizyah is for the cancellation of killing.' Thus, those not subject to killing (slaves, women, etc.) are not liable for jizyah. The obligation is rooted in the threat of death, which is lifted by conversion.
This explains the exemption of slaves: they lack the capacity for war, so no killing threat exists.
3
Identify the temporal condition
Eligibility is assessed at the time the Imam imposes jizyah. If someone later gains capacity (freed, reaches puberty, recovers sanity), jizyah is not imposed until the next year.
This prevents retroactive liability.
4
Trace the qiyas argument
The proof: jizyah is wajib as a punishment (for unbelief), and punishment is dropped by Islam, analogously to capital punishment (qatl). When a dhimmi converts, the death penalty is no longer applicable, so jizyah—its substitute—also falls.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) argue that jizyah for the completed year remains due, as it is compensation for protection already provided. The Hanafi response emphasizes its punitive nature, not compensatory.
The practical difference: under the Hanafi view, conversion even on the last day of the year cancels the entire year's jizyah.

The Inviolability of the Dhimmi's Covenant: Offenses That Do Not Void
Ruling with proof on the inviolability of the dhimmi covenant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the dhimmi's covenant is not voided by certain major offenses, derive the rational proof (qiyas) for this rule, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the punishment for cursing the Prophet.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's covenant remains intact despite grave sins; original disbelief did not void it, so later sins cannot either.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies that certain serious crimes committed by a dhimmi—such as refusing to pay jizyah, murder, or cursing the Prophet—do not break the covenant of protection. Instead, the dhimmi is punished for each crime while his protected status continues. The reasoning is a powerful qiyas: since the dhimmi's original disbelief (kufr) did not prevent the contract, a later sin cannot have a stronger effect. We will also explore an important intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a habitual curser should be killed or only disciplined.

Guided Path
1
List the Offenses
Identify the seven offenses listed in the text that a dhimmi might commit: refusing to pay jizyah, killing a Muslim, tempting a Muslim from his religion, highway robbery, cursing the Prophet, cursing the Quran, cursing Islam, and adultery with a Muslim woman.
All these are grave sins, yet the text rules they do not void the covenant.
2
State the Core Ruling
The core ruling is that the covenant (ahd) remains valid despite these offenses. The dhimmi is punished individually but his status is preserved.
This is the opposite of what one might assume—that such crimes would expel him from dhimmah.
3
Understand the Proof (Dalil)
The proof is a rational analogy (qiyas): the dhimmi's original disbelief (kufr muqarin) did not prevent the covenant from being established, so a subsequent sin (which is similar in nature) cannot cancel it. This is a form of istihsan and qiyas al-awlawi (a fortiori).
The text states: لأن كفره المقارِنَ لم يَمْنَع العهد، فالطارئ لا يَرْفَعُه
4
Distinguish Punishment from Covenant Status
Note that although the covenant is not voided, the dhimmi is still punished: jizyah is forcibly collected, qisas applies for murder, hadd for adultery, and ta'dib for cursing.
The contract is not a free pass; it only means the dhimmi remains a dhimmi.
5
Analyze the Ikhtilaf on Cursing the Prophet
Earlier Hanafi authorities (al-Hawi) held that cursing is punished with ta'dib (discretionary punishment, not death). Later scholars (Ibn al-Humam, al-Khayr al-Ramli) ruled that a habitual curser should be killed, and a sultanic decree adopted this view. The mufta bihi is killing if habitual.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by later authority and state order.

Ruling on Initiating Fight Against Rebels: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on initiating war against rebels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi positions on initiating combat against rebels and identify which view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Do not initiate fighting against rebels until they start — but some Hanafis disagree when they gather and fortify.

This passage from Sharh al-Quduri presents a clear ruling: the imam should not initiate fighting against rebels (bughat) until the rebels start. The reasoning is to exhaust excuses and establish proof, following the example of Ali (RA) who sent a debater to the Khawarij at Harura before battle. However, an intra-Hanafi dispute arises: the scholar Khwaharzade argues that if rebels gather and fortify, it is permissible to initiate because the effective cause (dalil) is their assembly and refusal. This lesson unpacks both positions and identifies the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling from the matn
Notice the phrase 'ولا يبدؤهم بقتال حتى يبدؤوه' – the imam must not initiate; he waits for the rebels to start fighting.
This is the default ruling in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
2
Understand the rationale behind the base ruling
The text gives two reasons: i'dhar (providing excuse) and ihtijaj (establishing proof). Ali's action is cited as the prophetic model.
The goal is to ensure that war only occurs after all peaceful means are exhausted.
3
Examine Khwaharzade's counterposition
Khwaharzade states that it is permissible (ja'iz) to initiate when the rebels gather and fortify, because the ruling is tied to the effective cause (dalil) – their assembly and refusal. Waiting might make defense impossible.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
Al-Hidayah gives precedence to al-Quduri's statement, making the non-initiation view the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Khwaharzade's view is recorded but is considered marjuh (weaker) in the later tradition.

Key Terms
الغنيمة (al-ghanīmah): Spoils of war taken from belligerent disbelievers by force.
الجزية (al-jizyah): A poll tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects (ahl al-dhimma) in an Islamic state.
