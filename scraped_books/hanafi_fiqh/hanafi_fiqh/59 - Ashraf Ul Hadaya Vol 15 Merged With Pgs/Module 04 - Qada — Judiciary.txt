Re-testimony by Absent Heir in Murder Case
Opening ruling on re-testimony by absent heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on whether an absent heir must re-establish testimony when he arrives after another heir has already testified.

Core Idea
An heir who arrives later must present his own testimony anew to claim qisas.

This lesson examines a key procedural question in murder cases: if one heir testifies successfully, does a later-arriving heir benefit from that testimony or must he start afresh? The Hanafi school addresses this via the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the opening ruling: a murdered person has two sons. One son is present and testifies, establishing the killer's identity. Then the second son arrives. The question is whether the second son must repeat the testimony.
2
Recall the legal principle on joint rights
The right to qisas belongs to all heirs collectively. The testimony of one heir does not automatically bind the others; each heir's claim is independent.
3
Apply the Hanafi ruling on re-testimony
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must re-establish the testimony when he arrives, because his right to demand qisas is separate. The Sahibayn hold that the initial testimony suffices for both, as the killer's liability is established.
The passage only mentions the existence of the ikhtilaf without detailing the proofs.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
Although not explicitly stated, the later Hanafi position often favours the Sahibayn's view for practical ease, but the text here merely presents the dispute.

Forgiveness and Testimony with Three Heirs in Murder
Rulings on forgiveness proof and testimony with three heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling on testimony and forgiveness when three heirs are involved, including the killer's proof of forgiveness and the division of blood money.

Core Idea
A killer may prove an absent heir's forgiveness to drop qisas; testimony of one heir about another's forgiveness is void.

This lesson expands on the previous one by introducing additional complexities: three heirs, one of whom forgives the killer, and the killer's ability to prove that forgiveness. The passage provides rulings on the validity of testimony and the distribution of diyat when the killer confirms or denies the testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario with three heirs
There are three heirs. The killer claims that the third heir (absent?) forgave him. The question is how this affects the claims of the other two heirs.
2
Analyse the killer's proof of forgiveness
If the killer brings evidence (bayyina) that the absent heir forgave him, then the present heir's claim drops and qisas is cancelled.
3
Understand the testimony of one heir about another's forgiveness
If one heir testifies that the third heir forgave, that testimony is void (batil) because a witness cannot testify about another's waiver. The forgiveness is attributed to the testifying heir himself.
4
Apply the division rules for diyat
If the killer confirms the testimony of two heirs (i.e., admits they are truthful), diyat is divided between them (with some difference). If he denies both, the third heir (the one who allegedly forgave) gets one-third diyat, and the other two get nothing. If the accused confirms the two heirs, the killer is liable for one-third diyat.

Nature of Qisas Inheritance: Khilafah vs Wirathah
Dispute on whether qisas is inherited or vests directly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between khilafah and wirathah in the context of qisas and state the procedural consequence regarding representation of heirs according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Qisas is not inherited like property — it vests directly in each heir, so one heir cannot represent others.

This lesson delves into a key intra-Hanafi dispute: is the right to qisas inherited from the victim like a debt, or does it arise independently for each heir after death? The answer determines whether one present heir can pursue the claim on behalf of absent heirs. We will examine the proofs of both sides and the practical difference in court procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural issue
Understand the core question: when one heir is present and others absent, can the present heir represent all in establishing the qisas claim?
This hinges on the nature of the right.
2
Define khilafah and wirathah
Wirathah: the right first belongs to the deceased and is inherited. Khilafah: the right vests directly in heirs without ever being owned by the deceased.
This conceptual distinction is key to the dispute.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn analogize qisas to debt and diyah: since it is compensation for the self, the victim owns it, so heirs inherit it via wirathah.
They cite the victim's ability to forgive before death as evidence.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues qisas is a right to an act (qatl), not property; the deceased cannot own an act, so it vests directly in heirs via khilafah.
He uses the net analogy to show the deceased can own property (net catch) but not acts.
5
Apply to the subsidiary ruling
When the killer brings evidence that an absent heir has forgiven, the claim becomes about property (diyah), and the present heir becomes a representative.
This shows the principle: when the right converts to property, wirathah applies.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key procedural dispute within the Hanafi school: when only one heir of a murder victim is present in court and the killer has already confessed (or evidence has been presented regarding liability), can that single present heir pursue the qisas claim on behalf of all other absent heirs, or must each heir appear and establish the claim individually? The Sahibayn hold the former view: qisas is a compensation for the victim's life, so it belongs to the victim in principle, and upon death passes to the heirs via inheritance. Therefore, one heir represents all others, just as one creditor can represent co-creditors in a debt claim. Abu Hanifa holds the latter view: qisas is not a property right but a right to an act (qatl), and the deceased cannot own an act; ownership of qisas arises directly for the heirs after death without passing through the deceased. Hence, each heir must independently establish his claim; one heir cannot represent another. The passage then adds a subsidiary ruling: if the killer brings evidence that the absent heir has forgiven the qisas (converting it to diyah money), then the present heir does become a representative because the claim is now about property (the diyah), not the act. The same applies to a jointly owned slave killed intentionally.

Disagreement on Time, Place, or Weapon Voids Homicide Testimony
Explains when witness disagreement renders testimony void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that witness disagreement on essential details (time, place, weapon) voids testimony in homicide cases and apply it to given scenarios.

Core Idea
If two witnesses disagree on the day, location, or weapon of a killing, their testimony is void—they are testifying to different crimes.

In Islamic court, testimony must be consistent on the core facts. When witnesses describe the same killing in contradictory ways—different days, different cities, or different weapons—the ruling is clear: their testimony is void. In this lesson, we examine the reasoning behind this rule as transmitted from Imam Muhammad in Al-Hidayah, and learn to distinguish essential from minor differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that if the two witnesses differ on the day, the city, or the instrument of killing, the testimony is void (batil).
2
Understand the Reasoning
A killing cannot occur twice; each differing description would constitute a separate incident. Thus, each witness stands alone, providing only one witness per incident, which is insufficient for proof.
The text says 'al-qatl la yu'ādu wa lā yukarraru' (the killing is not repeated nor repeated).

Apply the Analogical Extension
The same rule applies when one witness specifies the weapon and the other professes ignorance; the unrestricted (muṭlaq) and restricted (muqayyad) versions are different, so the testimony is void.
This is explicitly mentioned in the passage.
4
Distinguish Essential vs. Minor Differences
Only differences that alter the legal nature of the killing (e.g., weapon type changes 'amd to shibh al-'amd) void testimony. Minor details like color of clothing do not affect the ruling.
The passage does not discuss nonessential details, but by analogy, they are not included in the rule.

Testimony for Homicide Without Weapon: Istihsan Overriding Qiyas
Contrasts qiyas rejection with istihsan acceptance of testimony when witnesses don't know the weapon

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi istihsan to accept testimony when witnesses do not know the weapon and explain why diyah is imposed rather than qisas.

Core Idea
Absolute killing testimony is actionable; istihsan accepts it and imposes diyah, overriding strict qiyas.

This passage presents a classic Hanafi example of istihsan overriding strict qiyas. Two witnesses testify to a killing but admit ignorance of the weapon used. By qiyas, such testimony would be rejected due to uncertainty. However, istihsan accepts the testimony and imposes diyah, demonstrating the flexibility of Hanafi jurisprudence in achieving equitable outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Two Competing Rulings
The act is testimony regarding a killing where the weapon is unknown. The passage states that by qiyas the testimony is rejected, but by istihsan it is accepted and diyah is imposed.
Qiyas: rejection; Istihsan: acceptance with diyah.
2
Understand the Qiyas Argument
The qiyas argument is that killing differs based on the weapon, so ignorance of the weapon makes the subject of testimony unknown. Therefore, testimony should be rejected.
Missing essential particular (weapon) invalidates testimony.

Analyze the Istihsan Reasoning
Istihsan relies on two points: (1) The witnesses testified to absolute killing (muthlaq), which is not ambiguous (mujmal), so the least obligation (diyah) is due. (2) Their ambiguity in testimony is interpreted as concealment for the accused, akin to the prophetic permission to lie for reconciliation.
Muthlaq is actionable; concealment is praiseworthy.

Since the killing is intentional but the weapon is unknown, qisas (retaliation) cannot be applied. The minimum obligatory consequence is diyah, which is imposed on the killer's own wealth, not on the 'aqilah, because the act is intentional.
Diyah is wajib on perpetrator's wealth.
5
Recognize the Tarjih (Preference) of Istihsan
The Hanafi school adopts the istihsan ruling, overriding the qiyas. This shows that istihsan is a valid source of law that can produce a different result from strict analogy.
Mufta bihi: istihsan position.

Difference Between Iqrar and Shahadah in Qisas
Explains the effect of denial on confession vs testimony in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of denial (takdhib) on confession versus testimony in qisas and explain the underlying legal principle.

Core Idea
A confessor's fisq does not invalidate his confession, but a witness's fisq does invalidate his testimony.

In qisas cases, the legal guardian (wali) may deny the statements of confessors or witnesses. This denial has different effects: it renders both confessors and witnesses fasiq (sinful), but while a fasiq's confession remains valid, a fasiq's testimony becomes invalid. This lesson explains the reasoning behind this rule, rooted in the nature of confession as a self-imposing act versus testimony as a report requiring 'adalah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Consider a murder case where two individuals confess to the crime, each claiming the other did it. Alternatively, two witnesses testify that a specific person is liable for qisas. The wali denies both the confessors and the witnesses.
The denial is called takdhib.
2
Apply the ruling on takdhib
According to Hanafi fiqh, the wali's denial causes the confessors and witnesses to become fasiq (morally corrupt). However, the legal effect differs: the confession remains valid, but the testimony becomes invalid.
Fisq is the state of being a fasiq.
3
Distinguish the effect
Confession (iqrar) is a statement made against oneself, establishing liability regardless of the confessor's moral character. Testimony (shahadah) is a statement about another person, requiring the witness to be 'adil (upright). Hence, a fasiq's confession is accepted, but a fasiq's testimony is rejected.
The principle: 'al-iqrar la yaftaqir ila 'adalah' (confession does not require probity).
4
Understand the rationale
The rationale is that confession is a form of self-harm (ilzam al-nafs), which is considered honest even from a sinful person. Testimony, on the other hand, is a report that can harm others, so it requires the reporter to be trustworthy.
This is a settled rule in the Hanafi school.

B) Their confessions remain valid and both are liable for qisas.
C) Only one of them is liable because the denial creates uncertainty.
D) The case is dismissed due to contradiction.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, denial (takdhib) by the legal guardian makes both confessors and witnesses fasiq, but a fasiq's confession is valid while a fasiq's testimony is invalid.

Purpose and Form of Testimony for the Wall Order
Explanation that testimony is only precautionary, not a condition for liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the purpose of ishhad in the wall demolition case, describe its proper form, and identify when it becomes valid.

Core Idea
Testimony is precaution, not condition; it proves notice, not liability.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah, the author explains why testimony (ishhad) was mentioned in the rule about the leaning wall. Many students mistakenly think testimony is required for the owner to become liable. The text clarifies that testimony is merely a precautionary tool to prove that the neighbor had given notice, in case the owner later denies it. This lesson dissects the ruling and its practical form.

The phrase establishes that prior notification was given.

5

Proof is required only when denial is anticipated; timing ensures relevance.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the mention of 'ishhad' (testimony) in the previous ruling. The author of Al-Hidayah mentioned testimony not as a necessary condition for liability, but as a means to establish the prior notification in case the owner denies it. It is therefore a matter of precaution (ihtiyat), not a requirement. The proper form is that the person demanding demolition says to witnesses: 'Bear witness that I have previously notified this man to demolish his wall.' Importantly, such testimony cannot be given before the wall actually leans or becomes dangerous, because before that, there is no transgression (ta'addi) on the part of the owner, so the basis for liability does not exist. This guards against premature legal action.

Key Terms
الإشهاد (al-ishhad): Testimony or formal witness-taking, used here for precaution to prove that the owner was asked to demolish the wall.
