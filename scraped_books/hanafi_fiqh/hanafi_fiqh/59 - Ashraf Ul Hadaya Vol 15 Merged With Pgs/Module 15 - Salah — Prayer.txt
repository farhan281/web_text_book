Hunting Rulings: The Decisive Moment of Shooting
Explains rulings when hunter changes state between shooting and arrow hitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the hunter's state at the time of shooting determines the permissibility of the hunt and the liability for penalties in scenarios involving conversion, apostasy, and ihram.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the shooting itself is the slaughter — so the hunter's state at shooting decides the ruling.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a critical question in Hanafi fiqh: when does the hunter's personal status — Muslim or non-Muslim, muhrim or halal — actually matter for the hunt? Is it at the moment the arrow strikes, or when it is released? The Hanafi answer is clear: the shooting is the act of dhabh (ritual slaughter), so the legally relevant moment is the release of the projectile. This lesson will walk through the specific scenarios and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The passage presents two pairs of scenarios: (1) a Magian who shoots then becomes Muslim, and a Muslim who shoots then apostatizes; (2) a muhrim who shoots then becomes halal, and a halal who shoots then becomes muhrim. Note the change in status between shooting and arrow impact.
These scenarios illustrate the same principle applied to different conditions.
2
Extract the central principle
The key principle is stated: "المعتبر حال الرمي" (the state at shooting is considered) because "الرمي هو الذكاة" (shooting is the slaughter). This means the act of dhabh occurs at the moment of release, not at the moment of death.
This principle is unique to the Hanafi school.
3
Apply the principle to the first pair
If a Magian shoots, then becomes Muslim before the arrow hits: at shooting he was not Muslim, so the dhabh is invalid — the meat is not halal. Conversely, if a Muslim shoots then apostatizes: at shooting he was Muslim, so the dhabh is valid — the meat is halal despite later apostasy.
The condition for valid dhabh is Islam at the time of shooting.
4
Apply the principle to the second pair
If a muhrim shoots, then becomes halal before the arrow hits: at shooting he was in ihram, so hunting is prohibited and the penalty (jaza) is due for the transgression. If a halal shoots, then enters ihram before the arrow hits: at shooting he was halal, so no transgression occurred and no penalty is due.
The liability for penalty is tied to the state at shooting, not the state at death.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school considers the state at the moment the animal dies (when the arrow strikes) as decisive. So they would rule differently in these cases. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the Hanafi reasoning.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi and based on the notion that shooting is the dhabh.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings on hunting when the hunter's legal status changes between the act of shooting and the arrow hitting the prey. The principle is that the validity of the hunting (and consequent permissibility of the meat) depends on the hunter's state at the moment of shooting, which is considered the act of slaughter (dhabh). The passage first discusses a Magian (fire-worshipper) who shoots an animal then converts to Islam before the arrow hits. According to the Hanafi school, the animal is not permissible to eat because at the time of shooting, the hunter was not a Muslim, and Islam is a condition for valid slaughter. Conversely, if a Muslim shoots then apostatizes, the animal is permissible because at the time of shooting, the hunter was a qualified Muslim. The same principle applies to a muhrim (one in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah): if a muhrim shoots while in ihram, even if he becomes halal before the arrow hits, he is liable for the penalty (jaza) for hunting in ihram, because the transgression occurred at the time of shooting. But if a halal (non-muhrim) shoots and then enters ihram before the arrow hits, no penalty is due, because the act was done in a permissible state. This illustrates that the decisive moment is the shooting, not the infliction of death.

Key Terms
رمي (ramī): The act of shooting an arrow or projectile at game. In this passage, it is equated with the act of slaughter (dhabh) for hunting.
ذكاة (dhabh): The ritual slaughter of an animal for it to be halal. In hunting, the shooting itself constitutes the dhabh.
إحرام (iḥrām): The state of consecration for Hajj or Umrah, during which hunting is prohibited and penalties apply.
جزاء (jazā’): The penalty or compensation due for hunting while in the state of ihram. It may involve sacrificing an animal or feeding the poor.

In Hanafi fiqh, the decisive moment for hunting rulings is the time of shooting, not the time of death, because the shooting itself constitutes the ritual slaughter.
