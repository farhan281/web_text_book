Woman Cuts Man's Hand, Marries Him, Then He Dies
Imam Muhammad's ruling on marriage after injury

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on mahr al-mithl and diya when a marriage is contracted for an invalid consideration.

Core Idea
Marriage and diya are separate: a woman still owes diya even if married in lieu of injury.

This lesson examines a specific case from Imam Muhammad where a woman cuts a man's hand and the man marries her in exchange for the hand. The man later dies from the injury. The ruling clarifies the interplay between marriage dower (mahr) and blood money (diya). You will learn how the Hanafi school determines mahr al-mithl when the contracted consideration is invalid, and how liability for diya depends on whether the injury was intentional or erroneous.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the issue
The woman cuts the man's hand, and he marries her 'in exchange for his hand' — meaning he accepts the marriage as compensation for the injury. Later he dies from the wound. What are the legal consequences?
Focus on two separate obligations: the marriage dower and the blood money.
2
Determine the dower (mahr)
The marriage consideration (waiver of the right to the hand) is not a valid dower because it is not property. Therefore she is entitled to mahr al-mithl, the standard dower for a woman of her status.
Mahr al-mithl is determined by factors like family, age, beauty, etc.
3
Determine diya liability based on intent
If the cutting was erroneous (khata), the diya is on her 'aaqilah (tribe/agnates). If intentional (amd), the diya is on her own wealth.
The 'aaqilah is a group of male agnates who share responsibility for accidental killings/injuries.
4
Confirm the separation of obligations
The marriage and the diya are independent. The marriage does not cancel the diya, nor does the diya affect the dower.
This refutes a possible misconception that the marriage substitutes for the diya.

Key Terms

دية (diya): Blood money; financial compensation for homicide or injury.

Woman Cuts Man's Hand Then Marries Him: Ruling on Mahr and Diyah
Ruling on marriage and diyah when woman marries after cutting hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Abu Hanifa's principle that pardon of a limb does not pardon resulting death to determine diyah liability when marriage includes the injury as mahr.

Core Idea
Pardon of the limb is not pardon of the killing: sirayah creates separate liability.

This lesson examines a nuanced case where a woman cuts a man's hand, then marries him in exchange for that hand, and the man later dies from the wound. The central question is whether the marriage and the implicit pardon of the hand also forgive the diyah for the resulting death. Following the Hanafi school, we will see that the marriage is valid, but the mahr specification is invalid, and diyah remains due according to the type of cutting.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the legal problem
Identify: a woman cuts a man's hand, then the man marries her in exchange for the hand (i.e., waives the compensation as mahr). Later the man dies from the injury. Determine the rulings on marriage validity, mahr, and diyah.
2
Identify the ruling on marriage and mahr
The marriage is valid because there is offer and acceptance. However, the mahr specification (the value of the hand) is invalid because the man's right at the time of marriage was either qisas (if intentional) or arsh (if mistaken), neither of which are proper mahr. Therefore, mahr al-mithl becomes compulsory.
3
Apply the principle of sirayah to determine diyah
The wound spreads (sirayah) and causes death. Abu Hanifa holds that pardon of the hand does not extend to pardon of the resulting death. Thus, the marriage does not waive the diyah. Diyah is now due for the death.
4
Distinguish diyah liability based on intention
If the cut was mistaken (khata'), diyah is on the woman's 'aqilah. If intentional ('amd), diyah is from her own wealth because intentional injury carries personal liability.
5
Recognize the legal reasoning (talil)
The reasoning is: 'pardon of the hand is not pardon of what results from it.' This is a Hanafi principle that prevents the marriage from implicitly covering the death.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific case: a woman cuts a man's hand, then the man marries her in exchange for that hand (i.e., he waives the compensation for the hand as mahr). Later, the man dies from the wound. The ruling, according to Imam Muhammad and Abu Hanifa, is that the marriage is valid, but the specification of mahr (the compensation for the hand) is invalid because at the time of marriage, the man's right was to qisas (if intentional) or arsh (if mistaken), but the hand was not yet fully compensated. In intentional cutting, the right is to qisas, which is not property and cannot be mahr; in mistaken cutting, the arsh (compensation) could be property but the man's right was not yet fixed as his exclusive property because the victim's right to arsh is a financial right that transfers to heirs upon death. However, the man dies from the injury, so the cutting becomes the cause of death. Since the marriage only pardoned the hand injury, not the subsequent death, diyah becomes due. The diyah is on the woman's 'aqilah if the cutting was mistaken (since diyah for khata' is on the 'aqilah), and from her own wealth if intentional (since 'amd carries personal liability). This is Abu Hanifa's view: pardon of the limb does not imply pardon of resulting death, so the marriage does not cover the killing.

Key Terms
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The standard mahr for a woman of her status, due when the specified mahr is invalid.
دية (diyah): Blood money for killing or injury; here, full diyah for death.
عاقلة (‘aqilah): The tribal group or helpers who pay diyah for unintentional killings or injuries.
سراية (sirayah): The spread of a wound to cause death; here, the hand wound leads to death.

Invalid Mahr Specification and Its Effect on Marriage
Ruling on marriage where mahr is compensation for injury that later causes death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when mahr is specified as compensation for an injury that later causes death, and distinguish the intentional (amd) versus accidental (khata) scenario.

Core Idea
A mahr that is not property—like qisas—is invalid; marriage remains valid but mahr al-mithl and diyah are due separately.

Imagine a woman cuts off a man's hand, and he then marries her, making the compensation for that injury her mahr. Later, the wound kills him. Is the marriage valid? What mahr does she get? And does she owe diyah? The Hanafi school provides a precise answer based on whether the cutting was intentional or accidental. This lesson unpacks that ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Scenario
Read the passage to understand the facts: a woman (Zaynab) cuts a man's (Zayd's) hand. He proposes marriage with the compensation as mahr. She accepts. Later, Zayd dies from the wound.
This is a combination of criminal injury (jinayat) and marriage (nikah).
2
Determine the Nature of the Liability
If the cutting was accidental, the liability is arsh (compensation) – which is property. If intentional, the liability is qisas (retaliation) – which is not property. This distinction is central to the mahr ruling.
Property (mal) is a condition for valid mahr (mahr saihh).
3
Assess the Validity of the Marriage and Mahr Specification
The marriage itself is valid because the essential elements (offer and acceptance) are present. However, the specification of mahr (tasmiah) is invalid because the specified object either does not exist (arsh extinguished by death) or is not property (qisas). In either case, mahr al-mithl becomes due.
Even if the specified mahr is invalid, the marriage is not void; the woman is entitled to the proper mahr of her peers.
4
Analyze the Effect of Death on the Liability
When the injury causes death, the original compensation for the hand (arsh or qisas) is subsumed by the diyah (blood money) for death. The marriage agreement did not cover death, so diyah is owed separately. Whether diyah is on the woman personally or her tribe depends on intent: accidental -> aqila; intentional -> her own property.
Imam Abu Hanifa's key point: forgiveness of the hand does not imply forgiveness of what results from it.
5
Apply the Qiyas for Set-off (Muqasah)
In the intentional case, since both mahr al-mithl and diyah are monetary obligations on the same person, they are set off if equal. Any surplus is paid to the other party. In the accidental case, no set-off because diyah is on the aqila, not the woman.
This ensures justice so that neither party gains unfairly from the two overlapping obligations.

But Zayd said: 'Marry me, and that right of mine (the hand's compensation) upon you is the mahr.' The woman accepted, so the marriage was performed, and on the woman the arsh (compensation) was obligatory – that is, five thousand dirhams – that (amount) is taken as the mahr. But the matter happened that the husband (Zayd) died. What is the ruling? And Zayd's death was caused by this cutting of the hand. He (the author) said: The marriage is still valid, but the specification (tasmiah) of the mahr is not valid; therefore, for the woman, mahr al-mithl (proper mahr) is obligatory, and Zayd's heirs will receive diyah. Now, upon whom is the diyah obligatory? He said: If Zaynab cut the hand accidentally, then the diyah is upon Zaynab's tribe (aqila); if she cut it intentionally, then the diyah is upon the woman's property (mal). 
The reason according to Imam Abu Hanifa:
Wa hadha 'inda Abi Hanifah li-anna al-'afw 'an al-yad idhalam yakun 'afwan 'amma yahduthu 'anhu 'indahu fa al-tazawwuj 'ala al-yad la yakun tazawwujan 'ala ma yahduthu minhu
Translation: And this is according to Abu Hanifa because forgiveness of the hand, when according to him it is not forgiveness of what results from it, so marriage upon the hand is not marriage upon what results from the cutting of the hand. Detailed explanation: According to Imam Sahib, the reason is what has passed – that according to him, forgiveness of cutting the hand does not constitute forgiveness of killing. Similarly, according to him, marriage in exchange for the penalty of the hand is not marriage in exchange for killing. When in the marriage the compensation for the hand was specified as the exchange, that specification (tasmiah) became null because the thing specified was not Zayd's right (since it was extinguished by the marriage?). And when the specification was not valid, mahr al-mithl becomes obligatory. In such a case this is the ruling, the details of which are mentioned in Hidaya Volume 2.
Ruling on intentional cutting of hand:
Thumma al-qat'u idha kana 'amadan yakunu hadha tazawwujan 'ala al-qisas fi al-taraf wa huwa laysa bi malin fala yasluhu mahran la siyamma 'ala taqdir al-suqut fayajibu mahr al-mithl wa 'alayha al-diyah fi maliha li-anna al-tazawwuj wa in kana yatadammanu al-'afwa 'ala ma nubayyin insha' Allahu ta'ala lakinna 'an al-qisas fi al-taraf fi hadhihi al-surah wa idha sara tabayyana annahu qatl al-nafs wa lam yatanaqqahu wa fatajibu al-diyah wa tajibu fi maliha li-annahu 'amd
Translation: Then if the cutting was intentional, this is marriage upon qisas for a limb, and qisas is not property, so it cannot serve as mahr, especially if it is estimated as falling (due to the marriage). Thus mahr al-mithl is obligatory, and upon her is diyah from her property because the marriage, though it includes forgiveness as we will explain if Allah wills, is forgiveness of qisas for the limb in this scenario. When the injury spread (to the soul), it became clear that it was killing of the soul (not mere cutting), and the forgiveness did not cover it, so diyah is obligatory, and it is obligatory from her property because it is intentional.

Translation: The analogy is that qisas should be obligatory as we have explained. When mahr al-mithl is obligatory for her and diyah is upon her, set-off (muqasah) occurs if both are equal. If the cutting was accidental, then this is marriage upon arsh (compensation) of the hand. When it spreads to the soul, it becomes clear that there is no arsh for the hand and that the specified (mahr) is non-existent, so mahr al-mithl is obligatory, just as if he married her upon what is in the hand and there is nothing in it. Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya addresses a complex scenario at the intersection of injury (jinayat) and marriage (nikah). Zaynab cuts off Zayd's hand, creating a liability for compensation (arsh or diyah). Zayd then proposes marriage, making that compensation the mahr. The marriage is validly contracted, but before anything is paid, Zayd dies from the wound. The author explains the ruling under two possibilities: accidental (khata) or intentional (amd).

If the cutting was accidental: Zaynab's liability is arsh (compensation) for the hand, but since the injury caused death, the arsh is subsumed under diyah (blood money). The marriage is valid, but the specified mahr (the arsh) is not valid because the right to arsh was extinguished when the death occurred. Therefore, mahr al-mithl (proper mahr) is due to Zaynab. The diyah (for the death) is upon Zaynab's aqila (tribe) because accidental death triggers diyah on the aqila.

If the cutting was intentional: Zaynab owes qisas (retaliation) for the hand. Qisas is not property, so it cannot be valid mahr. Even if qisas is waived by the marriage (since marriage implies forgiveness of the claim), it still is not property. So mahr al-mithl is obligatory. Additionally, since the injury led to death, diyah is due from Zaynab's own property (not aqila) because it is intentional and thus qisas could apply, but here it is transformed into diyah due to the nature of the death. The marriage only forgives the hand qisas, not the death qisas.

Imam Abu Hanifa's reasoning is highlighted: forgiveness of the hand does not imply forgiveness of what results from it (death). Therefore, marriage upon the hand's compensation does not cover the death compensation.

The passage also presents a qiyas (analogy) for the intentional case: since mahr al-mithl and diyah are both monetary, if they are equal, they cancel out. If one exceeds, the difference is paid. For the accidental case, no set-off because diyah is on the aqila, not on Zaynab personally, while mahr is on her.

Key points: The tasmiyah (specification of mahr) is invalid if the specified item is not legally owned by the husband or is not property. The marriage itself remains valid, and mahr al-mithl becomes due. The death from the injury creates a separate liability (diyah) that is not covered by the marriage agreement.

Key Terms
ارش (arsh): Compensation for a bodily injury that is not a fixed amount (like diyah); determined by the value of the lost function.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for homicide or bodily injury; in this passage, qisas for the hand (limb).
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper mahr owed when no valid mahr is specified; determined by the mahr of women of similar status.
دية (diyah): Blood money paid for homicide or severe injury; here for death resulting from the cut.
تسمية (tasmiah): Specification of mahr in the marriage contract.

Marriage on Qisas: When Injury Becomes Homicide
Ruling on marriage with qisas as dower when injury becomes fatal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling when marriage is contracted on qisas as dower and the wound becomes fatal, distinguishing between amd and khata' and applying set-off rules.

Core Idea
Qisas cannot be dower – when specified mahr is invalid, mahr al-mithl becomes wajib.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary addresses a complex scenario where a marriage is contracted on the basis of qisas for an intentional injury, but the injury later causes death. The Hanafi school rules that qisas is not valid as mahr because it is not property, so mahr al-mithl is due. Additionally, the woman owes diyah for the homicide, and a set-off may occur. We will unpack the legal reasoning and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Identify the factual scenario: a woman intentionally cuts a man's hand, the man marries her on the qisas of that hand as mahr, and the wound spreads causing his death. State the two rulings: (1) the specified mahr (qisas) is invalid, so mahr al-mithl is wajib; (2) the woman owes diyah for the homicide (amd).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The passage uses qiyas to the ruling of marriage on wine or swine. In the matn, Imam Muhammad says: 'صار كما اذا تزوجها على خمر او خنزير' – it becomes as if he married her on wine or swine. Identify this as the proof: qiyas from the established case where non-property items are invalid as mahr.
The qiyas is jaliyyah (clear analogy).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof proves the ruling)
The effective cause (illa) is that both wine/swine and qisas are not permissible as property (mal mutaqawwim) for mahr. Since mahr must be valuable property, any specified mahr that is not property is invalid, and mahr al-mithl becomes wajib. The same reasoning applies to qisas.
Commentary also notes that even if qisas had not been dropped by the marriage, it still could not be mahr.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih: other schools may treat qisas as valid mahr or differ on diyah liability. For example, some Shafi'i opinions might allow it. The Hanafi position is based on the principle that mahr must be mal (property).
The passage itself does not state the opposition, but the school difference is acknowledged.
5
Apply the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The mufta bihi is: mahr al-mithl is wajib on the husband's estate to the woman; diyah is wajib on the woman from her own wealth (since it is amd); if equal, set-off (muqassah) occurs; any excess is paid to the heirs or to her. In khata', diyah is on the 'aqilah and no set-off.
This scenario also illustrates the rule that by making qisas mahr, the husband consents to its dropping, so qisas falls entirely.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with detailed legal reasoning from the Hanafi school regarding a marriage contracted on the condition of qisas (retaliation) for an intentional injury that later becomes fatal. The ruling is given by Imam Muhammad in the matn. The commentator explains: If a woman cuts off a man's hand intentionally, qisas of the hand is due. If the man then marries her on that qisas as mahr, the qisas is not valid as mahr because it is not property. Therefore the mahr specified is void and mahr al-mithl becomes due. Even if the qisas had not been dropped, it still could not be mahr; here it is dropped anyway by the marriage contract (since the man's offer and woman's acceptance of marriage on that basis amounts to waiver of qisas). Thus mahr al-mithl is wajib. Additionally, if the wound spreads and causes death, diyah (blood-money) becomes wajib upon the woman because it is amd (intentional killing). The diyah is from her own wealth (not the 'aqilah). A set-off (muqassah) occurs if the mahr and diyah are equal; otherwise the excess is paid. In case of khata' (accidental), the ruling differs: the man married on the 'arsh (compensation) of the hand, but when the wound causes death, the 'arsh is deemed nonexistent, so mahr al-mithl is due but no set-off because diyah in khata' is on the 'aqilah (tribe) and mahr is on the woman. The passage concludes with a direct quote from Imam Muhammad's statement in the matn: if the man marries the woman on the hand and what arises from it, or on the injury, and then dies from it, and the cut was amd, then she gets mahr al-mithl because qisas is not suitable as mahr. It becomes as if he married her on wine or swine—nothing is due on her (for the qisas) because by making qisas mahr, he consented to its dropping, so it drops entirely.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation for intentional bodily harm; a right of the victim that is not property.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The standard dower for a woman of her status when the specified dower is invalid or not specified.
دية (diyah): Blood-money paid for homicide or injury; in intentional killing it is from the offender's wealth; in accidental it is on the 'aqilah.
مقاصة (muqassah): Set-off; mutual cancellation of debts when each party owes the other.

Marriage in Exchange for Intentional Severance of Hand: Qisas as Invalid Mahr
Ruling with qiyas proof on mahr al-mithl and waiver of qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when qisas is stipulated as mahr in a marriage and explain why the woman owes neither qisas nor diyah.

Core Idea
Qisas is not property, so it cannot be mahr; the marriage is valid but mahr al-mithl is due.

This lesson examines a specific case from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: a woman intentionally cuts off a man's hand, and then he marries her in exchange for that act. Since qisas (retaliation) is a personal right and not considered property (mal) in Islamic law, stipulating it as mahr is invalid. The marriage itself remains valid, but the woman is entitled to mahr al-mithl (the proper dower), and crucially, she owes nothing—neither qisas nor diyah—because the husband's acceptance of qisas as mahr constitutes a complete waiver of his right to retaliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? (Marriage with qisas as mahr) What is the overall hukm? (Marriage valid, mahr al-mithl wajib)
The ruling addresses both the validity of the marriage and the financial consequences.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does Imam Muhammad cite? (Qiyas to marriage with wine or pork as mahr). Identify the analogy.
The analogy is explicit in the text: 'صار كما اذا تزوجها على خمر او خنزير'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the analogy work? Both qisas and wine/pork are not property, so they cannot be valid mahr. Hence mahr al-mithl is due.
The common illa (cause) is 'not being property (ghayr mal)'.
4
Note the consequence of waiver
Why does the woman owe no qisas or diyah? Because the husband agreed to qisas as mahr, which is a waiver. Since qisas is not property, the waiver is absolute—no substitution (diyah) arises.
This is a key distinction from cases where the victim waives qisas in exchange for money.
5
Evaluate the cross-madhhab dimension
How might other schools view this? Shafi'is agree qisas cannot be mahr, but they might consider diyah still due if the act was intentional. Hanafis say no diyah because of the absolute waiver.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether the woman pays diyah or not.

Commentary
This is a ruling from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani explaining the case of a woman who intentionally cuts off a man's hand, and then the man marries her in exchange for that act or its consequences. The key point is that the marriage is valid, but since qisas (retaliation) is not considered property (mal) in Islamic law, it cannot serve as a valid mahr (dower). Therefore, the woman is entitled to mahr al-mithl (the proper dower of her peers). The man's acceptance of qisas as mahr constitutes a waiver of his right to qisas, and since qisas is not property, the waiver is absolute, and the woman owes nothing—neither qisas nor diyah (blood money). The author draws an analogy to marriage in exchange for wine or pork, which are not valid mahr because they are not property in Islamic law. The commentary elaborates that if the man had waived qisas on condition that it becomes property, that condition is void because blood cannot be property, so the waiver remains absolute. Thus, in this 'amd (intentional) case, the woman gets her mahr al-mithl and is free from any liability for the death.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional injuries; a personal right of the victim or heirs.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): Proper dower equivalent to women of her status; wajib when stipulated mahr is invalid.
تسمية (tasmiyah): Stipulation of dower in marriage contract.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a marriage contracted in exchange for diyah after an accidental injury, and explain why the excess over mahr al-mithl becomes a bequest limited to one-third of the estate.

Core Idea
In accidental injury, diyah is property, valid as mahr; but terminal illness makes any excess over mahr al-mithl a bequest.

In the previous lesson, we saw that when a woman intentionally cuts a man's hand, the marriage in exchange for qisas extinguishes both qisas and diyah. Here, the cutting is accidental (khata'), so diyah (blood money) is due from the 'aqila. The husband marries the woman, waiving the diyah as mahr. But because the husband is in marad al-mawt (terminal illness), any waiver exceeding mahr al-mithl is treated as a bequest (wasiyyah) and is limited to one-third of his estate. This lesson unpacks these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case: a woman accidentally cuts a man's hand, and he dies from the injury. He had married her in exchange for the diyah owed by her 'aqila.
Accidental (khata') triggers diyah, not qisas.
2
Determine the hukm on diyah
Diyah is wajib on the 'aqila (the woman's tribe). The husband, as the victim, can waive this debt.
Diyah is property, so it can validly be mahr.
3
Apply the marad al-mawt rule
Since the husband died from the wound, he was in marad al-mawt at the time of marriage. Any financial concession beyond what is necessary (like mahr al-mithl) is considered a bequest (wasiyyah) and subject to the one-third limit.
Marriage is an essential need, so the waiver up to mahr al-mithl is valid even in marad al-mawt.
4
Distinguish between intentional and accidental
In 'amd, qisas (not property) is involved, so its waiver extinguishes both. In khata', diyah is property, so its waiver as mahr is valid, but only up to mahr al-mithl; excess is a bequest.
This contrast is the key legal point.
5
Derive the practical ruling
The 'aqila pays diyah. The husband's waiver up to mahr al-mithl is valid. Any surplus becomes a bequest, valid only up to 1/3 of the husband's estate unless heirs approve.
Mufta bihi: as above.
