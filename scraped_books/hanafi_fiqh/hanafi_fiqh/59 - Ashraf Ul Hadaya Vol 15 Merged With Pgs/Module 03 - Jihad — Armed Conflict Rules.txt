Shooting Arrows: Determining Liability by the Time of Shooting
Rulings on shooting arrows considering the state of the target at different times

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that liability for an arrow shot is determined by the target's status at the time of shooting, and distinguish between cases involving apostasy, freedom, and ihram.

Core Idea
Liability for an arrow attaches at the moment of release, not impact.

In this passage, Al-Hidayah presents a series of cases involving delayed arrow strikes. The common thread is the Hanafi principle that the legally relevant moment is when the arrow is shot, not when it hits. Understanding this helps resolve complex scenarios where the target's status changes between release and impact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
From the varying cases, extract the overarching Hanafi rule: liability is determined at the moment of shooting, not hitting.
2
Apply to apostasy cases
If the target was Muslim at shooting, diyat is due even if he later apostatizes. If he was an apostate at shooting, no diyat even if he later embraces Islam.
This shows the principle's operation with religious status.
3
Apply to freedom status
If a slave is shot and later freed before being hit, the shooter owes diyat for a free person (according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) or for a slave (according to Imam Muhammad). Note the disagreement.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the valuation.
4
Apply to witness withdrawal
If a witness retracts before the arrow hits, liability is canceled, because the basis of liability (the certainty of the act) is removed.
Illustrates the principle that liability is contingent on the status at shooting.
5
Apply to game and ihram
For hunting, the shooter's state at shooting determines halal status and penalty. A Magian's shot is not halal even if he converts before hit. A muhrim's shot incurs penalty even if he leaves ihram before hit.
Extends the same principle to hunting law.

Commentary
This chapter from the Book of Murder discusses how the state of the target at the time of shooting affects liability. The key principle is that the relevant moment is the time the arrow is shot, not when it strikes. A Muslim who shoots at a person who later apostatizes before being hit still owes diyat because the target was Muslim at the time of shooting. Conversely, if the target was apostate or harbi at the time of shooting, then becomes Muslim before being hit, no liability. This shows that liability is determined by the status at the moment of the act (ramy), not the result. The text also covers cases involving slaves: if a slave is freed before the arrow hits, liability is to the new free status. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Muhammad's proof vs. Shaykhayn's proof. Additionally, if a witness withdraws before the arrow hits, liability is canceled. For game, the eater's status at the time of shooting determines if the game is halal. A Magian's shot is considered slaughter by a non-Muslim, so if he becomes Muslim before hitting, the game is not halal. For a pilgrim in ihram, the penalty for hunting applies if he shoots while in ihram, even if he leaves ihram before the arrow hits. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi use of istihsan and the critical timing of the act.

Key Terms
رمی (ramy): The act of shooting or throwing an arrow at someone.
مرتد (murtadd): A person who leaves Islam; killing an apostate may be permissible but liability for damages varies.
محرم (muhrim): A person in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah, who is prohibited from hunting.

Hanafi Ruling on Ghurra for Fetal Death
Inter-madhhab dispute on ghurra liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ghurra for fetal death, identify the istihsani dalil, and explain the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school obligates ghurra on the 'aqila for fetal death, treating the act as khata' due to shubha.

This lesson focuses on the core Hanafi ruling for compensation when a fetus is killed by a blow to the mother. We will examine the legal nature of the obligation (ghurra), the proof based on istihsan, and the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i who places liability on the striker's own wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is striking a pregnant woman causing miscarriage. The Hanafi ruling is that ghurra (five camels or equivalent) is wajib upon the 'aqila.
Ghurra is not full diyat; it is a specific compensation for fetal death.
2
Classify the legal nature
The act is treated as khata' (mistake) due to shubha (doubt) about the fetus's status. Thus liability falls on the 'aqila, not the striker's own wealth.
Shubha arises because the fetus's creation is hidden and the striker intended to hit the mother, not the fetus.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof (istihsan)
The Hanafi proof is istihsan: the hadith on ghurra places it on the 'aqila, and qiyas is set aside due to the exceptional nature of the fetus.
Istihsan departs from strict analogy that would make the striker personally liable.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
Shafi'i argues the act is intentional (amd) toward the mother, so liability rests on the striker's own wealth. Hanafi responds that the harm to the fetus is unintentional, and the fetus's status is uncertain.
The practical difference: 'aqila pays over three years; striker pays immediately.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The Hanafi position (ghurra on 'aqila) is the relied-upon ruling in fatwa, based on the strength of the istihsan proof and sunnah.
This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Scenarios of Diyat and Ghurra for Fetal Death
Detailed scenarios of diyat for fetal death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the different diyat obligations in various scenarios of fetal death, including when the fetus is born alive, when the mother dies before or after delivery, and for a slave woman.

Core Idea
If the fetus is born alive and then dies, full diyat is due; if stillborn, ghurra only; if mother dies, diyat for her plus possible ghurra.

This lesson explores the detailed scenarios presented in the passage: from the basic ruling to complex cases involving the mother's death, the child being born alive, and the special rules for a slave woman. We will learn how to apply the principles of ghurra and diyat in each situation.

Guided Path
1
Scenario 1: Fetus stillborn
If the fetus is miscarried dead, only ghurra (five camels) is due on the 'aqila. No full diyat.
Male and female fetuses are equal in ghurra.
2
Scenario 2: Fetus born alive then dies
If the fetus emerges alive and then dies from the injury, full diyat (100 camels) is due, as it is considered a separate human life.
This is because the fetus was born alive, establishing full personhood.
3
Scenario 3: Mother dies first, then fetus delivered dead
If the mother dies from the blow and later a dead fetus is delivered, the striker owes full diyat for the mother. There is a dispute about whether ghurra for the fetus is also due. Hanafi: no ghurra if the fetus died before the mother? (passage mentions statements of jurists).
The passage notes ikhtilaf: some say ghurra is due, others not.
4
Scenario 4: Mother dies, fetus born alive then dies
If the mother dies from the blow, the child is born alive and then dies, the striker owes two diyats: one for the mother and one for the child (full diyat for each).
Both are treated as independent lives.
5
Scenario 5: Slave woman's fetus
For a slave woman, the diyat for a fetus that is born alive and then dies is less than full diyat, proportional to the slave's value. The passage also mentions a case where the master emancipates the fetus.
Imam Abu Yusuf has a specific view on the emancipation case.

No Qisas in Limbs Between Man, Woman, Free, and Slave
States Hanafi ruling and Shafi'i dispute on qisas in limbs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas in limbs and explain why it differs from the Shafi'i position using the property analogy.

Core Idea
Qisas in limbs requires exact value equivalence; Shariah valuations differ so qisas does not apply.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute regarding retaliation (qisas) for injuries less than death. The Hanafi school holds that qisas does not apply between a man and a woman, a free person and a slave, or two slaves. The Shafi'i school generally allows it, analogizing limbs to lives. We will explore the Hanafi proof based on treating limbs as property with fixed values.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and categories
The text states no qisas in three categories: man/woman, free/slave, two slaves. Note the phrase 'ولا قصاص بين الرجل والمرأة...'.
The ruling is absolute within these categories.
2
Contrast with Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i generally allows qisas in these cases, except when a free man cuts a slave's limb. Their proof is that limbs are subordinate to lives, so the same equality applies.
The exception aligns with their stance on qisas for life.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof: limbs are treated like property (amwal), so equality requires equal value. Shariah has fixed valuations (taqwim) – e.g., a man's hand = 500 dinars, a woman's = 250 dinars. Hence, no tamathul.
This is a qiyas but with a different illa (property vs. life).
4
Address the hypothetical objection

Core Idea

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents two specific cases where qisas is not applied despite serious injury. Guided Path

The passage mentions cutting a hand from half the forearm (a bone fracture) and a jai'fah wound (penetrating to body cavity). Note that both are serious, intentional injuries.

Qisas requires exact similitude between the injury and the retaliation. If that is impossible, qisas is not applied.

Cutting from half the forearm breaks a bone. There is no standardized way to break a bone exactly the same way, so equality cannot be ensured. Hence, no qisas.

Zaid is terminally ill. Marriage is a basic need, so the mahr al-mithl is valid from the entire estate. The excess (diyah minus mahr al-mithl) is a disposition beyond basic needs, so it is treated as a bequest, effective only up to one-third of the estate.
4
Calculate the 'aqila's liability
Given diyah = 10,000 and mahr al-mithl = 7,000, the excess is 3,000. If Zaid's estate is at least double the mahr (i.e., 14,000), the excess is fully dropped. If the estate is only 3,000, only one-third of the excess (1,000) is dropped, and the 'aqila pays the remaining 2,000.
The condition for full drop is that the excess is within one-third of the total estate; the text expresses it as estate double the mahr.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding the interaction between diyah (blood money) and mahr (dower) when a man commits manslaughter against a woman and then marries her with the diyah as the dower. The author begins by stating the basic scenario: Zaid kills Zainab by mistake, and then marries her with the full diyah as her mahr. Since diyah is wealth, it is valid to make it a dower. The key issue is that Zaid is terminally ill (marad al-mawt) at the time of marriage. The commentary explains the Hanafi rule: a terminally ill person's transactions that fulfill basic needs (like marriage) are valid from the entire estate, while non-basic needs (like gifts) are limited to one-third, like a bequest. Thus, Zaid's marriage is valid, and her mahr al-mithl (proper dower) is deducted from the diyah that the 'aqila owes. The 'aqila is liable for diyah (10,000 dirhams), but since the woman's proper dower is 7,000, that amount is dropped from the 'aqila's liability. The remaining 3,000 is the excess diyah over the proper dower. Since Zaid made the entire 10,000 as mahr, the 3,000 excess is considered a disposition of the terminally ill, so it is treated as a bequest to the 'aqila (since they are not heirs). If Zaid's estate is double the excess (i.e., at least 6,000), the entire 3,000 is dropped; if not, only one-third (1,000) is dropped, and the 'aqila must pay the remaining 2,000. The commentary then provides the reasoning from the Arabic text: the proper dower is lifted from the 'aqila because they bear responsibility on her behalf—it would be unjust for her to claim from them for her own crime. The excess is a bequest because the 'aqila are not killers and are eligible for bequest. Finally, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) also hold this view in the case of marriage on the hand (i.e., where the mahr is forgiveness of a wound), because forgiving the hand implies forgiving subsequent consequences.

عاقلة (‘āqilah): The group of male agnate relatives who are liable to pay diyah on behalf of a killer who committed manslaughter or quasi-intentional homicide.
دية (diyah): Blood money; financial compensation paid to the heirs of a homicide victim in cases of manslaughter or quasi-intentional killing.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower; the amount of dower equivalent to that given to women of similar status to the bride, used when no specific dower is stipulated.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Marriage on the Hand
Intra-Hanafi dispute on marriage on the hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Sahibayn's position that the same ruling on mahr deduction applies when the marriage is contracted on the hand, and identify the practical difference from Abu Hanifa's view.

Core Idea
Forgiving the hand implies forgiving its consequences; thus the rule for mahr deduction is the same in all cases.

The passage concludes with a brief intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Hanifa reportedly holds that the rule for deducting mahr al-mithl differs when the mahr is forgiveness of a wound (marriage on the hand), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that forgiving the hand includes forgiving what arises from it, so the same rule applies. This lesson examines that difference.

But here the diyah will not be dropped; rather the diyah is obligatory because Zaid made the diyah as dower, and since diyah is wealth, it can serve as dower. However, Zaid in this case will be considered a patient of mortal illness. The rule is that a patient of mortal illness who does actions that fall under basic needs is considered with full wealth, i.e., in such transactions he is like a healthy person. As for transactions not among basic needs, he is considered a patient of mortal illness and his dispositions are effective only up to one-third, i.e., they have the status of a bequest. Marriage is among basic needs, so the woman's proper dower is considered from the entire wealth, while gifts etc. are not basic needs, so they are considered from one-third. In any case, the 'aqila is liable for diyah here, i.e., ten thousand dirhams, and the woman's proper dower is seven thousand dirhams. So seven thousand are dropped from the 'aqila, leaving three thousand. Zaid had made the entire diyah as dower, so these three thousand should also be dropped. However, the excess over seven thousand is a disposition of Zaid while he is a patient of mortal illness, so it takes effect only from one-third of his estate. So if Zaid's estate is double the seven thousand, then these three thousand are also dropped from the 'aqila. If Zaid has no other wealth besides three thousand, then one thousand of them are dropped, and the woman's brothers will pay two thousand dirhams from Zaid's estate. In this phrase the author has expressed this meaning. The reason for lifting the proper dower from the 'aqila: 'And it is lifted from the 'aqila because they bear responsibility on her behalf, so it is impossible that she should recourse against them for her own crime. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding the interaction between diyah (blood money) and mahr (dower) when a man commits manslaughter against a woman and then marries her with the diyah as the dower. The author begins by stating the basic scenario: Zaid kills Zainab by mistake, and then marries her with the full diyah as her mahr. Since diyah is wealth, it is valid to make it a dower. The key issue is that Zaid is terminally ill (marad al-mawt) at the time of marriage. The commentary explains the Hanafi rule: a terminally ill person's transactions that fulfill basic needs (like marriage) are valid from the entire estate, while non-basic needs (like gifts) are limited to one-third, like a bequest. Thus, Zaid's marriage is valid, and her mahr al-mithl (proper dower) is deducted from the diyah that the 'aqila owes. The 'aqila is liable for diyah (10,000 dirhams), but since the woman's proper dower is 7,000, that amount is dropped from the 'aqila's liability. The remaining 3,000 is the excess diyah over the proper dower. Since Zaid made the entire 10,000 as mahr, the 3,000 excess is considered a disposition of the terminally ill, so it is treated as a bequest to the 'aqila (since they are not heirs). If Zaid's estate is double the excess (i.e., at least 6,000), the entire 3,000 is dropped; if not, only one-third (1,000) is dropped, and the 'aqila must pay the remaining 2,000. The commentary then provides the reasoning from the Arabic text: the proper dower is lifted from the 'aqila because they bear responsibility on her behalf—it would be unjust for her to claim from them for her own crime. The excess is a bequest because the 'aqila are not killers and are eligible for bequest. Finally, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) also hold this view in the case of marriage on the hand (i.e., where the mahr is forgiveness of a wound), because forgiving the hand implies forgiving subsequent consequences.

Key Terms
على اليد (‘alā al-yad): A marriage contracted with the mahr being forgiveness of a physical injury (e.g., cutting off the hand).
العفو عن اليد عفو عما يحدث منه (al-‘afw ‘an al-yad ‘afw ‘ammā yahduthu minhu): The maxim: Forgiving the hand is forgiving what arises from it. Used by the Sahibayn to extend the mahr deduction rule.
