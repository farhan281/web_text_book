Child's Liability in Homicide with a Weapon
Ruling on child's liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the liability of a child who causes death with a weapon and identify the basis for the obligation of diyah.

Core Idea
A child who causes death owes diyah from his own property, even though he lacks criminal responsibility.

This lesson examines the first part of the passage, which addresses a scenario where a child holds a weapon and someone dies as a result. In Hanafi law, a minor who causes homicide is not subject to qisas (retaliation) but is liable for diyah (blood money) as compensation. This ruling underscores the principle that liability for harm can arise from actions regardless of legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the first part of the passage: a child had a weapon and someone died. Note that the child is the direct agent of the act.
The weapon could be a knife, gun, or any tool.
2
Determine the liable party
The passage says 'بچہ پر منمان ہے' meaning the child bears the diyah. Understand that the child himself is liable, not his guardian (though the guardian may pay from the child's assets).
In Hanafi fiqh, the child's estate is liable.
3
Understand the legal basis
The ruling is based on the principle that harm caused by a person's direct action creates liability for compensation, even if the actor lacks intention or capacity for criminal punishment.
This is a tort-based liability.
4
Distinguish from adult liability
Compare with an adult: an adult who kills intentionally may face qisas or diyah, while a child only owes diyah. The child is not subject to hadd or ta'zir punishments.
The passage does not mention adult rules, but it's useful context.

Liability for Placing an Obstacle That Causes an Animal to Kill
Ruling on daman for obstacle causing animal to kill

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a person places an obstacle in the road causing an animal to kill, and identify the liable party.

Core Idea
The one who placed the obstacle bears full liability, not the animal's owner.

This passage from the classical Hanafi text presents a clear case of causation (sabab) in tort law. It illustrates that when a human action — placing an object in a public thoroughfare — directly triggers a chain of events leading to death, the human agent is held financially responsible. Understanding this ruling helps you grasp key Hanafi principles of liability (daman) and the role of direct causation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and consequence
The act is placing an object in the road. The consequence is that it touches an animal, the animal shies, and kills someone.
2
Determine the liable party
The passage explicitly states the person who placed the object is liable.
The liability is financial (daman), typically full diyah.
3
Understand the legal basis
The liability is based on the principle that the person created a direct cause (sabab) by wrongfully obstructing the road.
The animal's reaction is involuntary; the human agent initiated the deadly chain.
4
Remember the ruling for similar scenarios
This ruling applies when the object is placed in a public road and the animal's reaction is involuntary.
If the animal was intentionally goaded, liability shifts.

Qisas is the Primary Punishment for Intentional Murder
Hanafi ruling on qisas with proof and response to Shafi'i views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas for intentional murder, identify the conditions under which diyah is permissible, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Qisas is fard ayn for intentional murder; diyah requires the killer's consent.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah establishes the Hanafi position that qisas (retaliatory execution) is the default and obligatory punishment for intentional murder. The text contrasts this with two views of Imam al-Shafi'i, providing both transmitted and rational proofs. Understanding this ruling is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to criminal justice, where the victim's family holds significant control over the punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is intentional murder (qatl amd). The ruling: qisas is fard ayn upon the killer.
Fard ayn means an individual obligation on the killer; it cannot be transferred to another.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the Quranic verse (2:178): 'كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الْقِصَاصُ فِي الْقَتْلَى'. A hadith is also cited: 'Intentional killing carries retaliation'.
The hadith is alluded to but not quoted verbatim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'kutiba' indicates obligation (wujub). The phrase 'fi al-qatla' is general, but Hanafis restrict it to intentional killing because full deterrence (zajr) and compensation (jabr) are only achieved through intentionality.
The Hanafi interpretation uses other evidence to specify the general text.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i has two views: (1) the heir can take diyah without the killer's consent because it saves life; (2) either qisas or diyah is obligatory, with the heir choosing.
Both views differ from the Hanafi position.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school rejects both Shafi'i views. Qisas is the only default; diyah requires the killer's consent. Rational arguments: no equivalence between life and property, uncertainty that the heir will not retaliate after taking diyah.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position as stated.

Commentary
This passage addresses the Hanafi ruling on the punishment for intentional murder (qatl amd). It begins by establishing that the primary penalty is qisas (retaliation), which is a fard ayn upon the killer. The legal heirs (awliya) have the right to forgive or make a settlement, but diyah (blood money) cannot be forced on the killer without his consent. The text contrasts this with Imam al-Shafi'i's two views: first, that the heir may demand diyah without the killer's consent because it saves the killer's life; second, that either qisas or diyah is obligatory and the heir chooses. The Hanafi school rejects both, holding that qisas is the exclusive default, and diyah requires the killer's agreement. The Hanafis then provide proofs: the Quranic verse (2:178) and a hadith, plus rational argument based on the lack of equivalence between life and property. Qisas maintains equivalence and serves as deterrence (zajr) and compensation (jabr) for the heirs. The text also addresses two objections: first, why diyah is allowed in accidental killing (due to impossibility of qisas and need to preserve respect for life); second, that designating property as a means to remove destruction is uncertain because the heir might still seek retaliation. Thus, the Hanafi position is consistent.

Killing with Blunt Iron and Balance Weight: Qisas or Diya?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for blunt iron instrument killings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions (Imam Abu Hanifa’s two narrations and the Sahibayn’s view) on qisas for killings with blunt iron instruments and balance weights, and identify the practical consequence of requiring a wound.

Core Idea
Blunt iron kills: Sahibayn say qisas; Abu Hanifa says qisas only if wound occurs.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about killings with the blunt side of an iron tool or an iron balance weight. While the sharp edge always triggers qisas, the blunt side raises a disagreement: do we consider the iron itself as a weapon (Sahibayn) or require actual wounding for the ultimate penalty (Abu Hanifa's second narration)? Understanding this nuance sharpens your ability to apply the Hanafi criteria for intentional murder ('amd) versus quasi-intentional (shibh al-'amd).

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed rule
Start with the common ground: if the sharp edge causes a wound, the cause of qisas is complete — this is undisputed. Note that the passage begins by stating this baseline.
2
Isolate the point of dispute
The disagreement arises when the blunt side (munjh) is used without causing a wound. The Sahibayn hold it is still intentional murder ('amd) because iron is inherently a weapon. Abu Hanifa has two narrated positions — the first agrees with the Sahibayn, the second requires a wound for qisas.
The second narration is more nuanced: without a wound, the 'complete corruption' (ifsaad kaamil) is not achieved.
3
Trace the basis for each position
Sahibayn rely on the nature of iron itself as a weapon (aala qatl) — the instrument qualifies as deadly regardless of its shape. Abu Hanifa's second position looks at the effect: actual wounding is needed to prove the intent to kill or the severity of harm.
This difference reflects deeper usul principles about how 'amd is defined: by instrument vs. by outcome.
4
Apply the same logic to the balance weight
The passage explicitly analogizes the iron balance weight (tarazu ka baat) to the blunt iron tool. The same disagreement applies: Sahibayn impose qisas; Abu Hanifa makes it conditional on a wound.
This shows consistency in the school's reasoning across similar instruments.
5
Determine the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Sahibayn's view, any killing with a blunt iron object (without wound) leads to qisas. Under Abu Hanifa's second narration, if no wound, qisas is not wajib — instead, diya would be due (as shibh al-'amd).
The mufta bihi is not settled in this text; later Hanafi works often adopt the Sahibayn view for blunt iron that is commonly used as a weapon.

The author first states the agreed-upon rule: if the sharp edge causes a wound, the cause of qisas (retaliation) is complete. The Obligation to Kill a Muslim Who Draws a Sword Against Muslims
Stating the obligation to kill a Muslim who brandishes a weapon against other Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim who draws a sword against Muslims, cite the primary dalil, and explain why the ruling is wajib.

Core Idea
Drawing a sword against Muslims removes one's inviolability and makes it obligatory upon the community to kill the aggressor.

This lesson examines a critical ruling that addresses the extreme case where a Muslim's life is threatened by another Muslim. The Hanafi school, relying on a prophetic hadith, declares it obligatory to kill the aggressor, even if he is Muslim, because his act of rebellion (baghy) strips him of his legal protection (ismah). We will unpack the proofs and rationale behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm
The act is a Muslim drawing a sword (or equivalent weapon) against other Muslims. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) upon the Muslims to kill him.
The passage uses words like 'عليهم' (upon them) and 'حق' (duty/right) to indicate obligation, not mere permissibility.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary proof is the hadith: 'مَنْ شَهَرَ عَلَى الْمُسْلِمِينَ سَيْفًا فَقَدْ أَحَلَّ دَمَهُ' (Whoever draws a sword against Muslims has made his blood permissible).
This is a hadith ahad, but its meaning is clear and categorical (qat'i al-dalalah).
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The key phrase is 'أَحَلَّ دَمَهُ' (made his blood permissible). This explicitly removes the protection (ismah) of the Muslim's life, making it lawful to shed his blood.
The hadith does not distinguish between the aggressor being Muslim or not; the act of drawing the sword is the cause of losing inviolability.
4
Understand the secondary rationale
Besides the hadith, the text gives two additional reasons: (1) He is a baghi (rebel), and rebellion invalidates ismah. (2) Killing is the only effective means to repel the threat (daf' al-harm).
The combination of these reasons strengthens the ruling and explains why it is wajib rather than merely permissible.
5
Recognize the authority and scope
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah (al-Mabsut and al-Jami' al-Saghir by Imam Muhammad). It is mufta bihi. The passage also touches on contingent factors like whether the act occurs in a settlement or on a road, day or night, and with what weapon.
These conditions affect the application but not the core obligation.

Multiple Murderers Killing One Person: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Hanafi proof and response to Shafi'i on multiple murderers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that multiple guardians can jointly execute qisas on a single murderer and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on the principle of tamathul.

Core Idea
Death is indivisible: each wound that could cause death attributes full causation, allowing joint qisas execution.

In this passage, we encounter a rich inter-madhhab dispute over retaliation (qisas) when multiple murderers kill a single person. The Hanafis argue that each guardian of the victim can participate in executing the murderer, with each considered to have fully exacted their right. The Shafi'is object, citing the requirement of exact similarity (tamathul) between crime and punishment. By understanding the Hanafi reliance on the indivisibility of death (ghayr mutajazzi), you will see how the school navigates apparent conflict with qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling
What is the practical ruling in dispute? Is it permissible for multiple guardians to jointly execute a single murderer in retaliation for killing one victim, and if so, is any additional compensation (diyah) due?
The Hanafi position: multiple guardians may execute jointly and each gets full retribution; no diyah. Shafi'i: only one guardian's right is satisfied; others require diyah.
2
State the Hanafi position and its core proof
The Hanafi school argues that death is indivisible (ghayr mutajazzi). Since each wound inflicted by a guardian is capable of causing death, the exit of the soul is attributed to each guardian fully. This establishes tamathul (similarity) between the crime and the punishment.
The proof rests on the nature of death and the concept of 'wasf al-kamal' (attribute of perfection) – each guardian's act is complete and sufficient.
3
State the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that qisas requires exact similarity (mumathalah). A single murderer's death cannot equal multiple murders committed by the same person. In the scenario of multiple murderers killing one victim, the hadith of Umar overrides qiyas, but here no such text exists, so qiyas must apply.
Shafi'i analogizes from the first chapter (multiple murderers, one victim) where without hadith, qiyas would prevent retaliation from all murderers due to lack of tamathul.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response extends the principle of indivisibility. Just as in the case of multiple murderers killing one victim (where each murderer is executed with full effect due to the indivisibility of death), here too each guardian's execution is fully attributed. The purpose of qisas – preservation of life (ihya') – is achieved, so no additional compensation is needed.
The Hanafis treat both scenarios symmetrically via the shared property that death is indivisible.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, multiple guardians can jointly participate in executing the murderer, and the execution counts as full satisfaction for each guardian's right. Under Shafi'i law, only one guardian's right is fulfilled through execution; the others must receive diyah (blood money) from the murderer's estate.
This affects how the courts handle multiple plaintiffs in qisas cases.

Commentary

However, the Hanafis hold that analogy actually supports their position due to the indivisibility of death.

Death of the Murderer Before Retaliation: Qisas Drops
Ruling that retaliation drops when the murderer dies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that qisas drops when the murderer dies, explain the qiyas to the death of a slave perpetrator, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-position.

Core Idea
If the murderer dies, qisas drops — the subject of execution is gone, so the right is extinguished.

In this passage, al-Quduri states a clear ruling: when a person upon whom retaliation (qisas) is obligatory dies before execution, the right to qisas is dropped entirely. The reason is that the very object of execution — the murderer's body — no longer exists. The commentator supports this with an analogy (qiyas) to a slave who commits an offense and dies before being handed over. The Shafi'i school disagrees, arguing that the obligation is not exclusively qisas but one of two alternatives, so diyah becomes due when qisas is impossible. We will explore the Hanafi proof and its response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is the death of a murderer who was subject to qisas. The ruling: qisas becomes saqit (dropped/void).
The ruling is a legal consequence, not an obligation.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? The author uses qiyas: the death of a slave perpetrator before being handed over is analogous. In both cases, the subject of execution is lost.
The analogy is based on the loss of the mahall al-istifa.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How exactly does this analogy prove the ruling? In the case of the slave, the right to hand over the slave is extinguished because the object is gone. Similarly, the right to qisas requires the physical existence of the murderer. When he dies, the right vanishes.
The illa is the disappearance of the mahall al-istifa.
4
Note the Shafi'i ikhtilaf
Who disagrees and what is their proof? Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the original obligation is not specifically qisas but one of two alternatives (qisas or diyah). When qisas becomes impossible, diyah becomes obligatory.
The Shafi'i view is based on the idea that the right of the victim is to have the crime redressed, not necessarily via execution.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon and why? The Hanafi school holds that the primary obligation is qisas; diyah only replaces it by mutual agreement (sulh). Without such agreement, and with the murderer dead, the right is lost.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position as stated in al-Quduri.

Core Idea
When a hand is cut by two, neither loses a hand—divisibility blocks qisas, half diyah suffices.

In cases of joint bodily harm, the Hanafi school applies a nuanced rule: if two people cut off one hand, neither is subject to qisas (retaliation), because the hand is divisible. This lesson examines the Hanafi position, the Shafi'i counterargument based on analogy with life, and the key concept of tamathul (exact equivalency).

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of a medical practitioner (cupper or surgeon) when a patient dies as a result of a permitted operation. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that since the patient gave permission, the practitioner is not liable. Imam Abu Hanifa disagrees, arguing that the patient's right was limited to cutting (qat'), not death. If the act results in death, it is an unjust killing (qatl bi-ghayr haqq) because the outcome exceeded the scope of permission. Therefore, although the death penalty (qisas) is dropped due to a doubt (shubha) — because the act was initially permitted — financial compensation (diya) is obligatory. Imam Abu Hanifa distinguishes the cases cited by the Sahibayn (e.g., an Imam commanding a punishment, a hired person) where the actor is obligated (mukallaf) and thus not liable for unintended consequences. In the medical case, there is no obligation (wujub) or contract (ilitzam); rather, pardoning is recommended (mandub), so the act is like a mubah (permissible) act such as hunting, where accidental harm incurs diya. The commentary explains this reasoning in detail, emphasizing that the permission was for a specific act, and exceeding that is unjust.

Killer Confirms Both Witnesses: Diyah Divided into Thirds
Ruling on diyah distribution when killer confirms both witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah distribution when the killer confirms both witnesses, and apply the principle that an acknowledgment against oneself is valid but claims against others require proof.

Core Idea
When a killer confirms two witnesses, he acknowledges two-thirds of the blood-money, but his claim of the third's forgiveness is rejected unless proven.

This lesson explains a scenario from the Book of Homicide (Jinaayaat) in Al-Hidayah where two witnesses testify that a killer owes blood-money to a third person. The killer confirms the witnesses but claims the third person forgave him. The commentator clarifies the Hanafi ruling: the killer's acknowledgment binds him for two-thirds, but his claim of forgiveness is not accepted because the third party denies it. Thus the killer remains liable for the full diyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
There is a killer (Zaid), two witnesses, and a third person (Zaahid) who claims blood-money. The witnesses testify that the killer owes diyah to Zaahid.
The third person is the one against whom the testimony is given (mashhud 'alayh).
2
Determine the effect of the killer's confirmation
By confirming both witnesses, the killer acknowledges two-thirds of the blood-money to them. This acknowledgment is valid and binding on him.
The acknowledgment is against his own interest, so it is accepted.
3
Analyze the killer's additional claim
The killer also claims that Zaahid's right has dropped due to forgiveness. This claim is against Zaahid's interest and is unsubstantiated.
The claim is not supported by evidence.
4
Apply the principle of burden of proof
Since Zaahid denies forgiveness, the killer's claim is not accepted. The burden of proof lies on the claimant (killer) to prove the forgiveness.
In Hanafi fiqh, the default is that rights are not dropped unless proven.
5
State the final distribution
The blood-money is divided into three equal shares: one for each of the two witnesses and one for Zaahid. The killer must pay all three shares.
The killer is liable for the share of the denying party (Zaahid).

Killer Denies Both Witnesses: Forfeiture of Share
Ruling when killer denies both witnesses and they claim property transformation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when both the killer and the third witness deny the two witnesses, and explain why the two witnesses forfeit their share of diyah.

Core Idea
Admission of dropped qisas is binding; unproven claim of property transformation fails.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi homicide law where the killer denies both witnesses, and the two witnesses themselves admit that the right to qisas has been dropped. The question is whether they can then claim their share has transformed into property (diyah). The passage provides a clear ruling: they get nothing, while the remaining heir receives one-third of the diyah. Understanding the distinction between self-binding admissions and claims against others is essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Three witnesses (Khalid, Sa'id, Zaahid) testify against the killer. The killer denies both Khalid and Sa'id. Additionally, Zaahid also denies them. Khalid and Sa'id admit that qisas was dropped, but claim their share became property.
This is a case of both denial by the killer and a third witness.
2
Distinguish admission from claim
Khalid and Sa'id's admission that qisas is dropped is against themselves, so it is accepted. Their claim that their share turned into property is against another (Zaahid or the killer), so it requires proof.
Hanafi principle: acknowledgment (iqrar) against self is binding; a claim against another needs evidence.
3
Analyze the legal effect of their admission
Since they admitted qisas dropped, their right to qisas is forfeited. They cannot transform it into property without proof. Their claim of forgiveness by Zaahid is denied by Zaahid, so it is treated as if they themselves initiated forgiveness of their own share.
The dropping of qisas is attributed to them, so their share is extinguished.
4
Determine the distribution
With Khalid and Sa'id's share forfeited, only Zaahid's share remains. Zaahid receives one-third of the diyah (the full diyah is divided among the three; one-third goes to Zaahid). The killer pays one-third diyah to Zaahid.
The other two-thirds? The text only mentions one-third for Zaahid. The rest? Possibly waived or not discussed. The passage focuses on the witnesses' forfeiture.
5
Understand the underlying reasoning
The key is that Khalid and Sa'id's admission is self-binding, but their alleged claim against Zaahid is unproven. Therefore, their right is gone, and only Zaahid's remains. This ruling prevents witnesses from shifting the burden of proof.
Hanafi fiqh emphasizes that one cannot claim a right from another without evidence if one has already admitted the contrary.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The killer is bound by his own failure to confirm — this implies an acknowledgment of one-third.

This lesson completes our series of witness confirmation scenarios. Here, only the accused (mashhud 'alayh) confirms the two witnesses; the killer does not. The killer's silence or denial triggers a legal acknowledgment, making him liable for one-third of the diyah to the confirming party. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi principle that a party's admission can create financial obligation even without full proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties

2
Determine the ruling
State the hukm: the killer must pay one-third of the diyah to the mashhud 'alayh. This is wajib.
The one-third is the share of the confirmatory party, not the witnesses.
3
Trace the legal basis
Understand why the killer is liable. Since the killer did not confirm the witnesses, his failure is treated as an implied acknowledgment that he owes Zaahid's share. The legal principle: a person is bound by his own acknowledgment (إقرار).
This is not a punishment for the killer, but a consequence of his own admission.

Recall the other three scenarios: if both killer and mashhud 'alayh confirm, the witnesses get nothing; if neither confirms, the killer pays full diyah; if only the killer confirms, he pays two-thirds. Here, only the mashhud 'alayh confirms, so the killer pays one-third.
The amount varies based on who confirms the witnesses.

This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah, thus relied upon in Hanafi fatwa. It is accepted as the mufta bihi position.

Testimony of Fatal Beating While Bedridden: Qisas Required
Ruling on qisas when witnesses testify to a fatal beating causing bedridden state

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for establishing qisas through testimony of a fatal beating and apply the principle that testimony is equivalent to direct observation.

Core Idea
Testimony of beating with a cutting instrument causing bedridden death proves intentional killing for qisas.

In Hanafi law, proving intentional killing for qisas does not require an eyewitness to the fatal moment. Testimony that a victim was beaten, remained bedridden until death, and the blow was with a wounding instrument suffices. This passage from al-Hidayah explains how such testimony is treated as equivalent to direct observation, establishing causation and intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Witnesses testify that the accused struck the victim, and the victim remained bedridden (sahib firaash) from that beating until he died. The ruling: qisas (retribution) is wajib (obligatory) if the beating was intentional.
2
Locate the legal principle
The text states: 'What is established by testimony is like what is established by direct observation.' This principle (al-thabit bi al-shahada ka al-thabit bi al-mu'ayana) is the proof that testimony carries the same weight as eyewitnessing for establishing the causal link between beating and death.
This is a foundational usul principle in Hanafi law.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The principle equates testimony with direct observation. Thus, if witnesses testify to the beating and the subsequent bedridden state until death, the causal connection (death due to beating) is legally established as if they had seen the entire event.
The continuity of bedridden state is the key indicator of causation.
4
Note the condition of the instrument
The text specifies that the witnesses must testify that the blow was with a 'shay' jaarih' (a cutting/wounding instrument). This indicates intent to cause death (amdan) because such weapons are inherently dangerous.
A cutting instrument distinguishes intentional homicide from accidental or less harmful strikes.
5
Apply to the case
If witnesses provide testimony meeting these conditions (intentional beating, bedridden state, cutting instrument), qisas becomes wajib on the accused. The ruling is derived from the principle that testimony, like direct sight, establishes the cause of death.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position in the Hanafi school.

Contradictory Testimony on Qisas and Diyah
Explains ruling when two witnesses admit qisas ended but claim financial compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on contradictory testimony to determine the killer's liability when witnesses admit qisas has fallen but claim financial compensation.

Core Idea
Witnesses' admission of qisas fall is binding, but their financial claim is unproven unless evidence; killer's liability depends on his own acknowledgment.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario from the Book of Crimes (Kitab al-Jinayat): two witnesses initially claim the right to qisas for an intentional killing, but then admit that the right has been replaced by financial compensation (diyah). The accused denies any commutation. How does Hanafi fiqh resolve this contradiction? The ruling hinges on the distinction between a valid admission (which extinguishes qisas) and an unproven claim (which requires proof). We will trace the conditions that trigger the killer's obligation to pay diyah to the accused alone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their positions
There are three key parties: the two witnesses (Khalid and Sajid), the accused (Zahid), and the killer. The witnesses admit qisas has ended but claim they are owed diyah. The accused denies any financial obligation. The killer's role depends on his acknowledgment.
2
Distinguish between the valid admission and the unproven claim
The witnesses' admission that qisas has fallen is legally valid because it is an iqrar against their own right. However, their claim that the right transformed into a financial claim is a new assertion that requires proof. Without evidence, it is unacceptable.
This follows the rule that iqrar against oneself is binding, but a claim against another (the accused) needs proof.
3
Analyze the effect of the accused's (Zahid's) confirmation
If the accused alone confirms the witnesses' statement, then the killer becomes liable to pay one-third of the diyah to the accused. This is because the killer's own acknowledgment (iqrar) of the killing, combined with the accused's confirmation, establishes the obligation.
If the accused does not confirm, the killer is not bound by the witnesses' claim.
4
State the final ruling and its conditions
The ruling: When two witnesses admit qisas has fallen but claim financial compensation, and the accused alone confirms them, the killer must pay one-third of the diyah to the accused. The claim of diyah for the witnesses themselves is rejected unless proven.
This is the mufta bihi position as per Zahir al-Riwayah.

Types of Property for Diyah Payment: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah property types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on permissible property types for diyah payment, explain their proofs, and apply the rule of aggravation (tashdid) correctly.

Core Idea
Diyah payment types are disputed within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa restricts to camels and coinage; the Sahibayn add cows, sheep, and garments.

This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute on the types of property from which diyah (blood money) may be paid. While the base text states Abu Hanifa's limitation to camels, gold, and silver, the two companions (Sahibayn) expand the list to include cows, sheep, and garments based on the practice of Caliph 'Umar. Understanding this dispute is crucial for applying diyah correctly, especially regarding aggravation (tashdid) in semi-intentional killing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The dispute concerns which types of property can be used to pay fixed diyah amounts. Abu Hanifa allows only camels, gold, and silver; the Sahibayn also allow cows, sheep, and garments in specified quantities.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that estimation (taqdeer) requires known monetary value. Cows, sheep, and garments have variable values, making them unsuitable for fixed diyah. Camels are an exception due to well-known traditions (aathar mashhoorah) that override qiyas.
Gold and silver have known value; camels are established by transmitted reports despite variable value.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn cite the action of Caliph 'Umar, who fixed diyah for each type: 200 cows for cow-owners, 1000 sheep for sheep-owners, and 200 hullah for garment-owners. This is narrated by Imam Muhammad in Kitab al-Athar with a sound chain.
The Sahibayn consider 'Umar's decision as a binding precedent.
4
Analyze the response and resolution
Abu Hanifa's followers interpret 'Umar's action as a settlement (sulh) rather than a fixed estimation. The author of Ashraf al-Hidayah notes that the correct view is that the permissibility of cows, sheep, and garments is exclusively the Sahibayn's position, and it is the relied-upon view in later fatwa.
Imam Muhammad's own narration includes all types, but Abu Hanifa restricted to camels and coinage.
5
Apply the rule of aggravation (tashdid)
Aggravation of diyah for semi-intentional killing is only achieved when paying with camels (80 camels in four categories). If diyah is paid from other types, aggravation cannot occur because the transmitted method is specific to camels.
Thus, the practical difference: paying with cows, sheep, or garments loses the aggravation element.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the types of property from which diyah (blood money) can be paid. The base text from al-Quduri states that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, diyah is only established from three types: camels, gold (dinars), and silver (dirhams). The two companions (Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) add that diyah can also be paid from cows (200 cows), sheep (1000 sheep), and garments (200 hullah, each hullah being two garments). Their proof is the action of Caliph 'Umar, who fixed these amounts for the respective wealth-holders. The commentary clarifies that this ruling applies to both accidental killing (qatl khata') and semi-intentional killing (shibh al-amd) for Abu Hanifa, but the aggravation of diyah (tahghiz) only applies when paying with camels due to the specific division of eighty camels into four age-categories. If diyah is paid from other types, aggravation cannot be achieved because the transmitted method of aggravation is specific to camels. Abu Hanifa's proof is that estimation (taqdeer) requires a known monetary value; cows, sheep, and garments have variable values, so they are unsuitable for fixed diyah. Camels, though also variable, are established by well-known traditions (aathar mashhoorah) that override qiyas. The passage then discusses a statement by Imam Muhammad in al-Ma'aqil that settlement (sulh) for more than 200 garments or cows is invalid, indicating these are fixed amounts. Some scholars interpret this as the consensus of all three imams, but the author prefers the view that it is only the opinion of the Sahibayn, citing Imam Muhammad's own narration from 'Umar that includes all types, which Muhammad says everyone accepts, but Abu Hanifa restricts to camels and coinage. Thus, the difference remains that Abu Hanifa limits diyah payment to camels, gold, and silver, while the Sahibayn also allow cows, sheep, and garments.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money or monetary compensation paid by the killer (or his 'aqilah) to the victim's heirs for unintentional or semi-intentional homicide. This passage discusses the types of property from which diyah can be paid.
تغليظ (tashdid): Aggravation of diyah, which applies in semi-intentional killing (shibh al-amd). It is achieved by paying 100 camels, with 80 of them divided into four age categories (30 hiqqah, 30 jadha'ah, 20 bint laboon, 20 ibn laboon). This aggravation is only possible when paying with camels.
صاحبين (Sahibayn): The two companions: Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the principal students of Imam Abu Hanifa. Their positions are recorded alongside Abu Hanifa's in the Zahir al-Riwayah and are often given weight in later Hanafi fatwa.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Muḍiḥah Causing Loss of Eyes
States the ruling on qiṣāṣ for a wound causing loss of eyes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on qiṣāṣ for a muḍiḥah wound causing loss of eyes, explain the mufta bihi ruling, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
A single wound causing multiple injuries yields no qiṣāṣ per Abu Hanifa, qiṣāṣ per Sahibayn, diyah per later scholars.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: when a muḍiḥah wound results in loss of both eyes, does qiṣāṣ apply? Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn differ, and later scholars settled on diyah for both. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how Hanafi jurisprudence balances retaliation and compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its components
The act is inflicting a muḍiḥah wound that causes loss of eyes. Separate the wound injury from the eye injury.
Muḍiḥah is a head wound exposing bone.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position
No qiṣāṣ for either; diyah for eyes and ʿarsh for the wound.
ʿarsh is a fixed compensation for wounds.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Qiṣāṣ for the wound, diyah for the eyes.
Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the offender pays diyah+ʿarsh; under Sahibayn, qiṣāṣ is executed for the wound.
Practical consequence for the offender.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Later scholars (mashāyikh) adopt diyah for both.
This is the relied-upon position.

No Partial Qiṣāṣ When Full Qiṣāṣ Is Inapplicable
Ruling on partial qiṣāṣ when the full act is not subject to qiṣāṣ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that partial qiṣāṣ is not allowed when the injury does not warrant qiṣāṣ and apply it to scenarios like munaqqilah wounds and joint cuts.

Core Idea
Partial retaliation is not permitted when the full act does not justify qiṣāṣ.

This lesson examines a key limitation on qiṣāṣ: if an act of injury does not give rise to qiṣāṣ in its entirety, the victim cannot demand a lesser form of retaliation. The ruling stems from the principle that qiṣāṣ must be available for the whole act; cherry-picking a part is not allowed. We'll analyze the examples from the text—cut joints and head wounds—to understand the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its qiṣāṣ status
First, determine whether the original act of injury is subject to qiṣāṣ. If not, then the victim cannot demand qiṣāṣ for any part of it.
This is the core condition: the act must be a valid cause for qiṣāṣ in its entirety.
2
Examine the victim's request
The victim requests a partial retaliation: cutting only the joint, breaking only the fractured part, or inflicting a muḍiḥah wound instead of a munaqqilah. This is an attempt to circumvent the inapplicability of qiṣāṣ.
The demand focuses on a portion of the injury that would normally be subject to qiṣāṣ if done separately.
3
Apply the rule: no partial qiṣāṣ
Since the original act does not justify qiṣāṣ, the victim's request is denied. The act is treated as a whole; partial retaliation is not an option.
The Hanafi school views this as a logical extension: if the whole is not punishable by qiṣāṣ, the part that would be qiṣāṣ-worthy in isolation loses its independent status.
4
Compare with analogous cases
Consider the example of a munaqqilah wound: it does not incur qiṣāṣ, so the victim cannot demand a muḍiḥah wound (which does incur qiṣāṣ) by waiving the excess. This illustrates the principle uniformly.
The analogy reinforces that the ruling is not arbitrary but based on the nature of the injury as a single legal event.

Sahibayn's Qiyas: Act in Two Locations as Two Crimes
Provides Sahibayn's qiyas argument that the finger cut and eye loss are two separate acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Sahibayn's qiyas argument that an act in two locations constitutes two independent crimes and apply it to the muḍiḥah-eye loss case to determine qiṣāṣ and diyah liability.

Core Idea
An act affecting two body parts is two separate crimes; doubt in one does not bar qiṣāṣ for the other.

In this lesson, we examine the Sahibayn's reasoning in an intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn argue that a muḍiḥah wound and resultant eye loss are separate acts because they occur in two different locations. They support their view with a powerful qiyas (analogy) to an arrow that kills two people: just as qiṣāṣ is due for the first direct victim, qiṣāṣ is due for the direct wound, independent of the indirect eye loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Dispute Context
Recall the earlier case: a person strikes another on the head, causing a muḍiḥah wound that also results in loss of eyesight. Abu Hanifa treats this as a single act, so no qiṣāṣ because of doubt. The Sahibayn disagree.
The dispute hinges on whether the act is one or two.
2
Understand the Core Principle
The Sahibayn assert: 'al-fi‘lu fī maḥallayni fa-yakūnu jīnāyatayni mubtada‘atayni' – the act is in two locations, so it is two independent crimes.
This principle isolates liability: doubt in one location does not spill over to the other.
3
Deconstruct the Qiyas (Arrow Analogy)
The asl (original case): a person intentionally shoots an arrow at a man; it hits him and passes through to kill another. Qiṣāṣ is wajib for the first victim because the act is direct. The far‘ (new case): the muḍiḥah wound is directly inflicted, while the eye loss follows indirectly.
The illa (effective cause) is the act occurring in two distinct anatomical locations.
4
Apply the Illa to the Far‘
Just as the arrow's first hit is a separate crime from the second, the wound on the head is one crime and the eye damage is another. Therefore, qiṣāṣ applies to the wound (direct), and diyah applies to the eyes (indirect).
This yields a practical split: qiṣāṣ for the wound, diyah for the eyes.
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa gives diyah for both (no qiṣāṣ). The Sahibayn give qiṣāṣ for the wound plus diyah for the eyes. The later mashāyikh (mufta bihi) adopted the view of diyah for both, rejecting the Sahibayn's position.

Head Wound with Eye Damage: Qisas or Diyat? (Imam Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn)
Presents and discusses proofs for both sides in a dispute about qisas and diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on qisas for a spreading head wound and explain the key difference in their reasoning using the concept of saari.

Core Idea
A spreading wound (saari) defeats qisas because exact equivalence becomes impossible — only monetary compensation remains.

In criminal liability, unintended consequences of a single blow raise questions of proportionality. This lesson examines the Hanafi dispute on an intentional head wound that unintentionally damages the eyes. Imam Abu Hanifa's concept of saari (spreading) provides the core argument for replacing qisas with monetary compensation. The Sahibayn counter that the act affects two locations, allowing separate punishments.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the two positions
Understand the scenario: a person intentionally strikes another's head, causing a mudihah wound that also unintentionally damages both eyes. Identify the dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa says no qisas (only arsh for head + diyat for eyes); Sahibayn say qisas for the head wound + diyat for the eyes.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that the act occurs in two separate locations (head and eyes), making them two independent crimes. Doubt in one (unintentional eye damage) does not extend to the other. Their analogy: an arrow that passes through one person and kills another — qisas for the first, diyat for the second.
Note the concept of ta'addud al-mahall (multiplicity of place) leading to ta'addud al-jarimah (multiplicity of crime).
3
Analyze Imam Abu Hanifa's proof
Imam Abu Hanifa argues that the first wound is saari (spreading to the eyes). Qisas requires exact equivalence, and the attacker cannot control the spread. Therefore, qisas is impossible and monetary compensation (arsh/diyat) becomes wajib. Furthermore, the act is one (single blow) and the place is unified (head and eyes connected), so the unintended effect casts doubt on the entire act.
Key term: saari – a wound whose effect spreads; the inability to ensure equal retaliation bars qisas.
4
Compare the practical outcomes
Under Imam Abu Hanifa: the offender pays arsh for the head and diyat for the eyes, no qisas. Under Sahibayn: the offender faces qisas for the head (a retaliation wound) and diyat for the eyes. The thamarah al-khilaf is whether physical retaliation is applied to the head.
In practice, the Hanafi school generally follows Imam Abu Hanifa's view on spreading wounds.

Doubt in one (unintentional eye damage) does not extend to the other. They analogize to shooting an arrow that kills two persons: qisas for the first intentional kill, diyat for the second unintentional kill. Then it presents an objection and response: the objection argues that since the eye injury was unintentional, doubt arises and qisas should be dropped. The Sahibayn respond that they don't impose qisas for the eyes, only for the head wound which was intentional, so no doubt. Then Imam Abu Hanifa's proof: the first wound is 'saari' (spreading) — the blow's effect spreads to the eyes. Qisas requires exact equivalence, and the attacker cannot control the spread of the wound, so equivalence is impossible; therefore monetary compensation is wajib. Also, the act is one (a single blow), and the place is unified (the head and eyes are connected), so the unintentional effect at the end casts doubt on the beginning. This differs from killing two separate persons (where acts are separate) and from accidental knife drop. The commentary explains that because the attacker cannot ensure the wound spreads in the same way as was done to him, qisas is impossible, so diyat is imposed.

Key Terms
ساری (saari): A wound that spreads its effect to another part of the body, such as a head wound causing eye damage.
ارش (arsh): Fixed monetary compensation for wounds that are not subject to qisas. It is less than full diyat.
شجہ (shajjah): A head wound. There are grades: mubarriha, damiyah, simhaaq, mudihah, etc. This passage focuses on mudihah (exposing bone) and munqilah (displacing bone).

Child's Liability for Property Destruction
Ruling on child's liability with proof from principle of accountability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a minor's liability for destroying property and explain why intent is irrelevant in rights of people (huquq al-ibad).

Core Idea
A child is liable for property destruction because intent is irrelevant in civil liabilities.

A common misunderstanding is that children are never held accountable for damage because they lack legal capacity. This lesson clarifies a key Hanafi principle: in financial matters (huquq al-ibad), liability follows from the act itself, not from the actor's intention. The passage from Al-Hidayah presents Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's ruling on a child destroying property, along with its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the passage and locate the ruling: the child is liable (ضامن) for destroying property. Note that this applies only when the property is not a deposit (أمانة).
The Arabic phrase 'ضمن' indicates legal liability.
2
Understand the Exception
The ruling explicitly excludes destruction of a deposit (الوديعة). The child is not liable for damaging entrusted property unless negligence is proven.
Deposits are a trust (أمانة), so liability requires a breach of trust.
3
Analyze the Proof
The passage provides the basis: 'لأن الصبي يؤاخذ بأفعاله' – the child is held accountable for his actions. This is contrasted with statements, for which he is not accountable.
The proof is a general principle (qiyas jali).
4
Extract the Principle
The second part of the proof: 'وصحة القصد لا معتبر بها في حقوق العباد' – the validity of intention is not considered in rights of people. This is the core doctrine.
In Hanafi usul, huquq al-ibad are based on objective acts, not subjective states.
5
Note the Cross‑Madhhab Difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafiʿi) require valid intention (or legal capacity) for liability; this leads to different practical rulings.
This ikhtilaf stems from a usul disagreement on the role of intention in civil matters.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the liability of a minor (sabi) for destroying property. The ruling states that if a child destroys property that is not an amanah (deposit), he is liable to compensate the owner. The reason provided is that in the Hanafi school, a child is held accountable for his actions in matters of property rights (huquq al-ibad), even if he is not accountable for his statements. This is because financial liability is based on the act itself, not on the validity of intent. The passage clarifies that this applies only when the property was not entrusted to the child as a deposit, because in the case of deposit, the child is not liable if he destroys it without negligence, as the deposit is not his responsibility. The commentary further explains that the child's lack of valid intention does not exempt him from liability in civil matters, as opposed to religious obligations where intention is crucial. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
صبي (sabi): A minor child who has not reached puberty; legally capable of acts causing financial liability but not of verbal contracts.
ضمان (daman): Legal liability to compensate for loss or destruction of property.
ودیعة (wadi'ah): Deposit; property entrusted to another for safekeeping without compensation.
