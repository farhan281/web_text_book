Inconsistency in Murder Testimony: Time, Place, and Instrument Variations
Rulings on inconsistencies in murder testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that any material inconsistency in witness testimony invalidates it for murder cases, and differentiate between testimony and confession.

Core Idea
In murder cases, witness testimony must be unanimous in all material details; any inconsistency creates doubt and invalidates it.

The text presents scenarios where witnesses agree that the accused caused death but differ on time, place, or instrument. The Hanafi school demands precision to avoid miscarriages of justice. This lesson dissects these rules, contrasts them with confession, and explains the underlying rationale of istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenarios
List the variations given: witnesses differ on night vs. day, one city vs. another, bound vs. unbound instrument, or ignorance of the weapon.
These are all material details in murder cases.
2
Apply the Rule of Unanimity
According to Hanafi fiqh, testimony must be unanimous (ta'lif al-shahada) in all essential particulars. Any material inconsistency invalidates the entire testimony.
The core fact (accused struck victim) alone is insufficient; details matter.
3
Understand the Rationale
Qisas is a severe punishment, so doubt (shubha) must be avoided. Inconsistency creates doubt, thus testimony is rejected.
This is stricter than in non-capital cases.
4
Compare with Confession
The text introduces a case where two persons each confess to the same murder. Confession (iqrar) is binding on each confessor individually, so the guardian can execute both.
Confession does not require external consistency; it is a unilateral admission.
5
Note the Istihsani Dalil
The rule for multiple confessions is based on istihsan (juristic preference) over strict analogy, allowing execution of both confessors.
This shows flexibility in confession compared to testimony.

Re-establishment of Evidence by Absent Heir in Murder Cases
Intra-Hanafi dispute on re-establishment of evidence by absent heir in murder case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position and the Sahibayn's position on whether an absent heir must re-establish evidence of intentional murder, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Each heir's claim to qisas requires independent proof (Abu Hanifa), not joint benefit from one presentation (Sahibayn).

In murder cases (qatl amd), the right to demand qisas belongs to all heirs jointly. But does a single heir's presentation of evidence suffice for all, or must each heir independently prove the murder? This passage from Bab al-Shahadah fi al-Qatl presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn on this procedural question. Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly applying the law of evidence in cases of multiple heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the nature of the case
The murdered man has two sons: one present (shahid) and one absent (gha'ib). The killing is intentional (amd). The present son establishes evidence (bayyinah) of the murder. Then the absent son arrives. The question is whether he must present evidence again.
The case is about qisas (retaliation), not diya (blood money).
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must re-establish the evidence (i'ada al-bayyinah). The judge cannot rule for qisas until the absent heir presents his own evidence.
This is based on the principle that each claimant's proof is independent for corporal claims.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the absent heir does not need to re-establish evidence. Once evidence is established by one heir, it benefits all joint claimants, and the judge can rule for qisas upon the absent heir's arrival.
The Sahibayn view the right as joint, so one proof suffices.
4
Identify the consensus exceptions
For unintentional killing (qatl khata) and for debt owed to the father, there is consensus (ijma') that re-establishment is not required. In these cases, the claim is financial (diya/debt), not corporal.
This highlights the distinction between corporal and financial claims.
5
Apply to the given example
In the text: Zayd kills Bakr. Bakr has two sons, Khalid (present) and Sajid (absent). Khalid presents evidence. Under Abu Hanifa, Sajid must re-present evidence before qisas; under Sahibayn, the judge can rule without new evidence. The accused is kept in custody pending resolution.
The practical difference is whether the absent heir must take action or not.

Commentary
This passage from 'Bab al-Shahadah fi al-Qatl' deals with the procedural requirement for establishing intentional murder (qatl al-amd) in court when the heirs are multiple. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that each heir must independently present evidence (bayyinah) for the claim of qisas. If one son is absent and the present son establishes evidence, the absent son must re-establish it upon arrival before the judge can rule for qisas. The Sahibayn disagree: once evidence is established, the absent son benefits from it and does not need to repeat it, because the right is joint. However, for unintentional killing (qatl al-khata') or debt, there is consensus that re-establishment is not required, perhaps because the claim is for property (diya) rather than corporal punishment. The text provides a practical example: Zayd kills Bakr; Khalid (present) and Sajid (absent) are heirs. Khalid cannot get qisas alone; he must wait for Sajid. Under Abu Hanifa, Sajid must present evidence again; under the Sahibayn, once present, the judge can proceed. The passage ends by noting that the accused is kept in custody until the matter is resolved.

بینہ (bayyinah): Evidence, specifically the testimony of two witnesses or equivalent proof
اعادہ (i'ada): Re-establishment or repetition of evidence
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation, the right of the heirs to demand execution of the murderer

Absent Heir and Re-Testimony in Murder: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on re-testimony by absent heir in murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether an absent heir must re-establish evidence for intentional murder and apply the Sahibayn's qiyas reasoning that qisas is inherited like debt.

Core Idea
Absent heir must re-testify in intentional murder under Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn deem it unnecessary because qisas is inherited like debt.

This passage addresses a critical intra-Hanafi dispute on whether an absent heir must re-establish proof of murder for qisas when a present heir has already done so. The disagreement hinges on whether the right to qisas is inherited (like debt and diyah) or arises independently in each heir. The Sahibayn analogize qisas to debt and diyah, allowing one heir to represent all in litigation. Understanding this distinction clarifies when multiple heirs must each present witnesses and when one suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Consider a person intentionally killed, leaving two sons: one present, one absent. The present son establishes evidence (bayyinah) of murder. The absent son later arrives. Does he need to present evidence again?
Assume the murder is intentional ('amd); the ruling differs for accidental killing and debt.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa rules the absent heir must repeat the evidence. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that the absent heir does not need to re-establish evidence; the first heir's testimony suffices for all.
Both positions are from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof (qiyas)
The Sahibayn argue that qisas is a substitute ('iwad) for the person of the deceased, so ownership follows the same pattern as diyah and debt. Since debt and diyah are inherited, qisas is also inherited (bi-tariq al-wirathah). In inheritance, one heir can represent all in litigation.
The key text: 'ان القصاص طريقه طريق الوراثة كالدين' (qisas follows the method of inheritance like debt).
4
Distinguish wirathah from khilafah
Wirathah (inheritance): the right is first established for the deceased and then passes wholly to each heir. Khilafah (succession): the right is established directly in the heirs without prior ownership of the deceased. Only in wirathah can one heir act for all.
Abu Hanifa apparently considers qisas to be by khilafah, hence each heir must independently prove.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must come and testify, delaying execution of qisas. Under Sahibayn, the judge can proceed immediately after the present heir's testimony (though the accused may be detained). Later fatwa prefers the Sahibayn's view for ease.
Consensus: for accidental killing and debt, no re-testimony is required.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding testimony in murder cases. It presents the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on whether an absent heir must re-establish proof of murder when a present heir has already done so. Abu Hanifa holds that each heir must independently prove the murder for qisas to be enforced, hence the absent heir must repeat the testimony. The Sahibayn argue that qisas is a substitute for the person (nafs) of the deceased and is inherited like debt; thus one heir's testimony suffices for all, as the right belongs to the entire estate. The passage supports the Sahibayn's view by analogy to inheritance (wirathah) as opposed to mere succession (khilafah), noting that in inheritance one heir can represent all in litigation. The distinction between these two types of right acquisition is key: inheritance transfers the right fully to each heir, while succession establishes it independently in each heir without prior transfer from the deceased. The Sahibayn's reasoning is that qisas is an iwad (substitute) for the person, and ownership in the substitute follows ownership in the original person, similar to diyah and debt. Since the right exists first for the deceased and then passes to the heirs by inheritance, one heir can act on behalf of others. The passage concludes with the unanimous rulings for accidental killing and debt, emphasizing that the disagreement is only in intentional murder. The Sahibayn's proof includes: if qisas is converted to fines, it belongs to the deceased's estate; and if the deceased pardons before death, qisas falls, showing the right was his property to begin with.

Key Terms
بينة (bayyinah): Evidence or testimony, specifically the testimony of witnesses to prove murder.
قصاص (qisas): Retributive justice in murder, i.e., the right of the heirs to demand execution of the killer.
وراثة (wirathah): Inheritance: the transfer of a right from the deceased to the heirs as successors in ownership.
خلافة (khilafah): Succession: a right established directly in the heirs without first being established for the deceased.
عوض (iwad): Substitute; in this context, qisas is a substitute for the person (nafs) of the slain.

Testimony of Killing Without Knowledge of the Instrument: Istihsan over Qiyas
Ruling on testimony of killing without instrument; istihsan imposes diyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony of killing without knowledge of the weapon and explain the istihsan rationale distinguishing mutlaq from mujmal.

Core Idea
Absolute testimony of killing is accepted; ignorance of weapon does not invalidate it — istihsan imposes diyah.

In cases where witnesses confirm the act of killing but cannot specify the weapon, a tension arises between strict analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan). This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling, which by istihsan accepts the testimony and imposes diyah (blood money). We will dissect the reasoning, including the distinction between absolute (mutlaq) and ambiguous (mujmal) statements, and the principle of concealment (satr) for reconciliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Qiyas Counterargument
State the Hanafi ruling: by istihsan, diyah is due. Contrast with the qiyas that would reject the testimony because killing varies by instrument, making the subject matter unknown.
The qiyas is based on the idea that ignorance of the weapon creates uncertainty in the crime.
2
Analyze the Istihsan Rationale via Mutlaq vs Mujmal
The witnesses testified to absolute killing (qatl mutlaq), which is not ambiguous (mujmal). In Hanafi usul, mutlaq is operative as is, so the minimum consequence (diyah) applies. The passage uses the analogy of freeing a slave in kaffarah being absolute.
Mutlaq is known in its essence but applies to multiple individuals; mujmal is unknown in essence.
3
Examine the Concealment (Satr) Analogy
The vagueness in testimony is interpreted as concealment for the accused, similar to the hadith permitting lying for reconciliation (islah dhat al-bayn). This prevents the testimony from being labeled false.
The analogy justifies accepting the minimal responsibility.
4
Apply the Distinction Between Mutlaq and Mujmal
The passage illustrates with the cow of Bani Israel: the command was mutlaq, allowing any cow. Similarly, absolute testimony of killing operates despite lack of instrument detail.
This shows that mutlaq does not require specification to be actionable.
5
Determine Practical Outcome and Liability
Since the killing is treated as 'amd (intentional) by default, diyah is paid from the killer's own wealth, not the 'aqila. This differs from accidental killing.
The default presumption in killing is 'amd unless evidence indicates otherwise.

Core Idea
Confession remains valid despite partial denial; testimony is rejected entirely if the beneficiary denies any part.

Two people confess to the same murder, yet the wali can execute both. The answer lies in how the Hanafi school treats the effect of accusing a confessor versus a witness of lying.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct scenarios: (1) two individuals each confess to killing the same person; (2) two sets of witnesses testify that two different individuals each killed the victim.
