Both Rahin and Murtahin Must Unite for Qisas of Mortgaged Slave
Ruling on joint qisas demand for a mortgaged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that both the rahin (mortgagor) and murtahin (mortgagee) must jointly demand qisas for a mortgaged slave, and explain the rationale based on protecting each party's rights.

Core Idea
Qisas for a mortgaged slave requires both owner and creditor to act together – neither alone can demand it.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a mortgaged slave is intentionally killed while in the murtahin's possession. The ruling illustrates how the Hanafi school balances the ownership rights of the rahin with the possessory rights of the murtahin. The requirement that both must join in demanding qisas ensures that the murtahin's security interest is not arbitrarily extinguished.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Rights
Recognize that the rahin is the owner of the slave, while the murtahin holds possession as security for a debt. Ownership gives the rahin authority, but the murtahin's right of custody creates a legal interest.
The murtahin is not an owner; his right is limited to retaining possession until the debt is paid.
2
State the Rule
The ruling: neither the rahin alone nor the murtahin alone may demand qisas; both must join. If one acts without the other, the demand is invalid.
This is a condition (shart) for the validity of qisas in this context.
3
Analyze Why the Rahin Alone Cannot Act
The rahin alone cannot demand qisas because doing so would invalidate the murtahin's right to hold the slave as collateral. The murtahin's right would be extinguished without his consent, which is unjust.
Invalidation of the murtahin's right occurs because qisas leads to the death of the slave, removing the collateral.
4
Analyze Why the Murtahin Alone Cannot Act
The murtahin alone cannot demand qisas because he is not the owner of the slave. The right to demand qisas belongs to the guardian (wali) of the victim, which is the owner. The murtahin lacks the requisite authority.
Ownership is a prerequisite for qisas guardianship.
5
Understand the Dropping of the Murtahin's Right
When both join, the murtahin's right is dropped with his consent. However, the right is not permanently extinguished because reconciliation or creation of doubt (shubha) could later affect the case.
The murtahin's consent is necessary to avoid injustice; the possibility of later change preserves flexibility.

Ruling on the Amount of Compromise Money and Its Immediate Payment
States that the compromise amount is not fixed and is due immediately unless deferred by agreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the flexibility of mal al-sulh amount and explain why it is due immediately by default.

Core Idea
Sulh money: not fixed by text, due immediately by default — it's a contractual debt like mahr.

This passage clarifies two key aspects of the compromise payment (mal al-sulh) in cases of qisas. First, the amount is not predetermined by Shari'ah; it is whatever the parties agree, just like in khul'. Second, if the timing of payment is not mentioned, it becomes due immediately (naqdan), following the same principle as mahr and sale price. This ruling distinguishes sulh from diyah, which is legislatively fixed and paid in installments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
We are looking at the payment (mal al-sulh) agreed upon in a compromise settlement for qisas. The ruling covers two sub-issues: the amount and the timing of payment.
The act is the sulh contract itself, but the ruling focuses on the obligation that arises from it.
2
State the two key rulings
First, the amount can be any value, little or much, because no fixed text defines it. Second, if the parties do not specify whether payment is immediate or deferred, it is due immediately (naqdan).
These are default rules; parties can agree otherwise.
3
Locate the analogy (qiyas)
The text draws an analogy to khul' (divorce by redemption) for the flexibility of amount, and to mahr and sale price for the immediacy of payment. The common factor (illa) is that these are contractual debts arising from mutual agreement with no fixed amount in revelation.
Khul' and sulh both involve a payment in exchange for a right (release from marriage or from qisas).
4
Contrast with diyah
Diyah (blood money) is contrasted because it is not a contractual debt but an obligation imposed by Shari'ah. Therefore, diyah has a fixed amount and is paid in installments over three years, not immediately.
This distinction reinforces the principle: contractual debts are due immediately; Shari'ah-imposed debts may have different rules.
5
Understand the legal reasoning
The key principle is 'mal wajib bil aqd' (property obligatory by contract) defaults to immediacy, unlike 'mal wajib bish-shar'' (property obligatory by Shari'ah) which may have special rules as in diyah.
This principle is used in many areas of Hanafi fiqh, such as mahr and price in sales.

Right to remove obstacles from public roads
Establishes the right of any individual to remove constructions obstructing a public road

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on removing obstructions from the main public road and explain the qiyas (analogy) with shared property that justifies it.

Core Idea
Any person may remove an obstruction from a public road because the right of passage is a common right, like co-ownership.

This lesson from Al-Hidayah addresses a practical issue: what happens when someone builds a structure encroaching on a main road. The Hanafi school empowers any individual to act, not just authorities. We will trace the ruling and its proof through a clear analogy with shared property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited act and the ruling
The act: building a latrine, drain, gutter, or bench onto the main road. The ruling: any person from the general public may remove it.
Focus on the act being an encroachment on the main road (al-tariq al-a'zam).
2
Recognize the basis of the right
Each person has a right to passage for themselves and their animals. This right gives them authority to remove obstructions.
The right is not just a privilege but a legal entitlement (haqq).
3
Examine the qiyas (analogy)
The text draws an analogy with shared property (al-milk al-mushtarak): if one co-owner builds something without permission, the other co-owner has the right to demolish it.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy) because the illa is evident.
4
Apply the illa (effective cause)
The common element is a shared right (haqq mushtarak). In both cases, the right is held by multiple parties, so any party may act to preserve it.
The illa is extracted by tanqih al-manat: the common right is the operative cause.
5
Note the limitation
The ruling applies only to the main road (tariq a'zam), not narrow alleys. The structure must cause harm or obstruction.
Distinguish from zuqaq (alley) where rules may differ due to limited users.

Ruling on Building in a Public Road: Harm as a Decisive Factor
Ruling with proof on building in a public road

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on building structures in a public road and apply the condition of no harm to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
Construction in a public road is permissible unless it causes harm; harm makes it prohibitively disliked.

Building a structure like a latrine or a bench in a public road raises questions of rights and harm. The Hanafi school, based on a clear prophetic hadith, sets a simple yet profound rule: as long as no harm comes to the passersby, the builder may benefit. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and the scope of permissible use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is building a structure (e.g., latrine, bench) in a public road. The ruling: permissible (mubah) if no harm, prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) if harm occurs.
The text uses 'يسع' (it is permissible) and 'كره' (it is disliked).
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof is the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar fi al-Islam'. This is a primary textual evidence (dalil naqli) from the Sunnah.
The hadith is cited directly in the passage.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The negation of harm (darar) and mutual harm (dirar) indicates that any action causing harm is prohibited. Here, harmful construction is disliked (makruh tahrimi) because the prohibition is not absolute but based on harm.
Hanafi usul: the imperative to avoid harm gives a ruling of prohibition, but the degree (tahrim vs tanzih) depends on context. Here, the structure itself is not inherently harmful, so the ruling is makruh tahrimi.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf differ on whether residents can prevent construction even without harm. Imam Muhammad (the author) holds that prevention without valid reason is obstinate (mut'annit) and not allowed.
This intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf will be detailed in a later lesson. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position follows Imam Muhammad.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah adopts Imam Muhammad's view as the preferred position, making it the mufta bihi for fatwa. The condition of harm is the decisive criterion.
The authority_weight is zahir_riwayah, as it appears in the core books of Imam Muhammad.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on man' in public road

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the right to prevent construction in a public road and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
A harmless structure in a public road: can anyone force its removal? Abu Hanifa says yes, Muhammad says no.

Building in a Private Alley: The Requirement of Unanimous Consent
Ruling on construction in a private alley

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on construction in a private alley and identify the condition (permission of all residents) that makes it permissible.

Core Idea
In a private alley, unanimous consent is required for any construction because the alley is jointly owned.

The Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between public thoroughfares and private dead-end alleys. In a private alley (darb ghayr nafidh), the residents collectively own the space. Therefore, any construction that encroaches on the alley—even if not harmful—requires the explicit permission of every co-owner. Guided Path
1

The passage contrasts a thoroughfare (tariq nafidh) with a non-thoroughfare (darb ghayr nafidh). Understand the legal implications of each.
In a public road, permission is impractical; in a private road, it is obligatory.
2

Imam Muhammad rules that no resident may build a latrine or water spout extending into the alley without the consent of all residents.

The alley is 'mamlukah' (owned) by the residents. The evidence is the right of shuf'a (pre-emption) when a house in the alley is sold.
Shuf'a proves shared ownership because it only exists among co-owners.
4
Contrast with the public road
In a public road, a resident may build as long as it does not harm others, because obtaining unanimous consent is impossible.
The law presumes each as a sole owner by legal fiction (hukman).
5

For any disposition (tasarruf) in a private alley, the condition is unanimous permission. Without it, the act is impermissible.
This includes building, extending, or even temporary structures.

Liability for Two Rights: The Case of the Permitted Slave
Imam Muhammad's ruling on double value liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a permitted trading slave commits a crime and owes debt upon emancipation, and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind the obligation of two full values.

Core Idea
Freeing a slave with unpaid debt and crime liability destroys two rights, each demanding full value from the master.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhun lah). The slave has both a debt and a crime liability attached to his person. When the master frees the slave without knowledge of these encumbrances, the master becomes responsible. The key is that two distinct rights—one for the creditors and one for the victim's heirs—are each guaranteed by the entire value of the slave individually. The master must pay two full values but can satisfy both from the same 'neck' by a specific order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and obligations
Recognize the three parties: the slave (ma'dhun lah), the master (who frees him), the creditors (debt-holders), and the victim's heirs (awliya' al-jinaya). The slave carries two obligations: repayment of debt and compensation for the crime (diya or qisas).
The slave's permission to trade gives him capacity to incur debts but his person remains the ultimate security.
2
Determine the master's liability
Upon freeing the slave without knowledge, the master must pay two full values (qimatayn): one for the debt-holder and one for the victim's heirs. Each is a separate obligation.
The value is the market price of the slave at the time of emancipation.
3
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The master 'destroyed two rights' by freeing the slave. Each right is 'guaranteed by the entire value individually'—meaning if only one right existed, it would still require the full value. Hence, two rights require two full values.
This is an application of the principle that a right attached to a specific asset (here, the slave's person) is secured by the entire asset.
4
Note the order of satisfaction
The text states: 'payment to the heirs and sale for the creditors.' First, the slave (or his value) is given to the victim's heirs; then the slave (if taken as qisas) or his remaining value is sold to pay the creditors. This order prioritizes the crime victim.
The phrase 'one neck' indicates both rights can be satisfied from the same slave successive.
5
Recognize the qiyas basis
The ruling is derived by analogy (qiyas) from the case where only one right (e.g., only debt) exists and the master frees the slave: then the master pays one full value. Here two rights exist, so the rule extends to two full values.
The common illa is that each right is individually guaranteed by the entire value.
