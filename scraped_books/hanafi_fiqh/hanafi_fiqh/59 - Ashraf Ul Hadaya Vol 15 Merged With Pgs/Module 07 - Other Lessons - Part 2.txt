Liability for Shooting a Slave Who Is Freed Before Injury
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation when a slave is freed after being shot but before death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad in the case of a slave freed after being shot, and explain the legal reasoning behind the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
The shooter owes full value of the slave, not just depreciation, because the act determines liability at the time of the shot.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about a unique scenario: a slave is shot, freed by his master before the arrow strikes, and then dies. The three great Imams differ on what the shooter must pay. Understanding this case sharpens your grasp of causation (siraaya), ownership, and the timing of liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Recognize that the sequence is: act of shooting, emancipation of the slave by the master, then the arrow hits and the slave dies. Both the act and the death are certain, but the ownership status changed between the act and the death.
2
State the position of each Imam
According to the text, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the shooter owes the full value of the slave (as a slave) to the master. Imam Muhammad holds that he owes only the depreciation in value caused by the injury (the difference between the slave's value before and after the shot).
Abu Yusuf agrees with Abu Hanifa, so the two are called the shaykhayn.
3
Analyze the key concept: siraaya (transmission of injury)
Imam Muhammad argues that emancipation cuts the siraaya, so only the direct harm (depreciation) is compensable. The shaykhayn argue that the shooter becomes a killer from the moment of the shot, and since the slave was owned then, full qimah is owed; siraaya is not relevant because the act itself makes him a killer.
The shaykhayn distinguish this from amputation or wounding where only part is destroyed.
4
Determine the operative ruling
In the Hanafi madhhab, the view of the shaykhayn (full value) is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi), as it is the stronger proof and the well-known transmission.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
For example, if the slave was worth 1000 dirhams before the shot and 800 after the shot due to injury, Muhammad's view gives 200, the shaykhayn give 1000. The difference is substantial.
This shows the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute (intra_hanafi_dispute) concerning the liability of someone who shoots an arrow at a slave, the slave's owner frees him before the arrow strikes, and then the slave dies from the wound. The three imams differ: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the shaykhayn) hold that the shooter owes the full value of the slave (as a slave) to the former master. Imam Muhammad holds that he owes only the depreciation in value caused by the shooting (the difference between the slave's value immediately before the shot and after the shot but before death). The core reasoning revolves around the concept of siraaya (transmission of injury) and whether emancipation (itq) cuts the causal chain. Muhammad argues that 'itq cuts the siraaya, so only the direct injury (the depreciation) is compensable. The shaykhayn argue that the shooter is a killer from the moment of the shot, and since the slave was owned then, full qimah (slave's market value) is owed. They distinguish this from cases of amputation or wounding where only part is destroyed. The passage does not explicitly state the mufta bihi, but in the Hanafi madhhab, the view of the shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is generally relied upon in such cases. The Urdu commentary further explains that the shaykhayn's analogy to amputation is flawed because there the subject changes (the slave becomes free after emancipation), while here the subject does not change (the slave was free at time of death but owned at time of shot).

Key Terms
رمي (ramy): Shooting an arrow or projectile at someone; the act of launching the missile.
سراية (siraayah): Transmission or spread of injury; the legal continuation of a harmful act's effect from the original wound to subsequent death.
عتق (itq): Emancipation of a slave; freeing by the master.
ضمان (daman): Liability or compensation for harm or damage.

Liability for Shooting a Slave Who Becomes Free Before Impact
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for arrow shot at slave who becomes free before impact

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions on liability when a slave is shot and freed before impact, and evaluate the arguments of the Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
Liability is fixed at the time of shooting, so emancipation before impact does not reduce the obligation.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi debate on a nuanced question: if someone shoots an arrow at a slave, and the master frees the slave before the arrow strikes, what is the shooter's liability if the slave dies? Imam Muhammad argues that only the reduction in value is owed, analogizing to a case of cutting a slave's hand. The Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) contend that the shooter owes the full value because the act is complete at the time of shooting. Imam Zufar holds a third view, demanding diyah since the victim is free at death. We will unpack the reasoning and the tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Dispute
What is the point of contention? The key issue is whether the shooter's liability is determined at the moment of shooting (ramy) or at the moment of impact (isaba).
2
Examine Imam Muhammad's Analogy
Imam Muhammad argues that just as cutting a slave's hand followed by emancipation only results in compensation for the hand's value, shooting a slave followed by emancipation should only result in compensation for the reduction in value caused by the shooting act.
This relies on the concept of seraya (transmission) being cut off by a change in the victim's status.
3
Analyze the Shaykhayn's Rebuttal
The Shaykhayn reject the analogy. In the cutting case, there is partial destruction of the locus (the hand), and after transmission, the compensation would go to a different person (the freed slave). In shooting, the act is direct and aims at the whole person; there is no partial destruction before impact. Thus, the shooter is considered a killer from the moment of shooting.
They emphasize that the status of the victim at the time of the act determines liability, not subsequent changes.
4
Understand the Time-of-Act Principle
The text clarifies that shooting itself, before impact, does not destroy anything; it only affects people's desires. Therefore, no compensation is due before impact. But once the arrow hits and kills, the liability is traced back to the shooting act, and since the victim was owned at that time, the full value is owed to the master.
This is why there is no change in locus (mahall) in this case, unlike in the cutting analogy.
5
Note Zufar's Position and the Author's Ruling
Imam Zufar considers the time of impact, so he would require diyah (blood money) because the victim is free at that moment. The author rejects this, stating that the proofs for the time of shooting are established.
The mufta bihi position is that of the Shaykhayn: full value to the master.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability when someone shoots an arrow at a slave, and the slave is freed by his master before the arrow strikes. The key issue is whether the shooter's liability is determined at the time of shooting or at the time of impact. Imam Muhammad argues that the shooter only owes compensation for the reduction in the slave's value caused by the shooting itself, because the transmission of the fatal injury (seraya) is cut off by the change of status (emancipation). He analogizes to a case where a hand is cut and then the slave is freed and dies: the cutter owes only for the hand's damage, not full value. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Verbal differences among Hanafi imams do not change rulings when both agree on the essential meaning.

In the classification of head wounds (shijaj), Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad disagree on the order and definition of two types: mutalahimah and baadi'ah. However, this is a purely linguistic variation; both imams agree on the legal assessment (hukumat 'adl). Understanding such intra-madhhab differences prevents unnecessary confusion and reinforces that the school's unity lies in practical rulings rather than terminological preferences.

Identify the two positions

The author presents Abu Yusuf's view as the primary reference.

Compare the definitions of mutalahimah

The essential medical condition is similar.
3
Recognize the legal consequence
Check the ruling for both wounds: according to the text, both require hukumat 'adl (fair estimation of diyat). Thus, the terminological difference produces no practical variation in the mufta bihi.
This is confirmed by the author's statement: 'this is a difference of wording not affecting meaning and ruling.'
4
Determine the tarjih
The author (Ashraf al-Hidayah) does not give preference to either version; both are accepted as valid transmissions from the two imams. The mufta bihi is that both wounds are treated identically.
The dispute is classified as 'ikhtilaf lafzī' (verbal difference).
5
Apply the principle to other intra-Hanafi differences
Generalize: when two Hanafi authorities differ only in wording or arrangement without affecting the core legal effect, the ruling remains unchanged. This preserves the school's coherence despite multiple narrations.
This is a common feature in Hanafi fiqh (e.g., definitions of ghusl, wudu, etc.).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define daamighah, explain why it is excluded from the ten shijaj in Hanafi fiqh, and apply the classification principle to head wounds.

Core Idea
Daamighah is omitted from shijaj because its typical fatality shifts the ruling to homicide, not wound damages.

This passage introduces daamighah, a head wound that reaches the brain. Although it could be considered an eleventh shajjah, Imam Muhammad deliberately omitted it. The reason? Because such a wound almost always results in death, moving it from the category of 'wound-specific diyat' to the laws of killing (qisas or full diyat). Understanding this classification is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi law of injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wound and its defining feature
Daamighah is a head wound that penetrates the skull to the brain. Note the precise anatomical criterion: yussilu ila al-dimagh.
This distinguishes it from aamah (wound reaching the membrane) and other shijaj.
2
Recognize the legal consequence of the definition
Because the wound reaches the brain, it almost always causes death. The text says it 'usually results in death' (تقع قتلاً في الغالب).
This is not a minor injury; it is typically fatal.
3
Understand the classification shift
Once the injury is fatal, it ceases to be a mere shajjah (a non-fatal head wound) and becomes an act of killing (qatl). The ruling changes from diyat for wounds to the more severe penalties for homicide.
Shijaj are defined as non-fatal wounds; fatal wounds are outside that category.
4
Explain the reason for omission
Imam Muhammad did not list daamighah among the ten shijaj because its ruling is not distinct from homicide. There is no need for a separate provision; it is covered by qisas/diyat for death.
The passage emphasizes: 'since the ruling of daamighah is not distinct from the ruling of killing'.
5
Apply the principle to a practical scenario
When encountering a head wound, first check if it penetrates the brain. If it does, treat it as a potential homicide case, not as a shajjah with graded diyat.
This classification affects the entire legal response: liability, punishment, and damages.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Leader is liable for trampling; joint liability if driver present at side; split liability if driver in middle.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common tort scenario in pre-modern transport: when a line of camels (qitar) causes harm by trampling, who bears the liability? The Hanafi school provides detailed rules distinguishing the leader (qa'id), who controls from the front, and the driver (sa'iq), who drives from behind. The key is causation with transgression (ta'addi). By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the liable parties and the basis for apportionment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the act? Leading a line of camels. What is the ruling? The leader is liable (daman) for what the camels trample. Note the proof: causation with transgression (ta'addi) is a cause for liability.
2
Distinguish between killing and property damage
If a person is killed, the blood-money (diyah) is upon the 'aqilah (tribal guarantors). If property is damaged, compensation is from the leader's own wealth. This distinction is based on the nature of the harm.
3
Apply joint liability when a driver is present at the side
If both a leader and a driver are present and the driver is at the side of the camels, both are jointly liable for all harm caused. This is because each controls the entire line through the connected reins (azimma).
4
Apply split liability when the driver is in the middle
If the driver positions himself in the middle and holds only one rein, he is liable for what is behind him (those camels are under his control as a leader), and both are liable for what is in front (the leader leads them, the driver drives them from behind). The reins disconnection separates control.
5
Identify the underlying reasoning (ta'lil)
The basis for liability is 'causation with transgression' (tasbib bi-wasf al-ta'addi). The leader has a duty to safeguard and the ability to do so; negligence makes him a transgressor. This applies to both leader and driver.

Liability for a Slave's Death After Usurpation: The Principle of Sirayah
Explains two scenarios of master cutting slave's hand and subsequent death after ghasb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that usurpation (ghasb) severs the causal chain (sirayah) of a pre-existing injury to determine liability for a slave's death.

Core Idea
Usurpation severs sirayah like a sale, shifting liability to the usurper for the diminished value.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi ruling in tort law: when a master cuts his slave's hand and the slave later dies after being usurped, who is liable? The answer depends on the timing of the usurpation. By studying the two scenarios presented in the text, you will learn how the Hanafi school uses analogy (qiyas) to sale (bay') to resolve the issue of causal spread (sirayah). Understanding this ruling will sharpen your ability to trace causal chains in liability cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage carefully to distinguish the two cases: (1) master cuts hand before usurpation, then slave dies in usurper's possession; (2) master cuts hand after usurpation (while slave is in usurper's possession), then slave dies.
Pay attention to the order of events: cut first vs. cut second.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
In the first scenario, the usurper is liable for the value of a one-handed slave (aqta'). In the second, the usurper owes nothing. Note the practical difference in liability.
The amount is not the full value but the diminished value.
3
Understand the proof (dalil): analogy to sale
The text states that usurpation (ghasb) is like sale (bay') because both transfer ownership. Sale severs the causal chain (sirayah). Therefore, after usurpation, the death is attributed to a heavenly affliction, not the original cut.
The operative phrase: 'الغصب قاطع للسراية لأنه الملک کالبيع' – usurpation cuts off sirayah because it is ownership like sale.
4
Apply the principle to both scenarios
In scenario 1, usurpation occurs after the cut, so it severs sirayah; the usurper pays. In scenario 2, the cut occurs after usurpation, so no severance occurs; the master remains liable (as the one who inflicted the wound), and the usurper is free.
The second scenario also involves the master retaking possession (istirdad), which further supports the discharge of the usurper.
5
Consider the cross-madhhab dimension
This ruling is mukhtalaf fih. Other schools may not accept that ghasb severs the causal chain like a sale. The Hanafi position relies on qiyas jaliy (clear analogy). Recognize that the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is the allocation of liability.
No detailed opposing proof is given in this passage, but the disagreement is legitimate.

غصب (ghasb): Usurpation or wrongful possession of someone else's property without consent.
ضامن (damin): Liable to pay compensation or guarantee the value of the property.

Mudabbar's Liability After Two Crimes and Recourse from Usurper
Ruling on mudabbar's price when two crimes occur, one during usurpation and one after return, with intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the master's liability for a mudabbar who commits two crimes, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the disposition of the half value recovered from the usurper, and apply the ruling to similar ghasb scenarios.

Core Idea
A master who prevents surrender of a mudabbar must pay full value; the half from the usurper goes to first victim per Shaykhayn, but Imam Muhammad says master keeps it.

This lesson unpacks a scenario where a mudabbar is usurped and commits a crime, then after return commits another. The master is liable for the full value, divided equally. But there is a subtle dispute on what the master does with the half he recovers from the usurper: does it go to the first victim or stay with the master? Understanding this dispute sharpens your skill in tracing the thamarah al-khilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and sequence of events
Clearly map the three actors: the master (mawla), the usurper (ghasib), and the mudabbar slave. Note the two crimes: first in usurper's possession, second after return to master.
The mudabbar is a slave whose tadbir (promise of freedom after master's death) makes him unsellable and untransferable.
2
Determine the master's primary liability
The master must pay the full value of the mudabbar, split equally between the two victims (guardians of the crimes). The reasoning: by making him mudabbar, the master disabled himself from surrendering the slave, thus nullifying the victims' right to the slave's person.
Only one value is due because only one slave is involved, but two equal claims exist.
3
Analyze the master's right of recourse
The master recovers half the value from the usurper because the first crime occurred in the usurper's hand; the usurper's liability shifts to the master's payment, so the master can seek reimbursement for that half.
This is analogous to a case where half the slave became due due to a cause in the usurper's possession.
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute on the recovered half
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (Shaykhayn): the master must give that half to the first victim, then seek recourse from the usurper. According to Imam Muhammad: the master keeps that half for himself after paying full value to both victims.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is who ultimately bears the cost of the first crime.
5
Compare the two positions and extract the mufta bihi
The text presents both views without explicit tarjih, but the Shaykhayn's view is mentioned first. In later practice, the view of the Shaykhayn may be relied upon, but the difference remains legitimate.
This dispute highlights how differing premises about the nature of recourse can lead to different outcomes.

Commentary
This passage addresses a scenario where a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) is usurped, commits a crime while with the usurper, is returned to the master, and then commits another crime with the master. The ruling: The master (owner) is liable for the full value of the mudabbar, to be divided equally between the two crime victims (or their guardians). The reason is that the master, by making the slave a mudabbar, has disabled himself from surrendering him to the victims (since a mudabbar cannot be sold or handed over for criminal liability), and he does not choose to ransom the slave (i.e., pay compensation to avoid surrender). Thus he effectively nullifies the victims' right to the slave's person, so he must pay the value. Only one value is due because only one slave is involved. The value is split equally because both crimes are equal in their legal cause. The master may then seek recourse from the usurper for half the value, since that half is due because of the crime committed while the slave was in the usurper's possession. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the Shaykhayn), the master must give this half to the first victim and then seek recourse from the usurper for that amount. According to Imam Muhammad, the master keeps this half for himself after he has already paid the full value to both victims.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whom the master has declared to be free upon the master's death (tadbir). Such a slave cannot be sold or transferred.
جنایت (jinayah): A crime or tort, especially one that incurs financial liability (diyah or damages).
رجوع (rujoo'): Recourse or claim for reimbursement from a party who caused the loss.
