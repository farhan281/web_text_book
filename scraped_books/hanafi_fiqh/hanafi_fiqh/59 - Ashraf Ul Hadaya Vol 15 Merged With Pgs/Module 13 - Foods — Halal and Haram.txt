Hunting by a Magian who becomes Muslim before the arrow hits
Ruling on permissibility of game based on shooter's status at time of shooting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game shot by a Magian who converts before the arrow hits and explain why the moment of shooting is the criterion for dhabh eligibility.

Core Idea
Permissibility of game depends on shooter's eligibility at the moment of shooting, not later conversion.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a Magian shoots an animal, becomes Muslim before the arrow strikes—is the meat halal? The Hanafi ruling hinges on the precise timing of the act that constitutes dhabh (slaughter) for game. We will analyse the two cases presented and extract the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contrasted scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) a Magian shoots, then converts before the arrow hits; (2) a Muslim shoots, then apostatizes before the arrow hits. Note the rulings stated.
The Magian is not a Kitabi and thus lacks eligibility for dhabh.
2
Determine the ruling for each case
State the ruling: in the first case the game is not halal; in the second it is halal. This is the explicit ruling from the text.
The ruling is based on the principle: 'al-mu'tabar hal al-ramy'.
3
Locate the 'illah (effective cause)
The text states: 'li-anna al-mu'tabar hal al-ramy fi haqq al-hill wa al-hurmah'—the considered state for permissibility is the state of shooting. Also 'al-ramy huwa al-dhabh'—shooting is the dhabh.
This makes the shooter's eligibility at the time of shooting decisive.
4
Apply the principle to both cases
Since shooting is the dhabh, the shooter must be eligible at that moment. The Magian was not eligible at shooting, so conversion later does not help. The Muslim was eligible at shooting, so later apostasy does not harm.
This distinction is crucial for fatwa practice.

Commentary
This passage addresses the permissibility of eating game shot by a person who changes religious status between shooting and the arrow hitting. The ruling is based on the principle that the act of shooting is considered the effective slaughter (dhabh) for the game. Therefore, the shooter must be eligible (i.e., Muslim or Kitabi) at the time of shooting. If a Magian (who is not eligible because Magians are not considered People of the Book in Hanafi law regarding dhabh) shoots game and then converts to Islam before the arrow hits, the game is not halal because the shooter lacked eligibility at the moment of shooting. Conversely, if a Muslim shoots and then apostatizes before the arrow hits, the game is halal because the shooter was eligible (Muslim) at the time of shooting. This is consistent with the broader Hanafi rule that the legal status of the act is determined at the moment of the act (shooting).

Key Terms
ذكاة (dhabh): Slaughter, including shooting as a means of killing game; requires the shooter to be qualified (Muslim or Kitabi).
أهلية (ahliyyah): Legal capacity/eligibility; here, the status of being a Muslim or Kitabi necessary for valid dhabh.

Key Takeaway
Game shot by a non-eligible person is not halal even if he becomes eligible afterwards; the moment of shooting determines permissibility.
