Shibh al-Amd and Deprivation of Inheritance: The Hanafi-Maliki Dispute
The killer in shibh al-amd is deprived of inheritance, and Malik's rejection of the category

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school deprives the killer in shibh al-amd of inheritance and contrast this with the Maliki position.

Core Idea
Doubt (shubhah) drops qiṣāṣ but not the killer's deprivation of inheritance.

This passage continues the discussion on qatl shibh al-amd (quasi-intentional killing) and its effect on inheritance. The Hanafi school rules that the killer is deprived of inheritance, just as in intentional murder, because doubt only affects the severest penalty (retaliation), not the civil consequences. Imam Malik, however, rejects the entire category of shibh al-amd, considering all intentional killings as plain ʿamd. This lesson parses the argument and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that shibh al-amd entails deprivation of inheritance (ḥirmān al-mīrāth) for the killer. Note that this is connected to the previous discussion on the definition of shibh al-amd.
The ruling is that the killer does not inherit from the victim.
2
Understand the Principle
The text gives the reason: doubt (shubhah) is effective in dropping qiṣāṣ but not in dropping the deprivation of inheritance. This is a key usul principle: doubt mitigates the harsher penalty but does not remove the lesser civil consequence.
Doubt here refers to the uncertainty about intent in shibh al-amd.
3
Recognize the Maliki Objection
Imam Malik denied knowledge of shibh al-amd altogether, implying he does not recognize it as a distinct category. For him, any intentional killing with a weapon-like object is ʿamd requiring qiṣāṣ.
Malik's position is based on his methodology of considering only well-known categories.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that the hadith evidence (already presented in earlier lessons) establishes shibh al-amd. The text says 'the proof against him is what we narrated originally'.
The hadith about the whip and stick is the primary evidence for the Hanafi view.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: In a killing with a stick, Hanafis rule shibh al-amd (no qiṣāṣ, heavy diyah on ʿāqila, killer excluded from inheritance). Malikis rule ʿamd (qiṣāṣ or diyah on killer, killer excluded from inheritance but for different reason).
Both schools agree on exclusion from inheritance, but the basis and other consequences differ.

Deprivation of Inheritance for the Accidental Killer
Ruling on accidental killer's deprivation of inheritance with talil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the accidental killer is deprived of inheritance and explain the talil (legal reasoning) based on the presence of sin.

Core Idea
The accidental killer is barred from inheritance because his act involves sin, making deprivation a valid consequence.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a key rule in Islamic inheritance law: the killer does not inherit from the victim. While intentional murder obviously prevents inheritance, the accidental killer is also prevented. The author explains the rationale: the presence of sin (ithm) in the accidental killing—due to negligence—justifies the deprivation. He then contrasts this with a case where the killer intentionally strikes but misses the intended spot, to highlight that the accidental killing still carries sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The accidental killer (qatl khata) does not inherit from the victim. This is the explicit ruling of the text.
2
Locate the reason (illah)
The author states 'because there is sin in it' (lan fihi ithman). The sin (ithm) is the cause for deprivation.
Sin here refers to negligence (tafrit), not intentional wrongdoing.
3
Understand the principle
Deprivation of inheritance (hurman) is suspended on sin; wherever sin exists, deprivation is valid. This is a talil argument: the ruling follows from the presence of sin.
The text says: 'so suspending deprivation on it is correct.'
4
Note the contrast with intentional miss
In the intentional miss case, qisas is wajib, not just deprivation. The contrast shows that even in accidental killing, sin exists, so deprivation is appropriate. The intentional miss case has greater sin (intent) and thus a stronger punishment.

Intentional Striking Missing Target: Entire Body as One Locus
Ruling on intentional striking that misses target: qatl amd and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intentional striking of any part of the body, even if the blow lands on another part, constitutes qatl amd (intentional killing) and explain why qisas is wajib.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the entire body is one locus: intent to strike any part makes the resulting death intentional killing (qatl amd).

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a subtle case: a person intentionally aims at a specific body part (e.g., the foot) but misses and hits another part (e.g., the forehead), causing death. The author rules that this is intentional killing (qatl amd) and qisas is wajib. The rationale: the entire body is considered a single locus (mahal wahid), so the intent to strike the body exists. This contrasts with the accidental killing case where no intent exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario is intentional striking of a body part that misses the intended spot but hits another part, causing death.
2
Understand the ruling
The ruling is that qisas (retaliation) is wajib.
This is because the killing is considered intentional (qatl amd).
3
Identify the reason (illah)
The reason is that the entire body is like a single locus; intent to strike some part of the body constitutes intent to strike the body as a whole.
The text says: 'wa jami' al-badan ka al-mahal al-wahid' (the entire body is like a single locus).
4
Contrast with accidental killing
In accidental killing, there is no intent to strike the body, only negligence. Therefore, the ruling is different: deprivation of inheritance and diyah, not qisas.

Ghasb of a Free Child: Liability by Istihsan
Hanafi ruling on liability for seized free child with istihsan proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a free child dies in the usurper's custody and explain the istihsan basis over qiyas.

Core Idea
Liability for a seized free child arises from causation (tasbib), not the invalid seizure itself.

This passage from the Book of Blood Money addresses a nuanced case: when a person seizes a free child and the child dies in his possession. The Hanafi school distinguishes between different causes of death, imposing diyah on the 'aqila only for location-specific dangers introduced by the usurper's transgression. The ruling is established by istihsan, overriding the qiyas that would negate liability entirely. Understanding this reveals how Hanafi jurists prioritize causation over formal invalidity, and how istihsan operates as a source of law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
List the two scenarios: (1) sudden death or fever → no liability; (2) death from lightning strike or snakebite → diyah on the usurper's 'aqila.
The diyah is not on the usurper directly but on his 'aqila (male agnate relatives) because the killing is by causation (tasbib), not direct (mubashara).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Zufar and al-Shafi'i hold no liability in either case via qiyas: a free person cannot be the object of ghasb, so seizure is legally nonexistent; thus no liability can attach.
They analogize to a minor mukatib (who is free in possession but not in person) – if seized, no liability.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The analogy applies a fortiori (al-awla): if a minor mukatib (free in possession) triggers no liability, then a free child (free in both person and possession) should trigger none.
This is a qiyas jali apparent analogy.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Liability arises from destruction (itlaf), not from the invalid ghasb. The usurper's act of transferring the child to a place where lightning/snakes are common is a transgressive condition that removes the guardian's protection; this condition is treated as the cause itself (shart al-illa yanzilu manzilat al-illa).
This is the istihsan argument. Analogy to digging a well in a public road.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, the Hanafi school imposes diyah on the 'aqila for death by location-specific causes; the opposing schools impose no liability at all, leaving the victim's family uncompensated.
The difference stems from whether one treats the transgression as a causal link (Hanafi) or rejects it (Zufar/Shafi'i).

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Blood Money (Kitab al-Diyat) in Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a ruling on the liability of a person who seizes (ghasb) a free child and the child dies in his custody. The passage states the Hanafi position, contrasts it with the qiyas-based view of Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i, and provides the Hanafi istihsan justification. The ruling distinguishes between two causes of death: sudden death or fever (no liability), and death by lightning strike or snakebite (diyah on the usurper's 'aqila). The Hanafi school (based on the matn of Imam Muhammad) holds that the usurper is not liable merely by virtue of the seizure (ghasb) of a free person, since a free person cannot be truly owned or possessed. However, if the child dies due to a cause that the usurper introduced by moving him to a dangerous location, then the usurper is liable by causation (tasbib) — i.e., his act of transferring the child to a place where lightning or snakes are common is considered a transgressive act that removes the guardian's protection. This follows the juristic principle that a condition accompanied by transgression is treated as the cause itself, analogous to digging a well in a public road. The opposing school argues that since a free person cannot be the object of ghasb, there can be no liability at all (by analogy to a minor mukatib). The Hanafi response relies on istihsan, emphasizing that liability arises from destruction caused by the usurper's act, not from the invalid seizure. The passage also notes that if the usurper moves the child to a place where fever or disease is common, he would also be liable, extending the principle consistently.

Key Terms
غصب (ghasb): Seizing/appropriation of property (or a free person) without right and without the owner's permission. For a free person, it is legally invalid.
عاقلة ('aqila): The male agnate relatives (asabah) of the killer who share liability for paying diyah in cases of semi-intentional or accidental killing.
استحسان (istihsan): A juristic preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a stronger ruling based on subtle evidence or necessity. Here, it is used to impose liability despite the invalid ghasb.
دية (diyah): Monetary compensation paid to the heirs of a victim in cases of unlawful killing, as an alternative to qisas (retaliation).
إتلاف تسبيبا (itlaf tasbib): Destruction by causation, where the act sets in motion a chain leading to harm, as opposed to direct destruction (itlaf mubashara).
