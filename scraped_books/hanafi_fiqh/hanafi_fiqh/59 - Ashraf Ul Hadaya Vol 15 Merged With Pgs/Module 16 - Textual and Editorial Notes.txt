Acceptance of Slave's Word in Killing Dispute
Ruling on acceptance of slave's statement in killing dispute with qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a freed slave's status at killing is disputed and apply the analogy (qiyas) to similar cases of claimed minority or insanity.

Core Idea
The slave's word is accepted because he denies liability by attributing the killing to a known state (slavery) incompatible with liability.

In this passage from the Hidayah, Imam Muhammad addresses a specific dispute: a freed slave and the victim's relative disagree on whether the slave was free or enslaved at the time of the killing. The ruling turns on the principle that the person denying a claim is credible if they attribute the act to a known state that negates liability. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the qiyas (analogy) used to support it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The freed slave claims he killed the victim while still a slave; the victim's relative claims he was free. Determine the legal consequence of each claim: if slave, liability on master; if free, liability on slave himself.
2
Locate the ruling
The text states explicitly: 'the word is the word of the slave.' Understand that this is a procedural ruling on whose statement is taken as true.
3
Understand the reason given
The slave is 'denying liability' (munkir li al-ḍamān) because he attributes the act to a 'known state incompatible with liability' (ḥālah maʿhūdah munāfiyah li al-ḍamān).
This is key: the denial is not mere denial but based on a known fact.
4
Identify the proof: Qiyas
The text draws an analogy (qiyas) to cases where an adult says he divorced while a child or insane. In those cases, the word is also accepted because the state (minority/insanity) is known and negates liability.
The qiyas is from a clear original case (aṣl) to the new case (farʿ).
5
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The effective cause (ʿillah) is the known incompatibility of the state with legal liability. This ʿillah is present in both the original and the new case, making the analogy valid.
The ʿillah is jaliyyah (clear) because the state is objectively verifiable.

قال وإذا أعتق العبد فقال لرجل قتلت أخالك خطأ وأنا عبد وقال الآخر قتلته وأنت حر فالقول قول العبد لأنه منكر للضمان لما أنه أسنده إلى حالة معهودة منافية للضمان إذ الكلام فيما إذا عرف رقه والوجوب في جناية العبد على المولى دفعا أو فداء وصار كما إذا قال البالغ العاقل طلقت امرأتي وأنا صبى أو بعت دارى وأنا صبى أو قال طلقت امرأتي وأنا مجنون وقد كان جنونه معروفا كان القول قوله لما ذكرنا
He said: If a slave is freed and then says to a man, 'I killed your brother by mistake while I was a slave,' and the other says, 'You killed him while you were free,' then the word is the word of the slave, because he is denying liability, since he attributes it to a known state incompatible with liability. The discussion is when his enslavement is known, and liability for the slave's crime is upon the master by surrendering him or paying ransom. It becomes like when a sane adult says, 'I divorced my wife while I was a child,' or 'I sold my house while I was a child,' or 'I divorced my wife while I was insane, and his insanity was known,' then his word is accepted for the reason we mentioned.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling from Imam Muhammad regarding a dispute between a freed slave and the victim's relative. The slave claims he committed the killing while still a slave, which would place liability on his former master. The relative claims he was free, making the slave himself liable. The text rules that the slave's word is accepted because he is denying liability (damān) by attributing the act to a known state (slavery) that negates his personal liability. The proof is an analogy (qiyās) to cases where an adult claims an action was done during minority or insanity, conditions incompatible with legal responsibility. In all such cases, the person's statement is taken as truthful because they are denying a claim (inkār) and have a plausible basis. The Hanafi position is that the default is to accept the word of the person denying liability, with an oath, as per the general principle 'al-bayyinah ʿalā al-muddaʿī wa al-yamīn ʿalā man ankara'.

Key Terms
ضمان (ḍamān): Financial liability or responsibility for compensation (diyah or arsh).
عاقلہ (ʿāqilah): The collective group (usually male agnates) who pay diyah in cases of accidental killing.

The Freed Slave-Girl's Word: Scope and Exceptions
Ruling on whose word is accepted when master claims injury occurred during slavery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose word is accepted in disputes between a master and freed slave-girl, and explain the internal ikhtilaf between Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
The freed slave-girl's word is accepted for physical injuries and property, but not for intercourse and earnings, by istihsan.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced ruling regarding disputes after emancipation. When a master claims injury occurred during slavery and the freed slave-girl claims it occurred during freedom, the default rule is that her word is accepted. However, exceptions exist for intercourse and earnings, based on istihsan. Within the Hanafi school, Imam Muhammad further restricts the ruling to only physically traceable items. Understanding this internal ikhtilaf sharpens one's ability to apply procedural principles in similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Claims
The master freed a slave-girl, then claims he cut her hand while she was still his property (so no liability). She claims it happened after freedom (so he must compensate).
Other claims like property taken, intercourse, and earnings are also considered.
2
State the Default Rule of Shaykhayn
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the freed slave-girl's word is accepted for all claims except intercourse and earnings.
This includes physical injuries and any property taken from her.
3
Recognize the Istihsan Exception
Intercourse and earnings (ghullah) are excluded from her word because the state of slavery is not incompatible with liability for these — istihsan overrides the default analogy.
Earnings belong to the master during slavery; intercourse is a right of the master.
4
Understand Imam Muhammad's Position
Imam Muhammad holds that the master only guarantees a physically traceable thing (like a severed limb) that he admits was in her possession. For other claims, the master does not guarantee anything because he denies liability by attributing the act to a known state (slavery).
This is a more restrictive view.
5
Trace the Qiyas and Istihsan Reasoning
The default analogy (qiyas) is that the freed person's word is accepted, as in the case of a male slave claiming killing during slavery. Istihsan introduces exceptions for intercourse and earnings (Shaykhayn) or restricts it to traceable items (Muhammad).
The illah is attributing the act to a known state incompatible with liability.

Source Text

قال ومن أعتق جارية ثم قال لها قطعت يدك وأنت أمتى وقالت قطعتها وأنا حرة فالقول قولها وكذلك كل ما أخذ منها إلا الجماع والغلة استحسانا وهذا عند أبي حنيفة وأبي يوسف رحمهما الله. وقال محمد لا يضمن إلا شيئا قائما بعينه يؤمر برده عليها لأنه منكر وجوب الضمان إسناد الفعل إلى حالة معهودة منافية له كما فى المسئلة الأولى وكما فى الوطى والغلة وفى الشيء القائم أقر بيدها حيث اعترف
He said: Whoever frees a slave-girl and then says to her, 'I cut your hand while you were my property,' and she says, 'You cut it while I was free,' then the word is her word. And likewise everything taken from her except intercourse and earnings, by istihsan. And this is according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, may Allah have mercy on them. And Muhammad said: He does not guarantee except for something physically traceable, which he is ordered to return to her, because he is denying the obligation of guarantee by attributing the act to a known state incompatible with it, as in the first case, and as in intercourse and earnings. In the case of the physically traceable thing, he admitted that it was in her possession where he acknowledged.
Source pages p. 279

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute after a female slave is freed. The master claims he cut her hand while she was still his property (so no liability), but she claims it happened after freedom (so he must pay compensation). The ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is that her word is accepted for physical injuries and for any property taken from her, except for intercourse (where the master's word is taken) and earnings (income generated during slavery, where the master's word is taken). This is based on istihsan (juristic preference). Imam Muhammad differs: he holds that the master is only liable for a physically traceable thing (like a severed limb) that is still extant and can be returned; for that, the master's admission of possession leads to liability. For other claims, the basic principle of the first case (the slave's word accepted) applies. The passage shows a nuanced ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school regarding the extent to which the female's word is accepted after emancipation.

Key Terms
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; a method of departing from strict analogy for a more equitable result.
غلة (ghullah): Earnings or income generated by the slave while in ownership.
