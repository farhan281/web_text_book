Navigating Qisas Topics in Ashraf al-Hidayah
Listing of chapter headings and topics on qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main topics covered in Volume 15 of Ashraf al-Hidayah and locate where specific qisas rulings are discussed.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap to mastering Hanafi qisas rulings.

This volume covers the law of retaliation (qisas) in detail. Before diving into the nuanced rulings, it is essential to know the structure of the volume. This lesson will help you navigate the contents and understand the scope of topics, from equality conditions to specific weapons and scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Scan the TOC for major thematic sections
Quickly read through the entire table of contents to identify broad categories such as qisas for life, qisas for less than life, diyah, and procedural rules.
Notice recurring phrases like 'قصاص', 'دیت', and names of imams.
2
Identify recurring legal issues
List the key issues that appear multiple times, such as equality between parties (male/female, free/slave), specific weapons (sword, gun, spade), and killing in special contexts (battlefield, drowning).
These are the core problems that fuqaha debated.
3
Note the presence of evidence citations
Observe where the TOC explicitly mentions 'دلیل' (evidence) of specific imams like Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, Imam Muhammad, and Imam Shafi'i. This indicates where comparative fiqh discussions occur.
These sections are particularly important for understanding inter-madhhab differences.
4
Recognize the TOC patterns for navigation
The TOC is organized hierarchically. Main headings (e.g., باب القصاص) are followed by sub-headings. Page numbers are provided. Use this to quickly locate a topic when studying.
The OCR has scattered numbers; cross-reference with the actual book if possible.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (fihrist) for Volume 15 of Ashraf al-Hidayah, the Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. It lists the chapter headings and sub-topics related to qisas (retaliation) and related issues such as diyah (blood money), rules of killing, and differences among fuqaha. The topics cover various scenarios: equality in qisas between men and women, free persons and slaves, sane and insane, and the rulings for specific cases like killing by a tool, drowning, and battlefield accidents. The list includes references to evidence (dalil) of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, Imam Muhammad, and Imam al-Shafi'i, as well as qiyas and istihsan. This is primarily a structural overview and does not provide detailed rulings. Students should use this as a guide to locate the full discussions in the volume.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for homicide or bodily injury, a legal punishment prescribed by Islamic law.
دیت (diyah): Blood money or financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs in cases of homicide or injury where qisas is not applicable.
دون النفس (dun al-nafs): Less than life; referring to bodily injuries that do not result in death.

Introduction to the Laws of Qisas and Diyat in the Hanafi School
Table of contents for qisas and diyat topics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main topics covered in Volume 15 of Ashraf al-Hidaya and explain the scope of qisas and diyat in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The laws of qisas and diyat govern retribution and compensation for murder and injury, balancing justice with mercy.

This lesson introduces you to Volume 15 of Ashraf al-Hidaya, which is dedicated to the detailed fiqh of qisas (retribution) and diyat (blood money). The table of contents reveals the breadth of scenarios covered, from group murder to multiple injuries, and highlights the systematic method of Hanafi jurists in deriving rulings. By understanding the structure, you will be better prepared to study the specific cases and proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Recognize that the volume covers qisas (retaliation) and diyat (blood money) as the two central themes. The table of contents lists cases where qisas is applied, where diyat substitutes, and the conditions for each.
Qisas applies primarily to intentional killing and intentional bodily harm; diyat applies when qisas is not possible or is waived.
2
Classify the listed scenarios
Group the entries into categories: single vs. multiple offenders, single vs. multiple victims, intentional vs. mistaken acts, and cases involving confession or forgiveness. This shows the comprehensiveness of Hanafi fiqh.
Entries like 'A group murdered a person' and 'One person killed a whole group' illustrate varying liability.
3
Note juristic disagreements
Observe that the index mentions opinions of Imam Shafi'i and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). This signals that the volume will present proofs and responses, which is a hallmark of Hanafi scholarly method.
Disagreements are noted for topics like multiple offenders cutting a hand or the effect of death after retribution.
4
Understand the volume as a roadmap
Realize that the table of contents is not a summary of rulings but a guide to the detailed discussions. Each entry corresponds to a section where the ruling, proof, and objections are elaborated.
Students should use this index to navigate and review specific topics.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for a chapter on jawaih (injuries) from the Hanafi fiqh work Ashraf al-Hidaya. It lists numerous rulings related to qisas (retaliation) and diyat (blood money) for various bodily harms: fingers, teeth, head wounds, loss of senses, etc. It also includes disagreements among the Hanafi imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), proofs, and special cases like the fetus (janin). The text serves as an overview of the topics covered in detail in the subsequent pages. Students should note the variety of issues addressed, including the role of 'aqila (tribe), the effect of doubt in intentional killing, and the distinction between arsh (compensation) for wounds and full diyah. The passage is not a continuous ruling but a list of headings summarizing the content.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional bodily harm, e.g., an eye for an eye.
دیت (diyah): Monetary compensation paid to the victim or heirs for homicide or bodily injury.
ارش (arsh): Compensation for wounds that do not have a fixed diyah, assessed based on severity.
عاقلہ ('aqila): The tribal group or akin who are collectively responsible for paying diyah in cases of mistake.
غرہ (ghurrah): Blood money for a fetus that is miscarried due to injury, typically set at one-twentieth of full diyah.
موضحہ (mudihah): A wound that exposes the bone.
جنین (janin): Fetus; unborn child.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction to the Book of Homicides (Kitab al-Jinayat). The author first explains the placement of this book after the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn). The connection is that pledge protects property, while the laws of homicide protect life. Since property is a means to protect life, the means (property) is given precedence. The term 'jinayat' (singular: jinaayah) is defined both linguistically and legally. Linguistically, it means any evil action. Legally, in this context, it refers specifically to a forbidden act that affects either life or property. This definition excludes acts that only incur sin without tangible harm. The commentator clarifies that this is the technical meaning intended in the book.

Classification of Crimes in Hanafi Fiqh
Introductory classification of crimes by object of harm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of crimes against others and list their subcategories as defined in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Crimes against others are classified by object: life, limb, honor, or property.

This opening passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah lays the foundational taxonomy of crimes (jinayat) in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding this classification is essential because each category carries distinct legal consequences, from mandatory retaliation (qisas) to fixed punishments (hudud) or discretionary penalties (ta'zir). We will trace the author's division from the highest class—crime against life—down to crimes against property, noting which subcategories are discussed in later volumes of al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the broadest division
Crimes are divided into those against oneself and those against others. This lesson focuses on the latter, as the first is not discussed here.
Crimes against oneself (e.g., suicide) are not within the scope of this book.
2
Name the four objects of harm
Crimes against others are classified by what is harmed: life, limb, honor, or property.
Each category leads to different legal rulings.
3
List the means of harming life
Four means are specified: killing (qatl), crucifixion (salb), burning (harq), and drowning (gharaq).
These are ways to commit a crime against life.
4
List the means of harming limbs
Three means: amputation (qat'), blinding (karr), and disfiguring (shabah).
These result in physical injury to a limb.
5
Distinguish crimes against honor
Two subcategories: qadhf (false accusation of zina) incurs a hadd punishment in this world; ghaybah (backbiting) is a sin punished in the Hereafter.
Qadhf is legally actionable; ghaybah is not punishable by human courts.
6
List crimes against property
Three subcategories: usurpation (ghasb), a generic crime (jinayat), and theft (sariqah).
Ghasb and sariqah are detailed in later volumes of al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage is an introductory classification of crimes (jinayat) from the Hanafi fiqh work Ashraf al-Hidayah. The author begins by dividing crimes into two broad categories: crimes against oneself and crimes against others. Since the book focuses on offenses against others, the first category is set aside. Crimes against others are then classified into four types based on the object of harm: life, limb, honor, and property. Each of these is further subdivided. For crimes against life, there are four means: killing, crucifixion, burning, and drowning. For crimes against limbs, three: amputation, blinding, and disfiguring. For crimes against honor, two: slander (qadhf), which incurs a fixed corporal punishment (hadd) already detailed in the second volume of al-Hidayah, and backbiting (ghaybah), which is sinful and subject to Divine punishment. For crimes against property, three: usurpation (ghasb), whose rules are in the third volume; a generic category of crime (jinayat); and theft (sariqah). The passage concludes with a citation from 'Uqud al-Jawahir al-Munifah and an acknowledgment. This classification sets the stage for the detailed rulings on homicide, bodily harm, and related offenses that follow in the Book of Crimes.

Key Terms
جنایت (jinayat): Crimes or offenses, generally against persons or property, which incur worldly punishments (hudud, qisas, ta'zir).
قتل (qatl): Homicide, intentional killing, one of the types of crime against life.
صلب (salb): Crucifixion, a method of killing often prescribed as a hadd punishment for certain crimes.
حرق (harq): Burning, a method of killing, though generally discouraged in Islamic law as only Allah punishes with fire.
غرق (gharaq): Drowning, a method of causing death, used as a punishment or in retaliation (qisas).
قطع (qat'): Amputation, severing of a limb, a type of bodily harm against limbs.
کر (karr): Blinding, causing loss of sight, a specific form of injury to the limb (the eye).
شبح (shabah): Disfiguring, causing a permanent scar or disfigurement, a crime against the limb.
قذف (qadhf): Slander, specifically false accusation of illicit intercourse (zina), which incurs the hadd punishment of eighty lashes.
غیبت (ghaybah): Backbiting, speaking ill of someone in their absence, a sin with no prescribed worldly punishment but subject to Divine accountability.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation, wrongful seizure of another's property without right, a crime against property.
سرقه (sariqah): Theft, taking property secretly from a protected place (hirz), which incurs the hadd punishment of amputation of the hand.

Definition of Diyat and Arsh
Defines diyat and arsh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitions of diyat and arsh and distinguish between them based on the type of harm.

Core Idea
Diyat is the substitute for a life; arsh is for lesser harm.

Before diving into the detailed rulings of the Book of Diyat, we must first understand its key terms. This lesson clarifies the fundamental distinction between diyat (blood money for homicide) and arsh (compensation for injuries), as defined by the Hanafi school. Grasping this distinction is essential for all subsequent discussions on liability and compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Read the Arabic text and locate the definitions of 'diyat' and 'arsh'. Note that diyat is defined first.
The passage explicitly states: 'دیت شریعت میں اس مال کو کہتے ہیں جو نفس کا بدل قرار دیا گیا ہے'.
2
Parse the definition of diyat
Recognize that diyat is 'wealth made the substitute for a life'. This implies it is a fixed compensation owed in cases of homicide.
The phrase 'بدل قرار دیا گیا' indicates it is legally established as a substitute.
3
Parse the definition of arsh
Arsh is defined as 'wealth that is wajib for something less than a life'. This covers all injuries short of death.
Arsh is proportional, unlike the fixed diyat.
4
Contrast the two terms
Note that the key difference is the subject of compensation: diyat for life, arsh for lesser harm. Qisas (retaliation) is the primary right; diyat is its substitute.
The commentator explains that qisas is given precedence because protection of life is paramount.
