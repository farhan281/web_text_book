Combining Haqiqah and Majaz in Two Words
Response to Abu Yusuf's proof by explaining combination of two words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that combining literal and metaphorical meanings in two separate words is permissible and apply it to the case of combining a vow and an oath.

Core Idea
Literal and metaphorical meanings can coexist in separate words without contradiction.

Imam Abu Yusuf raised an objection that combining a vow (nadhr) and an oath (yamin) in one expression would require mixing literal and metaphorical meanings, which is invalid according to Hanafi usul. The author (Nur al-Anwar) refutes this by showing that the two meanings attach to different words: 'Allah' is used metaphorically for the oath, and 'alayya' is used literally for the vow. Thus, no impermissible combination occurs. This principle is supported by an analogy from gift with condition (hibah bi-shart al-iwad), where two aspects (donation and compensation) can coexist without contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Understand the objection: Combining a vow and an oath in one expression (e.g., 'Lillahi 'alayya sawm') might seem to require mixing literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings in a single utterance, which is generally invalid.
The objection is attributed to Imam Abu Yusuf.
2
Understand the response
The author responds by pointing out that the two meanings are attached to different words: 'Lillahi' (by Allah) is metaphorical and represents the oath; 'alayya' (upon me) is literal and represents the vow. Thus, the combination is allowed because the meanings are separated into distinct words.
Combining haqiqah and majaz in one word is invalid, but in two separate words it is permissible.
3
Recognize the principle
State the Hanafi usul principle: Combining literal and metaphorical meanings is impermissible only when they are intended from a single word. When each meaning is assigned to a different word, there is no contradiction.
This principle is derived from the passage and is consistent with general Hanafi usul.
4
Apply the analogy
Consider the example of hibah bi-shart al-iwad (gift with condition of compensation). A gift is normally a donation (tabarru'), but with a condition of compensation, it takes on aspects of a transaction (mu'awada). Both aspects coexist without contradiction because they apply to different dimensions of the same act.
Similarly, the oath and vow coexist in the same expression because they are attached to different words.
5
Confirm the application
In the phrase 'Lillahi 'alayya sawm', 'Lillahi' is metaphorical (oath), 'alayya' is literal (vow). The combination is valid, and both obligations (if the oath is broken or the vow unfulfilled) apply separately.
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position (mufta bihi).

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hāshiyah-style) responding to a proof of Imam Abu Yusuf. The author explains that when someone combines a vow (nadhr) and an oath (yamin) in a single expression like 'for Allah, upon me is fasting', it might seem that he is combining literal and metaphorical meanings in one phrase. The objection raised is that combining haqiqah (literal) and majaz (metaphorical) in a single utterance is invalid. The author clarifies that this combination is not in a single word but in two separate words: 'Allah' (by Allah) which is an oath (yamin) used metaphorically, and 'upon' which is the literal wording for a vow (nadhr). Thus, the combination is permissible because the two meanings are assigned to different words. The author uses an analogy from gift with condition (hibah bi-shart al-iwad) to show how two aspects (donation and compensation) can coexist without contradiction. This is a Hanafi principle of combining multiple legal aspects in one transaction.

Key Terms
حقیقت (ḥaqīqah): Literal meaning of a word as per its original usage
مجاز (majāz): Metaphorical meaning of a word used beyond its literal sense
یمین (yamīn): Oath sworn by Allah's name
نذر (nadhr): Vow to perform an act of worship
