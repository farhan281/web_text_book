Ruling on Lost or Dying Hady: Voluntary vs Obligatory
Ruling on voluntary vs obligatory hady when lost or dying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for hady that is lost, develops a major defect, or dies en route, distinguishing between voluntary and obligatory types.

Core Idea
For voluntary hady, no replacement if lost; for obligatory hady, replacement is wajib.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses critical scenarios every pilgrim may face: what to do if the sacrificial animal (hady) is lost, develops a defect, or dies on the way to the Haram. The ruling hinges on whether the hady was voluntary (tat̤awwuʿ) or obligatory (wājib). The Prophet's instruction to Najiya al-Aslami provides the proof for the voluntary case, while the principle of continuing liability governs the obligatory. Understanding these distinctions ensures that the pilgrim's devotion is properly fulfilled and the meat benefits the poor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of hady
First, note that hady can be voluntary (tat̤awwuʿ) or obligatory (wājib). The ruling for loss, defect, or death changes based on this classification.
Voluntary hady is an act of extra devotion; obligatory hady is required due to a vow or as part of Hajj tamattu'/qiran.
2
Apply the rule for loss or major defect
For a voluntary hady, if it is lost or develops a major defect, no replacement is required because the act was tied to that specific animal. For obligatory hady, replacement is wajib because the liability remains until the animal reaches the Haram.
A major defect (ʿayb kathīr) prevents fulfillment of the obligation.
3
Analyze the case of incapacitation on the way (ʿaṭabah)
If the animal (here, a camel) becomes incapacitated en route, the rule differs: for voluntary, slaughter it, mark it, and give the meat to the poor; the rich may not eat. For obligatory, substitute another animal and dispose of the dead one as you wish.
The marking (dyeing the collar and striking the hump) identifies it as hady so only the poor eat.
4
Examine the Prophetic proof
The hadith of Najiya al-Aslami (commanded by the Prophet) establishes the procedure for voluntary hady that dies en route. The proof is a Sunnah ahad with zanni thubut and dalala.
The command indicates permissibility and recommended practice.
5
Understand the wisdom behind the ruling
Though the meat is normally only allowed after reaching the Haram, charity to the poor is preferred to leaving it for beasts because it contains devotion (qurbah).
This is an exception based on maslahah (public benefit).

If it is lost, it will not be wajib along with it. And there will be need for another. And with the defective one, do as you wish because it has become part of his remaining property. Explanation: If a person takes a hady but it dies, then if it is voluntary, another hady is not wajib because the act of obedience and devotion is attached to that specific animal and it is lost. If it is obligatory, another is wajib in its place because the obligation remains on his liability; merely purchasing it does not clear his liability until the hady reaches its place. If a major defect appears in the hady, another must be substituted because the obligation is not fulfilled with a major defect. So a second hady is necessary, and he may do as he wishes with the defective one because it becomes part of his property. If the camel dies on the way: if it was voluntary, he should slaughter it, dye its sandal with its blood, and strike its hump, and neither he nor the rich should eat from it. The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) commanded Najiya al-Aslami to do this. By 'sandal' is meant its garland. The benefit is that people will know it is a hady, so the poor will eat from it but not the rich. This is because permission to eat it is conditional upon it reaching its place. So it should not be permitted at all before that, except that giving it as charity to the poor is better than leaving it as prey for beasts, and there is a kind of devotion in it, and devotion is the goal. If it was obligatory, he should substitute another in its place, and do as he wishes with the slaughtered one because it is no longer suitable for what it was designated for, and it is his property like his other possessions.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the status of the hady (sacrificial animal) when it is lost or dies on the way. The author first distinguishes between voluntary (tat̤awwuʿ) and obligatory (wājib) hady. For a voluntary hady, if it is lost or dies, no replacement is required because the act of devotion was tied to that specific animal. For an obligatory hady, a replacement must be brought because the obligation remains on the person's liability until the animal reaches its designated place (the Haram). Similarly, if a major defect appears in the animal, the obligation is not fulfilled, so a substitute is necessary, and the defective animal becomes the owner's property to dispose of freely. The text then addresses the specific case where the badana (camel) becomes incapacitated (ʿaṭabah) on the way. For a voluntary animal, the Prophet (peace be upon him) instructed Najiya al-Aslami to slaughter it, dye its collar (qilādah) with its blood, and strike its hump so that people know it is a hady. The rich are prohibited from eating it, but the poor may eat it as charity, which is preferred to leaving it for beasts. For an obligatory animal, a substitute must be sent, and the owner may do whatever he wishes with the dead animal since it is no longer valid for its purpose and remains his property. The proof is the hadith of the Prophet's command to Najiya.

Key Terms
هدي (hady): The sacrificial animal brought for Hajj, whether camel, cow, or sheep.
بدنة (badanah): A camel designated as hady.
تطوع (tat̤awwuʿ): Voluntary, not obligatory.
نعل (naʿl): The garland (qilādah) placed on the hady animal, not a shoe.

Garlanding the Hady: Types and Conditions
Ruling on types of hady eligible for garlanding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which types of hady require garlanding (taqlīd) and for which animals it is not sunnah according to the Hanafi school, and explain the reasoning based on the nature of the sacrifice.

Core Idea
Only dam nusuk (ritual sacrifices) on camels are garlanded; compensatory and penalty blood are not.

The practice of garlanding (taqlīd) the sacrificial animal during Hajj is a sunnah that serves to announce the animal as a ritual sacrifice. However, not every hady is garlanded. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the types of hady based on their purpose and the animal species. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Hidayah, clarifying when garlanding is recommended and when it is not, and why goats are excluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is taqlīd (garlanding) of the hady. The ruling is sunnah (recommended) for specific types of hady.
This is a sunnah, not wajib.
2
Distinguish the types of hady
Categorize hady into: (a) dam nusuk (tat̤awwuʿ, tamattuʿ, qirān) and (b) compensatory/penalty (iḥṣār, jināyāt).
Dam nusuk is a ritual sacrifice; the others are for deficiencies or offences.
3
Apply the condition of animal species
According to the text, 'al-hady' means the badanah (camel). Goats are not garlanded because there is no benefit in it.
The custom of the Prophet ﷺ and the Sahaba was to garland only camels.
4
Understand the rationale
Garlanding publicizes the animal as a sacrifice. Penalty blood (iḥṣār, jināyāt) should be concealed; hence no garlanding.
The reasoning is based on maṣlaḥah and following the sunnah.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some schools may garland goats or all hady. For Hanafis, it is not sunnah.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling on which types of hady (sacrificial animals in Hajj) should be garlanded (taqlīd) and which should not. The author explains that garlanding is done for voluntary (tat̤awwuʿ) hady, as well as for hady of tamattuʿ and qirān, because these are 'dam nusuk' (blood of a ritual), and garlanding serves to announce and publicize that it is a sacrificial animal. In contrast, the hady for iḥṣār (prevention from continuing Hajj) and for jināyāt (offences committed during Hajj, like hunting) are not garlanded. The reason is that the cause of these is an offence, which warrants concealment; iḥṣār blood is compensatory, thus it follows the same category. The text further clarifies that when al-Quduri says 'al-hady', he specifically means the badanah (camel), as it is customary to garland a camel, not a goat. According to the Hanafi school, garlanding a goat is not sunnah because there is no benefit in it, as it does not serve the purpose of public identification. This ruling is based on the principle of maṣlaḥah (public benefit) and following the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) with the Sahaba.

Key Terms
تقلید (taqlīd): Garlanding the hady animal with a leather or cloth necklace to mark it as a sacrifice.
دم نسک (dam nusuk): Blood (sacrifice) that is part of the Hajj rituals, as opposed to penalty blood.
دم احصار (dam iḥṣār): The sacrifice required when a pilgrim is prevented from completing Hajj.
دم جنایات (dam jināyāt): The sacrifice required for violations committed during Hajj, such as hunting.

Standing at Arafah on the Wrong Day: Qiyas vs Istihsan
Determining validity of Hajj when people stand at Arafah on the day of Nahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on standing at Arafah on the wrong day and evaluate the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas.

Core Idea
When the people of Arafah err on the day, istihsan saves their Hajj from invalidity due to impossibility of correction.

Explanation: It is the custom of authors to mention some rare issues related to previous chapters at the end of the book, and they establish a separate section with the title 'Scattered Issues' or 'Miscellaneous Issues'. The author of Al-Hidayah also followed this custom and established the title 'Scattered Issues'. The issue is that the people of 'Arafah performed Wuquf on one day, and a group testified that the people performed Wuquf on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah. The point is that Wuquf 'Arafah is obligatory on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, and with dawn of the 10th the time for Wuquf ends, so these people's Wuquf did not occur, and when Wuquf did not occur, Hajj did not occur. The author says that this testimony is not accepted; rather, the Wuquf did occur and their Hajj is complete. Qiyas requires that Wuquf 'Arafah would not be sufficient for them, because if people performed Wuquf on the 8th of Dhul Hijjah, i.e., before the time, and then some witnesses testified that the Wuquf was before time, it would not be valid and must be repeated at the proper time. Similarly, in this case it would not be valid. The author of Al-Hidayah says that in the mentioned case, the Wuquf not being valid is because Wuquf is an act of worship specific to time (from after noon of the 9th to dawn of the 10th) and place (the plain of 'Arafah). So without both, Wuquf is not valid. Therefore, when witnesses testify that the Wuquf occurred on the 10th, due to the absence of the time, the Wuquf is not valid, and when Wuquf is not valid, Hajj is not valid. The reason for istihsan is that this testimony is based on negation because when the witnesses said that the pilgrims performed Wuquf on the day of Nahr, it is as if they testified that their Hajj did not occur. And this testimony is based on a matter that does not fall under the judge's jurisdiction. Because Hajj is not under the judge's ruling, and any testimony based on negation and on a matter that does not fall under the judge's jurisdiction is not accepted. When the testimony is not accepted, then for them, performing Wuquf on the day of Nahr is considered valid legally. And when Wuquf is valid, Hajj is also valid. The second proof is that it involves a general calamity because such differences occur frequently. Therefore, avoiding it is difficult. And because the testimony was given after Wuquf 'Arafah, compensation is impossible unless they are ordered to repeat Hajj the next year, and ordering repetition involves clear hardship, as the difficulties and complications are obvious and need no explanation. And Allah has pardoned hardship. Therefore, it is necessary to suffice with the Wuquf 'Arafah that occurred at the time of confusion. Unlike the case where people performed Wuquf on the 8th of Dhul Hijjah, that Wuquf is not valid. Because without severe hardship, compensation is possible to some extent in that the confusion will be removed the next day on the day of 'Arafah. Because from the 8th of Dhul Hijjah to dawn of the 10th there is much time, and it is very unlikely that among millions of people no one would know the correct date. So in that case compensation is possible. The second proof is that if Wuquf 'Arafah is delayed beyond its time, there is a precedent for its permissibility. For example, making up missed Ramadan fasts and prayers are permissible even though delayed. Similarly, Wuquf 'Arafah on the day of Nahr is permissible. But there is no precedent for the permissibility of advancing it. So if Wuquf is performed on the day of Tarwiyah and later discovered, that Wuquf is not valid and must be repeated on the day of 'Arafah. If someone objects that sadaqat al-fitr and zakat are valid when paid before time, providing a precedent for advancing, the answer is that these two are contrary to qiyas. The leading jurists said that if witnesses testify that people performed Wuquf on the day of Nahr, the judge should not hear this testimony and should say: 'The people's Hajj is complete, go home.' Because there is nothing in this testimony except causing fitna. And the hadith states: 'Fitna is sleeping; may Allah curse whoever awakens it.' So this testimony has no benefit except that hearing it will become famous among people and cause distress in the hearts of common Muslims that their Hajj was valid or not. Then their hearts will be disturbed. The author of Al-Hidayah says: When witnesses testify to sighting the moon at the end of the time of 'Arafah, from which it is known that today is the day for Wuquf 'Arafah, while there remains some or all of the night from the time of Wuquf, and it is not possible for the imam to perform Wuquf with all or most people in the remaining part of the night because all or most people have dispersed and cannot gather in that time, or if they gather and start moving they would arrive at 'Arafat after dawn, then this testimony was effectively given after the time had passed. Therefore, the ruling is that the imam should not act on this testimony. This indicates that if it is possible for the imam to perform Wuquf with most people, then acting on this testimony is necessary.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf Ul Hadaya (the Urdu commentary on Al-Hidayah) deals with two related scenarios concerning the validity of Wuquf 'Arafah and the acceptance of witness testimony about its timing. It is a ruling with proof, using both qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). The passage is an expansion on Al-Hidayah's text, explaining the reasoning behind the Hanafi position. 

First scenario: A large group of people performed Wuquf 'Arafah on a day they believed to be the 9th of Dhul Hijjah. Later, some witnesses testified that the people actually performed Wuquf on the 10th, implying that their Wuquf was invalid because it was after the prescribed time. The Hanafi ruling is that this testimony is not accepted, and the Wuquf (and thus the Hajj) is considered valid. The author presents qiyas, which would invalidate the Wuquf by analogy to performing Wuquf before the time (on the 8th). Since Wuquf is time-specific, performing it after the time seems similarly invalid. However, istihsan overrules qiyas for two reasons: (1) The testimony is based on negation (negating the occurrence of Wuquf) and relates to a matter (Hajj) that is not subject to judicial decree, so it is not admissible. (2) Accepting such testimony would cause a general calamity (ʻumūm balwā) because it would require millions of people to repeat Hajj, causing immense hardship (ḥaraj). Since the testimony came after Wuquf, compensation is nearly impossible, and Islam removes hardship. This is contrasted with Wuquf performed too early (on the 8th), where compensation is possible by repeating on the 9th, so that Wuquf is invalid. 

Second scenario: Witnesses testify to sighting the crescent moon on the evening of 'Arafah, which would indicate that the following day is the 10th, meaning that the current day should be the 9th and Wuquf should be performed that night. However, if the imam cannot gather most people to perform Wuquf in the remaining part of the night, he should not act on this testimony. The ruling again prioritizes avoiding chaos and hardship. If it were possible to gather a large group and perform Wuquf, the testimony would be acted upon. 

The passage also includes a practical directive from the leading jurists (mashāyikh) that the judge should not even hear such testimony to prevent fitna, citing a hadith: 'Fitna is sleeping; may Allah curse whoever awakens it.' Thus, the Hanafi school deals with these cases pragmatically, ensuring the ease of the community.

Key Terms
وقوف عرفات (Wuquf 'Arafat): Standing in the plain of 'Arafat on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, a rukn of Hajj.
قیاس (Qiyas): Analogical reasoning: applying the ruling of an original case to a new case due to shared illa.
استحسان (Istihsan): Juristic preference: departing from qiyas in favor of a stronger evidence or to avoid hardship.
ابتلائے عام (Ibtila-e-'Aam): General calamity or widespread difficulty that affects a large number of people.
حرج (Haraj): Hardship or difficulty, which the Shari'ah aims to remove.

It is a ruling with proof, using both qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). The passage is an expansion on Al-Hidayah's text, explaining the reasoning behind the Hanafi position.

Since the testimony came after Wuquf, compensation is nearly impossible, and Islam removes hardship. If it were possible to gather a large group and perform Wuquf, the testimony would be acted upon.

1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: repeating only the missed Jamrah is valid (já'iz). It is better to repeat all three to observe the sunnah order, but not required.
The ruling is from Imam Muhammad, as recorded in al-Hidayah, and is mufta bihi.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that repeating only the missed Jamrah is insufficient; all three must be repeated because the stoning is prescribed in order.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the broader framework of ibadat.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i compares this to sa'y before tawaf or beginning with Marwah before Safa – both invalid by consensus due to breach of order. He argues that stoning, being prescribed in order, follows the same rule.
The analogy is based on the assumption that order is essential in stoning.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school distinguishes: each Jamrah is an independent act (qurbah maqsudah binafsiha), so validity does not depend on precedence. Sa'y, on the other hand, is subordinate to tawaf, and its beginning and end are defined by text, making order essential there.
The key distinction is subordination vs. independence.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In the Hanafi school, the pilgrim only needs to restone the missed Jamrah. In the Shafi'i school, all three must be restoned.
This difference affects many pilgrims who may need to know how to correct their stoning.

Explanation of the issue: A pilgrim on the 11th of Dhul Hijjah stoned the middle and last Jamrah but left the first, even though on this date stoning all three is wajib. Then this pilgrim began to repeat the stoning on the same day. If he only stoned the missed Jamrah and did not repeat the other two, it is valid because he brought the original stoning into its time. At most, he left the order which is sunnah. The order was that the first Jamrah be stoned first, but in this case it was done later. Nevertheless, the sunnah order has been lost, and losing it does not necessitate any penalty. If he repeated all three, it is very good because the sunnah order is observed. An analogy: If a pilgrim performed tawaf around the Ka'bah but missed the Hateem, and then he only repeated the Hateem, it is valid. If he repeated the entire tawaf, it is good. Imam al-Shafi'i said that repeating only the first Jamrah is not sufficient; rather repeating all three is necessary, because stoning all three is prescribed in order. When the order is lost, it is as if he did not perform what was prescribed. This is like if someone performed sa'y before tawaf, which is invalid by consensus due to lack of order. Or if a pilgrim began sa'y with Marwah instead of Safa, that is also invalid by consensus. So similarly, if the first Jamrah is stoned after the middle and last, it is invalid because order is lost. Our proof: Stoning the Jamrah is in itself an act of worship intended for itself, so its validity is not dependent on precedence of some over others.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the stoning (ramy) of the Jamarat on the second day of Tashreeq (11th Dhul Hijjah). The scenario: A pilgrim stoned the middle and last Jamrah but omitted the first, and then on the same day repeated only the first Jamrah. According to Imam Muhammad (Hanafi), this is valid (já'iz) because each Jamrah is an independent act of worship (qurbah maqsudah binafsiha); the order (tartib) is only sunnah, not wajib. If he repeats all three in order, that is better (hasan). Imam al-Shafi'i holds that it is not sufficient; he must repeat all three because the stoning is prescribed in order, analogous to sa'y before tawaf or starting with Marwah before Safa, which are invalid by consensus due to order. The Hanafi response distinguishes stoning from sa'y: sa'y is subordinate to tawaf and its order is based on text, whereas each Jamrah is an independent act. The commentary provides an analogy with tawaf: if one omitted the Hateem, repeating only that part is valid. The passage also explains that the order is sunnah, and missing it does not require any penalty (tawān). This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
رمی (ramy): Stoning the Jamarat, an obligatory act (wajib) during Hajj.
جمرہ (jamrah): One of the three pillars symbolizing Satan, to be stoned with pebbles.
ترتیب (tartib): The order of stoning the Jamarat (first, middle, last).
قربت مقصودہ (qurbah maqsudah): An act of worship that is intended for its own sake, not subordinate to another.

Ruling on Missing One Jamarah: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Hanafi and Shafi'i dispute on repeating missed jamarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on repeating a missed jamarah and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the concept of qurbah maqsudah.

Core Idea
Each jamarah is an independent act of worship; order is sunna, not wajib.

This lesson examines a dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the validity of repeating only the missed jamarah when the prescribed order is broken. A pilgrim who stones the middle and last jamarah on the second day of Tashriq but misses the first, then later stones only the first, has he fulfilled his obligation? The Hanafi answer is yes because order is sunna, not a condition. We will walk through the proofs and the Shafi'i objection.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi’i regarding the validity of repeating only the missed jamarah when one omits the first on the second day of Tashriq. The Hanafi matn authored by Imam Muhammad states that if someone throws at the middle and last jamarah but misses the first, and then later throws only the first (without repeating the other two), it suffices, i.e. the obligation of ramy for that day is fulfilled. The reason is that the prescribed order (tartib) is only a sunna, not a condition of validity. He made up the missed act within its time (the same day), so it is valid. The only thing lost is the recommended sequence, which does not incur any penalty. The commentator then presents the Shafi’i view: all three must be repeated because the entire ramy is prescribed with order as an integral whole; breaking the order nullifies the act, analogous to performing sa’y before tawaf or starting sa’y from Marwah instead of Safa. The Hanafi response distinguishes each jamarah as an independent act of worship (qurbah maqsudah), so validity does not depend on order. The analogy to sa’y is rejected because sa’y is subordinate to tawaf (tabi‘) – tawaf is the primary act, sa’y is dependent. In contrast, each jamarah is independent; none is subordinate to another. The end of sa’y is textually fixed to Marwah, so starting there is impermissible, but ramy has no such fixed order of precedence. The commentator adds a supporting analogy: if someone performs tawaf but omits the Hatim portion and repeats only that, it is valid; the same principle applies here. The thamarah (practical result) is that in the Hanafi school, repeating only the missed jamarah suffices, while in the Shafi’i school, all three must be repeated. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the Hanafi view as stated in the matn.

Rights of the Purchaser of a Muhrima Slave Girl: Hanafi vs. Zufar
Intra-Hanafi dispute on purchaser's authority over muhrima slave girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the purchaser's right to release a muhrima slave girl, explain Imam Zufar's opposing argument, and evaluate the Hanafi response using qiyas and talil.

Core Idea
The purchaser inherits the seller's right to release, but not the seller's promise.

This passage presents a dispute within the Hanafi school between the majority (represented by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam Zufar regarding the sale of a slave girl who is in a state of ihram with her owner's permission. While the general ruling permits the purchaser to release her and have intercourse, Zufar objects, drawing an analogy with marriage. The resolution hinges on the principle of qiyas and the distinction between rights that transfer and those that do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act under discussion? The sale of a slave girl in ihram. According to the Hanafi school, the purchaser may release her from ihram (by cutting hair or clipping nails) and may have intercourse with her.
The ruling is permissive (mubah) but with a recommendation to avoid intercourse out of respect for Hajj.
2
State the opposing view
Imam Zufar holds that the purchaser cannot release her or have intercourse because ihram is a contract that preceded his ownership.
Zufar's position is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab dispute.
3
Analyze the opposing proof
Zufar uses qiyas: just as a purchaser cannot nullify the marriage of a married slave girl, he cannot nullify the ihram of a muhrima slave girl. Both are contracts that existed before the sale.
The marriage analogy is the core of Zufar's argument.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi response distinguishes: in marriage, the seller could not nullify the marriage, so the purchaser cannot. But in ihram, the seller had the right to release her (though disliked due to breaking a promise), so the purchaser, standing in the seller's place, inherits that right.
The key distinction is the transferability of the seller's authority. Also, the purchaser is not bound by the seller's promise.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: according to Hanafi, the purchaser can release and have intercourse; according to Zufar, he cannot release her, so he must either wait for her to complete Hajj or return her as defective.
Also, the method of release: cutting hair/clipping nails vs. intercourse, with the latter being permissible but less preferred.

Buyer's Right to End Ihram of a Purchased Muhrimah Slave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on buyer's right to end ihram of a purchased slave woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi majority position on the buyer's right to perform tahlil for a muhrimah slave and critically evaluate Imam Zufar's opposing analogy with marriage using qiyas analysis.

Core Idea
A buyer stands in the seller's shoes: the principle of qa'im maqam transfers the right to end ihram.

This lesson tackles an intra-Hanafi disagreement: when a slave woman is sold while in ihram (with the seller's permission), may the buyer end her ihram and have intercourse? Imam Abu Hanifa and the majority say yes, because the buyer succeeds to the seller's authority. Imam Zufar dissents, arguing that ihram, like marriage, is a pre-existing contract the buyer cannot dissolve. We will dissect both proofs and trace the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Issue
State the exact legal question: does the buyer of a muhrimah slave girl (who had permission for ihram) have the right to perform tahlil (end her ihram) and have intercourse?
The slave's ihram was validly assumed with the seller's permission.
2
State the Majority Hanafi Position
Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf & Muhammad) hold that the buyer may perform tahlil and have intercourse. The reason: the buyer is qa'im maqam (stands in the place) of the seller, who had that right.
3
Examine Imam Zufar's Counterargument
Imam Zufar analogizes ihram to marriage: just as a buyer of a married slave cannot dissolve the marriage, so too he cannot dissolve the ihram 'contract' that predates his ownership.
Note the type of qiyas: qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on similarity as a binding contract.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Rebuttal
The majority distinguishes ihram from marriage: the seller himself could not dissolve a marriage if he permitted it, but he could dissolve ihram. Therefore, the buyer inherits only what the seller had: the right to tahlil, not the right to divorce.
Key distinction: the seller had authority over ihram but not over a marriage he allowed.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If the buyer may perform tahlil, ihram is not a defect allowing return. If he may not (per Zufar), ihram is a defect and the buyer can rescind the sale.
This difference in ruling directly affects the buyer's option to return the slave.

He sold a slave woman who was in ihram, even though he had given her permission for ihram. Then the buyer may make her halal (i.e., end her ihram) and have intercourse with her. Imam Zufar said: He does not have that right because this is a contract (ihram) that came before his ownership, so he cannot dissolve it, just as if he bought a married slave woman. Our proof is that the buyer stands in the place of the seller, and the seller had the right to make her halal, so likewise the buyer, except that it is disliked for the seller because it involves breaking a promise. This meaning is not found in the case of the buyer, unlike marriage, because the seller did not have the right to dissolve the marriage if it was contracted with his permission, so likewise the buyer does not have that right. And if he has the right to make her halal, he cannot return her due to a defect according to us, but according to Zufar he can return her because he is prevented from intercourse with her. And in some copies it is mentioned 'or he has intercourse with her'. The first (with 'wa') indicates that he makes her halal without intercourse by cutting hair or trimming nails, then he has intercourse. The second (with 'aw') indicates that he makes her halal by intercourse, because it is not free from preceding contact that effects tahlil. It is better to make her halal without intercourse out of reverence for the matter of Hajj. And Allah knows best.

Explanation: The issue is that a person sold his muhrimah slave woman despite having given her permission for ihram, then this sale is valid. And if the buyer is not in ihram, it is permissible for him to make her halal and have intercourse. Imam Zufar said the buyer does not have this right. Imam Zufar's proof is that ihram is a contract that occurred before the buyer's ownership, so the buyer cannot dissolve it, just as if someone bought a married slave woman of another, the buyer cannot divorce her and then have intercourse. However, if the buyer was unaware of the marriage, he can return her due to the defect of marriage. Similarly, he cannot make the muhrimah halal and have intercourse. But he can return her due to the defect of ihram. Our proof is that the buyer stands in the place of the seller, and the seller had the right to make the muhrimah slave halal and have intercourse. So likewise the buyer gets this right. However, it is disliked for the seller to make her halal because when he gave permission for ihram, it was as if he promised not to have intercourse, and now he breaks that promise. The buyer, not having given such permission, is not guilty of breaking a promise, so it is not disliked for him. Unlike marriage: the buyer cannot take a married slave woman into his possession because the seller himself did not have the right to dissolve the marriage if he had permitted it, so likewise the buyer does not have that right. Therefore, analogy of muhrimah to married woman is not correct. The author of Hidayah says: When the buyer is permitted to make the muhrimah slave halal, then according to us he cannot return her due to the defect of ihram, because he cannot say that being muhrim makes her useless for him. However, according to Imam Zufar, the buyer can return her due to the defect of ihram because Imam Zufar holds that the buyer cannot break the ihram to have intercourse, so ihram is a hindrance to intercourse and thus considered a defect, giving the right to rescind the sale. The first copy with 'wa' in the text indicates that he first makes her halal without intercourse by cutting hair or nails, then has intercourse. The second copy with 'aw' indicates that he makes her halal by intercourse, because intercourse is not free from preceding touching that effects tahlil. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the sale of a slave woman who is in ihram with the owner's permission. The majority view (Imam Abu Hanifa and others) holds that the buyer may legally end her ihram (tahlil) and have intercourse with her, because he stands in the place of the seller, who had that authority. However, it is disliked (makruh) for the seller to do so because he had implicitly promised not to have intercourse by giving permission for ihram. The buyer is not bound by that promise. Imam Zufar disagreed, arguing that ihram is a pre-existing contract that the buyer cannot dissolve, analogous to a marriage contract. The text then discusses the practical consequences: if the buyer can perform tahlil, according to the majority he cannot return the slave due to a defect (since he can use her); Imam Zufar, however, allows return because ihram prevents intercourse. The passage also notes a textual variant in Jami' al-Saghir: some manuscripts have 'wa yujami'aha' (and he has intercourse) indicating that tahlil is done separately before intercourse, while others have 'aw yujami'aha' (or he has intercourse) implying that intercourse itself can effect tahlil. The preferred view is to perform tahlil without intercourse out of respect for Hajj.

Key Terms
محرمہ (muhrimah): A female slave who has entered the state of ihram by assuming it with the owner's permission.
تحلیل (tahlil): The act of ending the state of ihram, allowing the slave woman to be approached for intercourse.
قائم مقام (qa'im maqam): Legal succession; the buyer stands in the place of the seller regarding rights and obligations that are not personal.

Sale of a Muhrimah Slave Woman and the Buyer's Right to Release Her from Ihrām
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sale of muhrimah slave woman with textual variant analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the buyer of a muhrimah slave woman and evaluate Imam Zufr's counterargument.

Core Idea
The buyer of a muhrimah slave woman may release her from ihrām because he stands in the seller's place — ihrām is not a defect.

When a slave woman enters ihrām with her owner's permission and is then sold, a dispute arises: can the new owner release her and have intercourse? The Hanafi majority says yes, while Imam Zufr disagrees. This passage from Al-Hidayah presents the evidence and the textual variant in the source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
Determine what is being sold (a muhrimah slave woman) and the contested issue (can the buyer release her and have intercourse?).
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The buyer stands in the place of the seller (qā'im maqām), and the seller had the right to release her. Hence the buyer inherits that right.
The seller's act is makrūh due to breaking an implied promise, but this does not affect the buyer.
3
State Imam Zufr's position and proof
Imam Zufr analogizes to a married slave woman: the buyer cannot annul the marriage. Similarly, he cannot annul the ihrām contract. The buyer can only return her due to the defect (ʿayb) of ihrām.
Imam Zufr considers ihrām a contract that predates the sale.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response and the thamarah al-khilaf
The analogy to marriage fails because the seller could not annul the marriage (since he gave permission), whereas for ihrām the seller could release her. Therefore, the buyer, as his substitute, can also release her. The practical difference: Hanafi allows the buyer to keep and release; Imam Zufr forces return or abstinence.
The textual variant (aw vs wa) is also discussed, with the preferred method being release without intercourse to preserve the sanctity of Hajj.

Commentary
This passage discusses a scenario where a man sells his slave woman who has entered the state of ihram (with the owner's permission). The sale is valid. The question is whether the buyer can release her from ihram (by performing tahallul) and then have intercourse with her. According to the Hanafi school (Imam Abu Hanifa and his followers), the buyer may do so because he stands in the place of the seller (qā'im maqām), and the seller himself had the right to release her (though it is makrūh for the seller due to breaking his implied promise not to have intercourse). Imam Zufr holds that the buyer cannot release her by himself; instead, he can only return her due to the defect (ʿayb) of ihram, because ihram is a contract that occurred before the buyer's ownership, and he has no right to annul it. Imam Zufr analogizes to a married slave woman: a buyer cannot annul the marriage. The Hanafi response distinguishes: in marriage, the seller himself could not annul the marriage (since it was contracted with his permission), whereas for ihram, the seller could release her. Therefore, the analogy is invalid. The text then notes a textual variant in Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaghīr: one manuscript reads 'wa yujāmiʿahā' (and he has intercourse with her), meaning he releases her through non-intercourse means first, then intercourse; another reads 'aw yujāmiʿahā' (or he has intercourse with her), suggesting she is released directly by intercourse. The author explains that even in the second reading, release occurs before intercourse through lustful touch (mass bi shahwa), which also requires immediate release. The preferred (awla) method is to release her through cutting hair/nails without any intimate contact, to preserve the sanctity of Hajj and ihram.

Key Terms
محرمہ باندی (muhrimah bāndī): A female slave who has entered the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah.
احرام (ihrām): The sacred state of consecration for Hajj/Umrah, which prohibits certain acts including sexual intercourse.
حلال کرنا (halāl karnā): To release someone from ihram (make halal), i.e., perform tahallul so that previously prohibited acts become permissible.
عیب (ʿayb): A defect in an item being sold that entitles the buyer to return it. Here, ihram is considered a defect by Imam Zufr.
مس بشہوۃ (mass bi shahwah): Lustful touch, which causes tahallul (release from ihram) in a muhrimah woman.

Making a Muhrimah Slave Woman Halal by Touching with Desire
Concluding discussion on how intercourse affects a muhrimah slave woman's permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a muhrimah slave woman becomes halal by musa bi-shahwah (touching with desire) and explain the preferred (awla) practice to preserve the sanctity of ihram.

Core Idea
Touching with desire makes a muhrimah halal just like intercourse.

This passage concludes the chapter on Hajj by addressing a subtle point: if a man has intercourse with his muhrimah slave woman, she becomes halal. However, the Hanafi analysis shows that she actually becomes halal before the intercourse due to the necessary preceding touching with desire. The author then distinguishes between the legal effect and the recommended practice to preserve the sanctity of ihram. Understanding this distinction highlights the Hanafi method of qiyas and the consideration of higher objectives (maqasid) in recommended practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its immediate effect
The passage discusses a man who has intercourse with his muhrimah slave woman. According to the text, she becomes halal (permissible for intercourse). But the author argues that she actually becomes halal before the intercourse, because touching with desire (musa bi-shahwah) necessarily precedes intercourse and itself makes her halal.
Focus on the sequence: musa bi-shahwah occurs first, then intercourse. The ruling is that musa bi-shahwah is legally effective.
2
Understand the qiyas: Touching with desire is like intercourse
The Hanafi position, stated as 'we say', holds that musa bi-shahwah is analogous to intercourse. Both are prohibited acts in ihram and both cause janabah. Therefore, just as intercourse makes a muhrimah halal, so does musa bi-shahwah. The text uses this qiyas to explain why she becomes halal before actual intercourse.
The basis of the analogy: both acts involve sexual desire and break the ihram prohibition.
3
Distinguish between the legal ruling and the preferred practice
Although musa bi-shahwah legally makes her halal, the author recommends (awla) that the husband should make her halal by some other means (e.g., freeing her or waiting until her ihram ends) rather than using intercourse, touching, or kissing. This preserves the sanctity and grandeur of Hajj and ihram.
The legal ruling is permissibility, but the recommended practice is caution to maintain the reverence of the sacred state.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding discussion of Kitab al-Hajj in the Urdu commentary Ashraf al-Hidayah. It addresses the ruling on a slave woman who is in ihram (muhrimah) and how she may become permissible (halal) for her husband. The author presents a scenario: if a man has intercourse with his muhrimah slave woman, she becomes halal. However, the author notes that before intercourse, touching with desire (musa bi-shahwah) necessarily occurs. Since touching with desire also makes a muhrimah halal (as established earlier in the text), she becomes halal before actual intercourse. Thus, the intercourse that follows is with a woman who is already halal. The Hanafi position (stated as 'we say') is that touching with desire is analogous to intercourse; therefore, making her halal through touching is effectively the same as making her halal through intercourse. Nevertheless, the preferred (awla) course of action is to make the slave woman halal by some means other than intercourse, touching, or kissing, in order to preserve the sanctity of Hajj and ihram. Directly using intercourse to exit ihram diminishes that sanctity. The passage ends with supplications and the author's signature.
