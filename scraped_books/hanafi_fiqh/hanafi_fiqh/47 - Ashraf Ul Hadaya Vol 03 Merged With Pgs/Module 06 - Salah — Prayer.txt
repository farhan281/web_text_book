Medicine in the Urethra: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Fasting Nullifiers
Intra-Hanafi dispute on medication in urethra during fast.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether medication inserted into the urethra breaks the fast and identify the anatomical assumption underlying each position.

Core Idea
The bladder as a barrier or a passage determines the nullification of fast—a matter of anatomy, not pure fiqh.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about a specific medical scenario: a fasting person inserting medicine into the urethra. While Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf reach opposite conclusions, the difference hinges not on legal principles but on an anatomical fact—whether the urethra has a direct passage to the stomach. Al-Hidayah classifies this as a question of tashriḥ al-abdān (anatomy), not fiqh. Understanding this helps you see how some ikhtilāfāt are factual rather than methodological.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is whether inserting medicine (iqṭār) into the urethra (iḥlīl) during fasting nullifies the fast. State Abu Hanifa's view (no nullification) and Abu Yusuf's view (nullification).
Muhammad's position is reported inconsistently; some sources have him with Abu Hanifa, others with Abu Yusuf.
2
Extract the underlying anatomical assumption
Abu Yusuf assumes a passage (manfadh) exists between the urethra and the stomach, evidenced by urine exiting. Abu Hanifa assumes the bladder (mathānah) is a barrier (ḥā'il), and urine only seeps (yatrashaḥ) through.
The difference is purely factual, not legal.
3
Apply the thamarah al-khilāf
The practical consequence is that the ruling depends on medical reality. If modern medicine confirms a direct passage, Abu Yusuf's view applies; if not, Abu Hanifa's view applies.
Al-Hidayah states this is not from the realm of fiqh, so no tarjīḥ is given.
4
Note the muftā bihī today
Most later Hanafi fatwā collections defer to medical experts. The mufti should inquire about the anatomical connection and then apply the corresponding view.
The text does not specify a muftā bihī; it leaves it to external knowledge.

Intercourse during i'tikaf is haram by Quranic decree; its means are also haram by analogy, unlike fasting where only intercourse nullifies.

This lesson examines a key ruling from Al-Hidayah: the prohibition of sexual relations and their precursors during i'tikaf. You will see how the Hanafi school derives the rule from Surah al-Baqarah, then extends it via qiyas to touching and kissing, and finally contrasts this with the rules of fasting. Unlike i'tikaf, during fasting only intercourse is prohibited and invalidates the fast; touching and kissing do not. The reason given: the essence of fasting is abstaining from nullifiers (its rukn), not from prohibited acts, so the prohibition does not extend to means.
This distinction is subtle but important for correct application.

The Hanafi position is mufta bihi. The practical effect: a mu'takif must avoid not only intercourse but also any intimate acts that could lead to it.
Contrast with schools that limit the prohibition to intercourse only.

The Two Rak'ahs Before Ihram: Ruling and Proof
Explains the sunnah of two rak'ahs before ihram with prophetic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the two rak'ahs before ihram, explain the proof from the hadith of Ibn Abbas, and apply the condition that a recently performed fard prayer suffices.

Core Idea
The two rak'ahs before ihram are sunnah mu'akkadah, not wajib; a prior fard prayer can substitute for them.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling of the two rak'ahs our Prophet (peace be upon him) prayed before entering the state of ihram. This rule is derived from clear prophetic practice and is a sunnah, not an obligation. Understanding its exact legal status—sunnah mu'akkadah—is crucial for correctly performing the Hajj or Umrah rituals and avoiding unnecessary worry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is praying two rak'ahs immediately before entering ihram. The Hanafi ruling is that it is sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah), not wajib or a condition of ihram.
This means performing it brings reward, but omitting it does not invalidate ihram.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The passage cites two proofs: the hadith of Jabir (which mentions prayer but not number of rak'ahs) and the hadith of Ibn Abbas in Abu Dawud, which explicitly states that the Prophet prayed two rak'ahs at Dhu al-Hulayfah before making ihram.
The explicit mention of two rak'ahs in Ibn Abbas's hadith resolves any ambiguity in Jabir's narration.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's action (praying two rak'ahs) is a proof of sunnah, not obligation, because according to Hanafi usul, actions of the Prophet in worship are probative for istihbab (recommendation) unless there is additional evidence for wujub.
The hadith is ahad, which in Hanafi usul yields zanni thubut and zanni dalala, indicating sunnah rather than fard or wajib.
4
Note the conditions and substitution
The two rak'ahs should not be prayed at a time when prayer is makruh (e.g., after Fajr or 'Asr). If a fard prayer was performed before ihram, that fard suffices and no separate two rak'ahs are needed.
This substitution is a unique Hanafi leniency; other schools may require the two rak'ahs specifically.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence
The thamarah (practical outcome) is that a pilgrim may pray a fard prayer at the miqat and then enter ihram without additional prayer, which is particularly useful when time is constrained.
It also clarifies that the two rak'ahs are not a condition for ihram; ihram is valid even if they are omitted entirely.

The Three Prohibitions of Ihram: Rafath, Fusuq, Jidal
Ruling on avoiding three specific sins during ihram with Quranic proof

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Rafath, fusuq, and jidal are absolutely prohibited during Hajj, with fusuq especially severe in ihram.

When you enter ihram for Hajj or Umrah, you take on a sacred state that requires special conduct. The Quran explicitly commands avoiding three categories: rafath, fusuq, and jidal. Understanding what these mean is essential for every pilgrim. This lesson unpacks their definitions and the legal proof behind the prohibition.

Waiting for Raml When Crowded During Tawaf
Explains ruling when crowd prevents raml in first three rounds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waiting for raml when unable due to crowding and distinguish it from the case of istilam.

Core Idea
Raml has no substitute; if unable due to crowding, the pilgrim must wait to fulfill it.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains a key distinction in tawaf rituals: raml (quick walking) must be performed even if it means waiting, because it cannot be replaced. In contrast, if istilam (kissing the Black Stone) is impossible, one can simply face it. Why the difference? Because raml has no badal (substitute) in Hanafi fiqh, while istilam does. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that raml in the first three rounds of tawaf is wajib (obligatory) for men according to the Hanafi school.
Raml is not a pillar but a wajib; its omission requires a penalty if done without excuse.
2
Understand the problem scenario
The passage addresses a situation where extreme crowding prevents the pilgrim from performing raml. The question is: should he skip it, wait, or substitute?
The crowding must be genuine and unavoidable.
3
Apply the principle of no substitute
Since raml has no badal (alternative act), the pilgrim must stand still and wait until he can perform raml properly. He should not proceed with tawaf without raml.
The ruling is based on the nature of raml as an act that cannot be replaced.
4
Contrast with istilam
Contrary to raml, istilam (kissing the Black Stone) has a valid substitute: facing the Stone (istiqbal) and gesturing. Therefore, if unable to kiss, the pilgrim does not wait but simply faces and continues.
This difference highlights the core concept of badal in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Reflect on the illa (reason)
The underlying reason is the presence or absence of a substitute. Acts with a known badal may be omitted in favor of the substitute; acts without it require waiting or compensation.
Understanding badal helps predict similar rulings for other Hajj rituals.

The Two Rak'ats After Tawaf: Wajib or Sunnah?
Inter-madhhab dispute on the ruling of two rak'ats after tawaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the two rak'ats after tawaf and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the imperative hadith.

Core Idea
The two rak'ats after tawaf are wajib in the Hanafi school because the imperative in the hadith indicates obligation.

After completing tawaf, the pilgrim prays two rak'ats. Is this an obligatory act or a recommendable sunnah? The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ, and the dispute rests on how we interpret the Prophet's command. In this lesson, you will trace the proof and the counter-argument, sharpening your skills in identifying dalil and understanding madhhab differences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling on the two rak'ats after tawaf according to the Hanafi school. The text clearly says 'it is wajib in our view'.
Wajib means obligatory but with a less definitive proof than fard.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position
What does al-Shafi'i say? He considers the act sunnah, and his basis is the lack of explicit evidence for obligation.
Sunnah here means recommended, not obligatory.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is the hadith in the imperative form: 'Let the one performing tawaf pray two rak'ats for each seven rounds.' In Hanafi usul, al-amr li al-wujub — a command indicates obligation.
Also the Qur'anic verse about taking Maqam Ibrahim as a place of prayer is cited.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i likely relied on the hadith of the Bedouin where the Prophet mentioned only five daily prayers as obligatory. Hanafis respond that this hadith is either abrogated or not applicable because consensus exists on other obligatory prayers (janazah, eid).
The objection is based on a general principle that only the five prayers are fard.
5
Determine the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: For Hanafis, omitting these rak'ats is sinful (though tawaf remains valid); for Shafi'is, there is no sin in leaving them.
This affects the pilgrim's obligation and potential compensation.

Why Congregation Is Not Required for Combining at Muzdalifah
Explanation of a fiqh ruling with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on congregation for combining Maghrib and Isha at Muzdalifah and explain the legal distinction between delaying and advancing prayers in deriving the ruling.

Core Idea
Delaying a prayer to qada time follows analogy, so textual conditions are relaxed; advancing a prayer is contrary to analogy, so conditions are strictly applied.

In the previous lesson, we learned that the Prophet ﷺ combined Zuhr and Asr at Arafat with congregation, and Maghrib and Isha at Muzdalifah also with congregation. But is congregation a condition for the validity of the combination at Muzdalifah? Imam Abu Hanifa argues that it is not, and his reasoning hinges on a critical distinction: at Arafat, Asr is advanced before its time (contrary to analogy), so the text’s condition of congregation must be observed; at Muzdalifah, Maghrib is delayed after its time (in accordance with qiyas), so the condition is not necessary. This lesson unpacks that subtle yet powerful argument.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify what Abu Hanifa rules regarding congregation for the combination of Maghrib and Isha at Muzdalifah. The answer is simple: congregation is not a condition; it is valid without it.

Note that at Arafat, congregation is a condition for combining Zuhr and Asr. Contrast this with Muzdalifah: why the difference? The key is ta'khir (delay) vs taqdim (advancement).
Maghrib is delayed; Asr is advanced.

Abu Hanifa argues that delaying a prayer to its qada time is in harmony with the general principle that qada is allowed for all prayers. Since it follows qiyas, strict adherence to the specific textual condition (congregation) is not required. Conversely, advancing a prayer is against qiyas, so when the text permits it, all its conditions (including congregation) must be observed literally.
This is a qiyas-based argument: the 'illa is conformity to or deviation from the general rule of prayer times.

This shows a general Hanafi principle: textual conditions are strictly applied when the act itself is exceptional (against qiyas).

Other schools may require congregation for any combined prayer regardless of delay/advancement. The Hanafi position is based on the technical distinction between types of jam'. 1
Identify the act and the penalty range
Determine what the Muhrim is doing (applying pure olive oil) and the range of possible penalties: dam (sacrifice) according to Abu Hanifa, sadaqah according to the Sahibayn, or nothing according to Shafi'i if applied elsewhere than hair.
The dispute arises because olive oil is not a clear perfume but has some beneficial effects.
2
Follow the proof of the Sahibayn
Understand why Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani consider olive oil a deficient violation: it is primarily food (mat'um), and its benefits (killing lice, removing unkemptness) are incidental, not its primary purpose.
This is the basis for sadaqah, not dam.
3
Follow the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa
Understand why Abu Hanifa considers olive oil a complete violation: it is the origin of perfume (asl al-tib), has a mild scent, and provides multiple benefits (kills lice, softens hair, removes dirt). The totality makes it akin to perfume.
Being edible does not negate this, as saffron (za'faran) is both edible and a perfume.
4
Contrast the Shafi'i/Maliki view
See how al-Shafi'i (and Malik) differentiate based on location: applying to hair removes the required unkemptness (sha'ath), so dam; applying elsewhere does not, so nothing.
The Hanafi response argues that removing unkemptness is prohibited from any part, so the location distinction is unfounded.
5
Apply the exception for medicinal use
Note that if olive oil is used for treatment (e.g., on a wound or cracked foot), no penalty is due, because the condition of using it as a perfume is absent. This contrasts with using musk for treatment, which would incur penalty.
The ruling is conditional on the intention (perfuming vs. medicinal) per the maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha'.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, the Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah, covers rulings for a Muhrim (one in Ihram) regarding the application of certain substances to the head, beard, or body, and the resulting penalties. The passage type is an inter-madhhab dispute with preceding ruling statements. The author first addresses dyeing the head with wasma (a plant dye). According to the relied-upon position, if a Muhrim dyes his head with wasma for any purpose other than treatment, nothing is due because wasma is not a perfume. However, if he uses it to cover his head (e.g., to relieve a headache), he incurs dam because covering the head in Ihram is prohibited by consensus, irrespective of the substance used. The author then clarifies a nuance from al-Mabsut and al-Jami' al-Saghir: dyeing the head alone or the beard alone each independently necessitate dam; both together are not required. The main body of the passage is a detailed dispute regarding the application of pure olive oil. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that dam is due because olive oil, though primarily edible, is the origin of perfume, contains a mild scent, kills lice, softens hair, and removes dirt and unkemptness, thus constituting a complete violation (jinayah kamilah) akin to perfume. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it a deficient violation because it is not inherently a perfume or adornment; the benefits are incidental, so only sadaqah is due. Imam al-Shafi'i, and according to the text also Imam Malik, differentiate based on location: using it on the hair triggers dam because it removes the characteristic unkemptness (sha'ath) which the pilgrim is commanded to maintain (as per the hadith 'the pilgrim is unkempt and dusty'), but using it elsewhere on the body does not remove unkemptness, so nothing is due. The Hanafi authority (Abu Hanifa) responds that olive oil does remove unkemptness from any part of the body, and its being edible does not negate its perfuming potential, as exemplified by saffron which is both edible and a perfume. The dispute is confined to pure (unperfumed) olive and sesame oils. If the oil is blended with a known perfume like violet or lily, all schools agree that dam is due because it becomes a perfume. This entire discussion applies only when the oil is used for perfuming or beautification. However, if a Muhrim uses olive oil purely for medicinal purposes, such as treating a wound or a cracked foot, no penalty is incurred, because the substance is not perfume in itself, and the condition of using it as a perfume is absent. This is contrasted with using musk or other pure perfumes for treatment, where the ruling would differ because the substance is inherently perfume. The key lesson is that the ruling depends on both the substance's intrinsic properties and the intention/usage context. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa) prioritizes the complete benefit derived from olive oil, equating it to perfume in its effects, while the Sahibayn and other schools give weight to its primary classification as food or its limited application. The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school on this issue is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, as it is the position of the Imam and the one most commonly relied upon in practice.

Commentary
This passage presents a classical inter-madhhab dispute regarding the penalty (jaza') for a muhrim (person in ihram) who hunts game. The passage first states the position of the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), which is the relied-upon Hanafi madhhab: the penalty is the market value (qimah) of the killed game, assessed by two just men at the nearest settlement. The muhrim then has three options: (1) use that value to buy a sacrificial animal (hady) and slaughter it in the Haram, (2) use it to buy food and give it to the poor at the rate of sadaqat al-fitr (half sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates/barley per poor person), or (3) fast one day for each poor person that would have been fed. This view emphasizes that the Quranic phrase 'jaza'un mithlu ma qatala' refers to value, not a specific animal, because the absolute 'mithl' (likeness) must be in both form and meaning, which is impossible here, so it is interpreted figuratively as value, a concept well-known in Islamic law (e.g., compensation for property). The opponents—Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (the third great Hanafi imam) and Imam al-Shafi'i—argue that the penalty is the 'mithl' from livestock (na'am) in the sense of an animal that resembles the killed game in form and appearance. They cite the verse (Quran 5:95) literally. They provide examples: for a deer (zhabi) a sheep, for a hyena (dhab') a sheep, for a hare (arnab) a young female goat ('anaq), for a jerboa (yarbu') a kid (jafrah), for an ostrich (na'amah) a camel (badanah), for a wild ass (himar al-wahsh) a cow (baqarah). They also cite hadith: the Prophet said 'The hyena is game, and for it is a sheep.' They argue that if the intended were value, the Companions would not have obligated specific animals. However, Imam Muhammad agrees with the Shaykhayn for animals that have no livestock equivalent (e.g., sparrow, pigeon), where value is paid. Imam al-Shafi'i goes further, obligating a sheep even for a pigeon based on a supposed similarity in drinking style and sound. The passage concludes with the Shaykhayn's rebuttal: the absolute 'mithl' cannot be literal, so it is value; the word 'na'am' includes both wild and domestic; the hadith about the hyena is meant for estimation, not specification. This dispute shows the importance of usul al-fiqh principles: how to interpret ambiguous commands, the role of qiyas, and the departure of the Shaykhayn from literal meaning via istihsan (juristic preference) to achieve practical consistency.

Key Terms
جزاء الصيد (jaza' al-sayd): The penalty required from a muhrim who kills game while in ihram.
النعم (al-na'am): Livestock, including camels, cows, sheep, and goats. In this context, the verse uses 'min al-na'am' meaning from among livestock.
المثل (al-mithl): Likeness or equivalent. In this dispute, Shaykhayn interpret it as value equivalence; Muhammad and Shafi'i interpret it as physical resemblance.

Ruling with proof: compensation for breaking ostrich egg in ihram, with istihsan vs. qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking an ostrich egg for a muhrim and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in the case of a dead chick.

Core Idea
Breaking an ostrich egg in ihram incurs wajib compensation; if a dead chick emerges, by istihsan the chick's value is owed.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah, we encounter a nuanced ruling that illustrates two key principles of Hanafi jurisprudence: the treatment of potential game (the egg) and the preference of istihsan over strict qiyas. A muhrim who breaks an ostrich egg must pay its value, supported by narrations from Ali and Ibn Abbas. But what happens if a dead chick emerges? The apparent qiyas would limit compensation to the egg, but the Hanafi school, through istihsan, requires the value of the chick. Understanding this distinction deepens your appreciation of how Hanafi jurists balance textual evidence with practical considerations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basic ruling
The act is breaking an ostrich egg while in ihram. The ruling: the muhrim must pay its value (qimah). This is based on a narration from Ali and Ibn Abbas, and on the reasoning that the egg is the origin of game, having potential to become game, thus treated as game by precaution.
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Examine the exception: dead chick emerges
If a dead chick is found inside the broken egg, by strict qiyas the muhrim would owe only the egg's value because the chick's life is unknown. However, by istihsan (juristic preference), he owes the value of the chick.
This is the key point of the passage.
3
Understand the illa for istihsan
The basis for istihsan is that the egg is naturally prepared (mu'add) to produce a live chick. Breaking it before its time is the cause of the chick's death, so the death is attributed to the breaker by precaution (ihtiyatan).
The illa is the causal link between the breaking and the death.
4
Apply to analogous case: pregnant deer
The passage concludes with an analogous case: if a muhrim strikes the belly of a pregnant deer, causing it to abort a dead fetus and die, he owes the value of the deer. This reinforces the principle that a direct action causing death triggers full compensation.
The deer case is a furu' derived from the same reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling for a person in ihram (muhrim) who breaks an ostrich egg. The passage is a ruling with proof, citing narrations from Ali and Ibn Abbas and employing both qiyas and istihsan. The author begins by stating the ruling: breaking an ostrich egg requires payment of its value. The proof is twofold: a narration from Ali and Ibn Abbas, and a legal reasoning that the egg is the origin (asl) of game, having the potential to become game; thus it is treated as game by precaution (ihtiyatan) as long as it is not spoiled. Then the text discusses the scenario where a dead chick emerges from the broken egg. By qiyas (analogy), one would only owe the egg's value because the chick's life is unknown; it may have already been dead. However, by istihsan (juristic preference), the chick's value is owed. The reasoning for istihsan is that the egg is naturally prepared for a live chick to emerge; breaking it prematurely is the cause of its death, so the death is attributed to the breaker by precaution. The passage concludes with an analogous case: if a muhrim strikes the belly of a pregnant deer, causing it to abort a dead fetus and die, he owes the value of both the deer and the fetus. This illustrates the same principle: the action is the cause of death, so full compensation is due. The Hanafi school follows the istihsan position here, which is the relied-upon view.

Animals Exempt from Hunting Prohibition for a Muhrim
Lists animals exempt from hunting prohibition for a Muhrim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which animals a muhrim may kill without penalty, state the hadith basis, and apply the Hanafi principle of genus to include all dogs and mice.

Core Idea
Five (or six) harmful creatures are exempt from the hunting prohibition due to explicit prophetic permission; Hanafis extend by genus.

When a Muslim enters ihram, hunting becomes prohibited. However, the Prophet (peace be upon him) exempted certain harmful animals (fawasiq). This passage lists those animals, explains the hadith basis, and includes Abu Hanifa's principle that the genus (jins) is considered, thereby including all types of dogs and mice as exempt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempted animals from the hadith
Read the hadith quoted: five (or six) harmful creatures that can be killed in halal and haram. List them: kite, snake, scorpion, mouse, vicious dog; plus crow in the second narration. Note that the wolf is added via interpretation.
The first hadith has five, the second has six; both are authoritative.
2
Understand the basis: prophetic permission
The Prophet's explicit permission (or command) to kill these animals in ihram lifts the general prohibition of hunting. This is a dalil naqli from Sunnah ahad, with zanni thubut but qati dalala.
The exemption is specific to these animals; for others, the general prohibition remains.
3
See the expansion: wolf as vicious dog, crow limited to carrion-eaters
The author explains that 'vicious dog' is interpreted as the wolf, and 'crow' means the carrion-eating crow that initiates harm. The magpie ('aq'aq) is not included because it does not initiate harm and is not called a crow.
This shows how the ruling is refined by identifying the effective cause (initiating harm).
4
Apply Abu Hanifa's principle of genus
Abu Hanifa says the genus (jins) is considered, so all dogs (vicious or not, tame or wild) and all mice (domestic or wild) are equally exempt. This is a broader inclusion than the literal text.
This principle extends the exemption beyond the explicit wording, a characteristic Hanafi use of istihsan or consideration of meaning.

Ruling on a Muhrim with Game in His House or Cage
Inter-madhhab dispute on a muhrim's obligation regarding game in his house or cage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a muhrim who already has game in his house or cage, explain the Shafi'i opposing view, and apply the Hanafi proof using the practice of the Companions.

Core Idea
A muhrim need not release game already in his house or cage—mere possession is not active engagement (ta'arruḍ).

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a person enters the state of ihram while game is already in his house or in a cage. The question is whether he must release it. The Hanafi school says no; Imam al-Shafi'i says yes. Understanding the distinction between active pursuit (ta'arruḍ) and mere custody is key to grasping the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the explicit Hanafi ruling from the passage: a muhrim is not required to release game that is in his house or cage.
The ruling is explicitly stated: 'فَلَيْسَ عَلَيْهِ أَنْ يُرْسَلَهُ'.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that release is obligatory, comparing it to having the game in hand.
The opposition is stated: 'وَقَالَ الشَّافِعَى عَلَيْهِ أَن يُرْسِلَهُ'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Shafi'i reasoning: keeping the game in possession (imṣāk) constitutes ta'arruḍ, similar to holding it in the hand.
The basis is qiyas: possession equals active engagement.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Examine the Hanafi proof: (1) the established practice of the Companions (عادَة فاشِيَة) who did not release, and (2) the definition of ta'arruḍ as direct interference—confinement by the house/cage is not ta'arruḍ.
The Hanafi counter is that the Companions' custom is a valid proof (ḥujjah), and ownership alone does not mean active engagement.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafi—muhrim keeps the game without sin; Shafi'i—must release or incur penalty.
Also note the secondary Hanafi opinion: if the cage is in the hand, release becomes wajib but must be done without waste.

Whoever enters ihram while having game in his house or in a cage with him, he is not required to release it. Al-Shafi'i said: he must release it because he is actively engaging with the game by keeping it in his possession, so it becomes like when it is in his hand. Our evidence: The Companions used to enter ihram while having game birds and domesticated animals in their houses, and it is not reported that they released them; rather, the widespread custom was to not release them, and that is one of the proofs. Also, what is obligatory is to refrain from actively engaging, and he is not actively engaging from his side because the game is secured by the house or cage, not by him, though it is in his possession. And if he were to release it into a desert, it would still be his property, so the continuance of ownership is not considered. And it is said: if the cage is in his hand, releasing it becomes binding upon him, but in a way that does not cause waste.

Commentary
This passage contains an inter-madhhab dispute regarding a person in ihram who already has game in his house or a cage. The Hanafi position, as stated by the author, is that he is not obligated to release it. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that release is obligatory, comparing it to having the game directly in hand. The Hanafi proof relies on the practice of the Companions (رضي الله عنهم) who would enter ihram with game in their houses and did not release them, establishing a widespread custom (عادَة فاشِيَة) which itself is a valid proof. Additionally, the obligation is to avoid active pursuit (تعرض) of game; keeping it locked in a house or cage does not constitute active engagement since the confinement is not by the muhrim's direct action. The point about ownership remaining even after release shows that the mere fact of ownership does not necessitate release. A secondary view holds that if the cage is actually in the muhrim's hand, then release becomes wajib, provided it is done without wasting the animal (since waste is haram). This ruling is from the chapter on Hajj and applies to those in ihram.

Key Terms
تعرض (ta‘arruḍ): Active engagement or interference with game, such as hunting, trapping, or handling.
عادَة فاشِيَة (‘ādah fāshiyah): A widespread and established practice of the righteous predecessors, considered a valid proof (ḥujjah) in Hanafi uṣūl.

Permissibility of 'Umrah Throughout the Year Except Five Days
States that 'Umrah is valid year-round but makruh on five specific days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five days when 'Umrah is makruh and explain the Hanafi ruling that the act remains valid despite the karahah.

Core Idea
Umrah is valid all year, but disliked on five Hajj days — the act is sound, the karahah is external.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the timing of 'Umrah. While 'Umrah can be performed at any time, there are five specific days in the year when performing it is considered makruh tanzihi. You will learn the days, the evidence, and the intra-madhhab difference with Abu Yusuf, as well as the key point that the 'Umrah remains valid if performed on those days.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and exceptions
State that 'Umrah is permissible year-round but excepted on five specific days. Note the overall ruling is makruh tanzihi on those days.
2
List the five days
Memorize: Yawm 'Arafah (9th Dhul Hijjah), Yawm al-Nahr (10th), and the three days of Tashreeq (11th-13th).
These are the five days.
3
Examine the dalil
The hadith of 'Aisha (ra) shows her practice of disliking 'Umrah on these days. This is a zanni dalil. Also the logical reason: these days are for Hajj.
4
Consider the intra-madhhab difference
Abu Yusuf holds that before noon on 'Arafah it is not makruh. But the relied-upon position (azhar) is dislike for the entire day.
The commentator sides with the majority.
5
Understand the validity despite karahah
Even if performed, the 'Umrah is valid and the person remains in ihram because the karahah is due to an external factor (tazim of Hajj).
The act itself is sound.

وَالْعُشْرَةَ لَا تَفُوتُ وَهِيَ جَائِرَةً فِي جَمِيعِ السَّنَةِ إِلَّا خَمْسَةَ أَيَّامٍ يَكْرَهُ فِيهَا فِعْلُهَا وَهِيَ يَوْمُ عَرَفَةَ وَيَوْمُ النَّحْرَ وَأَيَّامُ التَّشْرِيقِ لِمَارُوِيَ عَنْ عَائِشَةَ أَنَّهَا كَانَتْ تَكْرَهُ الْعُمَرَةَ فِى هَذِهِ الْأَيَّامِ الْخَمْسَةِ وَلِأَنَّ هَذِهِ أَيَّامُ الْحَجِّ فَكَانَتْ متعينَةٌ لَهُ وَعَنْ أَبِى يُوسُفَ أَنَّهُ لَاتَكَرَهُ فِي يَوْمِ عَرَفَةَ قَبْلَ الزَّوَالِ لِأَنَّ دُخُولَ وَقْتِ رُكن الْحَجِّ بَعْدَ الزَّوَالِ لَا قَبْلَهُ وَالْأظْهَرُ مِنَ الْمَذْهَبِ مَا ذَكَرْنَاهُ وَلَكِنْ مَعَ هَذَا لَوْأَدَّاهَا فِي هَذِهِ الْأَيَّامِ صَحَ وَيَبْقَى مُحْرِمًا بِهَا فِيهَا لِأَنَّ الْكَرَاهَةَ لِغَيْرِهَا وَهُوَ تَعْظِيمُ أَمرِ الْحَجِّ وَتَخليصُ وَقِتِه لَهُ فَيَصحُ الشروع
And 'Umrah is not lost (i.e., it does not become invalid by time), and it is permissible in the entire year except five days in which its performance is disliked (makruh): the Day of 'Arafah, the Day of Nahr (sacrifice), and the days of Tashreeq. Because it is narrated from 'Aisha that she disliked 'Umrah on these five days, and because these are the days of Hajj, so they are specific for it. And from Abu Yusuf that it is not disliked on the Day of 'Arafah before noon because the time of the pillar of Hajj (standing at 'Arafah) enters after noon, not before. The more apparent (azhar) of the school is what we have mentioned. But despite this, if one performs it on these days, it is valid and he remains in ihram during them because the dislike is due to something external, namely the veneration of the affair of Hajj and dedicating its time to it; therefore beginning it is valid.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on the timing of 'Umrah. 'Umrah is not confined to a specific season like Hajj; it can be performed at any time of the year. However, there are five days when performing 'Umrah is makruh tanzihan (disliked, not prohibited): the Day of 'Arafah (9th Dhul Hijjah), the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah, Eid al-Adha), and the three days of Tashreeq (11th, 12th, and 13th Dhul Hijjah). The proof is the practice of 'Aisha (ra) and the reason that these days are dedicated to Hajj rituals. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that on the Day of 'Arafah before noon, 'Umrah is not makruh because the pillar of Hajj (wuquf) begins after noon. However, the relied-upon position (azhar al-madhhab) is that the dislike applies to the entire day, including before noon. Despite the karahah, if one performs 'Umrah on these days, the 'Umrah is valid and the person remains in ihram. The karahah is due to an external factor (respect for Hajj time) not inherent to 'Umrah itself, so the act is valid but disliked.

Key Terms
یوم عرفہ (Yawm 'Arafah): The 9th day of Dhul Hijjah, the day when pilgrims stand at 'Arafah; a day on which performing 'Umrah is makruh.
یوم نحر (Yawm al-Nahr): The 10th day of Dhul Hijjah, the day of sacrifice (Eid al-Adha); performing 'Umrah is makruh on this day.
ایام تشریق (Ayyām al-Tashrīq): The 11th, 12th, and 13th days of Dhul Hijjah, following the Day of Nahr; 'Umrah is makruh during these three days.
کراہت (karāhah): Legal dislike (makruh). In this context, it is makruh tanzihi, meaning the act is valid but discouraged due to external factors.
