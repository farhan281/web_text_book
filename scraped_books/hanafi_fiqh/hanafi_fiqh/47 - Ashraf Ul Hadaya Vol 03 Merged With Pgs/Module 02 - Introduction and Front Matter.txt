Table of contents listing chapter headings for fasting rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major sections of the Hanafi Book of Fasting as outlined in the table of contents and relate them to the broader structure of al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap: it maps the major rulings on fasting, from sadaqah al-fitr to the nullifiers and expiation.

The passage before you is not a set of fiqh rulings but a fihrist (index) of the Kitab al-Sawm in Ashraf al-Bidaya. This commentary on al-Hidayah organizes the discussion into logical units: starting with sadaqat al-fitr, then the rules of the fast itself, and finally a detailed chapter on what necessitates qada and kaffarah. Use this table to orient yourself before diving into the detailed text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three major sections
Notice that the list begins with a section on sadaqat al-fitr (alms of breaking the fast), then moves to 'Kitab al-Sawm' proper with sub-topics like types of fasts, intention, moon sighting, etc., and ends with a chapter on what necessitates qada and kaffarah (nullifiers and expiation). This tripartite structure is typical of Hanafi fiqh works.
Sadaqat al-fitr is often treated before the main fasting rules because it is a related obligation.
2
Locate key sub-topics within the main section
Within the core of the Book of Fasting, scan for intention (niyyah), the day of doubt (yawm al-shakk), moon sighting (ru'yat al-hilal), times of fasting, and the definition of fasting. These are foundational concepts before moving to nullifiers.
3
Recognize the pattern of a fiqh chapter
Hanafi fiqh chapters typically proceed from obligations, to conditions, to invalidators. Here you see intention (obligatory), then what breaks the fast (invalidators), followed by expiation. This pattern aids memorization and inference.
Memorizing the chapter structure helps you anticipate what rulings to expect.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for the Book of Fasting (كتاب الصوم) from Ashraf al-Bidaya, which is an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. It lists the major topics covered in the fasting section, beginning with sadaqat al-fitr (alms of breaking the fast), then the rules of fasting itself: types of fasts, intention, sighting the moon, the day of doubt, times of fasting, definition, and then a chapter on what necessitates qada (make-up) and kaffarah (expiation). The list includes various rulings on what breaks the fast, differences with Imam Shafi'i, and other details. This serves as a roadmap for the student to navigate the detailed discussions in the main text.

Key Terms
الصوم (al-Sawm): Fasting; the act of abstaining from food, drink, and sexual intercourse from dawn to sunset with intention.
الفطر (al-Fitr): Breaking the fast; also refers to sadaqat al-fitr, the alms given at the end of Ramadan.
الكفارة (al-Kaffarah): Expiation; a penalty for intentionally breaking a Ramadan fast without a valid excuse, involving freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor persons.

Navigating the Fihrist: Understanding the Table of Contents
Table of contents for Sawm, I'tikaf, and Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main fiqh topics covered in Volume 3 of Ashraf al-Hidayah and locate any subtopic by its page number using the fihrist.

Core Idea
A table of contents is a roadmap to a classical text — a skill for navigating fatwa sources.

The fihrist (table of contents) may not contain rulings, but it is the key to finding them. In Ashraf al-Hidayah, Volume 3, the fihrist lists the chapters of Sawm (Fasting), I'tikaf (Spiritual Retreat), and Hajj (Pilgrimage). Each entry is a heading for detailed discussions. Learning to read a fihrist is the first step in accessing the treasures of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main chapters
Look at the fihrist headings: clearly three sections: Sawm, I'tikaf, and Hajj. These are the organizing units of the volume.
Each chapter is a major topic in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Read the subheadings under each chapter
Under each chapter heading, there are subheadings listing specific legal issues, often with page numbers and mentions of different imams' views.
These subheadings give you the precise topic covered on that page.
3
Use page numbers to track the content
The page numbers in the fihrist correspond to the commentary text. To find a ruling on, say, a traveler's fast, look for the page number next to that subtopic.
Always cross-check with the main text for full context.
4
Note references to other madhahib
Several subheadings mention 'Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint' or 'Imam Malik's viewpoint'. This indicates where the Hanafi text compares positions.
This helps you quickly find points of ikhtilaf (disagreement).
5
Recognize the function of the fihrist
The fihrist is not a source of detailed rulings; it is an index. It tells you where to read about a specific issue, not the ruling itself.
Treat it as a navigational tool, not a legal text.

If a sick person (in the state of illness) or a traveler (in the state of travel) dies, making up the fast is not obligatory; if the sick person recovers or the traveler becomes a resident, then both die, making up is obligatory. Making up Ramadan fasts can be done consecutively or separately. If one delays making up until the next Ramadan begins, then he should first keep the fasts of the second Ramadan, then make up the missed ones. A pregnant woman and a nursing mother, if they fear for their children or themselves, may break the fast and make up later.
Definition of shaykh fani (elderly person unable to fast), ruling on giving fidyah for shaykh fani. If a person upon whom making up Ramadan fasts was obligatory dies after making a will, the guardian should give from his side for each day the equivalent of sadaqat al-fitr to a poor person. The guardian cannot fast on behalf of the deceased nor pray on his behalf.
If one begins a nafl prayer or a nafl fast and then breaks it, he must make it up – Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint. During a Ramadan day, if a child reaches puberty or a disbeliever accepts Islam, they must refrain from eating and drinking for the rest of the day. If a traveler intends to break the fast, then before noon he comes to the city and then intends to fast, that fast will be valid. If unconsciousness occurs in Ramadan, making up that day is not obligatory. If unconsciousness occurs from the first night of Ramadan, making up the entire month is obligatory.
If unconsciousness persists for the entire Ramadan, he makes up the entire month. If insanity occurs for the entire Ramadan, making up is not obligatory – Imam Malik's viewpoint. Ordering of Zakat after Salah in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains why the chapter on zakat follows salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi fiqh books order the chapter on zakat immediately after prayer, citing textual and chronological evidence.

Core Idea
The order of fiqh chapters follows Quranic sequence, prophetic hadith, and chronological legislation, not mere analogy.

Have you ever noticed that in Hanafi fiqh texts, the chapter on zakat comes right after salah, not fasting? This might seem odd since both salah and fasting are physical acts of worship. In this lesson, we explore the reasoning behind this arrangement, uncovering how the Hanafi school prioritizes textual evidence over qiyas (analogy) and takes into account the historical order in which these obligations were revealed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of worship
Read the opening of the passage and note how the author divides acts of worship into physical (salah, sawm), financial (zakat), and composite (hajj).
This categorization sets up the expectation that similar types should be grouped together.
2
Recognize the apparent qiyas
If we use qiyas (analogy), we would expect fasting to follow prayer because both are physical acts. The author explicitly states this.
This is the default logical progression, but it is overridden.
3
Analyze the Quranic order
Look at the verse cited: 'Aqim al-salata wa atu al-zakata' – Allah pairs salah and zakat directly. This provides a strong textual basis for placing zakat immediately after salah.
Quranic sequence is a primary proof in Hanafi methodology.
4
Analyze the hadith order
The hadith of the five pillars lists shahada, then salah, then zakat. This further supports the sequence.
Sunnah corroborates the Quranic order.
5
Evaluate the chronological argument
The passage notes that according to Mulla Ali Qari, zakat was made fard before fasting. This chronological precedence also favors placing zakat before fasting.
Some scholars differ on the exact timing, but the predominant view supports this order.
