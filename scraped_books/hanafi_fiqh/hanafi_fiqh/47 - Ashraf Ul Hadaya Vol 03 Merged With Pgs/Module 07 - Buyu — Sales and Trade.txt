Land Remains 'Ushri When Recovered by Muslim
Ruling on land tax status after reversion via shuf'a or fasid sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that land remains 'ushri when recovered from a dhimmi via shuf'a or upon annulment of a fasid sale, and explain the legal reasoning behind each scenario.

Core Idea
'Ushri land recovered by a Muslim retains its 'ushri status; a dhimmi's intervening ownership is legally nullified.

In Hanafi fiqh, land classification as 'ushri or kharaji has significant implications. A common scenario arises when a Muslim sells 'ushri land to a dhimmi, then later recovers it. Does the land change to kharaji? This passage clarifies that it does not; it reverts to 'ushri. Understanding the underlying legal principles—transfer of transaction and voiding of defective sale—is key to applying this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two recovery scenarios
Recognize the two ways a Muslim may recover 'ushri land from a dhimmi: by exercising the right of pre-emption (shuf‘ah) or because the sale is invalid (fasid) and the land is returned to the seller.
Shuf‘ah applies when a co-owner or neighbor buys the property from the dhimmi; fasid sale occurs when there is a defect in the contract, such as uncertainty in price.
2
Trace the reasoning for shuf‘ah
The transaction transfers to the pre-emptor as if he bought it from the Muslim seller. Therefore, the land never effectively passed to the dhimmi; it is as if the Muslim pre-emptor purchased directly from the Muslim owner.
This preserves the original 'ushri status because ownership did not change to a dhimmi in a valid manner.
3
Trace the reasoning for fasid sale
A fasid sale is annulled (faskh), and the contract is considered void ab initio—as if it never happened. The Muslim seller regains the land with all prior rights, including its 'ushri classification.
The Arabic phrase 'جُعِلَ الْبَيْعُ كَأَنْ لَمْ يَكُنْ' (the sale is made as if it never existed) captures this key principle.
4
Understand the overarching principle
A valid, binding sale would have changed the land to kharaji, but in these scenarios the Muslim's right was never fully severed due to the defect or the shuf‘ah clawback. Therefore, the original 'ushri status persists.
This reflects the wider Hanafi principle that ownership only changes through a sound, final contract.

Stability of Land Classification After Return via Shuf'ah or Fasid Sale
Ruling with reasoning on land classification after transfer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the classification of 'ushri land transferred to a dhimmi and returned via shuf'ah or fasid sale, and derive the ruling by applying the principles of retroactive invalidity and contractual substitution.

Core Idea
Land classification follows the owner's status; a voided sale is as if it never was.

When a Muslim sells his 'ushri land to a non-Muslim, does it become kharaji? The Hanafi school rules that if the land returns to a Muslim via shuf'ah or because the sale was fasid, it remains 'ushri. This passage explains the legal reasoning behind this stability of classification, grounded in the principles of shuf'ah transfer and the retroactive annulment of fasid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize that the passage deals with two ways the land can come back to a Muslim: through shuf'ah (pre-emption) by another Muslim, or by return to the original seller due to the sale being fasid (defective).
Both scenarios involve the land coming back into Muslim ownership after being temporarily under a dhimmi.
2
Analyze the shuf'ah case
Understand the legal fiction: the shafi' (person exercising shuf'ah) is treated as if he bought the land directly from the original Muslim seller. Thus, the dhimmi's intermediate ownership is disregarded.
The principle: al-shuf'ah tahduru al-'aqd (pre-emption retroacts the contract).
3
Analyze the fasid sale case
A fasid sale is one that must be rescinded; it does not transfer valid ownership. The return voids the contract retroactively, so the Muslim seller never lost rightful ownership.
Hanafi rule: al-bay' al-fasid yaruddu ka-annahu lam yakun (a fasid sale is returned as if it never was).
4
Apply the common rationale
In both cases, the Muslim's right to the land never truly ended. Since the land never permanently left Muslim ownership, its 'ushri status remains unchanged.
The passage explicitly states: "Wa li-anna haqq al-muslim lam yanqati' bi-hadha al-shira' li-kawnihi mustahaqq al-radd."

Buying and Selling in the Mosque During I'tikaf
Ruling on the permissibility of transactions without goods and the dislike of bringing goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mu'takif buying and selling in the mosque, distinguishing between permissible and disliked actions, and apply the proof from hadith.

Core Idea
A mu'takif may trade in the mosque without goods due to need, but bringing goods is disliked; for others, all trade is disliked.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school regarding commercial transactions within the mosque during the sacred state of i'tikaf. While the general principle discourages worldly activities in the mosque, necessity (hajah) creates an exception for the mu'takif under specific conditions. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is buying and selling in the mosque. For the mu'takif without bringing goods, it is permissible (la ba'sa). For the mu'takif bringing goods, it is disliked (makruh). For non-mu'takif, it is disliked generally.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith: 'Keep your children away from your mosques... and your buying and selling.'
Hadith is ahad, so zanni al-thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command to avoid buying and selling indicates dislike, but the mu'takif's need overrides via istihsan, making it permissible without goods.
Need (hajah) permits what is otherwise disliked.
4
Note the scope
The exception is limited to the mu'takif and only when goods are not physically brought into the mosque.
Bringing goods is always disliked.

Grammatical Proof for Authority of Two Just Men: The Hanafi Refutation
Hanafi response to opponent's grammatical proof about the phrase of compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi grammatical argument for why the hunter, not the two just men, chooses the type of compensation for hunting in ihram.

Core Idea
Proper 'atf requires unity of declension; thus the two just men only assess value, not choose the penalty.

This lesson examines a classical ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of the Qur'anic penalty for hunting in ihram (al-Ma'idah 5:95). The dispute turns on a fine point of Arabic grammar: whether the words 'kaffarah' (compensation) and 'adlu dhalika' (its equivalent) are coordinated with 'hadyan' (sacrificial animal) by the particle 'aw' (or). The Hanafi school refutes the coordination, restricting the role of two just men to valuation only.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling: the hunter himself has the authority to choose among hady, it'am, or siyam once the two just men estimate the value of the game.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that the opposing school (Shafi'i) argues that the two just men have authority over all three options because they are coordinated by 'aw'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the opponent's grammatical argument: 'hadyan' is accusative (mansub) and is the object; 'kaffarah' and 'adlu dhalika' are coordinated with it, so they share the same ruling.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: 'hadyan' is accusative, while 'kaffarah' and 'adlu' are nominative (marfu'). Grammatical coordination ('atf) requires unity of declension, so the coordination is invalid. They are actually coordinated with 'jaza'' (penalty), which is unstated.
This is a purely linguistic argument based on the rules of Arabic grammar.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: under the Hanafi school, the hunter chooses; under the opposing view, the two just men would choose. The Hanafi position is relied upon.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the interpretation of Qur'anic verse 5:95 regarding the penalty for hunting in ihram. The opponent (likely Shafi'i) argues that the grammatical structure of the verse gives two just men authority over all three options: a sacrificial animal (hady), feeding the poor (it'am), or fasting (siyam). Specifically, they claim the accusative word 'hadyan' is coordinated with 'kaffarah' and 'adlu dhalika' by the particle 'aw', so the same ruling applies to all. The Hanafi response, as given here, refutes this by pointing out a grammatical error: 'hadyan' is accusative (mansub) while 'kaffarah' and 'adlu' are nominative (marfu'), so proper Arabic coordination ('atf) is invalid when declensions differ. Instead, these terms are coordinated with 'jaza' (penalty), which is unstated, maintaining unity of declension. Slaughter of Hady and It'am: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling and inter-madhhab dispute on location of hady and it'am

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits it'am outside Makkah while Shafi'i restricts it, using the distinction between rational and non-rational acts of worship.

Core Idea
Non-rational worship is place-bound; rational worship is universal.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute about where to slaughter the hady and where to feed the poor as compensation for hunting. The Hanafi school holds that hady is restricted to Makkah, but feeding can be done anywhere. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, analogizing feeding to hady. The Hanafi response hinges on a fundamental usul distinction: some acts of worship are ta'abbudi (non-rational), others are ma'qul (rational). Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the limits of qiyas in Hanafi legal thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its textual basis
The matn states that hady cannot be slaughtered except in Makkah, citing the Qur'anic phrase 'hadyan baligh al-ka'bah'. Identify what this phrase implies about the location.
The Hanafis interpret 'baligh al-ka'bah' as reaching the Haram area, not the literal Ka'bah building.
2
State the Shafi'i objection and its analogy
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that feeding (it'am) should also be restricted to the Haram because it shares the same purpose as hady: providing ease for Haram residents. He uses qiyas (analogy) to link the two.
The common cause (jami') is توسعة على سكان الحرم (providing ease to the inhabitants of the Haram).
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafis reject the analogy because hady is a non-rational act of worship (قربت غیر معقولہ) while charity is a rational act (قربت معقولہ). Non-rational acts are performed exactly as prescribed and cannot be analogized.
This is a methodological point: qiyas is only valid between similar categories; the distinction between ta'abbudi and ma'qul is a Hanafi usul principle.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Hanafi law, a hunter who chooses to feed the poor can do so anywhere; under Shafi'i law, the food must be given to the poor of Makkah. This is the real-world impact of the dispute.
This illustrates that madhhab differences often have practical outcomes for worshippers.

Which Animals Are Acceptable for Hady? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute over Young Livestock
Ruling on the type and age of animals valid for hady, with a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the age of animals valid for hady and explain the basis of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan's opposing view.

Core Idea
For hady, only animals that meet the udhiyah age standards are acceptable according to the majority Hanafi view.

This passage addresses a key question for a hunter in ihram: if he chooses to offer a hady (sacrificial animal) as compensation for hunting, what kind of animal is acceptable? The text presents an internal Hanafi disagreement: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require animals that satisfy the conditions of udhiyah (e.g., at least one year for sheep/goats), while Muhammad ibn al-Hasan accepts younger livestock based on the practice of the Companions.

Commentary
This passage discusses what type and age of animal is acceptable for the hady in the penalty for hunting. The matn states that what suffices for udhiyah suffices for hady, i.e., the animal must meet the conditions for a valid sacrifice: at least one year for sheep/goats, two years for cows, five years for camels (according to Hanafi). However, Muhammad ibn al-Hasan and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that young livestock (صغار النعم) i.e., lambs or kids that are less than a year old but have reached the age of being weaned (jadh'a or thani?) are acceptable because the Companions (sahaba) are reported to have given a young she-goat ('anaq) and a young male goat (jafrah? or jadha'). The text cuts off before giving the full position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, but the beginning of their view is 'yajuz' (it is permissible). Possibly they allow older animals but not young ones? The standard Hanafi position is that only animals that meet the age and soundness conditions for udhiyah are valid for hady. The text is incomplete, so we cannot conclude the full ruling. The passage is a typical intra-Hanafi dispute with Muhammad differing from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Key Terms

صغار النعم (sighar al-ni'am): Young livestock, specifically young sheep and goats that have not yet reached one year of age.
