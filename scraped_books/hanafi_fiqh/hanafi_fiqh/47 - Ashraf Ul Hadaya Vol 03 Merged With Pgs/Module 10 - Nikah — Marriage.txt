Ihram by Proxy for an Unconscious Person: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of ihram by proxy for unconscious person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the validity of ihram by proxy for an unconscious person, explain the proofs for each position, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of the dispute.

Core Idea
Companionship in Hajj implies implicit permission for necessary acts, making proxy ihram valid even without explicit instruction.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hadaya presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of ihram assumed by a travel companion for a person who becomes unconscious. The core question is whether implicit permission (dalalah al-idhn) can be inferred from the relationship of companionship in the Hajj journey. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi method of using context and purpose to derive rulings, and it has practical implications for pilgrims who may become incapacitated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First scenario: a person becomes unconscious and his companions assume ihram for him. Abu Hanifa says valid; Sahibayn say invalid. Second scenario: with prior instruction, it's valid by consensus.
Note the two scenarios clearly.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
They argue that permission must be explicit or based on knowledge of permissibility. Since common people may not know that one can authorize ihram by proxy, implicit permission cannot be assumed. Thus, no valid permission.
Their argument hinges on the condition of knowledge for implicit permission.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
He argues that the contract of companionship implies mutual assistance in all necessary matters. Ihram is the purpose of the journey, so seeking help in it is implicitly understood. Knowledge of permissibility is derived from the evidence of companionship.
Abu Hanifa derives knowledge from the act of seeking help, not from prior knowledge of fiqh.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
According to Abu Hanifa, the unconscious person becomes a muhrim and can continue Hajj; according to Sahibayn, he is not muhrim and must assume ihram himself upon regaining consciousness.
This practical difference affects the validity of subsequent Hajj acts.
5
Note the tarjih status
The passage does not explicitly give tarjih, but later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Hanifa in cases of necessity, though Sahibayn's view is also considered.
No explicit tarjih in this text; it presents both arguments.

Translation: And the one upon whom unconsciousness occurred – so his companions recited talbiyah on his behalf – according to Abu Hanifa it is permissible, and the Sahibayn said it is not permissible. And if he had ordered a person to assume ihram on his behalf when unconsciousness occurs or he sleeps, then the one ordered assumed ihram on his behalf, then by consensus it is valid, such that when he regains consciousness or awakens and performs the actions of Hajj, it is permissible. The proof of the Sahibayn: he neither assumed ihram himself nor gave permission to another to assume ihram. This is because he did not give explicit permission, and implicit permission is dependent on knowledge, and many jurists do not know the permissibility of permission for ihram, so how would the common people know? Contrary to the case when he explicitly ordered another. And the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa: This person contracted companionship with his companions, so he sought assistance from each of the companions in every matter he was unable to do himself. And ihram is the specific purpose of this journey, so implicit permission for ihram is established. And knowledge is obtained by considering the evidence, and the ruling is based on evidence. Explanation of the case: If a person becomes unconscious and then one of his travel companions assumes ihram on his behalf, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa it is permissible, in the sense that the companion's ihram is original for himself and by way of proxy for the unconscious person. And the Sahibayn said this is not permissible. The second case: A person ordered a companion: 'If I become unconscious or sleep, then assume ihram on my behalf.' Then the one ordered assumed ihram in that state – by agreement of the Hanafis it is valid, such that when the orderer regains consciousness or awakens and performs the acts of Hajj, it is permissible without a new ihram. The conclusion: If he gave prior order, then by agreement the proxy's ihram on his behalf is valid; otherwise there is disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. In the disputed case, the proof of the Sahibayn: This person neither assumed ihram himself nor gave permission to another to assume ihram by way of proxy. That he did not assume ihram himself is obvious, and that he did not give permission to another is because permission is either explicit or implicit, and there is no third form. Here both types of permission are absent. The absence of explicit permission is obvious because he did not explicitly appoint anyone as his proxy for ihram. And implicit permission is not found because implicit permission is dependent on knowledge, i.e., first one must know that ihram is achieved by giving permission for ihram, and the permissibility of permission for ihram is not known to many jurists, let alone the common people. So when the permissibility of permission is not known, there will be no implicit permission. And when permission is found neither explicitly nor implicitly, it is as if no permission to assume ihram was given. And when there is no permission from him, how can another's ihram by way of proxy be valid? The conclusion: In this case, ihram is found neither as an original act nor by proxy. When neither is found, this person will not be considered a muhrim, and another person's assumption of ihram on his behalf will not be valid according to Shariah. Contrary to the case when he ordered another to assume ihram on his behalf, because in that case permission is found, so the proxy's ihram is valid.

Imam Abu Hanifa's proof: When this person contracted companionship with his travel companions, he sought help from each of them in every matter he was unable to do himself. And the specific purpose of this journey is ihram, and he is unable to do it due to unconsciousness, so it is as if he implicitly sought their help in assuming ihram. Thus implicit permission for ihram is established. As for the objection that implicit permission is dependent on knowledge, we say that knowledge is established by considering the evidence. That is, his seeking help in every matter is evidence that he knows it is permissible. In any case, knowledge is obtained by considering the evidence, and the ruling is based on evidence. So it is established that he has knowledge, and when knowledge is established, implicit permission is also established.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of ihram assumed by a proxy for a person who becomes unconscious without prior explicit permission. The author, Ashraf al-Hadaya, explains two scenarios. First, if a person becomes unconscious and his travel companions recite talbiyah (assume ihram) on his behalf without any prior instruction, Imam Abu Hanifa holds it valid while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold it invalid. Second, if the person had previously ordered a specific individual to assume ihram for him in case of unconsciousness or sleep, then all Hanafis agree it is valid. The dispute centers on whether implicit permission (dilalah al-idhn) can be inferred from the relationship of companionship in a Hajj journey. The Sahibayn argue that permission must be explicit or based on knowledge; since the common people may not know that one can authorize ihram by proxy, implicit permission cannot be assumed. Imam Abu Hanifa counters that the contract of companionship implies mutual assistance in all necessary matters, and since ihram is the core purpose of the journey and the person is unable to perform it himself, seeking help in ihram is implicitly understood. He adds that knowledge can be derived from the evidence (the contract of companionship), so the condition for implicit permission is met. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but later Hanafi fatwa often follows Imam Abu Hanifa's view in such cases of necessity, though the Sahibayn's caution is also considered. The passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of using implicit meaning (dalalah) and istihsan (juristic preference) over strict analogy.

Key Terms
دلالۃ الاذن (dalalah al-idhn): Implicit permission inferred from context or circumstances, as opposed to explicit verbal permission
رفاقت (rifaqah): Companionship in travel, specifically for Hajj, which implies mutual assistance and tacit understanding of each other's needs
صباحبين (al-Sahibayn): The two disciples: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the principal students of Abu Hanifa
