Prohibitions of a Sick Person: Preferential Repayment and Confession to an Heir
Explains invalid actions of a sick person toward creditors and heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a sick person's preferential debt repayment and confession to an heir, explain the Shafi'i counterargument using the hadith, and apply the ruling to a scenario.

Core Idea
A sick person cannot favour a creditor or confess to an heir – all must be treated equally.

In death illness (marad al-mawt), Islamic law restricts a person's freedom to dispose of wealth to protect the rights of creditors and heirs. This lesson examines two specific prohibitions: repaying one creditor over others, and making a confession (iqrar) of a debt or property to an heir. We will study the Hanafi ruling, the supporting hadith, and a conflicting Shafi'i opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two actions and the Hanafi ruling
Read the matn: it prohibits specifying a creditor for repayment and making a confession to an heir. Note that both are invalid (batil) unless confirmed by others.
The exception: if the other creditors/heirs confirm, it becomes valid.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
Find the hadith cited: 'لا وصية لوارث، ولا إقرار له بالدين'. Understand its force: it explicitly prohibits a will or confession to an heir.
This is a zanni hadith (ahad) but relied upon in the Hanafi school.
3
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i (in one opinion) argues that confession is an expression of truth, and the sick person is more truthful in illness. He analogizes to confession to a stranger.
This shows the ikhtilaf: is the confession a mere declaration of truth or a disposition like a will?
4
Analyse the Hanafi response and practical outcome
The Hanafis counter with the explicit hadith and the principle that the rights of heirs attach to the estate during marad al-mawt. The practical difference (thamarah): Hanafi says confession is void; Shafi'i says it is binding.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the validity of a sick person's (marīḍ) actions in his death illness (marad al-mawt). The matn states two rulings: (1) It is not valid for a sick person to repay the debt of one creditor while neglecting others, because this harms the rights of other creditors. (2) It is not valid for a sick person to make a confession (iqrār) of a debt or an asset in favor of an heir, unless the other heirs confirm it (i.e., they waive their right to object). The commentary elaborates that even giving dower or wages cannot be preferential unless the debt was incurred during the illness and proven by evidence. The Shāfi‘ī school, in one opinion, holds that such a confession is valid because it is an expression of truth and the sick person is more likely to be truthful due to the gravity of the situation. The Hanafī response relies on a hadith from al-Daraqṭuṭnī: 'No will to an heir nor confession of debt to him.' Additionally, Hanafī reasoning states that during death illness, the rights of all heirs attach to the estate; thus preferential treatment amounts to nullifying those rights. The commentator then discusses whether the exception in the matn (‘unless the rest confirm’) also applies to creditors. He notes that according to al-Hidāyah and other works, a sick person may repay a debt incurred during the illness if proven by evidence, as this does not harm other creditors. The key point is that the exception is primarily about confession to an heir, not about preferential repayment. The discussion ends with an invitation to ponder the subtleties.

Key Terms
إقرار المريض (iqrār al-marīḍ): A confession made by a person during his death illness (marad al-mawt) concerning a debt or property right.
غريم (gharīm): A creditor to whom the sick person owes a debt.
وارث (wārith): An heir who is entitled to a share of the estate upon death.

Effect of Subsequent Lineage on Prior Confession
Explains effect of subsequent proof of lineage on prior confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a prior confession is void when the beneficiary is later proven to be an heir, and distinguish when a confession remains valid despite subsequent marriage.

Core Idea
The validity of a dying person's confession depends on the beneficiary's status at the time of confession, not later events.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents a nuanced ruling: a confession made during death illness to a stranger may become invalid if the stranger is later proven to be an heir. Conversely, a confession to a foreign woman remains valid even if she is later married. The key is the timing of the status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) confession to a man who later turns out to be a son, and (2) confession to a woman who later becomes a wife.
2
Understand the effect of proving lineage
When the man's lineage is established, he becomes an heir. Under Hanafi law, a confession during death illness to an heir is void.
3
Apply the rule to the first scenario
Therefore, the confession to the man is void (bāṭil). The text explicitly says 'batala mā aqarra bihi'.
4
Apply the rule to the second scenario
The confession to the woman remains valid because at the time of confession she was a foreigner (ajnabiyyah). Subsequent marriage does not affect it.
The commentary emphasizes 'because she was a foreign woman at the time of confession'.

And if he confesses to something for a man, then his [the man's] lineage becomes established from him, his confession is void, and what he confessed to a foreign woman is valid, then he marries her. The author's words: 'And if he confesses...' mean if the sick person confesses to something for a foreign man, then he confesses that he is his son and his lineage is established, that confession of the thing is void because he has become an heir, and the confession of a sick person to his heir is not valid. Commentary
This passage states a ruling: If a sick person (al-marīḍ) makes a confession (iqrār) of a property right to a man who is a stranger (ajnabī), and then it becomes known that the man is actually his son (the lineage is established), that confession is void (bāṭil). The reason is that once the lineage is proven, the man becomes an heir (wārith), and as established in the previous ruling, a sick person cannot validly confess in favor of an heir. However, if the sick person also confessed something to a foreign woman (non-relative), that confession remains valid even if he later marries her. The timing matters: at the moment of confession, she was a stranger, so the confession is effective. The marriage does not retroactively invalidate it. This shows that the validity of a confession during death illness depends on the status of the recipient at the time of the confession, not subsequently.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): A confession of a right or obligation, here made by a sick person regarding property.
نسب (nasab): Lineage; the established relationship of fatherhood.
أجنبية (ajnabiyyah): A woman who is not a relative within the prohibited degrees; a stranger.
