Resolving Pronoun Ambiguity in Kitaba Contracts
Grammatical correction of pronoun reference in matn of Al-Wiqayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve ambiguous pronoun references in Hanafi matn texts and explain their legal implications.

Core Idea
A pronoun referring to the wrong party can invert the legal ruling—precision in language is precision in fiqh.

A single ambiguous pronoun can corrupt the meaning of a legal text, leading to incorrect rulings. In this passage, the commentator 'Imād al-Ri‘āyah dissects a grammatical ambiguity in Al-Wiqayah regarding the subject of the verb 'kataba' in the context of kitaba contracts. By carefully testing both possible interpretations, he arrives at the correct reading that preserves the legal distinction between emancipation and kitaba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous pronouns
Locate the two occurrences of 'kataba' (كاتب) in the matn: 'كاتب الولد' and 'كاتب الأم'. The subject pronoun (doer) of each is implicit and ambiguous.
The ambiguity lies in whether the doer is the seller (البائع) or the buyer (المشتري).
2
Test the first interpretation: both pronouns refer to the buyer
If 'كاتب الولد' and 'كاتب الأم' both refer to the buyer, the phrase 'أو كاتب الأم' would mean 'or the buyer wrote the mother's kitaba'. But this contradicts the preceding context, which discusses the seller's sale of the child. The conjunction fails.
The commentator argues this leads to an incoherent meaning.
3
Test the second interpretation: both pronouns refer to the seller
If both refer to the seller (the 'man' in 'من باع'), then 'كاتب الولد' means the seller wrote the kitaba for the child. However, the commentator notes this would blur the distinction between emancipation and kitaba, as the earlier emancipation case involved the buyer freeing the child.
The correct distinction must be between the buyer's emancipation and the buyer's kitaba, not the seller's kitaba.
4
Apply the commentator's resolution
The commentator concludes that 'كاتب الولد' refers to the buyer (المشتري), and 'كاتب الأم' refers to the seller (the 'man' in 'من باع'). This yields a coherent meaning: the buyer writes the kitaba for the child, while the seller writes the kitaba for the mother in a separate transaction.
This resolution preserves the logical flow and the legal distinction.
5
Consider an alternative reading
The commentator also mentions a 'precious point': the phrase 'أو كاتب الأم' might refer to a separate case where the seller sold the mother (with the child) and the buyer wrote her kitaba. This avoids the need for the previous resolution, but the commentator leans toward the first resolution as more precise.
This alternative is optional but shows the depth of scholarly analysis.

Interpretation of 'Five in Five' in Iqrār: The Dispute over 'في'
Intra-Hanafi dispute on meaning of 'في' in monetary confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the different Hanafi positions on the meaning of 'خمسة في خمسة' in a confession and explain the practical difference in the amount owed.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the meaning of 'في' in a confession is determined by the speaker's intention, but if multiple plausible meanings exist, the confession may be invalid.

When someone confesses 'I owe you five in five,' the amount owed depends on the intended meaning of the particle 'في.' This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explores the intra-Hanafi dispute among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Zufar, examining how the custom of arithmeticians and Quranic usage inform the interpretation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle ambiguous language in legal confessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its legal context
The phrase 'خمسة في خمسة' occurs in iqrar (confession of debt). Identify the core issue: what does the particle 'في' mean?
2
List the interpretations and their resulting amounts
Multiplication (ضرب) → five dirhams (the confession is for 'five multiplied' as a description, not the product). Intending 'with' (مع) → ten. Zufar: always ten because 'في' means 'with.'
Confusingly, the text says multiplication yields five, not twenty-five.
3
Identify the positions of the Hanafi imams
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: if multiplication, five; if 'with', ten. Muhammad: in garment analogy, owes eleven. Zufar: always ten. Hasan ibn Ziyad: rejects multiplication.
4
Analyze the Quranic evidence for polysemy
'في' appears in Quran meaning 'among' (فادخلي في عبادي), 'on' (في جذوع النخل), and possibly 'with'. This shows the particle has multiple plausible meanings.
5
Determine the final operative ruling
Because 'في' has multiple equally plausible meanings, the confession is ambiguous and la yajib (not obligatory) unless the speaker clarifies. Abu Hanifa retracted his earlier view.
The custom of arithmeticians only applies to measured items, not weighed (dirhams).

Sulh with denial is valid in Hanafi law because the Quranic generality and the hadith permit all forms of sulh unless they change halal to haram.

This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on sulh (compromise) when the defendant denies the claim or remains silent. وعند الشافعي لا يصح إلا في صورة الإقرار ؛ فلا يجوز الصلح مع إنكار أو سكوت ؛ لما تقدَّم من الحديثِ المذكور ، فإنّ الصلح مع إنكار أو سكوت بهذه الصفة ؛ لأن بدل الصلح كان حلالاً على الدافع، حراماً على الآخذ ؛ لكون المدعى عليه منكراً، وعدم ثبوت حق المدعي بالبينة، فينقلب الأمر، ويكون حراماً على الدافع وحلالاً على الأخذ.
The Book of Sulh: It is a contract that removes dispute, valid with acknowledgment, silence, and denial. Know that sulh is of three types: first, with acknowledgment; second, with silence; third, with denial — because the opponent at the time of the claim either remains silent or speaks in response, and that response is not free from negation or affirmation. All three types are permissible due to His saying: 'And sulh is better' (al-Nisa 4:128). The word 'sulh' here is defined by 'al', so it implies that every sulh is good, and every good thing is lawful, so every sulh is lawful. This verse, although revealed regarding spousal reconciliation, the consideration is the generality of the wording, not the specificity of the cause. Also, this statement came as a rationale, and the cause is not restricted to the place of the ruling, so it is general. It cannot be said that applying it to all is difficult, because sulh after an oath, sulh of a depositary, and sulh of one who claims marriage from a woman who denies it are not valid, so it must be restricted to the first meaning, which is sulh with acknowledgment. We say: leaving a general application in some places due to an impediment does not necessitate leaving it when there is no impediment. Moreover, Abu Dawud narrated from Abu Hurayra: 'Every sulh among Muslims is permissible except a sulh that makes something permissible that is unlawful or makes something unlawful that is permissible.' According to al-Shafi'i, it is valid only in the case of acknowledgment; so sulh with denial or silence is not permissible due to the aforementioned hadith, because the consideration (badal) of the sulh would be permissible for the giver and unlawful for the receiver, since the defendant denies and the plaintiff's right is not established by evidence, so the matter reverses and it becomes unlawful for the giver and lawful for the receiver.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Kitab al-Sulh (Book of Compromise). The author defines sulh as a contract that removes dispute and states that it is valid in three scenarios: when the defendant acknowledges the claim, when he remains silent, or when he denies it. This is a matn statement. The commentary then elaborates: sulh is categorized based on whether the opponent (the one being sued) acknowledges, remains silent, or denies. The proof for permissibility in all three cases is the Quranic verse 'wa al-sulhu khayr' (And compromise is better), which is general (amm) according to the Hanafi principle that 'ibrah li-umum al-lafz' (consideration is given to the generality of the wording). This generality is not negated by the fact that the verse was revealed about spousal reconciliation, and the underlying reason (illa) is not restricted to that context. The commentary then addresses a possible objection: some forms of sulh are invalid (e.g., after an oath, with a depositary, or in a marriage claim). The Hanafi response is that exceptions occur only when a specific impediment exists and do not undermine the general principle. Additionally, the hadith 'Every sulh among Muslims is permissible...' supports the permissibility of all forms of sulh unless they change the halal/haram status. The passage then presents the Shafi'i position: sulh is only valid with acknowledgment, not with silence or denial. The Shafi'i reasoning is that in cases of denial, the exchange becomes unlawful for one party because the plaintiff has no proven right. The Hanafi position, relying on the general proofs, remains that all three types are permissible, and the Shafi'i restriction is rejected.

إقرار (iqrar): Defendant's acknowledgment of the claim.
سكوت (sukut): Defendant's silence when faced with a claim.
إنكار (inkar): Defendant's denial of the claim.

Linguistic Clarification of 'Ribh' (Profit)
Linguistic clarification of the word 'ribh'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two valid pronunciations of 'ribh' and explain its meaning as surplus in the context of Hanafi mudarabah contracts.

Core Idea
The word 'ribh' has two valid pronunciations: rabih (with two fatḥas) and ribh (with kasra-sukūn), both meaning gain or surplus.

This brief commentary note clarifies the correct linguistic form of a key term in Hanafi fiqh: 'ribh' (profit). Understanding its pronunciation and meaning is essential for correctly reading contracts like mudarabah. The commentator draws from authoritative lexicons to confirm that both forms are valid and that the word denotes surplus above capital.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its context
Locate the word 'ribh' as it appears in the definition of a mudarabah partnership contract. The term is central to understanding profit-sharing.
2
Note the two pronunciations
The text explicitly states that 'ribh' can be pronounced with two fatḥas (rabih) or with a kasra and sukūn (ribh). Both are valid.
This is a lexical detail that avoids misreading in contracts.
3
Confirm the meaning from lexicons
The passage cites al-Ṣirāḥ and al-Miṣbāḥ, and quotes al-Azharī, confirming that the term means 'surplus' or 'gain' in trade.
Understanding that it denotes surplus is crucial for distinguishing it from capital or loss.
4
Understand the technical usage
In Hanafi fiqh, 'ribh' is the surplus above capital that is shared according to the partnership agreement. It is not synonymous with unlawful interest (ribā).
This definition aligns with its general Arabic meaning but with a specific contractual application.
