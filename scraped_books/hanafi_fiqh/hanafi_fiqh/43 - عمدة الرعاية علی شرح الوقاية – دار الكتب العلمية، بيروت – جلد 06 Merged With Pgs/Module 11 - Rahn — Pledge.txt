The General Rule: Agency Terminated by Death or Insanity
General rule of termination and intra-madhhab dispute on self-dismissal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule that agency is terminated by the principal's death or insanity and explain the rationale based on the revocable nature of agency, and also state the Hanafi position on the agent's self-dismissal without the principal's knowledge.

Core Idea
Agency is revocable; death or insanity voids it, and self-dismissal requires the principal's knowledge.

This passage begins by stating the general rule of agency termination. The commentary explains that agency is a revocable contract, so its continuation requires the principal's ongoing capacity. Beyond death and insanity, there is a key dispute about whether an agent can unilaterally dismiss himself. The Hanafi school holds that such self-dismissal is ineffective without the principal's knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states: 'And the agency is voided' (وتبطل الوكالة). Determine what events cause this voiding from the context – it refers to death and insanity of the principal.
The phrase is a general statement; the exceptions are discussed later.
2
Understand the rationale
The commentary explains that agency is a revocable (jā'iz ghayr lāzim) contract. Therefore, its continuation has the same effect as initiation, requiring the principal's legal capacity. Death and insanity end capacity, thus voiding the agency.
This is a qiyas (analogy) to the requirement for initiating a contract.
3
Analyze the self-dismissal dispute
The text contrasts the Hanafi position with that of al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad. According to Hanafis, if an agent dismisses himself without the principal's knowledge, he is not dismissed (لا ينعزل). The opposing schools hold that self-dismissal is effective. Malik also has a narration considering harm to the principal.
The practical effect: under Hanafi law, the agent remains bound until he communicates his resignation to the principal.
4
Connect to the broader rule
The self-dismissal issue is a special case of the general rule that termination events require knowledge or a valid cause. Here, the agent's unilateral act without knowledge does not terminate because it would prejudice the principal's interests.
This exception is an application of istihsan in some interpretations.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the termination of agency. The author first states that agency is voided (i.e., terminated) by certain events. The commentary explains that agency is a revocable contract (jā'iz ghayr lāzim), so it is subject to termination. The agent may also terminate his own agency, but only with the principal's knowledge, unlike the Shafi'i and others who hold that self-dismissal is effective without knowledge. The main point is that the general rule that agency ends with the death or insanity of the principal has exceptions. Several cases are listed: (1) When a mortgagor appoints a trustee or the mortgagee to sell the mortgaged property upon maturity, that agency is not terminated by the principal's death or insanity because it is a necessary (lāzim) agency linked to the rights of the mortgagee. (2) The agent for a sale with immediate payment (al-wakīl bi al-amr bi al-yad) is similarly not terminated. (3) The agent for litigation at the request of the opponent is terminated by death/insanity. (4) The agent for divorce is terminated by the principal's death by istihsan (juristic preference) not by qiyas. These exceptions are cited from al-Tanwir, al-Bazzaziyya, and al-Bahr. The key distinction is whether the agency is binding (lāzim) or revocable; only revocable agencies are terminated by death/insanity.

عزل (ʿazl): Dismissal of the agent by the principal or by self-dismissal.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; here, the Hanafi preference for protecting the principal over strict analogy.

Exceptions to Termination: When Agency Survives Death or Insanity
Exceptions to the general rule of termination; binding vs. revocable agencies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cases where a Hanafi agency is not terminated by the principal's death or insanity, and explain the distinction between binding (lāzim) and revocable (jā'iz) agencies.

Core Idea
A binding agency linked to a third party's right, like a mortgage, survives the principal's death or insanity.

While the general rule states that agency is voided by death or insanity, the Hanafi school recognizes important exceptions. These involve agencies that are binding (lāzimah) because they are tied to the rights of a third party, such as a mortgagee or a buyer. This lesson explores those exceptions and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle and the exceptions
The text begins by reiterating the general rule from the previous lesson, then explicitly states: 'But several cases must be excepted from this principle.' List the exceptions mentioned: (1) mortgage sale agency, (2) agent for hand-to-hand payment, (3) agent for litigation (this one is NOT excepted; it is terminated), (4) agent for divorce (terminated by death via istihsan).
Note that litigation and divorce agencies are still terminated, showing that not all agencies linked to third parties are binding.
2
Understand the key distinction: binding vs. revocable
The passage concludes by stating that binding agencies (الوكالة اللازمة) are treated differently. In a binding agency, the authority cannot be revoked by the principal's death or dismissal. The mortgage sale agency is an example of a binding agency because it secures the mortgagee's right to sell upon default.
Al-Samani restricted the general rule by adding 'which is not binding' (التي هي ليست بلازمة).
3
Analyze the reasoning for each exception
For mortgage and hand-to-hand payment agencies, the agency is linked to a right of a third party (the mortgagee or the buyer). To protect that right, the agency must continue despite the principal's death or insanity. For litigation and divorce agencies, the agency is still linked to the principal's personal capacity; death ends that capacity, so the agency terminates.
The divorce agency termination is by istihsan, not qiyas, meaning strict analogy would suggest it continues, but juristic preference ends it to avoid confusion over the marital status.
4
Connect to the broader fiqh framework
This distinction between binding and revocable agencies is a recurring concept in Hanafi law. It reflects a balance between protecting third-party rights and respecting the principal's loss of legal capacity.
The same principle applies in other contexts, like waqf or guarantees.

Supervening Undividedness: Pledge vs Gift
Intra-Hanafi dispute on supervening undividedness in rahn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why supervening undividedness (shuyu' tari') invalidates a pledge (rahn) but not a gift (hibah) in the Hanafi school, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between riwayat al-asl and the narration of Ibn Sama'ah.

Core Idea
Supervening undividedness destroys a pledge because capacity requires full ownership; a gift remains valid because ownership alone suffices.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah sharpens a critical distinction between two contracts: rahn (pledge) and hibah (gift). While both involve property, they react differently when the property becomes undivided (shuyu'). Understanding this reveals the role of capacity (mahalliyyah) and possession (qabd) in Hanafi contract law. The passage also presents an intra-Hanafi debate: the original narration (riwayat al-asl) versus a narration from Abu Yusuf via Ibn Sama'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the original narration (riwayat al-asl) that supervening shuyu' invalidates the continuation of the pledge, and the opposing narration from Abu Yusuf (via Ibn Sama'ah) that it does not.
This is the core intra-Hanafi dispute.
2
Examine the opposing argument
Abu Yusuf argues that the ruling for continuation is easier than for initiation, so analogically (qiyas) supervening shuyu' should not invalidate, just as it does not invalidate a gift.
The analogy (tashbih) is with hibah.
3
Analyze the original narration's rebuttal
The prohibition in rahn is due to lack of capacity (mahalliyyah) – the pledged item must be fully owned by the pledgor. This requirement applies equally at initiation and continuation, unlike hibah where capacity hinges on ownership and possession differs.
Capacity is a continuous condition unique to rahn.
4
Distinguish the role of capacity in rahn vs hibah
In rahn, the pledged item must remain fully owned by the pledgor; any undividedness destroys capacity. In hibah, ownership can coexist with undividedness, and possession (qabd) is only needed at initiation to protect the donor from undue burden.
This explains why taking back part of a gift is valid but annulling part of a pledge is not.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if a pledged item becomes undivided, according to the original narration the pledge becomes invalid; according to Abu Yusuf it remains valid. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the original narration.
This matters for fatwa: a pledge with supervening undividedness is void.

Commentary
This passage compares hibah (gift) with rahn (pledge) regarding supervening shuyu' (undividedness). In hibah, supervening shuyu' does not invalidate the gift because the focus is on the act of taking possession (qabd) at initiation; once ownership is established, subsequent events like the donor taking back part do not affect validity. In rahn, however, there is a dispute within the Hanafi school. The original narration (riwayat al-asl) holds that supervening shuyu' prevents the continuation of the pledge because the pledge's capacity (mahalliyyah) requires that the pledged item remains fully owned by the debtor; if part becomes undivided, the pledge becomes defective. A second narration from Abu Yusuf via Ibn Sama'ah argues that the ruling for continuation is easier, so supervening shuyu' should not invalidate, analogous to hibah. The text explains the reasoning: in hibah, the purpose (ownership) can be achieved with undividedness, and qabd is only required at initiation to prevent imposing a burden on the donor. In rahn, the prohibition of undividedness applies equally to initiation and continuation because the pledge must be fully owned by the pledgor at all times. This difference explains why taking back part of a gift is permissible but annulling part of a pledge is not.

Key Terms
رواية الأصل (riwayat al-asl): The original narration attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, contrasting with the narration of Ibn Sama'ah from Abu Yusuf.
بقاء الرهن (baqa' al-rahn): Continuation of the pledge after it is validly initiated; the condition of the pledged item must remain intact.
الشيوع الطارئ (al-shuyu' al-tari'): Supervening undividedness – ownership of a property becoming shared after the contract was validly initiated.

Ruling on Gifting an Undivided Share and Effect of Division
Commentary on gifts of common shares and comparison with rahn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifting an undivided share (mushā‘) and explain why subsequent joint ownership invalidates a mortgage (rahn) but not a gift.

Core Idea
A gift of a common share is valid only after division and delivery—possession must be free of commonality.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift (hibah) requires possession (qabḍ) for completion. This lesson examines the special case of gifting an undivided share (mushā‘) in joint property. While a common share cannot be delivered directly, it becomes possible if the property is first divided and then the specific share is handed over. The passage also explores the limit: you cannot gift flour while it is still mixed in wheat, because the gifted item does not exist until separated. Finally, the commentator contrasts this with mortgage (rahn), where even subsequent joint ownership (shuyū‘ ṭāri’) invalidates the contract, sparking an intra-Hanafi debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
The parent text states: if someone gifts half of a common share, then divides and delivers, the gift is valid. This is the core ruling.
The gift is conditionally valid.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling regarding gifts of undivided shares (mushaa) and gifts of items not yet separated from their source. The main ruling states that if someone gifts half of a property that is held in common (shuyuu), and then the property is divided and the specific share is delivered, the gift becomes valid. This is because, in the Hanafi school, the completion of a gift requires possession (qabd), and at the time of possession there is no longer any commonality. However, if someone gifts flour that is still mixed in wheat, or oil in sesame seeds, the gift is not valid because the gifted item (flour or oil) does not exist at the time of the contract; it only comes into existence through processing (grinding or pressing). But if the donor processes it (e.g., grinds the wheat into flour) and then delivers, the gift becomes valid. Similarly for butter (samn) in milk. The commentator then compares this with the case of rahn (mortgage), where subsequent joint ownership invalidates the mortgage, unlike hibah. This leads to an intra-Hanafi dispute: in the original narration (zahir al-riwayah), subsequent joint ownership prevents the continuation of the mortgage, while in a narration from Ibn Samaca from Abu Yusuf, it does not prevent continuation because the rules for continuation are easier than for initiation. The commentator explains the reasoning for the original narration: the prohibition is due to lack of capacity for the mortgage to continue in a common share, and capacity is required at both initiation and continuation. In contrast, for hibah, the common share can be owned, and possession is only required at initiation to avoid imposing an unwanted delivery obligation; once ownership is established, possession is not needed for continuation, which is why partial revocation is possible. The commentator also clarifies that if the donor delivers the gift while it is still common, the donee does not own it, so his dispositions are ineffective, and the donor's dispositions remain valid. The critical point is that possession must be free of commonality at the time of delivery, not at the time of the contract. Finally, the non-existence of the gifted item at the time of the contract is explained: potential existence is not considered.

Key Terms
مشاع (mushā‘): An undivided share in a jointly owned property.
قبض (qabḍ): Physical possession that completes the gift; required for validity in Hanafi school.
شيوع طارئ (shuyū‘ ṭāri’): Joint ownership that arises after the contract, e.g., by inheritance.
