Appointing an Agent to Prove Hudood and Qisas: The Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tawkeel for ithbat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on appointing an agent to prove hadd and qisas and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Proving a hadd claim is separate from executing it; agency for proof is valid.

This lesson explores a key intra-Hanafi disagreement: can a person appoint an agent to prove a hadd or qisas claim before a judge? The answer hinges on whether 'proving' is considered part of the penalty itself or a separate legal act. We will examine the arguments of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and see how the school resolved the difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the positions
What is the specific legal question? State the two main positions: Abu Hanifa permits tawkeel for ithbat of hadd and qisas; Abu Yusuf does not. Note the reported view of Muhammad.
The dispute applies when the principal is absent; in their presence, it is valid by consensus.
2
Understand the Hanafi proof (shubha and Quran)
The text begins with 'li-na' (our proof). Hudood and qisas are dropped by shubha. The absent principal may have pardoned (Quran encourages pardon), creating a shubha. This shubha prevents execution by an agent but does not prevent establishing the claim.
This proof shows that the shubha only affects istifa' (execution), not ithbat (proving).
3
Follow Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues that the purpose of ithbat is istifa', so if istifa' cannot be delegated, neither can ithbat. The agent is a substitute (badal), and substitutes have no place in hudood, as seen with testimony upon testimony, judge's letter, etc.
He draws an analogy to secondary evidence, which is also a substitute and not accepted in hudood.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response and the tarjih
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the tawkeel includes litigation (khusumah), which is not the hadd itself. Litigation is a separate act that can be delegated like any other right. The text then states the more apparent view (al-azhar) that Muhammad agrees with Abu Hanifa, making it the relied-upon position.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa, the agent can validly prove the claim and the principal executes; under Abu Yusuf, the appointment is void.
5
Apply to a scenario
Consider a plaintiff who cannot attend court due to travel. He appoints a wakeel to present evidence and prove a qisas claim. According to the mufta bihi (Abu Hanifa), the wakeel can do so, and the plaintiff may execute the qisas after the right is established.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of appointing an agent (wakil) to establish (ithbat) fixed penalties (hudood) and retaliation (qisas). Abu Yusuf analogizes the agent to a substitute (badal), and substitutes do not apply to hudood, as they are not established by secondary evidence. Abu Hanifa argues that tawkeel includes litigation (khusumah), which is not the hadd itself, so it is valid like other rights. The same difference applies to the appointment for response (jawab) on behalf of the accused. The passage ends by noting that tawkeel for ithbat of zina and drinking is invalid by consensus.

The Basic Ruling on Claiming Istilad
Well-known view on master's claim of istilad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the well-known Hanafi view on why a master's or husband's claim of childbirth is not a separate legal claim, and explain the role of iqrar.

Core Idea
Lineage is established by the man's admission (iqrar); a separate claim by him is legally unnecessary.

This lesson unpacks a key nuance in Hanafi family law: when a master or husband claims that his slave-concubine or wife gave birth to his child, the well-known view holds that such a claim is not a separate legal proceeding. Why? Because the man's own admission of paternity (iqrar) already establishes lineage, making a formal claim redundant. We will explore this principle and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claim
The claim involves either a master and his slave-concubine (umm walad) or a husband and wife. The claim is that she gave birth to his child, alive or dead.
The term 'istilad' literally means requesting a child, but in legal context it refers to the claim of childbirth.
2
Understand the well-known view
The text states that the claim by the man is 'not conceivable' because lineage is established by his own admission (iqrar). If he acknowledges the child as his, that admission is legally binding and her denial is irrelevant.
This is based on the principle that iqrar is a self-imposed proof in matters of lineage.
3
Distinguish between claim and admission
A formal claim (da'wa) would require the other party to deny, but here the man's own statement suffices. The text hints that the man is not really claiming lineage—he is merely confirming what his iqrar already does.
This nuance is important: the master's 'claim' is redundant, not invalid altogether.
4
Apply to the basic scenario
If a master says 'this child is mine' about a child born to his slave-concubine, that is an iqrar. The child becomes his, the mother becomes umm walad, and her denial is irrelevant.
This is the standard Hanafi ruling.

Commentary
This passage discusses the claim of istilad, meaning a claim of childbirth. The Reverse Scenario: Claiming Diyah After Istilad
Exceptional scenario of master's claim for diyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the exceptional scenario where a master's claim after childbirth is valid, and distinguish between a claim of lineage and a claim of financial liability.

Core Idea
A master can validly claim blood-money (diyah) from a freed slave-concubine when the child dies, even if lineage is not in dispute.

In the previous lesson, we saw that a master's claim of lineage is typically unnecessary. But Al-Barjandi presents a reversal: the master claims blood-money (diyah) from the mother for the child's death. This is a financial claim that requires first establishing the child's existence—which the mother denies. This lesson explores that nuance and its legal mechanics.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reverse scenario
A slave-concubine becomes pregnant. Just before delivery, the master frees her. She accepts the child (meaning she acknowledges it as the master's). Then the child dies, and the master claims diyah (blood-money) from her.
The child's death triggers a financial liability, not lineage.
2
Identify what is being claimed
The master is not claiming lineage—that was already established by his iqrar or her acceptance. He is claiming diyah, which is a compensation for killing (here, possibly due to negligence or intentional act).
Diyah is a financial penalty in Islamic law for bodily harm or death.
3
Determine the legal hurdle
For the diyah claim to proceed, the existence of the child must be proven. The mother denies the child's existence (perhaps she claims miscarriage or that the child never lived). So the court must first resolve the factual dispute about the child's birth.
Here, the master's claim is a genuine legal action because it requires proof of the child's birth.
4
Distinguish from the basic scenario
In the basic scenario, lineage is established by iqrar alone; in this scenario, the existence of the child is contested and must be proven by evidence.
This shows that the master's 'claim' can be valid when it concerns something other than lineage.

Nullification of Evidence in Marriage Claims (Tahatur)
States the ruling and rationale for nullifying both evidences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two men claim marriage to the same woman with equal evidence and explain the rationale based on non-shareability.

Core Idea
Equal marriage claims cancel each other because a woman cannot be shared.

This lesson addresses a core procedural ruling in Hanafi fiqh: what happens when two men each claim to be married to the same woman and both produce equally strong evidence? The ruling of tahatur (mutual nullification) is based on the fundamental principle that marriage is exclusive and cannot be shared, unlike property which allows joint ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two men each claim marriage to the same woman. Both present credible evidence (bayyinah) of equal strength and simultaneously.
The woman is alive and no preponderance (tarjih) exists.
2
Understand the rationale
Marriage cannot be shared (la yuqbal al-ishtirak), unlike property (milk) where partnership is possible. Therefore, the evidence cannot be reconciled.
The text uses qiyas: just as two creditors share an estate, property claims can be shared, but marriage cannot.
3
Apply the ruling
Both evidences are nullified (tahatur). The judge separates the claimants from the woman, and if the marriage was not consummated, no mahr is due.
This is the default rule when conditions are met.
4
Note the comparison with property
The same situation in property would lead to dividing the property or establishing partnership, because sharing is possible. This contrast highlights the unique nature of marriage.
This clarifies why the ruling is specific to marriage.

Purchase vs. Marriage Dower: Who Has the Stronger Claim?
Ruling that purchase and dower are equal when both present evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani in the dispute over conflicting claims of purchase and dower, EXPLAIN the proofs behind each position, and EVALUATE which position is likely the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Purchase and dower are equal as exchange contracts; but Muhammad prefers purchase to give effect to both evidences.

This passage addresses a procedural dilemma: when a buyer and a wife both present valid evidence for the same property, which claim prevails? The Hanafi school is internally divided. Abu Yusuf holds that purchase and dower are equal (sawa') and the property is divided. Muhammad al-Shaybani argues that purchase should be preferred (awla) and the husband pays the value. The text provides detailed proofs for each view, grounded in the nature of exchange contracts (mu'awada) and the principle of giving effect to both evidences as far as possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State clearly what Abu Yusuf and Muhammad each rule when a buyer and a wife claim the same property.
Abu Yusuf: property divided equally. Muhammad: buyer takes property, husband pays value.
2
Analyze Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues that working with both evidences (bayyinatan) is possible only if purchase is preferred. Why? Because marriage with another's property is valid (the husband owes value), but selling another's property without permission is invalid. Thus, preferring purchase saves both proofs.
This is a practical reasoning: avoiding invalidating either evidence.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's proof
Abu Yusuf counters that both contracts are mu'awada (exchange) establishing ownership immediately. Even if purchase seems stronger (property for property), dower ownership is 'mutakad' (strengthened): it survives destruction before delivery and allows disposition before possession. Thus, if not preferred, at least equality.
The strengthened ownership of dower is a key Hanafi concept.
4

Abu Yusuf responds that the purpose of a cause is its ruling. If marriage is assumed to be later (after the sale), it cannot retroactively create ownership. So giving preference to purchase based on a hypothetical later marriage is invalid.
This highlights the principle of sequential causes.
5

The thamarah al-khilaf: if property is awarded to buyer (Muhammad), husband pays its value; if divided (Abu Yusuf), wife gets half. The judge's ruling depends on which view is adopted.
The text leans toward Abu Yusuf, but the mufta bihi is not explicitly stated.

Core Idea
When purchase and mahr clash on one property, the Hanafi mufta bihi splits: equality (Abu Yusuf) over purchase priority (Muhammad).

This passage addresses a classic Hanafi conflict: two exchange contracts – purchase and marriage – claiming the same property. How do we resolve? The matn declares equality, but the commentary reveals a sharp intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of the nature of ownership in exchange contracts and the limits of judicial reconciliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conflicting Claims
One person claims to have purchased a specific item; a woman claims that same item was her dower (mahr) from marriage to the same man. The judge must decide who gets it.
Note that both claims are based on valid contracts: purchase (exchange of property) and marriage (with mahr).
2
State the Rival Positions
Abu Yusuf (and some reports of Abu Hanifa) says the property is divided equally. Muhammad al-Shaybani says the purchase claim is preferred, and the woman gets only the value of the item.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; both are within the school.
3
Trace Each Imam's Proof
Muhammad argues that both claims can be reconciled by prioritizing purchase: marriage on another's property is valid (its value is owed), but prioritizing marriage would invalidate the sale. Abu Yusuf counters that both contracts are exchange contracts establishing ownership; since marriage ownership is stronger (not invalidated by destruction), it cannot be weaker, so they must be equal.
The key analogy: both contracts simultaneously confer ownership; delayed marriage would not give ownership, so they are treated as concurrent.
4
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
Under Abu Yusuf: the property is split half to the woman (as part of mahr) and half to the purchase claimant. The woman also gets a claim for half the value against the husband. The purchase claimant can reclaim half the price or rescind. Under Muhammad: the purchase claimant gets the whole item, and the woman gets its value from the husband.
The mufta bihi is Abu Yusuf's view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about what happens when a man claims to have purchased a specific item and a woman claims that same item was her dower (mahr) from her marriage to that man. The basic ruling from the matn is that they are equal: neither is preferred, and the property is divided between them. However, the commentary reveals a disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Imam Muhammad argues that the purchase should be given priority because it is possible to reconcile both claims: we treat the marriage as valid but the husband owes the value of the mahr since he cannot deliver the specific item. The purchase claim is stronger because marriage on a specific item owned by another is permissible and the husband owes its value, whereas giving priority to the marriage would invalidate the sale since selling someone else's property is void. Abu Yusuf counters that both contracts are exchange contracts that establish ownership themselves. An objection is raised that purchase involves exchange of property for property and guarantee, while marriage involves exchange of property for something non-property (the benefit of the wife). The Hanafi response under Abu Yusuf is that ownership in the dower is stronger: it is established by the contract itself and not invalidated by destruction before delivery, unlike ownership in a purchase which is not fully effective until delivery. Therefore, the marriage claim is not weaker, and at best the two are equal. The final resolution is that the property is split: half to the woman as part of her mahr (since she deserves half of the named dower) and half to the purchase claimant, and he can either reclaim half the price or rescind the sale. This is the relied-upon position.

الشراء (al-shira'): Purchase; a contract of exchange of property for property.
العقد معاوضة (al-aqd mu'awada): An exchange contract where each party gives and receives something of value.

Rules of Lineage Claim for a Sold Slave-Girl
Clarifying legal rulings for claims of paternity in sale of umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for lineage claims in the sale of a slave-girl, distinguishing between the three timeframes and the effects of claims by seller and buyer.

Core Idea
The timing of birth relative to sale determines who can claim lineage and the child's status.

This lesson explores the Hanafi rules for claiming lineage (nasab) when a sold slave-girl gives birth. The timing of the birth—before six months, between six months and two years, or after two years—critically affects whether the seller or the buyer can establish paternity and whether the child becomes free. The passage also addresses a dispute between the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) when the parties disagree about the elapsed time since the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three timeframes
Distinguish between birth less than 6 months, between 6 months and 2 years, and more than 2 years after the sale. Note that 6 months is the minimum gestation for a viable child in Hanafi law.
2
Determine who can claim in each timeframe
For each timeframe, identify whether the seller, the buyer, or both can make a valid claim, and under what conditions (e.g., need for confirmation).
3
Understand the two types of claims
A buyer's claim can be either a claim of manumission (da'wa tahrir) or a claim of istilad. These differ in whether the child is considered free-born and whether the claimant acquires wala'.
Da'wa tahrir occurs when the child is born within 6 months; da'wa istilad occurs when the child is born after 6 months.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
When the seller and buyer disagree on the time since sale and both provide evidence, Abu Yusuf favors the buyer's evidence while Muhammad favors the seller's. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
The mufta bihi is Abu Yusuf's view.
5
Apply to a scenario
Practice applying the rules to a concrete case: given the birth timeframe and who claims, determine the outcome (validity of claim, status of child, effect on sale).

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āya is a commentary (sharḥ) on the chapter 'Da‘wā al-Nasab' (Claim of Lineage) from al-Wiqāya. The author opens by explaining why this chapter follows the chapter on claims of wealth — lineage claims are less frequent but still important. He then defines key terms: 'nasab' means lineage from the father's side (though it can include mother's side), and its plural is 'ansāb'. The core ruling concerns a slave-girl (jāriya) who is sold and then gives birth while with the buyer. The timing of the birth relative to the sale determines the validity of lineage claims by the seller or buyer. If the birth occurs less than six months after the sale, the seller's claim (or his acknowledgment testified by witnesses) establishes lineage and makes the slave-girl an umm walad, rescinding the sale. If the buyer claims, it is a claim of manumission (da‘wā taḥrīr) giving him wala' over the child. If both claim, the seller's claim takes precedence; if successive, the earlier. If the birth occurs between six months and two years, the seller's claim requires the buyer's confirmation, while the buyer's claim alone suffices and is a claim of istīlād (making the child free but without wala'). If both claim, only the buyer's claim is valid. If the birth occurs after two years, the seller's claim again needs buyer confirmation; the buyer's claim alone is valid as istīlād; if both claim, buyer's claim prevails. The text also clarifies that 'mubī'a' refers to a slave-girl sold only once, to avoid confusion in cases of multiple sales. The author then discusses a disagreement among later scholars about the time period: Abu Yusuf holds that if both parties dispute the duration but bring evidence, the buyer's evidence is preferred; Muhammad holds that the seller's evidence is preferred. Finally, it notes that a claim of lineage before birth is suspended until live birth.

أم ولد (umm walad): A slave-girl who has borne a child to her master, thus becoming free upon her master's death according to Hanafi law.
دعوى تحرير (da‘wā taḥrīr): A claim that results in the child's freedom and establishes wala' for the claimant (buyer).

Seller's vs Buyer's Claim of Paternity After Sale of a Slave Woman
Ruling on seller's and buyer's claim of paternity after sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving conflicting claims of paternity between a seller and buyer of a slave woman who gives birth, and explain why the seller's claim of istilad takes precedence.

Core Idea
The seller's claim of istilad trumps the buyer's claim of tahrir — conception in ownership outranks freeing.

When a sold slave woman gives birth, both seller and buyer might claim paternity. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi criteria for determining whose claim prevails, the types of claims (istilad vs tahrir), and the practical consequences for the sale and the child's lineage. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the conditions and proofs behind these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time frames
Note that the seller's claim is automatically valid if the child is born less than six months from the sale; if more than two years, it requires buyer confirmation.
These two clear-cut cases avoid prolonged disputes.
2
Distinguish the two types of claim
Understand the difference between da'wa al-istilad (based on conception in ownership) and da'wa al-tahrir (based on freeing). Istilad is stronger because it goes back to the time of conception.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
3
Apply the priority rule
When both claim, the seller's istilad claim takes precedence because it is based on conception during his ownership.
The buyer's claim is limited to the present.
4
Analyze the dispute with Zufar and al-Shafi'i
They argue the seller's claim contradicts the sale. Hanafis allow it because conception is a hidden matter (amr khafi).
This is an application of istihsan.
5
Determine the practical outcome
If seller's claim is valid, the sale is rescinded, the woman becomes umm walad, and the child belongs to the seller. If buyer's claim prevails, the sale stands and the child is the buyer's.
The sale of an umm walad is invalid.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary on a matn regarding the claim of lineage (da'wa al-nasab) when a sold slave woman gives birth. The passage type is a ruling with proof, as it states the legal rule and then provides reasoning and intra-madhhab differences. The author first clarifies the time conditions: if the child is born less than six months (half a year) from the sale, the seller's claim is valid; if more than two years, the seller's claim requires the buyer's confirmation. The buyer's claim alone is always valid as a claim of istilad (conception in his ownership). The commentary then addresses a potential objection about the wording 'since she was sold' and explains it applies only to a first sale. It further elaborates on cases where the seller and buyer dispute the time period, citing differences between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The passage defines two types of claim: da'wa al-istilad (claim based on conception in ownership) and da'wa al-tahrir (claim based on freeing). The former is given priority because it goes back to the time of conception. The commentary also covers joint ownership and the effect of the seller's claim: it rescinds the sale and returns the price because an umm walad cannot be sold. The disagreement with Zufar and al-Shafi'i is mentioned: they consider the seller's claim contradictory to the sale, but the Hanafi position is that the nature of conception is hidden, so contradiction is overlooked. The overall ruling demonstrates the Hanafi preference for certainty in lineage and the principle that the seller's claim of istilad overrides the buyer's claim of tahrir.

Key Terms

This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter on claims of lineage. It addresses a situation where a Muslim and a non-Muslim both claim paternity of a child. The Hanafi position, as presented here, is that the Muslim claimant is preferred over the non-Muslim. The reasoning is that it is obligatory to consider the child's best interest (نظر للصبي). The ruling is a benefaction to the child, and Allah does not blame the benefactor. The case of marriage is distinguished because its purpose is procreation, not just lineage.

Child's Lineage When Both Spouses Claim
Ruling on lineage when both spouses claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on child lineage when both spouses claim the child, and apply the conditions of firash and yad to determine attribution in sequential claims.

Core Idea
When both spouses claim a child, the default is both are parents; lineage cannot be split like property.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary on Al-Hidayah examines the case where a husband and wife both claim a child as theirs. The central ruling is that the child is considered the son of both due to the marital bed (firash) or joint possession (yad). However, the timing of the claims and the presence of an apparent marriage affect the outcome. We will walk through the ruling, its reasoning, and the nuanced scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Ruling
When both spouses claim the child simultaneously, the ruling is 'فهو ابنهما' (he is the son of both). This is the starting point.
2
Understand the Legal Basis
The child is attributed to both due to the apparent circumstances: either both have physical possession (yad) of the child, or the marital bed (firash) establishes the husband's paternity and the mother's maternity is evident.
Neither can invalidate the other's claim alone.
3
Analyze the Analogy with Joint Possession
The text analogizes to a garment in the hands of two men each claiming ownership. The garment goes to both, but unlike property, lineage cannot be partitioned. The child is not 'split'; each is a full parent.
Key distinction: lineage does not admit shares.
4
Examine Sequential Claims
If one spouse claims first that the child is from a third person (not the other spouse), the subsequent claim of the other spouse may or may not establish dual lineage, depending on the presence of an apparent marriage (nikah zahir).
Two sub-scenarios: husband first vs. wife first.
5
Apply the Ruling to Concrete Cases
Given a set of facts (who claimed what, when, and whether marriage is apparent), determine whether the child is attributed to both, only one, or neither.
Practice with the examples in the passage.

Settlement in a Marriage Claim: Impermissible
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sulh in marriage claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred Hanafi ruling on settlement in a claim of marriage and explain the reason behind its impermissibility, as well as describe the alternative minority view.

Core Idea
The husband cannot pay the wife to end a marriage claim.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: can a woman who claims marriage against a man settle that claim for money? The author, following al-Zahidi and the chosen view of Al-Wiqayah, holds it is impermissible. We will unpack the reasoning and the alternative view that some Hanafi scholars permit it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a settlement (sulh) where a man pays a woman who claims he is her husband, in exchange for her dropping the claim. The preferred ruling is that this settlement is not permissible (لم يجز).
This ruling comes from some copies of Mukhtasar al-Quduri and is chosen by the author of Al-Wiqayah.
2
Understand the two possible interpretations of the payment
The payment can be viewed in two ways: (1) as compensation for a separation (furqa) between them, or (2) as a mere payment to drop the claim without any actual separation.
Tag, this dichotomy drives the analysis.
3
Follow the reasoning for impermissibility
If the payment is for a separation: the husband does not receive any benefit from the separation, so he should not pay; it is the wife who benefits. If the payment is not for a separation: the claim remains, so the money is a bribe (rishwa) with no legitimate consideration. Hence the settlement is invalid.
The author calls it 'pure bribery without removing a dispute'.
4
Analyze the minority alternative view
Some Hanafi scholars (from other copies of Mukhtasar al-Quduri) permit it by treating the payment as an increase in mahr, followed by khul' on the original mahr. Then the settlement becomes a valid khul' divorce.
In this view, the man effectively increases the mahr and then divorces by khul', retaining the increase. The original mahr is forfeited.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under the preferred (impermissible) view, the woman must return the money and her claim remains; under the minority view, the marriage ends by khul' and she keeps the payment as an increased mahr.
The author chooses the first view as the relied-upon (muftā bihī).

His statement: 'And kinship of mahram'; meaning: kinship of mahram prevents revocation of the gift because of what we narrated from the hadith of Umar: 'Whoever gives a gift to a relative, he has no right to take it back...' the hadith. If one gives to a mahram without blood relation, like his foster brother, the mothers of women, stepdaughters, his brother who is a slave of a stranger, or to his brother's slave, he may revoke his gift according to Imam Abu Hanifa; because ownership does not fully vest in the relative, evidenced by the fact that the slave is more entitled to what is given to him if he needs it. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say: He does not revoke in the first case, and he revokes in the second.
If the slave and his master are both blood mahram relatives of the donor, then there is no revocation for the donor by agreement, according to the most correct view; because the gift, whichever of them it falls to, prevents revocation. This is transmitted by 'Al-Munh' from 'Al-Mabsut'.
Qadi Khan said in his Fatawa: If one gives to his brother and a stranger something, and they both take possession, he has the right to revoke. End. Apparently he means regarding the portion of the stranger, because blood relation prevents revocation by agreement.

Commentary
This is an expansion of the ruling that a mahram kinship relation prevents the donor from revoking a gift. The author provides two proofs: the hadith of Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) that a gift to a relative cannot be taken back, and rational reasoning that the purpose of such a gift is to maintain ties, and revocation would sever ties, which is prohibited. This ruling applies regardless of the donee's religion (Muslim, dhimmī, or musta'min).

Then the text addresses a nuance: if the donee is a mahram but not a blood relative (e.g., foster brother, mother of one's wife, stepdaughter), there is a disagreement between the imams. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the donor can revoke because the mahram relationship without blood does not fully establish ownership (the slave has a prior right to what he needs). The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) say no revocation for the first type (non-blood mahram), but yes for the second (when the mahram is a slave of a stranger or a brother's slave).

The text then clarifies that if both the slave and his master are blood mahram relatives, there is unanimous agreement that no revocation is allowed.

Finally, a fatwa from Qadi Khan is quoted: if a gift is given jointly to a brother and a stranger, the donor can revoke only the stranger's portion, because the brother's portion is protected by kinship.

Key Terms
قرابة المحرمية (qarābat al-mahramiyya): Kinship by reason of being mahram, i.e., a relationship that prohibits marriage, such as blood relatives and certain in-laws.
رجوع (rujūʿ): Revocation of a gift by the donor after the donee has taken possession.
ذو رحم (dhū raḥim): Blood relative, specifically a mahram relative.
