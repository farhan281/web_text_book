Dispute Over Work: Free or for Hire? Three Hanafi Views
Ruling on dispute over whether work was done for free or for wage, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on wage entitlement in a dispute between owner and craftsman and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
Custom and professional reputation can transform a volunteer into a wage-earner.

When an owner claims a craftsman worked for free but the craftsman demands a wage, whose word prevails? The default rule favours the owner, but the Hanafi school recognizes exceptions based on prior dealings and customary practice. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi disagreement among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, and shows why later fatwa adopts Muhammad's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
The dispute is about entitlement to wage (istihqaq al-ajr) when the owner denies any obligation. Determine what each party claims and the burden of proof.
The owner's denial is a defensive claim; the worker's claim is for entitlement.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its rationale
Abu Hanifa holds that the owner's word is taken because the apparent circumstance (zahir) of being a professional is only valid for defense, not to establish entitlement. Understand why he restricts the use of zahir.
His view preserves the default rule that one cannot be forced to pay without clear proof.
3
Explain Abu Yusuf's exception
If the craftsman is a regular associate (mu'amila) – meaning they have a history of paid work – then a wage is due. The prior dealing substitutes for explicit stipulation.
The custom of settling accounts indicates the work was for hire.
4
Explain Muhammad's exception
If the craftsman is known as a professional in that craft for a wage (e.g., runs a shop), the wage is obligatory and his word is taken. The apparent circumstance of opening a shop is equivalent to specifying a wage.
This view gives weight to public reputation and trade custom.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its practical consequence
Al-Zayla'i states that the fatwa is on Muhammad's view. In practice, a known professional who opens a shop is entitled to a wage and his word on the amount is accepted.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Rescission of Hire Due to Defect: Option, Not Automatic Void
Opening of chapter with ruling, proof, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on defects in hire contracts and apply the condition that only defects affecting benefit give the option to rescind.

Core Idea
A defect in the hired object gives the hirer an option to rescind—the contract does not automatically become void.

This lesson opens the chapter on rescission (fasḵẖ) of hire contracts. The commentator explains why the chapter appears late in the book and lays down the central principle: a defect that causes loss of benefit (fawt al-manfaʿa) gives the hirer a right to rescind, but the contract itself remains valid unless the hirer chooses to terminate. The proof is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) to the sale of defective goods before delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the ruling: A hire contract can be rescinded due to a defect that causes loss of benefit. Note that the contract does not become void automatically; instead, the hirer has an option (khiyār).
The text says 'hiya tufsakh biʿayb fawt al-nafʿ', and then clarifies that it is an option.
2
Understand the proof (qiyas)
Trace the analogy: In sale, a defect before delivery gives the buyer an option to return. In hire, the subject matter is benefits, which arise gradually. A defect occurring before the hirer takes possession of future benefits is analogous to a defect before delivery in sale, so the option is established.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) with the ʿilla being 'defect before delivery affecting the subject matter'.
3
List the conditions and preventers
The option exists only if the defect affects the benefit (not merely cosmetic). It is lost if: (1) the hirer uses the benefit after knowing the defect (consent), or (2) the lessor removes the defect before rescission.
The text explicitly mentions these conditions and preventers.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Some early scholars held the contract becomes void automatically. The majority (ʿāmmat al-mashāyikh) and the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) are that it gives an option. The commentator states the correct view is the first (option).
This ikhtilaf is within the Hanafi school; the tarjih is by later authorities like al-Zaylaʿī.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Consider a hired house where a wall collapses making a room unusable (affects benefit). The hirer may rescind for the remaining period or continue at full rent if he uses it. If only a cosmetic defect occurs (e.g., paint peels), no option arises.
This distinction is critical in practice.

Cancellation of Lease due to Defect Causing Loss of Benefit
Explanation of lessee's option to cancel lease due to defect affecting benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a defect in a leased property gives the lessee an option to cancel only if it causes a substantial loss of the contracted benefit, and apply this rule to different scenarios.

Core Idea
A lease is not automatically canceled by a defect—only a defect causing significant loss of benefit triggers an option to cancel.

In the chapter on cancellation of lease (faskh al-ijarah), the author explains the precise conditions under which a lessee can terminate the contract. The passage clarifies that the lease itself does not dissolve automatically upon a defect; rather, the lessee is given an option (khiyar) to cancel if the defect substantially impairs the benefit. This ruling is based on an analogy (qiyas) to the option in sale for defects occurring before delivery. We'll unpack the rule, the proof, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central rule
The passage states that a lease 'is cancelled due to a defect that causes loss of benefit.' Immediately clarify that this cancellation is not automatic—it is an option (khiyar) for the lessee. The majority view is that the lease is not rescinded by the defect itself; the lessee may choose to cancel or continue.
The intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: some early scholars held automatic rescission, but the relied-upon view is the option.
2
Examine the proof (dalil)
The proof is qiyas to sale: just as a defect in a sold item before delivery gives the buyer an option to return it, similarly, a defect in the leased property that affects future benefits gives the lessee an option. The 'illa (effective cause) is that the subject matter (benefit in ijarah, goods in sale) is impaired by a defect occurring before full delivery.
The analogy is explicit in the text: 'كما إذا حدث العيب في المبيع قبل القبض'.
3
Distinguish between defects that affect benefit and those that do not
The passage gives examples: a slave becoming ill or a house partially collapsing affect the benefit, so option exists. But a slave losing one eye (if hired for service) or a house losing a nonessential wall do not affect the benefit, so no option. The condition is that the defect must cause 'fawt al-manfa'ah' (loss of benefit).
This distinction is crucial: the contract is on the benefit, not the property itself.
4
Consider scenarios of partial loss or return of benefit
If the defect is later removed by the lessor, the option ceases. If the lessee uses the property after knowing the defect, he is deemed to consent and must pay the full rent. For a mill, if water supply is completely cut off, no rent is due for that period. If water decreases but not fully, only an excessive decrease (e.g., grinding less than half) gives the option.
The rule accommodates gradual benefit and partial restoration.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter on cancellation of lease (ijarah). The author explains that lease is cancelled due to a defect that causes loss of benefit, but this cancellation is not automatic; rather, it gives the lessee an option (khiyar) to cancel. The proof is an analogy (qiyas) to sale: just as a defect in a sold item before delivery gives the buyer an option to return it, similarly, a defect in the leased property affecting future benefits gives the lessee an option. However, if the lessee uses the property after discovering the defect, he is deemed to have accepted it and must pay the full rent. If the lessor removes the defect before cancellation, the option ceases. The author then clarifies that the lease itself does not automatically rescind upon the defect—this is the position of the majority of Hanafi scholars. The option can be exercised by the lessee alone, without a judge or the owner's presence. The passage then details what constitutes a 'defect that causes loss of benefit': it must actually impair the benefit (manfa'ah). Defects that do not affect the benefit, like a slave losing an eye (if hired for service) or a house losing a non-essential wall, do not give an option. Examples are given: destruction of a house, water supply cut off, or a mill wheel breaking. For a mill, if water is completely cut off, no rent is due for that period. The passage also notes that if both habitation and milling are contracted, rent for habitation remains due even if milling is impossible. The overall ruling is clear: the lessee has an option only when the defect substantially undermines the contracted benefit.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah on Sharh al-Wiqayah, dealing with defects in lease contracts (ijarah) and the hirer's right of rescission (khiyar al-faskh). The author explains the matn which lists examples of defects that give the hirer an option to rescind: demolition of the house, cutting off of the land's water supply, defect in the mill, or any defect that impairs the benefit. The commentary then elaborates on each.

First, 'demolition of the house' is simply defined as ruination. Second, 'cutting off of land's water' includes cases where the land is rain-fed; if rain stops, no rent is due even if the contract is not rescinded (al-ashahh). Third, for the mill, if the hirer does not rescind and water returns, the full rent is due, but rent is reduced proportionally for the interruption period. Imam Muhammad says that only an excessive decrease in water gives the right of rescission; a non-excessive decrease is tolerated because lease periods are not free from minor fluctuations. Al-Quduri defines excessive as grinding less than half of normal. Al-Tahtawi distinguishes decrease from complete interruption; in the latter, the hirer may rescind even for half grinding, but if he continues, full rent is due.

The passage then covers defects like illness of a hired slave and a gallsore on an animal's back. If the slave works less than before, the hirer has option to return; if unable to work at all, no rent is due. By analogy with the mill, working less than half triggers the option. For a gallsore, it impairs the animal's benefit.

A key intra-Hanafi dispute is whether such a defect automatically dissolves the contract (yanfasikh) or merely gives the hirer an option to rescind (lahu al-faskh). Some scholars like al-Quduri held that it dissolves automatically, drawing analogy to destruction of the subject matter before delivery. However, the preferred view (as chosen by al-Sarakhsi and others) is that it does not dissolve automatically because the benefit is not entirely lost; it can still be utilized in some way, and the defect may be remedied later (as in the example of a house collapsing and being rebuilt). The hirer's option falls if he benefits from the defective item or the lessor removes the defect before the hirer exercises his option.

In summary, the hirer has a khiyar (option) to rescind when a substantial defect impairs the contracted benefit. The contract remains valid until rescinded; if the hirer accepts the defect or the lessor fixes it, the option lapses.

Key Terms
فسخ (faskh): Rescission or annulment of a contract by one party due to a defect or deficiency in the subject matter.
خيار (khiyar): Option or right of choice given to the hirer to either continue or rescind the lease upon occurrence of a substantial defect.
نقصان فاحش (nuqsan fahish): Excessive deficiency – defined as grinding less than half of normal capacity, or similar reduction in benefit.
منفعة (manfa'ah): Benefit or utility that is the subject matter of a lease contract; its impairment triggers the option.

Rescission of Ijara: Defects, Stipulation, and Excuses
Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on rescission grounds in ijara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on rescission of lease by defect, stipulation, sight, and excuse, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A defect in a leased object gives an option to rescind, not automatic rescission.

When a leased item becomes defective, does the contract automatically end or does the lessee have a choice? The Hanafi school distinguishes between total loss of benefit and partial impairment. This lesson examines the grounds for rescission — defect, stipulation, sight, and excuse — and the reasoning behind the Hanafi view, including the key dispute with the Shafi'i school.

'Like the illness of a slave and the back sore of an animal. Then if he (the lessee) makes use of the defective thing or the lessor removes the defect, his option (khiyar) is forfeited.' [3] 'Like the illness of a slave and the back sore of an animal' – He said 'is rescinded' because the contract does not automatically rescind due to the possibility of benefit in some other way, but the lessee has a right to rescind. 'Then if he makes use of the defective thing or the lessor removes the defect, his option is forfeited' – i.e., the lessee's option. That is, the defect does not totally destroy the benefit, rather it impairs it such that one can still benefit in a general sense. So if the wall of the house collapses or the boy becomes one-eyed without impairing [use], the lease is not rescinded, as they have explicitly stated. From his statement 'like the illness of a slave': if one hires a slave for service and the slave becomes ill, if he can still work but less than before, the lessee has an option of return; if he does not return and the term expires, the rent is due. If he is completely unable to work, no rent is due. By analogy to the mill question, it should be said: if he works less than half his work, he has the option of return. This is mentioned in al-Fatawa al-Bazzaziyya. (2) His statement 'and the back sore of an animal' – with fath on the ba: a wound on the back or hoof of the animal, as mentioned by al-Tahawi quoting Ibn al-Athir. [3] His statement 'does not rescind' – Some say it rescinds because the subject matter, the specific usufructs, has been lost before taking possession, so it is like the destruction of a sold item before taking possession, or the death of a hired slave. This is what al-Quduri, the author of al-Tuhfa, and al-Aqta' chose, but non-rescission is more correct. Its evidence is what the commentator mentioned: 'due to the possibility of benefit in another way.' Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi and Shaykh al-Islam gave fatwa accordingly, and Hisham narrated from Muhammad: if one hires a house and it collapses, then the lessor rebuilds it, the lessee cannot refuse acceptance, nor can the lessor. End quote. This also is evidence that the contract did not rescind. (4) His statement 'due to possibility...' – its summary: The benefit is not totally lost because there is possibility of benefit in another way; moreover, the benefit has been lost in a manner that its return is conceivable, so it resembles the case of an absconding slave in sale. [0] His statement 'his option is forfeited' – because the cause obligating return has disappeared before rescission, and the contract renews moment by moment, so no option exists thereafter.

'And [rescission may occur] by stipulation (khiyar al-shart), by sight (khiyar al-ru'ya), and by excuse ('udhr).' [1] His statement 'and by...' – conjuncted to his statement 'by defect': i.e., the lease is rescinded by stipulation, by sight, and by excuse. Because stipulating a condition in a lease is valid in our school, and the first period of the lease is considered with it from the time the option falls. [2] His statement 'and by excuse' – it is not rescinded without it except when it is for consumption of a physical object, like contracting for copying (al-istiktāb), in which case the owner of the paper can rescind it without harm. Its origin is in sharecropping (al-muzāra‘a), where the owner of the seed can rescind but the worker cannot. This is in al-Ashbāh. [3] His statement 'And according to al-Shāfi‘ī...' – i.e., al-Shāfi‘ī said: The lease is not rescinded by option of stipulation, nor by option of sight, nor by excuses. Because stipulation is not valid in a lease in his view: if the option is for the lessee, he cannot return the subject matter in its entirety, since some usufruct is necessarily consumed during the option period; if for the lessor, he similarly cannot deliver completely because the usufruct of the past period cannot be delivered, and he cannot split the transaction before completion because it is not completed with the option of stipulation, unlike sale because in sale the entire object can be returned since the sold item is a physical thing that remains. The option of sight is also not valid in lease in his view because leasing something unseen is not permissible due to ignorance. And by excuses, he also does not rescind the lease except for defect, because usufructs are like physical objects in his view.

Our [Hanafi] argument: Regarding option of stipulation, the lease is a commutative contract where possession is not due in the session, and it admits rescission by mutual agreement (iqāla), so stipulating an option is permissible as in sale. The common ground is that both fulfill a need: since it is a commutative contract and transaction, it requires deliberation to avoid deception. And the loss of part of the subject matter in lease does not prevent return by option of defect by consensus, so likewise by option of stipulation, unlike [the case in] sale.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on rescission of lease (ijara) deals with the grounds for rescission. It begins by addressing defects that impair but do not destroy the benefit, such as a slave falling ill or an animal developing a back sore. The author stresses that the contract does not automatically rescind; rather, the lessee has an option to rescind. If the lessee uses the defective thing or the lessor remedies the defect, the option is forfeited because the cause for rescission no longer exists. The commentary then cites intra-madhhab differences: some earlier Hanafi scholars (like al-Quduri) held that the contract rescinds automatically when the benefit is lost, but the stronger position is non-rescission because some benefit remains, and the benefit may return (as in the case of rebuilding a collapsed house). This is supported by fatwa from al-Sarakhsi and others. Then the text introduces three other grounds for rescission: khiyar al-shart (option by stipulation), khiyar al-ru'ya (option of sight, i.e., when the object was unseen), and 'udhr (a valid excuse). These are valid grounds in the Hanafi school. The passage then presents a detailed disagreement with the Shafi'i school. The Shafi'i view denies rescission by stipulation, sight, or excuse, arguing that the lease contract does not allow such options because the subject matter (usufruct) is consumed over time and cannot be fully returned. The Hanafi response draws an analogy to sale (bay'), where these options are permitted: both are commutative contracts involving deliberation to avoid deception, and the partial loss of usufruct does not prevent return by option any more than it prevents return by defect (khiyar al-'aib), which is agreed upon.

Key Terms
خيار العيب (khiyār al-‘ayb): Option to rescind the lease contract due to a defect that impairs the benefit.
خيار الشرط (khiyār al-sharṭ): Option granted by stipulation in the contract to rescind within a specified period.
خيار الرؤية (khiyār al-ru’ya): Option to rescind upon seeing the leased object if it does not meet expectations.
العذر (al-‘udhr): A valid excuse that allows rescission of a lease contract.

This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether a lease (ijarah) contract can be rescinded on three grounds: option of condition (khiyar al-shart), option of sight (khiyar al-ru'yah), and excuses (a'dhar). The author first states the Hanafi position that these three grounds do permit rescission. He then presents the Shafi'i view, which rejects all three. Al-Shafi'i argues that an option condition (khiyar al-shart) is invalid in lease because the lessee cannot return the benefit already consumed during the option period, and the lessor cannot deliver the benefit that has passed. Option of sight is invalid due to ignorance (jahalah) of the object. Excuses do not allow rescission except for defect because benefits are treated like physical things. The author then provides the Hanafi proofs. For option of condition: lease is a commutative contract like sale, and the need for deliberation justifies options. Loss of some benefit does not prevent return, as is agreed in the case of defect. For option of sight: the ignorance here does not lead to dispute because the lessee can return the object immediately; the hadith 'Whoever buys something he has not seen has the option when he sees it' applies to lease as purchase of benefits. For excuses: an excuse (like inability to use the property due to circumstances) is analogous to a defect before delivery in sale. The author also mentions the view of Shuraih that lease is non-binding, and responds by comparing lease to the binding contract of salam.

خيار الشرط (khiyar al-shart): An option stipulated in the contract allowing either party to rescind within a specified period.
خيار الرؤية (khiyar al-ru'yah): The right to rescind upon seeing the object if purchased unseen.
عذر (udhr): An excuse that prevents continuation of the contract without undue hardship.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary (sharh) on al-Wiqaya, expanding on the concept of 'udhr (excuse) in the context of ijara (lease/hire) contracts. The author states that if an excuse arises which results in harm not consented to in the contract, the contract may be rescinded. The definition of the excuse (udhr) is: the occurrence of harm (damage or loss) to one of the contracting parties in his person or property that was not stipulated or consented to by the contract. The passage then mentions an internal Hanafi dispute: whether the contract is automatically rescinded (yanfasikh) or requires an act of rescission (yafsur). The majority of Hanafi mashayikh hold that it requires rescission, though there are two narrations. Two key Hanafi sources, al-Ziyadat and al-Jami' al-Saghir, differ on whether rescission requires a judge's decree or the lessor's consent. A reconciling view is that when the excuse is hidden (e.g., debt), judicial decree is needed to prove it; when the excuse is apparent (e.g., death), it does not. The passage provides four examples: (1) hiring a tooth puller when the pain stops; (2) hiring a cook for a wedding feast and the bride dies; (3) the lessor incurs a debt that can only be paid by selling the leased property; (4) the hirer travels when the contract was for service in the city or was absolute. In each case, continuing the contract would cause unconsented harm. The commentary elaborates on each example: for the tooth, the extracted tooth would be healthy; for the cook, the food would no longer be for the wedding; for the debt, the lessor will be imprisoned (harm of imprisonment); for travel, service in travel is harder than in the city. The author also notes that the term 'tooth' (dirs) is masculine and 'sin' is feminine, and that the restriction to these specific excuses implies that without such clear harm, there is no right of rescission. This passage is crucial for understanding the Hanafi position on excuses in contracts: rescission is a remedy for unforeseen harm, but the procedure (automatic or via rescission) depends on the nature of the excuse.

عذر (udhr): An unforeseen event that causes harm to one of the contracting parties in his person or property, not consented to by the contract, and which justifies rescission of the contract.
فسخ (faskh): The act of annulling or rescinding a contract, as opposed to its automatic termination (infisakh).
لزوم ضرر (luzum dharar): The occurrence of harm that is necessary (inevitable) if the contract remains in effect, and which was not stipulated or consented to in the contract.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary (sharh) on al-Wiqaya, expanding on the concept of 'udhr (excuse) in the context of ijara (lease/hire) contracts. The author states that if an excuse arises which results in harm not consented to in the contract, the contract may be rescinded. The definition of the excuse (udhr) is: the occurrence of harm (damage or loss) to one of the contracting parties in his person or property that was not stipulated or consented to by the contract. The passage then mentions an internal Hanafi dispute: whether the contract is automatically rescinded (yanfasikh) or requires an act of rescission (yafsur). Excuses for Rescinding an Ijarah Contract
Enumeration of specific excuses with rational justifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state five recognized excuses for rescinding an ijarah contract in the Hanafi school and explain the common principle of unanticipated harm.

Core Idea
A binding ijarah can be rescinded only when continuing it causes unanticipated harm not agreed upon.

Ijarah (lease or hire) is a binding contract, but the Hanafi school recognizes that unforeseen circumstances can make performance oppressive. This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah enumerates specific excuses—such as a tooth becoming pain-free, death of a bride, bankruptcy, or a hirer's change of travel plans—where rescission (faskh) is permitted to prevent harm. The underlying principle is that harm not contemplated at contract formation gives the affected party the right to exit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and the excuse
From the list, pick one scenario (e.g., hiring a cook for a wedding feast) and note the specific excuse (e.g., death of the bride).
Each excuse is a concrete example of an unforeseen event.
2
Determine the harm if contract continues
Analyze what loss or difficulty would occur if the contract were enforced. For the cook example, cooking non-wedding food wastes the hirer's money without benefit.
3
Verify the harm was unanticipated
Check whether this harm was implicitly or explicitly assumed in the contract. The parties did not agree to cook for a non-wedding; thus the harm is extra-contractual.
If the harm was foreseeable or accepted, rescission is not allowed.
4
Apply the ruling: permissible rescission
The Hanafi position holds that the affected party may rescind the contract to avoid the unagreed harm. This is a right, not an obligation.
Rescission is mubah (permissible) when the excuse is proven.
5
Differentiate intra-madhhab or cross-madhhab views
Note that Abu Hanifa and his students agree on these excuses. The Shafi'i school generally requires mutual consent or a breach of condition, not merely hardship.
This difference stems from how each school balances contract bindingness versus harm prevention.

Automatic Dissolution of Ijarah upon Death
Ruling with proof on death dissolution and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that Ijarah dissolves automatically upon the death of a party who contracted for himself, distinguish the cases of agents and co-lessees/co-lessors, and evaluate the inter-madhhab dispute with the three Imams.

Core Idea
Death dissolves Ijarah because the right passes to the heir who did not contract; the three Imams disagree, analogizing to sale.

The second part of the passage tackles death. The Hanafi school stands apart from the other three schools in holding that Ijarah dissolves upon death of either party if they contracted for themselves. The reasoning is that the benefit or hire passes to the heir, who cannot be bound by a contract they did not enter. This lesson examines the rule, its proof, and the exceptions for agents, guardians, and co-lessees.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling for self-contracting parties
The text says 'ينفسخ بموت أحد المتعاقدين عقدها لنفسه' – Ijarah dissolves automatically upon the death of a party who contracted for himself. Identify that this is automatic dissolution, not requiring a court order.
This is specific to the one who contracted for himself (li nafsihi).
2
Understand the Hanafi dalil (proof)
The proof is aqli: if the contract remained, the benefit or hire would become due to the heir who did not contract, which is impermissible. The text says: 'تصير المنفعة المملوكة أو الأجرة المملوكة لغير العاقد مستحقة بالعقد'.
This is a reason based on transfer of ownership (milkiyyah) to the heir.
3
Contrast with the three Imams' position
The opposing school (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) holds that Ijarah does not dissolve upon death, analogizing it to a sale (bay'). They argue that just as sale transfers ownership to the heir, Ijarah should transfer the right to benefit.
The Hanafi rebuttal is that benefits are consumed over time and cannot be inherited like a physical object.
4
Apply the exception: contract for another (agent, guardian)
If the party contracted as an agent, executor, or waqf administrator, death does not dissolve the Ijarah because the contract was always for the benefit of a third party. The text says 'فإن عقد الإجارة لغيره فلا تنفسخ'.
The reason is that the benefit was already for a non-contracting party, so the scenario that triggers dissolution (transfer to heir) does not occur.
5
Handle co-lessees/co-lessors and Zufar's dissent
If one of the co-lessees or co-lessors dies, the Ijarah becomes void only in his share, remaining valid for the other. Zufar argued that the entire contract becomes void due to shuyu' (joint ownership), but the majority holds that the condition of no shuyu' is only required at the start of the contract.
The analogy to witness testimony in marriage is used: conditions are considered at inception, not continuation.

Joint Lease Upon Death of One Party
Intra-Hanafi dispute on termination of joint lease upon one party's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one party in a joint lease dies, explain Zufar's opposing view, and apply the qiyas to testimony in marriage to justify the ruling.

Core Idea
In a joint lease, only the deceased's share is void — the condition of non-jointness is required only at inception, not continuously.

When a jointly owned property is leased and one of the lessors or lessees dies, does the contract collapse entirely? This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority and Imam Zufar, with a powerful analogy to marriage testimony that resolves the issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the lease is joint (ijara mushtaraka) — either multiple lessors or multiple lessees on a single undivided property.
Jointness here means undivided co-ownership (shuyu‘) of the leased object or the rental obligation.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The lease becomes void only in the share of the deceased party; it remains valid for the share of the surviving party.
This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
3
Understand Zufar's objection
Zufar argues that jointness (shuyu‘) is a preventer (mani‘) that invalidates the entire lease from the beginning, so death should void the whole contract.
Zufar views shuyu‘ as a defect that persists and cannot be cured.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof (qiyas)
The Hanafi response: absence of jointness is a condition (shart), like the presence of witnesses in marriage. Conditions are only required at the inception of the contract, not during its continuance.
Analogous: if witnesses die after marriage, the marriage remains valid.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under the Hanafi view, the lessee pays rent separately to the surviving party and to the deceased's heirs. Under Zufar's view, the lease must be renegotiated.
This practical difference affects ongoing contracts.

Liability for Fire on Leased Land: The Rule of Calm and Turbulent Winds
Ruling with proof on fire liability in Ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for fire spreading from crops on leased land and identify the condition (wind turbulence) that triggers liability.

Core Idea
Liability for fire spread depends on anticipation: calm winds = no liability, turbulent winds = liability.

In this passage, we examine a specific case of fire damage in the context of Ijarah (lease). The question is: if a lessee burns the harvested crops on his leased land, and the fire spreads to a neighbor's property, causing damage, is he liable? The Hanafi answer hinges on whether the winds were calm or turbulent, revealing a key principle about indirect causation (tasbib) and the role of anticipation of harm. We will walk through the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Default Ruling
The act is burning harvested crops on one's own leased or borrowed land. The default ruling, according to the matn, is that if the fire spreads to a neighbor, the burner is not liable. Note this as the baseline.
The default applies when the winds are calm, as the commentator later clarifies.
2
Introduce the Exception: Turbulent Winds
The commentator adds an important qualification: if the winds are turbulent (mudtaribah) on that day, the burner is liable. This changes the ruling from no liability to liability based on the circumstances.
The rationale is that the burner should have anticipated the spread on a windy day.
3
Understand the Legal Basis: Tasbib vs Mubasharah
The lack of liability in calm winds is justified by the concept of tasbib (indirect causation). For liability to arise from indirect causation, there must be transgression (ta'addi). Since the burner acted within his rights (burning his own crops), there is no transgression, so no liability. Contrast this with direct causation (mubasharah) like shooting an arrow.
This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi tort law.
4
Apply the Principle to the Turbulent Wind Case
When winds are turbulent, the burner is considered to have transgressed because he knowingly exposed others to risk. The anticipation of harm creates a duty to refrain, so the act becomes a transgression, triggering liability.
The act itself is lawful, but the context makes it prohibited (makruh tahrimi) due to potential harm.

Direct vs Indirect Causation: Tasbib, Mubasharah, and the Analogies
Analogy-based proof for the distinction between tasbib and mubasharah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct action (mubasharah) and indirect causation (tasbib) in Hanafi tort law, and apply the rule that direct action always incurs liability while indirect requires transgression.

Core Idea
Direct harm always triggers liability; indirect harm requires transgression to be liable.

This passage uses two powerful analogies to clarify the difference between direct and indirect causation: digging a well on one's own property (tasbib) and shooting an arrow (mubasharah). Understanding these analogies is key to grasping the Hanafi approach to tort liability. We will analyze how these analogies support the ruling on fire spread.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Analogies
The text compares burning crops (tasbib) to digging a well on your own land where someone dies, and contrasts it with shooting an arrow on your own land that hits someone (mubasharah). Recognize that both actions occur on one's own property, yet the rulings differ.
The well case: no liability (tasbib without transgression). The arrow case: liability (direct act).
2
Extract the Rule for Tasbib
For indirect causation (like digging a well or burning crops on one's own land), there is no liability unless there is transgression (ta'addi). Since the actor is acting within his right, there is no transgression, so no liability.
The key phrase is 'شرط الضمان التعدي'.
3
Extract the Rule for Mubasharah
For direct causation (like shooting an arrow that directly hits a person), liability exists even without transgression because the act itself is the direct cause of harm. There is no need to prove ta'addi.
The arrow case illustrates 'مباشر، فلا يشترط فيه التعدي'.
4

Burning crops is tasbib, not mubasharah, because the fire spreads indirectly via wind. Therefore, without transgression (calm wind), no liability. But if the burner knew the wind would carry the fire (turbulent wind), his action becomes transgression, and liability arises.
This shows how the same act can shift from no liability to liability based on context.
5
Understand the Common Thread: Ta'addi and Knowledge
Transgression in tasbib is established when the actor knows or should know that harm is likely. The anticipation of harm is the basis for liability. This is a nuanced approach balancing property rights and responsibility.
Contrast with strict liability views.

Special Cases: Fire on the Road and the Role of Intervening Causes
Expansion of the ruling with additional factual scenarios from al-Nihayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi criteria for liability in fire torts when the actor places fire in a public place (transgression) and when an intervening cause (wind) supersedes the initial act.

Core Idea
Transgression in act triggers liability even if harm is indirect; an intervening cause can sever liability.

The passage concludes with two additional scenarios from al-Nihayah: placing a live coal on the road (transgression) and wind lifting the coal (intervening cause). These cases refine our understanding of transgression and causation. We will examine how they fit into the broader framework of tasbib and ta'addi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the First Scenario: Placing Coal on the Road
If someone places a live coal on the public road (a transgression because the road is not his property) and it burns something, he is liable. Note that this is also indirect causation, but because the act itself is a transgression, liability attaches.
Transgression (ta'addi) exists here because he placed the coal where he had no right.
2
Identify the Second Scenario: Wind Lifts the Coal
If the wind lifts the coal from the road and it burns something elsewhere, he is not liable. Why? Because the wind is an intervening cause that supersedes his act; the harm is attributed to the wind, not to him.
The phrase 'الريح نسخت فعله' means the wind cancelled/annulled his action as the cause.
3
Apply the Principle of Intervening Causes
In Hanafi law, if a supervening natural element (like wind) directly causes the harm, the original actor is not liable even if his initial act was transgressive, because the causal connection is broken.
This is similar to modern concepts of novus actus interveniens.
4
Contrast with the Main Fire Case
In the main case, the wind does not break causation; rather, it is the means by which the fire spreads. The actor is liable when he knows the wind will carry the fire. In the coal case, the wind lifts the coal to a new location, breaking the chain.
The distinction is: in the main case, the fire spreads via wind as a foreseeable means; in the coal case, the wind moves the coal itself, an unpredictable movement.
5
Synthesize the Ta'addi Criteria
Transgression can arise from the place of the act (public road) or from the timing (windy day). In both, the actor knew or should have known of the risk. But if a new independent cause intervenes, the original act no longer counts as the legal cause.
Thus, liability is not absolute; it requires both causation and fault, with fault being negated by intervening causes.

Fire damage from hired land: no liability if winds calm; liability if winds disturbed.

Validity of a Work-Sharing Contract in Tailoring (Shirkat al-Sana'i')
Ruling on contract where a tailor/dyer engages someone to bring work and share profits half-half

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the validity of a tailor/dyer contract for work-sharing (half profit) and distinguish between the qiyas and istihsan arguments, and between shirkat al-wujuh and shirkat al-sana'i' classifications.

Core Idea
A contract to share work and profit half-half is valid by istihsan as a partnership of crafts, not an invalid hire.

This lesson examines a common guild practice: a tailor allows someone to bring customers in exchange for half the profit. While analogy (qiyas) would invalidate it due to unknown wages, Hanafi jurists uphold it by juristic preference (istihsan) as a partnership of crafts. We will trace the reasoning, the qiyas objection, and the internal debate over whether it is a partnership of credit or of crafts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and the initial ruling
Read the opening statement: 'If a tailor or dyer seats in his shop someone who presents work to him for half [the profit], it is valid.' Note that the ruling is 'sahih' (valid) by istihsan, not by qiyas.
The contract involves one party providing the shop and skill, the other bringing customers.
2
Analyze the qiyas argument for invalidity
The qiyas compares this to a miller's hire where payment is a measure of wheat (unknown). Since the earnings from the work are unknown at the time of contract, qiyas would render the contract invalid due to gharar (uncertainty) in the wage.
Al-Tahawi preferred this qiyas over istihsan.
3
Understand the istihsan argument for validity
Istihsan recharacterizes the contract as a partnership of crafts (shirkat al-sana'i'), where both parties contribute: one by accepting work (using his prestige) and the other by performing the work. The uncertainty in profit does not harm because the benefit is organized and custom supports it.
The commentary emphasizes that this is not a hire (ijarah) but a partnership; the work is shared.
4
Examine the classification dispute: shirkat al-wujuh vs shirkat al-sana'i'
Al-Hidayah classified this as shirkat al-wujuh (partnership of credit), but al-'Ayni and the commentator object because shirkat al-wujuh involves buying and selling, absent here. The correct classification is shirkat al-sana'i' (partnership of crafts).
The commentator notes that specifying one party for acceptance does not negate that both share work.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If classified as shirkat al-wujuh, the contract might be subject to different rules (e.g., requiring capital). As shirkat al-sana'i', it is a valid, customary partnership where each party's contribution is recognized.
The mufta bihi is validity by istihsan as shirkat al-sana'i'.

Hiring a Worker for Half the Output: The Hanafi Istihsan Exception
Ruling on hiring a worker for a share of the output

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a worker for a share of the output, explain the istihsan reasoning that overrides qiyas, and identify the conditions that make such a contract valid.

Core Idea
Hiring for an unknown share of output is valid by istihsan because custom and need justify the uncertainty.

In Hanafi fiqh, the default rule (qiyas) would invalidate a contract where the wage is unknown (jahalah). However, necessity and custom have led Hanafi jurists to permit certain arrangements, such as the miller's qafiz. This lesson examines a ruling from the Book of Hire where one person hires another for half of whatever work produces. We will see how istihsan allows this exception, and the reasoning based on partnership analogy and custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its apparent problem
The contract is a hire (ijarah) where the wage is a share of the output – an unknown amount at the time of contract. Normally, such uncertainty (jahalah) would invalidate the contract under qiyas.
The wage is 'half of what comes out of the work' – unknown.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis (istihsan)
Despite the uncertainty, the Hanafi school declares this contract valid by istihsan (juristic preference). Istihsan overrides qiyas here due to custom and public need.
Istihsan is a secondary source in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the reasoning: analogy to partnership of crafts
The commentators argue that specifying one party to accept work does not exclude the other. This mirrors a valid partnership of crafts (shirkat al-sana'i') where both may share work and profits, even if the exact shares are unknown.
Qafiz al-tahhan (miller's measure) is a parallel: paying a share of flour is customary.
4
Note the role of custom (urf) and need (haja)
Such contracts are widespread in trades and necessary for economic activity. Hanafi jurists consider ‘urf and haja as valid grounds for istihsan, making the ruling practical and flexible.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi’i) reject due to gharar (excessive uncertainty).
5
Recognize the mufta bihi status and modern mapping
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the contract is permissible (ja'iz). This ruling applies to modern profit-sharing arrangements, e.g., a barber sharing earnings with an assistant.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin (later fatwa literature).

Commentary on the validity of hiring a camel with an undefined howdah, contrasting Hanafi istihsan with Shafi'i qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi use of istihsan to validate a hire contract with ancillary uncertainty and distinguish it from the Shafi'i qiyas ruling.

Core Idea
A hire is valid despite unknown details when the main object is known and custom resolves the rest.

Contract law demands certainty to avoid disputes, but what about inevitable minor unknowns? In this lesson, we examine a classical dispute over hiring a camel with a howdah (litter) whose exact size and weight are unspecified. The Hanafi school, using istihsan (juristic preference), upholds the contract as valid, while the Shafi'i school rejects it as void due to gharar (uncertainty). Understanding this case reveals how Hanafi fiqh balances textual principles with practical custom.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the contract is valid (sahih) by istihsan. Note the reasoning: the rider is the primary object, and the howdah is subsidiary.
The Hanafi view relies on istihsan to override the strict qiyas that would prohibit the contract.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school holds the contract invalid because the howdah is unknown in length, width, and weight, which leads to gharar.
The Shafi'i use qiyas on the principle that uncertainty in the subject matter invalidates the contract.
3
Follow the Hanafi proof (istihsan)
The Hanafi proof uses an analogy to the company of artisans (shirkat al-sana'i'), where one partner accepts work and hires the other for an unknown share of output. That contract is valid by istihsan due to need. Similarly, the camel hire is needed for travel, and uncertainty is resolved by custom.
The proof combines analogy (qiyas) with a preference (istihsan) based on custom and necessity.
4
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Hanafi law, the contract is enforceable: the camel owner must provide customary equipment and stop for obligatory prayers. Under Shafi'i law, the contract is void and must be rescinded.
This shows the real-world impact of the ikhtilaf.
5
Understand the role of custom in removing gharar
The howdah's unspecified details are resolved by referring to customary norms. If the howdah is customary, the contract is valid; if not, it may become problematic.
Custom (urf) is a key tool in Hanafi ijarah to cure minor uncertainty.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the permissibility of hiring a camel to carry a howdah (mahmil) and riders. The author first draws an analogy to a company of artisans (shirkat al-sana'i') where one accepts work and hires the other for a share of the output, which is initially unknown but allowed by istihsan due to necessity. Similarly, hiring a camel with an undefined howdah is valid by istihsan. Al-Allamah Azmi Zadah clarifies that the reference to partnership of wujuh (shirkat al-wujuh) in al-Hidayah is not the technical definition but rather a partnership based on reputation. The commentator then details the obligations: the hirer must provide a customary howdah, while the camel owner must supply equipment like girths and halters; if any of these perish in the hirer's possession, he is not liable. The camel owner must assist with loading, driving, and making the camel kneel for women and the infirm. For prayer and purification, he must stop; for eating and supererogatory prayer, he need not stop since they can do it on the camel. The Shafi'i school opposes, holding the contract invalid due to uncertainty (gharar) in the howdah's dimensions. Hanafi scholars argue that the rider is the primary object, and his body is generally known; subsidiary uncertainty is removed by custom. The passage also addresses a related issue: if a person says to a house usurper 'vacate or I'll rent it to you monthly' and the usurper does not vacate, the rent is due. Finally, if a camel is hired to carry provisions and the hirer eats some, he must restore the equivalent; some Shafi'is disagree citing travel custom, but the Hanafi position relies on the contract's specification and shared custom.

Key Terms
شركة الصنائع (shirkat al-sana'i'): A partnership where one partner accepts work and the other performs it, sharing the profit (here half the output).
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference to deviate from strict analogy (qiyas) for a stronger reason, such as custom or necessity.
محمل (mahmil): A large litter carried by a camel for passengers, common for Hajj travel.
مكاري (mukari): The person who rents out the camel; the camel owner in a hire contract.

Validity of Ijarah When the Property is Usurped (Ghasb)
Rulings on ijarah with ghasb exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which an ijarah contract with a usurper is valid and identify when it is invalid due to denial of ownership.

Core Idea
Ijarah with a usurper is valid unless the usurper denies ownership, even if proof later appears.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: a person usurps property and then agrees to rent it. According to the Hanafi school, such a contract can be valid as ijarah, but there is a critical exception. The key is the consent of the usurper: if he acknowledges the owner's title and agrees to a wage, the contract stands. However, if he denies the owner's title, even if the owner later proves it, the contract is invalid because consent is lacking. This distinction protects property rights while allowing commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on ijarah with ghasb
The passage states that if a usurper (ghasib) uses a property and agrees to a wage, the contract is valid as ijarah. Recognize that the default ruling is validity.
2
Recognize the exception: denial of ownership
The ruling does not apply if the usurper denies the owner's title (juhud al-milk). Even if the owner later brings evidence, the denial invalidates the ijarah because the usurper's consent is not genuine.
The text says 'except if he denies his ownership' (illa idha jahada al-ghasib milkahu).
3
Analyze the effect of admission with denial of intent
If the usurper admits ownership but says 'I do not intend this wage' (la aqusid bihadha al-ajr), the ijarah also fails because he is not pleased (rida) with the contract.
The passage adds this as a second exception: 'or he admits ownership to him but says: I do not intend this wage.'

Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling from the commentary on al-Wiqayah. It addresses two main issues: first, the validity of contracts when the subject matter has been usurped (ghasb), and second, the validity of contracts when added (idafah) to a future time. Similarly, if the usurper admits ownership but states he does not intend the wage, there is no valid ijarah. The second part lists various contracts that are valid when added to a future time, such as ijarah, its rescission, muzara'ah (sharecropping), mu'amalah (musaqah), wakalah (agency), kafalah (suretyship), mudarabah (partnership), qada (judgeship), imarah (governorship), waqf (endowment), isa' (appointing an executor), wasiyyah (bequest), talaq (divorce), and itaq (manumission). Contracts Valid When Added to a Future Time (Idafah)
Rulings on validity of contracts added to future time with inter- and intra-madhhab disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that most contracts are valid when added to a future time and identify the inter-madhhab and intra-madhhab disagreements.

Core Idea
Contracts added to future time are valid because benefits arise gradually, unlike tangible goods.

This passage details the Hanafi position on contracts that begin at a specified future date. Unlike the Shafi'i school, which analogizes benefit to a physical object, the Hanafis allow ijarah, muzara'ah, wakalah, kafalah, mudarabah, and even waqf and divorce to take effect from a future time. The rationale is that these contracts involve benefits that come into existence over time, not a present tangible. The passage also highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding rescission of ijarah and dismissal of an agent in a forward agency.

Guided Path
1
List the contracts the passage declares valid when added to a future time
The passage explicitly mentions: ijarah (lease), faskh (rescission) of ijarah, muzara'ah (sharecropping), mu'amalah (musaqah), wakalah (agency), kafalah (suretyship), mudarabah (sleeping partnership), qada (judgeship), imarah (governorship), waqf (endowment), isa' (appointing executor), wasiyyah (bequest), talaq (divorce), and itaq (manumission).
Note that some of these are not strictly financial contracts, but the principle of idafah applies.
2
Understand the Hanafi rationale for validity
The passage says 'because its absolute form occurs as added, because its conclusion renews according to the occurrence of benefit' (li-anna itlaqaha yaqa'u mudafan li-anna in'iqadaha yatajaddadu bihasabi ma yahdathu min al-manfa'ah). In other words, benefits (manafi') are not present at the moment of contract; they arise over time, so it is permissible to specify a future start date.
This rationale distinguishes benefit from tangible property (ayn).
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
The Shafi'i school invalidates ijarah added to future because they analogize benefit to a tangible object (al-manfa'atu 'indahu ka'l-ayn). Since selling a non-existent future object is invalid, they say ijarah for future benefit is also invalid.
This is an inter-madhhab dispute; the Hanafi school is the majority view.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on two specific points
First, regarding rescission of ijarah (faskh) added to future: al-Sarakhsi held it valid, but the fatwa is against it (it is not valid). Second, regarding wakalah (agency) added to future: Abu Yusuf holds that the agent cannot be dismissed before the time; Muhammad holds he can be dismissed. The relied-upon view is that revocation is valid by consensus, as stated in Majma' al-Anhur.
These are internal differences among Hanafi scholars; the mufta bihi (relied-upon) view is specified.
5
Apply to examples
For practice, work through the examples given: ijarah from beginning of Ramadan, wakalah 'sell my slave tomorrow', kafalah 'whatever is due to you from so-and-so is on me' (valid as an original obligation), mudarabah after capital reaches twenty, etc.
The template for each: specify the future event clearly.
