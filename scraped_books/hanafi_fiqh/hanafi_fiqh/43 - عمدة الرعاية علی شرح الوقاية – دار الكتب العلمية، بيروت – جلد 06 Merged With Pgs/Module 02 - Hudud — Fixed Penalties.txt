Agency for Hudud and Qisas: The Absence Rule
Ruling on invalidity of agency for hadd and qisas when principal is absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for hudud and qisas in the principal's absence, explain the shubha reasoning, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's position from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Shubha (possibility of pardon/denial) blocks agency for hudud and qisas when the principal is absent.

In Hanafi law, agency (wakalah) is generally valid for giving and receiving rights. However, there is a critical exception: hudud and qisas cannot be executed by an agent if the principal is not present. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its rationale based on shubha, and the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar). Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your appreciation of how shubha operates in criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Rule
The passage first states a general rule: agency is valid for giving and receiving every right. This establishes the default permissibility.
The exception is phrased as 'except in taking possession of hadd and retaliation in the absence of the principal.'
2
Extract the Exception and Its Conditions
The exception has two conditions: (1) it concerns hadd or qisas, (2) the principal is absent from the court. If present, agency is valid by consensus.
The text explicitly notes that presence makes it valid by consensus (bil-ittifaq).
3
Analyse the Shubha Reasoning (Talil)
The invalidity is based on shubha: in qisas, the victim might pardon; in slander (qadhf), the accused might confirm; in theft, the claimant might claim property instead. These possibilities create uncertainty, so execution by agent is blocked.
Shubha prevents the punishment even if the crime is proven; it is a protective principle.
4
State the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds invalidity; the three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar) hold validity except for theft. They analogize to financial rights, which are delegable in absence.
The passage gives the opposing dalil: 'because it is a right of the servant...'
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: if a principal appoints an agent to execute qisas while absent, Abu Hanifa says agent cannot act; the three say agent can.
This affects how a mufti or qadi handles such cases.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi proof and contrasts it with the view of the three other schools.

Agency for Proving Hudud: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on agency for proving hadd and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and Abu Yusuf's positions on agency for proving hadd and qisas and explain the key difference in their reasoning.

Core Idea
Agency for proving hudud is valid because litigation is separate from execution.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a plaintiff can appoint an agent to prove a hadd or qisas claim in court. Imam Abu Hanifa permits it, while Imam Abu Yusuf forbids it. The disagreement hinges on the nature of the agent's role—whether it is litigation (which can be delegated) or a means to execution (which cannot). Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi imams reason from principles of agency and substitutability.

Guided Path
1
State the two Hanafi positions
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the validity of agency for proving (ithbat) hadd and qisas. Abu Hanifa says it is valid; Abu Yusuf says it is invalid.
Note that the disagreement is only when the principal is absent; all agree it is valid when present.
2
Follow Abu Yusuf's argument
Trace his reasoning: proof is a means to execution; agency for execution is invalid in absence, so agency for proof is also invalid. He analogizes to other substitutes (testimony upon testimony, etc.) not accepted in hudud.
Substitutes (abdal) are generally rejected in hudud due to the need for certainty.
3
Analyse Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa argues that agency covers litigation (khusumat), which is separate from execution. The agent only proves; the principal executes. Litigation is a distinct act that can be delegated like other rights.
This distinction between ithbat and istifa is crucial to the Hanafi position.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a plaintiff can appoint an agent to prove his claim in his absence; under Abu Yusuf, he cannot, and the agent's testimony would not be accepted.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as Muhammad al-Shaybani is said to agree with him.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that settlement (sulh) is not permissible for claims involving fixed punishments (hudud), such as those for adultery, theft, or drinking wine. The reason is that hudud are rights of Allah (huquq Allah), not the personal rights of the individual bringing the complaint. Therefore, a person cannot accept money to drop or not pursue a hadd case. The commentary gives examples: if someone catches an adulterer and accepts money not to take him to the judge, the settlement is invalid and the money must be returned. Similarly, if a victim of slander (qadhf) settles for money to pardon the slanderer, the settlement is void. An analogous case is a divorced woman claiming lineage of her child; she cannot settle for money because lineage is the child's right, not hers. Thus, any compromise involving a right of Allah or a third party's right is invalid.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment determined by Islamic law for certain crimes (adultery, theft, drinking alcohol, slander).
حق الله (haqq Allah): A right that belongs to Allah, meaning a public interest, not subject to individual discretion.

Invalidity of Sulh for Hadd Claims and Ma'dhun's Self-Settlement
Explains invalidity of sulh for hadd and ma'dhun self-settlement with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two cases where sulh is invalid according to the Hanafi school and explain the underlying legal principle for each.

Core Idea
Sulh cannot compromise rights of Allah or another's property — hudud belong to Allah, and a slave's person belongs to the master.

In this passage, the author explains two critical limitations on the validity of sulh (amicable settlement). While sulh is generally encouraged to resolve disputes, it is not permissible when it involves a claim of hadd (fixed punishment) because hudud are rights of Allah that cannot be waived or exchanged for money. Additionally, a ma'dhun (authorized slave) cannot make sulh regarding his own person because his trading permission does not extend to matters concerning his person, which is owned by his master. Let us examine the legal reasoning behind these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Read the text and list the two scenarios where sulh is declared invalid. One involves a claim of hadd, and the other involves a ma'dhun slave making sulh about his own person.
Both are explicit in the passage: 'لا يجوز الصلح عن دعوى حد' and 'إذا قتل مأذون آخر عمداً وصالح عن نفسه لا يجوز'.
2
Analyze the first case: sulh for hadd claim
State the ruling (sulh is batil) and the proof: hadd is a right of Allah, not a personal right, so it cannot be subject to compromise. The commentary gives examples of theft, adultery, and slander.
Key principle: حقوق الله لا تدخل تحت الصلح (rights of Allah cannot be entered into sulh).
3
Analyze the second case: sulh by ma'dhun for his person
State the ruling (sulh is invalid) and the proof: a ma'dhun's permission to trade does not include his own person (رقبته), which is owned by the master. Therefore, he cannot transact regarding it.
Contrast with sulh for trade goods, which would be valid.
4
Apply the underlying principle via analogy
The text analogizes with a divorced woman claiming lineage: she cannot take compensation to waive the child's right to lineage. Similarly, one cannot compromise a right belonging to another (Allah or the master).
This reinforces that only the true owner of a right can waive it.
5
Conclude the practical effect
In both cases, the sulh is batil (void from the outset). Any money paid under such sulh must be returned to the payer. The hadd punishment remains enforceable.
The ruling protects the inviolability of divine commands and ownership rights.

Commentary
This passage states two cases where sulh is invalid. First, sulh cannot be made for claims of hadd (fixed punishments) because hudud are rights of Allah; they cannot be waived or exchanged for money. The commentary gives examples: if a person catches a thief and accepts money not to report him, the sulh is invalid and the money must be returned. The commentary draws an analogy with lineage: a wife cannot sell the child's lineage because it is the child's right, not hers.
