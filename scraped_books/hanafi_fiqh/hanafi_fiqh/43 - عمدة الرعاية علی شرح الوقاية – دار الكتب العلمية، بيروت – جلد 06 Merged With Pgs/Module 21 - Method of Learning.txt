The Book of Gifts: Definition, Proofs, and Ruling
Opens the Book of Gifts with linguistic definition and legal proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hibah, identify its evidence from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma, and explain the ruling that giving is mandub and acceptance is sunnah.

Core Idea
Giving a gift is recommended, accepting is sunnah — a contract transferring ownership without compensation.

The Book of Gifts is placed after the Book of Loans because gift is compound (object+benefit) while loan is simple (benefit only). The author provides the linguistic roots of 'hiba', the legal definition of tamlik al-'ayn, and then establishes its legitimacy through Quranic verses, prophetic hadiths, and scholarly consensus.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the placement and linguistic meaning
Understand why the Book of Gifts follows the Book of Loans: gift transfers ownership of the object, loan transfers only benefit. Note the linguistic derivation of 'hiba' from 'wahaba' meaning to give without compensation.
Linguistic debate: some scholars object to 'wahaba' taking two objects; jurists justify it by analogy with 'a'ta'.
2
Understand the legal definition
The author defines hibah as 'tamlik al-'ayn' — transfer of ownership of a physical object. This excludes loans (benefit only) and sales (with compensation).
The definition is taken from the matn (primary text) being commented on.
3
Examine the evidence (dalil)
Identify the four proofs: (1) Quran 4:4 permitting acceptance of a gifted portion of dower, (2) Hadith of the Prophet accepting a gift of a foreleg/trotter, (3) Hadith on exchanging gifts to foster love, (4) Scholarly consensus (ijma).
The verse is from Surah Nisa; the hadiths are from Bukhari and Muwatta Malik.
4
Determine the ruling
Based on the evidence, giving is mandub (recommended) and acceptance is sunnah. The text states explicitly: 'فهي مندوبة' and 'وقبولها سنة'.
Exception: acceptance is not sunnah if the gift is haram or involves reproach (minna).
5
Appreciate the virtue and cause
Gift-giving is an attribute of perfection; Allah describes Himself as 'al-Wahhab'. Its cause is the desire for good—either worldly (love, praise) or otherworldly (reward).
The passage quotes Quran 38:9 and 59:9 to emphasize generosity.

Commentary
This passage is the opening commentary on the Book of Gifts. The author first explains the appropriate placement of this chapter after the previous one on loans (ariyah): that was a transfer of benefit without compensation, while gift is a transfer of ownership of the object without compensation; the loan is simple (only benefit), while gift is compound (object and its benefit). Then he provides a detailed linguistic analysis of the term 'hiba', citing multiple lexicons and grammarians, including the debate on whether the verb 'wahaba' can take two objects directly. The fuqaha's usage is defended by treating it as semantically equivalent to 'a'ta' (to give). The legal (shar'i) definition is then introduced: what the author (of the matn) stated, i.e., 'transfer of ownership (tamlik) of an object.' He then establishes the legitimacy (mashru'iyya) of the gift contract through four proofs: 1) The Quranic verse from Surah Nisa (4:4) which permits eating a gift of dower willingly given. 2) The hadith from Bukhari where the Prophet said he would accept an invitation and a gift of a foreleg or trotter. 3) The hadith from the Muwatta encouraging shaking hands and exchanging gifts to remove hatred. 4) Consensus (ijma') of the scholars. The passage further extols the virtue of giving gifts as an attribute of perfection, as Allah describes Himself as 'al-Wahhab' (the Bestower). It concludes that giving gifts is recommended (mandub) and accepting is sunnah, unless there is an impediment like the gift being from unlawful sources or intended to reproach. The motive for giving is either worldly (love, praise) or otherworldly (reward).

Key Terms
الهبة (al-hibah): In language: giving something beneficial to another without compensation. In Sharia: a contract transferring ownership of an object (tamlik al-'ayn) without compensation.
تمليك العين (tamlik al-'ayn): Transfer of ownership of a physical object (as opposed to usufruct).
العارية (al-'ariyah): Loan of a commodity for use without compensation; mentioned in the preceding chapter.
