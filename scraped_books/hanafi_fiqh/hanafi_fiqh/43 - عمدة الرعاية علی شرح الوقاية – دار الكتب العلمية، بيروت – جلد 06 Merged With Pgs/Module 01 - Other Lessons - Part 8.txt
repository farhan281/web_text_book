Gift with Invalid Conditions or Exceptions
Explains rulings on gifts with invalid exceptions and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifts made with void conditions or exceptions and explain why the gift remains valid while the condition is nullified.

Core Idea
A gift with a void condition remains valid; the condition is nullified automatically.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains an important Hanafi principle: when a gift is accompanied by an invalid exception or condition that contradicts the contract's nature, the gift itself does not become void. Instead, only the offending condition is invalidated. This ruling is derived from the hadith on 'umra (life grants) and applies to various scenarios, such as gifting a slave woman while excluding her pregnancy, or requiring the donee to return the item. Understanding this rule clarifies how Hanafi law protects the validity of transfers of ownership while disregarding non-compliant stipulations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
First, recognize that the act under discussion is a gift (hiba) with an attached exception or condition. The ruling given is that the gift is valid (sahih), while the exception or condition is void (bāṭil).
2
Analyze the scenarios
The passage lists several examples: gifting a slave woman except her pregnancy, or with conditions of return, emancipation, or becoming umm walad; and gifting a house with a condition of returning part or compensating. In each case, the gift is valid.
The exception of pregnancy is treated as a void condition because the unborn child is not independently ownable.
3
Understand the rationale
The reasoning is twofold: (a) For exceptions, the excepted item (pregnancy) cannot be a separate subject of gift, so the exception becomes a void condition. (b) For other conditions, they contradict the contract's default requirement (absolute ownership) and are thus void. The gift remains valid because ownership is suspended on a physical act (qabd), which is not invalidated by a void condition.
The condition is fāsid (void) but does not affect the underlying contract in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Locate the proof
The author cites the hadith of Jabir (may Allah be pleased with him) in which the Prophet ﷺ ruled that a life grant ('umra) belongs to the donee and his heirs, nullifying the donor's condition of return. This serves as a dalīl (evidence) that conditions contrary to the contract are void while the gift stands.
The hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud and al-Nasa'i, and is a Sunnah ahad with zanni thubut but qat'i dalāla according to Hanafi usul.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may hold that a gift with a void condition is invalid altogether. The Hanafi position is distinct: it preserves the gift and nullifies only the condition, as evident from the hadith.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilāf.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya on the topic of gifts (hiba) with conditions or exceptions. The author explains that if someone gives a gift with an invalid exception (like excluding the unborn child of a slave woman) or with a condition that contradicts the contract's requirement (like return, emancipation, becoming umm walad), the gift itself remains valid, but the exception or condition is nullified. The reasoning is twofold: (1) For exceptions, the excluded item (e.g., pregnancy) cannot be a separate subject of a gift because it is not known property; therefore, the exception transforms into a void condition. (2) For other conditions, they conflict with the contract's default of absolute ownership without restrictions. The Hanafi principle is that void conditions do not invalidate the gift because ownership is contingent on the physical act of delivery (qabd), and physical acts are not invalidated by such conditions. The author supports this with the hadith of 'umra (life grant), where the Prophet declared that the grant stands and the condition of return is void, establishing the analogy for other conditions.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna'): An exception made in a gift contract, e.g., excluding the pregnancy from the gift of a slave woman.
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A condition that contradicts the requirement of the contract, such as requiring the return of the gifted item.
عمرى (umra): A life grant in which the donor gives property to another for life, with a condition that it returns to the donor or his heirs upon the donee's death.

Gift with Exception or Condition: Ruling and Reasoning
Commentary on gift validity with exceptions/conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifts with invalid exceptions/conditions and explain why the gift remains valid.

Core Idea
A gift with a void condition remains valid because it is a gratuitous contract—invalid conditions nullify only commutative contracts.

When you gift something but add a condition—like 'I gift you this house but you must return part of it'—what happens? The Hanafi school teaches that the gift itself is valid, but the condition is void. This passage from the commentary on Al-Hidayah explains the reasoning behind this rule, supported by hadith and legal analogy. You will learn the distinction between gratuitous and commutative contracts and when invalid conditions actually nullify a contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being discussed? Gifting with an exception (e.g., 'except her pregnancy') or a condition (e.g., 'on condition that you return it'). The ruling is that the gift is valid but the exception/condition is void.
The act is a gift (hibah), which is a gratuitous contract.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does the author cite? The hadith on 'umra (life grant) narrated by Jabir: 'Whoever gives a life grant, it belongs to him and his heirs.' This shows that conditions contrary to the nature of a gift are void, but the gift itself stands.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud and al-Nasa'i.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The condition in 'umra (that the property returns after the donee's death) contradicts absolute ownership, so it is void. Similarly, any condition that restricts absolute ownership in a gift is void.
The analogy is drawn from the invalid condition in 'umra.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Who disagrees? Ahmad and Abu Thawr hold that the exception of pregnancy is valid and the gift without the pregnancy is valid. Their proof: analogy to freeing a slave where separate children can be excepted.
The Hanafi response distinguishes pregnancy from a separate child.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Why is the Hanafi position preferred? Pregnancy is like a part (limb) of the mother, so excluding it is invalid. Also, freeing is not a transfer of ownership, so the analogy fails. The gift remains valid and includes the pregnancy.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya on the Hidayah's chapter on gifts (hibah). The matn states: 'Whoever gifts a slave woman except her pregnancy, or on condition that the donee returns her, frees her, makes her an umm walad, or gifts a house as charity on condition of return of part or compensation, the gift is valid but the exception/condition is void.' The commentator explains the reasoning: For exception of pregnancy, because an exception only works when the excepted thing can be contracted separately, but pregnancy is not property and is part of the slave; thus it becomes a void condition. For other conditions, they contradict the nature of gift (absolute ownership). The gift remains valid because it is a gratuitous contract (tabarru'), and void conditions do not nullify such contracts, unlike commutative contracts (like sale). A hadith on 'umra (life grant) supports that conditions contrary to the nature of the contract are void. The commentator also addresses the views of Ahmad and Abu Thawr, who say the gift without the pregnancy is valid, and refutes them by analogy: the pregnancy is like a limb. Finally, the passage divides dispositions involving pregnancy into three categories: those where the exception is void (like gift, marriage), those where the whole contract is void (sale, lease), and those where exception is valid (bequest).

الهبة (al-hibah): A gift; a gratuitous transfer of ownership from one person to another without compensation.

Rulings on Invalid Conditions in Gifts (Hiba)
Rulings on gifts with invalid conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifts with invalid conditions and explain why the gift remains valid while the condition is void, distinguishing between tabarru'at and mu'awadat.

Core Idea
A corrupt condition does not invalidate a gift (tabarru') but is itself void; the gift stands free of it.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary addresses a common scenario: a donor adds a condition to a gift that is not in accordance with Islamic contract law. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between gratuitous contracts (tabarru'at) and exchange contracts (mu'awadat). In gifts, the corrupt condition does not nullify the gift; rather, the condition is void and the gift is valid. The commentator explores several applications, including the famous case of excepting the unborn child of a gifted slave woman, and provides a three-part categorization of contracts in relation to corrupt conditions.

What is the distinction between 'return' (رد) and 'compensation' (عوض) in the conditions, and why does it matter? The wording determines whether the condition is void or possibly valid.
If the donor stipulates compensation, the condition is void but the gift remains valid.

The muftā bihī position is that conditional ibrā’ is invalid.

Commentary
This passage begins the section on Ijarah (leasing/hiring) by examining the linguistic derivation and correct Arabic usage of the term. The author, commenting on a base text, quotes multiple Arabic lexicographers to settle the proper form and meaning. The key point of dispute is whether the verbal noun is إجارة (ijarah) or إيجار (ijar), and whether the active participle is مؤاجر (mu'ajir, from the pattern fā'ila) or مؤجر (mu'jir, from the pattern af'ala). The preferred Hanafi position, supported by early commentators like Qadi Zadah and later by al-Shami, is that the correct term for the contract is إيجار (ijar) because it is the sale of benefits (bay' al-manafi'), while إجارة (ijarah) properly refers to the wage itself (al-ujrah). The form إجارة as a verbal noun is not attested in classical usage. The quotation from al-Misbah al-Munir decisively states that 'ājartu' (with alif) yields the active participle mu'jir, not mu'ajir; the latter is a mistake used only in vulgar speech. The passage also distinguishes between the agent who hires out (mu'jir) and the one who receives wages (ajir). It further explains the transitive verb patterns: the verb from af'ala takes one object (I hired out the house), while some tribes use the fā'ala pattern with two objects (I hired out to Zayd the house). The ultimate relied-upon view is that إيجار is the technical term for the contract, and the correct active participle is مؤجر.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijarah): The term for the wage or the contract; here distinguished as the wage (al-ujrah) rather than the contract.
الإيجار (al-ijar): The verbal noun meaning the contract of hiring out, considered the correct term in Hanafi fiqh.
المؤجر (al-mu'jir): The one who hires out (the lessor) from the root آجر.
المؤاجر (al-mu'ajir): The active participle from فاعل, considered an error by Hanafi scholars.
الأجر (al-ajr): Compensation for service, synonymous with wages.

Linguistic Foundations of Ijarah: Correct Arabic Verb Forms
Lexical analysis of verb آجر and its derivatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the correct Arabic verb آجر (aajara) for renting property from incorrect forms, and state the active participle مؤجر (mu'jir).

Core Idea
The correct Arabic for 'rent out' is آجر, not مواجر; its active participle is مؤجر, not مؤاجر.

This lesson clarifies a common linguistic error in the terminology of ijarah (rental contracts). Understanding the precise verb forms ensures correct expression in fiqh discussions and prevents ambiguity in legal documents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Root and Verb Form
Recognize that the verb for 'rent out' is آجر (aajara) on the pattern أفعل, not فاعل. The active participle must follow the pattern مُفْعِل (mu'jir).
The footnotes explain that the error stems from substituting the hamza with waw, yielding مواجر, which is incorrect.
2
Distinguish Between آجر and أجر
Note that آجر (with alif madd) is used for renting property or animals, while أجر (without madd) means 'to hire a person'. This distinction is important in Hanafi fiqh.
The source explicitly states the difference between mu'jir (one who rents out) and ajir (one who is hired).
3
Analyze Transitivity
Observe that آجر is transitive to two objects: one can say 'ajartu Zaydan al-dar' (I rented Zayd the house) or invert the objects. This parallels the verb أعطى (to give).
Understanding the two-object construction helps in drafting clear rental contracts.
4
Examine the Noun الأجرة
Study the noun ujrah (rent) and its plural forms (ujar, ujur). Note that it is used as a verbal noun (masdar) for ijarah.
The plural patterns are similar to ghurfa/ghuruf and fals/fulus.

Acts That Can and Cannot Be Appended to Future Time
List of contracts and legal acts with ruling on their validity when made muḍāf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any contract or legal act as valid or invalid for iḍāfah (appending to a future time) according to the Hanafi school and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Appending to future time is valid only for acts that cannot be fully executed immediately.

In Hanafi law, not every contract or legal declaration can be postponed to a future time. The guiding principle is whether the act can be executed at the present moment. If it can, there is no need to delay it; if it cannot (like lease, which requires time to pass, or bequest, which only takes effect after death), postponement is valid. This lesson surveys the lists of valid and invalid muḍāf acts and the reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that the passage separates acts into those that are valid when made muḍāf and those that are not.
2
List the valid acts
From the text: lease, its cancellation, sharecropping, agency, suretyship, mudarabah, appointment of judge, appointment as governor, appointment of executor, bequest, divorce, emancipation, and endowment.
Also included: appointment of an executor (īṣā') and others from the context.
3
List the invalid acts
From the text: sale, its ratification, its cancellation, division, partnership, gift, marriage, return (to wife), settlement for property, and remission of debt.
These are immediate transfers of ownership (tamlik).
4
Understand the rationale
Valid acts involve periods of time, are inherently future (bequest), or are matters of authority/undertaking. Invalid acts are immediate transfers (tamlik) that can be executed now.
The footnote in the commentary explicitly states this reasoning.
5
Note the agency exception
Agency is valid when muḍāf, but there is a dispute on revocation before the time; that is treated in the next lesson.

Revocation of an Appended Agency: Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute in agency revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding revocation of a muḍāf agency, explain their proofs, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
An appended agency can be revoked before its term, but only if the agent knows.

When an agency (wakala) is appended to a future time (e.g., 'Sell my slave tomorrow'), can the principal revoke it before that time? The two disciples of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ. The later Hanafi authorities, as recorded in Majma' al-Anhur, resolve this by comparing the agency to other revocable acts like divorce and emancipation. This lesson unpacks that debate and the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
A principal says to an agent, 'You are my agent to sell my slave tomorrow.' Then, before tomorrow, the principal revokes the agency.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that the revocation is ineffective because the agency is considered effective from the moment of appointment.
3
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad holds that the revocation is effective because agency is revocable like other acts (divorce, emancipation).
4
Analyze the tarjih
Majma' al-Anhur supports Muhammad, citing consensus and analogy to divorce, emancipation, and endowment.
Condition: the agent must be informed.
5
Conclude with mufta bihi
The operative position is that revocation is valid with the condition that the agent is informed.
