Conditions for Sub-Agent and Joint Agency in Talaq/Itq
Conditions for sub-agency and joint agency in talaq/itq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid appointment of a sub-agent and derive the ruling when two agents are appointed for talaq or itq, distinguishing joint delegation from individual authority.

Core Idea
A sub-agent requires principal's permission; two agents in talaq/itq must act jointly unless individually authorized.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies two related agency issues: when an agent may appoint a sub-agent, and how two agents must act when delegated for divorce or manumission. The key principle is that matters requiring opinion (ra'y) demand joint deliberation, while routine tasks may allow individual action. Understanding these rules prevents invalid divorces and ensures the principal's intent is fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on sub-agency
Examine the text's statement: sub-agent is valid only with principal's permission or a directive to act by opinion. Note the consequences: with permission, sub-agent is agent of principal; without permission, action is suspended on ratification.
The sub-agent is not affected by the first agent's death or removal, but both end with principal's death.
2
Analyze the ruling for two agents in talaq/itq
Focus on the phrases 'طلقاها إن شئتما' (delegation) and 'طلقاها جميعا' (jointly). Determine when joint action is required vs. when individual action is permitted.
The word 'جميعا' mandates conjoint action; each agent cannot act alone.
3
Apply to scenarios with specified vs. unspecified objects
If the divorce or manumission is for a specific wife/slave, joint action is needed because it involves opinion (ra'y). For unspecified cases (e.g., one divorce not named), joint action is also required.
The text cites al-Siraj al-Wahhaj for this distinction.
4
Understand the underlying proof (talil)
The reasoning (qiyas) is based on preventing chaos (shaghab) in litigation and the need for joint deliberation in matters requiring opinion. General agency allows separate acts, but talaq/itq and litigation are exceptions.
The dalil is zanni in transmission and meaning, relying on analogy.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of al-Wiqayah regarding agency. It begins by explaining why two agents cannot be appointed together in litigation: joint action leads to chaos (shaghab). However, in other matters like sale, two agents may act if the principal delegates authority. The core ruling is about appointing a sub-agent: a sub-agent is valid only with the principal's permission, either explicitly or by saying 'act according to your opinion.' If permitted, the sub-agent is directly agent of the original principal, so he is not affected by the death or removal of the first agent, but both are terminated by the principal's death. Without permission, the sub-agent's actions are conditional on the principal's ratification. The passage then addresses two agents in talaq and itq: if the husband says 'Divorce her if you both wish,' it is a delegation requiring joint action. If he says 'Divorce her jointly' (jami'an), no single agent can act alone. If one gives one divorce and the other two, no divorce occurs because the specified number must come from each jointly. The same applies to manumission. The commentary adds a nuance: when the talaq or itq is for a spesific slave/wife or for compensation (badal), joint action is required because it involves opinion; for unspecified cases, joint action is also needed.

توكيل الوكيل (tawkeel al-wakeel): Appointing a sub-agent by the original agent.
تفويضا (tafwid): Delegation of authority to two agents to act jointly in talaq.
شغب (shaghab): Chaos, turmoil, or disturbance resulting from joint action in litigation.

Core Idea
In a claim of fay' before the ila' period ends, the claimant's statement establishes the return.

This lesson addresses a specific procedural ruling in family law: when a third party claims that a husband returned to his wife during the period of ila', and the husband denies it. The timing of the dispute determines whose word is accepted. Understanding this rule helps clarify how the Hanafi school handles evidentiary burdens in marital vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is fay' (return) during ila'. The dispute is between a claimant who asserts the husband returned and the husband who denies.
2
Note the timing condition
The ruling applies when the dispute arises before the ila' period ends.
3
State the ruling
The fay' is established by the claimant's statement alone.

Exception of Attached Parts in Acknowledgment
Ruling on invalidity of exception for subordinate parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on excepting subordinate parts in an acknowledgment and distinguish between valid and invalid exceptions.

Core Idea
A subordinate part cannot be excepted in acknowledgment; the whole remains with the claimant.

In Hanafi law, an acknowledgment (iqrar) with an exception is only valid if the excepted part is independent. This lesson examines the ruling when a person attempts to exclude a part that is customarily inseparable—like a seal from a ring or a tree from a garden. Through the examples provided, we will see that such exceptions are invalid, but a separate ownership claim can achieve the intended effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the exception
Consider the case of an acknowledgment with an exception, e.g., 'This ring is for you except its seal.' The act is iqrar with istithna.
2
Determine if the excepted part is subordinate
Assess whether the excepted part is customarily an integral part of the whole. The seal is not an independent item; it is subordinate (tab'i) to the ring.
3
Apply the ruling
According to the Hanafi school, if the part is subordinate, the exception is invalid (la yasihh). The entire ring is considered acknowledged.
4
Contrast with a valid alternative
If the person claims separate ownership (e.g., 'The ring is for him, the seal is for me'), the statement is valid because it is not an exception but a separate claim.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The principle of subordination (tab'iyya) means that subordinate parts are automatically included in the acknowledgment of the whole, so they cannot be excepted.

Commentary

Defining Death-Illness (Marad al-Mawt) in the Hanafi School
Defining death-illness and its conditions for valid confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of death-illness, identify its key conditions, and explain why the criterion of overwhelming probability (ghalabah) is relied upon.

Core Idea
Death-illness is legally defined not by bed rest but by overwhelming probability of death, preventing the patient from managing affairs.

In the chapter on the sick person's confession (iqrār al-marīḍ), the Hanafi jurists first distinguish between debts incurred in health and those in illness. The central concept is 'death-illness' (marad al-mawt), which triggers special rules: gratuitous dispositions are valid only up to one-third of the estate, and divorces intended to evade inheritance are invalid. This lesson unpacks the precise legal definition of death-illness and its conditions, based on the text of 'Umdat al-Ri‘āyah.

Guided Path
1
Contextualize the passage
The passage begins by distinguishing debts of health (unconditional) from debts of illness (requiring a known cause). This sets the stage for defining death-illness.
The author separates the rulings of the sick person because they differ from the healthy in matters of gratuitous dispositions.
2
Extract the core definition
Identify the phrase 'the state in which a man becomes evading divorce, and his gratuitous disposition is not executed except from one-third, that in which destruction is predominant.' This is the legal test.
The term 'fāran bi-al-ṭalāq' (evading divorce) is a key indicator: if a person divorces his wife to deprive her of inheritance, it is presumed death-illness.
3
Analyze the conditions
The text lists multiple criteria: inability to manage affairs, inability to go out for needs, inability to pray or walk, worsening condition. The relied-upon view is that of Abu al-Layth: the consideration is overwhelming probability (ghalabah), not bedriddenness.
For men, the criterion is inability to go out to the mosque or shop; for women, inability to climb to the roof. Pregnant women are considered healthy unless active labor begins.
4
Understand the relied-upon view
The commentator endorses the view from al-Dhakhīrah and al-Tashīl: ability inside the house is irrelevant; the overriding factor is the predominant probability of death.
Chronic conditions like tuberculosis or paralysis are considered death-illness only if they are worsening.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the sick person's confession (إقرار المريض). The author first distinguishes between a debt from health and a debt from illness: a debt from health is accepted without condition, while a debt from illness requires a known cause and knowledge without admission. The key legal concept defined here is 'death-illness' (مرض الموت), which triggers special rulings, such as gratuitous dispositions being valid only from one-third of the estate. The text provides a comprehensive definition: it is a condition where the patient is likely to die, prevents him from managing his affairs or leaving the house, and he is considered to be evading divorce. Various opinions from earlier works are cited: some measure by ability to pray, walk, or work; Abu al-Layth emphasizes overwhelming probability (غلبة) over being bedridden. For women, the criterion is inability to go to the roof. The passage also covers those with chronic conditions like tuberculosis, paralysis, etc., as long as the condition worsens. Finally, a 'known cause' for the debt includes expenses like medicine and doctors' fees. The relied-upon position is that death-illness is defined by the predominant probability of death, regardless of being bedridden.

Key Terms
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): A legal term for an illness where the patient is likely to die and is unable to manage his affairs or leave the house, triggering restrictions on gratuitous dispositions.
إقرار (iqrār): A legal admission or confession of a right or debt against oneself.
فاراً بالطلاق (fāran bi-al-ṭalāq): A person who divorces his wife to avoid her inheritance rights; this is an indicator of death-illness.

Completion of the Lunar Year
Ruling on completing the lunar year with logical and hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct completion date of the lunar year and explain the logical and textual evidence.

Core Idea
The lunar year ends on 10th Dhul Hijjah, not 11th, to avoid two Eids in one year.

The author begins by correcting a potential misunderstanding about the lunar year's end. He argues that if the year were completed on the 11th, the 10th would be included, leading to two Eid al-Adha celebrations in the same year—an impossibility. He then cites the hadith 'Complete the counting of Sha'ban' to support the principle that when in doubt, a month should be completed to thirty days. This ruling forms the basis for counting months in 'iddah, as discussed in the next lesson.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Some might think the lunar year ends on the 11th of Dhul Hijjah. The author refutes this.
2
Understand the logical proof
If the year ended on the 11th, the 10th would be part of the completion, causing Eid al-Adha to occur twice in one year, which is impossible.
Eid al-Adha occurs on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah.
3
Examine the hadith
The hadith 'Complete the counting of Sha'ban' (Bukhari, Muslim) is used to show that when uncertain about a month's start, one should complete it to thirty days.
This hadith is applied analogically to the completion of the year.
4
Apply the principle
The principle is that the first month of a period can be completed by adding days from the next month. Here, the year is completed by ensuring it ends on the 10th.
This principle is also used in 'iddah calculations.

The year is completed on the 11th of Dhul Hijjah, but the correct view is that the year is completed on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah in any case, because if it were completed on the 11th, the 10th would be included in the completion of the year, thus necessitating the repetition of Eid al-Adha in one year: one at the beginning of the period and the second at its end. Have you heard that Eid al-Adha is repeated in one year? 'Complete the counting of Sha'ban,' narrated by Bukhari and Muslim. And it is not excusable except in one month, which is the first month, and it is possible to complete it from the last, so it is completed, and the rest remain on the original principle. According to Abu Hanifa: The first month is completed by its days and its night from the month connected to it, so the second month begins necessarily by days, and so on until the end of the period: each month is thirty days, and the year is three hundred and sixty days. This is analogous to the 'iddah: if the divorce occurs at the beginning of the month, the months are considered by lunar months; if in the middle, then by days regarding the separation, and in the 'iddah likewise according to the Imam. According to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), the first is completed by the last, and the middle months by lunar months.

Commentary
This passage addresses the completion of the lunar year and its analogy to the counting of 'iddah. The author begins by clarifying that the lunar year ends on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah, not the 11th, to avoid the repetition of Eid al-Adha within the same year. A hadith from Bukhari and Muslim is cited: 'Complete the counting of Sha'ban,' indicating that when in doubt, the month of Sha'ban should be completed to thirty days. The discussion then shifts to the Hanafi position on counting months in 'iddah. According to Abu Hanifa, if divorce occurs at the beginning of a month, months are counted by lunar months; if in the middle, days are used. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the first month is completed by adding days from the subsequent month, and the intermediate months are counted by lunar months. This difference stems from the interpretation of the evidence. The ruling is part of the zahir al-riwayah.

Key Terms
العدة (al-'iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband before remarrying.
التفريق (al-tafriq): The separation between spouses due to divorce or annulment.
شعبان (Sha'ban): The eighth month of the Islamic lunar calendar.
عيد الأضحى (Eid al-Adha): The Festival of Sacrifice celebrated on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Counting 'Iddah Months
Intra-Hanafi dispute on counting 'iddah months when divorce occurs mid-month

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's view and the Sahibayn's view on counting 'iddah months when divorce occurs mid-month, and state the practical consequence of each.

Core Idea
When divorce occurs mid-month, Abu Hanifa counts the first month by days; the Sahibayn complete the first month by borrowing from the last.

The passage extends the principle of completing months to the context of 'iddah counting. After establishing that the lunar year ends on the 10th, the author presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the calculation of 'iddah when a woman is divorced in the middle of a lunar month. The disagreement centers on whether the first incomplete month should be counted by days (Abu Hanifa) or completed to a full lunar month by adding days from the subsequent month (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). This difference has practical implications for when the 'iddah period ends.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Divorce occurs in the middle of a month, not at the beginning.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
The first month is counted by its remaining days, and subsequent months are also counted by days (30 days each), leading to a total of 360 days for three months.
The year is 360 days according to this view.
3
State the Sahibayn's view
The first month is completed to a full lunar month by adding days from the last month; the intermediate months are counted by lunar months.
This view uses the lunar calendar for middle months.
4
Note the analogy
The principle of completing a month is the same as in the lunar year completion.
5
Determine the practical difference
Under Abu Hanifa, the 'iddah ends earlier if the first month is short; under the Sahibayn, the first month is always 30 days, potentially extending the 'iddah.
This affects when the woman may remarry.
