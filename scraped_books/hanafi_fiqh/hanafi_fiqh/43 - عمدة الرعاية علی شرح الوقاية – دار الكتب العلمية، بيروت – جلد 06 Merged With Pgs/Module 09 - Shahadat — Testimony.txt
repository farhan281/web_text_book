Restraining the Agent's Hand Based on Evidence of a Woman or Slave
Ruling with explanation on restraining agent's hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on restraining an agent's authority when a woman or slave brings evidence of divorce or emancipation, and explain why the right is not immediately established.

Core Idea
Evidence from a woman or slave restrains the agent but does not prove the right until the principal appears.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi agency law where a woman or slave claims that the absent principal divorced her or emancipated him. The agent's authority is suspended, but the underlying rights are not immediately established. This distinction protects the absent principal's right to defend himself while still allowing interim measures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
Recognize the agent (وكيل), the absent principal (موكل غائب), the woman (claiming divorce), and the slave (claiming emancipation). Each party has a distinct legal interest.
2
Determine the immediate legal effect
When the woman or slave brings evidence (بينة) to the agent, the agent's hand is restrained (قصر يد) – meaning he cannot proceed with the transfer.
The evidence does not need to be conclusive; it only needs to be plausible to suspend the agent's authority.
3
Understand the limitation
The divorce or emancipation is not established by this evidence. The text explicitly states 'بلا طلاق وعتق' (without divorce and emancipation). The agent's restraint is a temporary measure.
4
Link to the absent principal's appearance
The phrase 'حتى يحضر الغائب' (until the absent person appears) indicates that the divorce or emancipation only takes effect when the principal arrives and the evidence is re-established in his presence.
This ensures the absent principal's right to challenge the evidence.
5
Recognize the implicit qiyas
The commentator states the ruling is 'كما' (just as) a previous case, indicating an analogy. The same principle applies: evidence from a woman or slave restrains the agent but does not prove the substantive right until the principal is present.
This is an example of qiyas khafi (implicit analogy).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with its explanation from the commentary. It discusses a scenario where an agent (وكيل) is appointed by an absent principal (موكل غائب) to transfer the principal's wife and slave to another location. The woman claims the principal divorced her, and the slave claims he was emancipated. They bring evidence before the agent. The ruling is that the agent's hand is restrained (i.e., he cannot proceed with the transfer) without the divorce and emancipation being established. The evidence does not prove the divorce or emancipation until the principal himself appears. When the principal appears, the evidence must be re-established in his presence. The commentary explains that the phrase 'until the absent person appears' modifies 'without divorce and emancipation', meaning the divorce and emancipation do not take effect until the absent person arrives and the evidence is re-presented. This is consistent with a previous case where re-establishment of evidence was required.

Key Terms
وكيل (wakīl): A person appointed to act on behalf of another (the principal) in a legal matter.
قصر يد (qaṣr yad): To prevent the agent from exercising his authority over the property or action in question.
بينة (bayyinah): Legal evidence, usually testimony of witnesses or documentary proof.
غائب (ghā'ib): A person who is absent from the location where the legal matter is being addressed.

Oath Formulas for Non-Muslims
Defines terms and states oath formulas for non-Muslims with linguistic expansion and ruling on place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct oath formulas for each non-Muslim category (Jew, Christian, Magian, idol worshipper) and explain why the Hanafi school prohibits swearing in their places of worship.

Core Idea
Non-Muslims swear by Allah in a formula tailored to their creed; judges do not enter their places of worship.

In Hanafi courts, when a non-Muslim party is required to take an oath, the judge uses a formula that invokes Allah in a manner appropriate to the litigant's faith. This lesson presents the prescribed oaths for Jews, Christians, Magians, and idol worshippers, and then explains the ruling on where such oaths may be administered—specifically, the prohibition on entering their places of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the litigation context
Recognize that these oath formulas are used when a non-Muslim is a party in a lawsuit and must take a decisive oath (yamin). The judge administers a formula that mentions a specific divine act relevant to that religion.
The formulas are designed to make the oath binding on the non-Muslim's conscience.
2
Learn the four oath formulas
Study each formula: Jew – 'by Allah who revealed the Torah to Moses'; Christian – 'by Allah who revealed the Gospel to Jesus'; Magian – 'by Allah who created fire'; Idol worshipper – 'by Allah the Exalted'. Understand that the Magian formula is unique because they venerate fire.
The text adds that if a non-Muslim swears by just 'Allah' and refuses the specific formula, it is still sufficient.
3
Analyze the ruling on place of oath
The base text states 'they are not sworn in their places of worship'. The commentary explains this is because it is makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) for a judge to enter such places, which are considered 'assemblies of devils'.
The prohibition is not because the judge has no right to enter, but due to the spiritual harm and association with falsehood.
4
Contrast with other schools
The Hanbali and Maliki schools allow swearing in their places of worship if the non-Muslims venerate those places and fear false oaths there. The Hanafi school maintains the prohibition as the relied-upon position.
This ikhtilaf is based on whether the deterrent effect outweighs the prohibition of entering.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a judge who must administer an oath to a Christian defendant. According to the Hanafi ruling, the judge should not go to a church to do so; he can administer it in the court with the Christian formula.
The mufta bihi ruling is that if a Muslim habitually attends church, he may be disciplined (ta'zir).

Oaths for Zoroastrians and Idolaters: Formulas and Places
Clarifies oath formulas and place restrictions for non-Muslims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oath formulas for Zoroastrians and idolaters, explain why they do not swear in their places of worship, and compare the Hanafi position with the Hanbali/Maliki view.

Core Idea
Zoroastrians and idolaters swear by God, but never in their places of worship—entering is a gathering of devils.

This lesson clarifies the specific oath formulas for non-Muslims in Hanafi jurisprudence and addresses the critical issue of where such oaths should be administered. The core ruling is that Zoroastrians swear 'by God who created fire' and idolaters swear 'by God Most High', but both are prohibited from swearing in their places of worship. The commentary explains the rationale and contrasts with opposing views from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath formulas
Read the passage to find the exact oath phrasing for a Zoroastrian (Majusi) and an idolater (Wathani). Note that both invoke God, but the Zoroastrian specifically mentions the creation of fire.
The formula for Majusi uses 'who created fire' to align with their belief system without endorsing fire-worship.
2
Understand the location ruling
The passage states 'they do not swear in their places of worship'. Recognize that this is a prohibition, not a mere suggestion. Determine the legal category (hukm) of this prohibition.
The text clarifies it is makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) because these places are gatherings of devils.
3
Analyze the rationale
The prohibition is not because the judge has no right to enter, but because entering such places is harmful to one's faith. The commentary from al-Bahr supports this with a ta'zir ruling for Muslims who frequent churches.
4
Examine the opposing view
The passage mentions that Abu al-Khattab al-Hanbali and one Maliki narration allow swearing in places venerated by non-Muslims if it deters false oaths. Consider the Hanafi response that the deterrence does not override the prohibition.
This shows a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different weighing of harms.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
Conclude that the Hanafi mufta bihi is to avoid entering such places altogether. In practice, the oath is administered outside the place of worship.
The tarjih is based on the precedence of avoiding evil (sadd al-dhara'i').

Ruling on Two Outsiders' Evidence with Date Matching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when two outsiders present dated evidence on animal progeny, judgment goes to the one whose date matches the animal's age, and if unclear, they share equally.

Core Idea
Matching the date to the animal's age gives the evidence preponderance; otherwise, the animal is shared.

In Hanafi fiqh, when two non-possessors (outsiders) each claim ownership of an animal's offspring and both bring dated evidence, the judge must decide. The key is to compare the dates with the animal's age. This lesson explains the ruling and the underlying reasoning, including an illuminating analogy with a house in joint possession. Understanding this helps clarify how evidence is weighed in cases of conflicting claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the condition
The text states: if two outsiders present evidence on the progeny of an animal and they date it, judgment is given to the one whose date matches the animal's age. Note the condition: the date must correspond to the animal's age. If it does not, or if both dates match equally, the animal is shared.

The commentator explains: because the sign of truth (علامة الصدق) appeared for the one whose date matches, so his evidence is preponderated. This analogy explains why the outsider's evidence is preferred in the progeny case; it's not about possession but about who is considered an outsider.

You can now apply this rule to other situations: e.g., two outsiders claim a foal with different birth dates; check the foal's age from veterinary records. If one date matches, that claimant wins. If both match or neither, the foal is shared.

The Ruling on Contradictory Evidence: Do Both Become Invalid When Age Contradicts Both Dates?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on whether contradictory dates invalidate both evidences, explain the reasoning behind each, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
When both dates are contradicted by the animal's age, both evidences are not invalid; possession determines the outcome.

In cases where two parties present evidence on the progeny of an animal, each giving a date, but the animal's actual age contradicts both, a dispute arises within the Hanafi school. Some early scholars held that both evidences become invalid, while later authorities maintained they remain valid. This lesson examines the reasoning and practical implications of each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two parties (outsiders or one outsider and a possessor) present evidence on the progeny of an animal, each specifying a date. The animal's age contradicts both dates.
The scenario implies both dates are wrong, but the parties may have made an honest mistake.
2
State the initial view (al-Hakim al-Shahid)
Both evidences are invalid because falsehood of both parties is manifest; the animal remains with the current possessor.
This view is reported in al-Hidaya.

The evidences are not invalid. The ruling depends on possession: if one party has possession, that party wins; if both are outsiders, they share equally.

Under the initial view, the possessor always wins. Under the preferred view, possession is decisive but not absolute: if both are outsiders, they share.
This difference is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

The Role of the Pronoun in Iqrār
Ruling on when statements with pronoun are iqrār

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a response containing a pronoun (ḍamīr) referring to the claimed item constitutes iqrār, and distinguish between binding and non-binding responses.

Core Idea
No pronoun, no iqrār – the ḍamīr alone makes a reply a binding admission.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every reply to a claim is an acknowledgment (iqrār). The key is whether the response contains a pronoun that refers back to the claimed item. This lesson unpacks the linguistic criterion and its legal consequences, including the exception for mocked statements and the differing views of other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the operative condition
The matn states that phrases like 'intaqadtuhā' (I criticized it) with the pronoun 'hā' are iqrār, while the same without the pronoun are not. Identify the condition that triggers iqrār: the presence of the pronoun linking the reply to the claim.
2
Understand the reasoning (wajh al-dalālah)
The commentator explains that the pronoun functions as a reference (kināya) to the claimed amount (alf). This makes the speech a continuation of the claim, turning it into an admission. Without the pronoun, the speech is independent and can be interpreted as anything else, so no liability attaches.
This is a linguistic proof based on the nature of pronoun reference.
3
Examine the mockery exception
If the defendant made the statement mockingly and witnesses confirm that, it is not binding. However, a bare claim of mockery by the defendant is not accepted – similar to a divorce case where a man says 'I meant divorce from a fetter'.
This shows that the court looks at external evidence, not just the speaker's intention.
4
Apply the general principle to independent vs. dependent answers
An independent answer that can function both as initiation and reply is treated as initiation to avoid imposing liability by doubt (shakk). A non‑independent answer like 'yes' always becomes iqrār because it cannot stand alone.
This is the core principle: favour the interpretation that does not create liability when doubt exists.
5
Compare with the Shāfiʿī/Ḥanbalī position
Al-Shāfiʿī and Aḥmad hold that even without the pronoun, phrases like 'intaqada' are not iqrār because they might be mockery or exaggeration. The Hanafī reply relies on the clear reference created by the pronoun, which removes doubt.
The practical difference (thamarah) is that Hanafīs require the pronoun; the other schools do not consider it iqrār in either case.

Ruling on 'min thaman' as Exception or Retraction: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Analysis of 'min thaman' as retraction vs valid explanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether 'min thaman' is a retraction or a valid exception, and state the practical consequence (thamarah) of each view.

Core Idea
'Min thaman' is a retraction according to Imam Abu Hanifa; a valid exception when connected according to Sahibayn.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, a confession qualified by 'min thaman' (from the price of) raises a critical question: does it retract the confession or merely clarify its cause? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah presents the intra-Hanafi dispute, analyzing the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. Understanding this ikhtilaf illuminates how the school weighs immediate obligation versus the specification of a cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confessional statement and its qualification
The confessor says: 'I owe you one thousand dirhams from the price of a slave.' The qualification 'min thaman' (from the price of) immediately follows the confession of debt.
The key is whether this qualification is uttered in the same breath (mawsul) or with a pause (mafsul).
2
Understand Imam Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Imam Abu Hanifa argues that the price of an unspecified slave is not obligatory on the buyer until after receipt (qabd). The qualification thus negates the immediate obligation implied by the first part of the statement, making it a retraction (ruju'). The confession becomes invalid, and the thousand dirhams are due immediately.
The analogy: an unspecified price is like a consumed item because there is no way to enforce payment until the specific item is produced.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's distinction
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the qualification is connected (mawsul), it is a valid explanation of the cause. The confessor is believed, and the debt is not due until the slave is produced. If disconnected (mafsul) and the confesee denies the cause, the qualification is treated as a retraction. The practical consequence differs: under Imam's view, the thousand is due immediately; under Sahibayn's, it is suspended until the slave is produced.
The Sahibayn's reasoning: the first part implies immediate obligation, but a connected explanation (bayan taghyir) can change the implication. If the confesee confirms the cause, it is even accepted when disconnected.

Ruling on Confession Qualified by Prohibited Price Source (min thaman khamr)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on effect of confession qualified by prohibited price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a confession qualified by a prohibited price source, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A confession qualified by a prohibited price is binding according to Imam Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn distinguish based on whether the qualification is connected.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about the effect of a confession that specifies the debt is from a prohibited source, such as the price of wine. The core issue is whether such a qualification constitutes a retraction of the confession, rendering it invalid, or merely an explanation that does not affect the obligation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi principles in matters of testimony and financial claims.

Guided Path
1
State the Imam's position
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if someone says 'I owe you 1000 dirhams from the price of wine' (or similar prohibited items), the thousand is binding regardless of whether the exception is connected or disconnected. The Imam views the exception as a retraction of the confession, which is invalid.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the exception is connected (i.e., uttered without pause), nothing is binding because the final phrase clarifies that he never intended obligation. If disconnected, the exception is a retraction and the debt stands.
The Sahibayn analogize to saying 'in sha Allah' after a confession.
3
Identify the dalil for each
Imam's dalil: The price of prohibited items is not obligatory in Islamic law, so the exception is a retraction; the first part of the statement explicitly imposes obligation. Sahibayn's dalil: The connected exception indicates intent; it is like 'in sha Allah' which, when connected, negates obligation.
The commentator argues that the analogy with 'in sha Allah' is flawed because that is a condition, not an exception.
4
Analyze the commentator's response
The commentator of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains that 'in sha Allah' is a condition that cannot be known, and conditioning is a type of change (tabaddul), so it is valid when connected. But the price of prohibited items is not a valid condition; its mention nullifies the confession, and nullification is retraction, which is invalid even if connected.
Thus the analogy fails, supporting the Sahibayn's view that connected exception shows no intent.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam's view, the debt is always due. Under the Sahibayn's view, if the exception is connected, nothing is due; if disconnected, the debt is due. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sahibayn.

Ruling on Debt Described as Defective Currency in Confession
Effect of describing debt as defective currency after confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a confessor describes the debt currency as defective and the confesee denies it, and apply it to determine the obligatory payment.

Core Idea
A confessor must pay good currency unless the confesee confirms the defect.

This lesson examines a specific ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa regarding the description of currency in a confession of debt. When someone confesses a sum of dirhams but then claims they are defective, the default rule is that he must pay sound, good dirhams — regardless of whether his description was uttered in the same breath or separately. The confesee's denial of the defect makes the confession binding for good currency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
The act is iqrar (confession) of a debt with a qualification about the currency's quality. The case involves a debtor who confesses a sum of dirhams from a specific cause (price of goods or loan) and then describes them as defective.
Confession (iqrar) is a binding declaration in Hanafi law.
2
Determine the default rule for unqualified debts
In Hanafi law, the default for an unqualified debt is sound (jiyad) currency. If a debtor confesses a debt without specifying the quality, the obligation is for good dirhams.
This principle protects the creditor's right to sound currency.
3
Analyze the effect of the description
The confessor's claim that the currency is defective (zuyuf, buhraja, ustuqa, or rasas) is an attempt to reduce his liability. Since the confesee (the one to whom the confession is made) denies the defect and insists the currency is good, the confessor's claim is not accepted.
The confesee's denial is key; if he confirms the defect, the ruling changes.
4
Apply the ruling to connected and disconnected descriptions
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that it makes no difference whether the description of defect is uttered in the same breath (mawsulan) or with a pause (mafsulan). In either case, the confessor must pay good dirhams.
This is unlike some other rulings where connection matters.
5
State the final obligation
Based on the above, the confessor must pay good (jiyad) dirhams. The ruling is based on the principle that confession is binding for the default obligation unless the confesee agrees to a lesser quality.
This is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa; other Hanafi imams may differ.

Woman’s Acknowledgment of a Child: Conditions and Post-Death Ratification
Commentary expanding conditions for valid acknowledgment and post-death ratification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a woman's acknowledgment of a child to be valid, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the husband's ratification after her death.

Core Idea
A woman's acknowledgment of a child binds only herself; to implicate the husband, his ratification or a midwife's testimony is needed.

This lesson unpacks a ruling from al-Wiqayah on how a woman's claim of a child's lineage is validated. While her mere statement may establish the child's maternal lineage, it cannot alone impose paternity on a husband. The commentary clarifies the roles of the marital bed, the midwife's testimony, and the critical intra-Hanafi dispute about whether the husband can ratify after the wife's death. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying the law of lineage acknowledgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principal ruling and its conditions
The matn states that a woman's acknowledgment of a child requires either the husband's ratification or the testimony of a midwife. Recognize that this condition applies only when there is a marital bed (firāsh) or when the claim involves the husband's lineage.
The marital bed is the basis for establishing paternity; the midwife only specifies which child is born.
2
Understand the function of the marital bed and midwife's testimony
The child belongs to the husband by the hadith 'al-walad li al-firāsh'. The midwife's testimony is needed only for specification, not for proving lineage itself. For a woman in ‘iddah from divorce, the husband's ratification or testimony of two men (or man+two women per Abu Hanifa) is required. For ‘iddah from death, the heir's ratification suffices.
If the woman has no husband and is not in ‘iddah, some mashāyikh accept her mere acknowledgment for maternal lineage only.
3
Analyze the post-death ratification scenario
The matn states that ratification after the acknowledger's death is generally valid, except for the husband's ratification after the wife's death. This exception is the core of the intra-Hanafi dispute.
Ratification after death is valid for others because lineage persists beyond death.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute (Imam vs. Sahibayn)
Imam Abu Hanifa argues that the marriage bond ceases entirely with the wife's death (husband can marry her sister, cannot wash her body), so his ratification cannot revive it. The Sahibayn argue that effects like inheritance and ‘iddah persist, so ratification should be valid. The presenter defends Abu Hanifa, noting that ratification refers back to the time of acknowledgment when inheritance did not yet exist.
The difference hinges on whether the marriage's legal effects survive death sufficiently to support a retroactive ratification.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
The commentary (Umdat al-Ri'ayah) favors Imam Abu Hanifa's view, making the husband's post‑death ratification invalid. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The Sahibayn's view is not adopted due to the weakness in their analogy.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah on the matn of al-Wiqayah regarding the conditions for a woman's acknowledgment of a child and the validity of ratification after death. The matn states two rulings: first, a woman's acknowledgment of a child requires either the husband's ratification or the testimony of a midwife; second, ratification after the death of the acknowledger is generally valid except for the husband's ratification after his wife's death. The commentator expands on each. For the first ruling, a single woman's testimony (the midwife) suffices because the need is only to specify the child, not to establish lineage, which is already established by the marital bed. If the woman is married or in 'iddah from divorce, the husband's ratification is needed; if she is in 'iddah from death, the heir's ratification suffices. If she is unmarried and not in 'iddah, her own statement establishes lineage according to some mashāyikh. For the second ruling, the passage details an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that after the wife's death, the husband cannot ratify her acknowledgment because the marriage bond is severed (he may marry her sister immediately, and he cannot wash her body). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the effects of marriage (inheritance and 'iddah) persist, so his ratification should be valid. The commentator defends Abu Hanifa's view, explaining that ratification refers back to the time of acknowledgment when inheritance did not yet exist, and that the 'iddah argument is analogous and also fails because 'iddah is established only after death. The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa.

Invalidity of Acknowledging Lineage from Non-Child Relationship
Matn stating that acknowledgment of lineage from a non-child is invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging lineage from a non-child relationship and identify why such an acknowledgment is invalid.

Core Idea
Lineage from a non-child relationship cannot be established by mere acknowledgment; it is legally invalid.

This lesson addresses a fundamental rule in Hanafi family law: acknowledgment of lineage (iqrār bi al-nasab) is only valid for direct parent-child relationships. Claiming a brother or uncle through mere statement has no legal effect. This ruling emphasises that lineage is a fixed, biological fact that cannot be created by declaration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relationship claimed
Recognize whether the claimed relationship is parent-child (e.g., son, daughter) or non-child (e.g., brother, uncle).
2
Recall the Hanafi rule
The rule: acknowledgment of lineage is only valid if the relationship is based on childbirth (wilād). Non-child relationships are excluded.
The Arabic term 'wilād' refers to the physical act of birth, establishing direct descent.
3
Apply and conclude
If the relationship is not parent-child, the acknowledgment is invalid (lā yaṣiḥ). Broader legal consequences (e.g., inheritance) do not follow.
The matn uses 'kā' (like) to illustrate with brothers and uncles.
