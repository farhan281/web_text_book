Nukul in Qisas: Limbs vs. Life – An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nukul in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the effect of nukul in qisas claims for limbs and life, and explain the reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
Nukul in limb claims triggers retaliation; for life, only imprisonment – due to limbs being like wealth, life being sacred.

This lesson examines a critical intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal effect of a defendant's refusal to take an oath (nukul) in qisas cases. The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, turns on the nature of nukul and the classification of the right involved. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi methodology in balancing the rights of servants and the rights of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the scope of the dispute
Recognize that nukul occurs when a defendant refuses to take a sworn oath after the plaintiff lacks witnesses. The dispute is whether nukul can establish qisas for intentional injury (limbs) and for killing (life).
Qisas is retaliation in kind; a severe penalty.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that for limb claims, nukul leads to immediate qisas; for life claims, it leads to imprisonment until confession or oath. His basis: limbs are like wealth (created for protection), so outlay (bazl) applies; life is fundamental and cannot be taken on nukul alone.
The analogy (qiyas) is that limbs are analogous to wealth.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that nukul is an admission with doubt (shubha) – the defendant may avoid a false oath out of piety. Therefore, it cannot establish qisas in any case; only monetary compensation (diya or arsh) is required.
They treat nukul as iqrar fihi shubha, a legally doubtful admission.
4
Analyze the qiyas (analogy) that distinguishes limbs from life
Limbs are analogized to wealth because they serve as protection for the self. Wealth can be disposed of by the owner; similarly, a limb can be cut if needed (e.g., gangrene). Thus, nukul in limb claims is treated like admitting a monetary claim, so qisas applies. Life cannot be analogized because it is the foundation, not a protective covering.
This qiyas is jaliyyah (evident); the illa is that both are created for protection.
5
Address the objection (thief's hand) to solidify the principle
An objection is raised: if nukul proves qisas for limb, why does it not prove cutting a thief's hand? The response: cutting a thief's hand is a pure right of Allah, which cannot be established by doubt (shubha). But qisas for a limb is a right of the servant, which can be established by doubt.
This distinction between huquq al-ibad and huquq Allah is key in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of nukul (refusal to take an oath) in qisas cases. The base text states that limbs are treated like wealth in terms of compensation, unlike the self (life). According to Abu Hanifa, if the defendant refuses to swear (nukul) in a claim for qisas of a limb, he is immediately subjected to qisas (retaliation in kind). However, for a claim of qisas for life, he is imprisoned until he either confesses and is executed, or takes an oath and is freed; if he persists in refusing, he remains imprisoned indefinitely. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that nukul is an admission clouded by doubt (shubha), so it cannot establish qisas at all, in both life and limb cases. Instead, monetary compensation (diya or arsh) is required. The commentary elaborates the reasoning: limbs are analogous to wealth because they are created for protection, and thus treatment follows wealth in permissibility of cutting and in compensation. A possible objection (cutting a thief's hand by nukul) is refuted by distinguishing between rights of servants (which can be established by doubt) and pure rights of Allah (which cannot). The passage emphasizes the practical difference: in qisas for life, nukul leads to imprisonment; in qisas for limb, Abu Hanifa imposes immediate qisas, while the Sahibayn require compensation.

Key Terms
نكول (nukul): Refusal of the defendant to take a sworn oath (yamin) when requested by the plaintiff, after the plaintiff lacks witnesses.
إقرار فيه شبهة (iqrar fihi shubha): An admission that is not conclusive because it may arise from piety (tawarru') rather than truth.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional injury or killing.

Sulh for Jinayat: Validity and Conditions
Explains validity of sulh for intentional and unintentional homicide with proofs and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh for jinayat (intentional and unintentional) and identify the conditions for its validity, supported by Quranic proof and analogy with nikah.

Core Idea
The validity of sulh for qisas and diyah is rooted in Quranic forgiveness and the principle that compensation is permissible like mahr in marriage.

Sulh (amicable settlement) is a vital mechanism in Islamic law to resolve disputes without litigation. This passage from ‘Umdat al-Ri‘ayah focuses on the validity of sulh in cases of homicide and bodily injury (jinayat), both intentional and unintentional. It provides the Quranic basis from Surah al-Baqarah, explains the interpretive reasoning of early scholars, and draws an analogy with marriage to clarify the legal framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The text states that sulh is valid for claims arising from jinayah, whether intentional or unintentional, and whether based on acknowledgment, denial, or silence. Note the exception for claims of hiring an asset, where sulh is not valid.
This establishes the general principle.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The Quranic verse (2:178) is cited, and the tafsir of Ibn Abbas, Dahhak, and Hasan is referenced to show it applies to sulh for intentional blood.
The verse speaks of 'afw (forgiveness) and just follow-up.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The key word 'afw is interpreted as allowing compensation in lieu of qisas. The verse is explained in detail to show how it implies a just demand and payment, and that partial forgiveness suffices to drop qisas.
The word 'shay' (something) indicates partial forgiveness is valid.
4
Understand the qiyas with nikah
The text analogizes taking compensation in sulh to taking mahr in marriage. Whatever is valid as mahr is valid as compensation in sulh; the opposite applies. However, unlike marriage, sulh does not require a default compensation (mahr al-mithl) because qisas has no financial substitute by default.
This analogy justifies the permissibility of any agreed amount.
5
Distinguish intentional vs unintentional
For intentional (qisas), increase over diyah is permissible because the obligation is not financial but a right to qisas, which is determined by contract. For unintentional, increase over diyah is not allowed due to riba, unless the sulh is for a different type of property or after a judge's decree.
This distinction is crucial for applying the ruling correctly.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the ruling that sulh (amicable settlement) is valid for claims arising from jinaya (homicide or bodily injury), whether the offence is intentional ('amd) or unintentional (khata'), and whether the claim is based on acknowledgment, denial, or silence. The proof for the validity of sulh in intentional homicide is the Qur'anic verse (Al-Baqarah 2:178), which refers to 'afw (forgiveness) followed by a just demand and payment. The commentators (Ibn 'Abbas, al-Dahhak, al-Hasan) explain that this verse was revealed concerning sulh for intentional blood. The author elaborates on the interpretation of the verse, noting that 'afw can mean partial forgiveness or a payment in lieu of qisas. The passage then draws an analogy with marriage: just as it is permissible to take compensation (mahr) in marriage, it is permissible to take compensation in sulh for qisas, and whatever is valid as mahr is valid as compensation. However, unlike marriage where mahr al-mithl is due if no amount is specified, in 'afw from qisas nothing is due unless agreed, because qisas has no substitute except by agreement. The passage then distinguishes between intentional and unintentional homicide. For unintentional homicide, the obligation is financial (diyah), so sulh is valid, but an increase over the prescribed diyah amounts is not allowed due to riba, as it is like settling a debt for more than its amount. However, for intentional homicide (qisas), an increase or decrease over diyah is permissible because the obligation is not financial but a right to qisas, which is determined by the parties' agreement. Finally, the passage discusses conditions: if the sulh is for a debt in the dhimmah, possession must be taken in the session to avoid a debt-for-debt exchange. If a judge has already decreed a specific diyah amount, then a sulh for another type with an increase is permissible because the right becomes specified.

Key Terms
الصلح (al-sulh): An amicable settlement or compromise between parties, often involving payment in lieu of a claim.
الجناية (al-jinayah): A harmful act against the person, including homicide (nafs) and bodily injury (ma dunaha).
العفو (al-'afw): Forgiveness, which may involve waiving qisas in exchange for diyah or other compensation.
القود (al-qisas): Retaliation; the right of the victim's heirs to take the life of the killer in intentional homicide.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood money; prescribed financial compensation for homicide or injury.
