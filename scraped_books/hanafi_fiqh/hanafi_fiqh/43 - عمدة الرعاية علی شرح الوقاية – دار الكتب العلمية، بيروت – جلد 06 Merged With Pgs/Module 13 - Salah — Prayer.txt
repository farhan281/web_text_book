Termination of Agency by Death and Continuous Insanity: Defining Junūn Muṭbiq
Commentary on termination by death and definition of continuous insanity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafī ruling that agency terminates by death or continuous insanity, and explain the intra-Hanafī dispute on the duration of continuous insanity.

Core Idea
Continuous insanity terminates agency like death, but its duration is disputed: one month, a day/night, or a full year.

Agency (wakālah) is a contract that requires legal capacity. When either the principal or agent dies, or becomes continuously insane, the contract terminates because legal capacity is lost. However, what exactly constitutes 'continuous insanity' (junūn muṭbiq) is debated among the Hanafī imams. In this lesson, we explore their arguments and the relied-upon view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
State that agency is invalidated by death of either party or by continuous insanity (junūn muṭbiq).
2
Define the key term
Define junūn muṭbiq as all-encompassing, continuous insanity, distinguished from occasional episodes (ṣarʿ).
The term 'muṭbiq' literally means covering like clouds covering the sky.
3
Map the intra-Hanafī dispute
List the positions: Abū Ḥanīfa (1 month or 6 months in another report), Abū Yūsuf (1 month or >1 day/night), Muḥammad (1 year).
Each imam bases his view on different acts of worship (fasting, prayer, zakāh).
4
Apply the tarjīḥ (preference)
Note that Muḥammad's view is al-ṣaḥīḥ (correct) because a full year with all seasons indicates permanence, and less than a year does not prevent zakāh.
This is the muftā bihī for fatwā.

Commentary
This is a critical refinement (hāshiya) on the ruling that agency terminates by death or continuous insanity. The author first defines continuous insanity (junūn muṭbiq) as insanity that is constant. He presents the intra-Hanafī dispute on its duration: Abū Yūsuf holds it is one month because that nullifies fasting; another narration from him says it is more than a day and night because that nullifies the five prayers. Muḥammad holds it is a full year, which is the correct (ṣaḥīḥ) position, because a full year with changing seasons indicates permanence, and less than a year does not prevent the obligation of zakāh, so it is not analogous to death. Abū Ḥanīfa's position is reported as one month in one source and six months in another. The commentary also clarifies that death of either principal or agent terminates the agency, but if the agent dies, the right of return for a defect passes to his heirs. The author defends the text's wording 'death of one of them' as appropriate.

Sadaqah to Two Persons: Reconciling Abu Hanifah’s Two Narrations
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sadaqah to two wealthy persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on giving charity to two wealthy persons and reconcile the two riwayat from Abu Hanifah.

Core Idea
Sadaqah to two wealthy persons is invalid due to shuyu'; but to two poor persons it is valid, as the poor act as Allah’s deputies.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah addresses a subtle point in the law of charity: does giving a lump sum to two persons create a valid transfer? The answer differs based on whether the recipients are wealthy or poor, and within the Hanafi school there are two narrations from Abu Hanifah. Understanding how later scholars reconciled these narrations is essential for grasping the Hanafi method of resolving internal ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
Determine what is being given (ten dirhams), to whom (two wealthy or two poor persons), and the basic legal classification (sadaqah/hibah).
The passage also considers hibah alongside sadaqah.
2
State the two positions
According to one riwayah of Abu Hanifah, sadaqah to two wealthy persons is invalid; according to another, it is valid but metaphorically a gift. The Sahibayn hold it valid.
The apparent view (al-azhar) is that Abu Hanifah has two riwayat.
3
Examine the dalil for each position
The hadith 'Charity falls into the Hand of the Merciful' supports validity for the poor; the analogy with gifting a house supports validity for the Sahibayn.
The hadith is cited to show no shuyu' when the true recipient is Allah.
4
Understand the reconciliation
Commentators like Kamal al-Dirayah interpret that the charity mentioned in the original text refers to charity to wealthy persons, thereby removing contradiction.
This interpretation aligns both riwayat consistently.
5
Apply the ruling in practice
The mufta bihi position is that sadaqah to two wealthy persons is invalid, while sadaqah to two poor persons is valid by consensus.
The underlying principle is the presence or absence of shuyu' in possession.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on giving charity (sadaqah) or a gift (hibah) to two persons, focusing on the dispute between Imam Abu Hanifah and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The text begins by stating the matn: 'Like giving charity of ten to two wealthy persons (ghaniyan) and valid to two poor persons (faqiran).' The commentary then explains that according to Abu Hanifah—in one narration from al-Jami' al-Saghir—giving charity to two wealthy persons is invalid due to common ownership (shuyu'), because each recipient would own an undivided share. The Sahibayn, however, consider it valid because in their view there is no common ownership when the transfer is made to multiple persons collectively, analogous to gifting a house to two people. The commentary further clarifies that Abu Hanifah differentiates between sadaqah (charity) and hibah (gift) when given to wealthy persons: sadaqah to the wealthy is treated as a gift metaphorically, and a gift to a poor person is treated as charity. However, in the narration of al-Asl (the original text), Abu Hanifah rules that both sadaqah and hibah to two wealthy persons are invalid. The commentator reconciles this apparent contradiction by stating that Abu Hanifah has two narrations on this issue. The basis of the first (al-Asl) is that the completion of sadaqah requires actual possession (qabd), and when given to two persons, each receives a common share, so possession is incomplete. The basis of the second (al-Jami') is that sadaqah is intended solely for Allah, and the poor recipient acts as a deputy (na'ib) of Allah, so there is no common ownership because Allah is One. Thus, sadaqah to two poor persons is valid by consensus. The hadith 'Charity falls into the Hand of the Merciful before the hand of the poor' supports the idea that the true recipient is Allah, eliminating shuyu'. Finally, the text mentions that some scholars interpret the charity mentioned in the original text as referring to charity to wealthy persons, thereby removing the conflict between the two narrations. This passage is a good example of how Hanafi scholars reconcile ikhtilaf within the madhhab by recognizing multiple valid riwayat from the founder.

Key Terms
صدقة (sadaqah): Charity given voluntarily; in this passage, distinguished from hibah by its intention for Allah's pleasure and non-revocability.
هبة (hibah): Gift, a transfer without compensation; when given to a poor person, it is considered sadaqah.
شيوع (shuyu'): Common ownership; in this passage, it invalidates sadaqah/hibah when given to two wealthy persons because possession is not complete.
نيابة (niyabah): Deputyship; the poor person acts as Allah's deputy in receiving charity, so no shuyu' exists.

Dispute on Hiring for Adhan and Imamah
Inter-madhhab dispute on validity of hiring for adhan and imamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on hiring for adhan and imamah, identify the opposing school's proofs, and explain the Hanafi rebuttals.

Core Idea
A hire for adhan or imamah is invalid because acts of worship belong to the doer, not the hirer.

This lesson examines a classical dispute over whether a person can be hired (ijara) to perform the call to prayer (adhan) or lead prayer (imamah). The Hanafi school considers these purely devotional acts, and thus hiring for them invalidates the contract. Other schools, notably Shafi'i and Maliki, allow it under certain conditions. We will analyze the proofs from both sides and understand the Hanafi reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling on hiring for adhan and imamah: the contract is invalid (fasid). Acts of worship are not subject to hire.
The ruling applies to both adhan and imamah as they are devotional.
2
Identify the opposing school and their position
The Shafi'i and Maliki schools hold that hiring for these acts is valid as long as the act is not personally obligatory on the hired person.
The dispute turns on whether these acts are considered services or worship.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponents cite: marriage for Quran, ruqya reward from Abu Sa'id, stipend from treasury, and need for Hajj delegation.
Each proof is presented as analogical reasoning.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis refute each proof: marriage hadith not explicit; ruqya is ja'ala not ijara; stipend is not wage; delegation is not hire.
The core Hanafi principle is that acts of worship are attributed to the doer, not the hirer.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis cannot contract a fixed salary for these roles, while Shafi'is allow it.
In practice, Hanafi mosques often use gifts or honorariums instead of wages.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed inter-madhhab dispute regarding the validity of hiring (ijara) for the acts of adhan (call to prayer) and imamah (leading prayer). The matn states that these are not valid (lam tasihh). The commentator identifies this as the position of Imam Ahmad, and also of several early scholars (Ata, al-Dahhak, etc.). The opposing school, represented by al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad in one narration, holds that hiring for such acts is valid as long as the act is not specifically obligatory upon the hired person. They provide proofs: the hadith where the Prophet married a man for what he knew of the Quran (implying that teaching Quran can be a dowry, so it can be a wage); the hadith of Abu Sa'id al-Khudri who performed ruqya for a wage and the Prophet approved; the permissibility of taking stipends from the treasury; and the need for delegation in Hajj. The Hanafi response (lana) provides several proofs: the hadith of Abd al-Rahman ibn Shibl forbidding eating from the Quran; the hadith of Ubadah ibn al-Samit warning against accepting a bow for teaching Quran; the hadith of Uthman ibn Abi al-As commanding a mu'adhdhin who does not take wages; and a rational proof: acts of worship are attributed to the doer, so the hired person's work is for himself, not for the hirer, analogous to fasting and prayer. The Hanafis also refute the opposing proofs: the marriage hadith is not explicit about teaching being the dowry; it could be a gift; and the ruqya hadith falls under ja'ala (reward for a task) not ijara (hire), which has a wider scope.

Key Terms
الإمامة (al-imamah): Leading congregational prayer as an imam.
الأذان (al-adhan): The call to prayer.
الجعالة (al-ju'alah): A reward promised for achieving a specific result; differs from ijara in scope.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school holds that hiring for acts of worship like adhan and imamah is invalid (fasid) because worship is attributed to the doer, and the wage becomes impermissible.
