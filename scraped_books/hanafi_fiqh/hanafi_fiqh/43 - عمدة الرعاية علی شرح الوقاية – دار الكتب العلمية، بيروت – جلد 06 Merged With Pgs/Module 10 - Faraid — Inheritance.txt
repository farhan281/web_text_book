Depositary's Duty When Facing Claims of Agency, Inheritance, or Purchase
Expanding on rulings for depositary when facing claims of agency, inheritance, purchase, and debt collection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a depositary when someone claims agency, inheritance, or purchase, and distinguish when verification is binding.

Core Idea
A depositary cannot hand over based on verification alone; only inheritance and debt collection allow transfer.

When a depositary is approached by someone claiming to be an agent, heir, or buyer of the deposit, how should he respond? Hanafi law draws sharp distinctions based on the nature of the claim and the status of the owner. This lesson unpacks the rules from the classical commentary of al-Wiqaya, clarifying when the depositary must hold back and when he may hand over.

Guided Path
1
Identify the depositary's default position
The depositary is a trustee (amin) and must protect the deposit. He is not allowed to hand it over to anyone without clear authorization from the owner.
2
Analyze the agency claim
If someone claims to be an agent to collect the deposit, the depositary must not hand it over even if he verifies the claim, because his verification is an admission against the owner (who is absent). This is the madhhab, though Abu Yusuf held a different view.
Silence is not verification; the depositary is not bound by his own tasdiq.
3
Contrast inheritance vs. purchase claim
If the claimant says the owner died and left the deposit as inheritance, and the depositary verifies, he must hand it over because both agree on death, so the claimant is the owner. But if the claim is purchase from a living owner, the depositary must not hand over, as the owner is alive and capable of ownership.
The key difference: death vs. life of the owner.
4
Examine the debt collection exception
If an agent is appointed to collect a debt and the debtor claims he already paid the creditor, the agent must pay the debtor and make the creditor swear, not the agent. This is because debts are fungible (mithliyyat), unlike deposits (specific property).
Deposits are 'ayn (specific), debts are dayn (fungible).
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf is reported to say that the depositary should hand over to the agent if he verifies the claim. However, the relied-upon madhhab (mufta bihi) is the opposite: he must not hand over.
The text states 'هذا هو المذهب' (this is the madhhab).

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary on 'al-Wiqaya', discussing various situations where a depositary (the person holding a deposit) must or must not hand over the deposit to someone claiming to be an agent, an heir, or a buyer. The commentary expands on rulings from the base text. The passage type is a series of ruling statements with some intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. The author first discusses the case where a debtor pays the agent without verifying his agency; if the absent principal denies, the debtor has recourse against the agent. This applies whether the debtor explicitly denies or remains silent, as silence is not verification. Then, for a depositary, if someone claims to be an agent of the owner to collect the deposit, the depositary must not hand it over even if he verifies the claimant, because his verification is an admission against a third party (the owner). There is a narration from Abu Yusuf that he should hand it over, but the relied-upon madhhab is the opposite. Next, if someone claims that the owner died and left the deposit as inheritance to him, and the depositary verifies that, he must hand it over because both agree on the owner's death, so the claimant is the owner. In contrast, if someone claims to have purchased the deposit from the living owner, the depositary must not hand it over, as the owner is alive and capable of ownership; the depositary's verification would be an admission against the owner. Finally, for an agent appointed to collect a debt, if the debtor claims he already paid the creditor, the agent must pay the debtor and make the creditor swear that he did not receive it, not the agent. The ruling distinguishes between deposits (specific property) and debts (fungible equivalents).

Ruling on Ambiguous Household Goods After Death of a Spouse
Intra-Hanafi ruling on ambiguous household goods after spouse's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the disposition of ambiguous household goods after a spouse's death and identify the practical consequence of each view.

Core Idea
The ruling on ambiguous household goods after a spouse's death is disputed within the Hanafi school: each imam weighs possession, custom, and succession differently.

When one spouse dies, household items that could suit either gender (mushkil) raise a conflict between the surviving spouse and the deceased's heirs. This lesson examines the three Hanafi imams' positions on who gets these items, revealing how custom, possession, and succession principles interact in inheritance disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed items
Define 'mushkil' as items suitable for both men and women, which creates ambiguity in ownership after death.
Items clearly gendered are not disputed; they remain with the appropriate spouse.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the surviving spouse gets the mushkil items, relying on the survivor's prior possession.
This is presented as 'madhhab al-Imam' and likely the mufta bihi view.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf gives the wife enough for a standard trousseau from the mushkil items (based on custom), and the rest goes to the husband (or his heirs).
Custom overcomes the husband's presumptive hand.
4
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad gives all mushkil items to the husband during life and to his heirs after death, regardless of which spouse dies first.
This strongly favors the husband's side.
5
Note the practical difference
If husband dies first, under Abu Hanifa the wife gets all mushkil; under Muhammad, husband's heirs get them; under Abu Yusuf, wife gets trousseau value and heirs get the rest.
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear in this scenario.

Acknowledgment for a Fetus: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Stating a Cause
Intra-Hanafi dispute on iqrar li al-haml

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on acknowledgment for a fetus and explain the arguments of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
For a fetus, an acknowledgment without a valid cause is invalid according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, because ambiguity between inheritance and bequest destroys its validity.

This lesson examines a classical intra-Hanafi dispute: when someone acknowledges a debt or property for a fetus, must he specify the cause (inheritance or bequest)? The majority of the school requires it; Imam Muhammad permits it without. We will walk through the proofs, the analogies, and the practical three-scenario summary that resolves the issue for the mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Issue
What is the legal act being discussed? It is an iqrar (acknowledgment) made for a fetus (haml). The condition in dispute is whether the acknowledging person must state a valid cause (sabab salih) such as inheritance or bequest.
The fetus is a potential legal subject that can inherit and receive bequests if born alive.
2
State the Two Positions
Abu Yusuf (and Abu Hanifa) say: if no valid cause is stated, the acknowledgment is invalid. Muhammad says: it is valid even without stating a cause.
The text mentions that al-Shafi'i has two transmissions, and Ahmad and Malik follow Muhammad.
3
Analyze Muhammad's Proof
Muhammad argues that the acknowledgment is a binding proof (hujja) that must be enforced as much as possible. It can be interpreted as having a valid cause because inheritance or bequest is possible. He analogizes to the acknowledgment of a trade-permitted slave, which is valid despite possible corruption.
The slave analogy: a slave allowed to trade can acknowledge debts even if not from trade, because permission creates a general capacity.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Proof and Response
Abu Yusuf counters that for the fetus, there are two equally likely causes (inheritance and bequest), creating ambiguity, unlike the slave where trade is the single clear context. He gives another analogy: a purchase with unclear allocation between two slaves, which is invalid. He also notes that a fetus can only own through bequest, so only one cause is truly valid, but a general acknowledgment fails to specify it.
The fetus's limited capacity (only bequest) actually strengthens Abu Yusuf's argument: the only valid cause is bequest, so a general acknowledgment is insufficient.
5
Apply the Three-Scenario Summary
The text concludes with three scenarios: (1) if the cause is not mentioned, there is ikhtilaf; (2) if a valid cause is mentioned, consensus on validity; (3) if an invalid cause is mentioned, consensus on invalidity. The mufta bihi is that stating a valid cause is required.
The condition that the fetus be born alive within six months is mentioned but incomplete in the text; it is a separate requirement.

Commentary
This passage discusses the conditions for the validity of an acknowledgment (iqrar) made for the benefit of a fetus (haml). The author presents a dispute among Hanafi masters. The issue: when a person acknowledges a debt or property for a fetus without specifying the cause, is it valid? According to Abu Yusuf (and reportedly Abu Hanifa agrees), the acknowledgment is only valid if the acknowledging person states a valid cause, such as inheritance or bequest. If he says 'I inherited from his father' or 'so-and-so bequeathed this to him,' it is valid. If he says nothing or gives an invalid cause like 'I gave it as a gift' or 'I bought it for him,' it is not valid. Imam Muhammad holds that the acknowledgment is valid even without stating a cause, because the acknowledgment itself is a binding proof, and it can be interpreted as having a valid cause since inheritance or bequest is possible. He analogizes to the acknowledgment of a trade-permitted slave, which is valid even if the reason for the debt might be outside trade. Abu Yusuf responds that for the fetus, there are two possible causes (inheritance or bequest), and neither is more likely, so the acknowledgment is ambiguous and invalid. He compares to a case where someone buys two slaves and then the transaction becomes invalid because the price cannot be allocated. The passage concludes by summarizing three scenarios: (1) if the cause is mentioned, there is disagreement; (2) if a valid cause is mentioned, consensus on validity; (3) if an invalid cause is mentioned, consensus on invalidity. Then it discusses the condition that the fetus must be born alive within less than half a year from the acknowledgment for it to be valid, but this part is cut off.

Debts Precede Inheritance Even If They Consume All Property
Ruling that all debts precede inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of debts that take precedence over inheritance and explain the Hanafi proof based on attachment of creditors' rights.

Core Idea
All debts, whether from health or sickness, are paid before inheritance — even if nothing remains for heirs.

In this passage, the Hanafi school establishes a fundamental rule: the debts of a deceased person must be satisfied before any inheritance is distributed. The text identifies three distinct categories of debts — those incurred in health, those from a known cause during illness, and those acknowledged during terminal illness — all of which share the same priority. The proof is built on the principle that creditors' rights become attached to the property of a terminally ill person, similar to how a mortgagee's right prevents the mortgagor from disposing of the property without consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of debts
The passage explicitly lists three types: (1) dīn al-ṣiḥḥa – debt contracted in health; (2) dīn al-maraḍ bi-sabab ma‘lūm – debt of illness from a known cause; (3) dīn al-maraḍ alladhī ‘ulima bi-mujarrad al-iqrār – debt of illness known merely by acknowledgment. Recognize that all three are treated equally regarding priority over inheritance.
These categories cover all possible debts, ensuring no gap in protection for creditors.
2
State the primary ruling
The matn declares: 'And all [debts] are [given precedence] over inheritance, even if they encompass all of his property.' This means the estate is first used to pay all debts; heirs only receive what remains, if anything.
This applies whether the debt is small or consumes the entire estate.
3
Analyze the proof: attachment of creditors' rights
The commentary explains that a sick person's acknowledgment of debt is valid because creditors' rights have become attached to the property. The person is incapacitated and cannot earn, so the property must satisfy the debts. Analogous to mortgaged property – the mortgagor cannot dispose of it because another's right is attached.
This principle is derived from the general rule: an acknowledgment that nullifies another's right is not considered.
4
Address the objection concerning health debts
One might ask: if creditors' rights are attached in sickness, why is an acknowledgment of a health debt also valid? The answer: in health the person can earn, so creditors' rights are not attached to specific property until illness. Once terminally ill, all debts become attached to the estate.
This distinction preserves the priority of all debts equally at the time of death.
5
Consider the exception for essential needs like marriage
The text notes that a sick person may still contract a marriage with a proper dower because marriage is an essential need. This does not invalidate creditors' rights since the dower is reasonable and the right remains in the monetary value.
This exception is limited – any excessive dower would be void.

A terminally ill person cannot acknowledge a debt to an heir without other heirs' consent — the hadith prohibits it.

Understanding this dispute reveals how textual evidence and attachment of heirs' rights shape Hanafi rulings.

Sick Person's Acknowledgment and When the Recipient Becomes an Heir
Ruling on validity of iqrar when recipient becomes heir through new cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the time of acknowledgment (not death) is the basis for suspicion (tuhma) and determine the validity of an iqrar when the recipient becomes an heir after the acknowledgment.

Core Idea
A sick person's acknowledgment to a non-heir is valid if no suspicion existed at the time of issuance.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies a key fiqh principle: when a sick person acknowledges a debt or right to someone who is not an heir at that moment, the acknowledgment is valid even if that person later becomes an heir (e.g., through marriage or wala). The Hanafi school distinguishes this from cases where the recipient was already an heir or where the cause of inheritance existed at the time of acknowledgment. Zufar disagrees, focusing on the time of death, but the Hanafi position prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The validity of a sick person's acknowledgment (iqrar) to a foreign woman whom he later marries. The Hanafi ruling: the acknowledgment is valid and not invalidated by the later marriage.
The burden is on the acknowledgee: she must prove the debt, but the suspicion (tuhma) is the obstacle, not the debt itself.
2
Locate the dalil and principle
The Hanafi proof: at the time of acknowledgment, there was no cause of suspicion (tuhma) between them. A later event (marriage) cannot retroactively invalidate a binding acknowledgment. The principle: 'al-ibra li-waqt al-iqrar la li-waqt al-mawt' (consideration is given to the time of acknowledgment, not death), except when the heir becomes an heir through a pre-existing cause.
This principle is stated in al-Bahr and other works.
3
Analyze the three scenarios
The passage divides the case into three: (1) recipient is heir at acknowledgment but not at death (valid), (2) recipient is heir at both times but the link is severed and reestablished (Abu Yusuf says invalid due to suspicion, Muhammad says valid), (3) recipient is not heir at acknowledgment then becomes heir by a new cause (valid) or by an old cause (invalid). Walk through each with the examples given.
The intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad only affects scenario 2.
4
Compare with opposing view (Zufar)
Zufar argues that since the woman becomes an heir at death, suspicion arises and the acknowledgment should be invalid. The Hanafi response: the acknowledgment was issued to a non-relative (ajnabi), so it is binding and cannot be undone, unlike a gift (hiba) which is treated as a bequest (wasiyya) and thus considered from the third at death.
The distinction between iqrar (binding upon issuance) and hiba (revocable until death) is crucial.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: If the sick man acknowledges a debt to a woman, then marries her and dies, according to the Hanafi school (except Zufar) the woman takes the acknowledged amount from the estate. According to Zufar, she would not. The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position.
This ruling applies only if the acknowledgment is not a bequest (wasiyya). If it is a bequest, it is limited to one-third and subject to heirs' consent.

Acknowledgment of Paternity of a Boy with Unknown Lineage
Ruling on a sick person's acknowledgment of paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid acknowledgment of paternity (binuwwa) and explain why it is not invalidated by marad al-mawt.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a boy with unknown lineage as one's son establishes lineage and inheritance, even in terminal illness, because lineage is a primary need free from suspicion.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a critical question: Can a terminally ill person (marad al-mawt) validly acknowledge a boy of unknown lineage as his son? While deathbed declarations often raise suspicion of fraud, the Hanafi school rules that paternity is a fundamental need, and the acknowledgment is valid if certain conditions are met. We will examine the ruling, its rationale, and the disagreement with Shafi'i and Maliki scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
First, understand that the act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of paternity for a boy whose lineage is unknown. The default ruling: the boy's lineage is established from the acknowledger, and he inherits.
This applies even if the acknowledgment occurs during marad al-mawt.
2
Understand the rationale for validity
Lineage is a primary need (haja asliyya), and there is no suspicion (tuhma) in it. Therefore, the acknowledgment is not treated like a gift or bequest that would be limited to one-third of the estate.
This distinguishes it from other deathbed declarations.
3
Learn the exception: when the boy is already known to be the son
If the boy is already known to be the man's son (e.g., through previous evidence), a second acknowledgment is invalid. Why? Paternity relates back to the time of conception (zaman al-'uluq), so the sonhood was already established. The new acknowledgment is redundant and thus void.
This is the point of the cross-madhhab dispute.
4
Note the cross-madhhab disagreement
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik hold that even if the boy is known to be the son, the acknowledgment is not invalid, provided there is no suspicion of fraud. The Hanafi school insists on invalidity due to redundancy.
The practical difference: Under Hanafi, the second acknowledgment has no effect; under Shafi'i/Maliki, it might reinforce lineage but doesn't change inheritance.
5
Apply the conditions for valid acknowledgment
For the acknowledgment to establish lineage, three conditions must be met: (1) the boy has no known lineage, (2) the boy is of such age that he could be the son of the acknowledger, and (3) the boy confirms the acknowledgment (if he is of sound mind).
If any condition is missing, the acknowledgment fails to establish lineage.

Confession of Lineage After Death in Hanafi Law
Ruling that confession of lineage after death is invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a confession of lineage made after death and explain the conditions under which the confessed person may inherit.

Core Idea
A posthumous confession of lineage is invalid for establishing kinship, but may transfer wealth like a will when no other heir exists.

This passage examines a unique fiqh issue: a person confesses to a lineage relationship (e.g., 'he is my brother') after his death. Such a confession cannot create a new lineage, as lineage is established only by birth or evidence. However, Hanafi jurists recognized that the confession might affect the distribution of the confessor's estate. The commentary explores the dual nature of this confession and its treatment as a quasi-will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basic ruling
The act is a confession of lineage (iqrar bi al-nasab) made after the confessor's death. The ruling is that it does not establish the lineage (batil for lineage purposes).
2

The passage notes an exception regarding the husband's confirmation after the wife's death, but clarifies that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, even that is invalid.
3

The confession contains two things: (1) establishing lineage (affects others) and (2) granting entitlement to wealth (affects only the confessor). The first is rejected; the second may be accepted if no harm to others.

If the confessor has any other heir (even distant), the confessed person does not inherit. Only if there is no heir does the confessed person inherit, and even then it is treated like a will: it can be retracted and is subject to the one-third limitation when there are other potential claimants.

Acknowledgment of Brotherhood and Inheritance
Ruling on acknowledgment of brother and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of brotherhood and explain the condition under which the acknowledged person inherits.

Core Idea
An acknowledged brother inherits only if no other heir exists, because iqrar cannot impose lineage on others.

When a person acknowledges another as his brother, does that automatically entitle him to inheritance? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced answer: the acknowledgment is binding on the declarant's own property but does not establish lineage against others. Therefore, the acknowledged person inherits only when there is no other heir. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, including the analogy to a will and the distinction between rights over property and rights over lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The matn states that the acknowledged brother 'inherits only in the absence of another heir, even if distant.' Locate this condition in the passage and note that it is the key qualification.
The condition applies even to distant heirs like those related through clientage (wala').
2
Distinguish between lineage and property
The passage explains that acknowledgment concerns two things: lineage (nasab) and entitlement to property. Lineage cannot be imposed on others, so it is not established. Property, however, is the declarant's own right, so his acknowledgment is accepted regarding it.
This is why the acknowledged person does not become a true brother in lineage; he only gains a right to inheritance under the condition.
3
Apply the condition to scenarios
Analyze the effect of the condition: if the declarant has any known heir (close or distant), the acknowledged person does not inherit. If there is no heir, he inherits because the declarant has authority over his property similar to a will.
The commentator analogizes this to a will (wasiyyah), even allowing the declarant to retract the acknowledgment.
4
Understand the proof (dalil aqli)
The proof is based on qiyas (analogy): just as a person may bequeath all his property when there is no heir, he may give it by acknowledgment. The acknowledgment is like a will in that it is revocable and only effective when no other heir exists.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the principle of non-harm to others.

Hanafi Ruling on Co-Heir's Acknowledgment of Debt Receipt
Ruling on acknowledgment of debt receipt by one co-heir with implications for inheritance shares.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a co-heir's acknowledgment of debt receipt, apply the analogy (qiyas) to the buyer-slave case, and compare the Maliki position while identifying the practical difference.

Core Idea
An heir's acknowledgment binds only his own share; his co-heir's claim remains unaffected.

When a deceased person had a debt owed to him, and one of his heirs admits that the debt was partially repaid, does this admission affect the inheritance shares? The Hanafi school provides a precise answer based on the principle that an heir has authority only over his own portion. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its logical proof through qiyas, and the contrasting view of Malik and Ibn Abi Layla.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Consider a deceased (Zayd) who had a debt claim of 100 dirhams against Amr. Zayd has two sons. One son acknowledges that Zayd received 50 dirhams. Determine the legal implications for the inheritance of that debt claim.
The acknowledgment is made after the father's death, not during his lifetime.
2
State the Hanafi Ruling
The acknowledging son forfeits his right to the debt claim entirely; the remaining 100 dirhams (if the full debt is not acknowledged) or 50 dirhams (if partial) go to his brother. The acknowledging son gets nothing from the debt.
This applies even if the brother denies the acknowledgment.
3
Analyze the Qiyas (Analogy)
The Hanafi jurists analogize this to a buyer who acknowledges that the seller freed the slave before the sale. The acknowledgment is binding on the buyer (the slave is free at his hand) but not on the seller (the buyer cannot recover the price). Similarly, the heir has authority over his own share but not over his brother's share.
The effective cause (illa) is the heir's wilayah (authority) over his own share only.
4
Examine the Maliki Position
Malik and Ibn Abi Layla hold that the acknowledgment spreads across the entire estate proportionally. Thus, the acknowledging heir receives a share of what he admitted, and the denial by the co-heir is disregarded in distribution.
They treat the acknowledgment as a statement affecting the whole estate, similar to a general admission.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi Response to Maliki View
The Hanafis argue that accepting the Maliki view leads to circular reasoning (dor): if the acknowledging heir takes from his brother, the brother would then claim from the debtor, who would then sue the acknowledging heir, causing an infinite loop. To avoid this, the Hanafis treat the acknowledging heir's share as destroyed (halik).
This response relies on the principle that inheritance is secondary to debt repayment.

Condition for Paying in One Currency in Takharuj
Explains the specific condition when paying in one currency in takharuj involving multiple types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid payment in one currency in takharuj and apply it to determine when the settlement is sahih or batil.

Core Idea
Paying one currency in takharuj requires exceeding the heir's share of that type to avoid riba.

Takharuj is a mechanism for heirs to settle shares without physical division. However, when the estate contains both gold and silver together with other goods, paying an outgoing heir with only one type of currency triggers a potential riba concern. This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies the precise condition: the payment must exceed the heir's share of that specific currency; otherwise the settlement is invalid. It also explains how the possession requirements differ based on whether the heir holding the estate repudiates ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is takharuj where the estate contains both currencies (gold and silver) and other goods, and the heir is paid with one currency only. The ruling: invalid (la) unless the paid amount exceeds his share of that currency.
This is a conditional invalidity: if the condition is met, it becomes valid.
2
Locate the dalil
The underlying proof is the hadith prohibiting riba al-fadl: exchanging the same genus in unequal amounts. The settlement is treated as a sale (bay'), and if the payment equals or is less than the share, it becomes an exchange of gold for gold with no excess, which is riba.
The text argues that the settlement cannot be remission (ibra') because non-liable assets cannot be remitted, so it must be a sale.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illa) is the potential for riba when the same type is exchanged. Since the payment is in the same genus as part of the heir's share, any excess or equality without excess constitutes either riba or an invalid giveaway.
If the payment exceeds the share, the excess is a sale of that difference to the heir, which is valid because the currencies are different genera (gold vs. silver) or other goods.
4
Note the possession requirement
Since the exchange involves different currencies (gold and silver), it is considered sarf and requires hand-to-hand possession in the sitting. However, if the heir in possession repudiates (jahid), his prior possession becomes a guarantee and suffices; otherwise, new possession must be taken.
The principle: a guaranteed possession substitutes for a non-guaranteed one, but not vice versa.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The condition is strictly applied: paying one currency requires exceeding the share of that type to avoid riba. This is the operative position for fatwa.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is mentioned in this passage.

Core Idea
A settlement transferring debt in inheritance is void unless structured as exchange or discharge; three stratagems avoid riba.

The text explains that settlement cannot be by ibra' (remission) for physical assets because ibra' only applies to debts. Also, an exchange (mu'awada) would involve riba unless the consideration exceeds the share of ribawi items. Understand this double reasoning.
Riba arises if equal or less is given for a ribawi item like gold or silver.

Imam Abu Hanifah holds that the entire settlement (debt and asset parts) is void because it's a single transaction (safqah wahidah), even if the debt share is specified. This dispute affects the outcome: under Imam, total reversion; under Sahibayn, partial validity.

To make the settlement valid, three stratagems are presented: (1) stipulate release (ibra') of debtors from the debt, so it's a discharge; (2) heirs voluntarily pay the excluded heir his share of the debt; (3) heirs lend the excluded heir his share and settle on the remainder. Identify each.
The first two cause harm to the heirs; the third is preferred.
5

According to al-Hidayah and al-Zaylai, the third stratagem (lending) is the most appropriate. It avoids harm to the heirs while ensuring the excluded heir gets his share from the debtors.
Note the remaining 'old harm' that physical assets are better than debt.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proofs and legal stratagems from the chapter on settlement (sulh) specifically regarding debts in an inheritance. The core ruling is that if the heirs exclude one of them and stipulate that the debts in the estate belong to the remaining heirs, the settlement is void. The reasoning is that the estate consists of physical assets ('ayan) and debts (dayn). Settlement by way of remission (ibra') is invalid for physical assets because ibra' can only release a debt, not a physical thing. Therefore, the settlement must be by way of exchange (mu'awada). However, a simple exchange would involve giving the excluded heir some physical assets in return for his share of debts, but since he also gives up his share of physical assets, there is an unequal exchange that leads to riba unless the consideration exceeds his share of the physical assets. The text then explains that if the consideration is equal to or less than his share of ribawi property (gold/silver), the settlement is void—except in the case of denial (manakara) where the money is given to settle a dispute, so riba does not apply, though there is a differing view. Next, the passage discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifah and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding when the settlement is void in both debt and asset. According to Imam, if the debt share is not specified, the whole transaction is void because it is a single sale. The two companions hold that the non-debt part can be valid if the shares are specified, analogizing to the case of selling a slave mixed with a free person. Finally, the text presents three stratagems (hiyal) to make the settlement valid: (1) stipulate that the debtors are released from the debt and cannot be pursued; (2) the heirs voluntarily pay the excluded heir's share of the debt; or (3) the heirs lend the excluded heir his share of the debt and settle regarding the rest. The first two cause harm to the heirs, so the preferred stratagem is the third. The passage ends by noting that even with these stratagems, there remains a loss because physical assets are better than debts.

Compromise with an Unknown Estate (Sulh on Majhul Tarika)
Internal Hanafi debate on validity of sulh on unknown estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of sulh when the estate is unknown and apply the condition that jahala does not invalidate if it does not lead to dispute.

Core Idea
Jahala invalidates sulh only if it leads to dispute; possession of estate prevents dispute.

When heirs compromise over an unknown estate of non-measured items, a dispute arises: is it valid? The core issue is whether the ignorance (jahala) invalidates the compromise. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning, contrasting views that treat it as a sale vs. absolution, and explains why the more correct view permits it when the estate is already possessed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the specific scenario
The estate is unknown (majhula) and not of measured/weighed commodities. It is in the hands of the remaining heirs. Ask: what is the legal nature of the compromise?
The heirs are compromising among themselves.
2
Recognize the two possible legal natures
The compromise could be a sale (bay') or an absolution (ibra'). List the implications: sale requires known consideration; ibra' applies only to debts.
The opponent argues: if sale, unknown consideration invalidates; if ibra', invalid for specific assets.
3
Apply the rule on jahala
The Hanafi rule: jahala invalidates a sale only if it leads to dispute (niza'). Since the estate is already in the hands of the other heirs, no delivery is needed, so no dispute arises.
Thus the jahala is tolerated.
4
Contrast the opposing view
Some scholars hold that jahala always invalidates a sale. Understand their proof: any sale of unknown item is invalid.
The response: absolute jahala does not automatically invalidate; only dispute-causing jahala does.

Compromise and Division with Encompassing and Non-Encompassing Debt
Ruling on sulh and qisma with estate debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between encompassing and non-encompassing debt and state the Hanafi ruling on compromise and division in each case.

Core Idea
An encompassing debt voids any compromise or division; a partial debt only withholds its amount by istihsan.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains how debt affects the validity of compromise (sulh) and division (qisma) of an estate. The key distinction is whether the debt covers the entire estate or only part of it. The Hanafi school uses istihsan to avoid hardship on heirs while protecting the creditor's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What are the acts being considered? Compromise (sulh) and division (qisma) of an estate. For an encompassing debt, they are void (batil). For a non-encompassing debt, they are valid (sahih) by istihsan, but the portion equal to the debt is withheld.
Encompassing debt = debt equals or exceeds total estate value.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof for the non-encompassing case is istihsan (juristic preference), which overrides strict qiyas. The qiyas would stop the entire estate. The istihsan reasoning: the creditor is not harmed (heirs still must pay) and heirs would be harmed if the whole estate is suspended.
The mashayikh (later Hanafi authorities) adopted istihsan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does istihsan override qiyas? Because the estate is rarely free of a small debt, and the creditor may be absent. Stopping the whole estate causes unnecessary hardship to heirs, while the creditor's rights are preserved by withholding the debt amount.
The rationale is avoiding harm (darar).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The intra-Hanafi dispute: Qiyas would require stopping the entire estate (waqf al-kull). Istihsan allows stopping only the amount of the debt (waqf qadr al-deen). The early Hanafis leaned toward qiyas; later mashayikh preferred istihsan.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that with non-encompassing debt, compromise is valid and the debt amount is withheld. With encompassing debt, compromise is void. The tarjih authority is the mashayikh, and later Hanafi works (e.g., al-Hidayah) follow istihsan.
Exceptions: if a guarantor pays or a specific asset is set aside, valid even with encompassing debt.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi istihsan-based ruling on inheritance when the estate is burdened with non-exhaustive debt and apply it to a scenario.

Core Idea
Heirs inherit immediately despite debt to avoid hardship, but must pay the debt later.

This lesson examines a key ruling in Hanafi faraid: what happens when a deceased person's estate has debts that do not exhaust the entire estate? The strict analogy (qiyas) would prevent heirs from taking ownership until all debts are paid, but the Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) to allow immediate inheritance, avoiding hardship for the heirs. The passage explains the reasoning and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
The estate has debt that does not exhaust it (ghayr mustaghriq). The question is whether heirs can take ownership before the debt is paid.
This is a common scenario; people often have small debts.
2
State the Strict Analogy (Qiyas)
Every part of the estate is burdened with debt without priority, so it is treated as if fully engrossed (mustaghriq). Thus, ownership cannot pass to heirs until the debt is cleared.
This is the default ruling if only qiyas is considered.
3
Identify the Hardship (Haraj)
People almost always have some debt. Preventing ownership entirely would cause extreme hardship (haraj) and potentially leave heirs with nothing even if the debt is small.
Haraj is a key trigger for istihsan in Hanafi usul.
4
Apply Istihsan
To avoid hardship, the Hanafi school prefers the ruling that heirs may own the estate immediately, but they must later pay the debt from the estate. This overrides the strict analogy.
Istihsan is a recognized source of law in the Hanafi school, though rejected by others.
5
State the Final Ruling
Heirs inherit (sahih) but are responsible for paying the debt. The division of the estate is valid, and the debt is settled from the estate.
The mufta bihi position is as stated in the sharh.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the situation of an estate burdened with debt that does not completely exhaust the estate (ghayr mustaghriq). The base text states that every part of the estate is technically burdened with debt, so division before payment is not allowed. The commentator (sharh) provides the ruling based on istihsan: since people usually have some debt, preventing heirs from owning anything until all debts are paid would cause hardship. Therefore, the Hanafi ruling is that the heirs may inherit the estate immediately, but they must later pay the debt from the estate. This is to avoid nullifying the division if the debt turns out to be small. The ruling balances the rights of creditors with the needs of heirs.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Here, istihsan refers to preferring the ruling that avoids hardship (haraj) for the heirs over the strict analogy (qiyas) that would prevent inheritance before debt payment.
مستغرق (mustaghriq): When the debt completely exhausts the estate (mustaghriq), every part is fully encumbered. Here, the non-exhaustive debt (ghayr mustaghriq) is treated similarly for the purpose of division, but istihsan allows heirs to take ownership.
