Ruling on Husband's Possession of Gift for Minor Wife
Explains who may take possession of a gift to a minor girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a husband may validly take possession of a gift for his minor wife, and distinguish when the father or a stranger may also take possession.

Core Idea
A husband's possession of a gift for his minor wife is valid only after the wedding night.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies who may take possession of a gift given to a minor girl. The key ruling is that the husband can do so only after the wedding night, when his obligation to support her begins. The father, due to his inherent guardianship, can take possession at any time. Additionally, a stranger raising the child may act as guardian if no one else is available. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly completing a gift (hiba) to a minor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Persons
The act is taking possession (qabd) of a gift given to a minor girl. The persons involved are the father, the husband, and a stranger raising the child.
The gift is not complete until valid qabd occurs.
2
Determine Husband's Ruling
The husband's possession is valid only after the wedding night (zifaf). Before that, he has no authority because he is not yet responsible for her maintenance.
The condition is zifaf, not intercourse with someone like her.
3
Determine Father's Ruling
The father can take possession at any time, even after the wedding night, because his guardianship (wilaya) is inherent and continuous.
The father's possession is valid even if the husband also takes possession.
4
Determine Stranger's Ruling
A stranger who is raising the child can take possession if there is no guardian, as it is a pure benefit (naf' mahd) for the child.
The stranger acts in place of the guardian when absent.
5
Note Additional Conditions
For the husband, no requirement of capability of intercourse. The condition is simply zifaf. Also, if the girl herself is rational (ta'qil), she may take possession directly.
This is stated as correct (al-sahih) by al-Zayla'i and others.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the commentary explaining who may validly take possession (qabd) of a gift given to a minor girl. The text clarifies that the husband of the minor girl can take possession of the gift on her behalf, but only after the wedding night (zifaf). Before the wedding night, the husband does not have the authority to take possession because he is not yet responsible for her maintenance. The father, however, can take possession at any time due to his guardianship. Additionally, an unrelated person who is raising the child can also take possession if there is no guardian, as this is a pure benefit for the child. The passage also includes a lexical note on the word 'tifl' (child), defining its usage in Arabic and noting that it applies to both male and female until puberty. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the husband's possession after the wedding night is valid even in the presence of the father, as stated by al-Zayla'i.

Key Terms
زوج الطفل (zawj al-tifl): The husband of a minor girl who has been gifted something.
قبض (qabd): Taking physical or constructive possession of the gifted property, which is necessary for completion of the gift.
زفاف (zifaf): The wedding night, marking the commencement of the husband's responsibility to support his wife.
طفل (tifl): A minor child, used for both male and female until puberty.

Liability for Combined Permissible and Prohibited Acts: The Circumciser's Diyat
Explaining the ruling of half diyah when death results from permissible and prohibited acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyat when death results from combined permitted and prohibited acts, and explain the reasoning based on proportional liability and istihsan.

Core Idea
When death results from both a permitted and a prohibited act, diyah is proportionate, paradoxically less upon death than upon recovery.

This passage presents a seemingly paradoxical ruling: a circumciser who cuts the glans (prohibited) along with the foreskin (permitted) owes full diyah if the patient recovers, but only half diyah if the patient dies. The Hanafi school justifies this through proportional liability and the unique status of the glans as a complete limb. We will examine the objections raised from general qiyas and the responses that affirm this ruling as a rare but sound application of istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the apparent paradox
The circumciser cuts both the foreskin (permitted) and the glans (prohibited). The ruling: upon recovery, full diyah for the glans is due; upon death, half diyah for the soul is due. Note the paradox: more is owed on recovery than on death.
This is the core factual pattern.
2
Understand the first objection from general qiyas
The objector argues that this contradicts the usual rule in diyat: greater harm leads to greater liability. Here, death (greater harm) results in less compensation than recovery (lesser harm).
The objection is based on a qiyas from other diyat cases.
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's reasoning from al-Nawadir
Upon recovery, the glans is a complete limb with a fixed diyah, so full compensation is due. Upon death, the soul is destroyed by two acts: one permitted (foreskin cut) and one prohibited (glans cut), so liability is halved proportionally.
This reasoning establishes the rule of proportional liability.
4
Address the second objection regarding unequal contribution
The objector says cutting the glans is more likely to cause death than cutting the foreskin, so it should be treated like cutting the hand with slitting the neck (where the slitting alone causes death, so full liability remains). The response: each act may or may not cause death, and the exact contribution is not measurable, unlike slitting the neck which is inevitably fatal.
The disparity is not measurable, hence proportional liability applies.
5
Conclude with the principle and authority
Thus the ruling is based on istihsan and Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Nawadir. It is a rare issue (min al-andar) but the mufta bihi follows this view, as recorded in al-Hidayah, al-Tabyin, and al-Inaya.
This ruling is part of the Nawadir (non-Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah) but is relied upon.

Commentary
This passage deals with the ruling of liability when a person's death results from a combination of a permitted act and a prohibited act. The author explains that if a circumciser cuts only the foreskin (permitted) but also cuts the glans (prohibited), and the patient recovers, full blood money is due for the glans. If the patient dies, only half blood money is due because death was caused by two acts: one permitted (foreskin cut) and one prohibited (glans cut). This seems paradoxical: more is due on recovery than on death. The objection is raised that this contradicts the usual rule in blood money where greater harm leads to greater liability. The answer, based on Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Nawadir, is that upon recovery, the glans is a complete limb with a fixed compensation, so full blood money is due. Upon death, the soul is destroyed by two acts, one permitted, so liability is half. Another objection is that the prohibited act (glans cut) is more likely to cause death, so it should be treated like cutting the hand with slitting the neck, where the slitting alone causes death. The answer is that the glans cut may or may not cause death, and the exact contribution is not measurable, unlike slitting the neck which is inevitably fatal. Thus the ruling is based on istihsan and the principle of proportional liability.

Key Terms
الحشفة (al-hashafa): The glans of the penis; a complete limb in diyat calculations.
دية (diyah): Blood money; financial compensation for killing or injuring a body part.
