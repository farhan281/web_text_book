Prohibition on Mudarib Purchasing Slaves Subject to Manumission
Explanation of the mudarib's restrictions on buying slaves with potential manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two prohibitions on mudarib regarding slave purchases that trigger manumission, explain the rationale, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the effect of manumission on the capital owner's share.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot buy a slave that would be freed upon purchase, as it defeats the purpose of profit through trade.

In a mudaraba, the mudarib is entrusted with capital to trade. But what if he uses partnership funds to buy a slave who would be automatically manumitted due to kinship or a prior oath? This lesson unpacks the restrictions, the reasoning behind them, and an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether manumission is divisible when only part of the slave is owned by the mudarib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibited purchases
First, buying a slave who is manumitted upon the capital owner (rabb al-mal) due to kinship or oath. Second, buying a slave who is manumitted upon the mudarib himself, but only if there is profit (the slave's value exceeds the capital).
The first prohibition is absolute; the second depends on the presence of profit.
2
Understand the rationale
The mudaraba contract is legislated for profit through repeated transactions. Buying a slave that becomes free immediately ends the possibility of trade, contradicting the purpose. This is unlike general agency (wakala), which is absolute.
The reasoning is a form of istihsan: preferring the purpose of mudaraba over absolute freedom of the agent.
3
Analyze the consequence of violation
If the mudarib buys such a slave, the purchase is for himself, not the partnership. He is liable to the capital owner for the price paid from mudaraba funds.
This applies whether the manumission was upon the capital owner or upon the mudarib (when profit exists).
4
Examine the condition of profit for the second prohibition
The prohibition on buying a slave manumitted upon the mudarib only applies when the slave's market value exceeds the original capital. If the value is equal or less, the mudarib does not own any portion, so no manumission occurs and the purchase is valid.
The text gives examples with multiple slaves; each must individually exceed the capital for manumission to occur.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on divisibility of manumission
When the mudarib buys a slave with profit, his portion becomes free. Abu Hanifa holds manumission is divisible: only the mudarib's share frees, and the capital owner's share becomes corrupted (fasid). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold manumission is indivisible: the entire slave becomes free.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is Abu Hanifa's view, as it prevents total loss for the capital owner.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is an expansion on the rules of mudaraba (silent partnership) regarding the purchase of slaves who would be manumitted upon purchase by either the capital owner (rabb al-mal) or the mudarib himself. The commentary follows the matn of al-Wiqayah. The first ruling states that the mudarib may not purchase a slave who would become free automatically upon being purchased by the capital owner, whether due to kinship or a prior oath. The reasoning is that mudaraba is for generating profit through repeated transactions, but purchasing such a slave ends the possibility of trade because the slave becomes free immediately. Unlike a simple agency (wakala) which is unrestricted, mudaraba is specifically restricted to tradeable assets. If the mudarib violates this and buys such a slave, the purchase is for his own account, not the partnership, and he is liable to return the price to the capital. A second ruling prohibits the mudarib from purchasing a slave who would be manumitted upon himself (the mudarib) if there is profit in the mudaraba. The reason is that if the slave's value exceeds the capital, the mudarib's share of the slave becomes free, which corrupts the capital owner's share (according to Abu Hanifa) or also frees it (according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Therefore, the purchase is invalid for the partnership, and the mudarib is liable. However, if there is no profit (i.e., the slave's value does not exceed the capital), the purchase is valid because the slave remains part of the capital and can be sold. If profit appears after the purchase (e.g., the slave's value increases), then the mudarib's share is freed without liability, as he had no hand in the increase. The passage also clarifies that 'profit' in this context means the slave's market value exceeding the original capital, not necessarily the overall profit of the mudaraba. The text provides examples: if the capital is 1000 and the mudarib buys a slave worth 1000 or less, no manumission occurs. If he buys multiple slaves, each must individually exceed the capital for manumission.

عتق (itq): Manumission of a slave; setting a slave free.
ربح (ribh): Profit; excess of the slave's value over the original capital in the context of mudaraba.

Profit Distribution in Authorized Sub-Mudarabah
Explains profit division when owner authorizes delegation with conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the profit shares for the owner, first mudarib, and second mudarib in an authorized sub-mudarabah under two different profit-sharing agreements.

Core Idea
The owner's share is fixed; the first mudarib's sub-contract deducts from his own share, not the owner's.

This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah explains how profit is divided when the capital owner permits the first mudarib to sub-contract with a third of the capital. Two scenarios are examined: when the owner stipulates half of the total profit, and when he stipulates half of the profit earned by the first mudarib alone. Understanding these rules ensures that all parties receive their rightful shares without causing injustice to the capital owner or the workers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two profit-sharing agreements
The owner either says 'ما رزق الله بيننا نصفان' (half of total profit) or 'ما رزقك الله فهو بيننا نصفان' (half of the first mudarib's profit). These trigger different distributions.
The wording 'between us' vs 'for you' is crucial.
2
Calculate the first scenario
When the owner gets half of total profit, the first mudarib gets a sixth, and the second mudarib gets a third. The second's third comes from the first's share (since first gave third to second), and the owner's half is secure.
Total fractions: 1/2 + 1/6 + 1/3 = 1.
3
Analyze the legal principle
The owner's share is inviolable; the first mudarib can only reduce his own portion. The analogy of hiring a subcontractor (khayyāt) shows that the first mudarib benefits from the second's work.
The first mudarib's profit is pure even though he did not work.
4
Calculate the second scenario
When the owner says 'what Allah provides for you', the owner gets half of the first mudarib's profit (which is two-thirds of total), so one-third of total. The first mudarib gets one-third, and the second mudarib gets one-third as stipulated.
Equal thirds result from this wording.
5
Confirm the purity of the profit
The profit is halal for all because the owner earns by capital, the mudaribs by work. Even if the first mudarib did not work, the second's work is attributed to him.
This is based on the analogy of hiring.

Dispute on Contract Type: Loan vs Mudarabah or Bida'ah
Ruling on whose word is taken in contract type dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whose word is accepted in a dispute between capital owner and mudarib regarding contract type, and apply the principle of the denier being believed with oath.

Core Idea
In a contract type dispute, the mudarib's word is accepted with oath because he denies liability.

When a capital owner and a mudarib disagree on whether the contract was a loan, an investment, or a deposit, the Hanafi school has clear rules for whose statement is binding. This passage from al-Hidayah explains that the mudarib is believed when he denies the owner's claim, and that the owner's evidence is preferred if both bring proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The capital owner (rabb al-mal) claims the contract was a loan (qard). The mudarib claims it was bida'ah (investment with no profit share) or wadi'ah (deposit). Determine who is asserting a claim that imposes liability.
2
Determine who is the denier
The mudarib denies the owner's claim of a loan, which would impose liability on him for repayment. The general principle is that the denier's statement is taken with oath.
3
Apply the principle that the denier is believed with oath
Since the mudarib is denying a claim that would make him liable (daman), his statement is accepted after he takes an oath. This is analogous to other disputes where a party denies a claim.
4
Consider the rule when evidence is presented
If only one party brings evidence, it is accepted. If both bring evidence, the owner's evidence is preferred because it establishes the mudarib's liability.

Dispute on Specification: Owner Claims He Specified a Trade Type
Ruling on mudarib's word when owner claims he specified trade type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the owner claims he specified a trade type and the mudarib denies, and explain why the default is generality.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, generality is the default — the mudarib is believed unless the owner proves specification.

In mudarabah contracts, the scope of trade is a common point of dispute. The owner may later claim he restricted the mudarib to a specific trade, while the mudarib insists he was given full discretion. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on whose word is accepted, grounded in the principle that the default (asl) in mudarabah is generality of trade. Understanding this default protects the mudarib from unjust claims and ensures contractual certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Recognize the two conflicting claims: the mudarib says the owner gave complete discretion (iṭlāq), the owner says he specified a particular trade (takhṣīṣ).
The scenario assumes no written contract or witnesses initially.
2
State the ruling and its basis
The mudarib is believed with his oath. The basis is the default of generality (ʿumūm) in mudarabah – the contract inherently permits all trades unless restricted by an explicit condition.
The owner must prove the restriction; specification is an exception.
3
Analyze the legal example
Consider the example: if the owner says 'Take this capital as mudarabah for half the profit' without mentioning a trade, the mudarib may engage in any trade. If generality were not the default, the contract would be invalid without specifying the trade.
This example shows that the very validity of an unrestricted mudarabah depends on generality.
4
Understand the evidence rules
If both parties bring witnesses, the later-dated evidence prevails. If no dates or equal dates, the owner's evidence is preferred because he is the capital provider and his claim is less likely.
This is a secondary procedural rule from the commentary.

Ruling on One Depositee Handing Over a Divisible Deposit to the Other
Explains the ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on one depositee handing over a divisible deposit to the other.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether one of two joint depositees may hand over a divisible deposit to the other, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) in terms of liability if the deposit is lost.

Core Idea
Handing over a divisible deposit to a co-depositee without the owner's consent: Abu Hanifa requires division; the Sahibayn permit it.

In this lesson, we examine a classic intra-Hanafi dispute from the chapter on deposits (wadi'ah). When two people are jointly entrusted with a deposit, does one have the right to give the entire deposit to the other for safekeeping? The answer depends on whether the property is divisible or not, and on which Hanafi imam's view we follow. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between the ruling of the school founder and his two foremost disciples, and to see how a single difference in the understanding of the owner's consent leads to a different outcome in liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the legal question
We have two joint depositees. The act: one hands over the entire deposit to the other. The question: is this permissible? And if lost, who is liable?
Distinguish the act of handing over from the validity of the deposit contract.
2
Distinguish between divisible and indivisible deposits
The text first separates the ruling based on whether the property can be divided without loss. Indivisible items (e.g., a single animal) can be handed over entirely by agreement. Divisible items (e.g., wheat, money) trigger the dispute.
Divisibility is a key factor in determining the scope of owner consent.
3
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and his proof
Abu Hanifa: It is not permissible for one depositee to hand over the whole divisible deposit; they must divide it. If he does so, he is liable for half. His proof: the owner consented to joint custody; the action of two on a divisible thing affects only a part, so handing over the whole exceeds consent.
The principle 'fi'l al-ithnayn idha udifa ila ma yaqbal al-tajzi' yatanawal al-ba'd la al-kull' is central.
4
State the Sahibayn's ruling and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: It is permissible to hand over the whole; no liability. Their proof: each depositee owns his share, so taking it is not a transgression, analogous to a partner in a debt demanding his share from the debtor.
They draw an analogy from debt to deposit, emphasizing ownership of share.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and practical outcome
The practical difference is liability: if lost, Abu Hanifa says the giver pays half; the Sahibayn say no one pays. The text notes that if both depositees agree to keep with one, it is permissible, suggesting a practical leaning toward the Sahibayn.
The thamarah is the liability consequence. Understanding this helps in applying the correct fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn regarding the rulings of deposit (wadi'ah) when there are two depositees. It addresses the specific issue: if one of the two depositees hands over the deposit to the other, what are the rules? The commentary distinguishes between divisible and indivisible property. For indivisible property (e.g., a single slave or garment that would be damaged by division), one depositee may hand over the entire deposit to the other with the latter's permission. For divisible property (e.g., wheat, dirhams, cloth), the ruling differs according to Abu Hanifa and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). According to Abu Hanifa, the depositee may not hand over the whole to the other; rather, they must divide it and each keep his own half. If one hands over the whole, he is liable for half the value if it perishes. According to the Sahibayn, he may hand over the whole, and he is not liable. The reasoning: The Sahibayn argue that each depositee is the true owner of his share, so taking it is not a transgression, akin to a partner in a debt demanding his share. Abu Hanifa argues that the owner's consent is only for both of them jointly keeping the whole deposit, not for one keeping it alone. Since the deposit is divisible, the owner did not consent to one having exclusive possession. The commentary also discusses analogous cases: mortgagees and agents for purchase have similar rulings. Additionally, it covers the scenario where the owner forbids the depositee from handing over the deposit to his household. If the depositee hands it over to someone he could have avoided (e.g., a third party), he is liable; but if he hands it over to someone he cannot avoid (e.g., his slave who must tend to the animal, or his wife who typically keeps such items), he is not liable, as the prohibition is impractical. The text notes that if both depositees agree that one should keep it until the owner arrives, it is permissible without disagreement. This passage is from a commentary on Al-Hidayah, a central text of the Hanafi school.

Classifying the Craftsmen Partnership: Shirkat al-Sana'i' vs. Shirkat al-Wujuh
Critical refinement of al-Hidayah's classification by correcting it to shirkat al-sana'i'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shirkat al-sana'i' and shirkat al-wujuh, explain why the Hanafi school validates craftsmen partnership by istihsan, and state the practical difference in profit-sharing rules.

Core Idea
A partnership where one uses reputation and the other does the work is shirkat al-sana'i' not shirkat al-wujuh, valid by istihsan despite qiyas.

Al-Hidayah classifies a contract where one partner secures work through his reputation and the other performs the labor as a partnership of reputation (shirkat al-wujuh). But later authorities object: this contract lacks buying and selling, the hallmark of shirkat al-wujuh. Instead, it is a partnership of crafts (shirkat al-sana'i'), where both partners share the work. The ruling: it is impermissible by qiyas (analogy) due to uncertainty (jahl) but permissible by istihsan (juristic preference) because it is customary and beneficial. This lesson will walk through the internal Hanafi debate and the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
Identify the contract described: one partner uses reputation to acquire work, the other performs the work. The respected position (mufta bihi) is that it is valid as shirkat al-sana'i' by istihsan.
The contract is not ijarah (hire) because both share the work.
2
Identify the Opposing View within the School
Al-Hidayah classifies it as shirkat al-wujuh. Also, pure qiyas would render it void due to uncertainty (jahl) in the wage or output.
The opposing view is from an earlier authoritative text, not from a different school.
3
Follow the Opponent's Proof
Al-Hidayah's reasoning: one party uses his reputation (wajahah), which resembles shirkat al-wujuh where partners buy on credit using reputations. Qiyas analogizes the uncertainty to contracts with unknown compensation, which are void.
Qiyas compares this to hiring with unknown wages.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Response (Istihsan)
The commentator and al-'Ayni argue that shirkat al-wujuh specifically involves buying and selling goods on credit, not accepting work. Since this involves work, it is shirkat al-sana'i'. Istihsan overrides qiyas because the contract is customary (urf) and fulfills a need, and the uncertainty (jahl) is tolerated as the benefit is clear.
The phrase 'specifying a thing by mention does not indicate negation of what is other than it' shows that assigning roles does not exclude partnership in other functions.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf and Tarjih
Practical difference: if classified as shirkat al-wujuh, profit-sharing may follow different rules (e.g., no liability for losses, only credit purchases). As shirkat al-sana'i', both partners share work, profit, and loss. The relied-upon position is shirkat al-sana'i' per later authorities.
Azmi Zada reconciles by saying al-Hidayah used 'wujuh' loosely to mean reputation, not the technical partnership form.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the classification of a specific type of partnership. The text begins by quoting al-Hidayah, which interprets a contract where one person uses his reputation (wajahah) to obtain work and another performs the work with his skill (hadhaqah) as shirkat al-wujuh (partnership by reputation). The commentator objects, stating that this is actually shirkat al-sana'i' (partnership of craftsmen), because the work is upon both parties even if their roles differ. The ruling is that such a contract is impermissible by qiyas (analogy) but is valid by istihsan (juristic preference) due to custom and necessity. The author then provides the reason: the contract is not an ijarah (hire) because in shirkat al-sana'i', the work is shared; specifying one person for acceptance and another for work does not negate the partnership in both functions. Istihsan overrides the qiyas analogy to invalid contracts due to uncertainty (jahl), because custom and public benefit justify it. The commentary then cites al-'Ayni's criticism of al-Hidayah: shirkat al-wujuh is a partnership to buy and sell on credit, which involves no actual labor, whereas this contract is about work, not trade. Finally, Allamah Azmi Zada defends al-Hidayah by arguing that the term 'wujuh' was used loosely to mean reputation-based acceptance, not the technical partnership form, but acknowledges that the technical term should be understood as per jurists' conventions.

Key Terms
شركة الصنائع (shirkat al-sana'i'): A partnership where both partners contribute work; each performs a specific task (e.g., one accepts work, the other performs it).
شركة الوجوه (shirkat al-wujuh): A partnership where partners buy on credit using their reputations and sell for profit; no capital is contributed.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference overriding analogical deduction (qiyas) in favor of a more suitable ruling due to custom, necessity, or public interest.
الجهالة (al-jahalah): Uncertainty or ignorance regarding essential terms of a contract, such as the amount of wage or work.
