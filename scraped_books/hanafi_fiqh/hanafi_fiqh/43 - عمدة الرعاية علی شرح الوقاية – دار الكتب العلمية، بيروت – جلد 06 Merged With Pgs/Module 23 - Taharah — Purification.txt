Women Entering the Bathhouse: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-madhhab dispute with later refinement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on women entering the bathhouse and apply al-Shami's detailed ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The Hanafi ruling varies with time and individual piety—karahah depends on awrah exposure, not the act itself.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi debate over whether it is disliked (makruh) for women to enter public bathhouses. The early relied-upon view said no karahah, but later scholars like Ibn al-Humam declared absolute karahah due to widespread awrah exposure. Finally, al-Shami refined the ruling, conditioning it on the individual's conduct. Understanding this evolution shows how Hanafi fatwa adapts to social change.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
Extract the three views from the passage: (1) al-Ashbah's early view of no karahah absolutely, (2) Ibn al-Humam's view of absolute karahah in his time, (3) al-Shami's detailed view conditioned on lowering gaze and not exposing awrah.
Note the shift from unconditional to conditional.
2
Understand the basis for each
For each view, identify the underlying reasoning. The early view assumed modesty was maintained. Ibn al-Humam pointed to verified awrah exposure in his time. Al-Shami argued the cause (awrah exposure) is not universal; it depends on the individual's behavior.
The principle is that the ruling follows the effective cause (al-hukm yaduru ma'a al-illah).
3
Analyse al-Shami's refinement
Al-Shami expands the scope to both men and women entering bathhouses. He introduces two conditions: (a) the entrant lowers their gaze so as not to see others' awrah, and (b) they do not expose their own awrah. If both conditions are met, no karahah; otherwise, karahah.
This is a fatwa detail (tafṣīl) that balances necessity and piety.
4
Apply the conditional ruling
Test a scenario: A woman enters a women's bathhouse but keeps her gaze lowered and covers her awrah. According to al-Shami, there is no karahah. In contrast, if she looks around or uncovers, it becomes makruh.
The ruling applies to both genders, though the passage initially discusses women.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi is al-Shami's detailed view because it accounts for both the social context and individual responsibility. It is the most comprehensive and practical for fatwa today.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin (later scholars).

He said in al-Ashbah: It is disliked for her to enter the bathhouse according to one opinion, and it is said: except if she is ill or postpartum, and the relied-upon view is that there is no dislike absolutely. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute over the ruling on women entering public bathhouses. The early relied-upon view in al-Ashbah stated no karahah, but later, in al-Durr al-Mukhtar, Ibn al-Humam declared absolute karahah in his time due to the prevalence of awrah exposure. Al-Shami, in his gloss, refines this: karahah depends on whether the individual lowers their gaze and avoids exposing their own awrah. If they do, there is no karahah; otherwise, it is disliked. This shows how Hanafi fatwa adapts to changing social conditions. The underlying principle is the prohibition of looking at or revealing awrah. The mufta bihi today is the detailed view of al-Shami, as it balances necessity and piety.

Key Terms
عورة (ʻawrah): Body parts that must be covered; in Hanafi, for women before other women, it is from navel to knee except what is customarily uncovered.
كراهة (karāhah): Dislike; a legal category between permissible and forbidden, meaning the act is discouraged but not sinful.
تفصيل (tafṣīl): A detailed, conditional ruling as opposed to a general absolute one.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling on entering a bathhouse is conditional: no karahah if the entrant lowers their gaze and does not expose their awrah; otherwise, it is disliked.
