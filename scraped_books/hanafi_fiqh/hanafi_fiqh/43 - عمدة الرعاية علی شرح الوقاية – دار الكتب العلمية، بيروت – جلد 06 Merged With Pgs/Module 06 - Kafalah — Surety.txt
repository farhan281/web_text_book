Loss of the Guarantor's Debt and Its Effect
Expansion on loss of guarantor's debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on loss of the guarantor's debt in the specific scenario involving a Maliki judge exonerating the principal, and distinguish between the interpretations of kafalah as literal guarantee vs. hawalah.

Core Idea
Loss of the guarantor's debt occurs only in a specific scenario with a Maliki judge, not generally.

This passage expands on a ruling about the loss of a guarantor's debt. The commentator clarifies that the loss is attributed to taking the guarantor in a particular situation: when a judge following the Maliki view exonerates the principal debtor by mere guarantee, and then the guarantor dies insolvent. This distinction is crucial because it determines whether the agent can take a guarantor without risk of loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario of loss
The passage describes the form of loss (ṣūrat al-tawā): the agent raises the case to a judge who holds the Maliki view that the principal is exonerated by mere guarantee. The judge rules accordingly, then the guarantor dies insolvent.
This is the only scenario where loss is attributed to taking the guarantor.
2
Analyze the two interpretations of 'kafalah'
The commentator presents two views: (1) 'kafalah' here means hawalah (transfer of debt) because loss occurs in hawalah; (2) it means literal guarantee, and loss occurs by death of both guarantor and debtor insolvent.
The commentator rejects the hawalah interpretation.
3
Understand the rejection of hawalah
In hawalah, the debt is not lost by death of the transferee (muḥāl ʿalayh); the creditor can revert to the transferor. Loss only occurs if both die insolvent, which is similar to guarantee. Thus interpreting 'kafalah' as hawalah is invalid.
This reasoning shows why hawalah does not fit the scenario.
4
Adopt the preferred interpretation
The preferred view is literal kafalah, and the loss is specifically attributed to taking the guarantor in the Maliki judge scenario. The loss would not have occurred if the agent had not taken a guarantor.
This is the muftā bihī position.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf)
If 'kafalah' meant hawalah, the agent would be unable to take a guarantor in this scenario; if literal guarantee, he can. The Hanafi school takes the latter.
This affects the agent's right to take a surety.

Or the loss of what is upon the guarantor: The pronoun in 'is lost' refers to the collateral. The form of the loss: that he raises the incident to a judge who holds the opinion of the principal's exoneration by the mere guarantee, as is the madhhab of Malik, so he judges the exoneration of the principal himself, then the guarantor dies insolvent. His saying: 'upon the guarantor'; it is said: the intended meaning of guarantee here is transfer of debt (hawalah), because loss occurs in it and not in guarantee; and it is said: the intended meaning is the literal guarantee, and loss occurs in it by the death of the guarantor and the guaranteed being insolvent. And in 'al-Nihayah': the intended meaning here is loss attributed to taking the guarantor, because if he did not take a guarantor, it would not occur as in the collateral; and loss is mentioned here without attribution to taking the guarantor, because if he also did not take a guarantor, loss would occur by the death of the debtor. And interpreting it as transfer of debt is invalid, because debt does not become lost in it by the death of the transferee insolvent, but rather he returns to the transferor, and loss only occurs by both dying insolvent, so it becomes like guarantee. And the most likely is that by 'loss' is meant loss attributed to taking the guarantor, that he raises the matter to a judge who holds the opinion of the principal's exoneration by the mere guarantee, as is the madhhab of Malik, so he judges the exoneration of the principal, then the guarantor dies insolvent.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the ruling mentioned in the base text: 'Or the loss of what is upon the guarantor.' The commentator first clarifies that the subject of the verb 'is lost' refers back to the collateral mentioned earlier. He then explains the scenario of loss: the agent raises the case to a judge who follows the Maliki view that the mere act of giving a guarantee exonerates the principal debtor. The judge rules accordingly, then the guarantor dies insolvent, so the debt is lost. The commentator then discusses two interpretations of the word 'guarantee' (kafalah) in this context. First opinion: it means hawalah (transfer of debt), because loss occurs in hawalah but not in kafalah. Second opinion: it means literal guarantee, and loss occurs by both the guarantor and the guaranteed dying insolvent. The commentator cites al-Nihayah to clarify that the loss here is attributed to taking the guarantor, not to the absence of taking one. He rejects the hawalah interpretation because in hawalah the debt does not become lost by the death of the transferee; rather, the creditor can revert to the transferor. Loss only occurs if both die insolvent, which is similar to guarantee. Finally, the commentator gives the preferred view: the most likely interpretation is that loss is attributed to taking the guarantor, with the specific scenario as described (judge following Maliki view).

Key Terms
التوى (al-tawā): Loss of the debt due to the guarantor's death insolvent after a judge exonerates the principal.
الكفالة (al-kafālah): A contract where a person guarantees the debt of another; here it is disputed whether it means literal guarantee or hawalah.
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): Transfer of debt from one debtor to another; the creditor can revert to the original debtor if the transferee dies insolvent.
