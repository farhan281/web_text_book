Kafalah bi al-Darak as Acknowledgment of Ownership
Ruling that kafalah bi al-darak is treated as acknowledgment of seller's ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a guarantee of recourse and mere witnessing in terms of legal acknowledgment, and apply the Hanafi ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A guarantee of recourse is an acknowledgment of the seller's ownership; witnessing alone is not.

In Hanafi law, when a third party guarantees the buyer against defects or eviction (kafalah bi al-darak), this guarantee carries legal implications. The passage from al-Hidayah clarifies that such a guarantee is treated as an acknowledgment that the seller owned the property. This distinguishes it from simple witnessing, which does not constitute acknowledgment, because witnessing can occur even if the seller is not the true owner. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining who is estopped from later claiming ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act under discussion is kafalah bi al-darak — a third party guarantees the buyer recourse if the seller's title fails or the property is defective.
2
Understand the ruling
This guarantee is treated as taslim (acknowledgment) by the guarantor that the seller owned the property at the time of sale. The reasoning: the guarantee implies confidence in the seller's title, so it is equivalent to an admission.
3
Compare with witnessing
Mere witnessing and sealing of the deed does not constitute acknowledgment. Even if the witness writes 'I witnessed that he sold', it does not estop him from claiming ownership because witnessing can be done for preservation, not as an admission.
4
Note the exception
If the witness writes explicit phrases like 'sold while owning it' or 'sold a final and binding sale', then it becomes an acknowledgment. However, if he writes only upon the mutual acknowledgment of the parties, it is not.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario, determine whether the act constitutes an acknowledgment: guarantee → yes; mere witness → no; witness with explicit ownership phrase → yes.

وذكر في "الهداية" في كتاب الكفالة قبل الفصل في الضمان :قال ومن باع دارا وكفل عنه رجل بالدرك فهو تسليم؛ لأن الكفالة لو كانت مشروطة فيه، فتمامه بقبوله، ثم بالدعوى يسعى في نقض ما تم من جهته، وإن لم تكن مشروطة فيه فالمراد بها أحكام البيع وترغيب المشتري فيه؛ إذ لا يرغب فيه بدون الكفالة، فنزل منزلة الإقرار بملك البائع، ولو شهد وختم ولم يكفل لم يكن تسليما، وهو على دعواه؛ لأن الشهادة لا تكون مشروطة في البيع ولا هي إقرار بالملك؛ لأن البيع مرة يوجد من المالك، وتارة من غيره، ولعله كتب الشهادة ليحفظ الحادثة بخلاف ما تقدم، قالوا: إذا كتب في الصك باع وهو يملكه، أو باع بيعا باتا نافذا وهو كتب شهد بذلك، فهو تسليم إلا إذا كتب الشهادة على إقرار المتعاقدين. (فتح، تكملة)
And it is mentioned in al-Hidayah in the book of kafalah before the chapter on dhaman: He said: Whoever sold a house and a man guaranteed him for darak (recourse), it is a taslim (acknowledgment) because if the guarantee was stipulated in it, its completion depends on acceptance, then by the claim he seeks to annul what was completed from his side. And if it was not stipulated, the intent is the rulings of the sale and encouraging the buyer, because no one would desire it without guarantee. So it is treated as an acknowledgment of the seller's ownership. If he witnessed and sealed but did not guarantee, it is not a taslim, and his claim stands because witnessing is not stipulated in the sale nor is it an acknowledgment of ownership, because sale sometimes occurs from the owner and sometimes from others, and perhaps he wrote the witness to preserve the event, unlike the previous. They said: if he wrote in the deed 'he sold while owning it' or 'sold a final and binding sale', and he wrote 'he witnessed that', it is a taslim unless he wrote the witness upon the acknowledgment of the two contracting parties. (Fath, Takmila)

Commentary
This passage from al-Hidayah discusses the legal effect of kafalah bi al-darak (guarantee of recourse) in a sale. If a third party guarantees the buyer against defects or eviction, this is treated as an acknowledgment (taslim) that the seller owned the property at the time of sale. This is because the guarantee implies confidence in the seller's title. However, if the person merely witnesses the sale and seals the deed without guaranteeing, it does not constitute an acknowledgment, because witnessing can be done for various reasons, and the sale might have been made by a non-owner. The exception is if the witness writes explicitly 'he sold while owning it' or 'he sold a final sale' then it becomes an acknowledgment. But if the witness writes only upon the mutual acknowledgment of the parties, it is not an acknowledgment.

Key Terms
الكفالة بالدرك (al-kafāla bi al-darak): The guarantee of recourse; a commitment to indemnify the buyer if the sold property is defective or the seller's title fails.
تسليم (taslīm): Acknowledgment or admission; here it means an implicit acknowledgment of the seller's ownership.
