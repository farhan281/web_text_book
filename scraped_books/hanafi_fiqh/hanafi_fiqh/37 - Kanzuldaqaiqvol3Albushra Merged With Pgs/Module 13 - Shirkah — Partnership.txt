Distinguishing 'Min Mālī' (Gift) from 'Fī Mālī' (Partnership/Debt)
Ruling on the legal effect of 'min mālī' vs 'fī mālī'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal effects of 'min mālī' and 'fī mālī' in Hanafi law, and identify the correct ruling for each phrase.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, 'min mālī' signals a gift, while 'fī mālī' signals an existing right—partnership or debt.

This passage from Sharḥ al-Kāfī illustrates how subtle linguistic differences can change the legal outcome. The prepositions 'min' and 'fī' carry distinct legal consequences: one indicates a transfer of ownership (gift), the other indicates a pre-existing share or obligation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly categorizing acknowledgments and transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase
Determine whether the speaker used 'min mālī' (from my wealth) or 'fī mālī' (in my wealth). The preposition is key.
2
Case 1: 'min' phrase
If the phrase is 'min mālī' or 'min darāhimī hādhihi', it is a gift (hiba). The statement alone does not transfer ownership; delivery (qabd) is required for validity.
The gift is not valid (lā tajūz) unless the giver hands over the item.
3
Case 2: 'fī' phrase without disclaimer
If the phrase is 'fī mālī' or 'fī darāhimī hādhihi' and no disclaimer is added, it is an acknowledgment of partnership (shirkah). The other person has a share in that wealth.
4
Case 3: 'fī' phrase with disclaimer
If the phrase includes 'lā ḥaqqa lī fīhi' (no right for me in it), it becomes an acknowledgment of debt (dayn). The speaker admits the amount is owed to the other person.
The disclaimer clarifies that the speaker claims no ownership, indicating a debt.
5
Apply the ruling
Based on the phrase and context, determine the legal relationship: gift (needs delivery), partnership (immediate share), or debt (obligation to repay).

Commentary
This passage from Sharḥ al-Kāfī provides three distinct rulings based on prepositional phrases. First, if someone says 'he has in my wealth (fī mālī) one hundred dirhams' or 'in these dirhams of mine one hundred dirhams,' it is an acknowledgment of partnership (shirkah), meaning the other person has a share in that wealth. Second, if someone says 'he has from my wealth (min mālī) one thousand dirhams' or 'from these dirhams of mine one hundred,' it is a gift (hiba), which is a voluntary transfer and is not valid unless the giver actually hands over the item. Third, if someone says 'he has in my wealth one thousand dirhams, I have no right in it,' it is an acknowledgment of debt (dayn). The key difference is the preposition: 'fī' indicates a share or existing right (partnership or debt), while 'min' indicates a gift (a transfer from one's property). Also, the addition of 'lā ḥaqqa lī fīhi' clarifies that it is a debt, as he disclaims any ownership.

Key Terms
من مالي (min mālī): A phrase indicating a gift (hiba) of part of one's wealth.
في مالي (fī mālī): A phrase indicating a share or right in the wealth, either as partnership or debt.

Gift of Undivided Property: The Divisibility Condition
Ruling on gifting undivided property that can or cannot be divided

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school distinguishes between divisible and indivisible undivided property for hiba and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A gift of undivided property is valid in Hanafi law only if the property cannot be divided — otherwise division is required.

In this lesson, you will examine a key disagreement between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the gift (hiba) of undivided property (musha'). The Hanafi school imposes a condition of divisibility, while the Shafi'i school draws an analogy to sale. By the end, you will understand the Hanafi evidence based on the practice of the Caliphs and the rationale behind the distinction.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two categories: divisible and indivisible musha'. For divisible (e.g., share of a house), hiba is invalid without prior division; for indivisible (e.g., share of a slave), hiba is valid with deficient possession.
The criterion: can a judge compel division?
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i (and Ahmad, Malik in a narration) holds that hiba of musha' is always valid, as it is a transfer of ownership like sale.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i school uses qiyas: just as sale of an undivided share is valid, so is hiba.
They see no reason to differentiate between sale and gift.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafis argue that hiba is different from sale because it requires possession (qabd), and for divisible property, full possession is impossible without division. They cite the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs and a hadith of 'Ali (though weak).
The Hanafi position is based on a specific legal reasoning (istihsan?) rather than strict qiyas.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, gifting a share in a house without prior partition is invalid; under Shafi'i law, it is valid immediately.
This affects estate planning and joint ownership arrangements.

No Pre-emption for Division Among Partners
Ruling on shuf'ah for division among co-owners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why division (qisma) does not trigger shuf'ah and distinguish it from an absolute exchange (sale).

Core Idea
Division among co-owners is separation, not sale; pre-emption applies only to absolute exchange.

In Hanafi law, the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) arises only when a property is transferred through a sale or similar absolute exchange. But what about when co-owners simply divide their jointly owned property among themselves? This lesson explains that such a division does not give neighbours or others a right of pre-emption, because legally it is considered a separation of existing shares, not a transfer of ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal classification
The act under discussion is the division of jointly owned property among co-owners (qisma). Determine whether this act is a sale or another type of transaction.
2
Analyse the nature of qisma
Recognise that qisma is considered an ifraz (separation) of shares that already belong to the partners. It involves an element of compulsion (jabr) because a partner can force division.
This distinguishes it from a voluntary sale.
3
Apply the condition for shuf'ah
Recall that shuf'ah is legislated only for absolute exchange (mubadala mutlaqa), i.e., a sale where property is exchanged for property without any other element. Qisma does not meet this condition.
Therefore, shuf'ah does not apply.
4
State the ruling
Conclude that when co-owners divide a property among themselves, neighbours or other non-partners have no right of pre-emption.

Division is not an exchange; it is an allotment of previously owned shares.

Key Terms

Taking Share in Absence: Mithli vs Non-Mithli
Ruling on taking share in absence for homogeneous property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mithli and non-mithli property regarding the ruling on taking a share in the absence of the partner, and state the condition of possession.

Core Idea
A partner may only take his share in the absence of the other if the property is mithli and in joint possession.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies a key distinction in partnership law: when can a partner unilaterally take his share? The answer depends on the nature of the property—homogeneous (mithli) or heterogeneous (non-mithli)—and on a crucial condition of joint possession. We will explore the rationale and conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two property types
The passage distinguishes mithli (homogeneous) and non-mithli (heterogeneous) property. Understand the definitions.
Examples of mithli: grains, oils, money. Non-mithli: livestock, real estate.
2
State the ruling for each type
In mithli, the partner may take his share in the absence of the other; in non-mithli, he may not.
The ruling is a branching from the nature of division.
3
Analyze the condition of possession
The commentary adds that the mithli property must be in the joint possession (maqbud) of both partners. If not in possession, the ruling does not apply.
This condition is from later sources (al-Hamawi, al-Shalabi).
4
Understand the reasoning
In mithli, division is separation (ifraz) and identification of one's share, so unilateral taking is permissible. In non-mithli, division is an exchange (mubadala) due to value disparity, so unilateral taking is not allowed.
The commentary from Fath explains this rationale.

Coercion to Divide in Same-Type Property
Ruling on compelling a reluctant partner to divide in same-type property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a reluctant partner can be compelled to divide jointly owned property according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Compulsion to divide joint property applies only when it is of the same type (mutahid al-jins), not when types differ.

The Hanafi school permits a qadi to force a reluctant partner to divide jointly owned property, but this compulsion is not unconditional. The key condition is that the property must be of a single type, such as all sheep or all wheat. This lesson examines why this condition exists and what falls under it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is compulsion (jabr) by the qadi to force a reluctant partner to divide joint property. The ruling: it is wajib (obligatory) for the qadi to compel when conditions are met.
This is not a universal right; it is triggered by a request from one partner.
2
List the condition for compulsion
The property must be mutahid al-jins (same type). This includes both mithli (homogeneous) and qimi (heterogeneous but same genus). The condition is fulfilled regardless of whether the property is exactly alike or merely of the same kind.
Example: all wheat (mithli) or all sheep (qimi but same genus).
3
Understand the underlying rationale (illa)
Division of same-type property involves an exchange (mubadala). The Hanafi school analogizes this to a debtor compelled to sell property to repay a debt, because both involve a right of another person that justifies compulsion.
The illa is the attachment of another's right to the property.
4
Identify the exception
Compulsion does not apply when the property consists of different types (e.g., sheep and camels) because equivalence in division is impossible due to great disparity in value and objectives.
Voluntary division is always permissible if both partners agree.
5
Apply the rule to practice
When faced with a request for partition, first check if the property is of one type. If yes, the qadi can compel; if no, he cannot, but may mediate a voluntary agreement.
The ruling aims to prevent injustice and balance rights.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the matn with commentary from Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and Fath. It addresses when a reluctant partner can be forced to divide jointly owned property. The key distinction is whether the property is of one type (mutahid al-jins) or of different types. If it is of one type — whether homogeneous (mithli) like grains or heterogeneous (qimi) like animals of the same genus — the partner can be compelled because division here entails an exchange (mubadala), and compulsion is allowed when a right of another is involved, analogous to a debtor compelled to sell his property to pay debts. If the property is of different types (e.g., sheep and camels), compulsion is not allowed due to lack of equivalence; however, voluntary division is permissible. The Hanafi school applies this rule to prevent injustice.

جبر (jabr): Compulsion by the qadi to force a reluctant partner to divide the property.

Fee Distribution in Property Division: By Heads or By Shares?
Ruling on appointing a divider and the dispute over distributing fees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Abu Hanifa's position on fee distribution, identify the opposing view of the Sahibayn, and explain the reasoning behind each position using the concept of tamyiz.

Core Idea
The divider's fee is for the uniform work of distinction, so it must be split equally by heads, not by share size.

When joint property is divided, a divider (qasim) may be appointed. Ideally, his salary is from the public treasury, but if not, the partners pay his fee. A key dispute arises: should the fee be divided equally among partners (by heads) or proportionally to each partner's share? This lesson explores the arguments and the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios of divider appointment
First, note that the passage distinguishes a divider paid by bayt al-mal (public treasury) from one paid by the partners. Understand the rationale: division resembles judiciary, so a public salary is ideal; otherwise, the fee is on the beneficiaries.
The appointment itself is recommended (mandub).
2
State the issue in dispute
When the fee is paid by the partners, how is it divided among them? The text states: 'according to the number of heads, not according to shares.' This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Shafi'i hold it is by shares.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Present Imam Abu Hanifa's argument
Imam argues that the fee is in exchange for the act of distinction (tamyiz), which is uniform regardless of share size. Separating a small share from a large one requires the same work. Therefore, the fee is divided equally.
He also notes the practical difficulty of calculating proportional fees with fractions.
4
Present the Sahibayn's argument
The Sahibayn analogize the fee to expenses of ownership (mu'unat al-milk), such as fees for measuring, weighing, or digging a well. These are proportional to the benefit received. Hence, the divider's fee should be by shares.
Their analogy: if one partner has 1/3 and the other 2/3, the fee is split 1/3 and 2/3.
5
Analyze Imam's response to the analogy
Imam distinguishes the cases: measuring/weighing fees for division are themselves disputed, and if not for division, the work varies; digging a well involves moving soil, which varies. The work of distinction in property division is collective and uniform, so the fee does not vary.
Imam's view is considered more sound and is mufta bihi in the school.
6
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: in a case with shares 1/3 and 2/3, Imam says the fee is half-half, while the Sahibayn say 1/3-2/3. The relied-upon position is Imam's view (by heads).
Later Hanafi texts prefer Imam's view for convenience and to avoid dispute.

Commentary
This passage covers two related issues: (1) the recommendation to appoint a divider (qasim) paid from the public treasury to avoid fees; (2) the dispute over how to distribute the fee among partners if the divider is paid by them. The Hanafi school recommends appointing a divider whose salary is from bayt al-mal, similar to a judge, because division benefits the public. If that is not possible, a divider is appointed and his fee is distributed among the partners. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the fee is divided equally by heads (bi-adad al-ru'us) regardless of each partner's share. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that it is divided proportionally to each partner's share (qadr al-ansiba), and this is also the view of Imam al-Shafi'i. The reasoning: Sahibayn analogize it to expenses of ownership (mu'unat al-milk) like measuring and weighing fees, which are proportional. Abu Hanifa argues that the fee is for the work of distinction (tamyiz), which does not vary with the share amount; distinguishing a small share from a large one requires the same effort. He also notes the practical difficulty of calculating fractions. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view, as it is simpler and avoids dispute.

Key Terms
قاسم (qasim): The person appointed to divide jointly owned property among the partners.
بعدد الرؤوس (bi'adad al-ru'us): Distribution of the fee equally among the number of partners, regardless of their shares.
تمييز (tamyiz): The act of distinguishing or separating each partner's share in the division process.
مؤونة الملك (mu'unat al-milk): Expenses of ownership that vary with the share size.

Qualities of the Divider and Rules of Appointment
Requirements for the divider and prohibition of predetermined appointment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three essential qualities of a divider in Hanafi law and identify when partners may divide property themselves without a court-appointed divider.

Core Idea
A divider must be just, trustworthy, and knowledgeable; but adult partners can divide themselves if all agree, except when a minor is involved.

In Islamic property law, dividing jointly owned assets often requires an expert divider. The Hanafi school specifies three essential qualities for such a person: justice, trustworthiness, and knowledge of division procedures. However, the law also grants flexibility—if all co-owners are adults and consent, they may divide the property themselves. Understanding these rules prevents disputes and ensures fair and efficient partitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions for a valid divider
The text states a divider must be 'adl (just), amin (trustworthy), and 'alim bi al-qismah (knowledgeable in division). Note that these are customary requirements, not strictly legal obligations.
The commentary clarifies that the 'obligation' is wujub 'urfi (customary), meaning strongly recommended.
2
Understand the prohibition of predetermined appointment
The text says 'no single divider is predetermined'. This prevents a judge from forcing a specific divider onto the parties, as that could lead to inflated fees.
The rationale is to avoid the divider arbitrarily increasing his fee beyond the market rate.
3
Recognize the prohibition of partnership among dividers
Dividers are forbidden from forming a partnership because it would lead to monopolistic pricing and reduce competition.
Without partnership, each divider hastens to offer his services, fearing loss of opportunity.
4
Identify the exception for self-division
If all parties are adults and agree, they may divide the property themselves without a divider, because division has an element of exchange (mubada'a) and no compulsion.
This is permissible (ja'iz) provided the division is fair and all consent.
5
Note the minor's special case
If a minor is among the co-owners, self-division is not valid because the adults have no authority over the minor's share. A guardian or court-appointed divider is needed.
The minor's representation requires a lawful guardian or a qadi's oversight.

Commentary
This passage states the conditions for a valid divider (qasim) and additional rules regarding his appointment. The divider must possess three qualities: (1) 'adl (justice), because his role is akin to a judge; (2) amanah (trustworthiness), since his statement is relied upon; (3) knowledge of division methods ('ilm bi al-qismah), as competence depends on it. The commentary notes that the 'obligation' here is not a legal obligation but a customary one (wujub 'urfi) meaning preference. Additionally, no specific individual is forced upon the partners as divider; they are free to agree. If they agree, they may divide themselves without a divider, except if a minor is involved, as they lack authority over him. Finally, dividers are prohibited from forming a partnership for this work, because it would lead to higher fees due to laxity; they should compete individually. The Hanafi school emphasizes these regulations to ensure fairness and efficiency in division.

Key Terms
عدالة (adala): The quality of being just, avoiding major sins and not persisting in minor sins, required for the divider.
أمانة (amana): Trustworthiness, i.e., honesty and reliability in declaring the division.
القسام (al-qsām): Plural of qasim, meaning the dividers engaged in the profession of dividing property.
