Attending a Walimah with Play and Singing: Hanafi Rulings
Ruling on attending a wedding feast when play and singing occur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on attending a wedding feast when play and singing occur, and distinguish the conditions under which one must stay, leave, or not attend.

Core Idea
Responding to walimah is sunnah, but if sin is on the table or you are a leader, you must leave; otherwise, stay.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a common scenario: you are invited to a wedding feast (walimah) and there is play and singing. The ruling depends on when the sin occurs—before, after, or on the table—and on your own status in the community. The author uses a Quranic proof for the case of sin on the table, and clarifies that the sunnah of attending does not require you to remain in the presence of open sin.

Guided Path
1
Determine timing of sin relative to attendance
Identify whether the play/singing were known before attending, occur after sitting, or are present on the dining cloth. The ruling differs for each scenario.
The passage uses the word 'thamma' to indicate existence at the time of invitation.
2
Apply ruling for sin occurring after sitting
If play/singing start after you have sat down, you stay and eat because responding to the walimah is sunnah. You should try to prevent the sin if possible.
Analogous to not abandoning funeral prayer due to a wailing woman.
3
Consider the attendee's status: muqtada bih or not
If you are a person whose actions are followed (muqtada bih), e.g., a scholar, and you cannot prevent the sin, you must leave. Otherwise, you may stay.
This prevents setting a bad example and 'disgrace to religion'.
4
Apply ruling for sin on the dining cloth
If the play/singing are literally on the table (ma'idah), you must not sit. This is based on Quran 6:68: 'Do not sit with the wrongdoing people after being reminded.'
The verse is used as a direct proof (dalil).
5
Apply ruling for known sin before attending
If you know beforehand that there will be play/singing, you should not attend at all, as the obligation to respond is lifted when evil is present.
The passage explicitly states: 'he does not attend'.

Commentary
This passage gives the ruling on attending a wedding feast (walimah) where there is play (la'b) and singing (ghina'). The key distinctions are based on timing and location. If the play and singing occur after the person has attended (i.e., they start later), he should sit and eat, because answering the invitation is a sunnah that should not be abandoned due to the bid'ah of others. Ruling on Attending a Wedding Feast with Prohibited Entertainment
Commentary on attending a wedding feast with prohibited entertainment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on attending a walimah where prohibited play and singing are present and apply the distinctions based on role model status, location of sin, and prior knowledge.

Core Idea
Attend if sin appears after arrival unless you're a role model; avoid if sin is on the table or known beforehand.

Attending a wedding feast (walimah) is a recommended sunnah in the Hanafi school. However, when prohibited entertainment like play and singing is present, the ruling becomes nuanced. The classical Hanafi texts provide clear conditions: one may sit and eat if the sin occurs after arrival and he is not a role model, but must leave if he is a role model, and must not sit if the sin is on the dining table. This lesson unpacks these conditions and their basis in the Quran and analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is responding to an invitation to a walimah. The default ruling is sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah). Determine this from the text.
The text contrasts with some schools that consider it wajib.
2
Distinguish scenarios based on timing of sin
The ruling depends on whether the prohibited play and singing occur before or after arriving, and whether they are on the dining table or elsewhere. Identify each scenario in the text.
The text mentions: sin after arrival, sin on the table, and sin known beforehand.
3
Apply the role model condition
Even if sin occurs after arrival, if the invitee is a community leader (muqtada bihi) and cannot prevent the sin, he must leave. Determine if the person is considered a role model.
The text uses the analogy of not abandoning funeral prayer due to a wailer.
4
Locate the dalil for sin on the table
The verse (Quran 6:68) prohibits sitting with wrongdoers after being reminded. If the sin is on the dining table, this verse applies directly.
This overrides the sunnah of responding to the invitation.
5
Evaluate the prior knowledge condition
If the invitee knows beforehand that prohibited entertainment will be present, he should not attend, as responding is not obligatory in the face of a known sin.
This differs from the scenario where sin appears unexpectedly after arrival.

Ruling on Looking at Spouse's Private Parts
Ruling with proof: permissibility of looking at spouse's private parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a man looking at his wife's and lawful slave woman's private parts, identify the hadith proof, and explain the recommended practice.

Core Idea
Looking at spouse's private parts is permissible; the hadith exception proves it, but caution is better.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies a common fiqh question: may a husband look at his wife's private parts? The answer is not simply 'no' – it's permissible, with proof from the Prophet's command. However, the text also advises restraint, balancing permissibility with piety.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a man looking at the private part of his wife and his slave woman. The ruling is mubah (permissible), whether with or without desire.
The text also covers the reverse: woman and slave woman looking at husband/master.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith: 'Lower your gaze except from your wife and your slave woman.' This is an ahad hadith, so it is zanni al-thubut but qat'i al-dalalah due to the clear exception.
The hadith is cited in the passage.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The exception clause (إلا عن زوجتك وأمتك) explicitly permits looking at wife and slave woman. The word 'your' (أمتك) conditions that the slave woman must be lawful for intercourse.
The passage clarifies that a slave woman must be of a religion allowing intercourse (believer or kitabiyya) and not a mahram.
4
Note the recommended practice
Although looking is permissible, it is better (الأولى) not to look generally because it may cause forgetfulness. However, during intercourse, looking is recommended for greater pleasure.
This is a point of preference, not a ruling change.

Core Idea
A man may look at the places of adornment of his mahram, but not the back, belly, or thigh.

The Quran commands women not to display their adornment except to certain relatives. How do we determine what constitutes 'adornment'? The Hanafi school interprets it as the places where adornment is worn, leading to a specific list of permissible body parts. The Shafi'i school takes a broader view. 4
Contrast with the Shafi'i position

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage addresses the prohibition of combining two sisters for sexual relations, whether in marriage or slave ownership. Borrowing a Garment to Pawn It and Violating Conditions
Ruling on borrowing for pawn and liability for violating conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on borrowing a garment for pawning, explain the liability when the borrower violates the lender's conditions, and apply the qiyas between milk al-yad and milk al-ayn.

Core Idea
Borrowing for pawn is valid; violating conditions makes both borrower and pledgee liable at the lender's option.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school regarding borrowing a garment specifically to pawn it. The key distinction is between ownership (milk al-ayn) and mere possession (milk al-yad). When a lender specifies conditions, violating them constitutes transgression, giving the lender a choice of recourse. We will analyze the qiyas (analogy) that supports this ruling and explore the scenarios of violation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is borrowing a garment to pawn it. The Hanafi ruling is that it is sahih (valid). Note that the contract is valid because the lender voluntarily grants the right of possession (milk al-yad).
The ruling distinguishes between the validity of the contract and post-violation liability.
2
Locate the dalil (qiyas)
The proof is analogical reasoning (qiyas). The voluntary grant of mere possession (milk al-yad) is analogized to the voluntary grant of full ownership (milk al-ayn). Just as a gift of ownership is valid, so is a grant of possession for pawning.
This qiyas is explicit in the text: 'yuqāsu al-tabarru' bi milk al-yad waḥdahu 'alā al-tabarru' bi ithbāt milk al-'ayn wal-yad jamī'an.'
3
Analyze the conditions and violation
If the lender specifies an amount, type, or place of pawning, and the borrower violates that specification, both the borrower and the pledgee become transgressors. The lender has the option to hold either liable.
Specification is beneficial because it protects the lender's interests (ease of payment and higher recovery at destruction).
4
Determine the liability options and outcomes
If the lender holds the borrower liable, the borrower becomes the owner of the garment by paying compensation, and the pawn contract is between him and the pledgee. If the lender holds the pledgee liable, the pledgee can recover from the borrower both the compensation and the debt.
The text also details three scenarios based on the garment's value relative to the named debt, affecting the amount of liability.

This passage is a detailed ruling from the Hanafi school on borrowing a garment for the purpose of pawning it. Marriage on a Cut Injury: Mahr and Diya
Ruling on marriage dower and diya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the dower (mahr) and blood money (diya) when a woman marries a man on the condition of a cut injury, and determine the set-off rules for intentional and inadvertent cases.

Core Idea
Marriage on a cut injury pardons only the cut, not resulting death – creating separate mahr and diya obligations.

This lesson examines a complex fiqh scenario where injury, pardon, and marriage intersect. A woman cuts a man's hand, then marries him with the cut as the specified dower. If he later dies from the injury, the initial pardon of the cut does not cover the death. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules for the resulting mahr and diya obligations, distinguishing between intentional and inadvertent injury, and whether the victim dies or recovers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, note the two main cases: the cut is intentional ('amd) or inadvertent (khaṭa'). The husband may die from the injury or recover. These distinctions determine the liability.
2
Determine the initial liability for the cut
For intentional cut, the liability is retaliation (qiṣāṣ) – a personal right, not property, so it cannot be valid dower. For inadvertent cut, the liability is monetary compensation (arsh), which can be dower.
Retaliation between genders does not apply to limbs in Hanafi law, but the principle here is that qiṣāṣ is not property.
3
Analyze the effect of marriage on the cut
Marriage on the cut includes a pardon of the original liability for the cut (qiṣāṣ or arsh). But this pardon does not extend to any later death arising from the cut; the death is a separate liability.
The marriage is treated as consent to drop the claim for the hand, not for the life.
4
Derive the obligations upon death
If intentional and death occurs, she owes mahr mithl (because the specified dower – qiṣāṣ – is invalid) and diya for the life from her own wealth (since 'amd, not borne by 'āqila). If inadvertent, the specified dower (arsh) becomes void when death occurs (since arsh for hand is subsumed by diya for life), so again mahr mithl is due; diya is on her 'āqila.
The two obligations (mahr she owes to his heirs, diya they owe to her) may be set off if equal.
5
Consider the recovery scenario
If he recovers, the liability remains the arsh of the hand (even in intentional cases, by consensus), which is valid dower, so she gets no extra mahr. No death, no diya.
Recovery confirms the injury was limited to the hand.

Woman Cuts Man's Hand, Marries Him, He Dies: Mahr and Diyah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of pardon in marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Abu Hanifa's principle that pardon for a limb does not pardon subsequent death, and calculate mahr al-mithl and diyah in this scenario, distinguishing the view of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Pardon for a limb does not pardon death arising from it; marriage on retaliation yields mahr al-mithl, not the hand.

This lesson unpacks a complex scenario where a woman injures a man and then marries him on that hand as dower. When the husband later dies from the injury, multiple legal obligations arise: mahr, blood money, and the effect of implied pardon. We will examine Abu Hanifa's reasoning and the disagreement with his two companions, focusing on the crucial distinction between pardon for a limb and pardon for its consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exact scenario
Determine whether the cutting was intentional or accidental, and whether the husband died from the injury. The ruling differs based on these two variables.
The passage treats intentional and accidental cases separately; note the different outcomes for mahr and diyah.
2
State Abu Hanifa's principle on pardon
Abu Hanifa holds that pardon (afw) for a specific limb injury does not extend to any death that results from it. Marriage on the hand likewise does not imply marriage on what develops from it.
This is the core of the intra-madhhab dispute: the Sahibayn are implied to disagree, holding that pardon for the limb does extend to the death.
3
Analyze the intentional case
When the cutting is intentional, its legal consequence is qisas (retaliation) for the limb, which is not property. Thus it cannot serve as valid mahr. The marriage contract is still valid, but mahr al-mithl becomes obligatory. Additionally, the woman must pay diyah (full blood money) for the death because the implied pardon did not cover the death. Since it was intentional, the diyah comes from her own wealth, not her 'aqlah.
Key point: The woman owes both mahr al-mithl and diyah; they are set off if equal, otherwise balance paid.
4
Analyze the accidental case
In accidental cutting, the liability is arsh (compensation) for the hand, which is property. Thus naming it as mahr is valid. However, when death occurs, the arsh is subsumed; mahr al-mithl becomes due. The diyah for accidental killing is on the woman's 'aqlah, but since the woman's mahr is from the husband's estate, and the diyah is effectively transferred to her as mahr, the liability of the 'aqlah is reduced.
The passage also discusses the bequest scenario when mahr al-mithl is less than diyah.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf with Sahibayn
Although not detailed here, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that pardon for the limb does extend to the death. Practically, this would mean that in the intentional case, the diyah would be reduced or forgiven entirely because the marriage implied a full pardon. The text presents Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon one.
The difference affects the amount the woman ultimately owes: she pays full diyah under Abu Hanifa, but less (or none) under Sahibayn.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh: the gesture and writing of a person who is permanently mute (akhras) are legally equivalent to spoken words in all contracts and transactions. The passage also contrasts the mute with a person whose tongue is temporarily tied (mu'taqal al-lisan) due to illness, clarifying that their gesture is not valid unless the condition becomes prolonged. We will explore the reasoning, the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school, and the practical implications.

The general rule is based on necessity and analogy to speech.

Note the exception: 'لا في حد'. Understand why hudud are excluded: they are repelled by doubts, and gesture/writing are open to interpretation. Also note that in qisas, the victim's gesture is valid because it is a personal right.

The difference is based on permanence of incapacity and potential negligence.

The Hanafi position is more cautious due to the possibility of negligence.

When Does a Wife's Prevention of Entry Become Nushuz?
Ruling on wife refusing to let husband enter her home as nushuz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a wife preventing her husband from entering her home, identify when it constitutes nushuz leading to loss of maintenance, and distinguish the exception when she is demanding separate accommodation.

Core Idea
Prevention of entry is nushuz unless the wife claims her right to separate housing.

The marital relationship includes rights and duties regarding housing and obedience. The Hanafi school carefully delineates when a wife's refusal to let her husband enter her home is considered disobedience (nushuz) and when it is a legitimate assertion of her rights. This lesson examines the precise conditions that make her act nushuz and the exception that preserves her maintenance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Default Ruling
The act is the wife preventing her husband from entering her home while he resides with her. The default ruling: such prevention is nushuz (disobedience) because she is unreasonably withholding herself.
2
Determine the Condition for Nushuz
The prevention is nushuz only when the wife intends to stay in her house alone, without a valid right. If she instead intends to move him to his own house (demanding separate accommodation), it is not nushuz.
The husband is obligated to provide accommodation, so demanding it is a valid right.
3
Apply the Exception
If the prevention is to enforce her right to a separate home, the act is not nushuz. This is analogous to her imprisoning herself to collect her dower (mahr), which is permissible.
4
Note the Consequence
If the prevention is nushuz, the wife loses her right to maintenance (nafaqah) for the duration of her prevention. If it is not nushuz, maintenance continues.
5
Summarize the Ruling
The key distinction is the wife's intention and whether she has a valid right. Without a valid right, prevention is nushuz and she loses maintenance. With a valid right (like demanding separate accommodation), it is not nushuz.

Core Idea
A marriage contract requires explicit offer and acceptance; phrases that merely state a fact or suitability are insufficient.

This lesson examines three classic Hanafi scenarios involving Persian phrases used to express marriage. Nushuz: When Preventing the Husband Enters Disobedience

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of nushuz in the context of preventing the husband from entering, identify the exceptions, and apply the ruling to determine when maintenance is forfeited.

Core Idea
Preventing the husband from entering her house is nushuz unless she is exercising a valid right or the prevention is outside her control.

This passage from *Kanz al-Daqa'iq* defines nushuz specifically as the wife's act of preventing the husband from entering her home while he lives there. The ruling turns on the reason for prevention. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correctly applying maintenance (nafaqah) laws in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is the wife preventing her husband from entering her house when he is already living with her there. The default ruling is that this constitutes nushuz (disobedience), which is haram and results in loss of maintenance (nafaqah).
The key element: she prevents entry while he is residing in her house.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (talil)
The passage explains it is nushuz because she has imprisoned herself without right. The analogy is to imprisoning herself in someone else's house — she is withholding access to herself unjustly.
The illa (cause) is the unjust self-imprisonment.
3
Recognize the exceptions based on the wife's rights
Exceptions: (1) she prevents to assert her right to housing (when he wants to move her), (2) she restrains herself to collect her mahr, (3) she is physically prevented by debt or a usurper. In these, it is not nushuz.
Refusal of intercourse in his house is also not nushuz because he can force intercourse.
4
Apply the ruling to determine maintenance
If nushuz is established, maintenance stops. If an exception applies, maintenance continues because she is not disobedient.
In the debt/usurper case, the loss is beyond her control.
5
Distinguish from other scenarios
Compare the case where she lives in his house and refuses intercourse — this is not prevention because he can legally compel intercourse (in Hanafi law). Also, if she prevents him to move to his house, it's not nushuz because providing housing is his duty.
These distinctions prevent over-application of the nushuz ruling.

When a wife gifts her mahr to her husband and later dies, a dispute may arise between the husband and the heirs over whether the gift was made during health or during a death-illness. This affects whether the gift is treated as a valid transfer or a bequest subject to restrictions. The Hanafi ruling, supported by istihsan, gives preference to the husband's statement. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this preference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the ruling
State the scenario: wife gifted mahr to husband, died; heirs claim gift was during death-illness, husband claims in health. The ruling in the matn is 'al-qawlu lahu' – the husband's statement is accepted.
The ruling does not consider evidence; if evidence is brought, the husband's evidence is preferred per the commentary.
2
Analyze the qiyas argument
Understand the analogy: the gift is a new event (haditha). The general principle is that events are attributed to the nearest time (al-hawadith tudafu ila aqrab al-awqat). Since the nearest time before death is the death-illness, the qiyas would favor the heirs. This would make the husband's statement secondary.
This is a jaliyy (clear) analogy; the illa is the newness of the event.

The Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) here. The key factor: both parties agree that the mahr fell (sagata) from the husband due to the gift. In death-illness gifts to heirs, ownership transfers but with a potential obligation of return (dam). Since the mahr fell by agreement, the heir's claim of return is a new claim. The husband denies it, and the principle is that the denier's statement is taken (al-qawlu li al-munkir). Therefore, istihsan overrides qiyas.
The commentary from Fath and Takmila highlights this as the ground for istihsan.
4
Consider the evidentiary aspect
The passage notes that if the husband brings evidence (bayyina) that the gift was in health, his evidence is preferred over the heirs' evidence. This aligns with the procedural rule that the stronger evidence (or the evidence of the party whose claim is contrary to the presumption) is accepted.
This is an additional layer: the ruling applies in the absence of evidence, but if evidence exists, it is preferred.

Learning Objective

When a husband threatens his wife to force her to gift her mahr or to agree to a khul', the Hanafi school draws a sharp line between personal status and financial obligations.
