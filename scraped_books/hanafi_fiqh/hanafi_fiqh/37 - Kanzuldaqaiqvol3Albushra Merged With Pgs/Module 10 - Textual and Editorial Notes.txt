Three Types of Lineage Claims (Da'wa al-Nasab)
Defining the three types of lineage claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of da'wa al-nasab and state the legal effect of each.

Core Idea
The strength of a lineage claim depends on ownership at conception.

In Hanafi law, not all lineage claims are equal. The key factor is whether the claimant owned the mother at the time of conception. This passage introduces three types: the strongest establishes both lineage and umm walad status; a weaker one only establishes lineage for the present; and a special one concerns a father claiming his son's slave's child. Understanding this classification is essential for applying the detailed rulings in the chapter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the passage and list the three types of lineage claims. Note their Arabic names: da'wa al-istilad, da'wa al-tahrir, da'wa al-shubha.
Memorize the Arabic terminology; they are used throughout the chapter.
2
Analyze the basis of each type
For each type, determine whether the claimant owned the mother at the time of conception. This is the distinguishing factor.
Istilad requires ownership at conception; tahrir does not; shubha involves a father claiming his son's slave's child.
3
Determine the legal effect
Identify which types establish the mother as umm walad. Only da'wa al-istilad does because it asserts ownership at conception.
Da'wa al-tahrir does not make the mother umm walad; da'wa al-shubha establishes lineage but has its own conditions.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario with facts about ownership at conception, decide which type of da'wa applies and what legal consequences follow.
Practice by creating hypotheticals.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of lineage claims in Hanafi law. The first, da'wa al-istilad, is when the claimant asserts that the child was conceived while the mother was in his ownership. This is the strongest claim because it establishes both lineage and the mother's status as umm walad. The second, da'wa al-tahrir, is a mere claim of lineage without asserting ownership at conception; it only applies to the present moment but does not confer the status of umm walad. The third, da'wa al-shubha, is specific to a father claiming the child of his son's female slave; here lineage is established even without the son's confirmation, provided the female slave was in the son's ownership continuously from conception to the claim. This categorization is foundational for understanding the subsequent rulings in the chapter.

Key Terms
دعوى النسب (da'wa al-nasab): A legal claim asserting paternal lineage to a child.
دعوة استيلاد (da'wa al-istilad): A claim that the child was conceived while the mother was in the claimant's ownership, establishing both lineage and the mother's status as umm walad.
دعوة تحرير (da'wa al-tahrir): A claim of lineage that is confined to the present, without asserting ownership at conception; it does not make the mother umm walad.
دعوة شبهة (da'wa al-shubha): A claim by a father to the child of his son's female slave, where lineage is established even without the son's confirmation, given continuous ownership.

The Binding Effect of a Prior Lineage Confession
Ruling on a boy's lineage when the possessor first attributes him to an absent person then claims him

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a later paternity claim after a prior confession, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A confession of lineage for another, once made, cannot be undone — it bars the confessor's own later claim.

This passage from the chapter on claims (da'wa) addresses a delicate issue: when a man in possession of a boy first attributes him to an absent person and later claims him as his own son. The ruling hinges on the legal strength of a confession (iqrar). We will examine how Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on whether the absent person's denial can void the initial confession, and why the school ultimately follows Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the two conflicting claims
A man (the confessor) has a boy in his possession. He first says the boy is the son of an absent person (X). Later, he says the boy is his own son. Determine the legal status of the later claim.
2
Understand the three scenarios regarding the absent person's response
The absent person may: (1) confirm the confession, (2) deny it, or (3) remain silent. The ruling differs by scenario. Confirm and silence lead to consensus that the later claim is invalid.
Consensus applies only to the first and third scenarios.
3
Focus on the dispute: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn when the absent person denies
If the absent person denies being the father, Abu Hanifa holds the later claim is still invalid. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue the denial voids the prior confession, making the later claim valid. Trace the reasoning of each side.
This is the core intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's qiyas to the slave freedom case
Abu Hanifa analogizes to a case where someone confesses that another's slave is free, and the owner denies. The confession remains effective against the confessor: if he later buys the slave, the slave becomes free. Similarly, the lineage confession persists against the confessor, barring his own claim.
The qiyas is based on the principle that a confession is a binding proof against the confessor (hujja fi haqq nafsihi).

Claim of lineage. He said: he is the son of so-and-so, then said: he is my son, he is not his son, even if he denies that he is his son. And if the boy is in the hand of a man, and the person in possession confesses that he is the son of the absent so-and-so, then says: he is my son, he is not his son at any time, neither presently nor in the future. Commentary
This passage opens a discussion of the rules of claiming lineage (da'wa al-nasab) within the chapter on claims. The text presents a case: a man in whose possession a boy is found first declares that the boy is the son of an absent person, then later claims the boy as his own son. The ruling is that the boy is not his son, neither at the time of the second claim nor for the future. The commentary explains the reasoning: the initial confession establishes the lineage for the absent person, and such a confession, once made, cannot be undone by a later contradictory claim. The absent person's reaction is considered: if he confirms or remains silent, the confession stands, blocking the later claim by consensus. If the absent person denies, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that the later claim is still invalid because the confession remains effective against the confessor himself (it is hujja fi haqq nafsihi). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) argue that the denial voids the confession, clearing the way for the later claim. The text follows with Abu Hanifa's analogy: if someone confesses that another's slave is free and the owner denies, the confession does not revert; the confessor is bound by it such that if he later acquires the slave, he becomes free. Similarly, the confession of lineage persists, preventing the later claim. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as indicated in the classical sources. This ruling demonstrates the Hanafi principle that a confession of lineage is a strong legal acknowledgment that cannot be rescinded by the confessor.

نسب (nasab): Lineage or paternity; legal relationship by blood.
جحد (juhud): Denial or rejection of a claim, here the absent person's denial of being the father.

Connected vs. Disconnected Exception in Acknowledgment
Explanation of the rules of exception in acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on connected exception in acknowledgment, identify the intra-Hanafi difference on acknowledgment of debased coins, and apply the mufta bihi position on interruptions due to necessity.

Core Idea
An exception in acknowledgment must be connected; but necessity may connect the broken.

This lesson examines the procedural rules of exception (istithnā') in the context of acknowledgment (iqrār). The central principle is that an exception must be uttered immediately after the acknowledgment to be credited. However, the Hanafi school debates whether a break due to necessity invalidates the connection, and also whether the exception in a special case of debased coins is treated differently. We will trace the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and see which position is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule on exception
Identify the default ruling: if the person says 'except that it decreases' immediately (connected), he is believed; otherwise not.
The exception must be uttered in the same breath as the acknowledgment.
2
Examine the dispute on ziyūf (debased coins)
When someone acknowledges a debt of debased coins without mentioning the transaction, there is a reported consensus and a dispute within the Hanafi school. Abu Hanifa holds that even if the exception of 'ziyūf' is connected, the acknowledger is not believed and must pay good coins (jiyād). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if connected, the exception is accepted.
The dispute is about the credibility of the exception, not the validity of the acknowledgment itself.
3
Analyze the effect of interruptions due to necessity
If a break occurs because of natural necessities like breath, cough, or someone covering the mouth, Abu Yusuf considers the exception still connected. This view is preferred for fatwa because it accommodates human speech patterns.
The other two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad?) might differ, but the text does not specify their view on necessity.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The fatwa is given with Abu Yusuf's view on interruption: the exception is considered connected even with such breaks, making it valid.
This is a practical concession to avoid hardship.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqā'iq addresses the rules of exception (istithnā') in the context of acknowledgment (iqrār). The core principle is that an exception must be connected (muttaṣil) to the acknowledgment to be accepted; if disconnected (munfaṣil), the acknowledgment stands without the exception. The text then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the case where a person acknowledges a debt of 'debased coins' (ziyūf) without specifying the transaction. Some scholars claim consensus that if the exception of debased quality is connected, the acknowledger is believed; but others report that there is disagreement: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the acknowledger is not believed even if connected, and must pay good coins (jiyād); according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), he is believed if connected, not if disconnected. Finally, the text resolves a practical issue: if a break occurs due to natural necessity like breath, cough, or someone covering the mouth, Abu Yusuf considers it still connected (and this is the fatwa position), because people need to speak at length and cannot always utter the exception in one breath. This shows the Hanafi school's pragmatic approach to procedural rules.

وصل/فصل (waṣl/fasl): Connection or disconnection of the exception from the acknowledgment.

Definition and Types of Wala' in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition and classification of wala' as a legal concept.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define wala' (patronage) according to Hanafi jurists, distinguish its two types, and state its linguistic derivation.

Core Idea
Wala' is a bond of mutual help and inheritance, established by emancipation, with two types: emancipation and mutual alliance.

The Book of Patronage (Kitab al-Wala') is placed after the Book of the Mukatab because patronage is an effect of emancipation that follows after the mukatab's freedom. This lesson focuses on the definition, derivation, and types of wala' as outlined in the opening of the chapter. Understanding these foundations is essential before examining the detailed rulings.

Guided Path
1
Tracing the Etymology
Identify that 'al-wala' is derived from 'al-wali' (closeness) or 'al-muwalah' (mutual help). Note how the linguistic root points to the legal concept.
The fatha on the waw is distinctive.
2
Identifying the Legal Definition
Contrast the lexical meaning (exchange, help, connection) with the juristic meaning: mutual help (tanāsur) through emancipation or contract.
3
Distinguishing the Two Types
List the two types: wala' al-'itaqa (patronage of emancipation) and wala' al-muwalah (patronage of mutual alliance). Note the cause of each.
The text focuses on the first type.
4
Locating the Pillars and Conditions
Identify the rukn (the declaration or contract) and the shart (emancipator must be free and Muslim).
These are prerequisites for wala' to be valid.
5
Summarizing the Ruling
State that the ruling includes bearing criminal liability (aql) during the patron's life and inheritance after death.
This summarizes the legal effect.

The Book of Patronage. Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates. This book is an exposition of the rulings of patronage. It is with fatha on the waw, derived from al-wali meaning closeness or from al-muwalah. (Mulla Miskeen) He placed the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab because patronage is among the effects of the mukatab due to the removal of ownership of the person upon payment of the kitaba. Although it is among the effects of emancipation, the reasons for the order of previous books brought the mukatab to this position, so it was necessary to delay the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab so that the effect does not precede the cause. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 138/8) Know that the discussion of patronage has aspects: first, its derivation; second, its evidence; third, its cause; fourth, its lexical meaning; fifth, its meaning according to jurists; sixth, its pillar; seventh, its condition; eighth, its ruling. As for the first: it is derived from al-wali, which is closeness, i.e., the occurrence of the second immediately after the first without interval, or from al-muwalah; it is said: wali al-shay' when it occurs after it without interval, and it is mufa'ala from al-wilayah with fatha, which means help and love. Its evidence is his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," and his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage is like the link of lineage," i.e., a connection like the connection of lineage. Its cause is emancipation according to the majority because the master bestowed favor upon his slave by emancipation. The more correct view is that its cause is emancipation from his ownership because it is attributed to him, and attribution indicates specification, and because whoever inherits his relative, that relative becomes free by him and his patronage belongs to him, even though there is no emancipation from his side. As for its lexical meaning: it is an expression for exchange and help, or an expression for connection and friendship, and the friend is called wali due to mutual help and cooperation for his beloved and friend. According to jurists, it is an expression for mutual help, whether by emancipation or by a contract of mutual alliance. As for its pillar: his saying: "I emancipate him," or he owns the relative, or he contracts mutual alliance. It is required that the emancipator be eligible for inheritance, i.e., free and Muslim. Its ruling: that he bears the liability for criminal acts during the life of his emancipator, and inherits from him after his death. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 128/8) Patronage: Know that patronage used in the terminology of Sharia, by which mutual help occurs, is of two types: first, patronage of emancipation, also called patronage of grace; its cause is emancipation from his ownership whether it occurs by his action, i.e., emancipation and what is legally similar to emancipation such as purchasing a relative, accepting a gift, etc., or without his action, such as inheriting his relative. Second, patronage of mutual alliance; its cause is a contract of mutual help and friendship between them. The purpose of each is mutual help. The Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, established both types of patronage, saying: "The client of a people is from them, and their ally is from them." The author began first with explaining the first category, saying: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," i.e., virtual kinship and help are established for the one who emancipates the slave. This mentioned text is the wording of the hadith narrated by the six imams from Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, from the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." This hadith by its generality includes every emancipator, whether through tadbir, kitaba, istilad, or owning a relative. This is a transmitted proof. Its rational proof (qiyas): that the slave is legally as if dead; do you not see that many rulings reserved for the living, such as being a judge, witness, and ownership of wealth, are not established for him? So emancipation is a revival of him, because the rulings of revival are established for him, like revival by giving birth. So he inherits just as the father inherits from his child, and for this it is called patronage of grace. The emancipator may be Muslim or dhimmi. = By tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative, and the condition of sa'ibah is void. And if he emancipates a pregnant woman from her husband who is a slave, it does not transfer. (Answer to 'law') The subject, its predicate: a man's slave-woman. Because they inherit from each other through patronage like Muslims. Emancipation is general, whether voluntary or obligatory like expiatory emancipation, whether for money or not. (Collected from commentaries and glosses) By tadbir: i.e., he made a tadbir of his slave, then he died, and he became free from one-third of his wealth. (Ayni) And by kitaba: i.e., he made a kitaba with his slave and he paid the kitaba amount, so he became free. (Ayni) And istilad: i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation is by istilad, i.e., the master had a child from his slave-woman and then died, and she becomes free from all his wealth. This is conceivable. But it is objected: how can patronage be for the master through tadbir and istilad, when they become free after his death? The answer is: this applies when the master apostatizes and joins the abode of war, such that it is judged that his mudabbar and umm walad become free, then he returns as a Muslim and then his mudabbar and umm walad die; then patronage belongs to him. The better way is to say: what is meant is that the establishment of patronage for the 'asaba of the master is only due to its establishment for the master, for he is entitled to it, and the cause of emancipation from him, then it passes from him to his 'asaba. (Fath with addition) And owning a relative: i.e., he owns a relative of mahram lineage. [ ] i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation occurred by the master owning his relative, i.e., he owns his father or son by purchase, gift, bequest, or the like, so he becomes free by him, due to the generality of the hadith. The conclusion is that each of tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative is an emancipation by which patronage is established. The woman is like the man in all that, due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Women have no share of patronage except what they emancipate," to the end of the hadith. (Fath and Ayni with addition) And 'the condition of sa'ibah etc.': meaning, if the master emancipates his slave and stipulates that he will not inherit from him, the condition is void because it contradicts the ruling of Sharia, so he inherits from him, just as in lineage if he stipulates that he will not inherit. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 130/8) According to Ahmad, the condition of sa'ibah is valid. Sa'ibah is derived from saba, i.e., he ran and went every direction, and by it is meant emancipation without patronage. (Ayni, Takmila and others) Al-qinn: the slave owned by another man; she and her fetus become free. (Ayni) 'It does not transfer etc.': meaning, if a man emancipates a slave-woman who is pregnant from her husband who is a slave owned by another, while her marriage exists, and she gives birth to the child in less than six months, the patronage of the fetus does not transfer from the patrons of the mother to the patrons of the father ever; because the fetus is freed by the emancipation of its mother, and the emancipation of its mother is intended, so similarly he is freed as intended because he is part of the mother, and the master performed emancipation on all her parts intentionally, and patronage does not transfer from the emancipator due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." The existence of the fetus at the time of emancipation is known by her giving birth in less than six months from the time of emancipation to ascertain the time of conception. (Ramz al-Haqa'iq: 304/2) Similarly, if she gives birth to two children, one in less than six months and the other in more, and between them less than six months, patronage is established for the patrons of the mother and does not transfer to the patrons of the father because they are twins. We only said: 'and she gives birth to the child in less than six months' because if she gives birth at six months or more, the ruling is not so, as will come later. We only conditioned the emancipation of the slave-woman while the marriage of the slave husband exists because after the marriage this detail does not apply, and describing the husband as a slave is not for exclusion but is by way of example, because the ruling for a mukatab or mudabbar husband is similar. Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Patronage (Kitab al-Wala') which is the final book in the chapter on emancipation. The author first explains that 'al-wala' (patronage) is a legal relationship of inheritance that is established through emancipation. The word is derived from al-wali (closeness) or al-muwalah (mutual help). The text then systematically covers eight aspects: derivation, evidence, cause, lexical meaning, technical meaning, pillar, condition, and ruling. The primary evidence is the prophetic hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' (patronage belongs to the one who emancipates) reported by the six major hadith collections. Additionally, a rational proof (qiyas) is provided: a slave is legally 'dead' in many respects (cannot be a judge, witness, or own property), so emancipation is like bringing him to life, similar to how a father's relationship with his child gives inheritance rights. The cause of patronage is emancipation from the master's own ownership. The text distinguishes two types: wala' al-'itaqa (patronage of emancipation) and wala' al-muwalah (patronage of mutual alliance). The former is the focus here. The pillar (rukn) is the declaration of emancipation or contract of alliance. The condition is that the emancipator be eligible to inherit (free Muslim). The ruling is that the patron bears criminal liability (aql) and inherits. The text then expands on the different means of emancipation that establish patronage: tadbir (declaration that a slave will be free after master's death), kitaba (contract to purchase freedom), istilad (having a child with a slave-woman), and owning a relative (e.g., buying one's father). It clarifies that a condition to exclude inheritance (sa'ibah) is void in the Hanafi school (contrary to Ahmad Ibn Hanbal). A specific case is discussed: if a man emancipates a pregnant slave-woman whose husband is a slave owned by another, the child's patronage remains with the mother's patrons (the emancipator) and does not transfer to the father's patrons, because the child is freed as part of the mother. This is only true if the birth occurs within six months of emancipation, proving the child was conceived before.

Key Terms
الولاء (al-wala'): A legal relationship of mutual help and inheritance between the emancipator and the freed slave, established by emancipation.
التناصر (al-tanāsur): Mutual help and support, the essence of wala' according to jurists.
العقل (al-'aql): Liability for blood money or criminal acts; one of the rulings of wala'.

Evidence for Wala': Hadith and Qiyas
Evidence (dalil) for wala' from hadith and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to cite the primary evidence (hadith) for wala', explain its rational proof (qiyas), and evaluate the epistemological strength of the evidence.

Core Idea
The hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' is the foundational text; its generality and qiyas confirm that emancipation creates a patronage relationship.

The previous lesson defined wala'. Now we turn to the evidence that establishes this legal concept. The author cites a famous hadith and a rational analogy. Understanding the strength and implications of this evidence is crucial for deriving rulings and resolving disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identifying the Primary Dalil
Locate the hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' and note that it is narrated by the six major hadith collections from Aisha. Understand its status as a widely transmitted ahad hadith.
Though ahad, it is muttafaq 'alayh in meaning.
2
Analyzing the Wajh al-Dalalah
Break down the Arabic: 'al-wala'' with definite article 'al' indicates the whole category, and 'li' (for/to) indicates exclusive ownership. Thus, patronage belongs exclusively to the emancipator.
This is a clear textual indication (qat'i al-dalala).
3
Examining the Rational Proof (Qiyas)
Understand the qiyas: a slave is legally 'dead' (e.g., cannot own property, be a witness). Emancipation 'revives' him, similar to a father giving birth. Therefore, the emancipator inherits as a father does from his child.
This is a reasoning from analogy.
4
Considering the Scope of the Evidence
The hadith covers all forms of emancipation (tadbir, kitaba, istilad, owning a relative). The qiyas supports this general application.
No exception mentioned in the text.
5
Epistemological Assessment
The hadith is zanni al-thubut (ahad) but qat'i al-dalala (clear meaning). The qiyas provides additional support. In Hanafi usul, such evidence is sufficient to establish a ruling.
Knowing this helps in weighing evidence against other schools.

The Book of Patronage. Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates. This book is an exposition of the rulings of patronage. It is with fatha on the waw, derived from al-wali meaning closeness or from al-muwalah. (Mulla Miskeen) He placed the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab because patronage is among the effects of the mukatab due to the removal of ownership of the person upon payment of the kitaba. Although it is among the effects of emancipation, the reasons for the order of previous books brought the mukatab to this position, so it was necessary to delay the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab so that the effect does not precede the cause. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 138/8) Know that the discussion of patronage has aspects: first, its derivation; second, its evidence; third, its cause; fourth, its lexical meaning; fifth, its meaning according to jurists; sixth, its pillar; seventh, its condition; eighth, its ruling. As for the first: it is derived from al-wali, which is closeness, i.e., the occurrence of the second immediately after the first without interval, or from al-muwalah; it is said: wali al-shay' when it occurs after it without interval, and it is mufa'ala from al-wilayah with fatha, which means help and love. Its evidence is his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," and his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage is like the link of lineage," i.e., a connection like the connection of lineage. Its cause is emancipation according to the majority because the master bestowed favor upon his slave by emancipation. The more correct view is that its cause is emancipation from his ownership because it is attributed to him, and attribution indicates specification, and because whoever inherits his relative, that relative becomes free by him and his patronage belongs to him, even though there is no emancipation from his side. As for its lexical meaning: it is an expression for exchange and help, or an expression for connection and friendship, and the friend is called wali due to mutual help and cooperation for his beloved and friend. According to jurists, it is an expression for mutual help, whether by emancipation or by a contract of mutual alliance. As for its pillar: his saying: "I emancipate him," or he owns the relative, or he contracts mutual alliance. It is required that the emancipator be eligible for inheritance, i.e., free and Muslim. Its ruling: that he bears the liability for criminal acts during the life of his emancipator, and inherits from him after his death. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 128/8) Patronage: Know that patronage used in the terminology of Sharia, by which mutual help occurs, is of two types: first, patronage of emancipation, also called patronage of grace; its cause is emancipation from his ownership whether it occurs by his action, i.e., emancipation and what is legally similar to emancipation such as purchasing a relative, accepting a gift, etc., or without his action, such as inheriting his relative. Second, patronage of mutual alliance; its cause is a contract of mutual help and friendship between them. The purpose of each is mutual help. The Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, established both types of patronage, saying: "The client of a people is from them, and their ally is from them." The author began first with explaining the first category, saying: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," i.e., virtual kinship and help are established for the one who emancipates the slave. This mentioned text is the wording of the hadith narrated by the six imams from Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, from the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." This hadith by its generality includes every emancipator, whether through tadbir, kitaba, istilad, or owning a relative. This is a transmitted proof. Its rational proof (qiyas): that the slave is legally as if dead; do you not see that many rulings reserved for the living, such as being a judge, witness, and ownership of wealth, are not established for him? So emancipation is a revival of him, because the rulings of revival are established for him, like revival by giving birth. So he inherits just as the father inherits from his child, and for this it is called patronage of grace. The emancipator may be Muslim or dhimmi. = By tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative, and the condition of sa'ibah is void. And if he emancipates a pregnant woman from her husband who is a slave, it does not transfer. (Answer to 'law') The subject, its predicate: a man's slave-woman. Because they inherit from each other through patronage like Muslims. Emancipation is general, whether voluntary or obligatory like expiatory emancipation, whether for money or not. (Collected from commentaries and glosses) By tadbir: i.e., he made a tadbir of his slave, then he died, and he became free from one-third of his wealth. (Ayni) And by kitaba: i.e., he made a kitaba with his slave and he paid the kitaba amount, so he became free. (Ayni) And istilad: i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation is by istilad, i.e., the master had a child from his slave-woman and then died, and she becomes free from all his wealth. This is conceivable. But it is objected: how can patronage be for the master through tadbir and istilad, when they become free after his death? The answer is: this applies when the master apostatizes and joins the abode of war, such that it is judged that his mudabbar and umm walad become free, then he returns as a Muslim and then his mudabbar and umm walad die; then patronage belongs to him. The better way is to say: what is meant is that the establishment of patronage for the 'asaba of the master is only due to its establishment for the master, for he is entitled to it, and the cause of emancipation from him, then it passes from him to his 'asaba. (Fath with addition) And owning a relative: i.e., he owns a relative of mahram lineage. [ ] i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation occurred by the master owning his relative, i.e., he owns his father or son by purchase, gift, bequest, or the like, so he becomes free by him, due to the generality of the hadith. The conclusion is that each of tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative is an emancipation by which patronage is established. The woman is like the man in all that, due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Women have no share of patronage except what they emancipate," to the end of the hadith. (Fath and Ayni with addition) And 'the condition of sa'ibah etc.': meaning, if the master emancipates his slave and stipulates that he will not inherit from him, the condition is void because it contradicts the ruling of Sharia, so he inherits from him, just as in lineage if he stipulates that he will not inherit. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 130/8) According to Ahmad, the condition of sa'ibah is valid. Sa'ibah is derived from saba, i.e., he ran and went every direction, and by it is meant emancipation without patronage. (Ayni, Takmila and others) Al-qinn: the slave owned by another man; she and her fetus become free. (Ayni) 'It does not transfer etc.': meaning, if a man emancipates a slave-woman who is pregnant from her husband who is a slave owned by another, while her marriage exists, and she gives birth to the child in less than six months, the patronage of the fetus does not transfer from the patrons of the mother to the patrons of the father ever; because the fetus is freed by the emancipation of its mother, and the emancipation of its mother is intended, so similarly he is freed as intended because he is part of the mother, and the master performed emancipation on all her parts intentionally, and patronage does not transfer from the emancipator due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." The existence of the fetus at the time of emancipation is known by her giving birth in less than six months from the time of emancipation to ascertain the time of conception. (Ramz al-Haqa'iq: 304/2) Similarly, if she gives birth to two children, one in less than six months and the other in more, and between them less than six months, patronage is established for the patrons of the mother and does not transfer to the patrons of the father because they are twins. We only said: 'and she gives birth to the child in less than six months' because if she gives birth at six months or more, the ruling is not so, as will come later. We only conditioned the emancipation of the slave-woman while the marriage of the slave husband exists because after the marriage this detail does not apply, and describing the husband as a slave is not for exclusion but is by way of example, because the ruling for a mukatab or mudabbar husband is similar. Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Patronage (Kitab al-Wala') which is the final book in the chapter on emancipation. The author first explains that 'al-wala' (patronage) is a legal relationship of inheritance that is established through emancipation. The word is derived from al-wali (closeness) or al-muwalah (mutual help). The text then systematically covers eight aspects: derivation, evidence, cause, lexical meaning, technical meaning, pillar, condition, and ruling. The primary evidence is the prophetic hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' (patronage belongs to the one who emancipates) reported by the six major hadith collections. Additionally, a rational proof (qiyas) is provided: a slave is legally 'dead' in many respects (cannot be a judge, witness, or own property), so emancipation is like bringing him to life, similar to how a father's relationship with his child gives inheritance rights. The cause of patronage is emancipation from the master's own ownership. The text distinguishes two types: wala' al-'itaqa (patronage of emancipation) and wala' al-muwalah (patronage of mutual alliance). The former is the focus here. The pillar (rukn) is the declaration of emancipation or contract of alliance. The condition is that the emancipator be eligible to inherit (free Muslim). The ruling is that the patron bears criminal liability (aql) and inherits. The text then expands on the different means of emancipation that establish patronage: tadbir (declaration that a slave will be free after master's death), kitaba (contract to purchase freedom), istilad (having a child with a slave-woman), and owning a relative (e.g., buying one's father). It clarifies that a condition to exclude inheritance (sa'ibah) is void in the Hanafi school (contrary to Ahmad Ibn Hanbal). A specific case is discussed: if a man emancipates a pregnant slave-woman whose husband is a slave owned by another, the child's patronage remains with the mother's patrons (the emancipator) and does not transfer to the father's patrons, because the child is freed as part of the mother. This is only true if the birth occurs within six months of emancipation, proving the child was conceived before.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared effective cause (illa).
العموم (al-'umum): Generality: the hadith covers all emancipators, including those via tadbir, kitaba, istilad, or owning a relative.

Sa'ibah Dispute and Ruling on Pregnant Slave
Specific rulings on the condition of sa'ibah and emancipation of a pregnant slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the condition of sa'ibah (it is void) and apply the rule of patronage when a pregnant slave is emancipated.

Core Idea
the condition of sa'ibah is void; the fetus's patronage follows the mother's patron when born within six months.

This lesson addresses two practical issues: first, a master who tries to stipulate that the freed slave will have no patron (sa'ibah), and second, the case of emancipating a pregnant slave whose husband is a slave. Both cases test the boundaries of the hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa'.

Guided Path
1
Defining Sa'ibah
Understand that sa'ibah is when the master emancipates the slave with the condition that no inheritance will follow. The term derives from 'saba' meaning 'to run free'.
This is a pre-Islamic practice that the text addresses.
2
Stating the Hanafi Position
Learn that such a condition is void (laghw) – the patronage remains with the emancipator. The hadith is unconditional.
Analogy: one cannot stipulate that a son will not inherit from his father.
3
Comparing with Imam Ahmad's View
Note that Imam Ahmad considers the condition valid. Understand the consequence: in Hanbali fiqh, the freed slave has no patron and his estate goes to the public treasury (bayt al-mal).
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
4
Analyzing the Pregnant Slave Case
Examine the rule: if a man emancipates a pregnant slave whose husband is a slave, the fetus is freed with the mother, and its patronage stays with the mother's patrons if born within six months.
Six months is the minimum pregnancy duration.
5
Identifying the Legal Basis
The fetus is considered part of the mother, so the emancipation covers it. The hadith prevents transfer of patronage to father's owners.
This is consistent with the general principle.

The Book of Patronage. Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates. This book is an exposition of the rulings of patronage. It is with fatha on the waw, derived from al-wali meaning closeness or from al-muwalah. (Mulla Miskeen) He placed the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab because patronage is among the effects of the mukatab due to the removal of ownership of the person upon payment of the kitaba. Although it is among the effects of emancipation, the reasons for the order of previous books brought the mukatab to this position, so it was necessary to delay the Book of Patronage after the Book of the Mukatab so that the effect does not precede the cause. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 138/8) Know that the discussion of patronage has aspects: first, its derivation; second, its evidence; third, its cause; fourth, its lexical meaning; fifth, its meaning according to jurists; sixth, its pillar; seventh, its condition; eighth, its ruling. As for the first: it is derived from al-wali, which is closeness, i.e., the occurrence of the second immediately after the first without interval, or from al-muwalah; it is said: wali al-shay' when it occurs after it without interval, and it is mufa'ala from al-wilayah with fatha, which means help and love. Its evidence is his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," and his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage is like the link of lineage," i.e., a connection like the connection of lineage. Its cause is emancipation according to the majority because the master bestowed favor upon his slave by emancipation. The more correct view is that its cause is emancipation from his ownership because it is attributed to him, and attribution indicates specification, and because whoever inherits his relative, that relative becomes free by him and his patronage belongs to him, even though there is no emancipation from his side. As for its lexical meaning: it is an expression for exchange and help, or an expression for connection and friendship, and the friend is called wali due to mutual help and cooperation for his beloved and friend. According to jurists, it is an expression for mutual help, whether by emancipation or by a contract of mutual alliance. As for its pillar: his saying: "I emancipate him," or he owns the relative, or he contracts mutual alliance. It is required that the emancipator be eligible for inheritance, i.e., free and Muslim. Its ruling: that he bears the liability for criminal acts during the life of his emancipator, and inherits from him after his death. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 128/8) Patronage: Know that patronage used in the terminology of Sharia, by which mutual help occurs, is of two types: first, patronage of emancipation, also called patronage of grace; its cause is emancipation from his ownership whether it occurs by his action, i.e., emancipation and what is legally similar to emancipation such as purchasing a relative, accepting a gift, etc., or without his action, such as inheriting his relative. Second, patronage of mutual alliance; its cause is a contract of mutual help and friendship between them. The purpose of each is mutual help. The Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, established both types of patronage, saying: "The client of a people is from them, and their ally is from them." The author began first with explaining the first category, saying: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates," i.e., virtual kinship and help are established for the one who emancipates the slave. This mentioned text is the wording of the hadith narrated by the six imams from Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, from the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." This hadith by its generality includes every emancipator, whether through tadbir, kitaba, istilad, or owning a relative. This is a transmitted proof. Its rational proof (qiyas): that the slave is legally as if dead; do you not see that many rulings reserved for the living, such as being a judge, witness, and ownership of wealth, are not established for him? So emancipation is a revival of him, because the rulings of revival are established for him, like revival by giving birth. So he inherits just as the father inherits from his child, and for this it is called patronage of grace. The emancipator may be Muslim or dhimmi. = By tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative, and the condition of sa'ibah is void. And if he emancipates a pregnant woman from her husband who is a slave, it does not transfer. (Answer to 'law') The subject, its predicate: a man's slave-woman. Because they inherit from each other through patronage like Muslims. Emancipation is general, whether voluntary or obligatory like expiatory emancipation, whether for money or not. (Collected from commentaries and glosses) By tadbir: i.e., he made a tadbir of his slave, then he died, and he became free from one-third of his wealth. (Ayni) And by kitaba: i.e., he made a kitaba with his slave and he paid the kitaba amount, so he became free. (Ayni) And istilad: i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation is by istilad, i.e., the master had a child from his slave-woman and then died, and she becomes free from all his wealth. This is conceivable. But it is objected: how can patronage be for the master through tadbir and istilad, when they become free after his death? The answer is: this applies when the master apostatizes and joins the abode of war, such that it is judged that his mudabbar and umm walad become free, then he returns as a Muslim and then his mudabbar and umm walad die; then patronage belongs to him. The better way is to say: what is meant is that the establishment of patronage for the 'asaba of the master is only due to its establishment for the master, for he is entitled to it, and the cause of emancipation from him, then it passes from him to his 'asaba. (Fath with addition) And owning a relative: i.e., he owns a relative of mahram lineage. [ ] i.e., patronage belongs to the one who emancipates even if the emancipation occurred by the master owning his relative, i.e., he owns his father or son by purchase, gift, bequest, or the like, so he becomes free by him, due to the generality of the hadith. The conclusion is that each of tadbir, kitaba, istilad, and owning a relative is an emancipation by which patronage is established. The woman is like the man in all that, due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Women have no share of patronage except what they emancipate," to the end of the hadith. (Fath and Ayni with addition) And 'the condition of sa'ibah etc.': meaning, if the master emancipates his slave and stipulates that he will not inherit from him, the condition is void because it contradicts the ruling of Sharia, so he inherits from him, just as in lineage if he stipulates that he will not inherit. (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 130/8) According to Ahmad, the condition of sa'ibah is valid. Sa'ibah is derived from saba, i.e., he ran and went every direction, and by it is meant emancipation without patronage. (Ayni, Takmila and others) Al-qinn: the slave owned by another man; she and her fetus become free. (Ayni) 'It does not transfer etc.': meaning, if a man emancipates a slave-woman who is pregnant from her husband who is a slave owned by another, while her marriage exists, and she gives birth to the child in less than six months, the patronage of the fetus does not transfer from the patrons of the mother to the patrons of the father ever; because the fetus is freed by the emancipation of its mother, and the emancipation of its mother is intended, so similarly he is freed as intended because he is part of the mother, and the master performed emancipation on all her parts intentionally, and patronage does not transfer from the emancipator due to his saying, peace be upon him: "Patronage belongs to the one who emancipates." The existence of the fetus at the time of emancipation is known by her giving birth in less than six months from the time of emancipation to ascertain the time of conception. (Ramz al-Haqa'iq: 304/2) Similarly, if she gives birth to two children, one in less than six months and the other in more, and between them less than six months, patronage is established for the patrons of the mother and does not transfer to the patrons of the father because they are twins. We only said: 'and she gives birth to the child in less than six months' because if she gives birth at six months or more, the ruling is not so, as will come later. We only conditioned the emancipation of the slave-woman while the marriage of the slave husband exists because after the marriage this detail does not apply, and describing the husband as a slave is not for exclusion but is by way of example, because the ruling for a mukatab or mudabbar husband is similar. Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Patronage (Kitab al-Wala') which is the final book in the chapter on emancipation. The author first explains that 'al-wala' (patronage) is a legal relationship of inheritance that is established through emancipation. The word is derived from al-wali (closeness) or al-muwalah (mutual help). The text then systematically covers eight aspects: derivation, evidence, cause, lexical meaning, technical meaning, pillar, condition, and ruling. The primary evidence is the prophetic hadith 'al-wala' liman a'taqa' (patronage belongs to the one who emancipates) reported by the six major hadith collections. Additionally, a rational proof (qiyas) is provided: a slave is legally 'dead' in many respects (cannot be a judge, witness, or own property), so emancipation is like bringing him to life, similar to how a father's relationship with his child gives inheritance rights. The cause of patronage is emancipation from the master's own ownership. The text distinguishes two types: wala' al-'itaqa (patronage of emancipation) and wala' al-muwalah (patronage of mutual alliance). The former is the focus here. The pillar (rukn) is the declaration of emancipation or contract of alliance. The condition is that the emancipator be eligible to inherit (free Muslim). The ruling is that the patron bears criminal liability (aql) and inherits. The text then expands on the different means of emancipation that establish patronage: tadbir (declaration that a slave will be free after master's death), kitaba (contract to purchase freedom), istilad (having a child with a slave-woman), and owning a relative (e.g., buying one's father). It clarifies that a condition to exclude inheritance (sa'ibah) is void in the Hanafi school (contrary to Ahmad Ibn Hanbal). A specific case is discussed: if a man emancipates a pregnant slave-woman whose husband is a slave owned by another, the child's patronage remains with the mother's patrons (the emancipator) and does not transfer to the father's patrons, because the child is freed as part of the mother. This is only true if the birth occurs within six months of emancipation, proving the child was conceived before.

Key Terms
السائبة (al-sa'ibah): A slave emancipated with the condition that he has no patron; the emancipator forfeits inheritance rights.
القن (al-qinn): A male slave owned by another; used here to describe the husband's status.

Marking and Decorating the Quran: Permissibility and Dispute
Ruling on marking and adorning the Quran with proof and cross-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on adding markers, dots, and gold adornment to the Quran and explain the evidence and cross-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Practical aids like dots and markers are permissible; adornment is permissible but a dispute exists with the three other imams.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq discusses whether it is allowed to add practical markings (like verse markers and diacritical dots) and decorative elements (like gold or silver) to the Quran. The Hanafi school generally permits these as useful aids, but there is a notable disagreement with the other three major madhhabs. Understanding this ruling requires balancing the principle that the Quran's text is tawqifi (fixed by revelation) against the practical needs of preservation and accessibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts in question
The passage covers two categories: practical markings (ta'shir for verse grouping, nuqat for i'rab) and adornment (tahliya with gold, silver, etc.). Identify each as a separate ruling.
Ta'shir is marking every ten verses; nuqat are dots for grammatical endings.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each
For practical markings: permissible (ja'iz), even commendable. For adornment: permissible but some say fatwa is on dislike (karaha).
The author uses 'yajuz' (it is permissible) for both, then notes the opposing view for adornment.
3
Analyze the evidence for markings
The Hanafi proof: (1) Preservation of verses and i'rab is a valid need; (2) Non-Arabs cannot read without dots; (3) The hadith of Ibn Mas'ud 'strip the Quran' is contextualized to his time when oral transmission sufficed.
The hadith is a Qawl Sahabi, zanni in both transmission and meaning.
4
Analyze the evidence for adornment
Adornment is analogized to decorating mosques (qiyas) if not from waqf funds; it shows veneration. The three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) consider it disliked based on a broader application of 'stripping' the Quran.
The disagreement is not intra-Hanafi but cross-madhhab.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
For markings, the Hanafi position is clear: permissible. For adornment, there is a difference; some later scholars (the 'qil' in the text) give fatwa on dislike, so one should be cautious.
The author's own view leans to permissibility, but he faithfully reports the other view.

(Tatimmah al-Bahr al-Ra'iq 8:408) And adorning it: meaning it is permissible to adorn the Quran with gold water, silver, lapis lazuli, and the like, because that is a form of veneration, like the carving and adorning of mosques if it is not from waqf funds. According to the three (Imams), all of this is disliked; it is said: with that fatwa is given. (Ayni)
Source pages p. 345

Commentary
This passage rules on the permissibility of adding practical marks to the Quran. The author explains that placing a sign every ten verses (ta'shir) and adding dots (nuqat) for i'rab is permissible because it helps preserve the revelation and assists non-Arab readers. The hadith of Ibn Mas'ud advising to 'strip the Quran' is contextualized: in his time, the Quran was transmitted orally without need for such aids; later needs justified them. The ruling extends to writing surah names, verse counts, and even dividing into juz' (ahzab). As for adorning (tahliya) the Quran with gold, silver, etc., the author states it is permissible because it shows veneration, analogous to decorating mosques provided it does not come from waqf funds. However, the three other imams (Abu Hanifa's companions? Actually ' الثلاثة' likely refers to the three major mujtahid imams besides Abu Hanifa: Shafi'i, Malik, Ahmad) consider it disliked, and some say fatwa is given accordingly. The author's own view appears to permit it, but he notes the opposing view.

Key Terms
تعشير (ta'shir): Marking every ten verses with a sign.
نقط (nuqat): Dots placed on letters to indicate diacritical marks (i'rab).
تحلية (tahliya): Adorning with gold, silver, or other precious materials.
توقيفية (tawqifiyyah): Determined solely by revelation, not subject to human reasoning.
جردوا القرآن (jarridu al-Qur'an): Strip the Quran (of embellishments/additions) – a command from Ibn Mas'ud.

Checkpoint
What is the Hanafi ruling on adding gold water to the mushaf?
A) It is forbidden because it changes the revealed text.
B) It is permissible based on analogy to mosque decoration, though the other three imams dislike it.
C) It is only allowed if the mushaf is for personal use.
D) It is mandatory to show veneration.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school permits practical markings on the Quran as aids for preservation and recitation, and permits adornment (gold, etc.) as veneration, but with awareness of the cross-madhhab view of dislike.
