Boy in Hands of Muslim and Christian: Fatherhood vs Slavery
Ruling on conflicting claims of paternity and slavery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a Muslim and a Christian simultaneously claim a boy as slave and son respectively, and explain the istihsan reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Freedom and Islam are preferred over slavery and disbelief in conflicting claims.

In Islamic law, the status of a child in disputed custody involves weighing claims of paternity, ownership, and religion. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a case where a Muslim and a Christian both claim a boy—one as his slave, the other as his son. The Hanafi ruling resolves the conflict by prioritizing freedom and Islam, applying the principle of istihsan even when the father is a non-Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
Note that the boy is physically in the joint custody of a Muslim and a Christian. The Christian claims the boy is his son; the Muslim claims he is his slave. Both claims are made simultaneously.
The simultaneity is crucial.
2
Determine the ruling for simultaneous claims
The ruling states that the boy is free and becomes the Christian's son. Freedom is instantaneous, and the boy is considered a Muslim in terms of property (musliman mālan) because the proofs of tawhid are clear to every rational person.
The boy is not enslaved; he is attributed to the Christian father despite the father being a non-Muslim.
3
Understand the istihsan reasoning
The ruling is based on istihsan: choosing the option that combines freedom and Islam. If the boy were a slave of the Muslim and a son of the Christian, he would not gain freedom. Thus, giving him immediate freedom while being Muslim and a son of the Christian is preferable.
Istihsan overrides the apparent implication of the Muslim's possession.
4
Analyze the exceptions and nuances
If the Muslim had claimed the boy as his son instead of slave, the boy would be his son due to preference for Islam (tarjīḥan lahu bi-l-islām). If the Muslim's claim of slavery preceded and was not simultaneous, the boy would be considered a slave of Allah (free and Muslim).
The commentary expands on these variations.

Child's Crime and Mukatab's Kitabah: No Implied Inability
Ruling on qadi's decision not implying mukatab's inability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a qadi's ruling of blood-money against the mother's patrons does not constitute a declaration of the mukatab's inability, and state the Hanafi position on recourse for the mother's patrons.

Core Idea
The qadi's ruling confirms the kitabah; freedom is not retroactive before payment.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario where a mukatab dies leaving a child and a debt. The child commits a crime, and the qadi awards blood-money against the mother's patrons. Does this imply the mukatab's inability? The Hanafi school clarifies that the ruling actually confirms the kitabah, and the freedom only occurs upon payment. We also explore a subsequent dispute on whether the mother's patrons can reclaim the blood-money.

Guided Path
1
Establish the scenario and the qadi's action
Identify the facts: mukatab dies leaving a child from a free woman, leaves a debt sufficient for kitabah (not cash), child commits a crime, qadi awards blood-money against the mother's patrons.
2
Analyse the legal implication of the qadi's ruling
Why does this not constitute a ruling of inability? Because the mukatab is not free until payment of the kitabah; the debt is not yet collected. The qadi's award confirms the kitabah by temporarily attaching wala' to the mother's side.
The key principle: freedom is only established at the moment of payment, not retroactively.
3
Trace the effect on wala' upon the mukatab's eventual freedom
When the kitabah is paid, the child's wala' transfers to the father's patrons. Wala' follows lineage like nasab.
Wala' is initially with the mother's patrons because the father is not free, but it shifts upon the father's freedom.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on recourse
The mother's patrons paid the blood-money; can they reclaim from the father's patrons? There are conflicting views: some say yes (al-Takmila, al-Durr al-Mukhtar), others say no (al-Fath, al-Zaylai).
5
Apply the reconciling detail from al-Nihaya and al-Mi'raj
The detail: no recourse if payment was made during the mukatab's life; recourse if payment was made after the father's death before the kitabah payment. This resolves the apparent contradiction.
The reasoning: freedom is only at the last moment of life, not retroactive during life, but after death, freedom is backdated to the moment of death.

Repeating Oaths to Complete Fifty in Qasamah
Ruling on repeating oaths when the number of men is less than fifty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on repeating oaths in qasamah when the number of oath-takers is insufficient and explain the textual basis from the practice of Caliph Umar.

Core Idea
Fifty oaths are a textual requirement; repetition is mandatory until the number is complete.

In qasamah, the law requires fifty oaths to establish liability for blood-money. But what if the locality does not have fifty men willing to swear? This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explains that the oath is repeated on the available men until the count of fifty is reached. The foundation is the practice of Caliph Umar (ra), who faced forty-nine men and had one repeat his oath to complete the number.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qasamah (swearing oaths) in homicide. The ruling is that if the number of men is less than fifty, the oath is repeated on them until fifty oaths are sworn. This is wajib.
The hukm level is wajib, not fard, as it is based on the practice of a Caliph, which in Hanafi usul is binding but not of the same certainty as Quran.
2
Understand the condition triggering repetition
The condition is that the number of male oath-takers from the locality is incomplete (nâqis). The text states 'if the number is not complete' (لم يتم العدد). Repetition is a means to fulfill the textual requirement.
Repetition is not required if the number is already fifty; then each swears once.
3
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The proof is the action of Caliph Umar (ra). When forty-nine men came to him in a qasamah case, he repeated the oath on one man until fifty were complete, then awarded blood-money. This establishes the precedent.
The dalil is a Sunnah ahad (practice of a Caliph) with zanni thubut but qati dalala for the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the ruling to different scenarios
Scenario 1: Only one man available – he swears fifty oaths. Scenario 2: Forty men – each swears one oath, then ten men swear a second oath. Scenario 3: Fifty men – each swears once; no repetition allowed.
The guardian (wali) cannot compel repetition if the number is already complete.
5
Note the limitation
Once fifty oaths are sworn, no further repetition is permitted. The purpose of repetition is to fulfill the required count; once fulfilled, it ceases.
This prevents unnecessary burden on the oath-takers.

Exclusion of Minors, Insane, Women, and Slaves from Qasamah
Ruling excluding certain persons from qasamah with two legal reasons (ta'lil).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which individuals are excluded from qasamah and explain the two legal reasons for their exclusion.

Core Idea
Qasamah requires capable supporters and sound speech—minors, the insane, women, and slaves fail both conditions.

Qasamah is a unique procedure in Islamic criminal law for establishing liability in homicide cases. The Hanafi school strictly limits participation to free adult males. This passage clarifies why four categories of people cannot participate. We will examine the two legal arguments (ta'lil) that justify their exclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the excluded categories
List the four types of individuals upon whom qasamah is not obligatory: minors, the insane, women, and slaves.
2
Understand the first reason: lack of nusrah
These individuals are not among the people of support (ahl al-nusrah); they are followers (atbaa'), and support cannot be established through followers. Qasamah is a form of community support.
3
Understand the second reason: lack of sound speech
Minors and the insane are not among the people of sound speech (ahl al-qawl al-sahih), and the oath is a form of speech. By extension, women and slaves are also excluded because they lack the capacity for valid testimony.
The text explicitly mentions minors and insane for the second reason, but the same reasoning applies to women and slaves due to similar legal incapacity.
4
Recognize the authority and practical effect
This ruling is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school, meaning it is the relied-upon position. The exclusion is not because of untruthfulness but due to legal capacity based on nusrah and valid speech.

Additionally, for minors and the insane, their statements are not considered legally valid speech, and since the oath is a form of verbal testimony, they cannot participate. The authority weight is from the matn with commentary from al-Hidayah and other sources.

No Qasamah for Body Without External Mark or Bleeding from Natural Orifices
Ruling on when qasamah and blood-money are not required due to lack of signs of homicide.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where qasamah applies based on the presence of external marks or the location of bleeding on a dead body, applying the Hanafi criterion.

Core Idea
Blood from natural orifices (nose, mouth, anus) does not trigger qasamah; blood from eyes and ears does, as it indicates violence.

In Hanafi criminal law, the presence of external signs of violence on a dead body determines whether qasamah (oath-taking procedure) and diyah (blood money) are imposed. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies a specific nuance: bleeding from natural orifices does not raise suspicion of homicide, while bleeding from unnatural orifices like the eye or ear does, because such bleeding is unusual and typically results from external force. Understanding this distinction helps in applying the rules of qasamah correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The passage presents two scenarios: a body with no visible mark of violence, and a body with blood flowing from natural orifices (nose, mouth, anus). For both, there is no qasamah or diyah.
The absence of an external sign (athar) is the key.
2
Note the exception for eye and ear
The passage explicitly states that the ruling is different if blood flows from the eye or ear. In those cases, qasamah applies because such bleeding is not natural except from a beating.
This exception is critical for the distinction.
3
Understand the reason (talil)
Blood from the eye or ear does not usually exit except due to apparent beating (al-darb zahiran). Thus, it is treated as evidence of homicide, triggering the laws of qasamah.
The rule is based on customary experience (ghalib al-ada).

The commentary then adds a critical condition from later sources (al-Tatarakhaniyyah): the liability on the owner only applies if the guardian (wali) of the victim specifically accuses the owner of the murder. If the guardian accuses someone else, the owner is not liable. This condition prevents unjust liability. The authority weight for the dispute is from the founding imams, while the condition is from later fatwa works.

Key Terms
الدار (في هذا السياق) (al-dar): A house or dwelling, considered as property with a specific owner.
اليد (في مسألة الدار) (al-yad): Control or possession; the owner has yad over the property, the resident has more specific yad over the interior.

Qasamah: Liability on Original Settlers, Not Later Residents
Ruling specifying the liable group for qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify who qualifies as ahl al-khittah for qasamah liability and distinguish them from later residents and property owners.

Core Idea
Qasamah liability is tied to the original settlement community, not to later residents or purchasers.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial qualification in Hanafi qasamah law: only the original settlers (ahl al-khittah) bear the collective responsibility for unsolved homicides. The text clarifies that later residents, tenants, purchasers, and even those who acquire property through gift or inheritance are not included. Understanding this distinction ensures correct application of qasamah in communities with diverse property histories.

Commentary
This final passage specifies who exactly constitutes the 'people of the locality' (ahl al-mahallah) for the purpose of qasamah. It is not every current resident or owner; rather, it is limited to the ahl al-khittah — the original settlers who were granted land by the Imam after the conquest of a city. Later residents (tenants, borrowers) and those who acquire property through purchase, gift, etc., are not included. The term 'khittah' refers to the original division of land. The rationale is that the obligation of nusrah (support) is tied to the original settlement and its foundational community. This ruling ensures that only those who have a historical and communal stake bear the collective responsibility of qasamah.

Key Terms
الخطة (al-khittah): The original allotment of land by the Imam to settlers after the conquest of a city; ahl al-khittah are those original settlers.
