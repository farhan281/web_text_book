Cleanliness of Sadaqat Paid by Mukatab
Ruling with proof from hadith and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on charity paid by a mukatab, identify the proof from the hadith of Barirah, and explain the difference between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding ajz.

Core Idea
Charity paid by a mukatab becomes clean for the master because ownership changes, as proven by the hadith of Barirah.

This passage addresses a nuanced fiqh issue: when a mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission) pays charity to his master, does that charity become permissible (tayyib) for the master? The author presents the ruling, the proof from the Prophet's statement about Barirah, and then an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding the situation where the mukatab becomes unable to pay the kitaba before the payment reaches the master. There is also a theoretical objection and response. By the end, you will be able to articulate the ruling and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a mukatab paying charity to his master. The ruling is that it is clean (tayyib) for the master to use.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Barirah: 'It is charity for her and a gift for us.' This is the primary textual evidence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet distinguished between the same item being charity (forbidden to him) and a gift (permissible), indicating that a change of ownership changes the ruling.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Muhammad holds that the charity is clean even if the mukatab becomes unable before payment and the master is wealthy. Abu Yusuf disagrees.
5
Evaluate the theoretical objection
An objection is raised: how can ownership change when the master already owned the slave? The author critiques one answer and suggests a possession-based explanation.

And whatever the mukatab paid from charities and then became unable, it is clean for his master. Know that whatever the mukatab paid to his master from charities, while the master is one for whom charity is permissible, it is clean for him because ownership has changed, and change of ownership is like change of substance, so it becomes like another substance. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the hadith of Barirah. It addresses the issue of a mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission) who pays charitable funds (sadaqat) to his master and then becomes unable to pay the kitaba (ajz). The question is whether the charity becomes clean (tayyib) for the master, i.e., permissible for him to use. The author first states the ruling: it is clean. The reason given is change of ownership; the charity was initially owned by the giver, but upon transfer to the master, ownership changes, similar to how a substance changes identity. This is supported by the Prophet's ﷺ statement about Barirah: a piece of meat given to her as charity was then given to the Prophet as a gift, and he said it was charity for her but a gift for him. The ruling is ijma' (consensus) among the imams if the payment occurred before ajz. If the ajz occurred before payment, Imam Muhammad holds the same ruling even if the master is wealthy, while Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees. The author also draws an analogy: a poor person who receives zakat and then becomes wealthy does not have to return it; it becomes pure. Similarly, a wayfarer who receives charity and then reaches his wealth. The passage then presents a theoretical objection: how can ownership change when the master already owned the slave? The answer given is that the master's ownership was overridden by the mukatab's right; with ajz, that right disappears and full ownership returns. The author critiques this answer and suggests a possession-based explanation. Overall, this is an expansion of the matn ruling with proofs and intra-Hanafi differences.

Key Terms
طيب (tayyib): Permissible, clean, lawful for the master to use; not requiring disposal.
تبدل الملك (tabaddul al-milk): Change of ownership; the transfer of property rights such that the item is considered legally different.

Permissible Defects in Sacrificial Animals
Ruling on sacrificing animals with specific non-prohibitive defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which defects do not invalidate an udhiyah sacrifice according to the Hanafi school and cite the evidence from the practice of Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Core Idea
A missing horn or castration does not ruin the sacrifice; only defects that affect health or meat quality matter.

This lesson clarifies a common misunderstanding: not every imperfection in a sacrificial animal renders it invalid. The Hanafi school, based on the practice of Ali (RA), identifies several defects that are overlooked. Understanding these rulings helps you select a valid animal without unnecessary strictness.

Guided Path
1
Identify each defect category
List the four defects mentioned: hornless (jam'aa), castrated (khasi), mad (thawla'), mangy (jarba').
The passage also discusses broken horn and the distinction between castration types.
2
Understand the evidence for each ruling
For hornless: the horn serves no purpose. For castrated: the practice of Ali (RA). For mad: conditional on ability to graze. For mangy: conditional on fatness and skin intact.
Note that Abu Hanifa preferred castrated for better meat.
3
Distinguish between permissible and impermissible defects
Use the conditions stated: madness must not prevent grazing; mange must not damage skin. If conditions not met, invalid.
The passage also mentions that a broken horn is valid unless the break reaches the skull.
4
Apply the ruling to a novel scenario
Given a modern scenario (e.g., a polled sheep, a castrated goat, a mentally ill cow that cannot graze), determine validity.

Prohibited Defects in Sacrificial Animals
Listing defects with proof and elaboration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defects that invalidate a sacrificial animal and apply the Hanafi rules for quantity thresholds in missing ear/tail cases.

Core Idea
Sacrifice is invalid if the animal has a major structural defect; missing parts are assessed by quantity lost.

This lesson examines the specific physical defects that render an animal invalid for udhiyah (sacrifice). The rulings are based on prophetic hadith and detailed Hanafi criteria, including threshold disputes among the early imams. Understanding these rules ensures your sacrifice is valid according to the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defects from the hadith
List the four defects explicitly mentioned in the hadith of Bara' ibn 'Azib: obviously one-eyed, obviously sick, obviously lame, and emaciated with no marrow.
These are the foundational prohibitive defects.
2
Analyze lameness criteria
Determine when lameness invalidates: if the animal cannot place the fourth foot on the ground without leaning or lifts it, it is invalid. If it places it lightly with some weight, it is valid.
The definition comes from al-Hawi, cited in the passage.
3
Apply the threshold rule for ear/tail
Text states 'most of the ear/tail lost' invalidates. Hanafi imams differ on what constitutes 'most': Abu Hanifa: >1/3 lost; Sahibayn: >1/2 lost. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is Sahibayn's view.
The principle 'most has the ruling of the whole' is used.
4
Evaluate the ruling for eye loss
For the eye, the criterion is loss of most of the sight, not the physical eye. A practical method for measuring sight loss is described.
Similar threshold disputes apply (1/3, 1/2).
5
Consider the nuance of karahah
Al-Fath notes that the hadith of Ali about ear cuts might indicate dislike (karahah) rather than impermissibility, but the general ruling remains that major cuts invalidate.
This is a subtle point; the majority view is that it invalidates if most is lost.

Species Permitted for Sacrifice
Limiting sacrificial animals to prescribed species with rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the species permissible for udhiyah according to the Hanafi school and identify which animals are excluded, including the ruling on buffalo and hybrids.

Core Idea
Sacrifice is confined to camels, cows (including buffalo), sheep, and goats; wild animals and hybrids from wild mothers are invalid.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies the precise categories of animals that qualify for the udhiyah sacrifice. Unlike many areas of fiqh where qiyas (analogy) expands rulings, here the text specifically restricts sacrifice to domestic livestock. Understanding this limitation is crucial for ensuring the validity of one's worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is udhiyah? Which species are explicitly listed in the passage? The text mentions camels, sheep, goats, and cows (including buffalo as a type).
The ruling is that only these species are valid; others are invalid.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage states that permissibility is 'known from the Law by text contrary to analogy.' The implied proof is the Quranic verse commanding sacrifice of livestock (an'am) on the day of Nahr, e.g., Surah al-Hajj 22:34.
The Quranic text specifies sacrificial animals among the an'am (camels, cows, sheep, goats).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the text limit the species? The principle is 'iqtisar ala mawridih' (confining to the source). Since the ruling is based on a specific text and is contrary to analogy, qiyas is not allowed to expand it to other animals.
This is a ta'abbudi (devotional) ruling, not subject to analogical reasoning.
4
Apply the principle to buffalo and wild cow
Why is buffalo included? Because it is a type of cow (baqar), which is mentioned in the text. Wild cow (baqar al-wahsh) is excluded because the text refers to domestic livestock only.
The term 'baqar' in the Quranic verse is understood as the domestic species (ahlī).
5
Determine the hybrid rule
For a hybrid born from a domestic and a wild parent, the ruling follows the mother's species. If the mother is domestic, the hybrid is valid; if wild, it is not.
This is a general principle in Islamic law: the offspring's species follows the mother.

Learning Objective

Give at least one-third in charity; keep and eat the rest.

Disposal of Udhiyah Skin and Prohibition of Butcher's Wage
Stating rules for skin disposal and prohibition of butcher's wage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for disposing of the udhiyah skin and explain why giving the butcher's wage from the udhiyah is prohibited.

Core Idea
Give skin in charity or make useful items; never sell it for personal gain or pay the butcher from it.

The udhiyah skin is part of the sacrificed animal and must be treated with respect. The Hanafi school outlines specific rules: it must be given in charity or converted into a useful item, and it cannot be sold for personal use. Additionally, paying the butcher from the meat or skin is prohibited as it resembles a sale. This lesson unpacks these rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the primary ruling on the skin
The text says: 'ويتصدق بجلدها' — the performer of udhiyah should give the skin in charity, because it is part of the animal.
Charity is the default.
2
List permissible uses besides charity
The skin may be made into items like a bag (جراب), sieve (غربال), water-skin (قربة), or tablecloth (سفرة). One may also buy such items with the skin or its price.
These are durable, beneficial items.
3
Identify prohibited transactions
One cannot sell the skin and spend the money on oneself or family. One cannot buy consumable items (like food) with the skin because those are not durable.
The intent must not be personal gain (تمول).
4
Understand the substitute principle
The substitute (بدل) has the ruling of the substituted; so if sold, the proceeds must be used for charity.
Selling for charity is permissible.
5
Explain the prohibition of butcher's wage
Al-Takmilah states: 'لا يعطي أجرة الجزار منها شيئا' — the butcher should not be paid from the udhiyah. This is because it is in the meaning of a sale (معاوضة), and the udhiyah is purely for devotion.
Paying the butcher from the meat or skin is like selling a part for his labor.
