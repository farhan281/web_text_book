Intention for Qada of Ramadan Without Specifying the Day
Ruling on generic intention for qada fast of Ramadan without specific day

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on general intention for qada of one or more Ramadans and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding specification.

Core Idea
A general intention suffices for qada of one Ramadan; for multiple, the more correct view requires specifying the year.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq addresses a practical issue: what if someone owes missed Ramadan fasts and intends to make them up without specifying which day or which Ramadan? The matn initially permits a general intention, but the commentary reveals a critical intra-Hanafi difference. The ruling hinges on how we understand the Quranic basis for the Ramadan fast and the nature of intention in qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the base ruling
The act is making intention (niyyah) for qada of Ramadan without specifying the exact day. The matn states it is valid (sahih). Note that this applies to one missed Ramadan.
The day is left unspecified, meaning a general intention to fast 'for Ramadan' suffices.
2
Understand the Quranic proof
The commentary cites Surah Al-Baqarah 2:185: 'So whoever among you witnesses the month, let him fast it.' The command is attached to 'the month,' which is a single unit. Thus, a general intention to fast the month covers all its days.
The proof's strength: the dalala is zanni because it is not explicit about qada, but it establishes the principle of the month as a unit.
3
Expand to multiple Ramadans
The matn then says the same applies if one intends for two Ramadans (i.e., without specifying which year). The commentary notes that some mashayikh hold this view, drawing an analogy to making up missed prayers.
This is the point of ikhtilaf.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
The commentary states that the more correct view (al-ashahh) restricts general intention to only one Ramadan. For two or more Ramadans, one must specify the year (e.g., 'Ramadan of 1445'). The analogy to prayer is also nuanced: for prayer, you must specify which prayer (e.g., Zuhr) and its day, unless you intend the first or last obligation upon you.
The disagreement is among Hanafi scholars; the more correct view is considered stronger.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The operative position for fatwa is the more correct view: general intention is valid for one Ramadan, but for multiple Ramadans, specification is required. This is the view the commentary endorses.
This is a fatawa mutakhkhirin ruling, referenced to Ramz al-Haqaiq.

Commentary
This passage discusses the validity of intention (niyyah) in making up missed Ramadan fasts (qada). The matn states: if one intends to make up Ramadan without specifying the day, it is valid. Similarly, if one owes qada for two Ramadans and intends generally without specifying the year, it is also valid, analogized to making up missed prayers. The commentary explains the reasoning: the Ramadan fast is attached to witnessing the month, and the month is a single unit (thirty days). The Quranic verse (2:185) is cited as proof. Therefore, a general intention suffices. However, the commentary then presents an internal Hanafi difference: some mashayikh hold that a general intention is valid only for one Ramadan, but not for two; for two, one must specify the year. The analogy to prayer is also discussed: for qada prayer, one must specify the particular prayer (e.g., Zuhr) and its day, unless one intends the first or last obligation upon him, which then automatically specifies the first or last. The more correct view (al-ashahh) is that general intention is valid only for one Ramadan; for multiple Ramadans, specification is needed. The passage ends with a partial discussion on prayer.

Swallowing Another's Saliva While Fasting: Friend vs. Stranger
Ruling on a fasting person swallowing someone else's saliva

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on swallowing another's saliva while fasting, distinguish between the cases of friend and non-friend, and explain the qiyas reasoning based on repulsiveness.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, swallowing the saliva of a friend while fasting incurs kaffarah, but swallowing that of a stranger only requires qada—because the soul's repulsion matters.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, with commentary from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, tackles a nuanced scenario: what if a fasting person swallows someone else's saliva? The ruling turns on a subtle psychological factor—whether the saliva comes from a close friend or from a stranger. By the end, you'll see how Hanafi scholars use the concept of 'repulsiveness' (ta'afuh al-nafs) to analogize this to other substances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its consequence
Start by recognizing that swallowing another person's saliva while fasting is an act that invalidates the fast. The consequence depends on whether the saliva is from a friend or a non-friend.
2
Distinguish the two scenarios
Note the two rulings: if the saliva is from a close friend (sadiq), both qada and kaffarah are required; if from a non-friend, only qada. This distinction is the core of the passage.
A 'friend' here means someone the person is comfortable with, so the saliva is not repulsive.
3
Trace the qiyas (analogy)
The passage uses an analogy: stranger's saliva is like dough (repulsive), friend's saliva is like bread (desirable). Identify the original case (asl) of swallowing a repulsive substance and its ruling (only qada).
The asl is the case of swallowing dough or similar repulsive things.
4
Extract the effective cause (illa)
The illa is whether the soul finds the substance repulsive (ta'afuh al-nafs) or not. Stranger's saliva is repulsive, friend's saliva is not. This illa determines whether kaffarah is due.
This is a khafiyyah (hidden) illa, extracted via tanqih al-manat.
5
Apply the qiyas to derive the ruling
Apply the illa to the new case (far'): swallowing stranger's saliva → repulsive → only qada. Swallowing friend's saliva → not repulsive → like consuming desirable food → qada + kaffarah.
The hukm far' is the same as the asl for repulsive cases, and opposite for non-repulsive.

Commentary
This passage deals with the effect of swallowing another person's saliva while fasting. The ruling distinguishes based on the relationship: if the saliva comes from a close friend (sadiq), the soul does not find it repulsive, so it is considered like a desirable food (like bread), and thus swallowing it invalidates the fast and requires both qada (make-up) and kaffarah (expiation). If it comes from a stranger or someone not a friend, the soul finds it repulsive (like dough or similar things that are naturally disliked), so it only breaks the fast requiring qada, but not kaffarah. The reasoning is based on istihsan (juristic preference) using analogy: things that are normally desired (like food) incur kaffarah; things that are repulsive (like dirt) only require qada.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffarah): An expiation required for intentionally invalidating a Ramadan fast; in Hanafi fiqh, it is fasting for two consecutive months (siyam shahrayn mutatabi'ayn) or feeding sixty needy persons.
صديق (sadiq): A close friend or intimate companion; the text uses this to indicate someone the person is comfortable with, so they do not find their saliva repulsive.

Swallowing Saliva of Another During Fasting
Ruling on kaffarah vs qada for swallowing another's saliva

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on swallowing another's saliva while fasting, and explain why the consequence differs based on whether the person is a friend or not.

Core Idea
Swallowing a friend's saliva requires kaffarah; a stranger's requires only qada.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from Kanz al-Daqaiq regarding swallowing another person's saliva during a fast. The Hanafi school makes a nuanced distinction based on the relationship between the two individuals, using the criterion of repulsiveness (istiqdhār). Understanding this ruling helps you see how custom (urf) and analogy (qiyas) are applied in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is swallowing the saliva of another person while fasting. Two rulings are given: if the person is a friend, kaffarah is wajib; if not, only qada is wajib.
Note that the fast is broken in both cases (qada is required), but kaffarah is added only for friend's saliva.
2
Locate the proof
The commentary provides an analogy (qiyas): friend's saliva is like bread (desirable), stranger's is like dough (repulsive). The ruling for bread is that swallowing it requires kaffarah; for dough, only qada.
This is an example of istihsan based on custom.
3
Trace the illa
The effective cause is whether the soul finds the substance repulsive or not. For a friend's saliva, it is not repulsive, so it resembles desirable food. For a stranger's, it is repulsive, like dough.
The illa is derived from the attribute of repulsiveness (ta‘āfuh al-nafs).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other madhahib may not make this distinction and may consider all external saliva as breaking the fast requiring kaffarah, or they may have different criteria.
The Hanafi position is based on urf and istihsan.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq. The matn states a concise ruling: swallowing the saliva of another person during fasting requires kaffarah (major expiation) only if the person is a close friend; otherwise, only qada (making up the fast) is required. The commentary explains the rationale (ta'lil) using the concept of 'repulsiveness' (istiqdhār). In Hanafi fiqh, if the swallowed substance is something that people normally find repulsive (like dough or dirt), it only breaks the fast and requires qada, not kaffarah. If the substance is desirable (like bread), it requires kaffarah. Since a friend's saliva is not repulsive to the swallower (due to familiarity), it is analogized to bread, thus kaffarah is due. A stranger's saliva is repulsive, analogized to dough, so only qada. This distinction is based on custom and human nature, and is an example of istihsan (juristic preference).

بزاق (bizāq): Saliva, spittle
صديق (ṣadīq): A close friend whose saliva is not repulsive to the one swallowing
تعافه النفس (ta‘āfuhu al-nafs): The soul finds it repulsive/disgusting
