Definition and Proof of Shuf'ah (Preemption)
Defines shuf'ah and establishes its ruling with hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shuf'ah and explain its legal proof from the hadith of Jabir.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is a compulsory right for the undivided partner, proven by the Prophet's judgment.

Shuf'ah (preemption) is a unique right in property law that allows a co-owner to compulsorily acquire a sold share to prevent harm. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition and the primary textual proof from the Sunnah. Understanding the precise definition and its dalil is essential for applying shuf'ah correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hukm (ruling level)
Determine whether shuf'ah is fard, wajib, or something else. The text indicates it is 'wajib' for the partner, not fard, because it is a right of the individual based on a zanni dalil.
2
Analyze the definition's components
Break down the definition: 'tamlik al-buq'ah jabran 'ala al-mushtari bima qama 'alayh'. Understand each term: tamlik (ownership), buq'ah (property), jabran (compulsorily), mushtari (buyer), bima qama 'alayh (the price paid).
Note that 'jabran' distinguishes shuf'ah from ordinary sale.
3
Locate the primary dalil
Identify the hadith of Jabir (RA) that the Prophet (PBUH) gave judgment (qada) with shuf'ah in every undivided partnership. Recognize this as a dalil naqli (textual evidence).
The hadith is ahad, giving zanni thubut and dalala.
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah (proof indication)
Explain how the hadith proves the ruling: the Prophet's judgment (qada) implies obligation, and the statement 'he is more entitled to it' gives a right that can be enforced even without the buyer's consent.
5
Address the ikhtilaf about neighbors
The text raises an objection: the hadith only mentions partners, yet Hanafi extends to neighbors. Follow the Hanafi response: another hadith establishes the neighbor's right, so the restriction is not absolute.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf with the Shafi'i school.

Commentary
This passage defines shuf'ah (preemption) and presents its legal ruling with proof. The author begins with the linguistic meaning: al-shaf' means joining, and the shafi' is the one who adds the purchased property to his own. In legal terminology, shuf'ah is the ownership of the property compulsorily over the buyer for the price that the buyer paid. It is wajib (obligatory) for the partner who did not partition. The wisdom behind shuf'ah is to prevent ongoing harm from bad neighbors. The condition is that the property be immovable, either upper or lower floors. The proof is the hadith of Jabir (may Allah be pleased with him) that the Prophet (peace be upon him) ruled shuf'ah in every undivided partnership. An objection arises: the hadith only mentions the partner, not the neighbor. The response is that another hadith establishes the neighbor's right, so the restriction from the first hadith is not absolute. This shows that shuf'ah is established for both partners and neighbors.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf'ah): Ownership of the property compulsorily over the buyer for the price that the buyer paid, to prevent harm from neighbors.
الخليط (al-khalit): The partner who has not yet partitioned the jointly owned property.
جبرا (jabran): Compulsorily, meaning the acquisition is enforced on the buyer regardless of his consent.
مشتري (mushtari): The buyer of the property from the co-owner.

Definition of Karahiyah and the Ruling on Makruh Tahrimi
Defines karahiyah and explains the legal status of makruh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of karahiyah, distinguish between makruh tahrimi and makruh tanzihi, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the legal status of makruh tahrimi.

Core Idea
Makruh tahrimi is closer to haram but not haram; the relied-upon Hanafi position preserves the default permissibility of things.

This lesson introduces the Book of Karahiyah from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. We will define karahiyah linguistically and technically, and then examine a key dispute within the Hanafi school: is every makruh tahrimi actually haram? Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly classifying acts and issuing fatwas.

Guided Path
1
Define karahiyah linguistically and technically
Identify the linguistic meaning (opposite of desire/pleasure) and the technical meaning (a disliked act that one should avoid). Note that the book is named after this category even though it includes non-makruh rulings.
The author notes that explaining the makruh is more important due to the obligation of avoiding it.
2
Identify the two types of makruh: tahrimi and tanzihi
From the passage, see that makruh tahrimi is closer to haram (according to the two shaykhs) while makruh tanzihi is closer to halal by agreement. Understand that the dispute only concerns tahrimi.
Khalaf ibn Yahya's view (makruh closer to halal) is mentioned but not the relied-upon position.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Muhammad al-Shaybani held that every makruh tahrimi is haram. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold it is not haram but closer to haram. Examine the proofs: Muhammad uses the strength of evidence and absence of definitive permission; the two rely on default permissibility. Conclude with the mufta bihi view of the two shaykhs.
The commentator suggests the dispute is verbal; the practical difference is whether sin is recorded.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Karahiyah (Disliked Acts) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author defines karahiyah linguistically as the opposite of desire and pleasure. He notes that although the book contains rulings that are not disliked, it is named so because explaining disliked things is more important due to the need to avoid them. The passage then presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute: According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, every makruh (tahrimi) is actually haram, while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that makruh tahrimi is merely closer to haram, not actually haram. The commentator clarifies that this dispute is largely verbal: Muhammad avoids calling it haram only because no definitive (qat'i) text exists, whereas the two shaykhs uphold the default permissibility of things due to the legal maxim 'al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibahah' (the original ruling for things is permissibility). The passage also contrasts makruh tahrimi with makruh tanzihi, which is closer to halal by agreement. The commentator analogizes the makruh-haram relationship to the wajib-fard relationship in aqidah.

Key Terms
الكراهية (al-karahiyah): Linguistically, the opposite of desire and pleasure; technically, a disliked act that one should avoid.
المكروه (al-makruh): An act that is disliked, with a tahrimi type being close to haram due to strong evidence, but not actually haram.
