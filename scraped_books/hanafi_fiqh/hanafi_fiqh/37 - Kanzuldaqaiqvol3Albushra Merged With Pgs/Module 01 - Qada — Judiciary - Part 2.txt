Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Effect of Dates in Outsider Claims
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dating evidence in outsider property claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on dated vs undated evidence in outsider property claims and identify the thamarah al-khilaf for each scenario.

Core Idea
In Hanafi evidence law, a dated deed does not automatically outrank an undated one — Abu Hanifa and Muhammad each challenge the primacy of dating.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a classic intra-madhhab dispute among the three Hanafi founders (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the weight of dated evidence when two outsiders (kharajan) claim property. The ruling depends on whether both claim from the same seller or different sellers, and whether only one party's evidence carries a date. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi approach to evidence hierarchy and the principle that absolute ownership claims have independent force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Scenario 1: both outsiders claim from the same seller (the current possessor). Scenario 2: each claims from a different seller. The rule for dating differs between them.
2
Recall the default rule when both have dates
If both have dated evidence, the earlier date prevails in both scenarios. This is agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.
This is the only point al-Karkhi considered mutaffaq alayh.
3
Analyze the dispute when only one has a date — Scenario 1
If only one outsider has a date and the other does not, the imams split: Abu Hanifa says equal, Abu Yusuf says dated is preferred, Muhammad says undated is preferred.
The core issue: does a date increase certainty, or does absolute evidence prove deeper ownership?
4
Analyze the dispute when only one has a date — Scenario 2
In Scenario 2 (different sellers), all imams agree that the dated evidence is preferred, because it proves a specific time of acquisition from a unique seller.
This unanimity is because ownership stems from different origins, so date clarifies priority.
5
Understand the possessor's role
When the possessor (dhu al-yad) has an earlier date than the outsider, the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) prefer the possessor; Muhammad holds them equal. This shows the possessor's date can overcome the outsider's claim.
Muhammad's view: both evidences are absolute, so date is irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of dates (tarikh) when two outsiders (kharajan) claim ownership of property. In the first scenario, both claimants present evidence of purchase from the same person (the possessor). If both have dates and one is earlier, the earlier prevails. If only one has a date, Abu Hanifa says they are equal because the undated evidence could be earlier. Abu Yusuf says the dated one is preferred because its certainty is stronger. Muhammad says the undated is preferred because absolute evidence shows ownership from the beginning. In the second scenario, both claim purchase from different sellers. If both have dates and one is earlier, the earlier prevails. If only one has a date, the one with date is preferred because he proves a specific time of acquisition. The text also discusses the case where the possessor (dhu al-yad) has a dated evidence that is earlier than the outsider's: the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) prefer the possessor, while Muhammad holds they are equal. The underlying issue is the weight given to absolute versus dated evidence in ownership claims. The text refers to al-Karkhi and the author of al-Amālī for different versions of the imams' positions. The commentary (by 'Ayni and Fath) adds that if the author had explicitly mentioned the case of one party dating, it would have been clearer, as having a date does not automatically give precedence because the other might have an earlier undocumented ownership.

Key Terms
خارج (kharaj): A claimant who is not in possession of the property; an outsider.
ذو اليد (dhu al-yad): The person currently in possession of the property.
ملك مطلق (milk mutlaq): Absolute ownership without specification of time or cause.

Priority in Claims: The Role of Dates in Evidence (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on date priority in ownership claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi rules on dated and undated evidence to determine priority between two external claimants in ownership disputes.

Core Idea
Date matters only when claimants agree on a specific transaction; in absolute ownership, Abu Hanifa ignores it.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq addresses a common scenario in qada: two outsiders each claim ownership of property currently held by a possessor, or both claim to have purchased it from a third party. When should a dated proof prevail? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on the weight of dates in evidence. We will unpack the two main scenarios and the positions of the three imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The text presents two distinct situations: (1) both claimants assert ownership against the possessor, and (2) both claimants assert purchase from a third party (not the possessor). Distinguish them clearly.
In scenario 1, the possessor is a party; in scenario 2, both claimants are outsiders to the current possessor.
2
Analyze the baseline rule
If both claimants have no date, or both have equal dates, the property is split equally between them in both scenarios. This is the default.
3
Examine the effect of a single date in scenario 1 (absolute ownership)
When only one claimant provides a date, Abu Hanifa disregards it—they are still equal. Abu Yusuf prefers the dated one. Muhammad prefers the undated one. Understand each imam's reasoning.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: absolute ownership claim (milk mutlaq) implies ownership from the origin; the undated might be older.
4
Examine the effect of a single date in scenario 2 (purchase from a third party)
Here, all three imams agree that the dated claimant prevails. The reason: both claim purchase from the same person, so the one who proves an earlier transaction with that person is more entitled.
This is the key practical difference between the two scenarios.
5
Apply the rule of the possessor's earlier date
If the possessor also produces a dated proof (showing earlier ownership than the external claimant), then according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs), the possessor wins. Muhammad rejects the possessor's proof entirely in absolute ownership.
This reflects the procedural advantage of possession (dhu al-yad) combined with an earlier date.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the role of dates in ownership claims. The matn states: if two claimants both have dated proofs of purchase from the same seller, the earlier date wins; if only one has a date, the dated one wins only in the second scenario (both claiming from a third party). The commentary clarifies two scenarios: (1) both claim from the possessor, (2) both claim from a different person. Al-Karkhi and al-Amali record differing positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad. In absolute ownership claims (not based on purchase), Abu Hanifa disregards a single date because the undated could be older; Abu Yusuf prefers the dated one; Muhammad prefers the undated one. In the second scenario (purchase from a third party), the dated one is clearly preferred because it establishes receipt from the owner at a specific time. The author notes that if one claimant has taken possession, they are equal only if both have no date or equal dates; otherwise the earlier date wins. The rule on the possessor's earlier date: if the possessor's proof shows an earlier date than the external claimant, the possessor wins according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf; Muhammad rejects the possessor's proof in absolute ownership entirely.

Possessor Prevails in Offspring and Non-Recurring Claims
States possessor prevails in offspring and non-recurring ownership claims, citing hadith of Jabir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the possessor is preferred in offspring and non-recurring ownership claims, citing the hadith of Jabir and distinguishing istihsan from qiyas.

Core Idea
When both parties prove offspring, the possessor wins by istihsan, not qiyas.

In judicial disputes over ownership, evidence weighing can be complex. This lesson examines a specific rule from Hanafi fiqh: if both an external claimant and the current possessor prove that they own the offspring of an animal (or a non-recurring cause of ownership), the possessor is preferred. This ruling is based on the prophetic hadith of Jabir and the principle of istihsan, overriding the qiyas that would favor the external. Understanding this rule clarifies when possession itself becomes a decisive factor in evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two main disputes: (1) when both parties prove that an animal gave birth in their ownership (al-nitaaj), and (2) when they prove a non-recurring cause of ownership (sabab milk la yatakarrar) such as spinning, weaving, or milking.
Non-recurring causes are analogous to offspring.
2
State the ruling and its basis
In both scenarios, the Hanafi position is that the possessor (dhu al-yad) is preferred. The ruling is based on istihsan (equitable preference) derived from the hadith of Jabir, where the Prophet ruled for the possessor when both presented proof of offspring.
The hadith is a direct evidence (dalil naqli) that overrides qiyas.
3
Understand the dispute with Ibn Abi Layla
Ibn Abi Layla held the opposite view by qiyas: the external claimant's proof should prevail because it establishes something non-apparent (ownership from a non-recurrent cause), whereas the possessor's proof is redundant due to possession. Hanafis respond with the hadith, showing that the Prophet gave weight to possession itself.
This illustrates usul al-fiqh: qiyas can be overridden by a stronger textual proof.
4
Apply the exception
The rule applies only when the dispute is solely about the offspring or the non-recurring cause. If the parties also dispute the mother's absolute ownership, the possessor is not automatically preferred; the mother's ownership must be settled first.
This prevents the rule from being misapplied to unrelated ownership claims.
5
Distinguish recurring causes
Recurring causes (e.g., building, planting) do not give preference to the possessor. For such causes, the external claimant wins because the possessor's proof of a recurring act does not outweigh the external's proof of absolute ownership. If the cause is ambiguous, experts are consulted; if still unclear, the external prevails.
This shows the scope of the rule: it is limited to non-recurring causes analogous to offspring.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings on two types of claims: (1) when both parties prove offspring (نتاج) of an animal, and (2) when they prove a non-recurring cause of ownership. The author states by consensus (istihsan) that the possessor is preferred over the external claimant, citing the hadith of Jabir where the Prophet ruled in favor of the possessor. The qiyas of Ibn Abi Layla would favor the external, but istihsan based on the hadith overrides that. The commentary clarifies that 'offspring' means the animal was born in the ownership of the claimant or his predecessor. Importantly, this preference applies only when the dispute is about the offspring alone; if they also dispute the mother's absolute ownership, the possessor is not automatically preferred. For non-recurring causes (e.g., spinning, weaving, shearing, milking), the same rule applies because they are analogous to offspring. Recurring causes (e.g., building, planting) are different—the external claimant is preferred because the possessor's proof of such recurring acts does not outweigh the external's proof of absolute ownership. If the nature is unclear, experts are consulted; if still ambiguous, the external wins. The final ruling: if the external proves absolute ownership and the possessor proves purchase from that external, the possessor wins, consistent with the principle that the possessor's proof of receipt is stronger.

Preferring the Possessor's Evidence in Claims of Origin
Ruling on conflicting evidence in origin claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on evidence preference in claims of birth (nataj) and non-recurring causes, and apply the distinction between absolute ownership and origin claims.

Core Idea
In birth claims, the possessor's evidence plus possession outweighs the outsider's evidence alone.

When two parties dispute over the origin of property—such as claiming an animal was born in their ownership—how does a Hanafi judge decide? This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains that the possessor (dhu al-yad) is preferred in claims of birth and non-recurring causes, based on a Prophetic hadith and istihsan. However, in absolute ownership claims, the outsider is preferred. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim type
Determine whether the dispute is about the origin of property (birth, non-recurring cause) or absolute ownership. This classification determines the ruling.
Claims of origin include nataj (birth of animal), spinning, weaving, milking—causes that do not recur.
2
Determine possession
Identify which party currently possesses the disputed property. The possessor is dhu al-yad; the non-possessor is al-kharj (outsider).
Possession alone is not decisive—it preponderates the evidence only in origin claims.
3
Apply the core rule
If both parties present evidence on origin, the possessor is preferred due to the combination of his evidence and possession. If the claim is about absolute ownership, the outsider is preferred because his evidence establishes something contrary to the apparent (possession).
This rule is based on istihsan, supported by a hadith of Jabir.
4
Check for exceptions
If the claimed cause of ownership is recurrent (e.g., planting, building), the outsider is always preferred, even in origin claims. If the dispute involves the mother animal as well, the possessor loses his preference.
Recurrent causes include cultivation, construction—things that can be done multiple times.
5
Handle date conflicts
If both parties have dates, the earlier date is preferred according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad; if only one has a date, Abu Yusuf prefers the one with a date, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider the evidence equal.
This intra-Hanafi difference applies only in absolute ownership claims, not in origin claims.

Commentary

The core ruling is that the possessor's evidence is preferred, and he retains the property. This ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and is supported by a hadith where the Prophet (s.a.w.) judged in favor of the possessor in a similar dispute. Intra-Hanafi dispute on conflicting evidence without date

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Preference Not Given by Number of Witnesses
Explanation of the principle that more witnesses do not outweigh in conflicting evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on preferring witnesses by number and explain the rationale based on strength of cause (illa).

Core Idea
More witnesses do not outweigh others; strength of cause, not count, decides.

This passage addresses a classic dispute: when two sets of witnesses conflict, should the larger number prevail? The Hanafi school says no, drawing analogies from hadith and Quranic interpretation. The key is that preference is based on the strength of the legal cause (illa), not multiplicity. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind the Hanafi position and contrasts it with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core Hanafi rule
State the ruling when one litigant brings two witnesses and the other brings four; both are equal in the Hanafi view.
The rule applies when both sets are otherwise equally credible.

Recognize that preference is by strength in the cause (illa), not by count. The Hanafi position is that quantity does not enhance the probative value.
The analogy with hadith reports illustrates that internal strength matters more than external multiplicity.

Division of Property: Manaza'a vs 'Awl
Ruling on conflicting partial and total ownership claims with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi methods of manaza'a and 'awl in property division and apply them to cases of conflicting claims.

Core Idea
In a claim dispute, the all-claimant's uncontested half goes to him; the contested half is split equally between both claimants.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi dispute about dividing property when one claimant asserts full ownership and another asserts half ownership, both with evidence. The Imām Abū Ḥanīfa uses a contest (munāzaʿah) approach, while the Ṣāḥibayn prefer proportional reduction (ʿawl). Understanding the difference is crucial for a qāḍī adjudicating such cases.

Guided Path
1
State the claim scenario
A house is in a third party's possession. Claimant A says the whole house is his; claimant B says half is his. Both provide evidence.
The possessor is not one of the claimants; the dispute is between two external claimants.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's method (manaza'a)
The all-claimant is uncontested in half, so that half is surrendered to him fully. The remaining half is contested equally, so that contested half is split equally between them. Result: all-claimant gets 3/4, half-claimant gets 1/4.
The uncontested portion is determined by the smaller claim's share.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's method ('awl)
Treat the whole claim as 1 and the half claim as 0.5, summing to 1.5. Reduce proportionally: all-claimant gets 2/3, half-claimant gets 1/3.
This resembles inheritance reduction (awl) where shares are scaled down.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: all-claimant receives 3/4 under manaza'a but 2/3 under 'awl. The half-claimant gets 1/4 vs 1/3. The method chosen changes the shares significantly.
In court, the judge must decide which method to apply.
5
Consider other schools
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that both evidences cancel and the possessor's oath decides, giving the whole house to the possessor if he swears.
This is a minority view; the Hanafi school relies on evidence.

Four Types of Legal Division (Qisma)
Categorization of division methods with examples of consensus and intra-madhhab disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of legal division (qisma) in Hanafi fiqh and identify which cases fall under each type, including the points of consensus and disagreement.

Core Idea
Division is either by 'awl or manaza'a, with consensus on some cases and ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on others.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq presents a comprehensive categorization of legal division methods. You will learn the four types based on the method used ('awl or manaza'a) and whether the method is agreed upon or disputed. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly distributing property, debts, and claims in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary methods
Note that the two main methods are 'awl (proportional reduction) and manaza'a (contest). 'awl reduces shares proportionally when claims exceed the asset; manaza'a gives uncontested portions to the claimant and divides contested portions equally.
These are the two distributive methods used in Hanafi fiqh for dividing claims.
2
Memorize the eight consensus cases for 'awl
There are eight cases where 'awl is used by consensus: inheritance, debts, bequests under one-third when combined, muhaba (preferential treatment), mu'azzala (loose dirhams), su'aya (service), a slave who gouges an eye and kills, and a mudabbir slave with similar injuries.
These are settled and not subject to ikhtilaf.
3
Understand the single consensus case for manaza'a
There is one case where manaza'a is used by consensus: when two fudulis (unauthorized agents) sell the same slave to different buyers, and the master ratifies both sales. The price is divided by manaza'a: the buyer of the whole gets three-quarters, the buyer of half gets one-quarter.
This is the only case with unanimous agreement on manaza'a.
4
Recognize the three cases where Abu Hanifa uses manaza'a and the Sahibayn use 'awl
In these cases, Abu Hanifa prefers manaza'a while the two Sahibayn prefer 'awl. The three cases are: (1) the case of the book (the one discussed in this chapter), (2) bequest of all wealth with heirs' permission, and (3) bequest of a specific slave and half of that slave with no other property.
The thamarah al-khilaf affects how shares are calculated.
5
Recognize the five cases where Abu Hanifa uses 'awl and the Sahibayn use manaza'a
In these cases, Abu Hanifa applies 'awl while the Sahibayn apply manaza'a. The five cases involve a ma'dhun slave (permitted to trade) with debts, a slave who commits multiple homicides, a mudabbir slave committing similar offenses, and a umm walad killing her master and a stranger.
Again, the method determines the final distribution.

Core Idea
Actual use trumps mere control until evidence overrides.

When two people claim the same object, who gets it? The Hanafi school gives priority to the one who is actually using it—riding or wearing—over the one who merely holds a part. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this rule, its limitations, and the role of evidence.

Priority in Claims: Load on Animal and Beams on Wall

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on priority in disputes over animals and walls based on load/beams, explain the istihsan reasoning, and evaluate the Shafi'i objection.

Core Idea
Physical marks of customary use give priority in claims, even if other possibilities exist.

In disputes over property, physical evidence and customary use play a key role. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents two scenarios: a load on an animal and beams on a wall. The Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to give priority to the person displaying such marks, overriding a strict qiyas that would treat both claimants equally. Let's explore the reasoning and the inter-madhhab disagreement with al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two types of disputes: (1) two men dispute over an animal, and one has a load on it; (2) two men dispute over a wall, and one has beams (judhu') on it for roof support.
These are examples of 'signs of ownership' based on customary use.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each
In both cases, the owner of the load/beams is preferred over the other claimant, because they are the ones using the property in the customary manner.
If both have loads/beams, they are equal and the property is shared.
3
Trace the proof: istihsan vs qiyas
By qiyas, both claimants would be equal (Abu Hanifa's reported view). But istihsan (juristic preference) overrides this, giving preference to the one with the mark, because custom considers such marks as proof of ownership.
Istihsan is based on the idea that less than three beams is an incomplete proof, so three beams become like a nisab (threshold).
4
Analyze the nisab of three beams
If each claimant has three beams, the wall is shared. If one has three and the other less, the one with three is preferred. Three is the minimum for a roof, so it is a complete proof.
Quantity beyond three does not matter; three is the threshold.
5
Evaluate the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
Al-Shafi'i argues that beams could be borrowed or usurped, so their presence is not proof. The Hanafi response: the possibility is remote; placing beams is a sign of ownership.
The dispute is about the weight of circumstantial evidence.

Definition and Conditions of Iqrar (Acknowledgment)
Definition of iqrar, its conditions, rukn, and evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of iqrar, identify its three conditions (adult, sane, voluntary), and distinguish iqrar from testimony and claim.

Core Idea
Iqrar is a declaration of another's right against oneself — distinct from testimony and claim.

This lesson opens the Book of Acknowledgment (Iqrar) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. We will examine the precise Hanafi definition, the conditions that must be met for an iqrar to be valid, and the evidence from the Quran, Sunnah, consensus, and reason. Understanding the distinction between iqrar, testimony, and claim is crucial for grasping its legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the definition
The passage defines iqrar as 'declaration of the establishment of a right of another over oneself.' Identify the genus (declaration) and differentia (against oneself). Note how this excludes testimony (against another) and claim (for oneself).
The definition uses 'haqq' (right) absolutely to include both property and nullifications like divorce.
2
Extract the conditions of the muqirr
The text lists: adult (baligh), sane (aqil), and voluntary (taa'). Being free is not a condition for validity, but a slave's acknowledgment may be deferred in financial matters.
The condition 'mukallaf' implies legal responsibility; thus minors and the insane cannot make a valid iqrar.
3
Understand the rukn and hukm
The rukn is the word or equivalent (e.g., writing to an absent person). The hukm is the manifestation (zuhur) of the acknowledged right for enforcement, not its initial creation.
This contrasts with insha (origination) — iqrar is ikhbar (declaration).
4
Examine the evidence
The Quranic verse (2:282) commands dictation, implying the debtor's acknowledgment is binding. The Sunnah cites the case of Ma'iz and al-Ghamidiyyah, stoned on their own iqrar. Ijma and rational argument further support.
The rational argument relies on the presumption that a sane person does not lie against their own interest.
5
Note special cases
A slave's iqrar for hudud/qisas is immediately valid; for financial matters it is deferred until emancipation. An authorized minor's iqrar is valid only for trade-related matters.
These exceptions protect the rights of others (master, creditors) while preserving the general validity of iqrar.

Responses as Acknowledgment of Debt
Explains which responses constitute acknowledgment of a claimed debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which verbal responses to a debt claim constitute acknowledgment (iqrār) according to the Hanafi school and explain the role of the pronoun in this ruling.

Core Idea
A defendant's response that includes a pronoun referring to the claimed debt is acknowledgment; without it, it is not.

When someone is accused of owing a debt, their reply can be legally binding. This lesson explores a key principle from Hanafi fiqh of qada: whether a response to a claim of debt is an acknowledgment (iqrār) depends on the presence of a pronoun linking it to the claimed amount. We will examine when such a response obliges payment and how the Hanafi school differs from the Shafiʿi and Hanbali positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and response
Start with the scenario: a plaintiff says 'you owe me one thousand dirhams.' The defendant replies with a phrase. Identify whether the phrase contains a pronoun (like 'it') that refers back to the claimed thousand.
2
Apply the pronoun rule
If the response includes a pronoun (e.g., 'weigh it' or 'count it'), it is acknowledgment according to the Hanafis, because the pronoun indicates the debt is recognized. If there is no pronoun (e.g., 'I weigh' or 'I count'), it is not acknowledgment.
The exception is if the response is clearly in jest, proven by witnesses.
3
Distinguish inter-madhhab differences
Note that the Shafiʿi and Hanbali schools hold that even with a pronoun, 'weigh it' or 'count it' is never acknowledgment. For 'I have paid you,' all three Hanafi imams agree it is acknowledgment.
This difference stems from how strictly they interpret the link between the response and the claim.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle of default
The text states a principle: a response that can be a reply to an initiation is made a response; a statement that can stand alone is treated as an initiation. This prevents imposing a debt by doubt.
This is a usul principle (al-asl) applied to the classification of ambiguous statements.
5
Expand via the list in Sharḥ al-Kāfī
The passage gives a list of phrases that are acknowledgment: 'yes,' promises to pay, instructions to take possession, admitting non-availability, etc. These all implicitly recognize the debt without rejection.
This list shows that acknowledgment can be inferred from context, not only from explicit admission.

Interpreting Ambiguous Numbers in Acknowledgments

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When 'one hundred and a dirham' is said, the spoken item interprets the number by custom, not the debtor's choice.

How do you interpret a statement like 'I owe you one hundred and a dirham'? The Hanafi school, by istihsan, reads it as 101 dirhams, because custom treats the specified item as an explanation of the ambiguous number. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the key distinction between measured/weighed items and counted goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and the ambiguity
The act is an acknowledgment of a debt (iqrar). The ambiguity is in the number 'one hundred' without specifying the type. The debtor adds a specific item ('a dirham') after it.
The issue is whether the item interprets the hundred or the hundred remains separate.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis
For measured/weighed items like dirhams, the hundred is interpreted as dirhams by istihsan (juristic preference), based on custom. The debtor owes 101 dirhams.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here.
3
Present the Shafi'i qiyas and the Hanafi rebuttal
Qiyas would require the debtor to explain the ambiguous number, because conjunction implies separation. The Hanafi response: custom makes the item a clarifying phrase, not a separate entity.
The Hanafi evidence is based on frequent usage (ta'ammul).
4
Extend the rule to other cases
'One hundred and a garment': the garment interprets the hundred, so he owes 100 garments. 'One hundred and three garments': both numbers are ambiguous, so the following item explains both, hence 103 garments. 'Half a dirham and a dinar and a garment' (all unspecified): he owes half of each.
The principle: if all items are unspecified, they share equally; if one is specified, the half applies only to the specified.

Attaching 'I Did Not Receive' to an Acknowledgment: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on connecting 'not received' to acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn regarding a non-receipt condition attached to an acknowledgment and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A condition contradicting an acknowledgment is a retraction, not a valid explanation.

Acknowledging a debt but then claiming non-receipt of the item raises a critical juristic question: does the condition qualify the debt or retract it? In this lesson, we examine a dispute between the eponymous founder of the Hanafi school, Abu Hanifa, and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), on whether connecting a phrase like 'I did not receive it' to an acknowledgment of debt for an unspecified slave is valid. The disagreement hinges on the difference between a permissible explanatory change and a prohibited retraction.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Identify the case: a person acknowledges 'I owe one thousand from the price of a slave' but adds 'I did not receive it.' The slave is unspecified. Determine the legal status of the added condition.
The condition can be connected (waṣl) or separated (faṣl) from the acknowledgment.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the condition is invalid whether connected or separated. He considers it a retraction (rujūʿ) of the acknowledgment, which is void. Therefore, the thousand is binding without exception.
His reasoning: the first part imposes an obligation; denying receipt contradicts it – a retraction is not allowed.
3
Identify Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn distinguish between connection and separation. If the condition is connected (e.g., said in one breath), it is valid as an explanatory change (bayān taghyīr), similar to an exception (istithnāʾ). Thus, nothing is binding. If separated (with a pause), it is invalid, and the acknowledgment stands.
They analogize to exceptions like 'I owe you ten except one.'
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the condition is connected, according to the Sahibayn the acknowledger is not liable, while according to Abu Hanifa he is liable. If separated, both agree he is liable (unless the condition is interpreted differently).
This directly affects court judgments in such cases.
5
Determine the muftā bihi
The passage indicates that Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon (muftā bihi) position in the Hanafi school, supported by Zufar and al-Hasan, and it is the one adopted for fatwa.
Later Hanafi works generally follow Abu Hanifa here.

Rulings on Acknowledgment from Invalid Transactions and Defective Dirhams
Rulings on acknowledgment from illegal transactions and defective dirhams, compared with ghasb and wadi'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the binding nature of an acknowledgment from an invalid transaction versus a valid one, and apply the Hanafi rules regarding later descriptions of defective dirhams, including the effect of connection and the exception for ghasb and wadi'a.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment from an invalid transaction binds absolutely, but a later claim of defective dirhams may be a retraction or explanation depending on connection and context.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqāʾiq addresses situations where an acknowledgment of debt references an invalid transaction (e.g., price of wine) or describes the dirhams as defective. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the absolute binding effect of the acknowledgment and the possibility that a later qualification may be a retraction. The Ṣāḥibayn differ from Abū Ḥanīfa on when the later statement is accepted. Additionally, the rules for ghasb and wadi'a differ because no soundness of the items is implied. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying fiqh in judicial contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that the case is about an acknowledgment (iqrār) that either stems from an invalid transaction or describes the dirhams as defective. The core question is whether the later qualification is a valid explanation or an impermissible retraction.
The acknowledgment itself creates a debt; the issue is the effect of the qualification.
2
State the positions of Abū Ḥanīfa and the Ṣāḥibayn
Abū Ḥanīfa holds that the later description is a retraction and thus ineffective, making the sound dirhams binding absolutely. The Ṣāḥibayn hold that if the description is connected, it is a permissible explanation and the acknowledger is believed; if separated, it is a retraction.
This applies to both invalid transactions and defective dirhams, except ghasb/wadi'a.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talīl)
Abū Ḥanīfa argues that the absolute contract implies soundness, so later defect claim is a retraction. The Ṣāḥibayn argue that the later statement can be an explanatory change, like an exception, and is valid if connected.
The analogy with istithnā' (exception) and the sale of defective goods is used.
4
Apply the rule to ghasb and wadi'a
In ghasb (extortion) and wadi'a (deposit), the acknowledger is believed absolutely because no soundness is implied; one may extort or deposit defective items. Thus the later description is always accepted, whether connected or separated.
This is an exception to the general rule.
5
Compare with the Shāfiʿī and Ḥanbalī position
Al-Shāfiʿī and Aḥmad hold that sound dirhams are binding absolutely in all cases, without accepting the later qualification. This highlights the Hanafi specificity.
The Hanafi school provides more nuanced rulings based on context.

Liability in Possession Disputes: Deposit vs. Usurpation, Loan, or Sale
Rulings on liability when dispute arises over nature of possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the statement of the denier (al-qawl li-man yunkir) in disputes over the nature of possession, and determine liability based on whether the taking was admitted with or without permission.

Core Idea
Admission of taking creates liability; the taker must prove permission, and the owner's claim prevails unless both agree on permission.

This passage from *Kanz al-Daqaiq* addresses a common judicial scenario: a person acknowledges receiving property but disputes the legal basis. The Hanafi school applies the principle that the burden of proof lies on the claimant, and the denier's statement is accepted with oath. The key is whether the taking was admitted to have been with or without permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the admission and the dispute
The taker admits taking the property. The dispute is over the nature: deposit vs. usurpation / loan / sale. Determine what each party claims.
In all scenarios, the taker acknowledges physical possession.
2
Determine who is the denier
The denier is the party who denies the other's claim. The denier's statement is given weight with oath. In deposit vs. usurpation, the owner denies the deposit claim (he claims no permission), so the owner is the denier. In deposit vs. loan/sale, both agree the taking was with permission, so the taker denies the specific contract (loan/sale) and is the denier.
The principle 'al-qawl li-man yunkir' applies to the party who denies the claim.
3
Apply the presumption of liability
Because the taker admitted taking, he is presumptively liable under the hadith 'The hand is liable...' He must prove permission to shift liability. If he cannot prove permission, he is liable.
The hadith is the basis for the initial liability.
4
Apply the ruling per scenario
In deposit vs. usurpation: the owner (denier) is believed with oath, so the taker is liable unless he proves deposit. In deposit vs. loan/sale: the taker (denier) is believed with oath, so he is not liable unless the owner proves the contract.
The difference hinges on whether permission (idhn) is admitted or contested.

Ruling on Depositary's Refusal to Swear for Joint Claimants
Ruling on deposit when depositary refuses oath for two claimants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a depositary refuses oath for two claimants and calculate the resulting payments.

Core Idea
A depositary's refusal to take an oath (nukul) is treated as an admission, creating liability for the full claimed amount.

When two people each claim they deposited the same sum with a depositary, and the depositary denies both, a judicial oath is required. If the depositary refuses to swear for both claimants, Hanafi law treats this refusal as an admission of both claims. This lesson examines the precise ruling and the additional payment that arises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
Two claimants each assert they deposited 1000 dirhams with the same depositary. The depositary denies both claims.
2
Determine the depositary's response
The judge requires the depositary to take an oath for each claimant. The depositary refuses (nukul) for one or both.
Nukul (refusal to swear) is a key legal concept in Hanafi qada.
3
Apply the rule of nukul
Nukul is treated as conclusive proof (hujja) like an admission (iqrar). It establishes the claimant's right.
This principle is unique to Hanafi fiqh in certain contexts.
4
Distinguish refusal for one vs both
If he refuses for one only, that claimant gets the existing deposit. If he refuses for both, the deposit is divided equally, but an extra payment is due.
The depositary must pay an additional sum because his nukul establishes both claims fully, but the physical deposit can only satisfy one.
5
Calculate the final payment
Existing deposit (1000) split equally between the two claimants (500 each). Since each's full claim is 1000, the depositary must pay an additional 1000, also split equally (500 each). Total: each gets 1000 (500 from deposit + 500 from depositary).
The depositary ends up paying 1500 total: the 1000 already in his hand goes to the claimants, plus an extra 500 to each.

Ruling on a plaintiff who denies evidence then brings it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between acceptable and unacceptable contradictions in a plaintiff's statements and apply the Hanafi rule on evidence after denial.

Core Idea
A plaintiff's earlier denial of evidence does not bar later proof if the contradiction can be explained by forgetfulness.

This lesson examines a common courtroom scenario: a plaintiff who initially says 'I have no evidence' but later produces it. The Hanafi school accepts the later evidence, reconciling the apparent contradiction through the possibility of forgetfulness and subsequent recall. However, this rule has limits: if the plaintiff says 'I have no right,' later claims are rejected, and a statement like 'this house is not mine' does not create a bar. Understanding these distinctions is essential for proper application of qada rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial statement
Determine what exactly the plaintiff says: 'I have no evidence,' 'I have no right,' or 'This is not mine.' Each triggers a different ruling.
The nuance lies in whether the plaintiff denies the evidence or denies the right.

Apply the principle of tanaqud
Contradiction (tanaqud) only nullifies a claim if it entails annulling an established right. If no right was established, the contradiction is reconciled by forgetfulness.

An admission (iqrar) to an unknown person (majhul) is void; thus saying 'this house is not mine' does not establish a right for anyone, so later proof is allowed.
4
Derive the ruling
If initial statement denies evidence or testimony, later evidence is accepted due to possible forgetfulness. If initial statement denies the right itself, later claim is rejected due to contradiction.
