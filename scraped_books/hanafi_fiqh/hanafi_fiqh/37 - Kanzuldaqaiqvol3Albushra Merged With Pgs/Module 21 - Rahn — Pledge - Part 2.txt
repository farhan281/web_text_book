Sale of Pledge by Guardian After Pledgor's Death
Ruling on continuity of pledge and sale by guardian.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling regarding the sale of a pledge by the guardian of a deceased pledgor and identify the conditions under which it is permissible.

Core Idea
A pledge survives the pledgor's death; the guardian may sell it with the pledgee's permission to settle the debt.

When a person who has given a pledge (rahn) dies, the pledge does not become void. It remains with the heirs. This lesson explains who has the authority to sell the pledge to repay the debt, focusing on the role of the guardian or executor, and the necessity of the pledgee's permission. The commentary from al-Fath adds an important nuance regarding the heirs being minors or adults.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal status of a pledge after the pledgor's death
Understand that the pledge is not invalidated by death; it continues as a security with the heirs.
This is based on the principle that the pledge is a contract that survives the parties.
2
Determine who may sell the pledge
The guardian (executor) of the pledgor, or a guardian appointed by the judge if there is no executor, has the authority to sell.
The guardian stands in the place of the deceased.
3
Identify the condition for sale
The sale requires the permission of the pledgee (creditor), because the pledge is a security for the debt.
Without the pledgee's consent, the sale cannot occur.
4
Distinguish between adult and minor heirs
If the heirs are adults, they are responsible for redeeming the pledge themselves. If they are minors, the guardian acts on their behalf.
This distinction appears in the commentary from al-Fath.

Substitute for a Destroyed Pawn and the Executor's Role
Rulings on injury to pawn and executor's role after pledgor's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over the pledgor's obligation when a pawned slave is killed and a substitute is given, and identify the mufta bihi position based on the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
When a pawn is destroyed and a substitute given, the debt remains fully owed according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, because the substitute stands in place of the original.

This passage examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: if a pawned slave is killed by another slave and the killer is given as compensation, does the pledgor's debt reduce? Three positions emerge from Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Zufar. The text also addresses the executor's role after the pledgor's death. Let's analyze the reasoning behind each view.

Guided Path
1
Set the scenario
Understand the facts: a slave worth 1000 is pawned, its value drops to 100, it is killed by a slave worth 100, and the killer is given as substitute.
2
Map the intra-Hanafi positions
Identify the three views: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (full debt owed), Muhammad (pledgor's choice), and Zufar (substitute becomes new pawn for its value).
3
Evaluate the qiyas argument
Analyze Muhammad's analogy to sale before delivery and usurpation. Understand why Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf reject it: the substitute is treated as the original 'flesh and blood'.
4
Connect to executor's authority
Note the ruling: after pledgor's death, the executor sells the pawn with the pledgee's permission; if no executor, the judge appoints one.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling when a pawned slave is killed by another slave. The text begins by explaining a previous phrase, then presents the core case: a slave worth one hundred kills a pawned slave (originally worth one thousand but depreciated to one hundred). The killer slave is given as compensation. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (عندهما), the pledgor must pay the full thousand to redeem the pawn; the substitute does not reduce the debt. According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, the pledgor has a choice: either pay the full debt and take the pawn, or hand over the substitute slave to the pledgee and be free of any further obligation. According to Zufar, the substitute becomes a pawn for one hundred only. The text then provides the reasoning for each position. Muhammad analogizes to a sold item destroyed before delivery or a usurped item killed in the usurper's hand, where the buyer/owner has a choice. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that the substitute stands in the exact place of the original (flesh and blood), so no change in the pawn occurred; therefore the debt remains. Zufar's view is that the pledgee's hand is for recovery, and the substitute is only a tenth, so the debt reduces proportionally. The text then discusses the executor's sale of the pawn after the pledgor's death, stating that the executor may sell with the pledgee's permission, and if no executor exists, the judge appoints one. This ruling is based on the principle that the pledge remains valid after death, and the executor acts in place of the testator. The passage ends with a note from al-Fath that if the heirs are adults, they are responsible for releasing the pawn.

Change of Pawn: Grape Juice, Wine, and Vinegar
Ruling on pawned grape juice transformation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a pawn remains valid when its substance changes, and identify the Hanafi criterion for measuring increase or decrease in the pawn.

Core Idea
A pawn is not voided by natural transformation if value is restored; quantity, not market value, governs the debt.

Imagine you have pawned a barrel of grape juice. It ferments into wine, then naturally turns to vinegar. Does the pawn still secure the debt? The Hanafi school rules that if the transformation is natural, the pawn continues. But what if someone forces the conversion? And what if the parties are of different religions? This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains the principles, including a subtle debate among Hanafi jurists about whether to measure by quantity or value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Start with the base case: grape juice pawned for ten dirhams, worth ten. It naturally ferments to wine, then naturally turns to vinegar, still worth ten. The ruling: the pawn remains valid.
Natural transformation (by itself) preserves the pawn.
2
Analyze forced conversion
If the wine does not turn to vinegar by itself, can the pledgee convert it? The ruling depends on the religion of the parties. If both are Muslim or the pledgor is Muslim, the pledgee may convert it; if the pledgor is a disbeliever, the Muslim pledgee may not.
Conversion by pledgee is permissible only when wine holds no value for the pledgor (i.e., if pledgor is Muslim).
3
Determine liability for wrongful conversion
If the pledgee converts the wine without right (e.g., when pledgor is disbeliever), he becomes a guarantor of the value of the wine on the day of conversion.
This is analogous to usurping a dhimmi's wine and converting it.
4
Understand the criterion for increase/decrease
The passage raises a question: Is increase or decrease measured by market value or by physical quantity? The preferred Hanafi view (from al-Zayla'i) is that for measured items like juice and vinegar, quantity (volume/weight) is the gauge, not value. A decrease in value alone does not reduce the debt unless quantity is reduced.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference; the relied-upon position is the quantity criterion.
5
Apply the principle of continuance
The text argues that wine is a valid subject of sale in continuance (e.g., if juice bought ferments before delivery, the contract remains valid with an option). By analogy, it is a valid subject of pawn in continuance, so the pawn remains valid after transformation back to a valuable item.
The key is that the original contract was valid, and the return to value restores full validity.

Increase of the Pledge: Ruling on Offspring, Fruit, Milk, and Wool
Ruling on offspring, fruit, milk, wool as part of pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on natural increase (namā') of a pledged item and explain its legal basis.

Core Idea
Natural increase of a pledged property belongs to the pledgor but is also part of the pledge — the debt is secured against both.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rule on the increase (namā') of a pledged item. When an animal gives birth or a tree bears fruit after being pledged, does the increase become part of the security? The answer is yes — and here is why the Hanafi school extends the pledge right to the increase.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify the two-part rule: (1) the increase belongs to the pledgor (owner), (2) it is also part of the pledge (marhun) along with the original item.
The increase is not owned by the pledgee; it remains the pledgor's property.
2
Understand the Legal Basis
The pledge right is 'mu'akkad' (strong) and extends to ancillaries (taba'). The increase is ancillary to the original because it is generated from its substance.
This is a key Hanafi principle: the strength of the right causes it to spread to inseparable attachments.
3
Distinguish Types of Increase
Divide increases into two categories: (a) natural increase (offspring, fruit, milk, wool) and compensation for part of the substance (arsh, 'aqr) — these are included; (b) earnings, wages, gifts — these are not included.
The distinction is based on whether the increase arises from the substance itself or from external activity.
4
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Shafi'i agrees with Hanafi on inclusion. Maliki holds that fruit on a tree is not included unless it was attached at the time of the contract.
The practical consequence: under Maliki law, harvested fruit is not subject to the pledge; under Hanafi it is.

The Hide of a Pledged Sheep: Continuing Pledge After Death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the hide of a pledged sheep that dies, explain its proportion to the original pledge, and distinguish the intra-madhhab dispute regarding tanning with valuable materials.

Core Idea
The hide of a dead pledged sheep remains a pledge for its value proportionally, because the pledge is confirmed by destruction.

When a pledged sheep dies naturally, the pledge does not vanish entirely. Instead, the hide, if tanned, becomes a new pledge for its value at death. This lesson unpacks the ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentaries, showing how the original debt continues to secure the hide, and why tanning with valuable materials creates a legal dispute within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core case
A sheep worth ten dirhams is pledged for ten dirhams. It dies naturally (not slaughtered). Its hide is tanned and worth one dirham. Determine the legal status of the hide as a pledge.
The value at death is crucial; if the hide is worth two dirhams, the pledge is for two dirhams.
2
Trace the reasoning from the commentaries
The hide is a pledge because the original pledge is 'confirmed by destruction' (yataqarrar bi al-halak). The debt remains, so the surviving asset replaces the lost asset proportionally.
This contrasts with a sale where the contract is rescinded by destruction before possession.
3

If the tanning uses valuable materials, two opinions exist: (1) the original pledge is voided and the hide becomes a pledge for the tanning increase only; (2) the original pledge remains because it is stronger. The second is preferred.
Tarjih: the original debt-based right is stronger than the right arising from the tanning.
4

In a sale, if the sold sheep dies before possession, the contract is rescinded and the hide does not become sold. In a pledge, the contract survives because the debt remains owed.
This illustrates the principle that pledge is a security contract, not a transfer of ownership.

Increasing Collateral vs Increasing Debt in Pledge
Ruling on increasing the pledged property and increasing the debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the validity of increasing collateral and increasing debt in a pledge according to the Hanafi school, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad vs Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Adding collateral is valid; increasing debt on the same pledge is invalid because it creates commonality (shuyu‘) in the pledge.

In the Hanafi school, a pledge (rahn) is a security contract that allows flexibility under certain conditions. This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a key point: while you can add more items to an existing pledge without changing the debt, you cannot increase the debt without adding new collateral. An intra-Hanafi disagreement exists, with Abu Yusuf arguing by analogy to sale that both increases should be permissible. Let's walk through the reasoning.

Core Idea
Offspring of a pledge perishes for free; if the original perishes, the offspring is redeemed by its share of the debt.

When property is pledged as collateral, any offspring or increase (nama') born to it raises questions of ownership and debt coverage. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with commentary (sharh) on the scenario where a debtor substitutes one pledged item for another. The ruling: if a slave is pledged for 1000, and the debtor gives another slave of equal value to replace it, the first slave remains a pledge until the creditor returns it to the debtor. The second slave is held as a trust (amanah) until the creditor decides to make it the substitute. If the second slave perishes before substitution, the creditor is not liable because it is a trust. (2) Possession: There is a difference of opinion on whether a new formal possession (qabd) is required when the second slave becomes the substitute. One view requires it because the creditor's hand over the second is a trust hand, not a pledge hand. The other view says it is not required because the pledge is a gratuitous contract like a gift, and the trust possession suffices. The relied-upon position is not explicitly stated, but both views are transmitted. The authority weight is from later sources (Takmilat al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, Ayni's commentary).

Substituting a Pledged Item: Liability and Trust Status
Ruling on replacing a pledged item and the status of each

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the status of the original and replacement items when a pledge is substituted, and explain the internal dispute regarding the need for new possession.

Core Idea
The original pledge stays in liability until returned; the replacement is a trust until formally substituted.

Imagine you have pledged your watch to secure a loan, and later you want to replace it with a ring of equal value. What happens to each item legally? This passage from the Hanafi fiqh tradition provides a clear ruling: the original pledge remains in the mortgagee's liability (daman) until formally returned, while the replacement is held as a trust (amanah) until the substitution is complete. Additionally, there is an important internal debate among Hanafi scholars about whether a new physical possession (qabd) is required for the substitution to take effect. This lesson unpacks these rulings and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: a debtor pledges an item, then delivers a different item of equal value as a replacement. Both parties agree to the substitution.
The value condition is specified in the source: each item is worth the same amount.
2
Determine the status of the original pledge
The original item remains in the mortgagee's liability (daman). It does not automatically exit daman upon delivery of the replacement. The mortgagee is liable for it until it is physically returned to the pledgor.
Reason: the debt and possession that created the daman still exist.
3
Determine the status of the replacement
The replacement is held as a trust (amanah) from the moment of delivery until it is officially substituted for the original. If it perishes before substitution, the mortgagee is not liable unless negligent.
This is because the replacement was not taken with the intention of securing the debt; it is a temporary trust.
4
Analyze the internal dispute on qabd
Two Hanafi views: (1) A new formal possession (qabd) is required for substitution, because the mortgagee's hand over the replacement is a trust hand and cannot substitute for the lien hand. (2) No new qabd is needed, as pledge is a gratuitous contract like a gift, and trust possession can substitute for trust possession.
The text does not resolve which view is preferable (mufta bihi).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Book of Pledge, discussing the scenario of replacing a pledged item. The author first states the general rule: the original pledged item remains in the mortgagee's liability (daman) until it is formally returned to the pledgor. The replacement item is considered a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's possession until it is officially substituted for the original. The commentary then addresses a potential question: if the replacement perishes before the substitution, is the mortgagee liable? The answer depends on whether the original has been returned. If it perishes before the original is returned, the mortgagee is liable for the first (since it was in his daman) but not for the second (since it is still amanah). If it perishes after the original is returned? The text includes a gloss clarifying that if the replacement perishes before the mortgagee returns the original, he is not liable. Then it presents an internal Hanafi dispute (between two qeel) on whether a new formal possession (qabd) is required when the substitution takes place. One view requires new qabd because the mortgagee's hand on the replacement is a trust hand, and a trust hand cannot substitute for the mortgagee's lien hand. The other view, considered stronger by some, says new qabd is not required because pledge is a gratuitous contract like gift, and only the financial value is in daman; trust possession can substitute for trust possession. The passage ends by restating that the replacement is amanah.
