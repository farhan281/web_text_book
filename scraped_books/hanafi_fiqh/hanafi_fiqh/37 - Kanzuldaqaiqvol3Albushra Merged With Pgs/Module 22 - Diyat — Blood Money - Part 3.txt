Determining Liability for a Corpse: Qasamah in Mosques, Roads, Deserts, and Rivers
Rulings on qasamah and diyah for corpses in various locations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for qasamah and diyah to corpses found in various locations by identifying custodial control and location type.

Core Idea
Liability for qasamah follows custodial control: public spaces fall on treasury; private or neighborhood spaces on inhabitants or owners.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq presents the Hanafi classification of locations where a murdered person might be found without evidence. The ruling on qasamah (oaths) and diyah (blood-money) is determined not by the act of murder itself but by who has custodial responsibility over the place. Understanding these categories is essential for accurately assigning liability in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Location
Read the passage and note the four main location types: neighborhood mosque (masjid mahallah), congregational mosque/road (jami'/shari'), desert (bariyah), and riverbank (shati'). Also note the subcategories: small river, owned desert, etc.
The commentary divides each location further.
2
Determine Custodial Control
For each location, ask: who has management or preservation (tadbir, hifz) over it? The inhabitants of the neighborhood for a neighborhood mosque; the imam (government) for public roads and congregational mosques; no one for open desert or large river; the owners of the bank or nearest village for a riverbank.
If the location is owned privately, the owner is responsible.
3
Apply the Ruling
If custodial control is held by a specific group or person, qasamah (fifty oaths) is required from them, and diyah is paid by their 'aqila or treasury. If no one has control, the blood is wasted (yuhdar) and no liability arises.
For public spaces, diyah comes from bayt al-mal, not from any individual.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Difference
The commentary mentions a difference between al-Qahistani and al-Fath regarding the builder of a neighborhood mosque. Al-Qahistani restricts liability to when the builder is unknown; otherwise the builder is liable. This shows how the principle of custodial responsibility is interpreted.
The relied-upon position seems to follow al-Fath (general liability on inhabitants).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from Kanz al-Daqaiq on the laws of qasamah (oaths of compurgation) when a slain person is found in various locations. The basic principle is that the obligation to swear oaths (qasamah) and pay blood-money (diyah) falls on those who have custodial responsibility (tadbir) over the place.  

If the corpse is found in a neighborhood mosque, the people of that neighborhood bear the qasamah and diyah because they are responsible for its management. The commentator clarifies that if the mosque is built for strangers, the builder assumes responsibility. A difference is noted: according to al-Qahistani, this ruling applies only when the builder is unknown; if known, the builder is liable.  

In a congregational mosque (jami') or a public road (shari'), no qasamah is imposed because no specific individual or group has exclusive control. Instead, the diyah is paid from the public treasury (bayt al-mal), which represents collective public property. The same applies to public bridges and markets that are not attached to neighborhoods.  

If the corpse is found in a desert (bariyah) or the main stream of the Euphrates, the blood is wasted (yuhdar) — no liability because no one has control. However, if the river is small enough to entitle pre-emption (shuf'ah), the owners of the riverbanks are liable. Similarly, if the desert is owned or close to a village, the owner or villagers must pay.  

If the body is found detained on a riverbank (shati'), the nearest village is responsible because they use the bank for water and livestock.  

The commentary also distinguishes between private and public ownership of the bank, and between large and small rivers. The Hanafi school's nuanced approach is evident: liability follows control and benefit, not mere proximity.

Key Terms
قسامة (qasamah): Fifty oaths taken by the aggrieved party or the accused to determine liability in a murder case where no clear evidence exists.
محلة (mahallah): A neighborhood or locality; a subdivision of a city with its own inhabitants.
شاطئ (shati'): The bank of a river; the shoreline.

Effect of Wali's Claim on Qasamah in Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qasamah procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, according to the relied-upon Hanafi position, a wali's claim against one specific inhabitant does not drop qasamah from the rest, and distinguish this from the opposing view of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Claiming against one inhabitant does not exonerate all; qasamah remains on the neighborhood.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentaries (al-‘Ayni, Fath, etc.) examines how the wali's claim alters qasamah obligations. You'll learn the procedural rule: a claim against an outsider drops qasamah from the neighborhood, but a claim against a specific inhabitant does not. The text also notes an intra-Hanafi disagreement where Abu Yusuf held a contrary view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wali's claim scenarios
The passage distinguishes: (1) claim against an outsider, (2) claim against a specific inhabitant. State the ruling for each.
Outsider = not from the neighborhood; inhabitant = from the neighborhood.
2
Understand the rationale for the difference
Why does claiming against an outsider drop qasamah? Because the oath is only binding when directly claimed; by claiming an outsider, the wali implicitly exonerates the neighborhood. But claiming against an inhabitant does not drop it because the qasamah is to prove the killer is among them, and specifying one does not negate that others could be involved.
The claim against an inhabitant does not mean the wali has proven the case; it's just an accusation.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf (in a non-authoritative transmission) held that claim against one inhabitant drops qasamah from the rest, reasoning that the wali has identified the killer. The relied-upon position (Muhammad and Abu Hanifa) rejects this, because the identification was not judicially confirmed.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the majority view.
4
Apply the qasamah oath rule
If one mustahlaf says 'Zaid killed him,' he must swear 'I did not kill him and I know no killer other than Zaid.' According to Muhammad, he swears about knowledge; Abu Yusuf says no need because he confessed to Zaid's involvement.
The text adopts Muhammad's view as preferred.
5
Extend to fighting groups and mutawwalin
If a group fights with weapons and leaves a dead body, qasamah is initially on the neighborhood unless the wali claims against the fighters. But if the fighters were acting under a legitimate interpretation (mutawwalin) or were non-Muslims/kharjites, no qasamah arises.
Justified killing (e.g., in battle) exempts from liability.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of rulings on how the wali's (guardian of the slain) claim affects qasamah. It is an intra-Hanafi dispute because Abu Yusuf differs from Muhammad (and presumably Abu Hanifa) on one point.

First: If the wali claims against a person who is not from the neighborhood where the corpse was found, the qasamah drops from the neighborhood people. The logic is that the oath (yamin) is only required when there is a direct claim; by claiming against an outsider, the wali implicitly exonerates the neighborhood. He cannot later claim against them.

Second: If the wali claims against one specific individual from the neighborhood, the qasamah does not drop from the rest of the neighborhood. This is because the qasamah on the neighborhood is to determine that the killer is among them, not that they all killed. Claiming against one does not contradict that; he might be the killer, but others might be complicit. However, there is a transmission from Abu Yusuf that the qasamah and diyah do drop from the rest—this is an intra-Hanafi difference.

Third: If a group of people are fighting with swords and a dead body is left behind, the qasamah is initially on the neighborhood where the body is found, because the body is among them and they have a duty of preservation. But if the wali claims against the fighting group or a specific member, the neighborhood is exonerated. Note that mere claim against the fighters does not prove liability; evidence (bayyinah) is needed. Also, if the fighters were acting under a legitimate interpretation (mutawwalun) or were non-Muslims/kharjites, no qasamah arises because the death is considered justifiable.

Fourth: If one of the accused (mustahlaf) swears and says 'Zaid killed him', he is to swear: 'I did not kill him and I do not know any killer other than Zaid.' This is because by naming Zaid, he is not accepting the murder himself. According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, he must also swear about knowledge. But Abu Yusuf says he does not need to swear about knowledge because he already confessed to Zaid's involvement—the confession suffices. The text prefers Muhammad's view.

Throughout, the Hanafi school emphasizes that qasamah is a means to determine liability and that claims have specific procedural effects.

Key Terms
ولي (wali): The legal guardian of the slain person, typically a close male relative, entitled to claim qasamah and demand diyah.
مستحلف (mustahlaf): The person from whom an oath is demanded in a qasamah proceeding; one of the accused inhabitants.
متأولين (mutawwalin): Those who act on a legitimate interpretation (ta'wil) of law, e.g., in a battle or defense; they are not liable for unintended death.

Core Idea

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explains the intricate rulings of qasamah (compurgatory oaths) when a corpse is found. Guided Path
1
Identify the claim scenarios
Read the first part of the passage which states: claim against an outsider drops qasamah; claim against a specific inhabitant does not. Understand these two rules clearly.
The wali is the heir of the deceased.
2
Analyze the rationale
The commentary explains that claiming an outsider drops qasamah because the oath is tied to the claim. Since the wali did not claim against the inhabitants, they are exonerated. Conversely, claiming a specific inhabitant does not drop because qasamah exists to uncover the killer from among the inhabitants, and a single accusation does not negate the possibility of others.
Abu Yusuf's non-authoritative view holds it drops even for a specific inhabitant.
3

The passage adds: if two groups fight and a body is found, qasamah is on the mahallah unless the wali claims against the fighting group (or a specific fighter). In that case, neither the mahallah nor the fighters bear qasamah until proof is established. Also check if the fighters were acting legitimately (muta'awwilin) – then no liability.
Swords is metonymy for any weapon.
4
Examine the oath requirement
When a mustahlaf (one asked to swear) accuses a specific person (e.g., Zaid), the oath must negate his own guilt and affirm that he knows no other killer besides Zaid. This is Muhammad's position. Abu Yusuf says swearing on knowledge is unnecessary since he already identified a killer. The relied-upon view is Muhammad's.
This ensures no other killer escapes liability.

Core Idea
The 'Aqilah are the group liable for diyat; Hanafis say the diwan, Shafi'is say the 'asabat.

This lesson opens the Book of al-Ma'aqil, clarifying the identity and liability of the 'Aqilah (the group that pays blood money) in cases of accidental and quasi-intentional killing. Locate the dalil

Apply the hierarchy

Commentary
This is the opening of the Book of al-Ma'aqil (the liability group for blood money). The text begins with a discussion of the proper title of the book. Some scholars argue it should be 'al-'Awaqil' (plural of 'Aqilah) rather than 'al-Ma'aqil' (which is plural of 'ma'qilah' meaning diyat), since the content focuses on who bears the diyat (the 'Aqilah), not the diyat itself which was already covered in the Book of Diyat. The author defends the title by explaining that 'ma'aqil' can be a metaphor for 'people of the ma'aqil' (the diyat payers). The term 'ma'qilah' refers to the diyat (blood money) itself, so-named because it prevents bloodshed, deriving from 'to hobble' (aqala al-ba'eer). The ruling stated: every diyat obligated by the very act of killing (i.e., in qatl shibh al-'amd and qatl al-khata') is upon the 'Aqilah of the killer. The 'Aqilah are defined as the group who pay the diyat. The proof for this is the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding the fetus where he said: 'Stand and pay his blood-money' (fidau), and the practice of 'Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) who judged that diyat for accidental killing is upon the 'Aqilah, and this was accepted by the Companions, forming an ijma'. An exception is made for diyat that becomes obligatory by confession or settlement — that remains on the killer's personal wealth because he does not deserve leniency. The text then addresses the identity of the 'Aqilah. According to the Hanafi school, the 'Aqilah are the people of the diwan (military register) — i.e., the army. This is based on 'Umar's practice. In contrast, al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad hold that the 'Aqilah are the 'asabat (agnatic relatives) because that was the practice at the time of the Prophet and cannot be abrogated. The Hanafis respond with the evidence of 'Umar's consensus. The text further explains that if the killer is not from the people of the diwan, then the 'Aqilah are the people of his craft (hirafah), and if not, then the people of his alliance (half).

أهل الحرفة (ahl al-hirafah): The people of the killer's craft or profession, who form the 'Aqilah if he is not from the diwan.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-madhhab (Hanafi) ruling and an inter-madhhab dispute with al-Shafi'i. The Hanafi position is that the killer (who caused accidental death) is considered one of the 'aqila members; he must pay his share of the diyah along with his tribe. The reasoning: diyah is spread among many people; excluding the killer while charging others is unjust. The killer's support for the victim is even stronger (since he caused the harm). Al-Shafi'i argues that the killer is excused (he did not intend harm) and thus should not pay anything; since the whole diyah is not on him, the part should also not be. The Hanafi response: requiring the full diyah from the killer alone would be oppressive, but requiring a small portion is not; moreover, the killer's responsibility is even greater because he is the cause. They cite the Quranic verse 'No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another' to argue that the killer should not bear the burden of others' shares, but he does bear his own. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the killer contributes.

Scope of 'Aqila Liability: Freed Slaves, Mawla, and Excluded Crimes
Rulings on 'aqila of freed slave, client by clientage, and exclusions for certain liabilities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on which cases the 'aqila is liable for diyah and which cases are excluded, and apply the exception when the 'aqila confirm a confession.

Core Idea
The 'aqila bears diyah only where mutual support exists; intentional acts and acknowledgments without confirmation are excluded.

This passage consolidates several key rulings about the scope of 'aqila (‏العاقلة) responsibility in Hanafi law. It clarifies who bears diyah for a freed slave, a client by clientage, and lists specific cases where the 'aqila does not bear liability. Understanding these boundaries is essential for correctly apportioning blood-money obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the 'aqila for a freed slave
The 'aqila of a manumitted slave is the tribe of his patron (the one who freed him). The text states this is supported by the hadith 'Mawla al-qawm minhum' and analogy to mutual support.
The patron's tribe stands as the 'aqila because the freed slave's support comes from them.
2
Identify the 'aqila for mawla al-muwalaat
A client by clientage (mawla al-muwalaat) has his diyah borne by his patron and the patron's tribe. This resembles the patronate of emancipation (walā' al-'itāqah). Note the Shafi'i disagreement.
The text says the patron's tribe is responsible; the Shafi'i view is that nothing is obligatory upon them.
3
List the exclusions from 'aqila liability
The 'aqila does not bear: (a) crimes committed by a slave (liability on the master), (b) intentional killing (killer pays from his own wealth), (c) diyah by compromise (sulh) or confession (unless confirmed), and (d) injuries less than arsh al-mūḍiḥah (wound exposing bone). The proof is a hadith: 'لا تعقل العواقل عمدا ولا عبدا ولا صلحا ولا اعترافا ولا ما دون أرش الموضحة'.
Rationale: a slave does not participate in mutual support, and compromise/confession lack the 'aqila's consent.
4
Apply the exception for confession confirmation
If the 'aqila confirm the confessor's admission, they become liable because confirmation is an acknowledgment from them. Also, if evidence (bayyinah) establishes the crime, the 'aqila are bound even without confirmation.
This exception shows that the 'aqila's liability can arise through their own action or external proof.

Aqila Liability for Killing a Slave
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute on 'āqilah for slave's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the 'āqilah bears diyah for the accidental killing of a slave, evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument, and identify the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
The 'āqilah bears diyah for accidental killing of a slave because the slave is human; lesser injuries are property.

This lesson examines a specific rule from the Hanafi law of diyat: when a free person accidentally kills a slave, the 'āqilah (agnatic helpers) must pay the diyah. This ruling is contested by Imam al-Shafi'i, who holds that the 'āqilah never bears for a slave, because the slave is property. We'll walk through the Hanafi proof based on the human nature of the slave, the interpretative difference over the relevant hadith, and see how the two schools differ in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the exact scenario: a free person accidentally kills a slave. According to the matn, the diyah is on the 'āqilah. Note that this applies only to killing, not to injuries less than death.
The commentator specifies that the 'āqilah does not bear for injury to a slave below the level of death.
2
Identify the Shafi'i opposition
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the 'āqilah does not bear diyah for a slave at all; the killer must pay from his own wealth.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (qiyas)
The Hanafi proof is qiyas: the slave is an Adamī (human), so analogically similar to a free person. Since diyah is compensation for a human life, not for property, the 'āqilah bears it. Distinguish from injuries less than death, which are treated as property damages.
The commentary emphasises that diyah is 'badal al-ādamī' (substitute for the human), not 'al-māl' (property).
4
Analyze the Shafi'i proof (hadith) and Hanafi response
The Shafi'i rely on the hadith 'La ta'qil al-'āqilatu 'amdan wa lā 'abdan jīnāya'. The Hanafis interpret 'abd here as the crime of a slave (jīnāyat 'abd), not a crime against a slave. Thus, the hadith means the 'āqilah does not bear for intentional crimes nor for the slave's own crime. The Hanafis apply the same hadith to crimes committed by the slave, not against the slave.
The Hanafi interpretation is based on the grammatical construct (idāfah) and consistency with other proofs.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Hanafi, if a free person accidentally kills a slave, the 'āqilah pays. Under Shafi'i, the killer pays from his own wealth. This affects who bears the financial burden.
