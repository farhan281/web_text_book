The Principle of Halat al-Ramy: Liability Determined at the Time of Shooting
Establishes principle of halat al-ramy and applies to four branches.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that the moment of the act (ramy) is determinative for liability in killing, and apply it to the four branches of apostasy, conversion, emancipation, and witness retraction.

Core Idea
Liability in killing is fixed at the act of shooting, not at death — the target's later change of status does not alter the shooter's responsibility.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi jinayat (homicide law): al-mu'tabar halat al-ramy. The rule is that the shooter's liability is determined by the state of affairs at the time he releases the arrow, not when it strikes. This principle has far-reaching consequences in cases where the victim's status changes between the act and the death. We will explore the rule itself, its rational basis, and its application to four specific scenarios drawn from the classical fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the principle and its rationale
The text opens with 'al-mu'tabar halat al-ramy' (the considered state is the time of shooting). Understand that this is because the shooting is the shooter's act under his control. The impact is merely a consequence.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on causality and control.
2
Apply to the apostasy case
If a Muslim shoots at another Muslim, and the target apostatizes before the arrow hits, Abu Hanifa holds that diyah is due; Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say nothing is due. Compare the reasonings: Abu Hanifa focuses on the act, Sahibayn on the state at destruction.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is central here.
3
Apply to the conversion case
If a shooter aims at a harbi or apostate who then converts to Islam before the arrow hits, there is consensus that no liability arises. This is because at the time of shooting the target had no inviolability.
Ijma on this branch.
4
Apply to the slave emancipation case
If a shooter aims at a slave who is emancipated before the arrow hits, Abu Hanifa says the shooter owes the slave's value (since at shooting he was property). Muhammad says only the depreciation due to the wound, and Zufar says full diyah (since at impact he was free).
Three opinions within Hanafi school.
5
Apply to the witness retraction case
If a convicted adulterer is stoned, and a witness retracts after the stone is thrown but before it hits, the stoner is not liable because at the time of throwing the blood was mubah. However, the retracting witness may be liable for compensation.
Separates liability of shooter from that of witness.

Commentary
This passage opens a new chapter (bab) on the principle that the legally relevant moment for determining liability in killing is the time of the act (shooting), not the subsequent events before death. It is a ruling with proof, as it provides both the rule and the rationale. The author states the principle: 'al-mu'tabar halat al-ramy' (the considered state is the time of shooting). He then applies it to four branches. The first branch: if the target apostatizes after being shot but before the arrow hits, according to Abu Hanifa, the shooter must pay blood money because the act occurred when the target was still a Muslim. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree, saying no liability because at the moment of impact the target had no inviolability. The second branch: if the target converts to Islam after being shot, the shooter owes nothing by consensus, because the shooting was aimed at a non-protected person. The third branch: if the target is a slave and is emancipated after being shot but before impact, Abu Hanifa holds that the shooter must pay the slave's value (since at the time of shooting he was a slave), while Muhammad says only the depreciation in value is due, and Zufar says full blood money because at impact he was free. The fourth branch: if a person is stoned by order of the court, and a witness retracts after the stone is thrown but before it hits, the stoner is not liable because at the moment of throwing the blood was permissible. However, the retracting witness may be liable for compensation. The commentary draws from various sources and clarifies that the principle is based on the shooter's act being under his control. The Hanafi position is presented along with intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Key Terms
حالة الرمي (halat al-ramy): The moment when the shooting occurs; the state of the target and shooter at that time is determinative for liability.
القيمة (al-qima): The market value of a slave; owed by the shooter if he kills a slave, as per Abu Hanifa.
السراية (al-siraya): The causal chain from the act to the death; emancipation cuts it off, reducing liability to depreciation only according to Muhammad.

Definition and Ruling of Qasamah
Defines qasamah and states its ruling: fifty oaths by the accused locality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qasamah, state its ruling (wajib), identify its conditions and implications, and explain the intra-Hanafi variation regarding specific accusation.

Core Idea
Qasamah: fifty oaths by the accused locality, not the plaintiff, to determine diyah when the killer is unknown.

Qasamah is a unique legal procedure that balances the rights of the victim and the accused when a killer remains unknown. This lesson unpacks its definition, ruling, and conditions according to the Hanafi school, along with an important intra-madhhab difference narrated from Abu Yusuf. Understanding qasamah is essential for grasping how Islamic law preserves blood inviolability while protecting the innocent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the legal classification of qasamah. According to the Hanafi school, it is wajib when the killer is unknown and the body shows signs of foul play.
Wajib means obligatory; its proof is from the Sunnah (consensus).
2
Analyze the legal definition (shar'an)
Deconstruct the definition: it consists of oaths (ayman) taken by the people of the locality where the body is found, each swearing they did not kill and know no killer.
The definition excludes the plaintiff (wali); he does not swear.
3
List the conditions for applicability
Qasamah applies when: (1) a dead body is found with marks of violence, (2) the killer is unknown (not identified by confession or clear evidence), and (3) the claim is against the locality or an unspecified person. If the killer is known, qasamah drops.
The condition 'unknown killer' is explicitly mentioned in the text.
4
Describe the procedure
The wali (guardian) selects fifty men from the locality. Each swears individually: 'By Allah, I did not kill him, and I know no killer.' Upon their oaths, the 'aqila pays the diyah over three years.
The oath is sworn individually ('I'), not collectively ('we').
5
Note the intra-Hanafi variation
A narration from Abu Yusuf (and also from Abu Hanifa) states that if the claim is against a specific person, qasamah is dropped; instead the accused swears a single oath. The general ruling (fifty men from the locality) remains mufta bihi.
This difference affects whether the whole locality or a specific individual bears the oaths.

Commentary
This passage introduces the book of Qasamah, which deals with cases where a slain person is found and the killer is unknown. It is a definition and ruling statement. The term 'qasamah' linguistically means something put in place of oaths; legally it refers to oaths sworn by the people of a locality where a slain body is found. Each swears: 'I did not kill him, and I know no killer.' The ruling: Fifty men from the locality are chosen by the legal guardian (wali) and they swear. Upon their oaths, the 'aqila (paternal relatives) of the locality pay the diyah over three years, whether the killing appears intentional or accidental. The guardian himself does not swear. The passage also notes that if the killer is known, qasamah is dropped and the case proceeds normally. There is an intra-Hanafi variation from Abu Yusuf that if the claim is against a specific person, qasamah is not applied; instead the accused swears one oath. The commentary by al-Fath and al-Ayni explains details: the selection of the fifty is the guardian's right, and he may choose suspects or pious men; the oath wording differs—each swears individually using 'I', not 'we', to avoid falsehood if he alone killed. The wisdom (hikmah) of qasamah is to protect the accused from retaliation and to ensure the victim's blood is not wasted, while emphasizing the gravity of bloodshed through repeated oaths.

Key Terms
قسامة (qasamah): A legal procedure where fifty men from the locality where a slain body is found swear that they did not kill and do not know the killer, leading to diyah on their 'aqila.
ولي (wali): The legal guardian of the slain person, who has the right to demand blood-money or retaliation and selects the fifty oath-takers.

Liability of the 'Aqila for Accidental Killing of a Slave
Ruling and rebuttal on 'aqila liability for slave's killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that the 'aqila bears diya for a free man's accidental killing of a slave and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A slave's life is human, so the 'aqila bears diya; only limb injuries are property.

When a free man accidentally kills a slave, who must pay the compensation? The Hanafi school distinguishes between the life of a slave (treated as a human life) and limb injuries (treated as property). This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning, contrasts it with the Shafi'i view, and clarifies the basis of the 'aqila's liability.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the precise ruling: when a free man accidentally kills a slave, the diya is upon the 'aqila (the killer's 'clan' or guarantors).
This applies only to killing, not to limb injuries.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Shafi'i school holds that the 'aqila does not bear for a slave at all, whether life or limb; the killer must pay from his own wealth.
Their basis: slaves are property, and the hadith 'la ta'qilu al-'awaqilu ... wa la 'abdan janiyah'.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i dalil: the hadith is interpreted as blanket exclusion of slaves from 'aqila liability, and the slave is considered mere property.
The hadith is authentic; the dispute is over its application.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: the hadith refers to intentional injury of a slave, not accidental. Moreover, the diya for life is a substitute for a human being (badal al-adami), not property, so the 'aqila bears it by qiyas to a free person.
The distinction between life and limbs is key: limbs are property, life is human.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Hanafi, the 'aqila pays diya over three years; under Shafi'i, the killer pays the slave's market value immediately from his own wealth.
This affects who bears the financial burden and the timeline.

Commentary
This passage addresses the liability of the 'aqila when a free man accidentally kills a slave. The Hanafi position is that the 'aqila must pay the diya, because a slave is a human being (adami), so his life is compensated like a free person's, not as mere property. The diya for the slave is a substitute for a human being, not for lost wealth. This is distinguished from injuries below the life (i.e., limb injuries), which are treated as property damages and not borne by the 'aqila. The Shafi'i view is that neither the life nor the limbs of a slave are borne by the 'aqila; rather, the killer pays from his own wealth. The Hanafi response relies on qiyas (analogy) to free persons: since the slave is human, the same principle of the 'aqila's liability applies. The hadith 'la ta'qilu al-'awaqilu 'amdan wa la 'abdan janiyah' is interpreted to refer to intentional homicide of a slave, not accidental. So the Hanafi school takes the hadith as applying to intentional acts, not accidental killing of a slave. The commentary concludes that the slave's injury necessitates handing him over (if killed, compensation), unless the master ransoms him.

Key Terms
بدل الآدمي (badal al-adami): The compensation (diya) for taking a human life, which is considered a substitute for the person, not for property.
أطراف العبد (atraf al-'abd): Limb injuries or dismemberment of a slave, which are not borne by the 'aqila according to Hanafi.
