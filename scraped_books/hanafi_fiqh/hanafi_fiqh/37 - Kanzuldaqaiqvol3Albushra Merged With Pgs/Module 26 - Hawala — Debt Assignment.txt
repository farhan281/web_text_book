Executor Accepting Hawala for Minor's Property
Ruling on executor accepting hawala for minor's benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor accepting a hawala (debt transfer) on behalf of a minor and explain the condition of benefit that must be satisfied.

Core Idea
An executor may accept a hawala for a minor only if beneficial—guardianship is based on the ward's interest.

This lesson examines a short but important ruling from the Hanafi fiqh: an executor (wasī) may accept a hawala (transfer of a debt) on behalf of a minor's property only if it is beneficial (khayr) for the minor. The ruling highlights the principle that guardianship (wilāyah) is a trust that must be exercised in the ward's best interest. The passage also touches on related issues of division and sale, but we will focus on the hawala ruling itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is the executor's acceptance of a hawala (transfer of a debt owed to the minor to another debtor).
The Arabic term used is 'احتياله' which refers to accepting a hawala.
2
Determine the ruling
The text states it is valid (صح) – the acceptance is legally effective.
Validity is not absolute; it depends on the condition.
3
Identify the condition
The condition is that the hawala must be better (خير) for the minor. The phrase 'لو خيرا له' makes benefit a prerequisite.
If the hawala is not beneficial, the executor should not accept it.
4
Understand the legal basis
Guardianship (الولاية) is exercised for the ward's benefit (الولاية نظرية). The executor must act in the minor's best interest, and accepting a beneficial hawala fulfills that duty.
This principle underlies many Hanafi rulings on guardianship.

Commentary
This short passage addresses the validity of an executor accepting a hawala (transfer of debt) on behalf of a minor. The ruling is that the executor may accept a hawala if it is beneficial (khayr) for the minor, i.e., if the new debtor is more creditworthy than the original. The guardian's authority (wilaya) is based on acting in the minor's best interest. The text then digresses into clarifying the validity of division (qisma) for measurable items, comparing it to sale, and then discusses the sale of a slave by an executor in the absence of creditors. It confirms that the executor, like the deceased, can sell validly even in the absence of creditors because the creditors' right is to the monetary value, not to the specific asset. This is contrasted with a slave authorized to trade, where the master cannot sell him because creditors have a right to the slave's earnings.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawalah): Transfer of a debt from one debtor to another; the executor may accept it for the minor if beneficial.
الولاية نظرية (al-wilayah nazariyyah): Guardianship is based on consideration of benefit; the executor must act in the minor's interest.
