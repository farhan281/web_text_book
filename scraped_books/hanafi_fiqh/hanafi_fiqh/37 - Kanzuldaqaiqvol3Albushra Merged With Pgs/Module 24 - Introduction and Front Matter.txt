Definition of Diyat (Blood Money) — Linguistic and Shar‘i Meaning
Defines the term diyat linguistically and technically

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definitions of diya in Hanafi fiqh and explain why the chapter on diyat follows jinayat.

Core Idea
Diya is the monetary substitute for a life, linguistically from 'wada' and technically distinct from arsh.

The opening of Kitab al-Diyat in Kanz al-Daqaiq defines the central term 'diya'. The author provides its linguistic origin as a verbal noun and its technical meaning as the monetary compensation for a life, clarifying that it is a proper name for blood-money, while arsh covers other injuries. He also explains the chapter's placement after jinayat because diya is one of its consequences, but retaliation (qiwas) was presented first due to its emphasis on preserving life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root
Find the verbal noun from which diya is derived. The text states it is derived from 'wada' (he exacted blood-money).
Linguistically, diya is a verbal noun used as a name for the payment.
2
State the technical definition
In sacred law, diya is the payment given to the heirs in exchange for the life of the victim. It is a proper name (علم) for the substitute for the soul (بدل النفس).
The text uses 'عبارة عما يؤدي' — an expression for what is paid.
3
Differentiate diya from arsh
Note that diya is specifically for taking a life, while arsh is for injuries less than homicide. The text says 'وهو الأرش' pointing to arsh as a related but distinct term.
Arsh is compensation for wounds, diya for life.
4
Explain the ordering of chapters
The author mentions diyat after jinayat because diya is a consequence of injury to a human being, but qiwas (retaliation) was presented first because it better fulfills the meaning of giving life (إحياء).
The precedence of qiwas over diya is due to its stronger connection to preserving life.

The Book of Diyat: Diyat is plural of diya. Linguistically it is a verbal noun: 'he exacted the blood-money' (wada) means the killer gave the blood-money and gave the heir the money that is the substitute for the soul. Then that money was called diya, denominated by the verbal noun, as in Al-Maghrib. In sacred law: an expression for what is paid. This term became a proper name for the substitute for souls to the exclusion of others, and it is arsh. The substitute for the soul is called diya because it is usually paid; rarely does its forgiveness occur due to the greatness of the sanctity of the human being. Then mentioning diyat after jinayat (injuries) is apparently appropriate because diya is one of the consequences of injury to a human being, preserving him from retaliation. But since the meaning of giving life (ihya) is more in retaliation, the explanation of qiwas (retaliation) is presented before diya. Completion, Fath, Mulla Miskin.

Commentary
This passage is a definition_ruling. The author begins the Book of Diyat by defining the term. Linguistically, diya is a verbal noun from wada, meaning the payment given to the heirs in place of the slain person. In sacred law (shar‘), it has become a technical term for the monetary compensation for taking a life, distinct from arsh (compensation for injuries). The author explains why diyat is placed after jinayat in the book: it is one of the two consequences of homicide (the other being qiwas). Since retaliation (qiwas) emphasizes the principle of preserving life (ihya), it takes precedence in order. The passage sets the stage for the detailed rules that follow.

Key Terms
دية (diya): Monetary compensation for taking a life, equivalent to the value of the victim's life.
أرش (arsh): Compensation for physical injuries less than homicide, like wounds.

Key Takeaway

The Glosses of Kanz al-Daqa'iq: A Case Study in Hanafi Textual Transmission
Colophon of Kanz al-Daqa'iq glosses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the significance of the glosses on Kanz al-Daqa'iq and identify the key scholars involved in its transmission.

Core Idea
A Hanafi matn is completed not by its author alone, but by generations of commentators.

This lesson delves into the colophon of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, a foundational Hanafi fiqh text. While not a fiqh ruling, this passage reveals how Hanafi scholarship preserves and transmits legal knowledge through commentaries and glosses. Understanding this process is crucial for appreciating the authority and accuracy of the texts we study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main text and its author
Locate the name of the original matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) and its author (Imam Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi). Note the praise given to the text and author.
The author died in 710 AH.
2
Understand the problem addressed
The colophon states that earlier editions lacked sufficient glosses (hawashi). Some had partial glosses but were insufficient. Recognize the need for comprehensive annotations.
Glosses clarify difficult passages and cross-reference rulings.
3
Trace the chain of scholars
Identify the two scholars mentioned: Maulawi Muhammad Ahsan al-Nanautawi (who began the work but died) and Maulawi Muhammad Habib al-Rahman al-Diyubandi (who completed it). Understand the personal and scholarly connection.
The first scholar composed a poetic lament.
4
List the sources used
The completed gloss drew from major Hanafi works: al-Hidayah, al-Durr al-Mukhtar, Radd al-Muhtar, etc., as well as linguistic works. Note the reliance on authoritative references.
This shows the layered nature of Hanafi scholarship.
5
Appreciate the request for forgiveness
The colophon ends with a plea for readers to overlook errors. Recognize this as scholarly humility and a reminder that no work is perfect.
This reflects the adab (etiquette) of scholarship.

نحمدك يا من منه الهداية إلى كنز الدقائق والوقاية عن الخطأ في فهم رمز الحقائق ونصلي ونسلم على سيدنا ومولانا محمد النبي الأمين الأمان، وعلى آله وأصحابه ما لمع القمران.
أما بعد، فإن علم الفقه لا يخفى على العاقل جلالة اعتباره ونبالة أسفاره سيّما السفر المسفر الحقائق، أعني به "كنز الدقائق"، فإنه متن متين وبالضبط قمين وكيف لا ؟ وقد صنفه الإمام الهمام والبحر الطمطام أبو البركات بن محمود النسفي - برّد الله مضجعه وبارك جمعه ولذلك أكب العلماء بشرحه قرى وأمصارا، وشاع المكتوب والمطبوع منه مرارا، لكن بعض نسخه كان معرى عن الحواشي، وبعضه وإن كان محشى بنصف العيني والمستخلص، لكن لم يك مزيل الغواشي؛ لاشتماله على الزوائد واشتغاله عن الفوائد، فتوجه إلى ترتيب حواشيه وإزالة غواشيه الفاضل الشهير في الزمن المولوي محمد أحسن النانوتوي – عامله الله بالأحسن فلم يوفق لإتمامه وتفويض الخيام عن اختتامه، فرثي بهذه الأبيات: يا عين ما لك تذرفين بأدمع وملئت دمعا مثل حوض مترع والقلب أيضا في البكاء مواتئ تنعين فقد وحيد فقد تنعين مدقق متفطن متدرب تنعين فقد إمام أرباب التقى حسن لوفاته عصر متبحر أورع متورّع السجايا السيد المتبرع قد كان ماحي بدعة وضلالة ويحب كل موحد متشرع مات العلوم كثيرة قد كان أحسن في العلوم الأجمع فحباه ربي بالسرور ونضرة وألوف مغفرة بيوم ثم تممّه من كتاب الهبة إلى آخر الكتاب الصاعد إلى مصاعد العرفان المولوي محمد حبيب الرحمان الديوبندي، مجزع فلخص حواشيه من شرح الطائي، والعيني، والمستخلص وملا مسكين وحاشية فتح المعين، وتكملة البحر الرائق، والنهر الفائق والدرّ المختار وحاشيته رد المحتار والهداية والكفاية والبداية والوقاية، والقاموس، والصحاح، والمنتخب، والصراح، وكذلك من غير ذلك، فالمرجو من الخلان أن ينظروا إليه بنظر العفو، فإن الإنسان لا يخلو عن الخطأ والنسيان.
Print Colophon: We praise You, O from whom is guidance to Kanz al-Daqa'iq and protection from error in understanding the symbol of realities, and we send blessings and peace on our master and lord Muhammad, the trustworthy prophet, the trust, and on his family and companions as long as the two moons shine. As for after, verily the knowledge of fiqh is not hidden from the intelligent: its greatness and nobility of its travels, especially the journey that unveils realities, I mean Kanz al-Daqa'iq. Indeed, it is a solid matn and worthy of precision, and why not? The imam, the ocean, Abu al-Barakat ibn Mahmud al-Nasafi - may Allah cool his resting place and bless his compilation - authored it. Therefore, scholars devoted themselves to its commentary in towns and cities, and it became widespread in manuscript and print many times. But some copies were bare of glosses, and some were glossed with half of al-'Ayni and al-Mustakhlas, but that was not sufficient to remove obscurities because of extraneous matter and preoccupation from benefits. So the famous scholar of the time, Maulawi Muhammad Ahsan al-Nanautawi - may Allah deal with him kindly - turned to arranging its glosses and removing its obscurities, but he was not able to complete it and left its conclusion. So he lamented with these verses: 'O eye, why do you shed tears, filled with tears like a brimming tank? The heart also in weeping mourns the loss of a single one, mourns the loss of a scrutinizer, sharp, trained. You mourn the loss of an imam of the people of piety, Hasan, in his era, a deep scholar, more pious, abstemious, the generous master. He was an eradicator of innovation and error, loving every monotheist and follower of Shari'a. The sciences, many, have died; he was excellent in all sciences. So my Lord granted him joy and freshness and thousands of forgiveness on a day.' Then Maulawi Muhammad Habib al-Rahman al-Diyubandi, upset, completed it from the Book of Hibah to the end of the book ascending to the heights of knowledge. He summarized its glosses from Sharh al-Ta'i, al-'Ayni, al-Mustakhlas, Mulla Miskin, Hashiyat Fath al-Mu'in, Takmilah al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, al-Nahr al-Fa'iq, al-Durr al-Mukhtar and its gloss Radd al-Muhtar, al-Hidayah, al-Kifayah, al-Bidayah, al-Wiqayah, al-Qamus, al-Sihah, al-Muntakhab, al-Sirah, and others. So we hope from our friends that they look upon it with a forgiving eye, for man is not free from error and forgetfulness.

Commentary
This passage is a front matter (colophon) detailing the print history of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It begins with praise of Allah and salawat upon the Prophet. It then extols the importance of fiqh and specifically the matn Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Imam Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi. The text explains that previous editions lacked sufficient glosses, so the scholar Maulawi Muhammad Ahsan al-Nanautawi began work on a new gloss but died before completion. He composed a eulogy in Arabic poetry mourning his own inability to finish. The task was then taken up by Maulawi Muhammad Habib al-Rahman al-Diyubandi, who completed the gloss from the Book of Hibah to the end. He compiled annotations from numerous sources, including major Hanafi commentaries and linguistic works. The colophon concludes with a request for the readers' forgiveness for any errors.

Key Terms
كنز الدقائق (Kanz al-Daqa'iq): A renowned Hanafi fiqh matn by Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi, covering rulings concisely.
الحواشي (al-hawashi): Glosses or marginal notes added to a matn or sharh for clarification.
المتن (al-matn): The original concise text, e.g., Kanz al-Daqa'iq.
الشرح (al-sharh): A detailed commentary on a matn, e.g., al-Hidayah.
