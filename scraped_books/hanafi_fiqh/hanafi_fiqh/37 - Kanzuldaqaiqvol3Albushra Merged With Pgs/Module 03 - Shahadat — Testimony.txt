Precedence of Possessor's Evidence in Ownership Disputes
Explains the precedence of possessor's evidence over external evidence in absolute ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on precedence of evidence when a possessor and external claimant both present evidence, and distinguish between absolute ownership and ownership by cause.

Core Idea
In absolute ownership, the possessor's evidence is preferred because the hand itself is proof of ownership.

When two parties claim ownership of the same property and both present evidence, how should a judge decide? The Hanafi school distinguishes between absolute ownership and ownership by a specific cause. This lesson explores the Hanafi position and the basis for preferring the possessor's evidence, as well as the disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi rule that in absolute ownership, the possessor's evidence is preferred over the external claimant's evidence.
The hand (al-yad) is considered proof of absolute ownership.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i does not automatically prefer the possessor's evidence; he treats both evidences as equal or requires further investigation.
The Shafi'i view is not detailed here but is implied.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand that the Shafi'i school may argue that both evidences should be equally considered, as the external claimant's evidence could be stronger.
The text does not provide the Shafi'i dalil in full.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes absolute ownership (where the hand is proof) from ownership by cause (where consensus prefers the possessor's evidence).
The analogy with offspring (al-nataj) illustrates the distinction.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in a Hanafi court, the possessor wins if both bring evidence; in a Shafi'i court, the judge may weigh both or require additional proof.
This difference applies only to absolute ownership, not ownership by cause.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the precedence of evidence when two claimants present evidence, one a possessor and the other an external party. The ruling is that the possessor's evidence is preferred in cases of absolute ownership. However, in specific cases like offspring (نتاج), the hand does not indicate ownership of the offspring, so if the possessor brings evidence that he owns it by cause, his evidence is preferred by consensus. The difference with Shafi'i is confined to absolute ownership, not to cases of ownership by cause.

Dividing Conflicting Claims: Munaaza'ah vs 'Awl
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dividing property between two claimants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on dividing property with conflicting evidence, and explain the methodological basis (munaaza'ah vs 'awl) that leads to the different results.

Core Idea
Uncontested portions go to the uncontested claimant first; only the contested portion is divided.

When two claimants present valid evidence for different shares of a property, how should it be divided? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute over the method—one approach prioritizes uncontested possession, the other applies proportional reduction. Understanding this difference reveals a deeper principle about how contestation and evidence interact in property claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and claims
Note the house is in the hand of a third party. Claimant A proves half, Claimant B proves the whole. Both have valid evidence.
This is not a case of possession by either claimant.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and method (munaaza'ah)
Abu Hanifa divides by munaaza'ah: the claimant of the whole is uncontested in half, so that half is given to him. The contested half is divided equally, yielding 3/4 and 1/4.
The key is that the uncontested portion is isolated first.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and method ('awl and mudarabah)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad apply 'awl: the total claim is 1.5, so the base rises from 2 to 3. The claimant of the whole gets 2/3, the claimant of half gets 1/3.
This treats all shares as competing fractions, similar to inheritance.
4
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In Abu Hanifa's view, the claimant of the whole gets 3/4; in the Sahibayn's view, he gets 2/3. The difference is 1/12 of the property.
This is a tangible outcome that affects the parties' shares.
5
Note the cross-madhhab context
Malik agrees with the Sahibayn in one narration. Al-Shafi'i discards both evidences and lets the possessor swear, effectively keeping the whole.
In practice, a Hanafi judge would follow Abu Hanifa's view as per later tarjih.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the division of a house when two claimants present evidence. The scenario: a house is in the hand of a third party. Claimant A claims half, Claimant B claims the whole. Both have valid evidence (bayyinah). According to Abu Hanifa, the division is by way of munaaza'ah (dispute): the claimant of the whole is uncontested in half of the house, so that half is given to him without dispute. The remaining half is contested between the two, so it is divided equally. Thus the claimant of the whole gets three-quarters (the uncontested half plus half of the contested half), and the claimant of half gets one-quarter (half of the contested half). According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), the division is by 'awl (proportionate reduction) and mudarabah (sharing): the claim is for a whole and a half (1 and 0.5), totaling 1.5. The base of the case is 2, which rises to 3 (by 'awl). The claimant of the whole gets 2/3, and the claimant of half gets 1/3. This is also a view of Malik. Al-Shafi'i is reported to have said that both evidences are invalidated and the possessor (who has de facto possession) swears as to half, and if he swears, the entire house remains with him.

Key Terms
المنازعة (al-munaaza'ah): A method of dividing contested property where uncontested portions are given to their claimants first, and only contested portions are divided.
العول (al-'awl): Proportionate reduction of shares when the total of fixed shares exceeds the base denominator.
المضاربة (al-mudarabah): Here, a method of sharing where each party takes a number of shares proportional to their claim.

Preference for the External Claimant's Evidence in Disputed Property
Explains the preference for the external claimant's evidence in a joint possession dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on preference of evidence when two claimants dispute a property and apply it to scenarios where one claimant is external and the other is a possessor.

Core Idea
The external claimant's evidence beats the possessor's — possession does not trump proof in Hanafi qada.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, when two parties claim the same property, the court must decide whose evidence to accept. The principle stated in this passage establishes a clear hierarchy: the evidence of the claimant who is external (not in possession) is preferred over the evidence of the one in possession. This rule prevents the possessor from using his possession as a shield. The commentary by Ayni and others unpacks this principle through a specific case: two men disputing a house, one claiming half, the other the whole.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
State the rule: the evidence of the external claimant (al-kharij) is preferred over the possessor (dhu al-yad). This is the fundamental maxim of this passage.
The maxim is: بينة الخارج أولى من بينة ذي اليد.
2
Understand the rationale
Recognize that each claimant's claim is directed to what is in his own hand to avoid injustice. If a person claims what he already holds, he is not unjust; but if he claims what is in another's hand, he must prove his right.
The passage says 'لئلا يكون في إمساكه ظالماً' — to avoid being unjust in holding.
3
Apply to the partial claimant
The one claiming half does not claim anything from the other's hand; his hand is rightful for his half. His evidence is only needed for what is in his hand.
He is a possessor regarding his half, so his hand is 'يد محقة' (rightful hand).
4
Apply to the full claimant
The one claiming the whole claims both his own half and the other's half. For the other's half, he is external, so his evidence prevails over the possessor's evidence.
Regarding the other's half, he is 'خارج' (external), so his bayyinah is preferable.
5
Derive the final judgment
The full claimant gets the whole house: half by abandonment (since the partial claimant did not claim it) and half by judgment based on his preferred evidence.
The passage concludes: 'فيسلم له الكل نصفها بالترك لا على وجه القضاء، ونصفها على وجه القضاء'.

Dispute Over Animal Offspring with Dates
Ruling with proof on dated evidence in offspring disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for deciding between two claimants with dated evidence in a dispute over animal offspring, distinguishing cases with and without dates, and considering possession.

Core Idea
When both claimants provide dates, the judge prefers the one whose date matches the animal's age; if unclear, possession decides.

This passage from the Hanafi text addresses a specific evidentiary problem: two parties both claim ownership of an animal's offspring and present evidence with dates. The judge must determine which evidence to accept, considering the animal's known age and who holds possession. The ruling demonstrates the Hanafi school's reliance on practical indicators (zahir al-hal) and the effect of possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two claimants both present evidence for the same offspring, each giving a date of birth or ownership.
Assume both are external to possession unless stated otherwise.
2
Check if the animal's age matches a date
If the offspring's known age matches one claimant's date, that claimant wins.
The judge uses external evidence (e.g., veterinary age estimation) to verify.
3
If age is unclear or matches neither, consider possession
If the age cannot be determined or matches neither date, possession decides: if both are external, the offspring is divided; if one possessor, that possessor wins.
This applies only when both claimants are external or when one is the possessor.
4
Handle contradiction of both dates
If the animal's age contradicts both dates (e.g., animal is 5 but both dates say 3), two views exist: the initial view invalidates both evidences; the preferred view (al-asahh) does not invalidate but uses possession.
The preferred view is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Rulings on Iqrār with Exceptions and Their Variations
Rulings on iqrār with retraction and its legal consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqrār with retraction and apply it to scenarios involving trusts, ownership, and conflicting amounts or descriptions.

Core Idea
A retraction in iqrār does not void the first iqrār; the confessor is liable to compensate the second if possession was acknowledged.

Iqrār (judicial confession) is a powerful legal tool, but what happens when the confessor immediately retracts or qualifies it? The Hanafi school provides detailed rules: the first iqrār stands, but the confessor may owe a guarantee to the second person depending on the nature of the acknowledgment. This lesson unpacks the key scenarios from the source text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the iqrār and the retraction
Read the confessor's statement carefully. Determine the initial iqrār (e.g., 'This thousand is a trust for X') and the subsequent retraction (e.g., 'no, rather a trust for Y').
The retraction can be explicit using words like 'لا بل'.
2
Assess whether the first iqrār acknowledged possession
If the first iqrār involves a trust or deposit, it implies the confessor had possession of the item. If it is a mere ownership statement (e.g., 'it is for X'), no possession is acknowledged.
Possession (yad) is key to the ruling.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule on retraction
If possession was acknowledged, the first iqrār is valid and the property goes to the first person. The retraction does not invalidate it, but the confessor must give an equivalent to the second person because his iqrār created a claim and his retraction harmed the second.
The confessor is liable for the like (mithl) to the second.
4
Distinguish the case without possession
If the first iqrār was a pure ownership statement (e.g., 'it is for X, no rather for Y'), the confessor is not liable to the second because the first iqrār did not involve possession of the item; the retraction is ineffective and the testimony for the second is not accepted.
The ruling differs because there is no guarantee for the second.
5
Handle variations: unspecified property, same muqarr lahu, and derivative products
If the property is unspecified, liability still applies. If the muqarr lahu is the same person and amounts conflict, the greater amount or better quality is binding. If someone acknowledges that a debt or trust is for another, it is an iqrār but the right of receipt remains with the confessor.
Also, iqrār of ownership of an origin includes its derivatives (e.g., milk from a cow).

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of rulings on iqrār (judicial confession) involving exceptions or retractions (istithnā'). Ruling on evidentiary requirement for 'aqilah liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the evidentiary requirement for imposing qasamah on the 'aqilah and distinguish between possession and ownership as proofs.

Core Idea
Possession indicates ownership but is inconclusive; witness testimony is required to hold the 'aqilah liable.

This lesson examines the evidentiary standard for 'aqilah liability in qasamah cases. The text from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies that mere possession of a property where a body is found is insufficient to impose liability; witnesses must testify that the property belongs to the possessor. The commentary analogizes to the pre-emption right (shuf'ah), which likewise requires proof of ownership, not mere possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling

2
Determine the required condition
What is sufficient? Witness testimony establishing that the property belongs to the possessor. What is insufficient? Mere possession (yad).
Possession is only an indicator, not conclusive proof.
3
Understand the reasoning
Why is possession insufficient? Because it is uncertain (muhtamalah) regarding ownership. The commentary analogizes to shuf'ah, where pre-emption requires proven ownership.
Legal effects (liability) require certainty or strong probability; possession does not meet this threshold.
4
Note the scope of the rule
According to Abu Hanifa, this rule applies whether the victim is the owner of the house or someone else. Later discussion may detail exceptions.
The text says 'عند أبي حنيفة على ما يأتي.'
5
Connect to Hanafi principles
The ruling reflects the principle that uncertain evidence cannot impose liability on the 'aqilah. This is consistent with the Hanafi approach to criminal liability requiring clear proof.
This is a procedural safeguard.

Testimony of Inhabitants in Qasamah: Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-madhhab dispute on testimony competency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on the admissibility of testimony from inhabitants of the mahallah in qasamah cases, and state the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Potential litigant or actual? That single shift changes everything about witness competency.

This lesson examines an intra-madhhab dispute on whether inhabitants of a mahallah can testify in qasamah (sworn oath) cases regarding homicide. At stake is the critical distinction between actual and potential litigant status, which determines witness competency. You will explore how Abu Hanifa and his two disciples each apply the same agreed principles to reach different conclusions, and how the analogy of an agent in litigation clarifies their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario and the Two Positions
Identify the two factual situations: testimony against non-inhabitants (outsiders) and testimony against another inhabitant. Then state what each imam rules in each situation.
Abu Hanifa rejects all; Sahibayn accept against outsiders but reject against insiders.
2
Identify the Agreed Principles
Both sides agree on two principles: (1) a person who has become a litigant in a case cannot testify in that case; (2) a person who has not become a litigant can testify. The dispute is how to classify the inhabitants.
The principles are not in dispute; their application to this specific case is.
3
Apply the Principles to the Inhabitants
Abu Hanifa holds that the inhabitants have already become litigants because the wali's claim in qasamah makes them all parties. The Sahibayn hold that they are only potential litigants (urudiyyah) unless actually accused.
The key term is 'urudiyyah' – potentiality of becoming a litigant.
4
Examine the Analogy to the Agent (Wakil)
The text illustrates with the wakil in litigation: if the wakil has appeared in court, his testimony is later rejected; if he never appeared, it is accepted. Abu Hanifa compares the inhabitant to the wakil who appeared (actual litigant); Sahibayn compare to the wakil who never appeared (potential).
This analogy shows the illa (cause) is not just potential but actual engagement in the dispute.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Practical Consequence
The relied-upon view for testimony against outsiders is Sahibayn's (accepted); for testimony against insiders, it is Abu Hanifa's (rejected). The thamarah al-khilaf is clear: in the same case, the admissibility of evidence flips depending on whom the witnesses testify against.
Recognizing which view applies to which scenario is essential for practicing fatwa.

Ruling on testimony of executors regarding another executor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony of executors regarding a co-executor and explain the istihsan reasoning that overrides the qiyas.

Core Idea
Testimony of executors is accepted only if the claimed person asserts—istihsan overrides suspicion of self-interest.

When two executors testify that the deceased appointed a third person as co-executor, strict analogy (qiyas) would reject their testimony because they stand to benefit from sharing responsibility. However, Hanafi law employs istihsan to accept the testimony—but only if the named person himself claims the appointment. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the default rule
Two existing executors testify that the deceased appointed a third person (Zaid) as co-executor. Normally, testimony of someone who derives personal benefit is rejected due to tuhma (suspicion of partiality).
The benefit here is having a co-executor to share burden or protect their administration.
2
Understand the qiyas ruling
By strict analogy (qiyas), the testimony should be rejected because the executors have a personal interest in adding a co-executor.
This is the default ruling if no istihsan applied.

Istihsan prevails: if Zaid (the named person) himself claims that he was appointed, then the executors' testimony is accepted. The reason is that the judge would be obligated to appoint a third executor anyway, so their testimony merely saves the judge the trouble of selection.

The situation is analogous to a case where the deceased left no executor and the judge appoints one initially. Here, the appointment is effectively made by the judge through acceptance of the testimony.
This analogy shows that the testimony does not create a new benefit but facilitates an existing obligation.
5
State the final ruling and its condition
Final Hanafi position: testimony of executors is accepted by istihsan only if the named person claims the appointment. Otherwise, it is rejected and the judge appoints a third executor independently.
The mufta bihi is the istihsan ruling.

Testimony Invalid Due to Self-Interest (Tuhma)
Ruling on testimony with self-interest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when two witnesses' testimony would cancel their own debt and identify the principle of tuhma (suspicion) that invalidates it.

Core Idea
Testimony is invalid if the witness directly benefits by cancelling their own liability.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq presents a straightforward case: two witnesses claim a deceased owes them 1000, but two other witnesses testify that the first two owe the same amount. Since the first pair's testimony would eliminate their own debt, it is rejected due to self-interest (tuhma). Understanding this ruling helps grasp the Hanafi principle that testimony must be free from any personal gain or loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
There is a deceased person (the defendant). Two witnesses (call them Witnesses A and B) claim the deceased owed them 1000. Two other witnesses (C and D) claim that A and B owe them 1000.
The two sets of claims are connected: the second claim, if accepted, would make A and B debtors of C and D.
2
Determine the conflict of interest
If A and B's testimony is accepted, the deceased's estate would pay them 1000. But if C and D's testimony is also accepted, A and B would have to pay C and D 1000. Thus, accepting A and B's testimony would indirectly cancel their own debt, benefiting them.
The benefit is direct and immediate: the estate's payment to A and B would offset their debt to C and D.
3
Apply the principle of tuhma
Hanafi law rejects testimony where the witness stands to gain or avoid loss. This is the principle of tuhma (suspicion of partiality). Here, A and B clearly benefit, so tuhma applies.
Tuhma is a key condition for testimony validity: the witness must be neutral and impartial.
4
State the ruling
The testimony of Witnesses A and B is invalid (batil). Their claim against the deceased is rejected entirely.
The ruling does not address the validity of C and D's testimony separately; it only states the consequence for the first pair.

General Ruling: Testimony Requires Seeing and Hearing
General ruling on hidden witness testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony based solely on hearing an acknowledgment and identify the reason for its invalidity.

Core Idea
Hearing alone is insufficient for testimony because voices can resemble each other, causing uncertainty.

Imagine you hide witnesses to catch an acknowledgment. If they both see and hear the person, their testimony is valid. But what if they only hear his voice without seeing him? The Hanafi school holds that such testimony is invalid due to potential voice similarity. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios

2

Voice similarity introduces probability, not certainty.
3

Knowledge implies certainty, not mere probability.
