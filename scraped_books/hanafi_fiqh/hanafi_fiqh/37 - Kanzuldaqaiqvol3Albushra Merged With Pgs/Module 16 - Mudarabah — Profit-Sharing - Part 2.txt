Mudarib's Expenses on Murabahah Sale Basis
Ruling on what expenses the mudarib can include in the cost price when selling on murabahah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which expenses the mudarib may include in the cost price when selling on murabahah and apply the Hanafi principle to new cases.

Core Idea
Only goods-related expenses that increase value or are customary count in murabahah cost.

In a murabahah sale, honesty about cost is critical. The mudarib must know exactly what counts as part of the capital. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi rule: only expenses directly on the goods are included; personal expenses are not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The mudarib sells goods on murabahah (cost-plus-profit). The ruling: he counts only what he spent on the goods, not on himself.
This ensures the profit is calculated on the true capital.
2
List includable expenses
Examples: carrying charges (hamlan), broker's fee (ujrat al-simsar), fuller's fee (qassar), dyer's fee (sabbagh), and similar customary costs.
These increase the goods' value or are customary among traders.
3
State the governing principle
Anything that causes an increase in capital—actually or constructively—is added. Customary expenses also count.
The principle distinguishes value-adding costs from personal costs.
4
Note the exception: mudarib's own funds
If the mudarib uses his own money to shorten or carry the goods, that does not become part of the cost unless agreed. It is treated as a separate matter.
This prevents mixing personal funds with partnership capital without permission.

Mudarib's Expenses: Travel vs. Residence
Ruling on mudarib's expenses with proof and intra-Hanafi debate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which mudarib expenses are charged to the mudaraba capital and apply the distinction between travel and residence to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Travel expenses fall on capital; residence and incidental needs like medicine fall on the mudarib.

A mudarib is entrusted with capital but not personal expenses. However, travel for trade is necessary—so Hanafi fiqh distinguishes expenses by location and necessity. This lesson explores the rule, its proof, and an internal debate among the Hanafi imams on what counts as 'incidental'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? The mudarib spending money during the mudaraba. What is the ruling? Expenses during travel for the mudaraba are taken from the capital; expenses during residence in the mudarib's original homeland are from his own wealth.
The 'original homeland' (watan asli) determines residence.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) based on the 'illa (effective cause): travel is for the sake of the capital, so its costs are on the capital; residence is for personal reasons, so its costs are personal.
The same logic applies to incidental items like medicine.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The 'illa is that travel directly benefits the capital. Normal expenses (food, lodging) are 'certain' needs of travel. Medicine, however, is 'incidental'—its occurrence is not certain, so it falls on the mudarib's own responsibility.
This reasoning is used by Sahibayn to exclude medicine.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that medicine, alkali, and oil are from capital because they repair the body for work. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that medicine is from the mudarib; alkali and oil are from capital (Muhammad excludes oil).
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) follows Sahibayn.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: if the mudarib gets sick and buys medicine, under the mufta bihi he pays from his own pocket; under Abu Hanifa's view it would be from capital.

Mudarib's Voluntary Spending: No Reimbursement After Capital Exhausted
Ruling when mudarib uses personal funds after capital exhausted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a mudarib using personal funds after capital exhaustion is a volunteer (mutatawwi') with no right to reimbursement, and explain the underlying reasoning of unauthorized loan.

Core Idea
When capital is exhausted, the mudarib's additional spending is volunteerism, not a reimbursable expense.

In mudarabah, the mudarib is entrusted with capital to trade. But what happens when he uses his own money to complete a transaction after the capital is spent? The Hanafi school rules that such spending makes him a volunteer (mutatawwi'), meaning he cannot claim reimbursement from the owner. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this rule and its implications for mudarabah contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the facts: the mudarib has spent all the mudaraba capital on goods, then uses his own money to cut or transport those goods, after the owner told him to 'act on your opinion.'
The owner's phrase 'act on your opinion' does not authorize additional personal spending on the owner's account.
2
Understand the key condition
Note the critical condition: the entire capital is exhausted. This means no mudaraba funds remain to cover the cutting or transport. Had some capital remained, the mudarib could have used it for such expenses.
The ruling hinges on the complete depletion of capital.
3
Apply the ruling and reasoning
The mudarib is deemed a volunteer (mutatawwi') for his personal spending. Legally, his action constitutes an unauthorized loan to the owner, which is invalid (batil). Hence, he cannot claim reimbursement.
The ruling also applies if he buys goods for more than the capital – the excess is his own responsibility.

Mudarib as Co-Owner When Dyeing Cloth: The Principle of Physical Mixing
Ruling with proof by qiyas jaliy on mudarib dyeing cloth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mudarib dyes the mudaraba cloth with his own dye, explain the legal reasoning (illa), and distinguish it from services like cutting.

Core Idea
Adding physical property (dye) creates co-ownership; services do not.

In mudaraba, the mudarib may sometimes add his own property to the partnership asset, such as dyeing cloth with his own dye. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that this makes him a co-owner proportional to the increase in value, contrasting it with services like cutting or carrying, which do not create co-ownership. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the principle of mixing physical property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
State what the mudarib does (dyes the cloth with his own dye) and what the ruling is (he becomes a partner in the increase in value caused by the dye).
The text specifies red dye, but the principle applies to any color except black (historically).
2
Identify the illa (legal cause)
The dye is a physical property (ayn mal qa'im) that persists and mixes with the mudaraba property. This mixing creates co-ownership by necessity.
This is based on qiyas jaliy with mixing similar properties like grain.
3
Contrast with services
Cutting or carrying are services, not physical property. They do not create co-ownership. The mudarib would only be a volunteer (mutatawwi') for such services.
This distinction is crucial: services do not mix property, so no partnership.
4
Note the effect of authorization
If the owner said 'act on your opinion', the mudarib is authorized and does not become a guarantor. Without such authorization, he would be a usurper (ghasib) and liable.
Authorization affects liability, not the co-ownership itself.
5
Consider time-dependent change
The text notes that black dye caused decrease historically, but now it may cause increase. Rulings can change with time (taghayyur al-ahkam bi-taghayyur al-azman).
This shows fiqh responsiveness to custom (urf).

Mudarabah Calculation: Profit Shares and Liability on Lost Asset
Faraid-style calculation of liability in mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the liability of the mudarib and the owner when an asset purchased with realized profit is lost before payment.

Core Idea
Profit realization transforms ownership; subsequent loss is shared according to new ownership proportions.

This lesson examines a classic mudarabah case from Ramz al-Haqa'iq. The mudarib, after earning profit, purchases a slave that is lost before payment. The key is understanding how the profit split creates distinct ownership shares, which then determine each party's liability for the lost asset.

Guided Path
1
Identify Initial Capital and Profit Share
Note the capital given to the mudarib (1000 dirhams) and the agreed profit share (half, i.e., 50% for the mudarib).
The remaining 50% goes to the owner (rabb al-mal).
2
Calculate Profit After Sale
The mudarib buys cloth (bazz) with the 1000 and sells it for 2000. The profit is 2000 - 1000 = 1000 dirhams. Split this profit: mudarib gets 500, owner gets 500.
The original capital remains owner's property.
3
Determine Ownership of the Purchased Slave
The slave is bought for 2000 (the total in hand). The owner's total claim is capital (1000) + his profit share (500) = 1500, which is 3/4 of 2000. The mudarib's claim is only his profit share of 500, i.e., 1/4. Thus, the slave is owned 3/4 by the owner and 1/4 by the mudarib.
Ownership percentage equals the proportion of the purchase price each party contributed.
4
Apply Loss According to Ownership
The 2000 is lost before paying the seller. Each party bears the loss proportionately: mudarib loses 1/4 of 2000 = 500, owner loses 3/4 = 1500.
Liability is joint but not equal—it follows ownership shares.
5
Compute Each Party's Total Payment
The seller must be paid the full price of 2000. The mudarib pays 500, the owner pays 1500. This matches the calculated loss share.
The owner's total payment is 1500, not the original capital alone.

Core Idea
A mudarib who exhausts capital becomes a volunteer; if he mixes his own dye, he becomes a partner in the increase.

In a mudarabah, the mudarib's authority is tied to the capital. What happens if he goes beyond it? This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses two scenarios: when the mudarib is told to act at his discretion but spends all the capital, and when he adds his own dye to the goods. Understand the rationale

Mudarabah Loss: Proportional Liability When Profit Creates Ownership
Liability distribution when mudarib's profit creates co-ownership and asset is lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a mudarib purchases an asset with realized profit and the asset is lost before payment, and apply the proportional ownership basis to calculate the loss share.

Core Idea
Profit realized in mudarabah makes the mudarib co-owner of assets purchased with it, sharing loss proportionally.

In this scenario, a mudarib receives 1000 capital, trades to 2000, buys a slave, then loses the 2000 before paying. The Hanafi school holds that the mudarib’s profit share (500) gives him ownership in the slave, making him liable for that portion of the loss. This lesson walks through the reasoning and practical calculation.

Guided Path
1
Trace the transactions
Start with the original capital: 1000 dirhams. The mudarib buys cloth (bazz), sells it for 2000, netting a profit of 1000. Then he buys a slave for 2000 – but before paying, the 2000 is lost.
The profit of 1000 is realized before the purchase.
2
Identify profit ownership
Since the profit share was half, the mudarib earns 500 of the profit. That 500 makes him owner of that portion of the mudarabah property. When he spends 2000 on the slave, he buys 500 (1/4) for himself and 1500 (3/4) for the mudarabah.
Ownership is proportional to the source of funds.
3
Apply liability principle
When the 2000 price is lost, each owner bears loss proportionally. The mudarib is liable for 1/4 of 2000 = 500. The capital owner is liable for 3/4 = 1500. The mudarib pays his 500 directly; the owner pays 1500.
This is the core ruling: liability follows ownership.
4
Effect on mudarabah status
The mudarib’s quarter of the slave exits the mudarabah because it is now his personal property and liable for its share of the price. The owner’s three-quarters remain in the mudarabah as trust property.
Mudarabah property is a trust (amanah); personal liability contradicts that status.
5
Murabahah restriction
Because the slave is partly owned by the mudarib personally, he cannot sell it on murabahah (cost-plus sale) at a price higher than 2000, as that would include his own share and misrepresent cost.
If sold for 4000, the mudarabah share (3/4) yields 3000; after deducting capital (2000), 500 profit is split, and the mudarib takes his 1000 personal share.

Murabahah Sale Between Mudarib and Capital Owner: The Ka'l-Ma'dum Principle
Ruling on mudarib's murabahah sale of a slave bought from the capital owner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a sale between mudarib and capital owner is considered non-existent (ka'l-ma'dum) for determining the cost basis in a murabahah sale.

Core Idea
A sale between mudarib and capital owner is ignored for murabahah; the cost basis is the original purchase price.

Murabahah is a trust-based sale requiring full disclosure of cost. When a mudarib buys an asset from the capital owner, or vice versa, a potential conflict arises: the disclosed cost might be inflated. The Hanafi school resolves this by treating such a sale as legally non-existent (ka'l-ma'dum) for murabahah purposes, anchoring the disclosed price to the original cost. This lesson explores two symmetrical scenarios and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Identify the parties and the transactions
In the first scenario, the capital owner (rabb al-mal) originally bought a slave for 500. The mudarib later buys that same slave from the owner for 1000. In the second scenario, the mudarib buys a slave from a third party for 500 and then sells it to the owner for 1000. Note the flow of ownership in each case.
The key is to distinguish the original acquisition from the intermediate sale.
2
Step 2: Determine the disclosed price for murabahah
If the mudarib in scenario 1 wants to sell the slave on murabahah, he cannot use his purchase price (1000). Instead, he must use the owner's original cost (500). Similarly, in scenario 2, if the owner wants to sell on murabahah, he must use the mudarib's original purchase price (500), not the price he paid (1000).
The intermediate sale is ignored for trust-based sales.
3
Step 3: Apply the ka'l-ma'dum (considered non-existent) principle
The sale between agent (mudarib) and principal (capital owner) is treated as if it never happened for the purpose of murabahah. This is because murabahah is built on trust (amanah) and avoiding even the suspicion of betrayal (khiyanah). Allowing the intermediate price would create ambiguity about the true profit margin.
This principle overrides the general validity of the sale itself; the sale is valid but its price is disregarded for murabahah.
4
Step 4: Derive the ruling for the opposite case
The same logic applies symmetrically: whether the mudarib buys from the owner or the owner buys from the mudarib, the cost basis for a subsequent murabahah sale is the first acquisition price from an independent party.
The rule is consistent: the agent-principal sale is ka'l-ma'dum.

Loss Distribution and Slave Ownership in Mudarabah
Ruling on loss allocation and division of slave ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the distribution of loss and slave ownership in a mudarabah when the mudarib's share becomes guaranteed, and apply the rule for murabaha pricing.

Core Idea
When a mudarib's share becomes guaranteed, it exits the mudarabah; the remaining capital is a trust, and murabaha must be based on actual cost.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a nuanced scenario in mudarabah where the mudarib incurs a personal loss that attaches to an asset purchased with mudarabah funds. You will learn how losses are allocated between the mudarib and the capital owner, how the mudarib's proportional ownership in the asset is determined, and why that share exits the mudarabah due to the principle of incompatibility between guarantee and trust. Finally, you will see the correct method for pricing a murabaha sale based on the actual purchase price.

Guided Path
1
Trace the loss distribution
Identify the two phases of loss: first, a 2000 loss from the initial capital equally split (1000 each); second, an additional 1000 loss borne solely by the owner. Calculate each party's total loss.
The mudarib's total loss is 500, the owner's total loss is 1500.
2
Determine the mudarib's ownership in the asset
When a slave is purchased for 2000, the mudarib's personal claim of 500 (from his loss) entitles him to 1/4 of the slave. This fraction becomes his personal property.
The mudarib's share is proportional to his personal liability relative to the purchase price.
3
Apply the principle of incompatibility (tanaf)
The mudarib's share exits the mudarabah because it is guaranteed against him, while mudarabah capital is a trust. They cannot coexist. The owner's share remains in the mudarabah.
This is a key Hanafi principle: an item cannot be both mamun (guaranteed) and amanah (trust).
4
Apply the murabaha pricing rule
When selling the slave on murabaha, the mudarib must base the sale price on the actual purchase cost (2000), not on the total capital (2500).
The total capital includes additional funds not used for this particular asset.
5
Calculate profit distribution
If sold for 4000, the mudarabah's share is 3/4 of 4000 = 3000. Subtract the capital 2500, leaving 500 profit to be split per the profit-sharing ratio. The mudarib's personal share (1/4) yields 1000 profit for him alone.
This example illustrates the combined profit allocation.

Murabaha Sale When Mudarib Buys from Owner
Ruling on murabaha sale when mudarib buys from capital owner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that an internal sale between mudarib and capital owner is disregarded for murabaha cost disclosure, and state the underlying rationale of trust and agency.

Core Idea
When the mudarib buys from the owner, the transaction is treated as non-existent for murabaha — cost must be based on the owner's original price.

Murabaha is a sale built on trust: the seller must disclose the true cost. But what happens when the seller is the mudarib who bought the item from the capital owner? This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling that the internal sale is ignored, preserving the integrity of murabaha.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
The owner originally bought a slave for 500 dirhams. Later, the mudarib buys the same slave from the owner for 1000 dirhams. When the mudarib wants to sell it on murabaha, what cost should he disclose?
The internal price is not the real cost.
2
Identify the mudarib's role
The mudarib is the owner's agent (wakil). A sale to oneself is not a genuine transfer; it is like selling to himself.
Agency creates a legal identity between principal and agent in this context.
3
Apply the rule
For murabaha, the disclosed cost must be the owner's original cost (500), not the inflated internal price (1000). The internal sale is considered non-existent (ka'l-ma'dum).
Murabaha is founded on trust; using the inflated price would involve deception.
4
Consider the reverse scenario
If the mudarib buys a slave for 500 from a third party and sells it to the owner for 1000, then the owner sells it on murabaha, the cost must be 500, not 1000.
The same principle applies regardless of direction.

Distribution of Fida' When a Mudarabah Slave Kills Accidentally
Ruling on fida' distribution when a mudarabah slave kills accidentally

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the proportional shares of ransom (fida') when a mudarabah slave kills accidentally, and state the conditions under which the slave must be handed over (daf').

Core Idea
Ransom is proportional to ownership shares—a mudarib who owns one quarter pays one quarter of the ransom.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a detailed scenario in mudarabah: a slave purchased with partnership capital accidentally kills someone. The ruling demonstrates how the ransom obligation is distributed between the owner and the mudarib based on their ownership shares, and explains the key distinction between handing over the slave (daf') and paying ransom (fida'). Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly allocating liabilities in joint ownership situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify scenario and ownership shares
Slave bought for 1000, worth 2000. Profit 1000 split 50-50: owner's capital 1000 + half profit (500) = 1500 = 3/4 share; mudarib's profit share 500 = 1/4 share. So slave owned 3:1.
2
Determine victim's heirs' options
Heirs can demand the slave (daf') or ransom (fida'). Daf' ends mudarabah; fida' keeps slave but requires payment.
3
Calculate ransom distribution
Fida' is a burden of ownership, so divided proportionally: owner pays 3/4, mudarib pays 1/4.
Based on the principle that ransom follows ownership.
4
Explain service schedule after fida'
After paying ransom, the slave serves the owner 3 days and the mudarib 1 day per week, reflecting ownership shares.
The slave remains jointly owned but not as mudarabah property.
5
Identify condition of presence for daf'
Both owner and mudarib must be present for daf' because the mudarib has a right of ownership (like a mortgagee). For fida', presence not required unless one refuses.
Analogy to mortgaged slave killing accidentally.

Repeated Loss of Purchase Money in Mudarabah: Trustee Non-Liability
Ruling on liability for lost purchase money before payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the mudarib is not liable for lost trust money before payment to the seller and state the owner's obligation to provide replacement funds indefinitely.

Core Idea
The mudarib is a trustee: loss before payment is the owner's risk, not the mudarib's liability.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a striking scenario in mudarabah: the mudarib loses the purchase money before paying the seller, and the owner must keep sending replacement funds without end. The ruling hammers home a foundational Hanafi principle: the mudarib holds the capital as a trust (amanah), not a guarantee. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly allocating risk in profit-sharing partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction and the parties
Recognise that the owner (rabb al-mal) provides capital to the mudarib (working partner) to buy a slave. The mudarib buys the slave but the price (thaman) is not yet handed to the seller.
The price is the same 1000 dirhams originally given.
2
Locate the key event: loss of the price before payment
The money is lost before it reaches the seller. The mudarib has not yet paid the seller; the capital is still in his possession (as a trust).
Loss could be theft, destruction, etc., not due to negligence.
3
Apply the amanah principle
Since the mudarib holds the money as an amanah (trust), he is not liable for its loss unless he was negligent. The text explicitly says 'because the money is a trust in his hand.'
This is a core Hanafi rule: trustees do not guarantee trust property unless there is ta'addi (transgression) or tafrit (negligence).
4
Recognise the owner's obligation to replace the lost money
The owner must give another 1000 dirhams to the mudarib so that the mudarib can pay the seller. This is because the original debt to the seller remains; the loss does not extinguish the obligation.
The owner bears the risk until actual payment to the seller is made.
5
Trace the infinite regress
If the replacement fund is also lost before payment, the owner must provide yet another, and so on indefinitely. This illustrates that the mudarib’s non-liability is absolute and the owner alone bears the risk of loss until the seller receives the money.
Such repeated loss is highly improbable but legally possible; the ruling highlights the principle.

Apportioning Ransom for a Slave's Accidental Killing in Mudarabah
Ruling on distributing ransom (fida') for a slave purchased with mudarabah capital.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rule for apportioning ransom (fida') for a slave purchased with mudarabah capital who commits accidental killing, and explain the reasoning based on ownership proportions.

Core Idea
Ransom for a slave's accidental killing is shared between owner and mudarib proportionally to their ownership shares in the slave.

In mudarabah, a slave purchased with partnership capital may commit an accidental killing. The owner and mudarib must decide between surrendering the slave or paying ransom. This lesson explores how the ransom is apportioned and why it follows the ownership ratio, which is derived from the capital and profit shares.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the mudarib buys a slave for 1000 dirhams when its actual value is 2000. This means there is a profit of 1000 embedded in the slave's value.
The capital is 1000, and the slave's market value is 2000, so profit = 1000.
2
Calculate Ownership Shares
The owner (rabb al-mal) owns the capital of 1000 plus half the profit (500) = 1500, which is 3/4 of the slave's value. The mudarib owns half the profit (500) = 1/4 of the slave's value.
Ownership is, therefore, three-fourths for the owner and one-fourth for the mudarib.
3
Understand the Legal Principle
The ransom (fida') is a burden of ownership (mu'unat al-milk). When a jointly owned property incurs a liability, it is borne in proportion to ownership shares.
Thus, the ransom is divided in the same ratio as ownership.
4
Apply the Ratio to Ransom
Since the ownership is 3:1 (owner:mudarib), the ransom is apportioned accordingly: three-fourths on the owner and one-fourth on the mudarib.
The slave then compensates by serving the owner three days and the mudarib one day.
5
Consider the Edge Case
If the slave's value equals the capital (no profit), the mudarib has no ownership, and the entire liability falls on the owner. The mudarib cannot be forced to pay ransom.
The mudarib may still choose to pay ransom to preserve the mudarabah property, if he expects future profit.

Core Idea
A mudarabah slave with joint ownership rights requires both owner and mudarib present for surrender, akin to a mortgaged slave.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a key rule in mudarabah: if a slave purchased with mudarabah capital commits accidental killing (jinayah khatā’), he can only be surrendered (daf‘) to the victim's family when both the capital owner (rabb al-māl) and the mudarib are present. The ruling is derived via qiyas (analogy) to a mortgaged slave. Understanding this joint authority is essential for managing liability in mudarabah contracts.

Presence of both is a shart (condition) for the act of daf‘.
2

The asl is a mortgaged slave (al-marhūn). If a mortgaged slave commits accidental killing, it is not surrendered except in the presence of both the mortgagor (rāhin) and mortgagee (murtahin).

Qadi Khan adds that the mudarib alone has no authority for surrender or ransom.

In practice, both parties must be involved in decisions affecting the mudarabah asset. The joint ownership right (haqq malk) of the mudarib is not just a managerial role but a substantive property right that limits the owner's unilateral action.
This contrasts with other schools that may treat the mudarib as a mere agent.

Dispute on Capital Amount in Mudarabah: Whose Word Prevails?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on evidentiary rule in mudarabah capital disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose word is accepted in a dispute over capital amount in mudarabah, explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, and apply the rule to cases involving both capital and profit disputes.

Core Idea
The mudarib's word on the amount received is accepted, not the owner's.

When a mudarib and the capital owner disagree on how much capital was handed over, whose statement carries weight? The Hanafi school addressed this through an internal debate, starting with a rule that favored the owner before Abu Hanifa retracted to the view that the mudarib's word is accepted. This lesson walks through the reasoning (talil) that led to the final position, and explains how it applies when both parties also dispute profit shares or bring evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The mudarib claims he received a smaller capital (e.g., 1000) and earned profit, while the owner claims he gave a larger capital (e.g., 2000). The disagreement is about the quantity of capital handed over.
The dispute is over the amount received, not the terms of the mudarabah.
2
State the initial and final Hanafi positions
Initially, Abu Hanifa and Zufar held that the owner's word is accepted because he denies the mudarib's partnership claim. Later, Abu Hanifa retracted and agreed with Abu Yusuf and Muhammad that the mudarib's word is accepted.
The final position (mufta bihi) is the mudarib's word on the amount received.
3
Analyze the talil (reasoning) for the retraction
The core of the dispute is the quantity received. The recipient (mudarib) is better placed to know what he actually received, so his statement about the amount is preferred, whether he is a trustee or a guarantor.
This distinguishes from cases where the dispute is about the nature of the transaction (e.g., loan vs. mudarabah) where the owner's word is accepted.
4
Extend the rule to profit disputes and evidence
If the dispute also involves the profit share, the owner's word is accepted for the profit amount. If either party brings evidence, it is accepted; if both bring evidence, the owner's proof prevails for the capital amount, and the mudarib's proof for the profit share.
This shows the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the dispute.
5
Apply the rule to a contemporary scenario
In a modern halal investment partnership, if the investor claims he gave $50,000 but the entrepreneur (mudarib) says he received only $40,000, the entrepreneur's word is accepted. The investor would need to prove his claim with evidence.
The rule upholds the principle that the recipient's word on quantity is trusted unless disproven.

Dispute over Capital Amount: Mudarib's Word Accepted
Resolves a mudarabah capital dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose statement is accepted in a mudarabah dispute over the capital amount and explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's retraction.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the mudarib's word prevails on capital received; the owner's word on profit share.

When the mudarib and owner disagree about the capital given, clear procedural rules are needed. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents the final Hanafi position, including an intra-madhhab shift from Abu Hanifa's initial view that the owner's word (like Zufar's) is taken, to the later consensus that the mudarib's word is accepted regarding the amount received. You will learn the reasoning and the practical consequences for profit disputes and evidence rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The mudarib claims he received 1000 and made 1000 profit; the owner claims he gave 2000 with no profit. The core issue is the amount of capital received.
The dispute focuses on the quantity received, not the existence of the contract.
2
State the initial and final Hanafi positions
Initially Abu Hanifa agreed with Zufar: the owner's statement is taken because the mudarib claims profit and the owner denies it (denier's word). Later, Abu Hanifa retracted and adopted the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the mudarib's statement is taken on the amount received.
This retraction is significant for understanding how Hanafi rulings evolve.
3
Examine the reasoning for the final position
The dispute boils down to the amount received (al-miqdar al-maqbood). The mudarib is the receiver (qabid), and his word on the quantity received is authoritative by default, whether he is an amin or damin. This is analogized to any case where the receiver denies receipt entirely.
The principle 'al-qawl qawl al-qabid' applies.
4
Distinguish the profit component
If the dispute also includes the profit amount, the owner's statement is accepted regarding the profit share. This is because the owner denies the mudarib's claim of profit, and the mudarib is the claimant on that part.
The ruling is not absolute; it splits the issues.
5
Understand the evidence rules
If one party brings evidence, it is accepted. If both bring evidence, the owner's evidence prevails on capital increase, and the mudarib's evidence prevails on profit increase.
This provides a hierarchy when proof is available.

Dispute over Mudarabah vs. Bida'a: Whose Word Prevails?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on contract nature: mudarabah vs. bida'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for whose statement is accepted in a mudarabah vs. bida'a dispute, explain the rationale of 'reversal of the cause', and identify the intra-Hanafi difference with Zufar.

Core Idea
When mudarib claims mudarabah and owner claims bida'a, the owner's word is taken as he denies the additional right.

When a mudarib and capital owner disagree on the nature of their contract, the ruling depends on who is claiming an additional right beyond what the other admits. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies a specific scenario: the mudarib claims it was a mudarabah (profit-sharing), while the owner claims it was a bida'a (simple agency without profit share). The Hanafi position gives precedence to the owner's statement, and the reasoning—called 'in'ikas al-'illa'—explains why the opposite scenario yields the opposite result. We will also examine a disagreement from Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute scenario
Note the two claims: the mudarib says the contract is mudarabah with half profit, and he has already earned profit; the owner says it is bida'a (agency trade). Determine which party is claiming an additional right (profit share) and which is denying it.
The mudarib claims a right to profit; the owner denies that right.
2
State the general Hanafi rule
The owner's statement is accepted when the mudarib claims mudarabah and the owner claims bida'a. The reasoning: the mudarib is claiming an extra benefit (profit share) that the owner denies; thus the owner is the denier (munkir), and his word is taken with an oath.
This follows the principle that the denier's statement is preferred over the claimant's when no evidence is provided.
3
Analyze the reversal scenario (in'ikas al-'illa)
If the roles were reversed—e.g., the mudarib claims the capital was a loan (qard) and the owner claims it was mudarabah or bida'a—the mudarib's statement is taken. The cause ('illa) reverses: in the first case, the mudarib claims an additional right; in the reversed case, the owner claims an additional right (that the mudarib owes him a share or duty).
The 'reversal of the cause' shows consistent logic: the one who denies the additional claim has his statement accepted.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar
On the sub-issue of generality vs. specificity (e.g., mudarib claims he had general authority to trade in anything, owner claims it was restricted to a specific type), the Hanafi position gives the mudarib's word, but Zufar gives it to the owner. Understand why the Hanafi view is that the mudarib's statement is preferred here: he is the one who carried out the work and knows best the scope.
This shows that within the Hanafi school there can be differences on subsidiary rulings even when the main rule is agreed.
5
Apply the evidence rule
If either party produces evidence (bayyinah), it is accepted. If both produce evidence, the owner's evidence is preferred because it confirms his denial of the mudarabah, which is a more comprehensive negation.
The preference is based on which evidence proves more: the owner's evidence negates the mudarib's claim of partnership.

Burden of Proof: Mudarabah vs Bidha'ah
First dispute scenario: mudarib claims mudarabah, owner claims bidha'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule 'al-qawl li-man yunkir' to resolve disputes between mudarib and rabb al-mal regarding the nature of the contract (mudarabah vs bidha'ah).

Core Idea
When a mudarib claims mudarabah and the owner claims bidha'ah, the owner's word is taken.

In mudarabah disputes, the burden of proof falls on the party making an extra claim. When both agree the mudarib took money with permission, but the mudarib claims he is entitled to profit share, the owner's denial is given weight. This lesson examines the first scenario from Kanz al-Daqaiq: the mudarib claims a mudarabah with half profit, while the owner insists it was a consignment (bidha'ah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
The mudarib has 1000 dirhams and claims the contract was mudarabah with half profit, and that he made 1000 profit. The owner claims it was bidha'ah (commodity consignment), meaning no profit share for the mudarib.
Both agree the mudarib took the money with the owner's permission.
2
Apply the principle 'al-qawl li-man yunkir'
The mudarib is claiming an extra obligation (profit share) which the owner denies. Since the denier's statement is given weight, the owner's word is believed.
The mudarib's claim imposes an extra condition on the owner, so the owner's denial is sufficient.
3
Understand the legal consequence
If the owner's word is taken, the contract is treated as bidha'ah: the mudarib does not share profit and may only receive a fee if agreed. The mudarib must return any profit earned to the owner.
If evidence is produced later, it can overturn the presumption.

If the mudarib said: 'You lent it to me', and the owner said it is a deposit or bidha'ah or mudarabah, then the owner's statement is believed, and the mudarib's evidence (beyond his own testimony) is his evidence because the mudarib claims ownership (of the profit) while the owner denies it. If it were the opposite, the mudarib's statement would be believed. If you say: What is the difference between the first case and this case, where in the first the owner's statement is believed and in its opposite the mudarib's statement is believed? I say: The difference is the reversal of the cause because both have agreed that the taking was with permission, and the owner claims (an extra condition) while he (the mudarib) denies it, as in al-Idah. Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq with its commentary by Mulla Miskin deals with disputes between the mudarib (investment manager) and rabb al-mal (capital owner) regarding the nature of their contract. The base matn states: When a mudarib has one thousand dirhams and claims it was a mudarabah with half profit sharing, and claims profit of one thousand, but the owner claims it was a bidha'ah (commodity consignment, where the mudarib does not share profit), then the owner's word is taken. The commentary explains that this is because the mudarib is claiming an extra obligation (compensation for work or partnership) which the owner denies, so the principle 'al-qawl li-man yunkir' (the word of the denier) applies. The passage then covers various scenarios: (1) Dispute over the amount of capital or share of profit: the owner's word is taken. (2) If the mudarib claims generality (any trade) and the owner claims a specific trade, or the owner claims mudarabah in a specific type and the mudarib denies it, the mudarib's word is taken because the owner is claiming a restriction on the mudarib's authority. (3) Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) differs in the last scenario, saying the owner's word is taken. However, the relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa: the mudarib's word is taken. (4) If the mudarib claims the money was a loan and the owner says deposit, consignment, or mudarabah, the owner's word is taken, but the mudarib's evidence (beyond his own testimony) is his evidence because he claims ownership (of the profit). If the roles are reversed, the mudarib's word is taken. The commentator explains the distinction: in the first case, both agree the mudarib took with permission, and the owner is the one claiming an extra condition (profit share), so he must prove it. In the later case, the mudarib is claiming the owner transferred ownership (loan), so the burden is on him. Finally, if both produce evidence, the owner's evidence is preferred because it proves more (i.e., it affirms the mudarabah with conditions).

Key Terms
مضاربة (muḍārabah): A partnership where one party provides capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides labor (mudarib), with profit shared according to a pre-agreed ratio; loss borne by the capital owner.
بضاعة (biḍāʿah): Consignment: the owner sends goods for sale, and the seller acts as an agent without sharing profit; any profit belongs entirely to the owner, and the agent may receive a fixed fee or commission if agreed.
قول (qawl): Statement or claim; in legal disputes, 'al-qawl li...' indicates whose claim is given weight in the absence of evidence.

Disputes Over Trade Restrictions: Mudarib's Word vs Owner's Specification
Intra-Hanafi dispute on burden of proof when owner alleges trade restriction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position (mudarib's word is taken) when the mudarib claims general trading authority and the owner claims a specific trade, and explain Zufar's opposing view.

Core Idea
When the owner claims a restriction on the mudarib's trade, the mudarib's denial is believed.

The mudarib is entrusted with the capital and knows what he is authorized to do. If the owner later claims he restricted the trade to a certain type, the mudarib's word that no restriction was placed is given weight. This lesson covers the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Zufar on this issue, as presented in Kanz al-Daqaiq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Two subcases: (1) The mudarib claims he was given general authority to trade in anything, and the owner claims he restricted it to a specific trade. (2) The owner claims mudarabah was for a specific type of trade, and the mudarib says no such specification was made. In both, the owner is claiming a restriction on the mudarib's authority.
The owner is the one asserting an extra condition (restriction).
2
Apply the principle 'al-qawl li-man yunkir'
Since the mudarib denies the restriction, his statement is taken. The mudarib is the denier.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa. Zufar disagrees.
3
Understand Zufar's opposing view
Zufar says the owner's word is taken because the owner is more knowledgeable about the terms he set. The Hanafi response: the mudarib is the one who knows what he was authorized to do; the owner's claim adds a condition and he must prove it.
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's.
4
Know the rule when both produce evidence
If either produces evidence, their evidence is accepted. If both produce evidence, the owner's evidence is preferred because it proves more (it confirms a specific restriction).
This applies to all disputes, not just this scenario.
