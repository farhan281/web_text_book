Oath for the Thief and Effect of Nukul on Liability and Hadd
Ruling with proof via ijma and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on administering an oath to the thief and explain how nukul affects property liability versus hadd amputation.

Core Idea
Nukul establishes property liability but never hadd amputation — doubt prevents the strictest penalties.

This passage addresses a unique scenario in Hanafi criminal procedure: when a theft victim seeks to recover their property rather than enforce the hadd penalty. The law allows the thief to be made to swear an oath; refusal results in civil liability but never amputation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how Hanafi fiqh balances strict hudud with evidentiary doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules that the thief is made to swear an oath (yastahlif al-sariq). Determine the legal category (wajib) and the condition that restricts this to theft only.
Other hudud (e.g., zina, qadhf) are excluded by consensus.
2
Locate the condition for the oath
The oath is administered only if the plaintiff's intent is to recover the money (mal), not to enforce the hadd. Note how the judge's procedure changes based on intent.
Imam Muhammad adds a procedural rule: the judge asks the plaintiff's aim.
3
Analyze the consequence of nukul
If the defendant refuses the oath (nukul), he becomes liable for the stolen property (daman). But amputation (qat') is not established. Understand why: daman can be based on doubt (shubha), while hadd requires clear proof.
The text explicitly states: 'he is not amputated'.
4
Trace the qiyas proof
The passage draws an analogy to the testimony of one man and two women in theft. Their testimony establishes liability but not amputation because it contains shubha. Nukul is analogous in containing doubt.
This is a qiyas khafi (subtle analogy) based on the shared illa of shubha.
5
Note the ijma on other hudud
The text asserts that by consensus (ijma), oaths are not administered in other hudud. This reinforces that the rule is specific to theft and only for property claims.
This is a dalil of ijma, which strengthens the ruling.

Sulh Invalid for Hadd Punishments
Explanation that sulh is invalid for hadd penalties because they are rights of Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that sulh is batil for hadd claims and explain the legal reasoning based on haqq Allah.

Core Idea
A hadd punishment is a right of Allah — no individual can accept compensation to drop the claim.

We already know that sulh (amicable settlement) is generally permissible to resolve disputes. But this rule has an important exception: sulh cannot be used to drop a hadd (fixed) punishment claim. Why? Because hadd punishments are the exclusive right of Allah (haqq Allah), not the personal right of the accuser. The accuser cannot trade away what does not belong to him. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sulh over a claim of hadd punishment (e.g., zina, drinking, false accusation). The ruling: the sulh is batil (invalid).
If the accuser accepts payment to drop the case, the sulh is void and he must return what he took.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is not a direct text but a qiyas (analogy). The author compares hadd claims to a public road: just as a private individual cannot accept payment to allow an obstruction on a public road (because the right belongs to the whole community), the accuser cannot accept payment to waive a hadd because the right belongs to Allah.
Dalil type: Qiyas. Asl: public road. Far': hadd claim. Illa: both involve a right that is not exclusively private.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illa) is that the right involved is haqq Allah (or haqq al-jama'ah for the road). Since the right does not belong to the individual, he cannot alienate it for a benefit.
The text states: 'لأن الحدود حق الله تعالى لا حق المرافع'
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The author notes an exception: the imam (ruler) can settle a hadd case for a payment because he has general authority (wilayah 'ammah). This does not contradict the principle; it is a specific dispensation.
Also distinguished: on a private road (tariq ghayr nafidh), residents can settle among themselves because the right is shared, not public.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
This is the standard Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah) and is also agreed upon by other schools. The mufta bihi is that sulh for hadd is batil.
Source: Ramz al-Haqaiq.

Sulh Not Valid in Hudud; Exception for Imam
Clarifying prohibition of sulh in hudud and imam's authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in hudud, explain the qiyas using the public road analogy, and identify the imam's exceptional authority to settle hudud.

Core Idea
Sulh is invalid in hudud because they are rights of Allah; only the imam may settle them.

This passage establishes a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: settlements (sulh) cannot be used to drop hudud punishments because these punishments are rights of Allah, not of any individual plaintiff. The author uses a powerful analogy with a public road to illustrate why private settlement is impermissible, then carves out an exception for the imam, who holds general authority over public interests. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between private claims (where sulh is valid) and public rights (where it is not).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the opening phrase: 'Sulh from a claim of property, as opposed to hudud'. Note that sulh is valid for property claims but invalid for hudud. What is the ruling for hudud? It is impermissible (haram) and the resulting contract is void.
The ruling is clear: sulh in hudud is batil (void).
2
Understand the Ratio Legis (Illah)
The text states: 'because hudud are the rights of Allah Almighty, not the right of the plaintiff.' Identify the illah: the right belongs to Allah, not the individual. Therefore, no individual can accept compensation to drop the case, as that would be taking a right that does not belong to him.
This is the core reason for invalidity.
3
Examine the Qiyas (Analogy)
The author draws an analogy with a public road: if a man has a structure on a public road, another man cannot accept payment to allow it to stay, because the right to the road belongs to the community. Similarly, hudud are a right of the entire Muslim community (as a right of Allah). Work through the analogy: asl (public road), far' (hudud), illah (right belongs to the public/Allah), hukm (sulh invalid).
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy).
4
Identify the Exception
The text makes an exception: the imam (ruler) can settle hudud for a consideration. Why? Because the imam has general authority (wilayah 'ammah) and can act for the community's interests, just as he can sell public property. Note the contrast: a private individual cannot settle, but the imam can.
The exception is based on the imam's authority.
5
Distinguish from Property Claims
The passage begins by saying sulh is valid in property claims. Understand why: property claims are rights of individuals, so the plaintiff can waive or settle them. Hudud are not. Also note the distinction between a public road (where only the imam can settle) and a private road (where residents can settle).
This reinforces the principle: sulh is valid where the right is privately owned.

Coercion to Consume Forbidden Things: Conditions for Permissibility
Ruling on permissibility of eating haram under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule on permissibility of consuming haram under coercion and distinguish between mild and severe coercion.

Core Idea
Severe coercion (ikrah mulji') permits the forbidden; mild coercion does not, but creates a shubhah that averts hadd.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on when a person may consume normally forbidden substances like pork or wine under duress. The key distinction is between mild coercion (beating, imprisonment) and severe coercion (threat to life or limb). The passage also clarifies the role of darurah and shubhah in averting punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of coercion
Read the passage and note the two categories: coercion that does not involve fear of death/limb (e.g., beating, imprisonment) and coercion that does (threat of killing or maiming). The ruling differs based on this.
2
Determine the ruling for each type
For mild coercion, the forbidden remains haram; for severe coercion, it becomes permissible (mubah). The passage states this clearly.
Note that the permissibility is conditional upon genuine fear of destruction.
3
Analyze the underlying principle
The prohibition from the Quran is lifted only in a state of darurah (necessity). The passage compares it to famine (makhmasah). The threshold is ikrah mulji'.
Some scholars attempted to quantify severity (e.g., forty lashes), but the correct view is subjective.
4
Examine the effect on hadd punishment
Even when mild coercion does not permit the act, it creates a shubhah (semblance of legality) that averts the hadd penalty for drinking wine. This is based on the principle that hudud are dropped by doubts.
5
Evaluate the dispute on sinfulness of enduring
The Hanafi majority holds that the coerced person who endures the threat (i.e., does not consume the haram) is sinful because he is neglecting a permissible act that would save his life. However, Abu Yusuf (one riwaya), al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad hold he is not sinful. Note the exception for ignorance of the ruling.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.

Commentary

If the person’s dominant presumption is that the threat will lead to death or loss of limb, it is permissible. Finally, it classifies types of coercion into sins: some are permissible (like uttering disbelief), some are still forbidden (like murder), and some are permissible but refraining is sinful (like eating haram under severe coercion). Ignorance of the ruling serves as an excuse.

Qisas for Parents and Grandparents: Hanafi vs Maliki
Explaining that mother/grandfather are like father in qisas ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school exempts all ascendants from qisas and evaluate the Maliki counter-argument.

Core Idea
No qisas for a parent who kills their child — the bond of lineage creates a legal impediment (shubha) that blocks retaliation.

This passage addresses a critical issue in Islamic criminal law: whether qisas (retaliation) applies when a parent kills their child. The Hanafi school holds that no ascendant — father, mother, grandfather, grandmother — can be executed for killing their descendant, based on a general hadith. Imam Malik, however, distinguishes between disciplinary killing and deliberate slaughter. Understanding this dispute reveals how different schools weigh the intention behind the act and the role of shubha.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: no qisas applies to any ascendant (father, mother, grandfather, grandmother) in all forms of killing their descendant. The source explicitly says they are like the father.
The ruling extends to all ascendants, not just the father.
2
Identify the Maliki objection
Imam Malik distinguishes between a killing that appears disciplinary (e.g., striking with a sword) — where no qisas applies — and a deliberate slaughter (e.g., cutting the throat) — where qisas is required.
The Maliki view introduces an intention-based exception.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is the general application (itlaq) of a hadith, likely 'La yaqtalu walidun bi waladihi' (A parent is not killed for a child). The negation is absolute, covering all cases.
The text refers to 'ma rawayna' (what we narrated), indicating a transmitted hadith.
4
Determine the practical difference
Under Hanafi law, a parent is never executed for killing their child. Under Maliki law, execution is possible if the killing is proven to be deliberate slaughter, not discipline.
This thamarah al-khilaf has significant implications for criminal sentencing.

No Qisas for Killing One's Own Slave or Partial Slave
Explains that killing one's own slave, mudabbar, mukatab, child's slave, or jointly owned slave does not incur qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of slaves for which qisas is not applied and explain the legal reasoning based on ownership and indivisibility.

Core Idea
Qisas drops entirely when the killer owns any part of the victim.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq and its commentary from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarify a key ruling: a person is not executed for killing his own slave, even if the ownership is partial or indirect. The reasoning draws on the principle that one cannot owe qisas to oneself and that qisas, being indivisible, cannot apply to a shared victim. This lesson unpacks the rule and its logical foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories
List the types of slaves mentioned: full slave, mudabbar, mukatab, child's slave, and partially owned slave. Note that the ruling applies to any situation where the killer has an ownership interest.
Mudabbar and mukatab are still slaves during the owner's life, so ownership subsists.
2
Understand the first reason: self-ownership
The commentator argues that if qisas were obligatory, it would be owed to the killer himself (as owner). Since one cannot impose punishment on oneself, qisas cannot be established.
This is analogous to the rule that a father is not executed for killing his child.
3
Understand the second reason: indivisibility
Qisas is a single punishment that cannot be split. If the killer owns part of the slave, qisas would apply only to the part he does not own, but since division is impossible, the entire qisas drops.
This applies even if the killer owns a very small share.
4
Note the hadith evidence
The passage mentions 'what we have narrated' (riwaya), indicating a textual proof (hadith) supports the ruling, though it is not quoted here.
The hadith is ahad but accepted in the school.

What the 'Aqila Does Not Bear and the Exception of Confirmation
Listing the types of injuries the 'aqila does not bear and the exception when they confirm confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of liability excluded from the 'aqila and explain the exception where their confirmation makes them liable.

Core Idea
The 'aqila only bears accidental diya for free persons; intentional, slave, compromise, and confession are on the killer alone.

The 'aqila is a support group that shares the burden of diya for accidental homicide. However, not every case falls on them. This lesson clarifies the boundaries of their liability, citing a prophetic hadith, and explains the one exception where their confirmation of the killer's confession makes them liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Excluded Categories
From the hadith and text, list the four types of homicide/injury for which the 'aqila is not liable: intentional (amad), slave injury (janiyat al-'abd), compromise (sulh), and confession (i'tiraf).
Each category has its own rationale.
2
Understand the Basis for Each Exclusion
For intentional: the killer's act is not excusable. For slave: no tribal support. For sulh and i'tiraf: no authority of 'aqila over the individual's actions.
The hadith explicitly negates liability.
3
Note the Exception for Confession
If the 'aqila confirm the killer's confession (by expressing belief or agreement), they become liable. Their confirmation is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrar) from them.
Alternatively, if evidence (bayyinah) is established, the 'aqila is liable without confirmation.
4
Distinguish Payment Terms
Diya by confession is deferred (mujjal, paid over three years), while diya by compromise is immediate unless deferred by stipulation.
This affects how the 'aqila must pay if they confirm.
