Spousal Disputes Over Household Goods: The Hanafi Rule and Its Contested Variations
Rulings on disputes between spouses over household items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi default rule for household goods disputes, identify the intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab disagreements, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each position.

Core Idea
Possession (yad) and apparent use (zahir) determine ownership; the husband's yad covers the wife's.

When spouses disagree over who owns household items, judges must decide based on apparent indicators. Hanafi law gives priority to possession (yad) but also considers custom ('urf) for items exclusive to one spouse. This lesson unpacks the rulings for when both spouses are alive, after death, and when one is a slave, along with the famous seven opinions (mubayyinah) that arise in such disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling for living spouses
The text states that if both spouses are alive and dispute over an item suitable for both (e.g., a carpet), the husband's claim is accepted with his oath. Items exclusive to the wife (e.g., makeup) go to her due to her apparent use. Understand the reasoning: the husband's possession (yad) covers his wife's hand; she lacks independent possession for common items.
This is the default Hanafi rule from the matn.
2
Analyze the post-death scenario
If one spouse dies and the heirs dispute with the living over items suitable for both, the living spouse gets them (per Abu Hanifa) because the deceased has no possession. This is the first of seven opinions listed. Note the intra-Hanafi variations: Abu Yusuf gives the wife's trousseau-like items to her heirs; Muhammad limits the living spouse's rights to his life; Zufar and Shafi'i/Malik divide equally.
The seven opinions are detailed; focus on the Hanafi positions.
3
Examine the slave spouse special case
When one spouse is a slave (including mukatab or permitted to trade), Abu Hanifa gives all household goods to the free spouse because the slave's possession is weak. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) treat a permitted slave as free in litigation, so they apply the same rules as free spouses. The commentary clarifies a textual variant: 'for the living' should be 'for the free' in some copies.
This shows how the underlying principle (yad) is affected by slave status.
4
Trace the reasoning: yad, zahir, and custom
The Hanafi ruling relies on two apparent indicators: possession (yad) and apparent use (zahir). For woman-specific items, custom gives her a stronger zahir than the husband's yad. For mixed items, his yad prevails. After death, the living's unopposed yad wins. Understand how these principles interact.
This step connects the rulings to usul al-fiqh concepts.
5
Evaluate the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Compare Abu Hanifa's position (living gets all mixed items) with Shafi'i/Zufar's (equal division). The concrete difference: in a dispute after the wife's death, her heirs get half of the mixed goods under Shafi'i but nothing under Abu Hanifa. This illustrates why the ruling matters in practice.
Focus on the real-world outcome, not just theoretical differences.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on disputes between spouses over household goods, with extensive commentary. The base ruling: If spouses disagree about an item suitable for both, the husband's claim is accepted with an oath because she is under his authority; items exclusively for the wife go to her due to her apparent use. If one dies, the living spouse gets items suitable for both, as the deceased has no possession. The passage then lists seven different opinions (hence 'Septenary') including positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar, al-Shafi'i, Malik, al-Hasan al-Basri, and Ibn Abi Layla. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is not explicitly given but Abu Hanifa's view is primary. Later, if one spouse is a slave, Abu Hanifa gives all to the free spouse, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad treat permitted slaves like free. The commentary resolves a textual variant in some copies: 'for the living' should be 'for the free' as per al-Sarakhsi.

Key Terms
يد (yad): Possession or control over an item; the hand of possession.
ظاهر (zahir): Apparent indicator or evidence that suggests ownership, such as possession or use.
تحالف (tahaluf): Mutual oath between disputing parties when evidence is lacking.
متاع البيت (mata' al-bayt): Household goods, chattels, and furnishings.
جهاز (jahaz): Trousseau; items brought by the wife to the marriage.

Sick Person's Acknowledgment to a Divorced Wife
Detailed ruling on sick person's iqrar to divorced wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sick person's iqrar to a divorced wife and identify the conditions that determine its validity.

Core Idea
A sick person's iqrar to a divorced wife is limited to the lesser of inheritance or debt due to suspicion of collusion.

When a sick person makes an acknowledgment (iqrar) to a woman he has divorced three times (or bain divorce) during his illness, Hanafi fiqh applies a nuanced ruling. The presence of suspicion (tuhma) that the divorce was orchestrated to benefit her at the expense of other heirs restricts the validity of the iqrar. This lesson unpacks the conditions—such as whether the divorce was at her request and whether she is still in her waiting period—that determine how much she may receive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
A sick person (marid) makes an iqrar of a debt to a woman he divorced three times (bain divorce) during his illness.
The divorce must be irrevocable (bain).
2
State the general ruling
She receives the lesser of the debt amount and her inheritance share.
This is the default rule when conditions apply.
3
Determine the conditions
Check: (a) Is she in her iddah? (b) Was the divorce at her request? If yes to both, suspicion exists; if iddah passed, no suspicion; if not at her request, iqrar invalid because she is an heir.
Also consider if she is a dhimmi (non-Muslim) who does not inherit.
4
Understand the rationale
The suspicion (tuhma) is that the marriage was ended to allow a larger iqrar. Limiting to lesser removes the incentive for fraud.
Hanafi law blocks means to fraud (sadd al-dhara'i').
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i holds iqrar entirely invalid; Hanafis allow it but restrict it.
The practical difference: in Hanafi, the wife gets something; in Shafi'i, nothing.

Commentary
This passage deals with the ruling of a sick person (marid) who acknowledges a debt to a woman he has divorced three times (or bain divorce) during his illness. The text states that the woman receives only the lesser of the inheritance she would get and the debt acknowledged. The reason is the presence of suspicion (tuhma) that the sick person may have divorced her to increase her share beyond her inheritance, thus defrauding other heirs. If the divorce occurred during the waiting period and at her request, the suspicion is strong, hence the limitation. If the waiting period has passed, the suspicion is gone, and the acknowledgment is valid fully. If he divorced her without her request, she is an heir, and acknowledgment to an heir is invalid because the sick person is 'fleeing' (farr) from inheritance. If she is a non-Muslim dhimmi who does not inherit, his acknowledgment is valid from the entire estate, and his bequest from one-third. The view of Imam al-Shafi'i is mentioned that the acknowledgment is not binding at all in this case, as he considers the door of acknowledgment closed for heirs. The Hanafi position relies on the principle of blocking suspicion (sadd al-dhara'i') while allowing iqrar where there is no suspicion.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or confession of a right or obligation, considered a conclusive proof against oneself.
تهمة (tuhma): Suspicion of fraud or collusion, which limits the validity of iqrar in certain cases.
بائن (ba'in): Irrevocable divorce, after which the husband cannot take back the wife without a new marriage.
عدة (iddah): Waiting period after divorce, during which the woman cannot remarry.

The Effect of Coercion on Manumission and Divorce
States that coercion does not invalidate manumission or divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of manumission and divorce under coercion and explain the rationale that distinguishes these acts from bilateral contracts.

Core Idea
Coercion does not invalidate manumission or divorce because consent is not required for these unilateral acts.

A common misconception is that coercion invalidates any legal act. However, Hanafi law draws a clear line: acts like sale and lease require the consent (riḍā) of the party, while manumission and divorce do not. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this distinction and its practical implications, including an analogy to acts done in jest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling
State clearly: If a person is coerced into manumitting a slave or divorcing his wife, the act is valid and takes effect (waqa‘a).
This is the position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand the rationale
Coercion does not remove legal capacity (ahliyyah). The invalidity of some acts under coercion is due to the absence of consent, which is a condition for those acts. But for manumission and divorce, consent is not a condition.
3
Distinguish from bilateral contracts
Contrast with sale, lease, and iqrar (acknowledgment), which require consent. Under coercion, those acts are invalid because consent is absent.
This distinction is central to the Hanafi position.
4
Apply the analogy to jest (hazl)
Manumission and divorce are valid even when done in jest, proving that consent is not required. The same logic applies under coercion.
This is a qiyas argument: the illa is the absence of a consent requirement.
5
Extend to marriage and dower
If a man is coerced into marriage, the marriage is valid. However, if the specified dower exceeds the proper amount (mahr al-mithl), the excess is void because consent to the excess is absent.
The text is incomplete on coerced women, but typically her consent is required.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a man claims his master freed him and is then killed by his guardian, and explain why the claimant gets nothing.

Core Idea
A claim of freedom forfeits slave-related compensation because the claimant's own statement is against his interest.

The first case in this passage addresses a man who asserts that his master emancipated him. Subsequently, his guardian (for example, the person responsible for him) kills him by mistake. The ruling is that the claimant receives nothing—neither the slave nor any monetary compensation—from the master, the clan, or anyone else. Core Idea
A husband cannot revoke a delegation of divorce because it is an oath; revoking conditional agency requires a double removal.

This passage addresses two distinct yet related issues in Hanafi fiqh: the revocation of a husband's delegation of divorce to his wife, and the technical procedure for revoking a conditional agency. Understanding these rulings clarifies the nature of legal acts that are akin to oaths and the mechanics of conditional agency revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and identify the two separate rulings: one about the husband's inability to revoke delegation of divorce, and another about the proper method to revoke conditional agency.

2
Analyze the reasoning for the first ruling
The passage explains that delegation of divorce is like an oath (yamin) because it suspends divorce on the wife's action. Oaths cannot be retracted, so the husband cannot revoke. Also, the wife is acting for herself, not as a true agent.

If a husband forces his wife to take khul' (divorce in exchange for her dower), what is the ruling?
A) Both divorce and compensation are void
B) Divorce is valid, but husband does not keep the dower
C) Divorce is valid only if wife agrees afterward
D) Divorce is invalid because of coercion

Key Takeaway
In forced khul', the divorce takes effect, but the wife is not liable for the compensation because consent is a condition for property transactions.
