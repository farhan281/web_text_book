Sulh Over Claims of Slavery: Manumission without Wala'
Ruling on sulh over a claim of slavery with manumission effect and limited wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh over a claim of slavery and explain why wala' does not automatically attach.

Core Idea
Sulh over a claim of slavery frees the person but does not grant wala' unless the freed person acknowledges the manumission.

When two parties dispute over whether one is a slave, they may settle through sulh. The Hanafi school validates this sulh, treating it as manumission for consideration. But a critical nuance arises: the claimant does not gain wala' (inheritance loyalty) because the respondent denies ever being a slave. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and the limited role of later evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
One party (claimant) alleges that the other (respondent) is his slave; the respondent denies and claims to be free-born.
The dispute is over status, not property.
2
Understand the dual nature of sulh
From the claimant's perspective, the sulh acts as manumission ('itq) for a consideration (the property received). From the respondent's perspective, it simply ends the litigation.
This dual characterization validates the sulh despite the respondent's denial.
3
Determine the absence of wala'
Wala' (inheritance loyalty) normally follows manumission, but here the respondent denies the manumission itself, claiming free birth. Therefore, no wala' attaches.
The key is the respondent's denial; acknowledgment would trigger wala'.
4
Evaluate the effect of later evidence
If the claimant later produces evidence proving the respondent was indeed a slave, that evidence is accepted only for establishing wala'—it does not revert the respondent to slavery.
The sulh's manumission is irreversible; evidence only adds wala'.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute: one person (claimant) alleges that another (respondent) is his slave; the respondent denies and claims to be free-born. If they settle (sulh) with the claimant taking some property from the respondent, this sulh is valid. The legal effect is that, from the claimant's side, the sulh functions as a manumission ('itq) for a consideration (the property received). From the respondent's side, it merely ends the dispute. Because the respondent maintains that he was always free, he does not acknowledge the manumission. Consequently, the claimant does not gain the right of wala' (inheritance loyalty) over the freed person. However, if after the sulh the claimant can produce evidence (bayyinah) that the respondent was indeed his slave, that evidence is accepted only for the purpose of establishing wala'—the respondent does not become a slave again, because he has already been declared free by the sulh (which is binding as a manumission).

Key Terms
الرق (al-riqq): Slavery; the status of being a slave.
الولاء (al-wala'): Inheritance loyalty: the right of the emancipator to inherit from the freed slave (in the absence of blood heirs).
