Ruling on Coercion to Consume Forbidden Substances
Ruling on permissibility of forbidden food/drink under coercion and measure of overwhelming coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between overwhelming (mulji') and non-overwhelming coercion and apply the Hanafi rule that only overwhelming coercion permits otherwise forbidden substances, while lesser coercion does not remove the prohibition but may still avert the hadd penalty.

Core Idea
Only overwhelming coercion threatening life or limb permits forbidden acts; lesser coercion leaves prohibition intact.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses the critical fiqh question: when does coercion justify consuming something that is normally haram? The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between threats to life or limb and lesser threats such as beating or imprisonment. The text establishes the principle based on the Quranic prohibition, and then discusses the measure of overwhelming coercion, rejecting a fixed number in favor of subjective assessment. Finally, it explains that even non-overwhelming coercion creates a shubha that prevents the hadd penalty for drinking wine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and the two categories of coercion
The acts are eating pork, carrion, blood, and drinking wine. Coercion is divided into: (a) what does not threaten life/limb (beating, imprisonment, shackling) – not permissible; (b) what threatens life/limb (threat of death or amputation) – permissible.
This is the core ruling.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The prohibition is from the Quranic nass (e.g., 5:3, 5:90). The exception of necessity (darura) is established by other proofs (e.g., Quran 2:173). The text mentions 'النص' (the text) as the source of prohibition.
Quranic evidence is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah of the proof
The absolute prohibition implies it remains unless a clear exception exists. Necessity (darura) is the exception, and ikrah mulji' is a form of darura. The imperative to avoid these things is general; the exception of necessity limits it.
The exception is derived from the principle that necessity permits the prohibited.
4
Note the ikhtilaf about the measure of overwhelming coercion
Some scholars (ba'dahum) fixed the threshold at forty lashes. The preferred Hanafi view (mufta bihi) rejects a fixed number because people's endurance varies. Instead, the criterion is the coerced person's prevailing opinion (ghalabat al-zann) that loss of life or limb will occur.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by the later masters.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih and its consequence on hadd
The relied-upon position is subjective assessment. Under lesser coercion (non-overwhelming), the act remains sinful but the hadd penalty for drinking wine is averted due to the shubha (semblance of permissibility) created by coercion. The text cites al-Muhit: the stronger coercion establishes actual ibaha, the weaker creates a shubha sufficient to avert hadd.
This shows the nuanced application of usul principles to penal law.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Imams of the Hanafi school. It states the general rule that forbidden things (carrion, blood, pork, wine) remain forbidden under coercion that does not threaten life or limb (such as imprisonment or beating). Only when the coercion is overwhelming (ikrah mulji') — namely the threat of death or amputation — do these things become permissible. The proof is that their prohibition is established by definitive texts (nass), and they are only permitted under genuine necessity (darura), which is the state of dire need like starvation. The commentary then addresses a dispute: some scholars fixed the threshold at forty lashes, but the preferred Hanafi view rejects numerical specification because people vary. The criterion is the coerced person's own prevailing opinion (ghalabat al-zann) that life or limb will be lost. Finally, the hadd penalty for drinking wine: under lesser coercion, even though the act is not permissible (fiskh), the hadd is averted due to the shubha (semblance of permissibility) created by the coercion. This is because the stronger coercion establishes actual ibaha, while the weaker creates a shubha, which is sufficient to ward off the hadd.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion that compels a person to do something against his will through threat of harm.
إكراه ملجئ (ikrah mulji'): Overwhelming coercion where the coerced fears loss of life or limb, such as threat of death or amputation.
ضرورة (darura): Necessity that permits the forbidden, such as starvation or fear of death.

Makruh Items and Jallalah Purification
List of makruh items and jallalah rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on donkey's milk, camel urine, and jallalah animals, and apply the purification process for jallalah.

Core Idea
Donkey's milk and jallalah meat are makruh, but the latter becomes halal after confinement and clean feeding.

This lesson covers the beginning of the chapter on disliked matters in eating and drinking. The passage identifies specific items considered makruh tahrimi—such as the milk of a female donkey and camel urine—and introduces the important concept of jallalah (animals that consume filth). You will learn the Hanafi position on these foods and the process by which a jallalah animal becomes permissible again.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disliked items
List the items explicitly mentioned as makruh: donkey's milk, donkey's meat, using donkey's milk for oil or perfume, and camel urine. Note that horse's milk is also makruh according to Abu Hanifa.
Donkey's milk and meat are makruh; camel urine is also included.
2
Understand the jallalah concept
Define jallalah: an animal that eats filth (impure substances) until its meat and milk develop a foul smell. Its consumption is makruh until it is purified.
The animal must have changed in smell/taste.
3
Learn the purification method
Know that the jallalah must be confined and fed clean fodder until the foul smell disappears. Abu Hanifa did not fix a time; other scholars suggested specific durations: 40 days for camels, 20 for cows, 10 for sheep, 3 for chickens.
Once the smell is gone, it becomes halal.
4
Consider the ruling on kid fed pig's milk
If a kid goat drank pig's milk but its meat did not stink, it is not considered jallalah and is permissible.
The ruling depends on the effect on the meat, not merely the ingestion.

Rulings on Donkey Milk, Horse Milk, and Jallalah Animals
States rulings on disliked meats, drinks, and jallalah animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that donkey milk and meat are makruh tahrimi according to the Hanafi school, explain the same for jallalah animals, and identify the condition that makes an animal jallalah and how it becomes permissible.

Core Idea
Donkey and jallalah animals are makruh tahrimi; purification requires confinement until the stench disappears.

In this passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq, we encounter several rulings on disliked meats and drinks. The focus is on the milk and meat of donkeys and horses, and the special case of jallalah animals—those that feed on filth. Understanding these rulings helps us navigate what is permissible and what is prohibitively disliked in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
List each item (donkey milk/meat, horse milk/meat, camel urine, jallalah camel/cow meat/milk, kid fed pig's milk) and state the hukm from the text: most are makruh tahrimi; kid fed pig's milk is permissible if meat unchanged.
The text uses 'kariha' for dislike, and the commentator (Ayni) clarifies it is makruh tahrimi.
2
Understand the reasoning
Donkey milk is disliked because it is generated from its meat, so the ruling follows the meat. Similarly, horse milk is disliked like its meat according to Abu Hanifa.
This is a qiyas: milk is derived from meat, so the ruling is the same.
3
Define jallalah and its condition
An animal becomes jallalah if it eats filth to the point that its meat and milk change and emit a foul smell. It is makruh to eat it or drink its milk until it is confined in a pure place and fed pure fodder until the stench disappears.
Abu Hanifa did not fix a specific duration; others suggested 40 days for camels, 20 for cows, etc.
4
Apply to the case of a kid fed pig's milk
According to Fatawa al-Walwalji, if a kid is fed pig's milk but its meat does not change, it is not considered jallalah and is permissible because the consumed milk is rendered negligible.
This shows that the condition for jallalah is a noticeable change, not mere consumption of impurity.

Commentary

Khamr: Definition and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Foaming
Defines khamr and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on foaming

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of khamr and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the condition of foaming (qadhf al-zabad) and its impact on hadd punishment.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires foaming for khamr; Sahibayn say mere fermentation suffices — a dispute over doubt versus certainty in establishing hadd.

This lesson introduces the definition of khamr (wine) according to the Hanafi school. While all intoxicants are prohibited, the Hanafis restrict the term 'khamr' to grape-based drinks. A key internal dispute arises regarding an additional condition: does the drink need to foam before it can be legally considered khamr? The answer determines when the severe hadd punishment applies.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi definition of khamr
Identify the linguistic and juristic definition of khamr from the passage. Note that in Hanafi fiqh, khamr is specifically the intoxicating drink from grapes, not all intoxicants.
Other schools define khamr broadly as any intoxicant.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Pinpoint the point of contention: Abu Hanifa requires foaming (qadhf al-zabad) for a drink to be considered khamr, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that mere fermentation (ishtidad) is sufficient.
This dispute is within the Hanafi school itself.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's rationale
Abu Hanifa argues that the rulings of khamr (prohibition and hadd) are definitive (maqtou' biha) and cannot be established by doubtful evidence. Foaming removes the doubt, providing certainty.
This reflects a cautious approach to applying hadd.
4
Examine the Sahibayn's position and evidence
The Sahibayn argue that the drink is called khamr before foaming, so fermentation alone suffices. They also cite that 'the three' (possibly including Imam Shafi'i) agreed on this.
They prioritize the common usage and consensus.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the precautionary view
The practical consequence: the hadd punishment for drinking khamr is only applied after foaming according to Abu Hanifa, while the Sahibayn would apply it upon fermentation. The relied-upon view (as per the text) is the precautionary one: for prohibition, fermentation suffices; for hadd, foaming is required.
This compromise balances certainty and practicality.

The Hanafi school defines khamr specifically as the intoxicating drink from grapes (or the juice that becomes intoxicating when fermented). Some narrations suggest all intoxicants are khamr, but the Hanafis interpret those Hadith metaphorically, arguing that each intoxicant has its own specific name. A key intra-Hanafi dispute is highlighted: Abu Hanifa requires that the grape juice must foam (qadhf al-zabad) to be considered khamr, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that mere fermentation (ishtidad) is sufficient. However, there is a consensus that boiling and fermentation are conditions. Some scholars differentiate: for the prohibition of drinking, mere fermentation suffices, but for the hadd punishment, foaming is required as a precaution. This passage also includes a discussion on why khamr was prohibited gradually and why it was allowed for previous nations.

This passage from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq defines three beverages that become haram only when intoxicating, distinguishing them from grape wine (khamr). Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings of hunting with dogs, focusing on cases where the dog strangles the prey without wounding, shares with an untrained or Magian's dog, or where the sending is interrupted by a shout from a Magian or Muslim. The author begins by stating that if the dog strangles the prey without wounding, it is absolutely forbidden (haram), regardless of whether it breaks a limb. This is because the condition for halal hunting is wounding that causes blood flow, and strangling does not achieve that. Abu Yusuf reports from Abu Hanifa that breaking a limb counts as an internal wound and thus makes the prey halal; however, the relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, forbidding it, as the breaking does not cause blood to flow. The text then discusses five cases where hunting is forbidden, explaining that in the first case the slaughter was optional, in the second there is no wound, and in the remaining cases the forbidden and permitted elements combine, so the forbidden prevails out of precaution. Next, the commentary addresses the scenario where a second dog returns the prey without wounding it, and the prey dies from the first dog's wound—this is disliked, with a difference of opinion whether it is tanzihi or tahrimi, the latter being preferred by al-Halwani. The text then moves to cases involving shouting (zajr). If a Muslim sends his dog and a Magian shouts at it, the sending remains valid because the shout is inferior to the sending and does not annul it; thus the prey is halal. Conversely, if a Magian sends the dog and a Muslim shouts, the sending is already invalid, so the shout cannot make it valid, and the prey is forbidden. This principle extends to anyone whose slaughter is invalid, such as a pilgrim in ihram, an apostate, an idolater, or one who deliberately omits tasmiyah. Finally, the passage covers the case where no one sends the dog, but it goes after the prey on its own, and a Muslim shouts and it responds—then the prey is halal by istihsan. Qiyas would forbid it because there is no sending, but istihsan considers the shout as a substitute for sending because the dog's obedience indicates an effective cause.

Hunting with Projectiles and Finding Prey Alive: Hanafi Rulings
Ruling with proof for projectile hunting and finding prey alive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for halal hunting with a projectile weapon and explain the ruling when the prey is found alive, including the evidence and scholarly differences.

Core Idea
A hunted animal is halal if wounded by a projectile with tasmiyah; if found alive, it must be slaughtered.

After completing the discussion on hunting with trained dogs, the text now turns to hunting with inanimate projectile weapons such as arrows and spears. The core principle is that a projectile that wounds and causes blood flow substitutes for normal slaughter, but only if the hunter pronounces tasmiyah and the animal's death is clearly caused by the weapon. A key additional rule: if the hunter reaches the prey alive, he must perform normal dhabh; otherwise the meat is forbidden. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof from the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim, and various scenarios that illustrate the conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting by shooting a projectile (arrow, spear, etc.) at game. The ruling: if the hunter says tasmiyah and the projectile wounds the animal, the meat is halal. If the animal is found alive, the hunter must slaughter it immediately; if he does not and it dies, it is haram.
This is the basic ruling from the matn.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim (Bukhari, Muslim, Ahmad): 'If you shoot your arrow, mention the name of Allah over it. If you find it killed, eat it, unless you find it has fallen into water, for you do not know whether the water killed it or your arrow.' The hadith establishes permissibility under conditions: tasmiyah, wounding, and certainty of cause.
The hadith also introduces an exception: if the prey falls into water, it is not halal due to ambiguity.
3
Apply the conditions to scenarios
The text expands on several scenarios: (1) hearing a sound and shooting, hitting different prey; (2) shooting at a bird and hitting another; (3) shooting at a domestic animal and hitting wild prey; (4) hitting the target after mistakenly thinking it was a human. In each case, the ruling depends on the intention and the nature of the prey. The general principle: the act must be genuinely intended as hunting (qasd al-sayd).
Abu Yusuf and Zufar add conditions based on the sound heard; the text generally follows the broader permissibility.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Intra-Hanafi: Abu Yusuf distinguishes based on the type of sound (e.g., pig vs. beast); Zufar conditions on the edibility of the heard prey. Cross-madhhab: Imam Malik opposes the case where a dog sent after specific prey catches another, but the Hanafi school holds it halal. The text presents the Hanafi position as the stronger view.
The differences are practical: e.g., if the sound is of a pig, the struck animal is not eaten according to Abu Yusuf.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is the general ruling from the matn: the prey is halal if tasmiyah is said and the projectile wounds, except if the animal is found alive (then slaughter is required) or if it falls into water (then halal is questionable). The details from Abu Yusuf and Zufar are noted but the text implies the wider permission.
Authoritative weight: zahir al-riwayah (the six core books).

Mixed Slaughtered and Dead Sheep: The Hanafi Ruling on Taharr and Ghalaba
Ruling on eating from a mixed flock of slaughtered and dead sheep with taharr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating from a mixed flock of slaughtered and dead sheep, explain the role of taharr, and compare the Hanafi position with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
If the slaughtered sheep outnumber the dead, taharr permits eating—probability replaces certainty.

Imagine a flock where some sheep are properly slaughtered (halal) and some are found dead (haram), and you cannot tell which is which. The Hanafi school allows you to eat after careful estimation (taharr) if the slaughtered ones are more numerous. This ruling is based on the principle of overwhelming majority (ghalaba) and contrasts with the Shafi'i requirement of certainty. In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling, its proof, and the inter-madhhab disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Key Question
The source presents a mixed flock of sheep: some halal (slaughtered) and some haram (dead). The key question is when, if ever, it is permissible to eat from this flock.
The ruling applies when there is no distinguishing mark on individual sheep.
2
Recognize the Hanafi Principle of Ghalaba
The Hanafi school relies on overwhelming majority (ghalaba) as a basis for permissibility. If the number of slaughtered sheep exceeds the number of dead, the predominant is halal, making it permissible to eat after taharr.
This principle is analogous to Muslim markets where most goods are halal even if some haram may exist.
3
Understand Taharr as an Investigative Process
Taharr is not random picking; it is a genuine effort to choose the likely halal sheep based on signs or probability. The eater must exert some effort to identify a slaughtered sheep.
If the dead are more, taharr is invalid and one must refrain entirely.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i Objection
The Shafi'i school holds that taharr is not sufficient because one can simply avoid eating altogether. The Hanafi response is that overwhelming probability (ghalaba) is equivalent to necessity in making things permissible.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf recognized in usul al-fiqh.

Leaving the location voids the condition; returning does not revive it.

Oaths are often tied to conditions of time or place. In this lesson, we examine a case where the condition is 'as long as I am in Bukhara'. The key point is that the condition is linked to continuous presence. Once the person leaves, the condition is considered terminated, and returning does not restart it. The phrase 'as long as I am in Bukhara' means the condition applies only during continuous presence. It is not a general condition like 'if I am ever in Bukhara'.

When he leaves Bukhara, the condition no longer applies because he is no longer 'in Bukhara'. The condition is voided and does not revive upon return.

Since the condition was voided when he left, doing the act after returning does not make him violate the oath. No penalty is incurred.

Recommended duration for Quran completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi recommendation to complete the Quran in forty days and the wisdom behind it.

Core Idea
Quran completion should allow reflection; forty days is the recommended minimum for deep understanding.

The passage addresses the question of how quickly a hafiz should complete the Quran. It advises a minimum of forty days to ensure proper reflection and understanding, rather than mere speed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommendation
The hafiz is advised to complete the Quran in forty days, reciting a hizb per day, sometimes a half or two-thirds of a hizb.
The text mentions variation due to finding a good stopping place.
2
Understand the wisdom
The purpose of recitation is to reflect on meanings (tadabbur). Forty days allows time for contemplation, as per Quran 4:82.
3
Note the allowance for extension
If the reader cannot finish at the exact end of a hizb, they may add a little to complete a proper stop, showing priority of correct recitation over strict clock.
