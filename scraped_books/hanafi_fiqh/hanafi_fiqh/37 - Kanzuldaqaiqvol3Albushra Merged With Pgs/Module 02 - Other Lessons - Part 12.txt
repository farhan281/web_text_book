Definition of Khuntha and the Primary Sign of Urination
Definition and primary ruling on khuntha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of khuntha, apply the primary sign of urination to determine gender, and explain the rule when urine comes from both organs.

Core Idea
Gender is determined by the organ from which urine first exits; frequency is irrelevant.

The fiqh of khuntha (hermaphrodite) is an important branch dealing with individuals whose gender is ambiguous. This lesson covers the definition and the primary method of gender identification based on urination, as established by the hadith of Ali (RA).

Guided Path
1
Define khuntha
Identify the person as one who has both male and female reproductive organs or is devoid of both but has ambiguous characteristics.
2
Identify the primary sign
The primary sign is urination. If urine comes from the male organ, the person is male; if from the female organ, female.
Based on the hadith: 'From where he urinates.'
3
Apply the rule for single organ
If urine comes from only one organ, gender is assigned accordingly.
4
Apply for both organs
If urine comes from both, the earlier urination determines gender; if simultaneous, the person is ambiguous (mushkil). Frequency is not considered.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Hermaphrodite (Khuntha) defines the term and lays out the method for determining the gender of a person born with both male and female organs (or none). The author defines khuntha as one who has both a penis (ذكر) and a vagina (فرج). The primary test is urination: if urine comes from the penis, the person is male; if from the vagina, female. If urine comes from both, the earlier urination determines gender; if simultaneous, the person is an ambiguous hermaphrodite (مشكل). Frequency of urination is not considered according to Abu Hanifa, but the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the organ with more frequent urine determines gender. After puberty, other signs take precedence: if a beard appears or he can have intercourse with women, he is male; if breasts, milk, menses, or pregnancy appear, she is female. The commentary also discusses the case of a person with no organs at all, who urinates from the navel: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say they do not know what to call such a person, while Muhammad says such a person is ruled as female. The hadith from Ali (رضي الله عنه) is cited: 'From where he urinates' as the basis for the primary sign. The passage then details the reasoning: the earlier urination indicates the original organ, and once that sign is established, it does not change. Abu Hanifa argues that frequency is not a reliable indicator because it depends on the width of the urethra, not on which organ is original. The two disciples analogize to the general principle in Shariah that the more frequent has the ruling of the whole, and thus preponderance is given by frequency. Abu Hanifa counters by analogizing to witnesses: two witnesses are not given more weight than four just because they are more numerous in number; similarly, frequency of urine does not override the fact of urination itself.

Key Terms
خنثى (khunthā): A person who has both male and female reproductive organs, or is devoid of both but has ambiguous characteristics.
مشكل (mushkil): An ambiguous hermaphrodite whose gender cannot be determined due to equal signs from both organs or simultaneous urination.
ذكر (dhakar): The male organ (penis) of the hermaphrodite.
فرج (farj): The female organ (vagina) of the hermaphrodite.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Frequency and Secondary Signs After Puberty
Intra-Hanafi dispute on frequency and secondary signs after puberty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding frequency of urination, and list the secondary signs that determine gender after puberty.

Core Idea
Frequency does not preponderate; secondary signs like beard or breasts override at puberty.

This lesson delves into the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether frequency of urination from one organ can determine gender when urine comes from both. After puberty, additional signs such as beard, breasts, menses, and pregnancy become decisive.

Guided Path
1
State the dispute on frequency
When urine comes from both organs, Abu Hanifa said frequency is irrelevant; the Sahibayn said the more frequent urine determines gender.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the fact of urination is a complete sign, and frequency does not indicate originality; it may be due to physical factors.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
They argue that more frequent urine indicates the original organ, analogizing to the principle that the more frequent has the ruling of the whole.
4
Understand the outcome of the dispute
Under Abu Hanifa, if frequency is equal, the person remains ambiguous. Under the Sahibayn, if one organ has more urine, that gender is assigned.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
5
List secondary signs after puberty
Signs of maleness: beard, ability to have intercourse with women, wet dream from male organ. Signs of femaleness: breasts, milk, menses, pregnancy.
These signs take precedence over urination signs after puberty.

Definition and Rulings on the Hermaphrodite (Khuntha)
Defines khuntha, methods to determine gender, and rulings for the ambiguous case.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq introduces the chapter on the hermaphrodite (khuntha). It begins with a definition: a khuntha is a person who has both male and female genitalia. The default method to determine gender is to observe urination: if urine comes from the male organ, the person is considered male; if from the female organ, female. If urine comes from both, the earlier urination is decisive. If both occur simultaneously and equally, the person is declared ambiguous (mushkil). Frequency of urination from one organ is not considered according to Abu Hanifa; the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that frequency indicates the primary organ. After puberty, additional signs determine gender: for males, a beard or ability to penetrate; for females, breasts, milk, menses, or pregnancy. If no signs appear or signs conflict, the person remains ambiguous. The ruling for an ambiguous khuntha is that he/she stands between the men's and women's rows during prayer, a slave girl is purchased from his/her wealth (or public treasury if poor) for circumcision, and then the slave is sold. In inheritance, the ambiguous khuntha receives the lesser of the two possible shares (i.e., the smaller of the male and female portions).

The Mute's Gesture and Writing as Legal Speech
Hanafi ruling on the legal capacity of a mute through gesture and writing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a mute's gestures and writing are equivalent to speech and classify the three types of writing with their legal effects.

Core Idea
A mute's gesture and writing substitute speech in all transactions, except hudud, due to permanent incapacity.

This passage establishes the fundamental Hanafi principle that a person born mute or permanently unable to speak (al-akhras) may transact all legal matters through gesture (ima') and writing (kitaba). The school treats these as fully equivalent to verbal expression because the incapacity is beyond the mute's control. However, writing is divided into three categories with distinct evidentiary weight. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly applying transactions involving mute parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the general ruling
Locate the ruling that the gesture and writing of a mute are like explicit speech (bayan). Note that this applies to marriage, divorce, sale, purchase, oaths, etc., but not to hadd penalties.
The initial sentence encapsulates the entire ruling.
2
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The text gives the reason: gesture is a means of expression for the capable; for the incapable, it becomes the primary means. Writing is treated like addressed speech at the time of incapacity.
This is an istihsan (juristic preference) based on necessity.
3
Distinguish the three types of writing
Writing is divided into formal (with heading), informal (without heading), and unclear (like writing on water). Only formal writing is automatically valid; informal requires additional evidence; unclear has no legal effect.
The terms 'mustabin marsum' and 'ghayr marsum' are key.
4
Apply the ruling to a case
Consider a mute person who writes a will. According to this ruling, if the writing is formal (addressed to someone), it is valid and enforceable.
This shows the practical application.

Commentary
This passage deals with the legal capacity of a mute person (akhras) to perform transactions through gestures (ima') and writing (kitaba). The ruling is that both are equivalent to verbal speech (bayan) in all legal matters except hudud (fixed punishments). This is because the mute is inherently incapable of speech, so his gestures and writing become his primary means of communication. The passage distinguishes three types of writing: (1) clear and formal (with heading like a letter) — valid for present and absent; (2) clear but informal (on walls, leaves) — requires additional evidence like intention or witnesses; (3) unclear (on air, water) — invalid even with intention. The exception is a person whose tongue is temporarily tied due to illness (mu'taqil al-lisan). For such a person, gestures are not automatically accepted because he had the chance to speak earlier and delayed; his condition is seen as negligence. However, if the condition persists for a long time (estimated as one year by al-Tamartashi), or lasts until death, then his gestures are accepted based on a narration from Abu Hanifa, and this is the relied-upon fatwa. The Shafi'i view is that gestures of such a person are valid, but the Hanafi response is that the mute is not negligent. The exception for hudud: gestures and writing are not accepted for establishing hadd penalties because hudud are repelled by doubts, and gesture/writing lack the certainty required. For example, if a mute accuses someone of zina (slander), his gesture is not sufficient for establishing the hadd of qadhf. Similarly, in qisas, the gesture is accepted because it concerns a personal right of the victim, which does not require strict verbal formulation. The passage concludes the chapter on 'Miscellaneous Issues' (masa'il shatta) of inheritance, covering various leftover rulings.

الكتابة (al-kitaba): Writing by the mute; three types based on clarity and formality.

The Temporarily Tongue-Tied Person and the Hadd Exception
Hanafi exceptions for temporary incapacity and hudud penalties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling for a temporarily tongue-tied person from that of a mute, and explain why gestures are not accepted for establishing hudud penalties.

Core Idea
A temporarily tongue-tied person's gesture is not accepted unless prolonged for a year or until death, and gestures never establish hudud penalties.

This passage addresses two important exceptions to the general rule that gestures substitute speech. First, for a person whose tongue becomes temporarily tied due to illness (mu'taqil al-lisan), the Hanafi school does not automatically accept his gestures because he could have spoken earlier and thus bears some negligence. Second, even the mute's gesture and writing are not accepted for establishing hadd penalties because hudud are repelled by doubts and require explicit verbal testimony. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for applying the correct ruling in cases of temporary incapacity and criminal accusations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exception for mu'taqil al-lisan
The text states that the gesture of a temporarily tongue-tied person is not like speech. Note the scenario: a patient with tied tongue nods to a will; the nod is not accepted.
This is contrary to the mute.
2
Understand the Hanafi rationale and the dispute
The Hanafi justification is that the mu'taqil was negligent by delaying his will until incapacity, whereas the mute is never negligent. The Shafi'i position accepts the gesture unconditionally. The Hanafi counter-argument emphasizes the need for the gesture to become habitual (prolonged).
Also note that Ahmad ibn Hanbal agrees with the Hanafi view.
3
Determine the condition for acceptance
If the tied-tongue condition persists for a year (according to al-Tamartashi) or until death, then the gesture becomes acceptable, based on a narration from Abu Hanifa. This is the mufta bihi.
Duration of prolongation is a key point.
4
Explain the hudud exception
The mute's gesture/writing is not accepted for hadd penalties because hudud are repelled by doubts. The gesture/writing lacks the certainty required. For qisas, however, gesture is accepted because it is a slave's right.
This highlights the different evidentiary standards for rights of Allah vs. rights of individuals.

Commentary
This passage deals with the legal capacity of a mute person (akhras) to perform transactions through gestures (ima') and writing (kitaba). The ruling is that both are equivalent to verbal speech (bayan) in all legal matters except hudud (fixed punishments). This is because the mute is inherently incapable of speech, so his gestures and writing become his primary means of communication. The passage distinguishes three types of writing: (1) clear and formal (with heading like a letter) — valid for present and absent; (2) clear but informal (on walls, leaves) — requires additional evidence like intention or witnesses; (3) unclear (on air, water) — invalid even with intention. The exception is a person whose tongue is temporarily tied due to illness (mu'taqil al-lisan). For such a person, gestures are not automatically accepted because he had the chance to speak earlier and delayed; his condition is seen as negligence. However, if the condition persists for a long time (estimated as one year by al-Tamartashi), or lasts until death, then his gestures are accepted based on a narration from Abu Hanifa, and this is the relied-upon fatwa. The Shafi'i view is that gestures of such a person are valid, but the Hanafi response is that the mute is not negligent. The exception for hudud: gestures and writing are not accepted for establishing hadd penalties because hudud are repelled by doubts, and gesture/writing lack the certainty required. For example, if a mute accuses someone of zina (slander), his gesture is not sufficient for establishing the hadd of qadhf. Similarly, in qisas, the gesture is accepted because it concerns a personal right of the victim, which does not require strict verbal formulation. The passage concludes the chapter on 'Miscellaneous Issues' (masa'il shatta) of inheritance, covering various leftover rulings.

Paying Land to Non-Owners to Give Kharaj
Ruling on imam's authority to manage kharaj land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the imam's authority to lease or sell kharaj land and identify the conditions under which each action is permissible.

Core Idea
Imam may lease or sell kharaj land to preserve fighters' rights, but owners retain surplus value.

This lesson examines a critical scenario in public finance: when landowners cannot pay kharaj. The Hanafi school provides a balanced solution that protects both the fighters' right to revenue and the owners' property rights, through leasing or sale by the imam. The passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explains the imam's options and the underlying reasoning, including an intra-Hanafi dispute on the permissibility of sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The landowners (owners of kharaj land) are unable to cultivate the land and pay the kharaj. This creates a conflict: the fighters (muqatilah) who are entitled to the kharaj revenue will lose their right if no action is taken.
2
First solution: lease the land
The imam may give the land to others on rent (i.e., lease it) so that they pay the kharaj from the rent. Any surplus rent after paying the kharaj is returned to the original owners.
Leasing is agreed upon by all Hanafi scholars.
3
Second solution: sell the land if no renter found
If the imam cannot find someone to rent the land, he may sell it to someone capable of cultivation. The sale price is used to pay any past due kharaj, and the remainder goes to the original owners.
There is an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether sale is permissible by all or only by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
4
Understand the legal reasoning
The imam cannot simply confiscate the land or give it away for free because ownership is preserved. However, the right of the fighters must be protected. Leasing and selling are less harmful than total loss of kharaj revenue.
The principle: loss to an alternative (the owner loses the land but gets compensation) is better than total loss (fighters lose all revenue).
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon view in the Hanafi school is that both leasing and selling are permissible for the imam. The dispute on sale is noted, but the matn and later fatwa support sale.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin (e.g., Ramz al-Haqaiq, al-Durr).

Core Idea
Silence, when immediate, is acceptance by custom – a key Hanafi principle in contracts.

This passage establishes that acknowledgment (iqrar) does not create ownership; it merely discloses. The core ruling is that silence in response to an offer of agency is deemed acceptance by custom (urf). The Hanafi school supports this with a qiyas to the gift of a debt: if the debtor remains silent, the gift is valid and the debt discharged. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly evaluating many transactional scenarios in Hanafi fiqh.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Is Acceptance Required for Waqf?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on acceptance in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on whether waqf requires acceptance by the beneficiary, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Waqf is established by the owner's declaration alone according to Abu Yusuf, while the majority requires silence as acceptance.

This passage extends the rule of silence as acceptance to waqf, but then introduces a disagreement. Al-Ansari held that if the beneficiary says 'I do not accept,' the waqf does not become invalid because it is for Allah. Al-Zayla'i attributes this view to Abu Yusuf, who held that waqf is effective by the mere utterance of the owner, without requiring any acceptance. This lesson unpacks that intra-madhhab difference and clarifies the operative ruling in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule from the passage
The author says: if someone declares his land waqf for another and the beneficiary remains silent, it is valid; if he says 'I do not accept,' it becomes invalid.
This is consistent with the earlier rule that silence implies acceptance.
2
Identify the opposing view
Al-Ansari said it does not become invalid because the waqf is for Allah; the beneficiary's rejection is irrelevant.
This view prioritises the religious nature of waqf over transactional acceptance.
3
Identify the author's attribution
Al-Zayla'i states that this is likely the view of Abu Yusuf, known for holding that waqf becomes effective by the mere saying 'I have made my property waqf.'
Abu Yusuf's position is based on the idea that waqf is an act of worship and charity, not a bilateral contract.
4
Understand the difference in principle
For Abu Yusuf, acceptance is not needed at all; for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, acceptance (by silence or explicit words) is required.
This difference stems from whether waqf is considered a contract or a unilateral dedication.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that waqf requires acceptance, and silence indicates acceptance. Abu Yusuf's view is a minority opinion, though it is sometimes relied upon in practice.
Later Hanafi jurists have given tarjih to the majority view, but the dispute remains known.

Dismissing an Agent Subject to a Conditional Agency
Explains the proper method to dismiss an agent when the agency is conditional upon dismissal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for permanently dismissing an agent when the agency is conditional upon prior dismissal.

Core Idea
Withdraw from the conditional agency first — otherwise each dismissal recreates the agency.

Imagine a principal tells his agent, 'Whenever I dismiss you, you are my agent.' Later, he wants to fire him for good. Simply saying 'I dismiss you' won't work because the word 'whenever' (kullamā) makes the conditional agency repeat indefinitely. The commentator explains the correct procedure: first withdraw the conditional clause, then dismiss the unconditional agency. Alternatively, the principal can pre-empt the problem by saying from the start, 'Whenever I appoint you, you are dismissed.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of agency involved
The scenario involves two agencies: an unconditional agency (currently active) and a conditional agency (suspended on the condition 'if I dismiss you, you are my agent'). Recognize that the conditional agency becomes effective upon dismissal.
2
Understand the effect of a simple dismissal
When the principal says 'I dismiss you,' the unconditional agency ends, but the conditional agency triggers, making the person an agent again. Thus, the dismissal is ineffective for permanent termination.
3
Recognize the implication of 'kullamā'
The word 'kullamā' (whenever) implies infinite repetition. Therefore, even if the principal dismisses the agent a thousand times, each dismissal re-activates the conditional agency, so the agent remains in place.
4
Formulate the correct dismissal procedure
The correct method is to first withdraw the conditional agency by saying 'I withdraw from the suspended agency,' and then dismiss the unconditional agency. This severs the conditional clause, preventing re-appointment.
The text also mentions an alternative: saying 'Whenever I appoint you, you are dismissed' from the start avoids the problem entirely.

Ruling on Well/Cesspool Damaging Neighbor's Wall
Ruling on liability for indirect damage from well/cesspool

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for indirect damage caused by a well on one's own property and distinguish when transgression (ta'addī) imposes liability.

Core Idea
No liability for indirect harm (tasbīb) from an act within own property unless transgression occurs.

This lesson examines a common neighbor dispute: if a well or cesspool on your property seeps moisture into your neighbor's wall, are you liable? The Hanafi ruling is that you are not compelled to remove it and not liable for damages, because the act is within your rights and the harm is indirect. However, the law advises kindness and draws a crucial distinction between indirect causation (tasbīb) and transgression (ta'addī).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is digging a well or constructing a cesspool on one's own property. The harm is moisture seeping into the neighbor's wall, potentially causing collapse.
2
State the ruling on compulsion and liability
The owner is not compelled to relocate the well/cesspool, and if the wall collapses, the owner is not liable for damages.
3
Understand the legal reasoning: property rights vs. harm
The act is within one's exclusive property (khālis mulkihī), so it is not considered transgression. Indirect causation (tasbīb) from a lawful act does not create liability.
The key principle: 'al-tasbīb lā yūjib al-ḍamān' (mere causation does not impose liability).
4
Distinguish tasbīb from ta'addī
Transgression (ta'addī) occurs when the act exceeds lawful limits, e.g., placing a stone on a public road or watering land excessively such that water overflows. In those cases, liability arises.
The exception from al-Fath: excessive watering that overflows onto neighbor's property is ta'addī and creates liability.

Racing and Betting: Permissibility and the Role of Istihsan
Ruling on racing with proof and conditions for betting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on racing, distinguish permissible one-sided betting from impermissible two-sided gambling, and explain the istihsan rationale and the muhallil exception.

Core Idea
Racing is permissible; betting from both sides is gambling, but one-sided betting is allowed by istihsan, and a third party can make two-sided betting permissible.

Racing competitions are part of Islamic tradition, as practiced by the Companions. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on musabaqah, focusing on when stake money (ju'l) is permissible and when it becomes forbidden gambling (qimar). The key distinction lies in whether the risk is mutual or one-sided, and a unique exception allows two-sided betting if a neutral third party (muhallil) is introduced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Determine the legal classification of racing itself. The text states that racing with horses, camels, feet, and archery is permissible (mubah). This is based on the hadith and practice of the Companions.
Racing is not inherently forbidden; it is a permissible activity with potential benefits for warriors.
2
Locate and analyze the primary dalil
Identify the hadith: 'La sabaqa illa fi khuff aw nasl aw hafir' (No prize except in hoof, arrowhead, or hoof). Understand that 'khuff' refers to camels, 'nasl' to archery, and 'hafir' to horses, mules, and donkeys. This hadith limits permissible betting to these specific competitive activities.
The hadith is ahad (single transmission) with zanni thubut and dalala, but it is accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Distinguish one-sided betting from two-sided betting
The text explicitly forbids stipulating a prize from both sides (e.g., each puts up a bet) because it constitutes qimar (gambling), which is haram. However, stipulating from one side only is permissible by istihsan, despite a qiyas that would also forbid it due to risk.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here because the one-sided bet lacks mutuality of risk, which is the essence of gambling.
4
Understand the muhallil exception
If both sides want to bet, they can bring a third party (muhallil) with a comparable horse. The condition is that the third party may win or lose; if he is sure to win or lose, it remains invalid. The muhallil removes the mutual risk because he does not definitely lose.
The muhallil makes the two-sided arrangement analogous to one-sided betting.
5
Note the legal enforceability
The permissibility discussed is one of permissibility (halal) but not enforceability. The judge cannot compel payment of a bet; it is a matter of voluntary fulfillment.
This prevents the courts from being used to collect gambling debts.

Etiquette of Du'a: Sending Blessings on Non-Prophets
Ruling on sending salawat and du'a for non-prophets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sending salawat and du'a for non-prophets and distinguish the appropriate forms for Companions, Followers, and others.

Core Idea
Prayer (salah) is reserved for the Prophet; others receive supplication appropriate to their station.

When we make du'a for the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, we use specific phrases like 'sallallahu alayhi wa sallam'. For others, the etiquette differs. The Hanafi school provides guidance on which forms of supplication are appropriate for different categories of people, from Companions to later generations. This lesson clarifies those distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Understand that sending salah (salawat) directly upon non-prophets is not permissible; it must be done in a subordinate manner (taba'an), such as 'upon his family and Companions'.
The word 'illa taba'an' indicates the only exception.
2
Understand the exception: taba'an
When including others with the Prophet, make it clear it is a follow-on, e.g., 'allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad wa 'ala aalihi wa sahbihi wa sallim'.
This preserves the exclusive veneration of the Prophet.
3
Examine the dispute on tarahhum for the Prophet
Note the two opinions on saying 'allahumma arham Muhammadan'. One group forbids it because it lacks the special veneration of salah and is redundant; another permits it.
The passage presents both without a definitive choice.
4
Apply the recommended forms for different groups
For Companions, ask for Allah's pleasure (ridwan); for Followers, ask for mercy (rahmah); for later generations, ask for forgiveness (maghfirah).
These forms are derived from the Companions' pursuit of pleasure and patience in trials.

The Basic Ruling: Permissible Contests and Prohibited Gambling
Explains the hadith proof and basic distinction between one-sided and two-sided stakes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three types of contests allowed for stakes and explain why two-sided stakes are prohibited as gambling.

Core Idea
Racing and archery with stakes are limited to camels, horses, and archery; two-sided stakes are haram gambling.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational hadith that restricts stakes in competitions. The Hanafi school uses this proof to permit contests only in specific animals and to prohibit gambling outright. Understanding this distinction is essential to avoid invalid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its scope
The hadith 'No stake except in a hoof, arrowhead, or hoof' limits stakes to three types. Identify the animals: camel (khuff), horse (hafir), and archery (nasl).
2
Understand the definition of jā'iz (permissible)
The text clarifies that permissibility here means the contest is not legally binding; the winner cannot force payment in court. The judge does not compel payment.
This is important: the stake is a voluntary reward, not a debt.
3
State the ruling on stipulating stakes from both sides
It is haram (forbidden) because it constitutes qimar (gambling), which is explicitly prohibited in the Quran. Both parties risk loss equally.
4
Identify the proof for prohibition
The prohibition is based on the general prohibition of gambling. The text mentions the linguistic meaning of qimar as mutual risk of increase or decrease.
5
Distinguish the permitted one-sided stake
If only one party offers a stake (conditionally), it is not gambling because only one side risks loss. This is allowed by istihsan, though qiyas would forbid it.
Istihsan is a Hanafi principle that gives preference to a more equitable ruling over strict analogy.

Commentary
The passage begins by ruling on horse racing and archery contests. The core ruling: the contest itself is permissible (jā'iz), but the stakes (ju‘l) are governed by strict rules to avoid gambling (qimār). The primary proof is the well-known hadith of Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him) that 'No stake except in a hoof, an arrowhead, or a hoof' – meaning only competitions involving camels, archery, and horses (including mules and donkeys) are allowed for stakes. The commentator clarifies that 'permissibility' here means the contest is halal but not legally binding; the winner cannot legally force the loser to pay unless agreed. The prohibition of stipulating a stake from both sides is based on the fact that it is gambling, which is explicitly forbidden. However, if the stake is from only one side (e.g., 'if you beat me, I pay you; if I beat you, you pay nothing'), it is not gambling because only one side risks losing. This is an exception by istihsān, though qiyās would prohibit that as well. The solution to allow a two-sided stake is to include a third party (muhallil) who is a fair competitor; then the two original contestants put their stakes, and the third party competes such that if he wins he takes both stakes, if he loses he gives nothing. This avoids gharar because the third party either gains or saves, never loses.

Stakes from One Side vs. Both Sides: The Principle of Istihsan
Explains the linguistic and methodological reasoning behind allowing one-sided stakes by istihsān.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits one-sided stakes by istihsān despite qiyas prohibiting them, and identify the linguistic basis for distinguishing qimar from one-sided stakes.

Core Idea
One-sided stakes are permitted by istihsān because they lack the reciprocal risk that defines gambling.

This lesson delves deeper into the Hanafi reasoning behind permitting stakes from one party while prohibiting two-sided stakes. The text explicitly contrasts qiyas with istihsān, showing how the school uses linguistic analysis and public benefit to reach a balanced ruling. Understanding this methodological move is key to appreciating Hanafi flexibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qiyas ruling on one-sided stakes
Strict analogy (qiyas) would prohibit one-sided stakes as well because it involves conditional ownership upon risk.
Qiyas looks at the presence of risk in both cases.
2
Understand the linguistic argument for istihsān
The term muqāmara (gambling) implies reciprocity (mufā‘ala), requiring both sides to risk loss. In a one-sided stake, only one party can lose, so it is not qimar.
Arabic morphology: mufā‘ala indicates a two-sided action.
3
Apply istihsān to override qiyas
Istihsān allows preferring the more equitable or beneficial ruling over strict qiyas. Here, the community need for friendly competition is considered.
Istihsān is a valid Hanafi source of law.

Note the practical consequence
The judge does not enforce payment of a one-sided stake because it is considered a voluntary reward (hibah), not a debt.
This is why the text says 'jā'iz' without legal entitlement.

The Third Party (Muhallil) Solution: Avoiding Gambling in Two-Sided Stakes
Explains the ruling and conditions for using a muhallil to make two-sided stakes permissible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the conditions and ruling for including a third party (muhallil) to make two-sided stakes permissible, and apply it to a scenario.

Core Idea
Two-sided stakes become permissible by introducing a third competitor who can win or lose, eliminating reciprocal gambling.

This lesson covers the clever Hanafi solution when two people both want to put up stakes: they can bring in a third party (muhallil) whose horse is a match. The third party either takes both stakes if he wins or pays nothing if he loses. This arrangement avoids qimār because the third party never loses wealth—only gains or saves. Understanding this requires careful attention to the conditions of the muhallil's ability.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem
Both parties want to stipulate stakes from both sides, which is normally haram. They need a lawful workaround.
2
Introduce the third party (muhallil)
They bring in a third person with a horse of equal ability. They say: 'If you beat both of us, both stakes are yours; if we beat you, you owe us nothing.'
The third party is called muhallil because he makes the contract permissible.
3
Check the condition of equal ability
The third party's horse must be such that it is possible for him to win or lose. If he always wins or always loses, the arrangement is invalid.
The condition ensures genuine uncertainty.
4
Understand why it is not gambling
The third party never pays; he only wins or ends with nothing. Thus, only the two original parties face risk, and that risk is not reciprocal (they compete against each other and the third).
The original two-sided condition between the two parties remains, but the inclusion of the third changes the overall nature.

Rulings on Caps, Black Clothing, and the Turban Tail
Declares the permissibility of caps and recommendation of black and turban tail.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on wearing caps, black clothing, and letting the turban tail hang, and identify which materials are disliked.

Core Idea
The Prophet’s practice establishes the permissibility of caps and recommendation of black and turban tail.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqāʾiq addresses three aspects of dress: caps (qalānis), the colour black, and the manner of wearing the turban. These rulings are derived from the Prophet’s own practice and are considered mustaḥabb or mubāḥ in the Hanafi school. The passage also highlights an intra-school difference on silk caps, which are ruled makrūh tanzīhan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Locate the three distinct subjects: (1) wearing caps, (2) wearing black clothing, (3) letting the turban tail hang down.
The passage begins with 'lā baʾs' (no harm) for caps, and 'nudiba' (recommended) for black and the turban tail.
2
Determine the hukm level for each
For caps: mubāḥ (permissible) generally, but silk caps are makrūh tanzīhan (disliked). For black clothing: mustaḥabb (recommended). For turban tail: mustaḥabb (recommended).
The passage explicitly states 'nudiba' for black and turban tail, indicating recommendation.
3
Locate the dalīl (proof)
The proof for caps is that the Prophet wore them (narrations in al-Dhakhīra). For black, the Prophet entered Mecca with a black turban. For the turban tail, the Prophet used to let it hang.
These are dalīl naqlī (textual proofs) from the Sunnah.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf on silk caps
Mullā Miskīn includes silk caps under general permissibility, but al-Durr al-Mukhtār excludes them and declares them disliked. Al-Fatḥ (Ibn al-Humām) sides with dislike, which is the muftā bihī.
The dispute is about whether the general ruling of permissibility applies to silk, given the prohibition of silk for men.
5
Note the muftā bihī (operative ruling)
The relied-upon ruling is: caps are permissible (silk caps disliked), black clothing is recommended, and letting the turban tail to mid-back is recommended.
This is the position given by al-Fatḥ and al-Durr al-Mukhtār.
