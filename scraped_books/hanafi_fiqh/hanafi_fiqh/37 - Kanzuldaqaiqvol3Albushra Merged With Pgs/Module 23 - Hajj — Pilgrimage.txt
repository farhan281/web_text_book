State at Shooting Determines Halal of Game
Applies the principle of considering state at time of shooting to permissibility of eating game.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for determining the permissibility of game based on the shooter's state at the time of shooting and apply it to cases involving apostasy or conversion.

Core Idea
The permissibility of game depends on the shooter's state at the moment of shooting, not later changes.

This lesson explores a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: the time of shooting (rami) determines whether game is halal. The passage from Al-Kanz expands on how the shooter's Islam or apostasy at that moment affects the ruling. Understanding this principle prevents common errors where later changes of state are mistakenly considered.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general rule
Recall that the permissibility of game is judged at the time the shooter releases the arrow, not when the arrow hits or later. This is because the shooting itself is the legal slaughter (dhabh).
The principle is derived from the fact that rami is within the shooter's control and is the cause of death.
2
Analyze the two cases given
Case 1: A Muslim shoots and then apostatizes before the arrow hits → game is halal. Case 2: A non-Muslim (Magian) shoots and then converts to Islam before the arrow hits → game is not halal. Identify the decisive factor: the shooter's state at the time of rami.
The apostate (murtad) is considered a non-Muslim, but his state at rami was Muslim, so the game is halal.
3
Grasp the rationale (dalil)
The text states: 'because the state at the time of shooting is considered regarding permissibility and prohibition, for the shooting is the slaughter; because it is his act and enters within the time of his ability, not the hitting.' The shooter's eligibility (being Muslim or kitabi) at the time of acting is what counts.
The hitting is not under the shooter's control, so it is not the basis for the ruling.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi exception
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ in the case where a Muslim shoots another Muslim and then apostatizes before the hit. They consider the shooter's later apostasy as discharging (making the target mubah). This exception does not affect the general rule for game.
The exception is based on the concept of discharging (ibra'), a distinct ruling.

Commentary
This passage applies the same principle to hunting. Part one: the permissibility of eating game depends on the shooter's state at the time of shooting, not his later state. If a Muslim shoots and then apostatizes before the arrow hits, the game is halal; if a non-Muslim shoots and then converts before the arrow hits, the game is not halal. The reason: the shooting is the effective slaughter (dhabh), and the shooter's qualification (muslim) at that moment determines permissibility. Part two: the obligation of jaza' (penalty) for a muhrim who hunts. If a muhrim shoots game and then becomes halal before the arrow hits, he must pay the penalty because the transgression (ta'addi) occurred when he shot while muhrim. Conversely, if a halal person shoots and then becomes muhrim before the arrow hits, no penalty is due, because the shooting was not a transgression. The passage concludes that this principle is unanimously agreed upon, except for the earlier case of apostasy after shooting a Muslim where Abu Yusuf and Muhammad departed from it due to their view of discharge.

الجزاء (al-jaza'): The penalty or compensation due for hunting in the state of ihram; it can be like the animal killed (by offering its equivalent in sacrifice) or a monetary value.

State at Shooting Determines Obligation of Jaza' for Muhrim
Applies the principle of considering state at time of shooting to obligation of jaza' for muhrim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for when a muhrim must pay jaza' for hunting, based on his state at the time of shooting, and apply it to cases of changing ihram status.

Core Idea
The obligation of jaza' depends on whether the shooter was muhrim at the time of shooting, not at the time of hitting.

This lesson addresses the second part of the passage: jaza' (penalty) for a muhrim who hunts. The principle is consistent: the state at the time of shooting determines the ruling. Even if the shooter later becomes halal (non-muhrim), the penalty remains wajib if he shot while muhrim. Conversely, if he shot while halal and later became muhrim, no jaza' is due.

Guided Path
1
Understand what jaza' is
Jaza' is the penalty (usually sacrificing a similar animal or its value) wajib on a muhrim who kills game. It is based on Quran 5:95. The passage here discusses when it becomes obligatory.
Jaza' is specific to ihram; it is not about eating the game but about the transgression of hunting while in ihram.
2
Analyze the two cases for jaza'
Case 1: A muhrim shoots game, then becomes halal before the arrow hits → jaza' is wajib. Case 2: A halal person shoots, then becomes muhrim before the hit → no jaza'. Identify the decisive factor: whether the shooter was muhrim at the time of shooting.
The transgression (ta'addi) is the act of shooting while muhrim, not the hitting.
3
Grasp the rationale (dalil)
The text explains: 'the penalty is obligatory due to transgression (ta'addi), which is the shooting while in the state of ihram.' The shooting is the act causing the transgression, and it is within the shooter's control.
The time of hitting is irrelevant for ta'addi because it is not an intentional act.
4
Link to the general principle
The passage states that the principle of considering the time of rami is agreed upon. This applies to both permissibility and jaza'. The exception by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad for shooting at a Muslim does not apply here.
This consistency shows the strength of the principle in Hanafi fiqh.

Executing a Will for Hajj: The Starting Point Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on starting point for Hajj of a deceased traveler who willed Hajj from his town

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the starting point for a deceased pilgrim's Hajj, distinguish between qiyas and istihsan reasoning, and apply the ruling to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
When a pilgrim dies en route, Abu Hanifa insists on Hajj from his town; the Sahibayn allow from the death place by istihsan.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the execution of a will for Hajj al-Islam. The core issue: where should the Hajj start for a person who died while traveling for Hajj and willed that it be performed from his town? Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ, with the former adhering strictly to the will's wording (qiyas) and the latter preferring flexibility (istihsan). Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school balances textual obligations with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its condition
Begin by understanding the baseline rule: if the deceased willed Hajj, the executor must hire a man from the deceased's town to perform it riding, only if the bequest (one-third of the estate) covers the full expenses. If not, the Hajj may start from a closer point where the third suffices.
This part is undisputed among the Hanafi imams.
2
Recognize the dispute trigger
The dispute arises when the deceased died while traveling for Hajj and willed that Hajj be done from his town. The question: does death in transit affect the starting point?
Abu Hanifa says no; the Sahibayn say yes.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's argument
Abu Hanifa applies qiyas: the will specifies the town because the original obligation was from the town. He cites the hadith that deeds cease with death (except three) to argue that the deceased's travel does not alter the obligation – the will must be honored exactly.
He interprets the exceptions as relating to afterlife rewards, not legal obligations.
4
Examine the Sahibayn's istihsan
The two companions use istihsan: the travel was an act of worship, so the obligation to cover that distance is lifted. Therefore, the deceased may be treated as if he were from the place of death, and the Hajj can start from there.
This deviation from qiyas is justified by the intent to facilitate the will's execution.
5
Apply the consensus exception
Note the exception: for a person with no fixed home (e.g., a perpetual traveler), there is consensus that Hajj starts from where he died, regardless of the will's wording.
This shows the dispute only applies when the deceased had a definite hometown.

Commentary
This passage deals with executing a will for Hajj al-Islam (the obligatory Hajj). The first ruling: if the deceased left a will for Hajj, the executor should hire a man from the deceased's own town to perform Hajj riding, if the bequest (one-third of the estate) is sufficient for the expenses. If the third is insufficient for the full expenses from the town, then the Hajj should be performed from a closer point where the third suffices. This is by istihsan (juristic preference) because the testator's intent was to fulfill the obligation; strict analogy would require adhering to the town specification, but istihsan allows flexibility. The second ruling: if a person left his town intending Hajj, died on the road, and willed that Hajj be performed from his town, the same rules apply. There is a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding where the Hajj should start for such a person. Abu Hanifa says it must start from his town, because the obligation was from the town, and death severs all actions; the hadith 'Every action ceases except three' is about rewards in the afterlife, not about legal obligations. The two companions say the Hajj may start from the place of death by istihsan, because the travel was itself an act of worship, and the obligation to cover that portion has been lifted; thus the deceased is considered as if he were from that place. The difference is only for those who have a fixed home; for a traveler without a fixed home, there is consensus that Hajj starts from where he died. Finally, the ruling also applies to a deputy (hired pilgrim) who dies en route: the same rules apply as if he were the original pilgrim: Hajj must be performed for him, from his town according to Abu Hanifa, or from the place of death according to the two.

Key Terms
حجة الإسلام (hijjat al-islam): The obligatory Hajj that every eligible Muslim must perform once in a lifetime.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; deviating from strict analogy (qiyas) for a more equitable ruling.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning; extending a ruling from a known case to a new case due to a common effective cause.

Ruling on Hajj Bequest When Funds Insufficient: Istihsan Over Qiyas
Ruling on execution of Hajj bequest when funds only reach partway, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi position on executing a Hajj bequest when funds are insufficient, including the liability of executors, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A bequest for Hajj is executed as far as funds allow, even if the starting point differs, by istihsan.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: a person bequeaths that Hajj be performed on his behalf from his hometown, but the one-third of his estate is insufficient to cover the expenses from that starting point. How should the executors proceed? The ruling hinges on a key principle of Hanafi fiqh: istihsan (juristic preference) overriding strict qiyas (analogy). We will explore the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and understand the practical consequences of their ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the default ruling by qiyas
Understand the facts: a deceased person bequeaths Hajj from his hometown. The one-third of the estate cannot cover the cost. By strict qiyas, because the specified condition (starting from hometown) is impossible, the bequest would be invalid. Recognize the problem: the testator's intent is to have Hajj performed, but the condition cannot be met.
Qiyas here would nullify the bequest entirely.
2
Apply istihsan and the new ruling
See how the Hanafi school uses istihsan to give effect to the testator's main objective: to have Hajj performed. The ruling: perform Hajj from where the funds reach (the point along the journey). This is permitted despite the unmet condition, because the aim is to execute the bequest as far as possible.
Istihsan is a recognized source in the Hanafi school, set aside strict qiyas.
3
Analyze the liability of executors when they choose a different starting point
If executors choose to perform Hajj from a place closer to Mecca than the hometown, they must guarantee any extra expenses because they deviated from the explicit instruction. If they choose a farther place, no liability, as they achieved the goal and more. Distinguish the two cases.
This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa.
4
Compare the position of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that Hajj should be performed from where the deceased died. Their reasoning: the journey with intention of Hajj was already an act of worship, and the obligation of Hajj was reduced by the distance covered. Thus, the starting point is the place of death. Understand the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): liability of executors differs.
The ikhtilaf applies only to someone with a fixed hometown. For a homeless traveler, there is consensus to start from where he died.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an intra-Hanafi dispute on executing a bequest for Hajj when the funds (from one-third of the estate) are insufficient to cover the full expense from the deceased's hometown. The author states that if the estate's one-third does not cover the cost from the deceased's town, the Hajj should be performed from the point the funds reach. This is istihsan (juristic preference). By strict qiyas (analogy), the bequest would be invalid because the condition of starting from the hometown is not met, but istihsan prevails because the testator's real intent is to have Hajj performed, and that should be fulfilled as far as possible. The passage then discusses a scenario where the executors choose to perform Hajj from a different location: if it is closer to Mecca than the hometown, they must bear any additional cost because they deviated from the explicit instruction; if farther, they are not liable as they achieved the goal and more. This is the position of Abu Hanifa. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) differ: they say Hajj should be performed from where the testator died, because his journey with the intention of Hajj was already an act of worship, and the obligation of Hajj was reduced by the distance he covered; thus the starting point is where he died, as if he were a resident there. Abu Hanifa's dalil is that the bequest specifically refers to Hajj from the hometown, and with death, actions cease (except ongoing charity, beneficial knowledge, and pious offspring). This ikhtilaf applies only to someone with a fixed hometown; for a traveler without a fixed home, there is consensus to perform Hajj from where he died. The final section clarifies that if someone commissioned to perform Hajj for another also dies en route, the same rule applies: Abu Hanifa says Hajj is performed for him from his own hometown; the disciples say from where the first died.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; setting aside strict qiyas in favor of a more equitable ruling to execute the testator's intent.
القياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; here, the strict analogy would invalidate the bequest because the condition of starting from the hometown is not met.

Lost Hajj Funds: Intention Over Allocation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on effect of losing money set aside for Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the effect of losing allocated Hajj funds and apply the principle that the purpose (qaṣd) of a bequest takes precedence over the specific means.

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, the purpose of a bequest outlives its allocated funds, so Hajj must still be performed from what remains.

Bequests for Hajj are common, but what happens when the money set aside for that purpose is lost before the pilgrimage? This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute that turns on whether the bequest attaches to the money itself or to its underlying goal. You will learn to distinguish the rulings of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad and understand why the principal's view is relied upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Start with the base case: a deceased person bequeaths that Hajj be performed from his estate. The executor divides with the heirs and sets aside funds, but those funds are lost. Now determine: is the bequest still binding?
The loss occurs after division and allocation.
2
State each Hanafi Imam's ruling
List the three positions: Abu Hanifa says perform Hajj from a third of what remains; Abu Yusuf says perform if only part of the third was allocated, otherwise void; Muhammad says never perform.
Use the names correctly: al-Imām is Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn are Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
3
Analyze the proof (dalīl) of each
Muhammad analogizes to the deceased himself setting aside money; Abu Yusuf focuses on the third as the locus (maḥall) of the bequest; Abu Hanifa emphasizes the purpose (qaṣd) of the bequest – division is a means, not an end.
The phrase 'القسمة لا تراد لذاتها بل لمقصودها' is the key principle.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, the executor must still arrange and fund Hajj from the remaining estate. Under the Sahibayn, the bequest is cancelled in whole or part, freeing the remainder for other uses.
The difference is in whether the bequest survives the loss.
5
Identify the muftā bihī (operative ruling)
The text presents Abu Hanifa's view first and emphasizes it with 'وهذا عند أبي حنيفة', indicating it is the relied-upon position in the school.
Most later Hanafi fatwa collections adopt Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (Ikhtilaf al-Imamayn) regarding a bequest for Hajj when the money allocated for it is lost before the Hajj is performed. The base scenario: the deceased bequeaths that Hajj be performed on his behalf from a third of his estate. The executor divides the estate, sets aside a specific amount for Hajj, but that money is lost. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that Hajj should still be performed from a third of whatever remains of the estate. Abu Yusuf differentiates: if the entire third was set aside and lost, the bequest is void; if only part was set aside, Hajj is performed from the remaining third up to the full third. Muhammad al-Shaybani says no Hajj is performed at all. Each provides a proof (dalil): Muhammad analogizes to the deceased setting aside money himself; Abu Yusuf focuses on the third as the subject of the bequest; Abu Hanifa emphasizes the purpose of the bequest (Qasd) over the specific allocation. The relied-upon position (Mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as indicated by the structure of the text and the phrase 'وهذا عند أبي حنيفة' (this according to Abu Hanifa) placed first.

Key Terms
الثلث (al-thuluth): The third of the estate available for bequests after debts and inheritance shares.
الحج (al-hajj): Pilgrimage to Mecca, here bequeathed as an act to be performed on behalf of the deceased.
الإمام (al-imam): In this context, refers to Abu Hanifa.
القسمة لا تراد لذاتها بل لمقصودها (al-qismatu la turādu li dhātihā bal li maqṣūdihā): The division (allocation) is not intended for its own sake but for its purpose.

Killing a Hajj Pilgrim as a Valid Excuse for Missing Hajj
Ruling that killing a hajj pilgrim excuses missing hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that killing a hajj pilgrim excuses missing hajj and explain the role of road safety (amn al-ṭarīq) as a condition for the obligation of hajj.

Core Idea
Killing a hajj pilgrim excuses missing hajj because road safety is a condition for its obligation.

Hajj is obligatory only when one has the ability (istiṭāʿa), which includes safety of the journey. This lesson examines a specific case where killing a fellow pilgrim compromises that safety, leading to a valid excuse for not performing hajj. We will explore the legal reasoning and the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether safety is a condition of obligation or performance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the Arabic text: 'قتل بعض الحاج، عذر في ترك الحج.' What is the ruling? It states that killing a hajj pilgrim is a valid excuse for abandoning hajj.
The ruling is clear: the killer is excused from performing hajj that year.
2
Understand the Legal Ground
The commentary explains the basis: safety of the road (amn al-ṭarīq) is a condition for hajj. Why does killing a pilgrim affect this condition? Because it indicates that the road is unsafe, thus the condition is absent.
The absence of the condition lifts the obligation.
3
Explore the Ikhtilaf
There is a dispute among Hanafi scholars: is safety a condition for the obligation to become due (shart al-wujūb) or a condition for valid performance (shart al-adāʾ)? In either case, if safety is absent, the person is excused.
Both views lead to the same practical result: no sin for missing hajj.
4
Determine the Consequence
The consequence is that the killer does not sin (lā yaʾtham) for not performing hajj that year. The obligation is not waived permanently; it remains for future years when safety is restored.
The excuse is only for the current year.
