The Mukatab's Crime and the Effect of Adjudication
Rulings on mukatab crime and effect of adjudication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the master's option when a mukatab becomes incapable after a crime, and explain the effect of adjudication on creating a debt for which the mukatab can be sold.

Core Idea
Adjudication of a mukatab's crime turns the compensation into a debt enforceable by sale.

This passage addresses the complex scenario of a mukatab who commits a crime and then becomes unable to pay his kitaba. The master's options and the effect of judicial ruling are clarified, along with a notable difference within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the master's initial option
When a slave is made a mukatab without the master knowing of a prior crime, and then the slave becomes incapable, the master chooses between handing over the slave or paying the lesser of value or blood money.
This is the original rule for a slave's crime.
2
Understand the effect of adjudication before incapacity
If the crime is adjudicated against the mukatab before incapacity, the lesser amount becomes a debt for which he is sold, according to the three imams.
Adjudication transfers the liability from the body to the value.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Zufar and an earlier opinion of Abu Yusuf hold that the kitaba prevents sale, so only value is owed without sale.
The dispute hinges on whether the kitaba is a stable or uncertain preventer.
4
Analyse the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof uses qiyas: the original rule is handing over; value only when impossible; the kitaba is uncertain, so transfer requires adjudication, analogous to an escaped slave.
The analogy to mudabbar is rejected because their kitaba is not subject to rescission.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The view of the three imams (debt and sale) is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
This is stated as 'عند علمائنا الثلاثة'.

It is said he becomes free if the remaining heirs set him free as long as the first does not retract. (Takmila al-Bahr al-Ra'iq 8:126). So when the effective cause for release becomes void, the effect also becomes void, unlike the manumission of all.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Mukatab deals with several rulings related to the death of the mukatab or his inability to pay, and the death of the master. The first ruling: If a slave commits a crime and the master later makes him a mukatab without knowing about the crime, and then the slave becomes incapable of paying the kitaba, the master has a choice: either hand over the slave to the victim's guardian or pay the blood money (the lesser of the slave's value or the blood money). This is because the master did not choose to ransom by making him a mukatab out of ignorance; and since the contract of kitaba is shaky (on the verge of being rescinded), the victim's right did not transfer from the slave's body to the value. Once the slave becomes incapable, the contract is rescinded, so the original remedy (pay or handover) applies. If the master knew of the crime when making the kitaba, he must ransom because he is considered to have chosen that option. The second ruling: If a mukatab commits a crime during the kitaba period and the crime is not adjudicated before he becomes incapable, the master again has the choice. Before incapacity, the mukatab owes the lesser of his value or the blood money because he cannot be handed over (kitaba prevents it) and he has right to his earnings. After incapacity, the rule reverts to that of a regular slave crime. The third ruling: If the crime is adjudicated against the mukatab before his incapacity, the lesser amount becomes a debt owed by the mukatab, for which he may be sold. This is the view of the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad). Zufar and an earlier opinion of Abu Yusuf hold that he owes his value and cannot be sold. The Hanafi proof: the original rule is handing over; resort to value only when impossible; here the preventer is uncertain due to possible rescission; so transfer from the original rule only by adjudication. This is analogous to an escaped slave who is not liable for value until adjudication. Fourth ruling: The death of the master does not dissolve the kitaba contract. It continues, and the mukatab pays the installments to the master's heirs. The heirs cannot demand immediate payment. Fifth ruling: If all the heirs set the mukatab free, he becomes free without paying the remaining price. This is based on istihsan (equity), treating it as a release from debt. They must free him in one session. Sixth ruling: If only some heirs free him, it is ineffective because they do not own him completely. The freeing by a minority cannot be treated as release or acknowledgement.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave who has entered a contract (kitāba) with his master to pay a sum in installments and thereby earn his freedom.
كتابة (kitāba): The contract of manumission by which a slave buys his freedom upon paying a fixed sum.
أرش (arsh): The blood money or compensation for injury, here specifically the lesser of the slave's value or the injury's blood money.

Continuation of Kitaba After Master's Death and Manumission by Heirs
Rulings on continuation of kitaba after master's death and manumission by heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the kitaba contract continues after the master's death and state the conditions for valid manumission by the heirs.

Core Idea
The kitaba outlives the master; only unanimous manumission by all heirs in one session grants freedom gratis.

The death of the master does not end the mukatab's contract; it continues with the heirs. The passage also details the rights of the heirs regarding manumission: if all free him together, he becomes free without paying, but partial freeing is void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on master's death
The kitaba is not dissolved by the master's death; it continues, and the mukatab pays the heirs on the same installments.
This is to protect the mukatab's right to freedom.
2
Understand the reason for non-dissolution
The kitaba is a right of the contract that does not transfer to the heirs' ownership; it remains under the master's ownership, so the master's death does not void it.
Analogous to tadbir and umm walad where the right continues.
3
Identify the condition for freeing by all heirs
If all heirs free the mukatab in one session, he becomes free gratis. This is based on istihsan, treating it as a release from debt.
In qiyas, he would not become free because the heirs do not own him.
4
Note the ruling on partial freeing
If only some heirs free him, the manumission is ineffective; he remains a mukatab, and the debt is not released.
The freeing cannot be treated as a release because it is not from all owners.
5
Apply the condition for freeing in one session
The freeing is only valid if done in one sitting; separate acts of manumission do not result in freedom.
This is a condition for the istihsan to operate.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Mukatab deals with several rulings related to the death of the mukatab or his inability to pay, and the death of the master. The first ruling: If a slave commits a crime and the master later makes him a mukatab without knowing about the crime, and then the slave becomes incapable of paying the kitaba, the master has a choice: either hand over the slave to the victim's guardian or pay the blood money (the lesser of the slave's value or the blood money). This is because the master did not choose to ransom by making him a mukatab out of ignorance; and since the contract of kitaba is shaky (on the verge of being rescinded), the victim's right did not transfer from the slave's body to the value. Once the slave becomes incapable, the contract is rescinded, so the original remedy (pay or handover) applies. If the master knew of the crime when making the kitaba, he must ransom because he is considered to have chosen that option. The second ruling: If a mukatab commits a crime during the kitaba period and the crime is not adjudicated before he becomes incapable, the master again has the choice. Before incapacity, the mukatab owes the lesser of his value or the blood money because he cannot be handed over (kitaba prevents it) and he has right to his earnings. After incapacity, the rule reverts to that of a regular slave crime. The third ruling: If the crime is adjudicated against the mukatab before his incapacity, the lesser amount becomes a debt owed by the mukatab, for which he may be sold. This is the view of the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad). Zufar and an earlier opinion of Abu Yusuf hold that he owes his value and cannot be sold. The Hanafi proof: the original rule is handing over; resort to value only when impossible; here the preventer is uncertain due to possible rescission; so transfer from the original rule only by adjudication. This is analogous to an escaped slave who is not liable for value until adjudication. Fourth ruling: The death of the master does not dissolve the kitaba contract. It continues, and the mukatab pays the installments to the master's heirs. The heirs cannot demand immediate payment. Fifth ruling: If all the heirs set the mukatab free, he becomes free without paying the remaining price. This is based on istihsan (equity), treating it as a release from debt. They must free him in one session. Sixth ruling: If only some heirs free him, it is ineffective because they do not own him completely. The freeing by a minority cannot be treated as release or acknowledgement.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave who has entered a contract (kitāba) with his master to pay a sum in installments and thereby earn his freedom.
كتابة (kitāba): The contract of manumission by which a slave buys his freedom upon paying a fixed sum.
أرش (arsh): The blood money or compensation for injury, here specifically the lesser of the slave's value or the injury's blood money.

Etiquette and Disliked Acts in Slaughter
Rulings on recommended and disliked acts in slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on recommended and disliked actions during slaughter and explain the legal effect of these actions on the meat's permissibility.

Core Idea
Disliked acts in slaughter cause extra pain but do not make the meat haram — only invalidating the proper cutting does.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq teaches us the etiquettes of slaughter commanded by the Prophet (ﷺ) and the actions that are disliked but still yield halal meat. The key distinction is between acts that are merely makruh and those that invalidate the slaughter entirely. We will examine the proof from hadith and the legal reasoning that extra pain does not negate halal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommended act and its proof
The text begins by stating that sharpening the blade is recommended (mandub). Find the hadith citation and understand how the command 'ليحد' indicates desirability.
The hadith: 'Indeed Allah has prescribed excellence in everything... let one of you sharpen his blade and give comfort to his animal.'
2
List the disliked acts and their rationale
The text lists several disliked acts: al-nakh' (reaching spinal cord), cutting the head, slaughter from the back, dragging the animal, skinning before the animal cools, and facing away from qiblah. Note that each is disliked due to causing extra pain.
The term 'al-nakh' has three interpretations, all disliked.
3
Analyze the tool types: sharp vs. blunt
The text divides tools into sharp (jāriḥa) and non-sharp. Sharp tools are of two types: sharp (ḥāddah) and blunt (kalilah). Slaughter with a sharp tool is permissible without dislike; with a blunt tool it is permissible but disliked due to delay causing extra pain.
This shows that the means of slaughter affect the ruling but not the permissibility of the meat.
4
Understand the key condition for slaughter from the back
Slaughter from the back of the neck is disliked but the meat is halal only if the animal remains alive until the veins are cut. If it dies before, the meat is not permissible because death occurred by non-slaughter means.
This is the only case in the passage where the meat becomes haram.
5
Summarize the underlying principle
All disliked actions are disliked because of extra pain (ziyādat al-alam). This does not render the meat haram. Only death before proper cutting (as in slaughter from back) invalidates.
The general rule: karāhah does not imply hurmah in slaughter.

Sunnah Methods of Slaughter and Ruling on the Fetus
Sunnah slaughter methods and intra-Hanafi fetus ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sunnah method of slaughter for camels, cows, and sheep, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the fetus of a slaughtered animal.

Core Idea
Sunnah slaughter matches animal anatomy; the fetus dispute turns on whether a hadith is literal or simile.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq covers two important rulings: the recommended (Sunnah) method of slaughtering different animals, and a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school about whether a fetus is rendered halal by the slaughter of its mother. Understanding these rulings requires clarity on the distinction between nahr and dhabh, and careful analysis of a key hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Sunnah methods
First, note that camels are to be slaughtered by nahr (cutting at the lower neck) while standing, and cows/sheep by dhabh (cutting under the jaw) while lying down. Long-necked animals like ostriches follow the camel's method.
The text also mentions that doing the opposite is disliked but permissible.
2
Understand the ruling on opposing methods
The text states that slaughtering a camel by dhabh or a cow/sheep by nahr is makruh tanzihi (disliked but not haram). This is because the purpose (blood flow) is achieved, but the Sunnah is abandoned.
Imam Malik's stricter view is mentioned but not adopted.
3
Recognize the dispute on the fetus
The second part introduces a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Abu Hanifa holds that the fetus is not made halal by the mother's slaughter; the Sahibayn say it is if fully formed.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with practical consequences.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa interprets the hadith 'ذكاة الجنين ذكاة أمه' as a simile (tashbih): the method of slaughter for the fetus is the same as for the mother, but the mother's slaughter does not cover the fetus. He also argues that the mother's death does not cause fetal blood to flow.
Zufar and Hasan ibn Ziyad agree with Abu Hanifa.
5
Compare with the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn take the hadith literally: the mother's slaughter suffices for the fetus. They are joined by the three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali). The mufta bihi in later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn for ease.

Timing of Udhiya and the Prayer Condition
Defines the time for udhiya and the ruling on slaughter before 'Id prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the time for udhiya, identify the condition of the 'Id prayer for city dwellers, and apply the legal trick (hila) to slaughter early.

Core Idea
Time for udhiya is three days, but city dweller must slaughter after 'Id prayer; others may slaughter at dawn.

In this lesson, we explore the timing of udhiya (sacrifice) as defined by the Hanafi school. The time begins at dawn on the 10th of Dhul Hijja and lasts for three days. A key condition is that city dwellers must slaughter after the 'Id prayer, based on a direct prophetic command. We also discuss a practical legal stratagem (hila) for those who wish to accelerate their sacrifice, and an istihsan ruling regarding prayer timing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time window for udhiya
The time begins at dawn (fajr) on the 10th of Dhul Hijja (Yawm al-Nahr) and ends at sunset of the 12th (the third day of Tashriq). This is based on the consensus of Companions.
Shafi'i holds four days; Hanafi three.
2
Note the condition for city dwellers
A person who resides in a city where 'Id prayer is held must slaughter after the 'Id prayer. Proof: hadith 'Whoever slaughters before the prayer, let him repeat his sacrifice.'
This condition is only for those upon whom 'Id prayer is obligatory.
3
Understand the proof (dalil)
The hadith is a direct command indicating that slaughter before prayer is invalid. Also, the Companions' statement that days of Nahr are three establishes the time period.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in practice.
4
Compare with other schools
Shafi'i adds a fourth day (13th). Malik and Ahmad require that city dwellers slaughter after the Imam's slaughter. Hanafi requires only after the prayer, not necessarily after the Imam.
The practical difference: in Hanafi, after the prayer is sufficient even if the Imam hasn't slaughtered.
5
Apply the legal stratagem (hila)
A city dweller who wants to slaughter early can send the animal to a place outside the city where a traveler would shorten prayer (approx. 48 miles). There, it can be slaughtered at dawn, as the condition of prayer does not apply to that location.
If the animal is in the city but the sacrificer is outside, the opposite ruling applies.

Age Requirements: Thani and Jadha' for Sacrifice
Rules on animal age for sacrifice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum age requirements for sacrificial animals (thani and jadha') and identify which animal types accept each category.

Core Idea
A thani is required for most animals; a jadha' (six-month sheep) is a valid exception for sheep only.

This passage establishes the basic age conditions for a valid udhiyyah. The thani (mature) animal is the default requirement across species, while the jadha' (young sheep) is accepted based on explicit prophetic permission. Understanding these age thresholds ensures that the sacrifice meets the sunnah criteria.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic rule
The text states 'جاز الثني من الكل' meaning the thani (mature animal) from all types (camel, cow, goat, sheep) is permissible. Determine the age for each species: thani from sheep/goat = 1 year, from cow = 2 years, from camel = 5 years.
Thani is the default minimum age for sacrifice.
2
Identify the exception
The text adds 'والجذع من الضأن' meaning the jadha' (young sheep) is permissible for sheep only. Note that jadha' is not accepted for goats or other species.
Sheep are an exception due to explicit hadith permission.
3
Understand the definition of jadha'
The commentary clarifies that jadha' from sheep is what has completed six months (majority view) or seven months (al-Za'farani). Know the difference between thani and jadha'.
The size condition: jadha' should be large enough to resemble a thani when mixed.
4
Apply the rulings
Given an animal species, determine if its age meets the thani or jadha' requirement. For sheep, both thani and jadha' are valid; for others, only thani.

Joint Ownership of a Sacrificial Animal: Conditions and Validity
Joint sacrifice rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for valid joint sacrifice and explain the rulings when a partner dies, is a non-Muslim, or intends only meat.

Core Idea
Joint sacrifice is valid only when all partners intend worship; any impurity of intent invalidates the entire sacrifice.

This passage addresses complex scenarios of seven partners sharing a camel or cow. It covers the consequences of a partner's death, the presence of a non-Muslim partner, and the effect of a partner intending mere meat. The underlying principle is that the shedding of blood must be solely for Allah, and the act cannot be partially devoted.

Guided Path
1
Scenario: Partner dies before slaughter
If one of seven partners dies and the heirs agree to sacrifice for all, it is valid. The sacrifice counts for the deceased because it is like charity, unlike manumission which creates wala'.
The permissibility relies on the heirs' consent and the intention of qurbah.
2
Scenario: Partner is non-Muslim or intends meat
If a partner is a non-Muslim or intends the meat for consumption rather than worship, the entire sacrifice of all partners is invalid. The reason: that partner's share is not qurbah, and the shedding of blood cannot be partially devoted.
Non-Muslims are not eligible to perform qurbah.
3
Scenario: Two persons sharing a cow/camel
Initially it seems invalid because each would own a half (3.5 shares out of 7), but the sounder view (al-aghall) is that it is valid because the half becomes qurbah through following the other's share.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by the tarjih in the text.
4
Understand the proof
The validity of sacrificing for the deceased is analogized to charity. The indivisibility of the act causes the impurity of one partner to spoil the whole.
Note the difference from manumission due to wala'.

Eating the Meat of Udhiyyah: Permissibility and Restrictions
Rules on eating sacrifice meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of eating from a voluntary sacrifice and explain the prohibition of eating from a vowed sacrifice.

Core Idea
Eating from a voluntary sacrifice is allowed; a vowed sacrifice must be entirely given in charity.

The passage clarifies the abrogation of the earlier prohibition on eating sacrifice meat after three days. It permits the sacrificer and others, rich or poor, to eat and store. However, this rule changes when the sacrifice is obligatory due to a vow (nadhr): then no one may eat from it, including the sacrificer, because it becomes like sadaqah.

Guided Path
1
General permissibility to eat
The text states the sacrificer eats from the meat, and it may be fed to the rich and stored. The hadith 'Eat, take provision, and store' abrogated the earlier prohibition of eating after three days.
This applies to both obligatory (required) and voluntary (sunnah) sacrifices, unless vowed.
2
The exception for vowed sacrifices
If the sacrifice is made obligatory by a vow (nadhr), the one who vowed cannot eat from it nor feed it to the rich. He must give it all in charity.
This is because it becomes sadaqah; the one who gives charity cannot benefit from it.
3
Penalty for eating from a vowed sacrifice
If the person eats from a vowed sacrifice, he must pay the value of what he ate.
The value is given in charity.

Rulings on Disposal of Hide and Slaughter by Owner and Kitabi
Matn and commentary on charity of hide, recommendation to slaughter oneself, and dislike of Kitabi slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the disposal of the sacrificial animal's hide, the recommendation for the owner to slaughter, and the ruling on Kitabi and night slaughter, and apply these rulings to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
The hide must be given in charity or turned into a useful item; the owner is recommended to slaughter; Kitabi slaughter is disliked but valid.

The Kanz al-Daqaiq commentary details the recommended practices (mandubat) of Udhiyyah. Beyond the basic obligation of sacrifice, there are important guidelines regarding the disposal of the hide, the person performing the slaughter, and the time. This lesson explores these rulings, clarifying what is recommended, what is disliked, and what is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling category
Recognize that the text discusses three distinct rulings: disposal of the hide, the person who slaughters, and the time. Each has a specific hukm level (mustahabb, makruh tanzihi, etc.) within the sunnah muakkadah framework of udhiyyah.
None of these rulings are fard or wajib; they pertain to recommended practice.
2
Locate the specific ruling on the hide
The text states 'he gives charity of its hide or makes from it something like a bag and sieve.' This means the hide must be disposed of charitably or converted into a useful object; it cannot be sold for personal use or given as wages to the butcher.
Selling the hide and donating the price is allowed, but selling for personal use is prohibited.
3
Identify the recommendation for the owner to slaughter
The text says 'it is recommended that he slaughter with his own hand if he knows that.' The evidence is the Prophet's action of slaughtering personally and his instruction to Fatima to witness her sacrifice.
If the owner cannot slaughter well, he should appoint another but witness the act.
4
Understand the ruling on Kitabi slaughter
The text says 'it is disliked that a Kitabi slaughter.' This is because sacrifice is a form of worship, and a Kitabi (Jew/Christian) is not of its people. However, if he is deputized, the slaughter is valid because he is qualified for dhabh. A Magian (fire-worshipper) is not qualified, so their slaughter is invalid.
The dislike is tanzihi (slight dislike), not tahrimi.
5
Note the additional ruling on night slaughter
The text concludes that slaughter at night is disliked (makruh tanzihi) due to the possibility of error in darkness, although it is permissible.
This ruling is mentioned in al-Fath from al-Qahistani.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the weft (luhma) determines the permissibility of mixed silk fabrics—pure silk weft is only allowed in war.

Core Idea
Visiting a sick dhimmi is permissible and a form of dawah; greet with 'wa alaykum' but do not initiate.

The Prophet himself visited a sick Jewish neighbor and called him to Islam, establishing a beautiful precedent for interfaith kindness. The Prohibition of Invoking by the Right of Creatures
Ruling on invoking by a creature's right in supplication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on saying 'bi-haqqi fulan' in supplication and explain the basis for the prohibition.

Core Idea
No creature has a right over Allah; invoking by a creature's right is impermissible.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq addresses a common practice in supplication: invoking Allah by the right or rank of a prophet, saint, or sacred object. The Hanafi school rules it impermissible, as it implies a right upon Allah. The text explores a minority view permitting it with interpretation, but the relied-upon position is prohibition based on precaution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is saying 'bi-haqqi fulan' (by the right of so-and-so) in supplication. The ruling is that it is impermissible (makruh tahrimi).
The text states 'la yajuz' (it is not permissible).
2
Understand the reason for prohibition
No creature has a right upon the Creator; Allah bestows mercy by His will, not out of obligation. The phrase implies a right that does not exist.
The text explicitly says 'la haqqa li-makhluq ala al-khaliq'.
3

The commentator in al-Shurunbulaliyya says the proof is a solitary report, so precaution dictates prohibition.
4

Since the evidence is a solitary report (khabar wahid) and zanni, precaution prefers prohibition to avoid implying a right upon Allah.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle of ihtiyat in matters of 'ibadat.

The Changing Ruling on Slave Women Traveling Without Mahram
Ruling on female slave and umm walad traveling without mahram, with later prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the original Hanafi ruling on female slaves traveling without a mahram and explain how societal corruption altered the fatwa to prohibition.

Core Idea
A female slave can originally travel without mahram, but widespread corruption changes the ruling to prohibition.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq addresses a nuanced fiqh issue: the traveling of female slaves (ama) and umm walad without a mahram. While the original ruling permits it based on the slave's legal status, later Hanafi scholars revised the fatwa due to changing social conditions. This lesson explores the original rationale and the principle of changing fatwa with changing times.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling and the types of slaves covered
The passage states that it is permissible for a female slave (ama) and a umm walad (slave mother of her master's child) to travel without a mahram. This also applies to mukataba (slave under contract of manumission), mudabbara (slave whose freedom is deferred), and a partially freed woman according to Imam Abu Hanifa.
The slave's legal status relaxes the mahram requirement.
2
Understand the legal basis: the slave is like a mahram
The ruling is based on the principle that a female slave is considered like a mahram for all men in matters of looking and touching. Since she is not subject to the same hijab rules as a free woman, she can travel with non-mahram men.
This analogy is key to the original permissibility.
3
Recognize the extension to other categories
The same ruling applies to umm walad, mukataba, mudabbara, and partially freed women because they all retain some degree of slavery. Imam Abu Hanifa analogizes the partially freed woman to a mukataba.
The common factor is the existence of slavery (al-riqq).
4

Limb Cut from a Live Animal: Hanafi Ruling and Exception
Explains the ruling on a limb cut off from a live hunted animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on a limb cut from a live animal, distinguishing between when the separated part is carrion and when the entire animal becomes halal due to the cut constituting dhabh.

Core Idea
A limb cut from a live animal is carrion unless the cut is so severe that survival is impossible, in which case the cut itself counts as slaughter.

The source passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a common hunting scenario: when a limb is severed from a live animal. The Hanafi school, supported by a hadith, rules that the severed limb is carrion. However, an important exception arises when the cut is so severe that the animal cannot survive—then the entire animal becomes halal. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this rule and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general ruling
The act is cutting a limb from a live animal. The general ruling according to the Hanafi school is that the cut limb is carrion (haram), based on the hadith: 'Whatever is cut from a live animal is carrion.'
This applies whether the animal later dies from the cut or not.
2
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that if the animal dies from the cut, the limb is halal because it is considered compulsory slaughter (dharurat). The Hanafi response distinguishes between optional slaughter (which kills immediately in ruling) and this case where the animal remains legally alive.
The key is the concept of 'hayy haqiqatan wa hukman' (alive in reality and in ruling).
3
Apply the Hanafi proof
The hadith is cited as dalil. The term 'alive' is absolute, covering both reality and legal construct. The remainder of the animal after the cut is considered alive in ruling because survival is plausible.
This is why the cut part is carrion—it was not removed by proper dhabh.
4
Identify the exception
If the cut is so severe that the remaining part cannot survive (e.g., splitting the animal in two, cutting off more than half the head, or cutting into three with the largest part towards the hindquarters), then the separated part is halal because the cut itself constitutes dhabh.
In such cases, the animal is 'alive in form only, not in ruling'—survival is not plausible.
5
Distinguish between ibanah and jarh
Complete separation (ibanah) of a limb usually makes it carrion. If the limb remains attached (jarh) and healing is plausible, it is not carrion. The ruling depends on whether the cut leaves the animal with a realistic chance of survival.
The passage also discusses striking the neck: if done from the front, it is halal but makruh; if from the back and death occurs before cutting the jugulars, it is not halal.

Ruling on Two Shooters Hunting the Same Game
Explains ownership and permissibility when two hunters shoot the same game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for determining ownership and permissibility when two hunters shoot the same game, and distinguish the view of Abu Yusuf from that of Muhammad.

Core Idea
If the first shot disables the game, it belongs to the first; otherwise belongs to the second.

When two hunters shoot the same animal, ownership depends on whether the first shot disabled the animal (ithkhan). If the animal could still escape, the second hunter's kill is valid and the meat is halal. But if the first shot disabled it, the animal is considered captured, and any subsequent kill by another makes it unlawful. This lesson explains the rule, its rationale, and an intra-madhhab difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the lifespan of the animal after a fatal wound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Distinguish between the first shot disabling the game (ithkhan) and not disabling it. In the first case, the game belongs to the first hunter; in the second, to the second.
Disability means the animal can be caught alive for voluntary slaughter.
2
Understand hizz al-imtina' (realm of resistance)
The animal is in hizz al-imtina' if it can still escape. In that state, compulsory slaughter (dharuri dhabh) through wounding is valid. If disabled, it exits this realm and voluntary slaughter becomes obligatory.
Compulsory slaughter is any wound that causes death; voluntary slaughter requires cutting the throat.
3
Apply the rule to the two scenarios
If first shot did not disable, second hunter's shot is a valid compulsory slaughter, making the game halal and his property. If first shot disabled, the first hunter is obliged to perform voluntary slaughter; if he does not and the second kills, the game becomes carrion (haram).
The second hunter becomes a killer of an already captured animal.
4
Consider the exception for a fatal first shot
If the first shot is so severe that the animal cannot survive (e.g., head severed), it is permissible even if the second kills it, because the remaining life is equivalent to that of a slaughtered animal.
This applies regardless of disability status.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
If the first shot is fatal but the animal lives for a day or less (more than a slaughtered animal), Abu Yusuf holds the second's kill is not haram, while Muhammad holds it is haram. The mufta bihi view tends towards the general rule: if first disables, second's kill is haram regardless.
The disagreement is about whether the animal is still considered in hizz al-imtina' after such a wound.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson addresses a key question in halal food: who may lawfully kill game? The Hanafi school sets a clear rule based on the hunter's qualification for slaughter (dhabh). Those who cannot perform voluntary slaughter cannot perform compulsory slaughter through hunting, except for the Kitabi (Jew or Christian), whose slaughter is valid.

The ruling is haram for game killed by the forbidden categories, and halal for game killed by a Kitabi.
