Valid Idafah: Contracts That May Be Added to Future Time
Listing of contracts valid when added to future time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which contracts are valid when added to a future time according to the Hanafi school and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Contracts that cannot be fully executed instantly (like ijarah, wakalah) are valid when added to future time.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between contracts that can and cannot be added to a future time (idafah). This lesson covers the first group: those that are valid. We will examine each type and the reasoning behind its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the valid contracts
List all contracts mentioned as valid when added: ijarah, faskh of ijarah, muzara'ah, mu'amalah, mudarabah, wakalah, kafalah, isa', wasiyyah, qada, imarah, talaq, itq, waqf.
Note that 'faskh' here refers to rescission of ijarah, not general termination.
2
Understand the common feature
These contracts either involve gradual benefit (ijarah), delegation (wakalah, qada), commitment (kafalah), future effect (wasiyyah), or unilateral action (talaq). None are immediate transfers of ownership.
Immediate tamlik is the key criterion for invalidity; its absence permits idafah.
3
Review the rationale for specific contracts
The commentary explains that mudarabah and wakalah are valid because they are 'from the category of authorization'; kafalah is a commitment; wasiyyah and isa' inherently apply after death; qada and imarah are appointments based on prophetic precedent; talaq, itq, and waqf are unilateral and can be deferred.
The hadith of Zayd ibn Harithah is cited for qada/imarah.
4
Note the difference between addition and condition
Addition must be explicit to a future time (e.g., 'from next month'), not conditional (e.g., 'if tomorrow comes'). The latter invalidates the contract.
This distinction is crucial for ijarah and talaq as per the commentary.

Commentary
This passage systematically lists which contracts may be added to a future time (idafah) and which may not. The Hanafi school distinguishes between contracts that are continuous or delegated (like ijarah, wakalah, qada) and those that are instant transfers of ownership (like sale, gift). The commentary provides the rationale: ijarah is valid because it comes into existence gradually; wakalah and mudarabah are forms of delegation; kafalah is a commitment; wasiyyah and qada are inherently future. In contrast, sale and its derivatives cannot be added because they are tamlik that can be done immediately. The passage also clarifies that for ijarah, the addition must be explicit (e.g., 'from next month'), while a conditional phrasing ('if tomorrow comes') is invalid. It also notes a difference among Hanafi scholars regarding the release of debt from kafalah.

Key Terms
مضافا (mudafan): A contract added to a future time, such that its effect starts at that future time.
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership; contracts that effect immediate transfer of ownership.
تولية (tawliyah): Delegation of authority; contracts like qada and imarah that involve appointment.

Invalid Idafah: Why Some Contracts Cannot Be Added
Listing of contracts invalid when added to future time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which contracts are invalid when added to a future time and explain why.

Core Idea
Contracts that are pure transfers of ownership (tamlik) cannot be added to future time because immediate execution is possible.

The second group of contracts are those that become invalid if added to a future time. This includes sale, gift, marriage, and others. The core reason is that these contracts involve tamlik that can be executed immediately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invalid contracts
List all contracts mentioned as invalid: bay' (sale), ijazatuhu (permission of sale by fuduli), faskhuhu (rescission of sale), qismah (division), shirkah (partnership), hibah (gift), nikah (marriage), raj'ah (revocable divorce return), sulh 'an mal (conciliation for property), ibra' al-dayn (release of debt).
Note that 'ibra' al-dayn' includes release of debt from kafalah, which is disputed.
2
Understand the common feature
All these contracts involve tamlik (ownership transfer) that can be done immediately. There is no need to add them to the future because they are possible now.
This is the opposite of the valid group where immediate tamlik is not possible.
3
Contrast with the valid group
Compare with ijarah (not tamlik), wakalah (delegation), wasiyyah (future), qada (appointment). The shared illa is the possibility of immediate tamlik.
The commentary uses this contrast explicitly.
4
Discuss the exception for ibra' from kafalah
Some Hanafi scholars allow ibra' (release) from kafalah to be added, though the majority position is that ibra' in general is invalid.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

Contracts Valid When Added to Future Time
Lists contracts valid/invalid when added to future time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate which Hanafi contracts are valid when added to a future time and state the underlying principle of tamlik vs. permission.

Core Idea
Contracts involving ownership transfer cannot be added to future time; those based on permission or delegation can.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi ruling: not all contracts can be tied to a future date. The passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq lists eleven contracts that are valid when added to a future time (mudaf) and nine that are not. The key is whether the contract involves immediate ownership transfer (tamlik) or mere permission/delegation. Understanding this distinction will sharpen your ability to classify contracts correctly and avoid common errors in transaction structuring.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the first sentence of the Arabic passage. It lists contracts that are valid 'mudafan' (added) and those that are not. Separate them mentally.
Valid: ijarah, mudarabah, wakalah, kafalah, isa', wasiyyah, qada', imarah, talaq, itq, waqf. Invalid: bay', its ratification, its rescission, division, partnership, gift, marriage, raj'a, sulh 'an mal, ibra' al-dayn.
2
Derive the criterion
Ask: What do the valid contracts have in common? They involve permission (itlaq), delegation, or effects that are inherently future (like wasiyyah after death). The invalid ones involve tamlik (ownership transfer) that can be executed immediately.
The commentary explicitly says: 'because they are tamlik and it is possible to execute them immediately, so no need for addition.'
3
Apply the criterion to each contract
For each contract in the valid list, confirm why it fits the permission/delegation category. For each invalid one, confirm why it is tamlik.
E.g., ijarah is usufruct, not ownership; talaq is an unilateral dissolution, not a transfer; waqf is a suspension of ownership.
4
Note the exception on ibra'
The passage adds a nuance: ibra' al-dayn (discharge of debt) is generally invalid when added, but ibra' from kafalah (suretyship) is valid according to some Hanafis. Recognise this as an internal ikhtilaf.
This shows that the criterion is applied with some flexibility for specific sub-types.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive listing from a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, detailing which contracts in Hanafi fiqh are valid when added to a future time (mudaf ila al-mustaqbal) and which are not. The author first enumerates the contracts that are valid with addition: ijarah (hire), mudarabah (sleeping partnership), wakalah (agency), kafalah (suretyship), isa' (appointment of executor), wasiyyah (bequest), qada' (judgeship), imarah (governorship), talaq (divorce), itq (emancipation), and waqf (endowment). Each is briefly explained: mudarabah and wakalah are valid because they involve mere authorization (itlaq); kafalah is an obligation of property from the outset, so it can be added and even suspended on a condition, but since it grants a right of claim, the condition must be customary, not absolute. Isa' and wasiyyah are inherently future-oriented as they apply after death. Qada' and imarah are pure delegation, so they can be suspended on conditions, citing the Prophet's succession order for commanders. Talaq, itq, and waqf are explicitly valid when added to a future time, with examples. The term 'mudafan' (added) is grammatically analyzed as a circumstantial adjective restricting all the mentioned contracts. Then the passage lists contracts that cannot be added: bay' (sale), its ratification by the owner after a fuduli sale, faskh (rescission), division (qisma), partnership (sharika), gift (hibah), marriage (nikah), return (raj'a), compromise for property (sulh 'an mal), and discharge of debt (ibra'). The reason is that these involve immediate transfer of ownership (tamlik) which can be executed presently, so addition is unnecessary. However, a nuance is noted: ibra' from a kafalah (discharge from suretyship) is valid when added according to some Hanafi scholars, so the prohibition on ibra' is specifically for normal debts. This differentiation is a key Hanafi principle: contracts that are mere permission or delegation can be added, while those that are ownership transfers cannot, unless the transfer is inherently impossible in the present (like wasiyyah).

Conditions for a Valid Muzara'ah Contract and Its Three Permissible Forms
Lists conditions of sharecropping and explains three permissible distributions of components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the essential conditions of muzara'ah and identify which of the three forms of distributing components is valid according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Muzara'ah is a lease (ijara) in origin, requiring specification of seed owner, type, share, land handover, and partnership in output.

Muzara'ah (sharecropping) is a common agricultural contract, but its validity depends on precise conditions. The Hanafi school treats it as a lease (ijara) initially, culminating in a partnership in the produce. This lesson unpacks the five essential conditions and three valid forms from the classical text, including the intra-madhhab difference on whether the contract's duration must be stated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is muzara'ah (sharecropping). The Hanafi ruling: it is valid (sahih) only if specific conditions are met.
2
List the essential conditions
From the text: (1) specification of seed owner (rabb al-badhr), (2) specification of seed type (jins al-badhr), (3) specification of the share of the party not providing seed (hazz al-ākhar), (4) handing over the land to the worker (takhliyah), (5) partnership in the produce (shirkah fī al-khārij). Duration must also be specified according to the mufta bihi.
Amount of seed need not be specified if the land's capacity is known.
3
Understand the proof (talil): analogy to ijara
The conditions are derived by analogy (qiyas) to a lease: each party's contribution determines what is being leased (land benefit or worker benefit). Thus, seed ownership defines the object of the lease.
The text uses examples like hiring a tailor to analogize the three forms.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on duration
Muhammad b. Salama transmitted that specifying duration is not required; the contract defaults to one year. However, the mufta bihi (relied-upon view) requires duration to be specified, as in al-Waqi'at, al-Durar, etc.
This ikhtilaf affects practice: modern fatwa generally requires duration.
5
Identify the three valid forms
The text provides three valid distributions of the four components (land, seed, oxen, work): (1) land + seed from one party, oxen + work from the other; (2) land from one party, the rest from the other; (3) work from one party, the rest from the other.
Each form corresponds to a different lease structure (leasing the worker, leasing the land, etc.)

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq with its commentary (sharh) from al-Fath and others explains the essential conditions for a valid muzara'ah contract in the Hanafi school. The text states that the contract must specify (1) the owner of the seed (rabb al-badhr), because the nature of the contract changes depending on who provides the seed—if the worker provides it, the contract is a lease of the land; if the landowner provides it, it is a lease of the worker's labor. (2) The type (jins) of seed must be known because it affects the wage and also because some seeds are more harmful to the land. The amount is not required if the land's capacity is known. (3) The share (hazz) of the party who does not provide the seed must be specified, as it is the consideration for his contribution. (4) The land must be handed over (takhliyah) to the worker so he can work without interference; if the landowner works alongside him, that defeats takhliyah and invalidates the contract. (5) There must be partnership in the output (shirkah fi al-kharij); specifying a fixed amount for either party invalidates the contract because it breaks the partnership. The text then explains the three valid forms of distributing the four components (land, seed, oxen, work). In the first form, the landowner provides land and seed, and the worker provides oxen and labor—this is a lease of the worker. In the second form, the landowner provides only land (or seed is from the landowner? Actually the text says 'seed owner' leases land) — the worker provides seed and labor? The text is somewhat garbled; the classical forms are: (a) land and seed from one party, oxen and work from the other; (b) land from one party, the rest from the other; (c) work from one party, the rest from the other. The commentary uses examples of hiring a tailor to analogize. There is also a disagreement about whether the duration must be specified; the mufta bihi requires it, though a rare transmission from Muhammad b. Salama does not.

Key Terms
المزارعة (al-muzara'ah): A contract where one party provides land and seed, the other provides work and oxen, and they share the produce.
رب البذر (rabb al-badhr): The owner of the seed; must be specified because it changes the nature of the contract.
حظ الآخر (hazz al-ākhar): The share of the party who does not provide the seed, which is the consideration for his contribution.
التخلية (al-takhliyah): Vacating or handing over the land to the worker so he can work without interference.
الشركة في الخارج (al-shirkah fī al-khārij): Partnership in the resulting produce; the contract is structured as ijara initially but becomes partnership at the end.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three invalid forms of muzara'ah and explain the reasoning based on the principle of taba'iyyah (subordination of benefits).

Core Idea
Sharecropping is invalid when the benefits of land, oxen, seed, and work are not compatible for subordination.

After learning the valid conditions of muzara'ah, we now examine the invalid forms. The key principle is that for a contract to be valid, the benefits of the contributed components must be compatible so that one can be subordinate to the other. When they are not, the contract is corrupted. This lesson covers the three invalid forms mentioned in al-Kanz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three invalid forms
List the three forms as given: (1) land and oxen from one party, seed and work from the other; (2) seed from one party, the rest from the other; (3) seed and oxen from one party, land and work from the other.
These are the three invalid forms from al-Kanz.
2
Analyze the first form
For form (1), the seed owner leased the land and stipulated the oxen on the landowner. The benefit of oxen (plowing) is different from the benefit of land (growing), so they cannot be subordinate to each other.
The condition of taba'iyyah requires unity of benefit.
3
Analyze the second form
For form (2), the worker is hired and cannot have the land subordinate to him because their benefits differ. It is analogous to the case where ox and land belong to one and the rest to another, which is also invalid.
The worker's benefit (labor) vs land's benefit (growing) are incompatible.
4
Analyze the third form
For form (3), both seed and oxen are from one party and land and work from the other. Since each alone (seed or oxen) would invalidate the contract when alone, combined it is even more clearly invalid.
The reasoning uses a fortiori argument (awlā).
5
Conclude the principle
The general principle is that the benefits of the components must be compatible for subordination; otherwise the contract is fasid.

The Additional Invalid Form and Logical Classification
Discusses an additional invalid form and provides a complete logical classification of all possible forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the form where oxen belong to one party and the rest to another is invalid, and describe Mulla Miskin's logical division of muzara'ah forms into seven types.

Core Idea
A form not in al-Kanz is also invalid; analogy cannot override the reliance on athar for the permissibility of muzara'ah.

The previous lesson covered the three invalid forms from al-Kanz. Now we examine an additional invalid form not explicitly mentioned there, and the Hanafi response to a proposed analogy in its favor. Finally, we study Mulla Miskin's comprehensive classification of all possible forms of sharecropping based on the four component parts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the additional form
The form is: oxen from one party, and all other components (land, seed, work) from the other party. This is not mentioned in al-Kanz but is discussed by commentators.
2
Analyze the proposed analogy
Some analogize this form to the valid form where only the worker contributes work (and the landowner provides everything else), or to the form where only the land is from one party and the rest from the other. They argue it should be permissible.
The proposed analogies are based on similarity: one component being from a party and the rest from the other.
3
Understand the Hanafi response
The response is that analogy (qiyas) would completely invalidate muzara'ah altogether, because the basic permissibility of muzara'ah is derived from tradition (athar), not qiyas. Therefore, we cannot use qiyas to extend to a case not covered by tradition.
This shows the limited role of qiyas in establishing foundational contracts.
4
Study Mulla Miskin's classification
Mulla Miskin states that sharecropping has four components: land, seed, work, oxen. By logical division, there are seven forms: four where one component belongs to one party and three where two components belong to one party. He lists which are valid and invalid.
Valid forms: (1) land from one, rest from other; (2) work from one, rest from other; (3) land and seed from one, rest from other. Invalid: all others.
5
Memorize the valid forms
From Mulla Miskin's classification, only three forms are valid: (a) one party provides land, the other provides everything else; (b) one party provides work, the other provides everything else; (c) one party provides land and seed, the other provides work and oxen.
These match the three valid forms from al-Kanz.

Invalid Distributions in Muzara'ah: When Factors Cannot Be Subsidiary
Explaining invalid factor combinations in muzara'ah and the subsidiarity rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three invalid forms of muzara'ah, explain the principle of subsidiarity (tab'iyyah), and apply the Hanafi rule that only forms supported by athar are valid.

Core Idea
Factors with different benefits cannot be subsidiary; only transmitted forms override the default qiyas prohibition.

In sharecropping (muzara'ah), the distribution of land, seed, bullocks, and labor determines validity. The Hanafi school restricts valid forms to those transmitted from the Companions. This lesson dissects the invalid combinations and the reasoning behind them, focusing on the principle that factors with distinct benefits cannot be made subsidiary to one another.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four factors of production
List the four components: land, seed, labor, and bullocks. These are the possible inputs in a muzara'ah contract.
Mulla Miskin categorizes seven possible distributions by rational division.
2
Classify the invalid forms described
From the passage, three specific distributions are explicitly called invalid: (1) land+bullocks from one party, seed+labor from the other; (2) seed from one party, the rest from the other; (3) seed+bullocks from one, the rest from the other. Understand the reason for each.
Also note a fourth form (bullocks from one, the rest from the other) discussed and rejected.
3
Apply the subsidiarity principle
For each invalid form, identify which factor is attempted to be made subsidiary (tabi'). The key rule: two factors with different benefits cannot be subsidiary. Bullocks (plowing) and land (growing) have different benefits, so they cannot be in the same party as subsidiary.
Similarly, land cannot be subsidiary to labor (worker is an employee, not a partner).
4
Recognize the role of athar over qiyas
Why are any forms allowed? Qiyas would prohibit all muzara'ah because the wage (share of produce) is unknown. The exception is based on transmitted practice (athar) from the Companions. Therefore, only forms explicitly found in the tradition are valid; analogy cannot extend beyond.
The fourth form (bullocks from one) is rejected because no athar supports it, despite apparent analogy with valid forms.
5
Summarize the valid vs. invalid distributions
From Mulla Miskin's seven-way division: valid forms are (a) land alone from one party, (b) labor alone from one party, and (c) land+seed from one party. All other distributions are invalid.
This is the mufta bihi position.

The Principle of Muzara'ah: Permissible Types and Their Proofs
Explaining the legal basis and rationale for permissible muzara'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of permissible muzara'ah according to the Hanafi school and explain why other sharecropping arrangements are invalid.

Core Idea
Only two types of muzara'ah are allowed: renting land or hiring a worker for a share of the produce.

Muzara'ah (sharecropping) is a common agricultural arrangement, but the Hanafi school strictly limits its valid forms to two specific contracts. This lesson unpacks the textual basis and reasoning behind these limits, drawn from Fath al-Qadir and the practice of the Companions. Understanding this principle will help you distinguish permissible muzara'ah from invalid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two permissible types
Read the text to identify the two contracts: (1) renting land for a share of its produce (seed on tenant), and (2) hiring a worker for a share of produce (seed on landowner).
2
Locate the textual evidence
Note the proofs: the practice of Abdullah ibn Umar for the first type, and the Khaybar treaty with Ali for the second type. Also, the continuous practice (ta'amul) of Muslims supports both.
3
Understand the qiyas objection
Realize that normally an unknown wage invalidates a contract. The share of produce is unknown at contract time, so qiyas would forbid both types. However, the text overrides qiyas for these two specific forms.
4
Apply the limitation
Any arrangement outside these two forms—e.g., renting a bullock for a share—remains subject to original qiyas and is invalid. The text makes an exception only for land and labor.

Invalid Stipulations and the Hila for Seed Removal in Muzara'ah
Listing invalid conditions and providing a legal device

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state four conditions that invalidate a muzara'ah contract and explain the Hanafi legal device (hila) that allows the seed owner to recover his seed through proportional shares.

Core Idea
Fixed stipulations cut partnership; use proportional shares based on typical yield to preserve validity.

In Hanafi muzara'ah, the contract is a partnership in produce. Any condition that assigns a fixed quantity to one party before division risks making that party take all if the yield is small. This lesson examines the four invalid stipulations, the hadith of Rafi' that supports the ruling, and the ingenious hila that allows seed removal through proportional sharing. We also cover the rulings on straw to complete the picture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Muzara'ah (sharecropping) is a contract of partnership. The ruling here is that certain conditions make the contract fasid (invalid).
The contract itself is not inherently invalid; the conditions spoil it.
2
List the four invalid conditions
The text lists: (1) stipulating fixed qafizes for one party, (2) stipulating that water from certain channels is for one party, (3) stipulating that the seed owner removes his seed off the top, (4) stipulating that a fixed land tax (kharaj tawzif) is deducted first before sharing.
Each condition introduces uncertainty that can cut partnership.
3
Understand the hadith proof
The hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij proves the invalidity of condition (2): the Prophet forbade a similar arrangement based on unpredictable water sources.
The hadith is narrated by Tahawi in Sharh Ma'ani al-Athar.
4
Learn the hila for seed removal
Instead of stipulating removal of the exact seed, the parties estimate the typical yield from that seed and reserve an equivalent proportion for the seed owner. Example: if seed is 1/10 of typical yield, the seed owner gets 1/10 of the total produce, and the rest is shared.
This is a proportional share (juz' shayi'), not a fixed amount.
5
Apply the rulings on straw
If they stipulate that straw goes to one party and grain to the other, it is invalid because the partnership is in grain. But if they split grain in halves and give straw to seed owner, or do not mention straw, it is valid.
Straw is a byproduct; its allocation must not defeat the partnership in the main produce.

A rental contract is for the benefit of the property; the lessee's sin does not transfer to the lessor.

Fiqh often confronts situations where a lawful contract may enable another's sin. Locate Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that the rental contract is for the benefit (manfa'ah) of the house itself. The rent becomes due upon delivery, and the lessee's personal sin does not transfer to the lessor. The contract is not inherently sinful.
This is a 'tālīl' argument: the legal cause (illah) of the contract is the benefit, not the use.
3
Locate the Sahibayn's dalil
The Sahibayn cite Quran 5:2: 'Do not cooperate in sin and aggression.' They argue that renting facilitates the sin, thus the lessor becomes a cooperator.
They view the contract as a means to a sinful end, which taints the contract itself.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a Hanafi following Abu Hanifa may rent without dislike; one following the Sahibayn would consider it makrūh. A mufti must choose which view to give as fatwa, often preferring the more cautious view of the Sahibayn.
The text does not explicitly state the mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often leans towards the Sahibayn's caution.

And renting a house: meaning it is permissible to rent a house to a disbeliever to take it as a place of worship or a fire temple for the Magians, or for wine to be sold therein in rural areas, and this is the statement of the Imam (Abu Hanifa). Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding renting a house to a non-Muslim for religious purposes (such as a church, temple, or place where wine is sold). Imam Abu Hanifa rules that such a rental is permissible. His reasoning is that the contract of rental (ijarah) is for the benefit of the property itself, and the rent becomes due upon delivery. The lessee's use does not transfer sin to the lessor. The lessee is a free agent choosing to commit sin. In contrast, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani) consider it makruh (disliked), citing the Quranic verse prohibiting cooperation in sin and aggression. They argue that renting facilitates the sin. The text also notes that the view of the two disciples is adopted by the other three imams (Shafi‘i, Malik, Ahmad). The relied-upon view in the Hanafi school may follow Abu Hanifa, but the later practice may lean towards caution.
