Executor's Rejection and Acceptance: When Can He Change His Mind?
Ruling on iterative rejection and acceptance of executorship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on an executor's later acceptance after initial rejection and explain the role of the judge in voiding the bequest.

Core Idea
Mere saying 'I do not accept' does not void executorship; only the judge's removal finalizes rejection.

This lesson examines a scenario where an executor initially refuses the role after the testator's death but later changes his mind. The Hanafi school balances the testator's interest against the executor's burden, allowing revocation of rejection until the judge intervenes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly happens when the executor says 'I do not accept' and then later accepts? The ruling is that the acceptance is valid if the judge has not removed him.
The testator is already deceased, so his interest is in the execution of his will.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is not from explicit text but from reasoning (dalil aqli): preventing harm to the deceased takes precedence over the executor's inconvenience.
The text states: 'because merely saying I do not accept does not void the bequest, as that would harm the deceased'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The harm of voiding the bequest is considered greater than the harm to the executor in remaining, especially since the executor's burden is compensated by reward.
This is an example of istihsan based on darar.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Zufar holds that rejection is valid immediately, so the judge may remove the executor after his refusal, unlike the Hanafi position.
The judge exercises ijtihad here; if he removes him, the acceptance is no longer valid.
5
Evaluate the mufta bihi
The relied-upon ruling is that acceptance is valid if the judge has not removed him, as per the majority Hanafi view.
This protects the testator's intent.

Commentary
This passage covers several rulings. First, if an executor initially refuses but later accepts before the judge removes him, his acceptance is valid. The judge's removal nullifies the bequest. Second, if a testator appoints a slave, disbeliever, or open sinner as executor, the judge replaces them – but there is a dispute: according to Al-Quduri the bequest is valid and then removed; Muhammad in al-Asl says it is void, though most interpret it as becoming void. If the disability (slavery, disbelief, minority) ends before removal, the person is not removed. Third, appointing one's own slave and minor heirs as executor is valid according to Abu Hanifa, but Abu Yusuf opposes, reasoning that a slave cannot have authority over the master. Abu Hanifa argues the slave is legally responsible and the minors lack disposition authority. Fourth, if an executor becomes incapable, the judge adds another or replaces him. Fifth, if two executors are appointed, they must act jointly; one acting alone is void. Abu Yusuf allows independent action if appointed separately.

Key Terms
بدل بغيرهم (baddala bi-ghairihim): The judge replaces a legally disqualified executor (slave, disbeliever, sinner) with another person.
ضم غيره إليه (damma ghayrahu ilayh): The judge adds another person to assist an executor who is incapable, rather than removing him.

Qualifications of Executors and Joint Action Requirement
Rulings on disqualification, replacement, and multiple executors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify disqualified persons, explain the dispute on appointing one's own slave, and state the rule on multiple executors acting jointly.

Core Idea
Slaves, disbelievers, and sinners are replaced by the judge; but appointing one's own slave for minor heirs is valid per Abu Hanifa.

This lesson covers who may be appointed as executor and who must be replaced. A key intra-Hanafi dispute concerns the appointment of one's own slave. Additionally, the passage addresses how incapacity is handled and the requirement that two executors act together.

Guided Path
1
List disqualified persons
The judge replaces a slave of the testator, a disbeliever, and an open sinner. There is a dispute: according to al-Quduri the bequest is valid but removed; according to Muhammad in al-Asl it is void (or becomes void).
If the disability ends before removal, the person is not removed.
2
Analyze the dispute on own slave
Abu Hanifa holds that appointing one's own slave as executor for minor heirs is valid. Abu Yusuf opposes, arguing slavery negates authority. Muhammad has two narrations.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the slave is responsible and the minors lack authority.
3
Understand the ruling on incapacity
If an executor becomes incapable, the judge adds another to assist, or replaces him if completely unable. The judge must verify the claim.
A capable and trustworthy executor cannot be removed by the judge.
4
Apply the joint action requirement
If two executors are appointed, they must act jointly; one acting alone is void. Abu Yusuf allows independent action if appointed separately.
The difference is based on whether they are appointed by one contract or separate contracts.

In Hanafi law, the role of an executor (wasi) is broader than in some other schools. The Rule and Exceptions for One of Two Executors Acting Alone
Outlines the general rule and exceptions for one executor acting without the other.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that one of two co-executors cannot act alone, identify the exceptions, and explain the preferred view on joint vs separate appointment.

Core Idea
One executor cannot act alone by consensus, except for urgent or mechanical tasks; separate appointment allows independent action.

When a testator appoints two executors, the default rule is that they must act together. However, Islamic law recognizes practical exceptions where delay would cause harm or where the act does not require personal judgment. This lesson examines the rule, its basis in consensus, and the range of exceptions, as well as an internal Hanafi debate about whether the rule applies when executors are appointed separately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its proof
The ruling is that one co-executor cannot act alone; the proof is consensus (ijma) as stated by al-Kaysani in the passage.
The text explicitly says 'فلا ينفرد أحدهما بالإجماع' (so one of them cannot act alone by consensus).
2
List the exceptions
The passage lists many exceptions: preparing the deceased, buying the shroud, urgent needs of minors, accepting gifts for them, returning a specific trust, paying a debt, executing a specific bequest, freeing a specific slave, and litigation concerning the deceased's rights.
These are all matters where acting alone is permitted.
3
Understand the legal basis for exceptions
Exceptions are allowed either due to urgency (e.g., preparing the deceased, because delay would cause deterioration) or because they are mechanical acts not requiring opinion (e.g., returning a specific trust, because it is simply handing over an identified item).
The commentary explains that these actions are 'not from the category of guardianship, but rather from the category of assistance.'
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on joint vs separate appointment
There is disagreement on whether the rule applies when the two executors are appointed separately (each by a separate contract). The preferred view (Abu al-Layth) is that it does not apply – each may act alone. Another view (Abu Bakr al-Iskaff) holds that the rule applies regardless.

The relied-upon position is that the rule of non-unilateral action applies only when the two executors are appointed in a single contract. If appointed separately, each may act independently. The invalidity of unilateral action is suspended pending the other's approval.
The passage also notes that 'what is meant by invalidity is that it is contingent on the approval of the other or his rejection.'

The Appointee of an Executor: Extending Authority to Both Estates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the executor's appointee, identify the Shafi'i counter-argument via qiyas to agency, and articulate the Hanafi rebuttal based on the original testator's implicit authorization.

Core Idea
An executor's appointee manages both estates—the testator's implied delegation authorizes the substitution.

This passage deals with the status of a person appointed by an executor after the executor's own death. The key question is whether this second appointee becomes executor only for the executor's estate or also for the original testator's estate. The Hanafi school affirms the broader scope, while the Shafi'i school limits it through an analogy to agency. Understanding this dispute requires analyzing the nature of executorship and the limits of implicit delegation.

What is the exact contested ruling? Does the person appointed by an executor also become executor for the original testator's estate?

The Hanafi school holds that the appointee is executor for both the executor's estate and the original testator's estate.
3

The Hanafi response is that executorship differs because the testator is dead and cannot appoint another, so implicit permission is necessary. The analogy to agency fails because the principal in agency is alive and can appoint directly.

Commentary
This passage addresses the status of a person appointed by an executor. The Hanafi ruling is that if an executor himself appoints another person to act as executor after his death, that appointee becomes an executor not only for the original executor's estate but also for the estate of the first testator. This is because the first testator, by appointing the original executor, implicitly authorized him to delegate the role. The testator is unable to attend to his own affairs after death, so the executor's appointment of a substitute is seen as continuing the original testator's intent. The Shafi'i school, however, disagrees: they draw an analogy to agency, where an agent cannot appoint a substitute unless explicitly permitted. According to them, the second appointee only handles the executor's estate, not the original testator's. Ahmad ibn Hanbal also held this view in one narration. The Hanafi response is that executorship differs from agency: the original testator (who is dead) cannot personally appoint a substitute, so the executor must have implicit authority to do so, whereas a principal in an agency contract is alive and can appoint another himself. Thus, the analogy fails. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is the relied-upon position.

Executor's Division of Estate: Validity and Consequences
Explains validity and effects of executor's division of estate with heirs and legatee.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid division of an estate by an executor between heirs and a legatee, and apply the Hanafi ruling to determine the consequences of loss or misallocation of the legatee's share.

Core Idea
The executor's division is valid only when representing heirs; acting for the legatee without authority makes the division non-binding.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq examines the legal framework for an executor dividing the deceased's estate between the heirs and a legatee. The core principle is that the executor acts as a representative of the heirs, not of the legatee. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining the validity of the division and the rights of the legatee if the property is lost. The passage also explores a classic case where a bequest for Hajj is made, revealing a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios of division
The passage describes two opposite scenarios: the executor dividing with the legatee on behalf of the heirs (valid), and the executor dividing with the heirs on behalf of the legatee (invalid). Recognize which party the executor represents in each case.
This distinction is the key to the entire ruling.
2
Understand the basis of the executor's authority
The executor is a successor (khalifa) of the deceased regarding the heirs, but not a general representative. He has authority to sell and preserve property for the heirs, but he does not represent the legatee without specific appointment.
The analogy of sale (qismah fi ma'na al-bay') is used to justify the executor's actions.
3
Analyze the consequences of loss in each scenario
If the division is valid (executor represents heirs) and the legatee's share is lost in the executor's hand, the legatee cannot claim from the heirs. If the division is invalid (executor represents legatee) and the share is lost, the legatee may claim one-third of what remains of the estate from the heirs, or hold liable the executor or heirs if they transgressed.
Note the distinction: invalid division does not transfer liability to the heirs if the property is in the executor's hand.
4
Examine the Hajj bequest case and the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The passage presents a specific case: a bequest for Hajj is made, the executor divides, and the allocated funds are lost. Abu Hanifa rules that Hajj is performed from one-third of the remaining estate. Abu Yusuf distinguishes based on whether the entire one-third was allocated. Muhammad says no Hajj at all. Understand the proofs each imam uses.
This ikhtilaf demonstrates different approaches to the purpose of division.
5
Consider the judge's division for an absent legatee
The judge can validly divide the estate on behalf of an absent legatee. If the property is lost in the judge's custody, the legatee has no recourse against the judge or the heirs.
The judge's authority differs from the executor's: the judge is a general supervisor for the incapable.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hawala acceptance requires clear benefit; if debtors are equal, it is not permissible.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses two guardianship scenarios: estate division leading to reclamation, and acceptance of debt transfer (hawala) for an orphan. Both rulings hinge on the guardian's duty to act in the minor's best interest. We'll unpack the legal reasoning and a key intra-Hanafi difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division reclamation scenario
Read the first part: executor divides estate, a slave falls to minor, executor sells it, price lost, then slave reclaimed. Note the two recourses: executor against minor's property, minor against other heirs.
This shows division is conditional on finality; reclamation invalidates it.

Permissible Price Discrepancy (Taghabun) in Executor's Sales
Ruling on permissible price discrepancy in executor's transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on taghabun yasir and ghaban fahish in executor's transactions and apply the distinction to a given case.

Core Idea
Slight price discrepancy is permissible; gross fraud invalidates due to lack of benefit.

When an executor sells or buys on behalf of an orphan, not every price difference is treated equally. The law balances the orphan's interest against practical reality. This lesson unpacks the standard of taghabun and the rationale behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the hukm: the executor's sale/buy is valid with slight discrepancy (taghabun yasir), invalid with gross fraud (ghaban fahish).
The key is the standard of 'what people tolerate'.
2
Understand the rationale
Why is slight discrepancy allowed? Because the executor cannot avoid it; requiring exact market price would block the door of executorship. Gross fraud is prohibited because it harms the orphan and guardianship demands benefit.
The proof is aqli (rational): based on the purpose of guardianship.
3
Distinguish between yasir and fahish
Taghabun yasir is the slight difference that market participants normally accept. Ghaban fahish is an excessive disparity that would be considered exploitation. The determination is by custom ('urf).
No fixed numerical threshold; it varies by time and place.

Executor's Sale for Absent Major and Real Estate for Debt
Ruling on sale for absent major and real estate for debt with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an executor may sell the property of an absent major, and apply the ruling on selling real estate to cover debts with awareness of the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
Executor may sell non-real estate for absent major; for real estate, sale is allowed only for debt—full estate if debt is total, partial according to Sahibayn.

What happens when an absent adult's property needs management? The executor steps in, but with limits. This lesson covers the scope of authority over movables vs real estate, and the dispute over selling all real estate for debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for absent major
The executor's sale on behalf of an absent major is valid for non-real estate (sawa' al-aqar). Rationale: the father has authority over non-real estate of the minor, so his executor inherits that authority.
For real estate, the father does not have authority, so the executor does not either unless for debt.
2
Examine real estate sale for debt
If the absent major has debt, the executor may sell real estate. Two scenarios: if the debt is comprehensive (mustaghriq), sell all by consensus; if not comprehensive, Sahibayn limit sale to the amount of debt, Abu Hanifa allows selling all.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: sale is by virtue of guardianship; if some is permitted, all is permitted.
3
Evaluate the dispute
Abu Hanifa sees the sale as a single delegation; Sahibayn see it as limited to necessity. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view according to later authorities.
Practical outcome: under mufta bihi, executor can sell entire property to satisfy even a small debt, but must act in best interest.

Executor Trading in Orphan's Property: Prohibition and Interpretation
Interpretation of prohibition on trading and dispute on profit disposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the apparent prohibition on trading and the correct interpretation that the executor may trade for the orphan but not for himself, and identify the profit distribution dispute.

Core Idea
The executor cannot trade for personal gain, but may trade for the orphan; profits from unauthorized trading are given in charity.

The matn says 'la yatajjar'—the executor does not trade. But later Hanafi works clarify this as a prohibition on self-dealing, not on benefitting the orphan. This lesson resolves the apparent contradiction and discusses the profit distribution dispute.

Guided Path
1
Read the matn and apparent meaning
The matn states 'wa la yatajjar' (and he does not trade). This seems to prohibit any trading by the executor with orphan property.
This would block many beneficial activities.
2
Identify the correct interpretation
Later scholars like Qadikhan explain the intended meaning: the executor does not trade for himself (li nafsihi) in the orphan's property. He may trade for the orphan.
The source text from Jami' al-Fusulayn supports this.
3
Understand the profit distribution dispute
If the executor trades (for himself) and profits: according to the Sahibayn, he guarantees the capital and gives the profit in charity; according to Abu Yusuf, the profit is his (no charity). The mufta bihi is that he gives profit in charity.
The executor also must guarantee capital if he trades for himself.
