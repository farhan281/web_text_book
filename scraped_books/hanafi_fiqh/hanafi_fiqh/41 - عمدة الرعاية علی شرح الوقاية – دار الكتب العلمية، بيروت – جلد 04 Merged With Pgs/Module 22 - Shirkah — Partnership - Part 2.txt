Invalidity of Partnership in Watering (Istisqa')
Explains invalidity of partnership in watering

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership in watering and explain why it is invalid due to the mubah nature of water.

Core Idea
A partnership over mubah property is void because ownership is not established.

Water from a river or well is ownerless (mubah) until someone physically acquires it. In this lesson, we examine a classical case from the Hanafi texts where two people attempt to form a partnership to draw and sell water. The ruling clarifies that such a partnership is invalid because the subject matter is not owned at the time of contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage discusses partnership for watering (istisqa'). The ruling is that such a partnership is void (batil). Identify what exact act is being ruled upon.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: water is mubah (ownerless). Since partnership requires owned property, the contract cannot be formed over mubah. Trace this reasoning.
This is a dalil aqli based on the definition of partnership.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: partnership is defined as a contract over owned property for profit. Water, being mubah, does not qualify as owned property until actual acquisition (ihraz). Therefore, any partnership to acquire mubah is invalid from its inception.
The condition of ownership at the time of contract is essential.
4
Note the consequence
Since the partnership is void, any earnings go to the worker alone, but he must pay the other partner ujrat al-mithl (hire-like compensation) for the use of his property (e.g., mule or water-skin).
This compensates for the loss of use, not a share of profit.

Termination of Contractual Partnership by Death and Apostasy
Lists causes of partnership termination and their legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the events that automatically terminate a contractual partnership and explain why knowledge of the other partner is not required.

Core Idea
Death of a partner ends the partnership immediately, as it automatically revokes the underlying agency.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the causes that invalidate a contractual partnership. The author covers death, apostasy, and also the effect of stipulating a fixed profit amount. The key principle is that contractual partnership depends on mutual agency and surety; when these are terminated by death or constructive death, the partnership ends without requiring the other partner's knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terminating events
The passage lists death of a partner and apostasy with a judge's ruling as events that invalidate (tabtul) the partnership. Also, stipulating a fixed profit amount for one partner makes the partnership corrupt (fasid).
Apostasy with joining dar al-harb is considered a constructive death.
2
Understand the legal basis
A contractual partnership inherently includes agency (wakalah) and, in mufawadah, surety (kafalah). These contracts are terminated by death automatically (constructive revocation). Thus, the partnership ends.
This is contrasted with partnership in ownership (shirkat al-milk), which continues after death.
3
Distinguish constructive vs. intentional revocation
Death is constructive revocation ('azl hukmi) — it takes effect immediately without the other partner's knowledge. Intentional revocation (e.g., dissolving the partnership) requires the partner's knowledge to be effective.
This is analogous to agency: the agent is not removed by the principal's revocation unless he knows, but death of the principal automatically ends the agency.
4
Apply the rule to apostasy
Apostasy with joining dar al-harb, when ruled by a judge, is a constructive death. Therefore, it also terminates the partnership immediately.
The passage references the chapter on apostates for this ruling.
5
Handle invalid profit stipulations
If a partner stipulates a fixed amount of profit for himself, the partnership becomes fasid (corrupt). The profit is then distributed according to capital shares, regardless of the condition.
This applies when capital is contributed by both sides. Other cases have different rules.

Dissolution of Contractual Partnership by Death or Apostasy
Ruling on dissolution of contractual partnership by death and apostasy

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Death and apostasy automatically end a contractual partnership; the surviving partner's ignorance does not change the ruling.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the invalidation of partnership. The key principle is that contractual partnership includes agency and suretyship, which end at death. Therefore, death (and apostasy by judgment) dissolves the partnership immediately, regardless of knowledge. This differs from intentional rescission which requires knowledge. Additionally, co-ownership (shirkat al-milk) is unaffected by death; property remains co-owned.

The passage lists two events: death of a partner and apostasy with joining Dar al-Harb if the judge rules accordingly.
Apostasy alone does not dissolve until a judgment is passed.

Contractual partnership includes agency and suretyship, which terminate with death. Ownership partnership is merely co-ownership and continues after death.

3
Understand legal dismissal vs. intentional dismissal.
Death and apostasy cause automatic dismissal (azl hukmi) without knowledge. Verbal dissolution (azl qasdi) requires the other partner's knowledge.
This parallels agency: an agent is not dismissed by the principal's word until he knows, but death instantly dismisses.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario.
If partner A dies while partner B is traveling, the partnership ends at the moment of A's death, not when B learns. Any profit after death belongs to A's heirs proportionally; B cannot continue the partnership without a new agreement.

Profit Distribution in Corrupt Partnerships
Ruling on profit distribution in corrupt partnerships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a profit-sharing condition in a partnership is invalid, profit is distributed according to capital shares, and identify the correct division of profit and wages in the three types of corrupt partnerships.

Core Idea
In a corrupt partnership, profit follows capital; the type of partnership (both capital, one capital, none) determines who gets profit and who gets wages.

This passage, continuing from the dissolution ruling, addresses corrupt partnerships. If the partners stipulate a fixed sum of profit for one, that condition is void; profit is split based on capital shares. The passage then details three scenarios: both contribute capital (profit per shares, no wage), neither contributes capital (profit for worker, wage for contributor), or only one contributes capital (profit for capital owner, wage for worker). This lesson focuses on these distribution rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invalid profit stipulation.
If the partners fix a lump sum of dirhams for one side, the condition is invalid. Profit must be proportionate to capital.
2
Apply the rule: profit is divided according to capital shares.
Even if they stipulated a different ratio, it is void; profit follows ownership of capital. Example: capital half-and-half, stipulation thirds: profit is half-and-half.
3
Distinguish the three types based on capital contribution.
The passage lists: 1) Both contribute capital: profit and loss shared per capital, no wage for worker. 2) Neither contributes capital (only labor): profit for the worker, the other gets wage of the like. 3) Only one contributes capital: profit for capital owner, the other gets wage of the like.
This is a key distinction for understanding who bears risk and who is compensated.
4
Apply to a given scenario.
For example, if one provides capital and the other works, profit goes to capital owner, worker gets market wage. This is like giving a donkey to someone to hire out: profit to owner, wage to worker.

Buying a Slave-Girl for Intercourse in a Mufawada Partnership
Ruling on a mufawada partner buying a slave-girl with permission for intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a mufawada partner purchasing a slave-girl with permission for intercourse and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Permission for a personal purpose in mufawada partnership can imply a gift, making the purchase exclusive to the buyer.

This passage addresses a nuanced situation in mufawada partnership: one partner buys a slave-girl with his partner's explicit permission to have intercourse. The dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples hinges on whether the permission implies a gift or gives the partner a right to recover half the price. Understanding this ruling clarifies how personal needs interact with partnership property in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
Recognize that the act is a mufawada partner buying a slave-girl with his partner's permission specifically for intercourse. The dispute is whether the buyer owns her exclusively or must compensate the partner.
The permission is key—without it, the purchase would be for the partnership.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the slave-girl is exclusively for the buyer without compensation. His proof: the permission for intercourse implies a gift (hibah) of the partner's share, so the buyer becomes sole owner.
The 'implied gift' (hibah bil-qarinah) is a Hanafi principle based on context.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the partner can recover half the price because the buyer used partnership funds for a personal need, analogous to buying food or clothing for oneself.
They analogize to essential personal needs that are automatically excluded from partnership.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to the Sahibayn
The response distinguishes essential needs (food) from non-essential ones (intercourse). Since intercourse is not automatically a partnership need, explicit permission for that purpose indicates a gift, not a loan.
Thus, the permission changes the nature of the transaction.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the buyer owns the slave-girl wholly; under the Sahibayn, he owes half the price to the partner. This affects how partners structure such purchases.
The later Hanafi fatwa follows Abu Hanifa's view.

Mufawad Purchases Slave-Girl with Permission for Intercourse
Intra-Hanafi dispute over ownership and reimbursement in mufawada purchase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions and explain the reasoning difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a mufawad partner's purchase of a slave-girl with permission for intercourse.

Core Idea
Permission for intercourse transforms a partnership purchase into a gift, making the buyer sole owner without compensation.

In mufawada partnership, each partner acts as agent and surety. Purchases normally become joint property. But when a partner buys a slave-girl with explicit permission to have intercourse, a specialized ruling applies. Abu Hanifa holds the buyer owns her alone, while the Sahibayn argue for reimbursement. This lesson dissects their proofs and the underlying principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling under dispute
Clearly state the scenario: a mufawad partner buys a slave-girl with his partner's explicit permission to have intercourse. What is the legal issue? Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn disagree on whether the buyer must compensate the partner.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa: the slave-girl is for the buyer alone with no compensation. His proof: the slave-girl initially enters the partnership, but the permission for intercourse necessitates a gift (hibah) of the partner's share.
Key: intercourse with a joint slave-girl is forbidden, so permission implies gift.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the partner has recourse against the buyer for half the price. Their proof: analogously to food purchases, the buyer paid a personal debt (his own share's price) from partnership funds, so the partner recovers half.
They use qiyas on the food exception.
4
Analyze the analogy to food purchase
Why does the food exception not apply here? Abu Hanifa distinguishes: food is a necessary need (luzum) and is excepted from partnership without condition. Intercourse is also a need but not necessary in the same way, so it requires explicit permission to be excepted. That permission triggers a gift, not a debt.
The gift reasoning overrides the food analogy.
5
Understand the gift reasoning and its implications
The permission for intercourse is construed as 'Buy the slave-girl for yourself; I gift my share.' This differs from a sale, which would create shared ownership again. The gift is complete, so the buyer owes nothing.
Practical result: no reimbursement, unlike food purchases.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is a commentary (sharh) on the text of al-Wiqayah, dealing with a specific case in the mufawada (general partnership). The ruling: If a mufawad partner buys a slave-girl with his partner's permission specifically to have intercourse with her, then according to Abu Hanifa, the slave-girl becomes the sole property of the buyer without any compensation to the partner. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that the partner can recover half the price from the buyer, because the buyer paid half of what was his personal debt (the price for his own share) from the partnership funds. The commentary then explains Abu Hanifa's reasoning: The slave-girl initially enters the partnership at the moment of purchase (since the mufawada partnership implies joint ownership in all purchases unless excepted). However, the permission for intercourse transforms the transaction into a gift of the partner's share to the buyer. This is because intercourse with a joint slave-girl is not permissible for either partner, so the only way to make it lawful is if the partner gifts his share. Consequently, the partner has no claim for reimbursement. The commentary contrasts this with the purchase of food or clothing, which are necessary needs and are excepted from the partnership by default (without explicit condition) because necessity makes them personal debts; thus, if bought from partnership funds, the partner recovers his share. The need for intercourse is also an original need but not as pressing as food, so it requires explicit permission to become a personal purchase. The text also clarifies that in a mufawada, each partner is liable for the price as principal or guarantor due to the kafalah inherent in the partnership. The commentary resolves a potential doubt: since the slave-girl entered the partnership, how can intercourse be lawful? The answer: the permission itself constitutes a gift of the partner's share, so at the moment of purchase the buyer becomes sole owner through that gift.

Making Intercourse Permissible in a Mufawada Partnership: The Gift Requirement
Explains the only way to permit intercourse with a slave-girl in mufawada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that intercourse with a slave-girl in a mufawada partnership is only lawful via a gift from the partner, and explain why a sale does not suffice.

Core Idea
In a mufawada partnership, intercourse with a jointly owned slave-girl is only lawful via a gift, not purchase.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in mufawada partnership where one partner purchases a slave-girl using partnership funds. The ruling distinguishes between the prohibition of intercourse with a jointly owned property and the necessity of a gift to transfer sole ownership. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the correct legal mechanism in such partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
Identify the specific act being ruled upon: intercourse (wat') with a slave-girl purchased in a mufawada partnership.
The act is initially prohibited due to joint ownership.
2
State the Ruling
State the Hanafi ruling: the only permissible method is a gift (hiba) from the partner to the buyer. A sale is not sufficient.
3
Understand the Proof
Understand why a sale fails: if the partner sells his share to the buyer, the share becomes jointly owned between the buyer and the seller (since the seller retains ownership in a new partnership), still prohibiting intercourse. Only a gift transfers full ownership without creating new joint ownership.
The proof is logical: the sale would restore the original joint ownership structure.
4
Apply the Consequences
If a gift is made, the buyer owes nothing extra. The seller can demand the full price from either partner due to the suretyship (kafalah) inherent in mufawada.
5
Compare with Exceptions
Note the exception for food and clothing: unlike the slave-girl, food and clothing purchased with partnership funds become the exclusive property of the buyer by necessity, and the partner can reclaim half the price from the buyer.
This exception highlights the stricter ruling for matters involving personal enjoyment like intercourse.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific ruling within the chapter on partnership (shirkah), focusing on the scenario of a mufawada partnership where one partner purchases a slave-girl using partnership funds. The text first states the ruling that the only way for the buyer to lawfully have intercourse with the slave-girl is through a gift (hiba) from the partner. The proof given is that if the partner sold his share to the buyer, the slave-girl would become jointly owned between the buyer and the seller (the partner), which does not resolve the prohibition. If it were established by purchase, the share would remain jointly owned, still preventing intercourse. Therefore, only a gift transfers the partner's share fully without creating joint ownership. Consequently, if the transaction is a gift, the buyer owes nothing extra to the partner. The text then explains the phrase 'And he takes each with its price' meaning the seller can demand the full price from either partner—the buyer originally, and the other partner by virtue of the suretyship that is inherent in a mufawada contract. This distinction highlights the principle that the ruling for slave-girls is stricter because of the prohibition of intercourse with jointly owned property.
