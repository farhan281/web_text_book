Assigning Punishments for Highway Robbery: Hanafi vs. Maliki
Commentary on the four punishment options and their assignment to crimes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the interpretation of 'aw' in the highway robbery verse and explain the thamarah al-khilaf with the Maliki school.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school assigns each highway robbery punishment to a specific crime—the Imam has no free choice.

In this passage, the commentator from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the Hanafi view on the four punishments for highway robbers. The key is the interpretation of the word 'aw' (or). While Imam Malik sees it as giving the Imam a free choice, the Hanafis read it as 'bal' (rather), which assigns each punishment to a particular type of crime based on the principle of proportionality. This lesson unpacks the proof and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual dispute
The Quranic verse (5:33) lists four punishments separated by 'aw'. The dispute is whether the Imam may choose any punishment or whether each is assigned to a specific crime.
The word 'aw' is the crux of the issue.
2
State the Hanafi position
Imam Abu Hanifa and his school hold that 'aw' here means 'bal' (rather), indicating disjunction and specific assignment, not free choice.
This is based on usul al-fiqh and linguistic analysis.
3
Examine the Hanafi dalil
The proof includes the Quranic context (the punishments match crimes in severity) and a hadith from Abu Burda that directly assigns each crime to a punishment.
The hadith is a clear textual evidence (dalil naqli).
4
Compare with the Maliki position
Imam Malik interprets 'aw' as genuine alternation, so the Imam may choose any punishment for any type of highway robbery.
This leads to a different application.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi law, the Imam must identify the crime type and apply the corresponding punishment. Under Maliki law, the Imam has discretion regardless of the crime.
Practical difference: e.g., a robber who only intimidates cannot be killed in Hanafi law but could be in Maliki.

Execution, Property, and Collective Liability in Highway Robbery
Detailed implementation of hadd: method, property forfeiture, and joint responsibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi method of executing the hadd for highway robbery, explain the ruling on property loss (daman), and apply the principle of collective liability.

Core Idea
The hadd cancels civil liability for lost property; collective liability binds all participants.

This passage details how the prescribed punishment (hadd) for highway robbery (hirabah) is carried out in the Hanafi school. It covers the method of execution by stabbing, the display of the body, the fate of stolen property, and the collective responsibility when one robber kills. Understanding these details is essential for correctly applying the hadd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the execution method
Note that the prescribed method is 'al-ba'j' – splitting the belly with a spear – and the body is left for three days. This is a form of mutilation, but the Hanafi school permits it as part of the divinely prescribed hadd.
Some may object on grounds of mutilation, but the commentator argues it is excepted from the prohibition because crucifixion is also legislated.
2
Understand the property ruling
If the stolen property is still extant, it must be returned to the owner. However, if it has perished, the robber is not required to pay compensation (daman) after the hadd has been applied, analogous to minor theft.
This reflects the principle that the hadd penalty extinguishes civil liability for the property.
3
Analyze the collective liability
If one of the robbers commits murder, the hadd applies to all participants, even if only one directly killed. This is because highway robbery inherently involves mutual support and assistance.
The commentary justifies this by the nature of hirabah, which is realized by some acting as supporters (ardaf).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on display duration
The apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) prescribes three days. Abu Yusuf held that the body should be left until it disintegrates as a stronger deterrent. The text prefers three days due to public harm from the smell.
This shows how later Hanafi authorities weight public interest (maslahah) against stricter deterrents.
5
Evaluate the proof for the hadd method
The commentator references the hadith of Abu Burda and general evidence for crucifixion. The method of stabbing is derived from the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The hadith is ahad in transmission but its meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalalah).
The dalil is considered zanni al-thubut but qat'i al-dalalah, meaning the ruling is wajib (not fard) due to the probabilistic transmission.

Hadd for Highway Robbery: Execution, Crucifixion, and Exceptions
Rulings on execution, crucifixion, and liability of highway robbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadd punishment for highway robbery in Hanafi fiqh, explain the imam's discretion among four methods, identify the conditions that drop the hadd, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on the duration of crucifixion display.

Core Idea
The hadd for highway robbery gives the imam discretion among execution methods, but specific conditions must be met for its application.

The punishment for highway robbery (muharaba) is one of the most severe hadd penalties. The Hanafi school derives the rulings from Quran 5:33, which prescribes execution, crucifixion, or cross-amputation. In this passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah, we examine the exact method of execution, the duration of displaying the body, and the important exceptions when the hadd is not applied. Understanding these details is crucial for correctly applying Islamic criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) is subject to a fixed hadd punishment. Determine the act and its legal category as a hadd offense.
The act must involve armed force or intimidation on a public road.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 5:33, which gives the imam a choice among execution, crucifixion, or cross-amputation. Identify how the passage references this verse implicitly.
The passage mentions 'crucifixion' as a legislated method, implying the Quranic basis.
3
Identify the imam's discretion
Abu Hanifa holds that the imam may choose among four options: amputation then killing, crucifixion, killing without amputation, or crucifixion without amputation. Understand the reasoning: the crime can be single or multiple.
This discretion is based on the verse's multiple punishments.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on crucifixion duration
The Zahir al-Riwayah position is three days; Abu Yusuf argues for leaving the body until it disintegrates. Evaluate the evidence for each view and identify the mufta bihi.
Harm to the public (smell) supports the three-day limit.
5
Recognize conditions that drop the hadd
The passage lists several cases where the hadd is not applied: if the robber repents before capture, if among them there is a minor or insane person, if a mahram is involved, if the robbery occurs within a city, or if the property is shared among the victims. Identify the principle behind these exemptions.
The concept of hirz (sanctuary) plays a role.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah discusses the punishment for highway robbers (muharibun). The text begins by stating that a convicted highway robber is to be executed by being pierced with a spear in his belly, and after death his body is displayed on a cross for three days. The commentator explains that the robber is not liable for any property he took that perished after his execution, analogizing this to the case of a thief in minor theft where the hadd punishment replaces liability. If one of the robbers directly kills someone, the hadd punishment applies to all the robbers because they share responsibility for the act of highway robbery (muharaba). Abu Hanifa holds that the imam has discretion in choosing the punishment: he may order crucifixion, killing by the sword, or amputation of the contrary hand and foot followed by crucifixion or killing. The passage then addresses the objection that piercing the belly is a form of mutilation (muthla) which is prohibited. The response is that this specific mutilation is an exception to the prohibition because crucifixion itself is legislated in the Quran and Sunnah. Regarding the duration of display, the apparent view (Zahir al-Riwayah) is three days, based on the harm that would be caused by a longer period. Abu Yusuf opines that the body should be left until it disintegrates as a stronger deterrent. The passage further clarifies that if the robber repents before capture, or if among the robbers there is a minor, an insane person, or a close relative (mahram) of the victim, or if the robbery is committed by one group against another, or in a city or between cities, then the hadd punishment is not applied. This is because the caravan is considered a sanctuary (hirz), and if the robbers themselves are part of that sanctuary (e.g., fellow travelers) or if the property is shared, the hadd falls. The overall principle is that the hadd punishment for highway robbery replaces both criminal and civil liability, so no compensation is due for wounds or lost property.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āyah, a commentary on Sharḥ al-Wiqāyah, discusses exceptions to the ḥadd punishment for highway robbery (qaṭʿ al-ṭarīq). It begins by stating that a stone and a stick are treated like a sword for the purpose of execution—this is because the punishment is a ḥadd, not qiṣāṣ, so it is carried out by the sword. Robbery within a city or between two cities does not constitute highway robbery in legal effect, as will be detailed later. In all these cases where ḥadd is not imposed, the perpetrators are not treated as highway robbers for punishment, but they still face ordinary criminal liability for killing, wounding, and taking property.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sariqa (Book of Theft), Bab Qat' al-Tariq (Chapter of Highway Robbery) is a commentary that treats several distinct legal scenarios connected by a single question: what happens when the hadd penalty for highway robbery is not imposed? The commentator (Sharh al-Wiqayah) and super-commentator (Umdat al-Ri'ayah) work together: the Sharh states the ruling and the Umdat refines it with proofs and intra-Hanafi disputes.

The passage opens by stating the general principle: in those scenarios where hadd is not obligatory, the wali (authorised guardian, whether the state authority or the victim's heir) has three options. If the killing was intentional ('amd), he may demand qisas (retributive execution). If the killing was unintentional (shibh 'amd or khata'), he takes diya (blood money). He may also simply forgive ('afw). This applies whether the robber took nothing or took less than nisab (the threshold for hadd). The reason given is that the Qur'anic verse on highway robbery (al-Ma'idah 5:33) specifies only four penalties corresponding to four crimes, and no fifth penalty is mentioned in either the Qur'an or hadith.

The first specific scenario where hadd is not imposed is tawba (repentance) before being captured. The Sharh quotes Qur'an 5:34: 'Except those who repent before you overpower them.' The exception is explicit that the hadd of highway robbery is not established if the robber repents before capture. This is a pure naqli proof.

The second scenario involves a group of highway robbers that includes a minor (sabi) or an insane person (majnun). Here we have an intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Yusuf holds that the sane adults who directly committed the crime are liable to hadd, while the minor and insane are exempt. His reasoning is that the direct perpetrator is the origin (asl) and any assistant is subordinate (tabi'); there is no defect in the act of a sane adult, so they are fully liable, and the defect in the subordinate does not affect them. Abu Hanifa, however, holds that no one in the group receives hadd, even if all were sane. His proof is that highway robbery is a single crime (jinaayah waahidah) committed by the entire group. If the act of some members is not legally effective (i.e., produces no hadd), then the act of the others becomes only part of the cause (ba'd al-'illah), so the full hadd cannot be established. The Sharh analogises (qiyas) this to the case of a mahram relative (dhi rahim muhrim) whose presence similarly prevents the hadd. The Umdat clarifies that if the non-sane are the direct perpetrators, all agree that hadd is not imposed.

The third scenario is when some members of a caravan commit robbery against others within the same caravan. The ruling is that this is not highway robbery because the entire caravan is considered a single secure place (harz waahid), like a single house. Therefore, the hadd for qat' al-tariq does not apply, and the matter goes to the wali.

The fourth scenario is highway robbery committed within a city (misr) or between two closely situated cities (like Kufa and Hira). The Sharh states that this is not considered highway robbery by ruling (hukman). The Umdat expands with the full discussion from al-Hidayah. By istihsan, the perpetrator is not a highway robber, even though by strict qiyas he would be (which is the position of al-Shafi'i). The rationale is that in a city or its immediate vicinity, help (ghawth) is normally expected to arrive quickly, so the robbery does not 'cut off' travelers in the sense intended by the law. Abu Yusuf has two narrations: one that hadd applies if outside but very close, and another that if weapons are used by day or night, they are considered highway robbers because weapons make help delayed. The Hanafi position is that the property is returned and the perpetrators are disciplined (ta'dib) and imprisoned, but the hadd penalty is not applied; if they kill, the matter goes to the guardians for qisas/diya.

The final note defines Hira as a city one mile from Kufa, and ghawth (help) is explained as meaning assistance, with a Persian gloss.

القود (al-qisas): Retributive punishment equal to the crime (qisas for intentional killing or wounding).
الأرش (al-arsh): Compensation for injury or loss; here includes diya for killing and payment for property damage.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sariqa (Book of Theft), Bab Qat' al-Tariq (Chapter of Highway Robbery) is a commentary that treats several distinct legal scenarios connected by a single question: what happens when the hadd penalty for highway robbery is not imposed? The commentator (Sharh al-Wiqayah) and super-commentator (Umdat al-Ri'ayah) work together: the Sharh states the ruling and the Umdat refines it with proofs and intra-Hanafi disputes.

The passage opens by stating the general principle: in those scenarios where hadd is not obligatory, the wali (authorised guardian, whether the state authority or the victim's heir) has three options. If the killing was intentional ('amd), he may demand qisas (retributive execution). If the killing was unintentional (shibh 'amd or khata'), he takes diya (blood money). He may also simply forgive ('afw). This applies whether the robber took nothing or took less than nisab (the threshold for hadd). The reason given is that the Qur'anic verse on highway robbery (al-Ma'idah 5:33) specifies only four penalties corresponding to four crimes, and no fifth penalty is mentioned in either the Qur'an or hadith.

The first specific scenario where hadd is not imposed is tawba (repentance) before being captured. The Sharh quotes Qur'an 5:34: 'Except those who repent before you overpower them.' The exception is explicit that the hadd of highway robbery is not established if the robber repents before capture. This is a pure naqli proof.

The second scenario involves a group of highway robbers that includes a minor (sabi) or an insane person (majnun). Here we have an intra-Hanafi dispute. Abu Yusuf holds that the sane adults who directly committed the crime are liable to hadd, while the minor and insane are exempt. His reasoning is that the direct perpetrator is the origin (asl) and any assistant is subordinate (tabi'); there is no defect in the act of a sane adult, so they are fully liable, and the defect in the subordinate does not affect them. Abu Hanifa, however, holds that no one in the group receives hadd, even if all were sane. His proof is that highway robbery is a single crime (jinaayah waahidah) committed by the entire group. If the act of some members is not legally effective (i.e., produces no hadd), then the act of the others becomes only part of the cause (ba'd al-'illah), so the full hadd cannot be established. The Sharh analogises (qiyas) this to the case of a mahram relative (dhi rahim muhrim) whose presence similarly prevents the hadd. The Umdat clarifies that if the non-sane are the direct perpetrators, all agree that hadd is not imposed.

The third scenario is when some members of a caravan commit robbery against others within the same caravan. The ruling is that this is not highway robbery because the entire caravan is considered a single secure place (harz waahid), like a single house. Therefore, the hadd for qat' al-tariq does not apply, and the matter goes to the wali.

The fourth scenario is highway robbery committed within a city (misr) or between two closely situated cities (like Kufa and Hira). The Sharh states that this is not considered highway robbery by ruling (hukman). The Umdat expands with the full discussion from al-Hidayah. By istihsan, the perpetrator is not a highway robber, even though by strict qiyas he would be (which is the position of al-Shafi'i). The rationale is that in a city or its immediate vicinity, help (ghawth) is normally expected to arrive quickly, so the robbery does not 'cut off' travelers in the sense intended by the law. Abu Yusuf has two narrations: one that hadd applies if outside but very close, and another that if weapons are used by day or night, they are considered highway robbers because weapons make help delayed. The Hanafi position is that the property is returned and the perpetrators are disciplined (ta'dib) and imprisoned, but the hadd penalty is not applied; if they kill, the matter goes to the guardians for qisas/diya.

The final note defines Hira as a city one mile from Kufa, and ghawth (help) is explained as meaning assistance, with a Persian gloss.

الغوث (al-ghawth): Help or assistance that is expected to arrive quickly, preventing the highway robbery from being effective.
المصر (al-misr): A city or large town; here used to denote a populated area where help is available.

Highway Robbery in Cities: A Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on qat' al-tariq in populated areas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position on when the hadd of highway robbery applies and explain the role of istihsan and ghawth in that ruling.

Core Idea
If help is likely to reach the victim, it is not highway robbery for hadd purposes.

The Hanafi school, through istihsan, restricts the harsh hadd penalty for highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) to situations where the victim is truly cut off from help. This lesson examines why robbery in a city or between close cities like Kufa and Hira is treated differently, contrasting the Hanafi view with that of Imam al-Shafi'i, who applies the same rule everywhere based on the literal act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Issue
Determine the precise legal question: does the hadd of highway robbery apply when the act occurs inside a city or between two nearby cities?
The text specifies cities close enough for help (ghawth) to reach quickly.
2
State the Hanafi Position and Its Basis
The Hanafi school says no; the culprit is not a 'qati' al-tariq' (highway robber) because the essence of the crime—cutting off passersby from aid—is absent. This ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas.
The availability of ghawth prevents the full hadd; instead, qisas, arsh, and ta'zir apply.
3
Present the Shafi'i Counter‑Argument
Imam al‑Shafi'i and the qiyas position hold that the act is highway robbery wherever it occurs, because the literal act of cutting the road exists.
This is the plain qiyas; it ignores the element of helplessness.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Rebuttal
The Hanafi response is that the 'reality' of cutting the road is to render travellers incapable of seeking help; this does not happen where help is at hand. The text then introduces nuances from Abu Yusuf regarding weapons and time of day.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al‑Khilaf)
Under the Shafi'i view, the hadd (amputation then crucifixion) is applied. Under the Hanafi view, the offender faces qisas, arsh, and discretionary punishment (ta'zir).
The difference is life‑changing for the accused.

Strangulation: Diyah or Qisas? Hanafi Distinction Based on Instrument
Ruling on diyah for strangulation and siyasa execution for repeat offenders

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on strangulation (diyah) and explain why the instrument (muthqal vs. muhaddad) determines whether it is 'amd or shibh 'amd.

Core Idea
Intention alone does not make killing 'amd; the instrument must be sharp and dissect.

Strangulation — squeezing the throat until death — is a real crime but its legal classification depends on more than just intent. The Hanafi school, led by Abu Hanifa, treats it as quasi-intentional (shibh 'amd) because the instrument is heavy, not sharp. The passage you are about to read lays out this core rule and then adds a policy dimension: habitual stranglers may be executed by the ruler as siyasa. We will work through the dispute with the Shafi'i school and even an internal dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the base ruling
The matn states: 'In strangulation is diyah, and whoever habitually does it, he is killed as a policy.' Locate this in the passage. Note that the first part gives the default penalty (diyah), and the second part is an exception for recidivists.
The ruling distinguishes between a single act and habitual behaviour.
2
Classify the instrument: muthqal vs. muhaddad
The passage explains that strangulation is killing with a heavy object (muthqal). Contrast this with a sharp weapon (muhaddad) that separates body parts. Abu Hanifa holds that only the latter yields 'amd (intentional) and qisas. The former yields shibh 'amd and diyah.
The key is the physical property of the instrument, not merely the intent to kill.
3
Trace the inter-madhhab dispute
The commentator notes that 'there is qisas in it according to other than Abu Hanifa,' i.e., al-Shafi'i. Al-Shafi'i considers any deliberate act causing death as 'amd regardless of instrument. The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi = diyah; Shafi'i = qisas.
This is the central ikhtilaf of the lesson.
4
Explore the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani depart from Abu Hanifa: if the instrument usually kills (even if not sharp), it is 'amd. The passage quotes al-Jurjani: 'if he intentionally strikes him with something that usually kills even if not sharp, it is also intentional.' This expands the scope of qisas.
The later Hanafi mufta bihi follows Abu Hanifa on this point, but the dispute shows the school's internal reasoning.
5
Understand the siyasa exception
The matn adds: 'whoever habitually does it, he is killed as a policy (siyasa).' The commentary says the ruler may execute such a person as ta'zir because he is spreading corruption. This is not a fixed hadd but a discretionary power to protect society.
This is an important bridge between fixed punishments and public policy.

Kitaba by the Son of an Apostate: Who Gets the Compensation?
Ruling on kitaba by son of apostate when father returns Muslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a son contracts kitaba with his apostate father's slave and the father returns Muslim, and explain the legal reasoning based on deputyship.

Core Idea
The son acts as the father's deputy; thus the kitaba's compensation and wala' belong to the father upon his return.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi fiqh involving apostasy, ownership, and the kitaba contract. When a father apostatizes and joins dar al-harb, his property is awarded to his son. If the son then makes a kitaba contract with the slave, and later the father returns as a Muslim, who receives the compensation and the wala'? The classical texts provide a clear ruling based on the principle of deputyship (khilafa). Understanding this ruling illuminates how Hanafi jurists treat retroactive ownership and agency in complex scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and sequence
Note the key events: the apostate father joins dar al-harb, a judge awards his slave to the son (due to legal death), the son contracts kitaba with the slave, and then the father returns as a Muslim.
The judge's ruling is based on the father's legal death, but the father's return revives him legally.
2
Understand the son's legal status
Recognize that the son is not the true owner of the slave; he is a deputy (khalifa) of the father. The father had empowered the son to manage his property, which is confirmed by the judge's award.
The son's authority comes from the father's implicit appointment and the judge's ruling.
3
Apply the principle of agency
When the father returns, the son's role becomes that of an agent (wakil). An agent's contracts are binding on the principal. Therefore, the kitaba contract and its effects (compensation and wala') revert to the father.
This is analogous to a principal ratifying an agent's earlier action.
4
Distinguish the case of prior payment
If the slave had already paid the kitaba sum before the father's return, the father does not get the compensation because the ownership had already transferred to the son through payment.
The ruling differs because the subject matter (the slave's freedom) is complete before the father's return.

Half Diyah When Amputee Loses Protection
Ruling on diyah when amputee apostatizes or joins Dar al-Harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of considering only the protected state of the limb when an injury leads to death after the victim loses protection (apostasy or joining Dar al-Harb) and calculate the diyah liability.

Core Idea
When a wound leads to death after the victim loses protection, only half diyah is owed for the injury on the protected limb.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario: a Muslim cuts off the hand of another Muslim, but before the wound heals, the victim apostatizes or joins Dar al-Harb and then dies from the cut. Intuitively, one might expect full diyah because the death resulted from the cut. However, the Hanafi school rules that only half diyah (the blood money for the hand) is owed, because the cutting occurred when the limb was protected, but the death occurred when the person was no longer protected. This lesson unpacks the proof and contrasts it with the standard rule of full diyah for injuries causing death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two distinct scenarios: (1) the amputee apostatizes and dies from the cut while still in apostasy; (2) the amputee joins Dar al-Harb and later returns as a Muslim but dies from the cut.
Both yield the same ruling.
2
Extract the ruling and its basis
The ruling: cutter owes half diyah from his own wealth. The proof: 'Because the cutting occurred on a protected limb, and the fatality occurred on an unprotected limb, so the cutting is considered, not the fatality.'
The rational proof (talil) hinges on the legal status of the victim at the time of each event.
3
Contrast with the Muslim victim case
If a Muslim cuts a Muslim's hand and the victim dies without losing protection, full diyah is owed due to sarayah (the injury spreads to death). Identify why the outcome differs: the victim's protected status remains throughout.
The key variable is ismah (inviolability).
4
Understand the special reasoning for the second scenario
For the case of joining Dar al-Harb, al-Hidayah adds that a judicial ruling of joining Dar al-Harb makes the victim legally dead, which severs the causal link (sarayah), and subsequent Islam creates a new protected life that does not revive the old injury.
This is a supplementary justification beyond the main proof.
5
Explain why liability is on the cutter's own wealth
The act of cutting is intentional (amd), and the aqila does not bear liability for intentional acts. Hence the diyah is from the cutter's personal property.
Contrast with accidental injury where aqila bears the burden.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the liability of a person who cuts off the hand of another, after which the amputee apostatizes and dies from the cut. It presents two scenarios: first, the apostate dies while still in apostasy; second, the amputee joins Dar al-Harb (enemy territory) and later returns as a Muslim but dies from the cut. In both cases, the cutter owes only half the blood money (دية اليد) for the hand, not the full blood money for the life. The proof is rational: the cutting occurred when the limb was protected (معصوم) because the person was a Muslim at that moment, but the death occurred when the limb was no longer protected due to apostasy or the legal death ruling upon joining Dar al-Harb. The fatality is attributed to the unprotected state, so the cutter is liable only for the injury on the protected limb. The payment is from the cutter's own wealth because it is an intentional act (عمد), and the 'aqila (tribe) does not bear liability for intentional acts. The commentary elaborates: if the amputation itself occurs after apostasy, there is no liability. Two points are clarified: the apostate's death must be directly from the cut without recovery; and if the cutting causes both an injury and death (like in the case of a Muslim victim), full blood money would be required due to fatality (سراية) because the person remained protected throughout. The commentator also notes that the proof regarding the 'unprotected limb' only directly applies to the first scenario; for the second (returning as Muslim and dying), al-Hidayah offers a different reasoning based on the legal death ruling upon joining Dar al-Harb, which severs the chain of fatality. The last point addresses why the blood money is not on the 'aqila: intentional acts incur liability on the individual.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A Muslim who has apostatized from Islam; here his blood and limb are no longer protected (غير معصوم).
سراية (sarāyah): The spreading of a wound or injury leading to death; the legal extension of liability from the initial injury to the resulting death.
دية (diyah): Financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs for homicide or bodily injury; here half diyah is for the hand (½ of full life diyah).
عاقلة (ʿāqilah): The 'tribe' or group of paternal relatives who bear blood money in cases of accidental killing; they do not bear intentional acts.

The Hanafi position rests on the minor's capacity: he knows good from bad, his declaration of faith reflects true belief or disbelief. Non-obligation does not prevent acceptance, analogous to a minor's prayer being accepted.
The text uses the analogy of prayer and Eid prayer for traveler to illustrate acceptance despite non-obligation.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf held that apostasy is not valid (since it is pure harm) but Islam is valid (pure benefit). The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) accept both symmetrically.
The mufta bihi is that both are valid.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi
The operative ruling: the apostasy of a discerning minor is legally recognized, he is compelled to return, but not killed. His Islam is also valid with full consequences (e.g., inheritance from Muslims, marriage restrictions).
This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the rulings of a discerning minor's (sabi mumayyiz) apostasy and Islam. It begins by stating the Hanafi position: his Islam is valid, he is forced to return to Islam if he apostatizes, but he is not killed if he refuses. The commentary explains the basis: the minor's apostasy is valid (hence he is compelled), but the death penalty is lifted for minors and women. The opposing view of al-Shafi'i and Zufar (and partially Abu Yusuf) is then detailed: they argue that apostasy is pure harm and therefore invalid for a minor who cannot choose harm, analogizing to invalid divorce and manumission. Abu Yusuf accepts the minor's Islam because it is pure benefit, while Shafi'i and Zufar reject even that due to the harmful consequences (e.g., inheritance disinheritance, marital separation). The Hanafi response is twofold. First, the minor's capacity for true belief and disbelief means his declarations should be accepted: he knows the difference between good and bad. Second, a rational argument: non-obligation does not prevent acceptance, analogous to a minor's prayer being accepted though not obligatory, or a traveler's Jumu'ah prayer. Then a textual proof (hadith) is cited: 'Ali ibn Abi Talib's acceptance of Islam as a child, which is well-established. The harm argument is rebutted by noting that Islam's primary purpose is benefit, and harms are merely incidental; similarly, apostasy's pure evil is accepted because evil is never excused. The commentary concludes with detailed references and a note that a further elaboration exists in the author's gloss on Al-Hidaya. Throughout, the Hanafi position maintains legal symmetry: since the minor can validly accept Islam, he can also validly reject it, and this is consistent with his developing legal personality.

Key Terms
صبي مميز (sabi mumayyiz): A child who has reached the age of discernment (around 7 years) and can understand the basics of belief and disbelief.
ارتداد (irtidad): Apostasy from Islam; a discerning minor's apostasy is considered valid in Hanafi law with the same consequences except the death penalty.
