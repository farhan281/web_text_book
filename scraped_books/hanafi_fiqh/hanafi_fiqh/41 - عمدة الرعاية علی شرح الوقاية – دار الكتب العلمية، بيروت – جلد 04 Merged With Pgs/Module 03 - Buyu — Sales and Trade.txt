Advancing Expiation Before Oath-Breaking: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on advancing kaffarah, with theoretical discussion on wujub vs wujub al-ada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on advancing expiation before oath-breaking, distinguish the Hanafi sabab-shart framework from the Shafi'i wujub/wujub al-ada distinction, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Expiation is invalid before the oath is broken; the breaking (hanth) is the cause, the oath only a condition.

This passage presents a clear inter-madhhab dispute: may one offer expiation for an oath before breaking it? The Hanafi school answers no, while the Shafi'i school permits it in financial expiations. The disagreement is rooted in how each school defines the cause of obligation (sabab) and whether a theoretical distinction between obligation itself (wujub) and obligation of performance (wujub al-ada) can justify advancing an act before its cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
Determine whether expiation (kaffarah) can be given before breaking the oath (hanth). The Hanafi position: not valid. The Shafi'i position: valid for financial expiations.
The dispute is over the timing of the expiation relative to the oath-breaking.
2
Trace the Hanafi dalil
The oath is sworn for sincerity (birr), as per Quran 5:89, and does not lead to expiation. The breaking (hanth) is the cause (sabab) of the obligation, while the oath is only a condition (shart). Expiation before hanth would be advancing an obligation before its cause, which is invalid by consensus.
Analogy with zakat: can give zakat before the year completes only after owning nisab (the cause), not before.
3
Identify the opposing school's dalil
The Shafi'i school allows advancement in financial expiations (e.g., freeing a slave, feeding, clothing) based on a distinction between the obligation itself (wujub) and the obligation of performance (wujub al-ada). They analogize to the price in a sale, which is due before delivery.
The Shafi'i view does not extend to bodily expiations like fasting, where they agree with Hanafis that advancement is invalid.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis reject the wujub/wujub al-ada distinction for expiation because the cause (hanth) is still required. For bodily acts, the distinction is impossible because the act itself is the performance. For financial acts, the cause must precede the obligation.
The author of al-Talwih explains that Shafi'is deny the distinction entirely, while some Hanafis deny it only in bodily acts.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a Shafi'i can free a slave or feed the poor immediately after swearing an oath, before any violation. A Hanafi must wait until the oath is broken.
This affects the validity of the expiation: Hanafis consider it invalid if offered early; Shafi'is consider it valid for financial types.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute over the permissibility of advancing expiation (kaffarah) before breaking an oath (hanth), followed by a theoretical discussion on the nature of obligation (wujub) versus obligation of performance (wujub al-ada). The Hanafi position, clearly stated, is that expiation cannot be offered before the oath is broken. The reasoning is that the oath is sworn for sincerity (birr), not for expiation; the cause (sabab) of the obligation of expiation is the act of breaking the oath (hanth), while the oath itself is merely a condition (shart). Therefore, advancing the expiation would be advancing an obligation before its cause, which is invalid by consensus. The text provides an analogy: just as zakat can be given before the year completes only after owning the nisab (the cause), similarly, expiation cannot precede hanth. The Shafi'i school disagrees specifically regarding financial expiations (like freeing a slave, feeding, clothing), arguing that a distinction can be made between the obligation itself (wujub) and the obligation of performance (wujub al-ada), so the expiation could be validly offered even if the cause has not yet occurred. The Hanafis respond that such a distinction is only possible in financial obligations, not in bodily acts of worship like fasting, because bodily acts are themselves the performance. The discussion then deepens on page 29 with a quotation from al-Taftazani's al-Talwih, explaining that Shafi'is generally do not differentiate between wujub and wujub al-ada, while some Hanafis deny the difference only in bodily acts. The author of al-Kashf attempts a technical distinction: wujub is the liability of the dhimma with the mental existence of the act, while wujub al-ada is the external realization. However, this is criticized as problematic because it implies liability of a heedless person. The author of the commentary (al-Sadr al-Shahid?) concludes that wujub is the liability of the dhimma, and wujub al-ada is the discharge of that liability. The practical conclusion is that for expiation, the Hanafi ruling stands: it must be after hanth.

Key Terms
الحنث (al-hanth): The act of breaking an oath intentionally, by doing what one swore not to do or vice versa.
الكفارة (al-kaffarah): A compensatory act, such as freeing a slave, fasting three days, or feeding ten poor persons, required for breaking a valid oath.
السبب (al-sabab): The cause that triggers the obligation of a ruling; here, the breaking of the oath (hanth) is the cause for expiation.
الوجوب ووجوب الأداء (al-wujub wa wujub al-ada): Wujub is the obligation itself (liability of the dhimma); wujub al-ada is the obligation to perform the act externally (discharge of the dhimma).

Vow of Hady from Wife's Spinning: Ownership Condition
Ruling on an oath to wear from wife's spinning as a hady

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for a vow of hady based on an oath to wear from wife's spinning, and apply the Hanafi rule that ownership at the time of oath is required.

Core Idea
A vow to give clothing as a hady only binds if the raw material was owned at the time of the oath.

Consider a weaver who says to his wife, 'If I wear from your spinning, then it is a hady (an offering to Mecca).' Later, he acquires cotton, she spins it, he weaves it, and wears the garment. Is he obligated to send it to Mecca? The Hanafi scholars (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) clarify that the vow depends on whether the husband owned the cotton at the moment of the oath. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the concept of 'cause of ownership', and the practical consequences for fulfilling such a vow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its object
The man says: 'If I wear from your spinning, it is a hady.' The object of the vow is the garment made from the wife's yarn. Note that the hady here means charity in Mecca.
2
State the condition for obligation
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), the hady is wajib only if the cotton (raw material) was owned by the husband at the time of the oath. Ownership at the time of wearing is irrelevant.
If he did not own it at the time of the oath, the vow is invalid because a vow must be on property or a cause of ownership.
3
Understand the reasoning (cause of ownership)
The text explains that the vow is only valid if it relates to owned property or a cause of ownership. The wife's spinning is not a cause of ownership for the husband; it is customary to spin his cotton. So it is as if he said 'from my cotton'.
The phrase 'cause of ownership' (سبب الملك) is key: the husband must have had ownership or a direct path to ownership at the time of the oath.
4
Distinguish between types of hady
If the vow is for a specific animal (sheep, camel), it must be slaughtered in the Haram and its value cannot be substituted. If it is for a garment, the garment itself or its value may be given as charity in Mecca.
Immovable property vowed as hady is fulfilled by donating its value.

Oath to Repay Debt: Payment with Defective Coins Does Not Fulfill
Ruling on oath fulfillment with coin quality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which forms of payment do not fulfill an oath to repay a debt according to Hanafi fiqh, and explain why defective or counterfeit coins are invalid.

Core Idea
An oath to repay a debt is only fulfilled by paying with sound currency; defective, counterfeit, or claimed coins do not suffice.

When someone takes an oath to repay a debt, the repayment must be in full with acceptable currency. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on paying with coins that are deficient in quality, fraudulent, or disputed in ownership. We will explore the definitions of key terms and understand why such payments fail to fulfill the oath.

Guided Path

Do Prohibited Acts Produce Legal Effects? The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on legal effects of prohibited acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that prohibited physical acts can produce legal effects and apply the distinction between qabīḥ li-‘aynihi and qabīḥ li-ghayrihi to examples.

Core Idea
A prohibited act can still produce a valid legal effect if the prohibition is due to an external reason, not inherent evil.

This lesson tackles a foundational usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi‘i schools: can a sinful, prohibited act nevertheless generate a valid legal consequence? The Shafi‘is argue that prohibition makes the act inherently ugly (qabīḥ li-‘aynihi), so no beneficial ruling can follow. The Hanafis counter that the ugliness is external (qabīḥ li-ghayrihi), allowing the act's essence to produce its normal legal effects. This disagreement has real-world implications across marriage, property, and worship.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi claim: prohibited physical acts (e.g., zinā, ghaṣb, sinful travel) still produce their usual legal effects (e.g., muṣāharah, ownership, rukhsah) because the prohibition is not tied to the act's essence but to an external description.
The effect attaches despite the sinfulness of the act.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Shafi‘i school holds that prohibition of a physical act renders it qabīḥ li-‘aynihi (inherently ugly), and such an act cannot serve as a basis for a beneficial legal ruling. They cite consensus that the inherently ugly cannot produce a legal effect.
Imam al-Shafi‘i is the explicit opponent in the text.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponent argues: if the act is prohibited, its ugliness is inherent (li-‘aynihi); the inherently ugly cannot yield a legal benefit (like ownership or affinity). Therefore, zinā cannot create muṣāharah, ghaṣb cannot give ownership, and a sinful journey cannot grant concessions.
Their proof relies on a consensus (ijmā‘) that the inherently ugly yields no ruling.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal: the ugliness is not for the act itself but for an external reason (li-ghayrihi). For example, zinā is prohibited but it causes a child, which is the basis for muṣāharah. Ghaṣb is prohibited but ownership via ḍamān is necessary to avoid combining substitute and original. The sinful traveler still gets concessions because the journey is a valid cause; sin is an external consideration.
The key is the difference between the act's essence (dhāt) and its description (waṣf).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequences: (1) In Hanafi, a child born of zinā establishes muṣāharah (prohibition of marriage to relatives); in Shafi‘i, it does not. (2) A usurper (ghāṣib) can own the usurped property by paying ḍamān in Hanafi; in Shafi‘i, he cannot. (3) A traveller on a sinful journey may shorten prayer and break fast in Hanafi; in Shafi‘i, he may not.
These differences affect everyday fiqh rulings.

Maximum Land Tax and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Increase
Ruling on maximum land tax and intra-Hanafi dispute over increase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the maximum land tax and state the intra-madhhab dispute regarding increasing the tax when land becomes more productive, identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Half of the produce is the maximum land tax; once set, it cannot be increased even if the land becomes more productive.

This lesson examines a key ruling in Hanafi fiqh concerning land tax (kharaj). While the maximum is fixed at half the produce, the question of adjustment arises when land productivity changes. The passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf over whether the tax can be increased when the land becomes more fertile. The relied-upon position favours stability, following the practice of Caliph Umar (ra).

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed ruling
The passage states that half of the produce is the utmost capacity (ghayah al-taqah). This is the maximum tax that can be imposed. Both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad agree on this limit. Additionally, if the land cannot bear the assessed tax in a given year, the tax must be reduced.
The half limit is based on the practice of Umar and his companions, considered a Sunnah.
2
Identify the point of dispute
The dispute is whether the tax can be increased if the land's productivity increases after the initial assessment. Muhammad allows it; Abu Yusuf prohibits it.
Both accept reduction; the disagreement is specifically on increase.
3
State Muhammad's position and dalil
Muhammad argues that increase is permissible because the tax is based on capacity (taqah). Since reduction is allowed when capacity decreases, increase should be allowed when capacity increases. This is a rational analogy (qiyas).
Muhammad's reasoning is that consideration is given to capacity; it should rise and fall accordingly.
4
State Abu Yusuf's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf argues that increase is not permissible, based on the established practice of Caliph Umar. When Umar was informed that the land had extra capacity, he did not increase the tax. This is a textual proof (athar).
The hadith of Umar is recorded by al-Bukhari and Abd al-Razzaq, indicating that once set, the tax remains even if capacity rises.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi scholars (e.g., in al-Kafi) have preferred the view of Abu Yusuf (and a narration from Abu Hanifa), stating that increase is not permissible. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin, and the tarjih is based on the textual proof from the Caliph's practice.

And half of the produce is the utmost capacity, and it is decreased if [the land] cannot bear its tax, and it is not increased if it becomes able according to Abu Yusuf, but it is permissible according to Muhammad. [1] His statement: 'and half' is a subject with the predicate 'the utmost capacity'; its summary is that the limit of the land's capacity is that the obligatory [tax] is half of what the land produces. So it is permissible to assess less than it, like a quarter, a fifth, and the like, and it is not permissible to increase beyond half, neither in proportional land tax nor in fixed land tax, just as it is not permissible to increase beyond the amount established from Umar and his companions for the jarib of vineyards and others. This is in al-Bahr. [2] His statement: 'and it is decreased' means: if a tax is assessed on land and it cannot bear it in a certain year due to scarcity of its yield, then the tax is decreased. This is indicated by the statement of Umar to Hudhayfah and Uthman ibn Hunayf, when he had sent them to survey the lands of Iraq and assess the land tax on them: 'How did you do? Do you fear that you have burdened the land with what it cannot bear?' They said: 'We have burdened it with a matter it can bear, with no great surplus in it.' He said: 'See that you have not burdened it with what it cannot bear.' They said: 'No.' This was recorded by al-Bukhari and others. [3] His statement: 'and it is not increased' means: if a tax is assessed on land according to its capacity, then its capacity increases due to increase in its produce, then according to Muhammad, increasing the tax is permissible just as decreasing is permissible, because consideration is given to capacity; so with its increase the tax is increased, and with its decrease it is decreased. According to Abu Yusuf, it is not increased because it is established from Umar that when he was informed of an increase in capacity, he did not increase [the tax] — as recorded by Abd al-Razzaq and others.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the adjustment of land tax (kharaj) when the land's productivity changes. The ruling is that the maximum tax cannot exceed half of the produce. It may be reduced if the land cannot bear the tax in a given year. The point of contention is whether the tax can be increased if the land becomes more productive. According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, increase is allowed because the tax is based on capacity, so it should rise and fall with capacity. He uses a rational argument: since the tax is set according to ability, when ability increases, the tax should increase accordingly. Abu Yusuf, however, argues that increase is not allowed, relying on the practice of Caliph Umar, who refused to increase the tax even when told of increased productivity. The relied-upon position (al-sahih) is the view of Abu Yusuf (and Imam Abu Hanifa in one narration) that increase is not permissible, as mentioned in al-Kafi. The passage also includes the textual proof from the hadith of Umar, showing that the initial assessment must be fair and not oppressive, and that reduction is mandatory if the land cannot bear the tax.

Key Terms
خراج (kharaj): Land tax imposed on conquered lands, paid by the cultivator, either as a fixed amount (kharaj muwazzaf) or as a share of produce (kharaj muqasamah).
طاقة (taqah): The capacity of the land to bear the tax, determined by its average produce. The maximum is half of the produce.
وظيفة (wazifah): The assessed tax amount imposed on the land, whether fixed or proportional.

Selling Weapons to People of Fitna: Ruling and Reasoning
Ruling on selling weapons to rebels and criminals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling weapons to people of fitna and explain why the prohibition is makruh tahrimi based on the principle of aiding sin.

Core Idea
Selling weapons to known rebels is makruh tahrimi because it directly aids sin.

In this lesson, we examine a specific fiqh ruling concerning sales that may facilitate wrongdoing. The classical Hanafi texts address whether a Muslim may sell weapons to those engaged in corruption or rebellion. The ruling hinges on the seller's knowledge and the principle that aiding sin is prohibited. We will explore the text, its commentary, and the analogies drawn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
State the act (selling weapons) and the hukm level (makruh) with the condition that the seller knows the buyer is from ahl al-fitnah.
The matn says: 'if he knows that he is from the people of fitna, it is disliked, otherwise not.'
2
Understand the condition of knowledge
The ruling is contingent on the seller's certainty. Without knowledge, no dislike applies because aiding sin is absent.
The commentary explicitly notes 'if he does not know... selling weapons is not disliked.'
3
Examine the expansion to other transfers
The same ruling applies to gifts, sales on credit, or any means of ownership. The prohibition is not limited to sales.
The text says 'likewise giving it as a gift and all other means of ownership.'
4
Analyze the analogy with raw materials
Selling raw materials like iron is not disliked, just as selling wood for musical instruments is not disliked. The sin is not directly committed with the raw material.
This is compared to selling musical instruments (makruh) vs. selling wood (not makruh).
5
Grasp the proof: aiding sin
The karahah is tahrimi because the act aids sin. The illa (effective cause) is direct assistance to crime.
Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq confirms the karahah is tahrimi due to reasoning of I'aanah ala al-Ma'siyah.

The Reward for Returning a Pawned Runaway Slave
Explaining the division of reward between pledgee and pawner when a pawned slave is returned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the pledgee's and pawner's liability for the reward (ja'l) when a pawned runaway slave is returned, and apply the proportional division rule.

Core Idea
The reward is divided proportionally: the pledgee bears a share equal to the debt-to-value ratio.

This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqayah explains a nuanced financial rule regarding pawned property. The reward (ja'l) for returning a runaway pawned slave is not automatically the sole responsibility of the pledgee. Instead, the shared liability reflects each party's interest in the slave's value. Let's walk through the analytical steps to understand the division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the return of a pawned runaway slave. The ruling is that a reward (ja'l) is due to the returner, and the liability for that reward is shared between pledgee and pawner based on the slave's value relative to the debt.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the rationale (talil) from al-Hidayah: the returner 'revived the monetary value' of the slave. The reward compensates for that revival, and the pledgee's right is limited to the amount of the debt.
The key phrase: 'al-istifa' wa al-ja'l bi-muqabala ihya' al-maliyyah'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reward corresponds exactly to the revived value. Since the pledgee only has a claim up to the debt amount, his share of the reward is capped proportionally. Any excess value belongs to the pawner, so he bears the remaining reward.
The analogy to 'price of medicine' and 'ransom from crime' shows the same proportional logic.
4
Analyze the expenses treatment
Expenses incurred on the escaping slave (e.g., food, shelter) are treated like those of a found object (luqatah): voluntary unless authorized by a judge. If the finder spends without court order, it is a donation; with court order and condition of reimbursement, he can recover from the owner.
This rule ensures that unapproved spending does not create unintended liability.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete case
Take a slave worth 200 dirhams pawned for a debt of 100 dirhams. If a reward of 10 dirhams is paid to the returner, the pledgee owes 5 dirhams (100/200 × 10) and the pawner owes 5 dirhams. If the slave's value equals the debt, the pledgee owes the entire reward.
The division is mathematical: pledgee's share = (debt / value) × total reward.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary on the ruling regarding the reward (ja'l) given for returning a pawned runaway slave. The commentator explains and expands the text of al-Wiqayah. The ruling distinguishes three scenarios: (1) If the slave's value is equal to or less than the debt owed to the pledgee (murtahin), the entire reward is borne by the pledgee because the benefit of the slave's value is entirely his for recovering the debt. (2) If the slave's value exceeds the debt, then the pledgee bears only the portion of the reward proportionate to the debt, and the pawner (rahin) bears the remainder, since the pawner's interest in the excess value is protected. The reward is compensation for 'reviving' the slave's monetary value by returning him, as stated in al-Hidayah: the pledgee's right is to the extent of the guaranteed amount. (3) The expenses incurred for the slave while lost are treated like those of a found object (luqata): if someone spends on him without court order, it is voluntary charity, but with a judge's order on condition of reimbursement, the spender may recover from the owner. The qualification 'from the journey period' is merely incidental; the reward is not restricted to that period but applies to any return. The entire discussion maintains consistency with the Hanafi position that a pawned item remains subject to the pledge despite the owner's death.
