The Layered Structure of a Hanafi Fiqh Text
Description of book's layout and contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main layers (matn, sharh, supercommentary) in a later Hanafi fiqh work and explain how each layer functions.

Core Idea
A later Hanafi fiqh text often has three layers: the original matn, a commentary, and a supercommentary – each building on the previous.

When you open a volume of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, you are not just reading one book. You are encountering a layered tradition: the core matn (Wiqayat al-Riwayah), its commentary (Sharh al-Wiqayah), and a supercommentary ('Umdat al-Ri'ayah). Additionally, a marginal commentary (Ghayat al-'Inayah) provides further elucidation. Understanding this structure helps you navigate the text and appreciate the scholarly chain that produced it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (base text)
The matn is the core text, here 'Wiqayat al-Riwayah fi Masa'il al-Hidayah' by Burhan al-Shari'ah. It is an abridgment of the Hidayah and is placed at the top of the page.
The matn is the authoritative foundation that later commentators expand upon.
2
Identify the first sharh (commentary)
Immediately following the matn, 'Sharh al-Wiqayah' by Sadr al-Shari'ah provides a detailed explanation. It is placed in the middle of the page.
The sharh clarifies ambiguous points, provides evidence, and may include inter-madhhab discussions.
3
Identify the supercommentary
'Umdat al-Ri'ayah' by Imam 'Abd al-Hayy al-Laknawi is a commentary on the sharh, not directly on the matn. It is placed after the sharh.
Supercommentaries often resolve disputes between earlier commentators and bring in later scholarly consensus.
4
Understand the marginal commentary
At the bottom of the page, 'Ghayat al-'Inayah' by Dr. Salah Muhammad Abu al-Hajj provides footnotes on 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah. This is a modern academic addition.
Marginal comments (hasiyah) are common in printed editions to clarify references or provide additional sources.
5
Recognize the scholarly chain
Each layer builds on the previous: matn → sharh → supercommentary. This reflects the transmission and refinement of Hanafi fiqh over centuries.
Knowing who wrote each layer helps you assess the authority and time period of the rulings.

Definition and Types of Oaths in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition and legal categories of oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of yamin and distinguish its three recognized types (laghw, ghamus, mun'aqida), including the inclusion of suspensive conditions (ta'liq).

Core Idea
An oath in Hanafi fiqh strengthens a statement by invoking Allah; suspensive conditions like 'if I do X, my wife is divorced' count as oaths.

Oaths are integral to Islamic law, serving to affirm statements or resolve intentions. The Hanafi school defines yamin with precision, encompassing not only direct swearing by Allah but also conditional suspensions that carry the same binding force. This lesson unpacks that definition and the three categories of oaths recognized by sharia.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions of yamin
Trace the term from its root meaning 'strength' to its application in fiqh as 'mentioning Allah's name or attribute to strengthen a statement.' Note the connection to the right hand and to the concept of qasam.
The legal definition excludes swearing by other than Allah, though swearing by divorce is included via ta'liq.
2
Understand why ta'liq (suspension) is included as an oath
Ta'liq links a disliked consequence to a condition, strengthening the resolve to avoid that condition. Hanafis consider this an oath due to its function and sharia/custom evidence, unlike Dawud al-Isfahani.
The text argues ta'liq provides 'security in the covenant' (وثيقة في العهد).
3
Distinguish the three sharia-recognized types of oaths
Laghw (inadvertent, without intent), ghamus (deliberately false about the past, major sin), and mun'aqida (binding oath about the future, requiring expiation if broken). The text clarifies these are the only ones with legal rulings.
Truthful statements about the past are not counted among these three.
4
Note the disagreement on ta'liq as an oath
Dawud al-Isfahani opposed considering suspensive conditions as oaths. The Hanafi response rests on sharia texts (e.g., curse on those who swear by divorce) and customary usage.
5
Apply the definition to classify examples
Practice distinguishing between laghw, ghamus, and mun'aqida, and identify whether a suspensive statement qualifies as an oath under Hanafi rules.
Focus on intent and the nature of the statement (past/future, deliberate/inadvertent).

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the chapter on oaths (kitab al-ayman) from the commentary 'Umdat al-Ri'aya. The author begins by defining the term 'yamin' linguistically, tracing it to the meaning of strength, and notes that the right hand is called yamin due to its greater strength. This meaning is carried over to the legal definition: an oath is a strengthening of a statement by invoking Allah's name or attributes. The author then explains the arrangement of chapters in the book: marriage is placed first because it is closest to worship, followed by divorce, then manumission, then oaths. This ordering reflects the relationships among these topics. The passage then clarifies that there are three types of oaths recognized by sharia: laghw (inadvertent oath), ghamus (false oath), and mun'aqida (binding oath). However, absolute oaths include more than these three, such as truthful statements about the past, which do not fall into any of these categories because they entail no legal consequence. The text also discusses suspension (ta'liq), where an oath is formed by attaching a condition, such as 'if I do this, then my wife is divorced.' This is considered an oath by the Hanafi school, contrary to some other scholars. The passage emphasizes that the sharia considers oaths in terms of accountability and legislates them through Quran, Sunnah, and consensus.

يمين غموس (yamin ghamus): A false oath about a past event, deliberately swearing falsely; it is a major sin.

First Male Muslim: Adult vs Child Conversion
Historical dispute about precedence of Abu Bakr and Ali, with legal implications for child conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the first adult male Muslim and the first child Muslim, and explain the Hanafi ruling that a discerning child's Islam is valid.

Core Idea
Abu Bakr was the first adult male Muslim; Ali was the first child Muslim, proving that a child's Islam is valid.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah discusses a famous historical debate: who was the first male to accept Islam after Khadija? By examining the evidence, scholars concluded that Abu Bakr was the first adult, while Ali, raised in the Prophet's household, was the first child to embrace Islam. This distinction carries important fiqh implications: the Hanafi school validates the Islam of a child who has reached the age of discernment (tamyiz).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting claims
The passage presents two views: one that Abu Bakr was the first male Muslim, and another that Ali was the first. Note which view is supported by the majority and by the evidence cited.
Ibn Ishaq and others hold that Abu Bakr was first; others claim Ali preceded him.
2
Analyze the evidence cited for Ali's precedence
The hadith of Abu Rafi' mentions the order of prayer: Prophet on Monday, Khadija on Monday evening, Ali on Tuesday. This suggests Ali prayed later, but does not definitively prove he converted before Abu Bakr.
The hadith is used by those claiming Ali was first, but al-Tabarani's narration is a single report.
3
Recognize the context: Ali's age
The passage then narrates the story of Ali being taken into the Prophet's care as a child. It explicitly states Ali was a minor (sabi) when he accepted Islam. This is key to resolving the dispute.
The text says: 'فعلى هذا يكون أوّل من أسلم من الرجال أبو بكر ، ويكون عليه أوّل صبي أسلم' (Thus the first man to become Muslim is Abu Bakr, and Ali is the first boy to become Muslim).
4
Distinguish between adult and child converts
The dispute is resolved by distinguishing between an adult (baligh) and a child (sabi). Abu Bakr was an adult, Ali was a child. Both are valid Muslims, but the category differs.
The Hanafi school holds that the Islam of a discerning child (mumayyiz) is valid, contrary to some schools.
5
Derive the legal principle: validity of a child's Islam
From this historical precedent, the Hanafi school derives that a child of discernment (tamyiz) can validly embrace Islam. This ruling is based on the Prophet's confirmation of Ali's Islam despite his young age.
This principle will be applied in the subsequent lesson on inheritance (mawarith) for a child convert.

Ibn Ishaq said: No man or woman preceded her (Khadija) by consensus of the Muslims. The first male to believe after her was the Siddiq of the Ummah, Abu Bakr. And it was said that Ali ibn Abi Talib became Muslim after Khadija but before Abu Bakr. Ibn Ishaq and others decisively stated this, citing the hadith of Abu Rafi' that the Prophet prayed on Monday, Khadija prayed at the end of it, and Ali prayed on Tuesday, reported by al-Tabarani. Commentary
This passage is a historical discussion within the context of the chapter on apostasy (Bab al-Murtadd), likely as a backdrop for the ruling on the validity of a child's Islam. The commentator presents a dispute about who was the first male Muslim: Ibn Ishaq and the majority hold it was Abu Bakr, while others claim Ali preceded him. The commentator supports the majority view by citing a hadith from al-Tabarani about the order of prayer on successive days. He then narrates the well-known story of the Prophet taking Ali into his care during a famine, showing that Ali was raised by the Prophet. This leads to the conclusion that Abu Bakr was the first adult male Muslim, while Ali was the first child Muslim, as he had not reached puberty. The passage also includes a famous poem attributed to Ali refuting Mu'awiyah's claims of virtue, highlighting Ali's unique relationship with the Prophet. Finally, the age of Ali at the time of his conversion is given as ten years according to Ibn Ishaq, with other opinions ranging from five to fifteen. The commentator notes that al-Hafiz preferred the age of ten. This information sets the stage for the subsequent ruling that the Prophet confirmed Ali's Islam despite his young age, and from this, important legal consequences follow, such as the inheritance issue discussed in the next lesson.
