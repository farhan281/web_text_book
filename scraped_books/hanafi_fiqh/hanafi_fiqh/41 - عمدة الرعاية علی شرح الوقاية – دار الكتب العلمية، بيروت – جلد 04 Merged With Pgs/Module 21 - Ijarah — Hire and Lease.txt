The Reward for Returning a Mortgaged Runaway Slave
Ruling on whom the reward falls upon for returning a mortgaged runaway slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who bears the reward (ju'l) for returning a mortgaged runaway slave and apply the principle of proportional liability based on the secured debt.

Core Idea
The reward for returning a mortgaged runaway slave falls on the mortgagee up to the value of the secured debt.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the ruling on ju'l (reward) for returning a mortgaged runaway slave. It clarifies that the mortgagee is liable for the reward because he revives the financial value of the slave as security. The amount is proportional to his debt; any excess is on the mortgagor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is returning a mortgaged runaway slave. The ruling is that the reward (ju'l) is upon the mortgagee (creditor), not the owner (mortgagor).
This is the key point – the obligation is on the one who benefits from the pawn's value.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to the mortgagee's obligation to pay medical expenses or ransom to save the slave from death or crime. Just as those expenses protect the secured value, the reward compensates for reviving the financial value.
The 'illa (effective cause) is 'ihya' al-maliyyah' (reviving the financial value).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The mortgagee revives the financial value of the slave by securing its return. Since the slave serves as collateral, the reward is compensation for that revival. Therefore, the reward is upon him.
This reasoning is from al-Hidayah.
4
Note the condition of proportionality
The mortgagee's liability is limited to the amount of his debt. If the slave's value exceeds the debt, the mortgagee pays reward only up to his debt, and the mortgagor pays the remainder.
Example: slave worth 200 dirhams, debt 100 dirhams, reward 50 dirhams → mortgagee pays 50 (since ≤100). If reward is 150, mortgagee pays 100, mortgagor pays 50.
5
Apply to analogous cases
The passage also applies the rule to other contexts: bequeathed slave with split usufruct, permitted debtor slave, usurped slave, gifted slave, and child's reward. In each, the reward is on the party who ultimately benefits from the slave's value.
This shows the generality of the 'illa.

His statement: 'And upon the mortgagee...' etc.; he said in Tanwir al-Absar: 'The reward of a mortgaged slave is upon the mortgagee if its value equals the debt or is less; if it is more, then upon the mortgagee up to the amount of his debt, and the remainder upon the mortgagor. And the reward of a slave bequeathed with his corpus to one person and his service to another is upon the owner of the service. When it ends, the owner of the service claims from the owner of the corpus, and the slave is sold for it. And the reward of a permitted debtor slave is upon the one to whom ownership is ultimately established, just as the reward of a usurped slave is upon the usurper, and of a gifted slave upon the donee, even if the donor withdraws, and of a child upon his wealth.' End. That is, if the mortgaged slave runs away and is returned from a journey distance, then the reward is upon the mortgagee. This is if its value equals the debt or is less. If it is more than the debt, then upon the mortgagee is (reward) up to his debt, and the remainder is upon the mortgagor. And the matter of its expenses is like that of a foundling (luqata). His statement: 'from a journey distance' – this is an accidental restriction; otherwise the reward is not specific to a journey distance, but is obligatory for less also as previously mentioned. His statement: 'upon the mortgagee'; he said in al-Hidayah: 'Because he revived its financial value by returning it, and that value is his right since the satisfaction and the reward are in exchange for reviving the financial value, so it is upon him. And the return during the life of the mortgagor and after his death are the same, because the mortgage is not voided by death. This is if it is more: up to the debt is upon him, and the remainder upon the mortgagor, because his right is to the amount guaranteed, so it is like the price of medicine and ransoming it from crime by ransom.' End. His statement: 'if it is more' – e.g., its value is two hundred dirhams, and the mortgagee's debt upon the mortgagor for which that slave was mortgaged is one hundred dirhams. His statement: 'like a foundling' – so if the taker spends on it without the judge's permission, it is charity; and with the judge's permission on condition of recourse, he may recover from the owner.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the reward (ju'l) for returning a mortgaged runaway slave. It begins by quoting Tanwir al-Absar, which states that the reward for returning a mortgaged slave is upon the mortgagee if the slave's value equals or is less than the debt. If the value exceeds the debt, the mortgagee is liable for reward only up to his debt amount, and the rest is upon the mortgagor. The quote then lists other cases: a slave whose corpus and service are bequeathed to different persons – reward on the service owner; a permitted debtor slave – reward on the ultimate owner; a usurped slave – on the usurper; a gifted slave – on the donee even if the donor revokes; a child's reward – on his wealth. The commentary then explains that if a mortgaged slave runs away and is returned from a journey, the reward is on the mortgagee. The restriction 'from a journey' is incidental; the reward applies even for shorter distances. The commentary from al-Hidayah provides the rationale: the mortgagee revives the financial value of the slave through its return, and since the slave secures his debt, the reward is compensation for that revival. The mortgagee's liability is proportional to the secured debt. If the slave's value exceeds the debt, only the debt portion is secured, so the reward is limited accordingly. This is analogized to medical expenses or ransom to save the slave. Finally, expenses for the slave's upkeep are treated like a foundling (luqata): if the finder spends with the judge's permission with an agreement of reimbursement, he can recover; otherwise it is charity.

Key Terms
جعل (ju'l): Reward given to the person who returns a runaway slave; in this context, the amount is proportional to the secured debt.
مرتهن (murtahin): The mortgagee (creditor) who holds the pawn (rahn) as security for a debt.
إحياء مالية (ihya' maliyyah): Reviving the financial value of the property; the mortgagee's act of returning the runaway slave restores its value as security.
