Testimony on Negation in Manumission: A Hanafi Intra-School Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on shahada 'ala al-nafy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Imam Muhammad accepts testimony on negation in the manumission case and contrast it with the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, applying the principle of qiyas jaliy.

Core Idea
Testimony on negation is accepted when the witness attests to a positive fact that necessarily implies the negation.

This lesson examines a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of testimony that implies a negative fact. The case involves a master who conditions his slave's freedom on not performing Hajj; witnesses testify that they saw the slave sacrifice in Kufa, which implies he did not perform Hajj. Imam Muhammad accepts this testimony, while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf reject it. Through qiyas, the reasoning behind each position is clarified, and the preferred view is identified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core case
Understand the scenario: a master says 'If you do not perform Hajj this year, you are free.' Two witnesses testify that the slave sacrificed in Kufa on Eid al-Adha. The condition for freedom is non-performance of Hajj. The testimony seems to prove the condition by implication.
The case hinges on whether the testimony is considered 'shahada 'ala al-nafy' (testimony on negation) or 'shahada 'ala al-ithbat' (testimony on affirmation).
2
State the positions and their proofs
Imam Muhammad accepts the testimony because the witnesses saw a positive fact (sacrifice in Kufa) that necessarily implies the negation of Hajj. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf reject it, arguing that testimony on pure negation is not accepted.
Muhammad's proof: the testimony is on an observable act, not a pure negation. The Sahibayn's proof: negation cannot be directly witnessed.
3
Analyze the analogy (qiyas)
The passage draws an analogy to a case where a master says 'If you do not enter the house today, you are free,' and a witness testifies that the slave did not enter (by seeing him outside). That testimony is accepted. The same reasoning should apply to the Hajj case.
The illa is that the witness observes a positive fact that makes the negation certain. The far' (Hajj case) shares this illa.
4
Evaluate the objection and response
The Sahibayn object that the sacrifice in Kufa does not necessarily imply not performing Hajj (e.g., he could have performed Hajj earlier or later). The response in al-Fath argues that the slave had no right to the sacrifice, so the only purpose is to prove negation, and since the witnesses saw the sacrifice, the negation is proven.
The supercommentary concludes that Muhammad's view has more weight (أوجه).
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The preferred view (mufta bihi) is that testimony on negation is accepted when based on a witnessed positive fact that necessarily implies the negation. This resolves the dispute in favor of freeing the slave.
The tarjih is based on the reasoning in al-Fath and the strength of the qiyas.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the acceptance of testimony that implies a negation. The case involves a master who tells his slave: 'If you do not perform Hajj this year, you are free.' Two witnesses then testify that they saw the slave sacrifice his offering in Kufa on the Day of Sacrifice. Imam Muhammad accepts this testimony because the witnesses directly attest to an observable event (the sacrifice in Kufa), which necessarily implies that the slave did not perform Hajj (since Hajj requires being in Mecca). Therefore, the condition for freedom is fulfilled. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, however, reject the testimony, arguing that it is essentially testimony about a negative fact (that the slave did not perform Hajj), and such testimony is generally inadmissible. The supercommentary defends Muhammad's position by arguing that negation can be testified to when the witness has direct knowledge of the circumstances that prove the negation, just like positive testimony. It draws an analogy from another case: if a master says, 'If you do not enter the house today, you are free,' and a witness testifies that the slave did not enter, that testimony is accepted because the witness saw the slave outside the house. The witness directly observes the positive fact of being outside, which implies the negation of entering. Similarly, in our case, the witnesses directly observe the sacrifice in Kufa, which implies the negation of Hajj. The commentary from al-Fath further supports Muhammad by noting that the slave has no right to the sacrifice or to going out, so the only purpose of the testimony is to prove the negation. The passage concludes by referencing the principles of usul al-fiqh: when one witness affirms and another negates, preference is given based on whether the negation is known by evidence or is merely based on original non-existence.

Testimony for Time-Barred Hadd: Except Qadhf
Explains that delayed testimony for hudud is rejected except for qadhf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects testimony for time-barred hudud (except qadhf) and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A witness's delay in testifying against a hadd creates suspicion—except when the penalty involves a human right, like qadhf.

The passage from al-Hidayah addresses a critical nuance: not all hudud are treated equally regarding time. While the Shafi'i school accepts testimony regardless of delay, the Hanafi school requires promptness for purely divine hudud. This distinction hinges on the nature of the right involved—whether it belongs exclusively to Allah or also involves a servant's claim.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Extract the ruling: testimony for a time-barred hadd (zina, theft, drinking) is not accepted, except for hadd qadhf. The condition is that the witness was close to the judge with no excuse.
The term 'hadd' here refers to its cause (the crime).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that hudud are not voided by passage of time; he analogizes them to rights of servants and to confession.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf in usul.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i argues: just as testimony is accepted for property rights even if delayed, and confession is accepted regardless of time, so should divine hudud be accepted.
He treats all hudud uniformly.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: the witness has a choice between testifying and concealing (both good deeds). Delay indicates either concealment (leading to suspicion of rancor) or sin (if not concealment). Thus a preventing factor exists. Confession is different because one does not harm oneself.
Qadhf is exceptional because it involves repelling disgrace—a human right.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In the Hanafi school, old hudud crimes cannot be established by testimony; in the Shafi'i school, they can.
This has practical implications for evidence in court.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the acceptance of testimony for hudud (fixed penalties) when the incident is old. The ruling states that if a witness testifies about a hadd that is time-barred while he was near the judge with no excuse, his testimony is not accepted—except for hadd qadhf (false accusation of zina). The reason is that qadhf includes a human right (refuting disgrace), which does not lapse by time. The commentary explains: The term 'hadd' loosely refers to its cause (e.g., zina, theft, drinking). 'Old' means the incident occurred long ago. 'Near his imam' means the witness was close to the judge with no valid excuse for delay. The Hidayah states the Hanafi principle: purely divine hudud (Allah's rights) are invalidated by passage of time. Shafi'i disagrees, analogizing them to human rights and confession. The Hanafi response: The witness had a choice between testifying and concealing; if he delayed for concealment, later testimony is suspicious (rancor/enmity); if he delayed without concealment, he is a sinner. Thus there is a preventing factor. Confession is different because a person does not harm himself. So zina, drinking, theft are purely Allah's rights; qadhf involves a human right and is not time-barred.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A fixed penalty prescribed by Allah for specific crimes (e.g., zina, theft, qadhf, drinking).
تقادم (taqadum): The passage of a long period of time since the commission of the act that is the cause of the hadd.
قذف (qadhf): False accusation of zina; its hadd is eighty lashes and includes a human right to refute disgrace.

The commentary details each phrase: 'bihadd' means the cause of hadd, 'mutaqadim' means the event occurred long ago, 'qarīban min imāmihi' means the witness was able to testify immediately but delayed. The author then cites al-Hidayah, which states the Hanafi principle: pure divine hadds (zina, drinking, theft) are invalidated by delay, opposing al-Shafi'i who analogizes them to human rights and confession. The Hanafi proof: a witness chooses between testifying and concealing; delay suggests either a bad motive (enmity) or sinfulness (if not for valid concealment), casting suspicion. Confession is different because one does not harm oneself. Qadhf is excluded because it involves a human right (honor), and the right of the claimant does not lapse by delay, as lawsuit is required and delay indicates no claim, not transgression.

Key Terms
حد متقادم (hadd mutaqadim): A hadd punishment (or its cause) that has been delayed for a long time without valid excuse.
حد قذف (hadd qadhf): The hadd punishment for false accusation of zina (qadhf), which includes a human right (right of the accused to clear their honor).
حق العبد (haqq al-'abd): A right belonging to a human being (as opposed to a pure divine right), which can be waived or pursued by the individual.

Delayed Testimony and Confession in Hadd Cases: Liability vs. Hadd
Expansion and explanation of two rulings on delay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on delayed testimony for theft and explain why confessing to a delayed hadd establishes the hadd penalty.

Core Idea
Delayed testimony for theft cancels hadd but preserves compensation; delayed confession for hadd is effective because self-incrimination lacks enmity.

In this passage, we examine two related but distinct scenarios involving delay (taqadum) in hudud cases. First, witnesses testify to a theft that occurred long ago. Second, an accused person confesses to a hadd crime after a delay. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between testimony (shahada) and confession (iqrar). While delay taints testimony due to possible enmity, confession is taken at face value because one does not harm oneself. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two separate cases: (1) delayed testimony about theft, (2) delayed confession about a hadd crime. Identify each and its ruling.
2
Analyse the theft testimony ruling
The text rules that testimony for a delayed theft establishes liability (daman) but not the hadd (amputation). Ask: why is hadd dropped? Because delay raises suspicion of enmity (shahadat al-ta‘an). Why is liability not dropped? Because property rights are human rights that do not lapse with time.
Contrast with Shafi‘i position: he accepts testimony for both.
3
Analyse the confession ruling
Confession to a delayed hadd (e.g., zina) leads to hadd being applied. The reason: the obstacle that taints testimony (enmity) does not exist in confession – a person does not accuse themselves out of malice.
Exception: drinking (shurb) as will be explained later.
4
Understand the underlying principle (qiyas jali)
The ruling on testimony is based on a qiyas: the presence of suspicion (tuhma) blocks acceptance. This suspicion is deemed nonexistent in confession because self-interest prevents false confession. Recognise that this is a pure qiyas on the ma‘na: enmity is the effective cause (illa) for rejecting delayed testimony, and its absence in iqrar makes it acceptable.

Definition of Taqadum for Drinking and Other Hadds
Defines how taqadum is determined for drinking (by smell) and for other hadds (by a month)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of taqadum for drinking and for other hadds, and distinguish the preferred Hanafi position on the time limit.

Core Idea
Taqadum for drinking is determined by disappearance of smell; for other hadds, it is one month (Muhammad's view) or left to the judge (Abu Hanifa).

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies how delay (taqadum) in testifying affects the acceptance of testimony in hadd cases. For drinking, the key indicator is physical: the smell of alcohol must still be present. For other hadd crimes like zina and theft, the criterion is temporal. The passage also reveals an important disagreement between the two founding Imams of the Hanafi school.
