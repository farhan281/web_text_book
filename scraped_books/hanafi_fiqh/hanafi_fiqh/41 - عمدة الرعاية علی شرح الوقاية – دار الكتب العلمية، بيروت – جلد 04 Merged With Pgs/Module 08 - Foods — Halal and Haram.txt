Ruling on Eating a Half-Ripe Date When Sworn Not to Eat Dates
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mudhnib date oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether a mudhnib date violates an oath and apply the principle of haqiqah vs. 'urf in oath interpretation.

Core Idea
A half-ripe date (mudhnib) breaks an oath if eaten, but only according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn rely on custom.

When a person swears not to eat ripe or unripe dates and then consumes a date that is partly both, does he break his oath? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on this issue, rooted in whether oaths are interpreted literally or by common usage. This lesson examines the arguments of Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three oath scenarios
The text presents three cases: (a) swearing not to eat ripe dates (rutab) and eating a mudhnib; (b) swearing not to eat unripe dates (busr) and eating a mudhnib; (c) swearing not to eat both. For each, Abu Hanifa rules the oath is broken; the Sahibayn distinguish based on the predominant state.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his literalist proof
Abu Hanifa holds that a mudhnib contains both ripe and unripe parts; therefore eating it constitutes eating both, violating any oath against either. He argues that each component is intentionally consumed, unlike a purchase where the whole is a single transaction.
His reasoning is based on haqiqah (literal reality) and the fact that eating involves consumption of each part.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their custom-based proof
The Sahibayn rule that if the date is predominantly unripe (busr mudhnib), it is still called busr in common usage, so an oath against ripe dates is not broken. Similarly, a predominantly ripe mudhnib is called rutab, so an oath against unripe dates is not broken. They analogize to purchase, where the minor part follows the major.
Their reasoning relies on 'urf (custom) and the analogy to a transaction involving a whole object.
4
Compare the analogy to purchase
Abu Hanifa distinguishes eating from purchase: in eating, each part is individually consumed and intended; in purchase, the transaction covers the entire object as a single unit, so the minor follows the major.
This highlights the difference between consumption and ownership as bases for legal categorization.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, kaffarah (expiation) is required; according to the Sahibayn, no kaffarah unless the predominantly matching counterpart is eaten. The later preferred opinion (mufta bihi) tends toward the Sahibayn's view based on custom.
See Fath al-Qadir, which deems Abu Yusuf's view stronger ('aqwa).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the ruling on oaths involving dates at different stages of ripeness. The central issue is the classification of a 'mudhnib' date—one that is partly ripe (rutab) and partly unripe (busr). The text begins by stating the general rule according to Imam Abu Hanifa: if a person swears not to eat ripe dates, or unripe dates, or both, and then eats a mudhnib date, he breaks his oath (yanth). Abu Hanifa's reasoning is that the mudhnib date contains both components (ripe and unripe), so eating it fulfills both names, thus violating any oath against either category.

However, the two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (referred to as 'the two' or 'Sahibayn'), disagree. According to them, if someone swears not to eat ripe dates and eats a mudhnib date that is predominantly unripe (busr mudhnib), he does not break his oath, because the date is still called 'busr' (unripe) in common parlance. Similarly, if he swears not to eat unripe dates and eats a predominantly ripe mudhnib date, he does not break his oath. Their reasoning is based on customary usage ('urf): the name 'ripe' or 'unripe' attaches to the dominant part, and the oath is understood according to the common meaning.

The commentary from al-Hidayah explains that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad analogize this to a purchase: if one buys a date, the entire date is considered one unit, and the minor part follows the major. But Abu Hanifa distinguishes: in eating, each component is individually consumed and intended, whereas in a purchase, the transaction covers the whole object.

The text further notes that according to al-Binayah, Imam Muhammad actually agreed with Abu Hanifa's ruling (that the oath is broken), creating a nuance. Then Fath al-Qadir comments that the dispute hinges on whether oaths are interpreted literally (haqiqah) or according to custom ('urf). If custom is considered, Abu Yusuf's view is stronger because in everyday language, a person eating a mudhnib date is not referred to as 'eating unripe' if it is mostly ripe.

Later sections from al-Mughrib and the commentary criticize al-Hidayah's definition of mudhnib, arguing that ripening begins from the stalk end (al-dhanab), not the other end. This botanical correction affects the force of Abu Hanifa's argument. Finally, al-Kafi provides a practical definition: a busr mudhnib is mostly unripe with some ripe; a rutab mudhnib is mostly ripe with some unripe. This passage demonstrates the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning, where linguistic precision, custom, and physical reality interact to determine the operative ruling.

Key Terms
مذنب (mudhnib): A date that is partly ripe (rutab) and partly unripe (busr). The term originally refers to the tail or stalk end (dhanab) where ripening begins.
حنث (hanth): The act of breaking an oath; if the sworn matter occurs, the oath is violated and expiation (kaffarah) becomes due.
يمين (yamin): An oath; a solemn vow to do or not do something, usually with the name of Allah. In this context, it is a conditional oath (al-yamin al-mun'aqidah).

Definition of Bishara and Ruling on Multiple Slaves Informing
Defines bishara and explains the ruling when multiple slaves inform the master separately.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions of bishara and apply the ruling when multiple slaves inform the master separately or simultaneously.

Core Idea
Only the first truthful joyful news is bishara; subsequent reporters are not mubashshir.

This passage defines the term 'bishara' (good news) with three essential conditions. Understanding these conditions is crucial when a master conditions freedom on receiving good news from his slaves. The ruling distinguishes between slaves who inform one after another versus all at once, based on both custom and a key usul principle about the word 'kull' (every).

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of bishara
Extract the three conditions that make a report bishara: joyful, truthful, and new to the hearer. Note what is excluded by each condition.
The passage explicitly lists these conditions and cites Quran (3:21) for linguistic usage.
2
Apply the definition to the scenario
Consider a master who says 'Every slave who brings me good news of X is free.' If slaves inform one after another, only the first qualifies as mubashshir; if they inform together, all qualify.
The key distinction is the timing: sequential vs. simultaneous.
3
Understand the usul principle of 'kull'
The word 'kull' (every) when followed by a nakira (indefinite noun) indicates general coverage of each individual independently ('umum al-infirād). This justifies freeing all when they inform simultaneously.
The passage contrasts 'kull' with 'al-jami'' (all) which implies collective coverage.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling on the term 'bishara' (good news). The commentator explains that bishara has three conditions: it must be joyful, truthful, and new to the hearer. Based on this, if a master says 'Every slave who brings me good news of such-and-such is free,' and his slaves inform him one after another, only the first slave is freed because he alone is the true 'mubashshir' (bearer of good news). However, if they inform him simultaneously, all are freed because each one is a mubashshir. The legal basis is the general application of the word 'kull' (every) when used with a nakira: it indicates each individual independently. The passage cites al-Tanqih and al-Tawdih for further detail.

Key Terms
البشارة (al-bishārah): A joyful, truthful, and new report given for the first time to the hearer.
كلّ (kull): When followed by a nakira indefinite noun, it indicates generality with individual independence ('umum al-infirād).

Ruling on Slaves Informing Together
States the ruling when multiple slaves bring good news simultaneously.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a slave sends another slave with good news, and determine which slave is freed based on whether the sender is mentioned.

Core Idea
When a slave sends another with good news, freedom depends on whether the sender is mentioned.

This lesson expands the general rule about slaves who bring good news to their master. When multiple slaves inform simultaneously, the mode of communication does not matter. But a special situation arises when one slave sends another as a messenger. The key distinction is whether the sender's role is 'mentioned' – if it is, the sender is freed; if not, the messenger is freed. Understanding this nuance prevents a misapplication of the master's offer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage deals with the master's declaration: any slave who brings good news is freed. The scenario is multiple slaves informing simultaneously, or one slave sending another as a messenger.
The modes (letter, message, oral) are all equivalent.
2
Apply the general principle
When slaves inform together, all are freed because each is a direct bearer of news. The mode does not affect the ruling.
3
Focus on the sub-case: slave sending a messenger
Identify that a slave (sender) dispatches another slave (messenger) to convey the news to the master.
4
Examine the condition of mentioning the sender
If the communication mentions the sender (e.g., 'Send my master the news that I, slave X, have good news'), then the sender is the true bearer and is freed. If not, the messenger is the apparent bearer and is freed.
The key is explicit mention; implied or indirect identification does not suffice.
5
Derive the ruling
State clearly: only the sender is freed if mentioned; only the messenger is freed if not mentioned.
This preserves the master's intent to free the one who actually brought the news.

Intention for Oath Expiation Must Accompany the Cause, Not the Condition
Explains the ruling on using a conditional emancipation for oath expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a conditional emancipation (upon purchase) is used as expiation for an oath, and explain the distinction between the cause ('illah) and condition (shart) in this ruling.

Core Idea
Intention for expiation must accompany the oath (cause), not the purchase (condition).

In Hanafi fiqh, when a person makes an oath and then phrases an emancipation conditionally on a purchase, the intention to expiate the oath must be made at the time of the oath, not at the time of the purchase. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this ruling, especially the distinction between the 'illah (cause) and shart (condition) in legal analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A person says 'If I buy Zaid, he is free' and intends to expiate an oath. The purchase is a condition for emancipation.
2
State the ruling
The emancipation does not count as expiation. The expiation remains due.
3
Understand the cause ('illah)
The cause of the emancipation is the oath, not the purchase. The purchase is merely a condition (shart) that triggers the emancipation.
In Hanafi usul, the 'illah is the reason for the ruling; the shart is a prerequisite.
4
Apply the condition for expiation
For expiation to be valid, the intention must accompany the 'illah (the oath), not the shart (the purchase). Since the intention occurred at the purchase, it is not effective.
5
Note the exception
If the intention had been made at the time of the oath (concurrent with the 'illah), the emancipation would suffice as expiation.

Commentary
This passage addresses a subsidiary ruling: If a man says 'If I buy so-and-so, he is free,' and then buys that person with the intention of fulfilling the expiation of an oath, it does not count as expiation. The reason is that for expiation, the intention must coincide with the cause (illah) – which is the oath itself. Here, the intention only occurs at the time of purchase (the condition for emancipation), not at the time of the oath. If the intention had been made at the time of the oath, it would have been valid. The rule is from al-Hidayah and al-'Inayah, and it is placed here because the issue involves an oath (yamin) and emancipation conditional on purchase.
