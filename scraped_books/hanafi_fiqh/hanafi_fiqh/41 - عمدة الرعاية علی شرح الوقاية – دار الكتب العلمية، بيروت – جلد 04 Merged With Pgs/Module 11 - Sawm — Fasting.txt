Breaking an Oath by Fasting for a Short Duration
Ruling on breaking an oath not to fast by fasting for one hour with intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on when a short fast breaks an oath, distinguishing between absolute and qualified oath wordings.

Core Idea
If the oath is absolute, any intentional fast breaks it; if it specifies a day/fast, only a full day counts.

A person swears not to fast, but then fasts for just an hour. Is the oath broken? The answer depends on the exact wording. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling, showing the critical role of intention and the distinction between absolute and qualified oaths. Understanding this prevents unintended oath-breaking and the obligation of kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath wording
Determine whether the oath is absolute (e.g., 'I will not fast') or qualified (e.g., 'I will not fast a day' or 'I will not observe a fast'). The wording determines the minimum duration of fasting required to break the oath.
Absolute: no time qualifier. Qualified: includes 'day' or 'fast' implying a complete unit.
2
Check intention
Ensure the person intended to fast (niyya). Without intention, refraining is not considered fasting legally or customarily, and the oath is not broken.
Intention must be present for the act to count as a valid fast (sawm shar'i).
3
Determine the duration of fasting
If the oath is absolute, any duration of purposeful abstinence (even an hour) counts as fasting and breaks the oath. If qualified, only a complete day's fast (from dawn to sunset) suffices.
A complete day means the entire daytime; a partial day does not break a qualified oath.
4
Apply the ruling
Conclude whether the oath is broken based on the above steps. If the conditions are met, hanath (oath-breaking) occurs and kaffarah becomes due.
Kaffarah for breaking an oath is feeding/clothing ten poor people or fasting three days.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on oaths related to fasting. If a person swears, 'I will not fast' (using a general expression without specifying duration), and then he observes a fast for even one hour with the intention of fasting, he breaks his oath. This is because the legally valid fast (الصوم الشرعي) begins as soon as one intends and refrains from nullifiers, even for a short time. The minimal duration to be considered a fasting person is a single moment of abstinence with intention. Therefore, fasting for an hour with intention constitutes a valid fast, and the oath is broken. However, if the person explicitly includes the word 'day' (يوماً) or 'a fast' (صوماً) in his oath, then he does not break the oath unless he completes a full day's fast. The reason is that when the oath is qualified with 'day', the default meaning is the complete daytime. Similarly, when 'fast' is used, it refers to the complete legally valid fast, which is a full day. The commentary clarifies that intention (نية) is crucial: if someone refrains from eating without intending to fast, he is not considered a fasting person (neither legally nor customarily), so the oath is not broken. The passage also addresses a potential objection: if the source verb 'to fast' is used, one might think it implies a full day. But the explanation is that when the oath is absolute ('I will not fast'), the action of fasting for any duration fulfills the definition of fasting, whereas specifying 'day' or 'fast' sets a minimum requirement. Thus, the ruling depends on the wording of the oath.

Key Terms
حنث (hanith): Breaking an oath by doing what one swore not to do, or not doing what one swore to do
صوم شرعي (sawm shar'i): Fasting as defined by Islamic law: abstaining from food, drink, and marital relations from dawn to sunset with intention
نية (niyya): Intention in the heart to perform the act of worship

Breaking an Oath Not to Fast by Fasting an Hour
Ruling and commentary on oath violation by partial fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on breaking an oath not to fast by fasting an hour, and explain the Hanafi proof from the Quran using the principle of al-sawm al-shar'i.

Core Idea
Intentional fasting for any duration breaks a general oath not to fast; the legal reality of fasting is refraining with intention.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling on oaths related to fasting. The central question is whether fasting for only a portion of a day can constitute a breach of an oath. The passage clarifies that the legal definition of fasting (al-sawm al-shar'i) focuses on the act of refraining with intention, not the duration. This principle leads to a distinct Hanafi position that may differ from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the act
What is the exact wording of the oath? Is it general ('I will not fast') or does it specify 'day' or 'fast'? What act is performed (fasting an hour)?
The ruling depends on the presence or absence of qualifying terms.
2
Determine the ruling
State whether the oath is broken. If broken, what is the consequence (kaffarah)? The text says 'hantha' (guilty of oath-breaking).
The ruling is wajib: the oath-breaker must perform kaffarah.
3
Locate the Hanafi proof
What evidence does the author cite? The verse from the Quran (2:187) and the reasoning that the phrase 'atimmu al-siyam' implies the possibility of incomplete fasting.
The proof is from the Quran, but its interpretation is debated.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse prove the Hanafi position? The imperative 'complete' presumes that the fast can be incomplete; similarly, a general oath refers to the reality of fasting, which is an act of refraining, not a full day.
This is a talil (reasoning) based on linguistic implication.
5
Note the objection and response
What is the opposing view (that 'fast' legally means a full day)? How does the author rebut it using the same verse?
The response shows the Hanafi method of interpreting legal terms when both literal and legal meanings exist.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school on oaths regarding fasting. The author states that if a person swears 'I will not fast' (without specifying a day) and then intentionally fasts for even an hour, the oath is broken. This is because the legal reality of fasting is simply refraining from nullifiers with intention; any amount constitutes the essence of fasting. However, if the oath includes the word 'day' or 'fast' (as a unit), it is not broken until a full day is completed. The commentary explains that 'with intention' excludes unintentional restraint. A key debate arises: the opponent argues that the legal meaning of 'fast' is a full day, so the oath should only be broken upon completing a day. The Hanafi response cites Quran 2:187, where 'fast' is applied to the act of fasting during the day, not necessarily the entire day, showing that the legal term can apply to partial fasting. Thus, the absolute oath ('I will not fast') is violated by any intentional fasting, while specifying 'a day' requires the full day.

Key Terms
الحنث (al-hinth): The breach of an oath, incurring the expiation (kaffarah) or the consequence of the oath.
الصوم الشرعي (al-sawm al-shar'i): Legal fasting: refraining from nullifiers with intention from dawn to sunset. However, the text argues that any intentional refraining constitutes its reality.
