Distinction Between Wujub and Wujub al-Ada' in Hanafi Fiqh
Analyzing the difference between obligation and obligation to perform

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between wujub and wujub al-ada' in Hanafi fiqh, explain the intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab positions, and apply the distinction to financial and bodily acts.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, wujub occupies the dhimmah; wujub al-ada' empties it — they are conceptually distinct.

This lesson explores a central conceptual distinction in Hanafi legal theory: the difference between the existence of an obligation (wujub) and the obligation to perform it (wujub al-ada'). While Shafi'is treat them as identical, Hanafis have debated whether and how they differ, especially in bodily acts like fasting and financial duties like expiation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly timing obligations and for grasping Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the core question? Does wujub exist independently of wujub al-ada', or are they the same? Note the different answers given by Shafi'is and Hanafis.
2
State the Shafi'i position
According to al-Taftazani, Shafi'is hold that wujub has no meaning except the necessity of performing the act. Thus wujub equals wujub al-ada'.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Some Hanafis (e.g., Shaykh Abu al-Mu'in) deny the distinction in bodily acts; others affirm it. Identify their arguments and the proposed definitions from 'al-Kashf' and the commentator.
Note the critique of the mental existence definition.
4
Understand the preferred Hanafi definition
The commentator defines wujub as 'occupation of the dhimmah with an act or property' and wujub al-ada' as 'the necessity of emptying the dhimmah'. Grasp how this avoids the critique of the earlier definition.
5
Apply the distinction to practical cases
Consider how the distinction affects the timing of expiation (kaffarah) in oaths. The commentator argues that even in financial acts, property is not the ultimate aim; performance is. So the distinction holds in both domains.
Compare with the Shafi'i view that would require immediate performance.

Al-Taftazani said in 'al-Talwih': 'Know that obligation (wujub) in the terminology of jurists, despite differences in their interpretations, essentially means an act such that its abandoner deserves blame in the present and punishment in the hereafter. Hence the Shafi'is held that it means nothing except the necessity of performing the act, and that there is no meaning of obligation without obligation to perform (wujub al-ada'), meaning the performance of the act, whether it be initial performance, make-up, or repetition. As for the Hanafis, some of them held that there is no difference between obligation and obligation to perform in bodily acts, to the extent that Shaykh Abu al-Mu'in went far in rejecting and denying it. For fasting, for example, is only refraining from fulfilling two desires during the day for Allah's sake, and refraining is the servant's act; when it occurs, performance occurs. If they were distinct, then the faster would be doing two acts: refraining and performing the refraining, and this is absurd. As for those who go for the distinction, some are content with examples, and some attempt verification. The author of 'al-Kashf' held that obligation itself is the occupation of the dhimmah with the mental existence of the act, and obligation to perform is bringing that act from non-existence to external existence; there is no doubt they are different. Similarly in financial matters, the original obligation is the liability of a property in the dhimmah, and obligation to perform is bringing it from non-existence to external existence... This is his statement. The apparent is that occupation of the dhimmah with the mental existence of the act or the conceptualized financial thing is merely an expression; because it is not valid to intend the conceptualization of the one under obligation, as he may be heedless like a sleeping person or a child, nor conceptualization in general, as there is no meaning to the occupation of the dhimmah of a sleeping person or a child with a prayer or property that exists in the mind of Zayd, for example. The author of the commentary (al-Sharh) held that obligation itself is the occupation of the dhimmah with an act or property, and obligation to perform is the necessity of emptying the dhimmah of what it is occupied with.' [1] His statement: 'pertains to property' – this is a conceptual distinction between obligation and obligation to perform in financial matters. So obligation itself is related to the property itself, as it is the duty in the dhimmah, and obligation to perform is related to the act, i.e., its performance. The clearest in distinction is what the commentator (al-Sharih) mentioned in 'Tanqih al-Usul': 'The difference between obligation itself and obligation to perform is that the first is occupation of the mukallaf's dhimmah with something, and the second is the necessity of emptying the dhimmah of what it is occupied with. So it must have a prior right in the dhimmah. Thus, when one buys something, the price is established in the dhimmah; as for the necessity of performance, it is at the time of demand based on the original obligation.' Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the distinction between wujub (obligation) and wujub al-ada' (obligation to perform) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, as part of the commentary on the chapter of oaths. The author begins by citing al-Taftazani from 'al-Talwih' who explains that the Shafi'is do not distinguish between wujub and wujub al-ada', holding that wujub is nothing but the necessity of performing the act. Among Hanafis, there are multiple views: some Hanafis (like Shaykh Abu al-Mu'in) deny the distinction in bodily acts, arguing that the act itself is its performance; others affirm the distinction. The author of 'al-Kashf' defines wujub as the occupation of the dhimmah with the mental existence of the act, and wujub al-ada' as bringing that act into external existence. The commentator (the author of the sharh) critiques this as merely a conceptual expression. The preferred view is that wujub is the occupation of the dhimmah with an act or property, and wujub al-ada' is the necessity of emptying the dhimmah. The text then notes that Shafi'is deny the distinction altogether, while some Hanafis deny it only in bodily acts. The final part of the passage clarifies that in financial matters, property is not the ultimate aim in rights of Allah; rather, performance is. Thus, even though wujub and wujub al-ada' can be conceptually distinct, in practical terms for acts of worship, the distinction may not have a tangible effect. The commentator also quotes from 'al-Tawdih' praising the distinction and offering a verification using the example of prayer: time being the cause, the necessity of the specific form (hay'ah) of prayer is wujub itself. Throughout, the passage follows the author's order: presenting the Shafi'i view, then Hanafi views, then a critique and preferred position.

Key Terms
الوجوب (al-wujub): The legal quality of an act such that its abandoner deserves blame in this world and punishment in the Hereafter; occupation of the dhimmah with an act or property.
وجوب الأداء (wujub al-ada'): The necessity of performing the act or discharging the duty, i.e., emptying the dhimmah of what it is occupied with.
اشتغال الذمة (ishtighal al-dhimmah): The state of the legal capacity (dhimmah) being occupied with a duty or liability.

Defining Idam in Oaths: Abu Hanifa vs. Muhammad al-Shaybani
Commentary on Al-Hidayah defining idam and presenting Hanafi internal dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi definitions of idam and apply them to determine whether an oath is broken in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Idam is only a subsidiary condiment, not a main dish—meat does not break an oath unless intended.

This lesson unpacks a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about the meaning of 'idam' in oath formulations. The difference between a narrow definition (dipped or mixed subsidiary) and a broad definition (anything commonly eaten with bread) has practical consequences: does eating roasted meat break an oath not to eat idam? By examining the linguistic and customary arguments, you will see how the Hanafi masters derived their positions.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed basis
The oath is about not eating 'idam'. Identify the context: a valid oath concerning a condiment.
2
Present the narrow definition
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: idam is only what is dipped or mixed as a subsidiary (taba'), like vinegar and salt. Meat is not idam.
3
Present the broad definition
Muhammad (and a narration from Abu Yusuf): idam is anything generally eaten with bread, including meat and eggs.
4
Analyze the proofs
Trace the linguistic derivation from mu'adamah (compatibility). Narrow view emphasizes full mixing (imtizaj); broad view emphasizes mere accompaniment.
Note the use of custom ('urf) in the broad definition.
5
Identify the practical consequence and tarjih
The thamarah al-khilaf: eating meat breaks the oath only under the broad definition. The text states 'al-sahih' indicating the narrow view is mufta bihi.
Exception: if the oath-taker intended meat, it becomes idam due to tashdid.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Al-Hidayah regarding the definition of idam in oaths. The base matn states that whatever is dipped in bread is idam, and salt is idam but not roast meat. The commentary then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that idam is only what is dipped into bread or mixed in such a way that it is eaten as a subsidiary (taba'), like vinegar, salt, and other liquids. They argue that 'ishtibagh' (dipping) implies physical mixing, and the term idam derives from mu'adamah (compatibility), which is fully realized in mixing. Meat, on the other hand, is eaten alone and thus not idam. Imam Muhammad (and a narration from Abu Yusuf) defines idam more broadly as anything eaten with bread in the customary way, including meat and eggs, because compatibility exists in eating together. The text concludes that the correct position (al-sahih) is that grapes and melon are not idam, implying the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is the relied-upon position. However, if one specifically intends meat in his oath, it becomes idam due to the strictness of oaths (tashdid).

اصطباغ (ishtibagh): Dipping (bread) into a liquid condiment.
تبعية (taba'iyyah): Subsidiarity: being eaten as an accompaniment, not as a main dish.

Definition and Time of al-‘Ishā’ and al-Sahūr in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines the terms al-‘ishā’ and al-sahūr with their time boundaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of al-‘ishā’ and al-sahūr as used in oaths and identify their time boundaries.

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, al-‘ishā’ spans from noon to midnight, not just the night prayer.

Legal terms often carry specialized meanings that differ from everyday usage. This passage clarifies two such terms—al-‘ishā’ and al-sahūr—as they appear in the context of oaths. Understanding these definitions is essential for correctly applying rulings related to oath fulfillment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Locate al-‘ishā’ and al-sahūr in the text. Note that they are being defined in relation to a preceding phrase 'منه' (from it), which refers to an earlier mentioned time.
The passage is from a commentary on a matn about oaths.
2
Determine time boundaries for al-‘ishā’
The text states 'من زوال الشمس إلى نصف الليل' — from the decline of the sun (noon) to midnight. This is the period covered by 'al-‘ishā’' in oath contexts.
The commentator supports this by noting that noon and afternoon prayers are called 'صلاتا العشاء' in hadith.
3
Analyze al-sahūr linguistically
Notice the two possible vowelizations: with fatḥa (السَّحُور) it is a noun meaning the food eaten at saḥar time; with ḍamma (السُّحُور) it is a verbal noun meaning the act of eating that food.
The time of saḥar is defined as the last sixth of the night.
4
Confirm with prophetic tradition
The hadith 'تسحروا فإن في السحور بركة' (Take suḥūr, for in suḥūr there is blessing) is cited, showing the term's usage and the merit of the meal.
This hadith is found in both Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī and Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim.

Invalidity of Specifying by Intention in Oaths of Wearing, Eating, Drinking
States the ruling on swearing not to act and intending a specific item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an intention to specify a particular item in an oath of wearing, eating, or drinking is invalid, and explain the linguistic reasoning based on the essence (māhiyyah) and the concept of muqtaḍā.

Core Idea
Intention to specify a particular item in an oath of wearing, eating, or drinking has no effect; the oath covers all instances.

When someone swears 'I will not wear', 'I will not eat', or 'I will not drink', they might think that intending a specific garment, food, or drink narrows the oath. The Hanafi school, however, holds that such an intention is completely ineffective, both legally and religiously. This lesson unpacks the linguistic and usul reasoning behind this ruling, focusing on the nature of the verb, the concept of māhiyyah (essence), and the role of implied necessity (muqtaḍā).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the ruling directly from the matn: if a person swears by saying 'if I wear, eat, or drink' and intends a specific item, his intention is not accepted at all, either in court or religiously. The consequence (e.g., freeing a slave) applies to any act of wearing, eating, or drinking.
Note: The phrase 'lam yuṣaddaq aṣlan' means complete rejection of the intention.
2
Understand the reason based on māhiyyah
The verb 'labisa' (to wear) is derived from 'lubs', which denotes the essence (māhiyyah) of the action, not individual instances. Similarly, 'akala' and 'shariba' denote the essence of eating and drinking. Since the oath is for prevention, what is negated is the absolute act, not a specific instance.
The essence (māhiyyah) is what is common to all individuals; it is not general like 'every instance' but rather the universal concept.
3
Examine the concept of muqtaḍā
The object worn, eaten, or drunk is a necessary concomitant (muqtaḍā) for the action to occur. However, the muqtaḍā is a meaning, not a wording, and thus has no generality (lā 'umūma lahu). Therefore, it cannot be specified by intention.
Because the necessity is satisfied by a single individual, there is no requirement to affirm all individuals.
4
Refine the concept with Fatḥ al-Qadīr
A deeper investigation clarifies that this is not truly iqtiḍā’ but rather omission of the object (ḥadhf al-maf‘ūl) for brevity. The object is a necessary part of the action, so its generality in the context of negation is a necessity of the act, not from wording, and thus does not accept specification.
This refinement prevents the misapplication of iqtiḍā’ to every verb-object relationship.
5
Address the objection about indefinite noun in negation
If one explicitly says 'I will not eat an eating (aklan)', the indefinite noun in the context of negation is indeed general and can be specified by intention. But the original oath uses only the verb, which denotes essence, so the objection does not apply.
The difference lies in the wording: the verb alone vs. the verb followed by a verbal noun in the negative.

The Lam of Ikhtisas in Oaths: Command vs. Ownership
Explaining the ruling of lam in oaths involving actions by others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the lam in an oath like 'bā'tu laka' implies the addressee's command and apply this to determine whether an oath is broken.

Core Idea
The lam in 'bā'tu laka' specifies the action to the addressee, requiring his command; without it, the oath is not broken.

This passage delves into a subtle but crucial rule about oaths involving actions that can be performed by someone else. The presence of the letter lam changes the legal meaning: it indicates that the action is specifically for the addressee, which in turn requires that the addressee's command be involved. Understanding this rule helps you determine when an oath is actually broken.

If the action occurs without the addressee's command (e.g., he sells the item himself), the oath is not broken because the condition for fulfillment was not met.
The swearer does not owe kaffarah.

This rule only applies to verbs where substitution is possible, like selling, buying, tailoring. For actions that cannot be delegated, different rules apply.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling concerning oaths that contain the Arabic letter lam attached to a verb that can be performed by someone else (e.g., selling, buying, dyeing). The author explains that when such a lam enters into the oath, it necessitates the addressee's command (amr), meaning that the swearer is swearing to do an action specifically for the addressee. Consequently, if the addressee acts without the swearer's knowledge or command, the oath is not broken. The commentary elaborates on the position of the lam (between verb and object) and its significance. It also discusses the condition that the verb must be one that can be delegated (niyabah). The passage cites various authorities like al-Bahr, al-Zahiriyya, and al-Fath to clarify nuances, such as the case where the swearer orders the addressee to buy something for his own son. The key point is that the lam indicates ikhtisas (specification) and requires that the action be performed by the swearer's command, not independently by the addressee.

Key Terms
لام (lam): The letter lam that enters upon a verb to indicate specification (ikhtisas) to the addressee, implying his command.
النيابة (al-niyabah): Substitution or delegation; the ability for one person to perform an action on behalf of another.
الاختصاص (al-ikhtisas): Specification; the lam makes the verb specific to the addressee, requiring the addressee's command.
