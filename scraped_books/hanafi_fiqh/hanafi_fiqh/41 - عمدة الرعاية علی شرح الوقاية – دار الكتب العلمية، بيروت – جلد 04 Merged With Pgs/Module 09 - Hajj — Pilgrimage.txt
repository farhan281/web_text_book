Oath to Walk to the Kaaba: Hajj or Umrah Required
Ruling on an oath to walk to the Kaaba, requiring Hajj or Umrah on foot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath to walk to the House of Allah and explain the reasoning based on customary practice.

Core Idea
An oath to walk to the Kaaba obligates Hajj or Umrah on foot due to customary understanding of the wording.

This lesson examines a specific oath: 'Upon me is walking to the House of Allah.' While the literal meaning is merely walking, Hanafi jurists rule that such an expression obligates a full Hajj or Umrah performed on foot. The reasoning relies on customary usage ('urf) which treats this vow as a commitment to the pilgrimage act itself, not just the mode of transport.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an oath or vow to walk to the House of Allah. The ruling is that it obligates Hajj or Umrah on foot (wajib). Note that the obligation arises from the vow, not from the original obligation of Hajj.
The ruling is specific to the wording 'upon me is walking to the House of Allah'.
2
Analyze the linguistic and customary interpretation
Understand that the phrase 'walking to the House of Allah' is interpreted metaphorically. Customarily, such a phrase is understood as a commitment to perform Hajj or Umrah, because 'walking to the Kaaba' implies the entire pilgrimage journey.
The text mentions that this is a 'linguistic metaphor and a customary reality' (majaz lughawi wa haqiqah 'urfiyyah).
3
Recognize the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The passage notes that without customary interpretation, analogy would require nothing to be obligatory because walking is not a specific act of worship. However, 'urf transforms the meaning, making the vow effective.
This shows the role of 'urf in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.
4
Apply the ruling to different locations
The ruling has practical details: if the person is not in Makkah, he must walk from his own house (preponderant view). If he is in Makkah, he must walk from the Haram to Arafat for Hajj, or go to the Hill for Umrah. This shows the precise implementation.
The dispute about starting point (house vs. miqat) is resolved by al-Nahr's preference.

Commentary
This passage continues the chapter on oaths, specifically regarding oaths to walk to the House of Allah. The author clarifies that such an expression ('upon me is walking to the House of Allah') creates an obligation to perform Hajj or Umrah on foot. The commentary explains that this wording, though a vow (nathr), is treated like an oath. The term 'House of Allah' is explained as a honorific addition, and a caution is given against anthropomorphic interpretations. The ruling is justified by customary usage (urf) making 'Aliyya (upon me) effectively a commitment to Hajj or Umrah. The final notes detail the practical implementation: from one's hometown if not in Makkah, and from the Haram or Hill if in Makkah. This ruling is from the Hanafi school, based on the principle that customary practice can elevate a literal expression to a legal obligation.

Key Terms

النذر (al-nathr): A vow, a commitment to perform an act of worship; here it is considered similar to an oath in its binding nature.

Testimony Proving Non-Performance of Hajj: Dispute on Acceptability
Intra-Hanafi dispute on accepting testimony that implies a negative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf from Muhammad al-Shaybani on accepting testimony that implies a negative, and apply the rule that testimony must correspond to the claim.

Core Idea
Testimony proving a fact that implies a negation is not accepted unless the witness has direct knowledge of the negation.

This passage examines a fiqh scenario where two witnesses testify that a master sacrificed his animal in Kufa, which implies he did not perform Hajj, thus freeing his slave. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf reject the testimony, while Muhammad accepts it. The disagreement is about whether testimony about a negation can be accepted and whether the testimony must directly address the claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal claim
Read the case: a master says, 'If I do not perform Hajj this year, you are free.' Witnesses later testify that the master sacrificed in Kufa. The slave claims freedom based on the non-performance of Hajj. Determine what exactly is being claimed and what the witnesses are testifying about.
The claim is that the master did not perform Hajj (a negative). The testimony is about a positive act (sacrifice in Kufa) that implies the negative.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf and their reasoning
According to them, the slave is not freed. They argue that testimony about a negation is not acceptable. Even if the testimony is about an existent act (sacrifice), it is not linked to the claim; it is 'testimony without a claim' (shahada bi-la mutalib).
Key principle: testimony must be in response to a specific claim.
3
State Muhammad al-Shaybani's position and his reasoning
Muhammad argues the slave is freed. His reasoning: the testimony is about an existent matter (sacrifice in Kufa) which necessarily implies the non-performance of Hajj. Testimony about negation is acceptable when the witness has direct knowledge, as in conditional manumission cases like the house-entry example.
Muhammad uses an analogy to prove acceptance.
4
Analyze the rebuttals and responses
The commentary presents a rebuttal: Muhammad's analogy is rejected because in the house-entry case, the witness directly observed the slave being outside, whereas here the witness only observed the sacrifice, which does not directly prove non-Hajj. Also, the claim is only about negation, and testimony must match the claim.
The passage in al-Fath objects that the slave has no right in the sacrifice, questioning the criterion of 'existent matter implying the claim'.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If testimony is accepted (Muhammad's view), the slave is freed. If not (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's view), the slave remains enslaved. This shows the real-world impact of the juristic disagreement.
The commentary seems to lean toward Muhammad's view, but no final tarjih is given.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of testimony that indirectly proves a negative (non-performance of Hajj). The scenario: a master says to his slave, 'If I do not perform Hajj this year, you are free.' Later, two witnesses testify that the master sacrificed his animal in Kufa on the day of Nahr. This testimony implies that the master was in Kufa, not in Mecca, so he did not perform Hajj. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that the testimony is not accepted and the slave is not freed, because it is testimony about a negation (that he did not perform Hajj). Muhammad al-Shaybani rules that the slave is freed because the testimony is based on a known existent matter (the sacrifice in Kufa), which necessarily implies the negation of Hajj. The commentary then explains the reasoning: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that testimony is only for proving existent matters, and here the claim is a negation. Even if the testimony is about an existent act (sacrifice), it is without a proper claim because the slave's claim is only about the negation of Hajj. Muhammad counters that testimony about negation is acceptable when the witness has direct knowledge, similar to cases like a conditional manumission based on non-entry into a house. The commentary in al-Fath objects that the slave has no right to the sacrifice, and the criterion should be whether the testified matter is an existent that necessarily implies the claim. The passage ends mid-argument, likely continuing in the source.

Key Terms
شهادة على النفي (shahāda ‘alā al-nafy): Testimony that directly or indirectly asserts the non-existence of an act.
شهادة بلا مطالب (shahāda bi-lā muṭālib): Testimony that does not correspond to the claim being made in the lawsuit.
