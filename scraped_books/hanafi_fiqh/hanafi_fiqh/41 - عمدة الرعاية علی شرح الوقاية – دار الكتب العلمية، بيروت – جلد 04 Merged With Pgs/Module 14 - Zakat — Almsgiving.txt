Nisab of Theft: Ten Dirhams or Equivalent
Commentary on the nisab for the hadd of theft, with defense against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi nisab for theft, identify the primary proof, and refute the two objections raised against the proof.

Core Idea
The hand of a thief is cut only for theft of ten dirhams or equivalent—a condition established by mutawatir hadith, not isolated reports.

In this passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, the commentator elucidates the nisab (threshold) for implementing the hadd of theft. He presents the Hanafi position that the stolen property must reach ten dirhams (or equivalent) and defends this ruling against objections that the evidence is weak or that the Quranic verse is absolute. This lesson will walk you through the proof structure and the response to criticisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The passage states that the nisab is the amount for which cutting is obligatory. Recognize that the ruling (hukm) is the cutting of the hand, and the condition (shart) is the nisab of ten dirhams or equivalent.
The nisab is a condition for the hadd, not the hadd itself.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'The hand is not cut except for a dinar or ten dirhams.' Identify this as the core proof and note its narrators and chains.
Multiple narrations strengthen the proof even if individual chains are criticized.
3
Review the supporting narrations
The passage lists several hadith about the shield (mijann) valued at ten dirhams. Understand that these establish the historical practice and the standard of value.
The shield serves as a tangible reference for the nisab.
4
Understand the objections
Two objections are raised: (1) the hadith chains are weak, (2) these are ahad reports that cannot restrict the absolute Quranic verse. Grasp the challenge to the Hanafi position.
The objectors imply the evidence is insufficient for a hadd punishment.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The author responds: (1) weakness is compensated by multiplicity, (2) the common meaning of the hadith (that there is a nisab) is mutawatir, so the restriction is qat'i (certain), not zanni (speculative).
This response defends the use of the hadith to specify the Quran without abrogation.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya on the matn of al-Wiqaya concerning the nisab (minimum threshold) for the hadd of theft. The author begins by restating the matn: 'And its nisab is the value of ten (dirhams).' He notes that the original commentator (al-Sharh) did not elaborate on this point because it is obvious. The first objection is that the commentator's reasoning would prove a broader claim; this is dismissed because the matter is clear. Then the author explains that the nisab for silver is ten dirhams, and for other metals the equivalent value. The primary evidence (asl) is the hadith: 'The hand is not cut except for a dinar or ten dirhams.' He then lists multiple narrations to strengthen the proof, including hadith from Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Abbas, Ayman, Umm Ayman, and others, all supporting the value of a shield (mijann) as ten dirhams or one dinar at the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him). After citing these, he addresses two objections. The first objection claims that the hadith specifying ten dirhams are weak. The author responds that not all chains are weak, and even if some are, the multiplicity of chains compensates. The second objection is that these are ahad (isolated) hadith, and using them to restrict the absolute Quranic verse (5:38) is against usul. The author refutes this by arguing that the hadith indicating that cutting is not for every theft but only above a nisab are mutawatir (mass-transmitted) in meaning, even if the specific wording about ten dirhams is ahad. Therefore, the restriction is based on certain (qat'i) evidence, not on speculative (zanni) reports. He concludes that this does not constitute abrogation of the Quran by ahad reports, but rather restriction by mutawatir reports.

Key Terms
نصاب (nisab): The minimum value of stolen property that makes the hadd of cutting the hand obligatory.
مجن (mijann): A shield; used as a standard commodity to determine the nisab value in the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him).
الظاهرية (al-Zahiriyyah): The Zahiri school of thought, which relies on the apparent meaning of texts without analogy or restriction.

Recipients of the Khums and Their Conditions
Explains khums recipients, conditions, and an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of khums recipients from Quran 8:41, identify the condition of poverty for orphans and near kin, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the shares of the Prophet and near kin after his death.

Core Idea
Khums has five shares; orphans and near kin deserve only if poor, and the caliphs dropped the Prophet's and kin's shares after his death.

This lesson examines the Quranic basis for distributing the khums (one-fifth of booty) and the conditions attached to each category. The passage clarifies that orphans, near kin, and wayfarers are eligible only if they are poor, and it addresses a historical dispute within the Hanafi school about whether the shares of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his relatives continue after his death. By understanding these rulings, you will be able to correctly apply the distribution rules in real cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary dalil
Locate the verse (Al-Anfal 8:41) cited as the basis for khums distribution. Note that it lists six categories: Allah, Messenger, near kin, orphans, poor, wayfarer.
Scholars agree that Allah's share is for blessing and combined with the Prophet's share, resulting in five practical shares.
2
Distinguish agreed vs. disputed shares
Recognize that the shares of orphans, poor, and wayfarer are agreed upon. The dispute centers on the Prophet's share (ceased at his death) and the near kin's share (whether it continues and under what condition).
3
Analyze the condition of poverty
Understand that orphans and near kin are eligible only if they are poor. The passage explicitly states that an orphan with sufficient wealth is not entitled. The near kin are also restricted to the poor among them.
The phrase 'وقدم فقراء ذوي القربي عليهم' indicates precedence of poor kin over other poor and wayfarer.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Read Abu Yusuf's narration from Ibn Abbas that the caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali) divided khums into three shares, dropping the Prophet's and kin's shares. This represents one position within the Hanafi school.
The commentator does not reject this report but presents it as part of the discussion. The later mufta bihi retains the kin's share for the poor among them.
5
Evaluate the objection and its answer
Note the objection: if orphans deserve only due to poverty, why mention them separately after 'poor'? The answer is to remove the misconception that orphans have no right because they do not fight (jihad).
The same rationale applies to wayfarers and near kin—they are mentioned to counter specific misconceptions.

This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the recipients of the khums (one-fifth) from war booty. Division of Khums and Prohibition of Sadaqah for Bani Hashim
Ruling on division of khums into three shares and prohibition of sadaqah on Bani Hashim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the division of khums after the Prophet's death and explain why the shares of the Prophet and his relatives were dropped.

Core Idea
Khums is divided into three shares because the shares of the Prophet and his relatives ceased with his death, as established by the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs.

The division of war spoils (khums) involves a key difference between the Hanafi school and others regarding the number of shares. While the apparent meaning of the Quranic verse and a hadith suggest five categories, the Hanafi school holds that only three remain after the Prophet's death. This lesson examines the evidence—particularly the practice of the Caliphs—that supports the Hanafi view, as well as the absolute prohibition of sadaqah for Bani Hashim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue – five shares vs. three shares
Recognize that the passage addresses whether khums is divided into five categories (as per Quran 8:41) or three (as per Hanafi school). The apparent text lists five recipients, but the Hanafi school argues that two categories—share of Allah (interpreted as share of the Prophet) and share of relatives—ceased after the Prophet's death.
The verse mentions 'for Allah, the Messenger, and the near relatives, orphans, the needy, and the wayfarer' (Quran 8:41).
2
Understand the proof from the Caliphs' practice
The passage cites the action of the Rightly Guided Caliphs as decisive evidence (dalil qati') that overrides the apparent meaning of the hadith. The Hanafi school treats this practice as a form of ijma.
The Caliphs (Abu Bakr, 'Umar, 'Uthman, 'Ali) after the Prophet's death discontinued the shares of the Prophet and his relatives.
3
Distinguish between the two indications of the hadith of compensation
The hadith has two aspects: establishing compensation for Bani Hashim (which remains) and specifying five shares (which is overridden). The Hanafi response preserves the compensation principle while rejecting the five-share division.
The hadith of 'iwad (compensation) is cited in al-Inayah: it gives two indications, but the five-share indication is abrogated by the Caliphs' consensus.
4
Apply the ruling to the prohibition of sadaqah on Bani Hashim
The passage also discusses whether Bani Hashim can receive sadaqah when khums is unavailable. The relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwayah) is absolute prohibition due to their honor and the impurity of sadaqah.
This prohibition is timeless, based on hadith in Sahih Muslim that sadaqah is 'the impurities of people'.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and its resolution
A rare narration from Abu Hanifa allows sadaqah when khums is unavailable, but this is not from Zahir al-Riwayah. The mufta bihi is the absolute prohibition.
The rare narration is from Abu 'Asimah, but it is not relied upon for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute concerning the division of khums (one-fifth of war spoils) and the entitlement of Bani Hashim. The central issue is whether khums is divided into five shares (for Allah, the Prophet, relatives, orphans, the needy, and wayfarers) as the apparent meaning of Quran 8:41 and a hadith about 'iwad (compensation) suggest, or into three shares (only for orphans, the needy, and wayfarers) as the Hanafi school holds. The Hanafi position relies on the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs (Abu Bakr, 'Umar, 'Uthman, 'Ali), who after the Prophet's death discontinued the shares of the Prophet and his relatives (dhawi al-qurba), distributing khums only to the three remaining categories. The text in al-Inayah presents an objection: if the hadith of compensation is authentic, it mandates five shares; if not, it cannot be used. The Hanafi response is that the hadith has two indications — establishing compensation and stating five shares — but the decisive evidence (the Caliphs' action) overrides the five-share division while preserving the compensation itself. Thus, khums is divided into three shares. Regarding Bani Hashim, the text notes a rare narration from Abu Hanifa via Abu 'Asimah that when khums is unavailable, sadaqah becomes permissible for them because the prohibition was tied to their right in khums. However, the relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwayah) is absolute prohibition of sadaqah for them, based on hadith in Sahih Muslim indicating that the prohibition is due to their honor and the impurity of sadaqah, which is timeless.

Key Terms
الخمس (al-khums): One-fifth of war spoils that is set aside for specific categories as per Quran 8:41.
العوض (al-'iwad): Compensation from khums given to Bani Hashim in lieu of their prohibition from taking sadaqah.
ذوي القربى (dhawi al-qurba): The near relatives of the Prophet (Bani Hashim) who are entitled to a share of khums.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, the commentator expands on the base text's claim that certain lands are 'ushri (subject to 'ushr tithe) rather than kharaj (land tax). The most striking case is Basra, which geographically falls within the kharaj region of Sawad, yet the Companions imposed 'ushr on it, setting aside analogy. Tax Classification of Revived Mawat: Proximity vs. Water Source
Ruling on tax classification of revived mawat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi criteria for determining whether revived land is 'ushri or kharaji.

Core Idea
Tax on revived mawat depends on proximity to land type or, per Muhammad, on water source.

After establishing ownership, the passage discusses how to classify revived land for tax purposes. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf use proximity to existing 'ushri or kharaji land, while Muhammad al-Shaybani looks to the water source. This affects the amount and type of tax.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two tax categories
'Ushri land is originally Arab land, paying 'ushr (tithe). Kharaji land is conquered land, paying fixed kharaj. These categories are inherited from historical context.
2
Apply the proximity criterion
If revived land is close to 'ushri land, it is 'ushri; if close to kharaji, it is kharaji. This is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
3
Apply Muhammad's water source criterion
Muhammad considers the water used: if from a well, spring, rainwater, or public river (Tigris/Euphrates), it is 'ushri; if from rivers dug by Persians ('Ajam), it is kharaji.
4
Identify the practical difference
The same land could be classified differently depending on which criterion is applied, leading to different tax liabilities.
For example, a plot near kharaji land but irrigated by a well: proximity makes it kharaji, water source makes it 'ushri.
5
Note the mufta bihi
The text does not give a clear tarjih; both views are transmitted. However, the earlier view (proximity) is more widely followed in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
The finger is six barley grains placed bellies together; the cubit's length varies (24 or 32 fingers) affecting tithe thresholds.

Stipend from Bayt al-Mal: Ownership and Effect of Death
Commentary on stipend ruling from Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a stipend (ata) from Bayt al-Mal is not owned before receipt and lapses with death, and apply this ruling to determine the consequences for heirs if the recipient dies before receiving the stipend.

Core Idea
A stipend from the treasury is a gift not owned until receipt — it cannot be inherited or transferred beforehand.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Hidayah clarifies the legal status of stipends (ata) given to judges, fighters, workers, and their dependents from the public treasury. The core ruling is that such stipends are considered gifts (silah) that are not owned until physically received. Consequently, if the recipient dies before receipt, the stipend lapses and does not become part of the estate. This lesson unpacks the categories of recipients, the half-year distinction, and the practical implications for inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of stipend recipients mentioned
The text lists judges, workers (e.g., scribes, coast guards, muhtasib, mufti, teacher without pay), fighters (muqatila) and their dependents, and offspring of these groups. Note that offspring receive an independent stipend, not as inheritance.
The commentary expands 'workers' to include various public servants.
2
Understand the half-year rule
If a recipient dies in the first half of the year (i.e., before six months), he is deprived of the stipend entirely. If he dies in the second half, it is recommended (mustahabb) to give the stipend to his heirs, but it is not an obligation.
The half-year point is a default; the ruler's discretion may apply.
3
Analyze the legal nature of the stipend
The stipend is defined as a 'gift' (silah), not a salary or deferred compensation. Therefore, it is not owned until actual receipt (qabd). Until receipt, it cannot be inherited, sold, gifted, or divided.
This is the key principle that drives the ruling on deprivation at death.
4
Distinguish from owned land grants
The commentary contrasts stipends with lands given by the Imam as ownership (tamlik). For such lands, all rules of ownership apply (inheritance, sale, etc.). The stipend is only a promise of a gift.
This distinction prevents misunderstanding that all treasury grants are alike.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a judge who dies in the third month of the year, the stipend registered in his name lapses; his heirs have no claim. If he died in the tenth month, the ruler may recommend giving it to the heirs, but they cannot demand it.
The practical consequence is that the stipend does not enter the estate.

Ruling on Raising a Foundling (Laqit) – Mandub or Fard?
States ruling and clarifies via qiyas and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on raising a foundling and identify the conditions under which it becomes obligatory (fard).

Core Idea
A foundling's life takes priority: picking him up is recommended but becomes fard when death is feared.

This lesson opens the Book of the Foundling (Kitab al-Laqit) in the Hanafi commentary. It establishes the default ruling as recommended (mandub) but introduces a critical exception: when the child's life is in danger, the ruling escalates to obligatory (fard). The author draws an analogy with lost property (luqata) and clarifies the finder's qualifications.

Guided Path
1
Define the Foundling
Identify the linguistic and legal definition of laqit: a child abandoned due to fear of poverty or accusation. Understand why this chapter follows jihad – life is at stake.
The term laqit is derived from 'picking up' from the ground, passive participle.
2
State the Default Ruling
The base ruling is mandub (recommended). The text says 'raf'uhu mandub' – raising him is desirable.
Mandub means rewarded if done, not sinful if omitted.

If his destruction (halak) is feared, the ruling becomes wajib (here meaning fard). The commentary clarifies that 'wajib' in the text means fard, not technical Hanafi wujub.

The analogy is drawn: lost property (luqata) also becomes fard to pick up when loss is feared. The effective cause (illa) is risk of loss (khawf al-diya' / halak).
The illa is jaliyyah (clear), extracted via tanqih al-manat.
5

The finder must be mukallaf (legally responsible); picking up by a child or insane person is invalid. However, a disbeliever, sinner, restricted slave, or interdicted person may validly pick up.

The Foundling's Freedom and Support from Bayt al-Mal
Ruling statement on the foundling's freedom and state responsibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings regarding the foundling's freedom, maintenance, and inheritance, and identify the conditions under which the public treasury (bayt al-mal) bears responsibility.

Core Idea
A foundling is free unless proven enslaved; the state supports him when he has no means.

This lesson examines the legal status of a foundling (laqṭ) according to the Hanafi school. The passage establishes that the child is presumed free, and his maintenance, liability for crimes, and inheritance are tied to the public treasury (bayt al-mal) under specific conditions. We will unpack the ruling, its conditions, and the evidence from the practice of the early caliphs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three core rulings
Read the first line of the matn: the foundling is free (unless evidence of slavery), his maintenance and jinaayah are from bayt al-mal, and his inheritance goes to bayt al-mal. These three rulings are the basis of the passage.
The term 'jinaayah' here refers to the compensation (diyah) due if the foundling commits a crime.
2
Understand the default presumption of freedom
The commentary explains that freedom is the original state for all human beings, especially in Dār al-Islām. This means the burden of proof is on anyone claiming slavery.
The only way to overturn this is by clear evidence (bayyinah) or the foundling's own confession after adulthood.
3
Examine the conditions for bayt al-mal liability
The commentary specifies that bayt al-mal is responsible only if the foundling has no property and no known relatives. If he has either, his maintenance comes from that source.
This prevents unnecessary burden on the treasury.
4
Analyze the evidence from the Companions
The passage cites reports from Ali and Umar (radiyallahu anhuma) that they spent on foundlings from bayt al-mal. This forms the proof by action (fi‘l al-sahābah), which is authoritative in Hanafi methodology.
The rule for jinaayah and inheritance is analogically extended from this practice.
5
Clarify the finder's role
The finder (multaqit) has no obligation to spend on the foundling unless ordered by the judge. If he volunteers, it is a donation; if the judge orders, it becomes a debt repayable later.
This protects the finder from being forced to bear an unwanted financial burden.

One Partner Paying Zakat of Another Without Permission: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability of zakat agent paying after principal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability of an agent who pays zakat on behalf of the principal after the principal has already paid, distinguishing between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The agent's mandate ends automatically when the principal pays, making the second payer liable even if unaware, per Abu Hanifa.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about zakat payment by one partner on behalf of another. The key issue is whether an agent, having permission to pay the principal's zakat, remains liable if he pays after the principal has already settled the obligation. Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree on the outcome when the agent is unaware of the prior payment. Understanding their reasoning illuminates Hanafi principles of agency, obligation, and the purpose of zakat.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Clearly describe the case: two partners each give permission to the other to pay their zakat. They pay successively. The second is unaware of the first's payment.
The key element is the agent's ignorance of the prior payment.
2
Identify the Disputed Ruling
State that according to Abu Hanifa, the second partner is liable (must compensate the first). According to Sahibayn, he is not liable if ignorant.
If the agent knows, all agree he is liable.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the agent's objective is to relieve the principal of the obligation (isqat). Once the principal pays, the mandate ends automatically (azl hukmi), even without the agent's knowledge.
Azl hukmi is a legal dismissal that does not require notification.
4
Analyze Sahibayn's Reasoning
Sahibayn argue that the agent's duty is simply to transfer ownership (tamlik) to the poor. He performed that duty, so no liability. They analogize to the case of slaughtering the blood of ihsar after the blockade ends.
They distinguish between performing an act and achieving a particular legal effect.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence
The difference matters when the agent is unaware: Abu Hanifa requires compensation; Sahibayn does not. Also, if both pay simultaneously, each compensates the other for the share they paid on their behalf.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf — the practical outcome of the legal difference.

'And if they pay together, each is liable for the portion of the other': like if each pays in the absence of his companion, and their payments coincide in one time, or it is not known which preceded the other, then each is liable for the share of the other.
[2] His saying: 'This according to Abu Hanifa...' etc. Al-Hidaya says: 'And upon this difference is the case of the one ordered (al-ma'mur) to pay zakat: if he gives charity to the poor person after the principal paid by himself. According to them (the two), he is ordered to transfer ownership (tamlik) to the poor person, and he has performed it; so he is not liable to the principal. This is because what is within his capacity is the transfer, not its occurrence as zakat, since that depends on the principal's intention; and he is only required to do what is within his capacity. And it becomes like the one ordered to slaughter the sacrificial animal of ihsar (blockade) if he slaughters after the ihsar has ceased; the pilgrim (hajj) is not liable, whether the agent knows or not. According to Abu Hanifa, he is ordered to pay the zakat, and what was paid did not occur as zakat, so he became disobedient. This is because the objective of the order is to relieve oneself from the duty; for apparently one does not undertake harm except to repel harm; and this objective was achieved by the principal's own payment; the agent's payment was devoid of it, so he is dismissed (ma'zul) whether he knows or not, because it is a legal dismissal (azl hukmi). As for the blood of ihsar, it is said it is upon this difference, and it is said there is a distinction: the blood is not obligatory upon him, for he can wait until the ihsar ceases; in our case, the payment is obligatory, so the absolution (isqat) is considered the objective in it, unlike the blood of ihsar.' [4] His saying: 'and their payments coincided'; thus in the manuscripts; the apparent is 'and their payment coincided' (wa ittafaqa ada'uhuma).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding one partner paying the zakat of another without permission. The matn states that it is not allowed. If both have permission and pay successively, the second is liable (must compensate the first) even if ignorant of the first payment, according to Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if the second is unaware, he is not liable. If both pay simultaneously or the order is unknown, each is liable for the other's share. The 'Hidaya' commentary explains the reasoning: Abu Hanifa views the agent's task as relieving the principal of the obligation; once the principal pays, the agent's mandate is automatically revoked (azl hukmi) even without knowledge. The Sahibayn view that the agent's duty is to effect transfer (tamlik) to the poor; doing so fulfills the order, so no liability, analogous to slaughtering the blood of ihsar after the blockade ends. The passage ends with a note on settling accounts between partners if they are equal in capital (offset) or else reclaim excess.

Key Terms
عزل حكمي (azl hukmi): Automatic legal dismissal without knowledge, applied by Abu Hanifa to the agent after the principal pays zakat.
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership to the recipient; Sahibayn see the agent's duty as tamlik to the poor.
دم الإحصار (dam al-ihsar): The sacrificial animal due when one is prevented from completing Hajj due to blockade or illness.

Partner Paying Zakat Without Permission: Liability and the Sahibayn Dispute
Ruling on a partner paying zakat of co-partner without permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a partner paying zakat for another without permission, and explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the liability of an unaware agent.

Core Idea
An agent who pays zakat on your behalf after you have already paid is liable to you, even if unaware.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah examines a scenario where two partners authorize each other to pay zakat on their respective shares. If one pays after the other has already discharged the obligation, is he liable? The Hanafi masters differ: Abu Hanifa holds him liable; the Sahibayn say he is not. The commentary unpacks the reasoning, contrasting it with analogous cases. The passage also clarifies the need for explicit permission and the effect of simultaneous payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Two partners authorize each other to pay zakat. One pays, then the other pays unaware the obligation is already fulfilled. The question is whether the second is liable.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Abu Hanifa: the second is liable because his payment did not count as zakat (failed to discharge the obligation). The purpose of the command is to escape liability, and since the agent's payment was void, he acted contrary and must compensate.
The proof is based on the purpose of the command (ta'lil).
3
State the Sahibayn position and its proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the agent is not liable because he was ordered to transfer ownership (tamlik), which he did. Whether the payment counts as zakat depends on the principal's intention (niyyah), which is beyond the agent's control.
They analogize to slaughtering a sacrificial animal for ihsar after the ihsar has ended.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: zakat is obligatory (wajib), whereas the blood for ihsar is not obligatory. Therefore, discharging the obligation is the primary purpose, and the agent's failure makes him liable.
This tafriq (differentiation) is key to the Hanafi tarjih.
5
Apply the ruling to simultaneous payment
If both partners pay together, each is liable for the portion of the other. If the partnership is equal (mufawada or 'inan with equal capital), they set off the amounts; otherwise, the one who paid more recovers the excess.
This shows the practical resolution of mutual liability.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling on a partner paying zakat on behalf of his co-partner. It begins by establishing that without explicit permission, such payment is invalid because zakat requires niyyah (intention), and general partnership does not imply permission for zakat payment. When permission is given and both pay consecutively, the second payer is liable to the first if the first's payment already fulfilled the obligation, even if the second was unaware. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), if the second was unaware, he is not liable. Their reasoning: the second was commanded to transfer ownership to the poor, which he did; the fact that it didn't count as zakat due to the principal's earlier payment is not the agent's fault. Abu Hanifa argues that the agent's payment was invalid as zakat, so he acted contrary to the command, thus guaranteeing the amount. The passage then notes that if both pay simultaneously, each guarantees the other's share. If the partnership is of equal capital (mufawada or 'inan with equality), they settle by set-off; otherwise, the one who paid more recovers the excess.

Key Terms
ضَمِنَ (ḍamina): The second partner becomes liable to pay the first partner the amount of zakat he gave, as if he had paid his own money without authorization.
الزَّكاة (al-zakāh): The obligatory charity on wealth, which requires the intention (niyyah) of the giver.
