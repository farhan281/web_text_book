Shibh Amd and Khata: Definitions and Legal Consequences
Defines two categories of homicide: shibh amd and khata, with their respective penalties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shibh amd and khata and state the legal consequences (diyah, kaffarah, sin, qisas) for each.

Core Idea
Shibh amd and khata both require diyah and kaffarah, but shibh amd incurs sin while khata does not, and neither permits qisas.

Homicide in Hanafi law is not monolithic. This passage introduces two categories—shibh amd (resembling intentional) and khata (mistake)—and their distinct consequences. Understanding these categories is essential because they determine whether the killer bears sin, whether qisas (retaliation) applies, and who pays the blood money.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage begins by introducing two types of homicide: shibh amd and khata. Identify the key terms and note the Arabic wording.
2
Define shibh amd by example
The text gives an example: deliberately striking with something that does not generally kill. Understand that the key element is deliberate action with a non-lethal means.
3
Define khata by examples
The text lists several scenarios: shooting at game and hitting a person, rolling over in sleep, etc. Recognize that these are all unintentional, based on mistake or accident.
4
List the legal consequences for each
For shibh amd: diyah on the aqila, sin, kaffarah, no qisas. For khata: diyah on aqila, kaffarah, no sin, no qisas. Note the difference: sin is present in shibh amd but absent in khata.
5
Understand the shared and distinct elements
Both share diyah and kaffarah; both exclude qisas; the only difference is sin.

Or khata, such as shooting at game and hitting a person, or rolling over on him in sleep and killing him, or a mount stepping on him while he is riding it, or falling from a roof onto him, or a stone falling from his hand and causing death—its consequence is kaffarah and diyah upon the aqila, and no sin in it.

Commentary
This passage classifies homicide into two categories: shibh amd (resembling intentional) and khata (error). For shibh amd, the penalty is diyah (blood money) on the aqila (the killer's paternal relatives), the killer bears sin and must pay kaffarah (expiation), but there is no qisas (retaliation). For khata, the same penalties apply—diyah on the aqila and kaffarah—but no sin. The text notes that the ruling on shibh amd is according to both opinions, likely referring to Abu Hanifa and his two companions, though all agree on these consequences.

Key Terms
شبه عمد (shibh amd): An act that resembles intentional killing: the perpetrator deliberately uses an instrument that is not normally lethal, resulting in death.
خطأ (khata): Unintentional killing through accident or mistake, such as shooting at game and hitting a person.
عاقلة (aqila): The group of paternal relatives (asabah) of the killer who are responsible for paying diyah in cases of homicide.
قود (qisas): Retaliatory punishment in kind for intentional homicide or injury.
دية (diyah): Monetary compensation paid to the victim's heirs for homicide or injury.
