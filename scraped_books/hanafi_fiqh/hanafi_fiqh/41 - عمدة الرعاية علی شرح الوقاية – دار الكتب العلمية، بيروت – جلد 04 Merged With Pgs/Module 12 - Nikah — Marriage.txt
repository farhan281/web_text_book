Umm Walad Purchased After Oath Does Not Fulfill Kaffarah
Ruling that an umm walad purchased after an oath to free her does not count for kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why purchasing an umm walad for kaffarah does not discharge the oath and distinguish it from purchasing a relative.

Core Idea
An umm walad's prior right to freedom makes her emancipation insufficient for kaffarah.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a man swears an oath to free a slave as kaffarah and suspends it on purchasing a particular slave. If that slave is his umm walad (who already has a right to freedom through bearing his child), the emancipation upon purchase does not fulfill the kaffarah. The commentary explains the reasoning and contrasts it with the purchase of a relative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and condition
Recognize that the man makes a suspended oath: 'If I buy you, you are free for my kaffarah.' The slave in question is his umm walad, who has borne his child through marriage.
The condition is the purchase.
2
Apply the ruling
Upon purchase, she becomes free due to the condition, but the emancipation does not count for the kaffarah.
The ruling is clear: free but not sufficient.
3
Understand the rationale
The umm walad already has a right to freedom through istilad (bearing a child). Thus, her emancipation is partly due to that prior right, not solely from the oath.
Istilad creates a right that precedes the purchase.
4
Distinguish from the case of a relative
Unlike the umm walad, a relative (e.g., a brother) is fully in slavery; purchasing him after a suspended oath results in complete emancipation that counts for kaffarah because no prior right exists.
The key difference: completeness of slavery at the time of purchase.
5
Note the linguistic aside
The passage clarifies the term 'tasarri' (taking a concubine) and its derivation from 'sirr' (secret).
Linguistic detail, not core to the ruling.

Qadhf Dropped for Eternal Prohibition Intercourse
Ruling on qadhf when intercourse is eternally forbidden, with Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between eternal and temporary prohibitions and apply the Hanafi rule that hadd al-qadhf is dropped when the accused person's intercourse is eternally prohibited.

Core Idea
Eternal prohibition creates doubt that drops hadd al-qadhf — even ownership cannot restore honor.

In cases where a person commits zinā with someone who is eternally forbidden to him, such as a foster sister, the Hanafi school holds that the one who accuses him does not receive the ḥadd punishment for qadhf. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this ruling and the intra-Hanafi dispute with al-Karkhi, who argued for applying hadd. Understanding this distinction requires analyzing the nature of the prohibition—whether it is inherent (ḥarām li-aynihi) or temporary (ḥarām li-ghayrihi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is accusing a person of zinā with his slave girl who is eternally forbidden (e.g., foster sister). The ruling from the matn is that hadd is not applied.
2
Classify the prohibition
The prohibition of fosterage is eternal (ḥurma mu'abbada) and falls under ḥarām li-aynihi (forbidden in itself). It cannot be removed at any time.
Distinguish from temporary prohibitions like menstruation or Magian religion.
3
Examine the ikhtilaf
Al-Karkhi argues that ownership exists, so the case is analogous to a Magian slave girl, and hadd should apply.
His dalil is qiyas based on ownership creating a semblance of lawfulness.
4
Apply the Hanafi rebuttal
The rebuttal is that the prohibition of fosterage is permanent and can never be lifted, unlike the Magian case where Islam could make the intercourse lawful. Therefore, the doubt (shubha) is stronger, and hadd is dropped.
This reasoning appears in al-Fath and is the relied-upon position.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: the accuser either receives 80 lashes (al-Karkhi) or is free from punishment (Abu Hanifa and majority).
The mufta bihi is that hadd is dropped.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the dropping of hadd al-qadhf when the accused person's illicit intercourse is of a type that is eternally forbidden (haram li-aynihi). The matn states three cases: intercourse with a jointly owned slave girl, intercourse with a slave girl who is eternally forbidden (e.g., foster sister), and intercourse that is not in full ownership. The commentary, from the 'Umda, expands by quoting Abu Hanifa's condition that the eternal prohibition must be established by ijma' or mashhoor hadith to be free of doubt. It then divides prohibition into two types: haram li-aynihi (in itself) and haram li-ghayrihi (for another reason). The former arises from two subcategories: intercourse without any ownership (absolute zinā or with a jointly owned slave) and intercourse with a woman eternally forbidden even if owned (like a foster sister). The correct ruling is that no hadd is applied to the one who accuses such a person. The commentary then addresses a dispute with al-Karkhi: he would apply hadd because ownership exists, equating it with a Magian slave girl. The 'Umda rejects this, arguing that the prohibition in fosterage is eternal and the substance was never lawful, unlike the temporary prohibition of a Magian slave girl which can be removed by Islam. Thus, the correct view follows Abu Hanifa that hadd is dropped.

حرام لعينه (ḥarām li-aynihi): An act that is forbidden in itself due to its inherent harm or impurity, such as zinā.

Qadhf for Zina During Kufr
Ruling that no hadd for qadhf of zinā during kufr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qadhf for zinā committed during kufr and explain the legal reasoning.

Core Idea
No hadd for accusing a Muslim of zinā committed before Islam.

This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'aya clarifies an important nuance in the law of qadhf. If a person accuses a Muslim woman of zinā but that act occurred before she embraced Islam, the ḥadd penalty for false accusation (qadhf) does not apply. The reasoning is that the woman's chastity (iḥsān) in Islamic law is only considered from the moment of her Islam; her past as a non-Muslim does not diminish her post-Islam status. The text expands this ruling to cover various scenarios – including whether the woman was a harbi or dhimmi, and where the zinā took place – and draws an analogy to accusing a freed slave of zinā committed while enslaved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
Read the opening phrase: 'ولا بقذف من زنت في كفرها'. What is the ruling? Who is being accused?
The ruling is that there is no hadd for qadhf. The accused is a woman who committed zinā while a disbeliever.
2
Analyze the scope (إطلاق)
The commentary states the ruling is 'absolute' (إطلاقه). Identify the cases it includes: harbi/dhimmi, dar al-harb/dar al-Islam, and whether the accusation was specific or general.
The ruling is not limited by the woman's sub-status or location of the zinā.
3
Understand the analogy (قياس)
The text gives an analogy: accusing an emancipated slave of zinā as a slave. Explain why this analogy fits: just as the slave had no capacity for iḥsān during slavery, the kāfira had no Islamic chastity during kufr.
The common illa (effective cause) is the absence of iḥsān at the time of the act.
4
Recognize the cross-madhhab dispute
The analytical toolbox indicates this is mukhtalaf fih (disputed). Other schools may apply hadd if the accuser intends to defame after Islam. Think about why Hanafis differ.
Hanafis focus on the act itself, not the intention behind the accusation, in this context.

Qadhf of a Mukatab Who Dies with Enough Wealth
Ruling that no hadd for qadhf of a mukatab who dies leaving sufficient wealth for kitaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qadhf of a mukatab who dies with sufficient wealth and explain the role of Sahabi ikhtilaf in creating doubt that drops the hadd.

Core Idea
Disagreement among the Companions over the mukatab's status creates legal doubt, and hadd is dropped.

In this lesson, we examine a specific case: what happens when a person accuses a mukatab (a slave under a manumission contract) of zinā, and then the mukatab dies leaving enough wealth to pay off his kitaba? The Hanafi ruling is that no hadd is applied, and the reason lies in a disagreement among the Companions about whether the mukatab is considered free at the moment of death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qadhf (false accusation of zinā) against a mukatab who dies leaving sufficient wealth to pay his kitaba. The ruling is that no hadd punishment is applied.
2
Locate the reason (illah) given in the text
The text states that hadd is only obligatory for qadhf of a free person. The status of this particular mukatab is uncertain.
The key condition for hadd al-qadhf is that the victim must be free.
3
Examine the Sahabi ikhtilaf
The Companions disagreed: Ali and Ibn Mas'ud considered the mukatab to have died free, while Zaid bin Thabit considered him a slave. This ikhtilaf is cited in the commentary.
Each opinion has its basis, but neither is declared definitively correct.
4
Apply the principle of doubt (shubha)
In Hanafi usul, punishments (hadd) are averted in cases of doubt. The existence of a legitimate ikhtilaf among authoritative figures creates shubha.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The accuser does not receive the hadd punishment. The ruling preserves the principle of caution in hudud.
The text does not discuss tazir, but the hadd is definitely dropped.

Qadhf Applied for Intercourse Temporarily Forbidden (Ḥarām li-Ghayrihi)
Ruling statement with expansion: When hadd is applied for qadhf of one whose intercourse is temporarily forbidden.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on applying hadd al-qadhf when the accused person's intercourse is temporarily forbidden (ḥarām li-ghayrihi) and apply it to given examples.

Core Idea
Hadd al-qadhf applies when the prohibition is temporary; it does not apply if the prohibition is permanent (ḥarām li-ʿaynihi).

This passage clarifies a nuanced rule in Hanafi jurisprudence regarding qadhf (false accusation of zinā). It states that the hadd punishment for the accuser is only dropped if the accused person's act was inherently and permanently forbidden (ḥarām li-ʿaynihi). If the prohibition is due to an external factor that can be removed, the accuser is still subject to hadd. This distinction is crucial for proper application of hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being judged
The passage discusses qadhf of a person who engaged in intercourse that is forbidden due to an external reason (ḥarām li-ghayrihi). Understand that the ruling is about the accuser, not the accused.
The accuser is the one who makes the false accusation of zinā.
2
Distinguish ḥarām li-ʿaynihi from ḥarām li-ghayrihi
Ḥarām li-ʿaynihi is inherently forbidden (e.g., zinā). Ḥarām li-ghayrihi is forbidden because of an external factor that can be removed (e.g., menstruation, disbelief). Hadd for qadhf is not applied if the act was ḥarām li-ʿaynihi; it is applied if it was ḥarām li-ghayrihi.
This distinction affects whether the accused person is considered to have committed zinā or not.
3
Apply the rule to examples
Work through the examples given: wife menstruating, Magian slave girl, mukateb slave girl, married slave girl, and slave girl purchased with a fasid contract. In each case, the prohibition is temporary, so hadd is applied to the accuser.
For the Magian, prohibition ends if she converts; for the mukateb, it ends if she pays and becomes free.
4
Understand the exception: fasid marriage vs. fasid purchase
A fasid purchase transfers ownership, so the prohibition is temporary (ḥarām li-ghayrihi). A fasid marriage does not transfer ownership, so the prohibition is permanent (ḥarām li-ʿaynihi). Consequently, hadd is applied in the purchase case but not in the marriage case.
Ownership is key: if the man owns the woman, the prohibition can be lifted (e.g., by converting or paying).

The Hanafi school determines death of the missing by death of peers, but practical necessity led later scholars to fix ages like 90 or 60.

When a person goes missing and their life or death is unknown, Islamic law must provide a mechanism to settle their property and allow their spouse to remarry. Commentary
This passage deals with the Hanafi ruling on when a missing person (mafqud) is legally considered dead. The author first presents the Zahir al-Riwayah position: the determination is based on the death of his peers (aqran). The commentator (the sharh) then argues that the most fitting method for fiqh is not to fix any specific age, as there is no text and no room for qiyas in matters of measure, similar to determining compensation for destroyed goods—it depends on the circumstances. The author of 'al-Bahr' criticized those who fixed ages, but 'al-Nahr' replied that investigating peers' deaths is impractical, so estimation by years was adopted for necessity. Later, 'Radd al-Muhtar' reconciles by stating that the fixed ages are interpretations of the 'death of peers' principle, not contradictions. The later scholars chose sixty years because most people die before that age, and rule is by the predominant. The passage then discusses the consequences: if the missing person returns alive before the period, he reclaims his property; after the period, his property is distributed as of that day, his wife observes a widow's iddah (four months ten days), and she may remarry. The need for a qadi's ruling is disputed; the chosen view is that it is necessary because death is probable, not certain.

Core Idea
The marriage bond is too strong to dissolve on mere probability; the wife waits until death is confirmed.

This passage addresses one of the most emotionally and legally intense scenarios in Islamic family law: a husband vanishes, and his wife does not know if she is married or widowed. The Hanafi school takes a rigorous position, requiring definitive knowledge before allowing remarriage, while other schools set a fixed period. Understanding the Hanafi reasoning reveals the school's commitment to preserving the marriage bond.

They rule that she must wait indefinitely until she has definitive knowledge of death or divorce, or until a judge decrees his death. Their evidence includes athar from Ali and Ibn Mas'ud, and qiyas on the strength of the marriage bond.

They hold that she must wait four years, after which she may remarry. They rely on other athar.

Under Hanafi law, the wife may wait many years or even decades; under Maliki/Shafi'i, she can remarry after four years.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute within the commentary on al-Wiqaya. The text first presents the ruling (matn): if a missing person (mafqud) returns alive before the expiry of the period set for judging him dead, he resumes all rights over his property and marital relationship. If the period has passed and he has been judged dead, then the judgment is retroactive to the end of the period: his property is distributed among heirs alive at that time, and his wife observes the death-waiting period (iddah) of four months and ten days, after which she may remarry. The commentator then expands on the ruling, explaining that the judgment of death requires a court decree (qada) because it is only probable. The core of the dispute is whether the wife may remarry after a fixed period if no news arrives. The Hanafi position, attributed to Abu Hanifa and the majority of the school, is that she must wait indefinitely until she has definitive knowledge of his death (either by witness, the decree of a judge after a specific period, or news of divorce). This is based on the principle that the marriage bond is strong and cannot be dissolved on mere probability. The Hanafi proof includes the athar (sayings of companions) from Ali ibn Abi Talib and Ibn Mas'ud, and the agreement of several Tabi'un, that she must wait until she knows. They also cite a marfu' hadith, 'She is his wife until the evidence comes to her,' but admit its chain is weak. The opponents, Imam Malik and Imam al-Shafi'i, rely on other athar and are reported to hold that the wife must wait four years (and then the judge may decree divorce). The commentary concludes that the Hanafi position is supported by principles (usul) and analogy (qiyas), not by strong marfu' hadith. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the Hanafi school is that the wife must wait until the missing person is confirmed dead by a judge's decree after the elapse of a period (typically one year for the Hanafi judges to estimate according to the longest life expectancy of peers, but later scholars preferred sixty years based on common age).

ففي موطأ مالك عن عمر: «أيما امرأة فقدت زوجها فلم تدر أين هو، فإنها تنتظر أربع سنين، ثم تعتد أربعة أشهر وعشرًا ثم تحل». وأخرج ابن أبي شيبة نحوه عن عثمان وابن عباس. وأخرج ابن أبي الدنيا وابن أبي شيبة وعبد الرزاق وغيرهم: «إن رجلاً أخذته الجن ففقد في عهد عمر، فأتته امرأته فأمرها أن تتربص أربع سنين، ثم أمرها أن تعتد، فإذا انقضت عدتها تزوجت». وفي رواية: «ثم أمر بعد التربص وليه أن يطلقها». وذكر صاحب الهداية: إن عمر رجع إلى قول علي، لكنه لم يثبت بسند يعتبر به. بل ذكر الزرقاني في شرح الموطأ أنه روي عن علي نحو ما أفتى به عمر وجابر. وفي هذا المقام على أصحابنا في تركهم هذه الآثار وأخذهم بأثر ابن مسعود وبالقياس أنه قد تقرر في مدارك أصولهم أن قول الصحابي فيما لا يعقل بالرأي في حكم المرفوع، فيقدم على غيره، ومن المعلوم أن أثر عمر وغيره يخالف القياس فيكون مرفوعًا حكمًا، فلا بد أن يؤخذ به ويقدم على الآثار الموافقة للقياس وعلى القياس. وبعد اللتيا والتي نقول: قد صرح جمع من أصحابنا كصاحب جامع الرموز وصاحب الدر المنتقى شرح الملتقى، وصاحب رد المحتار وغيرهم: بأنه لو أفتى حنفي في هذه المسألة بقول مالك عند الضرورة لا بأس به. وعلى هذا علمي، حيث أفتيت غير مرة بقول مالك ظنًا مني أنه قوي من حيث الدليل، ومع قطع النظر عند تقليد مذهب الغير جائز عند الضرورة اتفاقًا، ولست بمتفرد في ذلك، بل وافقت فيه جمعًا من الحنفية.
In the Muwatta of Malik from Umar: 'Any woman who has lost her husband and does not know where he is, she shall wait four years, then observe an 'iddah of four months and ten days, and then she is free to remarry.' Ibn Abi Shaybah extracted something similar from Uthman and Ibn Abbas. Ibn Abi al-Dunya, Ibn Abi Shaybah, Abd al-Razzaq and others extracted: 'A man was seized by jinn and disappeared during the time of Umar. His wife came to him, and he ordered her to wait four years, then ordered her to observe 'iddah, and when her 'iddah ended, she married.' In another narration: 'Then after the waiting period, he ordered her guardian to divorce her.' The author of al-Hidayah mentioned that Umar retracted to the saying of Ali, but this is not established with an acceptable chain. Rather, al-Zarqani in his commentary on al-Muwatta mentioned that something similar to what Umar and Jabir gave as a fatwa is narrated from Ali. At this point, regarding our scholars (Hanafis) leaving these athar and adopting the athar of Ibn Mas'ud and analogy, it is established in the foundations of their principles that the saying of a Companion in matters not subject to opinion is in the position of a marfu' (raised to the Prophet), so it is given precedence over others. It is known that the athar of Umar and others contradicts analogy, so it is marfu' by implication, and it must be acted upon and given precedence over athar that agree with analogy and over analogy. After all that, we say: A number of our scholars, such as the author of Jami' al-Rumuz, the author of al-Durr al-Muntaqa Sharh al-Multaqa, the author of Radd al-Muhtar, and others, have explicitly stated that if a Hanafi gives a fatwa in this matter according to the opinion of Malik in times of necessity, there is no harm. Based on this, my teacher [said]: I gave a fatwa more than once according to Malik's opinion, thinking it strong in terms of evidence, and apart from considering [the necessity], following another madhhab in times of necessity is permissible by agreement, and I am not alone in this; rather, I have been joined in it by a group of Hanafis.

Commentary
This passage discusses an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the ruling on a woman whose husband is missing (mafqud). The author first reports a famous athar from Umar in the Muwatta of Malik: the wife must wait four years, then observe an 'iddah of four months and ten days, after which she may remarry. Similar reports are cited from Uthman, Ibn Abbas, and others. The author notes that some reports indicate Umar retracted to Ali's view, but this is not authenticated. The Hanafi school (as stated by the author) does not follow these athar; rather, they follow the athar of Ibn Mas'ud and analogical reasoning (qiyas). The reason given is that in Hanafi usul, the saying of a Companion in matters not subject to personal opinion is considered marfu' (attributed to the Prophet) and thus is given precedence. Since the athar of Umar contradicts qiyas, it should be considered marfu' and followed, but the Hanafis still do not follow it because they have a different athar (Ibn Mas'ud) that agrees with qiyas, and they prioritize that. The author then states that in cases of necessity, it is permissible for a Hanafi to give a fatwa according to Malik's view, and he himself has done so. This shows a pragmatic approach within the Hanafi school: the operative position is based on Ibn Mas'ud and qiyas (the wife does not wait four years; other rules apply), but when necessity dictates, the Maliki view may be adopted.

Key Terms
المفقود (al-mafqud): A person whose whereabouts are unknown, whether dead or alive.
التربص (al-tarabbus): The waiting period for a woman whose husband is missing, as per the fatwa of Umar: four years.
العدة (al-iddah): The prescribed waiting period after divorce or death of husband; here specifically for a missing husband.
