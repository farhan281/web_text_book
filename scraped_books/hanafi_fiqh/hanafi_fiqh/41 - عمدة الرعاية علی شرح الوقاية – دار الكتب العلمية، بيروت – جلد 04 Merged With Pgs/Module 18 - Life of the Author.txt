Genealogy of the Prophet's Lineage from Hashim to Adnan
Traces prophetic genealogy from Abd al-Muttalib up to Adnan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the lineage of the Prophet from Abd al-Muttalib to Adnan and identify the key figures mentioned in the text.

Core Idea
The Prophet's lineage is verified up to Adnan, who is from the progeny of Isma'il son of Ibrahim.

This passage provides the genealogical background essential for understanding the fiqh ruling on the distribution of khums (the fifth of war spoils) to the Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib. Knowing the precise lineage of the Prophet helps identify who qualifies as a descendant entitled to these shares. The passage traces the noble lineage from the Prophet's grandfather Abd al-Muttalib up to Adnan, with careful attention to pronunciation and varying reports.

Guided Path
1
Start with Abd al-Muttalib
Identify Abd al-Muttalib as the grandfather of the Prophet and list his children, noting that Abdullah is the Prophet's father.
The passage mentions several names; focus on the key one: Abdullah.
2
Ascend to Hashim
Go back one generation to Hashim, noting his original name (Amr) and his nickname derived from crumbling tharid for the needy.
Hashim is known for providing food during famine.
3
Continue to Abd Manaf and Qusayy
Identify Abd Manaf (originally Abd Manat) and Qusayy, with the story of Qusayy's naming after being carried away by his mother.
Remember the pronunciation: Qusayy (diminutive).
4
Reach Quraysh and Adnan
Trace from Kilab to Adnan, noting that Quraysh is the tribe name (from Fihr) and that Adnan is the last verified ancestor.
Adnan is the final point where lineage is agreed upon.
5
Understand the verification point
The passage states that up to Adnan the lineage is verified; beyond there is disagreement. This is important for genealogical accuracy in fiqh contexts such as khums distribution.
The connection from Adnan to Isma'il is agreed, but details beyond Adnan are disputed.

Commentary
This is a biographical passage tracing the lineage of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib up to Adnan. The author discusses the number of Abd al-Muttalib's children, his age, and the death of the Prophet. He then traces the lineage further back through Hashim, Abd Manaf, Qusayy, and up to Adnan, noting various opinions and pronunciation details. The passage also includes notes on the sons of Abd Manaf, particularly identifying Abd Shams as the ancestor of the Umayyads and Nawfal as the ancestor of certain companions. This genealogical information is relevant for understanding the tribal affiliations of the early Muslims and the basis for the distribution of the khums among the Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, which is discussed in the following lesson.

Key Terms
عبد المطلب (Abd al-Muttalib): The grandfather of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him); his name was Shayba but became known as Abd al-Muttalib.
قريش (Quraysh): The dominant tribe of Mecca at the time of the Prophet; the tribe to which the Prophet belonged.
هاشم (Hashim): Great-grandfather of the Prophet; originally named 'Amr but nicknamed Hashim for crumbling tharid for his people during famine.
عبد مناف (Abd Manaf): Son of Qusayy and father of Hashim; originally called Abd Manat but name changed.
عدنان (Adnan): The furthest verified ancestor of the Prophet; from the progeny of Isma'il.

The Validity of Ali's Early Islam and the Death of Abu Talib
Biographical discussion with critical refinement of hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the preponderant Hanafi view on the validity of a child's conversion and the state of Abu Talib's death, and explain the evidence for both.

Core Idea
The Prophet confirmed Ali's childhood Islam; Abu Talib died a disbeliever according to Ahl al-Sunnah.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya addresses two intertwined issues: the validity of Ali's early conversion at about age ten, and the final state of his father Abu Talib. The commentator affirms that the Prophet ﷺ legally recognized Ali's Islam, leading to rulings such as non-inheritance from his disbelieving father. He then refutes the anomalous narration that Abu Talib died Muslim, citing consensus from the authentic hadith and Quranic verses. Understanding these points clarifies Hanafi positions on the legal capacity of minors and the inheritance barrier of differing religions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main issues
The passage discusses Ali's age at conversion (ten years, preponderant) and whether Abu Talib died Muslim or disbeliever.
2
Examine the evidence for Ali's valid Islam
The Prophet's confirmation and the practical consequence (Ali and Ja'far did not inherit from Abu Talib) prove that Ali's Islam was legally valid despite his youth.
Inheritance only applies when heir and decedent share the same religion.
3
Analyze the refutation of Abu Talib's Islam
The lone narration that Abu Talib died Muslim is shādh (anomalous) and contradicts the authentic hadith and numerous Quranic verses; hence, the majority view is that he died a disbeliever.
The commentator references his own treatise and cites the six authentic books.
4
Extract the key rules
A child's conversion is valid if accompanied by understanding; a disbeliever's death on disbelief means Muslim heirs do not inherit.
This aligns with Hanafi fiqh: a minor with ʿaql can validly convert, and the difference of religion is a complete bar to inheritance.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical note within the chapter on apostasy, addressing the early Islam of ‘Ali ibn Abi Talib and the death of his father Abu Talib. The commentator begins by affirming that the Prophet ﷺ confirmed ‘Ali’s Islam, which carried legal consequences. He then discusses a poem attributed to ‘Ali, praising his precedence in Islam at a very young age—ten years according to the preponderant view. A key argument is that because ‘Ali’s Islam was valid, he did not inherit from his father Abu Talib, who died a disbeliever. The commentator refutes the claim that Abu Talib died Muslim, citing that the lone narration is anomalous (shādh) and contradicts the authentic books (ṣiḥāḥ sitta) and numerous Qur’anic verses. The majority of Ahl al-Sunnah hold that Abu Talib died in disbelief. The commentator references his own treatise on the subject. On page 502, he continues with the poem and explains key words: ‘ṭaran’ means ‘all,’ ‘ghulāman mā balaghtu’ means he was a young boy not yet having reached puberty, and ‘al-awān’ means ‘time.’ The passage guards against the misunderstanding that ‘Ali’s conversion was invalid due to youth or that his father died Muslim.
