Oath on Exiting: Purpose vs. Destination
Contrasting rulings on exiting for purpose vs. destination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where an oath on 'exiting' is violated by intention at departure versus cases where arrival is required, and apply the Hanafi definitions of khuruj and ityan.

Core Idea
Khuruj (exit) is violated at departure with intention; ityan (coming) requires arrival.

This lesson examines two puzzling cases: a man who swears he will only go out for a funeral then does something else, and another who swears not to go to Mecca, starts out, then returns. Why is the first not in violation but the second is? The key lies in the Hanafi definition of 'khuruj' as a discrete act defined by intention at the moment of leaving, not by arrival. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying oath rulings in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Oath Scenarios
First scenario: oath 'I will not go out except to a funeral' – he goes out to a funeral, then to another matter. Second scenario: oath 'I will not go out to Mecca' – he goes out intending Mecca and returns. Note the different outcomes: no violation in first, violation in second.
The difference hinges on the wording: the first limits the purpose of exit, the second forbids a destination.
2
Define Khuruj (Exit) as per Hanafi Fiqh
Khuruj means separation from inside to outside with a specific intention at the moment of crossing the threshold. Arrival at the intended place is not required. The act is complete at departure.
This definition comes from the commentary, citing al-Bada'i' and al-Bahr.
3
Apply to the Funeral Case
When he left his house for the funeral, his exit was solely for the funeral. The subsequent going to another matter is a separate act, not a new 'exit' from the house. Hence, no violation of the oath.
The key is that the initial exit was already completed with the funeral intention.
4
Apply to the Mecca Case
His exit from the house with the intention of going to Mecca completes the act of khuruj to Mecca. Even though he returns before reaching Mecca, he violated the oath at the moment of departure.
The condition for violation is the intention at separation, not arrival.
5
Distinguish Khuruj from Ityan
The commentary notes that for verbs like 'come' (ityan), 'visit' ('iyadah), 'return' ('awd), arrival at the destination is required. This is because these actions necessitate reaching the place, unlike khuruj which is a one-moment event.
This distinction affects oath drafting: if one swears using 'ityan', the ruling differs.

Commentary
This passage, from the hashiyah on Sharh al-Wiqayah, presents two distinct rulings on oaths involving exiting. The first ruling: if someone swears 'I will not go out except to a funeral' and then goes out to a funeral, but subsequently goes to another matter, he does not violate the oath. The reasoning is that 'going out' is defined as separation from inside to outside with the specific intention of the funeral. Once the intention for the funeral is present at the point of separation, the exit is considered only for the funeral; the subsequent action is a separate movement not constituting a new 'exit' from the house. The commentary clarifies that 'going out' does not require reaching the destination—intention at the moment of crossing the threshold suffices. The second ruling: if someone swears 'I will not go out to Mecca' and then goes out intending Mecca and even returns before reaching it, he violates the oath. This is because the condition for violation is the act of exiting with the intention of Mecca, not actually arriving. The commentary distinguishes between 'khuruj' (exit) and 'ityan' (coming); for 'ityan', arrival is necessary. The passage also contrasts cases where the exit is by one's own command (violation) versus coerced or willing (no violation). The analysis draws from al-Bada'i' and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq to illustrate nuances, such as a woman who intends the mosque but changes her mind—no divorce, because the exit was for the mosque. Similarly, a man in his own courtyard is not considered 'outside' until he leaves the door. The commentary emphasizes that the key is the initial intention at the moment of separation, not the eventual action.

Key Terms
الخروج (al-khuruj): Separation from inside to outside, with intention at the moment of crossing the threshold; arrival at destination is not required.
الحنث (al-hanth): Violation of an oath by doing the sworn-against act.
الإتيان (al-ityan): Coming to a place; requires arrival (wusul) at the destination.

Oath on 'Coming' vs 'Going': Arrival Required for 'Ityan'
Distinguishing between 'ityan' and 'dhahab' in oath rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ityan' (coming) and 'dhahab' (going) in Hanafi oath rulings and apply the correct violation condition for each.

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, 'ityan' requires arrival; 'dhahab' violates upon departure.

This lesson examines a critical distinction in Hanafi jurisprudence: the difference between 'coming' (ityan) and 'going' (dhahab) in the context of oaths. While both involve movement towards a place, their legal consequences diverge. We will explore the textual proof from the Quran, the ruling's application, and the reasoning behind why 'dhahab' is treated like 'khuruj' (exiting).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the specific wording of the oath and whether the key term is 'ityan' (coming) or 'dhahab' (going). The ruling for violation differs: for 'ityan', violation occurs upon arrival and entry; for 'dhahab', violation occurs upon departure with intention.
In the source, 'la ya'tiha' requires entering; 'dhahab' is like 'khuruj'.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites Quran 20:47: 'Fa'tiya Fir'awn' – the command to 'come' (ita) implies arrival at the location (Pharaoh). This establishes the linguistic principle that 'ityan' necessitates reaching the destination.
The dalil is from Quran, qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala according to the text.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verb 'ita' in the Quranic verse is used to mean arriving at Pharaoh, showing that 'ityan' entails wusul (arrival). This linguistic proof is applied to oaths: if one swears 'I will not come to Mecca', he does not violate until he actually arrives and enters.
The Hanafi school uses this to argue that 'ityan' requires physical presence at the location.
4
Note the ikhtilaf and tarjih
The text states 'in the most correct view' (al-ashah) for 'dhahab' being like 'khuruj'. This indicates a difference of opinion; the preferred view is that 'dhahab' violates upon mere departure, not requiring arrival. Other views may require arrival for both terms.
The tarjih is from the Hidayah and supported by linguistic analysis of 'dhahab' as mere transition.
5
Apply to a practical example
The text gives an example: if a husband swears his wife will not come to a wedding, and she goes before the wedding festivities start (customarily not considered 'coming'), she does not violate. This illustrates that 'coming' is assessed by custom and arrival.
This example is from al-Dhakhira.

Invalidity of Specifying Particular Items in Oaths
States that if a swearer intends a specific item in a general oath, it is not accepted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding specification by intention in general oaths and explain the linguistic reasoning behind it using the concepts of mahiyyah and iqtida'.

Core Idea
An oath on a general action covers all instances; intention to specify is worthless.

In this passage, the commentator explains a crucial Hanafi principle: when a person swears a general oath (e.g., 'I will not wear'), a hidden intention to restrict it to a specific item is completely disregarded. This rule applies both in court (qada'an) and in one's personal religious duty (diyanatan). The proof is linguistic: the verb is derived from the essence (mahiyyah) of the action, not its individual instances. The only exception is what is necessary by implication (iqtida') to make sense of the oath, but that does not allow specification.

Guided Path
1
State the Ruling and its Scope
Identify the ruling: If someone swears 'I will not wear, eat, or drink' and intends a specific item, the intention is not accepted. This applies both judicially (in court) and religiously (before Allah).
The term 'lam yusaddaq aslan' negates belief entirely.
2
Identify the Linguistic Proof (Mahiyyah)
The proof is that the verb 'لبست' (I wear) is derived from 'اللبس' which denotes the essence of wearing, not a particular instance. Similarly for eating and drinking. The negation applies to the entire genus, not a specific individual.
Hanafi usul: negation of a generic noun negates all instances.
3
Understand the Role of Iqtida' (Implication)
The only necessary implication is that wearing requires a garment, eating requires food, etc. But this implication does not allow specifying a particular item; it merely makes the expression meaningful.
Iqtida' is necessary for coherence but does not create new legal scope.
4
Determine the Consequence
Because the specification is not accepted, the oath remains general. If the swearer later wears any garment (or eats/drinks anything), the oath is broken and the consequence (e.g., divorce, manumission) applies.
The consequence is triggered by the act itself, not by the intended item.
5
Contrast with Other Schools (Optional)
Some Shafi'is accept specification if the wording can bear it. Hanafis reject this because the wording is unambiguously general.
This ikhtilaf stems from differing views on the scope of general expressions.

Commentary
This passage parses the matn's ruling on specifying particular items in oaths. The matn states: if someone swears 'I will not wear, eat, or drink' and in their heart intends a specific garment, food, or drink, that specification is not accepted at all (la yusaddaq aslan). The commentator explains that this applies both judicially (in court) and religiously (before Allah), meaning the swearer is still bound by the general oath. The reason is that the negation applies to the essence (mahiyyah) of the action (wearing, eating, drinking), not to particular instances. The verb 'لبست' is derived from the root 'اللبس' which is a concept for the whole category, not specific items. Therefore, if the oath is broken, the consequence (e.g., divorce or manumission) occurs upon any wearing, eating, or drinking, regardless of intention. The only exception is by implication (iqtida'), which is necessary to make sense of the expression, because one cannot wear without a garment, etc. This implication does not provide scope for specifying a particular item. Thus, the ruling ensures that oaths are interpreted generally unless the wording itself is limited.

Key Terms
لم يُصَدِّق أصلاً (lam yusaddaq aslan): He is not believed at all, meaning neither in court (qada'an) nor in religious conscience (diyanatan).
الماهية (al-mahiyyah): The essence or the general concept of the action (wearing, eating, drinking) as opposed to specific instances.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtida'): Implication; a necessary inference from the wording to make it meaningful, but not a specification.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the Sahibayn regarding the validity of an oath when its subject matter becomes impossible, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Possibility of fulfillment (imkan al-birr) is a condition for the oath's validity; the Sahibayn require it at the moment of the act, Abu Yusuf does not.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on oaths, focusing on a water pitcher example. The disagreement hinges on whether the possibility of fulfillment (imkan al-birr) is a condition for the oath's validity and continuation, and how time restrictions affect the obligation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how Hanafi jurists differ on the same principle and to see the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf) in actual rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Sahibayn's Position
Identify the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the two) on both the unrestricted (mutlaq) and time-bound (mu'azzat) oath scenarios described in the passage.
Consider the two scenarios: no water initially, and water poured after the oath.
2
Identify Abu Yusuf's Difference
Recognize how Abu Yusuf's position differs from the Sahibayn, particularly in the case where no water existed at the time of the oath.
Abu Yusuf holds the oath is valid even without water, leading to hanth.
3
Follow Abu Yusuf's Proof
Understand the basis of Abu Yusuf's distinction: in unrestricted oaths, obligation is immediate, so inability leads to hanth; in time-bound, obligation is delayed, so no hanth before the time.
He argues that time-binding provides leeway (tawsi'ah).
4
Analyse the Sahibayn's Response
Examine why the Sahibayn require imkan al-birr for both inception and continuation, and how they apply this to the time-bound case (oath void at the last part due to impossibility).
They analogize to initiating an oath in the same impossible condition.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Identify the practical difference in rulings: when does kaffarah become wajib? According to Abu Yusuf, in the no-water scenario, kaffarah is due; according to the Sahibayn, no kaffarah because the oath is invalid.
The dispute affects whether the swearer must expiate or not.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding an oath about drinking water from a pitcher. The core issue is whether the oath is valid and whether breaking it (hanth) occurs when the water is no longer available. Two scenarios are discussed: (1) the pitcher has no water at the time of the oath, and (2) the pitcher has water initially but it is later poured out. Furthermore, the oath may be either unrestricted (mutlaq, no time specified) or time-bound (mu'azzat, e.g., 'today'). The disagreement is between Abu Yusuf on one side and the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on the other. Abu Yusuf holds that in the unrestricted oath, the swearer is obliged to fulfill immediately, so if the water is poured, he has failed and breaks his oath. In the time-bound oath, the obligation is at the end of the time, so if the water is poured earlier, he does not break it. The two hold that the possibility of fulfillment is a condition for the oath's validity and continuation. If there is no water initially, the oath never takes effect regardless of timing. If there is water initially but it is poured, the oath becomes void in a time-bound case because at the moment the act is due (the last part of the time), the water is gone. In an unrestricted case, the two also hold that if the water is poured, the oath is broken because the obligation was immediate and the subject matter is lost. The passage then records an objection from Fath al-Qadir comparing the two cases and an answer from al-Nahr and Shaykh Amin ibn Abidin, ultimately supporting the position of the Sahibayn. The practical consequence is whether kaffarah (expiation) is due.

Key Terms
يمين (yamin): An oath, legally a confirmation of an action or statement by mentioning Allah or a divorce/manumission.
حنث (hanth): Breaking an oath by failing to perform the sworn act.
بر (birr): Fulfilling the oath by performing the sworn act.
مطلق (mutlaq): An unrestricted oath with no time limit specified.
مؤقت (mu'azzat): A time-bound oath where a specific time limit is mentioned, e.g., 'today'.
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation for breaking an oath, such as feeding ten poor people, clothing them, or freeing a slave.

Oath on Hitting: Pulling Hair, Strangling, Biting
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of 'darb' in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether painful acts like pulling hair break an oath not to hit and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding playful intent.

Core Idea
Any intentional painful act breaks an oath not to hit, even if done playfully.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person swears not to hit someone, then engages in actions like pulling hair or biting. The central question is whether these count as 'hitting' (darb). The Hanafi school generally treats any painful act as hitting, but a minority view considers playful actions exempt. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the majority Hanafi view: pulling hair, strangling, and biting break the oath because they are painful acts, regardless of the label.
The text from Al-Hidayah states: 'because it (darb) is a name for a painful act, and pain has occurred.'
2
Identify the opposing view
Some Hanafis (reported as 'qil') hold that if done playfully (mula'abah), these acts are called 'joking' not 'hitting', so the oath is not broken.
This view is based on the conventional meaning of darb in non-violent contexts.
3
Follow the opposing proof
The opposing proof: in playful contexts, these acts are not intended to harm and are not conventionally called hitting; the oath's wording should be interpreted literally.
This relies on the linguistic understanding that 'darb' typically implies striking, not playful touch.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi majority counters that the defining element of darb is causing pain, not the specific manner or intention. Since the act caused pain, the oath is violated.
The oath is about the effect, not the label. Intent to harm is not required; causing pain suffices.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the majority view, kaffarah (expiation) is required immediately. Under the minority view, no kaffarah if done playfully.
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view, as it prevents evasion of oaths.

Commentary

The Hanafi position generally holds that the oath is broken in all cases because the act is still painful, but this text presents the differing view without explicit tarjih. The caution: students should know that the relied-upon position in the school is that any act causing pain, even playfully, breaks the oath, as indicated by the citation from Al-Hidayah.

Stillbirth and the Oath of Childbirth: An Intra-Hanafi Analysis
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath fulfillment by stillbirth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding oaths of childbirth and apply the mufta bihi position to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A stillborn child does not fulfill an oath conditioned on a living child according to Abu Hanifa.

Oaths involving childbirth often raise nuanced questions about what constitutes fulfillment of the condition. This lesson examines a specific scenario: a man says to his pregnant slave-girl, 'If you give birth to a child, that child is free.' She first delivers a stillborn, then later a live child. Does the oath apply to the stillborn, the living child, or neither? The Hanafi masters differ on this point, revealing the importance of precise wording in legal conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Oath Wording
Read the oath: 'If you give birth to a child (walad), he is free.' Note that the condition is the birth of a 'walad', not just any birth.
The term 'walad' is ambiguous but can be contextually restricted.
2
Distinguish the Two Scenarios
Contrast this oath with the general oath 'If you give birth, then you are such-and-such' where stillbirth counts as birth. Here the focus is on the child being freed.
The first scenario (stillbirth breaks the oath) is agreed upon; the dispute is only about the second.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āyah is a detailed commentary on a ruling from al-Wiqāyah concerning oaths involving the term 'ākhar' (last). The passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute, where the commentator expands upon the positions of Imām Abū Ḥanīfa and the Ṣāḥibayn (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad al-Shaybānī).

The first part deals with a man who swears 'the last slave I buy is free.' He buys one slave, then another, then dies. The question is: which slave is free? The commentary explains that the word 'ākhar' (with a long alif and broken kha) means the subsequent individual, requiring a prior slave. Without a prior purchase, the second slave cannot be 'ākhar.' Thus, the first slave purchased is not freed, and the second is freed only if there was a preceding first purchase. The key principle is that 'ākhariyyah' (lastness) requires both a preceding item and the absence of a later item. Death of the master establishes that no later purchase will occur, thus confirming the lastness.

The second part addresses the timing of the freeing. According to the matn (the base text), Abū Ḥanīfa holds that the last slave is freed on the day of purchase (from all wealth), while the Ṣāḥibayn hold it is on the day of death (from one-third wealth). The commentary defends Abū Ḥanīfa's view: the attribute of lastness was present at the time of purchase, but it was not certain until death. Death is a revealer (muʿarrif), not a creator of the attribute. Therefore, the freeing relates back to the time of purchase (saḥīḥ tarājuʿ). The practical consequence is that if the slave's value exceeds one-third of the estate, Abū Ḥanīfa's view frees him fully (since it is a gift from a healthy person), while the Ṣāḥibayn's view limits it to one-third (as a bequest from a dying person).

The third part extends this reasoning to an oath of triple divorce: a man says 'the last woman I marry is divorced with three.' He marries two women and dies. All agree the second wife is divorced because she is the last. The dispute is about timing: Abū Ḥanīfa says the divorce occurs at the time of marriage (because the lastness is established retrospectively), while the Ṣāḥibayn say at death. Additionally, the issue of 'fār' (fleeing husband) arises: Abū Ḥanīfa holds the husband does not become fār (i.e., his action is not considered an evasion of divorce rules), while the Ṣāḥibayn consider him fār. The commentary supports Abū Ḥanīfa throughout, emphasizing that the legal effect follows the objective fulfillment of the condition, not mere intention.

فار (fār): A husband who deliberately divorces his wife to deprive her of rights, considered in suspensive divorce contexts.

This seemingly subtle distinction has major implications for the quantum of property (all wealth vs. one-third) and for inheritance, mahr, and iddah in the case of divorce.

Abu Hanifa responds that death is merely a revealer of what was already true from the time of the act. The attribute existed but was not acted upon until certainty; death does not create the attribute, it confirms it.

Core Idea
The timing of a conditional divorce determines inheritance and dower—Abu Hanifa fixes it at marriage, Sahibayn at death.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about a man who says 'The last woman I marry is divorced thrice.' He marries two women and then dies. The key issue: when does the divorce of the first wife take effect? Abu Hanifa holds at marriage, so the husband is not a fār (one evading inheritance) and she does not inherit. Both positions are from within the Hanafi school. The passage does not specify which is muftā bihī; both are transmitted in Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah. Later practice often follows the Sahibayn to protect inheritance rights.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the madhhab, not a disagreement with another school.

Ruling on an Oath to Divorce All Wives When One Wife Asks
Clarifies the ruling when a man responds to his wife's accusation by swearing to divorce all his wives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a talaq oath uttered in response to a wife's accusation, distinguishing between the apparent ruling (qada'an) and the ruling in conscience (diyanatan), and identify the conditions under which the responding wife is divorced according to Abu Yusuf and later scholars.

Core Idea
An oath to divorce all wives, spoken in reply to one wife, excludes her in conscience despite the apparent generality.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text addresses a common yet nuanced scenario: a man, accused by his wife of marrying another woman, retorts 'Every woman I have is divorced.' The literal wording suggests all wives are divorced, but the context of the reply and the man's intention can alter the ruling. The Hanafi school, through the fatwa of Abu Yusuf, provides a balanced answer that considers both the apparent meaning and the underlying purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its apparent ruling
The act is the husband's utterance 'كل امرأة لي طالق' in response to his wife's accusation. The apparent ruling (zahir) is that all wives are divorced because 'كل' is a word of generality (amm) that includes every wife.
This is the literal interpretation that would be enforced in court (qada'an) unless evidence contradicts.
2
Examine Abu Yusuf's counter-argument
Abu Yusuf argues that the specific wife who prompted the oath is not divorced. His proof: the statement was a reply (jawab) to her accusation, so the man's purpose (gharad) was to please her by divorcing other wives. The context restricts the generality of 'كل'.
This is a contextual specification (takhsis bi al-qarinah) based on the purpose of the speech.
3
Understand the qada/diyana distinction
The man's intention to divorce only other wives is khilaf al-zahir (contrary to the apparent meaning). Therefore, it is not accepted in court (qada'an), but it is valid in conscience (diyanatan) before Allah. This is a core Hanafi principle: courts judge by external evidence, while intention matters religiously.
If the man had no specific intention, the apparent generality prevails even in conscience.
4
Locate the fatwa and later refinement
According to al-Bazdawi, the fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view: the wife who prompted is not divorced. However, later scholars (Shams al-A'imma) add a condition: if there was prior dispute indicating anger, then the wife is also divorced, because the statement was made in anger, not merely to please.
This refinement shows that the context of anger overrides the assumption of reassurance.
5
Apply the principle to a similar case
The passage gives an analogy: if a man says 'Do you have a wife other than this woman?' and he replies 'Every woman I have is divorced,' the specific woman is not included because the phrase 'other than this woman' explicitly excludes her. Similarly, the context of reply in the original case implicitly excludes the accuser.
This illustrates how contextual indicators (qara'in) can specify the general word 'كل'.

The wife who prompts a husband to say 'كل امرأة لي طالق' is not divorced according to the Hanafi fatwa, unless the statement was made in anger due to prior dispute.

The Number of Confessions Required for Theft: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on one confession vs. two

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the number of confessions for theft hadd and explain why Abu Yusuf's initial qiyas is rejected.

Core Idea
One confession suffices for theft hadd; the exception of zina cannot be extended by analogy.

This passage addresses an important intra-Hanafi dispute: how many confessions are needed to establish the hadd (fixed punishment) for theft? While it may seem intuitive to analogize from zina, where four confessions are required, the Hanafi school maintains that a single confession is sufficient. Understanding this ruling reveals the school's methodology in handling textual exceptions and the principle that a person's confession is binding on themselves.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the number of confessions required to establish the hadd of theft. The base ruling states that one confession suffices, which is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Abu Yusuf analogizes confession to testimony, arguing that just as zina requires four witnesses or four confessions, theft (being a hadd) should require two confessions (since each confession equals one witness). The Hanafi response rejects this analogy: the rule requiring four confessions in zina is established by explicit text (Quran/Sunnah) and is exceptional; for other crimes, the general principle applies—a person's confession is binding on himself, so one confession is sufficient. The passage also notes Abu Yusuf's alternative view that the two confessions must be in separate sittings, but reports that he eventually retracted to the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). This shows the Hanafi commitment to the principle of iqrar (self-incrimination) and the limitation of qiyas when textual exception exists.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): Confession by the accused that he committed the theft; this serves as evidence for the hadd punishment.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning used by Abu Yusuf to extend the rule of zina (four confessions) to theft.
أصل (asl): The original principle that a person is held accountable by his own confession, which is not overridden by analogy to an exceptional case.
