Rulings on Female Apostates and Inheritance of Their Children
Hanafi ruling with proof on female apostates and inheritance of children

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on female apostates, explain the proof from hadith, and determine the inheritance of a child born to an apostate's enslaved woman based on the mother's religion and the time of birth.

Core Idea
A female apostate is not killed; her child's inheritance hinges on the mother's religion and conception timing.

The Hanafi school takes a distinctive position on female apostates: they are not executed but imprisoned, and their property rights remain intact. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof from the hadith prohibiting the killing of women, and the detailed rules for inheritance of children born to an apostate's enslaved woman. You will learn how the child's religion and inheritance rights depend on whether the mother is Muslim or kitabiyya and whether the birth occurs within six months of the apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on female apostates
Start by extracting the primary ruling: 'ولا تقتل مرتدة، وتحبس حتى تُسلِم'. This is the fundamental rule from which all other deductions follow.
Contrast with the Shafi'i position that female apostates are killed.
2
Locate and evaluate the proof (dalil)
The proof is the hadith 'النهي عن قتل النساء' (prohibition of killing women). Determine its type (ahad), thubut (zanni), and dalala (qati). Understand how this specific prohibition specifies the general command to kill apostates.
The Hanafi response to the opposing dalil is based on specification (takhsis).
3
Understand the rationale for valid transactions
The text links property inviolability (عصمة المال) to life inviolability. Since the female apostate's life is protected, her property remains protected. Hence, her earnings and transactions are valid and pass to her heirs upon death.
This is a key principle: عصمة المال تابعة لعصمة النفس.
4
Analyze the inheritance rules for children of an apostate's enslaved woman
The passage distinguishes two cases: (1) mother is Muslim: child inherits absolutely, regardless of birth timing; (2) mother is kitabiyya: child inherits only if born less than six months after apostasy. Trace the reasoning: the child follows the better parent in religion.
The six-month period corresponds to the minimum duration of pregnancy; conception during Islam makes the child Muslim.
5
Apply the rules to determine inheritance
Given a scenario with an apostate father, an enslaved mother (Muslim or kitabiyya), and a birth date relative to apostasy, decide whether the child inherits. Use the principles from step 4.
Remember: apostates do not inherit from each other; a Muslim child inherits from the apostate father.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi ruling with proof on the treatment of female apostates (murtaddah) and the inheritance of their children from enslaved women. The author begins by stating the core ruling: a female apostate is not killed, contrary to the Shafi'i school. Instead, she is imprisoned until she returns to Islam. Her transactions and earnings remain valid and pass to her heirs upon her death (real or legal, e.g., joining the abode of war). The proof for not killing her is the hadith prohibiting the killing of women, which specifies the general command to kill apostates. The commentary then explains the rationale: the inviolability of property is tied to the inviolability of life; since her life is protected (she is not killed), her property rights remain. Next, the passage discusses the case of an enslaved woman belonging to the apostate who gives birth. If the apostate claims the child, the child is established as his free son and inherits from him. This applies absolutely if the enslaved woman is Muslim, because the child follows the mother's religion (Islam) and thus can inherit from the apostate father. If the enslaved woman is Christian or Jewish, the inheritance occurs only if the child is born less than six months from the apostasy, meaning conception occurred while the father was still Muslim, making the child Muslim (following the better parent). If more than six months pass, the child follows the father's religion (now apostasy) and does not inherit because apostates do not inherit from each other. The commentary also clarifies that if the apostate joins the abode of war with his property, the same inheritance rules apply.

استيلاد (istilad): Claiming paternity of a child born to one's enslaved woman, which establishes the child as free and the woman as umm walad (mother of the master's child).

Inheritance from Apostate Father: Child's Entitlement
Explaining inheritance conditions for child of apostate father

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance of a child from an apostate father, distinguishing between cases based on the mother's religion and the timing of birth, and explain the rationale of following the better religion.

Core Idea
A child inherits from an apostate father only if the child is Muslim – determined by mother's religion or conception before apostasy.

This ruling from the matn of al-Wiqaya, expanded in 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, addresses a nuanced inheritance scenario: when the father apostatizes and a child is born thereafter. The key factor is the child's religion, which is determined by following the 'better' parent at birth. In Hanafi fiqh, this leads to different outcomes depending on whether the mother is Muslim or Christian, and the timing of birth relative to the father's apostasy.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two scenarios
Identify whether the mother is Muslim or Christian. The passage gives different rulings for each: unconditional inheritance if mother Muslim, conditional if mother Christian.
2
Identify the governing principle
The child follows the better religion of the parents (khayr al-abawayn dinan). Understand how this principle determines the child's religion at birth.
This is the ta'lil (causal reasoning) for the rulings.
3
Apply the timing condition for Christian mother
If the mother is Christian, the child inherits only if born within six months of the father's apostasy (minimum pregnancy period). This indicates conception occurred before apostasy, so the child is Muslim and inherits.
If born after six months, the child does not inherit because conception occurred after apostasy, and the child follows the apostate father.
4
Analyze the legal effect
The child inherits only if Muslim. If the child is considered apostate (by following the apostate father), the child does not inherit because an apostate cannot inherit from an apostate.
This explains why the child does not inherit in the Christian mother, late birth scenario.

A Muslim Does Not Inherit from a Non-Muslim
Legal ruling with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a Muslim does not inherit from a non-Muslim, cite the key hadith evidence, and identify the practical consequence.

Core Idea
A Muslim does not inherit from a non-Muslim, nor a non-Muslim from a Muslim.

This lesson examines the foundational rule of inheritance between Muslims and non-Muslims. The text recounts the case of Ali and Ja'far not inheriting from their father Abu Talib, who died as a disbeliever, to illustrate the principle that difference of religion prevents inheritance in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Faulty interpretation excuses liability, not inheritance.

This lesson examines a specific fara'id scenario: a rebel kills a just ruler. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents the ruling and highlights a key intra-Hanafi disagreement. By understanding the distinction between civil liability (daman) and inheritance, you will grasp why the majority Hanafi position deprives the rebel of inheritance even when he claims a faulty interpretation.

Inheritance of a Missing Person (Al-Mafqud)
Faraid case analysis of suspending inheritance shares of a missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate inheritance shares when a missing person is present, applying the Hanafi principle of suspending the uncertain share and granting the lesser of two possible shares.

Core Idea
The missing person's share is suspended — heirs receive only what is certain.

In Hanafi inheritance law, the status of a missing person creates uncertainty. The law does not simply disinherit the missing person or ignore their potential rights. Instead, it suspends their share and gives other heirs only what is guaranteed. This lesson unpacks the key principles and a detailed example from al-Hidayah, teaching you how to apply the rule of 'aqall al-nasibayn' (the lesser of two shares).

Guided Path
1
Identify all potential heirs
List every surviving relative who might inherit. The missing person is treated as a potential heir. In the example: two daughters, missing son, son's son, son's daughter.
The missing person's share is suspended, not denied.
2
Determine who excludes whom
If the missing person is alive, does he exclude any other heirs? In the example, the missing son would totally exclude his own children (grandchildren).
Hajb hirman (total exclusion) blocks inheritance completely.
3
Assign shares for two scenarios
Calculate what each heir would receive if the missing person is alive versus dead. Then give each present heir the lesser of the two amounts (the certain portion).
This is the 'aqall al-nasibayn' principle.
4
Suspend the uncertain remainder
After distributing the certain shares to present heirs, set aside the remaining property until the missing person's fate is known.
If no present heir can be certain of more, the excess is held by the court or a trustee.
5
Apply the same logic to pregnancy
The missing person is analogous to an unborn child (haml). The same principles of suspension and giving the lesser share apply.
Hanafi fatwa suspends for an unborn child the share of one son.

Learning Objective

The key principle is that in Hanafi fiqh, the presumption of continuity (istishab) is a valid proof for defending existing rights (daf') but not for establishing new rights (ithbat). After the waiting period where the missing person is presumed dead for the purpose of his wife's iddah, for his own wealth he is considered alive prior to the waiting period, so any heir who died during that period does not inherit from him because he was presumably alive. But regarding the wealth of others (e.g., if a relative died and the missing person was an heir), he is considered dead from the time of his loss, so his share of that inheritance is not given to him (since he is presumed dead), but is returned to the other heirs of that deceased. The commentary uses the example: if someone dies while the missing person is still missing, the missing person's share of that estate is stopped, and after the waiting period (or when his death is confirmed), that stopped share is given to the heirs of the missing person's testator (i.e., the heirs of the deceased from whose estate it was stopped) because the missing person is now known to have predeceased that deceased (or is treated as such). The ruling is based on the principle that istishab can prevent inheritance (daf') but cannot grant it (ithbat).

Key Terms
الاستصحاب (al-istishab): The presumption of continuity: keeping the previous state of affairs in the absence of evidence to the contrary.
ظاهر الحال (zahir al-hal): The apparent state of affairs that is presumed based on available evidence; here synonym for istishab.
المفقود (al-mafqud): A person missing and whose death is not confirmed.
