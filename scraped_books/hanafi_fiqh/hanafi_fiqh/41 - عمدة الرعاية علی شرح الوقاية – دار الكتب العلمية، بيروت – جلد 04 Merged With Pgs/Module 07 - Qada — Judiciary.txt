Oath Interpretation: Diyanah vs. Qada' on Real Ability
Explains that if one intends real ability in an oath, he is believed privately but not judicially.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between diyanah and qada' in oath interpretation and apply the Hanafi rule that a meaning contrary to customary usage is accepted privately but not in court.

Core Idea
In oaths, a meaning contrary to customary usage is accepted privately before Allah, but not in court.

When someone takes an oath using the word 'ability' (istita'ah), does he have to fulfill it only if he truly is able in the moment? Or does the oath refer to usual, possible ability? The Hanafi school distinguishes between what is accepted between the person and Allah (diyanah) and what is accepted in court (qada'). This lesson examines that distinction and the proof from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is an oath containing the word 'ability' (e.g., 'I will come if I am able'). The ruling is about the credibility of the swearer's interpretation: if he claims he meant the real ability (concurrent with the act), he is believed diyanatan but not qada'an.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The commentator cites Quran 3:97, which uses 'istata'a' in the context of Hajj. The verse is interpreted to show that the customary meaning of 'istata'a' is possible ability (soundness of means), not the real ability that is inseparable from the act.
The verse is qati' thubut but zanni dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof proves the ruling)
Because the Quranic verse uses 'istata'a' without qualification and it is understood by the community as possible ability, this establishes that the customary meaning is possible ability. Thus, if someone intends another meaning, it is contrary to the apparent, so the judge does not accept it.
The principle: words in oaths are interpreted according to custom.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
A minority opinion (qil) states that he should be believed qada'an too because the word is homonymous (shared between two meanings). However, the preferred view (al-awjah) is that the word has become specialized in the customary meaning, so the judge does not accept the other meaning.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view)
The preferred view is that he is not believed qada'an, as stated by al-Marghinani and others. The authority is from Fath al-Qadir and Fath Bab al-Inaya. The mufta bihi is diyanatan only.
Authority weight: zahir riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The matn states that if a person swears an oath involving 'ability' (istita'ah) and later claims he meant the real ability (which is concurrent with the act), he is believed diyanatan (between him and Allah) but not qada'an (in court). The commentator explains why: the word istita'ah has two meanings. First, the real ability that accompanies the act; second, the possible ability, which is soundness of means and removal of obstacles. The latter is the customary usage, as in the Quranic verse (3:97). If one intends the first meaning, it is contrary to the apparent, so the judge does not accept it. However, there is a minority view (qil) that he should be believed judicially too because the word is homonymous. But the preferred view (al-awjah) is that he is not believed qada'an because the word has become specialized in the customary meaning. The footnote references Fath al-Qadir and Fath Bab al-Inaya.

Key Terms
ديانة (diyānah): Acceptance of a claim between the person and Allah, regardless of court evidence.
قضاء (qaḍā'): Judicial ruling based on apparent evidence; not necessarily reflecting inner truth.
الاستطاعة الحقيقية (al-istiṭā'ah al-ḥaqīqīyah): The real ability that is concurrent with the action and is its complete cause; cannot precede the action.

Each Exit Requires Separate Permission: Linguistic Basis in Oaths
Explains condition for oath fulfillment with linguistic analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath 'do not go out except with my permission' and explain the linguistic reasoning that each exit requires separate permission.

Core Idea
The particle 'ب' means adhesion: each exit must be directly attached to a permission.

Oaths often contain exceptions like 'except with my permission'. How many permissions are needed? The Hanafi school answers by analyzing the Arabic construction. This lesson unpacks the linguistic reasoning behind the rule and explores a legal device that can simplify repeated permissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and condition
Read the phrase: 'لا تخرج إلا بإذنه' (do not go out except with his permission). The condition for fulfilling the oath (birr) is that each specific exit must have its own permission.
2
Analyze the exception structure
This is an istithnā' mufaragh (discontinuous exception). The implied meaning is: 'I do not exit an exit except an exit attached to my permission.' The general excluded-from (mustathnā minhu) is estimated as 'exit' in the negative context.
The indefinite noun 'خروجاً' in a negative context indicates generality.
3
Apply the linguistic rule of 'ب'
The preposition 'ب' here denotes adhesion (ilṣāq). So the permitted exit must be directly attached to the permission. Therefore, each exit requires its own permission; a previous permission does not adhere to a later exit.
The Hanafi position relies on the literal meaning of 'ب' for attachment.
4
Understand the legal device and ikhtilaf
A device (ḥīla) is to say: 'كلما أردت الخروج فقد أذنت لك' (whenever you wish to go out, I have permitted you). Abu Yusuf holds this suffices for all future exits; Muhammad disagrees, requiring explicit permissions. The practical consequence depends on which view is followed.
The text does not give a tarjīḥ; later Hanafi works may prefer one view.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The matn states that in an oath 'do not go out except with my permission', faithfulness requires that each exit have its own permission. The commentator explains: the construction implies 'do not go out except an exit attached to my permission'. The particle 'ب' (bi) here means adhesion (ilṣāq), so the exit must be directly attached to permission. This is an istithnā' mufaragh (discontinuous exception) where the general excluded-from is implied ('do not exit an exit except an exit...'). Since the indefinite in a negative context denotes generality, all exits remain forbidden unless each specifically is attached to permission. Thus, a single permission for one exit does not suffice for another. The footnote adds that a legal device (ḥīla) is for the husband to say 'Whenever you want to go out, I have permitted you' once; then future exits are considered with permission. There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Yusuf says this general permission works and subsequent prohibition is ineffective, while Muhammad says it does not work. The device is not in the main text but is a practical application.

Key Terms
الإلصاق (al-ilṣāq): Adhesion; the meaning of the preposition 'ب' indicating direct attachment of the action to the permission.
استثناء مفرغ (istithnā' mufaragh): A construction where the exceptive particle 'except' is followed by a single noun, implying an omitted general category.
الحيلة (al-ḥīla): A legal device to avoid breaking the oath while still achieving the desired outcome, e.g., giving general permission.
