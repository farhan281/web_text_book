The Foundling (Laqit): Freedom, Maintenance, and Inheritance
States the legal status of a foundling as free and rulings on maintenance and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the foundling's default status and explain the conditions under which his maintenance and inheritance are handled by the public treasury.

Core Idea
A foundling is free by default, his needs are the state's duty, and his estate goes to the public treasury — unless evidence of slavery or relatives emerges.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary on al-Wiqayah addresses the legal status of an abandoned child (laqit). It establishes three core rulings: the presumption of freedom, the state's responsibility for maintenance, and the inheritance pathway to the public treasury. These rulings reflect foundational Hanafi principles of istishab (presumption of continuity) and social welfare.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main rulings
Read the opening sentence: freedom default, maintenance from bayt al-mal, inheritance to bayt al-mal. These are the pillars of the passage.
The passage uses 'wa' (and) to connect them.
2
Understand the presumption of freedom
The text states 'He is free except by proof of his enslavement'. Freedom is the original status (istishab al-hurriyyah) especially in Dar al-Islam.
Compare to the default in other legal systems.
3
Analyze the source of maintenance
Maintenance (nafaqah) includes all necessities, even marriage expenses. It is from bayt al-mal when the foundling has no wealth and no relatives. Otherwise, from his wealth or relatives.
The proof is from the practice of Caliphs Ali and Umar.
4
Examine the finder's role
If the finder spends without a judge's order, he is a volunteer and cannot claim reimbursement. With a judge's order, it becomes a debt recoverable when the child grows up.
This prevents arbitrary claims against the child's future estate.
5
Apply the inheritance rule
If the foundling dies with no known relatives, his estate goes to bayt al-mal. The public treasury is the ultimate heir of those with no heirs.
This is consistent with Hanafi inheritance law.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the legal status of a foundling (laqit). It opens by stating that a foundling is considered free by default, as freedom is the original state of humans, especially in a Muslim land. This ruling is based on the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity). The only exception is if there is evidence of slavery, such as testimony or the foundling's own confession after adulthood. Next, the passage addresses the foundling's maintenance (nafaqah): it includes all necessities—food, clothing, shelter, medicine, even marriage expenses if the ruler marries him off. This maintenance is from the public treasury (bayt al-mal) because the foundling is a Muslim unable to earn and without family. The author supports this with athar (reports) from Ali and Umar, establishing that the caliphs provided for foundlings from the public treasury. However, if the foundling has personal wealth or known relatives, his maintenance comes from those sources. Regarding voluntary spending by the finder, the commentary clarifies that if the finder spends without judicial order, he is a volunteer (mutabarri') and cannot claim reimbursement. But if the judge orders him, it becomes a debt recoverable when the foundling matures. Finally, the inheritance of a foundling who dies without known relatives belongs to the public treasury, not the finder. The Hanafi position is that the public treasury is the heir of those with no heirs. The authority weight here is Zahir al-Riwayah, as these rulings are from the core texts of the school.

بيت المال (bayt al-mal): The public treasury of the Muslim state, responsible for welfare of those without means.
حجة الرق (hujjat al-raqq): Proof of slavery: evidence such as witnesses or confession that a person is a slave.

Foundling Custody and Lineage Claims: The Hanafi Approach
Prohibits forcibly taking a foundling and details lineage claim rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules on forced taking of a foundling and explain the Hanafi position on claiming lineage, including the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on multiple claimants.

Core Idea
The finder's custody is protected; lineage is established by description of a mark, and if more than two claim, the imams differ.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains two key rulings: no one may forcibly take a foundling from its finder, and lineage can be claimed by anyone who describes a distinctive mark. It then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on how many claimants can establish lineage when more than two step forward. We will unpack the reasoning, the use of istihsan over qiyas, and the practical consequences of the three positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary rulings
Begin by extracting the two main rules: (a) it is prohibited to forcibly take the foundling from the finder, and (b) lineage is established from whoever claims the child, especially if they describe a physical mark.
The prohibition protects the finder's right of custody (haqq al-hifdh) due to prior possession.
2
Understand the qiyas vs istihsan for the finder's claim
By analogy (qiyas), the finder's claim to lineage would be invalid due to contradiction (he first said it was a foundling, now claims it as his son). However, istihsan allows it because the finder assumes responsibility by this claim, and contradiction does not prevent lineage claims.
This is a classic example of istihsan overriding qiyas in the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the preference based on description
If two men claim the child, the one who describes a distinctive mark on the foundling's body (not on clothes) is preferred. The mark must be on the body because clothes are not attached permanently.
If neither describes a mark, or the description is false, they are equal unless one has prior claim in time or evidence.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on multiple claimants
If more than two men claim the child, the imams differ: Abu Hanifa says lineage is established for all claimants; Muhammad says for up to three; Abu Yusuf says for up to two. The text does not give a preferred view.
This dispute is based on differing interpretations of precaution (ihtiyat) and realism.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a case with four claimants, Abu Hanifa would recognize all four as sons, Muhammad would recognize three, and Abu Yusuf only two. This affects inheritance, guardianship, and other rights.
The mufta bihi (operative view) is not given in this text; later works may prefer one.

Commentary
This passage presents rulings on two aspects of foundling (laqit) law. First, it establishes that once a person finds a foundling, no one else may forcibly take it from him; the finder has a right of custody (haqq al-hifdh) due to prior possession. Foundling’s Religion: Place vs. Claimant (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Expansion of the Hidāyah ruling on foundling's religion under lineage claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the foundling's religion when a lineage claim is made and distinguish between the three narrations within the school.

Core Idea
The foundling's religion follows the abode unless the claim benefits the child without harm.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āya expands on the Hidāyah's treatment of a foundling's religion when a person claims paternity. While the default is that the foundling takes the status of the land of discovery, a claimant's lineage can be accepted without altering the child's religion if it would cause harm. However, within the Hanafi school, there is disagreement over what happens when the finder's religion conflicts with the place—three narrations are reported.

Guided Path
1
State the default principle
Identify the established rule: a foundling's religion is determined by the abode (dār) of discovery. In Muslim territory the child is Muslim; in dhimmi territory the child is dhimmi.
The default is based on the dominant religion of the land.
2
Analyze the exception for a claimant
When a person (e.g., a dhimmi) claims lineage, the claim is accepted but the child's religion remains as per the abode. This is istihsan: the claim benefits the child (lineage) without harming his Islam.
The claim is upheld for lineage but not for religion because changing religion would be harmful.
3
Identify the three narrations in conflict scenarios
When the finder's religion differs from the place (e.g., Muslim finder in dhimmi village), there are three views: (1) consider the place (al-Hidayah’s preferred view), (2) consider the finder (Ibn Samā‘a’s narration from Muhammad al-Shaybani), (3) consider the apparent Islam of the child.
These narrations are recorded in different chapters (Kitāb al-Laqīṭ vs. Kitāb al-Da‘wā).
4
Determine the muftā bihī position
The relied-upon position is that the place governs, due to its precedence and because it is the basis of the default rule.
This is the view in al-Hidayah and most later Hanafi sources.

Rulings on Foundling's Property and Finder's Authority
Stating the rulings on foundling's property and finder's powers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on property found with a foundling and identify the limits of the finder's guardianship according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The foundling's property belongs to him; the finder's authority is limited to necessities – not marriage, credit disposal, or hiring out.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah outlines the Hanafi position on the foundling's property and the extent of the finder's (multaqit) authority. Understanding these limits is crucial for anyone dealing with foundlings, as the finder's role is custodial, not parental. The passage addresses three key issues: ownership of property found with the child, its expenditure, and the finder's permissible actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main rulings
Read the passage and list the three distinct legal issues addressed: (1) property ownership, (2) expenditure of that property, (3) finder's authority over the child.
2
Analyze the property ownership ruling
Note that anything tied on the foundling is presumed to belong to the foundling. Consider why the ruling uses 'what is tied' and what the default presumption is.
This is based on apparent ownership (zahir al-hal).
3

Duration and Method of Announcing Luqata
Ruling on the duration and method of announcing found property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred Hanafi view on the duration of announcing found property and distinguish it from the Zahir al-Riwayah position.

Core Idea
The duration for announcing found property is not fixed; it depends on the finder's judgment until he is convinced the owner will not seek it.

When you find a lost item, you must announce it to find the owner. But for how long? The Hanafi school has an internal difference: the early transmitted position (Zahir al-Riwayah) prescribes a fixed year, while later authorities, led by Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, hold that the duration is left to the finder's reasonable estimation. This lesson unpacks the evidence for each view and explains why the flexible view became the operative fatwa.

Guided Path
1

2
Identify the internal opponent and their view
Recognize that the opposing view within the Hanafi school (attributed to Imam Muhammad, and also to Malik and Shafi'i) requires a full year of announcement for all luqata, regardless of value.
This is the position in Zahir al-Riwayah, the core transmitted texts.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Analyze the hadith 'Announce it for a year' (Bukhari and Muslim) as the primary dalil for the fixed-year view.
The imperative is taken literally by the opposing view.
4

Understand how the preferred Hanafi view reconciles the hadith: the year-long announcement applies to items of significant value as a precaution, while lesser items are left to estimation, supported by the hadith of Ali (ra) where the Prophet ordered only three days for a dinar.
Qiyas and istihsan (juristic preference) are used to limit the literal application.
5

Recognize the practical consequence: under the preferred view, the finder may stop announcing after a short period if confident; under the fixed-year view, he must wait a full year before using or disposing of the item.
This impacts the finder's liability and the owner's chance of recovery.

Core Idea
The finder's expense drops if the item is destroyed after detention—a ruling from Zufar, disputed by the three senior Hanafi imams.

This passage addresses a practical issue: a finder incurs expenses on a lost item, then detains it to recover those expenses. If the item is destroyed, does the finder still have a claim? The passage reveals that the relied-upon Hanafi position comes from Zufar, but it is contested by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad. Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the fate of the finder's expense (nafaqah) for a lost item if the item itself is destroyed. The core ruling states: if the finder has detained the item (for the purpose of recovering his expenses), and the item is destroyed, the reimbursement for those expenses is dropped. If the item is destroyed before such detention, the expense remains. The commentary identifies this as the apparent position in al-Hidayah, but reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute. Al-Quduri attributed this ruling to Zufar, while the three senior imams – Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani – hold that the expense does not drop upon destruction. Their reasoning: the finder's claim for expenses is a debt owed by the owner, and the found item is not a pawn such that its destruction extinguishes the debt. Later scholars note that the position of the three is more logically sound, though the mufta bihi (relied upon) position from al-Hidayah is Zufar's view. This passage thus illustrates the interplay between qiyas (analogy) and the actual transmission in the school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Accurate description makes delivery permissible, not obligatory; the finder may demand formal proof.

When a lost item's claimant describes its distinguishing marks correctly, is the finder compelled to hand it over? The Hanafi school says no — delivery is permissible but not judicially enforceable. This lesson examines the proof and the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State clearly: delivery is permissible (hal) but not wajib; the finder is not compelled by a judge.
The basis: distinguishing marks create probability, not certainty.
2
Examine the Shafi'i proof
Imam al-Shafi'i cites the hadith 'If its owner comes and knows its tie and number, give it to him.' He interprets the imperative as obligatory.
The operative word is 'fadfa' (give).
3
Analyze the Hanafi response

Under Shafi'i, a judge can compel delivery; under Hanafi, the finder can refuse and require formal proof.

Definition and Initial Ruling of the Missing Person
Defining the missing person and stating he is considered alive for his own benefit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a missing person (mafqud), state the initial Hanafi ruling regarding his status as alive for his own benefit and dead for others, and explain the legal basis of istishab.

Core Idea
A missing person is presumed alive to protect his own rights, but dead when that benefits others.

When a person's whereabouts are unknown and his life or death cannot be confirmed, how does the law treat him? This lesson introduces the chapter on the missing person in Hanafi fiqh. You will learn the precise definition, the initial ruling that balances his rights and the rights of others, and the foundational principle of istishab that underlies this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition of a missing person
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note that the missing person (mafqud) is defined as an absent person (gha'ib) whose trace is not known, meaning his life or death is uncertain. The definition excludes simply being away (e.g., a traveler) whose news is available.
The term 'mafqud' can also mean 'sought' – consider the dual meaning.
2
Understand the distinction: alive in his own right, dead in the right of others
The core rule: the missing person is treated as alive for matters that harm him (e.g., his wife cannot remarry, his wealth is not divided) and as dead for matters that benefit him (e.g., he is not entitled to new welfare payments meant for the living). This balances his rights and the rights of those affected by his absence.
The commentary emphasizes: 'he is considered alive in rulings that harm him, and dead in rulings that benefit him and harm others'.
3
Apply the rule to specific consequences
Three examples given: (1) his wife may not remarry – because declaring her a widow would dissolve the marriage (harmful to him). (2) His property is not divided among heirs – because inheritance requires death. (3) His lease contract is not nullified – because death would void it, but his death is unconfirmed.
Each consequence follows from the principle: his life is presumed to protect his interests.
4
Recognize the basis in istishab and its weakness
The presumption of continued life comes from istishab (presumption of continuity of the previous state). The text notes that istishab is a weak proof (hujja da'ifa), suitable only for repelling claims (daf‘) not for establishing new rights (ithbat). This means the missing person's status protects what he already has but does not grant new entitlements.
This distinction will become important when later rulings on inheritance, divorce, and property management are discussed.

The missing person (al-mafqud) presents a unique legal challenge: his status is uncertain. Commentary

Qadi's Duty to Appoint a Custodian for the Missing Person
Ruling with commentary on Qadi appointing a custodian for a missing person

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Qadi must protect the missing person's property and dependents like a guardian.

This passage explains the Hanafi ruling that the Qadi must appoint a representative (custodian) for a person who has disappeared and whose status is unknown. The custodian's role is limited to specific tasks: collecting debts, preserving property, selling perishable goods, and providing maintenance for certain relatives. The reasoning hinges on the Qadi being the general guardian (nazir li'l-ammah) and the missing person being unable to manage his own affairs, analogous to a child or insane person.

The Zahir al-Riwayah: Death of Contemporaries
Explains the Zahir al-Riwayah position and its rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Zahir al-Riwayah view on declaring a missing person dead and explain its methodological basis, including the inapplicability of qiyas to fixed periods.

Core Idea
The strongest Hanafi view avoids a fixed period; death is determined by the passing of contemporaries.

When a person goes missing, Islamic law must balance the rights of the missing person, his heirs, and his wife. The Hanafi school's most authoritative transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah) does not set a fixed number of years but instead looks to the death of the missing person's contemporaries. This lesson explains that position and its reasoning, grounded in the absence of textual evidence and the principle that analogy does not apply to fixed measurements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The core question: after what period should a missing person (mafqud) be declared dead? The answer affects inheritance, wife's remarriage, and other rulings.
2
Recognize Zahir al-Riwayah
According to the apparent madhhab, the missing person is considered dead when none of his contemporaries (aqran) remain alive. No fixed number of years is set.
Contemporaries are those born around the same time as the missing person.
3
Understand the proof
There is no explicit text (nass) fixing a period, and analogy (qiyas) is not applicable to measurements (muqaddarat). Therefore, the matter is referred to the death of peers.
This is analogous to valuations like the price of destroyed goods or dowry of the like.
4
Contrast with other narrations
Other narrations from Abu Hanifa (via Hasan ibn Ziyad) and the Sahibayn suggest fixed periods, but these are from the nawadir and not from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
The text criticizes these fixed-period views as based on unreliable customs or rare reports.

It begins by glossing the matn's phrase 'up to ninety years' and then launches into a detailed discussion of the different opinions within the Hanafi school on the period after which a missing person is ruled dead. The text presents the Zahir al-Riwayah position, which is that the missing person is considered dead when none of his contemporaries (aqran) remain alive. Istishab and the Missing Person: Property Rulings
Ruling on missing person's property with istishab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the missing person's property after the period and explain how istishab as a proof for defense (daf‘) determines the differential treatment.

Core Idea
Istishab preserves the missing person's own property from inheritance but prevents him from inheriting from others.

The missing person (al-mafqud) creates a unique legal challenge: should he be considered alive or dead? The Hanafi school resolves this with a nuanced application of istishab (presumption of continuity). This lesson unpacks the ruling that his own property is divided among his current heirs, while his share in others' estates reverts to their heirs, all grounded in the principle that istishab is a proof for defense, not for establishing new rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Contexts
The missing person's property is treated differently in two scenarios: his own property (which he owns) and the property of others (e.g., where he is an heir).
2
State the Ruling for Each Context
After the elapse of the specified waiting period, regarding his own property, he is considered alive (so his current heirs inherit now, not earlier heirs). Regarding others' property, he is considered dead from the time of loss (so his share is returned to the other deceased's heirs).
The matn says: 'His property is divided among those who inherit from him now, and regarding the property of others from the time he was lost, so what was held in abeyance for him is returned to those who inherit from the other at his death.'
3
Identify the Underlying Principle
The principle is istishab (presumption of continuity): 'The apparent state (zahir al-hal) is a proof for defense (daf‘) not for establishment (ithbat).' This is a Hanafi specific usul rule.
The commentator quotes al-Binaya: 'keeping what was as it was due to lack of evidence removing it.'
4
Apply Istishab to Each Context
For his own property: istishab of being alive prevents his earlier heirs (who died during the period) from inheriting—defense of the status quo. For others' property: istishab cannot establish his right to inherit from them, so his share lapses and goes to the others' heirs.
This is why he is 'alive in his own property, dead in the property of others.'
5
Note the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
The practical difference: if the missing person had a son and later his father died, the son inherits from the missing person's own property, but the missing person does not inherit from the father; the share held for the missing person goes to the father's other heirs.

This passage is from the chapter on the missing person (al-mafqud) and deals with the ruling on his property after a certain period.
