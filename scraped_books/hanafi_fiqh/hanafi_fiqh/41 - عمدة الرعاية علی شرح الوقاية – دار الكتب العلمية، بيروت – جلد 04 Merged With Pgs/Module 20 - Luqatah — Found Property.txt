Ruling on Picking Up a Foundling (Laqit)
Explains the ruling and conditions for picking up a foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on picking up a foundling and identify the conditions that make it obligatory (fard).

Core Idea
Picking up a foundling is recommended; it becomes a necessity when his life is in danger.

This passage from the Book of the Foundling (Kitab al-Laqit) addresses a question of life and moral duty: what should a person do upon finding an abandoned child? The author lays out a spectrum of rulings—from commendable to obligatory—based on the level of risk to the child. The commentary clarifies key terms and conditions, ensuring that the ruling is understood correctly and aligned with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Read the opening phrase: 'رفعه أحب' (picking him up is commendable). Note that the default ruling is mandub (recommended).
The term 'أحب' is used for mandub.
2
Understand the escalated ruling
Look for the conditional clause: 'وإن خيف هلاكه يجب' (and if his destruction is feared, it becomes obligatory). This introduces the exception.
The word 'يجب' is clarified later.
3
Examine the commentary's clarification of 'wajib'
Read the commentary from Al-Bahr: the word 'wajib' here means 'fard' (necessary obligation), not the technical Hanafi 'wajib'. This removes any perceived inter-madhhab disagreement.
The distinction is important to avoid confusion with other acts where 'wajib' is a separate category.
4
Note the conditions for the finder (multaqit)
From Al-Nahr: the finder must be mukallaf (legally responsible). He need not be Muslim, just, or sensible; even a disbeliever or restricted slave can validly pick up.
This expands the scope of the obligation.
5
Trace the analogy (qiyas) to lost property
The text says 'كاللقطة' (like lost property). Understand that the ruling for foundling is derived by analogy to luqatah, with the common cause ('illa) being the risk of loss.
The analogy extends from property to soul, preserving the principle of saving what is valuable.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Foundling (Kitab al-Laqit) discusses the ruling on picking up a foundling—an abandoned child. The author begins by stating: 'Picking him up is commendable, but if his destruction is feared, it becomes obligatory, like the lost property.' This establishes a spectrum of rulings based on necessity. The commentator then explains why this topic follows jihad in the book's arrangement: because souls and wealth are at risk, and the foundling (a soul) takes precedence over lost property (wealth). Linguistically, 'laqit' is derived from 'laqata' (to pick up) and refers to a child abandoned due to fear of poverty or accusation of adultery. The ruling: initially, it is recommended (mandub) to pick up a foundling, but once picked up, it becomes prohibited to abandon him again because preserving him becomes obligatory. The word 'yajib' (obligatory) here is clarified: it means fard (a necessary obligation) not just wujub in the technical sense. This aligns with other schools, removing any perceived disagreement. Conditions for the finder (multaqit) are implied: he must be legally responsible (mukallaf)—so a child or insane person cannot validly pick up a foundling. However, he need not be Muslim, just, or sensible; even a disbeliever, sinner, or restricted slave may pick him up, and in fact such persons are preferable because of the greater need. The analogy to lost property (luqatah) is drawn: picking up lost property is recommended but becomes fard when its loss is feared, provided the finder is safe from harm; if he fears for his own safety, leaving it is better. The same principle applies to the foundling.

Key Terms
اللقيط (al-laqīṭ): An abandoned child picked up from the ground; a verbal noun meaning 'something picked up'.
اللقطة (al-luqatah): Lost property belonging to someone else that is found.
الالتقاط (al-iltiqāṭ): The act of picking up a foundling or lost property.

Luqatah: Liability of the Finder and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
States ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the finder's liability in luqatah with and without witnesses, identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement, and apply the exceptions.

Core Idea
Without witnesses, the finder of a lost item is liable unless unable to obtain witnesses or fearing harm.

The finder of a lost item must handle it with care. According to the Hanafi school, taking it with witnesses establishes trust; without it, the finder becomes liable. However, Imam Abu Yusuf dissents. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proof, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Define luqatah and identify its excluded cases
You must first understand what counts as luqatah (property found lost, owner unknown, not mubah). Exclude harbi property, items in secure places, and mubah items. This sets the scope of the ruling.
The definition comes from the first footnote in the passage.
2
State the default ruling on liability
The matn clearly states: with witnesses = amanah (no liability); without witnesses = daman (liability). This is the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
Liability arises only if the owner later claims the item and the finder's intention to return is in question.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf differs: he holds the finder is not liable without witnesses. His basis is that the Shari' sanctions the act of taking with intention to return, so it remains amanah.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on interpretation of the hadith's imperative.
4
Apply the exceptions
If the finder is unable to get witnesses (none available) or fears harm from a tyrant, the requirement of witnessing drops and the finder is not liable by consensus.
This protects the finder when witnessing is not feasible.
5
Derive the practical consequence
In practice, a finder should always try to have witnesses (or record the find) to avoid liability. If witnesses are not possible, he may still take the item but accepts the risk of liability.
This reflects the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Luqatah (lost and found property) and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the finder's liability. The author first provides a linguistic definition of luqatah: it is property found lost, whose owner is unknown, and which is not permissible for the finder to take without proper procedure. Excluded are property of a harbi (enemy combatant), property found in a secure place like a house or trunk (as that indicates it is not truly lost but perhaps placed there), and mubah (ownerless permissible) items. The core ruling: if the finder takes the luqatah with witnesses to return it to the owner, it is a trust (amanah) in his hands and he is not liable for its loss unless negligent. If he does not have witnesses, he is liable if the owner later claims it and denies having received it. The text then notes the consensus that if the finder admits he took it for himself, he is liable by agreement of all scholars, including those of other schools, because that is akin to usurpation (ghasp). The intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that without witnesses, the finder is liable because his action lacked Shari' sanction. Abu Yusuf holds the finder is not liable because the Shari' permits the act of taking lost property with the intention to return, regardless of witnessing; the hadith commanding witnesses is interpreted as recommendatory, not obligatory. The text supports Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's view as the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). An important exception is noted: if the finder is unable to obtain witnesses (none available) or fears harm from a tyrant, he may omit witnessing and is not liable by consensus. The passage thus clarifies that the default rule for taking luqatah is that witnessing is required to establish trust status; failure to witness renders the finder potentially liable.

Key Terms
لقطة (luqatah): Property found lost whose owner is unknown and which is not permissible for the finder to take without proper procedure (i.e., not mubah).
أمانة (amanah): A trust; the legal status of the found item in the finder's hands when he takes it with witnesses and intention to return, such that he is not liable for loss without negligence.
ضمان (daman): Liability; obligation to compensate for loss or damage. In this context, the finder must pay the value of the item to the owner if he fails to return it and the owner denies receiving it.
إشهاد (ishhad): The act of making others witness the taking of the luqatah, so that the finder's intention to return it is established.

Ruling on finder's liability depending on intention and witnesses, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Without witnesses, the finder's liability is presumed; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold him liable, Abu Yusuf does not.

This passage from Kitab al-Luqata presents the ruling on found property (luqata) as a trust (amanah) if the finder takes witnesses to confirm his intention to return it. However, a key intra-Hanafi dispute arises when the finder does not take witnesses: does he become liable (daman) if the owner later denies his intent? Understanding this difference is essential for applying the correct ruling in practice.

State the positions

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Luqata opens with the ruling that found property (luqata) is a trust (amanah) if the finder takes it with witnesses (ishhad) for the purpose of returning it to its owner. If he does not take witnesses, he becomes liable (daman) if the owner denies that the finder took it for return. The author explains that if the finder admits taking it for himself, he is liable by consensus. If he does not admit that, the ruling differs: according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the finder must have taken witnesses to avoid liability; without witnesses, he is liable. According to Imam Abu Yusuf, the finder is not liable even without witnesses, because the mere intention to return suffices. The text then defines luqata as lost property whose owner is unknown and which is not permissible to take (i.e., not abandoned property or property of a war enemy). The proof for taking witnesses is the hadith reported by Ishaq ibn Rahwayh. The rationale for liability without witnesses is analogy to usurpation (ghasb): taking another's property without permission. The absence of liability with witnesses is because the law permits and even encourages the act, and there is no liability for a legally recommended or obligatory act. Finally, an exception is noted: if the finder is unable to find witnesses or fears harm, he is not liable by agreement. This is an intra-Hanafi dispute (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad vs. Abu Yusuf) with the relied-upon position being that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (i.e., liability without witnesses) as stated in the matn.

Key Terms
لقطة (luqata): Property found lost whose owner is unknown and which is not permissible to take (i.e., not abandoned or enemy property).
إشهاد (ishhad): Taking witnesses to testify that one has taken the found property for the purpose of returning it to its owner.
ضمان (daman): Legal liability to compensate the owner for the value of the lost property if it is lost or damaged while in the finder's possession without proper intent.

Core Idea
A perishable found item may be used after fear of spoilage, but the wealthy finder must give charity on behalf of the owner.

This lesson examines the Hanafi exception for found items that spoil quickly. Ruling on Perishable Luqatah: Definition, Charity, and Owner's Option
Expansion on perishable luqatah with proof and cross-madhhab comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on perishable luqatah, explain the finder's obligations and optional actions, and identify the cross-madhhab difference regarding found animals.

Core Idea
Perishable luqatah: define until spoilage, then charity or use; owner later approves or claims compensation.

When you find an item that spoils quickly—like food or ripe fruit—the standard procedure of announcing it for a full year would be pointless. Islamic law addresses this through rational specification and consensus. In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi ruling on such items, including the finder's duty, the options of charity or personal use based on wealth, the owner's subsequent rights, and the uniform treatment of all lost property including animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type and characteristic
First, recognise that the luqatah in question is perishable (e.g., food, some fruits), meaning it cannot last a full year of definition without spoiling.
Not all fruits spoil quickly; 'some fruits' indicates selectivity.
2
Understand the finder's duty: definition until spoilage
The finder must define/announce the item until he fears it will spoil. The general command to define for a year is specified here by rational indication (the purpose would be lost) and consensus.
Definition is wajib until the point of imminent spoilage.
3
Analyse the post-definition options
After definition without owner appearing, the finder has two main paths: (a) give it in charity (if he is wealthy), or (b) use it himself (if he is poor). The owner later has the choice to approve the charity and earn reward, or claim the value (daman) from the finder.
Charity for the wealthy is recommended/obligatory; use for the poor is permissible.
4
Examine the dalil and its specification
The primary dalil is the hadith: 'Recognize its tie and its container, and announce it for a year.' This imperative (amr) indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. However, for perishables, the ruling is specified by rational necessity (otherwise the item would be wasted) and by consensus of the scholars.
The hadith is ahad and zanni al-thubut, but its specification is accepted.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
In the Hanafi school, there is no distinction between found animals and other items—all are treated equally. According to Malik and al-Shafi'i, if a camel or cow is found in the desert, special rules apply. The Hanafi position is based on the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs, as narrated by Imam Muhammad.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both views are respected.

Expenses on Lost Property: Permission vs. Donation
States the ruling on expenses on lost property with and without judge's permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on expenses incurred on lost property and explain the legal distinction between spending with and without the judge's permission.

Core Idea
Finder's spending on lost property is a donation unless authorized by the judge, who holds general guardianship over absent owners.

This lesson addresses a common dilemma: when you find a lost item and spend money on it, can you get reimbursed? Hanafi fiqh provides a clear rule based on whether the judge's permission was obtained. The passage establishes that the judge's general authority (wilayah 'ammah) legitimizes debt, while the finder's independent act is merely voluntary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct cases: spending without the judge's permission and spending with his permission.
2
Classify the legal consequence
Without permission, the spending is a donation (tabarru') – the finder cannot claim reimbursement. With permission, it becomes a debt (dayn) on the owner.
Dayn is a financial liability that must be repaid.
3
Understand the rationale
The judge has general authority (wilayah 'ammah) over the property of absent persons, so his authorization creates a valid debt. The finder lacks such authority.
Wilayah 'ammah is a key concept in Hanafi fiqh for guardianship of absent persons.
4
Apply the distinction
In practice, a finder must seek judicial authorization before incurring expenses if they wish to have a right to reimbursement.
This protects the owner from unauthorized debts.

Commentary
This is a ruling on the expenses a finder may incur on a lost item (luqata). The matn (core text) establishes a clear distinction: if the finder spends without the judge's permission, it is considered voluntary charity (tabarru'), meaning he has no right to claim reimbursement from the owner. Key Terms

دين (dayn): A debt; a financial obligation owed by the owner to the finder when the latter spent with the judge's permission.
ولاية عامة (wilayah 'ammah): General authority, specifically the judge's guardianship over the property of absent persons.

Condition for Finder's Reimbursement on Found Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the requirement of a condition for reimbursement on found property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a finder of lost property can claim reimbursement for expenses, and identify the condition that must be present for this right.

Core Idea
The finder's right to reimbursement requires an explicit condition of recourse from the judge; a mere order to spend is insufficient.

In the Hanafi school, when someone finds a lost item and spends money to preserve it, can they later demand that the owner repay them? The answer depends on a specific condition set by the judge. This lesson explores the classical Hanafi position on this question, focusing on an intra-madhhab dispute about what constitutes sufficient authorization. Understanding this condition protects the rights of both the finder and the owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The dispute is about what is required for a finder to claim back expenses from the owner of found property: is a judge's mere order to spend enough, or must the judge explicitly stipulate a condition of recourse?
This is the central ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
2
State the two Hanafi views
The passage mentions two riwāyāt (transmitted opinions): one holds that the judge's order to spend automatically grants the right to recourse; the relied-upon view (al-aṣaḥḥ) requires the judge to explicitly stipulate the condition of recourse (shart al-rujū‘).
The stronger view is the one requiring explicit condition.
3
Trace the evidence for each view
The view requiring the condition relies on the principle that the owner's liability is not automatic—it must be created by an explicit stipulation. The opposing view draws an analogy with the runaway slave (ābiq), where the finder can hire out the slave without explicit condition, implying that the order itself suffices.
The reconciliation in the text shows the differing contexts.
4
Determine the muftā bihī position
The text explicitly says 'fī al-aṣaḥḥ' (in the most correct view), indicating that the condition must be explicit. This is the operative position for fatwa.
This is the tarjīḥ settled by later Hanafi authorities.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
For example, if a judge tells a finder 'spend on this lost book' but says nothing about recourse, the finder cannot later claim the money from the owner. If the judge says 'spend with the right to recover from the owner', then he can.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilāf—the practical consequence.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Al-Wiqayah regarding the rulings on found property (luqatah). It expands on two key scenarios: property with benefit and property without benefit. For property with benefit (e.g., an animal), the judge (or finder with permission) hires it out and spends the hire on its upkeep, preserving the owner's ownership without incurring debt. An analogy is drawn with a runaway slave (abaq), where the finder similarly hires him out. A dispute between Al-Hidayah and Al-Tabyin is reconciled: hiring is allowed if the slave has a distinguishing mark preventing escape or if the lessee is strong and trustworthy. For property without benefit, the judge permits spending only after the finder proves it is a lost item (to avoid usurpation) and stipulates the condition of recourse on the owner. The relied-upon (asahh) view is that this condition is necessary; a mere order to spend is insufficient. If spending is not beneficial (e.g., expenses may exceed value), the judge orders sale and preservation of the price. Finally, the finder may detain the found property until the owner reimburses the expenses, likened to a seller's right to retain goods until payment.

اللقطة (al-luqatah): Found property; wealth picked up from the ground or found without known owner, subject to specific rulings on preservation and return.
شرط الرجوع (shart al-ruju'): The condition stipulated by the judge when permitting spending on found property, allowing the finder to claim reimbursement from the owner.
الآبق (al-abiq): A runaway slave; mentioned as an analogy for found property with benefit, where the finder may hire out the slave and spend from the hire.

Sale of Lost Item and Finder's Right of Detention
Commentary expanding the ruling on selling a lost item and detaining it for expenses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a judge orders the sale of a lost item and explain the finder's right to detain it for expenses.

Core Idea
The finder may detain the lost item for his expenses, but only if the judge explicitly stipulated the right of recourse.

When a lost item cannot be preserved without expense, the judge may order the finder to spend, but with a crucial condition: the owner's reimbursement must be explicitly stipulated. If expenses risk consuming the item's value, the judge orders sale instead. This lesson unpacks the rulings on sale and detention in the Hanafi school from a key commentary. Understanding these conditions prevents wrongful claims and clarifies the finder's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two judicial options
Distinguish when the judge orders the finder to spend on the lost item versus when he orders its sale. The decision hinges on whether spending is 'more beneficial' or feared to consume the item's value.
The passage states: if ordering spending is beneficial (صلح), the judge orders it for a short period; otherwise, he orders sale.
2
Understand the condition for recourse
Recognize that the finder's right to claim reimbursement from the owner is not automatic. The judge must explicitly stipulate the right of recourse (شرط الرجوع) when ordering the finder to spend. Without it, the finder cannot demand repayment.
The commentary clarifies: an order to spend alone does not grant the authority to seek reimbursement.
3
Analyze the detention right
The finder may detain the lost item (حبسها) as security for his expenses. This is analogous to a seller detaining goods until payment. The detention is permissible until the owner reimburses the finder.
The commentary likens it to a sale: the owner obtains ownership 'from' the finder, so detention is justified.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a specific case, determine the judge's action based on the risk to the item's value and the presence of a stipulation. Then state whether the finder can detain the item.
Remember: if the judge did not stipulate recourse, the finder cannot detain or claim expenses, even if he spent with the judge's permission.

Perishing of Detained Lost Item: Expense Not Forgiven
Dispute among Hanafi scholars about expense forgiveness after item perishes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi ruling on whether the finder's expenses are forgiven when a detained lost item perishes, and explain why the analogy to a pledge is rejected.

Core Idea
Detention for expenses does not create a pledge; the debt remains even if the item perishes.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about the fate of expenses incurred by a finder of a lost item (luqatah) when the item perishes after the finder has detained it. The base text in Al-Hidayah suggests the expense is forgiven by analogy to a pledge, but later authorities clarify that this view belongs only to Imam Zufar, not to the founding imams. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between true contractual pledges and possessory liens in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
The base text states that if the lost item perishes after detention, the expense is dropped; if before detention, it is not. This is the starting point of the discussion.
This ruling is attributed to Al-Hidayah, but its authenticity as a Hanafi position is contested.
2

Handing Over Lost Item Upon Identification of Marks
Ruling on when it is permissible to hand over the lost item to a claimant who describes its distinguishing marks

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for permissibly handing over a lost item to a claimant and apply them to a scenario.

Core Idea
Handing over is only permissible when the claimant's description matches all marks completely.

When someone claims a lost item, the finder must ensure the claimant is the true owner. This lesson explains the key condition: the claimant must describe the item's distinguishing marks such that the description matches the item exactly. We will also examine the consequences when a second claimant later proves ownership with evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is handing over a lost item (luqatah) to a claimant. The ruling is that it becomes permissible (halal) when the claimant correctly describes the item's distinguishing marks.
2
List the conditions for permissibility
Primary condition: the claimant must describe the item's distinguishing marks (e.g., weight, number, container, tie-string). Alternatively, if the finder confirms the claimant's truthfulness, handing over is permissible even without marks.
The finder's confirmation (tasdiq) is a separate route.
3
Note the requirement for complete matching
The description must match the item entirely; partial matching is insufficient. For example, specifying only the container but not the weight does not suffice.
'Matching in some marks alone is not sufficient.'
4
Handle multiple claimants
If two claimants each correctly describe the item, handing over to both is permissible (likely each gets a share or judge decides).
The text says 'حل الدفع لهما'.
5
Understand the effect of later evidence
If after handing over, another person brings evidence (bayyinah) of ownership: if the item is still extant, the second claimant takes it; if destroyed, the judge may hold liable either the finder or the first recipient. If the finder is held liable, he can recover from the first recipient.
The finder's earlier confirmation is overridden by the court's judgment.

Core Idea
A poor finder may use the lost item; a rich finder must give it in charity.

Finding a lost item (luqata) raises the question: may the finder use it if the owner never appears? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on the finder's wealth. This lesson unpacks the two hadiths that seem to give conflicting instructions and shows how the scholars harmonized them.

The ruling is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

Legal rulings on taking fugitive and stray slaves, with conditions and ikhtilaf

Taking the fugitive slave is recommended; leaving the stray is better—unless loss is feared, then it becomes obligatory.

After establishing the definitions, the text turns to the legal rulings. The passage also notes that if one fears the slave will be lost, taking becomes fard (obligatory) and taking for oneself becomes haram. The commentary emphasizes that the stray slave remains in his place unlike the fugitive slave who moves away.

Commentary on the reward amount and conditions for returning a runaway slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the reward amount for returning a runaway slave, distinguish between the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and explain why Abu Yusuf's view is the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Forty dirhams is fixed for a three-day journey, and reduces proportionally – Abu Yusuf's view prevails over Muhammad's.

In this passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, the commentator expands on the reward (ju'ala) for returning a runaway slave. The text presents two intra-Hanafi views on the amount when the slave's value is less than forty dirhams, and the relied-upon position. Understanding this dispute clarifies how textual evidence overrides analogy in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Read the passage to find the two positions: Abu Yusuf holds that 40 dirhams is fixed regardless of the slave's value; Muhammad holds that if the slave is worth less than 40, the finder gets the value minus one dirham.
Look for the phrases 'لأبي يوسف' and 'وعند محمد'.
2
Analyze the dalil for each position
Abu Yusuf relies on the text (nas) that sets the amount at 40 dirhams; Muhammad focuses on the purpose (preserving owner's property) and uses a ta'lil (ratio) to reduce the amount.
Consider the role of textual evidence vs. reasoning in their arguments.
3

Later texts (e.g., Minah al-Ghaffar) prefer Abu Yusuf's view because it conforms to the text and is supported by al-Marghinani and al-'Ayni.
The tarjih mechanism: conformity to the explicit text outweighs teleological reasoning.
4
Examine the condition of testimony (ishhad)
The passage states that the finder must testify when taking the slave that he intends to return it, if able. Otherwise, no reward.
This is a condition for entitlement, not a pillar.
5

For returns less than three days, the reward is pro-rated from 40 dirhams. The Shafi'i school does not require a condition (stipulation) for the reward, but the Hanafi school insists on it based on the Companions' practice.
