Definition and Conditions of Waqf in the Hanafi School
Defines waqf and outlines its essential conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of waqf, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding ownership of the corpus, and list the general and special conditions for a valid waqf.

Core Idea
Waqf detains the corpus but ownership stays with the founder; only the usufruct is given as charity.

Waqf (endowment) is a pivotal institution in Islamic law for perpetual charity. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah defines waqf linguistically and legally, highlighting the famous disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two students over who owns the endowed property. Understanding this definition is essential before studying the detailed conditions and rulings of waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning and legal definition
Read the opening lines: waqf linguistically means detention (habs). Legally, Abu Hanifa defines it as detaining the corpus in the ownership of the founder while giving the usufruct as charity. Note the contrast with the Sahibayn's definition.
The text gives Abu Hanifa's view first; the Sahibayn's view is mentioned later.
2
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute over ownership
Compare Abu Hanifa's position (corpus remains in waqif's ownership) with that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (corpus is removed from human ownership, belonging to Allah). Consider the practical consequences: revocability, sale, inheritance.
This dispute affects many later rulings and is a hallmark of Hanafi waqf law.
3
Extract the general conditions of waqf
The passage lists conditions common to all charitable gifts: the founder must be sane, adult, free, not under interdiction, and the waqf must not be suspended on a future condition. Note also that Islam is not a requirement.
These conditions ensure the founder has legal capacity.
4
Understand the special condition for exiting ownership
According to Abu Hanifa, for a waqf to be valid (and remove the corpus from the waqif's ownership), it must be either added to after death (like a will) or have a ruling attached to it. Abu Yusuf disagreed with this special condition.
This is a unique point of disagreement between the imams.
5
Identify the rukn (pillar) of waqf
The pillar of waqf is the specific expression (lafz) used by the founder, such as 'This land is sadaqah muwaqqafah mu'abbadah upon the poor'.
The wording must indicate permanence and charity.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the Book of Waqf from the commentary on al-Wiqayah. It begins by defining waqf linguistically and legally. Linguistically, waqf means detention (al-habs). Legally, it is described according to Imam Abu Hanifa as the detention of the corpus (al-ayn) while retaining ownership of the corpus in the waqif (founder) and giving away its usufruct (manfa'ah) as charity. According to the two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, known as al-Sahibayn), waqf is detention of the corpus in the ownership of none other than Allah—meaning the corpus is removed from human ownership entirely. This is a significant intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that ownership remains with the waqif, while the Sahibayn hold that it is transferred to Allah's ownership. The practical effect relates to issues like whether the waqif can revoke or sell the waqf property. The passage then lists the general conditions of waqf, which are like those for other voluntary gifts: the founder must be sane, adult, and free. The endowment cannot be suspended (mu'allaq) on a future condition, e.g., 'If my son arrives, my house is sadaqah muwaqqafah'—this is invalid. Islam is not required; a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject) may validly endow upon his children. The founder must not be under legal interdiction (hajr) for foolishness. The passage then mentions a special condition for exiting ownership according to Abu Hanifa: the waqf must be either added to after death (like a will) or have a ruling attached to it—this is in disagreement with Abu Yusuf. The pillar (rukn) of waqf is the specific expressions used, such as 'This land of mine is sadaqah, muwaqqafah, mu'abbadah (perpetual) upon the poor.' The commentator then explains two points: 'al-ayn' must be valuable property, real estate, or movable items commonly transacted; and 'giving away usufruct' includes even general usufruct, such as endowment upon oneself (though that has its own rules).

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Linguistically: detention (al-habs). Legally according to Abu Hanifa: detention of the corpus in the ownership of the founder with charity of the usufruct.
حبس العين (habs al-ayn): Detention of the physical corpus (the property itself) so that it is not sold, given, or inherited.
التصدق بالمنفعة (al-tasaddaq bil-manfa'ah): Giving away the usufruct (benefit or income) of the property as charity, either to specific beneficiaries or for general purposes.
عاقلاً بالغاً حراً (aqilan balighan hurran): The founder must be sane, adult, and free. These are general conditions for all charitable gifts (تبرعات).
محجور عليه (mahjur alayh): A person under legal interdiction (hajr) due to foolishness or other reasons; such a person cannot perform a valid waqf.

Waqf: Definition and the Intra-Hanafi Ownership Dispute
Definition of waqf with intra-Hanafi ownership dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of waqf, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on ownership of the corpus, and explain the practical consequence of this dispute.

Core Idea
The waqf corpus remains the waqif's property (Abu Hanifa) or enters Allah's ownership (Sahibayn) — a dispute with real consequences for revocability.

Waqf, a perpetual charitable endowment, is a cornerstone of Islamic social finance. Yet its legal definition sparks a profound intra-Hanafi debate: who owns the corpus? This lesson unpacks the definitions, conditions, and the ownership dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the act and its ruling
Waqf is defined linguistically and legally. State the Hanafi legal definition: 'حبس العين على ملك الواقف والتصدق بالمنفعة' (detention of the corpus in the waqif's ownership, giving the usufruct in charity).
This is the definition according to Abu Hanifa.
2
2. Distinguish the two definitions
Abu Hanifa: corpus remains in waqif's ownership. Sahibayn: corpus enters the ownership of Allah (لا على ملك أحد غير الله). Note the subtle difference: the Sahibayn's definition removes it from any human ownership.
Some add 'حكم' (legal ruling) to clarify.
3
3. Examine the conditions and pillar
Conditions include waqif's capacity (sane, adult, free, not interdicted), no suspension (تعليق), and for Abu Hanifa, the waqf must be immediate or added to after death. The pillar (rukn) is the specific verbal expression.
The waqf is valid even from a non-Muslim.
4
4. Analyze the ownership dispute's practical impact
The thamarah al-khilaf: According to Abu Hanifa, the waqif retains ownership and can revoke or sell the corpus as long as the waqf's purpose is not contravened. According to the Sahibayn, the waqf is irrevocable and the corpus cannot be sold, inherited, or gifted.
Later Hanafi practice favors the Sahibayn's view for permanence.

Commentary
This passage defines waqf (endowment) according to the Hanafi school and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the ownership of the waqf corpus. The author begins by stating the definition: waqf is 'the detention of the corpus in the ownership of the waqif and the giving in charity of the usufruct.' He then quotes from Fath al-Qadir, which explains the connection between waqf and partnership, both preserving the original while benefiting from the increase. The linguistic and legal definitions are provided. Legally, according to Abu Hanifa, the corpus remains in the ownership of the waqif, but the usufruct is given as charity. According to the Sahibayn, the corpus is detained not in the ownership of any human but in the ownership of Allah (i.e., it is removed from human ownership entirely). The passage then outlines the conditions for a valid waqf: the waqif must be sane, adult, free, and not under interdiction; the waqf cannot be suspended (e.g., conditional on a future event); Islam is not a requirement (a non-Muslim may make waqf for his children). A specific condition according to Abu Hanifa is that the waqf be effective immediately or connected to death, while Abu Yusuf holds that it can be effective after death. The pillar (rukn) of waqf is its specific verbal expression. The author then elaborates on the terms 'corpus' (must be valuable property, real estate or movable) and 'giving in charity of the usufruct' (includes even temporary charity). He draws an analogy to a loan (ariyah) where the corpus remains with the lender but the usufruct is given. Based on his view that the corpus remains his property, Abu Hanifa allows the waqif to dispose of the waqf property (e.g., sell or lease it) as long as he does not conflict with the purpose, similar to a borrower's use of a loaned item. The Sahibayn, holding that ownership transfers to Allah, maintain that the waqf is irrevocable and cannot be sold or inherited. The author notes that some scholars add the word 'legal ruling' (hukm) to say 'detention in the legal ruling of the ownership of Allah' to clarify that it is neither in the waqif's ownership nor transferred to another. Ibn al-Humam approves of Imam Malik's definition that the corpus remains in the waqif's ownership but is inalienable (cannot be sold, inherited, or gifted), similar to the status of an umm walad (a slave who bears her master's child) or a mudabbar (a slave whose emancipation is deferred until the master's death).

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Legally, the detention of a corpus (عين) in the ownership of the waqif (according to Abu Hanifa) or in the ownership of Allah (according to the Sahibayn), with the benefit given in charity.
العين (al-'ayn): The corpus or physical asset of the waqf, which must be valuable property, either real estate or movable goods that are commonly transacted.
المنفعة (al-manfa'ah): The usufruct or benefit derived from the corpus, which is given in charity to the beneficiaries of the waqf.

The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute on Waqf: Ownership, Bindingness, and Practical Examples
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf definition and bindingness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on waqf ownership and bindingness, apply the rule of bindingness to examples of utilitarian endowments (sikaya, khan, cemetery), and distinguish the mosque case.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa treats waqf like a loan (ownership retained); the Sahibayn see divine ownership (immediately binding).

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah defines waqf and unpacks a well-known dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The disagreement is not about the validity of waqf – all three consider it a permissible and commendable act – but about its binding nature (luzum) and whether the endower retains ownership. Understanding this dispute is crucial to correctly apply waqf rules to different types of endowments, from mosques to watering places.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Find Abu Hanifa's definition: 'kal-ariyah' (like a loan) and the Sahibayn's definition: 'habs al-ayn ala milk Allah' (confinement of the asset to the ownership of Allah). What does each imply about ownership?
Abu Hanifa's definition retains ownership; Sahibayn's removes it.
2
Determine the point of disagreement
Both agree waqf is permissible (ja'iz). The dispute is on bindingness (luzum): Abu Hanifa says it is non-binding unless a judge decrees or it is made as a will; the Sahibayn say it is binding immediately.
Revocability vs. irrevocability.
3
Apply to utilitarian assets
For a watering place (sikaya), caravanserai (khan), ribat, or cemetery, Abu Hanifa says ownership does not leave until a judge rules. Why? Because the endower can still benefit (e.g., drink, stay, be buried).
Endower's retained benefit prevents automatic transfer.
4
Contrast with a mosque
A mosque is different: the endower forfeits all personal benefit (cannot pray there exclusively), so the asset becomes dedicated to Allah immediately without a judge's decree.
Complete cessation of benefit triggers immediate transfer.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The Sahibayn's view (binding immediately) is the relied-upon position in the Zahir al-Riwayah and is adopted by the majority of scholars, including the author of Al-Hidayah.
Fatwa is given on the binding view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature and bindingness of waqf. The author first presents the definition of waqf according to Abu Hanifa: it is like a loan ('ariyah) where the asset remains in the ownership of the endower but the benefit is given to the beneficiaries. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) define it as the confinement of the asset to the ownership of Allah, meaning it leaves the endower's ownership entirely. The text then gives examples: endowing on the poor, building a watering place (sikaya), a caravanserai (khan), a ribat (inn) for travelers, or making land a cemetery. According to Abu Hanifa, in all these cases the endower's ownership remains and the endowment is not binding until a judge rules on it or it is made as a bequest. The disciples hold it is binding immediately. The passage emphasizes that the dispute is not about validity (all agree waqf is valid) but about bindingness (luzum). The commentary explains that Abu Hanifa's view is based on the analogy to a loan, while the disciples see it as a transfer to divine ownership. The hidayah excerpt clarifies that for a mosque, ownership immediately transfers to Allah because the endower loses all right to benefit, but for other endowments like a cemetery or watering place, the endower can still benefit, so ownership remains until judicial decree. The text also discusses the conditions for valid waqf: it must be a charitable act (qurba), so endowing solely on the rich is invalid, but if it ultimately goes to the poor it is valid.

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): The confinement of an asset such that its benefit is directed to a charitable cause while the asset itself remains preserved. Abu Hanifa defines it as like a loan (ownership remains with waqif); the Sahibayn define it as confinement to the ownership of Allah.
العارية (al-ariyah): A loan of an item for use, where the item remains the property of the lender. Abu Hanifa analogizes waqf to this, meaning the waqif retains ownership.
حبس العين (habs al-ayn): The literal confining of the physical asset, preventing its sale, gift, or inheritance, while its benefit is given to the specified beneficiaries.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Bindingness of Waqf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf bindingness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on waqf bindingness (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Waqf is permissible for all Hanafi imams, but only binding (lazim) according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, not Abu Hanifa.

Waqf (endowment) is a well-known act of charity, but within the Hanafi school there is a key difference: while all three imams agree waqf is permissible, they differ on whether it is binding (lazim) upon declaration. This dispute has practical consequences for the waqif's ability to revoke the endowment and for the inheritance of the property. In this lesson, we unpack the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the evidence each uses, and the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.

Guided Path
1
Establish common ground: permissibility
Note that all three imams agree that waqf is permissible (ja'iz). The dispute is not on permissibility, but on bindingness (luzum). The text refutes the misconception that Abu Hanifa prohibits waqf.
Refer to al-Tarabulsy's quote: 'هو جائز عند علمائنا الثلاثة وأصحابهم'
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that waqf is like a loan (ariyah): the waqif retains ownership, may revoke (with karahah), and the property is inheritable. Binding occurs only by a judge's ruling or by making it a will (wasiyyah).
The text uses 'لا يزول ملك الواقف عنه' and conditions for binding.
3
Identify the companions' positions
Abu Yusuf: Binding by mere utterance; ownership leaves immediately, no delivery needed. Muhammad: Binding only after actual delivery (taslim) by the beneficiaries using the property (e.g., drinking, staying).
Note the difference between utterance and delivery.
4
Examine the evidence
The text cites the hadith 'لا حبس عن فرائض الله' as Abu Hanifa's proof. Also, the analogy to 'ariyah. The companions rely on general practice of the Companions and the principle of permanent charity.
The hadith is weak (zanni al-thubut), used as supporting evidence.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text states 'وهو الصحيح' referring to the binding view of the two companions. This is the position of the majority of scholars, and it is the operative ruling for fatwa.
Tarjih authority: Al-Tarabulsy and al-Hidayah affirm this view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the ruling of waqf (endowment). The central issue is whether waqf removes ownership immediately and becomes binding (lazim), or whether it merely transfers usufruct while ownership remains, allowing revocation. The text begins by stating a general rule: if someone dedicates a ribat, a cemetery, etc., ownership does not leave the waqif. The commentator clarifies that this refers to Abu Hanifa's position. The correct understanding is that all three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) agree on the permissibility of waqf, but they differ on its bindingness. Abu Hanifa holds that waqf is like a loan (ariyah): the waqif can revoke it during his lifetime (with karahah), and it is inheritable. It only becomes binding if a judge rules it or if it is made as a will (i.e., suspended on death). Abu Yusuf holds that ownership is removed by the mere utterance, without requiring delivery (taslim), making waqf binding immediately. Muhammad holds that ownership is removed when the beneficiaries actually use the property (e.g., drink from the water fountain, stay in the ribat), because delivery is a condition, and delivery of the type (opening for use) suffices. The passage also discusses the case of a mosque: unlike other waqf properties, a mosque becomes dedicated immediately without a judge's ruling because the owner has no remaining right of benefit. Regarding waqf suspended on death (e.g., 'if I die, it is waqf'), the correct view is that it is a binding will (wasiyyah lazimah) – the property does not exit ownership during life, but the waqif cannot sell it after death because that would invalidate the will. He may revoke before death. The passage refutes a misconception that Abu Hanifa totally forbids waqf; rather, he permits it but considers it non-binding. The evidence for Abu Hanifa's position is the hadith 'La habs 'an fara'id Allah' (No withholding from the shares of Allah), indicating that one should not tie up property from inheritance (i.e., waqf that prevents heirs from inheriting is not binding).

الرباط (al-ribat): A lodge built for the poor or travelers to stay, often for religious purposes.
اللزوم (al-luzum): Bindingness – the quality of an act that makes it irrevocable and legally required to be upheld.
التسليم (al-taslim): Delivery – in this context, handing over the waqf property to the beneficiaries or trustee, either by placing it in their control or by allowing them to use it.

This passage from the commentary on the chapter of Waqf deals with the conditions under which a waqf (endowment) becomes binding (lazim) and the effect of suspending it upon death (ta'liq bil-mawt). The author begins by stating the definition of waqf and then presents a legal ruling regarding waqf that is suspended upon death: there are two narrated positions from Imam Abu Hanifa. In one narration, such a waqf becomes binding immediately; in the other, it does not. The author of al-Mukhtar (likely al-Mukhtar li al-Fatwa) chose the second view, which is that it does not become binding. The text then addresses the inference of opponents who cited a report from al-Qadi Shurayh that the Prophet (peace be upon him) prohibited 'habs' (detention). This argument is refuted by noting that the prohibition applies only when such a detention infringes upon the rights of heirs; if it is done before any heir's right attaches, it is permissible, akin to giving movable property in charity. Further, the opponents argued that ownership in waqf remains with the endower because rights of people (like use) still apply; they concluded that ownership must be either with someone else or with the endower, and since the first is invalid, it remains with him. The Hanafi response is that the property leaves the ownership of the endower and enters the ownership of Allah, which does not negate the usufruct rights of people, just as in a mosque. The commentary then elaborates on the two narrations: Al-Hidayah and Al-Kitab clarify that according to Abu Hanifa, the endower's ownership does not cease unless a judge rules on it and it is suspended upon death. However, in the case of suspension upon death alone, the correct view is that ownership does not cease, but it becomes like a will for perpetual benefit and thus binding. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani), who hold that waqf is binding without the need for a judge's ruling, and the basis for this is the precedent of Prophet Abraham's endowment of the Ka'bah. For Abu Hanifa, there are only two ways for waqf to become binding: a judge's ruling. The text then supports the binding nature of waqf with the famous hadith of Umar ibn al-Khattab, where the Prophet instructed him to 'give charity with its principal, not to be sold, inherited, or gifted.' This hadith is cited by al-Bukhari, Muslim, and others. The discussion about the Ka'bah addresses a potential objection: if the Ka'bah was already endowed before Abraham, how can it be his endowment? The response is that the land was indeed endowed before, but the construction and building were made with Abraham's ownership and endowment. The text concludes by explaining what is meant by 'hakim' (judge): it refers to a judge appointed by the ruler (al-muwalla), not an arbitrator (muhakkam) chosen by the parties. This distinction is important because only the judge's ruling can remove the disagreement and make the waqf binding according to Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary on the rulings of waqf, specifically the conditions for a building to become a masjid and the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding when the ownership of the waqf property ceases. The author begins by explaining that if a person makes a cellar under a mosque for purposes other than the mosque's interests, the mosque does not become a valid mosque. Similarly, if one designates the middle of his house as a mosque and permits prayer there, it does not become a mosque because the path has not been separated (i.e., there is no exclusive access). The condition for a valid masjid is that both its lower and upper parts must be entirely dedicated as a mosque, cutting off all private rights. However, if the cellar or upper floor is endowed for the mosque's interests (like storage for the imam), it does not invalidate the masjid, analogously to the cellar of Bayt al-Maqdis. The author notes that this is the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya), but there are weak narrations in al-Hidayah. The text then clarifies that 'permission' (idhn) means permission combined with actual prayer; if no one prays there, even a mosque separated by a path would not be valid due to lack of delivery (taslim). If a space does not become a mosque, the owner retains the right to sell it and it is inherited. However, building a room for the imam above the mosque is permissible as it serves the mosque's interests, but after the mosque is established, any new construction is prohibited. The main dispute: Abu Yusuf holds that ownership of the waqf property ceases immediately upon the waqf declaration, without needing delivery (taslim) to a trustee, comparing it to manumission ('itq). Muhammad al-Shaybani requires delivery to the trustee and possession as a condition for the cessation of ownership. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Yusuf's view, as preferred by the verifying scholars. The commentary also explains that 'by the saying itself' means the mere utterance suffices, though cessation can also occur by action (like prayer in a mosque). The scholars of Bukhara followed Muhammad, but Abu Yusuf's view is considered more correct.

Key Terms
التسليم (al-taslim): The act of delivering the waqf property to the trustee or its equivalent (e.g., prayer in a masjid, burial in a cemetery) to effectuate the waqf.
إفراز الطريق (ifraz al-tariq): Separation of a path: designating an exclusive entrance or pathway to the masjid so that private rights are eliminated.
الوقف (al-waqf): An endowment of property for charitable purposes, where ownership is transferred to Allah and the property becomes inalienable.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Taslim in Waqf
Commentary on intra-Hanafi dispute on taslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the requirement of delivery (taslim) for the cessation of ownership in waqf, and identify the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Ownership of waqf property ceases by mere declaration (Abu Yusuf) or requires delivery (Muhammad); the mufta bihi is Abu Yusuf's view.

Within the Hanafi school, there is a well-known difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on when a waqf becomes effective. This lesson examines both positions, their proofs, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The dispute is whether waqf requires delivery (taslim) to a trustee for ownership to cease.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Ownership ceases immediately upon the declaration of waqf, such as saying 'Waqaftu'.
He compares waqf to manumission (itq), which is a relinquishment.
3
State Muhammad's position
Delivery (taslim) to the trustee and possession are necessary for the waqf to be effective.
He compares waqf to charity and zakat, where delivery is required.
4
Analyze the proofs
Abu Yusuf: qiyas to manumission. Muhammad: qiyas to charity. The Hanafi response rejects the charity analogy because in charity, making Allah the owner is not actualized as an independent aim.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The verifying scholars prefer Abu Yusuf's view, and the fatwa is upon it, although the scholars of Bukhara followed Muhammad.

Core Idea

Core Idea
A waqf without a perpetual beneficiary may be invalid — explicit mention of perpetuity is a required condition in the relied-upon Hanafi view.

The validity of a waqf (endowment) depends on several conditions, one of the most debated being the requirement to specify a perpetual beneficiary. In this passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, we encounter an important intra-Hanafi dispute: does the endower need to explicitly state that the beneficiary is perpetual (e.g., 'for the poor forever')? While all Hanafi imams agree that waqf must be perpetual, they differ on whether the words 'waqf' or 'sadaqah' alone imply perpetuity or require explicit reinforcement. This lesson will walk you through the reasoning of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and clarify the operative ruling (mufta bihi) for fatwa practice.

Abu Yusuf holds that explicit mention is not required. His reasoning: the words 'waqf' and 'sadaqah' themselves imply perpetuity, so no separate mention is needed.

Abu Yusuf's view, if followed, would send the proceeds to the poor when the children die, but this is not the operative position.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa restricts waqf to immovables; his students expand it to movables via custom and text.

Endowment (waqf) is a cornerstone of Islamic charitable infrastructure. The Hanafi school recognizes a fundamental debate: is it valid to endow movable property like animals, weapons, or books? The founding imam, Abu Hanifa, held that only immovables qualify for permanent endowment, but his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, developed expansions based on implicit followership and customary practice. Understanding this internal disagreement clarifies how Hanafi law evolves through textual proof and community usage.

Muhammad uses ta'amul (customary practice) as a source of law parallel to ijma'.

Both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad cite the hadith that Khalid endowed his horses and armor in the path of Allah. This textual proof allows departure from the analogy that movables lack permanence. Abu Hanifa considers this a special case for jihad.
The hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari serves as specific evidence (dalil khas) for movable waqf.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the ruling from al-Hidayah regarding the priority of waqf expenditures. The main ruling states that the revenue (irtafa'/ghalla) of a waqf must first be used for its maintenance and repair ('imara), even if the endower did not explicitly stipulate this. This is because the waqf is intended to be perpetual, and maintenance is necessary for its preservation. The condition of maintenance is thus implied by the very nature of waqf. The passage then discusses two sub-issues: (1) It is not permissible to divide the waqf property among the beneficiaries, because their right is in the usufruct (manfa'a), not in the substance ('ayn). Some scholars allowed division to maximize benefit, but the correct Hanafi position is against it. (2) The order of spending: after maintenance, the next priorities are the imam, teacher, lamp, mat, and other needs of the mosque or institution. If the waqf is specified for a particular group (e.g., the poor), then after maintenance, the revenue is spent on them. If the waqf is for a specified person (mu'ayyan) and ultimately for the poor, then the specified person is responsible for maintenance from his own wealth. If he refuses or is unable, the judge may lease the property, use the rent for maintenance, and then return the remaining income to the beneficiary. The broken materials from the waqf (e.g., fallen wood or bricks) are also to be used for maintenance. The commentary emphasizes that the goal is to preserve the waqf; no extravagance (like decorative painting) is allowed unless the endower prescribed it. The ruling is supported by the rational argument that the endower's intention of perpetuity necessitates maintenance.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): An endowment of a tangible asset in perpetuity, with the usufruct dedicated to a charitable or specified purpose.
ارتفاع (irtafa'): The revenue or yield generated by the endowed property, such as rent or produce.
عمارة ('imara): Maintenance and repair of the waqf property, including structural upkeep and necessary furnishings.
مصارف (masarif): The beneficiaries of the waqf who are entitled to receive the usufruct or income.

Rulings on Waqf Maintenance and Broken Materials
Ruling on waqf maintenance and disposal of broken materials

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for maintaining waqf property, apply the correct disposal of broken materials, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding mosque materials.

Core Idea
Broken waqf materials must be reused for maintenance or transferred to a similar waqf, never distributed to beneficiaries.

Waqf is a perpetual endowment; its corpus must be preserved. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies the obligations for maintaining waqf property and the strict rules for handling broken materials (naqd). It also addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about what happens when parts of a mosque become unusable. Understanding these rules ensures the continuity of the waqf's benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The main act is the maintenance (imarah) of waqf property. The ruling is that it is obligatory (wajib) on the beneficiary or from the waqf's income.
Maintenance preserves the corpus, which is essential for the waqf to continue.
2
Determine who bears the cost and when
If the waqf is for a specific person (e.g., a child) with ultimate benefit to the poor, that specific person must pay for maintenance. If he refuses or is poor, the judge rents the property and uses the rent for maintenance.
The judge acts as a supervisor to protect the waqf.
3
Trace the disposal of broken materials
Broken materials (naqd) must first be used for maintenance if needed. If not needed now, they are stored. If storage is risky or impossible, the judge sells them and keeps the proceeds for future maintenance.
The naqd is never distributed to beneficiaries.
4
Apply the same principle to mosque materials
If a mosque becomes ruined and its surrounding materials (e.g., straw, reeds) are no longer needed, they are transferred to the nearest mosque, ribat, or well. This follows the principle that waqf property must benefit a similar purpose.
Imam Muhammad disagrees, holding that it reverts to ownership.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon fatwa (Tanwir al-Absar) is that of Imam Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the materials remain waqf and go to the nearest similar waqf.
This is 'with it is the fatwa' (وبه يفتى).

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is a supercommentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah, dealing with the maintenance of waqf property. The author first states the ruling: if a waqf is for a specific beneficiary (e.g., children) and ultimately for the poor, the maintenance is the responsibility of that specific beneficiary (the one entitled to the benefit). This means he must spend from his own money to keep the property in repair. If he refuses or is unable because he is poor, the judge (qadi) has the authority to rent out the waqf property, use the rental income for maintenance, and then return the property to the beneficiary once repaired. This balances the rights of the waqif (endower) and the beneficiary. The second part deals with the broken materials (نقض) from the waqf building (e.g., fallen stones, wood). These must be spent on the same waqf's maintenance if needed immediately; if not needed now, they are stored for future repairs. If it is impossible or harmful to store them, the judge or trustee may sell them and keep the proceeds for future maintenance. Under no circumstances may the broken materials be distributed among the beneficiaries (like the poor), because they have no right to the corpus (ayn) of the waqf, only to its proceeds (ghallah). The passage then cites al-Bahr al-Ra'iq confirming this. Finally, it quotes Tanwir al-Absar with an analogous ruling: if a mosque becomes dilapidated and its surrounding area is no longer needed, it remains a mosque (it does not revert to ownership), and its usable parts (like straw, reeds) if no longer needed for that mosque are transferred to the nearest mosque, ribat, or well. This is the relied-upon fatwa (mufta bihi) according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, differing from Muhammad al-Shaybani who holds that it reverts to ownership. The passage emphasizes that waqf property and its proceeds must always be channeled to the closest similar charitable purpose.

عمارة (imārah): Maintenance and repair of waqf property to keep it functional.
مصرف (masrif): The beneficiary or the purpose for which the waqf income is designated (e.g., the poor, specific individuals).

Disposal of Unusable Waqf Property
Ruling on storage, sale, and use of waqf proceeds when property is ruined

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the disposal of waqf property when it becomes unusable, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding a superfluous mosque.

Core Idea
Waqf proceeds must be preserved for the waqf purpose; the corpus itself cannot be distributed.

When a waqf property falls into disrepair or becomes unusable, the trustee (mutawallī) or judge (ḥākim) must take specific steps to protect the endowment. This lesson examines the rules for handling proceeds, selling salvageable materials, and the special case of a mosque that is no longer needed. Understanding these rules prevents misallocation of waqf assets and preserves the founder’s intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem scenario
Determine that the waqf property is ruined or unusable, and repair is impossible or too costly. This triggers the special rulings.
The text assumes the property cannot be restored to useful condition.
2
State the general rule for proceeds
If repair is impossible, the salvageable materials (al-naqḍ) are sold, and the proceeds are spent on the waqf purpose (e.g., rebuilding) or preserved for future need. They are NOT distributed to beneficiaries.
The commentary stresses that beneficiaries have no right to the corpus (ʿayn), only to proceeds (ghalla).
3
Distinguish the case of a mosque
If a mosque becomes superfluous (people have moved away), the relied-upon (muftā bihī) position is that it remains a waqf mosque. It cannot be sold or revert to private ownership, even if no longer used for prayer.
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that it returns to the original owners – an intra-madhhab ikhtilāf.
4
Apply to other waqf institutions
For ribāṭ (hospices) and wells that become unusable, their proceeds are transferred to the nearest similar institution. Grass of the mosque also remains waqf.
The text shows the principle of preservation and redirection when direct use is impossible.

Commentary
This passage from a commentary on al-Wiqayah discusses rulings on waqf property that has become unusable. The text first states a general principle: if a waqf property generates proceeds (like rent) that cannot be spent on its intended purpose due to the property being damaged, those proceeds should be saved for future repair. If spending on repair is impossible (e.g., the property is completely destroyed), then the proceeds from selling the salvageable materials are used for the waqf purpose, but the proceeds cannot be distributed among the beneficiaries of the waqf (like the poor) because they have no right to the corpus itself. The commentary clarifies that 'yuddakhar' means to store up, and 'la yuqsam' means the rubble (or proceeds) cannot be given to beneficiaries. Then, quoting from 'Tanwir al-Absar', the text addresses the case of a mosque: if the mosque becomes unnecessary (people move away), it remains a mosque according to the relied-upon position. However, Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that it reverts to private ownership. Similarly, the grass growing on mosque land remains waqf according to Imam Abu Hanifa and 'the second' (likely Abu Yusuf), even if the mosque is no longer needed. As for ribats (hospices) and wells, if they cannot be used, their proceeds are transferred to the nearest similar institution.

A) Distribute the proceeds among the beneficiaries.
B) Sell the salvageable materials and use the price for the waqf purpose.
C) Abandon the property.
D) Give the proceeds to the judge as a gift.

Key Takeaway
Waqf proceeds must be preserved for the waqf purpose; the corpus cannot be distributed, and the mosque remains waqf even if superfluous.
