Strangulation and the Definition of 'Amd
Commentary on the ruling for strangulation and classification of murder types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on strangulation and explain why Abu Hanifa differs from his disciples and al-Shafi'i on the classification of murder as 'amd.

Core Idea
Strangulation is not 'amd because the instrument does not cut – only diyah applies.

You have probably assumed that any intentional killing is 'amd subject to qisas. But Hanafi law makes a subtle distinction: the instrument matters. This lesson unpacks the ruling on strangulation and the intra-madhhab dispute that arises from it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the matn's ruling on strangulation and habitual stranglers: diyah for the act, and possible ta'zir execution for habitual offenders.
2
Understand the basis
Why does Abu Hanifa classify strangulation as qatl bi al-muthaqqal? The instrument is non-sharp but normally lethal.
Distinguish from 'amd which requires a cutting instrument.
3
Locate the dispute
Who disagrees with Abu Hanifa and on what grounds? The two disciples and al-Shafi'i consider any normally lethal instrument as 'amd.
Sahibayn: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
4
Evaluate the thamarah
What practical difference does it make? Qisas vs diyah.
Also note the imam's discretionary power for habitual stranglers.
5
Note the mufta bihi
Which position is relied upon for fatwa? Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position, but the imam may execute as siyasah.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Al-Wiqayah dealing with the chapter on highway robbery. The matn states: 'And in strangulation is blood-money, and whoever habitually does it is killed by policy.' The commentator first clarifies that this ruling is specifically according to Abu Hanifa, as Shafi'i and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that strangulation is intentional murder (amd) requiring qisas. The commentator then explains key terms: 'al-khanq' (strangulation) is squeezing the throat; 'man i'tadahu' means a habitual strangler who may be killed by the Imam as a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) because he spreads corruption. The commentator defines 'qatl bi al-muthaqqal' (killing with a heavy object) as intentional killing with a non-sharp instrument that normally kills, like a large stone—this is not considered 'amd requiring qisas according to Abu Hanifa. A lengthy quotation from al-Jurjani's 'Sharh al-Fara'id al-Sirajiyyah' then systematically categorises murder into three types: (1) 'amd (intentional) using a sharp weapon or its equivalent, necessitating qisas, sin, and no expiation; according to the two disciples, any instrument that normally kills suffices for 'amd, even if not sharp. (2) shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional) using something not normally lethal, requiring diyah on the 'aqila, sin, and expiation, but no qisas—this is agreed upon by both imams. (3) khata' (accidental) with examples, requiring expiation and diyah on the 'aqila, with no sin. The passage thus grounds the ruling on strangulation within the broader Hanafi classification of homicide, demonstrating how the nature of the instrument determines the legal consequence.

Key Terms
الخنق (al-khanq): Strangulation: squeezing the throat, either with hands or a rope, causing death.
القتل بالمثقل (al-qatl bi al-muthaqqal): Killing with a heavy, non-sharp object that normally causes death, such as a large stone or club.
العمد (al-amd): Intentional murder: deliberately striking with a sharp weapon or its equivalent that separates body parts.
شبه العمد (shibh al-amd): Quasi-intentional murder: intentionally striking with something that does not normally kill, resulting in death.

You might think that all killings are either intentional or accidental. But Hanafi law adds a middle category: quasi-intentional (shibh amd). This lesson unpacks the threefold classification from the commentary of al-Jurjani, giving you a precise tool for categorizing homicides.

Imam's Role in Diyah and Qisas for a Victim with No Heir
Expands Imam's role in blood money and retaliation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Imam's options in cases of homicide with no heir and explain why pardon is prohibited.

Core Idea
For a victim with no heir, the Imam acts as public trustee: he may take blood money or execute retaliation but may not pardon without compensation.

This lesson examines a unique scenario: when a homicide victim has no legal guardian (wali), the Imam steps in. The text from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the Imam's powers and limitations, distinguishing between accidental and intentional killings. Understanding this ruling clarifies the public dimension of criminal justice in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage addresses two types of homicide: accidental (khatā') and intentional ('amd). Identify which applies to a victim without a legal heir.
The victim may be a Muslim or a musta'min who accepted Islam before dying.
2
Determine the Imam's action in accidental killing
For accidental killing, the text states the Imam takes the diyah from the 'āqila and deposits it in the public treasury. Confirm that the Imam does not execute qisas here.
Accidental killing only entails diyah, not retaliation.
3
Determine the Imam's options in intentional killing
For intentional killing, the Imam has a choice: either execute qisas (retaliation) or take diyah via compromise (ṣulḥ). He cannot pardon unconditionally.
The killer must consent to diyah; the Imam cannot impose it unilaterally.
4
Understand the rationale for the prohibition of pardon
The commentary explains that the Imam's authority is supervisory (wilāyah naẓariyyah), not proprietary. He cannot drop the public's right without compensation.
A private guardian may pardon, but the Imam represents the public treasury.

For accidental killing, the Imam collects the diyah (blood money) from the ‘āqila (the killer's tribal group or registered stipendiaries) and deposits it into the public treasury (bayt al-māl). For intentional killing, the Imam may either execute qiṣāṣ (retaliation) or accept diyah by way of ṣulḥ (compromise) with the killer's consent, but he cannot unconditionally pardon the killer because pardoning would forfeit the public's right without compensation. The commentary expands on the term ‘āqila—defining it as the people of the dīwān (military register) and the ‘aṣaba (agnates)—and explains that the Imam's authority is strictly supervisory (wilāyah naẓariyyah), not proprietary; hence he cannot drop the right to qiṣāṣ without exchange. The passage also notes that the ruling on expiation (kaffārah) is omitted here because it is already covered in the Book of Injuries. The underlying legal rationale (ta‘līl) is that Islamic sanctity (‘iṣmah) carries both a deterrent aspect (mu’limah) and a compensatory aspect (muqawwimah), and both qiṣāṣ and diyah rest on this dual inviolability.

Diyah for an Apostate's Death from a Qisas Cut: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Sara'yah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah liability when apostasy interrupts the causal chain of a qisas wound

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad on diyah for an apostate who dies from a qisas cut after returning to Islam, and identify the role of sara'yah in determining liability.

Core Idea
If apostasy intervenes, does it break the causal link? The ruling hinges on sara'yah's continuity.

This lesson delves into a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when an apostate whose hand was cut as qisas returns to Islam and dies from that cut, must the cutter pay full diyah (Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf) or half diyah (Muhammad & Zufar)? The difference hinges on whether apostasy nullifies the sara'yah (spread of the wound). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace causal chains in Hanafi criminal liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and its factual context
The case: a person intentionally cuts off another's hand as qisas. The victim then apostatizes, later returns to Islam, and dies from that cut. The key point is that the cut was done when the victim was Muslim (ma'sum), but apostasy intervened, then return to Islam occurred before death.
The victim's ismah (inviolability) is temporarily lost during apostasy.
2
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
They hold that the cutter must pay full diyah (diyat al-nafs). Their reasoning: the victim was ma'sum at the time of the cut and at the time of death from that cut; apostasy does not break the causal chain of sara'yah because the death is still directly from the original wound.
Full diyah is 100 camels or its equivalent.
3
State the ruling of Imam Muhammad and Zufar
They hold that only half diyah is due (diyat of the hand cut). Their reasoning: apostasy nullifies the sara'yah, making the wound's spread 'hadar' (worthless). Returning to Islam cannot revive the lapsed guarantee for the death, so only the original cut is compensated.
Half diyah is 50 camels or equivalent.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and understand the dispute's basis
The practical difference is the amount of diyah: full vs half. The dispute rests on whether sara'yah is interrupted by the victim's loss of ismah during apostasy. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
No explicit tarjih is given in this passage, but later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Hanifa.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute. The matn states a ruling: if an apostate whose hand was cut off as qisas (intentional retaliation) returns to Islam in Dar al-Islam and dies from that cut, the cutter must pay full diyat (blood money). The commentary explains that this is according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf because the victim was ma'sum (inviolable) at the time of the cut and at the time of the wound's spread (sara'yah) that led to death. However, Imam Muhammad and Zufar hold that half diyat is due. Their reasoning is that apostasy had intervened and nullified the sara'yah; returning to Islam cannot revive the lapsed guarantee. The commentary also clarifies why qisas is not applied: the apostasy creates a shubhah (legal doubt) that prevents qisas. Thus, the ruling follows the principle that full diyat applies only if the cut and the death both occur while the victim is ma'sum. The dispute centers on whether apostasy breaks the sara'yah or not.

Key Terms
سراية (sara'yah): The spread of an injury from the initial wound to the entire body, causing death. In this context, it refers to the death resulting from the qisas cut.
عصمة (ismah): Inviolability; the status of being protected by law from injury. A Muslim's blood is ma'sum.
ارتداد (irtidad): Apostasy; leaving Islam. In this passage, it creates a legal doubt (shubhah) that prevents qisas.
