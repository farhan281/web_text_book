Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rely on the general meaning of Quran 5:6 and the principle of taysir (ease). Abu Yusuf prioritizes the literal definition.

Under the relied-upon view, a person may perform tayammum on a clean stone or plaster wall. Under Abu Yusuf's view, only earth or sand would suffice.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is clear: broader availability of valid alternatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on delaying prayer when one hopes to find water and distinguish between mustahabb and wajib in this context.

Core Idea
Hoping for water makes delaying prayer recommended, not obligatory — seek the best purification but do not compromise the prayer time.

This short Q&A from a Hanafi manual clarifies a common situation: a person is in the prayer time but does not have water, yet expects to find water later. Should they wait? The Hanafi answer distinguishes between what is required and what is better. Understanding this distinction is key to applying fiqh correctly without falling into over-obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is delaying the prayer. According to the Hanafi school, the ruling (hukm) is mustahabb (recommended), not wajib (obligatory).
The text explicitly says 'لا يجب عليه ذلك، بل يُستحب له'.
2
Recognize the key term 'yustahabb'
'Yustahabb' indicates a level of desirability below sunnah. It is rewarded if performed, but no sin is incurred for omitting it. This is distinct from wajib, where omission is sinful.
Hanafi usul distinguishes mustahabb from sunnah; mustahabb is a pure recommendation.
3
Apply the ruling to the scenario
If a person delays hoping for water, they wait until the end of the prayer time. If water is found, they perform wudu and pray; otherwise, they do tayammum and pray. The prayer is valid in either case.
The delay is only recommended, so praying earlier with tayammum is also acceptable.
4
Consider the boundaries of the recommendation
The recommendation applies when there is genuine hope of finding water. If one is certain to find water, the ruling may still be mustahabb, but the incentive is stronger. However, if delaying would risk missing the prayer time entirely, the obligation to pray within the time takes precedence, and delay becomes impermissible.
This nuance is derived from the broader principle that obligations (wajibat) override recommendations (mustahabbat).

When does miscarriage cause nifas?
Ruling on whether a miscarriage causes nifas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule to classify post-miscarriage bleeding as nifas, hayd, or istihadah based on limb visibility and duration.

Core Idea
A miscarriage causes nifas only if the fetus shows formed limbs; otherwise classify as hayd or istihadah.

This lesson addresses a common sensitive question: does a woman who miscarries enter the state of nifas (postnatal bleeding)? The answer depends on how far the pregnancy had developed. The Hanafi school sets a clear anatomical marker: if any part of the baby's creation appears (hand, foot, finger, nail, hair), the bleeding is nifas. If not, the bleeding is treated like any other uterine bleeding—if it lasts three full days and nights and is preceded by a complete clean period, it is hayd; otherwise it is istihadah. Understanding this distinction ensures correct rulings for salah, fasting, and marital relations.
