Definition and Rulings of Diyah (Blood Money)
Definition of diyah and first ruling on its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of diyah and explain the Hanafi ruling for aggravated diyah (diyah mughalizah) in shibh 'amd, including its amount and categories.

Core Idea
Diyah is wajib upon the 'aqila for accidental killing — 100 camels in four categories for shibh 'amd.

This lesson opens the Book of Diyat (Blood Money). We first learn what diyah means linguistically (derived from 'wada') and legally (wealth obligatory on killer for accidental killing, quasi-intentional killing, or limb compensation). Then we dive into the first specific ruling: the aggravated diyah for quasi-intentional killing (shibh 'amd) is payable by the killer's agnatic relatives (the 'aqila) in the amount of 100 camels divided into four age categories, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. The lesson also clarifies that aggravation only applies when the diyah is paid in camels, not in cash or other commodities.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the linguistic and legal definition
Break down the linguistic derivation: diyah is on the pattern of 'idad, from the root wada (to pay blood money). Legally, it is the wealth obligatory on the killer for accidental (khata') or quasi-intentional (shibh 'amd) killing, or for limb injuries. Note that compensation for limbs is sometimes called arsh.
Memorize the scope: it covers both death and injury, but the ruling in this lesson focuses on shibh 'amd.
2
Identify the parties obligated in shibh 'amd
The text states: 'upon the 'aqila of the killer is an aggravated diyah, and upon the killer is expiation (kaffarah).' The 'aqila are the male agnatic relatives (asabah) who pay the diyah, not the killer personally. The killer must perform kaffarah (freeing a slave or fasting).
This is a key Hanafi distinction: the financial burden shifts to the tribe, while the killer bears a spiritual expiation.
3
Memorize the amount and categories of aggravated diyah
The aggravated diyah is 100 camels in four equal quarters: 25 bint makhad (1-year-old she-camel), 25 bint labun (2-year-old she-camel), 25 hiqqah (3-year-old she-camel), and 25 jadha'ah (4-year-old she-camel). This composition is specific to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
If diyah is adjudged from other than camels (e.g., money or cattle), there is no aggravation in the amount; the standard diyah applies.
4
Recognize the limitation of taghliz (aggravation)
The text explicitly states that aggravation is only in shibh 'amd. If the diyah is paid from non-camel assets, the taghliz does not apply. This means that in practice, if a judge orders compensation in cash, the amount remains the standard diyah (100 camels value) without the four-category requirement.
This is a practical nuance that affects how diyah is calculated in modern contexts.

Defining Diya (Blood Money) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defining diya linguistically and legally in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define diya linguistically and legally in Hanafi fiqh and identify its three triggering cases.

Core Idea
Diya is financial compensation for killing by mistake, quasi-intent, or cutting a limb.

In this lesson we unpack the term 'diya' (blood money). Understanding its linguistic root and legal scope is essential before studying the rulings on diyat. The passage clarifies when diya is owed and how it relates to 'arsh for limbs.

Guided Path
1
Identify linguistic derivation
Recognize that diya comes from the root w-d-y, meaning 'to pay blood money,' and note the pattern like 'idda with the first radical omitted.
The pattern omission is a common feature in Arabic nouns.
2
List the three legal triggers
Extract the three cases from the text: accidental killing (qatl al-khata), quasi-intentional killing (shibh al-amd), and cutting off a limb.
Limb cases are also called 'arsh.
3
Distinguish diya from 'arsh
Understand that diya is the general term for compensation; for limbs it may be specifically called 'arsh, but the passage treats them together.
Some later texts differentiate more strictly, but this definition includes both.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario, determine whether diya is owed based on the three triggers.
Check if the act falls under one of the three cases.

Rulings on Joint Cutting and Qisas
Explaining cases where multiple perpetrators cut a hand or one cuts hands of two

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal consequences (qisas or diya) when multiple perpetrators injure one victim or one perpetrator injures multiple victims, and explain why shibh amd does not apply to injuries.

Core Idea
Qisas is infeasible when multiple perpetrators cut one hand; they collectively owe half diya.

This passage from the matn addresses scenarios where cutting injuries involve multiple perpetrators or multiple victims. Understanding these rulings clarifies the limits of qisas and the role of diya when exact retaliation is impossible due to indivisibility or partial presence. It also establishes that the category of shibh amd (semi-intentional) only applies to homicide, not to bodily injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify each scenario
Read rulings 12, 13, and 14 separately. In 12, two men cut one hand. In 13, one man cuts the right hand of two men. In 14, a general principle about categories of injury is stated.
2
Determine qisas applicability
In scenario 12, qisas is not applicable because retaliation cannot be divided—you cannot cut half a hand from each perpetrator. In scenario 13, qisas is applicable for the victim who appears, because one hand can be cut from the single perpetrator.
Qisas requires complete equivalence and feasibility.
3
Identify the diya consequence
In scenario 12, the two perpetrators jointly owe half the diya of a full life (50 camels). In scenario 13, if both victims appear, they cut the perpetrator's hand and also take half diya to be split; if only one appears and cuts, the absent victim gets half diya from the cutter.
Half diya of a life is 5000 silver dirhams or 50 camels.
4
Note the general principle for injuries
Ruling 14 states that for injuries below the level of life, there is no shibh amd; only amd (intentional) and khata (accidental) exist. This means the classification of shibh amd is specific to homicide.
This affects the determination of diya amounts and the possibility of qisas.

Diya for Shibh Amd and Khata: Rulings and Amounts
Stating the rulings and amounts of diya for quasi-intent and accidental killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for diya in shibh amd and khata, and distinguish between mughalladha and ghayr mughalladha in terms of camel categories and monetary equivalents.

Core Idea
Aggravated and non-aggravated diya both use 100 camels; the difference is in the age categories (4 quarters vs 5 fifths).

This lesson presents the Hanafi rulings on blood money (diya) for two types of unlawful killing: quasi-intent (shibh amd) and accident (khata). The passage distinguishes between the aggravated diya (mughalladha) and the non-aggravated diya (ghayr mughalladha), specifying the exact camel categories and the alternative payments in gold and silver. Understanding these classifications is essential for applying diya in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of killing
Determine if the killing is shibh amd or khata based on the description.
Shibh amd involves intentionality but not with a deadly instrument; khata is accidental.
2
State the diya type
For shibh amd: aggravated (mughalladha); for khata: non-aggravated (ghayr mughalladha).
3
List camel categories
For mughalladha: 4 categories of 25 each (bint makhad, bint labun, hiqqa, jadha'a). For ghayr mughalladha: 5 categories of 20 each (bint makhad, ibn makhad, bint labun, hiqqa, jadha'a).
Note that ibn makhad (male) only appears in khata.
4
Note the monetary alternative
If not paid in camels, diya is 1000 dinars or 10,000 dirhams, without aggravation.
This applies to both types.
5
Identify the responsible parties
Diya is paid by 'aqila (paternal relatives); kaffarah is on the killer.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Permissible Property for Diya
Presenting the intra-Hanafi dispute on valid property for diya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi disagreement on permissible property types for diya and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa restricts diya to camels, gold, and silver; Sahibayn allow more types based on qiyas.

Diya (blood money) is a fixed compensation for unintentional killing. The Quran and Sunnah specify payment in camels, gold, or silver. However, a famous intra-Hanafi dispute arises: can diya be paid from other property types like cows, sheep, or garments? Imam Abu Hanifa holds a strict textualist view, while his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn), adopt a broader view based on analogy and customary acceptance. This lesson unpacks the dispute and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify Imam Abu Hanifa’s ruling: diya is established only from three types—camels, gold, and silver. Why? Because the texts are fixed and not open to analogical extension.
This view relies on the principle that diya is a fixed penalty ('uquba), not a financial transaction.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
State the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: diya can also be paid from cows (200 cows), sheep (1,000 sheep), or garments (200 hullal, each hullah being two pieces of cloth).
They consider diya as financial compensation (mal) that can be based on prevalent valuables.
3
Analyze the basis of each view
For Abu Hanifa, the basis is the text (nass) alone, with no room for qiyas. For Sahibayn, the basis is qiyas (analogy to camels) and custom ('urf) that accepts other widely traded goods.
This reflects a broader methodological difference over the scope of qiyas in 'uqubat.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if a judge (qadi) orders diya from cows, the verdict is valid according to Sahibayn but invalid according to Abu Hanifa. The aqila (tribe) must know which view the court follows.
This affects both litigants and judges in actual cases.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon position)
Later Hanafi fatwa often adopts Sahibayn's view for ease and practicality, especially where camels are scarce.
This is an example of tarjih based on maslahah (public interest) within the school.

Commentary
This passage records an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the types of property from which diya can be taken. Imam Abu Hanifa restricts diya to the three types explicitly mentioned in the Quran or hadith: camels (specified in the Sunnah), gold, and silver. This is based on the principle that diya is a fixed legal penalty (qadr shar‘i) which cannot be altered by analogy or custom. His two students, Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, however, extend the types to include cows, sheep, and garments (hullal) because they consider diya as a type of financial compensation that can be paid from widely accepted currency or valuable goods in the absence of camels. They provide conversion rates: 200 cows, 1,000 sheep, or 200 hullal (each hullah = two garments). This dispute has practical implications: if a judge orders diya to be paid from cows or sheep, the verdict follows the Sahibayn’s view, but according to Abu Hanifa such a verdict would be invalid. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the later Hanafi school is often the view of the Sahibayn due to ease and practicality.

The Ten Head Wounds and Their Diyah Rulings
Listing ten head wound types with diyah and qisas rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten types of head wounds (shijaj) and state the diyah, qisas, or discretionary compensation for each according to the Hanafi school, including the intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas.

Core Idea
Qisas is only wajib for intentional muḍiḥa; deeper wounds get fixed diyah, and shallow wounds get discretionary compensation.

Head wounds are classified into ten distinct types, each with its own legal consequence. The Hanafi school treats al-muḍiḥa uniquely—it is the only head wound where retaliation (qisas) is permissible if the act was intentional. For all other wounds, monetary compensation applies, either a fixed diyah amount or a judge-determined settlement. This lesson walks through the ten wounds, their definitions, and the rulings attached to each.

Guided Path
1
Identify and order the ten wounds
Read the list of ten wounds from the text. Note that they progress from superficial to severe. The first seven (al-hariṣa through al-muḍiḥa) involve the skin and scalp; the last three (al-hāshima, al-munqila, al-āmma) involve the bone or brain cavity.
Memorize the names and order—this is foundational for applying the correct ruling.
2
Distinguish the qisas rule for muḍiḥa
The text states that qisas is only for intentional muḍiḥa. All other shijāj (including those above and below muḍiḥa) do not carry qisas. Understand that the criterion is the ability to ensure exact equality in retaliation.
Qisas for muḍiḥa requires the wound to be intentional and exactly on the bone-exposing layer.
3
Learn the fixed diyah amounts for deep wounds
For al-hāshima (fracture), al-munqila (bone displacement), and al-āmma (reaching the brain sac), the diyah amounts are fixed: one-tenth, one-tenth plus half, and one-third of the full diyah, respectively. For penetrating wounds (jā’ifa), one-third each.
These amounts are independent of the victim's gender or social status.
4
Understand discretionary compensation for shallow wounds
Wounds below muḍiḥa (the first six) have no fixed compensation. Instead, a judge (ḥukūmat ‘adl) assesses a fair amount based on the severity. This is rooted in the practice of early scholars.
The absence of a fixed amount does not mean no compensation—it must still be awarded.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas
The text from al-Hidaya reports a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan. Abu Hanifa (the mufta bihi view) denies qisas for any shijāj except muḍiḥa because equality cannot be ensured. Muhammad argues for qisas in wounds before muḍiḥa because equality is possible. Evaluate the practical consequence: different rulings for the same wound.
The difference applies only to intentional wounds; accidental wounds always have diyah.

Multiple Injuries from a Single Mudiḥa: Inclusion of Arsh in Diyah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inclusion of wound compensation in diyah for faculties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on inclusion of arsh of a mudiḥa wound in diyah for loss of senses, and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The arsh of a mudiḥa is not included in diyah for sense loss; each sense is independent.

This passage from Sharh al-Kanz by al-Zayla‘i tackles a nuanced issue in diyat: when a single mudiḥa wound causes both the wound and loss of a faculty (mind, hair, hearing, sight), should the compensation for the wound be subsumed into the larger diyah? The Hanafi school splits internally on this. For mind and hair, inclusion is agreed; for senses, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that compensation for each remains separate, while Abu Yusuf differentiates between faculties connected to the head and those separate.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed rule for mind and hair
When a mudiḥa causes loss of mind or total loss of hair, the arsh is included in the diyah. Reason: loss of mind nullifies all bodily functions, making it like loss of life; for hair, partial loss merges into total loss.
This is unanimous among the Hanafis.
2
State the rule for senses (hearing, sight, speech)
For loss of a sense like hearing, sight, or speech from a mudiḥa, the arsh of the wound is NOT included in the diyah of the sense. Both compensations are due separately according to the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad).
Senses are independent faculties, not subsumed by a head wound.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Yusuf holds that the arsh is included in the diyah for hearing and speech, because these are functions of the head, but not for sight, which originates from the eye.
Thus under Abu Yusuf, for hearing/speech the victim gets only the diyah (arsh included); for sight both are due.
4
Understand the rationale behind the majority view
The basis is that each sense is a separate entity with its own complete diyah. Unlike loss of mind, which nullifies all benefits, loss of a sense is a specific deprivation. Therefore, the wound compensation remains separate.
The text contrasts loss of mind (subsuming all) with loss of hearing (independent).
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf in practice
If a victim loses hearing from a mudiḥa, the mufta bihi (relying on Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) mandates two payments: arsh for the wound + full diyah for hearing. Under Abu Yusuf, only the diyah for hearing is paid.
The practical difference is a full diyah amount.

Inclusion of 'Arsh in Diyah for Lost Senses: The Dispute of Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on separate vs. inclusive compensation for wound-causing-sense-loss.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether the 'arsh of a mudihah is included in the diya for lost senses, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of the dispute.

Core Idea
A wound and its resulting loss may be compensated separately or jointly — Hanafi masters differ.

This passage from Sharh al-Kanz presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on diyat. When a head wound (mudihah) leads to loss of a sense, should the fixed 'arsh for the wound be included in the larger diya for the sense? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no; Abu Yusuf says yes for hearing/speech. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists balance textual fixity and contextual justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and dispute
The key question: if a mudihah causes loss of a sense, do you pay 'arsh (for wound) + diya (for sense), or is 'arsh absorbed?
The mudihah is a wound that exposes bone, with a fixed 'arsh of 5 camels.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa & Muhammad: 'arsh is separate, not included. Abu Yusuf: included for hearing and speech, not for sight.
Abu Yusuf's view distinguishes between senses.
3
Apply the positions to each sense
For hearing and speech: under Abu Yusuf, diya minus 'arsh; under Abu Hanifa, full diya plus 'arsh. For sight: both agree full diya plus 'arsh.
Thus the practical difference only affects hearing and speech.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's: always separate. The passage cites the view without explicit tarjih, but later Hanafi works uphold the majority.
The principle: each injury has its own prescribed compensation.
5
Appreciate thamarah al-khilaf
Under the mufta bihi, the victim gets more total compensation. Under Abu Yusuf, less for hearing/speech.
Example: hearing loss — total under Abu Hanifa = 'arsh (5) + diya (5000); under Abu Yusuf = diya only (5000).

The ruling is based on the principle that creating a hazard in a public way entails liability. It is analogous to the well-known rule for digging a well.

The owner of private property is not liable for accidents caused by lawful structures on his land.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a person digs a well on his own property, and someone falls in and dies. The Hanafi ruling is clear: the digger bears no liability. The answer is 'la yadmanu shay'an' – he does not guarantee anything. No diya or compensation is owed.

A property owner has the right to use his land lawfully; he is not responsible for trespassers or accidental harm from structures that are not inherently dangerous.

Liability may arise if the well is placed in violation of regulations (e.g., too close to a neighbor's boundary or public road) or if dug with intent to harm.

Liability Conditions for a Leaning Wall on a Public Road

Core Idea
A wall owner is not liable for collapse unless warned, witnessed, and given time to act.

This lesson addresses a common urban problem: a wall leaning dangerously over a public road. Islamic law does not impose automatic liability on the owner; rather, it protects the owner from unforeseen liability by requiring explicit warning and proof. We will examine the precise conditions from al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a wall collapsing onto a public road causing death or property damage. The ruling is that the owner is liable for compensation (diya for death, property value for goods) under specific conditions.
Liability is not automatic.
2
List the three conditions from the text
The conditions are: (1) The owner was demanded to demolish (tuliba bi naqdihi). (2) Witnesses were set to the demand (ashhada 'alayhi). (3) The owner failed to demolish within a period he was able to.
The text includes witnesses in the condition; later clarification shows they are for proof.
3
Understand the role of witnesses
Witnesses are for proof of the warning, not a separate condition for liability. Without witnesses, liability may be harder to enforce but not automatically negated if warning can be proven otherwise.
The third Q&A clarifies that the condition is the warning, not the witnesses.

Core Idea
A master's liability for a slave's injury depends on his knowledge at the time of freeing or selling.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on when a master remains liable for injuries committed by his slave after he has freed or sold him. The key factor is whether the master knew about the injury at the time of the transaction. The rulings also cover mudabbar and umm walad, and the scenario of multiple injuries by the same slave.

Determine whether the master freed or sold the slave with or without knowledge of the slave's jinnayah. The ruling differs based on this.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the diyat amount for a male and female fetus of a slave woman and identify who receives the diyat for a fetus of a free woman versus a slave woman.

Core Idea
Diyat for a fetus depends on the mother's status: slave woman's fetus = fraction of her value; free woman's fetus = inherited.

This lesson focuses on a specific ruling from the Book of Diyat concerning a miscarried fetus. The passage gives two distinct rules: first, the amount of blood money for a fetus of a slave woman, which varies by gender; second, the destination of that compensation—whether it is inherited by the fetus's heirs (if the mother is free) or belongs to the owner (if the mother is a slave). Grasping this ruling clarifies the legal personhood of the unborn child and the financial responsibility for causing a miscarriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between a miscarried fetus of a slave woman and that of a free woman. The first scenario is specifically about a slave woman (الأمة), while the second covers both free and slave women regarding who receives the diyat.
2
For a slave woman's fetus, note the gender-based fractions
For a male fetus, the diyat is half of a tenth of the mother's value if she were alive. For a female fetus, it is a tenth of her value.
The fraction is applied to the hypothetical value of the mother, not the fetus itself.
3
Understand the calculation
The diyat is not a fixed amount; it depends on the mother's market value. For a male: (1/2) * (1/10) * mother's value = 1/20. For a female: 1/10 of mother's value.
4
Determine the recipient of the diyat
If the fetus was from a free woman, the diyat is inherited by the fetus's heirs according to their shares. If from a slave woman, the diyat belongs to her master, as the fetus is considered property.

Kaffarah for Killing: Definition and Exceptions
Ruling on kaffarah for shibh amd and khata; no kaffarah for killing slave or fetus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of killing that require kaffarah, identify the specific forms of kaffarah (freeing a slave or fasting), and list the exceptions (slave, fetus) and the distinction from zihar.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for killing is only for shibh al-amd and khata; it is freeing a slave or fasting two months, not feeding.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling on kaffarah (expiation) for unlawful killing as per the Hanafi school. The passage clarifies which types of killing incur kaffarah, what the kaffarah consists of, and importantly, which cases are excluded. It also distinguishes kaffarah for killing from that of zihar, emphasizing that feeding is not acceptable here.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of killing subject to kaffarah
The text states kaffarah is wajib for shibh al-amd (resembling intentional) and khata (accidental). Intentional killing (amd) does not require kaffarah.
2
Identify the prescribed kaffarah options
The verse from Surah An-Nisa (4:92) prescribes freeing a believing slave, or if unable, fasting two consecutive months.
3
Note the exceptions
No kaffarah is required for killing a male slave, a female slave, or a fetus; only diyah applies.
4
Compare with kaffarah for zihar
Unlike kaffarah for zihar, where feeding the poor is accepted, kaffarah for killing cannot be fulfilled by feeding.

Qasamah: Procedure and Eligibility
Ruling on qasamah procedure for unknown killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the qasamah procedure and list the persons excluded from taking the oath.

Core Idea
Qasamah: fifty eligible men swear innocence, but the locality still pays diyah.

This lesson explains the prophetic procedure called qasamah when a murdered body is found in a locality with no known killer. You will learn who selects the oath‑takers, who is disqualified, and what legal consequences follow.

Guided Path
1
Trigger condition
Identify when qasamah applies: a body is found in a locality and the killer is unknown.
This is not for every dead body; there must be signs of murder.
2
Selection by the guardian
The victim's guardian (wali) chooses the fifty men. They must be free, adult, sane, male Muslims.
The wali has this right; the judge does not select.
3
List disqualifications
Memorize who cannot be included: child, insane person, woman, slave, mudabbar, mukatab.
Mudabbar and mukatab are still slaves despite future freedom.
4
Content of the oath
The fifty men swear by Allah: 'We did not kill him, nor do we know his killer.'
They deny direct killing and knowledge.
5
Outcome despite the oath
Even after swearing, diyah (blood money) is imposed on the locality's aqila.
The oath does not absolve them; diyah is collective responsibility.

Question: A man is found killed in a locality, and it is not known who killed him. How is his killer sought? Answer: The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) established a way for that: it is to administer oaths to fifty men from the people of that locality, and this is called qasamah. Question: Who selects these fifty, and how are they sworn? Answer: The guardian (wali) selects them. Among them there should not be a child, nor an insane person, nor a woman, nor a slave, nor a mudabbar, nor a mukatab. And they swear by Allah: 'We did not kill him, nor do we know of a killer for him.' Question: So if they swear, are they completely absolved? Answer: No, they are not absolved; rather, the diyah is adjudged against the people of that locality.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on the procedure of qasamah (compurgation) when a murdered body is found in a locality with an unknown killer. The Prophet ﷺ prescribed that fifty men from that locality be selected by the victim's guardian (wali). These men must be free, adult, sane, male Muslims; thus excluded are children, insane, women, slaves, mudabbar (slaves whose freedom is deferred), and mukatab (slaves who have a contract of manumission). They swear that they did not kill the victim nor know who did. However, even after this oath, they are not fully exonerated; rather, diyah (blood money) is imposed on the entire locality (on their aqila, or support group). The Hanafi school holds that this diyah is to be paid by the aqila of the locality over three years.

Key Terms
قسامة (qasamah): A legal procedure where fifty men from a locality take an oath to deny knowledge of a murder, leading to diyah imposed on them.
ولي (wali): The guardian or heir of the victim; responsible for selecting the fifty men.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose owner has declared that he will be free upon the owner's death.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has made a contract with his owner to purchase his freedom.
عاقلة (aqila): The group of male relatives or tribe responsible for paying diyah.
