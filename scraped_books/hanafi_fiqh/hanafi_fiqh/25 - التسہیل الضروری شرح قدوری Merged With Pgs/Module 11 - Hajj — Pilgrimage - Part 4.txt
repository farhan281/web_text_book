Conditions of Hadi: Thani and Jadha'
Age requirements for hadi and exception for young sheep

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for a valid hadi animal and identify the exception for young sheep (jadha') with its requirement of visual resemblance to a thani.

Core Idea
A valid hadi must be a thani and defect-free; only sheep may substitute a jadha' that looks like a thani.

The hadi (sacrificial animal) offered during Hajj must meet specific age and health conditions. This passage from al-Tashil al-Daruri Sharh al-Quduri clarifies the minimum age requirements for different species and the exceptional allowance for young sheep known as jadha'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
The passage states that the animal must be a thani (second-year or older) and free from defects. These are the two overarching conditions.
2
Define thani for each species
For sheep, thani is one year old; for cattle, two years; for camels, five years. Memorize these age thresholds.
3
Note the exception for sheep
For sheep only, a jadha' (at least six months old) is permissible if it visually resembles a thani. This is a concession.
The exception does not apply to goats or other animals.
4
Understand the visual similarity condition
The jadha' must be such that an observer would be uncertain whether it is a thani or a jadha'. This ensures it is close in size and appearance.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi difference
The marginal note mentions that some scholars defined jadha' as more than a year, but the relied-upon view is six months with the visual condition.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

A valid hadi (sacrificial animal) must be free from defects. This passage details which defects make an animal unsuitable, clarifying that minor imperfections do not invalidate the sacrifice. Understanding these rules ensures your hadi is accepted.

Guided Path

The passage begins by stating that a hadi animal must be 'free from defect' (سليم من العيب), meaning sound in limbs. This is the overarching condition.

List the Major Defects

Slaughter Place and Animal Specification for Hadi
Specifies that hadi must be slaughtered within Haram and particularizes animals for expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that hadi must be slaughtered within the Haram and identify the specific animals required for two expiatory cases versus all other types of hadi.

Core Idea
Hadi slaughter is confined to the Haram; severe expiations require a camel or cow, while all other hadi permit a sheep.

The hadi must be offered correctly for it to be valid. This lesson clarifies two key rulings: where the hadi must be slaughtered and which animal is required for each type. Understanding these details ensures that the sacrifice is accepted and the expiation is fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the place requirement
Read the first Q&A: the hadi must be slaughtered specifically within the Haram (the sacred precincts of Mecca). Any slaughter outside is invalid.
This applies to all types of hadi without exception.
2
Distinguish the two severe cases
The second Q&A specifies two expiatory cases that require a full camel or cow: (1) one who performed Tawaf al-Ziyarah while junub and did not repeat, and (2) one who had intercourse after standing at 'Arafah but before halq and before Tawaf al-Ziyarah.
These are major violations; only a large animal suffices.
3
List the cases permitting a sheep
For all other types – dam al-ihsar, dam al-tamattu' wa al-qiran, dam jinayah, and voluntary hadi – a sheep is permissible.
Sheep includes goats as well according to Hanafi fiqh.

Apply the ruling
Given a scenario, determine the type of hadi and the correct animal, always verifying that slaughter occurs within the Haram.

Partnership in Hadi and Eating Its Meat
Rulings on shared hadi and eating its meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid partnership in hadi and identify which types of hadi meat may be eaten by the owner.

Core Idea
A shared hadi requires every partner to intend worship; if one seeks only meat, all are invalid.

In this lesson, we examine two connected rulings from the Hanafi school regarding hadi (sacrificial animals during Hajj). First, the conditions for sharing a single camel or cow among multiple persons. Second, the rules about the owner consuming the meat of different categories of hadi. Understanding these helps ensure the validity of the sacrifice and proper distribution of meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: partnership in slaughtering hadi, and eating from its meat. The ruling is that partnership is permissible under conditions, and eating is permissible only from certain types.
The passage is a Q&A format; extract the legal norms.
2
Note the condition for partnership
All partners must intend qurbah (devotion). If even one partner intends only to obtain meat (and not worship), the sacrifice is invalid for all.
The condition is explicit: 'كل واحد من الشركاء يريد القربة'.
3
Identify the types of hadi
List the categories: voluntary hadi (هدي التطوع), mut'ah and qiran hadi (هدي المتعة والقران), dam al-ihsar (دم الإحصار), and dam al-jinayat (دماء الجنايات).
The first three are for optional or obligatory sacrifices during Hajj; the last two are penalties.
4
Apply the eating rule
The owner may eat from voluntary, mut'ah, and qiran hadi, but not from dam al-ihsar or dam al-jinayat. Those must be given as charity.
The distinction is based on the nature of the obligation.

For tawaf in janabah without repetition and intercourse before tawaf after Arafat, only a camel or cow suffices; a sheep is enough for all other hady.

You have learned about various types of hady in Hajj: dam al-ihsar, dam al-tamattu', etc. The question often arises: which animal is required for which expiation? Miscellaneous Will Rulings: Mukatab, Revocation, and Bequest of Service
Three standalone rulings on wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a mukatab's will, explain how revocation works, and apply the rule for bequeathing service or usufruct.

Core Idea
A mukatab's will is invalid; revocation requires explicit statement; bequest of service or residence is valid but limited to one-third.

This lesson covers three standalone rulings from the 'Miscellaneous Issues' section of the chapter on wills. First, the will of a mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) is invalid even if he has wealth. Second, a testator may revoke a will only by explicit words; mere denial is not revocation. Third, bequeathing the service of a slave or the use of a house for a fixed period is valid; if the asset's value exceeds one-third, the legatee receives a proportional share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first ruling: mukatab's will
The text states: 'لا تصح وصية المكاتب وإن ترك وفاء' — the will of a mukatab is invalid even if he leaves sufficient funds. Reason: the mukatab is still a slave and does not fully own his property (his earnings belong to him but his person is not fully free).
This applies to slaves under kitabah; a free person's will is valid.
2
Understand revocation of a will
Revocation is possible only by explicit statement (صرح بالرجوع). Denying the will (جحد الوصية) does not constitute revocation.
The testator may change his mind; but simply denying it later is not legally effective.
3
Analyze the bequest of service or usufruct
Bequeathing the service of a slave or residence in a house for known years is valid, as is a perpetual bequest. If the slave's full value is within one-third, the legatee gets full service. If the slave is the only asset and exceeds one-third, the legatee receives a proportional share of service (e.g., one day per three).
The same principle applies to residence: if the house's value exceeds one-third, the legatee gets use for a proportional period.
4
Note the one-third limit
All wills are limited to one-third of the estate. The commentator clarifies that the phrase 'from where it suffices' in the Hajj section also implies this limit.
This is a fundamental rule of wasiyyah.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of rulings on wills (wasiyyah), focusing on the will to perform Hajj and miscellaneous issues. The teacher, in question-answer style, explains: If a person bequeaths for his obligatory Hajj (Hajjah al-Islam), the heirs must appoint a man from the deceased's town to perform it riding (if the bequest suffices). If the bequest is insufficient, they use the available funds to perform it as far as possible. A second scenario: if the person died en route after leaving for Hajj, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifah holds that the proxy should start from the deceased's town (like a fresh obligation), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that it should start from the place of death (since the obligation was already in progress). The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is the view of Abu Hanifah as per the matn of al-Quduri. Then come miscellaneous rulings: a mukatab's (slave under contract of manumission) will is invalid even if he has funds; revocation of a will is valid only by explicit statement, not by mere denial; bequeathing the service of a slave or usufruct of a house for fixed or indefinite years is valid. If the slave's full value exceeds one-third, the legacy is limited to one-third: for service, the legatee gets a proportional share (e.g., one day's service per week). If the legatee dies, the slave returns entirely to the heirs. Finally, the commentator notes that al-Quduri's phrase "from where it suffices" implicitly requires the Hajj expenses to be within one-third of the estate, as no will exceeds one-third without heirs' consent. The citation from Ghuniyat al-Nasik confirms that if the will is to perform Hajj without specification, one Hajj is performed from one-third of the estate to the extent of sufficiency.

Key Terms

الرجوع عن الوصية (al-ruju' 'an al-wasiyyah): Revocation of a will, which is valid only by explicit statement.
