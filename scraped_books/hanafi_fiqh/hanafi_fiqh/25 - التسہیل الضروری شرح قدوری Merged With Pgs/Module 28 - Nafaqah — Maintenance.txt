General Obligation of Maintenance and Categories of Recipients
Author enumerates categories of maintenance recipients and ten rulings for wife's maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of persons entitled to maintenance in Hanafi fiqh and identify the basic condition for a wife's entitlement.

Core Idea
Maintenance is obligatory on specific relatives — wives, children, divorced women, parents, and uterine relatives.

This passage begins the Book of Maintenance (Nafaqat) with a clear question: who must one support? The answer lists five categories. Understanding who is entitled helps you know your obligations in family and kinship relations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Read the first question-answer pair. The author states that maintenance (nafaqah) is wajib (obligatory) for five groups.
Wajib here means legally binding, not merely recommended.
2
List the five categories
Extract the groups: wives (zawjat), children (awlad), divorced women (mutallaqat), parents (walidayn), and uterine relatives (dhawu al-arham).
Uterine relatives are those related through females; their maintenance is subject to further conditions elsewhere.
3
Focus on wives: the base condition
The specific section on wives begins with the condition for entitlement: the wife must submit herself (sallamat nafsaha) in her husband's house.
Submission means making herself available for marital life, not necessarily sexual availability.
4
Note the absence of wealth condition
The text says 'whether the husband is wealthy or poor' (mūsiran kāna aw mu‘siran). This means the obligation is not contingent on the husband's financial ability; it is a debt.
However, the amount may vary based on means, as per other texts.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Maintenance (Nafaqat) in the Hanafi fiqh manual. The author first gives a general classification of those entitled to maintenance: wives, children, divorced women, parents, and uterine relatives. Then he focuses specifically on the rulings for wives' maintenance, presenting ten distinct cases (though points 4 and 5 are missing in this manuscript). The first ruling establishes the general rule: a wife is entitled to maintenance when she submits herself to her husband's home, regardless of the husband's financial status. The second ruling addresses nushuz (disobedience) – if the wife is rebellious and does not submit, she loses her right to maintenance until she returns. The third ruling covers three scenarios: (a) if she withholds herself to demand her mahr, she still gets maintenance; (b) if the wife is too young for intercourse, no maintenance even if she submits; (c) if the husband is too young but wife is adult, she gets maintenance. The sixth ruling (points 4-5 missing) deals with imprisonment or kidnapping – no maintenance due. The seventh ruling covers Hajj travel without husband – no maintenance, but if the husband goes with her, maintenance is due as residence not travel. The eighth ruling (unnumbered) says illness in husband's house does not negate maintenance. The ninth ruling: if a man marries a slave woman lodged by her master, he must support her. The tenth ruling: if a slave marries a free woman, her maintenance is his debt, enforceable by sale of his property unless his master pays.

Key Terms
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance, including food, clothing, and housing, owed by a person to his dependents.
نشز (nushuz): A wife's disobedience or rebellion by leaving the marital home without valid reason.

Ten Specific Rulings on Wives' Maintenance
Author enumerates ten specific rulings for wives' maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ten Hanafi rulings to determine when a wife is entitled to maintenance and when she loses that right.

Core Idea
A wife's right to maintenance depends on submission; nushuz, imprisonment, travel without husband, and certain marital statuses suspend or cancel it.

After stating the general rule, the author gives ten specific cases that illustrate exceptions and nuances. Memorizing these will help you handle real-life situations where maintenance is contested. The rulings cover nushuz, minors, imprisonment, travel, illness, and slave marriages.

Guided Path
1
Review the first ruling (general rule)
Start with ruling #1: maintenance is due upon wife's submission in the marital home. This is the baseline.
Remember: submission means living with husband and being available for marital relations.
2
Analyze rulings #2-3: nushuz and mahr disputes
Ruling #2: nushuz (disobedience) cuts off maintenance until return. Ruling #3: withholding submission to demand mahr does NOT cut off maintenance.
Important distinction: nushuz is leaving with no valid reason; withholding for mahr is a valid right.
3
Examine rulings on minors (missing 4-5)
The text has gaps: a wife who is a minor (cannot be enjoyed) gets no maintenance even if she submits; a minor husband with adult wife does owe maintenance.
The illah (legal cause) for maintenance is the ability to enjoy marital life; if the wife is too young, the husband does not benefit.
4
Work through rulings #6-8: external factors
Ruling #6: imprisonment for debt or kidnapping – no maintenance. Ruling #7: Hajj travel without husband – no maintenance, but if husband accompanies, maintenance is as residence (not travel) costs. Ruling (unnumbered): illness in marital home does not stop maintenance.
Illness is not a form of nushuz; she remains entitled.
5
Apply rulings #9-10: slave marriages
Ruling #9: marrying a slave woman provided a house by her master – husband owes maintenance. Ruling #10: slave marries free woman with permission – her maintenance is debt on slave, enforceable by sale unless master pays.
These show that maintenance obligation follows the husband's capacity, not his freedom status.

Ruling on Husband's Poverty and Maintenance
Question and answer on husband's poverty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband cannot afford maintenance and explain the wife's recourse.

Core Idea
Poverty does not dissolve marriage; the wife may borrow on the husband's credit.

A common concern in marriage is what happens if the husband cannot provide financial support. This lesson presents the clear Hanafi ruling: the marriage remains intact, and the wife is advised to borrow for her needs, with the husband liable to repay when able. Understanding this ruling prevents premature separation and protects the wife's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage presents a question: what happens if a husband is too poor to pay his wife's maintenance (nafaqah)? Recognize that the core issue is the effect of poverty on the marriage bond.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The answer clearly states: 'They are not separated.' Memorize this as the foundational rule. The marriage continues despite the husband's financial difficulty.
3
Explain the wife's recourse
The wife is instructed to borrow money for her maintenance on the husband's behalf. This creates a debt that the husband must repay when he becomes financially able.
The Arabic phrase 'استديني عليه' means 'borrow on his behalf' – essential terminology.
4
Note the underlying principle
The ruling is based on the Hanafi principle that the marriage bond is not severed due to poverty. Instead, the obligation of maintenance transforms into a debt, preserving the marriage.
Compare with other schools that may allow separation after a period.

Instead, the wife is advised to borrow money for her own maintenance, and the debt will be on the husband. When the husband becomes financially able, he must repay this debt. This is based on the principle that the marriage bond is not severed due to poverty, and the wife has a claim on the husband's future earnings.

Completing Maintenance After Affluence
Question about completing maintenance from poverty to affluence after court order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband's financial status changes from poverty to wealth after a court-ordered maintenance at the poverty level.

Core Idea
Maintenance is adjusted to the husband's current ability; a poverty-level order is not final if wealth increases.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from Hanafi jurisprudence on spousal maintenance (nafaqah). The key principle is that maintenance is tied to the husband's financial capacity, and a court order does not freeze the amount forever. When a husband becomes wealthy, the wife is entitled to have her maintenance upgraded accordingly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
The court ordered maintenance based on the husband's poverty (i'sar). This sets the initial amount.
2
Recognize the change in circumstance
The husband's financial status improves from poverty to affluence (iysar). This changes the legal basis for maintenance.
In Hanafi fiqh, maintenance amount is determined by the husband's capacity at the time of payment.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle of adjustment
The ruling states that the maintenance is 'completed' to the affluent level. This means the wife can demand the difference for the period after he became wealthy.
The Arabic phrase 'يتمم لها ذلك' indicates a continuation, not a new case.

Ruling on Court-Ordered Maintenance After Husband's Death
Question and answer on nafaqah arrears after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that court-ordered maintenance not paid before the husband's death drops and is not inherited, and explain why it differs from other debts.

Core Idea
A court-ordered maintenance debt drops at the husband's death; it is not inherited.

In this lesson, we examine a specific question: if a husband is ordered by a court to pay maintenance but dies before paying, what happens to that debt? The Hanafi answer is clear: the obligation is dropped. This ruling highlights a unique feature of maintenance (nafaqah) in Hanafi fiqh—it is not a debt that survives death. Understanding this principle helps distinguish nafaqah from other financial obligations that pass to the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The court ordered the husband to pay maintenance. Months passed without payment, then the husband died. The key point is that the debt was not paid before death.
This is a situation where the husband was alive when the order was made but died before performing.
2
Recall the Hanafi ruling
The answer states: 'سقطت النفقة في هذه الصورة' meaning the maintenance is dropped. Memorize this concise ruling.
The verb 'saqatat' (dropped) is the key term.
3
Understand the basis
Maintenance (nafaqah) is an ongoing personal obligation linked to the husband's life. It ceases at death and does not become a debt on his estate.
This distinguishes nafaqah from other debts like loans or sale prices.
4
Apply the ruling
If a similar scenario arises, the maintenance arrears cannot be claimed from the husband's estate. The wife has no recourse to recover them.
The principle applies regardless of the duration or amount of unpaid maintenance.

This is a unique feature of maintenance (nafaqah) in Hanafi law.

The Refundability of Advance Maintenance Upon Death
Intra-Hanafi dispute on refund of advance annual maintenance upon death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad on the refund of advance annual maintenance upon death and explain the practical difference (thamarah).

Core Idea
Advance maintenance is not refundable at death according to Abu Hanifa, but Muhammad apportions it.

When a husband gives his wife a lump sum for a year's maintenance, the marriage might end before the year is up. What happens to the unspent portion? This passage from a classical Hanafi manual records a dispute between the founding imam and his foremost student, highlighting a critical difference in how maintenance obligations are viewed.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Identify the factual situation: the husband gives a year's maintenance in advance, then either spouse dies before the year ends. The question is whether the unspent balance is recoverable.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa rules that nothing is taken back from the wife. She keeps the entire advance, even if she dies the next day. Consider the legal basis: the payment is treated as a completed transfer of ownership, not a daily entitlement.
Implicitly, the obligation is fulfilled upon payment.
3
Identify Muhammad's Position
Imam Muhammad holds that the advance should be apportioned. The wife keeps only the amount corresponding to the time that actually passed; the remainder belongs to the husband (or his estate).
This view sees maintenance as a daily obligation.
4
Compare the Practical Differences
Under Abu Hanifa, the wife's heirs keep all the money if she dies early. Under Muhammad, the husband recovers the unspent portion. This affects financial planning and expectations in marriage.

Core Idea

This brief 'faa'ida' clarifies a key procedural rule in Hanafi family law: when a judge orders maintenance (nafaqah) and the obligated party fails to pay for a period, the right to claim that past maintenance is extinguished (saqatat). The only exception is if the judge had previously permitted the dependents to borrow on the defaulter's account. This rule prevents the accumulation of unpaid maintenance as an automatic debt, encouraging dependents to seek judicial authorization promptly.

The ruling applies to children, parents, and relatives, as stated.
2
Identify the exception
The ruling changes if the judge gave permission (idhn) to the dependents to borrow money on the defaulter's account. In that case, the past maintenance does not drop; it becomes a debt that can be claimed.
The permission must be from the judge, not just anyone.
3

Without judicial permission, the dependents cannot later demand payment for that past period. The obligation is extinguished. With permission, the borrowed amount becomes a debt on the defaulter.

This rule incentivizes dependents to promptly seek court authorization to borrow for their needs, rather than letting arrears pile up. It provides clarity and prevents disputes over old claims.
