Ghusl on Friday, for Ihram, and Entering Makkah: Sunnah According to Hadith
Marginal hadith citation on ghusl occasions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three occasions for which ghusl is considered sunnah mu'akkadah according to the Hanafi school and identify the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Ghusl on Friday, before Ihram, and before entering Makkah is sunnah — confirmed by authentic hadith.

This lesson examines a marginal hadith citation found in a Hanafi fiqh text. It highlights three occasions when performing ghusl (ritual bath) is considered sunnah mu'akkadah: Friday, before entering Ihram, and before entering Makkah. The hadith cited by al-Hakim in al-Mustadrak confirms this practice as part of the Prophetic sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three occasions
Read the hadith text and list the three situations where ghusl is prescribed: Friday, intending Ihram, and intending to enter Makkah.
2
Recognize the hadith source and authenticity
Note that the hadith is narrated by al-Hakim in al-Mustadrak and declared sahih by the standards of Bukhari and Muslim, with al-Dhahabi's concurrence.
This strengthens the reliability of the practice.
3
Understand the Hanafi classification
According to the Hanafi school, ghusl on these occasions is sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah) — not wajib, but highly recommended and regularly practiced by the Prophet.
The ruling is derived from this and other hadiths.

Commentary
This marginal note references a hadith regarding ghusl for Friday and for entering ihram and entering Makkah. The citation from al-Hakim indicates the hadith is authentic by the standards of Bukhari and Muslim. The text is partially corrupt; the exact wording is unclear, but the legal point is that ghusl on these occasions is sunnah mu'akkadah.

Key Terms
سنة مؤكدة (sunnah mu'akkadah): An emphasized but non-obligatory practice; regularly done by the Prophet; leaving it is disliked but not sinful.
إحرام (ihram): The state of ritual consecration for Hajj or Umrah, entered with intention and talbiyah.
غسل (ghusl): Full-body ritual washing with water, required for janabah and recommended for certain occasions.

Definition of Hadr and Ibaha
Definition of prohibition and permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the terms hadr and ibaha in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish between them using the linguistic and technical meanings.

Core Idea
Hadr means prohibition, ibaha means permissibility — the fundamental legal categories of Hanafi fiqh.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational terms hadr (prohibition) and ibaha (permissibility) as defined in the Hanafi school. These two concepts are the poles of legal classification for all actions. The passage also clarifies a related term, aghaza, used in the rules of war.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Core Terms
Locate the terms hadr and ibaha in the passage. Note that they are opposites in meaning.
Hadr is the core prohibition term; ibaha is its opposite.
2
Understand the Linguistic Meaning
Read the definition: hadr linguistically means man' (prevention). The passive participle mahzur means that which is prevented (i.e., forbidden). Ibahah is not explicitly defined here, but by contrast it means permission.
Linguistic meaning often forms the basis for the legal definition.
3
Apply the Definitions to Examples
Consider the term aghaza provided in the passage: it is a technical term meaning 'to hasten in killing the wounded enemy.' This illustrates how a legal definition applies to specific actions in fiqh.
Technical terms in fiqh often have precise conditions derived from primary sources.

Commentary
This short passage defines key terms. The passage type is a definition ruling. Hanafi legal theory distinguishes between hadr (prohibition) and ibaha (permissibility). Hadr is linguistically man' (prevention, forbidding). Mahzur is the passive participle: that which is prevented, i.e., forbidden. Ibaha is permissibility. The text also explains the term 'aghaza' (to finish off the wounded) from the previous chapter, which is a technical term in the rules of war.

Key Terms
حظر (hadr): Linguistically prevention; technically, prohibition (as opposed to ibaha).
إباحة (ibaha): Linguistically permissibility; technically, the ruling that an action is permitted.
أجهز (aghaza): To finish off the wounded; to hasten in killing them in battle.
