Will of a Prodigal: Valid from One-Third
Ruling on a prodigal's will for charitable purposes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a prodigal's will for charitable purposes and identify the portion from which it is valid.

Core Idea
A prodigal's charitable will is valid, but only from one-third of his estate.

A prodigal (safīh) is under legal interdiction due to poor financial judgment. However, when it comes to religious bequests (waṣiyyah), Hanafi law recognizes his capacity to direct charitable gifts after death, albeit with the standard one-third limitation. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the person and act
The passage asks about a safīh (prodigal) who, while ill, makes a will for acts of devotion (qurb) and charitable causes (abwāb al-khayr). Recognize that a safīh is legally interdicted but here the act is a will.
Prodigal = a person who squanders wealth, hence restricted from financial transactions.
2
Determine the ruling
The answer states 'tajūz' (it is valid). Understand that the will is legally effective and must be executed after death.
Validity here means the will is binding to the extent allowed.
3
Identify the limitation
The will is valid 'min thulthi mālihī' (from one-third of his wealth). This is the standard limit for all bequests unless heirs approve more.
The one-third rule protects heirs' inheritance rights.
4
Consider the context
The will is for charitable purposes. Hanafi law restricts a prodigal's non-charitable wills (e.g., to a friend) but permits charitable ones within the one-third. Reflect on the wisdom: encouraging charity while preserving heirs' shares.
A prodigal's will for other purposes (non-charity) is not valid even from one-third.

The Prohibition of Taking Wages for Quran Recitation for the Deceased
States ruling with rational proof from later Hanafi authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking wages for Quran recitation to gift reward to the deceased and explain the reasoning based on sincerity and reward.

Core Idea
Taking wages for Quran recitation to gift reward to the deceased is haram because no reward is earned from hired recitation.

Many Muslims hire reciters to read Quran and gift the reward to deceased loved ones. But what does Hanafi fiqh say? This lesson examines the clear prohibition stated by Ibn Abidin and the underlying principle: sincerity is essential for reward. Without sincerity, no reward is generated, so there is nothing to gift.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is taking wages (ujrah) for reciting Quran specifically to convey reward (īṣāl al-thawāb) to the deceased. The ruling is haram (prohibited).
This is a clear ruling from Ibn Abidin, citing Taqi al-Din.
2
Understand the reasoning (dalīl ‘aqlī)
The reasoning is that recitation done for worldly wages lacks ikhlāṣ (sincerity). Scholars state that such recitation produces no reward (thawāb). If no reward is earned, there is nothing to gift to the deceased.
The logic is: reward requires sincerity → hired recitation lacks sincerity → no reward → no gift.
3
Distinguish from hiring for teaching
The passage notes that scholars differed on hiring for teaching (ta‘līm) Quran, but not on hiring for mere recitation. Teaching involves a tangible benefit (learning), so wages are permissible. Mere recitation for reward is different.
This distinction shows the specificity of the prohibition.
4
Apply the ruling in practice
If a family hires a reciter to read Quran and gift the reward, the contract is invalid and the payment is haram for the reciter. The family should instead perform good deeds sincerely and gift the reward themselves.
Alternative: the family can recite themselves or donate to charity in the deceased's name.

Definition and Rulings of Wasiyyah (Will)
Definition and Rulings of Will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of wasiyyah, distinguish its three categories of ruling (obligatory, recommended, invalid), and identify who may make or receive a valid will.

Core Idea
A will transfers ownership after death; its ruling depends on unpaid duties to Allah and people.

The Book of Wills begins by defining wasiyyah both linguistically and technically. The Hanafi jurists then explain when making a will is obligatory, recommended, or invalid, and answer common questions about who can bequeath and who can inherit. This lesson covers the foundational rulings every Muslim should know about estate planning in Islam.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and technical definition
Wasiyyah linguistically means requesting someone to do an act in one's absence. Technically, it is a transfer of ownership (tamlik) attached to after death, whether of a tangible item or a benefit.
The term 'isa'' is synonymous.
2
Identify the three categories of ruling
If the testator has unpaid obligations to Allah (zakat, hajj) or people (debts, trusts), making a will to fulfill them is obligatory (wajib). If no such obligations, bequeathing some wealth for charity is recommended (mustahabb). If debts consume all wealth, a will is invalid unless creditors forgive.
The ruling depends on the testator's financial and religious standing.
3
Note the conditions for validity
The testator must be of sound mind and adult (not a minor). The beneficiary must not be the testator's killer (directly). A will between Muslim and non-Muslim is valid.
Capacity and absence of prevention are key.
4
Apply the rulings to practical scenarios
Use the Q&A to determine permissibility of bequests to killers, minors, and non-Muslims. Recognize that obligations take priority over discretionary bequests.

Prohibits will to a direct killer of the testator

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a will made to a direct killer of the testator is invalid and prohibited, and identify the condition of direct involvement.

Core Idea
A will to a direct killer (even accidental) is invalid to prevent profiting from crime.

In Hanafi fiqh, a will (wasiyyah) is generally encouraged for bequests to non-heirs. However, there is a key exception: a will cannot be made for a person who directly caused the death of the testator. This ruling applies whether the killing was intentional or accidental, as long as the killer was the direct cause. Understanding this rule prevents a killer from benefiting from his own act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is making a will (wasiyyah) in favor of a person who killed the testator. The ruling is that such a will is not permissible (la tajuz).
This is a prohibition; the will is invalid.
2
Note the condition of direct involvement
The ruling applies only if the killer was directly involved (mubashir) in the killing. Indirect causes (e.g., ordering the killing) are excluded.
The text specifies 'mubashiran' (direct).
3
Understand the scope: intentional and accidental
The ruling includes both intentional ('amdan) and accidental (khata'an) killing. This is broader than some schools which only exclude intentional killers.
Hanafi position is comprehensive.
4
Grasp the underlying rationale (illah)
The prohibition prevents the killer from profiting from his crime—a principle to deter wrongdoing and maintain justice.
The illah is to block the means to benefit from killing.

Core Idea
The validity of appointing your own slave as executor depends on whether the heirs are adults.

This lesson examines a specific question in the law of wills (wasiyyah): can a testator appoint his own slave as executor? The Hanafi school gives a conditional answer based on the legal capacity of the heirs. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of a slave's capacity in testamentary matters.

The ruling hinges on whether there are any heirs who have reached the age of majority (kibar). Adult heirs have full legal capacity.

The slave is owned by the heirs after the testator's death. If there are adult heirs, they have authority over the slave, creating a conflict of interest. If all are minors, the slave can manage the estate until they reach majority.
The ruling protects the heirs' interests by avoiding a potential conflict of agency.

Other schools may have stricter views; this is a Hanafi leniency.

Core Idea

A testator may bequeath different fractions to different people. If the total exceeds one-third and no heir consents, the available one-third is divided proportionally. This lesson shows exactly how to split the third when one legatee is owed one-third and another one-sixth.

Multiply: legatee of one-third gets 2/3 × 1/3 = 2/9 of the estate; legatee of one-sixth gets 1/3 × 1/3 = 1/9 of the estate.

The general rule in Hanafi law is that a bequest cannot exceed one-third of the estate unless the heirs consent. However, Abu Hanifa recognized three exceptions where the one-third limit is applied proportionally among multiple beneficiaries, even if each would individually exceed one-third. This lesson unpacks those exceptions: favoritism in sale, partial manumission by working, and bequests of specific sums.

Core Idea
A bequest 'like a son's share' adds the legatee as an extra son; an unspecified 'sahm' defaults to the smallest heir share, but never less than one-sixth.

When a testator uses ambiguous language such as 'like the share of my son' or simply 'a share,' the Hanafi school provides clear rules to determine the legatee's entitlement. Bequests to Groups and Individuals: Distribution Rules
A series of fatwas on bequest distribution rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi rules for distributing bequests when the testator uses terms like 'children', 'heirs', or names individuals, including cases of death of a legatee or unspecified shares.

Core Idea
Bequest wording is literal: 'children' divides equally, 'heirs' uses inheritance ratio; if one legatee dies, the third goes to the survivor if named together.

This lesson covers the first set of bequest scenarios from the chapter on the legatee. You will learn how to interpret common bequest phrases and determine the correct distribution according to Hanafi fiqh. The rulings are based on literal meaning and customary usage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest phrase
Determine if the testator used 'children of so-and-so', 'heirs of so-and-so', or named individuals like Zayd and Amr. This step sets the distribution method.
The term 'children' (walad) is gender-neutral in bequests.
2
Classify the distribution method
For 'children' (walad), divide equally between males and females. For 'heirs' (waratha), divide according to inheritance ratios: male gets twice the female. For named individuals, divide as stated.
If testator says 'to Zayd and Amr, one-third', the phrase implies joint entitlement.
3
Handle death of a legatee
If a named legatee dies before testator, check the wording. 'To Zayd and Amr for one-third' gives the entire third to the survivor. 'One-third between Zayd and Amr' gives the survivor half of that third.
The difference hinges on whether the bequest is a single unit or divisible shares.
4
Apply rules for unspecified shares
If testator says 'a share' (sahm) without specifying, the legatee gets the smallest share of the heirs, but not less than one-sixth. If testator says 'a part' (juz'), the heirs decide the amount.
'Part' (juz') is undefined, leaving discretion to heirs.
5
Note timing of wealth
If the testator has no wealth at the time of bequest but later acquires wealth, the legatee is entitled to one-third of what he owns at death. The bequest takes effect at death.
The bequest is contingent on the existence of wealth at death.

The Invalidity of a Mukātab’s Will
First ruling: invalidity of a mukātab's will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the will of a mukātab and identify the reason for its invalidity.

Core Idea
A slave under contract to purchase his freedom cannot make a valid will, even if he has enough assets.

The first ruling in our passage addresses the legal capacity of a mukātab in matters of bequest. While a mukātab can own property and engage in trade, his ability to give away his wealth after death is restricted. This lesson explains why such a will is automatically invalid in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the person and the act
The ruling is about a 'mukātab' making a 'waṣiyyah' (will). A mukātab is a slave who has a written contract to earn his freedom by paying installments. The act is making a bequest of his property after death.
2
State the legal ruling
The passage says 'lā taṣiḥḥ waṣiyyat al-mukātab' – the will of a mukātab is not valid. This means it has no legal effect.
3
Consider the condition
The text adds 'wa in taraka wafā'' – even if he leaves sufficient assets (to cover both his manumission and the bequest). This shows the ruling is absolute; the presence of assets does not make it valid.
The reason is that a mukātab's legal capacity is incomplete; he is still a slave until fully free, and his right to dispose of property after death is suspended.
4
Connect to the underlying principle
In Hanafi fiqh, the mukātab has limited property rights during the contract period. His ownership is not full, and his will is akin to the will of a regular slave, which is also invalid without the master's permission. Here, even with assets, the will fails.

Revocation of a Will: Explicit Statement vs. Denial
Second ruling: revocation of a will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a will is revoked only by explicit verbal revocation, not by mere denial.

Core Idea
A testator can revoke his will anytime, but only clear words of revocation count—denial is not revocation.

The second ruling in our passage clarifies how a testator may cancel his will. While the testator has full freedom to change his mind, the mode of revocation matters. This lesson explains why mere denial is legally ineffective and what constitutes a valid revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its permissibility
The ruling is about 'rujūʿ an al-waṣiyyah' (revocation of a will). The passage says 'yajūz li-l-mūṣī al-rujūʿ ʿan al-waṣiyyah' – it is permissible for the testator to revoke the will.
2
Distinguish between explicit revocation and denial
The text gives two cases: (a) if he explicitly revokes (ṣarriḥa bi-al-rujūʿ), it is a revocation; (b) if he denies the will (jaḥada al-waṣiyyah), it is not a revocation.
3
Understand the legal effect of each
Explicit revocation cancels the will, so upon death the estate goes to heirs without the will's effect. Denial does not revoke; the will remains valid and must be executed if witnesses exist or if the denial is proven false.
4
Consider the rationale
In Hanafi fiqh, the will is a unilateral commitment (tabarruʿ) that can be revoked at any time before death because it is not binding. However, revocation must be clear because the will creates rights. Denial may be a lie or forgetfulness, so it does not extinguish the earlier commitment.

Bequest of Usufruct: Service and Dwelling
Third ruling: bequest of usufruct (service/dwelling)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on bequest of usufruct (service of a slave, dwelling of a house) including calculation of shared service and return upon legatee's death.

Core Idea
A bequest of usufruct (service or dwelling) is valid for a fixed term or perpetual, but the capital must come from one-third; otherwise, the usufruct is shared proportionally.

The third ruling addresses bequests of things that are not ownership of the asset itself, but the right to use it. The passage explains how such bequests are handled, especially when the testator has limited assets. It also covers what happens when the legatee dies before the term ends.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of the bequest
The bequest is of 'khidmat ʿabdihi' (service of his slave) and 'suknā dārihi' (dwelling in his house). These are usufruct (manfaʿah), not ownership of the slave or house itself.
2
State the general permissibility
Such bequests are valid for a fixed number of years or even perpetually (abad). This is allowed because usufruct is considered a form of property that can be bequeathed.
3
Apply the one-third rule
The passage outlines two scenarios: (a) If the slave's full value (as capital) falls within the one-third of the estate, the slave is handed to the legatee for service. (b) If the testator has no other assets, meaning the slave is the entire estate, then the slave's service is divided: heirs use him two days, legatee one day.
4
Determine the conclusion upon legatee's death
When the legatee dies, the slave 'returns completely to the heirs' (ʿād al-ʿabdu ilā al-wārithīna kāmilan). This implies that the usufruct bequest ends with the legatee's death, even if the term is not finished, because the usufruct is personal.
