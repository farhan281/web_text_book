The Foundling: Definition and Duty to Rescue
Ruling on picking up a found child and naming

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of a laqit and explain why picking up a foundling is a fard kifayah.

Core Idea
Picking up a foundling is a communal obligation to preserve life.

This lesson opens the Book of the Foundling with a clear definition and ruling. The finder must act immediately to save the child from harm. The term 'laqit' itself points to the action of picking up. We will unpack this simple yet profound duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage asks what the finder should do: 'He picks it up (yalqatuhu).' This is a command, and the Hanafi school classifies it as fard kifayah (communal obligation).
A communal obligation means if some Muslims fulfil it, the rest are absolved.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given: 'so that it does not get lost and is protected from destruction.' The obligation is rooted in preserving life (hifz al-nafs), a higher objective of Shari'ah.
This overrides other concerns like ownership or lineage.
3
Analyze the definition
The child is called laqit because it is picked up (ultuqita). The name derives from the action, emphasizing the finder's role.
This is a linguistic derivation common in fiqh nomenclature.

The public treasury, not the finder, bears the foundling's upkeep.

This brief Q&A from the matn addresses a crucial social welfare ruling: the financial responsibility for an abandoned child. The finder performs a commendable act of rescue, but the state's treasury must provide for the child's needs.

The state is responsible for the welfare of those without guardians, including foundlings, as a collective duty (wajib kifayah).

Resolving Conflicting Paternity Claims of a Foundling
Ruling on resolving conflicting paternity claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for determining paternity when two men claim a foundling.

Core Idea
Description of a hidden birthmark proves paternity; otherwise both are fathers.

When two men simultaneously claim a foundling as their son, how does Islamic law resolve the dispute? The Hanafi school provides clear rules: the claimant who accurately describes a distinctive physical mark on the child's body is given priority. If neither can describe such a mark, the child is considered the son of both. This ruling balances the need to establish lineage with the welfare of the foundling. In this lesson, you will learn the exact criteria and their application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Recognize that the scenario involves two men each claiming the foundling as his son. The ruling is triggered only when both claims are made.
2
Check for a described mark
Determine if either claimant describes a specific physical mark (sifah) on the child's body. This mark must be distinctive and not publicly known.
The sifah is proof of intimate knowledge.
3
Apply the priority rule
If one describes a mark, he is awla bihi (has more right) and the child is assigned to him. This resolves the conflict decisively.
The priority is absolute; the other claimant is dismissed.
4
Consider the scenario with no mark
If neither describes a mark, the child is declared the son of both. This is a literal legal recognition, not a physical division.
Both men are considered fathers for purposes of inheritance and maintenance.
5
Apply the precedence rule
If one claim was made earlier, that claimant takes the child. This rule applies even if no mark is described, but it may be superseded by a mark description.
Earlier claim is only considered when neither describes a mark.

Rulings on the Foundling (Al-Luqatah)
Question-answer rulings on foundling status, care, and disposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on the foundling's status by location, the finder's authority, and identify the intra-Hanafi difference on apprenticing.

Core Idea
A foundling's status is determined by location; the finder has limited care, not full guardianship.

This lesson covers the rulings on a foundling (al-laqit) – an abandoned child discovered by someone. The Hanafi school presumes the child's religion based on the location of discovery and restricts the finder's authority to basic preservation and spending on necessities. We also explore an internal Hanafi difference regarding whether the finder may apprentice or hire out the child.

Guided Path
1
Determine status by location
First, identify where the foundling was discovered: if in a Muslim city or village, the child is presumed Muslim even if a dhimmi claims him. If found in a dhimmi settlement or place of worship, the child is ruled a dhimmi.
This is a legal presumption (hukm wad'i) based on the dominant religion of the location.
2
Evaluate claims of lineage and ownership
Analyze the two claims: a dhimmi claiming paternity (lineage is established but child remains Muslim) and a person claiming the child as a slave (not accepted; child is free). Also a slave claiming paternity (accepted but child remains free).
The child's freedom is protected; only lineage may be established.
3
Identify property ownership
Property found tied to the child belongs to the child, not the finder, because the child is capable of ownership and has possession.
The finder only manages the property for the child's benefit.
4
Define the finder's authority limits
The finder (multaqit) cannot marry the child or dispose of his property, but can spend on necessities (food, clothing) and accept gifts on the child's behalf.
The finder's role is custodial, not that of a full guardian.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Al-Quduri permits apprenticing or hiring out the foundling, but Imam Muhammad (in al-Jami' al-Saghir) prohibits it, and this is the sounder view because full guardianship belongs to the judge.
The text notes 'huwa al-ashah' – the prohibition is relied upon.

Rulings on Lost Property (Luqata)
Question-answer rulings on lost property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on finding lost property, including the obligation to pick up, announcement periods, and disposal, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the announcement period.

Core Idea
Lost property is a trust; finder must announce and cannot own without charity.

This passage from Kitab al-Luqata covers the basic rulings on lost property: when to pick it up, how long to announce, what to do if the owner doesn't appear, and special cases like livestock and worthless items. Pay close attention to the disagreement among Hanafi authorities on the announcement period—this affects practice significantly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine when picking up lost property is obligatory (fard kifayah) vs. permissible (mubah). The key factor is fear of loss.
If loss is not feared, it remains permissible but not mandatory.
2
Note the conditions for the finder
When picking up, the finder should take witnesses and hold the item as a trust (amana). This protects both parties.
3
Understand the announcement period
The base ruling from Abu Hanifa: items under 10 dirhams require a few days; 10 dirhams or more require one year. But note the ikhtilaf: Imam Muhammad requires one year for all. A third view leaves it to the finder's judgment.
The text indicates the third view is considered correct (al-sahih).
4
Handle the charity option
If the owner does not appear after announcement, the finder gives the item in charity. If the owner appears later, the finder has a choice: let the charity stand or compensate the owner.
The finder does not automatically own the item; ownership cannot be transferred from an unknown person.
5
Apply to special cases
Livestock (sheep, cow, camel) may be picked up. Worthless items (date pits, peels) may be used without announcement but remain the owner's property.
Expenses on livestock are borne by the finder without reimbursement.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Found Property (Kitab al-Luqata) presents the rulings in a question-and-answer format, with additional commentary from al-Hidayah. It begins by defining luqata as wealth discarded in a place that a passerby might pick up. The ruling on picking it up depends on the risk of loss: if the item is likely to be lost, picking it up is obligatory (wajib) to preserve the owner's property; otherwise it is merely permissible (ja'iz). Upon picking it up, witnesses should be taken to confirm the finder's intention to preserve and return it; the item becomes an amanah (trust), meaning the finder is not liable for accidental loss unless negligent.

Next, the obligation of announcing (ta'rif) the found item is detailed. If the value is less than ten dirhams, announcement is made for a few days; if ten dirhams or more, for a full year. This is based on a narration from Abu Hanifah. Imam Muhammad estimates the basic ruling as a year without distinction, which is also the view of Malik and al-Shafi'i. However, the text notes a preferred opinion: no fixed amount is binding; the finder announces until he is sure the owner will not seek it, then gives it in charity. For perishable items, announcement is made until spoilage is feared, then charity is given. Announcement should be made in the location found and in the congregational mosque. For items clearly unwanted (like date pits), no announcement is needed; they may be utilized but remain owner's property because transfer of ownership from an unknown person is invalid.

If the owner appears after charity has been given, the finder has the option to let the charity stand or to compensate the owner (making the charity his own expense). For animals, the finder may pick them up. If he spends on the animal without judge's permission, he is a volunteer (no reimbursement). With permission, the expense becomes a debt on the owner. If expenses exceed the animal's value, the judge may handle it by renting it out, selling it, or authorizing spending. The finder may detain the animal until reimbursed if permitted. Regarding claims, the finder should not hand over the lost item without evidence; if the claimant describes distinctive marks, the finder may give it but cannot be compelled by the judge. Finally, charity from found property should go to the poor, and the finder may use the item only if he is poor; a rich finder may not use it.
