Qasamah: Special Cases and Exceptions
Rulings on qasamah when wali is involved, some refuse, or insufficient men

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for three exceptional cases in qasamah: when the wali is from the locality, when some selected men refuse to swear, and when there are insufficient men.

Core Idea
The wali is exempt from swearing if from the locality; refusers are imprisoned; oaths are repeated if men are few.

Qasamah is a procedure to resolve alleged murder when evidence is weak. This passage addresses three practical obstacles that may arise during the process. The Hanafi jurists provide clear rulings to ensure the oath requirement is fulfilled without compromising justice.

Guided Path
1
Isolate the three scenarios
The passage is structured as three questions. Identify each scenario: (1) wali is from the locality, (2) some selected men refuse to swear, (3) there are fewer than fifty eligible men in the locality.
Each scenario is a separate legal question with its own ruling.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1: the wali is not sworn and no diyah is adjudged against him even if he swears. For scenario 2: the refusing men are imprisoned until they swear. For scenario 3: the available men swear multiple times until fifty oaths are completed.
Imprisonment is coercive, not punitive.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The wali is exempt to avoid conflict of interest as a plaintiff. Imprisonment compels participation without punishing. Repetition ensures the required fifty oaths despite numerical shortage, preserving the procedure's integrity.
These rulings balance the rights of the victim, the accused, and the community.

Commentary

Signs of Killing in Qasamah
Rulings on signs of killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the physical signs that indicate homicide in Hanafi law and apply them to determine whether qasamah is required.

Core Idea
Only bleeding from eyes or ears, or clear marks of violence, indicate homicide; nose, mouth, and anus bleeding do not.

In cases of uncertain death, the Hanafi school provides specific signs to determine whether a person was killed. This lesson covers the rulings on qasamah based on the presence or absence of such signs.

Guided Path
1
Identify if there is any physical trace (athar) on the body
Check if the body has any wound, bruise, or sign of beating/strangulation.
2
If blood is present, identify the source
Determine whether blood comes from nose, mouth, anus (not indicative) or from eyes/ears (indicative).
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If no trace or non-indicative blood, no qasamah or diyah. If indicative blood or other signs, qasamah becomes wajib.

Commentary
This passage combines several rulings on when qasamah applies based on physical signs, plus a special case of a body found on an animal. First, if a dead body is found with no external mark of violence (no wound, no blood, no sign of beating or strangulation), it is presumed natural death, and no qasamah or diyah. Second, if blood flows from nose, anus, or mouth, these are natural occurrences (nosebleed, hemorrhoids, vomiting) and do not indicate homicide, so again no qasamah or diyah. However, if blood comes from eyes or ears, this is a sign of violence and the person is deemed killed, requiring qasamah. Third, if a body is found on an animal being driven, led, or ridden, the diyah falls on the aqila of the driver/rider/leader, not the locality, because the victim was in their custody (like inside their house). If multiple persons are involved (rider, driver, leader), they all share the diyah.

Liability for Body Found on an Animal
Rulings on liability for body on animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine who is liable for diyah when a body is found on an animal driven, led, or ridden, and explain the custody principle.

Core Idea
The person in control of the animal (driver, rider, leader) is liable for diyah because the victim was in their custody.

When a dead body is found on an animal, the Hanafi school places liability on the person controlling the animal, not on the locality. This lesson covers the rules for driver, leader, and rider.

Guided Path
1
Identify who is controlling the animal
Determine if the animal is being driven (sa'iq), led (qa'id), or ridden (rakib).
2
Apply the custody principle
The person in control has custody over the animal, so any body found on it is in their custody.
3
Determine liability
If one person is in control, that person's aqila pays diyah. If multiple, they share the diyah equally.
The locality is exempt.

Rulings on Qasamah When the Guardian Names a Suspect
Rulings on qasamah based on the guardian's accusation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on qasamah when the guardian names a specific suspect from the quarter versus from outside, and explain the legal consequence.

Core Idea
Qasamah remains on the residents if the guardian names an insider; it drops if an outsider is named.

When a murder victim is found in a locality, the residents must collectively swear (qasamah) to avoid liability. But if the victim's guardian points to a specific individual, does this affect the qasamah? The Hanafi school provides clear rules distinguishing between an accusation against a resident and an outsider. In this lesson, we parse the first two questions of the passage to understand when qasamah and diyah are dropped.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant and the accused
The guardian (wali) is making a claim of murder. He may name a specific person from the same quarter (mahallah) or from outside.
The quarter refers to the residential area where the victim was found.
2
Determine the location of the accused relative to the quarter
Is the accused a resident of that quarter or an outsider? This distinction is key.
3
Apply the ruling for an insider
If the accused is from inside the quarter, the qasamah does not drop from the residents. They must still swear.
The text says 'la tasqut' – it does not drop.
4
Apply the ruling for an outsider
If the accused is from outside, then both the qasamah and the diyah (blood money) drop from the residents.
The residents are no longer liable because the accused is not from their group.
5
Understand the rationale
The residents are only liable for murders within their quarter. If the guardian identifies an outsider, the residents are exonerated.

Definition of Aqila and Collection of Diyah
Defines aqila and how diyah is collected

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-'aqilah in Hanafi fiqh and state the method of collecting diyah from them.

Core Idea
The aqila pays diyah over three years: soldiers via diwan stipends, civilians via tribe capped at 4 dirhams per person.

This lesson clarifies the key term 'aqila'—the group responsible for paying diyah on behalf of an unintentional killer. In Hanafi law, the aqila changes depending on whether the killer is a soldier or a civilian. Understanding this definition is essential for correctly applying the rules of diyat and recognising who bears financial liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its derivation
The word 'aqila comes from 'aql (to tie/pay diyah). Define it as the group that pays diyah for the killer.
The question-answer format in the text is a teaching device.
2
Distinguish the two types of aqila
If the killer is a soldier enrolled in the diwan, the aqila is the diwan (military register). If he is a civilian, the aqila is his paternal tribe.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
3
State the payment method
Diyah is collected over three years. For soldiers, it is taken from their annual stipends. For tribesmen, each member pays at most one dirham and two daniqs per year, reduced if the tribe is large.
The three-year period starts from the date of judgement, not the killing.
4
Note the inclusion of the killer
The killer himself is counted as one of the aqila and contributes like any other member.
5
Recognise the exception for diyah by settlement
The phrase 'by the killing itself' excludes diyah that is agreed through a settlement (sulh) – such diyah is not obligatory on the aqila.
This is from the marginal note in the source.

Types of Diyah Borne by the 'Aqila
List of diyah types borne by the 'Aqila

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six types of diyah that the 'Aqila bears according to Hanafi fiqh and explain the rationale for each.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila bears diyah for quasi-intentional killing, accidental killing, killing by cause, acts of children/insane, and injuries to slaves by free persons.

This lesson details the categories of blood money that the 'Aqila is obligated to pay. Understanding these categories is crucial for determining when the tribe is liable and when the killer himself must pay.

Guided Path
1
List the six categories
Memorize the six items given in the passage: shibh al-'amd, qatl al-khata', killing by cause (e.g., balcony), child/insane intentional act treated as accidental, and free person's accidental injury on a slave.
The first two are the main categories; the others are extensions.
2
Understand 'quasi-intentional'
Shibh al-'amd is an act intended but without intent to kill, e.g., hitting with a stick not meant to kill. Its diyah is heavy (mghallazah).
Heavy diyah is typically 100 camels or equivalent.
3
Accidental killing
Qatl al-khata' is where there is no intent at all, e.g., a hunter shooting and hitting a person. The diyah is lighter.
The 'Aqila bears it regardless of the killer's ability to pay.
4
Killing by cause (bi-sabab)
If someone places an object in the road that causes death, it is considered a cause, and the 'Aqila bears the diyah.
This includes both rawshan (balcony) and mizab (waterspout).
5
Child and insane person
The intentional act of a child or insane person is legally classified as accidental, so diyah is on the 'Aqila.
This is because they lack full legal capacity.
6
Free person injuring a slave accidentally
If a free person accidentally injures a slave, the 'Aqila of the injurer bears the injury (i.e., pays the slave's diyah).
Note: the 'Aqila does not bear the slave's own jinnayah (acts).

Exclusions from 'Aqila Liability and Special Cases
List of diyah types NOT borne by 'Aqila and miscellaneous issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four cases where the 'Aqila does not bear diyah and state the three miscellaneous rules regarding the 'Aqila of freed slaves, clientage, and the minimum threshold.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila does not bear diyah for intentional killing with shubhah, filicide, slave's acts, confession, or settlement; and only bears amounts above half a tenth of full diyah.

Not all diyah payments fall on the 'Aqila. This lesson covers the exclusions—cases where the killer pays from his own wealth—and includes special rules about the 'Aqila of freed slaves, the patron of clientage, and a minimum threshold for 'Aqila liability.

Guided Path
1
Exclusion 1: Intentional killing with shubhah
If an intentional killing bypasses qisas due to a legal ambiguity (e.g., a son killing his father when the father was attacking), the diyah is on the killer, not the 'Aqila.
The ambiguity (shubhah) prevents retaliation but does not shift liability to the 'Aqila.
2
Exclusion 2: Father kills his son intentionally
If a father intentionally kills his son, the diyah is paid by the father in three years from his own wealth; the 'Aqila does not bear it.
This is because the father is excluded from qisas and also from 'Aqila liability in this case.
3
Exclusion 3: Injury by a slave
The 'Aqila does not bear an injury committed by a slave. The slave's master is responsible.
This is consistent with the earlier point that the 'Aqila bears injury to a slave, not by a slave.
4
Exclusion 4: Confession and compromise
If the killer confesses, the diyah is on him, not the 'Aqila, unless the 'Aqila confirms the confession. Also, diyah agreed upon in a settlement (sulh) is not borne by the 'Aqila.
The 'Aqila is not bound by the killer's admission.
5
Miscellaneous rules
1) The 'Aqila of a freed slave is the tribe of his patron. 2) The client of wala' (mawla al-muwalat) is liable via his patron and tribe. 3) The 'Aqila does not bear amounts less than half a tenth of the full diyah; below that threshold, the injurer pays alone.
The threshold is based on the full diyah (500 dirhams for a free man? Actually full diyah is 1000 dirhams in some riwayah; half tenth is 50 dirhams).
