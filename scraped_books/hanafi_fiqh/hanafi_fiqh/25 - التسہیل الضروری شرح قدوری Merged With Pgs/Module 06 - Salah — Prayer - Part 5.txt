Catching One Rak'ah in Friday Prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a latecomer who catches one rak'ah in Friday prayer and explain the conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Catching one rak'ah in Friday prayer makes it valid as Friday; you complete the missed rak'ah.

This passage gives a clear, concise ruling: if you join the Friday prayer and catch at least one full rak'ah, your prayer is counted as Friday prayer. You then stand up after the imam's salam and pray the remaining rak'ah individually. This ruling is based on the hadith that whoever catches one rak'ah has caught the prayer. Let's break down the legal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage addresses a latecomer (masbuq) who joins the Friday prayer after it has started. The key is whether he catches a full rak'ah with the imam.
Friday prayer is two rak'ahs.
2
State the ruling
If he catches at least one rak'ah, his prayer is valid as Friday prayer. He then completes the missed rak'ah individually after the imam's salam.
This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.
3
Define 'catching a rak'ah'
Catching a rak'ah means joining the imam before or during the ruku' of that rak'ah and performing that ruku' with the imam.
Hanafis define idrak as catching the ruku'.
4
Contrast with catching less
If he catches less than a rak'ah (e.g., joins after ruku' of first rak'ah), his Friday prayer is invalid; he must pray zuhr instead.
This will be covered in the next lesson.

The latecomer's Friday prayer depends on when he joins: Abu Hanifa says any part of the imam's prayer suffices; Muhammad requires most of the second rak'ah.

This lesson examines an internal Hanafi disagreement (ikhtilaf) about a latecomer who arrives after the Friday prayer's second rak'ah is complete. The ruling determines whether he completes two rak'ahs as Friday or four as zuhr. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two adhans for Jumu'ah: one at zawal to cease trade, one before the imam's khutbah.

Friday prayer has a unique structure with two calls to prayer. This lesson clarifies the timing and purpose of each adhan based on the Hanafi madhhab, helping you understand the sequence from zawal to iqamah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
State that the passage is about the number of adhans for Friday prayer: it is two.
2
Timing of the first adhan
Note that the first adhan occurs when the sun passes the meridian (zawal). It signals the time for Friday prayer and the obligation to cease trade.
Zawal marks the beginning of the time for Jumu'ah.
3
Timing of the second adhan
The second adhan is given when the imam ascends and sits on the minbar, just before the khutbah.
This adhan is called 'adhan bayn yadayhi' (before him).
4
Sequence after the adhans
After the second adhan, the imam delivers two sermons (khutbah) with a short pause between them. Then the iqamah is called and the prayer is performed.
5

Summarize that in the Hanafi school, both adhans are sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah), not wajib.

Eid prayer is wajib, not sunnah, for those obligated for Jumu'ah.

Those who are not obligated for Jumu'ah (e.g., women, travelers, sick persons) are not required to attend Eid prayer. However, it is recommended (mustahabb) for them to pray if they wish, but the prayer is not wajib.
This distinction is important for fatwa: a woman or traveler may attend Eid for reward, but she/he is not sinful for missing it.

Manner of Performing Eid Prayer
Detailed description of Eid prayer performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the complete manner of Eid prayer according to the Hanafi school, including the number of takbirs and recitation.

Core Idea
Eid prayer is two rak'ahs with extra takbirs: three in the first after thana', and three in the second after recitation, plus a fourth for ruku without raising hands.

The actual performance of Eid prayer is unique because of the additional takbirs. This lesson breaks down the detailed description provided in the passage, clarifying each step from the imam's takbirat al-ihram to the final taslim. Understanding these details ensures the prayer is performed correctly according to the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the number of rak'ahs and location
The prayer is two rak'ahs, performed in the desert (jabbana) outside the built-up area. The imam leads the congregation.
This is the original sunnah; today many pray in masjids as well.
2
Break down the first rak'ah
After takbirat al-ihram and thana' (subhanaka), three additional takbirs are said audibly with raising hands. Then ta'awwudh and basmalah silently, then Fatiha and a surah aloud, then complete ruku and sujud normally.
The three extra takbirs are wajib according to Hanafi; omitting them intentionally requires sajdah al-sahw.
3
Break down the second rak'ah
Stand up, say basmalah silently, recite Fatiha and a surah aloud, then say three takbirs audibly with raising hands, then a fourth takbir for ruku without raising hands, then complete the rak'ah.
The fourth takbir for ruku is part of the normal ruku transition; it is not additional.
4
Note the rulings on hands and audibility
Hands are raised with each additional takbir. Recitation is audible. The basmalah and ta'awwudh are silent.
This is consistent with Hanafi prayer rules.

Ruling on audibility of takbir on way to Eid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on audibility of takbir for both Eids and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
For Eid al-Adha, takbir is audible; for Eid al-Fitr, Abu Hanifa says silent, Sahibayn say audible.

This lesson examines a key difference within the Hanafi school: the audibility of takbir while walking to the Eid prayer. While all three imams agree on audible takbir for Adha, they differ for Fitr. The passage from al-Quduri and later fatwa works clarifies the relied-upon position.

Later fatwa sources like al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya and al-Ghiyathiyya prefer the position of silence for Fitr, making it the mufta bihi (operative ruling).

The dispute is only about audibility; the act of takbir itself is recommended (mustahabb) on both Eids. The practical consequence is that one may follow either view, but the relied-upon position is silence.

The Ruling and Method of Salat al-Istisqa' (Prayer for Rain) - Hanafi Position

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istisqa' prayer is recommended but not confirmed sunnah; supplication alone suffices.

This passage explains the legal status of the prayer for rain (salat al-istisqa') in the Hanafi school. It answers key questions: Is there a prayer? Is it obligatory? How is it performed? The text clarifies a famous statement of Abu Hanifa and provides the detailed procedure based on the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm
The act is the prayer for rain. The ruling: it is mashru' (prescribed) but not wajib. It is sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah (unconfirmed sunnah).

Locate the dalil
The proof is the Prophet's varying practice: sometimes he prayed the istisqa' prayer, other times he only made du'a on the minbar without prayer. Both are established from him.
This variation indicates the act is not a confirmed sunnah (mu'akkadah).
3
Analyze the proof
The Prophet did not consistently perform the prayer; he sometimes sufficed with du'a. Consistent practice is required for sunnah mu'akkadah. The hadith of Asma' about manumission during eclipse is mentioned as a parallel for specific acts on special occasions.

Praying Over the Grave: Three-Day Limit or Until Decomposition?
Intra-Hanafi dispute over the time limit for praying over the grave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the time limit for praying over the grave and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The later Hanafi view replaces the three-day rule with istihsan: pray until decomposition is probable.

The funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) is normally performed before burial, but if it was missed, can it be performed over the grave? The classical Hanafi matn by al-Quduri set a three-day limit. However, later Hanafi scholars in al-Durr al-Mukhtar refined this ruling using istihsan, removing the fixed limit and tying permissibility to the body's condition. This lesson examines the reasoning and the operative position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Recognize the scenario: a person is buried without the funeral prayer. The legal question is whether and for how long the prayer can be performed over the grave.
2
State the classical Hanafi ruling
Al-Quduri (the matn author) says: prayer is allowed only within three days. This is the initial position in the school.
This is a strict time limit.
3
Recognize the later refinement
In al-Durr al-Mukhtar, the ruling is revised: prayer is allowed out of istihsan as long as decomposition is not probable, without a fixed time limit.
This is considered the more correct (asahh) view.
4
Understand the istihsan basis
The rationale (illah) for permitting the prayer is the integrity of the body for supplication. This varies with climate, physique, and place, so a fixed three-day rule is arbitrary.
Istihsan overrides the original analogy or general rule.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The later view without a fixed time limit is the relied-upon position for fatwa, as transmitted by Ibn Abidin from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.
This reflects the Hanafi process of critical refinement over time.
