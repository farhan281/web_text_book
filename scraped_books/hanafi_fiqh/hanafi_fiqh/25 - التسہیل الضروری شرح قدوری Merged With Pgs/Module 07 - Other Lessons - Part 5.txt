Land Usurpation and Value Dispute in Hanafi Fiqh
Question-answer on ghasb and value dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Usurping land and building transfers ownership to the usurper upon paying its value; value disputes default to the usurper's word with oath.

This lesson examines two related scenarios from Hanafi fiqh: what happens when someone usurps a piece of land and builds on it, and how a dispute over the value of a hidden usurped item is resolved. The rulings illustrate the Hanafi principles of compensatory ownership and the burden of proof in financial disputes.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios
The passage contains two distinct rulings: first, usurping land (saha) and building on it; second, usurping an item and hiding it, then disputing its value.
The land ruling establishes transfer of ownership; the value dispute focuses on procedural evidence.
2
Apply the ruling on land usurpation
For land usurped and built upon: the original owner's ownership ceases entirely, and the usurper must pay the land's value.
This is a strict consequence: after building, the land becomes like a transformed asset.
3

When the parties differ on the value of the hidden item, the usurper's statement is accepted on oath. The owner's statement prevails only if he brings bayyina (two witnesses).
The default favours the usurper because he is the payor and bears the burden.
4

Once the usurper pays the value (whether by his own admission or by court order after evidence), he becomes the owner of the item.
Payment finalizes the transfer; the owner loses all claims to the item.

Liability for Decrease in Usurped Property
Ruling on liability for decrease in value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for decrease in usurped property and identify the basis for strict liability.

Core Idea
The usurper is liable for any decrease in value, regardless of fault, because unlawful possession triggers responsibility.

This passage from the chapter on usurpation (ghasp) establishes a fundamental principle: the person who wrongfully takes another's property (the ghasib) is strictly liable for any reduction in its value that occurs while it is in his custody. This rule does not require proof of negligence or intent—the mere fact of unlawful possession creates the obligation to compensate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the subject
The passage addresses 'the decrease' (al-nuqsan) that happens to 'the usurped property in the hand of the usurper'. First, note that the property is already under wrongful possession (yad al-ghasb).
The act here is not the usurpation itself, but the subsequent decrease.
2
State the ruling
The answer declares: 'upon him is the guarantee of the decrease' (faʿalayhi ḍamān al-nuqṣān). This means the usurper must pay compensation equal to the amount of decrease.
3
Identify the legal cause (ʿilla)
The liability is attached to 'the hand of the usurper'—the unlawful possession itself is the basis. No additional factor (like fault or direct act) is required.
This contrasts with a trustee (ameen) who is not liable for decrease without negligence.
4
Understand the scope
The term 'whatever decreases' (mā naqaṣa) is general—it covers any kind of reduction: partial damage, complete destruction, fall in market value, wear and tear, etc.
The only condition is that the decrease occurs while the property is in the usurper's possession.
5
Apply the principle
If the property is returned and then decreases, the usurper is no longer liable. But if the decrease happened during his possession, he must compensate even if he returns the property afterward.
This shows the temporal boundary: possession triggers liability regardless of later return.

Ownership Transfer by Complete Transformation
Ruling when usurped property is transformed losing its name

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a usurped object is transformed so that its name and main benefits are lost, and identify the condition that the usurper must pay the substitute before using it.

Core Idea
When a usurper changes an object's essence, ownership transfers but use requires compensation.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: what happens if someone usurps another's property and then transforms it so completely that its original name and primary utility are lost? For example, a living sheep becomes roasted meat, or wheat becomes flour. The ruling determines who owns the transformed item and what obligations remain. This principle is crucial for understanding liability and compensation in cases of material alteration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the key condition
The act is usurpation (ghasb) followed by transformation (taghyir). The condition that triggers ownership change is 'zawal al-ism' – the loss of the original name and most benefits of the object.
This condition distinguishes complete transformation from minor changes.
2
Determine the ownership consequence
When the condition is met, ownership of the original owner ceases (yazulu milk) and the usurper becomes the new owner (yamlik al-ghasib).
3
State the restriction on use
Despite owning the transformed object, the usurper is not permitted to benefit from it until he pays its substitute (badal) to the original owner.
The payment is compensation for the loss of the original property.
4
Apply the ruling to the given examples
Examples: sheep slaughtered and cooked → owner loses ownership, usurper owns the meat but must pay for the sheep; wheat ground into flour → similar ruling; iron made into sword → same.
These examples illustrate 'zawal al-ism'.

Ruling on usurped property with added value

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a usurper enhances the property, the owner may choose: take compensation for the original, or keep the item and pay for the enhancement.

This lesson presents a concise ruling from the Hanafi school on a common scenario: when someone usurps another's property and then adds value to it, such as dyeing cloth or mixing food. The law gives the original owner a choice (khiyar) to protect his rights without unjustly enriching either party. Understanding this khiyar is essential for resolving disputes over improved property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is ghasp (usurpation) followed by an addition (dyeing/mixing). The consequence is that the owner has a khiyar (option).
2
Option one: Claim original value
The owner may demand compensation equal to the value of the property before the addition (white cloth, plain saweeq) and surrender the altered property to the usurper.
3
Option two: Take the altered property
The owner may take the altered property but must pay the usurper for the added value (cost of dye, ghee).
4
Understand the rationale
This balances the rights of both parties: the owner does not lose his property, and the usurper is not forced to suffer a loss for his labor/materials if the owner benefits from them.
This khiyar is specific to increases that result from the usurper's own action.

Core Idea
In a value dispute after concealment, the usurper's word with oath is default – only decisive evidence shifts the burden.

This passage addresses a common scenario in property disputes: after an item is usurped and hidden, the parties disagree on its value for compensation. The Hanafi school provides a clear procedural rule that balances the possessor's knowledge and the owner's rights.

Guided Path

Identify the parties and the dispute

2
Apply the default presumption
The default rule is that the usurper's statement regarding value is taken, accompanied by his oath. This is because he is the defendant and in possession of the item.
The burden of proof is on the owner to overcome this default.
3

If the owner provides legal evidence (bayyinah) for a higher value, that evidence prevails over the usurper's statement. The evidence must meet Hanafi standards (e.g., two male witnesses).
The owner becomes the plaintiff when he claims more than the usurper admits.
4
Note the legal consequence
Once the usurper pays the determined value, he becomes the owner of the property (if it later reappears). This transfer of ownership finalizes the transaction.
This prevents future claims from the owner after compensation.

Owner's Option When Concealed Property Reappears

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Owner's option to reclaim property depends on how compensation was determined: only if usurper admitted with oath.

This lesson clarifies a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh where a usurper (ghasib) has compensated the owner for a concealed property that later reappears with a higher value. The key question is whether the owner can choose to void the compensation and take the property back. The answer hinges on how the compensation amount was established.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the property
The property was concealed (mughtayyaba) and the usurper (ghasib) paid compensation to the owner. Then the property reappears with a value greater than the compensation paid.
2
Distinguish the two scenarios
The text presents two categories: (1) compensation determined by the usurper's own statement with oath, and (2) compensation determined by the owner's statement, evidence, or the usurper's refusal to take oath (nukul).
The key difference is who provided the basis for the compensation amount.
3

In scenario 1, the owner has a choice: he may confirm the compensation and keep the property with the usurper, or take back the property and return the compensation. In scenario 2, the owner has no choice; the compensation is final and the property belongs to the usurper.
The ruling is based on the reliability of the evidence: the usurper's own admission binds him, while external proof is conclusive.
