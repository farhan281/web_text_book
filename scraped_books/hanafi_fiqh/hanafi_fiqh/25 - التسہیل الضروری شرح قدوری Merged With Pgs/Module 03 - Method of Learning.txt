Hierarchy of Hanafi Narrations and Inter-Madhhab Influence
Classification of Hanafi narrations and historical origins of other schools

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize Hanafi narrations into Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, and Fatawa/Waqi'at, and explain the historical influence of the Hanafi school on the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah is the backbone of Hanafi fatwa; Nawadir and Fatawa fill gaps with decreasing authority.

This passage from the introduction to a Hanafi fiqh text provides a crucial framework for understanding the sources of Hanafi law. It classifies narrations into three levels of authority and traces how the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools emerged through interaction with Hanafi scholars. Mastering this classification will help you evaluate the strength of different rulings within the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the passage and list the three categories: Zahir al-Riwayah, Nawadir, and Fatawa/Waqi'at. Note their distinguishing features.
Zahir al-Riwayah comes from the six core books of Muhammad al-Shaybani through reliable chains.
2
Understand the authority hierarchy
Zahir al-Riwayah is the most authoritative, being mutawatir or mashhur. Nawadir are weaker but still valid. Fatawa/Waqi'at are later deductions with the lowest authority.
This hierarchy determines which ruling is preferred when there is conflict.
3
Trace the historical links
The passage explains that al-Shafi'i and Ahmad ibn Hanbal's students studied under Hanafi scholars. Identify how this influenced the formation of their schools.
This shows the Hanafi school's role in the development of other madhhabs.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological and historical introduction. It first classifies the types of narrations in the Hanafi school. The primary category is Zahir al-Riwayah—the well-known transmissions from Muhammad al-Shaybani through reliable chains, either mutawatir or mashhur. The second category is Nawadir: rare transmissions that are not in the six core books but appear elsewhere by Muhammad or others. The third is Fatawa and Waqi'at: later jurists' rulings on novel issues not covered by earlier narrations. The passage then discusses how other madhhabs—Hanbali and Shafi'i—were influenced by Hanafi scholars. Imam al-Shafi'i studied with Hanafi companions, combining Hijazi and Iraqi methods, while Imam Ahmad's students also learned from Hanafi scholars, leading to the formation of their own schools.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-known and authoritative narrations transmitted from Muhammad al-Shaybani through trustworthy chains, either mutawatir or mashhur.
النوادر (al-Nawadir): Rare narrations from the early school founders that are not included in the six core books of Muhammad al-Shaybani.
الفتاوى والواقعات (al-Fatawa wa al-Waqi'at): Rulings derived by later mujtahids for new situations not covered by existing narrations from the early school.

Paying Zakat: The Role of Intention and Timing
Method of paying zakat with intention and at the time of payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the method of paying zakat on currency and trade goods, including the required amount and the timing of intention.

Core Idea
Zakat requires a concurrent intention; it can be at payment or when setting the amount aside.

This lesson focuses on the practical method of discharging the obligation of zakat on gold, silver, and trade goods. The passage clarifies the fixed rate of 2.5% and underscores the indispensable role of intention (niyyah). Understanding when and how to form this intention is key to ensuring the zakat is legally valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat on currency and trade goods. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) once conditions are met.
Zakat is a pillar of Islam.
2
Determine the amount
The amount is fixed at one quarter of one tenth (2.5%) of the zakatable wealth.
This rate is universal among all schools.
3
Identify the recipients
The zakat must be given to eligible recipients as defined in Quran 9:60.
Eligibility is detailed elsewhere.
4
Understand the role of intention
Intention must be concurrent either at the time of payment or at the time of setting aside the amount.
Hanafi: both timings are valid.
5
Note the consequence of omission
Without intention, the payment is not considered zakat but may be counted as voluntary charity.
The obligation remains unfulfilled.

Answer: He gives the amount that Allah has made obligatory in his wealth of zakat – which is one quarter of a tenth (1/40) – to those to whom it is permissible to give it, with an intention concurrent with the payment when he pays it, or with an intention concurrent with setting aside the prescribed amount.

Commentary
This brief passage explains the practical method of paying zakat. The amount is fixed at 1/40 of the zakatable wealth (equivalent to 2.5%). This must be given to eligible recipients (as defined in Quran 9:60). The crucial element is intention (niyyah). The intention must be made either at the time of actual payment to the recipient or at the time of setting apart the zakat amount (e.g., putting it in a separate fund). The intention distinguishes zakat from voluntary charity. The Hanafi school emphasizes that the intention should be made at the time of payment or setting aside, and it should be specific that this is the obligatory zakat.

Key Terms
ربع العشر (rub' al-'ushr): One quarter of one tenth = 2.5% = the standard zakat rate.
نية (niyyah): Intention to fulfill the obligation of zakat, distinguishing it from voluntary charity.
