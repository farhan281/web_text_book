Definition and Legal Nature of Waqf
Definition of waqf and intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of waqf and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership during endowment.

Core Idea
In waqf, the asset is detained and its benefit given in charity; ownership divides Abu Hanifa from his two disciples.

Waqf (endowment) is a foundational institution in Islamic law. This lesson unpacks its linguistic and legal definition as presented in the Hanafi tradition, highlighting a critical internal disagreement: does the founder retain ownership until a judge rules, or does ownership immediately transfer to Allah? Understanding this dispute illuminates practical consequences like revocation and sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is waqf (endowment). The text defines it and implies its validity, but the focus is on the nature of ownership, not the ruling's level.
Waqf is a valid (ja'iz) act in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Locate the definition and its elements
The definition has two parts: detaining the asset (habs al-'ayn) and giving the benefit in charity (tasadduq bi al-manfa'ah). The text adds 'or spending its benefit on whomever he wishes' from Fath al-Qadir.
The asset becomes inalienable; only the benefit is used.
3
Distinguish the two positions on ownership
Abu Hanifa holds that the founder retains ownership until a judge rules; after the ruling, ownership ceases. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that ownership transfers to Allah immediately upon declaration.
This is expressed by 'under the rule of the founder's ownership' vs. 'under the rule of the ownership of Allah'.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the waqf is revocable before a judge's ruling. Under the Sahibayn, it is irrevocable immediately. This affects inheritance, sale, and other transactions involving the asset.
The practical consequence (thamarah) is the binding force of the waqf.

Restriction (Hajr) of the Permitted Slave: Conditions and Acknowledgement
Rules on restricted slave's capacity and acknowledgement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that restrict a permitted slave and apply the ruling on his acknowledgement according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A permitted slave becomes restricted by public declaration or specific events; his acknowledgement of debts is valid only according to Abu Hanifa while restricted.

Before defining waqf, the text explains restrictions on the permitted slave ('abd ma'dhun). This lesson covers when a slave loses his trading capacity and the resulting dispute on the validity of his acknowledgements. Understanding these details is crucial for grasping the broader framework of property restrictions in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Locate the scenarios that automatically restrict the permitted slave: master's death, insanity, apostasy, joining enemy territory, slave's absconding, or female slave bearing her master's child. Each triggers restriction (hajr).
Note that the restriction must be made public among market people to be effective.
2
Determine the legal capacity of the restricted slave
Once restricted, the slave loses the capacity to trade freely. However, the text addresses whether his acknowledgement of debts, usurpations, and trusts is valid.
This is a key point: the restricted slave may still have some capacity to acknowledge obligations.
3
Analyse the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on acknowledgement
Abu Hanifa holds that the restricted slave's acknowledgement is valid and binding on the property in his hand. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is not valid. Identify their basis: Abu Hanifa sees the slave's hand as proof of ownership, while the Sahibayn consider the slave untrustworthy after restriction.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: under Abu Hanifa, claimants can recover from the property in the slave's hand; under the Sahibayn, they cannot.
4
Extend to the minor's permission case
The text analogises a minor permitted to trade by his guardian to the permitted slave. If the minor understands trade, his transactions are valid like the slave's. This shows the rule applies beyond slaves.

Definition of Waqf and the Ownership Dispute in Hanafi Law
Definition of waqf and intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership cessation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define waqf according to the Hanafi school and explain the three positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding when the waqif's ownership ceases.

Core Idea
Waqf restrains the substance in the waqif's ownership per Abu Hanifa, but the fatwa follows Abu Yusuf: it is complete by mere utterance.

This lesson examines the Hanafi definition of waqf and the central intra-madhhab dispute on when the waqif's ownership transfers. Understanding these three views is essential for grasping the legal nature of endowments and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Define waqf linguistically and legally
The text gives a two-part definition: linguistically 'restraint', legally 'restraining the substance under the ruling of the waqif's ownership and giving the benefit in charity.' Note that the substance remains owned by the waqif (at least initially).
This definition sets the stage for the dispute on ownership transfer.
2
Identify the wisdom (hikma) of waqf
Waqf is legislated to preserve the asset for continuous benefit and to provide ongoing reward. The hadith on three continuous good deeds is cited as proof.
This shows waqf is a recommended act (sunnah muakkadah).
3
Analyse the three Hanafi positions on ownership cessation
Abu Hanifa: ownership ceases only by judge's ruling or tying to death; the waqf is not complete without a perpetual end. Abu Yusuf: ownership ceases by mere utterance; the waqf is complete immediately. Muhammad: ownership ceases only after delivery to a trustee; waqf must have perpetual end.
Each view has different implications for revocability.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position and its practical effect
The text states the fatwa is on Abu Yusuf's view. This means in practice, waqf is considered binding upon declaration, and the waqif cannot revoke it.
Even though other views exist, the operative ruling for fatwa is Abu Yusuf's.

When Does the Waqif's Ownership Cease? The Three Hanafi Views
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waiver of ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions on when the waqif's ownership ceases and identify the mufta bihi position with its legal consequence.

Core Idea
The waqif's ownership ends by judge's decree, immediate speech, or delivery — only one is the fatwa.

The moment a waqf becomes binding is not simply a matter of uttering words. In the Hanafi school, a classic dispute between the three founding imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani — turns on when exactly the waqif's ownership of the endowed property is severed. Understanding these differences is key to knowing when the waqf is irrevocable and how to structure practical endowments.

Guided Path
1
State the three positions
Identify each imam's view on when the waqif's ownership of the endowed property ceases: Abu Hanifa requires a judge's decree or a will-like declaration; Abu Yusuf requires only the words of waqf; Muhammad requires appointment of a trustee and delivery.
These are all recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand each imam's condition
Delve into the precise condition each imam sets for ownership to cease: for Abu Hanifa, either a judge's ruling (hukm al-hakim) or a declaration contingent on death (ta'liq bi al-mawt); for Abu Yusuf, just the utterance of the waqf formula; for Muhammad, the appointment of a trustee (wali) and physical delivery (taslim).
Abu Hanifa's caution stems from the idea that ownership is a strong right not easily severed.
3
Identify the mufta bihi position
Recognize that the relied-upon position for fatwa is that of Abu Yusuf: ownership ceases immediately upon uttering the words of waqf. This is stated in the marginal note: 'وعليه الفتوى'.
Later scholars like al-Marghinani and Ibn Abidin confirm this tarjih.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider the practical difference: if ownership is not yet severed, the waqif could potentially revoke the waqf or deal with the property. Under Abu Yusuf's view, the waqf becomes binding instantly, preventing the waqif from changing his mind.
This has implications for the validity of subsequent transactions involving the property.
5
Compare with other schools
Note that the Shafi'i school typically requires delivery (qabd) for the waqf to be binding, similar to Muhammad's view, while the Hanafi fatwa makes it easier to establish a waqf.
This difference affects how endowments are documented and enforced.

The Perpetuity Condition in Waqf: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra‑Hanafi dispute on the perpetuity condition for waqf completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the perpetuity condition for waqf completion and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf validates a waqf even if its named beneficiaries expire, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require an unending ultimate purpose.

This passage dives into an internal Hanafi debate about whether a waqf must be perpetual to be complete. The three founders of the school — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani — hold different views. Your task is to understand each position, the evidence behind it, and which view is relied upon for fatwa today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
The core question is whether a waqf must end in a 'direction that never ceases' (jihah la tanqati') for it to be complete (tamam). This determines the validity of a waqf that names only a finite group (e.g., my children) without specifying what happens after they expire.
The condition is not about the initial beneficiaries but about the ultimate destination.
2
State the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad)
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the waqf is incomplete unless its final destination is a perpetual cause, such as the poor or a public good. They require the waqif to say something like '…then after them to the poor'.
3
State Abu Yusuf's minority view
Abu Yusuf holds that even if the waqif names a terminating group without mentioning a later perpetual cause, the waqf is valid. After the named group expires, the proceeds automatically go to the poor.
Abu Yusuf bases this on the idea that the poor are always present and are implied.
4
Examine the evidence (dalil) for each position
The text does not give detailed proofs but mentions Abu Yusuf's reasoning: waqf is like manumission (it becomes effective by mere utterance). Muhammad requires delivery to the trustee because it is a right of Allah. Abu Hanifa adds that a judge's decree or a will‑like declaration is needed for it to be binding.
The tarjih (preference) in the school has shifted to Abu Yusuf's view for fatwa.
5
Determine the thamarah al‑khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa/Muhammad, a waqf that says only 'for my children' without more is incomplete and does not become binding. Under Abu Yusuf, it is immediately binding and after the children die it goes to the poor. The fatwa today follows Abu Yusuf, which makes such limited‑term waqfs valid and enforceable.

The Hanafi school permits waqf with conditions that seem to contradict the full transfer of ownership, but the jurists debated whether the waqif may retain personal benefit or control. This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on two key conditions: reserving the income (ghalla) for the waqif, and retaining the trusteeship (wilayah). The later preference (tarjih) for Abu Yusuf's view is based on encouraging charitable endowments.

Identify the two conditions

These are independent issues, though they share a common dispute framework.

Distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad

Waqf Conditions: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Stipulations
Intra-Hanafi dispute on waqf condition validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the validity of conditions in waqf and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A condition in waqf is valid per Abu Yusuf but invalid per Muhammad; fatwa favors Abu Yusuf to encourage endowments.

When a person establishes a waqf (endowment), they often wish to impose conditions on how it is managed or who benefits. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement about whether such conditions are legally valid. This passage presents the views of Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Hilal, and explains which view is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disputed issues
The passage discusses two separate conditions: (1) any condition stipulated by the waqif at the time of endowment, and (2) specifically the condition that the waqif himself be the administrator (mutawalli). Distinguish between them.
The second issue is a particular application of the first, but has its own transmission from Hilal.
2
State the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
For both issues, Abu Yusuf holds that the condition (and the waqf) are valid. Muhammad holds that both are invalid. For the second issue, Hilal initially agrees with Abu Yusuf but later is reported to side with Muhammad.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: all three are Hanafi imams.
3
Understand the basis of the dispute
Some say the difference is rooted in whether possession and segregation (qabd wa ifraz) are required for the waqf to be complete. Others treat it as an independent issue. The exact illa is not given, but the difference affects enforcement.
The passage does not elaborate on proofs; the focus is on the ruling and its tarjih.
4
Note the tarjih (preference) and its rationale
Al-Sadr al-Shahid, the scholars of Balkh, and al-Hidayah all prefer Abu Yusuf's view. The fatwa is given accordingly 'to encourage people to endow' (targhiban li'l-nas fi al-waqf). This is a public interest consideration.
Tarjih is based on a policy (siyasa) of encouraging charitable endowments, not purely on textual strength.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
In practice, if a waqif stipulates any condition (e.g., that his son be the mutawalli, or that the proceeds be given to a specific cause), both the waqf and the condition are valid and enforceable according to the mufta bihi position.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of conditions stipulated by the waqif (endower) in a waqf (endowment). The first line states the core ruling: Abu Yusuf considers such conditions permissible, while Imam Muhammad considers them impermissible. Here, Abu Yusuf holds that both the waqf and the condition are valid, while Muhammad and Hilal hold both are invalid. The text notes a conflicting transmission from Hilal. The passage concludes that the relied-upon position is Abu Yusuf's view to encourage endowments.
