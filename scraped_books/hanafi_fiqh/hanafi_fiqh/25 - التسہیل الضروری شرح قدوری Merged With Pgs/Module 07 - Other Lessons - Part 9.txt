Conditions for Halal Hunting with Trained Animals
Definition and conditions for halal hunting with trained animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for halal hunting with trained animals according to the Hanafi school and apply them to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Hunting with trained animals is halal only if the animal is trained, the hunter says Bismillah, and the prey dies from the wound.

Hunting is a means of obtaining food, but Islamic law imposes specific conditions to ensure that the meat is halal. This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings on hunting with trained animals, based on Quranic verses and classical fiqh texts. You will learn what qualifies as a valid hunting animal, what must be said at release, and why wounding is essential.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of hunting
The passage defines hunting as the act of catching wild, untameable animals, whether edible or not. It also clarifies that hunting is permissible only for those not in ihram and outside the Haram.
This sets the scope: only non-owned wild animals are considered game.
2
Identify the permissible tools
The permissible tools are trained hunting animals (jawaarih mu'allamah), specifically mentioned as dogs, cheetahs, falcons, and hawks. The Quranic verse (5:4) is cited as the primary proof.
The Hanafi school generalizes from 'mukallibeen' to all trained predators and birds of prey.
3
Extract the conditions for halal consumption
The text lists: (1) the hunter must mention Allah's name when sending the animal, (2) the trained animal must wound the game, and (3) the game must die from that wound. If the animal is caught alive, proper slaughter (idhaka) is required.
The condition of wounding is emphasized; if the animal kills by suffocation or other means, the meat is not halal.
4
Note the exception for other methods
The passage quotes al-Hidayah: only predators with fangs and birds with talons are considered valid hunting animals. For any other tool or method, the game must be properly slaughtered if caught alive.
This restricts the ruling to the specified types of trained animals.
5
Review the Quranic evidence
Two verses from Surah al-Ma'idah are used: verse 2 permits hunting after ihram, and verse 4 explicitly permits using trained hunting animals. The term 'mukallibeen' is explained to mean training for hunting, covering all trained jawaarih.
The proof is definitive (qati' al-thubut) and clear in its indication (qati' al-dalalah).

Book of Hunting and Slaughter

Rulings of Hunting

Question: What is hunting?

Book of Hunting and Slaughter

Answer: Hunting is the act of hunting, and it is also applied to the hunted animal, which is the wild animal that is untameable by humans, whether edible or inedible. Hunting a wild animal that is not owned is permissible for one not in ihram and outside the Haram (sanctuary). Allah Almighty said: 'And when you have completed the rites, then hunt.' (Al-Ma'idah: 2)

Question: If a Muslim wants to hunt, how does he hunt?

Answer: It is permissible to hunt with a trained dog, a trained cheetah, a trained falcon, and all trained hunting animals (jawaarih). Allah Almighty said: 'Say, good things are made lawful for you, and what you have taught of hunting animals, training them as hounds, teaching them from what Allah has taught you.' (Al-Ma'idah: 4)

Question: If the hunter sends the trained dog or trained cheetah and it kills the game, is it permissible to eat it?

Answer: It is permissible to eat the animal whose consumption is allowed, if the hunter mentioned the name of Allah when sending the trained hunting animals. So if the trained animal takes the game, wounds it, and it dies, it is halal. Similarly, if he sends a trained falcon or trained hawk and mentions the name of Allah, and it takes a bird, wounds it, and it dies, its consumption is halal.

Question: Why did you restrict it to wounding and death?

Answer (in the text): 'And everything you have taught of predators that have fangs, and birds that have talons, there is no harm in hunting with it. And there is no good in anything other than that, unless you reach its proper slaughter (idhaka).' Thus in al-Hidayah from al-Jami al-Saghir.

'From what Allah has taught you': al-Zayla'i said in Sharh al-Kanz (6/50): 'al-mukallib' is the trained dog, then generalised to every trained hunting animal, whether a beast or a bird. The meaning of His saying 'mukallibeen' is training for hunting, and 'tu'allimoonahunna' is that you discipline them, so it includes all that is taught of hunting animals.

Definition and Ruling of Hunting (Sayd)
Defines sayd and states basic ruling of permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sayd (hunting) and state its ruling of permissibility with its conditions and evidence.

Core Idea
Hunting wild, unowned animals is permitted except for those in ihram or within the Haram.

This opening passage from the chapter on hunting (sayd) clarifies both the linguistic and legal meaning of the term. It then lays down the foundational ruling: hunting is mubah for a non-muhrim outside the Haram, supported by a clear Quranic imperative. Understanding this definition and its immediate conditions is the first step in mastering the detailed rules of hunting in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of sayd
Read the text's answer to 'what is hunting?' Note that it mentions both the act (al-istiyad) and the object (al-musayyad). The object is defined as a wild animal that resists human capture, whether edible or not.
This dual definition is important because rules may differ for the act vs. the animal.
2
Distinguish the two meanings
Separate the verbal noun (act of hunting) from the passive participle (the hunted animal). In fiqh, sayd can refer to either depending on context.
For example, the prohibition on a muhrim applies to both the act and the consumption of the hunted animal.
3
Extract the ruling and its conditions
The text states: hunting wild, unowned animals is mubah (permissible) for a non-muhrim and outside the Haram. Note the two restrictions: (1) not in ihram, (2) not in the sanctuary of Makkah.
The converse: for a muhrim or inside the Haram, it is haram.
4
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The text cites Quran 5:2: 'And when you are free from ihram, then hunt.' This imperative (fasṭādū) indicates permissibility after the prohibition of ihram is lifted.
In Hanafi usul, the imperative generally indicates obligation, but here it is interpreted as permission because the context shows it follows a prohibition.

The Three Conditions for Halal Prey from Trained Animals
States the conditions for halal prey from trained animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions required for prey caught by a trained animal to be halal, and identify when the prey becomes haram.

Core Idea
Three conditions: tasmiyah, wounding, death by wound—missing any makes the prey haram.

Hunting with trained animals is a legitimate means of attaining food, permitted by the Quran. The Hanafi school, however, insists on precise conditions to ensure the animal dies in a manner analogous to proper slaughter. Without meeting these conditions, the meat is unlawful.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling

(1) The hunter must utter tasmiyah (Bismillah) when releasing the animal. (2) The trained animal must wound the prey (i.e., cause a bleeding wound). (3) The prey must die as a result of that wound.
3

The same conditions apply to both four-legged beasts of prey and birds of prey, as the text explicitly extends the ruling from dogs and cheetahs to falcons and hawks.
4
Note the exception: caught alive
If the prey is caught but not killed (e.g., captured alive), it must be slaughtered in the normal manner. This is implied by the statement 'unless you are able to slaughter it'.

The Hanafi Proof and Generality of 'Mukallibin'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Quranic basis for hunting with trained animals and describe how the Hanafi school generalizes the term 'mukallibin' to all trained predators.

Core Idea
Quran 5:4 permits hunting with trained 'jawarih'; 'mukallibin' generalizes to all trained beasts and birds.

The primary evidence for the permissibility of hunting with trained animals is the Quranic verse in Surah al-Ma'idah. This lesson explores how the Hanafi school interprets the term 'mukallibin' (trainers of dogs) as a general term covering all trained predators, citing al-Hidayah and al-Zaylai for support.

Guided Path

Identify the primary dalil
The Quranic verse (5:4) is implicitly cited as the basis. The text refers to 'mukallibin' which is the key term.

Analyze the linguistic expansion
Al-Zaylai explains that 'mukallibin' originally meant 'dog-trainers' but the verse generalizes to all trained jawarih, as the verb 'tu'allimunahunna' (you teach them) indicates the training, not the animal species.
3
Confirm with the hadith/authority
The citation from al-Hidayah states that any trained beast with fangs or bird with claws is permissible. This is the practical application of the general ruling.
4
Understand the exception
If the animal is not trained (e.g., wild dogs or untrained birds), their hunting is not permissible unless you can slaughter the prey properly. This is the meaning of 'no good in other than that unless you are able to slaughter it'.

Ruling on Hunting with a Pellet (Bunduq)
Q&A ruling on hunting with a pellet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hunting with a pellet (bunduq) and explain the exception if the animal is found alive.

Core Idea
Killing by blunt force from a pellet is not a valid hunt; the animal is carrion unless slaughtered while alive.

This lesson addresses another common hunting tool: the pellet or slingshot. The Hanafi school distinguishes between cutting implements and blunt force instruments. Understanding this ruling prevents consuming improperly killed game.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states: if a hunter hits an animal with a pellet (bunduq) and it dies from the hit, it is not eaten. The pellet kills by blunt force, not by cutting.
2
Note the exception
If the hunter finds the animal alive after the hit, then he can slaughter it (dhabh), and it becomes halal. This shows that the pellet itself does not render the animal halal; only proper slaughter does.
3
Apply the reasoning
The pellet is like a blunt object; it causes death by mawqudhah (stunned/bludgeoned), not by dhabh (slaughter). Hanafis require the implement to be sharp and capable of cutting for a valid hunt.

It is a ruling-statement passage, as it gives the hukm without quoting proofs, though later commentary explains the reasoning. The first ruling: if an arrow hits game with its side (the broad part, not the tip) but does not wound it, the animal is not lawful to eat. The animal must be wounded for the arrow's hit to be considered a valid hunt. Second: hunting with a pellet (bunduq) is not valid if it kills directly; the animal is carrion. However, if the hunter finds it alive and slaughters it, it becomes halal. This is because a pellet kills by blunt force, not by cutting the throat, so it is not a proper slaughter. Third: if two hunters shoot the same animal, the first does not fatally wound it (does not take it out of its ability to escape), and the second kills it, the game belongs to the second and is eaten. The first hunter acquires no ownership because his shot did not render the animal helpless. Fourth: if the first hunter severely wounds the animal (so it is no longer able to escape), and the second kills it, the game belongs to the first but is not eaten (it is carrion). The second hunter is liable to the first for the value of the animal, minus the damage from the first wound. The reason the animal is not eaten is that the second did not perform a valid slaughter, as the animal was already overpowered (first hunter could have properly slaughtered it). Fifth: it is permissible to hunt animals that are not edible meat, for their skin, feathers, or to repel their harm. Sixth: the hunting of a person in ihram (Hajj/Umrah state), a Magian, an apostate, or an idol worshiper is not eaten even if they mention Allah's name. However, the hunting of a Muslim or a Kitabi (Jew or Christian) is permissible if they mention Allah's name at the time of releasing the arrow or animal. The commentary then explains terms: 'muthkhin' means inflicting severe injury; the reasoning for the ruling on two hunters is given: the second is considered the actual hunter if the first did not disable the animal; if the first did disable it, the second's act is not a valid slaughter because it was done after the animal was already in the first's possession; the first's ownership is established by wounding; and the ruling on non-edible animals is that they may be hunted for other benefits.

Conditions and Required Veins for Valid Slaughter (Dhabh)
Rulings including conditions, required veins, and tasmiyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for halal dhabh, apply the required minimum vein cut according to the ruling of Sahibayn, and distinguish between intentional and forgetful omission of tasmiyah.

Core Idea
Not all four veins are required; cutting trachea, esophagus, and one jugular suffices according to Sahibayn.

This passage from a classical Hanafi manual presents the foundational rulings of Islamic slaughter (dhabh). It defines the act, lists the conditions for the meat to be lawful, and addresses two critical issues: how many veins must be cut, and the requirement of mentioning Allah's name. A disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Sahibayn) on the minimum veins is resolved in fatwa on the side of the disciples.

Guided Path
1
Define dhabh
Identify the legal definition: cutting the veins in the specific area between the throat and the base of the lower jaw.
The area is defined as 'between the throat (labbah) and the base of the lower jaw (lahyayn).'
2
List the three essential conditions
From the passage: the animal must be of edible meat, the slaughterer must be Muslim or Kitabi, and the tasmiyah (mention of Allah's name) must be uttered.
These are prerequisites for the meat to be halal.
3
Identify the four veins and the dispute on minimum requirement
The four veins are trachea (halqum), esophagus (mari'), and two jugulars (wadajan). Abu Hanifa holds that cutting 'most of them' suffices; Sahibayn require at least trachea, esophagus, and one jugular.
The mufta bihi (relying upon) is the Sahibayn's view.
4
Analyze the tasmiyah ruling
Intentional omission makes the animal carrion; forgetful omission is excused and the meat is halal.
This applies equally to Muslim and Kitabi slaughterers.
5
Examine the proof for Kitabi slaughter
The Quranic verse (5:5) is cited: 'The food of those who were given the Scripture is lawful for you.' This includes their slaughtered meat, provided they do not invoke other than Allah.
The proof is a primary textual evidence (dalil naqli).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Hanafi fatwa: udhiyah is mustahabb for self, not wajib for child – zahir al-riwayah governs over nawadir.

This passage from Ibn Abidin's Hashiyah clarifies an intra-Hanafi dispute about the obligatory nature of udhiyah (sacrifice). Al-Quduri had stated that udhiyah is for oneself and one's young child, but later Hanafi jurists did not adopt this. The key question: is it wajib or mustahabb? And does it apply to one's child? By tracing the reasoning, we see the hierarchy of authority within the school: the core transmitted corpus (zahir al-riwayah) is given precedence over rare transmissions (nawadir). The fatwa is that udhiyah is mustahabb for oneself and not required for the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is udhiyah (sacrifice). The first issue is whether it is wajib or mustahabb for oneself. The second is whether it is required for one's young child.
The rules for udhiyah involve conditions like being free, Muslim, resident, and having the means (the affluence of fitr).
2
State the two conflicting views
View 1 (zahir al-riwayah): udhiyah is mustahabb, not wajib, and not required for the child. View 2 (al-Hasan's narration from Abu Hanifa): it is wajib and must be performed for one's child and orphaned grandchild.
Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad's narration is considered nawadir (rare transmissions), not part of the six core books.
3
Identify the tarjih criterion
The Hanafi school gives priority to zahir al-riwayah (the established corpus of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) over nawadir. The fatwa is based on this hierarchy.
This is a key principle of Hanafi fatwa: the six core books (Zahir al-Riwayah) are the primary source.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if mustahabb, omitting is not sinful; if wajib, omitting is sinful and requires compensation. Also, if wajib for the child, the guardian must sacrifice from the child's wealth.
This difference affects financial responsibility and sin.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The operative ruling (mufta bihi) is that udhiyah is mustahabb for oneself, not wajib, and not required for one's child. This is the view relied upon for fatwa today.
Al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Ibn Abidin affirm this.

قال صاحب "الجوهرة" ناقلا عن "الينابيع": حتى إنه إذا حلف بطلاق امرأته وقال إن لم آت البصرة فهي طالق، فلم يأتها حتى مات فلا ميراث لها إذا لم يدخل بها، ولا عدة عليها، وإن كان قد دخل بها فلها الميراث وعليها العدة أبعد الأجلين بمنزلة الفار، ولو ماتت هي لم تطلق؛ لأن شرط البر لم يتعذر بموتها. وهذه المسألة فرع كونه حانثا في آخر جزء من أجزاء حياته، فافهم.

قال في "البحر الرائق" (٣٣٨/٤): ولا خصوصية للإتيان، بل كل فعل حلف أنه يفعله في المستقبل وأطلقه، ولم يقيده بوقت، لم يحنث حتى يقع الإياس عن البر إلى أن (قال وقيد باليمين المطلقة؛ لأنها لو كانت مقيدة كقوله : إن لم أدخل هذه الدار اليوم فعبده حر، فإن الحنث معلق بآخر الوقت حتى إذا مات الحالف قبل خروج الوقت، ولم يدخل الدار، لا يحنث، وأما إذا مضى الوقت قبل دخوله، وهو حي عتق العبد، كذا في "غاية البيان".
Question: If a disbeliever takes an oath and then breaks it while still a disbeliever or after embracing Islam, is expiation obligatory? Answer: His oath is not considered a valid oath in the noble Sharia, so nothing is obligatory upon him if he breaks it.

Question: A man may swear an oath to do something impossible, for example saying, 'I will ascend the sky' or 'I will turn this stone into gold.' Does his oath become valid, and when is he judged to have broken it? Answer: His oath becomes valid in this case, and he breaks it immediately after the oath.

Question: He swore he would go to Basra, for example, but did not go until he died. When is he judged to have broken the oath? Answer: He is judged to have broken it in the last part of his life.

Breaking: Because he is not qualified for the oath; it is made to glorify Allah, and with disbelief he is not glorifying, nor is he qualified for expiation because it is worship. - al-Hidayah

'In the last part of his life' because the possibility of fulfilling the oath before that is hoped for, from al-Jawharah. This is the difference between this case and the previous one, i.e., the oath to ascend the sky or turn the stone into gold; there, fulfillment is not hoped for, so he breaks immediately. The author of al-Mukhtar said: In the case of 'I will ascend the sky' or 'I will turn this stone into gold,' he breaks immediately because fulfillment is possible in reality, but he breaks due to inability in custom.

The author of al-Jawharah, narrating from al-Yanabi', said: Even if he swears by the divorce of his wife and says, 'If I do not come to Basra, then she is divorced,' and he does not come until he dies, then she has no inheritance if he had not consummated the marriage, and no waiting period; if he had consummated, she inherits and observes the waiting period of the longer of the two periods, like a fleeing husband. If she dies, she is not divorced because the condition of fulfillment is not precluded by her death. This case is a branch of him being a breaker in the last part of his life; understand.

He said in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (4/338): There is no specificity to 'coming' [to Basra]; rather, any action he swears he will do in the future and leaves it unrestricted, not binding it to a time, he does not break until despair of fulfillment occurs. He restricted it to the unrestricted oath; because if it were restricted, like his saying, 'If I do not enter this house today, my slave is free,' then the breaking is tied to the end of the time, so if the oath-taker dies before the time expires and has not entered the house, he does not break. But if the time passes before he enters while he is alive, the slave is freed. This is from Ghayat al-Bayan.

Commentary
This passage presents three distinct rulings on oaths. First, an oath taken by a disbeliever is not valid in Islamic law ('Sharia'), because an oath is an act of glorifying Allah, and the disbeliever lacks the requisite belief. Consequently, breaking such an oath does not necessitate expiation (kaffarah), whether the violation occurs during disbelief or after embracing Islam. The second ruling addresses oaths to perform impossible acts, such as ascending the sky or turning a stone into gold. According to the Hanafi school, such an oath is validly concluded, but the person is deemed to have broken it immediately upon swearing, because fulfillment is impossible by custom. The third ruling concerns oaths to perform a future act without specifying a time, like swearing to go to Basra. In this case, the oath is not broken until the last moment of the oath-taker's life if he dies without fulfilling it, because before death there remains hope (raja') of compliance. This distinguishes it from the impossible act. The author then elaborates with a practical example: if a man swears by his wife's divorce that if he does not go to Basra, she is divorced, and he dies before going, the wife's inheritance and waiting period depend on whether the marriage was consummated. If the husband had not consummated, she neither inherits nor observes iddah; if consummated, she inherits and observes the iddah of a farrah (a woman whose husband has died or abandoned her). If the wife dies first, the divorce does not occur, as the condition of non-fulfillment was not precluded. Finally, a quote from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq generalizes the principle: for any unrestricted future act, breaking occurs only at despair of fulfillment, while time-bound oaths break at the end of the specified time.

Oaths Not to Speak: Speech and Time Interpretation
Rulings on oaths regarding speech and time words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between speech that breaks an oath not to speak and speech that does not, and apply Hanafi rules for interpreting indefinite and definite time expressions in oaths.

The question: He swore that he would not eat from this wheat, then he ate from its bread, does he thereby incur the oath? The answer: He does not incur it in this case according to Abu Hanifa, may he be pleased with him; rather he incurs it if he bites and eats it (the grain). And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: If he eats from its bread, he incurs the oath as well, because it is understood customarily. And for Abu Hanifa (the attribute?) that it has an actual used meaning, for it (the grain) is boiled, fried, and eaten by biting, and this (literal meaning) overrides the customary metaphor according to his principle. And if he bites them both, he incurs it according to both of them, which is the sound opinion due to the generality of the metaphor. From 'Al-Hidayah'.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding an oath not to eat from a specific quantity of wheat (al-ḥinṭa). The question is whether eating bread made from that wheat breaks the oath. According to Abu Hanifa, it does not; the oath is only broken if the raw grains are consumed by biting (qadam). This is because the literal reality (ḥaqīqa) of wheat is that it is a grain that can be boiled, fried, or eaten raw by biting, and this literal meaning takes precedence over the customary metaphor (majāz ‘urfī) of calling bread made from wheat 'eating wheat'. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Al-Shaybani) hold that eating the bread does break the oath because customarily, when one swears not to eat wheat, it is understood to include its products like bread. They argue that the metaphor is so common that it overrides the literal meaning. The text also notes that if the swearer bites the grains (both before and after processing?), he incurs the oath according to both. The muftā bihī (relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school, as per Al-Hidayah and other later works, is the view of Abu Hanifa, giving precedence to the literal meaning over custom in oaths due to the strict interpretation required. However, the text here states that the sound opinion (al-ṣaḥīḥ) is the view of the two companions for biting? There is ambiguity; the main point is the dispute.

Key Terms
الحنطة (al-ḥinṭa): Wheat, specifically the grain in its raw form.
القضم (al-qaḍm): To bite or chew, especially of raw grains.
