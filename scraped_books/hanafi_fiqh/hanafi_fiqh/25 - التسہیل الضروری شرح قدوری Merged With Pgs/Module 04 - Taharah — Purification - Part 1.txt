Definition and Categories of Taharah
Definition and categorization of taharah in Q&A format.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of taharah and distinguish between major and minor hadath.

Core Idea
Taharah is not just cleanliness but a legal condition for prayer requiring removal of hadath and najasah.

This passage opens the Book of Purification in the Hanafi legal manual by defining taharah and its two categories. The author uses a question-and-answer format to clarify why purification is placed first: because it is a condition for prayer, which is the second pillar of Islam. The definition covers both removal of ritual impurity (hadath) and physical impurities (najasah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the rationale for beginning with purification
Note that the passage explains why fiqh books start with purification: it is a condition (shart) for the validity of prayer, which is the second pillar of Islam.
This emphasizes the structural importance of taharah in Islamic jurisprudence.
2
Parse the legal definition of taharah
The legal definition has two components: removal of hadath (minor and major) and removal of najasah (impurities) from clothing, body, and place of prayer. Distinguish these from the linguistic meaning of cleanliness.
Linguistic cleanliness alone is insufficient for worship; legal purity is required.
3
Differentiate major and minor hadath
Major hadath requires full-body ghusl; minor hadath requires washing and wiping specific limbs (wudu). The text uses the verb 'iftarada' (to obligate) to indicate that these acts become obligatory when prayer is intended.
'Iftarada' signals a legal obligation (fard).
4
Memorize the first two obligatory parts of wudu
The passage lists the face (from hairline to chin, ear to ear) and the hands up to the elbows as obligatory to wash. This begins the detailed description of wudu.
These are fixed boundaries; later lessons will complete the list.

Definition of Purification and Obligatory Acts of Wudu
Introduces purification and states fard acts of wudu with textual evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of taharah, list the four fard acts of wudu, and explain the Quranic and hadith evidence for each.

Core Idea
Wudu has four fard acts: washing face, hands, feet, and wiping a quarter of the head; the rest is sunnah.

This passage from the sharh of al-Quduri opens the Book of Purification by first explaining why fiqh books begin with purification: it is a condition for prayer, the second pillar of Islam. It then defines purification both linguistically and legally, clarifying the types of ritual impurity (hadath) and the required actions. The core of the lesson is the enumeration of the obligatory (fard) acts of wudu, supported by a clear Quranic verse and a hadith that establishes the Hanafi position on wiping the head. By the end, you will have a precise understanding of what makes wudu valid according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Define purification legally
State the Hanafi legal definition of taharah, which covers removal of both ritual impurities (hadath) and physical impurities (najasah) from body, garments, and place of prayer.
Linguistically, taharah means cleanliness.
2
Distinguish between hadath akbar and asghar
Identify the two types of ritual impurity: major (janabah) requires full-body wash (ghusl); minor requires wudu (washing specific limbs and wiping the head).
The passage also explains the terminology differences: ghusl (damma) vs. wudu (fatha).
3
List the four fard acts of wudu
Enumerate from the passage: washing the face, washing the hands including the elbows, washing the feet including the ankles, and wiping the head (at least a quarter).
The passage emphasizes that missing even a spot the size of a hair invalidates wudu.
4
Analyze the Quranic proof
Locate the verse cited (Surah al-Ma'idah: 6) and identify the imperative verbs (fā'ghsilū, imsahū) that indicate obligation per Hanafi usul.
The verse mentions washing face and hands, wiping head, and washing feet.
5
Examine the hadith evidence for the head wipe
Understand how the hadith of al-Mughirah (wiping the forelock) supports that only a part of the head needs to be wiped for the obligation, distinguishing Hanafi from Shafi'i.
The hadith is used to interpret the Quranic 'wipe your heads' as a partial wipe.

Question: What is the meaning of purification linguistically and legally? Answer: Purification linguistically is cleanliness. Legally: it is removal of minor and major hadath, and removal of impurities from garments, bodies, and places of prayer. Question: What is major hadath? Answer: If washing the entire body is obligated upon a man or woman, that is major hadath; when washed, that hadath is removed, and this is called ghusl (with damma on ghayn) and ightisal. Question: What is minor hadath? Answer: If washing some limbs and wiping some is obligated upon a man or woman for the sake of performing prayer, that is minor hadath; when the hadath is removed from those limbs, purification is obtained thereby, and this is called ghasl (with fatha on ghayn) and wudu, and the man is mutawaddi'. Question: Explain those limbs that must be washed or wiped in wudu? Answer: They are: 1- The face from the hairline to the bottom of the chin, and from the right earlobe to the left earlobe. 2- The hands from the tips of the fingers to the elbows. 3- The feet from the tips of the toes to the ankles. So these limbs are washed in wudu, and a man is not mutawaddi' unless he washes all of them, and if a spot the size of a hair remains that water has not reached, he is not mutawaddi' until he washes that spot with water. 4- The wiped limb is the head; its complete coverage is sunnah. And for the fard, wiping a quarter of it suffices. Question: Are the ankles and elbows included in the washing? Answer: Yes, they are included in the fard of wudu. Question: From where did you learn the obligation of washing the three limbs and the obligation of wiping the head in wudu? Answer: Allah the Exalted explained them in His Book, saying: 'O you who have believed, when you rise to pray, wash your faces and your hands to the elbows, and wipe over your heads and [wash] your feet to the ankles' [al-Ma'idah: 1?]. Question: What is the evidence for the obligation of wiping a quarter of the head? Answer: The evidence for that is the hadith of al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah, may Allah be pleased with him, who said: 'The Prophet, peace be upon him, performed wudu and wiped over his forelock.' Question: What are the sunan of wudu? Answer: 1- Intention: he intends to remove hadath or to permit what is not permissible except with wudu. 2- Washing the hands up to the wrists three times at the beginning of wudu, and it is emphasized to wash them if... his forelock: narrated by Muslim [?]

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of al-Quduri opens the Book of Purification. It begins with a question about why fiqh books start with purification: because prayer—the second pillar of Islam—requires purification as a condition. The author then defines purification (taharah) linguistically as cleanliness and legally as the removal of both major and minor hadath (ritual impurity) and the removal of physical impurities (najasah) from garments, bodies, and places of prayer. Major hadath (janabah) requires a full-body wash (ghusl), while minor hadath (hadath asghar) requires washing specific limbs and wiping the head (wudu). The text then enumerates the obligatory (fard) acts of wudu: washing the face (from hairline to chin and between ears), washing both hands including elbows, washing both feet including ankles, and wiping the head (a quarter of the head suffices for the fard according to the Hanafi school). The author explicitly states that the ankles and elbows are included in the washing fard. The proof for these obligations is the Quranic verse (Surah al-Ma'idah: 6), and for wiping a quarter of the head, the hadith of al-Mughirah where the Prophet wiped his forelock. Finally, the text begins listing the sunan of wudu: intention (niyyah) and washing the hands to the wrists thrice at the start. The passage emphasizes that if even a spot the size of a hair is left unwashed, wudu is incomplete.

Key Terms
الطهارة (al-ṭahārah): In this passage, taharah is defined legally as removal of both ritual impurities (hadath asghar and akbar) and physical impurities (najasah) from body, garments, and place of prayer.
الحدث (al-ḥadath): Ritual impurity requiring ablution; major (akbar) requires ghusl, minor (asghar) requires wudu.
الوضوء (al-wuḍūʾ): Washing specified limbs with water for minor impurity; includes fard acts of washing face, hands to elbows, feet to ankles, and wiping a quarter of the head.
الغسل (al-ghusl): Full-body wash required for major impurity (janabah). Also used for wudu in some contexts.

Core Idea
The sunnah acts of wudu enhance the obligatory; knowing which are sunnah vs. mustahabb deepens legal understanding.

This passage enumerates the fifteen sunnah acts of wudu as taught in the Hanafi school. It then highlights a subtle difference among Hanafi scholars regarding which of these acts are categorized as sunnah (more emphatically recommended) versus mustahabb (less emphatic). Understanding this distinction refines your appreciation of Hanafi fiqh and helps avoid oversimplification.

Sunnahs of wudu
Answer: 1. Intention (niyyah): he intends to remove ritual impurity (raf' al-hadath) or to permit what is not permissible except with wudu.

3. Mentioning the name of Allah (tasmiyah) at the beginning of wudu.
4. Using the toothstick (siwak).
5. Rinsing the mouth (madmadah) three times.
6. Inhaling water into the nose (istinshaq) three times combined with blowing it out (istinthar).
7. Wiping the entire head (isti'ab al-ra's bi al-mash). Wiping the ears.
9. Interlacing the fingers (tahlil al-asabi').
10. Interlacing the beard (tahlil al-lihyah).
11. Repeating the washing up to three times.
12. Order (tartib) according to what is mentioned in the Noble Quran: first washes his face, then his hands to the elbows, then wipes his head, then washes his feet to the ankles.
13. Starting with the right side: he first washes his right hand then his left, and similarly he starts with the right foot then the left.
14. Performing the acts consecutively (muwalat).
15. Wiping the neck.
Wiping the neck: Al-Quduri counted washing the hands before inserting them into the vessel, tasmiyah, siwak, madmadah, istinshaq, ears, tahlil al-lihyah, tahlil al-asabi', and repeating washing up to three as sunnah. Sunnahs and Mustahabbats of Wudu: The Hanafi Hierarchy
Lists sunan and mustahabbat with intra-madhhab difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the sunan and mustahabb acts of wudu according to Imam al-Quduri and distinguish the reclassification by the author of Al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Not all recommended acts in wudu are equal: sunnah is stronger than mustahabb, and scholars differ on which is which.

In this lesson, we study the list of sunan (recommended acts) and mustahabb (less stressed acts) in wudu as presented by Imam al-Quduri in his Mukhtasar. The passage also notes a key intra-Hanafi difference: the author of Al-Hidayah reclassified some acts, swapping sunnah and mustahabb for certain items. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for proper fiqh classification and for teaching beginners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the full list of acts
Read the passage and note all the acts mentioned as related to wudu: washing hands before inserting into vessel, tasmiyah, miswak, madmadah, istinshaq, wiping entire head, wiping ears, takhlil al-lahyah, takhlil al-asabi', repeating washing three times, tartib, starting with right, muwalat, wiping neck, and intention.
This list includes both sunnah and mustahabb.
2
Separate according to al-Quduri's classification
Al-Quduri counts the first nine acts (from washing hands to repeating thrice) as sunnah, and the remaining six (intention, isti'ab al-ra's, tartib, starting with right, muwalat, wiping neck) as mustahabb.
Sunnah is more confirmed than mustahabb.
3
Note al-Hidayah's reclassification
Al-Hidayah moves tasmiyah from sunnah to mustahabb, and moves intention and tartib from mustahabb to sunnah.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school, there is ijtihad.
4
Understand the pedagogical reason
The author lists all under 'sunan' to avoid confusing beginners, but expects the teacher to explain the hierarchy.
Pedagogy requires clarity without causing confusion.
5
Summarize the hierarchy
Sunnah is more stressed than mustahabb. The exact list can vary between authorities, but the principle of ta'kid (emphasis) is key.
Students should learn the mu'tamad (relied-upon) classification.

The Ten Nullifiers of Wudu (Nawaqid al-Wudu)
Enumerates ten causes that break wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ten nullifiers of wudu and explain the specific condition for each nullifier (e.g., blood must spread, vomiting must fill the mouth, laughter must be in prayer with ruku' and sujud).

Core Idea
Ten specific actions break wudu; each has a precise condition—blood must spread, laughter must be in prayer, sleep must relax the body.

Wudu is a state of ritual purity that is broken by certain events. The Hanafi school enumerates ten nullifiers (nawaqid) in a clear list. This lesson will help you memorize and understand the conditions for each nullifier, which is essential for maintaining valid wudu for prayer.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the nullifiers
Group the ten items into categories: excretions (feces, wind, urine/fluids), flowing impurities (blood, pus, matter), loss of consciousness (sleep, fainting, insanity), and laughter.
This helps with memorization.
2
Examine the conditions
For each nullifier, note the specific condition stated: blood/pus must spread to a place needing purification; vomiting must fill the mouth; sleep must be in a position that relaxes control (lying down or leaning if would fall).
These conditions differentiate valid from invalid nullifications.
3
Understand qahqahah
Loud laughter nullifies wudu only in prayers that include ruku' and sujud (i.e., all prayers except janazah). Silent or slight laughter does not nullify wudu.
This is a distinctive Hanafi ruling.
4
Apply the final rule
If any nullifier occurs, a new wudu is required before praying. Use the list to check each situation.

The Five Causes of Obligatory Ghusl
Lists five causes of obligatory ghusl and distinguishes them from wudu-only conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five causes of obligatory ghusl and distinguish them from conditions that only require wudu.

Core Idea
Ghusl becomes obligatory due to sexual penetration, seminal discharge, and the end of menstruation or postpartum bleeding.

In this lesson, we explore the five triggers that make ghusl (the major ritual bath) obligatory. Understanding these causes is essential for every Muslim to maintain ritual purity. The passage also clarifies that certain fluids like madhi and wadi do not require ghusl, only wudu.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the five causes
Commit to memory the five items listed: (1) wet dream with semen discharge, (2) penetration of the glans (with or without emission), (3) conscious emission of semen, (4) end of menstruation, (5) end of postnatal bleeding.
The first three are related to sexual activity; the last two are exclusive to women.
2
Understand key terminology
Learn the terms 'junub' (person in major impurity), 'iltiqa al-khitanayn' (euphemism for penetration), and 'hashafa' (glans).
Penetration alone, even without emission, obligates ghusl per the Hanafi school.
3
Distinguish ghusl from wudu breakers
Note that madhi (pre-ejaculate fluid) and wadi (thick post-urine discharge) do not require ghusl; they only invalidate wudu.
This distinction is crucial for correctly identifying when ghusl is needed.
4
Apply the ruling
Given a scenario, decide whether ghusl is obligatory. The key is to check if any of the five causes has occurred.
For example, after intercourse, ghusl is required even if no semen is emitted.

The Obligatory Ghusl and Its Causes
Question: When does major ritual impurity occur? And what necessitates ghusl?
Answer: Major ritual impurity occurs due to one of the following matters that we mention below: 1 - A wet dream from a man or a woman if semen is discharged.
2 - Insertion of the glans (hashafa) of the man into the vagina or anus of a woman, or into the anus of a man, whether or not semen is emitted, and ghusl is obligatory on both the active partner and the passive partner. Jurists call insertion into the vagina 'meeting of the two circumcised parts' (iltiqa al-khitanayn).
3 - Emission of semen with gushing and desire from a man or woman. If any of these three occurs, it is said: the man and woman are junub (in a state of major impurity).
4 - Cessation of menstruation.
5 - Cessation of postnatal bleeding (nifas). So due to these matters, ghusl becomes obligatory, meaning washing the entire body such that no place the size of a hair remains without water reaching it.
Question: Is ghusl required for madhi or wadi?
Answer: Ghusl is not obligatory due to their emission; rather, they are nullifiers of wudu, as we mentioned.

Recommended Ghusl and Its Occasions
Lists occasions for sunnah ghusl with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four occasions for recommended ghusl (ghusl masnun) in the Hanafi school, cite the primary hadith evidence, and explain why the Hanafi position classifies it as sunnah mu'akkadah, not fard.

Core Idea
The Prophet affirmed that ghusl for Friday and Eid is better than wudu, so it is sunnah, not obligatory.

In this passage, the author addresses a common question: besides the obligatory baths (janabah, etc.), are there any recommended baths in Islam? The answer is yes, and the Prophet explicitly made ghusl sunnah for four specific occasions. We will examine these occasions, the hadith evidence, and how the Hanafi school derives the ruling of sunnah mu'akkadah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the occasions
List the four specific occasions mentioned in the passage: Friday (Jumu'ah), the two Eids (Fitr and Adha), entering ihram for Hajj/Umrah, and standing at Arafah.
These are the well-known sunnah ghusl occasions in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the hadith evidence
The passage cites two hadith: one from Samura ibn Jundub stating 'whoever performs wudu on Friday is good, and whoever performs ghusl, ghusl is better', and another from Ibn Abbas urging ghusl, perfume, and siwak for Friday.
Both hadith are narrated in Sunan and considered hasan.
3
Examine the wording for legal implications
The critical phrase is 'الغسل أفضل' (ghusl is better). In Hanafi usul, an imperative can indicate obligation, but when accompanied by a comparative indicating preference, it shifts to recommendation (sunnah).
The Shafi'i school interprets the imperative 'فليغتسل' as wajib; Hanafi views it as sunnah due to the qualifying phrase.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school considers Friday ghusl as wajib (obligatory) based on the imperative, while the Hanafi school holds it as sunnah mu'akkadah. This is a valid ikhtilaf with practical consequences.
The student should understand both positions and the reasoning behind each.
5
Apply the Hanafi principle of tarjih
The Hanafi school gives weight to the hadith that explicitly states 'better', and also considers that the Prophet's own practice was consistent with recommendation, not obligation. Hence the mufta bihi is sunnah.
This illustrates how Hanafi jurists prioritize evidence (dalil) and reconcile apparent conflicts.

Causes of Obligatory Ghusl and Ruling of Madhi and Wadi
Lists the three causes of obligatory ghusl and clarifies madhi/wadi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of obligatory ghusl in the Hanafi school and explain why madhi and wadi do not require ghusl.

Core Idea
Only three things make ghusl obligatory: janabah, menstruation, and postpartum bleeding. Madhi and wadi only break wudu.

In this lesson, we examine the causes that make the major ablution (ghusl) fard. The Hanafi school identifies three specific events that require a full body wash. It also clarifies that two other fluids—madhi and wadi—do not trigger ghusl but only nullify wudu. Understanding this distinction prevents unnecessary ritual burdens.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three broad causes
From the passage, extract the overarching categories: Janabah (from two subcauses), cessation of menstruation (hayd), and cessation of postpartum bleeding (nifas).
The passage numbers the subcauses differently, but the three headings are clear.
2
Examine the sub-causes of Janabah
Note that janabah arises from either (1) insertion of the glans into a private part (even without emission) or (2) emission of semen with gush and desire. Both trigger ghusl.
This covers both intercourse and nocturnal emission.
3
Confirm the definition of the ghusl obligation
The text states: 'washing of the entire body, such that not a place the size of a hair remains except that water has reached it.' This defines the requirement.
4
Analyze the madhi/wadi question
The passage explicitly asks and answers: madhi and wadi do NOT require ghusl. They are among the nullifiers of wudu.
This is a key distinction for daily practice.
5
Differentiate between mani, madhi, and wadi
Mani (semen) triggers ghusl. Madhi (pre-ejaculate) and wadi (prostatic fluid) only nullify wudu and require washing the affected area.
The passage does not define the fluids but distinguishes their rulings.

Core Idea
In ghusl, the sunnah adds sequence and completeness; only one full wash is fard.

Ghusl is a required purification for states of major impurity. While the fard obligation is simply to wash the entire body once with intention, the Prophet ﷺ taught a more complete method that earns extra reward. This passage details that sunnah method step by step, helping you perform ghusl in the most perfect way.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is ghusl (ritual bath), a purification required in states of janabah, menstruation, etc. This passage describes the sunnah (recommended) method, not the obligatory (fard) elements.

Washing Feet in Ghusl: When to Delay
Explains when to wash feet during ghusl based on water stagnation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios for washing feet during ghusl and identify which condition triggers the delay.

Core Idea
Feet are washed after moving away from stagnant used water—avoiding impure water is key.

When performing ghusl (ritual bath), you also perform wudu as part of it. But what if the water used for wudu collects in a puddle where you stand? Should you wash your feet while the water is still there? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi ruling based on whether the used water drains away or stagnates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two surface types
The passage mentions surfaces where used water does not stagnate (e.g., raised bed, stone, tiles) versus surfaces where it does stagnate.
Stagnation means the water collects and does not flow away.
2
Apply the rule for draining surfaces
If the surface drains, wash feet at the end of wudu as usual. No special delay needed.
3
Apply the rule for non-draining surfaces
If used water stagnates, delay washing feet until the end of ghusl, after stepping away from the puddle.
This avoids using water that is no longer purifying (tahir but not tahur).
4
Understand the rationale
Used water (al-ma' al-musta'mal) is pure but cannot be used for purification. Washing feet in stagnant used water would be undesirable.
The ruling is a practical application of this principle.

Hanafi Well Purification: Bucket Numbers and Procedures
Detailed procedure for well purification including bucket numbers and spring wells

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the number of buckets to remove from a well based on the size and condition of the dead animal, and handle continuous springs.

Core Idea
The required water removal from a well depends on the animal's size and decomposition, with standard medium bucket as measure.

This lesson details the Hanafi method for purifying a well after a carcass falls in. You'll learn the exact bucket numbers for large, medium, and small animals, how to adjust when the animal has decomposed, and how to handle wells that have a continuous spring.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the dead animal by size
Identify whether the animal is large (human, dog, sheep), medium (pigeon, chicken, cat), or small (mouse, sparrow).
Size matters, not species name.
2
Apply default bucket numbers
For large animals, remove all water. For medium, remove 40 buckets wajib, 50 mustahabb. For small, remove 20 wajib, 30 mustahabb.
These numbers are for a standard medium bucket.
3
Check for decomposition
If the carcass has swollen or disintegrated (intafakha or tafassakha), remove all water regardless of size.
Decomposition spreads impurity more widely.
4
Adjust for bucket size
If using a larger bucket, proportionally reduce the number of bucketfuls. For example, if a bucket holds 10 medium buckets, then for a small animal, 2-3 large buckets suffice.
The calculation ensures the same total volume.
5

If the well has a flowing spring that never empties, remove the estimated volume of water present, determined by two reliable experts.
The water is considered pure once the equivalent of the original water is removed.

Classifications of Su'r (Water Remnant) in Hanafi Fiqh
Q&A defining the purity status of su'r

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the su'r (remnant water) of humans, halal meat animals, dogs/pigs/predators, cats/free-range chickens/predatory birds/house animals, and donkeys/mules.

Core Idea
Human su'r is pure; cat's su'r is makruh; donkey's is doubtful; dog's is najis.

This passage presents the Hanafi classification of water leftover after a human or animal drinks. The rulings range from absolutely pure (tahir) to impure (najis), with intermediate categories of disliked (makruh) and doubtful (mashkuk). Understanding these categories is essential for daily wudu and purification, especially when only one water source is available.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of su'r
The passage lists five categories: human (tahir), halal meat animals (tahir), dogs/pigs/predatory beasts (najis), cats/chickens/predatory birds/house animals (makruh), and donkeys/mules (mashkuk). Make a mental table.
2
Distinguish tahir from mutahhir
Note: 'tahir' means pure but not necessarily purifying. Makruh water is tahir but its use is disliked; mashkuk water is pure but its purifying capacity is uncertain. Only tahir water that is also mutahhir (purifying) can be used for wudu unconditionally.
Other schools may equate tahir with mutahhir; Hanafis distinguish.
3
Apply the ruling for makruh su'r
If you have two waters: one makruh (e.g., cat's remnant) and one pure human remnant, you must use the human's. If only makruh water is available, you use it and do not do tayammum.
The ruling is makruh tanzihi – permissible but inferior.
4
Handle doubtful (mashkuk) su'r
For donkey/mule su'r, Hanafis rule it mashkuk. One must perform both wudu (using that water) and tayammum to exit the doubt. This is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
Other schools may rule it pure or impure outright.

Tayammum is performed identically for minor and major impurity: two strikes, face to hands.

In the absence of water or when its use is harmful, tayammum provides a valid purification for both minor (hadath asghar) and major (hadath akbar) impurity. This lesson walks through the precise method as outlined in the Hanafi tradition, emphasizing that the procedure is uniform regardless of the type of impurity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the intention
The first obligatory step is niyyah (intention). The person must intend to remove impurity or to permit the prayer. Without this, tayammum is invalid.
Intention is fard in tayammum, unlike wudu where it is sunnah.
2
First strike and face wiping
Strike both hands on pure earth, then wipe the entire face with the hands, ensuring every part including between hair and beard is covered.
The face is wiped with the hands leaving no spot untouched.
3
Second strike and hand wiping
Strike hands on earth a second time. Wipe the right hand from fingertips to elbow with the left hand, then the left hand similarly with the right hand.
The hands are wiped to the elbows, not washed as in wudu.
4
Confirm uniformity
The same steps apply for both minor and major impurity. There is no distinction in the performance of tayammum between the two.
This is a key point: janabah does not require extra steps.

Valid Materials for Tayammum and the Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Ruling on valid tayammum substances with intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on valid tayammum materials, distinguish the dissenting view of Abu Yusuf, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Any pure earth-like substance is valid for tayammum, but Abu Yusuf restricts it to earth and sand.

Tayammum is a purified substitute for wudu when water is unavailable. But what counts as 'earth'? The Hanafi school broadly accepts any substance from the genus of the earth, while a leading student of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, held a narrower view. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the reasoning behind the difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi majority position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad: any pure substance from the genus of the earth (jins al-ard) is permissible for tayammum. List the examples given: sand, plaster, lime, kohl, and even smooth stone.
2
Identify the opponent and his view
Abu Yusuf limits valid substances to pure earth (turab) and sand only. He excludes plaster, lime, kohl, and stone.
3
Analyse the basis of disagreement
The dispute hinges on the interpretation of 'sa'idan tayyiban' (good earth) in the Qur'an. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider all earth-like materials as included; Abu Yusuf takes a narrower literal reading.
4
Apply the Hanafi response
The majority holds that these substances are of the same genus and therefore valid, provided they are pure.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Yusuf's view, a tayammum with plaster or kohl would be invalid; under the majority view it is valid. The mufta bihi position is the wider permissibility.

السؤال: بماذا يتيمم؟
الجواب: يجوز التيمم بالتراب الطاهر، وبكل ما كان من جنس الأرض كالرمل والجص، والنورة، والكحل ، والحجر وإن كان أملس لا غبار عليه.

Question: With what does one perform tayammum?
Answer: Tayammum is permitted with pure earth, and with everything that is of the same genus as the earth, such as sand, plaster, lime, kohl, and stone even if it is smooth without dust. And it is a condition in all these things that they be pure. This is according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them). And Abu Yusuf (may Allah have mercy on him) said: It is not permitted except with earth and sand specifically, on condition that they be pure.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on the materials permissible for tayammum. According to Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, any substance that is from the genus of the earth (i.e., natural or derived from earth) can be used, provided it is pure (tahir). This includes sand, plaster, lime, kohl, and stone, even if smooth and dust-free. However, Abu Yusuf restricts it to pure earth (turab) and sand only. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, allowing a wider range of materials as long as they are pure.

رمل (raml): Sand.
جص (jiss): Plaster (gypsum).
نورة (nurah): Lime (calcium oxide).
كحل (kuhl): Antimony or kohl stone.
حجر (hajar): Stone, including smooth stone without dust.

Intention in Purification: Fard in Tayammum, Sunnah in Wudu and Ghusl
Ruling on intention in tayammum vs wudu/ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the necessity of intention (niyyah) in tayammum, wudu, and ghusl, and explain why intention is fard in tayammum but only sunnah in the other two.

Core Idea
Intention is fard in tayammum but only sunnah in wudu and ghusl – a distinctive Hanafi position.

This passage addresses a practical question: if dust settles on your face and hands without intention, does it count as tayammum? The answer reveals a key Hanafi distinction: intention is obligatory for tayammum but not for wudu or ghusl. Understanding this difference is crucial for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
Read the question: a person demolishes a house, dust reaches his face and hands without intention. The answer: this does not count as tayammum because intention is fard (obligatory) in tayammum.
The key term is 'fard' – omission invalidates the act.
2
Contrast with wudu and ghusl
The answer then provides counterexamples: if someone immerses in water without intending ghusl, it suffices. Similarly, rain covering the limbs without intending wudu suffices. This shows intention is not fard in wudu and ghusl.
Why the difference? The passage states: 'because intention is not fard in ghusl and wudu; rather it is sunnah.'
3
Derive the principle
From the contrast, derive the general principle: for water-based purifications (wudu/ghusl), physical contact with water suffices even without intention; for earth-based purification (tayammum), intention is essential to distinguish it from mere dust.
This is a Hanafi-specific ruling based on the nature of each purification.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools, such as the Shafi'i, hold that intention is fard in all purifications. Their proof includes the hadith 'Actions are judged by intentions.' The Hanafi school responds with the specific examples from practice.
Understanding the ikhtilaf helps appreciate the Hanafi evidence and ease.

السؤال: إذا هدم بيتا، وأثارت الريحُ الغبار، فوصل التراب وجهه ويديه، هل حصل بذلك التيمم، وجازت الصلاة به؟
الجواب: لا يكون بذلك متيمماً، ولا تحصل به الطهارة المطلوبة؛ لأن النية فرض في التيمم، أما لو انغمس في الماء ولم ينو الغسل يكفيه من الغسل، وكذا إذا أصاب المطر جميع أعضاء الوضوء، وسال الماء عليها، وأمر يده على رأسه، يكفيه من الوضوء وإن لم ينو الوضوء، وذلك لأن النية ليست بفرض في الغسل والوضوء، بل هي سنة فيهما.
Question: If a person demolishes a house, and the wind stirs up dust, and the dust reaches his face and hands, is he considered a mutayammim by that, and is prayer permissible with it?
Answer: He is not considered a mutayammim by that, nor does the required purification occur; because intention is obligatory (fard) in tayammum. However, if he immerses himself in water without intending ghusl, it suffices for his ghusl. Similarly, if rain hits all the limbs of wudu and water flows over them, and he passes his hand over his head, it suffices for his wudu even if he did not intend wudu. This is because intention is not fard in ghusl and wudu; rather it is sunnah in both.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes the role of intention in different purifications. If dust settles on a person's face and hands without his intention, it does not count as tayammum because intention is a fard (obligatory integral) of tayammum. However, if a person immerses in water (for ghusl) without intending it, or rain covers his limbs for wudu, the purification is valid even without intention. The reason: intention is not fard in wudu and ghusl; it is only sunnah (recommended). Thus, tayammum is unlike wudu/ghusl in this aspect.

Key Terms
نية (niyyah): Intention to perform the act of purification; in tayammum it is fard, in wudu/ghusl it is sunnah.
فرض (fard): Obligatory component; omission invalidates the act.
سنة (sunnah): Recommended but not obligatory; omission does not invalidate.

The Permissibility of All Acts with Tayammum
Ruling that tayammum permits all acts that are permissible after wudu or ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that tayammum permits all acts permissible after wudu or ghusl, and list the specific acts mentioned in the passage.

Core Idea
Tayammum is a full substitute for wudu and ghusl.

This passage answers a common question: once you have performed tayammum, what acts of worship are you allowed to do? The answer is comprehensive: tayammum makes permissible everything that wudu or ghusl would permit. This includes not only prayers but also touching the Qur'an, entering the mosque, reciting the Qur'an, and performing tawaf. The principle is that tayammum is a legal equivalent, so no distinction is made.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal effect
Recognize that tayammum is a purification substitute. The question asks about praying nafl, but the answer expands to all acts.
2
Note the equivalency statement
The author explicitly says there is no difference between purification by water and by tayammum. This is the key principle.
3
List the specific acts mentioned
Mentioned are: praying fard and nafl, touching the mushaf, entering the mosque, reciting the Qur'an, performing tawaf.
These are examples, not exhaustive.
4
Derive the general rule
Since tayammum is equivalent, any act that requires wudu or ghusl becomes permissible with tayammum, as long as the tayammum is valid.

السؤال: إذا تيمم فهل يجوز أن يصلي به النوافل ؟
الجواب: يجوز له أن يصلي بالتيمم ما شاء من الفرائض والنوافل، وأن يفعل كل ما يجوز بعد اغتسال الجنب ووضوء المتوضئ ؛ فإنه لا فرق بين الطهارة الحاصلة بالاغتسال أو التوضؤ، وبين الطهارة الحاصلة بالتيمم، فجاز للمتيمم مسُّ المصحف، ودخول المسجد، وتلاوة القرآن، وطواف البيت.
Question: If one performs tayammum, is it permissible for him to pray supererogatory prayers with it?
Answer: He may pray with tayammum whatever he wishes of obligatory and supererogatory prayers, and he may do everything that is permissible after the ghusl of a junub and the wudu of one who performed wudu; for there is no difference between the purification obtained through ghusl or wudu and the purification obtained through tayammum. Thus, it is permissible for the mutayammim to touch the mushaf, enter the mosque, recite the Qur'an, and perform tawaf of the Kaaba.

Commentary
This passage confirms that tayammum provides the same legal purity as wudu and ghusl. Once one has performed tayammum, he may pray any number of fard and nafl prayers, touch the Qur'an, enter the mosque, recite the Qur'an (even while in a state of janabah if tayammum was for that), and perform tawaf. The key point is that there is no difference in the legal effect of the purification; tayammum is a full substitute.

Key Terms
مس المصحف (mass al-mushaf): Touching the physical copy of the Qur'an.
طواف البيت (tawaf al-bayt): Circumambulation of the Kaaba.
