Customary Return of Borrowed Item Discharges Liability
Ruling on liability after customary return of borrowed item.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that customary return to the owner's stable or house suffices to discharge the borrower's liability and identify the condition that triggers this discharge.

Core Idea
Customary return to the owner's premises ends the borrower's liability.

This lesson examines a specific ruling on the return of a borrowed item (ʿāriyah). The Hanafi school holds that if the borrower returns the borrowed animal to the owner's stable or the slave to the owner's house, that constitutes valid delivery (taslīm mutaʿārif). Consequently, the borrower incurs no liability if the item is later lost or destroyed. This ruling underscores the role of custom (ʿurf) in determining legal obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is returning a borrowed item (dābbah or ʿabd). The legal consequence is that the borrower is not liable for subsequent loss.
Liability here means responsibility for compensation.
2
Understand the condition for discharge
The discharge is conditional upon 'customary delivery' (taslīm mutaʿārif). This means returning the item in a way that is considered sufficient by local custom.
Custom (ʿurf) plays a key role.
3
Apply to given scenarios
The text provides two examples: returning an animal to its stable and returning a slave to the owner's house. In both, the borrower is not liable.
The stable and house are customary places.
4
Recognize the role of custom
What constitutes 'customary' can vary by time and place. The common element is that the item is placed under the owner's control in a typical manner.
The Hanafi school uses istiḥsān to prioritize custom.
5
Conclude on liability
Once customary return is established, the borrower's responsibility ends. No further action (like informing the owner) is required.
Contrast with schools that require physical handover.

Who Bears the Cost of Returning a Borrowed Item?
Ruling on cost of return in aariyah with analogy to ghasp and ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on who bears the cost of returning a borrowed item (aariyah) and distinguish it from the rules for usurpation (ghasp) and hire (ijarah).

Core Idea
The borrower bears the cost of returning the borrowed item, just as the usurper bears it.

This lesson addresses a practical question in the contract of aariyah (gratuitous loan): who bears the cost of returning the item to its owner? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer based on the principle that the one who benefits from the use bears the related expenses. The ruling is compared to ghasp (usurpation) and ijarah (hire) to highlight the underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is returning a borrowed item (aariyah). The ruling is that the borrower (musta'ir) must bear the cost of returning it to the lender (mu'ir).
The cost is called ujrat al-radd.
2
Recognize the analogy with ghasp
The text draws an analogy with ghasp (usurpation): the usurper (ghasib) bears the cost of returning the usurped item. This shows that the one who wrongfully or gratuitously benefits must bear return expenses.
Both aariyah and ghasp involve the taker benefiting from possession.
3
Distinguish from ijarah
In ijarah (hire), the cost of returning the hired item is borne by the owner (lessor), not the lessee. This is because the owner receives compensation (ujrah) and thus bears the expense of return.
In ijarah, the benefit is compensated, so the owner bears the cost.
4
Apply the underlying principle
The principle is that the one who benefits from the use or possession bears the cost of returning the item. In aariyah and ghasp, the borrower/usurper benefits; in ijarah, the owner benefits via rental income.
This principle aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on benefit-based cost allocation.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may place the cost of returning a borrowed item on the lender, arguing the item is the lender's property. The Hanafi school, however, relies on the analogy with ghasp and the benefit principle.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Source Text

Question: Who bears the cost of returning the borrowed item? Answer: The borrower bears it, just as the cost of returning a usurped item is borne by the usurper. As for the cost of returning a hired item, it is upon the one who rented it out.
Source pages p. 300

Commentary
This ruling explains the allocation of expenses for returning the borrowed item. In aariyah, the borrower (musta'ir) must bear the cost of returning the item to the lender. The text draws an analogy with ghasp (usurpation): the usurper (ghasib) must also bear the return cost. In contrast, in ijarah (hiring), the cost of returning the hired item is borne by the owner (the one who rented it out, i.e., the lessor). This distinction is based on the nature of each contract: in aariyah and ghasp, the taker has the benefit, so they bear the cost; in ijarah, the owner provides the benefit for compensation and thus bears the return cost.

Lender Can Revoke Aariyah at Any Time
Ruling on revocation of aariyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revocation of aariyah and explain why the contract is considered ghayr lazim.

Core Idea
Aariyah is a gratuitous contract; the lender may revoke at will because it is ghayr lazim.

This brief Q&A from a Hanafi text addresses a key feature of the loan of benefit (aariyah). Unlike binding contracts, aariyah does not obligate the lender to continue the loan. The ruling is clear: the lender may revoke whenever he wishes. Understanding this helps distinguish aariyah from other contracts like ijarah (lease), which are binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act being ruled upon is the lender's revocation of aariyah. The ruling is that it is permissible (mubah) at any time.
The text uses the imperative 'له أن يرجع' which indicates permissibility.
2
Recall the nature of aariyah
Aariyah is a non-binding (ghayr lazim) contract. This means neither party is forced to continue; the lender can revoke, and the borrower can return the item without cause.
This contrasts with ijarah (lease), which is binding for the agreed term.
3
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is that revocation is absolute. Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require a valid reason if the borrower has taken possession for a specific purpose.
The practical consequence: in the Hanafi school the lender can demand return even if the borrower hasn't yet used the item.

Question: Does the lender have the right to revoke his aariyah? Answer: He may revoke whenever he wishes.
Source pages p. 300

Commentary
This ruling affirms that aariyah is a non-binding (ghayr lazim) contract. The lender (mu'ir) has the right to revoke the loan of benefit at any time he wishes, without needing the borrower's consent. This is because aariyah is a gratuitous contract and does not create a permanent obligation on the lender. However, the lender must provide a reasonable opportunity for the borrower to return the item. Some later jurists discuss whether the lender can revoke before the borrower has had a chance to benefit, but the core principle is that the contract is revocable.

Revocation of Aariyah with Building or Planting: The Two Hanafi Opinions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation for improvements upon revocation of aariyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on compensation when a lender revokes aariyah of land after the borrower has built or planted, and identify the conditions that determine which opinion applies.

Core Idea
In aariyah, if the borrower builds or plants, compensation depends on whether a term was fixed: if timed, lender pays decrease; if not timed, borrower removes at his own cost.

This passage from the Hanafi texts addresses a practical scenario: a lender revokes a loan of land (aariyah) after the borrower has invested in construction or planting. The Hanafi school offers two opinions (wajhan) based on whether the aariyah was timed. We will explore these opinions, their basis, and how later authorities reconciled them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
Determine whether the aariyah was timed (muwaqqatah) or untimed. This is the key factor that triggers different rulings.
The text begins with 'إن وقت العارية' (if it was timed) and 'وإن لم يكن وقت' (if not timed).
2
Understand Al-Quduri's Ruling
If timed, the lender must compensate the borrower for the decrease in value of the building or plants caused by their removal. If untimed, the lender can compel removal without compensation.
This is the view attributed to Al-Quduri in Al-Mukhtasar.
3
Understand Al-Hakim al-Shahid's Alternative
Al-Hakim al-Shahid holds that the lender must pay the full value of the building and plants, and they become his property, unless the borrower chooses to remove them himself.
This is cited from Al-Muntakhab and supported by Al-Hidayah.
4
See the Elaboration in Al-Kifayah
Al-Kifayah provides an example: if the building's value up to the stipulated period is 10 dinars and immediate removal value is 2 dinars, the borrower can claim 8 dinars as compensation for decrease.
This illustrates the first opinion's calculation of decrease.
5
Determine the Practical Difference
The thamarah al-khilaf is the amount of compensation: decrease vs. full value. The mufta bihi view is the first (decrease if timed, no compensation if untimed), but the second view is also considered.
The dominant view in later Hanafi works is Al-Quduri's.

Definition of Muzara'ah (Sharecropping)
Definition of muzara'ah with key condition on share distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definition of muzara'ah and explain why the share must be undivided (mushau').

Core Idea
Muzara'ah is a partnership in cultivation where the worker receives a fraction of the produce—not a fixed wage—to avoid uncertainty.

This lesson begins with a foundational definition: what is muzara'ah? We examine its linguistic roots and technical meaning as defined by Hanafi jurists. Understanding this definition is essential because the condition of an undivided share (mushau') determines the contract's validity and distinguishes it from other land arrangements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic origin
Recognize that muzara'ah is derived from zira'ah, meaning cultivation. The word form is mufa'ala, indicating a mutual action between two parties.
2
Parse the technical definition
Break down the definition: it is a transaction (mu'amalah) between the landowner (rabb al-ard) and a worker (rajul). The worker's duty is to cultivate the land.
3
Identify the key condition
The worker takes a share of the produce 'mushau'an' – an undivided fraction like one-third or one-fourth. This distinguishes muzara'ah from fixed-wage labor.
Mushau' is crucial to eliminate gharar (uncertainty).
4
Understand the rationale for mushau'
The undivided share ensures that both parties share the risk of the harvest. A fixed quantity would make it a rental of labor rather than a partnership, which is a different legal category.

Two conditions for valid muzara'ah according to Sahibayn

Identify the act and its ruling
Muzara'ah (sharecropping) is considered valid (sahih) by the Sahibayn when two specific conditions are met. Note that Imam Abu Hanifa holds the contract to be absolutely invalid, but the Sahibayn allow it under these conditions.
This ruling applies only to the permissible forms (al-suwar al-ja'izah) of muzara'ah.

The contract must specify a known period (muddah ma'lumah), e.g., one year, to avoid uncertainty about the duration of work. If the term is ambiguous, the contract is invalid.

Key Rulings on Muzara'ah (Sharecropping)
Question-answer rulings on sharecropping contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for valid and void sharecropping contracts and apply the rules regarding division of produce, compensation, and consequences of death or expiry.

Core Idea
In Hanafi sharecropping, produce follows the seed owner in a void contract; the worker gets only market wage, not a share.

This lesson unpacks a series of practical rulings on muzara'ah (sharecropping) from the Hanafi tradition. You will learn how to determine the division of produce when the contract is valid or void, who bears costs, and what happens if the term expires or a party dies. These rules balance the rights of landowner and worker, ensuring fairness and preventing exploitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract status
First determine whether the sharecropping contract is valid (sahih) or void (fasid). A valid contract follows stipulated shares; a void contract triggers special compensation rules.
The text lists conditions that void the contract, such as stipulating all harvest costs on the worker.
2
Apply the seed-owner rule for void contracts
If the contract is void but land produced, the entire crop belongs to the owner of the seed. The other party is entitled to ajr al-mithl (market wage) but subject to a cap if the seed was from the landowner.
Abu Hanifa caps the worker's wage at the stipulated share; Muhammad gives full ajr al-mithl.
3
Check compulsion to work
The seed owner (landowner if he provided seed) cannot be compelled to work; the other party can be compelled by the judge to perform the work.
This prevents the seed owner from being forced into labor but ensures the worker fulfills his duty.
4
Handle expiry before harvest
If the contract period ends before the crop ripens, the crop remains in place until harvest. The cultivator pays rent for the land share he occupied, and expenses are shared proportionally.
The goal is to avoid waste and ensure the crop reaches maturity.
5
Allocate harvest costs
Costs of harvesting, threshing, lifting, and winnowing are split according to each party's share in the produce. Stipulating all costs on the worker makes the contract void.
This prevents unfair burden on the worker.
6
Apply death rules
Death of a party voids the contract before cultivation. After cultivation, the crop is preserved: if landowner dies, worker keeps crop until harvest; if worker dies, heirs may complete but without wages, and landowner has options to buy out or share expenses.
The ruling protects the investment of labor and seed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Musaqah is valid by fatwa despite Abu Hanifa's disapproval, due to istihsan and public need.

Musaqah is a contract for tending fruit trees in exchange for a share of the harvest. While Imam Abu Hanifa considered it invalid by qiyas, his two disciples upheld its validity based on istihsan, and the fatwa follows them. This lesson examines the definition, the intra-Hanafi dispute, the essential conditions, and the scope of trees covered.

Guided Path
1
Define musaqah linguistically and technically

Understand the condition of fruit increase
The contract is valid only if the fruit can increase with the worker's effort. If the fruit is already mature or will not increase, the contract is invalid.
This condition ensures the worker's labor has a real effect.
5
Determine the scope of trees covered
Musaqah is valid for date palms, grapevines, other trees, and even eggplant roots. The principle is any tree whose fruit can increase with work.
Seasonal crops (rūṭab) are also included if they regrow.

If the owner of the land dies and the fruit is unripe, the worker may continue in it as he was doing before until the dates ripen, even if the heirs of the land owner dislike that, by istihsan. So the contract remains to prevent harm to him, and there is no harm on the other. If the worker insists on harm, the heirs of the other choose between dividing the unripe fruit according to the condition, or giving him the value of his share of unripe fruit, or spending on the unripe fruit until it ripens and then taking that from the worker's share of the dates. If the worker dies, his heirs may continue in it even if the land owner dislikes, because consideration is for both sides. If they want to harvest it unripe, the land owner has the three choices we mentioned. If both die together, the choice is for the worker's heirs because they stand in his place. If the worker's heirs refuse to continue, then the choice is for the land owner's heirs as we described. End with abbreviation.

Commentary
This passage, from Al-Quduri's matn and Al-Hidayah's commentary, rules on the invalidation of the musaqah (irrigation) contract. The matn states three rulings: (1) if the contract becomes invalid, the worker gets the equivalent wage (ujr al-mithl), not the agreed share; (2) the contract is voided by death of either party, analogized to muzara'ah; (3) it can be rescinded for excuses like ijara. After maturation, the worker gets his share. If the worker dies, his heirs may continue. If both die, the worker's heirs have priority. This istihsan overrides strict qiyas to avoid harm.

استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas to avoid hardship; here it allows continuation of the contract despite death.

Reviving Land Abandoned by a River
Q&A on reviving land abandoned by the Euphrates or Tigris

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which land abandoned by the Euphrates or Tigris (or similar rivers) can be revived and owned according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Land permanently abandoned by a river can be revived like dead land, but only if water cannot return and it is not another's harim.

Rivers sometimes change course, leaving behind dry land. Who can claim this land? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi ruling: the key is whether the water can ever return. If it cannot, the land becomes mawat (dead land) and can be revived with the Imam's permission, leading to ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the land
Consider land once covered by a river (like the Euphrates or Tigris) that has dried up because the river shifted.
2
Check possibility of water return
If the water could return naturally (e.g., the river might flow back), the land is not considered dead and cannot be revived by anyone else.
This preserves the original riverbed's potential.
3
If return is impossible
If the water cannot return (e.g., the river has permanently changed course), the land is treated as mawat (dead land) that can be revived.
4
Additional conditions
The land must not be within the harim of an inhabited area (عامر). Also, revival must be done with the permission of the Imam (ruler).
5
Result of revival
If someone revives it under these conditions, they own it.
Ownership is established through actual cultivation or construction (ihya).

Q&A on tahjir and the ruler's intervention after three years

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal effect of tahjir (marking land) and the time limit for development according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Tahjir gives a three-year priority to develop; if not developed, the land may be taken by the ruler.

Sometimes a person marks a piece of land (tahjir) with stones to indicate intent to revive it, but does not actually cultivate or build. Does this give ownership? This lesson clarifies that tahjir only grants a limited right of priority, and if the land remains undeveloped for three years, the ruler may reassign it.

1
Define tahjir
Tahjir is the act of marking the boundaries of a piece of land with stones, declaring an intention to revive it.
2
Identify the legal effect of tahjir alone
Tahjir alone does not confer ownership; it only gives a temporary right of priority to the marker.
The passage implies that ownership comes only after actual revival (ihya).
3
State the time limit
The ruler grants the person a respite of up to three years to develop the land.
4
Consequence of non-development
If after three years the land is still undeveloped, the ruler can take it away and give it to someone else who will develop it.
This prevents hoarding of land.

The Permitted Slave (Ma'dhun): Definition and Legal Capacity
Q&A defining the permitted slave and his legal capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that make a slave a ma'dhun and state the scope of his legal capacity in transactions.

Core Idea
General permission grants the slave full transactional capacity, but marriage and manumission remain with the master.

This lesson introduces the concept of the permitted slave (ma'dhun) in Hanafi law. Through a series of questions and answers, the passage defines who qualifies as a ma'dhun, what transactions he may conduct, and what prohibitions apply despite general permission. Understanding these distinctions is essential for grasping the Hanafi framework of legal capacity (ahliyyah) in commercial contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate the answer to 'Who is the permitted slave?' – he is the opposite of the interdicted (mahjur). The causes of interdiction are minority, slavery, and insanity. The ma'dhun is a slave whom the master has permitted to trade.
The passage assumes knowledge from the Book of Interdiction.
2
Determine the condition: general permission (idhn 'amm)
General permission confers full capacity in all types of trade. Even if the master specifies a restriction (e.g., only one type), the slave is still considered ma'dhun in all trades. But if permission is limited to a specific task (e.g., buy a garment), he is not a ma'dhun and is bound by that order only.
The default is general permission unless explicitly restricted to a single act.
3
List the permitted acts
The ma'dhun may buy, sell, pawn, and take in pawn. His confession (iqrar) regarding debts, usurpations, and deposits is valid.
Confession is effective because he has capacity for trade.
4
List the prohibitions and debt liability
Even with general permission, the ma'dhun cannot marry, marry off slaves he owns, make a kitabah contract, free slaves for payment, or give gifts (except small hospitality). Debts incurred in trade are attached to his person; he may be sold to pay creditors unless the master ransoms him. Any remaining debt after sale is pursued after emancipation.
Marriage and manumission are personal status matters reserved for the master.

السؤال : ما حكم استحقاق الأمة في حضانة ولدها؟ الجواب: ليس للأمة وأم الولد قبل العتق حق الحضانة، فإذا أعتقا فهما كالحرة في ذلك. الحضانة: لعجزهما عن الحضانة بالاشتغال بخدمة المولى، وإذا أعتقتا صارتا حرتين أوان ثبوت الحق، ودخلت تحت الأمة المدبرة لوجود الرق فيها وكذا المكاتبة داخلة تحت الأمة بالنسبة إلى الولد المولود قبل الكتابة، وأما إذا ولدته بعد الكتابة فهي أولى بحضانته من غيرها؛ لأنه صار داخلا في كتابتها، وأراد بالحق المنفي حق أمه الحضانة، قالوا: ولا يفرق بينه وبين للنهي عن ذلك. "البحر الرائق" (١٨٥/٤).
Question: What is the ruling on the entitlement of a slave woman to custody of her child? Answer: A slave woman and an umm walad have no right of custody before emancipation. If they become free, they are like a free woman in that regard. [Commentary:] Custody: due to their inability to perform custody because of being occupied with serving the master. When they are freed, they become free women and the right is established. And a mudabbara (slave promised freedom upon master's death) is included under slave woman because slavery exists in her. Similarly, a mukataba (slave in contract of emancipation) is included under slave woman with respect to a child born before the kitaba; but if she gives birth after the kitaba, she is more entitled to his custody than others because the child is included in her kitaba. The author intended by 'denied right' the mother's right of custody. They said: and one does not separate between him and [؟] due to the prohibition of that. (Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, 4/185).

Commentary
This passage gives the ruling that a slave woman (أمة) and an umm walad (a slave who bore her master's child) do not have the right of custody as long as they remain in slavery. Their servitude prevents them from having the capacity to care for the child because they are obligated to serve their owner. Once they are emancipated, they become like free women and acquire custody rights. The commentary extends this to categories: a mudabbara (slave whose emancipation is deferred to the master's death) is still considered a slave for custody purposes. A mukataba (slave under contract of emancipation) is considered a slave for a child born before the contract; but if the child is born after the contract, the child is part of her contract, and she becomes more entitled to custody than others. The reference clarifies that the 'denied right' refers to the mother's custody right, and there is a prohibition against separating the child from her.

Key Terms
أمة (ammah): A female slave owned by another person.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who gives birth to her master's child.
مكاتبة (mukataba): A slave who has entered into a written contract with the master to earn and purchase her freedom.
