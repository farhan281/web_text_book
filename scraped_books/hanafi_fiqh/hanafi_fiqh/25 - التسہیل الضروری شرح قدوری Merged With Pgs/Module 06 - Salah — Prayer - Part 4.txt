Forgetting the First Sitting: Return or Continue?
Ruling for forgetting the first sitting in 3 or 4 rak'ah prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for forgetting the first sitting by determining whether sajdah al-sahw is required based on the worshiper's proximity to standing or sitting.

Core Idea
Forgetting the first sitting: if closer to sitting, return and no sajdah; if closer to standing, continue and do sajdah.

In the Hanafi school, the first sitting (qa'dah) after the second rak'ah is a wajib (obligatory) element in three- and four-rak'ah prayers. What happens if you forget it and remember while still in the process of standing? This lesson examines the precise ruling based on your physical state at the moment of remembrance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the forgotten element
Recognize that the first sitting (al-qu'ud al-awwal) is a wajib in Hanafi fiqh for 3 or 4 rak'ah prayers.
This is distinct from the final sitting, which is a rukn.
2
Assess the state of remembrance
Determine whether the worshiper is closer to the sitting position or to the standing position at the moment of remembering.
The text uses 'closer' (أقرب) as a criterion.
3
Apply the ruling for closer to sitting
If closer to sitting: return to the sitting position, recite tashahhud, and continue the prayer. No sajdah al-sahw is required.
No compensation because the wajib was fulfilled by returning before adding an extra rukn.
4
Apply the ruling for closer to standing
If closer to standing: do not return, continue the prayer, and perform sajdah al-sahw after the last tashahhud before salam.
Sajdah al-sahw compensates for the missed wajib.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Contrast with the Shafi'i position, which requires returning even if fully standing, followed by sajdah al-sahw.
The difference stems from the status of the first sitting: wajib (Hanafi) vs sunnah (Shafi'i).

Forgetting the Last Sitting and Adding a Fifth Rak'ah
Ruling when one forgets last sitting and adds a fifth rak'ah in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one forgets the last sitting in a four-rak'ah prayer and adds a fifth rak'ah, and apply the correct course of action (return or add a sixth) based on whether prostration of the fifth has occurred.

Core Idea
If you prostrate for an extra rak'ah, your fard becomes nafl — add another to complete an even number.

In the Hanafi school, the last sitting (al-qa'dah al-akhīrah) is a fard obligation. Forgetting it while adding a fifth rak'ah creates a unique situation. This lesson clarifies exactly what to do: whether you can still save your fard prayer or if it transforms into nafl and how to complete it. We will also see a related scenario where a person mistakenly stands after the fourth thinking it's the second.

Guided Path
1
Identify the missed fard
The last sitting (al-qa'dah al-akhīrah) is a fard in Hanafi prayer. Forgetting it and standing for a fifth rak'ah triggers the ruling.
This applies to four-rak'ah prayers like Zuhr, Asr, Isha.
2
Check if prostration of the fifth has been performed
If the sajdah of the fifth rak'ah has not yet been done, the situation is salvageable: return to sitting, leave the fifth, and do sajdah al-sahw. If sajdah has been performed, the original fard is invalidated.
Prostration means the two sajdahs of the rak'ah.
3
Apply the ruling for each case
Case 1 (no sajdah): return to the last sitting, leave the fifth rak'ah, and prostrate for forgetfulness. Case 2 (sajdah performed): the prayer becomes nafl; add a sixth rak'ah to make the nafl even (since odd numbers are not preferred for nafl) and do sajdah al-sahw.
The text explicitly says 'batala farḍuhu' — his obligation is invalidated.
4
Extend to other cases (benefit)
The same rule applies analogously to two-rak'ah (Fajr) and three-rak'ah (Maghrib) prayers. If you forget the last sitting in those, the same division applies.
For Fajr, the last sitting is the only sitting after the second rak'ah; for Maghrib, after the third.
5
Handle the secondary scenario
If the person actually sat in the fourth, recited tashahhud, then stood thinking it was the second rak'ah, the ruling is the same: if no sajdah of the fifth, return; if sajdah performed, add a sixth. The extra rak'ahs become nafl.
This shows the ruling is based on the prostration event, not on the intention.

Doubts (Shakk) in Prayer
Rulings for various types of doubt during prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between first-time doubt and frequent doubt in prayer, state the ruling for each, and explain the role of tahrī and sajdah al-sahw according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Frequent doubts are resolved by predominant opinion or minimum certainty; first-time doubt requires restart.

Doubt in the number of rakʿahs during prayer is a common experience. The Hanafi school provides clear rules: a single occurrence demands restart, while chronic doubt (waswasa) is handled by reliance on what is most likely or on certainty. This lesson unpacks these rulings, including the role of sajdah al-sahw and the scholarly differences within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of doubt
First-time doubt versus frequent doubt. The passage distinguishes between a first occurrence of doubt and chronic doubt that occurs often.
First-time: restart; frequent: continue with adjustment.
2
Apply the appropriate principle for frequent doubt
If frequent, use tahrī (investigation to reach predominant opinion). If no predominant opinion, rely on certainty (yaqīn), i.e., the minimum number of rakʿahs.
Tahrī is a mental effort; if it yields no result, fall back to al-aqall (minimum).
3
Determine the need for sajdah al-sahw
In both frequent-doubt cases (tahrī or minimum), sajdah al-sahw is required to compensate for the uncertainty. First-time doubt does not involve sajdah because the prayer is restarted.
There is an intra-Hanafi difference: al-Quduri omitted sajdah, but al-Durr al-Mukhtar makes it obligatory.
4
Follow the detailed method for four-rakʿah prayers
The passage elaborates steps for doubt between first and second rakʿah: assume it is the first, then sit as if second, then pray third, sit as if fourth, etc., with sajdah al-sahw.
Three-rakʿah prayers are analogous.

Forgetting the Final Sitting and Adding an Extra Rak'ah
Q&A on adding a rak'ah after omitting final sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a person omits the final sitting and adds a fifth rak'ah, and apply the same rule by analogy to two- and three-rak'ah prayers.

Core Idea
Omitting the final sitting turns a fard prayer into nafl; add an extra rak'ah to make it even.

This lesson addresses a common mistake in prayer: forgetting to perform the final sitting and inadvertently adding an extra rak'ah. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that transforms the obligatory prayer into a supererogatory one, requiring a corrective addition. We will explore the precise rule and its application to various prayer lengths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The passage describes a person who forgets the final sitting (al-qa‘dah al-akhīrah) in a four-rak'ah fard prayer and performs a fifth rak'ah with prostration.
2
Determine the Legal Effect
The omission of a fard (the final sitting) voids the obligatory nature of the prayer; it becomes a nafl (supererogatory) prayer.
The act of adding an extra rak'ah is itself a mistake, but the prayer is not invalidated entirely.
3
Apply the Remedy
To complete the prayer correctly, the person must add a sixth rak'ah, making the total an even number of rak'ahs (six) suitable for a nafl prayer.
This remedy ensures the prayer is performed in pairs, as is recommended for voluntary prayers.
4
Extend by Analogy
The ruling is analogically applied to two-rak'ah (e.g., Fajr) and three-rak'ah (e.g., Maghrib or Witr) prayers: if one forgets the final sitting and stands for an extra rak'ah, the same principle applies – the fard becomes nafl, and one must add enough rak'ahs to make the total even.
For a two-rak'ah prayer, if you stand for a third, add a fourth to make four. For a three-rak'ah prayer, if you stand for a fourth, add a fifth to make a five-rak'ah nafl (though some later Hanafi sources may treat five as complete; the principle remains consistent).

Returning to Sitting After Erroneously Standing from Fourth Rak'ah
Q&A on returning to sitting when one stands erroneously

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on returning to the sitting position after mistakenly standing from the fourth rak'ah and identify the condition that allows return.

Core Idea
Return before the prostration of the fifth rak'ah, then perform sajdah al-sahw; prayer remains valid.

A common error in prayer is losing count. This passage addresses a specific scenario: after completing the fourth rak'ah and sitting for tashahhud, the person stands thinking it is the second rak'ah. The Hanafi ruling provides clear guidance on when to return and when the prayer becomes invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mistake
The person has completed the fourth rak'ah, sat for tashahhud, then stood up erroneously thinking it is the second rak'ah. He then remembers his error.
This is a mistake of addition, not omission.
2
Determine the condition for return
He must return to the sitting position as long as he has not performed the prostration (sajdah) of the fifth rak'ah. The key phrase is 'ما لم يسجد للخامسة' (as long as he has not prostrated for the fifth).
Prostration here means the two prostrations of a full rak'ah.
3
Understand the consequence if the condition is missed
If he prostrates for the fifth before remembering, the fard prayer becomes nafl (supererogatory). He must complete it as nafl and then pray a fresh fard prayer.
This is based on general Hanafi rules for added rak'ahs.
4
Note the compensatory action
After returning to the sitting position, he must perform sajdah al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness) at the end of his prayer. Then he completes the prayer with taslim.
Sajdah al-sahw is wajib for this mistake.

Core Idea
Sujood al-tilawah is wajib only when the recitation itself obligates it; the parties and context determine the requirement.

Sujood al-tilawah is a prostration performed upon reciting or hearing specific Quranic verses. The Hanafi school holds it as wajib (obligatory) under certain conditions. Rulings on Salat al-Marid (Prayer of the Sick)
Q&A rulings on prayer adaptations for the sick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a sick person's prayer across each level of physical incapacity, and apply the rule that only head gestures are accepted for ima.

Core Idea
The sick person's prayer adapts to ability but never drops; only head gestures are valid for ima.

This lesson presents a concise Q&A from the Hanafi tradition on how a sick person performs Salah when unable to stand, bow, prostrate, or sit. The rulings follow a clear hierarchy: from sitting to lying down to gesturing, with a strict rule that only head motions are accepted for ima. Understanding this sequence ensures you know the fiqh of concessions for the ill.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default concession
If the sick person cannot stand, he prays sitting (qā‘id) with actual ruku and sujud if possible. This is the first level of concession.
Sitting is the default position when standing is difficult; the prayer remains otherwise normal.
2
Apply the next level: inability to bow/prostrate
If ruku and sujud are impossible, the person sits and gestures (īmā’) with his head, making the sujud gesture lower than the ruku gesture. No object should be brought to the face.

3

If sitting is not possible, the person lies on his back (mustalqī) with feet toward the qibla and head elevated so the face faces the qibla, and prays with gestures. Lying on his side (muḍṭaji‘) with face toward the qibla is also permissible.
Both positions require the face to face the qibla; on the back, the feet must also point to the qibla.
4
Limit the gesture to the head only
Gestures are made only with the head. If the person cannot move his head, he does not gesture with eyes, eyebrows, or heart; instead, he delays the prayer until he is able.
The Hanafi school is strict: no substitute for head movement; delay is the only option.
5
Handle the special case: can stand but cannot bow
If a person can stand but cannot bow or prostrate, standing is not required. It is better to pray sitting with gestures. However, if he chooses to stand and gesture, his prayer is permissible.
The ruling shows that the inability to bow/prostrate cancels the requirement to stand; sitting is preferred but not obligatory.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi discussion on the definition of travel distance that triggers the concessions (rukhsa) like qasr (shortening prayer). The passage is a commentary and fatwa discussion, combining classical texts with a modern application.

The author begins by quoting al-Hidayah: the travel that changes rulings is a journey of three days by moderate camel pace or walking. This is the criterion. From Abu Hanifa, there is also an estimation by 'marahil' (stages), which is close. The correct view according to al-Hidayah is that it is not estimated by farsakhs, but this is disputed among later Hanafis. Al-Kifayah lists which rulings change: qasr, breaking fast, extending masah on khuff to three days, dropping Jumu'ah and 'Eid, dropping udhiyah, and restriction on free woman traveling without mahram.

Ibn Abidin notes that al-Hidayah's 'it is the correct' is to reject the estimation by farsakhs used by 'general scholars'. Yet later scholars differed on the farsakh equivalent: 21, 18, or 15 farsakhs. The fatwa is on 18 because it is the middle value. However, the Imams of Khwarazm gave fatwa on 15 (the third).

The passage then provides a practical fatwa for modern times. Since people use fast transportation, how do we apply the three-day measure? The author argues that the jurists ruled that fast travel (horse, mail) covering the distance in two days still triggers qasr. So the criterion is the distance, not the actual time. He then calculates: 18 farsakhs = 54 miles = 98 km; 15 farsakhs = 45 miles = 82 km; some use 16 farsakhs = 88 km. He leans toward 18 as middle.

A significant discussion follows: Ibn al-Humam in al-Fath considered that a person with karamah (miracle of folding distance) who travels a three-day journey in an hour would not shorten, because the 'illa (cause) for qasr is the presumption of hardship (mazannat al-mashaqqa), which is absent. The author refutes this: the cause itself is the travel of that distance, not actual hardship. Since the jurists allowed qasr for fast travelers (horse, mail) despite less hardship, the principle is uniform. Therefore, a person by karamah or by modern plane covers the same distance and must shorten. The author concludes that if planes existed in Ibn al-Humam's time, he would not have needed to use the karamah example.

الكرامة (al-karamah): A miracle granted to a saint (wali) by which they can cover great distances in a short time (tayy al-ard).

Intention to Stay and Traveler's Prayer
This passage explains how intention to stay affects the traveler's obligation to shorten or complete prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a traveler becomes a resident (must complete prayer) if he intends to stay 15 days, and remains a traveler if he intends less or indefinite.

Core Idea
Intending to stay 15 days changes the traveler to a resident, requiring full prayer.

How long must a traveler intend to stay in a place before he is no longer considered a traveler? The Hanafi school sets a clear threshold: 15 days. Learn the rule for different intentions and the case of indefinite stay, even for years.

Guided Path
1
Identify the threshold
If a traveler intends to stay in a town or village for 15 days or more, he becomes a resident (muqim) and must pray all four-rak'ah prayers completely.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools differ (4 days).
2
Apply for less than 15 days
If the intention is to stay less than 15 days, he remains a traveler and shortens his prayers.
3
Handle indefinite stay
If a traveler has no fixed intention to stay 15 days (e.g., says 'I will leave tomorrow' but never does), he remains a traveler even if he stays for years. He must shorten his prayers indefinitely.
This is based on the principle that the ruling follows the intention, not the duration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for the validity of Jumu'ah prayer and differentiate between the positions of Abu Hanifa and his companions regarding the sermon and congregation.

Core Idea
Jumu'ah requires a ruler-authorized imam, Zuhr time, a sermon before prayer, and a congregation of at least four including the imam.

This lesson examines the essential conditions for Jumu'ah prayer according to the Hanafi school. We will explore the requirement of authority, timing, the sermon, and the minimum congregation, noting the internal differences among the early Hanafi imams.

Guided Path
1

2
Understand the third condition – time

The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

In practice, Hanafi mosques follow the view of Abu Hanifa, requiring a minimum of four. However, some may follow Abu Yusuf if needed.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi and does not affect the overall validity across the school.

The Condition of Public Permission (al-Idhn al-ʿAmm) for Jumu'ah
Expansion and resolution of a condition from Nawadir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the status of public permission (al-idhn al-amm) as a condition for Jumu'ah, identify its source in Nawadir, and apply Ibn Abidin's resolution for multi-location situations.

Core Idea
Public permission is a widely practiced condition from Nawadir, not core texts, and is waived when Jumu'ah is held in multiple locations.

This lesson delves into a unique condition for Jumu'ah that is not found in the core Hanafi texts (Zahir al-Riwaya) but appears in later works: public permission (al-idhn al-amm). We will explore its meaning, its authority in the Nawadir, a practical problem in military camps, and the resolution offered by later jurists like Ibn Abidin.

Guided Path
1
Understand the meaning of al-idhn al-amm
Public permission means that the doors of the mosque are open and all people are allowed to enter and pray Jumu'ah. If a group locks themselves in to pray, it is invalid.
2
Identify the authority of this condition
Al-idhn al-amm is not mentioned in the Zahir al-Riwaya (e.g., al-Hidaya). It is from the Nawadir (rare narrations). Despite this, it is adopted in many later reliable works like al-Kanz, al-Wiqaya, etc.
Authority weight: Nawadir.
3
Recognize the practical problem in military camps
In military camps, only residents are allowed to enter; outsiders are prevented. This would violate public permission. The condition seems to invalidate Jumu'ah there.
4
Apply Ibn Abidin's resolution
Ibn Abidin distinguished: if Jumu'ah is held only in one place, public permission is required. If held in multiple locations, the condition is waived because no one is deprived of Jumu'ah entirely.
This resolution uses the reasoning (ta'lil) of prevention of loss (tafwit).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi is to consider public permission necessary in single-location situations, but not when multiple locations exist. This allows Jumu'ah in camps if the camp has its own mosque and others can attend elsewhere.
The fatwa is based on the non-essential nature of the condition (from Nawadir) and the general permissibility.

The second adhan is given when the imam ascends the minbar to deliver the khutbah. This is the established Hanafi practice. Some later scholars mention that the first adhan is the regular adhan, and the second is called the iqamah? But here both are called adhan. In Hanafi fiqh, the first adhan is sunnah, and the second is also sunnah but with the specific timing.

Key Terms
زالت الشمس (zalat al-shams): The sun passed its zenith; the time for Zuhr prayer begins.

Excused Persons Praying Zuhr in Congregation on Friday
Ruling on excused and imprisoned persons praying zuhr in congregation on Friday

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for excused persons praying Zuhr in congregation on Friday and explain why it is makruh.

Core Idea
Excused persons must pray Zuhr individually on Friday; congregation is makruh.

This short passage addresses a practical question: what should those who are excused from Friday prayer do for Zuhr? The clear answer is that they should pray individually, as congregational prayer on that day is disliked. The ruling preserves the unique status of the Friday congregation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is praying Zuhr in congregation on Friday by those who have a valid excuse from Friday prayer. The ruling is makruh (disliked).
Makruh here is tanzihi, not tahrimi.
2
Understand the reason for karahah
The reason is to avoid creating a separate congregation that resembles the Friday congregation, which undermines the unique obligation of Jumu'ah.
The permission of the ruler or his deputy is required for a valid Friday congregation.
3
Apply the correct practice
The excused should pray Zuhr individually (furada) and not in congregation.
This includes prisoners, the sick, travelers, etc.

Commentary
This is a clear ruling that for those who are granted a legitimate excuse from Friday prayer (such as severe illness, fear, or imprisonment), it is makruh tanzihi (disliked but not sinful) for them to gather and pray zuhr in congregation on Friday. Instead, they should pray zuhr individually. This is to avoid creating a separate congregation that might resemble the Friday congregation, which is reserved for the obligatory communal Friday prayer. The Hanafi school maintains that the legitimacy of Friday prayer requires the permission of the ruler or his deputy; thus, private congregations would undermine that.

Key Terms
المعذورون (al-ma'dhurūn): Those who have a valid religious excuse (udhr) such that Friday prayer is not obligatory upon them.
