List of penalties for omitting wajib acts and ruling on sequence violation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific penalty (dam or sadaqah) for each omitted wajib act in Hajj and explain the ruling when a mutamatti' or qarin violates the order of rituals.

Core Idea
Leaving a wajib in Hajj generally requires a dam, but some omissions only require sadaqah; violating order also incurs a dam.

In the Hanafi school, Hajj has a set of obligatory (wajib) acts. If a pilgrim omits or delays one, they must offer a compensatory sacrifice (dam) or charity (sadaqah). The following passage from a classic text details the specific penalties for various omissions and addresses what happens if the prescribed order of rituals is disrupted for a mutamatti' or qarin pilgrim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main categories of omission
The passage lists several wajib acts of Hajj (standing at Muzdalifah, sa'i, stoning, leaving Arafat early, Tawaf al-Wida'). For each, note whether the penalty is a dam (sheep) or sadaqah (charity).
Dam is the default; sadaqah is specified for minor omissions.
2
Apply the penalty for stoning violations
Leaving all stoning or most pebbles requires a dam; leaving one jamrah on one day requires sadaqah. Understand the threshold: 'most' vs. 'some'.
The text uses 'اكثر الحصيات' (most pebbles) and 'رمي إحدى الجمار' (one jamrah).

Analyze Tawaf al-Wida' penalties
Leaving four circuits or the entire tawaf requires a dam; leaving three circuits requires sadaqah. Note that returning to Makkah and performing the tawaf cancels the penalty.
The exception: if the pilgrim returns and performs tawaf, no penalty.
4
Understand the order violation for mutamatti' and qarin
If a mutamatti' or qarin slaughters before stoning Jamrat al-Aqabah, or shaves before slaughtering, they must offer a dam.
This applies specifically to those performing tamattu' or qiran Hajj.

Core Idea
Omitting a wajib in Hajj requires a penalty: blood sacrifice (dam), charity (sadaqah), or a camel (badanah) for severe cases.

Hajj comprises obligatory acts (wajibat) whose omission does not invalidate the pilgrimage but requires a compensatory penalty. This passage from Al-Tashil al-Daruri outlines the specific penalties for key omissions, distinguishing between major lapses (dam) and minor ones (sadaqah). Determine the prescribed penalty

The exception is Tawaf al-Ziyarah in janabah, which requires a camel (badanah).

Violation of Order in Hajj Rituals
Rulings on violating the required order of Hajj rituals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required order for mutamatti', qarin, and mufrid in Hajj, and identify when violating that order necessitates a dam.

Core Idea
Violating the required sequence in Hajj incurs a dam, even if each act is valid alone.

In Hajj, certain rituals have a prescribed order: stoning, then sacrifice (for mutamatti' and qarin), then shaving. Breaking this order—even if performed correctly—requires a blood sacrifice (dam) as compensation. This lesson clarifies the rules based on the type of Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hajj type
Determine whether the pilgrim is mutamatti' (Tamattu'), qarin (Qiran), or mufrid (Ifrad). Each has a slightly different required order.
Mutamatti' and qarin must sacrifice (hady) as part of their Hajj; mufrid does not.
2
State the required order for that type
For mutamatti' and qarin: first stone Jamarat al-Aqabah, then slaughter the hady, then shave or cut hair. For mufrid: first stone Jamarat al-Aqabah, then shave.
The order is wajib (obligatory) in the Hanafi school.
3
Identify the violation
If the pilgrim performs an earlier ritual after a later one (e.g., shaves before stoning, or slaughters before stoning), the order is violated.
The violation occurs even if both acts are eventually completed.
4
Apply the penalty
For any violation of order, the pilgrim must offer a dam (one sheep or goat) as a penalty. This is not a nullification of Hajj, but a compensation.
The dam is in addition to any other required sacrifices.

Commentary
This passage addresses the specific situation of a qarin (someone performing Hajj and Umrah in a single ihram) who violates one of the prohibitions of ihram. The general rule is that because the qarin has two ihrams (one for each rite), he must pay two penalties (دم) for a single prohibited act — one penalty for violating his Hajj ihram and one for violating his Umrah ihram. However, there is an important exception: if the qarin passes the miqat without first assuming ihram and then later assumes ihram for both Hajj and Umrah after passing the miqat, he only owes one penalty, because at the time of passing the miqat he was not yet a qarin (he had not yet combined the intentions). The text then cites al-Durr al-Mukhtar to clarify that not every act requiring a penalty causes multiplication. For example, leaving a wajib of Hajj or cutting plants of the Haram does not double for the qarin, because those are not considered 'violations upon the ihram' but rather violations of the Haram's sanctity. The same logic applies to obligatory charity (صدقة) imposed for certain infractions. Al-Shami's gloss confirms that cutting plants of the Haram also does not multiply the penalty for the qarin, because it is a fine unrelated to ihram. However, killing game in the Haram is different: if a qarin kills game in the Haram, he owes two values (one for each ihram), because the stronger sanctity (ihram) dominates, and the penalty is solely for violating the ihram, not the Haram. Crossing the Miqat without Ihram: Ruling and Exceptions
Ruling with proof on crossing miqat without ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for crossing the miqat without ihram, identify the dam penalty for a qarin, and explain the evidence and exceptions.

Core Idea
Crossing the miqat without ihram incurs sin and dam; the obligation applies to all entrants except those with frequent needs.

This lesson covers the Hanafi position on crossing the miqat without ihram. The text establishes that any afaqi Muslim who enters Mecca or the Haram must be in ihram; failure triggers a dam. We also explore the exception for frequent need, the evidence from hadith, and the disagreement with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Recognise that the act is crossing the miqat without ihram. The ruling is that it is prohibited (haram) and requires a dam (slaughter of a sheep) as a penalty.
The ruling applies to any afaqi (resident outside miqat) who is Muslim and mukallaf.
2
Determine the penalty for multiple ihrams
If the person is a qarin (performing hajj and umrah together) or a mutamatti' who has already brought a hady, the dam is doubled because two ihrams were violated.
Dam is multiplied only for violations specifically related to ihram (e.g., crossing miqat, not cutting plants).
3
Note the exception for frequent need
Those who enter Mecca repeatedly for essential livelihood (e.g., woodcutters, drivers) are exempt from the obligation to enter in ihram.
This exception is by analogy (qiyas) and based on the practice of Ibn Umar and the Prophet's silence about such people.
4
Examine the evidence
The main evidence is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Whoever intends hajj and umrah...' and the fatwa of Ibn Abbas that even merchants must be muhrim. Hanafis reject the mafhum al-mukhalafah argument that would limit obligation to those intending ritual.
Bukhari's chapter and the example of Ibn Umar's entry without ihram (due to returning from outside miqat) are also discussed.
5
Understand the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school generally does not require ihram for entering Mecca unless one intends hajj or umrah. Hanafis, Malikis, and Hanbalis require it, except for frequent need.
The difference leads to practical consequences: sin and dam for Hanafis versus no penalty for Shafi'is in many cases.

Book of Hajj. Crossing the miqat without ihram. Question: What is the ruling for one who did not perform ihram from the miqat while intending to enter Mecca? Answer: An afaqi, Muslim, mukallaf who intends to enter Mecca, or to enter the Haram even for trade or tourism, and crosses the miqat by land, sea, or air without ihram, then either performs ihram or does not: he has sinned and a dam is incumbent upon him. For the one upon whom ihram is incumbent (the qarin and likewise the mutamatti' who has brought the hady), two dams [are due]. Similarly, the ruling for charity is also doubled; due to his transgression against his two ihrams. Except for crossing the miqat without ihram as a disconnected exception: upon him is one dam, because at that time he is not a qarin. Al-Shami said in his hashiah: Also excluded is if he cuts the plants of the Haram: the penalty does not multiply for the qarin either. [Al-Bahr] said: Because it is from the category of fines; ihram has no connection to it, unlike killing game of the Haram if the qarin kills it: he is obliged to pay two values, because it is a transgression against ihram which is multiple, and one does not look at it being a transgression against the Haram because the stronger of the two sanctities subsumes the lesser, and ihram is stronger. Thus the obligation of the value is only due to ihram, not due to the Haram; the Haram is only considered when the killer is non-muhrim. A dam is incumbent upon him. Imam al-Bukhari placed a chapter in his book: 'Entering the Haram without Ihram' then said: Ibn Umar entered as a non-muhrim; and the Prophet [SAW] only commanded talbiyah for one who intends hajj and umrah, and did not mention it for woodcutters and others. Al-Hafiz said in al-Fath: The athar of Ibn Umar is reported by Malik in al-Muwatta from Nafi' who said: 'Abdullah ibn Umar came from Mecca, until when he was at Qudayd, news of the fitna reached him, so he returned and entered Mecca without ihram. His statement: 'He did not mention it for woodcutters and others' is from the speech of the author [al-Bukhari]; its summary is that he specified ihram for one who intends hajj and umrah, and he argued by the implication (mafhum) of his statement in the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Whoever intends hajj and umrah' – its implication is that one who goes to Mecca repeatedly without intending hajj or umrah is not obligated to perform ihram. Scholars have differed on this: The well-known position of the Shafi'i school is that it is not obligatory absolutely; in one opinion it is obligatory absolutely; and for those whose entrance is frequent, there is an organized difference of opinion, and it is more appropriate that it is not obligatory. The well-known position of the three Imams is obligation; and there is a narration from each of them that it is not obligatory, and that is the view of Ibn Umar, al-Zuhri, al-Hasan, and the Zahiris. The Hanbalis affirmed the exception for those with repeated needs. The Hanafis excepted those who are within the mawaqit. Ibn Abd al-Barr claimed that the majority of the Companions and Successors were upon the view of obligation. Ibn Qudamah said in al-Mughni: The mukallaf who enters without fighting or repeated need: it is not permissible for him to cross the miqat without ihram; and this is what Abu Hanifah and some of the Shafi'i's companions said. Some others said: ihram is not obligatory; and from Ahmad [there] is that which indicates that. It has been narrated that he [Ibn Umar?] entered it without ihram. Until he said: So when this mukallaf who enters without fighting or repeated need wishes to perform ihram after crossing the miqat, he should return and perform ihram from it; if he performs ihram from [a point] before it [i.e., after crossing], then upon him is a dam, like one who intends the ritual. Question: Is there any way for the sin and dam to be remitted? Answer: If one crosses the miqat without ihram, he is obligated to return to his miqat which he crossed, or to any miqat nearer or farther. The better is to return to the miqat which he crossed. So when he returns to the miqat and performs ihram from it for hajj or umrah, the sin and dam are remitted from him. I say: Since the madhhab of Abu Hanifah, Malik, and Ahmad is that ihram is necessary for one who intends to enter Mecca, even if he does not intend either of the two rituals – and this is a narration from al-Shafi'i – and it is the madhhab of the majority of the Companions and Successors as Ibn Abd al-Barr said, it was necessary for everyone who intends to enter the Haram or Mecca to perform ihram with one of the two rituals, especially umrah, if the season is not the season of hajj. Yes, if concession were given in that for those who need to enter frequently to earn what is needed for the maintenance of their families, like drivers, by analogy with woodcutters, it would have some merit. As for those whose entrance is not frequent, and likewise those who leave Mecca to beyond the mawaqit and then return, they must enter Mecca with ihram, because they are not like woodcutters. The argument for the non-obligation of ihram by his statement 'for one who intends hajj and umrah' is not correct according to those who do not consider argument by the concept of opposition (mafhum al-mukhalafah). Moreover, this hadith is narrated by Ibn Abbas, and he gave a fatwa that no merchant or person seeking any need should enter Mecca except while in ihram, as narrated by al-Tahawi in the chapter 'Entering the Haram, is it permissible without ihram?' There were with the Prophet [SAW] on the Conquest of Mecca a hundred thousand Companions or more, as narrated by al-Bayhaqi and others – refer to Awjaz al-Masalik Sharh al-Muwatta of Imam Malik. The madhhab of the majority of the Companions was the impermissibility of entering Mecca without ihram. So by that it is known that they did not argue for the permissibility of entering without ihram by his entering on the Day of Conquest like that; because they knew the difference between necessity and non-necessity. His entering on the Day of Conquest without ihram cannot be analogized to normal circumstances; because fighting was permitted for him on that day, and the permissibility of fighting necessitates the permissibility of crossing the miqat without ihram. Because the muhrim is commanded to uncover the head and face, while the fighter needs to wear armor, cover his head, etc. So when Allah permitted fighting in Mecca for His Messenger [SAW] and his Companions for a part of the day, He permitted them to cross the miqat without ihram as well. It is established in Sahih al-Bukhari from the Prophet [SAW] that he said: 'If anyone seeks a concession from you for the fighting of the Messenger of Allah [SAW], say to him: Allah permitted it for His Messenger but did not permit it for you; He only permitted it for me for a part of the day.' So since entering without ihram is not permissible for one who does not intend permissible fighting, as mentioned by Ibn Qudamah in al-Mughni, as for the entering of Ibn Umar without ihram, that was because he returned from the hal [area outside the miqat?] and did not cross the miqat, so it was permissible for him to enter Mecca without ihram. Among the people, some object to those who leave Mecca for Medina or other distant lands and then approach Mecca in ihram. This from these [objectors] is strange; because one who intends one of the two rituals is not permitted to enter Mecca without ihram, and this is by consensus. And there is no prohibition of frequent umrah in any authentic, explicit, marfu' hadith. So preventing them from performing umrah for one who comes to Mecca from distant lands is preventing good. And Allah is the granter of all good.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling on crossing the miqat without ihram. It begins with a question and answer: A Muslim who is resident outside the miqat (afaqi), legally responsible (mukallaf), who intends to enter Mecca or the Haram even for trade or tourism, and crosses the miqat by any route without ihram, commits a sin and must offer a dam (penalty of slaughtering an animal). If he later performs ihram, the dam is still due for the act of crossing without ihram. For a qarin (one performing hajj and umrah together) or a mutamatti' (one performing umrah before hajj) who has already brought a hady (sacrificial animal), crossing without ihram incurs two dams—one for each ihram violated. An exception is noted: if someone crosses without ihram but is not a qarin at that moment (e.g., he intends only one ritual later), only one dam is due. Al-Shami's hashiah adds that cutting plants of the Haram does not double the penalty for a qarin, because it is a fine unrelated to ihram; unlike killing game in the Haram, which does double because it is a transgression against the multiple ihrams of a qarin. The author then discusses the evidence from Bukhari and the hadith of Ibn Abbas about entering the Haram without ihram. The ruling varies among schools: the Hanafi, Maliki, and Hanbali positions generally require ihram for anyone entering Mecca, even without intending hajj or umrah; the Shafi'i school has multiple opinions. The Hanafis and Hanbalis exempt those with frequent needs (e.g., woodcutters, drivers). Ibn Qudamah's view is cited: a mukallaf not engaged in fighting or frequent need must not cross without ihram. If he does, he should return to the miqat and perform ihram, avoiding sin and dam. The author emphasizes that the Hanafi position is that ihram is necessary for anyone entering Mecca, except for those with frequent needs, analogized to woodcutters. The objection based on the Prophet's conquest of Mecca (when he entered without ihram due to the need for fighting) and Ibn Umar's entry without ihram (because he did not cross the miqat) are distinguished from normal cases. The passage concludes by criticizing those who prevent others from doing frequent umrah, which is not prohibited and is a good act.

Key Terms
ميقات (miqat): The designated boundary for entering the state of ihram; crossing it without ihram is prohibited.
دم (dam): A penalty of slaughtering an animal (sheep or goat) for violating a wajib of hajj/umrah or ihram.
قارن (qarin): One who performs hajj and umrah together with a single ihram.

Ruling on Passing the Miqat Without Ihram
Series of Q&A on passing miqat without ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on passing the miqat without ihram, identify the exceptions, and explain the inter-madhhab dispute regarding the obligation of ihram for entering Mecca without intention of Hajj/Umrah.

Core Idea
Passing the miqat without ihram is a violation requiring a sacrifice, but necessity permits entry without ihram in specific cases.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar tackles a critical question in Hajj and Umrah jurisprudence: what happens if someone passes the miqat without entering the state of ihram? Through a series of Q&A, the author establishes the general ruling, clarifies exceptions, and addresses the underlying inter-madhhab disagreement. The core issue revolves around whether ihram is required for all entries into Mecca, even for purposes other than pilgrimage. We'll explore the Hanafi position, its proofs, and practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling and its exceptions
The text first states the general rule: if one passes the miqat without ihram, it is wajib to return to a miqat and perform ihram. This removes both sin and the need for a sacrifice. An exception is made for those who enter frequently for livelihood, like woodcutters or drivers.
The exception is based on qiyas and the principle of necessity (darurah).
2
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi school, along with Malik, Ahmad, and a narration from al-Shafi'i, holds that ihram is wajib for anyone entering Mecca, even without intending Hajj/Umrah. Some scholars (other Shafi'i narrations) argue it is not obligatory, citing the hadith 'for whoever intends Hajj and Umrah' (mafhum mukhalafah). The Hanafi response rejects mafhum mukhalafah as a valid proof and cites the practice of most Companions.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: Hanafis require a sacrifice for entry without ihram; the opposing view allows entry without penalty.
3
Apply the ruling to various scenarios
The passage presents four scenarios: (1) if one does not return to the miqat but proceeds with ihram, sin and dam remain; (2) if one fears missing Hajj, he does not return but slaughters a sheep; (3) if one fears for life/property, the return obligation drops; (4) repeated entries without ihram require a separate Hajj or Umrah for each entry.
Intention matters: if the year passes, a specific intention for the due rite is required.
4
Evaluate the proof from the Prophet's Conquest
The text addresses a common misunderstanding: the Prophet's entry during the Conquest of Mecca without ihram was a special dispensation due to the necessity of fighting. This is not generalizable. The hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari explicitly restricts this permission to the Prophet alone.
The Companions, including Ibn Abbas, understood this distinction and required ihram for others.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi position and its implications
The Hanafi ruling respects the sanctity of the Haram while balancing practical needs. The exception for frequent entrants shows the school's use of istihsan and qiyas. The overall ruling underscores the importance of ihram as a condition for entering Mecca.
This lesson highlights how Hanafi fiqh handles textual evidence, analogical reasoning, and necessity.
