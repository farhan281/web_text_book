Core Idea
Trade goods zakat: three Hanafi valuation methods yield different nisab thresholds for the poor.

Zakat on trade goods is obligatory when their total value reaches the nisab of gold or silver. However, the method of valuation—how to determine which nisab applies—differs among the three founding Hanafi imams. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and the real-world effect on zakat liability.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the overall hukm: zakat on trade goods is wajib when value reaches nisab. This is agreed among the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the three positions
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad each propose a different valuation method. List them precisely from the source.
Focus on the operative phrase: 'يقومها بما...' for each.
3
Analyze the reasoning behind each
Abu Hanifa aims to benefit the poor (lower nisab). Abu Yusuf ties valuation to the original purchase currency. Muhammad always uses the prevalent local currency.
Their reasons are not fully stated in the source but can be inferred from the thamarah.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: the nisab threshold differs, affecting when zakat becomes due and how much is paid.
Abu Hanifa's view makes zakat due sooner; Muhammad's may delay it.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text does not give a tarjih, but later Hanafi fatwa often adopts Muhammad's or Abu Yusuf's view.
This is an open question; check later texts for the preferred position.

Definition, Formation, and Buyer's Options in Sale (Bay')
Definition of sale, its formation through offer and acceptance, and buyer's options

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a sale (bay'), explain its formation through past-tense offer and acceptance within the same session, and identify the buyer's options of defect and sight.

Core Idea
A valid sale requires mutual consent expressed via past-tense ijab and qabul in one sitting; after contract, the buyer has options for defect and unseen items.

This page from a Hanafi textbook presents two distinct topics: a concluding ruling on the distribution of sacrificial animals (hady) and the beginning of the Book of Sales. While the first topic is a specific application of hady rules, the main focus is the foundational definition and formation of a sale. The question-and-answer format clarifies that a sale is not binding until both offer and acceptance occur in the same session, and that the buyer is protected by options if a defect appears or if the item was purchased unseen.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the butcher's wages
The first question asks whether the butcher of the hady animal can be paid from the animal itself. The answer is no; the butcher must be paid from one's own wealth. This is supported by the hadith of Ali (ra) where the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded not to give the butcher from the animal.
This is a specific ruling for the hady animal, not a general principle for all slaughter.
2
Define a sale
The text defines sale as 'the exchange of property for property with mutual consent.' Identify the three key elements: exchange (mubadala), property (mal), and mutual consent (taradi).
This definition excludes barter of services; in Hanafi fiqh, only properties (amwal) can be sold.
3
Understand the formation of a sale
A sale is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) uttered in the past tense, e.g., 'I sold' and 'I bought'. This is a Hanafi requirement; other schools may allow other tenses.
The past tense indicates immediate and binding consent.
4
Recognize the session condition
The acceptance must occur in the same sitting (majlis) as the offer. If either party leaves the sitting before acceptance, the offer becomes void. This protects the offeree from being bound by a stale offer.
This is called khiyar al-majlis (option of the session) in the Hanafi school.
5
Identify the buyer's post-sale options
After a sale is completed, the buyer has two key options: (1) khiyar al-'ayb: if a defect appears in the item, the buyer may return it or keep it; (2) khiyar ar-ru'ya: if the item was purchased unseen, the buyer may confirm the sale or return it upon seeing it.
These options are granted to protect the buyer from hidden faults or misrepresentation.

Explains that acceptance must occur in the same session for the sale to be binding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule of khiyar al-majlis and explain why acceptance must occur in the same council.

Core Idea
A sale is not binding until acceptance occurs in the same session; leaving the council voids the offer.

This lesson explores the option of the council (khiyar al-majlis) in Hanafi fiqh. The passage explains that an offer alone does not bind the other party; instead, the offeree has the option to accept or reject only while both parties remain in the contract session. If either party leaves before acceptance, the offer becomes void. Understanding this ruling is essential for the validity of sales contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making an offer (ijab) in a sale. The Hanafi ruling is that the offer alone does not bind; acceptance is needed in the same council.
2
Understand the condition of the council
The offeree must accept while both parties remain in the same physical majlis. If either leaves before acceptance, the offer becomes void.
Leaving includes any deliberate departure from the place of negotiation.
3
Distinguish from other schools
Note that the Shafi'i school interprets the option as continuing until the parties physically separate, not just within the council.
This difference arises from different interpretations of the hadith on khiyar.

The Duration of Khayar al-Shart: Three Days or More?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the maximum duration of khayar al-shart.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that the option of condition cannot exceed three days, and explain the Sahibayn's different view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa limits khayar al-shart to three days; the Sahibayn allow longer if agreed.

The option of condition (khayar al-shart) is a key feature of sales, allowing parties to retain the right to cancel within a specified time. A critical question is how long this option can last. This lesson explores the classical Hanafi dispute on the maximum duration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is stipulating an option period in a sale. The basic ruling is that it is permissible (mubah) to set an option.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa sets a maximum of three days; any longer invalidates the option. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) allow longer periods if a specific duration is agreed and both parties consent.
The Sahibayn's view gives more weight to mutual agreement.
3
Understand the basis of each view
Abu Hanifa's restriction likely aims to prevent excessive uncertainty (gharar) that could lead to disputes. The Sahibayn emphasize freedom of contract and the validity of mutual consent.
Explicit dalil is not quoted; reasoning is inferred.
4
Note the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, a sale with a four-day option is invalid (fasid) because the option exceeds three days. Under the Sahibayn, it would be valid if both parties agreed to four days.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the difference).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Although the passage does not give a preference, later Hanafi practice (mufta bihi) generally follows Abu Hanifa's three-day limit as the standard ruling.
Some later fatwas may tolerate longer periods by mutual agreement, but the relied-upon position is three days.

Who Bears the Loss? Ownership and Liability in Khayar al-Shart
Rulings on ownership and liability during the option period for both seller's and buyer's option, including defect scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine who is liable if the goods perish during the option period, based on whether the seller or buyer has the option, and distinguish between fungible and non-fungible goods.

Core Idea
If the seller has the option, ownership stays with him; if the buyer has the option, a dispute arises over ownership, but liability for loss always falls on the buyer.

A key question when a sale includes an option: who owns the item during the option period? The answer determines liability if the item is lost or damaged. This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings for seller's option and buyer's option, including the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on buyer's ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify who has the option
Determine whether the seller or the buyer stipulated the option (khayar al-shart). This affects ownership during the option period.
2
Apply the rule for seller's option
If the seller has the option, the sold item remains in the seller's ownership. The buyer holds it as a trust (amanah). If it perishes in the buyer's hand, the buyer must compensate: pay the like (mithl) if fungible, or the value (qimah) if non-fungible.
3
Apply the rule for buyer's option
If the buyer has the option, ownership does not transfer according to Abu Hanifa; the buyer does not own it. According to the Sahibayn, the buyer owns it. However, in both views, if the goods perish, the buyer is liable to pay the full price (thaman).
The dispute on ownership does not affect liability for perishing; the buyer always pays.
4
Distinguish between permanent and temporary defects
If a permanent defect occurs (e.g., loss of a limb), the rule is the same as perishing: the buyer pays the price. If a temporary defect occurs (e.g., illness), the buyer retains the option; if the period expires with the defect still present, the sale becomes binding.
5
Summarize the liability rules
Liability for loss or permanent defect: buyer pays price (or like/value if seller's option). Temporary defect: option continues. The buyer's liability is universal regardless of who has the option.

Seeing Courtyard vs. Inner Rooms: Fatwa Changes with Custom
Intra-Hanafi dispute resolved by custom change

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between the matn authors and Imam Zufar regarding sufficiency of seeing only the courtyard for khiyar al-ru'yah, and apply the rule that fatwa changes when custom changes.

Core Idea
Fatwa on sufficiency of seeing only the courtyard changed because custom changed – a ruling based on 'urf may shift when 'urf shifts.

In the Hanafi school, the conditions for khiyar al-ru'yah (option of inspection) have been debated. The original position in the matn (e.g., al-Quduri) stated that seeing the courtyard alone terminates the option. However, Imam Zufar objected that one must enter and see the inner rooms. This lesson explores how the later authorities reconciled these views by recognizing that the earlier ruling was based on custom that no longer applies, thus making Zufar's view the operative fatwa today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the exact question being debated: does seeing only the courtyard of a house suffice to terminate the buyer's khiyar al-ru'yah, or must the inner rooms also be seen? The buyer has the right to cancel if the property does not match the description; but seeing the property terminates that right. The dispute is about how much visual inspection is needed.
2
State the matn position and its basis
The authors of the matn (e.g., al-Quduri) held that seeing the courtyard suffices. This ruling was based on the custom ('urf) in Kufa at that time – houses were built with uniform layouts, so viewing the exterior gave sufficient knowledge of the interior.
3
Present Imam Zufar's objection
Imam Zufar argued that houses differ in design; the exterior alone does not convey knowledge of the interior. Therefore, the buyer must enter and see the inner rooms to terminate the option. His proof is the reality of architectural variation.
4
Understand the reconciliation by later authorities
Al-Hidayah explains that the matn's answer was correct for its time and place because houses were uniform. But today, with greater variation, the ruling changes. Thus, the later authorities (al-Bahr, Jami' al-Fusulayn) state that fatwa is given on Zufar's view.
5
Apply the principle to modern fatwa
Using the principle that rulings can change with custom, apply Zufar's view in contemporary fatwa. Today, a buyer who only sees the courtyard has not sufficiently inspected the property and retains the right to cancel after seeing the interior.
