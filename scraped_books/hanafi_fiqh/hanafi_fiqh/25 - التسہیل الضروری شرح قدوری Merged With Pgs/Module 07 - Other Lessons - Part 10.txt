Learning Objective

Core Idea
An oath follows custom: bread from flour breaks the oath; raw flour does not.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common oath scenario: someone swears not to eat a certain flour, then eats bread made from it, or eats the raw flour directly. The Hanafi ruling hinges on whether the thing sworn is customarily eaten in that form. Let's unpack the reasoning and the rule.

The person swears not to eat 'this flour'. Note that flour is not customarily eaten raw.
The literal object is 'al-daqiq'.
2
Analyze the first scenario: eating bread
If the person eats bread made from that flour, the oath is broken. Because flour is primarily consumed as bread, the oath applies to its customary product.
This is the mufta bihi ruling.
3
Analyze the second scenario: eating raw flour
If the person eats the raw flour directly (istaf), the oath is not broken. Raw flour is not customarily eaten, so the literal meaning is applied.
'Istaffa' means to eat dry flour without moisture.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
In oaths, the literal meaning is given priority if feasible. If the literal is not customarily consumed, the oath is interpreted according to custom ('urf').
This principle is from Al-Hidayah.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may adhere strictly to the literal wording, so they might not consider bread as breaking the oath.
The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih.

An oath not to eat cooked food is broken by meat, not vegetables, because custom restricts the general term.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common question: if someone swears not to eat 'cooked food' (al-ṭabīkh), what exactly does that include? In Hanafi fiqh, the general term is specified by custom ('urf), so it refers primarily to meat cooked in water. However, the swearer's intention is also considered. Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the oath
The person swore not to eat 'cooked food' (al-ṭabīkh). The question is what counts as a breach.
2
Recognize the default ruling by custom
In Hanafi fiqh, 'cooked food' customarily means meat cooked in water. So eating cooked vegetables or lentils does not break the oath.
This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) in consideration of custom.
3
Apply the exception for intention
If the swearer intended a broader meaning (e.g., all cooked items), that intention is honored because it is a self-imposed severity (tashdīd).
Intention overrides the default custom.
4

Drinking the meat broth also breaks the oath because it contains meat particles and is called 'cooked food'.
Broth is considered part of the meat dish.

When you swear an oath on a specific thing, what happens if that thing changes over time? This passage from al-Hidayah tackles exactly that: a man swears not to eat the meat of a particular lamb, but the lamb grows into a ram. Does his oath still apply? The answer reveals a key principle in Hanafi oath jurisprudence: oaths are interpreted by the intended subject and its identity, not by temporary attributes.

The Indefinite and Definite ‘Dar’ in Oaths
Definition-based rulings for indefinite vs definite 'dar'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath involving an indefinite 'dar' (daran) versus a definite 'dar' (hadhihi al-dar) and explain the legal basis in the distinction between the plot and the building.

Core Idea
An indefinite ‘dar’ requires a building; a definite ‘dar’ retains its name even as a ruin.

Oaths about entering houses hinge on the legal definition of ‘dar’. The Hanafi school distinguishes between indefinite and definite references: the building is merely a description (sifah) of the land. This lesson unpacks the rule from al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Shami, showing how the same physical ruin leads to different outcomes depending on whether the oath named a specific house or any house.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath wording
Determine whether the oath uses an indefinite noun (e.g., 'a house' – daran) or a definite noun (e.g., 'this house' – hadhihi al-dar). This classification is the starting point for the ruling.
In Arabic, the indefinite (nakirah) and definite (ma'rifah) affect legal meanings.
2
Check the state of the building
Determine if the building is completely ruined (no building at all) or partially standing. For indefinite 'dar', the building is essential; for definite, the plot matters.
The source distinguishes 'dar' as land (arsah) plus a building as a description.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule for indefinite 'dar'
For an oath on 'a house' (daran), if there is no building, the oath is not broken because the name 'dar' requires a building to be present. The building is a necessary description for the indefinite.
The description (sifah) defines the indefinite noun.
4
Apply the Hanafi rule for definite 'dar'
For an oath on 'this house' (hadhihi al-dar), the name persists even if the building is destroyed or replaced, because the definite is identified by pointing, not description. Thus, entering the plot breaks the oath.
Always check if the condition or motive of the oath requires the building; if yes, then it may affect the ruling.
5
Consider exceptions
If the building was a condition or motive for the oath, even a definite 'dar' may require the building. But otherwise, the general rule applies.
The source says 'illa idha kanat shartan aw da'iyatan'.

The Nature of ‘Bayt’ and Special Cases in Oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when an oath about 'bayt' is broken, including cases of collapse, being inside, and entering places of worship.

Core Idea
‘Bayt’ is defined by function (overnight stay), not structure; places of worship are not ‘bayt’.

Unlike ‘dar’, the term ‘bayt’ focuses on purpose rather than land. A house becomes a ‘bayt’ only if it can be used for overnight stay. This lesson explores the rules for oaths involving ‘bayt’, including the peculiar situation of swearing not to enter while already inside, and why entering the Kaaba does not break an oath against entering a ‘bayt’.

Oaths on Beds: When Does an Extra Covering Break the Oath?
Fatwa answers on oaths about beds with coverings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where an added covering is considered a follower of the bed (breaking the oath) and where it is considered a separate entity (not breaking), and state the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Core Idea
A covering on a bed is not a follower; only a separate bed counts as a different subject.

This passage from the chapter on oaths addresses a common scenario: if someone swears not to sit or sleep on a specific bed, what happens when they use an extra covering or place another bed on top? The rulings hinge on whether the added layer is considered part of the original bed (a 'follower') or an independent bed. Within the Hanafi school, there is a difference of opinion between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf on this point. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying oath rulings in daily life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's subject
First, determine whether the oath is on a specific bed ('this bed') or an unspecified bed ('a bed'). This affects whether moving to another bed on top breaks the oath.
If unspecified, any bed counts; if specified, only that exact bed.
2
Classify the added item
Decide whether the extra layer is a separate bed or just a covering (like a carpet or blanket). A separate bed creates a new subject; a covering does not.
A covering is considered a 'follower' (tabi') of the original bed.
3
Apply the follower rule
If the added item is a covering, sitting or sleeping on it is still considered sitting/sleeping on the original bed, so the oath is broken. If it is a separate bed (and the oath was specific), the oath is not broken because the person is not on the original bed.
Exception: if the oath was unspecified, even a separate bed breaks the oath.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
For the specific case of a cover (qiram), Imam Muhammad holds that it is not a follower, so the oath is not broken. Abu Yusuf holds that it is placed for extra softness, making it a follower, so the oath is broken.
Both are valid Hanafi positions, but al-Jawhara declares Muhammad's view as the correct one.
5
State the mufta bihi
According to the relied-upon position (al-sahih), an added cover does not render the person as sitting/sleeping on the original bed for oath purposes, so the oath is not broken.
This is the view of Imam Muhammad.

Oath on Riding a Slave's Animal
Fatwa on riding a slave's animal in oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an oath to avoid a person's animal is tested by riding that person's slave's animal, and identify the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Riding a slave's animal is riding the master's property; the oath is not broken.

This lesson examines a specific oath scenario: a man swears he will not ride the animal of a certain person, but then rides the animal of that person's authorized slave. Does he violate his oath? The Hanafi school splits on this, but the relied-upon view holds that no violation occurs because the slave's animal belongs to the master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the act
Recognize that the oath is to avoid riding 'so-and-so's animal,' and the act is riding the animal of so-and-so's authorized slave (al-ma'dhun).
The slave's authorization for trade does not change ownership.
2
Understand the two positions
Note the difference: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say no oath-breaking; Muhammad al-Shaybani says yes, because the animal is attributed to the slave, and the oath referred to 'his animal.'
The text reports 'قولهما' (the two) and then 'قال محمد يحنث' – the two are likely Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
3
Recall the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (as in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira) is that he does not break his oath, because the true owner is the master.
This is consistent with the maxim 'al-'abd wa ma fi yadihi li-mawlahu.'
4
Apply the reasoning
Since the slave's acquisitions belong to the master, riding the slave's animal is effectively riding the master's animal – thus the oath is not breached.
The oath is against riding the master's animal, and the master's ownership extends to the slave's property.

Learning Objective

Continuation after an oath is a new act; immediate removal prevents violation.

This is specific to oaths; other legal contexts may treat continuity differently.

Meaning of 'Ability' (Istita'a) in Oaths
Fatwa on the meaning of 'ability' in oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the default meaning of 'ability' in an oath is physical health and absence of obstacles, and differentiate between diyanatan and qada'an acceptance of contrary intention.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'ability' means being healthy and unimpeded, not metaphysical power.

When someone swears, 'I will come if I am able,' what counts as inability? The Hanafi school clarifies that the customary understanding of ability (istita'at al-sihha) is physical health and the absence of obstacles like illness or authority. If the oath-taker intended a different (theological) meaning, it is accepted only in conscience, not in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the term 'ability'
Recognize that the oath includes a condition: 'if I am able' (in istata'a). The meaning of this term is at issue.
The term istita'a appears in the Quran and hadith with multiple meanings.
2
Understand the default meaning
The Hanafi school states that the default interpretation is 'ability of health and removal of obstacles' (istita'at al-sihha wa rafa' al-mawani'). This includes being free from illness, authority (sultan), madness, forgetfulness, etc.
This is the customary meaning (al-muta'araf) in oaths.
3
Distinguish from the theological 'true ability'
The text contrasts this with 'al-qudra al-haqiqiyya' – the true ability that Allah creates at the moment of the act. That is not the default meaning in oaths.
The theological concept is relevant for other contexts (e.g. divine decree), not for oaths.
4
Apply the ruling for failure due to obstacles
If the person fails to fulfill the oath due to a valid obstacle (e.g., illness), he does not break his oath (la yahnath). The oath is considered unfulfilled but not violated.
This is because the condition (ability) was not met.
5
Note the diyanatan/qada'an distinction
If the oath-taker intended the theological meaning (true ability), his intention is believed diyanatan (between him and Allah) but not qada'an (in court) because it contradicts the apparent customary meaning.
This respects the individual's conscience while maintaining a standard for legal rulings.

The Nawadir take it generally; the Zahir al-Riwayah restrict it to unspecified vows. The prevailing fatwa according to this text is the choice, especially for conditional vows not intended.

كفارة يمين (kaffarat al-yamin): The expiation for breaking an oath: feeding 10 poor, clothing them, freeing a slave, or fasting 3 days.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, etc.) representing the primary transmitted rulings of the Hanafi school.

Vows of Charity: The Hanafi Ruling on Giving All Wealth
Hadith proofs and istihsan analysis for vows of charity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling for a vow to give all wealth, apply the istihsan argument based on the zakat analogy, and state the exception for family needs.

Core Idea
A vow to give all wealth obligates only zakatable assets unless explicitly intended all; then all must be given but family necessities may be kept.

This lesson explores a classic Hanafi problem: someone vows to give all his wealth in charity. Does he truly have to give everything? The answer lies in balancing the literal wording of the vow with the underlying principles of obligation in Islam, particularly the model of zakat. We will trace the argument from hadith, through qiyas and istihsan, to the practical fatwa that protects the vow-taker and his family from hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hadith Foundation
Start with the hadiths cited: vows of obedience must be fulfilled; vows of disobedience must not and require kaffarah. This establishes the framework for all vow rulings.
The hadith from 'Imran ibn Husayn distinguishes two types of vows.
2
Understand the Qiyas Argument
A superficial qiyas would argue that a vow to give 'all wealth' means every single asset, because 'wealth' (مال) encompasses all movable and immovable property.
This would create extreme hardship.
3
Analyze the Istihsan Argument
The Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) by analogy with zakat: the obligation of charity (sadaqah) is attached only to certain categories of wealth—gold, silver, trade goods, and grazing animals. Therefore, the vow should be limited to those categories.
This is grounded in the principle that vows are interpreted in light of known obligations.
4
Apply the Scenarios of Intention
If the vow is general ('all wealth'), only zakatable assets are wajib. But if the vow explicitly intends all wealth (e.g., 'I give every dirham I own'), then all becomes wajib, with an exception for basic family needs until the person earns again.
The text says: 'if he intended by this vow all that he owns, all of that enters his vow, because he made it difficult on himself.'
5
State the Practical Fatwa
The mufta bihi: for a vow to give all wealth, the person must give zakatable assets (gold, silver, trade goods, grazing animals) even if less than nisab. If he intended all wealth, he gives all but may keep necessities for himself and his family until he earns again.
This is from al-Jawharat al-Nayyira and the Hidayah tradition.

Fulfilling a Vow to Give All Wealth: The Hanafi Exception
Q&A on the obligation of a vow to give all wealth

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a vow to give all possessions and explain the exception to retain subsistence.

Core Idea
A vow to give all wealth is binding, but you may keep enough to live on until you earn again.

You have vowed to give away everything you own in charity. Is that immediately binding? The Hanafi school says yes, but with a practical mercy: you may first retain enough to cover your own and your family's basic needs until you can earn new income. This lesson examines the ruling from al-Quduri's matn as presented in a Q&A format, highlighting the balance between fulfilling a vow and preventing hardship.

1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a nathr (vow) to give charity of everything one owns. The ruling is that it is obligatory (wajib) to fulfill it.
The hukm level here is wajib, not fard, because the vow is self-imposed; the foundation is zanni.

Continuity of Fasting in Kaffarah: Evidence and Dispute
Evidence for consecutive fasting in kaffarah and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi evidence for requiring consecutive fasting in kaffarah al-yamin and evaluate the Shafi'i/Maliki counter-argument.

Core Idea
The qira'ah of Ibn Mas'ud, treated as khabar mashhoor, establishes the requirement of consecutive fasting.

You may have noticed that the Qur'anic verse for kaffarah al-yamin (5:89) does not explicitly say the three days must be consecutive. Yet the Hanafi school requires consecutiveness. In this lesson, we examine the unique evidence behind this ruling—the recitation of Ibn Mas'ud—and the scholarly dispute with the Shafi'i and Maliki schools.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: the three days of fasting in kaffarah al-yamin must be consecutive (tataabu'). Separation is not valid.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opposing school and their view
The Shafi'i and Maliki schools hold that consecutiveness is not required; separation is permissible. Their proof is the absolute wording of the Qur'anic text.
Imam Ahmad is reported to require consecutiveness in one view.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponent argues that since the verse says 'three days' without 'consecutive', the literal meaning allows either consecutive or separate days.
This is a straightforward application of the principle that an absolute text (mutlaq) is to be left as is.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response relies on the qira'ah of Ibn Mas'ud, which adds the word 'mutatabi'at'. This qira'ah is considered like a khabar mashhoor, allowing it to add to the absolute text. The ruling remains even if the recitation is abrogated.
Early authorities (Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, etc.) affirmed consecutiveness.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under the Hanafi school, a person must fast three consecutive days; if any day is missed, the kaffarah is invalid and must be restarted. Under the Shafi'i school, the person may choose to fast consecutively or separately.
This difference matters in everyday fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the necessity of consecutive fasting (tataabu') when one chooses the fasting option in kaffarah al-yamin. The questioner points out that the Qur'anic verse (5:89) only says 'three days' without specifying consecutiveness. The Hanafi answer provides evidence from the recitation (qira'ah) of Ibn Mas'ud who read 'three consecutive days'. This reading is considered a khabar mashhoor (well-known report) by al-Hidayah, which allows adding to the general text of the Qur'an. Abu Bakr al-Jassas cites several early authorities (Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, Ibrahim, Qatadah, Tawus) who affirmed consecutiveness. The Hanafi position distinguishes between the recitation (which may be abrogated) and the ruling (which remains established). The opponents, Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik, hold that separation is permissible because the verse is absolute. The text notes that Imam Ahmad's apparent view requires consecutiveness. Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir elaborates on the fame of this qira'ah in Abu Hanifah's time. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a Hanafi must fast three consecutive days; a Shafi'i may fast them separately if he chooses.

Key Terms
قِرَاءة ابن مسعود (qira'ah Ibn Mas'ud): The recitation variant of Ibn Mas'ud that includes the word 'mutatabi'at' (consecutive) after 'three days'.
خبر مشهور (khabar mashhoor): A report transmitted by a few in the first generation but later widespread; has legal force near to tawatur in Hanafi usul.
تتابع (tataabu'): Consecutive days of fasting without break.

Paying Kaffarah Before Breaking the Oath
Ruling on pre-payment of kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pre-paying kaffarah and explain why it does not discharge the obligation.

Core Idea
Pre-paying kaffarah before breaking an oath is invalid—the obligation arises only upon the breach.

In Hanafi fiqh, the obligation of kaffarah for an oath is contingent upon the act of breaking the oath (hanth). This short ruling addresses a common question: if someone pays the expiation ahead of time, does it count? The answer from the Hanafi school is clear and illustrates a fundamental principle about the timing of obligations.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
The text explicitly states that if one presents the kaffarah before breaking the oath, it does not suffice and must be paid again after breaking.
2
Identify the underlying legal principle
The obligation (wujub) is conditional upon the occurrence of hanth. Payment before the condition is fulfilled is not valid for discharging the obligation.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that an obligation must be performed after its cause is realized.
3
Compare with Shafi'i position
Acknowledge that Imam al-Shafi'i is reported to allow pre-payment in general, but disallows when kaffarah is fasting.

Answer: If he presents the kaffarah before breaking the oath, it does not suffice for what becomes obligatory, and he must perform it a second time after breaking.

Commentary
This is a concise ruling on the timing of kaffarah in relation to the act of breaking an oath (hanth). The question is whether paying the expiation before breaking the oath fulfills the obligation. The answer is clear: the kaffarah only becomes obligatory at the moment the oath is broken. If one pays it beforehand, it does not count, and the person must pay again after breaking the oath. This is because the obligation (wujub) has not yet occurred at the time of the earlier payment; it is a conditional obligation contingent on the breach. The Hanafi position is stated explicitly, and a later note (seen on this page) adds that Imam al-Shafi'i allows it except when the kaffarah is fasting, but that is not part of this immediate ruling. This ruling underscores the principle that obligations must be performed after their cause (sabab) is fully realized.

Key Terms
حنث (hanth): The act of breaking an oath; doing what one swore not to do, or not doing what one swore to do.
