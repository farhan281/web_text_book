Mufawada: Effect of New Wealth and Capital Requirements
Explains two rulings: invalidation by new wealth and capital requirements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi rulings: (1) the effect of a partner acquiring new wealth on Mufawada partnership, and (2) the required type of capital for Mufawada to be valid, including the customary exception.

Core Idea
Mufawada requires absolute equality – any new wealth breaks it; capital must be currency.

This lesson distills two essential rulings on the Mufawada partnership from the Hanafi school. First, the strict equality requirement means that if a partner acquires new wealth through inheritance or gift, the partnership transforms into an 'Inan. Second, the capital must consist of specific currency; other goods are not valid unless customary practice accepts them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Q&A pairs
The passage has two separate questions and answers. The first deals with the effect of a partner acquiring new wealth. The second deals with the condition of capital type.
Memorize each ruling separately.
2
Understand the first ruling: new wealth invalidates Mufawada
If a partner inherits or receives a gift of property, the Mufawada becomes invalid and automatically converts to an 'Inan partnership. This is because equality is disrupted.
The new wealth does not need to be added to partnership capital; personal acquisition is enough to break equality.
3
Understand the second ruling: capital must be currency
Mufawada is only valid if capital consists of dirhams, dinars, or current coins. However, if people customarily deal with gold ingots or silver nuggets as currency, partnership with these is also valid.
The exception is based on 'urf (custom) having legal weight in commercial transactions.
4
Connect to the principle of musawah (equality)
Mufawada's essence is complete equality in capital, profit, and liability. New wealth disrupts this. Currency capital ensures fungibility for equality.
Understand why these rules exist: to maintain the strict equality required for Mufawada.

Defines 'Inan partnership, its ruling, capital condition, and clarifies mixing not required.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and ruling of 'Inan partnership and identify its conditions for validity.

Core Idea
'Inan partnership is an agency-based joint venture without surety; mixing capital is not required.

This lesson introduces the concept of 'Inan partnership (shirkat al-'inan), one of the primary partnership types in Hanafi fiqh. We will unpack its definition, ruling, and the specific conditions that make it valid, drawing a clear contrast with the Mufawada partnership.

Guided Path
1
State the definition of 'Inan partnership
Extract from the text the two forms: partnership in a specific trade or in general trade. Note that 'Inan implies mutual agency.
The name 'Inan literally means 'reins' – each partner has control like a horse's reins.
2
Identify the ruling and its legal basis
The text says it is ja'iz (permissible) and concluded on wakalah (agency), not kafalah (suretyship). Understand the implications: each partner is liable only for his own purchases.
3
List the conditions for validity
Capital must be in currency (dirhams, dinars, or customary equivalents like tibr and naqra). The contract (offer and acceptance) is also required.
This is similar to Mufawada regarding capital, but with one key difference.
4
Contrast with Mufawada: mixing not required
The text explicitly states that mixing assets is not required for 'Inan. This simplifies formation.
5

Capital, Profit, and Loss in 'Inan Partnership
Q&A rulings on capital, profit, and loss in 'Inan partnership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of disparity in capital and profit in 'Inan partnership and apply the rule regarding loss of capital to determine when the partnership becomes void.

Core Idea
In 'Inan partnership, disparity in capital and profit is allowed, and the partnership is void only if all capital is lost before any purchase.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary clarifies several practical aspects of 'Inan partnership. It addresses common questions about unequal contributions, partial capital, different currencies, and the effect of capital loss. Understanding these rulings helps partners structure their venture flexibly while knowing the precise point at which the partnership dissolves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissibility of disparity
Note that the text allows tafadul in capital (different amounts) and in profit (different shares) even with equal capital.
2
Examine partial capital contribution
Recognize that a partner may use only part of his wealth; full contribution is not required.
3
Determine validity with different currencies
The partnership is valid even if one gives dinars and the other dirhams; no conversion needed.
4
Analyze the effect of capital destruction before purchase
If both capitals are destroyed or one before any purchase, the partnership becomes void. If one destroyed after a purchase, the purchased item is shared, and the loss is shared proportionally.
Distinguish before and after purchase.
5
Apply the rule for loss after purchase
The partner who bought recovers the other's share of the price, meaning the loss is borne proportionally.

Invalid Stipulation of Fixed Salary in 'Inan Partnership
Ruling on invalid condition in 'Inan partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a fixed salary is stipulated in an 'Inan partnership and explain why it invalidates the contract.

Core Idea
A fixed salary stipulation makes the partnership invalid because profit must be shared proportionally.

Partnership in Hanafi law is built on profit-sharing in proportion to capital or labour, not fixed sums. This lesson examines the ruling when one partner tries to secure a guaranteed fixed amount for himself, which contradicts the very nature of partnership risk and reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Recognize that the stipulation is a fixed amount of dirhams for one partner, not a share of profit.
2
State the ruling
The text says 'la tasihh al-shirkah' — the partnership is invalid. This is a ruling of fasid (voidable) contract.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason given: 'it is possible that the profit may be only what was stipulated'. If actual profit equals that amount, the other partner gets nothing, which is unjust.
This highlights the need for uncertainty (gharar) in profit distribution.
4
Apply the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position: any stipulation of a fixed sum for a partner invalidates the partnership contract.
If such a condition is added, the contract becomes fasid and should be rectified or nullified.

Agency allows delegation of any contract one may do himself.

This shows agency is a facilitative institution.

The text does not explicitly state the consequence, but Hanafi jurists hold that conditions for validity render the contract void when unmet.

Therefore, the agency is invalid. Conversely, if the principal is incompetent (e.g., insane), even a capable agent cannot be appointed validly.

Agent Buying Excess Quantity: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on agent exceeding quantity instruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions (Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn) on an agent buying excess quantity at the same total price, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
An agent who buys more than instructed at the same total price binds the principal either partially (Abu Hanifa) or fully (Sahibayn) depending on benefit.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding an agent who exceeds the quantity instructed but at the same total price, where the market price per unit is identical. The disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn illustrates how different considerations—strict adherence to instructions versus the principal's benefit—can lead to different rulings in agency contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the factual scenario
The agent was instructed to buy ten ratls of meat for one dirham. Instead, he bought twenty ratls for one dirham, and the market price is ten ratls per dirham. Note the key elements: the total expenditure is the same, but the quantity is doubled.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the principal is bound to accept only ten ratls (the instructed quantity) for half a dirham (the proportional price). The reasoning is that the agent exceeded the limit of his authority in quantity, so the contract is valid only to the extent of the instruction.
The agent's authority is limited; the extra quantity is not binding.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), the principal is bound to accept all twenty ratls for one dirham. Their reasoning is that the agent acted in the principal's benefit by acquiring more goods for the same price; the principal's interest is served, so the entire contract is binding.
The principal benefits from getting more for the same cost.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is in what the principal must accept and pay. Under Abu Hanifa, the principal pays half a dirham for ten ratls; under the Sahibayn, he pays a full dirham for twenty ratls.
This affects the principal's liability and the agent's authority.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
Although the text does not give a definitive tarjih, in later Hanafi fatwa practice the Sahibayn's view is often adopted because it facilitates transactions and respects the principal's benefit.
The mufta bihi is frequently the Sahibayn's view.

Restrictions on Agent's Transactions with Relatives
Ruling on agent’s transactions with principal’s relatives – intra-Hanafi difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether an agent may transact with the principal's close relatives and identify the practical consequence of the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
The agent is restricted from transacting with the principal's close relatives due to potential conflict of interest.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the limitations on an agent appointed for sale or purchase. The key question is whether the agent may sell to or buy from certain relatives of the principal. Understanding this difference is crucial for drafting valid agency contracts and avoiding invalidity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principals
Who is the agent (al-wakil) and who is the principal (al-muwakkil)? The agent is appointed to act on behalf of the principal.
The agent is a fiduciary (amin).
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and rationale
Abu Hanifa prohibits the agent from transacting with the principal's father, grandfather, child, grandchild, wife, slave, and mukatab. The rationale is conflict of interest – the agent may not prioritize the principal's interest.
This is a precautionary restriction.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and condition
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow the transaction provided the price is fair market value (mithl al-qeema), except for the principal's slave and mukatab where they agree with Abu Hanifa.
They rely on the general permissibility of transactions.
4
Note the agreed exception
The slave and mukatab are under the principal's ownership or contractual tie, making any transaction with them a potential conflict.
Both parties agree on the exception for the principal's slave and mukatab.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, such a contract is void or voidable; under Sahibayn, it is valid if at market price (except slave/mukatab). This affects the legal standing of the transaction.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is often Abu Hanifa's for caution.

Agent Buying: Permissible Price and Taghabun
Ruling on agent's permissible purchase price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible price range for an agent buying on behalf of a principal and identify the boundary of taghabun using expert valuation.

Core Idea
An agent may buy at fair market value or with a slight excess that experts consider normal.

When you appoint someone to buy for you, you give them authority to make a purchase. But what price can they pay? The Hanafi school explains that the agent may buy at the market price or at a price slightly higher—within the range that people ordinarily accept. If the excess is too large, it is not allowed. This lesson clarifies that boundary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline price
The agent is permitted to buy at the equivalent price (mithl al-qeema), which is the fair market value.
2
Define permissible excess
An increase that falls within taghabun—a small disparity that people in ordinary transactions would accept.
Taghabun literally means 'mutual oversight' or 'discrepancy'.
3
Define impermissible excess
An increase that does not fall within taghabun means it is beyond what expert valuators (muqawwimin) would deem reasonable. Such an excess invalidates the agent's authority.
The boundary is determined by expert valuation, not arbitrary judgement.

However, if the excess is beyond that limit, it is not permissible because it constitutes manifest negligence. The second question defines the 'taghabun that is not overlooked' as a discrepancy that is so significant it falls outside the range of what valuators (muqawwimin) would deem reasonable. Learning Objective

Core Idea
An agent to collect debt may or may not have power to sue; Abu Hanifa says yes, Sahibayn says no.

This lesson addresses a practical question: if someone appoints you to collect a debt, do you automatically have the right to take the debtor to court? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), disagreed on this point. Understanding their reasoning helps clarify the scope of agency (wakalah) in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is appointing an agent to collect a debt. The question is whether this appointment also confers authority to litigate (khusumah). Identify the two positions.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Reasoning
According to Abu Hanifa, the agent for receipt is also an agent for litigation. Why? Because litigation is a necessary means to achieve the end of collection. Without it, the agent cannot enforce payment.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and Reasoning
The Sahibayn hold that agency for receipt does not include agency for litigation. They see litigation as a separate act requiring explicit authorization. The agent must be specifically empowered to sue.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Although the text does not explicitly state which view is relied upon, later Hanafi practice often adopts Abu Hanifa's view for practical efficiency. The agent can then proceed with litigation without separate appointment.

In any agency for an unspecified item, check whether the agent declared intention or used principal's funds. If neither, the item is the agent's property.
The agent's own money and silence default to agent ownership.

Deposit Claim by an Agent Must Be Proven
Ruling on deposit handover upon claim of agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a mere claim of agency, even if believed, does not obligate the depositary to hand over the deposit.

Core Idea
An agent's oral claim of authority is not sufficient to receive a deposit without proof.

This lesson addresses the first Q&A: a man claims to be an agent for receiving a deposit. Even if the depositary believes him, the depositary is not ordered to hand over the deposit. In Hanafi fiqh, mere assertion of agency is not legally binding.

The depositary is not ordered to hand over the deposit.

3
Understand the rationale
Agency requires clear proof; a mere claim does not create obligation. The depositary is not liable to deliver without verification.
Protects the owner's property from unauthorized claims.

Question: An agent sold by sale then guaranteed the price from the buyer, what is the ruling on his guarantee? Answer: His guarantee is void. Commentary
This passage presents three distinct rulings on agency (wakalah). The first ruling: a mere claim of being an agent, even if believed by the depositary, does not obligate the depositary to hand over the deposit. The agent must provide proof of agency. The second ruling: if an agent sells an item and then personally guarantees the price to the buyer, this guarantee is null and void (bāṭil) because it would result in the claimant and the claimed being the same person (the agent would be both demanding payment and obligated to pay). The third ruling: revocation of agency is only effective when the agent is informed. If the agent acts before knowing of the revocation, his actions are valid and bind the principal. This is based on the principle that agency is a contract that requires mutual knowledge of termination.

The Void Guarantee: Agent Cannot Ensure the Price
Ruling on agent's guarantee of sale price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an agent's personal guarantee of the sale price is void (bāṭil) in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
An agent's guarantee of the price is void because it would unite the roles of claimant and debtor.

The second Q&A deals with an agent who sells an item and then personally guarantees the price to the buyer. In Hanafi law, such a guarantee is null. This is because the agent, as representative of the seller, is already responsible for collecting the price; making him also the guarantor creates a logical contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
An agent sells an item, then personally guarantees the price from the buyer.
2
State the ruling
The guarantee is void (باطل).
Source: 'ضمانه باطل'.
3
Understand the rationale
The agent would be both the claimant (demanding the price) and the debtor (obligated to pay the guarantee), which is legally impossible.
The roles of agent (collector) and guarantor cannot merge.
