The Hanafi Legal Definition of Hajj
Defines Hajj linguistically and legally

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of Hajj and identify its three essential components: specific place, specific time, and specific action.

Core Idea
Hajj is defined as visiting the Ka'bah and 'Arafah at appointed times while in ihram.

This lesson opens the Book of Hajj with a precise definition. The Hanafi school defines Hajj by three elements: a specific place (the Ka'bah and 'Arafah), a specific time (the appointed times for tawaf and wuquf), and a specific action (entering ihram before the standing and tawaf). Mastering this definition ensures you know the essential requirements of the pilgrimage.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic meaning
Linguistically, 'hajj' means heading towards a great thing. This gives the sense of intention and purpose.
2
Grasp the legal definition
Legally, Hajj is visiting a specific place (makān makhṣūṣ), at a specific time (zamān makhṣūṣ), with a specific action (fi‘l makhṣūṣ).
This three-part formula is the core of the Hanafi definition.
3
Examine the specific place
The specific place is the venerated House (the Ka'bah) and the plain of 'Arafah. Both are essential.
Missing either invalidates the Hajj.
4
Identify the specific time and action
The specific time refers to the appointed times for tawaf and standing at 'Arafah. The specific action is being in a state of ihram with the intention of Hajj, which must precede the standing and tawaf.
Ihram with niyyah is a condition for validity.

Definition and Obligation of Hajj with Conditions
Defines hajj, states its obligation with proof, lists conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of hajj linguistically and legally, explain its obligation using the Quranic proof, and identify the conditions of obligation for men and women.

Core Idea
Hajj is a fard 'ayn upon every able Muslim once in a lifetime; denial of its obligation removes one from Islam.

This lesson introduces the foundational rulings of Hajj. We begin with the definition, then establish its obligation from the Quran, and finally outline the conditions that must be met for Hajj to be obligatory. Pay close attention to the extra condition for women.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish linguistic and legal definitions
Identify that linguistically hajj means 'intending a great place' and legally it is visiting the specific place (Kaaba and Arafat) at specific times with specific actions. Note the three elements: place, time, and action.
The legal definition sets hajj apart from mere visit; it is a composite act.
2
Identify the ruling and locate the dalil
Hajj is a pillar of Islam; denial is apostasy. The proof is Quran 3:97 which commands pilgrimage. Determine that the imperative and preposition 'ala indicate obligation in Hanafi usul.
The verse's dalala is qati; the condition 'man istata'a' restricts obligation to those able.
3
List conditions for a man
State the conditions: free, Muslim, adult, sane, healthy, safe route, ability to afford provisions and transport (ownership or hire), surplus wealth beyond housing and family needs for the journey.
These are shurut al-wujub (conditions for obligation), not conditions for validity.
4
Identify the extra condition for a woman
A woman requires a mahram (or husband) if the distance to Mecca is at least travel-shortening distance (approx. 78 km). Without a mahram at that distance, hajj is not obligatory.
If she has no mahram, the obligation is suspended until she can find one or the distance reduces.

The Three Pillars (Fara'id) and Obligations (Wajibat) of Hajj
Lists the three fara'id and the first wajib of Hajj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three fara'id of Hajj, explain the consequence of omitting a fard versus a wajib, and identify at least one wajib from the passage.

Core Idea
Fara'id invalidate Hajj if omitted; wajibat are compensable with a dam.

This passage, presented in a question-and-answer format, distinguishes between the fundamental integrials (fara'id) of Hajj and its obligations (wajibat). Understanding this difference is crucial: omitting a fard breaks the pilgrimage, while omitting a wajib can be fixed without repetition. The text explicitly names the three fara'id and begins the list of wajibat.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the structure
The passage uses a Q&A style. Identify the two questions: one about the fara'id and one about the wajibat.
The Q&A format helps memorisation.
2
Distinguish between fard and wajib
In Hanafi fiqh, a fard (pillar) is an integral whose omission invalidates the act. A wajib (obligation) is compulsory but its omission can be compensated with a dam (sacrificial animal).
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.
3
Memorise the three fara'id
List the three fara'id as given: (1) Ihram, (2) Wuquf bi 'Arafah, (3) Tawaf al-Ziyarah.
Ihram is the intention; Wuquf is the essence; Tawaf al-Ziyarah is the mandatory circumambulation.

Note the incomplete list of wajibat

The full list includes seven, but only this one is from the source.

Mawaaqit: Appointed Places for Ihram
Questions and answers on mawaaqit rulings for various locations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct mīqāt for individuals based on their location relative to the mawaaqit and the Haram, and state the rulings for early and delayed ihram.

Core Idea
Your mīqāt depends on residence: inside mawaaqit, al-hill, or al-Haram.

This lesson unpacks the key rulings on the mawaaqit, the fixed geographical boundaries for entering ihram. Through a series of questions and answers, the text clarifies the obligations for three categories of people: those living inside the mawaaqit, those between the mawaaqit and the Haram, and those inside the Haram itself. It also addresses the timing of ihram—whether it can be done early or must be delayed, and what penalty applies if one passes the mīqāt without ihram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three residency categories
Parse the first question-answer pair to list the three categories: (1) residing within the mawaaqit, (2) residing between the mawaaqit and the Haram (al-hill), (3) residing inside the Haram. For each, state the corresponding mīqāt.
Pay attention to the special case of Haram residents: Hajj vs Umrah differ.
2
Determine the ruling for early ihram
From the second Q&A, note that performing ihram before reaching the mīqāt is permissible (ja'iz) and even preferable (afdal) under two conditions: within the months of Hajj and security from prohibitions.
Early ihram is a sunna, not an obligation.
3
Determine the ruling for delayed ihram
From the third Q&A, understand that delaying ihram beyond the mīqāt is not permissible (la yajuz). If one does so, a dam (sacrificial animal) becomes wajib.
The penalty applies even if one remembers later and performs ihram inside the Haram.
4
Handle the case of no mīqāt on the route
From the fourth Q&A, learn that if a traveler's route does not pass through any of the five named mawaaqit, he must perform ihram at a point parallel (muhadha) to the nearest mīqāt.
This rule is crucial for air travel today.
5
Review the five specific mawaaqit
The text mentions Dhu al-Hulayfah (for those coming via Madinah), al-Juhfah (now abandoned), Qarn (for Najd and East), and Yalamlam (for Yemen). Note that people of Iraq use Dhu al-Hulayfah because they pass by Madinah.
These are the place mawaaqit; time mawaaqit are not covered here.

Commentary
This passage is a set of rulings on the mawaaqit (appointed places for entering the state of ihram), presented in a question-and-answer format. The mawaaqit are fixed geographical boundaries for those who live outside them; the text explains three categories of residents. First, a person living within the mīqāt itself (e.g., in Dhu al-Hulayfah) performs ihram from his place of residence. Second, one who lives between the mawaaqit and the Haram (the sacred precincts of Mecca) performs ihram from the open land (al-hill) between the mawaaqit and Haram; this is his mīqāt. Third, one who lives inside the Haram itself performs ihram from the Haram for Hajj, but for Umrah he must go outside the Haram to the hill. Raml and Idtiba': Rulings and Method
Question-answer on sunnah of raml and idtiba' with condition and method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of raml and idtiba' in the Hanafi school, explain the condition that triggers them, and describe their method.

Core Idea
Raml and idtiba' are sunnah only when sa'y follows, not for every tawaf.

This passage addresses a common observation: some pilgrims perform raml and idtiba' during tawaf. The Hanafi school clarifies that these acts are sunnah, but only when the pilgrim intends to perform sa'y after the tawaf. Understanding this condition ensures the sunnah is applied correctly, avoiding both omission and improper application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their ruling
Recognize that both 'raml' (brisk walk with shoulder movements) and 'idtiba'' (exposing the right shoulder) are sunnah – not wajib. This sets the baseline.
Sunnah means recommended; omitting it does not invalidate the tawaf.
2
Isolate the key condition
Notice that the ruling is conditional: raml and idtiba' are only sunnah for one who 'intends to perform sa'y between Safa and Marwa after his tawaf.' Without subsequent sa'y, they are not prescribed.
This condition applies to tawaf al-qudum and Umrah tawaf.
3
Understand the method of raml
Raml is performed in the first three circuits only. The method: shake the shoulders like a wrestler strutting and increase walking speed.

Understand the method of idtiba'
Idtiba' is performed in all circuits of the tawaf. The method: bring the edge of the upper garment from under the right armpit and place both ends over the left shoulder, leaving the right shoulder uncovered.

The condition is about intention at the time of tawaf, not actual performance later.

Stoning on the 13th: Obligation and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains obligation of stoning on 13th and the dispute over time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on stoning on the 13th and analyze the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the permissibility of stoning before noon.

Core Idea
Stoning on the 13th is sunnah unless dawn finds you in Mina, then wajib; stoning before noon is disputed: valid but makruh (Abu Hanifa) vs. invalid (Sahibayn).

The 13th of Dhul-Hijjah is the final day of stoning. The pilgrim has the option to leave after the 12th, but if he remains in Mina and dawn breaks, stoning becomes obligatory. This lesson explores the timing for this day and a notable intra-Hanafi disagreement: may one stone before noon?

Guided Path
1
Determine the obligation for the 13th
If the pilgrim chooses to stay in Mina after the 12th, stoning on the 13th is sunnah. However, if dawn of the 13th comes while he is in Mina, it becomes wajib.
The passage says: 'ولو طلع الفجر الثاني من اليوم الثالث عشر وهو في منى، وجب عليه رمي هذا اليوم أيضا.'
2
Identify the sunnah time and valid window
The sunnah time is after zawal (noon). The valid time extends from zawal to sunset. Stoning after sunset is invalid.
3
Know the dispute about stoning before noon
Abu Hanifa allows stoning before noon on the 13th, though it is makruh (disliked) because it contradicts the sunnah. His two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is not permissible and the stoning is invalid.
The text: 'ولورمى قبل الزوال جاز عند أبي حنيفة له لكنه مكروه... وقال صاحباه لا يجوز الرمي قبل الزوال في هذا اليوم أيضا.'
4
Analyze the proofs for each view
Abu Hanifa considers the time for the 13th to be broad; the sunnah is after noon but early stoning still fulfills the obligation. The Sahibayn analogize to the previous days, where stoning before noon is invalid.
The difference stems from whether the 13th is treated similarly to the 11th/12th or as a distinct day.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi scholars generally adopt the view of the Sahibayn. Therefore, the relied-upon position is that stoning before noon on the 13th is invalid.
This is the mufta bihi for fatwa.

The answer states that Qiran is superior to Ifrad (Hajj only) and Tamattu' ('Umrah followed by Hajj with a break in ihram). The description of Qiran begins with entering ihram from the miqat for both 'Umrah and Hajj simultaneously, reciting the intention after the two rak'ahs of sunnat al-ihram. Once talbiyah is recited, the person (qarin) is in ihram for both. Upon entering Makkah, he first performs tawaf for 'Umrah with idtiba' (uncovering the right shoulder) and raml (quick steps) in the first three circuits, then sa'y for 'Umrah. After this, he performs tawaf al-qudum (arrival tawaf) and sa'y for Hajj. He remains in ihram until the 8th of Dhul Hijjah (Yawm al-Tarwiyah), then proceeds to Mina, stands at 'Arafah, then Muzdalifah, stoning, etc. The extra requirement for a qarin is the hady (sacrificial animal) to be slaughtered on the day of Nahr after stoning the major Jamrah. He must not shave or shorten hair until after slaughtering. After that, he exits ihram completely except for marital relations, which become permissible only after tawaf al-ziyarah.

Fasting as Substitute for Hady in Qiran
Q&A on fasting substitute for hady in Qiran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a qarin must fast instead of slaughter, and identify the consequence of missing the scheduled fast.

Core Idea
If a qarin cannot afford a hady, he must fast ten days: three during Hajj and seven after returning home; missing the three during Hajj revives the obligation to slaughter.

In the Hanafi school, a qarin (one who combines Hajj and Umrah) is required to offer a hady (sacrificial animal). However, if he cannot afford it, Allah grants a substitute of fasting ten days. This passage from the Tasheel al-daruri Sharh al-Quduri details the timing of these fasts and what happens if the initial three fasts are missed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The ruling applies when the qarin cannot afford the hady. This is the prerequisite for the fasting substitute.
2
State the substitute
He must fast ten days total: three days during Hajj and seven days after returning to his family (or in Makkah).
3
Specify the timing of the three days
The three days during Hajj must include the Day of Arafah as the last. The fasts should be completed before the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah).
The text says 'آخرها يوم عرفة' (the last of which is the Day of Arafah).
4
Recognize the exception
If he misses fasting the three days until the Day of Nahr enters, the fasting option is forfeited and he must slaughter the hady.
The text says 'لم يجزئه الآن إلا ذبح الهدي' (Only slaughtering the hady is sufficient for him now).
5
Note the permissibility
The seven days can be fasted in Makkah before returning home; it is not required to wait until returning to family.
The text says 'نعم هذا جائز' (Yes, that is permissible).
