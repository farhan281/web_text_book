Explicit Words in Emancipation and the Effect of Specifying a Body Part
Explicit words for emancipation and body part rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the explicit words that effect emancipation, explain why intention is not required, and apply the rule that specifying a body part frees the entire slave.

Core Idea
Explicit words free a slave immediately—intention not needed; naming a body part frees the whole.

Emancipation (i'tāq) is a serious legal act that changes a person's status from slave to free. The Hanafi school holds that certain words are so clear that they effect freedom regardless of what the master intended in his heart. This lesson examines those explicit (ṣarīḥ) words and a related rule: if the master says 'your head is free,' the entire slave becomes free because such phrases are understood as referring to the whole person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit words
List the six Arabic phrases given in the passage that are considered ṣarīḥ (explicit) for emancipation.
These are: أنت حر, معتق, عتيق, محرر, حررتك, أعتقتك.
2
Understand the absence of niyyah
Recognize that because these words are ṣarīḥ, they convey the meaning directly without need for the master's internal intention (niyyah). This is a Hanafi principle: ṣarīḥ words are self-sufficient.
Contrast with kināyah (implicit) words where niyyah is required.
3
Apply the body part rule
When the master adds emancipation to a specific body part (e.g., 'your head is free'), the entire slave is freed because such expressions are idioms for the whole person.
The passage lists: head, neck, face, body, and for a female slave, her private part.

Expressions Treated as Explicit: 'Hadha Ibni', 'Hadha Mawlay'
Ruling that certain statements effect emancipation without intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three expressions that are treated as explicit for emancipation in Hanafi fiqh and explain why intention is not required.

Core Idea
Call a slave 'my son' or 'my master' and he goes free — these words are legally explicit even without intent.

This lesson covers three specific expressions that automatically effect emancipation in Hanafi law, even if the speaker did not intend to free the slave. The key principle is that these statements are 'mulhaq bi al-sarih' — they are legally equivalent to direct, explicit words of emancipation. Understanding this category is crucial because it expands the scope of what counts as a valid declaration of freedom beyond literal 'I free you'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three expressions
List the phrases: (1) 'Hadha ibni' (this is my son) — with the condition that the master persists in the claim, (2) 'Hadha mawlay' (this is my master), and (3) calling the slave 'Ya mawlay' (O my master).
For 'hadha ibni', persistence is required; the other two do not require persistence.
2
Understand the legal effect
These expressions cause emancipation automatically, without any intention (niyyah) of emancipation. The slave is freed immediately upon the utterance (and persistence for the first).
3
Grasp the underlying principle
These expressions are 'mulhaq bi al-sarih' (annexed to the explicit) because they unequivocally negate the status of slavery: a father does not own his son, and a master is not a slave.
The Hanafi school uniquely treats such kinayah (indirect) expressions as having the force of sarih (explicit) words.
4
Note the boundary condition
For 'hadha ibni', the master must persist in the claim (thabata 'ala qawlihi). If he says it only once and retracts, the ruling may differ (covered elsewhere).
This condition does not apply to 'hadha mawlay' or 'ya mawlay'.

Core Idea
Tadbir is a binding declaration that frees a slave upon the master's death, subject to the one-third rule.

Tadbir, or emancipation upon death, is a unique form of manumission in Hanafi law. In this lesson, we explore its definition, the immediate legal consequences for the slave (mudabbar), and the detailed rules governing freedom after the master's death, including the impact of debts and the one-third limit on bequests.

Once declared, the slave becomes mudabbar – he cannot be sold or gifted, but the master retains the right to use his services and hire him out.

Upon the master's death, the mudabbar's freedom depends on the estate: if his value is within one-third of the net estate, he is free without payment; if not, he works (si'ayah) to pay two-thirds of his value to the heirs; if debts cover the whole estate, he pays full value to creditors.

If tadbir is conditional on a future event (e.g., death from a particular illness), the slave is not mudabbar during the master's life and can be sold, but becomes free upon fulfillment of the condition, from one-third.
Conditional tadbir does not take immediate effect.

Chapter of Tadbir
Question: What is tadbir?
Answer: It is that the master says to his slave: 'When I die, you are free,' or says: 'You are free after me,' or 'You are mudabbar,' or says: 'I have made you mudabbar.'
Question: When he says that, what is the ruling?
Answer: The slave becomes mudabbar and is freed when his master dies. It is not permissible to sell him or give him as a gift. It is permissible for the master to use his services and hire him out.
Question: If he makes his female slave mudabbara, is it permissible for him to have intercourse with her during his lifetime or marry her to a man?
Answer: Both matters are permissible for him.
Question: If her master married her to a man and she bore a child, what is its ruling?
Answer: This child becomes mudabbar along with its mother.
Question: When the master who made tadbir dies, is the mudabbar freed for free with nothing, or does he have to work to pay his value to the heirs?
Answer: There is detail: if the mudabbar falls within one-third of the deceased's estate, he is free without anything. If he has no other property besides this slave, he works for the heirs for two-thirds of his value. And if the master had a debt that covers all his wealth, the slave works for the creditors for his entire value.
Question: If the master attached the tadbir to a condition, e.g., he said: 'If I die in this illness, or in this journey, or from such disease,' is he mudabbar?
Answer: He is not mudabbar in this form, and therefore it is permissible to sell him and give him as a gift. But if the master dies under the condition he mentioned, the slave becomes free like a mudabbar, i.e., from one-third, because the condition has been fulfilled.

Answer: When the master has intercourse with his female slave and she bears a child from him, it is said he has istalada her, and that female slave is called umm walad for him, and this is on condition that he acknowledges that it is his child, and his lineage is established from him in this case. Commentary
This passage is a question-answer exposition (masā'il) on the rulings of istilād and umm al-walad. It opens by defining istilād: it occurs when a master has intercourse with his female slave and she bears a child, provided the master acknowledges the child as his own. The slave then becomes 'umm walad' (mother of a child) with special legal status. The core ruling: during the master's life, the umm walad cannot be sold or given away; she remains a slave but the master may use her for service, hire her out, or marry her off. Upon the master's death, she becomes free from the entire estate (not just one-third) without needing to work for the heirs, even if the master had debts. Her children born after she becomes umm walad are free upon her freedom. If the master denies paternity, lineage is not established and she does not become umm walad. However, once he acknowledges one child, subsequent children are automatically considered his unless he denies them. A child born to a woman while owned by another, then later purchased by the biological father, makes her umm walad upon purchase. If the master's father has intercourse with the master's slave and claims the child, the child's lineage is established from the father, and the slave becomes his umm walad, but he must pay the value to his son (the owner). No additional 'aqr (dower for shubha) or child's value is required. If the grandfather (father's father) has intercourse with his grandson's slave and the father is dead, lineage is established from the grandfather; if the father is alive, it is not. In a co-ownership scenario: if one partner claims the child, the child's lineage is established from him, he becomes owner of the slave as umm walad, and he must pay the other partner half the 'aqr and half the value. If both claim, the child is attributed to both (legally impossible biologically but recognized as such for inheritance), the slave becomes umm walad for both, each pays half the 'aqr, and they offset amounts due. The text ends by defining technical terms: 'aqr is the dower paid when intercourse occurs under shubha (a mistaken belief of permissibility), and 'yataqāṣān' means each discharges his obligation against the other's.

Umm al-Walad in Complex Ownership Scenarios
Complex ownership scenarios for umm al-walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rulings of umm al-walad to cases involving third-party owners, co-owners, and multiple claims.

Core Idea
Umm al-walad status can arise even when the father was not the owner at time of intercourse, and co-ownership leads to shared rights and accounting.

This lesson explores scenarios where the biological father is not the original owner, or where two partners claim paternity. These cases test the boundaries of the umm al-walad rules and introduce concepts like 'aqr (dower for shubha) and mutual settlement.

Guided Path
1
Case: Intercourse with another's slave, then purchase
A man has intercourse with another's slave via marriage, she bears his child, then he purchases her. She becomes umm walad for him upon purchase.
The intercourse itself does not create umm walad status; ownership must follow.
2
Case: Father claims child from son's slave
Master's father has intercourse with the master's slave and claims the child. Lineage is established for the father, and the slave becomes his umm walad. He must pay the value of the slave to his son.
No additional 'aqr or child's value is due.
3
Case: Grandfather and grandson's slave
If the father is dead, grandfather's claim is accepted; if father is alive, grandfather's claim is not accepted.
The grandfather's claim is conditional on the father's death.
4
Case: Co-ownership with one claimant
If one co-owner claims the child, the child's lineage is established from him, the slave becomes his umm walad, and he must pay the other co-owner half of her 'aqr and half of her value.
The child's value is not due.
5
Case: Both co-owners claim
If both claim, the child is attributed to both, the slave becomes umm walad for both, each pays half the 'aqr, and they settle accounts.
This is a unique ruling where lineage is established to two fathers legally, even if biologically impossible.

Legal ruling on paternity and compensation in Q&A format.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paternity and compensation when a father impregnates his son's slave-girl, and identify which payments are required and which are not.

Core Idea
The father's paternity claim is accepted; he must pay the slave-girl's value to the son but owes no dower or child's value.

This lesson examines a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: a father has intercourse with his son's slave-girl, and a child is born. The father claims paternity. The Hanafi school rules that the lineage is established, the slave-girl becomes an umm walad for the father, and he must compensate the son by paying the slave-girl's market value. However, no additional payments like dower or child's value are required.

Determine the Ruling on Paternity
The Hanafi school establishes the child's lineage from the father who claims paternity. This is based on the presumption that the father's claim is credible.
The child is free and inherits from the father.

Determine the Status of the Slave-Girl
The slave-girl becomes an umm walad (mother of the child) for the father. She cannot be sold and becomes free upon the father's death.
This status transfers ownership-like rights to the father.

Identify the Compensation Required
The father must pay the current market value of the slave-girl to his son (the original owner). This compensates for taking the slave-girl as umm walad.
The payment is wajib (obligatory).
5
Confirm Exemptions from Additional Payments
The father is not liable for the 'aqr (dower) or the value of the child. The 'aqr is waived because the intercourse is not considered a transgression due to paternity, and the child is free.
Only the value of the slave-girl is due.

An invalid kitaba still yields freedom; the slave must compensate the value of the impermissible consideration.

In Hanafi fiqh, a kitaba (contract of manumission) is typically valid only if the consideration is lawful property (mal mutaqawwim). The passage rules that such a kitaba is fasid (invalid), yet if the slave actually delivers the prohibited items, he becomes free. This ruling is derived by analogy to a void sale where the sold item is destroyed. We will walk through the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Recognize that the case involves a kitaba (a contract in which a slave purchases his freedom in installments) where the consideration is wine or pig—items that are not lawful property for a Muslim. Understand that the contract's validity depends on the nature of the consideration.

The text states the ruling: 'al-kitaba fasida' (the contract is invalid). The reason is that wine and pig are not mal mutaqawwim (valuable property) for a Muslim, so the contract is fasid (voidable).
Fasid differs from batil in Hanafi law: a fasid contract may produce some legal effects if executed, while a batil contract produces none.

If the slave actually pays the wine or pig, the judge rules that he becomes free. This is because the objective of kitaba is manumission, and payment has occurred, even though the consideration was invalid. The contract, though fasid, is capable of effecting freedom once performed.

The reason: since the contract was invalid, the slave should have returned to slavery, but that is impossible after manumission. The text explicitly draws an analogy: 'as in a void sale when the merchandise is destroyed' (kama fi al-bay' al-fasid idha talafa al-mabi'). In a fasid sale, if the sold item is consumed or destroyed, the buyer must pay its market value. Similarly, here the wine/pig has been delivered (consumed by the master) and the slave cannot be returned to slavery, so he must pay the value.
The common illa (effective cause) is that a fasid contract has been executed and the subject matter cannot be returned, so compensation of value is due.

Joint Kitaba for Two Slaves: Rulings on Validity and Guarantee
Rulings on a single kitaba contract with two slaves, with and without guarantee.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a single kitaba contract for two slaves, distinguish between the scenarios with and without mutual guarantee, and explain the consequences of payment.

Core Idea
A single kitaba for two slaves is valid; if one pays all with guarantee, both are freed and he claims half.

This passage presents two specific rulings on joint kitaba contracts. The first scenario involves a single kitaba for two slaves without any guarantee, while the second adds a mutual guarantee clause. Understanding these rulings clarifies how joint obligations are handled in Hanafi law, particularly the effect of guarantee on liability and freedom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) a single kitaba for two slaves without any guarantee, and (2) a single kitaba with a stipulation that each slave guarantees the other.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
Both scenarios are valid (sahih). The text explicitly states 'the kitaba is valid' for both.
Validity is the same, but consequences differ.
3
Explain the effect of payment without guarantee
Without guarantee, both slaves must pay the full amount together. If they both pay, both become free; if they become incapable (ajz), both revert to slavery.
No mention of one paying for the other.
4
Explain the effect of payment with guarantee
With guarantee, if one slave pays the entire kitaba amount, he becomes free and his companion also becomes free. The paying slave can then claim half of what he paid from the other slave.
This is a case of mutual kafalah.
5
Note the recourse mechanism
The payer has the right to seek reimbursement for half from the non-paying slave. This is based on the principle of recourse (ruju').
The master is not involved; the slaves settle between themselves.

السؤال: كاتب عبديه كتابة واحدة بألف درهم، هل تصح هذه الكتابة؟ الجواب: نعم، الكتابة صحيحة، فإذا أديا ما عليهما عتقا، وإن عجزا ردًا إلى الرق. السؤال: كاتب عبديه على أن كل واحد منهما ضامن عن الآخر، ما حكم هذه الكتابة؟ الجواب: هذه الكتابة جائزة وأيهما أدى جميع مال الكتابة عتق هو وصاحبه، ويرجع الذي أدى على صاحبه بنصف ما أدى.
Question: He made a single kitaba contract for his two slaves with one thousand dirhams; is this kitaba valid? Answer: Yes, the kitaba is valid. When they both pay what is due from them, they become free; if they become incapable, they are returned to slavery. Question: He made a kitaba contract for his two slaves on the condition that each one is a guarantor for the other; what is the ruling on this kitaba? Answer: This kitaba is valid. Whichever of them pays all the kitaba money, he becomes free along with his companion, and the one who paid has recourse against his companion for half of what he paid.

Commentary
This passage presents two rulings on joint kitaba for two slaves. First scenario: the master writes a single kitaba for both slaves for a total amount of 1000 dirhams, without mention of guarantee. The contract is valid. Both must pay the amount together; if they both pay, both become free. If both become unable to pay (ajz), they revert to slavery. Second scenario: the kitaba contract includes a stipulation that each slave guarantees the other (kafalah). In this case, the contract is also valid. If one slave pays the entire kitaba amount, he becomes free and the other also becomes free. The paying slave can then claim half of what he paid from the other slave (i.e., contribution). This is a standard Hanafi ruling on joint obligations with mutual guarantee.

Key Terms
ضامن (damin): Guarantor; one who is liable for the debt of another.

Post-Kitaba Events: Manumission, Death of Master, and Heirs' Manumission
Rulings on what happens after kitaba: master's manumission, master's death, and heirs' actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each of three post-kitaba events: master's unconditional manumission, master's death, and partial manumission by heirs.

Core Idea
A kitaba contract is binding beyond the master's life; only full collective manumission by all heirs frees the mukatab.

After a kitaba contract is made, several events may affect its status. This lesson covers three key scenarios: the master voluntarily manumitting the slave unconditionally, the master's death, and attempts by heirs to free the slave. Understanding these rulings is essential for properly applying the law of kitaba.

Guided Path
1
Scenario 1: Master's Unconditional Manumission
Identify the ruling when the master manumits the mukatab after the kitaba contract. The slave is freed and the kitaba debt is waived.
Manumission is a stronger cause of freedom.
2
Scenario 2: Master's Death
Determine what happens to the kitaba upon the master's death. The contract remains valid; the mukatab continues paying installments to the heirs.
Kitaba is not dissolved by death.
3
Scenario 3: Heirs' Manumission
Evaluate the effect when one heir manumits vs. all heirs manumitting. A single heir's manumission is ineffective; only collective manumission by all heirs frees the slave.
All heirs collectively own the slave's kitaba.

Source Text

السؤال: كاتب عبده، ثم أعتقه منجزا، ما حكمه؟ الجواب: يعتق بإعتاقه، ويسقط عنه مال الكتابة. السؤال: كاتب عبده، ثم مات فهل تنفسخ الكتابة بذلك؟ الجواب: لا تنفسخ، ويقال للمكاتب : أد المال إلى ورثة المولى على نجومه. السؤال: إن أعتقه أحد الورثة، هل ينفذ عتقه؟ الجواب: إن أعتقه أحدهم لا ينفذ عتقه، وإن أعتقوا جميعا عُتق، وسقط عنه مال الكتابة.
Question: He made a kitaba contract with his slave, then he manumitted him unconditionally (munajjazan); what is the ruling? Answer: He becomes free by his manumission, and the kitaba money is waived from him. Question: He made a kitaba contract with his slave, then the master died; does the kitaba dissolve by that? Answer: It does not dissolve, and the mukatab is told: Pay the money to the master's heirs according to its installments. Question: If one of the heirs manumits him, does his manumission become effective? Answer: If one of them manumits him, his manumission does not become effective. But if all of them manumit him, he becomes free and the kitaba money is waived from him.
Source pages p. 404

Commentary
This passage covers three post-kitaba scenarios. First: if the master voluntarily manumits the slave after the kitaba contract, the slave becomes free immediately and the outstanding kitaba debt is waived. This is because manumission is a stronger cause of freedom. Second: if the master dies, the kitaba contract is not dissolved. The mukatab must continue paying the installments to the master's heirs. Third: if only one heir of the deceased master tries to manumit the slave, it does not take effect because all heirs collectively own the slave (the kitaba is an asset of the estate). However, if all heirs agree to manumit, the slave is freed and the debt is waived.

Key Terms
أعتقه منجزا (a'taqahu munajjazan): To manumit the slave unconditionally and immediately, as opposed to contingent manumission.
كتابة (kitaba): A contract of manumission by which a slave agrees to pay a sum to the master in installments and becomes free upon completion.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered into a kitaba contract.
