Learning Objective

Core Idea
Imam al-Quduri's Mukhtasar is a foundational Hanafi text of 12,000 issues, blessed and memorized for barakah.

This passage provides a concise biography of Imam al-Quduri, the author of the famous Mukhtasar. Learning about his life and the reverence for his work is essential for understanding the Hanafi scholarly tradition and the esteem in which this matn is held.

His chain shows direct connection to the early school masters.

Biography of Imam Abu al-Husayn al-Quduri
Abdullah
Al-Khatib said in his 'Tarikh Baghdad' (4/377): He is Ahmad ibn Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Ja'far ibn Hamdan, Abu al-Husayn, the jurist known as al-Quduri. He heard from Ibn Muhammad al-Hawshabi, and he narrated only a little. I wrote from him and he was truthful. He was among those who excelled in jurisprudence due to his intelligence. The leadership of the followers of Abu Hanifa ended with him in Iraq, his status became great, and his rank was elevated. He was eloquent in expression in discussion, bold in speech, and recited the Qur'an. I heard Abu Bashir Muhammad ibn Umar al-Wakil and Abu al-Qasim al-Tanukhi al-Qadi mention that the birth of al-Quduri was in the year 362 AH. Ibn al-'Imad said in 'Shadharat al-Dhahab' (3/233): Abu Bakr al-Khatib, author of 'Tarikh', narrated from him, and he authored in the madhhab the famous 'Mukhtasar' and other works. He debated with Shaykh Abu Hamid al-Isfarayini, a Shafi'i jurist, and he respected him greatly. In 'al-Fawa'id al-Bahiyya': Ahmad ibn Muhammad ibn Ahmad, Abu al-Husayn al-Baghdadi al-Quduri. It is said that his nisba (attribution) is to a village near Baghdad called Qadura, and it is said that it is attributed to selling pots (qudur). He is the author of the blessed Mukhtasar that circulates among students. He studied fiqh under Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Yahya al-Jurjani, from Ahmad al-Jassas, from Abdullah Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi, from Abu Sa'id al-Barda'i, from Musa al-Razi, from Muhammad. In it also from 'City of Sciences' that his nisba is to the manufacture or sale of pots, or it is the name of a village. Allah Almighty knows best what is correct. The author of 'Kashf al-Zunun' said: The author of 'Misbah Anwar al-Ad'iya' said: The Hanafis seek blessing by reading it during epidemics. It is a blessed book; whoever memorizes it will be safe from poverty. It was even said: whoever recites it under a righteous teacher, and he supplicates for him at the completion of the book with blessings, then he will possess dirhams equal to the number of its issues. In some commentaries of al-Majma', it contains twelve thousand issues. Similarly, the author of 'Miftah al-Sa'ada' (2/146) mentioned and said: This Mukhtasar is blessed by scholars, even to the extent that they have tried reciting it during times of distress and days of plague. In 'Kashf al-Zunun': Abu Ali al-Shashi used to say: Whoever memorizes this book is the most knowledgeable among our companions, and whoever understands it is the most understanding among our companions. It is well known that the author of al-Hidayah first wrote al-Bidayyah in fiqh, in which he collected the issues of al-Quduri and the issues of al-Jami' al-Saghir by Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani. The author of 'Miftah al-Sa'ada' (2/129) said: The author of al-Hidayah established a convention in his book, among which: he mentions the word 'qala' (he said) at the beginning of every issue if it is an issue from al-Quduri or al-Jami' al-Saghir or it is mentioned in al-Bidayyah; if it is mentioned in other sources, he does not mention 'qala'. Also, he mentions the issues of al-Quduri first, and the issues of al-Jami' al-Saghir at the end of the chapters. If there is any difference between them, he explicitly mentions the words 'al-Jami' al-Saghir' (until the end of what he said). The author of 'Miftah al-Sa'ada' (2/145) also mentioned that al-Quduri wrote a commentary on the Mukhtasar of al-Karkhi, and authored al-Tajrid in seven volumes containing the differences between al-Shafi'i and Abu Hanifa; he began dictating it in the year 405 AH. He also wrote the book al-Taqrib on issues of difference between Abu Hanifa and his students, stripped of proofs. Then he authored the second Taqrib, mentioning the issues with their proofs. Al-Hafiz al-Dhahabi said in 'Siyar A'lam al-Nubala' (17/575): He narrated from Ubaydullah al-Hawshabi and Muhammad ibn Ali ibn Suwayd al-Mu'addib. Al-Khatib and al-Qadi Abu Abdullah al-Damghani narrated from him. He died in Rajab in the year 428 AH at the age of sixty-six. Similarly, it is mentioned in Tadhkirat al-Huffaz (1086) that his death was in this year. Refer for his biography to al-Ansab by al-Sam'ani (10/352), Wafayat al-A'yan by Ibn Khallikan (1/86), al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah (12/40), Hadiyyat al-Arifin (1/73), and al-Jawahir al-Mudiyya (1/247).

Commentary
This passage is a biography of Imam Abu al-Husayn al-Quduri, the author of the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, a foundational text in the Hanafi madhhab. The biography begins by giving his full name and lineage: Ahmad ibn Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Ja'far ibn Hamdan, Abu al-Husayn al-Quduri. Al-Khatib al-Baghdadi describes him as a truthful and intelligent jurist who became the leader of the Hanafis in Iraq. His date of birth is given as 362 AH. He authored the famous Mukhtasar, which became a standard textbook for Hanafi students. The text also mentions that he debated with the Shafi'i jurist Abu Hamid al-Isfarayini, showing his scholarly stature. Various opinions are given about his nisba 'al-Quduri': it may refer to a village called Qadura, or to the trade of selling pots (qudur). The Mukhtasar is described as a blessed book that Hanafis recite during epidemics for blessings. It is said to contain 12,000 issues (masa'il). Abu Ali al-Shashi is quoted saying that whoever memorizes this book is the most knowledgeable among the Hanafis. The passage also explains the relationship between al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and later works: the author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) first wrote al-Bidayyah, combining issues from al-Quduri and al-Jami' al-Saghir. Al-Marghinani used a convention: he would mention 'qala' (he said) for issues from al-Quduri or al-Jami' al-Saghir, and he would present al-Quduri's issues first, then those of al-Jami' al-Saghir at the end of chapters. Al-Quduri also wrote other works: a commentary on al-Karkhi's Mukhtasar, al-Tajrid (on differences between Shafi'i and Hanafi), and al-Taqrib (on differences within the Hanafi school). The biography ends with the year of his death: 428 AH, at the age of 66. The author provides references for further study. This biographical entry serves to introduce the student to the author and his works, establishing the authority of the Mukhtasar as a core text in the Hanafi curriculum.

Key Terms
القدوري (al-Quduri): Abu al-Husayn Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Quduri, a prominent Hanafi jurist and author of Mukhtasar al-Quduri.
المختصر (al-Mukhtasar): The short compendium of Hanafi fiqh authored by al-Quduri, also known as Mukhtasar al-Quduri.
البداية (al-Bidayyah): A book by the author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) that compiled issues from al-Quduri and al-Jami' al-Saghir as a precursor to al-Hidayah.

'Kneaded it' means scrutinized the Imam's principles and fundamentals, and exerted effort in increasing derivation of branches and rulings from them: the student of the Greatest Imam, Abu Yusuf Ya'qub ibn Ibrahim, the Chief Judge; as al-Khatib narrated in his History, he was the first to compile a book on the principles of fiqh according to Abu Hanifa's school, and he dictated the issues, spread them, and disseminated Abu Hanifa's knowledge across the regions of the earth. 'Baked it' means increased derivation of branches, their refinement, purification, and editing such that they needed nothing else: Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani, student of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the editor of the Nu'mani school, upon whose juristic expertise there is consensus. 'So the rest of the people eat from his bread' means the bread of Muhammad which he baked from the dough of Abu Yusuf and the flour of Abu Hanifa. From the preface of al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar.
Source pages p. 9

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction that metaphorically describes the chain of transmission and development of Hanafi fiqh. It is taken from the preface of al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar. The metaphor of agriculture and baking illustrates the contribution of key figures. Abdullah ibn Mas'ud represents the initial planting as a companion who laid the groundwork. Alqamah then watered and clarified the knowledge. Ibrahim al-Nakha'i collected the scattered benefits. Hammad refined and clarified the rulings. Abu Hanifa organized fiqh into systematic chapters and books, making him the foundational codifier. Abu Yusuf expanded on Abu Hanifa's principles, deriving new rulings. Muhammad al-Shaybani perfected the school, editing and elaborating until the Hanafi fiqh was complete and self-sufficient. The final line emphasizes that all later scholars benefit from Muhammad al-Shaybani's work. This passage underscores the Hanafi school's claim of a continuous chain back to the Sahaba, establishing its authority.

زرعه (zara'ahu): Metaphorically means 'he planted it' in the sense of initiating the derivation of branches of fiqh

Imam al-Sha'bi: The Scholar Who Guided Abu Hanifah
Biographical entry establishing al-Sha'bi's credentials and influence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical details of Imam al-Sha'bi and explain his significance in the transmission of Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Al-Sha'bi was the oldest teacher of Abu Hanifah and directed him to pursue knowledge.

This passage introduces Imam al-Sha'bi, a towering figure among the tabi'in and a pivotal link in the chain of Hanafi scholarship. As the oldest teacher of Abu Hanifah, al-Sha'bi's fatwas and methodology directly shaped the founding generation of the Hanafi school. Understanding his authority and legacy helps us appreciate the rigour of the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Sha'bi's full name and lineage
The text gives his name as 'Amir ibn Sharahil al-Hamdani al-Kufi al-Sha'bi. Note the nisbahs: al-Hamdani (tribe), al-Kufi (city), al-Sha'bi (sub-tribe).
2
Note his scholarly attributes
He is described as imam (leader), hafiz (memorizer of hadith), faqih (jurist), and mutqin (precise). These titles establish his credibility.
3
List his teachers among the Companions
He narrated from several Companions: 'Imran ibn Husayn, Jarir ibn 'Abdullah, Abu Hurayrah, Ibn 'Abbas, 'A'ishah, and 'Abd (likely Ibn 'Umar or 'Abdullah ibn 'Amr).
This wide network shows his direct access to prophetic teachings.
4
Recognize his role in Abu Hanifah's education
The passage calls him 'the greatest teacher of Abu Hanifah' and al-Suyuti states that al-Sha'bi directed Abu Hanifah to knowledge and to attending scholarly circles.
This establishes a direct teacher-student link from the tabi'in to the founder of the Hanafi school.
5
Place his lifetime historically
Born during the caliphate of 'Umar (approx. 6 years into his reign, so around 18 AH/639 CE) and died around 110 AH/728 CE. This means he lived through the Umayyad period and was a contemporary of the later Companions.
