Definition and Types of Kafalah (Suretyship) in Hanafi Fiqh
Introduction to suretyship: definition, types, and rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define kafalah according to the Hanafi school, distinguish between kafalah bi-nafs and kafalah bi-mal, and explain the basic rulings for kafalah bi-nafs.

Core Idea
Kafalah: joining liability to another for demand — guarantees a person's presence or a debt.

Kafalah (suretyship) is a common contract in Islamic finance and personal dealings. The Hanafi school defines it precisely and divides it into two main types. In this lesson, we will parse the definition, identify the two types, and examine the detailed rules for kafalah bi-nafs (surety for the person) as presented in the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
Identify the exact wording of the Hanafi definition: 'ضم ذمة إلى ذمة في المطالبة' (joining one liability to another in demand). Understand that 'dhimma' is the legal liability of a person, and 'mutalaba' refers to the right to demand payment or appearance.
This definition distinguishes kafalah from transfer of debt (hawala), where liability is shifted.
2
Distinguish the two types
The text states kafalah is of two types: kafalah bi-nafs (surety for the person) and kafalah bi-mal (surety for property). Note the difference in obligation: one guarantees the presence of a person, the other guarantees payment of a debt.
Only kafalah bi-nafs is elaborated in this passage; kafalah bi-mal is mentioned but not detailed.
3
Recognize the verbal formulas for kafalah bi-nafs
Identify the specific Arabic expressions that conclude the contract: 'تكفلت بنس فلان' (I am surety for so-and-so), 'ضمنته' (I guarantee him), etc. These are explicit (sarih) offers indicating the intention to guarantee.
Hanafi law requires explicit wording; mere implication may not be sufficient.
4
Understand the obligation of the surety (kafil)
The surety is required to produce the principal (makful bih) when demanded. If a specific time is stipulated, he must produce at that time. Failure leads to imprisonment by the judge until he produces the person.
The obligation is to produce the person, not to pay money; this is the key difference from kafalah bi-mal.
5
Identify the modes of release (bara'a)
The surety is released by producing the principal in a place where the beneficiary can litigate, or by the death of the principal. The final question hints that delivery in a different location than stipulated does not release the surety.
Death extinguishes the dhimma of the principal, so the surety's obligation ends.

Definition and Division of Kafalah
Defines kafalah and divides it into two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of kafalah and differentiate between kafalah bi al-nafs and kafalah bi al-mal.

Core Idea
Kafalah joins the guarantor's liability to the debtor's; it covers either the person or the property.

This lesson introduces the chapter of kafalah (suretyship). The Hanafi school defines kafalah as the joining of liabilities for the purpose of claim. Understanding this definition and its two primary divisions is essential for grasping the rules of guarantee in transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its definition
Read the passage and note the exact wording of the definition of kafalah: 'ضم ذمة إلى ذمة في المطالبة'.
The key verb is 'ضم' (joining).
2
Parse the definition components
Break down 'ضم ذمة إلى ذمة في المطالبة': 'ضم' = joining, 'ذمة' = liability, 'إلى ذمة' = to another liability, 'في المطالبة' = for the purpose of claim. This means the guarantor's liability is added to the debtor's, not transferred.
The creditor may claim from either party.
3
Recognize the two divisions
Identify the two types: kafalah bi al-nafs (suretyship for the person) and kafalah bi al-mal (suretyship for property).
These cover different subject matters.
4
Apply the definition to examples
Think of real-life scenarios: co-signing a loan (kafalah bi al-mal) and guaranteeing a defendant's court appearance (kafalah bi al-nafs).
This clarifies the practical scope.

Kafalah bi al-Nafs: Contract, Obligation, and Release
Q&A on rulings of kafalah bi al-nafs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid phrases for contracting kafalah bi al-nafs, explain the guarantor’s obligation and the consequence of failure, and identify the conditions for release.

Core Idea
In kafalah bi al-nafs, the guarantor must produce the person or face imprisonment; release requires actual delivery in a suitable place or the debtor’s death.

Kafalah bi al-nafs is a guarantee of a person's presence, a common practice in Islamic finance and legal proceedings. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rulings on how this guarantee is contracted, what duties it imposes, and how the guarantor can be released. The passage uses a question-and-answer format to address key scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify Valid Phrases of Contract
Read the list of phrases that create a valid kafalah bi al-nafs. Note that any expression indicating guarantee of the person—whether general (‘I guarantee his person’) or partial (‘his head’, ‘his half’)—is sufficient.
The key is certainty of meaning; even metaphorical expressions like 'his neck' are valid.
2
Understand the Primary Obligation
The guarantor must produce the person when demanded. If a specific time is stipulated, production is due at that time. Failure to produce after demand leads to imprisonment by the judge.
This is a unique Hanafi enforcement mechanism.
3
Analyze Release Conditions
The guarantor is released by either (a) delivering the person to a place where the creditor can sue him, or (b) the person's death. Delivery in a suitable public space (like a market) is sufficient, but delivery in a remote area (wilderness) is not.
The rationale is that the creditor must have practical access to legal process.
4
Examine Mixed Guarantee
If the guarantor also promised to pay the debt if he fails to produce the person, failure triggers payment but does not cancel the personal guarantee. Both obligations remain.
This shows that personal and financial guarantees are separate but can coexist.

The Ruling and Definition of Kafalah bi al-Mal
Explains validity, phrasing, and conditions of kafalah bi al-mal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and legal ruling of kafalah bi al-mal and identify the condition that the guaranteed debt must be valid.

Core Idea
Kafalah bi al-mal is permissible even if the amount is unknown, as long as the underlying debt is valid.

This lesson introduces kafalah bi al-mal, a form of suretyship in Hanafi fiqh. The passage defines it as a guarantee to pay another's debt and declares it permissible. A key point is that the debt need not be specified as long as it is valid.

Guided Path
1
Define kafalah bi al-mal
Read the first answer in the passage: 'It is that a person guarantees... to pay what is upon him of property to the one who has the right.' This is the definition.
The guarantee is for a financial obligation.
2
State the ruling
The passage says: 'This is permissible in the Shari'ah.' The ruling is ja'iz (mubah).
Permissible, not obligatory.
3
Identify the condition
Note the clause: 'as long as the debt is valid (ṣaḥīḥ).' This means the underlying obligation must be legally recognized.
Examples: sale price, dower, tort compensation.
4
Recognize the leniency
The text explicitly allows the guaranteed item to be known or unknown. This is a Hanafi relaxation.
Other schools may require specification.

Answer: He is believed regarding himself and he is not believed regarding the surety.

Question: Is suretyship permissible without the permission of the principal debtor?

Question: Is it valid for the surety to demand [the debt] from [the principal debtor]?
Answer: It is not valid before [the surety] pays; if he pays on his behalf, it becomes permissible for him to demand [reimbursement] from him.

Answer: He should pursue the principal debtor until he frees him.

Question: The creditor released the principal debtor or collected his right from him. Does anything remain of the surety's suretyship?
Answer: The surety is discharged from the suretyship in this case and nothing remains upon him.

Dispute Resolution and Recourse in Suretyship
Procedural rules for disputes, recourse, and discharge in suretyship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving disputes between surety and debtor, determine when the surety has the right of recourse, and identify the effect of discharge of the debtor on the surety.

Core Idea
In disputes between surety and debtor, the surety's statement with oath is accepted if there is no evidence, and the surety's right of recourse depends on the debtor's permission.

This lesson covers practical aspects of suretyship: how disputes about the amount are resolved, the effect of permission on the surety's right to claim reimbursement, and the circumstances under which the surety is discharged. These rules ensure fairness and prevent unjust enrichment.

Understand the dispute resolution process

Recognize the effect of permission on recourse
If the surety acted with the debtor's permission, he can claim reimbursement after paying. Without permission, he cannot.
Permission creates a form of agency.

Know when the surety can demand reimbursement
The surety can only demand from the debtor after he has paid the creditor.
He cannot demand before payment.
4
Identify the surety's remedy if pursued by the creditor
If the creditor pursues the surety, the surety should pursue the debtor to free himself.
This is a practical remedy.

The surety's liability is secondary.

Creditor's Right to Choose in Kafalah
First ruling: creditor may demand from debtor or surety

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the creditor's right to choose in a kafalah contract and apply it to determine from whom the debt may be demanded.

Core Idea
The creditor may demand payment from either the debtor or the surety, at his option.

In the Hanafi school, a suretyship (kafalah) gives the creditor an additional party to pursue. This lesson explains that the creditor is not forced to demand from the debtor first; he may directly approach the surety. Understanding this basic ruling is essential before exploring more complex scenarios in kafalah.

State the ruling

Apply to a scenario
If Zaid guarantees a loan of 100 dirhams from Amr to Bakr, Amr (creditor) can demand payment from Bakr (debtor) or from Zaid (surety) without any precondition.
4
Recognize the absence of prior requirement
The creditor is not required to first exhaust remedies against the debtor before approaching the surety. The option is immediate.
This is the core of the ruling.

This lesson addresses a common situation in guarantees: when two people guarantee a debt and one ends up paying, can he get anything back from the other? The Hanafi rule is clear: he recovers half of whatever he paid. This ensures fairness between co-guarantors and prevents unjust enrichment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Two people act as guarantors (kafil) for a third person's debt. Critically, each also guarantees the other's guarantee obligation — meaning they are mutual co-guarantors.
This is different from joint debtors or simple co-signers.
2
Determine liability of each guarantor
Each guarantor is liable for the full debt jointly with the other, but since they are also each other's guarantor, their internal liability is equal — 50% each.
The rule of contribution applies between them.
3
State the rule of recourse
If one guarantor pays the creditor any amount (part or full), he has the right to recover half of that amount from the co-guarantor.
The text says: 'يرجع على شريكه بنصف ما أدى قليلا كان أو كثيرا' — he recovers half of what he paid, be it little or much.
4
Apply the rule to any payment amount
For any payment p, the paying guarantor recovers p/2 from the co-guarantor. This applies regardless of whether the payment was small or large.
There is no minimum or maximum; the half rule is universal.
5
Distinguish from joint debtors
In the previous lesson, two people were co-debtors (not guarantors) — they shared liability equally and each paid half directly to the creditor. Here, they are guarantors, so the paying guarantor must first pay, then seek reimbursement.
The distinction affects who pays whom and when.

Suretyship for Kitabah Debt Is Invalid
Ruling that guarantee for kitabah debt is invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suretyship for kitabah debt and explain why it is invalid using the condition of 'sound debt' (dayn sahih).

Core Idea
Suretyship (kafala) is only valid for a sound debt; the kitabah debt lacks full enforceability and cannot be guaranteed.

In this lesson, we examine a clear ruling from al-Quduri: a person cannot act as a surety for the debt owed under a kitabah contract (a slave's agreement to pay for freedom). The reason is that this debt is not a 'sound debt' (dayn sahih) in the Hanafi sense, meaning it is not a fully enforceable financial obligation. Understanding this condition is essential to correctly applying the rules of kafala.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage asks about suretyship (kafala) for the property of kitabah. The ruling is straightforward: it is not permissible (la yajuz).
This is a categorical prohibition; the contract is batil (void).
2
Recognize the condition for valid kafala
The reason given is that the kitabah debt is not a sound debt (dayn sahih). For kafala to be valid, the debt must be sound: known, certain, and fully enforceable.
What makes a debt 'sound'? It must be a fixed liability that cannot be unilaterally remitted by the creditor.
3
Apply the condition to the case
The kitabah debt is unique: the master can forgive it or adjust terms. Hence it lacks the stability of a regular debt, so it fails the dayn sahih condition.
This applies regardless of whether the guarantor is free or a slave.
4
Note the scope of the ruling
The ruling is universal: no one, free or slave, can give a valid guarantee for kitabah debt.
Other schools may differ; but the Hanafi position is clear.

Guarantee for Deceased with No Assets: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on kafalah for insolvent deceased

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the validity of a guarantee for a deceased debtor with no assets, and identify the mufta bhi position.

Core Idea
A guarantee for a debtor who left nothing is valid according to the Sahibayn but invalid per Abu Hanifa — later fatwa adopts the Sahibayn view.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: does a guarantee (kafalah) for the debts of a deceased person who left no assets hold legal weight? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, reached opposite conclusions. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your awareness of how Hanafi jurists reason from competing principles, and highlights the importance of the mufta bhi ruling in later practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and legal question
Read the passage to understand the situation: a man dies with debts and no estate; someone gives surety on his behalf. The question: is this surety valid?
2
Recall Abu Hanifa's ruling and infer his reasoning
Abu Hanifa says the guarantee is invalid. Although the text doesn't give his dalil, infer his principle: the debt is unenforceable because no estate exists; guarantee requires a valid obligation, which is lacking.
Abu Hanifa likely views the debt as effectively extinguished for want of assets.
3
Recall the Sahibayn's ruling and their reasoning
The Sahibayn say the guarantee is valid. Their reasoning: the debt itself is a valid obligation on the deceased; the guarantee creates a new, independent liability on the guarantor, which does not depend on the existence of the estate.
The Sahibayn see the debt as a ongoing legal obligation, not tied to assets.
4
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Hanifa, the guarantor cannot be held liable; according to the Sahibayn, he is liable. This affects creditors' security and the enforceability of guarantees.
The difference directly impacts whether a creditor can sue the guarantor.
5
Determine the mufta bhi position and its rationale
Later Hanafi fatwa commonly adopts the Sahibayn view. It facilitates credit, protects creditors, and upholds the validity of contracts made in good faith.
The tarjih is based on practical utility and the weight of the Sahibayn's authority in later Hanafi thought.

Kafalah is only valid for a valid debt; mal al-kitabah is not a valid debt.

This lesson examines a specific ruling on suretyship (kafalah) concerning the kitabah contract. A valid debt is one that is legally enforceable and binding. In Hanafi law, the slave's kitabah debt lacks full enforceability (e.g., no imprisonment or lawsuit against the slave), so it falls short.
