Automatic Emancipation of a Mahram Relative in Joint Ownership
Question-answer on joint ownership of a mahram relative slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on automatic emancipation of a jointly owned slave who is a mahram relative of one partner, and identify the co-owner's options.

Core Idea
Ownership of a mahram relative triggers automatic emancipation, even in joint ownership—the co-owner may free or seek compensation.

You already know that owning a mahram relative sets them free automatically. But what if you co-own such a slave with another person? This lesson tackles that precise scenario: two men jointly own a slave who turns out to be the son of one of them. Does the father's share alone become free? Must he compensate his partner? The answer clarifies the limits of automatic emancipation and the partner's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering condition
Recognize that the ruling applies only when one of the joint owners is a mahram relative (e.g., son) of the slave.
Mahram here means a relative within prohibited degrees of marriage.
2
Determine the automatic effect
The father's share of ownership is automatically cancelled and the slave becomes free in that proportion.
This happens by operation of law, not by the father's choice.
3
Assess the father's liability
The father does not owe any compensation to the co-owner for his share.
Because the emancipation is automatic, not a voluntary act.
4
State the co-owner's two options
The co-owner may either free his own share voluntarily, or require the slave to work to pay the value of that share (istis'a).
Istis'a means the slave is required to earn his freedom by working for the co-owner.

Validity of Emancipation Despite Blasphemous Intention
Ruling on emancipation with sinful intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that emancipation is valid even if the master declares a sinful intention, and explain why the utterance alone is effective.

Core Idea
A slave is freed by clear words of emancipation, regardless of the master's stated intention.

In Hanafi fiqh, the legal effect of certain acts depends on clear expression, not on the state of mind. This ruling protects the slave's right to freedom and prevents the master from denying emancipation by claiming a bad intention. Understanding this principle is essential for applying similar rules in other areas, such as divorce and vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is emancipation (ʿitq). The ruling is that it is valid and the slave becomes free immediately.
The focus is on the validity, not on the reward.
2
Recognize the master's statement
The master says 'I freed for Satan' or 'for the idol.' Despite the blasphemous object, the words of emancipation are clear.
The utterance is explicit, e.g., 'a'taqtu' or 'anta hurr.'
3
Determine the effective cause
In Hanafi law, the effect of emancipation is produced by the clear utterance itself, not by the intention. The slave’s right to freedom is established by the words.
This is based on the principle that legal consequences follow the outward expression (zāhir al-kalām) not the hidden intent.
4
Apply to similar cases
The same principle applies if the master says it jokingly, under duress, or with a wicked intention. The slave is freed.
Contrast with acts of worship where intention is a condition for reward.

Mutual Testimony of Emancipation: Abu Hanifah vs. the Two
Ruling on emancipation when each partner testifies against the other

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifah and the Two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the slave's payment obligation when joint owners mutually testify to emancipation, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When joint owners each testify the other freed his share, the slave is freed but must compensate — but whether he pays depends on the owners' wealth, per the Two.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi dispute arising from a peculiar scenario: two co-owners each testify against each other that the other freed his share. The testimony, being against interest, establishes freedom of the entire slave. But who bears the cost? Abu Hanifah holds the slave must pay both regardless of wealth; the Two differentiate based on the owners' financial status. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply principles of testimony, property rights, and compensation in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifah's position
Identify the ruling attributed to Abu Hanifah in the text: the entire slave is freed, and the slave must seek to pay each partner for his share, whether they are wealthy or poor.
This is the 'He said' position in the text.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's objection
The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differentiate based on the owners' wealth. If both are wealthy, no payment; if both poor, payment to both; if mixed, payment only to the wealthy one.
This is the 'Wa lahuma' position.
3
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference lies in whether the poor owner also receives payment. Abu Hanifah says always; the Two say only if wealthy.
The underlying principle: the wealthy owner's right to compensation is waived due to lack of need, according to the Two.
4
Determine the tarjih
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the view of the Two (mufta bihi). The text presents Abu Hanifah's view first but then records the Sahibayn's position.
The mufta bihi position is that if both owners are wealthy, the slave owes no payment.

Child of a Slave Woman by Her Master is Free
Ruling on child of slave woman by her master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the status of the child born to a slave woman from her master and explain the legal basis.

Core Idea
A master's child with his slave woman is free — the right of ownership cannot override the father-child bond.

This ruling addresses a specific scenario: when a master fathers a child with his own female slave. Unlike ordinary cases where the child follows the mother's status as property, here the father is the owner, and the child is free. Let's analyze the logic behind this exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mother's status
She is a slave (amah) owned by the father. Determine her legal capacity.
A slave is property, but also a person.
2
Identify the father's status
The father is the master (mawla) and a free man. He owns the mother.
3
Apply the ruling
The child is free (hurr). The father's freedom and paternity confer freedom on the child despite the mother's slavery.
This is an exception to the general rule that children follow the mother's status.

Source Text

ولد الأمة من مولاها حر. ولدها من زوجها مملوك لسيدها. ولد الحرة من العبد حر.
The child of a slave woman from her master is free. The child of a slave woman from her husband is owned by her master. The child of a free woman from a male slave is free.
Source pages p. 396

Commentary
These three rulings summarize the Hanafi position on the status of children born from relationships involving slaves and free persons. First: If a master fathers a child with his own slave woman, the child is free because the father owns the mother. Second: If a slave woman is married to a man (who is not her master) and has a child, that child belongs to the slave woman's master because the child follows the mother's status as property. Third: If a free woman marries a male slave and has a child, the child is free because the mother is free. These rulings are based on the principle that lineage and freedom follow the mother's status, except when the father is the master, in which case the child is free.

Definition and Basic Effect of Tadbir
Definition of tadbir and its legal effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of tadbir, identify its key legal effects, and apply the ruling that the mudabbar cannot be sold or gifted.

Core Idea
Tadbir is a binding declaration that frees the slave upon the master's death, protecting his future freedom.

Tadbir is a unique form of manumission in Hanafi fiqh, where the master defers the slave's freedom to after his own death. This lesson unpacks the definition from the matn and explains the immediate legal consequences: the slave becomes mudabbar, his eventual freedom is secured, and the master's right to sell or gift is restricted. Understanding this foundational ruling is essential for later discussions on inheritance and estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its definition
Recognize that tadbir is the master's declaration linking the slave's freedom to the master's death. The text gives four phrasings: 'When I die, you are free,' 'You are free after my death,' 'You are mudabbar,' or 'I have made you mudabbar.'
The common element is the linkage to the master's death.
2
State the immediate legal effects
Upon the declaration, the slave becomes mudabbar. The master cannot sell or give him away, but may continue to use his services or hire him out. The slave is not freed until the master dies.
The prohibition on sale/gift protects the slave's eventual freedom.
3
Distinguish tadbir from other forms of manumission
Unlike immediate emancipation (i'taq), tadbir defers freedom. Unlike kitabah (contract of manumission), there is no payment required. The mudabbar remains a slave during the master's lifetime with restricted alienability.
This distinction matters for inheritance rules and the slave's status.

Permissible Actions with a Mudabbar Female Slave
Q&A on mudabbar female slave rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on intercourse and marriage with a mudabbar female slave and explain why tadbir does not restrict these actions.

Core Idea
Tadbir defers emancipation, not ownership — the master retains full rights.

Tadbir (deferred emancipation) is a contract where the master declares that a slave will be free upon his death. Some might think this restricts the master's rights over the slave during his lifetime. This short passage from the Hanafi tradition clarifies that the master may still have intercourse with his mudabbar female slave or marry her off to another man, because ownership is unaffected until death.

Guided Path
1
Define tadbir and its legal effect
Tadbir makes the slave free upon the master's death, but during the master's life, the slave remains fully owned. This is the key principle.
The master retains all ownership rights except those specifically restricted by the tadbir agreement, which are none.
2
Identify the two actions questioned
The passage asks about intercourse (wat') and marriage (tazwij) of the mudabbar female slave.
Both actions involve the master's authority over the slave's body and marital status.
3
Apply the principle of retained ownership
Since tadbir does not reduce ownership, the master's rights remain intact. Thus, intercourse is permissible (as long as no other prohibition exists) and marriage to another man is permissible.
Marriage transfers the right to companionship to the husband, but the master's ownership continues; the slave remains mudabbar.
4
Note the distinction from other forms of partial freedom
Compare with a mukatab (slave under a contract of manumission): a mukatab is not fully owned, so intercourse is not permissible. Tadbir does not create partial freedom, only a deferred event.
This distinction helps solidify the rule.

The key point is that tadbir only defers emancipation; it does not affect the master's ownership rights during his life.

Conditional Tadbir (Ta'liq al-Tadbir)
Question about conditional tadbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of conditional tadbir and identify when the slave becomes mudabbar.

Core Idea
A conditional tadbir is valid; the slave becomes mudabbar only when the condition occurs.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario: the master makes tadbir conditional on a future event. The question is whether the slave becomes mudabbar immediately or only upon the condition's fulfillment. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying tadbir rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ta'liq al-tadbir — making emancipation conditional. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) and the slave becomes mudabbar when the condition is fulfilled.
This is analogous to conditional emancipation (ta'liq al-'itq).
2
Parse the condition
The condition must be a future contingency that is possible (e.g., death from a specific illness). The slave's status is suspended until the condition occurs.
If the condition is impossible (e.g., 'if you fly'), the tadbir is void.
3
Determine the moment of mudabbar status
The slave becomes mudabbar at the moment the condition is fulfilled, not before. If the condition never occurs, the tadbir is null and the slave remains ordinary.
This is a key difference from unconditional tadbir, which takes effect immediately.
4
Apply the ruling to the examples
For the mentioned examples (death from this illness, during this journey, from such a disease), the slave becomes mudabbar if and when the master dies accordingly.
If the master dies from a different cause, the tadbir is void.
5
Note the authority and completeness
This passage is from a question-and-answer format; the full ruling is not given in the window. However, based on Hanafi fiqh, the answer is as stated.
Refer to standard texts like al-Hidayah for detailed conditions.
