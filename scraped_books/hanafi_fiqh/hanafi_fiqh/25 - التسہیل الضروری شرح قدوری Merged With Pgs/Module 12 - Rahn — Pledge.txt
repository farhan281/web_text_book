Definition and Formation of Rahn (Pledge) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines rahn and explains its formation and completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define rahn, identify its pillars, and explain the role of possession in its completion.

Core Idea
Rahn is concluded by offer and acceptance but completed only by possession; without possession, the pawner retains an option.

This lesson introduces the chapter on rahn (pledge) from a classical Hanafi text. We will examine the definition of rahn, its essential pillars, and the critical role that possession (qabd) plays in completing the contract. Understanding the distinction between conclusion (in'iqad) and completion (tamam) is key to grasping Hanafi mortgage law.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
Break down the linguistic and shariah definitions of rahn and identify the key elements: detaining something as security for a right.
2
Identify the pillars of rahn
List the rukn (pillars) from the passage: rahn (item), rahin (pawner), murtahin (mortgagee), sighah (offer/acceptance), and qabd (possession).
3
Distinguish conclusion from completion
Note that rahn is concluded (in'iqad) by offer and acceptance but completed (tamam) by possession. Without possession, the contract is not fully binding.
4
Analyze the legal effect of possession
Understand that without possession, the pawner has the option to cancel; with possession, the mortgagee assumes liability (daman).

Definition and Contract of Rahn (Pledge)
Definition and initial rulings on rahn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define rahn, state the conditions for its validity, and apply the liability rules when the pledged item perishes.

Core Idea
Rahn is a contract of security: possession perfects it, and liability is limited to the lesser of value and debt.

This passage lays the foundation for understanding the contract of pledge (rahn) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by defining rahn both linguistically and legally, then explains how the contract is formed and completed. The crucial role of possession (qabd) is emphasized, along with the pledgor's right to withdraw before possession. The passage also details the liability regime when the pledged item perishes, distinguishing between scenarios based on value relative to debt. Finally, it clarifies what kinds of property cannot be validly pledged due to the requirement of distinguished possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of rahn
Read the linguistic and legal definitions. Note that the legal definition ties the detention to a right that can be recovered from the item.
The word 'rahn' linguistically means detention; legally it means security for a debt.
2
Distinguish contract formation from completion
Observe that the contract is formed by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul), but it is not completed until the pledgee takes possession (qabd) in a specific manner.
Possession must be collected, vacated, and distinguished.
3
Analyze the pledgor's option before possession
Understand that before possession, the pledgor can withdraw. Once possession occurs, the contract becomes binding and the pledge enters the pledgee's liability.
This protects the pledgor from being bound prematurely.
4

The excess is not guaranteed because it is held as trust.
5

Understand why musha' (undivided share) and other inseparable items (e.g., fruit without tree) cannot be pledged due to lack of distinguished possession.
This condition ensures the pledgee can physically control the item.

Question: If offer and acceptance exist but the pledgee has not taken possession, what is the ruling? Answer: As long as the pledgee has not taken possession, the pledgor has the option: if he wishes, he may hand it over; if he wishes, he may withdraw from the pledge. And when the pledgor hands over the pledge and the pledgee takes possession, it enters the pledgee's liability. [The phrase 'exists and means that it is not discontinued from the market' appears.] Question: Why was the pledge legislated? Answer: It was legislated for security and recovery of the debt, so that the right of the creditor is not lost. Thus, the pledge is only valid for a guaranteed debt. Question: The pledgee takes possession of the pledged item and it perishes in his hand, upon whom does the liability fall? Answer: We have explained that when the pledgee takes possession of the pledged item, it enters his liability. The jurists have stated that it is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt. Question: What is the meaning of their statement 'guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt'? Answer: Its meaning becomes clear with the following detail: If the pledged item perishes in the hand of the pledgee and its value and the debt are equal, the pledgee becomes as if he has received his debt legally. If its value is less than the debt, the debt is reduced by that amount, and the pledgee pursues the pledgor for the remainder, i.e., the remaining debt. If the value of the pledged item is more than the debt and it perishes in the hand of the pledgee, then what exceeds the debt is considered a trust (amanah) and is subject to the rulings of trusts. So if this pledge perishes in the hand of the pledgee, he does not guarantee this excess because trusts are not guaranteed, and the debt is entirely discharged because what corresponded to his debt from the pledged item perished with him. Question: You have mentioned that the pledge is completed when the pledgee takes possession of the pledge in a collected, vacated, and distinguished manner. Is there any benefit to this condition? Answer: This condition excludes everything that is not like that. Thus, it is not permissible to pledge jointly owned property (musha'), nor to pledge fruit on the tops of palm trees without the palm trees, nor to pledge crops in the land without the land, nor to pledge palm trees without the fruit, nor to pledge land without the crops.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Rahn (Pledge) with a definition both linguistic and legal. Linguistically, rahn means detaining an object; legally, it means making an object detained for a right that can be recovered from it. The contract is formed by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) and is completed by possession (qabd) by the pledgee (murtahin). The pledgee must take possession in a manner that is collected (mahuz), vacated (mufaragh), and distinguished (mumayyaz) – meaning the pledged item is fully separated and identifiable. Until possession, the pledgor (rahin) retains the option to withdraw. Once possession occurs, the pledge enters the pledgee's liability (daman). The purpose of rahn is to secure a debt, so it is only valid for a guaranteed debt (dayn madmin). If the pledged item perishes in the pledgee's hand, liability is based on the lesser of its value and the debt. Three scenarios: (1) Value equals debt – debt is deemed satisfied. (2) Value less than debt – debt reduced by value, pledgee claims remainder from pledgor. (3) Value exceeds debt – debt is fully satisfied, and the excess is treated as a trust (amanah); if the excess perishes, the pledgee does not compensate it because trusts are not guaranteed. The condition of possession being collected, vacated, and distinguished is crucial: it excludes musha' (undivided share), fruit without the tree, crops without land, trees without fruit, and land without crops, as these cannot be properly possessed and separated.

مرتهن (murtahin): The person who receives the pledge as security (creditor).
راهن (rahin): The person who gives the pledge as security (debtor).
قبض (qabd): Taking physical possession of the pledged item in a manner that is collected, vacated, and distinguished.
مشاع (musha'): Undivided share in joint ownership; not valid for pledge because it cannot be possessed separately.

Core Idea
A pledge is guaranteed by the lesser of its value or the debt.

The Hanafi school holds that when a pledge perishes in the pledgee's possession, the guarantee is limited to the smaller amount: either the pledge's value or the debt. This unique principle balances the interests of both parties. The passage explores this rule through three scenarios, making the legal logic clear.

Rahn with Trusts and Business Capitals
Rulings on invalid rahn with trusts and business capitals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that rahn is invalid if the debt is not guaranteed, and distinguish amanat from guaranteed debts.

Core Idea
Rahn is only valid for guaranteed debts; trusts and business capitals are not guaranteed.

This lesson examines which financial rights can be secured by a pledge (rahn). The Hanafi school emphasizes that rahn requires a guaranteed debt (dayn madmun). Trusts like deposits and loans for use are not guaranteed, and mudaraba and partnership capitals are also considered trusts. Therefore, rahn is invalid for these.

Core Idea
The creditor holds the entire pledge until the full debt is paid; partial payment releases nothing.

One common misconception in pledge contracts is that paying off part of the debt allows the rahin to retrieve a corresponding part of the pledge. State the ruling
The rahin cannot take either slave until the full debt is paid. He must take both together after full payment.
The pledge is indivisible for the purpose of release.

The creditor's right over the entire pledge continues until the debt is fully cleared. Partial payment does not fragment that right.
This is based on the principle that the pledge secures the entire debt as a single obligation.
4
Apply to similar cases
If a rahin pledges a single item and pays part, he cannot retrieve any portion of it. If he pledges multiple items, he cannot retrieve any of them until full payment.
The same rule applies whether the items are fungible or specific.

Core Idea
Sale of a pledge is suspended; freeing a pledged slave is effective but security shifts; consumption requires substitution.

This lesson covers several key scenarios where the pledgor (rahin) or a third party acts on the pledged property without the creditor's (murtahin) permission. Identify the act and the actor

Commentary
This passage covers rulings on various acts affecting the pledge. Rulings on Pledge Custody and Guarantee
Q&A rulings on pledge custody and liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible custodians for a pledge and identify when the pledgee becomes liable for the pledge's value.

Core Idea
The pledgee may keep the pledge with his household; depositing it outside makes him fully liable.

This lesson examines the rules governing how a pledgee (murtahin) must care for the pledged item. The Hanafi school provides clear guidance on who may handle the pledge and what happens if the pledgee steps beyond those limits. Understanding these rules protects both the creditor's security and the debtor's property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify permissible custodians
From the answer to the first question, list the persons with whom the pledgee may keep the pledge without incurring liability. The text mentions himself, his wife, his child, and his household servant.
All must be 'in his household' (fi 'iyalihi).
2
Recognize transgressive custody
If the pledgee keeps the pledge with someone outside his household (e.g., a neighbor) or deposits it with a third party, he becomes a transgressor (muta'diyan) and must guarantee the full value if the pledge is lost.
This liability is called daman al-ghasb (guarantee of usurpation).
3
Analyze the effect of returning the pledge
When the pledgee returns the pledge to the pledgor (rahin), the guarantee (daman) is lifted. If the pledge perishes in the pledgor's hands, it is lost without any compensation to the pledgee.
However, the pledgee may take it back later, and the guarantee resumes.
4
Understand post-mortem procedure
If the pledgor dies before repaying the debt, the executor (wasi) or, in his absence, a judge-appointed representative must sell the pledge to satisfy the debt. The surplus goes to the pledgor's heirs.
This ensures the pledgee's right is not lost.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Pledge presents a series of rulings concerning the pledge (rahn) in a question-answer format. The first ruling states that in a sale where a pledge is involved, the buyer has an option: he may either accept the pledge as is or cancel the sale, unless he pays the full price immediately or pays the equivalent of the pledge's value to the seller, in which case the pledge remains with the seller as security. Then, regarding custody of the pledge, the pledgee (the creditor holding the pledge) may keep the pledge with himself, his wife, his children, and his household servants. The liability is specifically the guarantee of usurpation (daman al-ghasb), meaning the full market value. If there is no executor, the judge appoints one. These are clear rulings from the Hanafi school, based on the need to preserve rights and prevent misuse.
