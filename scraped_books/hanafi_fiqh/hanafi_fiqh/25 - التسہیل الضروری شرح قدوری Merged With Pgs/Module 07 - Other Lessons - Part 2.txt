Interpreting Multiplication and Addition in Iqrar
Two rulings on interpreting multiplication and addition phrases in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the amount owed when a debtor uses the phrases 'five in five' and 'five with five' in an acknowledgment of debt, and explain the Hanafi rationale.

Core Idea
In iqrar, multiplication phrasing does not multiply the debt; addition phrasing does.

When someone acknowledges a debt using mathematical expressions, the way those expressions are interpreted can drastically change the amount owed. This lesson presents two rulings from the Hanafi school on common phrases used in iqrar: one involving multiplication and one involving addition. Understanding these rulings is essential for correctly determining obligations in financial acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phrases
The source presents two distinct phrases: 'five in five' (khamsa fi khamsa) and 'five with five' (khamsa ma'a khamsa). Recognize that the first is multiplication and the second is addition.
2
Determine the intended operation
The debtor's stated intention is given: for the first phrase, he intends multiplication; for the second, addition. In each case, the expression is interpreted as such.
3
Apply the Hanafi interpretation
For 'five in five' intending multiplication, the ruling is that only one 'five' is owed (i.e., five), not the product (25). For 'five with five' intending addition, the ruling is that ten is owed (the sum).
The word 'fi' (in) when used for multiplication is not effective in iqrar; the base amount is maintained.
4
Understand the rationale
The Hanafi school takes a strict construction in iqrar: ambiguous mathematical operations are interpreted in favor of the debtor unless clear evidence proves otherwise. Multiplication via 'fi' is ambiguous, so the default amount applies. Addition via 'ma'a' is straightforward.

In the second scenario, the debtor says 'khamsa ma'a khamsa' (five with five), intending addition. He then owes ten (i.e., five plus five). The distinction is critical: 'fi' indicates multiplication which is not effective in iqrar without explicit proof, while 'ma'a' indicates addition which is more straightforward and creates a combined obligation. These rulings show the Hanafi principle of strict construction in iqrar: ambiguous mathematical operations are interpreted in favor of the debtor unless the creditor can prove otherwise.

The Effect of 'Fi' in Hanafi Acknowledgments
Four legal scenarios on the implication of 'fi' in acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the relationship of items in an 'fi' acknowledgment and state whether both or only the primary item is owed.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, 'fi' can imply inclusion or separation based on customary usage.

The word 'fi' (in) is common in acknowledgments, but its legal effect is not uniform. This lesson examines four classical scenarios that reveal the principle: when the second item is a container or accessory, both are owed; when it is a location, only the primary item is owed. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly adjudicating iqrar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two items
In the acknowledgment, note the primary item (the thing acknowledged) and the secondary item introduced by 'fi' (in). For example, 'dates in a basket' — dates are primary, basket is secondary.
The second item is always the object of the preposition 'fi'.
2
Determine the relationship
Is the secondary item a container, accessory, or mere location? In the text, a basket is a container for dates, a stable is a location for an animal, a handkerchief is an accessory for a cloth.
Customary usage (urf) determines the nature of the relationship.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If the secondary item is integral to the primary (e.g., container, accessory), both are owed. If it is merely a location, only the primary item is owed.
This rule is derived from the text's consistent pattern.
4
State the ruling
Conclude what is incumbent upon the person: for dates in a basket, both; for an animal in a stable, only the animal; for a cloth in a handkerchief, both; for a cloth in a cloth, both.
The last two are considered accessories to each other.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acknowledgment 'upon me is a cloth in ten cloths.' While Abu Yusuf interprets the phrase as indicating a single cloth among ten, Muhammad understands it as acknowledging eleven cloths. The disagreement hinges on the meaning of 'fi' (in) and whether it implies inclusion or addition. Knowing which position is relied upon (mufta bihi) is essential for issuing fatwa in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed acknowledgment
The person says 'upon me is a cloth in ten cloths.' The question is what is owed: one cloth or more?
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that only one cloth is owed. He interprets 'fi' as 'from among' – the cloth is one of the ten.
3
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad holds that eleven cloths are owed: the ten cloths plus the one cloth mentioned as 'in' them.
4
Identify the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi works such as Al-Hidayah favor Muhammad's position as the relied-upon view for fatwa.
5
Explain the practical difference
The number of cloths owed differs: one under Abu Yusuf, eleven under Muhammad. The mufta bihi is eleven.

Specificity transforms a debt acknowledgment into a conditional obligation.

This prevents debtors from using vague references to avoid payment.

A connected qualification modifies the acknowledgment; a separate one does not.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about a debtor who acknowledges a debt from the price of goods but claims the dirhams are counterfeit. The key question is whether his claim is accepted. Two rulings on iqrar of pregnancy

Core Idea
Iqrar of pregnancy is valid and binding; ambiguity is resolved by interpreting it as a bequest or inheritance.

Can a person acknowledge that a slave-girl or an animal is pregnant for someone else? The Hanafi school answers clearly: yes, and it is binding. But what if the acknowledgment is vague — e.g., 'For the pregnancy of so-and-so, I owe a thousand dirhams'? The text from Al-Jawhara provides the resolution. This lesson unpacks both rulings.

If the acknowledgment is ambiguous, e.g., 'For the pregnancy of so-and-so, I owe one thousand dirhams' without further detail, then according to Al-Jawhara it is interpreted as a bequest (وصية) or inheritance (ميراث).
The interpretation favors validity by linking it to a known legal cause.

Priority of Lineage Acknowledgment Over Money
First Q&A on conflict between lineage and money acknowledgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person on his deathbed acknowledges money to a stranger and then claims the stranger is his son.

Core Idea
Lineage acknowledgment voids a prior money acknowledgment in death illness.

When a dying person makes conflicting acknowledgments, the law must prioritize. This lesson addresses the conflict between acknowledging money to a stranger and later claiming that same person as a son. The Hanafi ruling gives precedence to lineage, upholding the stronger right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acknowledgments
The person first acknowledges money to a stranger, then acknowledges that same person as his son. Note the sequence and the relationship.
The second acknowledgment changes the status of the recipient from stranger to son.
2
Determine the ruling for each
According to the text, the lineage acknowledgment is valid (sahih), and the money acknowledgment is voided (batil). Understand that lineage takes precedence.
The money acknowledgment is cancelled because it was based on the person being a stranger, which is overridden by the son's stronger claim.
3
Apply the principle
In any similar conflict during death illness, if lineage is acknowledged, it supersedes any prior financial acknowledgment to the same person.
This is specific to death illness (marad al-mawt) where special restrictions on bequests apply.

Question: He acknowledged money to a stranger during death illness, then said: 'He is my son'; what is the ruling on these two acknowledgments? Answer: His acknowledgment of lineage is valid, so the person is established as his son, and his acknowledgment of money is voided. Question: He acknowledged a boy as his son while he is of such age that such a birth is possible; what is the ruling? Answer: If this boy has no known lineage and he confirms his acknowledgment, his lineage is established from the acknowledger. Question: If the acknowledger is sick and acknowledges a boy as his son and his lineage is established, then the acknowledger dies, does this boy share in the inheritance with the other heirs? Answer: Yes, he shares in the inheritance. Question: Is it permissible for a man to acknowledge that so-and-so is his father, so-and-so is his mother, so-and-so is his wife, so-and-so is his child or his master? Answer: Yes, acknowledgment of all that is valid. Question: And what is the ruling on a woman's acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master? Answer: Her acknowledgment of all that is valid. Question: What is the ruling on her acknowledgment of a child? Answer: If the husband does not confirm her in that, her acknowledgment is not accepted; if the husband confirms her or a midwife testifies to her giving birth, the lineage is established from the husband. Question: And whoever acknowledges lineage other than parents and child, such as saying: 'This is my brother,' or 'This is my uncle,' is his acknowledgment accepted? Answer: It is not accepted in terms of lineage. As for inheritance, it is considered: if the acknowledger has a known heir, close or remote, he has priority over the recipient in inheritance; if he has no heir, the recipient inherits from him. (Explanation from Al-Hidayah: because it would impose lineage on someone else; since his lineage is not established from him, he does not compete with a known heir. Inheritance: because he has authority to dispose of his own wealth when there is no heir.)

Commentary
This passage collects rulings on acknowledgment of lineage (nasab) in Hanafi fiqh. The first case shows that if a dying person first acknowledges money to a stranger and then claims he is his son, the lineage acknowledgment takes precedence, and the money acknowledgment is void. This is because lineage is a stronger right and cannot be combined with a bequest to the same person. Next, to establish paternity through acknowledgment, the boy must be of an age that such a birth is possible, he must have no known lineage, and he must confirm the claim. Once established, the son inherits. A man can validly acknowledge parents, spouse, child, and master (mawla), and a woman can also acknowledge all of these except a child; her acknowledgment of a child is only accepted with the husband's confirmation or a midwife's testimony of birth. Finally, acknowledgment of non-direct lineage (brother, uncle) does not establish lineage but may affect inheritance if there is no known heir. This is because, as Al-Hidayah explains, acknowledging such lineage would impose a relative on someone else, and the claimant cannot compete with a known heir. However, if there is no heir, the acknowledgment gives the recipient a right to inheritance through the acknowledger's disposal of his own wealth.

A boy’s lineage is established only if birth is possible, he has no known lineage, and he confirms.

The possibility condition ensures the claim is biologically plausible.

If all conditions are met, lineage is established (thabata nasabuhu). If any condition is missing, the lineage is not established.
The absence of a known lineage prevents conflict with another claimed father.

Acknowledgment (Iqrar) of Lineage and Inheritance
Rules on iqrar for lineage and inheritance in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between types of acknowledgment (iqrar) in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rules for establishing lineage versus granting inheritance rights.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of a father or child establishes lineage; acknowledgment of a brother only confers inheritance rights.

This passage presents key rulings on the legal effect of acknowledging relationships. It differentiates between direct ascendants/descendants and other relatives, a critical distinction in Hanafi inheritance law. Understanding these rules prevents false lineage claims and ensures correct distribution of inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of iqrar
First, recognize that the passage separates acknowledgment into two categories: (1) direct lineage relationships (father, mother, child) and (2) other relatives (brother, uncle). The legal consequences differ sharply.
Direct lineage can establish nasab; other relatives only affect inheritance.
2
Analyze conditions for direct lineage
For a man's acknowledgment of a son, the text requires: the boy must have no known lineage, and he must confirm the acknowledgment. Only then is lineage established. Similarly, a woman's acknowledgment of a child requires the husband's confirmation or a midwife's testimony of birth.
These conditions protect against false claims and maintain family integrity.
3
Understand inheritance-only effect for other relatives
Acknowledging a brother or uncle does not establish lineage. However, if the acknowledging person has no known heir (including distant relatives), the acknowledged person inherits. If there is any known heir, that heir takes precedence.
This rule prevents iqrar from overriding established inheritance rights.
4
Apply the special case of a sick person
Even if the acknowledging person is sick (possibly in deathbed), the acknowledgment of a son is valid and the son inherits alongside other heirs. This shows that iqrar is binding even in critical conditions.
Deathbed acknowledgment is accepted for lineage but may be scrutinized for property.

السؤال: اشترى دارا بثمن، ثم دفع إلى البائع ثوبا، فبماذا يأخذ الشفيع الدار؟ الجواب: يأخذها بالثمن دون الثمن؛ لأن الثوب لم يقع عليه العقد وإنما ملكه بعقد ثان. السؤال : هل يسقط حق الشفعة في بعض الصور؟ الجواب: إذا باع دارا إلا مقدار ذراع في طول الحد الذي يلي الشفيع فإنه يبطل حق الشفيع بذلك؛ لأنه لم يوجد الاتصال بملك الشفيع وهذه حيلة يختارها الناس لإسقاط الشفعة. السؤال: هل هناك حيلة أخرى غير ما ذكرتم؟ الجواب: ذكر الفقهاء حيلة سوى ما ذُكر، وهي أن يبتاع من دار سهما بثمن، ثم يبتاع بَقِيَّتها فيحصل حق الشفعة للجار في السهم الأول دون السهم الثاني. السؤال: فهل تكره الحيلة في إسقاطها ؟ الجواب: لا تكره الحيلة عند أبي يوسف وتكره عند محمد رحما. بتسع السهم الثاني: قال صاحب "الهداية": لأن الشفيع جار فيهما إلا أن المشتري في الثاني شريك فيتقدم عليه، فإن أراد الحيلة ابتاع السهم بالثمن إلا درهما مثلا، والباقي بالباقي. وقال صاحب "الجوهرة": صورتها: رجل له دار تساوي ألفا فأراد بيعها على وجه لا يأخذها الشفيع، فإنه يبيع العشر منها مبتاعا تسعة مائة، ثم يبيع أعشارها بمائة، فالشفعة إنما تثبت في عشرها خاصة بثمنه ولا تثبت له الشفعة في التسعة الأعشار؛ لأن المشتري حين اشترى تسعة أعشارها صار شريكا فيها بالعشر عند محمد لمحمد أن الشفعة وجبت لدفع الضرر، فلو أبحنا الحيلة ما دفعناه، ولأبي يوسف أنه منع عن إثبات الحق فلا يُعد ضررا. من "الهداية".
Question: He bought a house for a price, then gave a garment to the seller; with what does the pre-emptor take the house? Answer: He takes it with the price, not the garment, because the contract was not made on the garment; rather he owned it through a second contract. Question: Is the right of pre-emption waived in some cases? Answer: If he sells the house except a cubit width along the boundary adjacent to the pre-emptor, then the pre-emptor's right is nullified because connection to the pre-emptor's property does not occur; this is a trick people choose to nullify pre-emption. Question: Is there another trick besides what you mentioned? Answer: The jurists mentioned a trick besides that: that he buys a share of the house for a price, then buys the remainder; thus the pre-emptor's right occurs in the first share but not the second. Question: Is the trick disliked? Answer: The trick is not disliked according to Abu Yusuf, but disliked according to Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them. By nine? The second share: The author of al-Hidayah said: Because the pre-emptor is a neighbor in both, but the buyer in the second is a partner, so he takes precedence. If he wants the trick, he buys the share for the price except one dirham, and the remainder for the remainder. The author of al-Jawharah said: Its form: A man has a house worth a thousand; he wants to sell it in a way that the pre-emptor does not take it: he sells a tenth for nine hundred, then sells the remaining tenths for one hundred; thus pre-emption is established only in that tenth at its price, and pre-emption is not established for the nine tenths; because the buyer, when he bought nine tenths, became a partner in it with the tenth. According to Muhammad, pre-emption is established to repel harm, so if we permitted the trick we would not repel it. According to Abu Yusuf, it prevents the establishment of a right, so it is not considered harm. From al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of tricks to nullify the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The text begins with a ruling: if someone buys a house and pays with a garment instead of cash, the pre-emptor takes the house with the original price, not the garment, because the garment was a separate transaction. It then explains a valid trick: selling the house except a small strip adjacent to the pre-emptor nullifies the right because the property does not adjoin the pre-emptor's land. Another trick is to first sell a small share, then the rest; the pre-emptor only has right over the first share, not the second, because the buyer of the rest becomes a partner. The core dispute is whether such tricks are disliked (makruh). Imam Muhammad considers them makruh because the purpose of shuf'ah is to prevent harm, and tricks defeat that purpose. Imam Abu Yusuf does not consider them makruh because the seller is merely preventing a right from being established, which is not harmful. The passage then provides further elaboration from the leading commentators, including examples with tenth shares and quotes from al-Hidayah and al-Jawharah. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated, but the disagreement is presented.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf'ah): The right of pre-emption; the right of a co-owner or neighbor to purchase a sold property by paying its price.
الحيلة (al-hīlah): A legal stratagem to achieve a permissible outcome while technically complying with the law.
الضرر (al-ḍarar): Harm; a key concept in the reasoning: Muhammad sees the trick as perpetuating harm, Abu Yusuf does not.
