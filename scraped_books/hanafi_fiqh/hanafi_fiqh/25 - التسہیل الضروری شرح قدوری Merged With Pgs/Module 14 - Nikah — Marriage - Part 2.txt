Intercourse with Two Slave Sisters by Right of Possession
Ruling on having intercourse with two slave women who are sisters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intercourse with two slave sisters and identify the permissible ways to subsequently engage with the other sister.

Core Idea
A master may not have intercourse with two slave sisters; after enjoying one, the other is unlawful until the first is removed from ownership.

In this brief Q&A from a classical Hanafi text, we encounter a clear ruling that extends the Quranic prohibition of marrying two sisters to the domain of milk al-yamin (right of possession). The text not only prohibits collecting both sisters but also provides lawful exits for the master who wishes to later have intercourse with the other sister. Understanding this ruling helps us see how Hanafi fiqh applies the principle of consistency between marriage and concubinage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited act
The man has two slave sisters; the act of having intercourse with both is prohibited. Start by recognizing that the hukm is haram.
The prohibition is not just for simultaneous intercourse but also sequential while both remain in his possession.
2
Recognize the conditional permission
The text allows him to enjoy whichever he wishes first, but after that, the other is forbidden unless he makes the first unlawful to himself. Understand the condition 'unless'.
The condition removes the bar – once the first is made permanently inaccessible, the second becomes permissible.
3
List the methods to make the first unlawful
The ways are: marry her to another man, make a kitabah contract (gradual emancipation), or remove her from ownership by freeing, gifting, selling, or giving in charity.
All methods effectively terminate the master's right of possession over the first sister, thus removing the prohibition of jam'.
4
Connect to the underlying legal principle
The prohibition is based on qiyas to marriage where two sisters cannot be married simultaneously. Hanafis apply the same hukum to concubinage because the right of possession is analogous to marriage in this respect.
This reflects the maxim 'al-haram fi al-milk ka al-haram fi al-nikah'.

السؤال: رجل له مملوكتان وهما أختان هل يجوز له أن يطأهما بملك اليمين؟ الجواب: لا يحل له وطؤهما كلتاهما، وله أن يستمتع بأيتهما شاء، فإذا استمتع بإحداهما فليس له أن يستمتع بالأخرى بعد ذلك ما لم يُحرِّم الأولى على نفسه. السؤال: كيف يحرمها على نفسه ؟ الجواب: يزوجها من رجل أو يكاتبها أو يُخرجها من ملكه بإعتاق أو هبة أو بيع أو صدقة.

Commentary
This passage deals with the prohibition of collecting two sisters in sexual relations even when both are slave women owned by the same man. The ruling states that he cannot have intercourse with both sisters simultaneously or sequentially while both remain in his possession. He may choose one, but after having intercourse with her, he cannot enjoy the other sister unless he first makes the first one unlawful to himself (i.e., removes the possibility of returning to her). The ways to make her unlawful are: marrying her off to another man, making a kitabah contract (which is a contract of gradual emancipation where the slave buys her freedom), or removing her from his ownership by freeing her, gifting her, selling her, or giving her in charity. This effectively separates the two sisters so that the prohibition of jam' (collection) is removed. This is based on the Quranic verse prohibiting collecting two sisters in marriage, analogically extended to concubinage (milk al-yamin). The authority is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

مملوكتين (mamlūkatayn): Two female slaves owned by a person, with full right of possession.
مكاتبة (mukātaba): A contract of kitabah: the slave agrees to pay a sum for her freedom in installments, becoming a mukatabah.

Guardian in Marriage and Order of Lineage Guardians
Definition of wali and order of lineage guardians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four causes of guardianship in marriage and recite the order of lineage guardians ('asabaat) in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The closest lineage guardian is a woman's own son, then grandson, then father — the order follows the 'asabaat hierarchy in inheritance.

This passage from a classical Hanafi matn defines who can act as a guardian (wali) in marriage and the order of precedence among lineage guardians. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for determining the validity of marriage contracts in the Hanafi school. The text lists the guardians in descending order of closeness, mirroring the inheritance order of 'asabaat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Causes
Read the opening sentence: guardianship is established by four causes. List them: lineage, wala', imamate, and ownership.
These cover all possible guardians.
2
Understand the Lineage Guardians
The text specifies that the lineage guardians are the 'asabaat (agnatic male relatives) in the order of inheritance.
The order is 'aqrab fa'aqrab (nearest then next).
3
Memorize the Order
The order begins with the woman's son, then grandson however low, then father, then grandfather however high, then full brother, then half-brother, then sons of brothers, then paternal uncles.
The text cuts off but the pattern continues similarly.
4
Apply the Principle
Given a set of relatives, determine the closest guardian by applying this order.
Remember: the son is closer than the father.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
According to the Hanafi school, an adult sane woman may contract her own marriage without a guardian, so this order applies primarily when a guardian is desired or for minors.
This contrasts with the Shafi'i school where a wali is required.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on guardians and competence (kafa'ah). It defines the wali (guardian) for marriage and outlines the four causes of guardianship: lineage (qarabah), clientage (wala' - due to freeing a slave), imamate (the ruler), and ownership (milk - over a slave). The focus here is on lineage guardians. They are the 'asabaat (agnatic male relatives) in the same order as inheritance. The order begins with the woman's own son (who is her 'asabah), then grandson, then father, then paternal grandfather, then full brother, then half-brother, then nephews, then uncles. The text uses 'aqrab fa'aqrab' (closest then next). This order is critical for determining who has the right to contract marriage on a woman's behalf. The passage cuts off mid-list, but the pattern continues with the next of kin. This is a foundational text in Hanafi marriage law. The authority is Zahir al-Riwayah.

العصبة (al-'aṣaba): Agnatic male relatives who inherit through 'asabah (residuary). They are the guardians for marriage.

Prohibition of Mother and Daughter After Zina
Questions on marriage prohibitions due to zina, step-daughter, sister of divorced wife, and slave sisters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that zina with a woman makes her mother and daughter permanently prohibited to the man and identify the conditions that trigger this prohibition.

Core Idea
Zina with a woman makes her mother and daughter permanently prohibited to the man.

This lesson clarifies that the prohibition of marriage due to zina extends beyond the woman involved to her mahram relatives, specifically her mother and daughter. The presence of desire (shahwa) is key.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is zina (illegitimate intercourse) or touching with desire. The ruling is that the woman's mother and daughter become prohibited (haram) for marriage.
The prohibition is permanent, like a mahram relationship.
2
Analyze the condition of desire
The text specifies 'or touches her with desire or she touches him similarly'. This means the prohibition applies even without full intercourse if there is lustful touch. Note that the condition is bilateral.
The phrase 'aw hiya mustahhu kathalik' shows intention matters.
3
Understand the scope of prohibition
The prohibition applies to the woman's mother and daughter, not all her relatives. This is a specific ruling derived from the Quranic principle that zina creates a similar prohibition to marriage.
Other relatives may become prohibited through marriage, not zina.
4
Contrast with marriage prohibition
In valid marriage, the wife's mother and daughter become mahram immediately. Zina creates the same prohibition, highlighting that illicit intimacy can also establish mahram relationships.
This ruling is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Mahr is wajib by marriage contract itself; a slave's mahr is a debt on his person.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational concept of mahr (dower) in the Hanafi school.
