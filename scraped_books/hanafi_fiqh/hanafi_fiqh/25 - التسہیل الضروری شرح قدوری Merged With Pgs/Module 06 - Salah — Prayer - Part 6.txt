How to Carry the Bier and Walking Pace
Ruling on carrying the bier and walking pace

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended method for carrying the funeral bier and the permissible walking pace according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Carry the bier swiftly but without trotting — haste with dignity.

After the funeral prayer, the deceased is carried to the grave. The Hanafi school prescribes a specific method to honor the deceased while hastening the burial. This lesson lays out the simple procedure for carrying the bier and the proper pace of walking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The passage addresses how men carry the bier and how they walk. It covers both multiple carriers and a single carrier.
2
State the method for multiple carriers
When several men carry, they each take hold of one of the four legs (or handles) of the bier.
3
State the method for a single carrier
If only one person lifts, they place the bier on their shoulders in this order: right front corner on right shoulder, right back on right shoulder, left front on left shoulder, left back on left shoulder.
4
State the walking pace
They walk swiftly (musri'in) but without trotting (khabab). Khabab is a rapid trotting gait resembling a horse; it is prohibited.

Rulings on Sitting in the Cemetery and Digging the Grave
Rulings on cemetery sitting and grave digging.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on sitting in a cemetery before burial and describe the preferred method of digging a grave.

Core Idea
Sitting is permissible but disliked before the bier is placed; lahd is the preferred grave method.

This passage from a classical Hanafi text addresses two practical concerns: the permissibility of sitting in a cemetery before the funeral and the proper method of constructing the grave. These rulings help the community maintain respect for the deceased and follow the sunnah of burial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Recognize that the passage first asks about sitting in the cemetery. The default ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz).
Permissibility is the default unless a prohibition is established.
2
Note the disliked situation
The passage adds an exception: sitting is disliked (makruh) before the bier is placed down from the men's shoulders. Understand that this is a temporal restriction.
The dislike is because it may show disrespect at that moment.
3
Identify the recommended grave depth
The grave should be dug to half a man's height or to the chest. Deeper is good but not obligatory.
Depth is a recommended practice, not a condition of validity.
4
Distinguish between lahd and shaqq
Lahd (a side niche facing qibla) is the preferred method. Shaqq (a trench in the middle) is permissible only when the soil is too soft for a niche.
This distinction reflects practical adaptation to soil conditions.
5
Summarize the two rulings
Recap: sitting is generally permissible but makruh before the bier is set; the grave should be dug deep and lahd is best, but shaqq is allowed for soft soil.

The preferred method is lahd: a niche dug on the qibla side of the grave where the body is placed. If the ground is soft and a niche cannot be made, then shaqq (a trench dug in the middle of the grave) is permissible. This reflects Hanafi practice.

Place the deceased on the right side facing qibla, recite the supplication, and untie the shroud.

This lesson covers the practical steps of placing the body in the grave, a final act of care for the deceased. Knowing the correct method ensures the burial is performed according to the Sunnah.

Question: How is the deceased entered into the grave? Answer: He is entered from the direction of the qibla, and the one who places him in his niche says: "Bismillah wa billah wa 'ala millati rasulillah" (In the name of Allah, by Allah, and upon the creed of the Messenger of Allah). He turns him to face the qibla on his right side like a person lying down. He unties the knots of the shroud that they tied for fear of it spreading, and he levels the unbaked bricks on the niche, then pours soil. Question: If they used baked bricks, wood, or reeds for leveling the niche, what is the ruling? Answer: Baked bricks and wood are disliked (makruh), but there is no problem with reeds.

Commentary
This passage details the procedure for placing the body in the grave. The deceased is entered from the side of the qibla. The person placing the body recites the supplication. The body is turned onto its right side facing the qibla, as one lies down. The knots of the shroud are untied to allow the body to rest naturally. Then, unbaked bricks (or similar) are arranged to cover the niche, and soil is poured. Regarding materials for the niche, baked bricks (aajur) and wood are makruh (disliked) because they resemble luxury or are wasteful, but reeds (qasab) are permissible as they are natural and simple.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah shape of a grave and identify which construction materials are prohibited (ma'siyah) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The grave should be humped and low; plastering or cementing is prohibited disobedience.

This lesson covers two key rulings on grave construction after burial: the recommended shape above ground and the prohibition of using permanent materials like plaster or cement. These rulings are based on prophetic practice and prohibition, and they form part of the Hanafi funeral etiquettes that preserve simplicity and avoid extravagance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act under discussion
The passage addresses two separate acts: (1) shaping the grave after filling it with soil, and (2) using plaster or cement on the grave. Recognize that these are distinct rulings.
2
State the sunnah shape
The grave should be made musannam (humped like a camel's back), not flat (musattah), and the height should not exceed one handspan (shibr).
This is the preferred method according to the Hanafi school.
3
Identify the prohibited actions
Plastering the grave (tajsis) with gypsum, cement, lime, or any similar permanent building material is prohibited (ma'siyah) because the Prophet prohibited it.
The prohibition includes any form of permanent construction that indicates extravagance or imitation of non-Muslim practices.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
The prohibition aims to prevent exaggeration in honoring the dead, preserve the dignity of graves, and avoid waste. The sunnah encourages simplicity and equality in death.
This rationale helps distinguish between permissible temporary markers and prohibited permanent structures.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on divisibility of emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on partial emancipation (Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn) and explain the thamarah al-khilaf in terms of sa'yah and options for the co-owner.

Core Idea
Emancipation is indivisible according to the mufta bihi position — freeing part frees the whole.

This passage tackles two related scenarios: a master freeing part of his own slave, and a partner freeing his share in a jointly-owned slave. The central question is whether emancipation (i'taq) can be partial. Abu Hanifa holds it is divisible, leading to partial freedom and the slave working to compensate the unfreed portion (sa'yah). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold it is indivisible, so freeing any part frees the entire slave without sa'yah. This difference generates distinct rulings for the co-owner's options. Let us break down the arguments and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) a master frees part of his slave, (2) one partner frees his share in a jointly-owned slave. Note that both revolve around the same legal principle.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its consequence
Abu Hanifa says emancipation is divisible. Thus, if a master frees part of his slave, only that part becomes free. The slave must perform sa'yah to earn the remaining value. For a joint slave, the freeing partner's share is freed; the other partner has three options if the freed is wealthy (free his share, seek compensation, or sa'yah), or two if insolvent (free his share or sa'yah).
The sa'yah concept is unique to Hanafi fiqh in this context.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its consequence
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold emancipation is indivisible. Freeing part frees the whole. Therefore, no sa'yah is required. For a joint slave, the only option for the other partner is to hold the freeing partner liable (if wealthy) or make the slave work (if insolvent); no option to free his own share because the whole is already free.
Their view aligns with Imam al-Shafi'i and is the mufta bihi.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is whether a partially freed slave is partially free (with sa'yah) or fully free (without sa'yah). In the joint ownership case, it affects the co-owner's options: Abu Hanifa allows the co-owner to free his share as a separate act, while the Sahibayn do not because the entire slave is already free.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the Sahibayn's view (full freedom without sa'yah) as the relied-upon position for fatwa. This is more cautious in granting freedom and simpler in practice.
Always cite the mufta bihi when giving fatwa.

Four Rulings on Emancipation: Mahram, Mutual Testimony, Corrupt Intent, and Master's Child
Four additional rulings on emancipation: joint mahram ownership, mutual testimony, corrupt intent, and child of master.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling in four miscellaneous emancipation cases: joint ownership of a mahram, mutual testimony without confession, corrupt intention, and the child of a master from his slave woman.

Core Idea
Emancipation is heavily favored: ownership of a close relative frees automatically, mutual testimony of partners frees the slave, and corrupt intent does not negate valid words.

In the previous lesson, you learned that owning a mahram relative triggers automatic emancipation. Here, the text extends that principle to joint ownership and also addresses three other distinct scenarios: when two partners testify against each other without admitting their own emancipation, when the master's intention is corrupt, and the status of a child born to a slave woman from her master. Each ruling illustrates how Hanafi fiqh prioritizes freedom (hurriya) even in complex or morally ambiguous situations.

Guided Path
1
Case 1 – Joint Ownership of a Mahram Son
Identify the scenario: two co-owners, one is the father of the slave. The father's share becomes free automatically by virtue of mahram relationship (ownership of dhu rahim muharram). The father is not liable for the partner's share; the partner has the option to free his share or require the slave to work for value (sa'yah).
This extends the rule from full ownership to partial ownership.
2
Case 2 – Mutual Testimony Without Confession
Two partners each testify that the other freed his share, but neither admits freeing his own. According to Abu Hanifa, the slave is completely free and must work for each of them (sa'yah) regardless of their wealth. The Sahibayn differentiate: if both are wealthy, the slave works but no one receives the value; if both are insolvent, he works for them; if one is wealthy and one insolvent, he works only for the wealthy.
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: the Sahibayn's view is often preferred in later fatwa.
3
Case 3 – Emancipation with Corrupt Intention
If the master says 'I free this slave for the devil' or 'for an idol,' the emancipation is still valid. The ruling depends on the explicit words of emancipation, not the intention behind them.
This contrasts with other acts where intention matters; here, freedom is given effect to favor liberty.
4
Case 4 – Child of Master from Slave Woman; Harbi's Slave Entering Islam
The child born to a slave woman from her master is free (because the master is the father, and paternity establishes freedom). Additionally, a slave belonging to a harbi who escapes to dar al-islam as a Muslim becomes free.
Both rulings affirm the Hanafi principle that freedom is the default when there is ambiguity or a cause favoring it.

Hiraz is either a secure place or a guardian; theft from either triggers the hadd of cutting.

In Hanafi fiqh, the hadd penalty for theft (cutting the hand) is not applied arbitrarily — it requires precise conditions. One of the most important conditions is that the stolen property was in hiraz (secure protection). This lesson defines hiraz and clarifies the two forms it can take, based on the classical Hanafi text.

Apply the ruling
If a theft occurs from any of these two types of hiraz, the hadd penalty of cutting becomes obligatory (wajib) on the thief, provided all other conditions are met.
The ruling is clear: presence of either type triggers the obligation.

Rulings on Nabidh: Cooking and Intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions that make nabidh halal: minimal cooking, non-intoxicating amount, and absence of amusement.

Core Idea
Nabidh becomes halal when minimally cooked, even if later it becomes strong, as long as it is not consumed for intoxication.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Honey/Fig/Grain Nabidh: Imam Muhammad's Stricter View
Presents Imam Muhammad's stricter ruling on honey/fig/grain nabidh and its consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Muhammad's position on drinks from honey, figs, and grains, and explain how it differs from the early Hanafi position, including the practical consequences for impurity and hadd.

Core Idea
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani rules that all intoxicating drinks from honey, figs, etc. are haram absolutely, pure or impure, and the mufta bihi view follows him.

This lesson dives into the final part of the passage, where the text explicitly references Imam Muhammad's stricter ruling. You will learn how the school's later fatwa shifted towards his view, affecting rulings on najasah (impurity) and hudud (fixed punishments).
