Initial Actions for the Dying Person
Instructions for those present with a dying person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four recommended actions for the dying person and identify their hukm level as mustahabb.

Core Idea
When death approaches, turn to qiblah and prompt shahada; after death, close eyes and tie jaws.

This lesson covers the initial rulings for a person in the throes of death (muhtadar). Based on the Hanafi school, these actions are recommended (mustahabb), not obligatory, but they carry great merit. The passage distinguishes between actions before death (turning to qiblah and talqeen) and after death (closing eyes and tying jaws). The same rules apply to men and women.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phases
Recognize that the passage separates actions into those before death and those after death. The phrase 'فإذا مات' marks the transition.
2
List the actions for each phase
Before death: turn to qiblah on right side, prompt shahada. After death: tie jaws, close eyes.
3
Determine the hukm level
These are not fard or wajib but mustahabb (recommended). The text implies permissibility of omission.
4
Note the gender equality
The passage explicitly states the same ruling applies to women: 'وحكم المرأة في ذلك مثل ذلك'.

Commentary

The answer describes three actions: First, the dying person should be placed on his right side facing the qiblah. This is based on the sunnah of making the dying person face the qiblah, as it is the direction of prayer and the desired state at death. This does not mean to repeat it if he says it, but to remind him gently. Third, after death occurs, those present should tie his jaws shut (to prevent the mouth from hanging open) and close his eyes. This passage sets the stage for subsequent rulings on washing, shrouding, and funeral prayer.

The Hanafi Method of Washing the Deceased
Procedure for washing the deceased with materials and method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the step-by-step procedure for washing the deceased according to the Hanafi sunnah and identify the materials used.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a communal obligation; the sunnah method involves three washings with water containing cleansing agents and camphor.

In this lesson, we examine the detailed procedure for washing a deceased Muslim as presented in the classical Hanafi text. The passage addresses the steps, materials, and variations when materials are unavailable. Understanding this procedure is essential for fulfilling the communal obligation with proper adherence to the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the preparatory steps
The body is placed on a fumigated bed, the private parts are covered, clothes removed, and wudu is performed without rinsing the mouth or nose.
The fumigation is sunnah; wudu is done for the body's purification.
2
Understand the sequence of washings
Three washings: first on the left side, then on the right side, then after sitting up and wiping the belly, a final washing on the left side with camphor.
The belly wipe expels any remaining matter; the washing is not repeated if something comes out.
3
Recognize the use of materials
Khitmi is used for head and beard, sidr or potash for boiling water, and camphor for the final washing.
These are sunnah; the permissibility of soap is mentioned.
4
Note the accommodations
If sidr, khitmi, or camphor are unavailable, plain water suffices. Soap is permissible as an alternative cleansing agent.
The obligation is to wash with water; other materials are recommended.
5
Summarize the ruling
Washing the deceased is fard kifayah; the method described is sunnah muakkadah. The three washings are the Hanafi relied-upon position.
Other schools may require more washings, but Hanafis fix three.

Section on washing the deceased
Question: When the deceased dies, how is he washed?
Answer: When they intend to wash him, they place him on a bed that is smoked with incense an odd number [of times], and they place a cloth over his private parts, and remove his clothes, and perform wudu for him without rinsing the mouth and without [sniffing water into] the nose, and wash his head and beard with marshmallow plant [soap]. Then he is laid on his left side, and washed until it is seen that water has reached the part adjacent to the bed. Then he is laid on his right side, and washed until it is seen that water has reached the part adjacent to the bed. Then the washer sits him up and leans him against himself, and wipes his belly gently. If something comes out of him, he washes it, and does not repeat his washing. Then he lays him on his left side, and washes him, pouring water over him that contains camphor. Thus the three [washings] are completed.
Question: Is something besides water used for cleaning?
Answer: Yes, water is boiled with jujube leaves or with potash and he is washed with it. If soap is used, it is permissible.
Question: If these things are not found?
Answer: Then pure water.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the method of washing the deceased, presented in a question-answer format. The teacher explains that the procedure begins by placing the body on a bed that has been fumigated with incense an odd number of times (preferably three, five, or seven) to remove odors. The private parts are covered with a cloth, and clothes are removed. Wudu is performed but without rinsing the mouth or nose, because the deceased cannot perform the act properly. The head and beard are washed with khitmi (a cleansing agent from marshmallow plant). The body is then washed three times: first on the left side, then on the right side, then after sitting the deceased up and gently wiping his belly (to expel any remaining matter, which is washed but does not require re-washing), the final washing is done on the left side with water mixed with camphor. The total washings are three. For cleansing, water boiled with sidr (jujube leaves) or hurdh (potash) may be used; soap is also permissible. If none are available, plain water suffices. This reflects the Hanafi reliance on the sunnah as transmitted from the Companions.

Key Terms
خطمي (khitmi): A plant (Althaea) used as a cleansing agent; here it is used to wash the head and beard of the deceased.
كافور (kafur): Camphor, a fragrant substance added to the water in the third washing.
سدر (sidr): Jujube leaves, used to boil water for washing the deceased.

Shrouding of a Man: Required and Sunnah Pieces
Rules for the shrouding of a man and woman with required and optional pieces

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three sunnah pieces of a man's shroud and explain the manner of shrouding.

Core Idea
The sunnah shroud for a man is three pieces: izār, qamīṣ, and lifāfa; two suffice, one is makruh.

Shrouding the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). This passage details the sunnah pieces for a man and the procedure for wrapping. Understanding these rulings ensures proper burial according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah pieces
The text states the sunnah shroud consists of izār, qamīṣ, and lifāfa. Note that the qamīṣ has no pocket, armpit openings, or sleeves.
Izār covers from head to foot; lifāfa is the outer wrap; qamīṣ from neck to foot.
2
Distinguish sunnah from sufficient
The sufficient shroud is two pieces (izār and lifāfa). In necessity, any available cloth suffices. Using only one cloth is makruh (disliked) without excuse.
This shows flexibility while maintaining the ideal.
3
Note the specifications of each piece
Izār: head to foot. Lifāfa: head to foot. Qamīṣ: from base of neck to foot, no pockets, armpit openings, or sleeves.
These specifications ensure complete covering.
4
Describe the shrouding procedure
Fumigate the shrouds odd times, spread lifāfa, then izār, place the dried body, apply hanūṭ on head/beard, and camphor on seven prostration points (forehead, nose, palms, knees, feet).
Drying prevents wetting the shroud.

Commentary
This passage explains the rules of kafan (shrouding). مساجد (masājid): The seven body parts that touch the ground during prostration (forehead, nose, hands, knees, feet).

Sunnah Shroud for Men and Women: Garments and Procedure
Explains the obligatory and recommended parts of the shroud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five pieces of the sunnah shroud for a woman, explain the procedure for shrouding a man and a woman, and identify the relied-upon Hanafi view on the placement of the breast-cloth.

Core Idea
The sunnah shroud for a woman is five garments; for a man, three; basic covering is obligatory, additional pieces are sunnah.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's matn details the sunnah method of shrouding for men and women. It explains which garments are obligatory, which are sunnah, and the order of dressing. The text also clarifies a disputed point about the placement of the breast-cloth, relying on the view from al-Ikhtiyar supported by hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify obligatory vs. sunnah elements
Recognize that the izār (loincloth) and lifāfah (wrapper) are obligatory for both men and women. For women, the khimār (headcover) is also obligatory. The shirt (qamīs for men, dir' for women) and breast-cloth (khirqah) are sunnah.
The text says the shroud is 'sunnah' meaning the complete set is recommended; the minimum is wajib.
2
List the female shroud pieces
Memorize the five pieces: dir' (shift), izār, khimār, lifāfah, and khirqah (breast-cloth). Understand that the dir' is the same as qamīs.
The khimār is three cubits long.
3
Understand the shrouding procedure step by step
Trace the order: spread lifāfah, then izār, place deceased, dress in dir', braid hair on chest, place khimār over head and braids, tie khirqah over the dir', then fold izār and lifāfah. Tie sides if needed.
For men, the shirt is put on first, then izār wrapped left then right, then lifāfah.
4
Note the dispute on the breast-cloth placement
Some Hanafi jurists place the breast-cloth over all shrouds, but Ibn Abidin chooses the view that it is tied over the shirt (dir'), based on hadith. This is the mufta bihi.
The dispute is an intra-Hanafi legitimate ikhtilaf.
5
Apply the ruling to real scenarios
In necessity, minimum is izār, lifāfah, and khimār for women; for men, izār and lifāfah. Using only two garments without excuse is makruh.

The Five Fixed Miqat for Hajj and Umrah
Lists five fixed Miqat with modern commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five Miqat locations and state the ruling for crossing them without ihram.

Core Idea
The Prophet ﷺ fixed five Miqat; crossing any without ihram is prohibited.

This lesson presents the five Miqat (boundaries) set by the Prophet ﷺ for entering ihram. Knowing your Miqat is essential for a valid pilgrimage; crossing without ihram incurs a sin and requires a sacrifice in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states it is not permissible (لا يجوز) to cross any Miqat without being in ihram. In Hanafi fiqh, this is a prohibition (haram) and missing it requires a dam (sacrifice).
The ruling applies to both residents of that region and those passing through.
2
Memorize the five locations
List the five Miqat: Dhu'l-Hulayfah (for Madinah), Dhat 'Irq (for Iraq), al-Juhfah (for Syria), Qarn (for Najd), Yalamlam (for Yemen).
Know both the classical names and modern equivalents (e.g., Abyar Ali for Dhu'l-Hulayfah).

The pledgor cannot reclaim a proportional share; only full payment to all creditors releases the asset entirely.

Causes and Scope of Interdiction in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition of interdiction and its three causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of interdiction in Hanafi fiqh and explain why interdiction applies to statements but not to liability for torts.

Core Idea
Interdiction restricts property disposal for minors, slaves, and the insane, but liability for damage remains.

Interdiction (hajr) is a protective measure in Islamic law that limits a person's ability to dispose of property due to incapacity. This lesson introduces the three causes of interdiction as outlined in the Hanafi school: minority, slavery, and insanity. We will also clarify an important nuance: interdiction applies to legal statements (contracts) but not to physical actions, meaning that even an interdicted person must compensate for damages they cause.

Guided Path

The passage clearly enumerates three causes: minority (saghir), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun). Write them down and note the rationale for each: lack of full legal capacity.

The passage answers a key question: interdiction applies to 'aqwal' (statements/contracts) but not to 'af'al' (physical actions). For a minor or insane person, transactions are invalid or suspended, but if they cause property damage, they are liable for compensation.
This distinction is crucial: a contract made by a minor is not simply void; it is suspended (mawquf) pending guardian approval.

The passage poses a scenario: a minor or insane person destroys property. The answer: compensation (daman) is obligatory from their wealth. This demonstrates the principle 'la hajra fi al-af'al' – interdiction does not cover actions causing harm.
This aligns with the broader Hanafi rule that liability for torts (itlaf) is independent of capacity for contracts.

Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on hajr (legal interdiction) from a Hanafi commentary on al-Quduri. It begins by defining hajr and listing its three causes: minority (sughr), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun). The interdictor restricts the legal capacity of the individual in certain transactions. For minors and slaves, transactions are not valid without guardian/master permission. Interpreting Words of Confession: Debt vs. Trust and Defensive Statements

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Whether a phrase confesses a debt or a trust depends on the exact wording; defensive statements implicitly admit the original debt.

The precise wording of a confession determines its legal effect. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: certain words like 'alayya' and 'qabli' automatically mean a debt, while 'indi' and 'ma'i' mean a trust. Additionally, common defensive responses such as 'I paid it' are treated as implied confessions of the debt itself, shifting the burden of proof. Identify the operative phrase

According to the Hanafi school: 'alayya' and 'qabli' are confessions of debt (dayn). 'indi' and 'ma'i' are confessions of trust (amanah). The practical outcome depends on this classification.

Connected Exceptions (Istithnā Muttasil) in Acknowledgment
Second, third, and fourth rulings on connected exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for valid and invalid connected exceptions in acknowledgments and calculate the remainder.

Core Idea
A connected exception is valid only if it leaves a remainder; otherwise it is void and the full acknowledgment stands.

When someone acknowledges a debt but immediately excludes a part, the law must determine whether the exception is valid. This lesson explains the Hanafi rule: as long as the exception leaves something behind, the acknowledgment is reduced; but if the exception swallows the whole, it is ignored.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledgment and the exception
The acknowledger states a debt and then excludes a specific amount or item in the same breath.
The exception must be connected (muttaṣil) in wording and meaning.
2
Check if the exception leaves a remainder
If the excepted amount is less than the total, the exception is valid and the remainder is binding. If the exception equals or exceeds the total, the exception is void and the full acknowledgment is binding.
If the exception is of a different type (e.g., dirhams except a dinar), the value of the excepted item is deducted.
3
Apply the corresponding ruling
For 'ten except one': valid, owes nine. For 'ten except ten' or 'ten except twelve': void, owes ten. For 'hundred dirhams except a dinar': deduct the value of a dinar.
The deductor takes the value at the time of acknowledgment.

Qualifications That Nullify or Are Ignored in Acknowledgment
Seventh, eighth, and ninth rulings on qualifications that nullify or are ignored

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify qualifications that render an acknowledgment void (inshā Allah) and those that are ignored (khiyār, exception of building).

Core Idea
Suspending an acknowledgment with 'if Allah wills' voids it; adding an option or an invalid exception is ignored.

Not every qualification attached to an acknowledgment is effective. Some qualifications, like 'if Allah wills,' completely nullify the declaration because they show indecision. Others, like an option to rescind or an exception of part of the property, are disregarded because acknowledgment is a binding act that does not allow such conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of qualification
Is it a suspension (inshā Allah), an option (khiyār), or an exception of an inseparable part (e.g., building from house)?
2
Apply the corresponding rule
If it is inshā Allah, the entire acknowledgment is void. If it is khiyār or an exception of a part that is physically part of the property, the qualification is invalid but the acknowledgment remains binding.
For inshā Allah, the speaker negates his intention; for khiyār, acknowledgment does not admit rescission; for building, the building is part of the house.
3
State the outcome
Inshā Allah → nothing due. Khiyār → acknowledgment binding, option void. Exception of building → both house and building are owed.

Interpreting Ambiguous Acknowledgments
Fifth and sixth rulings on ambiguous wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply rules for interpreting ambiguous acknowledgments such as 'one hundred and dirham' or 'one hundred and a garment.'

Core Idea
When an acknowledgment is ambiguous, the debt is set at the minimum that the words can bear, and the acknowledger explains the ambiguity.

Sometimes an acknowledgment uses unclear wording, like 'one hundred and dirham' (dropping the unit) or 'one hundred and a garment.' The law must determine exactly what is owed. This lesson covers the Hanafi rules for resolving such ambiguities.

Guided Path
1
Parse the ambiguous phrase
Identify what is omitted or unclear. In 'one hundred and dirham,' 'dirham' is joined to 'one hundred' without a unit for the hundred. In 'one hundred and a garment,' the hundred lacks a specification.
2
Apply the default interpretation
For 'one hundred and dirham,' it is interpreted as one hundred dirhams plus one dirham, totalling 101 dirhams. For 'one hundred and a garment,' the garment is due immediately, and the acknowledger is asked to explain what he meant by 'one hundred' (e.g., dirhams, dinars, etc.).
The default is to give effect to each part; ambiguity is resolved in favor of the minimum plausible amount.
3
Determine the binding amount
In the first case, 101 dirhams. In the second, one garment plus whatever the acknowledger specifies for the hundred (if he says dirhams, then 100 dirhams plus garment). If he does not explain, the court may decide.
The acknowledger's explanation is accepted unless the creditor proves otherwise.

Three rulings on valid and invalid exceptions in acknowledgment

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An exception in acknowledgment must be connected and cannot negate the whole; otherwise it fails.

This passage addresses three fundamental scenarios for making an exception (istithna) within an acknowledgment of debt (iqrar). Understanding these rulings is critical for correctly determining the amount owed when a debtor qualifies his admission. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines to distinguish valid from invalid exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is an exception (istithna) made during an acknowledgment. The ruling depends on whether the exception is connected and does not negate the entire principal.

Question: And if he said, 'The building of this house is mine and the land is for so-and-so,' is he believed in that?
Answer: Yes, he is believed.
Source pages p. 236

Commentary
This passage presents two related rulings regarding the acknowledgment of a house (dar) and its building (bina). In the first scenario, if a person acknowledges that a house belongs to someone else but then excepts the building from that acknowledgment, claiming the building for himself, the exception is invalid. He is required to hand over both the house and the building because when a house is acknowledged, it includes the building as an integral part; an exception of a component is not permissible once the whole is acknowledged. In the second scenario, if he declares, 'The building of this house is mine and the land (arsa) is for so-and-so,' he is believed. This is a separate statement of ownership, not an exception from an acknowledgment. Since he is claiming ownership of the building while also affirming that the land belongs to another, his statement is accepted as a self-disclosure (iqrar against himself regarding the land, and a claim regarding the building). The ruling distinguishes between a retrospective exception (which is void) and an original declaration of divided ownership (which is accepted).

Inclusion of Containers in Hanafi Iqrar
Four rulings on inclusion of containers in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a container or surrounding property is included in an iqrar (acknowledgment) based on the Hanafi interpretation of the particle 'fi' and custom.

Core Idea
In Hanafi iqrar, 'fi' includes the container only if custom treats them as a unit.

When someone acknowledges property 'in' something—like dates in a basket or a cloth in a handkerchief—how much is owed? This lesson examines four scenarios from At-Tashil ad-Daruri, where the answer depends on whether the container is considered part of the item or merely a location. We will see that the meaning of 'fi' is flexible, interpreted by context and custom, and even the great Hanafi imams disagreed on one case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledged item and context phrase
Read the iqrar statement and note the item (e.g., dates, animal, cloth) and the word 'fi' followed by a container or location.
2
Classify the context as container or location
Determine whether the context is a container typically associated with the item (e.g., basket for dates) or a mere location (e.g., stable for animal). Custom is the key.
3
Apply the inclusion rule
If the context is a container customarily part of the item, both are owed. If it is a location, only the item is owed.
The third scenario shows that 'in a handkerchief' includes the handkerchief because it is a container.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
For 'a cloth among ten cloths', Abu Yusuf says one cloth, Muhammad says eleven. Note their differing interpretation of 'fi' in this context.
The dispute centers on whether 'fi' means 'part of' or 'in addition to'.
5

While the text does not give Tarjih, later Hanafi works often adopt Abu Yusuf's view as more consistent with literal interpretation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson presents two rulings from Hanafi law on acknowledgments (iqrar). When someone acknowledges a debt or wrongful possession but later claims the specific goods are defective or the currency is counterfeit, his statement is legally considered. However, because the claim benefits him, an oath is required to confirm his honesty. These rulings protect the debtor from being forced to pay more than he owes while safeguarding the creditor's rights.
