Iqala: Definition, Ruling, and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Its Nature
Definition, permissibility, and intra-Hanafi dispute on nature of iqala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and ruling of iqala in Hanafi fiqh, distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether iqala is faskh or a new sale, and apply the practical consequence regarding the right of pre-emption (shuf'a).

Core Idea
Iqala is a permissible rescission with mutual consent; its nature as faskh or new sale differs for parties versus third parties.

Iqala (rescission of a sale) is a common transaction in Islamic law. While the basic ruling is clear—permissible with mutual consent for the original price—a significant intra-Hanafi dispute exists about its legal nature: is it a mere cancellation (faskh) or a new sale? The answer affects third-party rights, especially pre-emption. This lesson will unpack the definition, the ruling, and the three positions of the early Hanafi masters.

Guided Path
1
Define Iqala and its Essential Condition
Start by understanding the linguistic and legal definition of iqala: it is the lifting of a completed contract by mutual consent. The essential condition for its validity is that it must be for the same price as the original contract (mithl al-thaman al-awwal).
Note the term 'lafẓ' (word) iqala—the dispute only applies when the word 'iqala' is used; other words like faskh or radd change the ruling.
2
State the Basic Ruling
Iqala is permissible (ja'iz) with mutual consent. If a different price is stipulated, the stipulation is invalid but the iqala itself remains valid with the original price.
The condition of mutual consent (tarāḍī) is paramount; coercion invalidates the iqala.
3
Identify the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
The central issue: Is iqala a rescission (faskh) or a new sale (bay' jadīd)? There are three positions: Abu Hanifa splits the nature—faskh for the parties, new sale for third parties; Abu Yusuf views it as a sale generally with exceptions; Muhammad views it as faskh generally with exceptions.
The exceptions involve the timing of the iqala (before/after delivery) and any change in price.
4
Trace the Practical Consequence: Pre-emption (Shuf'a)
The practical difference is seen when the sold item is real estate. If the pre-emptor (shafī') waived his right during the original sale, but then the parties perform iqala, the pre-emptor can demand pre-emption again—because for him, iqala is a new sale.
This applies under Abu Hanifa's view; the Sahibayn's views would lead to different outcomes in certain scenarios.
5
Understand the Linguistic Condition
The dispute only applies when the word 'iqala' is used. If the contract is rescinded using words like 'faskh', 'radd', or 'tark', it is automatically considered a rescission (faskh) and not a sale.
If the word 'sale' (bay') is used, all agree it is a new sale by consensus.

Iqala: Perishing of Price or Goods and Partial Perishing
Rulings on iqala when price or goods perish, or partially perish

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which iqala is valid when the price or goods perish, and identify the recommended act regarding iqala.

Core Idea
Price loss doesn't hinder iqala; goods loss does; partial loss allows cancellation of the remainder.

This lesson builds on the basic permissibility of iqala by examining how the perishing of the price or the sold goods affects its validity. We also learn the ethical encouragement to grant iqala when asked, supported by a prophetic promise of forgiveness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios of complete perishing
Note that the passage treats perishing of the price (thaman) and perishing of the sold item (mabi‘) separately. Determine the ruling for each.
2
Understand the underlying logic
Reflect on why perishing of the price does not prevent iqala (because an equivalent can be returned) while perishing of the good does (returning the specific item is impossible).
This distinction is based on the nature of iqala as rescission, not a new sale.
3
Apply the partial perishing rule
When only part of the sold item perishes, iqala is permitted for the remaining part. The parties can agree to cancel for the undamaged portion.
This shows flexibility in iqala; the damaged part is treated separately.
4
Recognize the ethical encouragement
The hadith indicates that granting iqala to a regretful buyer is mustahabb, carrying great reward. This encourages leniency in transactions.
The reward is forgiveness of sins, not just worldly benefit.

Murabaha and Tawliya: Definitions, Condition, and Cost Disclosure
Definitions of murabaha and tawliya, condition, and cost inclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define murabaha and tawliya, identify the condition of mithli goods, and apply the cost-inclusion rule in murabaha transactions.

Core Idea
Murabaha and tawliya require a known original cost; only mithli goods ensure exact cost certainty.

This lesson introduces two specific types of sale: murabaha (cost-plus-profit) and tawliya (sale at cost). The Hanafi school imposes a key condition: the goods must be mithli (fungible) so the original cost is precisely known. Additionally, when the seller adds expenses that increase value, he must disclose them transparently.

Guided Path
1
Define the two sale types
First, understand the difference between murabaha (selling at cost plus profit) and tawliya (selling at exact cost). Both involve reselling the same good at the original price basis.
2
Identify the condition of validity
The text states that murabaha and tawliya are not valid unless the countervalue (al-'iwad) is of a type that has a standard measure (mithl), such as measured or weighed items. Recognize that this condition ensures the original cost is objectively known.
3
Understand the rationale
Why must the goods be mithli? Because the sale relies on the buyer knowing the exact original cost. For non-mithli (unique) goods, the cost cannot be verified with certainty, leading to potential gharar (uncertainty).
4
Apply the cost-inclusion rule
When the seller adds improvements (washing, dyeing, transport, etc.) that increase value, he must phrase his disclosure as 'it cost me' rather than 'I bought it for' to transparently include all costs. This avoids misrepresentation.

This passage from the Hanafi fiqh tradition addresses a practical concern in transactions: when you buy something by measure or weight, you must re-measure it yourself before using or reselling. Why? Core Idea
Ownership of the price transfers upon contract, so the seller may dispose of it before physical receipt.

A common question in sales: once a sale is concluded, can the seller use the money owed before actually receiving it? In Hanafi fiqh, the answer is clear and straightforward. This short ruling establishes a key principle about the relationship between ownership and possession.

Guided Path

The act in question is the seller's disposition (tasarruf) of the price before physically receiving it (qabd).
Disposition includes using, gifting, or transferring the debt.
2

The Hanafi ruling is that it is permissible (jāʾiz). This is directly from the text.
3

Ownership of the price transfers to the seller at the moment of the contract, even without possession. The price becomes a debt owed by the buyer.
This differs from specific goods where possession may be required.

The text does not mention exceptions, but Hanafi fiqh clarifies this.

Since the seller is the owner of the debt, any disposition is valid, provided no other impediment exists (e.g., interest).
This principle underlies many modern financial transactions.

Voluntary Increase After Sale: A Permissible Gift
Ruling on voluntary post-sale increase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on voluntarily increasing price or goods after a sale is complete and identify the condition that distinguishes it from a prohibited increase.

Core Idea
Voluntary post-sale increase is a gift, not a sale term — always permissible.

This lesson presents a concise Hanafi ruling: after a sale is finalized, either party may voluntarily add to the price or goods. At first glance, this might seem to contradict the principle that a sale is binding once concluded. However, the key is that the increase is not part of the contract but a separate charitable act (tabarru'). Understanding this distinction prevents confusion with prohibited riba or contractual modifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of increasing the price (by the buyer) or increasing the goods (by the seller) after the sale is complete. The ruling is 'ja'iz' (permissible).
2
Note the critical timing
The increase must occur 'after the completion of the sale' (ba'da tamam al-bay'). If it were stipulated during the contract, it would be invalid.
Timing is key: post-contract vs. in-contract.
3
Recognize the voluntary nature
The increase is not obligatory; it is a voluntary gift (tabarru'). The word 'yajuz' indicates permissibility, not obligation.
Voluntariness distinguishes it from a contractual condition.
4
Distinguish from riba
Some might think any post-sale increase is riba. However, riba involves an excess in a contractual exchange. Here, the increase is a separate gift, not part of the consideration.
5
Apply the ruling in practice
If a buyer voluntarily pays extra out of gratitude, it is valid. Similarly, a seller may add extra goods. The increase is considered a new gift (hiba).

Measuring Goods Before Sale: Hanafi Position and Proof
Proof and discussion on measurement before sale

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Selling food before measuring is makruh tahrimi, not haram, because the hadith is ahad and zanni.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the matn's ruling about deferring a debt. The core principle is that any immediate debt can be postponed to a future date if the debtor agrees, but loans (qard) are an exception. Defines two types of riba and cites multiple hadiths as proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define riba al-qard and riba al-bay' and identify the key hadith evidence that establishes their prohibition.

Core Idea
Riba is prohibited in two forms: stipulating excess in loans and exchanging like items with excess or on credit.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's matn provides concise definitions of two fundamental categories of riba: riba al-qard (loan riba) and riba al-bay' (sale riba). It then marshals multiple hadiths to establish the severe prohibition of riba, including the famous hadith of the six commodities. Understanding these definitions and their proofs is essential for recognising prohibited transactions in Islamic finance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Read the definitions of riba al-qard and riba al-bay' as given in the passage. Note the precise conditions: for riba al-qard, a stipulation of excess in repayment; for riba al-bay', selling a mukeel or mawzoon item by its own genus with excess, or by its own or another genus on credit.

Derivation of the ’Illa of Riba from the Six Commodities
Extraction of the legal cause of riba from the six-commodity hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ’illa of riba and apply it to determine the permissibility of an exchange involving measured or weighed items.

Core Idea
Riba’s ’illa is kayl or wazn combined with genus, extending the prohibition beyond the six commodities.

The hadith of the six commodities establishes rulings on riba, but why these six? Abu Hanifa identified the common trait: all are either measured (mukeel) or weighed (mawzoon). This lesson unpacks his deduction, showing how the ’illa generalizes the prohibition to all similar items and distinguishes between excess (fadl) and credit (nasi’ah).

Guided Path
1
Summarize the hadith ruling
Recall the Prophet’s command on six commodities: within the same genus, exchange must be equal and hand-to-hand; between different genera, excess allowed but still hand-to-hand.
Focus on the contrast between same genus and different genus rulings.
2
Identify the common attribute
All six commodities are either measured by volume or by weight. Abu Hanifa treats this as the shared effective cause (‘illa) that determines riba.
Gold and silver are weighed; wheat, barley, dates, salt are measured.
3
Derive the ’illa
The ’illa is kayl (volumetric measure) or wazn (weight) together with genus (jins). Thus, any mukeel or mawzoon item of the same genus triggers the same forbiddances.
This extends riba to all mukeel/mawzoon items, not just the six.
4
Determine the consequences
When both attributes present: excess and credit prohibited. When only genus present: credit prohibited but excess allowed. When both absent: both allowed.
Examples: cloth (neither mukeel nor mawzoon) allows both; slaves (same genus but not mukeel/mawzoon) allow excess but not credit.
5
Contrast with other schools
The Shafi’i school holds the ’illa to be currency (gold/silver) or food. Hanafi critiques: the hadith includes salt, which is not a currency, and excludes non-food measured items like oil.
This illustrates the importance of textual basis for ’illa extraction.

Ruling on Riba for the Six Items (Hadith of Ubadah)
States the Prophetic hadith on riba and its basic ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid exchange of ribawi items and apply the hadith of Ubadah to determine when riba occurs in same-genus and different-genus transactions.

Core Idea
Exchanging gold for gold? Must be equal and immediate – any excess is riba.

This lesson introduces the foundational hadith on riba al-fadl. The Prophet ﷺ specified six commodities and set precise conditions for their exchange. Understanding this hadith is crucial for identifying prohibited transactions in sales and recognizing the boundaries of permissible trade.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six items and the ruling
List the six commodities mentioned in the hadith. Recognize that the ruling applies specifically to these items and any others that share the same 'illa (effective cause) of being weighable or measurable food or precious metals.
The six are gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, and salt.
2
Understand the conditions for same-genus exchange
When exchanging an item for its own genus (e.g., gold for gold), three conditions are required: (1) equality in quantity (like for like), (2) equality in quality (equal for equal, meaning no premium for quality differences), and (3) spot delivery (hand to hand).
The phrase 'sawa'an bi sawa'' emphasizes strict equality.
3
Recognize the prohibition of excess and delay
The hadith explicitly states that any increase in same-genus exchange is riba. Additionally, delay in delivery (even if equal) is also riba (riba al-nasi'ah). Both giver and taker are equally sinful.
This applies to both parties – the one who gives more and the one who takes more.
4
Apply the rule to different-genus exchange
When the genera differ (e.g., gold for silver), the condition of equality is waived, but spot delivery remains mandatory. Excess is permissible as long as the transaction is immediate.
This is the basis for permissibility of selling gold for silver with a premium if done hand-to-hand.
5
Trace the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
The imperative 'sell not' (understood from context) combined with the explicit statement 'what increases is riba' provides clear evidence for prohibition. The condition 'yadan bi yad' is derived from the requirement of immediate delivery.
The dalala is qat'i (definitive) even though the hadith itself is ahad.

Hanafi Derivation of the Illa for Riba
Deduction of the legal cause for riba from the six ribawi items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal cause for riba and apply it to determine the permissibility of a sale with excess or delay.

Core Idea
Riba is prohibited when both genus and weight/measure coincide; if only one, only delay is prohibited.

Understanding the legal cause (illa) of riba is essential for identifying which transactions fall under its prohibition. The Hanafi school, through Abu Hanifah's istinbat from the six items mentioned in the hadith, derives a specific combination of characteristics. This lesson unpacks that derivation and its practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six items and their common attributes
List the six ribawi items (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt). Note that each is either measured by volume (mikyal) or weighed (mawzun). This is the starting point for deducing the illa.
The hadith enumerates these items to illustrate the illa, not to limit the ruling.
2
Understand the two components of the illa: genus (jins) and measure/weight (kayl/wazn)
Abu Hanifah deduced that the effective cause is the combination of two attributes: belonging to a common genus and being measured or weighed. Both must be present for the full prohibition.
Genus refers to the category (e.g., wheat is a genus, gold is a genus). Measure/weight refers to the method of trade.
3
Apply the rule to four scenarios
Work through the four combinations: (1) both genus and measure/weight present → excess and delay prohibited; (2) only measure/weight present (different genus) → excess allowed, delay prohibited; (3) only genus present → excess allowed, delay prohibited; (4) neither present → both allowed.
The examples in the text (cloth for cloth, wheat for barley) illustrate scenarios (3) and (2) respectively.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Contrast with the Shafi'i position, which holds that the illa for riba in gold/silver is being a medium of exchange, and for food items is being food. The Hanafi position extends prohibition to all measured/weighed items.
The difference leads to practical variations, e.g., on transactions involving non-food measured items like iron.
