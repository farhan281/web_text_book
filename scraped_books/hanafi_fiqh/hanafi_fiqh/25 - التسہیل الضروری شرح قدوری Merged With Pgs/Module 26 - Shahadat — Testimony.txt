Witness Requirements for Proving Fosterage
Conditions for establishing fosterage through testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the evidentiary requirements for establishing fosterage (rada') and identify why women alone are insufficient according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Fosterage (rada') requires two men or one man and two women; women alone cannot prove it.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain family matters require strong proof. Fosterage (rada') creates legal impediments to marriage, so its establishment demands a specific number of witnesses. This lesson explains the minimum testimony needed and the rationale behind excluding women's sole testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being established
The act is fosterage (rada'), which creates a legal relationship preventing marriage between the nursing mother and the child, and among the children.
Rada' is a separate legal relationship from blood kinship, but with similar marriage prohibitions.
2
State the two valid witness combinations
The text gives two options: two men OR one man and two women. These are the only acceptable combinations.
Note that the man's testimony is always required; women alone are not accepted.
3
Identify the rejected combination
Women alone (without a man) are explicitly rejected. This includes two women, three women, or any number of women without a male witness.
This ruling aligns with the general Hanafi principle in marriage and other non-property matters.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The Hanafi school treats the testimony requirement for rada' the same as for marriage (nikah). In both, women's sole testimony is not accepted because these matters involve a permanent prohibition (hurmat) and require stronger evidence.
In property matters, two women equal one man; here, women alone are insufficient.

Witnesses for Birth of Child in Iddah: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on witnesses for birth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the number of witnesses required to prove birth for lineage and identify the mufta bihi position with its exceptions.

Core Idea
Lineage requires two male witnesses or one male and two females, unless pregnancy is visible or husband acknowledges.

This lesson examines a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the evidentiary requirements for establishing lineage when a child is born to a woman in her iddah period. The issue hinges on whether the general rule for witness testimony applies or an exception is made for birthing events. We will explore the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn and determine the operative ruling for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Identify the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) as stated in the passage. Abu Hanifa requires two men or one man and two women; the Sahibayn require only one woman.
Note that both are within the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the exceptions
Abu Hanifa makes an exception for visible pregnancy (hubl zahir) or prior acknowledgment by the husband. Under these conditions, no witnesses are needed. The Sahibayn accept one woman without exceptions.
3
Analyze the evidentiary principle
Abu Hanifa's view aligns with the general Quranic rule for testimony (two witnesses or one man two women). The Sahibayn consider birthing as a women's affair where one woman is sufficient.
This is the core of the ikhtilaf.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, as it is stronger in analogy with general evidentiary standards. Later authorities like al-Marghinani prefer this view.
Thus, for fatwa, the rule is two witnesses unless an exception applies.

Theft is proven by a single confession or two male witnesses — no repetition needed.

The answer is concise: either the thief's own confession made once, or the testimony of two male witnesses. Understanding this is essential for correctly applying hudud.

The act itself (theft) is haram, but this lesson is about the evidentiary process.
2
Examine the first method: iqrar
The thief's confession (iqrar) is sufficient if made once. Note that no repetition is required.
Hanafi school: one clear confession is enough for hadd.

Examine the second method: shahadat
Testimony of two witnesses (shahidan). The text does not specify gender, but Hanafi fiqh requires male witnesses for hudud.
The witnesses must be ‘adl (upright) and free. Female witnesses are not accepted for hadd.
4
Compare with other schools
Some schools require multiple confessions or four witnesses for certain hudud, but Hanafi holds to this simple rule.
This is a point of ikhtilaf.

Ruling on Testimony and Its Obligation
Establishing the obligation of testimony and the exception for hudud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the obligation of testimony and identify when concealment is preferred over disclosure.

Core Idea
Testimony is fard kifayah in rights; for hudud, concealment is better.

This lesson opens the Book of Testimonies (Kitab al-Shahadat) with a clear question-answer format. The passage establishes that giving testimony when called is a communal obligation rooted in Quranic injunctions. However, it immediately introduces a critical distinction: for hudud (fixed penalties), the default recommendation is to conceal, except when testifying about theft where careful wording avoids invoking the hadd. Understanding this nuance is essential for applying the Hanafi approach to testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm
Locate the phrase 'أداء الشهادة فرض على الشهود'. Determine what hukm level 'فرض' indicates. In Hanafi fiqh, 'فرض' in this context means fard kifayah (communal obligation), not fard 'ayn.
Note: The text says 'فرض على الشهود' – an obligation upon witnesses, but it does not mean every individual must testify; if some fulfill it, others are absolved.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the Quranic verses cited: 2:282 and 2:283. Note that both are imperative forms: 'لا يَأْبَ' (let them not refuse) and 'لا تَكْتُمُوا' (do not conceal).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Apply the Hanafi usul principle: al-amr li al-wujub (the imperative indicates obligation). The verses command action (not refusing) and prohibition of concealment, both of which indicate wujub (obligation).
This is a zanni dalala; the verses are qat'i thubut but zanni in meaning, hence the obligation is fard kifayah, not fard 'ayn.
4
Note the exception for hudud
Read: 'وأما الحدود فيخير فيها الشاهد بين الستر والإظهار والستر أفضل'. This shows that for hudud, the witness has a choice, and concealment is better. Understand the rationale: covering faults is encouraged.
5
Observe the thamarah (practical consequence) in theft
The passage says: 'إلا أنه يجب أن يشهد بالمال في السرقة فيقول: أخذ المال، ولا يقول: سرق'. This is a practical application: one must testify that the person took property, but avoid saying 'he stole' to prevent triggering the hadd penalty unnecessarily.
This shows the Hanafi tendency to avoid hudud when possible.

The Four Categories of Testimony in Hanafi Law
Enumeration of testimony categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of testimony in Hanafi law and identify the required number and gender of witnesses for a given case.

Core Idea
Testimony requirements vary by case: four men for zina, two men for hudud/qisas, two men or one man and two women for other rights, one woman for private female matters.

Testimony is a cornerstone of Islamic legal procedure. The Hanafi school categorizes cases into four distinct tiers, each with specific requirements for the number and gender of witnesses. This taxonomy balances the need for certainty in serious crimes with practicality in everyday transactions and private matters. Mastering these categories is essential for understanding the admissibility of testimonial evidence in a Hanafi court.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the four categories
Learn the basic taxonomy from the text: (1) zina: four men, (2) qisas and other hudud: two men, (3) other rights (property and non-property): two men or one man and two women, (4) private female matters (birth, virginity, defects): one woman.
Use the mnemonic 'Zina 4, Hudud 2, Rights 1+2, Female private 1'.
2
Identify the legal category for each type of case
Given a case, determine whether it falls under hudud (e.g., zina, qadhf, theft), qisas (murder, bodily harm), other rights (contracts, marriage, divorce, agency, will), or private female matters that men cannot see.
Qisas and hudud are grouped together in the second category, even though they are distinct punishments.
3
Apply the rules to determine admissible witnesses
For a specific case, state the required number and gender of witnesses. For example, a debt case requires two men or one man and two women; a theft case requires two men; a birth case requires one woman.
If the conditions are not met, the testimony is not accepted.
4
Reflect on the rationale behind the gradation
Hanafi jurists justify stricter requirements for hudud and qisas due to the severity of punishments and the need for certainty (qat'). Women's testimony is accepted in other matters but with a 2:1 ratio, and alone in private matters where they are the only possible witnesses.
This reflects a balance between evidentiary rigor and practical necessity.

Conditions for Accepting Testimony
States the conditions for accepting testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for accepting testimony in Hanafi fiqh and explain why a witness saying 'I know' (a'lam) is insufficient.

Core Idea
Testimony requires both the witness's justice and the explicit wording 'ashhadu'.

This lesson examines the foundational conditions for testimony in Islamic court. The Hanafi school holds that testimony is a specific legal act with strict requirements: the witness must be upright (adil) and must explicitly declare 'I bear witness'. Saying 'I know' or 'I am certain' does not fulfill the requirement, because testimony is a declaration of bearing witness, not a mere statement of knowledge. Understanding these conditions is crucial for anyone involved in legal proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
Read the passage and extract the two conditions mentioned for accepting testimony: justice (adala) and the wording of testimony (lafz al-shahada).
2
Analyze the wording condition
The passage explicitly says that if the witness says 'a'lam' (I know) or 'atiqan' (I am certain) without saying 'ashhadu', the testimony is not accepted. Reflect on why: testimony is a specific legal declaration, not a report of knowledge.
3
Link to Hanafi usul
In Hanafi usul, the wording of testimony is a condition (shart) for validity. The imperative in the Quran and Sunnah for testimony uses the word 'shahada', implying the need for that specific articulation.
4
Note cross-madhhab perspective
While the Hanafi school insists on the wording 'ashhadu', other schools (e.g., Maliki) may accept synonyms. This difference stems from how strictly they interpret the requirement of lafz.

The Investigation Dispute: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on investigating witnesses' justice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Abu Hanifa's position from the Sahibayn's on investigating witnesses' justice and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa presumes apparent justice except in hudud/qisas; the Sahibayn demand investigation for all witnesses.

Whose Testimony Is Accepted in Hanafi Law
Lists rulings on witness acceptance and rejection with glosses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of witnesses whose testimony is accepted and rejected according to the Hanafi school, and identify the specific conditions that affect admissibility.

Core Idea
Testimony is rejected for worldly enmity and undignified conduct; accepted from dhimmis against each other and heretics not reaching kufr.

Witness credibility is crucial in Islamic court. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules on who may testify, balancing justice with social realities. This passage lists those categories, with glosses clarifying nuances like the difference between worldly and religious enmity and the treatment of heretical sects.

Guided Path
1
Identify rejected witnesses

Understand the enmity condition
Worldly enmity disqualifies because it tempts false testimony; religious enmity (e.g., for a Muslim against a dhimmi) is acceptable as it shows piety.
The gloss explains the rationale: worldly enmity is forbidden, so the witness is untrustworthy.
4
Understand the heresy condition
Testimony of ahl al-ahwa is accepted only if their heresy does not amount to kufr. The extreme Khattabiyyah sect is excluded because they believe in divinity of Ali and Jafar, which is kufr.
This condition is from al-Dhakhirah and Sharh al-Zayla'i.
5
Understand territorial rule for musta'mins
Testimony among musta'mins is accepted only if they are from the same territory (e.g., both Romans). If from different territories (Roman and Turk), it is not accepted.

Agreement of Witnesses: Wording and Meaning
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness agreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on witness agreement and apply it to scenarios of differing testimonies to determine acceptance or rejection.

Core Idea
Witnesses must agree in meaning; Abu Hanifa adds wording, Sahibayn accept lesser amount.

Testimony is a cornerstone of Islamic judiciary. This lesson explores a critical intra-Hanafi dispute: must witnesses agree in both wording and meaning, or is agreement in meaning sufficient? Through practical scenarios, you will learn how to apply each position and understand the practical consequences in court.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
Start with the first benefit: testimony must agree with the claim (al-muddai bihi). If it contradicts, it is rejected. Then note that Abu Hanifa requires agreement in both wording and meaning between witnesses.
This is the default principle before considering exceptions.
2
Analyze the first scenario
One witness says 1000, other says 2000. Determine the ruling according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. Abu Hanifa rejects both; Sahibayn accept the lesser (1000).
This is the core dispute.
3
Apply to similar scenarios
Work through the other three Q&A cases: (1) 1000 vs 1500 when plaintiff claims 1500 → accepted for 1000. (2) Both say 1000 but one adds partial payment → judge ignores payment claim. (3) Contradictory places (Mecca vs Kufa) → Sahibayn accept if meaning agreed (death on Yawm al-Nahr).
Each case clarifies the scope of agreement.
4
Summarize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, many testimonies are rejected; under the Sahibayn, the lesser common amount is accepted, allowing for more judgments.
Later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn.

Rulings on Subsidiary Testimony (Shahadat al-Far')
Explains acceptance conditions and procedure for testimony on testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for accepting subsidiary testimony, explain the proper procedure for establishing and performing it, and identify when it is rejected.

Core Idea
Subsidiary testimony is a secondary proof, accepted only when the original witness is unavailable, and never in hudud or qisas.

This lesson presents the Hanafi rules on subsidiary testimony (shahadat al-far'). This is a crucial procedural tool that allows cases to proceed when the original witnesses cannot personally attend court. Understanding its conditions, proper wording, and limitations is essential for ensuring that justice is served without compromising evidentiary standards.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope
Note that subsidiary testimony is not accepted in hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) cases, but it is permissible for financial claims and rights of people. The testimony of two subsidiary witnesses on two original witnesses is accepted, but not one on one.
The prohibition in hudud and qisas is based on the need for highest certainty.
2
State the conditions for acceptance
Subsidiary testimony is only accepted when the original witnesses are unable to testify personally: they are dead, absent on a journey of three days or more, or ill to the extent that they cannot attend the judge's court.
These conditions ensure subsidiary testimony is a last resort.
3
Learn the procedure for appointment (ishhad)
The original witness must say to the subsidiary witness: 'Bear witness upon my testimony that I testify that so-and-so made an acknowledgment to me of such-and-such, and he made me a witness upon himself.' Omitting 'he made me a witness upon himself' is still permissible.
The wording explicitly delegates the testimony.
4
Learn the procedure for performance (ada')
The subsidiary witness at court says: 'I testify that so-and-so made an acknowledgment to him of such-and-such, and he said to me: bear witness upon my testimony of such-and-such, so I testify to that.' This clearly indicates he is relaying the original testimony.
The subsidiary witness must not state direct knowledge.
5
Understand special scenarios
If the subsidiary witnesses verify the integrity of the original witnesses (ta'dil), it is permissible; if they remain silent, the judge investigates. If the original witnesses deny having appointed the subsidiary witnesses, the subsidiary testimony is not accepted.
Denial voids the foundation of the testimony.

Liability of Witnesses Who Retract After Judgment
Explains liability and legal effects when witnesses retract before or after judgment

However, if they retract after judgment, the judgment stands firmly, and the retracting witnesses become liable to compensate the defendant for the loss caused by their testimony. This ensures that the finality of judgments is preserved while holding witnesses accountable. The Judge Does Not Hear Testimony to Impugn Witnesses
Procedural rule on impugning witnesses.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The judge does not hear formal jarh evidence; he investigates witnesses privately.

In Hanafi court procedure, a defendant may attempt to discredit the plaintiff's witnesses by alleging they are sinful or biased. This passage clarifies that the judge does not allow such formal impeachment but instead conducts a confidential inquiry. This prevents frivolous attacks and maintains judicial efficiency.

Guided Path

2
Locate the alternative procedure
Instead of hearing the defendant's evidence, the judge asks about the plaintiff's witnesses in secret (sirr). This is a private inquiry into their credibility.
The secret inquiry is a procedural mechanism unique to Hanafi law.
3
Understand the rationale
Formal jarh proceedings could lead to delays and misuse. The judge's confidential investigation protects the reputation of witnesses and ensures the focus remains on the substantive case.
Other schools allow formal jarh evidence, but Hanafi prioritizes judicial discretion.

This passage from the chapter on retraction of testimony sets out a clear rule: liability for witnesses depends on the relationship between the price in their testimony and the actual market value. It's a simple but important principle that balances fairness with the need for reliable testimony.

Testimony on Negation Coupled with Affirmation
Conditions for accepting negative testimony with affirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which testimony on a negative fact is accepted in Hanafi law and apply the rule to the two case examples (inheritance and conditional freedom).

Core Idea
Testimony on a pure negative is not accepted, but if the negative is inseparably linked to a positive assertion, it becomes admissible.

In Hanafi qada (judicial procedure), testifying about a negative fact (nafi) is generally problematic because proving a negative is nearly impossible. However, the jurists recognized an exception: when the negative is necessarily tied to a positive claim (ithbat), the testimony is accepted. This lesson examines that exception through concrete cases from the later Hanafi work al-Jawhara.

Guided Path
1

First, note that the default ruling is that testimony on a pure negative—e.g., 'he did not do X'—is not accepted because it cannot be definitively proven.
2

The passage states that if the nafi is 'maqrunan bi-l-ithbat' (coupled with affirmation) and the case falls under judicial authority, the testimony is accepted. Recognize that the exception removes the general prohibition.
3
Analyze the inheritance example
Consider the testimony: 'This is the heir of so-and-so, and we know of no other heir.' The positive part ('this is the heir') automatically implies the negative ('no other heir'). Since the negative is tied to a positive, the testimony is accepted and all property is transferred.
4
Analyze the conditional freedom example
A master says, 'If you do not enter the house today, you are free.' Two witnesses testify that the slave did not enter. Here, the negative (not entering) is the condition for freedom. Because the condition is part of a valid contract (ta'liq), the testimony on the negative is accepted and the slave is freed.
5
Understand the authority and ikhtilaf
The source is al-Jawhara, a later Hanafi text (fatawa mutakhkhirin). The ruling is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school, but there is cross-madhhab ikhtilaf: some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) generally do not accept negative testimony even when coupled with affirmation.
