Definition and Conditions of Zakat Obligation in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines zakat linguistically and legally, states its obligation, and lists conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of zakat, identify its obligation level (fard), and list the conditions for its obligation.

Core Idea
Zakat is a farḍ ʿayn on every free, adult, sane Muslim who owns a nisab for a full lunar year.

This lesson introduces the Book of Zakat with its linguistic and legal definition, the ruling of obligation, and the precise conditions that trigger the duty. Understanding these foundations ensures that you correctly identify when zakat becomes obligatory and what distinguishes it from general charity.

Guided Path
1
Parse the linguistic and legal definition
Identify the two meanings: linguistically 'purification and growth'; legally a transfer of ownership (tamlik) of a specified portion of wealth to a poor Muslim for Allah's sake.
The legal definition emphasizes tamlik – mere spending without ownership transfer is not zakat.
2
Identify the ruling and its proof
The text states zakat is farḍ (obligatory) and a pillar of Islam. The proof is the Quranic verse 'Establish prayer and give zakat.' Denying its obligation is kufr.
The imperative 'ātū' indicates obligation per Hanafi usul.
3
List the conditions for obligation
Extract the seven conditions: free, Muslim, adult (baligh), sane (aqil), full ownership of nisab (mulk tamm), nisab amount owned, and completion of one lunar year (hawl).
All conditions must be present simultaneously; missing one removes the obligation.
4
Note the Hanafi emphasis on tamlik
Zakat requires transfer of ownership to the recipient; the giver must completely sever benefit from that portion.
This distinguishes zakat from other forms of charity (sadaqah).

Understanding the Hanafi Definition of Zakat
Definition of zakat linguistically and legally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of zakat and identify each of its constituent conditions.

Core Idea
Zakat is not charity but a specified obligatory transfer of ownership from nisab-wealth to the poor Muslim.

Zakat is one of the five pillars, yet many Muslims misunderstand its precise legal meaning. This lesson unpacks the classical Hanafi definition, highlighting the key elements that distinguish zakat from voluntary charity. Understanding these conditions is essential for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Differentiate linguistic from legal meaning
Recognize that the word zakah has both a general linguistic sense (taharah and nama') and a specific technical meaning in fiqh. The legal definition incorporates conditions that do not apply to the linguistic sense.
This distinction is common in fiqh: many terms have a shar’i meaning that differs from the literal.
2
Deconstruct the legal definition into its components
The definition: 'tamlik of owner of nisab a juz' mal specified by the Lawgiver from his wealth to a Muslim poor person for Allah, with qat' al-manfa'a.' Identify each element: the giver (owner of nisab), the action (tamlik), the amount (juz' mal specified), the source (from his wealth), the recipient (Muslim poor person), the intention (li-Llah), and the condition of complete severance.
Each component is a necessary condition; if any is missing, the payment is not zakat.
3
Explain the significance of 'tamlik'
Tamlik means transferring full ownership. The recipient must become the owner of the given amount. Merely allowing the recipient to use or benefit is insufficient. This distinguishes zakat from ibahah (permission) or i'arah (loan).
Hanafis strictly require tamlik, unlike some other schools that may allow consumption without ownership transfer.
4
Identify the required recipient
The recipient must be a Muslim who is poor (faqir). The definition explicitly says 'musliman faqiran'. This excludes non-Muslims and the wealthy. Also, the payment must be for the sake of Allah alone, not for any worldly return.
Giving zakat to a non-Muslim does not fulfill the obligation, but may be counted as sadaqah.
5
Understand the final condition: complete severance
The giver must completely cut off any benefit from the given wealth. For example, if the giver retains any right to use or reclaim the wealth, it is not zakat. This ensures the transfer is absolute and the wealth is truly relinquished.
This condition prevents disguised charity where the giver still benefits.

Commentary
This passage defines zakat in both linguistic and technical senses. Linguistically, zakat means taharah (purification) and nama' (growth). The term carries the idea that paying zakat purifies one's wealth and soul, and also causes the wealth to increase in blessing. Legally, the definition includes several key elements: (1) the owner must possess a nisab (minimum threshold of wealth), (2) he must transfer ownership (tamlik) of a specific portion (juz') of that wealth, (3) the portion is specified by the Lawgiver (i.e., the Shar'i), (4) the recipient must be a needy Muslim, (5) the payment must be solely for Allah, and (6) the giver must completely relinquish benefit from the amount given. This distinguishes zakat from mere charity or gifts.

النصاب (al-nisab): The minimum amount of wealth that triggers the obligation of zakat.
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership; the giver must make the recipient the owner of the zakat amount.

The Obligation of Zakat: Fard and Pillar
Declares zakat as fard and pillar with Quranic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of zakat in the Hanafi school and explain the Quranic proof for its obligation.

Core Idea
Zakat is fard (obligatory) by definitive proof; denying it is apostasy.

Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam. In this lesson, we examine its legal status as fard (obligation) and the Quranic evidence that establishes it. Understanding this foundational ruling is essential for every Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Zakat is ruled as fard (obligatory) and a pillar of Islam.
Fard is established by definitive dalil.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse: 'Aqimu al-salah wa atu al-zakah' (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:43).
This verse appears multiple times in the Quran.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'atu' indicates obligation (wujub) according to Hanafi usul.
Al-amr yufidu al-wujub unless otherwise specified.
4
Note the consequence of denial
Denying a fard proven by qat'i dalil constitutes kufr (apostasy).
This is because zakat is necessarily known as part of the religion.

Conditions for Zakat Obligation
Ruling statement of zakat conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five conditions for zakat obligation and identify which individuals are exempt.

Core Idea
Zakat is obligatory only on free, adult, sane Muslims who own a full nisab for one lunar year.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents the foundational conditions (shurut) for zakat to become obligatory. It is a classic ruling_statement that serves as a memorization anchor for the Hanafi position. Understanding these conditions is essential before applying any zakat calculations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is zakat, and it is fard on those meeting all conditions. Recognize the overall hukm level.
Zakat is fard 'ayn on every eligible individual.
2
List the personal conditions
From the text: freedom (hurr), Islam (muslim), adulthood (baligh), sanity (aqil). These relate to the person's status.
These are all shurut for obligation, not for validity of payment.
3
List the wealth conditions
Ownership of a complete nisab (minimum threshold) with full ownership (mulk tam) and passage of one lunar year (hawl).
The nisab is 200 dirhams of silver or 20 mithqals of gold.
4
Note the absence of gender condition
Gender is not mentioned; both males and females are equally obligated if they meet the conditions.
This is a common point of confusion.
5
Recognize implications for minors and the insane
Since adulthood and sanity are conditions, minors and the insane are not obligated to pay zakat even if they own nisab.
Shafi'is differ; they oblige the guardian to pay on their behalf.

The Complete Nisab: Definition and Four Categories
Defines the complete nisab in four categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of nisab and identify which one triggers zakat obligation.

Core Idea
Nisab is a threshold: own 200 dirhams of silver, 20 mithqals of gold, their value in currency, or trade goods worth either.

This passage from the Quduri matn gives the precise definition of a complete nisab. It lists four distinct types of wealth that, when owned, make zakat obligatory. The clarity of these numbers is foundational for all later rulings on zakat calculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question and answer structure
The text is a q&a: 'What is the complete nisab?' followed by an answer that lists four separate categories.
Each category is independent; owning one at its threshold triggers the obligation.
2
List the four categories of nisab
Mentally note: silver dirhams, gold mithqals, currency equal to either, and trade goods equal to either.
The first two are specific weights; the last two are value-based.
3
Understand independence of each category
If one owns 150 dirhams and 15 mithqals, neither alone reaches its respective nisab, so zakat is not due. They are not combined.
This is a common point of confusion.
4
Grasp the meaning of 'value' for currency and trade goods
Currency (nuqud ra'ijah) is measured by its purchasing power relative to silver or gold. Trade goods (mal al-tijarah) are valued at market price at year-end.
Modern paper money falls under this rule.

Source Text

السؤال النصاب الكامل ما هو ؟

Commentary
This passage specifies the nisab (minimum threshold) for zakat. There are three categories: (1) Silver: 200 dirhams (approx. 595 grams of silver). (2) Gold: 20 mithqals (approx. 85 grams of gold). (3) Any other currency (such as paper money or coins) whose value equals the silver or gold nisab. Additionally, trade goods ('urud al-tijarah) are measured by their market value at the end of the year; if the value reaches either nisab, zakat is due. This flexibility ensures that the obligation applies to modern economies as well.

مثقال (mithqal): A gold coin; nisab is 20 mithqals. Each mithqal approx 4.25g gold.
نقود رائجة (nuqud ra'ijah): Currency in circulation (paper money, coins) that is accepted as legal tender.
مال التجارة (mal al-tijarah): Goods purchased with the intention of resale for profit.

Applying the Nisab to Modern Currency and Trade Goods
Extends the nisab definition to modern contexts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the nisab rules to modern savings and business inventory.

Core Idea
Currency and trade goods are zakatable when their value reaches the silver or gold nisab threshold.

While the nisab of gold and silver is fixed by weight, modern wealth often consists of paper money or business stock. This lesson focuses on the third and fourth categories from the passage: circulating currency and trade goods. Understanding how to value them is key to fulfilling zakat correctly in contemporary settings.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of value equivalence
The text says 'value of either'—meaning if cash or trade goods are worth 200 silver dirhams or 20 gold mithqals, they constitute a nisab. You must know the current market price of silver and gold.
Most Hanafi muftis today use the silver nisab as the default because it is lower and thus more precautionary.
2
Calculate the silver nisab in local currency
Find the current price of 1 gram of silver, multiply by 595g (200 dirhams × 2.975g). If your cash exceeds that amount, you have a nisab in currency.
Check reliable sources; value changes daily.
3
Apply to trade goods
For business inventory, sum up all goods intended for resale at their current market value (not cost price). If the total reaches the nisab threshold, zakat is due on the entire value (not just profit).
This includes stock, raw materials, and finished goods.
4
Distinguish between cash and trade goods
Cash and trade goods are separate categories; if both are owned, each is evaluated independently. However, if only one type reaches nisab, zakat is due on that type only.
Combining cash and trade goods to reach nisab is not allowed; each must be assessed separately.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat for a person with debt and apply it to determine whether zakat is due.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri’s Matn covers the rulings of zakat on gold and silver. It begins with general questions: paying value (qiymah) in zakat is permissible; if someone gives all his wealth in charity without intending zakat, the obligation is dropped; if wealth perishes after zakat becomes obligatory, it is dropped entirely or proportionally, but if it is consumed (e.g., spent voluntarily), zakat remains. Then it fixes the nisab: silver: 200 dirhams (weight: each dirham is 14 qirats, 1 qirat = 5 barley grains, so 200 dirhams = 2800 qirats), paying 1/40 = 5 dirhams. Gold: 20 mithqals (each mithqal = 20 qirats), paying 1/40 = 1/2 mithqal. The key intra-Hanafi dispute concerns the calculation of zakat on excess above nisab. Actually the text says 2 qirats, which seems inconsistent; perhaps the author means 2 qirats per 4 mithqals? Clarification: On gold, zakat is 1/40. For 20 mithqals, that's 1/2 mithqal (=10 qirats). On excess, Abu Hanifa says no zakat until the excess reaches 4 mithqals, and then on that 4 mithqals, 1/10 of a mithqal? Actually the text: 'في الزائد في كل أربعة مثاقيل قيراطان' meaning on each 4 mithqals, 2 qirats. Since 1 mithqal = 20 qirats, 2 qirats of 4 mithqals (80 qirats) = 1/40, consistent. So the dispute: Abu Hanifa requires a complete new nisab increment (40 dirhams for silver, 4 mithqals for gold) before any zakat on excess; the Sahibayn say zakat is due on any excess proportionally (1/40 of the excess). The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn’s view due to ease and general practice. The passage also covers rulings on gold/silver jewelry and utensils: zakat is obligatory on all forms, regardless of use. For alloys (ghish): if silver predominates, the whole item is treated as silver; if alloy predominates, it is treated as trade goods (urud) — if intended for trade, value is considered; if not, no zakat unless the pure silver content reaches nisab. Modern weight equivalents are given with precaution: take the lowest to be safe. The text ends by citing al-Hindiyya for further detail.

ربع العشر (rub' al-ushr): One quarter of one tenth, i.e., 2.5% or 1/40, which is the zakat rate for gold and silver.
غش (ghish): Alloy or adulteration mixed with gold or silver.

Zakat on Gold and Silver: Nisab, Alloys, and Calculation
Rulings on zakat of gold and silver with intra-Hanafi difference and alloy rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on zakat for gold and silver, including the nisab amounts, the rate of zakat, the treatment of alloys and jewelry, and apply the proportional calculation method to determine zakat due on excess amounts.

Core Idea
Gold and silver in any form – coin, jewelry, or alloy – attract zakat; the dominant metal determines the ruling.

Zakat on gold and silver is wajib according to the Quran and Sunnah. But what exactly counts as zakatable gold and silver? Does jewelry count? What about alloys where the precious metal is mixed with other metals? This lesson unpacks the classical Hanafi rulings from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and later commentaries, giving you a clear framework to determine zakat on precious metals in any form, including modern weight equivalents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nisab and rate for pure gold and silver
From the text, note that the nisab for gold is 20 mithqals, with zakat of half a mithqal (1/40). For silver, the nisab is 200 dirhams, with zakat of 5 dirhams (1/40). These are the standard nisab amounts and rates from the zahir al-riwayah.
The rate is al-rub' al-ushr (quarter of a tenth), i.e., 2.5%.
2
Distinguish the intra-Hanafi disagreement on excess zakat
Understand the difference between Abu Hanifa's incremental calculation (zakat on each additional 4 mithqals at 2 qirats) and the Sahibayn's proportional calculation (zakat on any excess beyond nisab at the same rate). The mufta bihi is the proportional method.
This difference applies only to gold; for silver, the calculation is straightforward since the nisab and rates are given in dirhams.
3
Apply the ruling to jewelry and utensils
The text explicitly states that zakat is obligatory on gold and silver regardless of whether it is in the form of jewelry, utensils, or coins, and whether used or unused. This is the established Hanafi position.
The Shafi'i school differs, exempting mubah (permissible) jewelry, but the Hanafi school holds that all gold and silver are zakatable assets.
4
Analyze the ruling for alloyed metals
If a coin or object contains an alloy (ghishsh), determine which metal predominates. If the precious metal (gold or silver) predominates, it is treated as gold/silver for zakat. If the adulteration predominates, it is treated as trade goods (urud), subject to zakat only if intended for trade and its value reaches the nisab.
If the precious metal content itself can be separated and reaches the nisab, zakat may be due on that pure content alone.
5
Convert to modern weight and apply the precautionary approach
Classical weights: 1 mithqal = 100 barley grains, 1 dirham = 70 barley grains. Modern equivalents: gold nisab approx. 85g, silver nisab approx. 595g. Scholars differ on exact grams; the precaution is to use the smallest estimates to avoid missing zakat.
Thus, if you own 85g or more of gold (or 595g of silver) for one lunar year, zakat is wajib.

Mixing Gold and Silver for Zakat Nisab: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on mixing gold and silver for zakat nisab, and apply their methods to given examples.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa mixes by value; the Sahibayn mix by parts — the difference determines zakat liability.

Zakat on gold and silver individually is straightforward: nisab is 20 mithqals for gold and 200 dirhams for silver. But what if a person has less than nisab of each? May they combine them to reach nisab? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on this precise question. This lesson unpacks the views of Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, using a classic example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the condition under which zakat becomes wajib when a person owns both gold and silver, each below nisab individually.
The key point is whether the two can supplement each other to complete nisab.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Mix by value: add the value of the gold to the value of the silver. If the total value reaches the nisab of silver (200 dirhams equivalent), zakat is due.
Abu Hanifa considers value as the common measure of wealth.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Mix by parts (weight): each metal must contribute a fraction of its own nisab. The fractions together must sum to one complete nisab. For example, gold at 5/20 (1/4) and silver at 100/200 (1/2) sum to 3/4, not 1, so nisab is not reached.
The Sahibayn treat gold and silver as distinct categories.
4
Apply the example from the text
Take a person with 100 dirhams of silver and 5 mithqals of gold valued at 100 dirhams. Under Abu Hanifa: total value = 200 dirhams → nisab reached → zakat wajib. Under Sahibayn: silver is 1/2 nisab, gold is 1/4 nisab → total 3/4, not 1 → no zakat.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
5
Note the consensus case
If the person had 100 dirhams silver and 10 mithqals gold (value 100, but weight is 10 mithqals = 1/2 nisab of gold), then both methods agree: nisab is complete because by parts silver 1/2 + gold 1/2 = 1, and by value total is 200.
This shows that the difference only arises when the parts do not sum to a full nisab.

Benefit: It is not permissible in zakat of camels to give males like Ibn Makhad except by way of value for females, unlike cattle and sheep, for it is permissible in the required age to give males and females.
Source pages p. 118

Commentary
This faa'idah (benefit) clarifies a rule about what animals can be given as zakat. For camels, the zakat due is usually a female of a certain age (e.g., a two-year-old she-camel). It is not permissible to give a male camel of the same age (e.g., Ibn Makhad, which is a male camel under one year) unless the owner pays the value of the required female. However, for cattle and sheep/goats, both males and females of the required age can be given. This is because the zakat texts for camels specify females, while for cattle and sheep, the gender is not specified.

Key Terms
ابن المخاض (ibn al-makhad): A male camel under one year old; its mother is pregnant again (makhad).

Zakat on Nisab vs. Afw: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat calculation when surplus livestock perishes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad/Zufar on zakat liability when surplus livestock perishes, and apply the correct calculation to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Zakat is owed on the nisab only; loss of surplus does not reduce the obligation according to the relied-upon view.

This passage presents a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute about how zakat is affected when part of the livestock dies during the year. The key distinction is between the nisab (minimum threshold) and the 'afw (surplus that does not incur additional zakat until the next bracket). The disagreement yields different zakat calculations, illustrating a fundamental principle of Hanafi zakat law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
Read the answer in the passage: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say zakat remains on the nisab; Muhammad and Zufar say it reduces proportionally.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab one.
2
Understand Nisab and 'Afw
For camels, nisab is 5; the surplus (afw) from 6-9 does not increase zakat. The example uses 9 camels where 4 die, leaving 5 (the nisab).
'Afw is the excess that is 'pardoned' from additional zakat until the next bracket (10 camels).
3
Apply to the Camel Example
According to Abu Hanifa: zakat on the remaining 5 is a full sheep (same as if he had 5 all along). According to Muhammad: zakat is 5/9 of a sheep (proportional to the original 9).
Trace the reasoning: Muhammad divides the obligation across the entire herd.
4
Generalize the Principle
The dispute is about the 'attachment' of the obligation: attached to the nisab only (Abu Hanifa) or to the entire herd proportionally (Muhammad). The thamarah al-khilaf is the amount due.
This principle applies to all livestock where 'afw exists.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (Zahir al-Riwayah). The majority view is preferred.
This is the standard Hanafi fatwa position.

Commentary
This passage discusses a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute about how zakat is affected when part of the livestock dies. The key distinction is between 'nisab' (the minimum amount subject to zakat) and 'afw' (the surplus above nisab that does not incur additional zakat until the next bracket). Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that zakat is only on the nisab, so if the surplus dies, the zakat obligation on the remaining nisab remains unchanged. Muhammad and Zufar hold that the zakat obligation is spread over the entire herd proportionally, so if some animals die, the zakat decreases. The first example: nine camels (nisab for camels is five; the perished four are 'afw). According to Abu Hanifa, since the nisab of five remains, a full sheep is due. According to Muhammad, the obligation (which would be one sheep for five camels) is divided over nine, so 5/9 of a sheep remains. The second example (incomplete) with eighty sheep (nisab is 40) shows the same principle. This dispute affects the calculation of zakat when livestock numbers are reduced by death during the year. The practical consequence (thamar al-khilaf) is a different amount due.

Key Terms
العفو (al-'afw): The surplus amount of livestock above the nisab that does not incur additional zakat until the next bracket.
النصاب (al-nisab): The minimum threshold of wealth that triggers zakat obligation.
