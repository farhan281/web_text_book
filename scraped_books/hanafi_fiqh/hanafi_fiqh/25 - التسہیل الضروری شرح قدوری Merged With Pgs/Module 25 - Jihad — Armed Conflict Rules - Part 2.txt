Property of a Convert in Dar al-Harb: The General Rule and the Dispute on Usurped Property
Questions and answers on the fate of a convert's property in conquered territory with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling on the fate of a convert's property in dar al-harb upon conquest and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding usurped property held by a Muslim or dhimmi.

Core Idea
A convert's property becomes fay' upon conquest, but usurped property in Muslim/dhimmi hands is disputed: Abu Hanifa says fay', Muhammad says not.

This passage from the chapter on 'Ushr and Kharaj addresses the fate of property belonging to a person who converts to Islam within enemy territory. The first part establishes a clear rule: upon conquest, his real estate, family, and fighting slaves become fay' for the Muslim community. The second part introduces a nuanced dispute among the Hanafi imams regarding property that had been unlawfully taken (ghasb) from the convert and is now in the hands of a Muslim or dhimmi. Understanding this dispute illuminates the school's internal reasoning on the intersection of property rights and state sovereignty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The convert is living in dar al-harb (enemy territory) and the Muslims conquer it. The ruling applies upon conquest.
2
Identify items that become fay'
List: real estate, wife (including pregnancy), adult children, slaves who fought. All become fay'.
3
Distinguish the second scenario
The property was usurped (ghasb) from the convert and is now held by either a harbi, a Muslim, or a dhimmi.
4
Compare rulings by holder
If held by harbi, it's fay' regardless. If held by Muslim or dhimmi, Abu Hanifa says fay', Muhammad says not.
5
Note the source references
The disagreement is recorded in al-Siyar al-Kabir, and Abu Yusuf's alignment with Muhammad is mentioned in Sharh al-Jami' al-Saghir, sourced from al-Hidayah.

Classification of 'Ushri and Kharaji Lands with Dispute on Revived Land
Definition and categorization of lands subject to ushr or kharaj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ushriyya and kharajiyya lands and apply the classification to revived land according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Land classification determines produce tax; revived dead land's status depends on adjacency (Abu Yusuf) or revival method (Muhammad).

This passage from the chapter on ushr and kharaj provides a comprehensive categorization of lands into 'ushriyya (tithed) and kharajiyya (taxed). Understanding these categories is essential for determining the correct agricultural levy. The text also presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether revived dead land takes the classification of its surroundings or its method of revival, highlighting the interplay of consensus, analogy, and scholarly reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main land categories
Recognize that lands are either 'ushriyya (subject to ushr, a one-tenth tithe) or kharajiyya (subject to kharaj, a fixed land tax). Note the four subtypes of 'ushriyya and three subtypes of kharajiyya listed in the text.
The subtypes are based on how the land was acquired or its historical status.
2
Understand the basis of Basra's classification
Basra is 'ushriyya by consensus (ijma') of the Companions, even though analogical reasoning (qiyas) would make it kharajiyya due to adjacency. This shows that ijma' can override qiyas.
This is a key example of the Hanafi methodology where ijma' takes precedence over individual reasoning.
3
Analyze the dispute on revived dead land
Abu Yusuf rules that revived land takes the classification of the land it is adjacent to (hiz). Muhammad rules that the method of revival (digging a well or extracting a spring) determines its classification. Both views are presented without tarjih.
The text cuts off, but Muhammad's view likely makes it 'ushriyya if revived by personal effort.
4
Apply the classification to examples
Given a scenario of a land conquest, conversion, or revival, determine whether the land is 'ushriyya or kharajiyya based on the criteria given.
Pay attention to the exception: land conquered and distributed among soldiers becomes 'ushriyya, not kharajiyya.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed classification of lands into two categories: 'ushriyya (lands subject to the tithe of one-tenth on produce) and kharajiyya (lands subject to a land tax). The text first lists four types of 'ushriyya lands: (1) The original lands of the Arabian Peninsula (Arab lands), geographically defined from 'Udhayb to the far reaches of Yemen, bounded by Abyssinia, the Persian Sea, and the Euphrates, called Hijaz. (2) Lands whose inhabitants embraced Islam voluntarily without conquest. (3) Lands conquered by force (anwatan) that were distributed among the soldiers as spoils. (4) The land of Basra, which is 'ushriyya by the consensus (ijma') of the Companions, even though analogical reasoning (qiyas) would suggest it should be kharajiyya because of its adjacency to kharaj land. Rulings on Kharaj Land Tax
Three legal rulings on kharaj tax in conquered lands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three key rulings on kharaj and explain why kharaj is not combined with 'ushr nor repeated within a year.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a fixed annual tax on conquered land; it does not change with currency, does not combine with 'ushr, and does not repeat with multiple harvests.

In this lesson, we examine three foundational rulings on kharaj (land tax) from the Hanafi school. These rulings clarify the unique characteristics of kharaj as distinct from 'ushr (tithe) and establish its fixed, non-recurring nature. Understanding these principles is essential for grasping the Islamic fiscal system of conquered lands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three questions
The passage is structured as three Q&A on kharaj. Note each question and its answer.
The questions address: (1) effect of currency change, (2) combination with 'ushr, (3) repetition within a year.
2
Extract the ruling for each question
For each question, state the explicit ruling given in the answer. Write them in your own words.
Ruling 1: Kharaj does not change with currency. Ruling 2: No 'ushr on produce of kharaj land. Ruling 3: Kharaj is not repeated in one year.
3
Note the rationale for the third ruling
The marginal note provides reasoning: 'Umar (RA) did not impose kharaj repeatedly, unlike 'ushr which applies to every harvest. Understand the difference.
Key distinction: 'ushr is on the produce per harvest; kharaj is on the land per year.
4
Understand the key distinctions between kharaj and 'ushr
Reflect on the mutual exclusivity: a land cannot be subject to both. Kharaj is fixed annually; 'ushr is per produce.
This principle is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Rulings on Jizya (Poll Tax)
Definition, rates, exemptions, and special cases of jizya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and categories of jizya, list the graded rates for different wealth levels, identify the exempt groups, and apply the special rules for conversion and overlapping years.

Core Idea
Jizya is a protection tax exchanged for aman, with rates based on wealth and broad exemptions for women, children, and the incapacitated.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi rulings on jizya as presented in the matn. Jizya is not a punitive levy but a contractual obligation in return for security and exemption from military service. The text specifies graded rates, categories of exemption, and a unique case for Banu Taghlib.

Guided Path
1
Define jizya and its two types
Identify the definition of jizya as a levy taken from dhimmis for protection. Distinguish between treaty jizya (agreed amount) and imposed jizya (set by the Imam).
The underlying basis is the dhimma contract.
2
List the three rate categories
From the Imam's imposed jizya: rich pay 48 dirhams/year (4/month), middle class 24 dirhams/year (2/month), working poor 12 dirhams/year (1/month).
Monthly installment allowed for convenience.
3
Identify the exempt groups
Women, children, chronically ill, destitute unable to work, and monks who do not mix with people are completely exempt.
Exemption is based on lack of capacity or social role.
4
Explain the effects of conversion and overlapping years
If a dhimmi converts to Islam, any outstanding jizya is dropped. If two years pass without payment, only one jizya is taken due to indulgence (tahadul).
Conversion nullifies the obligation; overlapping does not double it.
5
Describe the Banu Taghlib exception
Because Banu Taghlib refused jizya out of Arab pride, 'Umar with Companions' consensus imposed double the zakat rate on them as a substitute, taken from both males and females but not children.
This is an exception based on historical circumstance and ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage deals with the fate of a Muslim's slave who has fallen into enemy hands (through capture, escape, or being overpowered). The first scenario: a slave is captured by kuffar; a Muslim trader buys him and brings him to Dar al-Islam. While in the trader's possession, the slave suffers an eye injury, and the trader receives compensation (arsh – blood money for injury). The original master (first owner) may reclaim the slave by paying the trader the purchase price (the amount the trader paid to the kuffar), but he does not get the arsh (which remains with the trader as compensation for the injury). The second scenario: the same slave is captured twice. First, a Muslim (buyer A) pays a thousand dirhams to the kuffar and brings the slave out. Then the slave is recaptured and another Muslim (buyer B) pays another thousand dirhams. The original master cannot take the slave from buyer B by paying the price; only buyer A can take him from buyer B by paying the price (since buyer A has a prior right). Additionally, if any buyer purchases the slave from kuffar and brings him to Dar al-Islam, the original owner can reclaim by paying the price if he wishes. These rules protect the investment of the rescuing Muslims while allowing the original owner to reclaim property. The passage concludes with a reference to Al-Hidayah.

مولى (mawla): Master or owner of a slave in this context.
أبق (abaqa): The act of a slave running away from his master.

Rulings on Rebels (Baghat)
Imam's treatment of rebels: call to obedience, rules of combat, property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on combat, property, and captives regarding rebels (baghat) and apply them to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Rebels are erring Muslims, not apostates—leniency governs combat, property, and captives.

This lesson covers the Hanafi fiqh of dealing with rebels (baghat). Unlike apostates, rebels are Muslims who have a misinterpretation. The Imam must first call them to obedience and clarify doubts. Fighting only begins if they start. Their property is safeguarded, and their offspring are not enslaved. Let's examine the rulings step by step.

Guided Path
1
Initial steps before combat
Identify what the Imam must do before engaging rebels: call them back to the community and clarify their doubts. Fighting is not to be initiated until they start.
2
Rules of engagement during combat
Determine whether the rebels have a fi'a (place of refuge). If they do, the Imam's army may finish off the wounded and pursue the fleeing leader. If they do not, these actions are prohibited.
Fi'a means a group to which they can rally.
3
Prohibitions on captives and property
Note the prohibitions: the offspring of rebels shall not be enslaved, and their property shall not be distributed as spoils.
This distinguishes them from non-Muslim enemies.
4
Handling of seized property and weapons
If the Imam's army takes rebels' weapons, they may use them if needed. Property seized from rebels is withheld until they repent, then returned.
5
Tax collected by rebels
If rebels collected kharaj or 'ushr from lands, the Imam does not collect it again. If the rebels spent it correctly, it suffices; if not, the people must make it up in their relationship with Allah.

Question: If a group of people abandon obedience to Islam and take over a city, how should the Imam deal with them? Answer: The Imam should call them back to the community (jama'ah) and clarify their doubts. He should not start fighting until they start. If they start, he should fight them until their group is dispersed. If they have a place of refuge (fi'a), he should finish off their wounded and pursue their fleeing. If they have no place of refuge, he should not finish off their wounded nor pursue their fleeing. Their offspring are not taken as captives, and their property is not distributed. Question: The Imam's army takes the weapons of the rebels; is it permissible for the Imam's soldiers to fight with their weapons? Answer: If Muslims need them, they may do so. Question: If the Imam's group gains control over the property of the rebels, what should the Imam do with this property? Answer: He should withhold it and not return it to them until they repent. When they repent, he returns it to them. Question: The rebels collect land tax (kharaj) and tithe ('ushr) from the lands they have taken over, then the Imam gains control; should he take it from them a second time? Answer: He does not take it a second time. If they spent it in its proper due, that suffices for the one from whom it was taken. If they did not spend it in its proper due, then the people must repeat it between themselves and Allah Almighty.

Commentary
This passage covers the Hanafi fiqh of rebels (baghat). The passage type is a ruling statement in question-answer format. The key distinction: rebels are not considered apostates; they are Muslims with a political grievance or misinterpretation. The Imam must first call them to rejoin the community and clarify their doubts. Fighting only begins if they initiate.
