The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Interdiction of the Spendthrift
Presents the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf/Muhammad on whether a free adult spendthrift can be interdicted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Abu Hanifa's position versus that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding interdiction of a free adult spendthrift, and apply the mufta bihi view to specific transactions such as sale, marriage, manumission, Hajj, and bequest.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa rejects interdiction; the Two Companions impose it, and their view is the relied-upon position.

A free adult who wastes his wealth on useless things is called a safih (spendthrift). The Hanafi school internally disagrees whether such a person can be legally interdicted (hajr) from disposing of his property. This passage from al-Tashil al-Daruri presents the views of Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, along with the practical consequences across several areas of fiqh. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to see how differing usul assumptions affect concrete rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The central question: can a free, adult, sane but wasteful person be interdicted (prevented from managing his wealth)? Note that Abu Hanifa says no; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say yes.
The disagreement is over the principle of hajr, not over the need to protect wealth.
2
State each position clearly
Abu Hanifa: no interdiction; the spendthrift's dispositions are valid, though wealth is not handed over until age 25 if not prudent. After 25, wealth is given regardless. The Two Companions: interdiction is imposed; sales are invalid without judicial approval; wealth is never handed over until prudence is observed.
Note the age threshold in Abu Hanifa's view – it acts as a practical safeguard.
3
Identify areas of agreement
Despite the principal difference, the three imams agree on several rulings: manumission is valid (the slave works for his value according to the Two); marriage is valid but dower is limited to mahr al-mithl; obligatory Hajj is permitted but funds go to a trustee; bequests from one-third are valid.
These agreements show a shared concern for the spendthrift's essential needs and religious obligations.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad – the spendthrift is interdicted. This is the view adopted by later Hanafi authorities.
The authority_weight is zahir riwayah, and this is a case of intra-school tarjih.
5
Apply the ikhtilaf to specific scenarios
For each transaction (sale, marriage, manumission, Hajj, bequest), state the ruling under the mufta bihi view. For example, a sale by a spendthrift is not valid unless the judge permits it; a marriage is valid but dower above the proper amount is void.
This step connects the theoretical dispute to practical fiqh applications.

Question: A man who is a spendthrift, adult, free, what is the ruling on his disposition of his wealth? Answer: Abu Hanifa said: The spendthrift, if he is sane, adult, free, is not placed under interdiction, and his disposition of his wealth is permissible, even if he is wasteful and corrupting, destroying his wealth in things that have no purpose or benefit for him, such as throwing it into the sea or burning it in fire. Question: Is there a limit for handing over the money of the spendthrift according to Abu Hanifa? Answer: Yes, there is a limit according to him. He said: When a boy reaches puberty but is not prudent, his wealth is not handed over to him until he reaches twenty-five years. If he disposes of it before that, his disposition takes effect. When he reaches twenty-five years, his wealth is handed over to him even if no prudence is observed from him. Question: What is the saying of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them) regarding interdiction on the spendthrift? Answer: They said: The spendthrift is placed under interdiction and prevented from disposing of his wealth. If he sells, his sale is not effective regarding his wealth. If there is a benefit in it, the judge may permit it. One who reaches puberty but is not prudent is not given his wealth according to them ever until prudence is observed from him. His disposition is not valid. Zakat is taken from his wealth, and it is spent on his children, wife, and those whose maintenance is obligatory upon him among relatives. Question: A spendthrift emancipates his slave, what do our three scholars say about that? Answer: His emancipation is valid according to Abu Hanifa, and similarly according to them, but they say: The slave works to pay his value. Question: A spendthrift marries a woman, what is the ruling on his marriage? Answer: His marriage is valid, and the dower is examined. If he specifies a dower for her, the amount of the proper dower (mahr al-mithl) is valid from it, and the excess is void. Question: A spendthrift intends to perform the Hajj of Islam (obligatory Hajj), is he permitted to do so? Answer: Yes, he is permitted to perform the obligatory Hajj and is not prevented from it. The judge does not hand over the expenses to him, but hands them over to a trustworthy person among the pilgrims, who spends on him for the expenses of Hajj. Question: A spendthrift is ill and makes bequests for acts of piety and good deeds, what is the ruling on this bequest? Answer: This bequest is valid from one-third of his wealth.
Source pages p. 230

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal restriction (hajr) on a free adult spendthrift (safih). Abu Hanifa holds that a free sane adult cannot be interdicted, even if he is wasteful. His financial dispositions are valid, though his wealth should not be handed over until age 25 if he is not prudent. After 25, it is given regardless of prudence. In contrast, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) argue that a spendthrift can be interdicted. He is prevented from disposing of his wealth; his sales are not valid without judicial permission. His wealth is not handed over even after 25 until prudence is observed. They also detail that zakat is taken from his wealth and maintenance is paid. Despite general interdiction, they agree on certain points: manumission of a slave by a spendthrift is valid according to all three, but the two companions require the slave to work for his value. Marriage is valid according to all, but the dower is restricted to the proper dower (mahr al-mithl); excess is void. The spendthrift is allowed to perform obligatory Hajj, but funds are given to a trustee. Bequests from one-third of his wealth are valid. This demonstrates the practical consequences of the difference in opinion.

Key Terms
السفيه (al-safih): A spendthrift; a person who wastes his wealth in useless or harmful ways.
الحجر (al-hajr): Legal restriction preventing a person from disposing of his property.
مهر المثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower; the amount appropriate for a woman of similar status.
الرشد (al-rushd): Prudence; the ability to manage one's affairs responsibly.

Marriage of a Prodigal: Validity and Dowry Limit
Ruling on marriage of prodigal and dowry limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the marriage of a prodigal (safīh) and apply the limitation of dowry to mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
A prodigal's marriage is valid, but any dowry exceeding her mahr al-mithl is void.

This short Q&A from a classical text addresses a common concern: can a person under financial interdiction (safīh) enter a valid marriage? The answer protects both the individual's personal status and financial interests. The marriage itself is valid, but the dowry is capped at the appropriate amount for the woman.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
The passage discusses the marriage of a safīh – a person who has been legally interdicted due to prodigality (wastefulness). Recognize that this is a special status affecting financial transactions, not personal ones.
The safīh is under hajr (interdiction) to protect his wealth.
2
Extract the ruling on validity
The marriage is explicitly ruled as jā'iz (valid/permissible). Financial incapacity does not prevent a valid nikah.
This shows that marriage is a personal right, not purely a financial transaction.
3
Analyze the dowry treatment
If the safīh names a specific mahr, only the amount equal to mahr al-mithl (the appropriate dowry for her social standing) is valid. Any excess is batil (void).
The void portion does not affect the validity of the marriage itself.
4
Define mahr al-mithl
Mahr al-mithl is determined by factors like the woman's age, beauty, family status, and local customs. It serves as a fair standard.
If no mahr is specified, mahr al-mithl is still due.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, a safīh who promises a dowry beyond mahr al-mithl is only required to pay the mahr al-mithl. The excess promise is legally unenforceable.
This protects the safīh from being pressured into excessive financial obligation.

Priority of Debts in Marad al-Mawt
Priority of debts in death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the priority order of debts during death illness and apply the rule to determine which debt is paid first.

Core Idea
Health debts and known illness debts take priority over debts acknowledged during terminal illness.

When a person in terminal illness acknowledges debts, the Hanafi school carefully prioritizes them to protect the rights of genuine creditors. This lesson covers the ranking of debts from health, illness by known causes, and acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of debts
List debts incurred during health, debts incurred during illness by known causes (e.g., medical expenses), and debts acknowledged (iqrar) during terminal illness.
Known causes include expenses necessary for treatment.
2
Apply the priority rule
Health debts and known illness debts are paid first from the estate. Only after they are satisfied can acknowledged debts be paid, if there is any remaining estate.
Acknowledged debts are treated like bequests; they are limited to one-third of the estate and are subordinate to actual debts.
3
Consider the absence of prior debts
If there are no health debts or known illness debts, the acknowledgment is valid and the recipient (muqarr lahu) takes priority over the heirs, similar to a bequest from the one-third.
This is because the iqrar is considered a disposition from the one-third share.

Question: A sick person acknowledged debts during his death illness while he had debts from his health, and debts incurred during his illness by known causes; which debts are given priority? Answer: The debt of health and the debt known by causes are given priority over what he acknowledged during his death illness. Question: If he had no debts incurred during health, what is the ruling on debts acknowledged during illness? Answer: His acknowledgment is permissible, and the recipient has priority over the heirs. Question: What is the ruling on a sick person's acknowledgment to some of the heirs? Answer: His acknowledgment to an heir is void unless the other heirs confirm it. Question: He acknowledged money to a foreign woman during his death illness then married her; what is the ruling on his acknowledgment? Answer: His acknowledgment of the money is valid, and it is not nullified by the marriage. Question: A man divorced his wife three times during his death illness, then acknowledged a debt to her and died; what does this woman take from his wealth? Answer: She takes the lesser of the debt and her inheritance from him.

Commentary
This passage contains a series of rulings on iqrar (acknowledgment) made during terminal illness (marad al-mawt). The Hanafi school treats such acknowledgments with caution because they resemble bequests and can harm the rights of genuine creditors and heirs. First, debts from health or known causes take priority over debts acknowledged during illness. Second, if there are no health debts, the acknowledgment is valid and the recipient takes priority over heirs (since it is considered a bequest from the remaining third of the estate). Third, acknowledgment to an heir is void unless all other heirs confirm it, because it would give the heir an advantage over others. Fourth, if a man acknowledges a debt to a woman during illness and then marries her, the acknowledgment remains valid; marriage does not nullify it. Fifth, if a man divorces his wife during illness (which is effective but treated as a bequest in some respects) then acknowledges a debt to her, she gets the lesser of the debt and her inheritance share, to prevent conflict of interest.

مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): A terminal illness from which the person is expected to die
دين الصحة (dīn al-ṣiḥḥa): Debt incurred during the period of health
دين معروف بالأسباب (dīn ma‘rūf bi-al-asbāb): Debt incurred during illness by known causes, e.g., medical expenses

Acknowledgment to an Heir in Marad al-Mawt
Ruling on iqrar to an heir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on acknowledging a debt to an heir during terminal illness and identify when it becomes valid.

Core Idea
Iqrar to an heir is void unless all other heirs confirm it.

Acknowledging a debt to an heir during terminal illness is suspicious because it could unfairly advantage that heir over others. The Hanafi school therefore deems such acknowledgment void unless the other heirs voluntarily confirm it. This lesson examines the rule and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether the recipient is an heir
Check if the person to whom the debt is acknowledged is entitled to inherit from the sick person.
Heirs include those listed in the Quran and Sunnah (e.g., children, spouse, parents).
2
Apply the general prohibition
If the recipient is an heir, the acknowledgment is void (bāṭil) by default.
The reason is to prevent the sick person from favoring one heir over others by turning a bequest into a debt.
3
Consider the exception
The acknowledgment becomes valid if all other heirs confirm (yusaddiqūnahu) it. Their confirmation indicates they believe the debt is genuine.
If even one heir refuses to confirm, the acknowledgment remains void.

Effect of Marriage and Divorce on Iqrar in Marad al-Mawt
Rulings on iqrar with subsequent marriage or divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on how subsequent marriage or divorce affects an acknowledgment made during terminal illness.

Core Idea
Marriage does not nullify a prior iqrar; after divorce, the divorced wife takes the lesser of her debt and inheritance.

Two nuanced scenarios arise when a person in terminal illness marries or divorces after acknowledging a debt. This lesson clarifies that marriage does not cancel a prior acknowledgment to a stranger, and that a divorced wife who is also a creditor receives the smaller amount to avoid double benefit.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the marriage scenario
A man acknowledges a debt to a woman during his terminal illness, then marries her. The acknowledgment remains valid.
Marriage does not retroactively nullify the debt; it was a genuine obligation.
2
Analyze the divorce scenario
A man divorces his wife three times during terminal illness, then acknowledges a debt to her and dies.
The divorced wife is still an heir? In Hanafi, a revocable divorce (talaq raj‘i) does not cut inheritance, but a triple divorce (talaq bā’in) does. However, if the divorce was during marad al-mawt, she may still inherit as a precaution (to prevent fraud).
3
Apply the 'lesser of two' rule
The woman receives the lesser of the acknowledged debt and her inheritance share from him.
This prevents her from getting both a debt and an inheritance, which would be excessive.

The Refinement of 'Min Aslabikum': Adopted vs Foster Sons
Critical textual refinement on the scope of 'min aslabikum'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi interpretation of 'min aslabikum' (from your loins) and distinguish the prohibition of marrying the wife of a foster son from the permissibility of marrying the ex-wife of an adopted son.

Core Idea
The Quranic phrase 'from your loins' excludes adopted sons but not foster sons from the prohibition of marrying their wives.

This lesson focuses on a critical textual refinement from the commentary of al-Hidayah on the verse prohibiting marriage to sons' wives. The phrase 'min aslabikum' (from your loins) appears to limit the prohibition to biological sons. However, Hanafi scholars clarify that this exclusion applies only to adopted sons (who are not biologically related), not to foster sons (who are related through breastfeeding). This distinction has significant practical implications for marriage law.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic Verse and Identify the Key Phrase
Locate the verse in the passage: 'And the wives of your sons who are from your loins' (Nisa: 23). Focus on the phrase 'min aslabikum' (from your loins).
The verse is cited in the context of affinity prohibitions.
2
Understand the Potential Ambiguity
The phrase 'from your loins' could be interpreted strictly as biological sons only. This would make the wife of an adopted son permissible and the wife of a foster son permissible as well, since a foster son is not biologically related.
But the passage quotes al-Hidayah to clarify the correct interpretation.
3
Analyze the al-Hidayah Quote
Read the quote: 'The mention of loins is to negate consideration of adoption, not to make permissible the wife of a son from breastfeeding.' Understand that the purpose of 'min aslabikum' is to exclude adopted sons only, not to create a loophole for foster sons.
Al-Hidayah is a foundational Hanafi commentary. Its ruling is authoritative.
4
Derive the Two Rulings
Based on the quote, derive: (1) Marriage to the wife of a foster son is haram, just as it is haram for a biological son. (2) Marriage to the ex-wife of an adopted son (after his divorce or death) is permissible.
The prohibition for foster sons is based on the principle that breastfeeding creates kinship analogous to lineage.
5
Apply the Principle
Consider a scenario: A man has a foster son from breastfeeding. That foster son marries a woman and later dies. The man cannot marry that widow because she is considered his son's wife. Now consider an adopted son (legally adopted, not biologically related). If that adopted son marries and later divorces, the man may marry the ex-wife.
This distinction is specific to the Hanafi school and relies on the interpretation of 'min aslabikum'.

Prohibited Women Due to Lineage
Enumerates women forbidden by blood relation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven categories of women permanently forbidden in marriage due to lineage and apply qiyas to derive subcategories.

Core Idea
Seven blood relations are permanently forbidden: mothers, daughters, sisters, nieces, aunts, grandmothers, granddaughters.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar enumerates the women whom a man may never marry because of blood relationship. Knowing these categories is essential for every Muslim, as marriage to any of them is haram. The text also teaches a method of analogical reasoning (qiyas) to extend the prohibition to all subcategories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Categories
Read the list: grandmothers, daughters, sisters, brothers' daughters, sisters' daughters, paternal aunts, maternal aunts. These are the seven main groups.
Each group has a plural form that includes multiple subcategories.
2
Understand the Inclusion Principle
The text says 'even if they ascend' (grandmothers above) and 'even if they descend' (granddaughters below). This means all generations in the direct line are included.
For example, 'daughters' includes daughters, granddaughters, great-granddaughters.
3
Apply Qiyas to Subcategories
The text uses 'analogize to this' for sisters and aunts. Apply the same principle to derive: full, paternal, and maternal sisters; full, paternal, and maternal nieces and aunts.
This demonstrates that the prohibition covers all siblings and their descendants regardless of the connecting parent.
4
Recognize Exclusions
Note that cousins (children of aunts/uncles) are not listed. The prohibition is limited to these seven classes; cousins are permissible to marry.
This is a common point of confusion.

Prohibited Women Due to Fosterage
Explains that fosterage creates similar prohibitions as lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that fosterage creates the same prohibitions as lineage, and identify the Quranic proof from Surah al-Nisa'.

Core Idea
Everything prohibited by blood is prohibited by milk.

This lesson introduces the foundational principle of fosterage (rada') in Islamic law. The text from al-Quduri establishes that the prohibitions of marriage due to lineage also apply equally to fosterage, based on the explicit mention in the Quran. We will examine the ruling, its proof, and the scope of the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The text states that it is 'haram' (unlawful) for a man to marry his foster mother or foster sister. This is the specific prohibition.
The ruling is derived from the general principle: prohibitions of lineage transfer to fosterage.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites the Quranic verse (4:23): 'And your mothers who suckled you and your sisters from fosterage.' This is the primary evidence.
The verse explicitly mentions two categories of foster relatives as prohibited.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof proves the ruling)
The verse uses the word 'your mothers who suckled you' and 'your sisters from fosterage'. Linguistically, the mention of these two implies that all relations that are mahram by blood are also mahram by fosterage, by analogy (qiyas) based on the common cause (illa) of kinship through milk.
This is a clear textual indication (dalala qati'a).
4
Note the scope and exceptions
The text says 'except some exceptions that will come in the chapter of fosterage'. This means the general rule has specific exceptions (e.g., marrying the foster mother's daughter from another husband who was not breastfed? Actually, that is not an exception; exceptions like marrying a foster brother's sister?). The exceptions are minor and do not undermine the general principle.
The exceptions will be covered in later lessons on fosterage.
5
Evaluate the authority and consensus
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is agreed upon across madhhabs (muttafaq alayh). The Quranic text is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala, so the ruling is definitive.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on this general principle.

Women Prohibited Through Marriage (Sihriyyah)
Details women forbidden through marriage relationships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of women permanently prohibited through affinity (sihriyyah) and identify the Quranic proof for each category, including the condition for stepdaughters.

Core Idea
Marriage creates permanent prohibition with specific in-laws, even without consummation — except for stepdaughters where consummation is required.

This passage presents the rulings on women who become permanently unlawful to a man through marriage relationships (sihriyyah). It lists four categories and cites the clear Quranic verses from Surah an-Nisa (4:22-23). The text also clarifies important nuances: the prohibition applies even without consummation except for stepdaughters, and adopted sons are not treated as biological sons for this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
Read the passage and list the four types of women prohibited through affinity: (1) any woman married by your father or grandfather, (2) wife of your son or grandson, (3) mother of your wife, (4) stepdaughter (daughter of your wife).
The prohibition applies regardless of whether the marriage was consummated, except for stepdaughters.
2
Match each category to its Quranic proof
For each category, find the verse cited: category 1 → Quran 4:22, categories 2-4 → Quran 4:23. Note that the prohibition is derived from explicit imperatives.
The proof for mother of wife is 'ummahatu nisaikum' and for stepdaughters is 'rabbaibukum'.
3
Understand the consummation condition for stepdaughters
The stepdaughter is prohibited only if you have consummated the marriage with her mother. If not, there is no prohibition. The phrase 'in your custody' is not a condition; it is merely a common situation.
This is a key distinction from the other categories where no condition applies.
4
Analyze the adopted son exception
The phrase 'from your loins' (min aslabikum) in the verse regarding sons excludes adopted sons. Thus, marrying the ex-wife of an adopted son is permissible after divorce or death.
The Hanafi school strictly limits the prohibition to biological sons based on this textual detail.
5
Apply the rulings to a test scenario
Consider a man whose father dies and leaves a widow (not the man's mother). Is it permissible for him to marry her? According to the first category, it is haram regardless of consummation.
This helps solidify the unconditional nature of the prohibition for that category.

Prohibited Women Due to Combination in Marriage
Forbids simultaneous marriage to certain close female relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibition of combining certain women in marriage, derive the ruling from the Quranic verse and hadith, and apply the universal maxim to identify prohibited combinations.

Core Idea
Combining two sisters or a woman with her aunt in marriage is haram; a universal maxim defines all such cases.

Marriage in Islam has strict rules about who can be married simultaneously. The Qur'an explicitly forbids marrying two sisters at once, and the Prophet ﷺ extended this to combinations involving aunts and nieces. This lesson explains the prohibition, the proofs, and the universal maxim that jurists formulated to cover all similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that combining certain women in marriage is haram (prohibited). Identify the act (jam') and the ruling level (haram).
The text uses 'يُحَرَّم' (it is prohibited).
2
Locate the first dalil (Quran)
The Quranic verse (4:23) explicitly forbids combining two sisters. Note the phrase 'an tajma'u bayna al-ukhtayn'.
This is a primary, qat'i proof.
3
Locate the second dalil (Hadith)
The hadith from Abu Huraira extends the prohibition to combining a woman with her paternal or maternal aunt. The hadith is ahad but its meaning is qat'i due to acceptance.
The hadith lists four specific prohibited combinations.
4
Understand the universal maxim (qa'ida kulliyya)
The jurists derived a maxim: any two women who, if one were male, would be mahram to each other (by blood or fosterage), cannot be married simultaneously to the same man.
This maxim covers all cases beyond the explicit texts.
5
Apply the maxim to new cases
Test the maxim: e.g., can a man marry a woman and her mother? No, because if one were male, they would be mother-son (mahram). Similarly, foster relationships follow the same principle.
The maxim is comprehensive and prevents any loophole.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the prohibition of combining certain women in marriage simultaneously (jam'). The primary example is combining two sisters. The proof includes both the Quranic verse and the hadith narrated by Abu Huraira which extends the prohibition to combining a woman with her paternal or maternal aunt. The jurists derive a universal maxim: any two women who would be mahram to each other if one were male (i.e., they share a blood or foster relationship that would forbid marriage between them if one were male) cannot be married to the same man simultaneously. For example, two sisters: if one were male, they would be brother and sister, so marriage between them is impossible. Hence, combining them in marriage is haram. The hadith specifically mentions that one cannot marry a woman and her paternal aunt (father's sister) or maternal aunt (mother's sister) together. The phrase 'the younger on the older' indicates that this applies regardless of seniority.

Prohibited Women Due to Disbelief in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling with proof on marriage prohibitions due to disbelief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriage with Kitabiyya and identify which categories of women are forbidden due to disbelief.

Core Idea
A Muslim woman cannot marry any non-Muslim; a Muslim man can marry a Kitabiyya but must avoid marriage with polytheists.

Marriage in Islam is a sacred bond, but it is restricted by faith. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on whom a Muslim may and may not marry based on the other's religion. We will see that polytheists are absolutely forbidden, while Jewish and Christian women (Kitabiyyat) are permissible with discouragement, and that these rules apply symmetrically to men and women.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is marriage (nikah). The ruling is that marriage with polytheists is haram for both men and women, while marriage to Kitabiyya is permissible for men but disliked (makruh tanzihi).
The ruling for Muslim women is absolute: no marriage with any non-Muslim.
2
Categorize the prohibited women
Prohibited women include polytheists (idol worshipers, Zoroastrians, and all other disbelievers except Kitabiyyat). The text explicitly mentions Majusiyyat (Zoroastrians) and Wathaniyyat (idol worshipers).
The prohibition applies equally to free and slave women.
3
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 5:5, which permits marriage to chaste women from the People of the Book. Polytheists are forbidden by Quran 2:221 (general prohibition).
Hanafi usul treats the Quranic text as qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala on some details.
4
Understand the karahah (dislike)
Marriage to Kitabiyya is permissible but discouraged due to potential religious discord and children's upbringing. Later Hanafi fatwa collections consider it better to avoid.
This karahah is tanzihi (not tahrimi), so the marriage is valid.
5
Apply to contemporary groups
The text extends the ruling to modern sects that claim Islam but are considered disbelievers (e.g., Qadianis, communists, those denying mutawatir). Marriage with them is also forbidden.
These classifications rely on takfir based on core creedal deviations.

The General Principle of Prohibited Combinations in Marriage
Ruling with proof, expanding specific prohibitions into a general principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi general principle for prohibited combinations in marriage and apply it to identify which combinations are haram.

Core Idea
Any two women who would be prohibitive to each other if one were male cannot be married simultaneously — a comprehensive Hanafi rule.

This passage builds from specific prohibitions (step-daughters, sisters, aunts) to a general principle that covers all cases. Understanding this principle is key to applying Hanafi law in marriage combinations. We will trace the dalil and how the jurists expanded the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core prohibition
Recognize that the text forbids combining two women in marriage. The hukm level is haram, as indicated by the explicit term and the imperative prohibition in the hadith.
The primary example given is two sisters, but the scope is wider.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The Quran (4:23) explicitly prohibits combining two sisters. The hadith from Abu Hurairah extends this to aunts and nieces. Note the hadith uses the imperative 'نهى' which in Hanafi usul indicates prohibition (tahrim).
The hadith is ahad, so its thubut is zanni, but the prohibition is established.
3
Understand the expansion through the general principle
The jurists derived a 'qāʻidah kulliyyah': any two women who would be prohibited from marrying each other if one were male (due to lineage or fosterage) cannot be combined. This covers not only blood relations but also foster relations.
This principle goes beyond the explicit texts by analogizing from the given cases.
4
Apply the principle to new cases
Test the principle: if a woman and her paternal aunt were both male, they would be uncle and nephew? No, but if one were male, the other would be a mahram? Actually, the principle says: if one were male, marriage between them would be prohibited due to blood or fosterage. So combining a woman and her paternal aunt is haram because the aunt's daughter (the cousin) would be prohibited if the aunt were male? Wait, the principle is: take two women, suppose one of them is male. If that male could not marry the other (due to being a mahram), then combining them is haram. So for a woman and her paternal aunt, if the aunt were male, she would be the woman's paternal uncle, and a paternal uncle is a mahram, so marriage would be prohibited. Hence combining is haram. Similarly for a woman and her foster sister.
This requires knowledge of mahram relationships.
5
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may restrict the prohibition to the specific cases mentioned in the hadith, not applying the general principle. This highlights the Hanafi commitment to rational extension (qiyas) in deriving rulings.
The Hanafi position is broader and more comprehensive.

Marrying a woman already bound by marriage or iddah is haram and invalid because it violates another's exclusive right.

Marriage is a sacred bond that establishes exclusive rights. The Shari'ah strictly protects these rights, making it unlawful to marry a woman who is already in the bond of another man or in her waiting period (iddah). If such a marriage is contracted, it is invalid (batil) and must be annulled. The marriage does not produce any legal effects.
The woman remains in her original marriage or iddah; the second marriage is void from the start.

Prohibition of Marrying Another's Wife or Woman in 'Iddah
Ruling statement on prohibited women rights of others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that marrying a woman who is another's wife or in 'iddah is haram and identify the three causes of 'iddah mentioned in the text.

Core Idea
A woman cannot be bound by two marriage contracts; her marriage or 'iddah is a right of her current husband.

Marriage is a sacred contract that establishes exclusive rights between spouses. Islamic law therefore prohibits any contract that would conflict with an existing marriage or the waiting period ('iddah) that preserves the remnants of that marriage. This lesson examines a foundational ruling from al-Quduri that states this prohibition clearly and lists the situations in which a woman is considered legally tied to another man.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage answers the question about prohibited women due to the right of another. The act is marrying a woman who is already married or in 'iddah, and the ruling is 'la yahillu' (not lawful), which is a clear prohibition (haram).
'La yahillu' in Hanafi fiqh indicates tahrim (prohibition).
2
Identify the two categories of prohibited women
The text specifies two categories: (1) another man's wife (zawjat rajulin akhar), and (2) a woman in 'iddah belonging to another man (mu'taddatuhu). Both are prohibited because they are in the right of another person.
The term 'mu'taddatuhu' covers all types of 'iddah.
3
Enumerate the causes of 'iddah mentioned
The 'iddah may arise from three causes: divorce (talaq), death of the husband (wafah), or consummation in an invalid marriage (dukhul fi nikaah fassid). The prohibition applies equally in all these cases.
Consummation in a fasid marriage creates an 'iddah similar to that of a valid marriage.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that a woman cannot be subject to two simultaneous marriage contracts. During 'iddah, she is still considered part of the previous husband's rights, so marrying her would violate that right.
This ruling is derived from Quranic verses and hadith, and is unanimous across the madhhab.

Permissibility of Combining a Woman and Her Step-Daughter
Ruling on permissibility of combining a woman and her step-daughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marrying a woman and her step-daughter simultaneously and explain the legal basis for its permissibility.

Core Idea
A step-daughter is not a mahram to her step-mother, so combining them in marriage is permissible.

This short passage from al-Quduri addresses a specific case: a man wants to marry a woman while also marrying her step-daughter—the daughter of the woman's previous husband from another wife. The ruling is clear and straightforward: it is permissible. Let's understand why this is allowed and what legal principle governs it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act in question is marrying a woman and simultaneously marrying her step-daughter (the woman's husband's daughter from another wife). The step-daughter is not the woman's biological daughter nor related by milk, but is the daughter of her husband from a different marriage.
Note that the step-daughter is not a mahram to the woman; the only relationship is through marriage to the same man (the woman's current husband).
2
Identify the potential prohibition
In Islamic law, there is a general prohibition against 'combining' (jam') certain women in marriage simultaneously, such as two sisters or a woman and her aunt. This prohibition is based on specific texts and aims to preserve harmony. The question is whether step-daughter falls under this prohibition.
The prohibited categories are explicitly listed in the Quran and Sunnah; step-daughters of the wife are not among them.
3
Apply the principle
Since there is no textual evidence prohibiting the combination of a woman and her step-daughter, and the step-daughter is not a mahram to the woman, the ruling is that it is permissible (mubah). The absence of a prohibition implies permissibility.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that all marriages are permissible unless explicitly prohibited.

Post-Divorce Marriage to Sister-in-Law During 'Iddah
Ruling on marrying the sister of a divorced wife before her 'iddah expires.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on marrying the sister of a divorced wife before her 'iddah expires and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
A divorced woman's 'iddah preserves a residual marital tie; marrying her sister during this period is forbidden.

After a divorce, a man might consider marrying the sister of his ex-wife. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi ruling: while collecting two sisters as wives is forbidden, the prohibition extends to sequential marriage during the first wife's 'iddah. The 'iddah period maintains a legal connection, making such a marriage invalid until it concludes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act in question
The act is marrying the sister of a woman whom the man has divorced.
2
Note the condition that triggers the prohibition
The prohibition exists only while the divorced wife is in her 'iddah (waiting period). Both revocable and irrevocable divorces have an 'iddah.
3
State the ruling clearly
It is haram (unlawful) and the marriage contract is fasid (defective) if performed during the 'iddah.
4
Understand the underlying reason
During 'iddah, the woman is still considered connected to the man in some respects; marrying her sister would be akin to collecting two sisters simultaneously, which is explicitly forbidden.
5
Apply the ruling consistently
The same rule applies whether the divorce was revocable (raj'i) or irrevocable (ba'in), as both require an 'iddah.

Source Text

السؤال: طلق رجل امرأته، هل يجوز له أن يتزوج بأختها؟ الجواب: إذا طلق رجل امرأته طلاقا باتا أو رجعيا، لم يجزله أن يتزوج بأختها حتى تنقضي عدتها.
Question: A man divorces his wife; is it permissible for him to marry her sister? Answer: If a man divorces his wife with an irrevocable or revocable divorce, he is not allowed to marry her sister until her waiting period expires.
Source pages p. 327

Commentary
This ruling addresses the prohibition of collecting two sisters in marriage, but here the issue is sequential: after divorcing one sister, may he marry the other? The answer is no, not until the divorced wife's 'iddah is completed. This applies whether the divorce is irrevocable (ba'in) or revocable (raj'i). The reason is that during the 'iddah, the woman is still considered to have some marital connection to the man; if he married her sister during that period, it would be like collecting two sisters, which is haram. Once the 'iddah is over, the prohibition ceases because the marriage tie is fully severed. This is a well-established ruling from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
طلاقا باتا (ṭalāqan bāttan): An irrevocable divorce that severs the marriage immediately, but still requires 'iddah.
