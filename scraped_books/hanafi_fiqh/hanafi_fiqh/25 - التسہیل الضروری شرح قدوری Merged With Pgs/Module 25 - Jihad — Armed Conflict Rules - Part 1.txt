Foundling's Religion: Location Determines Status
Question-answer format establishing default religion based on place of discovery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the religion of a foundling is determined by the location where he is found, distinguishing Dar al-Islam from dhimmi locations.

Core Idea
A foundling's religion is presumed from location: Muslim in Muslim territory, dhimmi in dhimmi territory.

This lesson presents a classic Hanafi ruling on a foundling (laqit) whose parentage is unknown. The key question: if a dhimmi claims the child, does the child follow the father's religion? The Hanafi answer is surprising: the child's religion is determined by where he was found, not by the claimant. This rule applies in both Muslim and non-Muslim areas, establishing a clear legal presumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) foundling in a Muslim city or village, (2) foundling in a dhimmi village or place of worship. Recognize the contrast.
Memorize the key locations: Muslim cities/villages vs. dhimmi villages, churches, synagogues.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1: the foundling is ruled Muslim, even if a dhimmi claims paternity. For scenario 2: the foundling is ruled dhimmi.
The ruling is declaratory (hukm wad'i), not a practical obligation.
3
Understand the role of lineage
Note that the dhimmi's claim of paternity establishes lineage (nasab) between them, but it does not change the child's religion. The child is still considered Muslim if found in Muslim territory.
Lineage and religion are separate in this case.
4
Grasp the underlying rationale
The location is a circumstantial indicator (qarina) of the child's likely religion. In Dar al-Islam, the default presumption is Islam. In dhimmi areas, the presumption follows the environment.
This is based on the principle of 'default Islam' (al-asl al-Islam) in Muslim lands.
5
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that a foundling is always Muslim, regardless of location, because the default human state is Islam. Hanafis make an exception based on environment.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both positions are based on differing usul.

Commentary
This passage deals with the religious status of a foundling when found in different territories. First: If a foundling is found in a Muslim city or village, even if a dhimmi (non-Muslim citizen) claims him as his son, the child's lineage is established (i.e., he becomes the dhimmi's son), but the child himself is ruled to be a Muslim. The rationale is that the child was found in Dar al-Islam (the abode of Islam), so the default presumption is that he is Muslim. The child's physical presence in Muslim lands overrides the father's religion for the child's own status. Second: If the foundling is found in a village of the dhimmis, or specifically in a Jewish synagogue or Christian church, then the child is ruled to be a dhimmi (i.e., a non-Muslim protected citizen). The location determines the religion because it indicates the likely religious affiliation of the community. This is a ruling based on circumstantial evidence (qarā'in).

Enslavement of Captives in Jihad: Ruling and Rationale
Ruling on enslavement in jihad with refutation of objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on enslaving captives in jihad and explain the practical justification for this ruling.

Core Idea
Enslaving captives is permissible for the ruler to prevent them from returning to fight and to avoid treasury burden.

This passage addresses a critical aspect of jihad: the fate of captives. The Hanafi school upholds the permissibility of enslavement, distinguishing it from execution or imprisonment. The text also provides a rebuttal to modern ethical objections, grounding the ruling in practical considerations of security and economics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is enslaving captives taken in jihad. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) for the Muslim ruler to do so, and once distributed, they become property of the fighters.
2
Locate the Evidence
The proof is the continuity of jihad until the Day of Judgment and the established practice of the Prophet and early community. This is an implicit dalil based on consensus and sunnah.
The text does not cite a specific verse or hadith but relies on the ongoing practice of jihad.
3
Understand the Wisdom
The rationale: sending captives back strengthens the enemy; imprisoning them is a financial loss. Enslavement neutralizes them and benefits the community.
This is a teleological (maqasid) justification.
4
Refute the Objection
The objection that enslavement is aggression is dismissed as weak. The Hanafi response shows that the alternative options are worse for the Muslims.

The Imam's Discretion Over Captives: Istirqaq in Jihad
Rationale for permissibility of enslaving captives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on enslaving captives and explain the rational proofs given for its permissibility.

Core Idea
Enslaving captives is permissible but not obligatory; the imam chooses what benefits Islam.

This passage addresses a key question of jihad: what happens to captured enemies? The Hanafi school, based on the ongoing nature of jihad, grants the imam the authority to enslave, ransom, or free them. Here we examine both the ruling and the rational defense against modern objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hukm
Read the ruling directly from the answer: 'jaza li amir al-mu'minin an yastaqriqahum' – it is permissible for the imam to enslave them. Note that it is not obligatory (laysa bilazim).
The ruling is ja'iz (permissible), not fard or sunnah.
2
Locate the Rational Proof (Dalil Aqli)
The text provides a reasoned argument: returning captives strengthens the enemy, imprisoning them burdens the treasury. Enslavement integrates them into Muslim homes, benefiting both master and slave materially and spiritually.
This is a maslaha-based reasoning.
3
Analyze the Wisdom
The passage highlights dawah benefit: the captive observes Islam, may embrace it, and history shows many slaves became scholars. This is a great favor (minnah) upon them.
Combines worldly and religious benefit.
4
Note the Choice
The imam is not forced to enslave; he may choose other options (ransom, manumission, exchange) as will be explained in the Book of Siyar.
Emphasizes flexibility.
5
Contrast with Shariah's Encouragement of Freedom
The passage ends by noting that Shariah encourages emancipation through kaffarat, tadbir, and mukatabah. This balances the permissibility of enslavement.
Avoids misunderstanding that Islam promotes slavery.

Qatl al-'Amd (Intentional Homicide)
Defines intentional homicide and its legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of qatl al-'amd and list its legal consequences (sin, qisas, diyah, no kaffarah, inheritance deprivation).

Core Idea
Intentional killing with a weapon mandates qisas and blocks inheritance; no expiation is required.

In this passage, we learn the precise definition of intentional homicide ('amd) in Hanafi law. Unlike other schools, the Hanafis require a weapon or its equivalent for an act to qualify as 'amd, which triggers qisas and other specific consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qatl al-'amd, which is haram and subject to qisas.
2
Define the instrument requirement
Note that it must be a weapon or something that effectively separates body parts, like a pointed stick or stone.
The Hanafi school is stricter than others on this point.
3
List the consequences
Consequences include sin, deprivation from inheritance, qisas unless the heirs pardon, and no kaffarah.
4
Note the absence of expiation
Unlike other categories, 'amd has no kaffarah because the sin is too severe.
Kaffarah is only for shibh 'amd and khata'.
5
Compare with other schools
The Shafi'i school considers any intentional act causing death as 'amd, regardless of the instrument.

Commentary
This passage defines the five categories of homicide in Hanafi law, a foundational topic in criminal fiqh. The first, 'amd (intentional killing), requires striking with a weapon or something that effectively separates body parts, such as a pointed stick, stone, or a type of arrow (knar). Its consequences include sin (ithm), deprivation from inheriting the victim (hurman an al-mirath), and qisas (retaliation) unless the victim's heirs pardon or accept diyah, with no kaffarah (expiation). The text then has a stray word 'يتعمد' likely part of the definition. The second type, shibh 'amd (semi-intentional), is where one intentionally strikes but with an object not normally lethal, like a small stick. Imam Abu Hanifa considers this shibh 'amd, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that if the object is heavy, it becomes 'amd. The consequence is sin and kaffarah (on both views), but no qisas; instead, a heavy diyah (diyah mughallazah) is payable by the 'aqilah (tribal guarantors). The third type, khata' (accidental), has two subtypes: error in intention (mistaking a human for game) and error in action (missing a target and hitting a human). Its consequence is kaffarah and diyah on the 'aqilah, with no sin. The fourth, ma ajra majra al-khata' (treated like accidental), covers acts like a sleeping person rolling onto someone, carrying the same ruling. The fifth, qatl sabab (killing by cause), involves creating a hazard (digging a well, placing a stone) that leads to death, requiring diyah on the 'aqilah but no kaffarah. The passage ends with a note from al-Hidayah clarifying that 'no sin' in accidental killing refers to the sin of killing specifically, though there may still be minor sin for negligence, and that the deprivation of inheritance applies only to intentional killing (with sin), not to accidental or cause-based killing.

Key Terms
قتل العمد (qatl al-'amd): Intentional killing by striking with a weapon or something that effectively separates body parts.
الحرمان عن الميراث (al-hurman 'an al-mirath): Deprivation of the killer from inheriting from the victim, applicable only in intentional killing (with sin).

Shibh al-'Amd (Semi-Intentional Homicide) and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Defines semi-intentional homicide and details the intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi definition of shibh al-'amd and describe the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the instrument.

Shibh 'amd: intentional striking with a non-lethal tool; the weight of the object can trigger disagreement on whether qisas applies.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that if the object is heavy (e.g., a large stone or log), it becomes 'amd because it is likely to cause death.
They consider the potential lethality of the object.

For the Sahibayn, shibh 'amd is striking with something that does not usually kill, not the absence of a weapon.

Qatl al-Khata' (Accidental Homicide)
Defines accidental homicide and its two subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of accidental killing (error in intention vs error in action) and state their legal consequences.

Core Idea
Accidental killing carries kaffarah and diyah on the 'aqilah, but no sin and no qisas.

Accidental homicide (khata') is divided into two subtypes: error in intention (mistaking a human for game) and error in action (missing a target and hitting a human). Both have the same legal consequences: kaffarah and diyah, but no sin. The text clarifies that 'no sin' specifically refers to the sin of murder, though some negligence may still exist.

1
Identify the two subtypes of khata'
The text mentions: (a) error in intention – e.g., shooting at what one thinks is game but turns out to be a human; (b) error in action – e.g., shooting at a target but hitting a human.
Both involve no intent to kill a human.
2
State the consequences common to both
Both require kaffarah (expiation) and diyah (blood money) paid by the 'aqilah.

Note the absence of sin
The text says 'لا مأثم فيه' (no sin in it). The commentary explains this means no sin for the killing, but there may be some sin for negligence (lack of caution).
4
Distinguish from other categories
Compare with 'amd (sin, qisas) and shibh 'amd (sin, heavy diyah). Khata' has no sin and a standard diyah.
5
Understand the role of the 'aqilah
The 'aqilah (male relatives) pay the diyah as a form of collective responsibility, not the killer individually.

Exceptions to Qisas: Father-Son and Master-Slave Relationships
States the exceptions to qisas based on familial and ownership relationships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cases where qisas is not applicable due to paternal or ownership relationships and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
Qisas is dropped for killing one's own child or slave because of strong proprietary or paternal bonds.

While qisas generally applies broadly, the Hanafi school recognizes specific exceptions where the killer is the father or the master of the victim. These exceptions are based on the elevated status of fatherhood and the nature of ownership. Similarly, if a son inherits a right of qisas against his father, the sanctity of fatherhood prevents its execution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relationships that exclude qisas
The passage lists exceptions: father killing his son, a person killing his own slave (mudabbar, mukatab), and a person killing the slave of his son.
The exception also includes the case where a son inherits qisas against his father – it drops.
2
Understand the reasoning for each exception
For father-son: due to the sanctity of fatherhood (حرمة الأبوة). For master-slave: the master owns the slave, so he would be effectively paying himself. For mudabbar/mukatab: they are still slaves with a promise of freedom, but ownership remains.
The rationale is that qisas would result in a benefit to the killer (inheritance or ownership) or conflict with inherent authority.
3
Apply to the case of inherited qisas against father
If a son inherits the right of qisas for his father's murder, he cannot execute his father. The sanctity of fatherhood overrides the right.
This is a direct application of the principle. The father's life is protected even from his son's demand for qisas.
4
Note what is not covered
These exceptions are specific; other relationships (e.g., brother killing brother, mother killing child) are not mentioned and thus fall under the general rule.
The passage does not discuss mother-son; by default, qisas would apply unless specified otherwise.

No Qisas for Child or Insane Person: Treated as Accidental Killing
States that child and insane persons are not subject to qisas; their act is accidental with diyah on the 'aqilah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a child or insane person is not subject to qisas for intentional killing and state the alternative penalty.

Core Idea
The intentional killing by a child or insane person is legally treated as accidental, so diyah is due on the 'aqilah, not qisas.

The final ruling in the passage addresses the incapacity of minors and the insane. Since they lack legal responsibility (taklif), their deliberate act is categorized as error (khata'), shifting the penalty from qisas to diyah (blood money) payable by the 'aqilah (paternal relatives). This reflects the Hanafi principle that punishment requires a responsible agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of persons: child and insane
The passage asks about a child (صبي) and an insane person (مجنون). These are individuals who do not have legal capacity (mukallaf).
The term 'child' here means before puberty; after puberty, the person becomes responsible.
2
Understand why their act is treated as accidental
The passage says 'عمد الصبي والمجنون خطأ' (the intentional act of a child and insane person is accidental). This is because capacity (taklif) is required for intentionality to be legally significant. Their actions are considered errors (khata') due to lack of accountability.
This is a legal fiction: their actual intent is ignored for purposes of qisas.
3
Identify the consequences: diyah on the 'aqilah
Instead of qisas, the penalty is diyah (blood money) payable by the 'aqilah (the paternal relatives of the killer). They bear the fine as a form of collective responsibility.
The 'aqilah typically includes male agnates. This spreads the burden.
4
Apply the principle to other cases
By analogy, any person lacking capacity (e.g., severe mental disability, intoxication not self-induced?) would be treated similarly. The Hanafi school extends this to those who are not mukallaf.
The passage is specific to child and insane, but the principle of lack of capacity can be extended.

Options for the Imam Regarding Captives

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Imam has three choices: execution, enslavement, or dhimmi status; ransoming is disputed within the Hanafi school.

The treatment of prisoners of war is a critical aspect of Islamic jurisprudence of jihad. This lesson presents the Hanafi ruling on the options available to the Imam, along with a notable intra-madhhab difference regarding ransoming captives. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the boundaries of state discretion in warfare according to Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three permissible options
Read the text to find the three actions the Imam may choose: killing, enslaving, or releasing as dhimmis. Note that all three are permissible (mubah) and the Imam has discretion (takhyir).
2
Identify the prohibited actions
The text explicitly prohibits two actions: returning captives to Dar al-Harb and unconditional release (mann). Understand the rationale: both would strengthen the enemy or harm Muslim interests.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on ransoming
Abu Hanifa prohibits ransoming (mufada'ah) captives in exchange for Muslim captives, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) permit it. Consider the basis: Abu Hanifa likely feared encouraging enemy capture, while the Sahibayn emphasized maslahah (benefit).
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sahibayn.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its application
Later Hanafi authorities adopt the Sahibayn's view due to maslahah. In practice, the Imam may exchange prisoners when it benefits Muslims.
