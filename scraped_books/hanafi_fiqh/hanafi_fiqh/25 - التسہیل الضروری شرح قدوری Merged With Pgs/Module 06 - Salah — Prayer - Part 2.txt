The Five Wajibat of Prayer
Lists the five wajibat of prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five wajibat of prayer and state the consequence of omitting each.

Core Idea
Wajibat are necessary acts in prayer; omission requires sujud al-sahw but does not invalidate the prayer.

In the Hanafi school, the acts of prayer are categorized into fard, wajib, sunnah, and others. The following passage lists five wajibat—acts that are necessary but not integral to the validity of prayer. Understanding these helps you know when sujud al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness) is needed to compensate for unintentional omission.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the five wajibat
The passage lists exactly five wajibat. Commit them to memory: recitation of Fatiha, adding a surah, preceding Fatiha, specifying recitation in the first two rak'ahs of fard prayers, and tranquility (itminan) in the pillars.
This list is from a concise matn; longer lists exist in other Hanafi works.
2
Understand the definition of wajib in Hanafi fiqh
Wajib is a level between fard and sunnah. It is established by a zanni (probable) evidence, while fard is from qati (certain) evidence. Omission of a wajib does not break the prayer.
Other schools consider Fatiha as fard; this affects the ruling on its omission.
3
Differentiate wajib from fard and sunnah
Fard: omission invalidates prayer. Wajib: omission requires compensation (sujud al-sahw) but prayer is valid. Sunnah: omission is not sinful and no compensation is required.
Intentional omission of a wajib is sinful but still does not invalidate.
4
Know the compensation for omission
If a wajib is omitted forgetfully, sujud al-sahw must be performed at the end of prayer. If omitted intentionally, the prayer is valid but sinful, and sujud al-sahw is insufficient; repentance is needed.
This compensation rule applies to all wajibat in prayer.

Commentary
This passage lists five wajibat (necessary acts) of prayer according to the Hanafi school. Unlike the fara'id, omission of a wajib does not invalidate the prayer but must be compensated by sujud al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness). The five wajibat are: (1) Recitation of Surah al-Fatiha in the first two rak'ahs of every prayer (and in all rak'ahs of non-fard prayers). (2) Reciting a surah or at least three short verses after the Fatiha. (3) Reciting the Fatiha before the additional surah/verses. (4) Specifying the recitation (i.e., reciting aloud or silently as prescribed) in the first two rak'ahs of fard prayers. (5) Tranquility (itminan) in the pillars—meaning pausing briefly in ruku', sujud, and the postures so that one is at rest. The text uses the open letter 'ه' (hā') for the number 5. This list is not exhaustive; other Hanafi sources add more, such as qa'dah as a wajib (though here it is fard), or the order of recitation. The key distinction from fara'id is that these can be compensated by sujud al-sahw if omitted forgetfully. If omitted intentionally, the prayer is still valid but sinful, and sujud al-sahw is not sufficient.

Key Terms
واجب (wajib): A necessary act in prayer that is less than fard; its omission requires sujud al-sahw if forgetful, and is sinful if intentional.
اطمئنان (itminan): Tranquility; remaining motionless for a brief moment in ruku', sujud, and standing after ruku' so that one is at rest.
سورة الفاتحة (Surah al-Fatiha): The first chapter of the Quran, considered the greatest surah. In prayer, it must be recited in every rak'ah.

Ruling on Qibla for the One in Makkah or Elsewhere
States the qibla ruling for those at Masjid al-Haram vs others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on facing the qibla for someone in the Masjid al-Haram versus someone elsewhere, and identify the condition that triggers exact facing.

Core Idea
In the Haram, face the exact Kaaba; elsewhere, direction suffices.

This lesson clarifies a crucial distinction in salah: where you pray determines how precisely you must face the qibla. The Hanafi school holds that being present in the Masjid al-Haram obligates exact alignment with the Kaaba's structure. For all other locations, including Makkah outside the Haram, facing the general direction is sufficient. Understanding this avoids common errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Find where the worshiper is: inside Masjid al-Haram or outside it.
The text uses 'hāḍiran fī al-masjid al-ḥarām' vs 'ghāʾib ʿanhā'.
2
State the ruling for each
For inside: 'lā budda min iṣābat ʿayn al-kaʿba' (must hit exact Kaaba). For outside: 'qiblatuhu jihat al-kaʿba' (his qibla is the direction).
Remember the key terms: 'ayn vs jiha.
3
Note the exception within Makkah
Even if in Makkah but not in the Haram, the ruling is direction, not exact.
This shows the ruling is based on proximity to the Kaaba, not administrative boundaries.
4
Apply the distinction
Understand that deviation in the Haram invalidates prayer; outside, ijtihad is allowed.
This has practical implications for those praying near the Haram.

جهة الكعبة (jihat al-Kaʿba): The general direction towards the Kaaba, determined by ijtihad or signs.

The Twelve Wajibat of Prayer and Their Ruling
Lists wajibat and their ruling in question-answer format

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the twelve wajibat of prayer and explain the consequence of omitting a wajib intentionally versus forgetfully.

Core Idea
A wajib omitted intentionally invalidates the prayer; if forgetful, sajdah al-sahw suffices.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between faraid (pillars) and wajibat (obligatory acts) in prayer. Wajibat are binding but a step below faraid. This lesson presents the canonical list of twelve wajibat from al-Quduri and the rule that governs their omission. Understanding this list is essential for every Muslim, especially for imams leading congregational prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the twelve wajibat
Memorize the list: each item is a distinct wajib. Group them mentally: recitation rules, actions (tranquility, sitting), utterances (tashahhud, salam, qunut, takbirs), and imam's voice levels.
The second sitting is fard; only the tashahhud in it is wajib.
2
Understand the consequence of intentional omission
If any wajib is left out deliberately, the prayer is invalid and must be repeated completely. This shows the wajib's binding nature.
3
Understand the consequence of forgetful omission
If a wajib is omitted due to forgetfulness, the prayer remains valid after performing sajdah al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness). This compensates for the lapse.
Sajdah al-sahw is two extra prostrations before the salam.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a scenario (e.g., imam recites silently in Fajr), determine whether the omission was intentional or forgetful, and state the required action.
Jahr in Fajr is a wajib (item 11).
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
Other schools may classify some of these as fard (e.g., Fatiha) or sunnah. Understand that Hanafi classification is based on the strength of evidence.

Commentary
This passage lists the twelve wajibat of prayer and explains their ruling. Wajibat are obligatory acts that are between faraid and sunan in the Hanafi school. They are established by evidence that is not as definitive as that for faraid. The key distinction: if a wajib is omitted intentionally, the prayer is invalid and must be repeated. If omitted forgetfully, sajdah al-sahw suffices to make the prayer valid. The twelve wajibat include the recitation of al-Fatihah, adding a surah or equivalent verses after it, and ensuring the order of recitation. Other wajibat are: tranquility (itmi'nān) in each rukn – though itmi'nān is itself a fard according to some, here it is listed as wajib; the first sitting (qa'dah ūlā) and the tashahhud in both sittings; the salam wording; qunut in witr; the extra takbirs in 'Eid prayers; and the imam's audible or silent recitation in specific prayers. Note that the second sitting is actually a fard (part of qa'dah akhirah), but the tashahhud in it is wajib; similarly, the first sitting is wajib, its tashahhud also wajib.

Key Terms
الاطمئنان (al-itmi'nān): Steadiness and tranquility in each physical pillar (rukn), such as pausing after movement.
القنوت (al-qunut): A special supplication recited in the witr prayer, particularly in the last rak'ah.
سجود السهو (sajdah al-sahw): Two extra prostrations performed before the final salam to compensate for forgetful omission of a wajib or other lapses.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Omitting a sunnah does not invalidate the prayer nor require sajdah as-sahw.

The prayer is built on obligations and recommendations. This passage enumerates the sunan — acts that the Prophet (peace be upon him) consistently performed but did not command as obligatory. Memorizing these is foundational for every Hanafi student. In this lesson, we will learn each sunnah, its placement in the prayer, and the ruling regarding its omission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
The passage lists 'sunan al-salah' — recommended acts. Understand that 'sunnah' here means recommended, not obligatory (wajib) or fard. Omitting a sunnah does not break the prayer nor require sajdah al-sahw.

For men: raise hands to the ears; for women: to the shoulders. Men place right hand on left under the navel; women (from other sources) place on chest. These differences are based on hadith and practice.

If a sunnah is forgotten, the prayer remains valid. There is no requirement for sajdah al-sahw. This distinguishes sunan from wajibat.

In this lesson, we will examine six specific obligatory acts (wajibat) of salah as identified by the Hanafi school. Sunan of Salah (20 Acts)
Lists twenty sunan acts of salah from takbir to salam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the twenty sunan acts of salah and identify their positions from takbir to salam.

Core Idea
Twenty sunan perfect the prayer; omitting them loses reward but not validity.

The prayer is built on pillars and obligations, but it is completed by sunan—recommended acts that bring perfection and reward. This lesson presents the twenty sunan as taught in the Hanafi school, covering actions from the opening takbir to the final salutations. Memorize them to elevate your prayer.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the sunan by phase
Group the twenty sunan into three phases: beginning (takbir to thana'), middle (ruku', sujud, sittings), and end (tashahhud, salawat, salutation intentions).
This helps organize the list for memorization.
2
Identify key acts
Note specific acts like raising hands, tasbih in ruku' and sujud, the sitting posture, pointing finger, and the intentions with salutations.
Focus on actions that are often confused with wajib or fard.
3
Distinguish roles
Understand how the sunan differ for the imam, follower, and one praying alone, especially the intentions with the two salutations.
The intentions are sunan, not wajib, but they complete the prayer.
4
Memorize using the numbering
Use the list of 21 items (though one may be a duplicate) to commit each sunan to memory. Practice writing them in order.
The source lists 21 items but states 20 sunan; item 21 may be a repetition of item 18-20.

The Four Adab of Salah
Lists four adab of salah regarding appearance and posture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four adab of salah and explain how each enhances khushū'.

Core Idea
Adab perfect the prayer without invalidating it; they are keys to khushū'.

Salah is not only about fulfilling obligations; its perfection lies in embodying recommended etiquettes (ādāb). This lesson unpacks four adab from a classical matn, teaching you how to refine your prayer through mindful posture and self-control. Mastering these details elevates your worship from valid to excellent.

Guided Path
1
Recall the first adab
The first adab concerns the hands: a man should take his palms out of his sleeves when raising them for takbīr. This ensures the forearms are visible, following the sunnah.
This applies only to men; women do not remove hands from sleeves.
2
Map the gaze positions
The second adab prescribes where to look during each posture: at the place of prostration (standing), at the top of the feet (bowing), at the tip of the nose (prostrating), at the lap (sitting), and at the shoulders (saying salām). Memorize each.
Gaze is recommended, not wajib; but it greatly aids concentration.
3
Understand suppressing cough
The third adab is to suppress coughing as much as possible. While coughing does not invalidate salah, controlling it maintains decorum.
If coughing is uncontrollable, salah remains valid.
4
Handle yawning
The fourth adab is to hold back or cover the mouth when yawning, as yawning is from Shayṭān. This prevents taking an ugly posture.
The hadith: 'Yawning is from Shaytan; when one of you yawns, let him withhold as much as possible.'

The Detailed Method of Prayer from Takbir to Sujud
Detailed method of prayer from opening takbir to sujud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the sequence of actions in prayer from the opening takbir to prostration and distinguish the rulings for the imam, follower, and individual.

Core Idea
Prayer is performed in a specific sequence: takbir, thana', ta'awwudh, basmalah, Fatihah, surah, ruku, standing, sujud.

This lesson presents the standard Hanafi method of performing prayer from its inception to the first prostration. It covers the opening takbir, the recitations, the bowing, and the prostration, with special attention to the roles of the imam and follower. Memorizing this sequence is essential for correct salah.

Guided Path
1
Open with Takbir
Stand, raise hands to ears, say 'Allahu Akbar' without prolonging. This is the opening takbir (tahrimah).
The takbir is fard.
2
Place Hands and Recite Thana'
Place right hand over left under navel. Recite the thana': 'Subhanaka Allahumma...' silently.
Thana' is sunnah; the follower does not recite.
3
Ta'awwudh and Tasmiyah
Recite 'A'udhu billahi min al-shaytan al-rajim' and 'Bismillahir Rahmanir Rahim' silently. Follower omits because he does not recite.
These are sunnah.
4
Recite Fatihah and Amin
Recite Surah al-Fatihah silently, then say 'Amin' silently even in loud prayers.
Fatihah is wajib. The follower is silent.
5
Recite a Surah
After Fatihah, recite any surah or three verses from the Qur'an.
This is sunnah.
6
Bow for Ruku
Say takbir while bending down without raising hands. Place hands on knees with fingers spread. Keep back straight and legs straight. Say 'Subhana Rabbi al-'Azim' three times (minimum).
Tasbih in ruku is sunnah; minimum once suffices but three is recommended.
7
Rise from Ruku
Rise saying 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' (imam or individual) then 'Rabbana lakal hamd' (individual). Follower says only 'Rabbana lakal hamd'.
Imam says tasmee', follower says tahmeed.
8
Prostrate
Say takbir, place knees, then hands, then forehead and nose between palms. Keep fingers together pointing to qiblah. Keep belly away from thighs. Say 'Subhana Rabbi al-A'la' three times (minimum).
Expose arms and keep toes facing qiblah.

Method of Performing Salah (Hanafi Way)
Step-by-step manual of salah actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe and perform the actions of salah step by step from takbir to taslim according to the Hanafi school, identifying each posture and recitation.

Core Idea
The Hanafi salah is a precise sequence of actions with specific postures, recitations, and roles for imam, follower, and lone worshipper.

This lesson walks you through the entire salah according to the Hanafi school, from the opening takbir to the final taslim. The text from Al-Quduri (with commentary) gives you the exact actions and recitations. Pay close attention to the distinctions between the imam, follower, and lone worshipper—these affect what is recited and by whom.

Guided Path
1
Beginning the prayer
Identify the opening takbir: said standing, hands raised to ears, no prolongation. After takbir, place right hand on left hand below the navel. Then recite thana', ta'awwudh, and basmalah silently.
The follower does not recite ta'awwudh or basmalah because he does not recite Quran behind the imam.
2
Recitation of Fatihah and surah
Recite Surah al-Fatihah, then say 'Ameen' silently (even in loud prayers). Then recite a surah or three verses. After recitation, say takbir and go into ruku' without raising hands.
Ameen is said silently in Hanafi school.
3
Ruku' and standing up
In ruku': place hands on knees with fingers spread, back flat, legs straight, head level. Say 'Subhana Rabbiyal 'Azim' three times. Then rise saying 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' (imam and lone worshipper) and/or 'Rabbana lakal hamd' (follower and lone worshipper). Stand fully upright.
The imam says only the tasmee'; the follower says only the tahmeed; the lone worshipper says both.
4
Sujud and sitting between prostrations
Go into sujud: knees first, then hands, then face between palms, fingers pressed together pointing to qiblah, nose and forehead on ground, arms raised, stomach away from thighs, toes facing qiblah. Say 'Subhana Rabbiyal A'la' three times. Then sit calmly on the left foot (iftirash) with right foot erect, hands on thighs, then prostrate again.
The sitting between prostrations must be calm and upright.
5
Second rak'ah and tashahhud
Stand for second rak'ah: like the first but without raising hands and without thana'/ta'awwudh. After second sajdah, sit in iftirash for tashahhud: recite the tashahhud of Ibn Mas'ud, point with right index finger (ring with thumb and middle finger). For a two-rak'ah prayer, send salawat, make dua, and give taslim twice. For four-rak'ah prayers, stand after tashahhud for third rak'ah without raising hands or reciting thana'/ta'awwudh.
The Hanafi tashahhud uses specific wording from Ibn Mas'ud. The pointing finger is a sunnah.

Completing the Prayer: Two, Three, and Four Rak'ahs
Enumeration of actions after tashahhud for different rak'ah counts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the correct sequence for completing prayer after tashahhud, depending on the number of rak'ahs intended.

Core Idea
After tashahhud, the prayer's completion branches by rak'ah count: for two, send blessings and salam; for more, stand without extra invocations.

The tashahhud is a pivot point in prayer. What comes next depends on the number of rak'ahs the worshipper intended at the start. This lesson clarifies the distinct actions for two-rak'ah (Fajr), three-rak'ah (Maghrib), and four-rak'ah (Dhuhr, Asr, Isha) prayers, as detailed in the Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Determine the intended rak'ah count
The worshipper's intention at takbirat al-ihram determines the prayer's structure.
The intention is mental; no verbal declaration needed.
2
If two rak'ahs: after tashahhud
Send blessings upon the Prophet, make a supplication using Quranic or Sunnah phrases, then give salam twice, right then left.
The salam should be intended for those on each side (imam, worshippers, angels).
3
If four rak'ahs: after first tashahhud
Stand for the third rak'ah without raising hands, without reciting thana or ta'awwudh. Complete the third and fourth rak'ahs like the first two, then sit for final tashahhud, blessings, supplication, and salam.
The final sitting is similar to the first tashahhud sitting (iftirash) according to the text.
4
If three rak'ahs (Maghrib): after first tashahhud
After the third rak'ah's tashahhud, send blessings, supplicate, and give salam; there is no standing after tashahhud.
Maghrib has only one tashahhud after the third rak'ah.

Ruling on Prostrating on a Fold of Turban or Excess Garment
Question and answer regarding sujud on a turban fold or garment excess

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prostrating with a fold of turban on the forehead, distinguish between valid and invalid scenarios, and list the conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Prostration valid but disliked if fold is on forehead; invalid if forehead doesn't contact ground.

This lesson addresses a common practical question: what if a worshipper prostrates while a fold of the turban or an extra piece of clothing lies between the forehead and the ground? The Hanafi school distinguishes between two scenarios based on the location of the fold and the presence of an excuse. We will examine the ruling, the conditions for validity, and the legal reasoning behind the dislike.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is prostration (sujud) while a fold of the turban or excess garment is on the forehead. The general ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) but slightly disliked (makruh tanzihan) unless there is an excuse such as extreme cold or heat.
Focus on the key term 'kur al-'imamah' (fold of turban).
2
Distinguish between two cases
Case 1: The fold is on the forehead (between forehead and ground). Case 2: The fold is only on the top of the head, not touching the forehead. In case 1, sujud is valid with karahah; in case 2, it is invalid because the forehead did not contact the ground.
The decisive factor is the location of the fold relative to the forehead.
3
Recognize the conditions for validity
For valid sujud with a fold on the forehead, three conditions must be met: (a) the fold is on the forehead (covering it wholly or partially), (b) the place of prostration is pure (tahir), and (c) the worshipper feels the firmness (hajm) of the ground through the barrier.
The third condition is often neglected by people.
4
Understand the exception of excuse
If there is a legitimate excuse (e.g., extreme weather, illness), the dislike is removed and the prostration becomes permissible without karahah.
The excuse must be genuine and not habitual.
5
Apply the ruling to practical scenarios
Use the conditions to evaluate common situations: for example, prostrating on a thick folded prayer rug or on a sleeve. Check if the forehead contacts the barrier and if the barrier is on the forehead.
The modern equivalent might be prostrating on a cushion or pillow.

The Obligation Levels of Recitation in Prayer
Obligation levels of recitation in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the rulings of recitation in prayer as fard, wajib, or sunnah and state the exceptions for the third and fourth rak'ahs.

Core Idea
Absolute recitation is fard, Fatihah is wajib, but in the third and fourth rak'ahs, Fatihah becomes sunnah.

This lesson focuses on the hierarchy of recitation obligations in the Hanafi school. You will learn that while reciting something in every rak'ah is obligatory, the specific rulings for al-Fatihah and a surah vary by rak'ah, and exceptions exist for the later rak'ahs of obligatory prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three levels of obligation mentioned
In the passage, find the terms 'fard', 'wajib', and 'sunnah' applied to recitation. Note that absolute recitation is fard, while Fatihah and a surah are wajib.
Remember the Hanafi distinction: fard is proven by qat'i evidence, wajib by zanni.
2
Note the exception for third and fourth rak'ahs
The passage explicitly says that in the third and fourth rak'ahs of obligatory prayers, recitation of al-Fatihah is sunnah, not fard or wajib.
This is a Hanafi-specific ruling; other schools may hold differently.
3
Understand the hierarchical relationship
Absolute recitation is fulfilled by either reciting Fatihah or a surah/three verses. Both are wajib, but you can choose one.
This shows the Hanafi view that the wajib elements are interchangeable for the fard requirement.
4
Recognize that specifying recitation in first two rak'ahs is wajib
The passage states that it is wajib to specify recitation in the first two rak'ahs of obligatory prayers.
This means you must recite something specific (Fatihah + surah) in those rak'ahs.
5
Note that preceding Fatihah over subsequent recitation is wajib
The order is wajib: al-Fatihah must be recited before any additional surah.
If you accidentally recite the surah first, you should repeat Fatihah and then the surah; sajdah al-sahw may be required if done intentionally.

The Follower's Recitation: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Ruling on follower's recitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the follower does not recite behind the imam and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the follower is completely silent behind the imam, even in silent prayers.

This lesson focuses on one of the most distinctive Hanafi positions: the follower (muqtadi) must not recite anything behind the imam, whether in audible or silent prayers. This differs from the Shafi'i school, which requires the follower to recite al-Fatihah behind the imam. We will examine the evidence and the practical implication.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Point 8 of the passage: 'لا يقرأ المقتدي خلف الإمام، لا في الصلاة الجهرية، ولا في السرية'. Memorize this.
This applies to all prayers, including silent ones.
2
Examine the hadith evidence
The passage ends with a reference to a hadith from Muslim: 'When he recites, be silent.' This is the primary proof for the Hanafi position.
The hadith is general, covering both jahr and sirr.
3
Understand the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school holds that the follower must recite al-Fatihah because it is fard for everyone. They rely on other hadith that command recitation of al-Fatihah.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the hadith 'When he recites, be silent' abrogates or specifies the general command to recite al-Fatihah for the follower.
The Hanafi school gives priority to the command of silence.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice, a Hanafi follower does not recite anything, while a Shafi'i follower recites al-Fatihah silently. This affects the follower's concentration and the imam's responsibility.
If a Hanafi follower recites, their prayer may be considered makruh or invalid according to some.

Sunnah Lengths of Recitation and Additional Rulings
Sunnah lengths and additional recitation rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah lengths of surahs for different prayers and recite the rulings on basmalah, ta'awwudh, ameen, woman's recitation, and the non-specification of surahs.

Core Idea
Sunnah recommends long surahs in Fajr and Zuhr, medium in Asr and Isha, short in Maghrib; basmalah and ameen are not recited aloud.

This lesson covers the recommended lengths of recitation (mufassal surahs) for different prayers and several additional rulings: the silent recitation of basmalah and ta'awwudh, the silent saying of ameen, the complete silence of women in prayer, and the fact that no specific surah is required for any prayer.

Guided Path
1
Learn the mufassal classification
The Quran from Surah al-Hujurat to the end is divided into long (طوال), middle (أوساط), and short (قصار). Memorize the boundaries: long from Hujurat to Buruj, middle from Tariq to Bayyinah, short from Zilzal to end.
This classification is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
2
Recall the sunnah lengths for each prayer
For resident: Fajr and Zuhr → long mufassal; Asr and Isha → middle; Maghrib → short. Traveler may choose any length.
These are sunnah, not wajib.
3
Review the rulings on audible supplications
Basmalah (بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم) and ta'awwudh (أعوذ بالله من الشيطان الرجيم) are not recited audibly. Similarly, ameen is not said audibly by the imam or follower.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools differ on ameen.
4
Note the ruling for women
A woman praying alone does not recite audibly even in audible prayers; she must recite silently.
This is based on the principle of modesty.
5
Understand that no surah is mandatory
There is no specific surah that must be recited in any prayer. Any surah is permissible. The Hanafi school does not require a particular surah for any rak'ah.
This contradicts some common practices that associate certain surahs with certain prayers.

Muqtadi's Silence and Imam's Recitation: Rulings and Proof
Rulings on recitation with proof for muqtadi's silence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the muqtadi's recitation behind the imam, cite the hadith proof, and apply the ruling to distinguish between wajib and sunnah recitation modes.

Core Idea
Muqtadi must stay silent behind the imam; the imam must recite aloud in specific prayers.

This lesson unpacks the key rulings on recitation (qira'ah) in prayer as presented in the classical Hanafi text. You will learn why the follower (muqtadi) does not recite at all behind the imam, supported by a direct prophetic command. Additionally, we will cover the imam's obligation to recite aloud or silently in different prayers, the sunnah lengths of recitation, and several related questions that clarify common scenarios. The central proof is a hadith narrated by Muslim that forms the basis of the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling on the muqtadi
Read the statement: 'لَا يَقْرَأُ الْمُقْتَدِي خَلْفَ الْإِمَامِ لَا فِي الصَّلَاةِ الْجَهْرِيَّةِ وَلَا فِي السِّرِّيَّةِ'. This clearly states that the follower does not recite any verses behind the imam, whether the prayer is audible or silent.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position of the Hanafi school.
2
Analyze the hadith proof
The author cites the hadith: 'وَإِذَا قَرَأَ فَأَنْصِتُوا' (When he recites, be silent). The command 'أنصتوا' (be silent) is an imperative, which in Hanafi usul indicates obligation (wujub). The muqtadi is therefore obliged to remain silent during the imam's recitation.
The hadith is classified as ahad (single-chain), thus its proof level is zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalalah, making the ruling wajib rather than fard.
3
Determine the imam's recitation modes
The passage specifies that the imam must recite aloud (يَجْهَرُ) in Fajr, Jumu'ah, the two Eids, and the first two rak'ahs of Maghrib and Isha. In all other rak'ahs, he recites silently. This is based on the established practice of the Prophet (sunnah mutawatirah).
The ruling is also wajib because it is established by continuous practice.
4
Learn the sunnah recitation lengths
For the imam and the one praying alone (munfarid), it is sunnah to recite specific sections of the Quran: longer surahs from al-Mufassal (Surah al-Hujurat to al-Buruj) in Fajr and Zuhr, medium surahs (al-Tariq to al-Bayyinah) in Asr and Isha, and short surahs (al-Zalzalah to end) in Maghrib. The traveler is exempt.
These are recommended, not obligatory; omitting them does not invalidate the prayer.
5
Review the additional rulings on Basmalah, Amin, and women
The passage answers common questions: the Basmalah and Ta'awwudh are recited silently even when the imam recites aloud; the word 'Amin' is said silently by both imam and follower; a woman praying alone does not recite aloud even in audible prayers. No specific surah is obligatory in any prayer.
These clarify that silence or softness is preferred in these acts.

Next, the imam's recitation modes are detailed: he must recite aloud in Fajr, Jumu'ah, Eid prayers, and the first two rak'ahs of Maghrib and Isha; he recites silently in Zuhr, Asr, the third rak'ah of Maghrib, and the last two of Isha. The person praying alone (munfarid) may choose whether to recite aloud or silently in prayers where the imam would recite aloud. It is sunnah for both imam and munfarid to recite specific lengths of the Mufassal surahs: longer sections (tiwal) for Fajr and Zuhr, medium sections (awsat) for Asr and Isha, and short sections (qisar) for Maghrib. The traveler is exempt from this and may recite arbitrarily. Several questions follow: The Basmalah and Ta'awwudh are recited silently even when the recitation is aloud; the word 'Amin' is also said silently by both imam and follower; a woman praying alone does not recite aloud even in audible prayers. Then it is clarified that no specific surah is obligatory for any prayer, but it is disliked to limit oneself to a single surah. The passage ends with the definition of the sections of al-Mufassal: tiwal from al-Hujurat to al-Buruj, awsat from al-Tariq to al-Bayyinah, qisar from al-Zalzalah to the end of the Quran.

Rulings of Witr Prayer: Method, Recitation, and Congregation
QA format rulings on Witr recitation, Qunut, and congregation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi method of Witr prayer, including the sequence of actions, recommended surahs, and rulings on Qunut and congregation.

Core Idea
Witr prayer: third rak'ah recitation, takbir with hands raised, quiet Qunut, then ruku'.

Witr prayer is a emphasized prayer in the Hanafi school, considered wajib. This lesson presents the detailed rulings on how to perform Witr, from the recommended surahs to the timing and delivery of Qunut. We will also cover when Witr is prayed in congregation and how the imam recites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act described is the Witr prayer, specifically the third rak'ah. In the Hanafi school, Witr is wajib (obligatory) based on the continuous practice of the Prophet and Companions.
Witr is not fard but wajib; the difference is in the strength of the proof.
2
Extract the method of the third rak'ah
From the text: after reciting al-Fatihah and a surah, one says takbir (Allahu Akbar) while raising hands to the ears, then recites the Qunut, then says takbir for ruku' and completes the rak'ah normally.
This sequence is specific to the third rak'ah; the first two rak'ahs are like regular prayer.
3
Identify the sunnah recitations
It is sunnah to recite Surah al-A'la in the first rak'ah, Surah al-Kafirun in the second, and Surah al-Ikhlas (plus optionally al-Mu'awwidhatayn) in the third.
Other surahs may be recited, but these are recommended.
4
Review the proofs for the method
The text provides multiple narrations from Ibn Mas'ud and others confirming the sequence: takbir then Qunut before ruku'. These are ahad hadiths with zanni thubut but qati dalala for the Hanafi school.
The raising of hands in the takbir for Qunut is also supported by narrations.
5
Apply additional rulings
Qunut is recited quietly even by the imam. It is sunnah to pray Witr in congregation during Ramadan after Tarawih. The imam recites aloud in all three rak'ahs. Qunut is not recited in other prayers except for a calamity (nazilah), then after ruku'.
These rulings distinguish the Hanafi practice from other schools.

Number of Rak'ahs in Nafl Prayers per Taslimah: Day and Night
First Q&A on number of rak'ahs for nafl prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the maximum rak'ahs per taslimah for day and night nafl, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on night nafl.

Core Idea
For day nafl, up to 4 rak'ahs per taslimah; for night nafl, the relied-upon view is 2, though Abu Hanifa allowed up to 8.

This lesson examines a key Q&A from the Hanafi fiqh text addressing the number of rak'ahs permissible in supererogatory prayers with one taslimah. It draws a clear distinction between day and night nafl and highlights an important intra-madhhab difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on the night prayer. Understanding this dispute is crucial for correctly applying the school's relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act is nafl (voluntary prayer). The ruling distinguishes between day (nahar) and night (layl) nafl. Recognize that the general hukm of nafl is sunnah mu'akkadah, but once started it becomes wajib to complete.
2
State the ruling for day nafl
The text states it is permissible to pray 2 or 4 rak'ahs per taslimah. Exceeding 4 is makruh (disliked). This is the unanimous Hanafi position.
Note: 'Makruh' here is tanzihi (slightly disliked), not tahrimi.
3
State Abu Hanifa's ruling for night nafl
Imam Abu Hanifa permits up to 8 rak'ahs with one taslimah, but exceeding 8 is makruh.
His evidence: general permissibility and hadith on night prayer.
4
State the Sahibayn's ruling for night nafl
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that night nafl should not exceed 2 rak'ahs per taslimah. They consider it impermissible to combine more than 2.
Their evidence: practice of the Prophet (PBUH) and to avoid resembling witr.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical application
Later Hanafi authorities, such as al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah, prefer the view of the Sahibayn. Thus, the relied-upon position for fatwa is 2 rak'ahs per taslimah for night nafl.
One may follow Abu Hanifa’s view if there is a need, but the school's tarjih is for 2.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A missed fard prayer must be prayed as soon as remembered, except at sunrise, zenith, and sunset.

This lesson covers a fundamental ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the obligation to make up a missed obligatory prayer immediately upon recall, while also respecting the three times when any prayer (including qada') is prohibited. The source passage is a concise question-answer format from a classical matn. Understanding this rule is essential for daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is 'qada' al-fawa'it' (making up missed prayers). The ruling: it is obligatory (fard) to perform the missed prayer as soon as one remembers it.
The obligation is independent of whether the miss was intentional or forgetful, though sin differs.
2

The exception is the three prohibited times (al-awqat al-thalatha): sunrise, zenith (sun at meridian), and sunset. During these, qada' is delayed until after the time passes.
These times are the same for all prayers, including voluntary ones.
3
Apply the rule
If someone remembers a missed fard prayer during a prohibited time, they must wait until the time ends and then pray it immediately.
No delay beyond the prohibited time is allowed.
