Preemption Voided by Settlement for Compensation
Ruling on settling preemption for compensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a preemptor settles his preemption right for compensation and explain why the settlement is void.

Core Idea
Preemption cannot be sold or traded; a settlement for compensation is void and the compensation must be returned.

When a person has the right of preemption (shuf'a), he may be tempted to give it up in exchange for money. This lesson clarifies that such a transaction is completely invalid in the Hanafi school. The right of preemption is a personal right, not a commodity, so any attempt to sell it results in the preemption being void, and the compensation received must be given back.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
The preemptor makes a settlement (ṣulḥ) by accepting compensation to relinquish his right of preemption.
The compensation can be money, goods, or any valuable.
2
State the Ruling
The preemption becomes void (bāṭil), and the preemptor must return the compensation he took.
The voidance is immediate and the compensation is considered unlawfully taken.
3
Understand the Rationale
Preemption is a personal right established to protect a co-owner from harm; it is not property and cannot be sold or exchanged for value.
This is based on the principle that rights not intended for transfer cannot be traded.
4
Contrast with Gratuitous Waiver
If the preemptor simply waives his right without compensation, the ruling may differ (not covered in this lesson).
The presence of compensation is the key factor that makes the settlement void.

Core Idea
Selling the property that gives preemption before judgment extinguishes the right.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi law of preemption (shuf'a). If a preemptor disposes of his own property that qualifies him for preemption before the court awards it, his right becomes void. Understanding this clarifies the connection between the property and the right.

The preemption becomes void (bāṭil). The preemptor loses his right to claim the property.

The right of shuf'a is attached to the specific property; selling that property severs the attachment and eliminates the basis for the right.
This prevents the preemptor from profiting from both the sale and the shuf'a.

Agency and Guarantee Effects on Preemption
Rulings on agency and guarantee in preemption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between different agency scenarios and explain how guaranteeing darak affects preemption rights.

Core Idea
An agent's preemption is lost if he sells as agent for himself, but preserved if he buys for another; a guarantor of darak loses preemption.

This lesson covers three nuanced scenarios involving preemption: an agent who is also a preemptor selling the property entrusted to him, a buyer's agent who himself is a preemptor, and a person who guarantees the seller against defects (damān al-darak). Each scenario yields a different ruling based on the extent of the person's involvement in the transaction.

Guided Path
1
Scenario A: Agent sells as representative
A man appoints an agent to sell his house. The agent is himself a preemptor. He sells the house (including his own preemption right). The ruling: his preemption is lost because the sale was completed from his side (i.e., he is the seller's representative).
The agent's intention to preserve his right is irrelevant; the act of selling extinguishes it.
2
Scenario B: Agent buys for his principal
A buyer's agent (who is also a preemptor) buys the house for his principal. He claims preemption for himself. The ruling: he retains his preemption because he is buying for another, not for himself. His agency does not void his own right.
Buying for another is not considered a forfeiture of one's own preemption.
3
Scenario C: Guarantor of darak (seller's liability)
A man guarantees to the buyer that the seller will cover any defects (darak). After the sale, this man claims preemption. The ruling: his preemption is void because the sale was completed from his side in the sense that his guarantee made him part of the transaction.
The guarantee creates a stake in the contract, thus preventing him from claiming a separate preemption right.

Rulings on preemption when sale includes option for seller or buyer

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Preemption requires transfer of ownership; seller's option delays it, buyer's option does not.

Preemption (shuf'ah) is a right to purchase a property that has been sold, but this right is not absolute when the sale includes an option (khiyar). This lesson clarifies when the preemptor can and cannot claim his right based on the type of option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of option
Determine whether the option belongs to the seller (khiyar al-bā'i‘) or the buyer (khiyar al-mushtarī). This classification determines the effect on ownership transfer.
2
Analyze the effect on ownership
For seller's option: ownership remains with the seller. For buyer's option: ownership transfers to the buyer, subject to the buyer's right to return.
3
Apply the preemption rule
Preemption is only valid when ownership has transferred from the seller to the buyer. Based on the ownership status, decide if the preemptor can claim.
4
Consider the second scenario
When the seller drops his option, the sale becomes binding, ownership transfers, and preemption becomes permissible.
5
Summarize the principle
The key principle is that khiyar al-bā'i‘ prevents transfer of ownership, while khiyar al-mushtarī does not. Preemption follows the ownership status.

Preemption in an Invalid Sale
Ruling on preemption in a fasid sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why preemption is not immediately available in a fasid sale and state the condition under which it becomes obligatory.

Core Idea
Preemption requires a valid transfer of ownership; a fasid sale does not transfer ownership until the right to rescind is dropped.

Preemption (shuf'a) allows a neighbor or partner to replace the buyer in a property sale. But what if the original sale was invalid (fasid)? Does preemption still apply? This lesson clarifies that preemption is suspended until the invalid element is rectified and the parties waive their right to rescind.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is a purchase of a house via a fasid (invalid) sale. The immediate ruling is 'no preemption' (la shuf'ata).
The fasid sale lacks a condition for validity but can be rectified.
2
Understand the right to rescind
Each contracting party in a fasid sale has the right to rescind (haqq al-faskh). This option prevents full ownership transfer.
This right is a consequence of the sale's invalidity.
3
Identify when preemption becomes obligatory
If both parties drop their right to rescind (asqata al-faskh), the sale becomes effective, and preemption becomes wajib (obligatory) for the preemptor.
The preemptor's right attaches only after the sale is enforceable.
4
Apply the underlying principle
Preemption is tied to complete ownership. In a fasid sale, ownership is not fully transferred until the defect is removed (here by dropping rescission).
This aligns with Hanafi emphasis on ownership certainty.

Additional Shuf'ah Rulings: Waiver, Partition, and Multiple Pre-emptors
Fatwa rulings on waiver, partition, and multiple pre-emptors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the circumstances under which a pre-emptor's right revives after waiver, distinguish between sale and partition regarding shuf'ah, and calculate the distribution among multiple pre-emptors.

Core Idea
Waiver of shuf'ah does not revive after court-ordered return, but does after mutual rescission; partition is not a sale; multiple pre-emptors share equally per capita.

The source text presents three additional rulings on shuf'ah: the effect of a pre-emptor's waiver when the buyer later returns the property, the exclusion of partition from triggering shuf'ah, and the method of distributing the property among multiple pre-emptors. These rulings clarify important boundaries of the pre-emption right.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the effect of waiver and return
Examine the scenario: the pre-emptor waived his right, then the buyer returned the property to the seller. The text distinguishes between court-ordered return and non-judicial return. If the return is by court order (due to defect or option), the waiver stands and the pre-emptor's right does not revive. If the return is without a court order or by mutual rescission (taqayul), the waiver is considered void, and the pre-emptor's right returns.
The reasoning is that a court-ordered return cancels the sale ab initio, but the pre-emptor's waiver was on the original sale; a mutual rescission is a new transaction, so the pre-emptor gets a fresh opportunity.
2
Understand partition's exclusion from shuf'ah
Partition (qismah) is the division of jointly owned property into separate shares. The text explicitly states that no shuf'ah arises for a neighbor from partition because partition is not a sale; it is merely a separation of existing rights. Shuf'ah only attaches to transfers of ownership through sale or equivalent contracts.
This is a key distinction: shuf'ah is a right to substitute in a sale, not a general right to acquire shares in any transaction.
3
Distribute among multiple pre-emptors
When a property has multiple pre-emptors and they all want to exercise their right, they divide the property equally among themselves (per capita), regardless of their original ownership shares. The example illustrates: if three co-owners own half, third, and sixth respectively, and the half-owner sells his share, the other two pre-emptors each get an equal portion of the sold half, not proportional to their original shares. The share of the owner of the sixth is not reduced.
The rationale is that the right of shuf'ah is personal to each pre-emptor, and they share equally as a group of entitled individuals.

Shuf'ah – Legal Stratagems (Hiyal) to Avoid Pre-emption and the Dispute on Their Disapproval
Rulings on payment method, hilah to avoid shuf'ah, and intra-Hanafi dispute on disapproval

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state two legal stratagems for avoiding shuf'ah and differentiate between the positions of Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on their disapproval.

Core Idea
Using a legal stratagem to avoid shuf'ah is permissible according to Abu Yusuf but disapproved by Muhammad—a key intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Jawharah presents two practical stratagems (hiyal) that sellers and buyers can employ to circumvent the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). It also includes a ruling about payment method: when a buyer pays with a garment, the pre-emptor takes the property for the original price, not the garment. The passage then raises a crucial moral question: is it permissible to use such stratagems? The Hanafi school is divided, with Abu Yusuf permitting them and Muhammad considering them makruh. Understanding this dispute is essential for knowing which rulings are operative in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the payment ruling
When a buyer pays with a garment instead of cash, the pre-emptor takes the house for the original price (thaman) not the garment, because the garment was acquired through a separate contract.
This establishes that the pre-emptor's right attaches to the price agreed in the original sale.
2
Extract the first hilah: excluding a small portion
The seller sells the house except for a small area (e.g., one cubit) along the boundary adjacent to the pre-emptor. Since the sold property is no longer fully contiguous with the pre-emptor's land, the pre-emptor's right is void.
This works by destroying the condition of ittisal (contiguity) required for shuf'ah.
3
Extract the second hilah: purchasing in stages
The buyer first purchases a small share (e.g., one-tenth) of the property, then later purchases the remaining shares. The pre-emptor's right attaches only to the first share because in the second purchase the buyer is already a partner and thus has priority over the neighbor.
This stratagem uses the rule that a partner in the property has a stronger right of pre-emption than a neighbor.
4
Analyze the karahah dispute
Imam Muhammad holds that using such hiyal is disapproved (makruh) because shuf'ah was established to repel harm (li-daf' al-darar); permitting hiyal would nullify this purpose. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that it is not disapproved because the hilah merely prevents the right from being established, which is not considered harm.
The disagreement is based on the concept of darar: does preventing a potential right constitute harm?
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf is that those who follow Abu Yusuf may use such hiyal without sin, while those who follow Muhammad should avoid them. The passage does not specify a mufta bihi (operative view), so the fatwa may depend on the context and later juristic preference.
Later Hanafi fatwa often leans towards Abu Yusuf in matters of muamalat, but the ikhtilaf remains valid.

If the agent separates before taking possession, the contract is void.

In this short passage, the author addresses a practical question: can a principal appoint an agent to execute a ṣarf (currency exchange) or salam (advance payment sale) contract? Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh when the settled amount is a delayed payment of the same debt versus when it involves a change in amount, currency, or timing, and explain the underlying principle.

Core Idea
Sulh on a debt is permissible only as partial forgiveness, not as a new exchange triggering riba or sarf.

This passage addresses common scenarios of sulh (settlement) where a debtor and creditor agree to modify the terms of an existing debt. The rulings distinguish between permissible postponement or partial forgiveness and impermissible transactions that resemble riba or violate currency exchange rules. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi fiqh in debt settlements.

Guided Path
1

For each case, identify the current status of the debt (prompt or deferred) and the proposed settlement terms (amount, timing, currency).
The four cases vary these parameters.
2

Determine whether the settlement is a pure postponement (tajil) of the same right, or a new exchange (mu'aowada) that could involve riba or sarf rules. Only pure postponement or partial forgiveness (without additional benefit to the creditor) is permissible.

Core Idea
If a term was set, lender compensates for depreciation; if no term, borrower removes at own cost.

Borrowing land (ariyah) is a revocable contract. A key question arises when the borrower has built or planted and the lender revokes. This passage from al-Hidayah lays out the standard Hanafi position: compensation depends on whether a term was agreed. It also notes an alternative view from al-Hakim al-Shahid and a practical calculation method from al-Kifayah.

1

2
Analyze the term-with-compensation scenario
When a term was set and the lender revokes early, the lender must compensate the borrower for the decrease in value of the building/planting caused by removal.
Compensation is for the depreciation (intiqāṣ) between the value at term and after removal.
3
Analyze the no-term scenario
If no term was fixed, the lender can force removal of the building/planting at the borrower's own expense, with no compensation.
The borrower assumed the risk by building without a term.

Debts of the Ma'dhun and the Dispute on Master's Ownership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on master's ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on debts of a ma'dhun slave and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the master's ownership of the slave's assets when debts exceed value.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun slave's debts attach to his person; when debts exceed his total value, Abu Hanifa suspends the master's ownership, but the Sahibayn affirm it.

In this lesson, we examine two critical rulings concerning a ma'dhun (permitted slave) who engages in trade and incurs debts. First, we learn that his debts are secured against his very person (raqabah), allowing creditors to have him sold. Second, we encounter a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on whether the master retains ownership of the slave's assets when debts surround both wealth and person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the first Q&A: debts of the ma'dhun are attached to his person (raqabah). The slave can be sold for creditors unless ransomed. Proceeds are distributed proportionally; remaining debt follows after freedom.
This rule is not disputed among the Hanafi imams.
2
Understand the second issue
The second Q&A introduces a condition: when debts 'surround' (exceed) both the slave's wealth and personal value. The question is whether the master owns the assets in the slave's hand.
The dispute arises only when debts exceed total value.
3
Isolate Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the master does not own those assets. Consequently, if the master frees a slave owned by the ma'dhun, the emancipation is invalid.
This view prioritizes creditors' rights over the master's property.
4
Isolate the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the master does own the assets even if debts are overwhelming. Therefore, the master can validly free the ma'dhun's slave, though creditors may still pursue their claims.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is that of the Sahibayn for practical stability.

Ruling on Hijr of the Ma'dhun Slave
Ruling on hijr and its effect on price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the master's interdiction of a ma'dhun slave and explain the consequence of delivering the sold item before receiving the price.

Core Idea
The master's interdiction renders the slave interdicted immediately; a price not received becomes void since a master cannot owe his own slave.

This lesson concludes the discussion on the ma'dhun slave. It addresses what happens when the master places an interdiction (hijr) on the slave, and also clarifies the legal implication of delivering the sold item before payment: the price is void and the master can reclaim the item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master placing an interdiction (hajr) on his ma'dhun slave. The ruling is that the slave becomes interdicted (mahjur) – he loses his capacity to trade.
The ruling is straightforward: the hijr is effective immediately.
2
Understand the consequence of delivery before payment
If the slave delivers the sold item before receiving the price, the price becomes void (batil). This is because the price would become a debt owed by the slave to the master, but a master cannot have a debt against his own slave.
The voidance does not cancel the sale entirely; it means the price cannot be claimed.
3
Analyze the legal rationale (ta'lil)
The rationale is that the master cannot be a creditor to his own slave. Therefore, if the slave delivers the item without receiving the price, it is as if the sale was without consideration – the sale is not permissible.
The voidance of the price means voidance of its delivery and claim, not voidance of the sale itself.
4
Identify the remedy
The master has the right to take back the sold item (istirjā' al-mabī'). This protects the master's interest.
This remedy follows from the voidance of the price.

Guardian's Permission for Minor to Trade
Ruling on guardian granting trade permission to minor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a guardian's permission for a minor to trade and identify the condition for its effectiveness.

Core Idea
A minor can trade effectively if his guardian permits it and he understands buying and selling.

This short Q&A establishes a key principle in Hanafi sales law: a minor is not entirely barred from trade. With the guardian's permission and sufficient understanding, a minor's transactions become valid. The ruling analogizes the minor to a permitted slave (ma'dhun), clarifying his legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the guardian granting permission to a minor to engage in trade. The ruling: this permission is valid (sahih).
The validity of the permission is unconditional.
2
Determine the effect of permission
Once permission is given, the minor's status in buying and selling becomes analogous to a permitted slave (ma'dhun): he can transact on his own.
Analogies like this help expand legal capacity in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Identify the condition for effectiveness
The minor's transaction is effective (nafidh) only if he understands buying and selling (ya'qilu al-bay'a wa al-shira'a). This means he comprehends the basic nature of exchange.
This condition distinguishes a discerning minor (mumayyiz) from a non-discerning one.

This condition ('ta'qil al-bay' wa al-shira') ensures that the minor has at least basic comprehension of exchange, similar to the concept of discerning minor (mumayyiz) in other contexts.

Foundling's Property and Finder's Spending Authority
Ruling on foundling's ownership and finder's spending limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that money found with a foundling belongs to the foundling and identify the extent of the finder's guardianship to spend on necessities only.

Core Idea
A foundling owns money found on him; the finder may only spend on essentials, not dispose of property.

In this lesson, we address the first two questions of the foundling passage. The Hanafi school affirms that a child, despite lacking management capacity, is fully capable of ownership (ahl al-milk). Therefore, any money found with the foundling is his property, not the finder's. The finder's role is limited: he may spend from that money only on the child's necessities, such as food and clothing. He cannot marry the foundling off or dispose of the property in a general sense.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal capacity of the foundling
Understand that even a child is ahl al-milk (capable of ownership).
2
Determine ownership of found property
Money found tied to the foundling is his property, not the finder's.
3
Distinguish between full and limited guardianship
The finder has wilayat al-infaq (spending guardianship) but cannot marry off or dispose of property.

Commentary
This passage covers several aspects of the finder's (multaqit) authority over the foundling and his property. First: If money is found on the foundling, it belongs to the foundling because the child is capable of ownership (ahl al-milk) and the money is in his possession. The finder cannot take it. Second: The finder does not have the power to marry off the foundling (since marriage requires a guardian (wali) and the finder is not the child's legal guardian in that capacity) nor to dispose of the foundling's property in a general sense. However, the finder does have limited guardianship (wilaya) to spend on the child's maintenance (nafaqa) from his money and to purchase necessities like food and clothing.
