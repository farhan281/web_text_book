Highway Robbery: Execution and Imam's Options
Two scenarios: killing without theft and killing with theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for highway robbers who kill (with or without taking property) and explain the imam's three options when both crimes are committed.

Core Idea
When highway robbery involves killing, the punishment is execution as a hadd; if property is also taken, the imam may choose among three harsh penalties.

This lesson completes the Hanafi treatment of highway robbery by examining the gravest scenarios: killing the victim without taking property, and the combined crime of killing and theft. In the latter case, Islamic law grants the imam a choice among three severe punishments, reflecting the serious threat to public security.

Guided Path
1
Scenario: killing without property
If the robbers kill a person but do not take property, the imam executes them as a hadd. This is a fixed punishment; forgiveness by the victim's heirs does not waive it.
The hadd for murder here is distinct from qisas; it is a penalty for the crime of highway robbery resulting in death.
2
Irrelevance of victim's family's forgiveness
Even if the heirs of the murdered victim forgive the killer, the punishment is not dropped. This is because the hadd is a right of Allah, not a private right.
In qisas (retaliation for murder), the heirs can forgive or accept blood money; here they cannot.
3
Scenario: killing and taking property
If the robbers both kill and take property, the imam has a choice among three punishments: (1) amputate hands and feet crosswise then execute or crucify, (2) execute only, (3) crucify only.
The imam may choose based on the severity of the crime and public interest.
4
The three options explained
Option 1 combines hadd amputation for theft with execution for murder, plus crucifixion as additional deterrence. Options 2 and 3 are simpler but still severe. All options are valid; the imam's ijtihad decides.
Crucifixion here means displaying the body after execution as a warning.
5
Fixity of the punishments
These are all hadd punishments; the imam cannot reduce them below the given options. The choice is only among the three, not outside them.
The severity reflects the gravity of highway robbery involving murder.

Core Idea
Crucifixion in Hanafi law is a swift death by spear, limited to three days.

Crucifixion (taslib) is one of the prescribed punishments for highway robbery in Islamic law. The Hanafi school provides specific details on how this punishment is to be carried out. This lesson examines the method and duration as outlined in classical Hanafi texts, focusing on the requirement to kill the condemned swiftly by piercing the abdomen and the prohibition of leaving the body beyond three days.

This limit ensures the punishment does not become excessive.

Effect of Non-Liable Persons on Hadd in Highway Robbery
Ruling on hadd when robbers include minor, insane, or relative of victim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a highway robbery group includes a minor, insane person, or blood relative, and explain the internal Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on whether the hadd drops for all.

Core Idea
Presence of a non-liable robber drops the hadd for all — shubha spares the group.

Highway robbery (hirabah) is a grave offense punishable by a fixed hadd. But what if the robber group includes a minor, an insane person, or a close relative of the victim? These individuals cannot be subject to the hadd due to incapacity or familial impediments. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains the Hanafi ruling: the entire group escapes the fixed penalty, and the case becomes a matter of private retribution (qisas). However, there is an internal disagreement within the school, with Abu Yusuf holding a different view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The passage asks what happens when among highway robbers there is a minor, an insane person, or a relative of the victim.
2
State the main ruling
According to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, the hadd drops from all remaining robbers; the victim's heirs have the option to demand qisas or pardon.
3
Understand the reasoning
The presence of a non-liable person creates a legal doubt (shubha) that prevents the hadd from being applied to the entire group.
4
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf holds that if the sane members directly committed the killing, the hadd still applies to them; the presence of the non-liable does not shield the others.
5
Note the practical difference
Under the majority view, all get the benefit of doubt; under Abu Yusuf, if the adults actually killed, they still receive the hadd.
6
Additional note on collective liability
The passage also confirms that if only one robber directly kills, the entire group is liable for execution (qisas), because the act is collective.

Core Idea
In highway robbery (hirabah), the entire group bears equal criminal responsibility — the murder of any one is the murder of all.

This short ruling from a classical Hanafi Q&A addresses a critical question: if multiple robbers attack, but only one actually kills, should the death penalty apply to the entire group? The answer is a decisive yes. This rule reflects the unique severity of hirabah as a collective crime against public security, where the act of one is attributed to all. Understanding this principle clarifies the Hanafi approach to liability in organized violent crime.

Recognize that the severity of hirabah warrants collective responsibility. The group's joint intention to rob and terrorize makes all members liable for any murder committed during the crime, regardless of who actually struck the blow.

Hadd Punishments for Highway Robbers (Qutta' al-Tariq)
States punishments for highway robbers in three scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of highway robbery and their respective hadd punishments as per the Hanafi school, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of minors or insane members on the punishment of the group.

Core Idea
Highway robbers face three distinct hadd punishments depending on whether they kill, take property, or both.

Highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) is a severe crime against public order, distinct from ordinary theft. The Hanafi school prescribes fixed punishments (hadd) that vary based on the robbers' actions: imprisonment if caught before any crime, amputation if they take property without killing, execution if they kill without taking property, and a range of options if they do both. This lesson unpacks these rulings and also examines an important intra-Hanafi disagreement about the effect of a minor or insane person among the robbers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the crime and its three main scenarios
Highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq) are those who go out intending to cut the road, capable of resistance. The passage presents three scenarios based on what they actually do: take property only, kill only, or both kill and take property. A fourth scenario is being caught before any act.
The scenario of being caught before any property or killing leads to imprisonment until repentance.
2
Learn the hadd punishment for each scenario
For property taken (reaching nisab of 10 dirhams per person): amputation of right hand and left foot. For killing only: execution as hadd, pardon by heirs ineffective. For both killing and taking property: Imam has discretion (amputate then execute/crucify, or execute only, or crucify only). Note the manner of crucifixion.
The amputation must be from opposite sides (right hand and left foot).
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the presence of a minor or insane person
The passage records a dispute between Abu Hanifa (with Zufar) and Abu Yusuf. Abu Hanifa holds that if a minor, insane person, or a relative of the victim is among the robbers, the hadd is dropped for all, and the case becomes qisas for the heirs. Abu Yusuf holds that only the actual killer's hadd is dropped if he is the minor/insane; the sane adults still receive hadd.
This dispute has practical consequences: under Abu Hanifa, the entire group escapes hadd, while under Abu Yusuf, the sane adults remain liable.
4
Examine the joint liability rule
Even if only one of the robbers personally killed the victim, the ruling of execution applies to all of them. This is because they are considered a single group acting together.
This applies only to the execution punishment; amputation for property is individual? The text implies joint liability for killing.
5
Understand the importance of nisab and capability
The hadd for property-based highway robbery requires that each robber's share amounts to 10 dirhams or equivalent, similar to the nisab for theft. Additionally, the robbers must be 'mumtani' (capable of resistance), which distinguishes highway robbery from ordinary theft.
This condition ensures that the hadd is only applied for organized, serious robbery.

Coercion to Zina and the Hadd Penalty: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Question-answer on coerced zina hadd penalty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hadd for coerced zina and explain why the Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
When coercion negates consent, the Hadd penalty is dropped according to the relied-upon Hanafi view.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the application of the fixed punishment (hadd) for zina when the act is committed under coercion. The question strikes at the heart of criminal liability: does coercion remove culpability entirely, or does the gravity of the sin override duress? Abu Hanifa distinguishes between coercion by the sultan and coercion by others, while his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue for a blanket exemption. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists weigh intent, consent, and the role of authority in criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the legal question
The passage concerns a person who is coerced (ikrah) to commit zina and whether the hadd punishment applies. The act is zina (unlawful sexual intercourse), and the ruling is about the hadd penalty.
Zina is a hudud crime; hadd is the fixed punishment (stoning or lashes).
2
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Abu Hanifa holds that hadd is obligatory unless the coercer is the sultan (state authority). The rationale is that coercion by a private individual does not create sufficient doubt (shubha) to lift the hadd, but coercion by the sultan is overwhelming and negates free will.
The sultan's coercion is considered more absolute, creating a shubha that drops the hadd.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that hadd is not obligatory under any coercion, regardless of who the coercer is. For them, coercion negates consent entirely, and hadd requires consent.
This view is based on the principle that hadd is lifted by shubha (doubt), and coercion itself creates doubt.
4
Analyse the difference in reasoning
The divergence hinges on whether coercion by non-sultan creates a valid shubha. Abu Hanifa sees only the sultan's coercion as sufficiently overwhelming; the Sahibayn see any coercion as undermining free will.
This is a dispute about the threshold of coercion required to create doubt.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah) prefer the Sahibayn's view: no hadd in all cases of coercion. This is the relied-upon ruling for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the principle of avoiding hadd in cases of doubt (shubha).

If he dies or is killed upon his apostasy, what he acquired during Islam transfers to his Muslim heirs, and what he acquired during his apostasy is fay' placed in the public treasury.
5- If he joins the abode of war as an apostate, and the judge rules his joining, his mudabbars and ummahat awlad become free, his debts are discharged, what he acquired during Islam transfers to his Muslim heirs, and the debts that were binding on him during Islam are paid from what he acquired while Muslim, and what debts were binding on him during apostasy are paid from what he acquired during apostasy.
6- Whatever he sold, bought, or disposed of from his properties during his apostasy is suspended. If he becomes Muslim, his contracts become valid. If he dies, is killed, or joins the abode of war, they become void. And if he returns to the abode of Islam as a Muslim after the judge's ruling of his joining, he takes whatever of his property he finds in the hands of his heirs as it is.
The female apostate, if she disposes of her property during her apostasy, her disposition is permissible.
An apostate or one who died in his apostasy takes it? The author of Al-Hidayah said: Because the heir only inherits from him due to his disengagement; but if he returns as a Muslim, he needs it, so he is given precedence over him. Unlike if the heir removed it from his ownership, and unlike his ummahat awlad and mudabbars; because the judgment is with a valid proof and cannot be overturned. And if he comes as a Muslim before the judge rules that, it is as if he never ceased to be Muslim.

Commentary
This passage is a chapter on the rulings of apostates (murtaddin) from the Hanafi fiqh manual. It begins with a question and answer format, instructing the reader to memorize the following rules. The initial commentary quotes Al-Hidayah regarding why Muslim free persons are not owned by the enemy, linking back to the previous section.

The key rulings are:
1. If a male apostates (ridda), he is first asked to repent and given three days in prison. If he returns to Islam, fine; otherwise he is executed.
2. If someone kills the apostate before the Islamic law is presented to him, it is disliked (makruh) but no retaliation (qisas) or blood money (diyah) is due, because the apostate's life is not protected.
3. A female apostate is not killed, but imprisoned indefinitely until she repents. This is a specific Hanafi position.
4. The apostate's property rights are suspended (mawquf). If he returns to Islam, his property reverts. If he dies or is killed upon apostasy, what he earned during Islam goes to his Muslim heirs; what he earned during apostasy becomes fay' (booty) for the Muslim treasury.
5. If the apostate joins dar al-harb and the judge confirms this, his mudabbars and ummahat awlad become free, his debts are cleared, and his Islamic earnings go to his heirs. Debts are paid from the appropriate earnings.
6. Contracts made during apostasy are suspended; they are valid if he returns, void if he dies or joins the enemy. If he returns after the judge's ruling, he can reclaim any property still in the hands of his heirs.
The text concludes with a note that the female apostate's property transactions are valid, and a clarification from Al-Hidayah about an apostate's property if he returns before the judge's ruling: it is as if he never left Islam.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A person who abandons Islam, either through explicit declaration or act of disbelief.
فئ (fay'): Property acquired from non-Muslims without war, or in this context, property of an apostate earned during apostasy that becomes public treasury.
حكم بلحاقه (hukm bi-lahiqihi): The judge's ruling confirming that the apostate has joined the enemy (dar al-harb).
