Death of the Pledgor: Execution of the Pledge
Procedural ruling on sale of pledge after pledgor's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the process of recovering a debt from a pledge when the pledgor dies, and identify the roles of the executor and the judge.

Core Idea
When the pledgor dies, the pledge must be sold to pay the debt; surplus goes to the heirs.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: the pledgor (debtor) dies before the debt is repaid. The pledge remains as security, and the debt becomes a liability on the estate. The Hanafi ruling clarifies the proper procedure: the executor sells the pledge, repays the debt, and any surplus is inherited by the heirs. If no executor exists, the judge appoints one to ensure the pledgee's rights are protected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger event
The ruling applies when the pledgor dies before the debt secured by the pledge is fully paid.
The pledge remains as security even after death.
2
Determine who conducts the sale
If the pledgor left an executor (waṣī), that executor is responsible for selling the pledge. If there is no executor, the judge (qāḍī) appoints one.
The pledgee does not sell the pledge directly.
3
State the purpose of the sale
The sale is solely to repay the debt. The proceeds are used to satisfy the claim of the pledgee.
4
Identify the disposition of any surplus
If the sale proceeds exceed the debt amount, the surplus becomes part of the deceased's estate and is distributed among the heirs according to inheritance laws.
This ensures the pledgee does not gain more than what is owed.

The pledgee does not automatically own the pledge; it must be sold through proper legal channels.

Acknowledgment of a Brother: Limited Inheritance Effect
Ruling statement on limited effect of brother acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of a brother after the father's death and explain the limited effect on inheritance.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of a brother does not prove lineage but entitles him to share in the acknowledger's inheritance portion.

In Hanafi inheritance law, a person may acknowledge another as his brother after their father's death. However, this acknowledgment does not establish full lineage (nasab) for all legal purposes. Instead, it creates a limited right: the acknowledged person shares the inheritance from the acknowledger's own share. This lesson unpacks the precise legal effect and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Act
Focus on the act: a son acknowledges another as his brother after the father's death. Note that the father is no longer alive to confirm or deny.
The acknowledgment is made by an heir (the son) after the father's death.
2
Distinguish Two Legal Effects
The text gives two outcomes: 1) Lineage (nasab) is not established. 2) The acknowledged person shares in inheritance. Understand that lineage affects many rights: inheritance, marriage prohibitions, guardianship. The sharing is limited only to this particular estate and only from the acknowledger's share.
Nasab is a comprehensive status; inheritance sharing is a partial financial right.
3
Identify the Legal Basis for Sharing
The sharing is not from the father's estate directly, but from the son's (acknowledger's) share. The son is treated as if he is giving a portion of what he inherited to the acknowledged brother. This is based on the principle that a person's acknowledgment is binding on himself, not on others.
The father's estate is distributed among true heirs first; the acknowledged brother only gets from the son's portion.
4
Apply to a Scenario
Suppose the father leaves an estate of 100 units, and the only surviving child is the acknowledging son (other heirs? assume no other children). The son inherits all. If he acknowledges a brother, he must give half (assuming a brother would have taken half if proven) to the acknowledged person from his own share? Actually, the text says 'shares with the acknowledger in the inheritance' – meaning they split the share the acknowledger would have received as if the brother were a true heir. In this case, the son would give half of what he received? But careful: the Hanafi position is that the acknowledged person receives a share equal to what he would have gotten if proven as a brother, but from the acknowledger's portion. So if the son is the sole heir, he gives half to the acknowledged brother.
The precise calculation depends on other heirs; the principle is that the acknowledger bears the cost.
5
Memorize the Core Maxim
The ruling can be summarized: 'إقرار المرء على نفسه جائز، ولا يتعدى إلى غيره' (A person's acknowledgment is valid against himself, but does not extend to others). Thus, lineage is not established against the father, but the acknowledger's own share is affected.
This maxim is foundational for many chapters of Hanafi fiqh.

The ruling: this acknowledgment does not prove the brother's lineage (nasab) in terms of legal rights like inheritance from the father or mutual inheritance between the two as full brothers. However, the acknowledged person does share the inheritance of the father with the acknowledger, but only from the acknowledger's share, not as an heir of the father directly. This is a specific legal mechanism: the acknowledger is treated as if he is giving a portion of his own inheritance to the acknowledged one, not that the acknowledged one becomes a true heir. The ruling follows the Hanafi principle that a person's acknowledgment of another's lineage is only effective regarding himself, not others, and does not retroactively establish lineage from the deceased father.

Sulh with an Heir: Tangible Estate
Ruling on sulh with tangible assets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of sulh with an heir when the estate consists of tangible assets (real estate, goods, gold/silver) and identify the conditions for validity in each scenario.

Core Idea
For tangible estates, sulh is valid regardless of amount, but mixed gold/silver requires payment exceeding the heir's share of that type.

This lesson examines the permissibility of a settlement (sulh) where the heirs buy out one co-heir from the estate using a portion of the estate itself. The answer distinguishes between different types of tangible assets: real estate, movable goods, gold, and silver. Understanding these distinctions is critical for estate administration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the estate type
First, determine whether the estate consists wholly of tangible assets (real estate, goods, gold, silver) or includes debts. This lesson covers tangible estates only.
Tangible assets include 'urud (goods) and 'aqar (real estate).
2
Apply the corresponding ruling
If the estate is real estate or goods, sulh is valid regardless of the amount given. If it is all silver and they give gold (or vice versa), it is also valid.
No further condition needed.
3
Check the special condition for mixed metals
If the estate contains both gold and silver (and possibly other items), the payment to the heir must exceed his share of that particular type. His share of that type is considered exchanged for its equivalent, and the excess covers the rest of his inheritance.
Without this excess, the sulh may be invalid due to ribawi exchange.

السؤال رجل مات وله ورثة فصالحوا أحدهم بمال أعطوه وأخرجوه من التركة، هل يجوز هذا الصلح؟
الجواب: في ذلك تفصيل فاجمع خاطرك واسمع:

Question: A man died leaving heirs, and they settled with one of them for a property they gave him and removed him from the estate, is this sulh permissible? Answer: In that is detail, so gather your mind and listen: 1) If the estate is real estate or goods, this sulh is permissible whether what they gave him is little or much. 2) If the estate is silver and they gave him gold, or it was gold and they gave him silver, it is also permissible. 3) If the estate comprises gold, silver, and other items, and they settled with him for gold or silver, then what they give him must be more than his share of that type, so that his share is for its like, and the excess is due to his right from the rest of the inheritance. 4) If the estate is a debt owed by people, and they settle with one of them for a property on condition that they remove him from his share of the debt and the debt becomes theirs, then this sulh is void. However, if they stipulate that the debtors be absolved of the debt and that they do not revert to them for the share of the one settling, then the sulh is permissible.
Source pages p. 286

Commentary
This is a detailed ruling on the permissibility of sulh (settlement) with one heir, giving him some property from the estate to remove him from his share. The answer provides four distinct scenarios. First, if the estate consists of real estate or movable goods (urud), the sulh is valid regardless of the amount given. Second, if the estate is exclusively silver or gold, and they give him the other metal, it is also valid. Third, if the estate is a mix including both gold and silver, then the sulh is valid only if the amount given to the heir exceeds his share of that particular type, because his share from that type is considered exchanged for its equivalent, and the excess is for the rest of his inheritance. Fourth, if the estate is a debt owed by third parties, then the sulh is void if they give him property to relinquish his share and take over the debt themselves, because this resembles a sale of debt with property which is problematic. However, if they stipulate that the debtors are absolved and will not be pursued, then the sulh becomes permissible. This ruling shows the importance of the nature of the estate in determining the validity of sulh.

Key Terms
تركة (tarka): The estate left by the deceased, including all assets and debts.
عروض (urud): Movable goods or chattels, as opposed to real estate (aqar) or currency.
دين على الناس (dayn 'ala al-nas): Debts owed by third parties to the deceased.
غرماء (ghurama): Debtors (i.e., those who owe the debt).

Key Takeaway
For tangible estates, sulh with an heir is valid, but when gold or silver is involved, the payment must exceed the heir's share of that type to avoid riba.

Sulh with an Heir: Debt-Based Estate
Ruling on sulh with debt-based estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling that sulh with an heir is void if the estate consists solely of debts owed by third parties, unless the stipulation of absolution is included.

Core Idea
Sulh over a debt estate is void because it resembles a sale of debt for property, but it becomes valid if the debtors are absolved.

This lesson covers the fourth scenario where the estate is not tangible assets but debts owed by other people. Such a sulh is inherently problematic because it involves trading a debt for property, which is subject to strict rulings. However, a condition can salvage it.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the case: debt estate
Identify if the estate consists entirely of debts owed by third parties (dayn 'ala al-nas). This is distinct from tangible assets.
Debts here are receivables, not physical property.
2
Apply the default ruling: void
The default ruling is that such a sulh is void (batil) because it is akin to selling a debt for property, which is not permissible in Hanafi fiqh.
The reason: uncertainty and potential riba.
3
Check for the salvaging condition
If the heirs stipulate that the debtors are absolved (i.e., they release the debtors from liability and agree not to pursue them), then the sulh becomes permissible.
This condition transforms the transaction into a valid settlement.

Inheritance of the Ambiguous Hermaphrodite: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the inheritance share of an ambiguous hermaphrodite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance share of an ambiguous hermaphrodite under Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
An ambiguous hermaphrodite inherits as a female until proven male; the Sahibayn adopt a compromise averaging male and female shares.

This passage presents a classic faraid dispute within the Hanafi school. When an heir's gender is uncertain, should we assign a default or split the difference? Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correct estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and scenario
The case involves a deceased leaving a son and an ambiguous hermaphrodite. The hermaphrodite's gender is unknown at the time of distribution.
The hermaphrodite is called 'khuntha mushkil'.
2
State Abu Hanifa's default-female rule and the example
Abu Hanifa treats the hermaphrodite as a female for inheritance unless proven otherwise. In the example, the son receives two shares and the hermaphrodite one share (i.e., 2/3 and 1/3).
The exception 'unless proven otherwise' means if evidence of maleness emerges, the male share applies.
3
State the Sahibayn's compromise rule and compute the shares
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that the hermaphrodite gets half a male's share plus half a female's share. In this scenario: male share (if both male) = 1/2 each; female share = 1/3 for hermaphrodite. Hermaphrodite gets (1/2)*(1/2) + (1/2)*(1/3) = 1/4 + 1/6 = 5/12; son gets 7/12.
The Sahibayn's approach averages the two possibilities.
4
Compare the practical differences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, shares are 2/3 son, 1/3 hermaphrodite. Under Sahibayn, shares are 7/12 son, 5/12 hermaphrodite. The difference affects the distribution.
The disparity is due to whether the hermaphrodite is treated as female or as an intermediate.
5
Identify the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The later Hanafi school adopts the view of the Sahibayn as the operative position because it is more precautionary in dealing with the ambiguity.
This tarjih is well-established in works like al-Hidayah and Radd al-Muhtar.

The Hermaphrodite Receiving a Male Share via Asabah – First Example
First faraid example from the text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance distribution for a deceased woman leaving a husband, both parents, and a child hermaphrodite, and explain why the hermaphrodite receives the residuary share.

Core Idea
A hermaphrodite may inherit the residue (asabah) that belongs to a male child.

In this example from al-Jawharah, we see a case where under Abu Hanifa's default of treating a hermaphrodite as female, the actual share received is the male child's asabah share. This occurs because the prescribed shares of the husband and parents do not exhaust the estate, leaving a residue that goes to the child as asabah.

Guided Path
1
List surviving heirs
Identify the heirs: husband, father, mother, and child hermaphrodite. Note that the child is ambiguous in gender.
The hermaphrodite is treated as female for entitlement defaults, but share assignment may differ.

The sequence ensures that essential obligations are fulfilled before any inheritance is allocated. Understanding this order is critical for correctly applying the rules of inheritance (faraid).

Guided Path
1
Identify the first step
Funeral expenses (kafan and preparation) are the first claim on the estate. They must be reasonable without extravagance or stinginess.
2
Pay off debts
All debts of the deceased are cleared from the remaining property after funeral expenses.
Debts include loans, unpaid dues, and any financial obligations.
3
Execute bequests
Bequests (wasiyya) are fulfilled from up to one-third of what remains after debts. If the bequest exceeds one-third, it requires heir consent.
The one-third is calculated after deducting funeral and debts.
4
Distribute to primary heirs
Fixed-share heirs (as-hab al-fara'id) receive their prescribed Quranic portions first.
Examples: 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, etc.
5
Proceed to subsequent heir categories
After fixed shares, the remainder goes to asabah (agnatic residuaries). If no asabah, the remainder is returned (radd) to fixed-share relatives proportionally. Then dhawi al-arham (uterine relatives), then mawla al-muwalah, then the legatee of all property, and finally bayt al-mal (public treasury).
Each category is considered only if the previous one is absent or exhausted.

Defining Hajb (Exclusion from Inheritance)
Definition and classification of hajb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hajb, distinguish between hajb hirman and hajb nuqsan, and identify examples of each.

Core Idea
Hajb is the exclusion of an heir by another; it can be total (hirman) or partial (nuqsan).

In the previous lesson, we learned about fixed-share heirs and agnates. Now we examine the rules that prevent some heirs from inheriting altogether or reduce their shares. Understanding hajb is essential for correct estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Define hajb linguistically and technically
Linguistically, hajb means prevention. Technically, it is the exclusion of an heir from inheritance due to the presence of another heir.
2
Differentiate between the two types
Hajb hirman is total exclusion: the heir receives nothing. Hajb nuqsan is partial reduction: the heir's share is decreased from a larger fixed share to a smaller one due to the presence of a particular heir.
Example: the husband's share is reduced from 1/2 to 1/4 when there is a child (nuqsan). A grandfather excludes full brothers completely (hirman).
3
Identify the key distinction between hajb and 'adam al-irth
Hajb is exclusion by a living heir; 'adam al-irth means the heir never inherits due to a lack of relationship or impediment.
For instance, a non-Muslim relative does not inherit due to difference of religion, not by hajb.

Core Idea
Hajb in Hanafi faraid reduces shares or excludes totally depending on the presence of stronger heirs.

Inheritance distribution in Islamic law is precise. The Hanafi school categorizes 'hajb' (blocking) into two types: reduction (nuqsan) and total exclusion (hirman). Inheritance of Two Paternal Cousins with One as Maternal Brother
Ruling for dual-relation heir in faraid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the shares when a deceased leaves two paternal cousins, one of whom is also a maternal brother.

Core Idea
A person who is both a paternal cousin and a maternal brother takes both shares: fard as a sibling and asabah as a cousin.

This lesson presents a specific case in Hanafi faraid where a single heir holds two distinct relationships to the deceased. As a maternal brother, he is entitled to a prescribed sixth; as a paternal cousin, he shares the remainder equally with the other cousin. Understanding this dual role is essential for correct distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their relationships
List the surviving heirs: two paternal cousins (sons of paternal uncle). One of them is also a maternal brother (uterine brother).
Paternal cousins are asabah bi-nafsihim; maternal brother is a prescribed sharer (fard).
2
Apply the fixed share for the maternal brother
The maternal brother receives his Quranic fard of 1/6 of the estate.
This is based on Quran 4:12 for uterine siblings when there is no descendant.
3
Distribute the remainder via asabah among the paternal cousins
After removing 1/6, the remaining 5/6 is divided equally between the two paternal cousins, as they are both asabah of the same degree.
The maternal brother also shares in this asabah portion because he is also a paternal cousin.
4
Combine the shares for the dual heir
The maternal brother receives 1/6 (fard) + 5/12 (half of remainder) = 7/12 of the total estate. The other paternal cousin receives 5/12.
This demonstrates that a single person can inherit in multiple capacities.

Full Brother Excluded When Fard Shares Exhaust Estate
Ruling where full brother is excluded by exhaustion of estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate inheritance shares for husband, mother, uterine siblings, and full brother, and explain why the full brother receives nothing.

Core Idea
When prescribed shares exhaust the estate, the full brother (asabah) receives nothing.

In this faraid case, the estate is completely consumed by the fard shares of three categories of heirs: husband, mother, and uterine siblings. The full brother, though a residuary heir, gets nothing because no residue remains. This illustrates the principle of istiqraq (exhaustion of the estate by prescribed shares).

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and their categories
List all heirs: husband (fard), mother or grandmother (fard), uterine siblings (fard), full brother (asabah). Note that uterine siblings are fard only if no child or agnatic descendant exists.
Uterine siblings are considered 'children of the mother'.
2
Apply Hajb (Exclusion) rules
Check if any heir is excluded. Here, no heir is fully excluded by another. The full brother is not excluded by presence of uterine siblings; he remains asabah.
Hajb hirman (total exclusion) does not apply here.
3
Assign prescribed shares
Husband gets 1/2 (because no child). Mother gets 1/6 (because there are siblings). Uterine siblings (more than one) get 1/3 collectively.
If only one uterine sibling, share would be 1/6.
4
Sum the shares to check residue
Compute total: 1/2 + 1/6 + 1/3 = 6/12 + 2/12 + 4/12 = 12/12 = 1. The entire estate is consumed.
No residue remains for the asabah.
5
Conclude the full brother's share
Since no residue, the full brother inherits nothing. This is the ruling: asabah get what remains after fard shares, and if nothing remains, they get nothing.
Some schools argue otherwise, but the Hanafi position is as stated.

Inheritance When Death Order is Unknown (Drowning/Collapse)
Rule for simultaneous death inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for inheritance when the order of death is unknown in a common disaster.

Core Idea
When death order is unknown, each deceased's estate goes only to their living heirs.

Imagine a father and son drown together. Who inherits from whom? The Hanafi school addresses this with a clear rule: if multiple relatives die in a common disaster and the order of death cannot be determined, no one inherits from the other deceased. Each estate is distributed only to heirs who are certainly alive. This lesson explains the rule and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disaster and the heirs
Determine that the deaths occurred in a common event (e.g., drowning, building collapse) and list all deceased persons and their potential heirs.
2
Assess knowledge of death order
Confirm that the order of death is unknown. If any evidence (e.g., one was heard crying out later) exists, that evidence is used; otherwise, the rule applies.
Hanafi requires yaqin (certainty) for inheritance; speculation is not enough.
3
Apply the rule to each estate
For each deceased person, treat their estate as if they died alone. Distribute it to their living heirs only, excluding the other deceased.
The other deceased is treated as non-existent because they are not proven to have survived.
4
Consider cross-madhhab differences
Note that the Shafi'i school may allow inheritance if there is evidence of survival by an instant. Hanafi strictly requires proof.
This difference affects distribution in ambiguous cases.
