Revocation of Agency Requires Agent's Knowledge
Ruling on revocation of agency and its effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that revocation of agency takes effect only when the agent is informed, and prior actions remain valid.

Core Idea
An agent's authority continues until he learns of revocation; his prior actions bind the principal.

The final Q&A clarifies when revocation of agency becomes effective. In Hanafi law, simply revoking agency in private is not enough—the agent must actually know. Until the agent receives notice, his actions on behalf of the principal are valid and legally binding on the principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Principal revokes the agent; (a) agent knows, (b) agent does not know.
2
State the rulings
(a) If the agent knows, his subsequent actions are not permissible (لا يجوز). (b) If he does not know, he remains agent and his actions are valid (جائز).
Source: 'إن عزله الموكل وبلغه عزله لا يجوز تصرفه، فإن لم يبلغه العزل فهو على وكالته'.
3
Understand the legal effect
Actions before knowledge are attributed to the principal (يقع التصرف على موكله).
The agent's acts bind the principal until the agent is informed.

Commentary
This passage presents three distinct rulings on agency (wakalah). The first ruling: a mere claim of being an agent, even if believed by the depositary, does not obligate the depositary to hand over the deposit. The agent must provide proof of agency. The second ruling: if an agent sells an item and then personally guarantees the price to the buyer, this guarantee is null and void (bāṭil) because it would result in the claimant and the claimed being the same person (the agent would be both demanding payment and obligated to pay). The third ruling: revocation of agency is only effective when the agent is informed. If the agent acts before knowing of the revocation, his actions are valid and bind the principal. This is based on the principle that agency is a contract that requires mutual knowledge of termination.

Hawalah: Definition, Conditions, and Recourse
Defines hawalah, its conditions, and the creditor's recourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hawalah, state its three conditions of validity, and explain when the creditor can revert to the original debtor.

Core Idea
Hawalah transfers a debt, freeing the original debtor, unless the creditor's right is lost.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling on hawalah (transfer of debt). The text defines hawalah, sets its conditions, and clarifies the creditor's position once the transfer is complete. You will learn the precise legal effects and the one exception where the creditor may revert.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Hawalah is the transfer of a debt from one person's liability (dhimma) to another's. It applies only to debts, not physical objects or rights.
2
Identify the three parties and their consent
The muhil (original debtor), muhtal (creditor), and muhtal 'alayh (new debtor) must all consent for the hawalah to be valid.
The text mentions 'consent of the two parties' but explains three – this is a verbal nuance covering all involved.
3
Understand the default effect
Upon completion of hawalah, the muhil is free from the debt. The muhtal can only claim from the muhtal 'alayh.
4
Note the exception for recourse
The muhtal may revert to the muhil only if his right is lost (tawa), i.e., if the new debtor becomes insolvent or denies the debt and the muhtal cannot collect.
Tawa (يتوى) means perishing or loss.

Question: Is the consent of both parties required for its validity? Third, it explains the legal effect: once hawalah is completed, the original debtor is discharged, and the creditor cannot revert to claiming from him unless his right is lost (tawa), which refers to the inability to collect from the new debtor due to insolvency or denial. This establishes the general principle that hawalah is a binding transfer.

How Tawa (Loss) is Established in Hawalah: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Defines tawa and presents an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios for tawa according to Abu Hanifa, explain the third scenario added by the Sahibayn, and apply the difference to a practical hawalah case.

Core Idea
Tawa occurs by denial with oath or death insolvent; Sahibayn add judge's declaration of insolvency.

In hawalah, the creditor's right to revert to the original debtor depends on 'tawa' – the loss of the right to collect from the new debtor. This short passage defines tawa and reveals a practical difference between Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle real-world debt transfers.

Guided Path
1
Define tawa in the hawalah context
Identify the legal term 'tawa' and its meaning: the creditor's inability to collect from the new debtor, which permits recourse to the original debtor.
Tawa is a condition, not a right itself.
2
List Abu Hanifa's two scenarios
From the text, write down the two situations that establish tawa according to Abu Hanifa: (1) the new debtor denies the hawalah and swears, and the creditor has no evidence; (2) the new debtor dies insolvent.
Note that in the first scenario, the creditor lacks proof to counter the denial.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's addition
Find the third scenario added by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: the judge declares the new debtor insolvent during his lifetime.
This scenario gives the creditor a chance to revert even before the debtor's death.
4
Contrast the two views
Compare the positions: Abu Hanifa restricts tawa to two final events (denial under oath or death), while the Sahibayn also allow it upon a judicial declaration of insolvency.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that under the Sahibayn, the creditor can reclaim earlier.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text does not choose, but later Hanafi fatwa often adopts the Sahibayn's view for its comprehensiveness. Identify which view is more protective of the creditor.
In practice, the mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position, as it offers an additional remedy.

This passage explains the concept of 'tawa' in the context of hawalah. Tawa means loss or destruction of the creditor's right to collect. When tawa occurs, the creditor (muhtal) can revert to the original debtor (muhil). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad add a third scenario: a judge's declaration of the new debtor's insolvency during his lifetime. This difference of opinion is practical: under the Sahibayn's view, the creditor can reclaim even before the debtor's death if the judge declares him bankrupt.

Muhil's Claim Rejected When Sued by Muhtal 'Alayh
Ruling on dispute between muhil and muhtal 'alayh after hawalah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when the muhtal 'alayh sues the muhil and the muhil claims the hawalah was for a debt owed to the muhtal 'alayh, and identify the basis for the ruling.

Core Idea
The muhil's contrary claim is not accepted; the hawalah act itself proves his debt to the muhtal.

In hawalah, once the transfer is made, the rights of the parties become fixed. A dispute may arise when the new debtor (muhtal 'alayh) demands payment from the original debtor (muhil), and the muhil claims the hawalah was actually for a debt he owed the new debtor. This lesson examines how the Hanafi school resolves such a conflict.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
The muhtal 'alayh (new debtor) sues the muhil (original debtor) for the amount of the hawalah. The muhil claims he made the hawalah because he owed the muhtal 'alayh, meaning the new debtor was actually his creditor.
2
Note the muhil's defense
The muhil says, 'You have no claim against me because I made the hawalah for a debt that I owed you.' This would mean the muhtal 'alayh was the original creditor and the hawalah was a payment of that debt.
3
State the ruling
The muhil's statement is not accepted, and he is liable to pay the like amount to the muhtal 'alayh.
The text says: 'لا يقبل في ذلك قول المحيل، وكان عليه مثل الدين'.
4
Understand the rationale
The hawalah itself is evidence that the muhil owed the muhtal (the original creditor), not the muhtal 'alayh. Accepting the muhil's claim would contradict the clear legal effect of the hawalah transaction.
The act of hawalah transfers the debt from muhil to muhtal; the muhil's claim would negate that transfer.

Source Text

السؤال طالب المحتال عليه المحيل بمثل مال الحوالة فقال المحيل: ليس لك علي شيء؛ لأني أحلت بدين لي كان عليك، هل يقبل قوله؟ الجواب: لا يقبل في ذلك قول المحيل، وكان عليه مثل الدين. السؤال: وإن طالب المحيل المحتال بما أحاله به وقال : إنما أحلتك لتقبضه لي، وأنكره المحتال وقال: أحلتني بدين لي كان عليك، كيف يقضى بينهما ؟ الجواب: القول في ذلك قول المحيل مع يمينه.
Question: The muhtal 'alayh (new debtor) sued the muhil (original debtor) for the like of the amount of the hawalah, and the muhil said: You have no claim against me because I made the hawalah for a debt that I owed you. Is his statement accepted? Answer: The statement of the muhil is not accepted in that, and he is liable for the like of the debt. Question: And if the muhil sued the muhtal (creditor) for what he transferred to him, and said: I only made you a transferee to collect it for me, and the muhtal denied and said: You transferred to me for a debt you owed me. Commentary
This passage presents two scenarios of conflicting claims in hawalah. First, when the new debtor sues the original debtor after the hawalah, and the original debtor claims he made the hawalah because he owed the new debtor (i.e., the new debtor was actually his creditor). The original debtor's claim is rejected; he must pay the debt again. This is because the hawalah itself is evidence that the original debtor owed the creditor (muhtal), not the new debtor. Second, when the original debtor sues the creditor claiming the hawalah was only for collection (i.e., the creditor was an agent), and the creditor claims it was for a debt owed to him. Here, the original debtor's statement with oath is given preference. The difference is that in the first case, the original debtor's claim contradicts the clear act of hawalah; in the second case, the nature of the hawalah is ambiguous, so the original debtor's oath is accepted.

Sulh with Acknowledgment: Sale or Hire?
Ruling statement classifying sulh with iqrar as sale or hire.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a sulh with acknowledgment as either a sale or hire according to the consideration type.

Core Idea
Sulh with acknowledgment is a commutative contract—either sale or hire.

In this lesson, we examine a concise ruling on sulh (settlement) when the defendant admits the claim. The key insight is that such a sulh is a purely commutative exchange, and its legal rules are determined by the nature of the consideration: property for property follows sale, property for usufruct follows hire. This classification is essential for applying the correct conditions, options, and invalidation rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine that the sulh involves acknowledgment (iqrar) by the defendant. This is a purely commutative contract (mu'awada), not a compromise on a denied claim.
Sulh without acknowledgment (sulh an inkar) has different rules.
2
Classify the consideration
Examine what is given in exchange. If the consideration is property (mal) for property, it is a sale. If it is property for benefits (manafi), it is a hire.
Benefits include usufruct of an asset, services, etc.
3
Apply the corresponding contract rules
Once classified, apply all rules of sale (bay') or hire (ijara) respectively. This includes conditions of validity, options, delivery, and payment.
For sale: known price, known object, offer/acceptance. For hire: known duration, known benefit, etc.
4
Note the practical consequence
If the classification is wrong, the sulh may be invalid (fasid). For example, treating a hire as a sale may miss the requirement of specifying duration.
The same rules of invalidation from sale/hire apply.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
السؤال: ما حكم الصلح مع الإقرار ؟ الجواب: إن وقع هذا الصلح عن مال بمال يعتبر فيه ما يعتبر في البياعات، وإن وقع عن مال بمنافع يعتبر فيه ما يعتبر في الإجارات.
Question: What is the ruling of sulh with acknowledgment? Answer: If this sulh occurs for property in exchange for property, the rules of sales are considered in it; and if it occurs for property in exchange for benefits, the rules of hires are considered in it.
Source pages p. 283

Commentary
This passage gives the ruling for sulh when the defendant acknowledges the claim. In such a case, the sulh is a purely commutative contract. If the consideration is property for property, it is governed by the rules of sale (e.g., conditions of valid sale, option of inspection, etc.). If it is property for benefits (usufruct), it follows the rules of hire (ijara), such as specification of duration and benefit. This distinction is because the nature of exchange differs.

Key Terms
البياعات (al-bayi'at): Rules of sale contracts, including conditions, options, and validity.
الإجارات (al-ijarat): Rules of hire contracts, including duration, rent, and usage.

Pillars and Condition of Possession in Hibah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A gift requires offer and acceptance as pillars, and possession in the same session for completion.

Hibah, or gift, is a voluntary transfer of ownership without compensation. In Hanafi fiqh, the contract is built on two pillars: offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). However, the gift is not fully binding until the donee takes physical possession (qabd) in the same sitting. This lesson unpacks these essential elements and the rule regarding possession after separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Pillars (Rukn)
Find the two integral components of the hibah contract as stated: offer and acceptance. These are the rukn – without them, the contract does not exist.
Rukn in Hanafi is essential for existence, unlike shart which is external.
2
Distinguish Pillars from Completion Condition
Note that after the pillars, the text adds a requirement for completion (tamam): possession (qabd). This is a separate condition that makes the gift binding (lazim).
In Hanafi, hibah is valid with only ijab and qabul, but it remains revocable until possession.
3
Understand the Rule of Qabd
Read the condition: if the donee takes possession in the session (majlis) – even without the donor’s permission – the gift becomes fully binding. If possession is taken after separation, it is invalid unless the donor permits.
The session is the same physical gathering where the contract occurred.
4
Apply the Rule to Scenarios
Consider two cases: (1) possession in the session with or without permission – valid; (2) possession after separation without permission – invalid; with permission – valid.
This highlights the importance of timing and permission.

Gift of Undivided Shares (Musha') and Mixed Substances in Hanafi Fiqh
Rulings on gifting undivided shares and mixed substances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on gifting undivided shares (musha') and mixed substances, and apply the condition of divisibility to determine validity.

Core Idea
A gift of an undivided share is valid only if the property cannot be divided without loss.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rules for gifting undivided shares (musha') and mixed substances. The key distinction is whether the property can be divided (yuqsam) without destroying its function. Understanding this distinction prevents common errors in hibah transactions and clarifies the remedial actions available.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property type
Determine whether the property is partible (yuqsam) or non-partible. Partible: can be divided without loss of function (e.g., land, grain). Non-partible: division destroys utility (e.g., a slave, a specific bird, a millstone).
The passage uses examples: al-'abd (slave), al-hamam (pigeon), al-raha (millstone) for non-partible.
2
Apply the rule for musha'
For non-partible property, a gift of an undivided share (musha') is valid outright. For partible property, such a gift is fasid (void) unless the share is physically separated (muqasaman) and delivered (mahuzan) to the donee.
Fasid here means void but capable of rectification; it is not batil.
3
Handle the mixed substances case
Gifts of mixed substances (e.g., flour mixed with wheat, oil mixed with sesame) are fasid because the gifted item is not clearly identified (majhul). The mixture prevents proper specification.
This is distinct from musha' because it involves two different types of property combined.
4
Recognize the remedial action
A fasid gift of a share in partible property becomes valid if the property is subsequently divided and the specific portion is delivered to the donee. This retroactive validation is unique to fasid contracts in Hanafi fiqh.
The text says: 'fa-in qusima al-musha' wa sallama ila al-mawhub lahi al-juz' alladhi wahaba lahu jaza.'

Commentary
This passage is a set of rulings on the law of gifts (hibah) from a Hanafi matn. It begins by stating that the pillars of hibah are offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). However, for the gift to be complete, possession (qabd) is required. The valid words for a gift include specific verbs like 'wahaba', 'naha', 'a'ta', etc. The word 'a'martuka' indicates a life grant ('umra), which is explained separately: it means giving something for the recipient's lifetime, upon the death of the donee it returns to the donor or his heirs; any condition that it returns to the donor invalidates the gift. The final sentence explains the 'umra: if someone gives an object for the donee's life, it becomes the donee's property, and upon his death it goes to his heirs; any condition that it will return to the donor is void. This lesson addresses special situations where the requirement of physical possession (qabd) may be fulfilled differently. We will examine cases where the donee already holds the object, where a guardian receives the gift for a minor, and where the minor himself can take possession. Multiple Parties in Hibah: Donors and Donees
Ruling on gifts with multiple parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on gifts involving multiple donors or multiple donees, and identify the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding a gift from one person to two donees.

Core Idea
Two donors to one donee: valid. One donor to two donees: invalid per Abu Hanifa, valid per Sahibayn.

This lesson addresses two scenarios: when two people jointly give a gift to one person, and when one person gives a gift to two people. The ruling differs according to Abu Hanifa and his two companions. Understanding this dispute helps in drafting valid gift contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Case 1: Two donors give one house to one donee. Case 2: One donor gives one house to two donees.
Note that in both cases, the object is one and undivided.
2
State the ruling for each case
For Case 1 (two donors, one donee): valid (sahih). For Case 2 (one donor, two donees): invalid according to Abu Hanifa, but valid according to Sahibayn.
The ruling depends on who the donors and donees are.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Hanifa: a gift of an undivided share to multiple donees is invalid because the shares are unspecified. Sahibayn: such a gift is valid; the donees own the object in shares and can divide it later.
The difference stems from the permissibility of gifting an undivided share (mushā‘).
4
Note the practical implication
In fatwa, the view of the Sahibayn is often preferred, making such gifts valid. However, the text reports both positions.
Memorize: two donors → valid; one donor to two → disputed.

Gift with Compensation (Hibah al-‘Iwad)
Ruling on gift with compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a gift with a condition of compensation is valid, requires mutual possession, and is treated as a sale with all associated rights and obligations.

Core Idea
A gift with compensation is legally a sale: requires mutual possession, allows return for defects, and triggers pre-emption.

This lesson examines a special type of gift: hibah al-‘iwad (gift with compensation). Unlike a pure gift, this transaction is akin to a sale. It requires possession of both the gift and the compensation. Once completed, it carries sale effects such as the right to return for defects and pre-emption for co-owners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
This is a gift with a condition that the donee gives something in return. It is not a pure donation; it has elements of exchange.
The term 'hibah al-‘iwad' literally means 'gift with compensation'.
2
Determine the requirement for validity
Mutual possession (taqābuḍ) of both the gift and the compensation is required. Both parties must take delivery of their respective objects.
This mirrors the requirement in a sale (bay‘) for reciprocal possession.
3
List the legal effects
Once valid, the contract is treated as a sale. This gives rise to: right to return for defect (khiyār al-‘ayb), option of inspection (khiyār al-ru’yah), and pre-emption (shuf‘ah).
These are sale-specific rights not found in a pure gift.

Definition of ghasb and rules for liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ghasb, identify the elements of liability, and apply the correct compensation (mithl or qimah) when the usurped item is destroyed.

Core Idea
Usurpation is wrongful possession; liability is strict: return the item or pay its like/value, with no distinction between halak and istihlak.

This passage from a Hanafi primer defines the prohibited act of ghasb (usurpation) and the consequences for the usurper. We will break down the precise legal definition, the procedure when the item is destroyed, and the method for determining compensation based on whether the property is fungible (mithli) or unique (qimi). The text emphatically states that liability arises regardless of whether the property perished by the usurper's act or by someone else's, eliminating any supposed difference between destruction (halak) and consumption (istihlak).

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the definition of ghasb
Identify each element: removal of rightful possession, establishing wrongful possession, property must be valuable, protected, and transportable, and the taking must be without the owner's permission.

2
Identify the primary ruling (رد العين)
The usurper must return the exact usurped item (the 'ayn) if it still exists. This is the default obligation.
Returning the item is the first priority; substitute only comes into play if the item is destroyed.

Mithli = goods measured by weight, volume, or count with uniformity; qimi = goods with significant variation.
5
Note the equivalence of halak and istihlak
The text explicitly states there is no legal difference between destruction (halak) and consumption (istihlak). Liability attaches in both cases, whether caused by the usurper or by another person.
This resolves a potential confusion: even if a third party destroys the item, the usurper remains liable to the owner.

Rulings on 'Umra and Ruqba
Intra-Hanafi dispute on 'umra and ruqba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for 'umra and ruqba, explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement, and apply the distinction between them.

Core Idea
'Umra is valid but ruqba is batil; conditional ownership on death is invalid.

This lesson addresses two related gift contracts: 'umra (a life-long gift) and ruqba (a gift conditional on the donor's death). Although both involve a condition tied to death, the Hanafi school treats them differently. We will examine the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf, and understand the legal reasoning behind the majority view.

Guided Path
1
Define 'umra and ruqba
Read the Arabic text and translation to grasp the basic definitions: 'umra is a gift for the duration of the donee's life; ruqba is a conditional gift where the donor says 'my house is yours as ruqba', meaning it becomes the donee's if the donor dies first.
Note that both involve a condition of death, but the phrasing differs.
2
Identify the agreed ruling on 'umra
All three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) agree that 'umra is valid. The condition of return upon the donee's death is invalid, but the ownership transfer remains effective as a gift.
The gift is not invalidated by the invalid condition.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute on ruqba
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold ruqba to be batil (invalid). Abu Yusuf disagrees, holding it valid. Identify the evidence each side uses: the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) permitting 'umra and rejecting ruqba, and the analogy to contingent ownership.
The dispute centers on whether the condition renders the contract void.
4
Understand the reasoning for batil
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that ruqba suspends ownership on a risk (ta'liq al-tamlik bi al-khatar) — the death of the donor — which is an uncertain event. This makes the contract invalid. If invalid, the property is considered a loan (ariyah) because the donee may use it.
Compare with 'umra where the condition is not suspensive but only a condition of return.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that ruqba is batil, following Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. In practice, a 'ruqba' transaction is not enforced as a gift; instead, it may be treated as a loan or the property remains with the donor.
This is established in Zahir al-Riwayah and confirmed by later Hanafi authorities.

The Hanafi Definition of Ghasb (Usurpation)
Defines ghasb with four essential elements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ghasb and identify its four essential elements.

Core Idea
Ghasb is wrongful possession removing rightful possession, over valuable, transportable property without consent.

Ghasb (usurpation) is a foundational concept in Hanafi fiqh, governing liability for unlawful taking. This lesson breaks down its precise legal definition, which serves as a memorization anchor for the entire book of ghasb. Understanding each element is critical for distinguishing it from theft, borrowing, and other possession-related rulings.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition
Read the Arabic definition aloud. Focus on the four key phrases: إزالة اليد المحقة (removing rightful possession), إثبات اليد المبطلة (establishing wrongful possession), في مال متقوم محترم قابل للنقل (over valuable, respected, transportable property), بغير إذن مالكه (without owner's permission).
This is a memorization anchor – commit it to heart.
2
Analyze each element
Break down each element: (1) Removal of rightful possession – the owner's control is ended. (2) Establishment of wrongful possession – the usurper takes control. (3) The object must be mal mutaqawwim (legally valuable), muhtaram (respected, i.e., permissible to own), and qabil li al-naql (transportable). (4) Lack of owner's permission – the taking is without consent.
Note that 'muhtaram' excludes items like carrion or alcohol.
3
Apply the definition to a case
Consider a scenario: A person takes someone's car without permission and drives it. Does this fit the definition? Check each element: removal of rightful possession (yes), establishment of wrongful possession (yes), the car is valuable, respected, and transportable (yes), no permission (yes). So it is ghasb.
If any element is missing, it is not ghasb.
4
Distinguish from related concepts
Compare ghasb with theft (sariqa) which requires taking secretly, and with borrowing without permission (which may be ghasb if it involves possession). Ghasb can be open or forcible.
The key is the unlawful transfer of possession.

But ghasb can include land as we see later). The key is unlawful taking with force or stealth.

Key Terms
الغصب (al-ghaṣb): Removing rightful possession by establishing wrongful possession over valuable, transportable property without owner's consent.
المال المتقوم (al-māl al-mutaqawwim): Property that is legally valuable and permissible to use (e.g., not carrion or alcohol).
محترم (muḥtaram): Respected, meaning the property is lawful to own and possess (not impure or prohibited).

Rulings on Destruction and Substitute in Ghasb
Explains the ruling when usurped item perishes and what substitute is due

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the primary duty of a usurper to return the item, explain the judicial procedure when destruction is claimed, identify the correct substitute (mithl vs qima) based on property type, and state that liability is uniform for destruction and consumption.

Core Idea
Usurper must return the exact item; if perished, mithl for fungibles, qima for non-fungibles; no difference between halak and istihlak.

This lesson clarifies the rulings when a usurped item is destroyed or consumed. The primary obligation is to return the very item. If the usurper claims it perished, the judge takes measures to verify, including imprisonment. Once destruction is confirmed, the judge orders a substitute. The type of substitute depends on whether the item has a standard like (mithli) — then a like is due — or not (qimi) — then its value. Importantly, there is no legal distinction between accidental destruction and consumption by the usurper; liability applies in both.

2
Understand the claim of destruction
When the usurper claims the item perished, why is he imprisoned? Because his claim is suspicious; imprisonment compels him to produce it if it exists.
The judge does not accept the claim at face value.
3
Distinguish the substitute types
For mithli items (like wheat), a like (mithl) is due; for qimi items (like animals), value (qima) is due.
This classification ensures just compensation.
4
Recognize the uniformity of liability
Whether the item perished naturally (halak) or was consumed by the usurper (istihlak), the guarantee (daman) is the same.
Both cases trigger the same obligation.
5

In a given scenario, identify the item type and determine the substitute.
Practice with real examples.

Answer: The principle is that the usurper must return the very usurped item; if he claims its destruction, the judge imprisons him until he knows that if it were in his possession he would produce it; then the judge orders him to pay its substitute. Rulings on Diminution and Transformation of Usurped Item
This is a ruling statement that expands on three scenarios of change to a usurped item.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and state the Hanafi rulings for three distinct scenarios of damage to a usurped item: pure decrease in value, complete transformation (istihlak), and incomplete transformation with an addition (such as dyeing or mixing).

Core Idea
Complete transformation transfers ownership; partial transformation gives choice; pure decrease requires compensation.

When an item is usurped and then changes, Islamic law provides a nuanced set of rulings to ensure justice for both the original owner and the usurper. This lesson focuses on three key scenarios derived from classical Hanafi texts: (1) a simple decrease in value, (2) a complete transformation that changes the item's identity and primary benefit, and (3) a partial change where something is added to the item. Each scenario triggers a different legal consequence regarding ownership and financial liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the passage carefully and list the three distinct situations described: pure decrease, complete transformation, and partial transformation with addition.
2
Determine the ruling for pure decrease
For any decrease in the value, quality, or utility of the usurped item that remains essentially the same, the usurper is liable for that decrease. The original owner retains ownership of the item.
3
Determine the ruling for complete transformation (istihlak)
When the usurper completely transforms the item so that it loses its original name and primary benefit, ownership of the new item transfers to the usurper. However, the usurper must pay a substitute (badal) to the original owner and cannot use the item until he does so.
Examples: grinding wheat, slaughtering a sheep, forging iron into a sword.
4
Determine the ruling for incomplete transformation with addition
When the item is not completely transformed but an addition is made (like dyeing a white garment red), the original owner has a choice (khiyar). He may either take the value of the original item and let the usurper keep the enhanced item, or take the enhanced item and pay the usurper the value of the addition.
Examples: dyeing a garment, mixing ghee with saweeq.
5
Apply the rulings to the examples in the passage
For each example given (sheep, wheat, iron, brass, garment, saweeq), confirm which scenario applies and state the corresponding ruling.

Question: What is the ruling on deficiency? Answer: Whatever decreases in the usurped item while in the usurper's hand, he is liable for the decrease. Question: He usurped an item and the usurped item changed by the act of the usurper, such that its name and major benefits ceased, like one who usurps a sheep and slaughters it and roasts or cooks it, or usurps wheat and grinds it, or iron and makes it into a sword, or brass and molds it into a vessel, what is the ruling? Question: He usurped a garment and dyed it red, or saweeq and mixed it with ghee, what is the ruling? Commentary
This passage addresses two scenarios: (1) When the usurped item decreases in value but remains essentially the same (nuqsan), the usurper must pay the difference. (2) When the usurper transforms the item so completely that its name and primary benefit change (istihalik), ownership transfers to the usurper. For example, a sheep becomes roasted meat, wheat becomes flour, iron becomes a sword, brass becomes a vessel. In these cases, the original owner can no longer claim the item; the usurper now owns it but must pay its value (badal) before using it. He cannot benefit from it until he compensates the owner. (3) A middle case: the item is not completely transformed, but an addition is made (dyeing a white garment red, mixing ghee with saweeq). Here, the owner has a choice (khiyar): either take the value of the original item (white garment, plain saweeq) and let the usurper keep the enhanced item, or take the enhanced item and pay the usurper the value of the addition (dye, ghee). Key Terms
النقصان (al-nuqṣān): The decrease in value, utility, or quality of the usurped item.
الإستهلاك (al-istihlāk): Complete transformation where the item loses its original name and primary benefit.
الخيار (al-khiyār): The option given to the owner in partial transformation to choose between taking the enhanced item and paying for the addition, or taking the value of the original and letting the usurper keep it.
البدل (al-badal): The substitute or compensation paid by the usurper to the original owner in case of complete transformation.
