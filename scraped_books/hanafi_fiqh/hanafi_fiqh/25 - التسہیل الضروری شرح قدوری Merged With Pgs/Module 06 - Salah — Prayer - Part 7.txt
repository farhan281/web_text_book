Core Idea
Sitting on a mat does not break an oath to not sit on the ground because the mat is a separate barrier; clothing is not.

In this lesson, we learn about the interpretation of oaths concerning sitting on the ground. The Hanafi school distinguishes between objects that are considered part of the ground and those that are separate barriers. We will examine the ruling and the reasoning behind it.

The text states that sitting on a mat does not break the oath. Understand the reasoning: the mat is a separate barrier from the ground.

Differentiate clothing
If the person sits on his own clothing, that is considered sitting on the ground because clothing is attached to the body and not a separate barrier.
The Arabic phrase 'tabi' lahu' indicates the clothing follows the person.

Oath on Sitting on a Bed: Effect of Accessories and Stacking
Ruling on sitting on a bed with a mat or another bed on top

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to sit on a bed is broken, distinguishing between a mat on top and another bed on top, and between a specific and indefinite bed.

Core Idea
A mat on a bed does not prevent breaking the oath; another bed on top does, if the bed is specific.

This lesson examines two scenarios related to oaths about sitting on beds. The Hanafi school provides clear distinctions based on whether the bed is specified or indefinite, and whether the object on top is an accessory or a separate piece of furniture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sworn object
Determine whether the oath refers to a specific bed (e.g., 'my bed') or an indefinite one (e.g., 'a bed'). This distinction changes the ruling.
Specific = معين, Indefinite = منكر
2
Check the nature of what is on top
Is the thing on top an accessory (like a mat) or a separate entity (like another bed)? Accessories do not change the identity of the bed; separate entities create a new object.
A mat is considered a covering; a separate bed is a distinct piece of furniture.
3
Apply the rule for specific bed
If the bed is specific, sitting on the same bed with a mat on top breaks the oath because the mat is part of the bed. Sitting on another bed placed on top does not break the oath because that is a different bed.
The fact that the upper bed is physically on top does not make it the same bed.
4
Apply the rule for indefinite bed
If the oath was on an indefinite bed (any bed), then sitting on any bed breaks the oath, even if there is another bed on top. The indefinite phrase covers all beds.
The upper bed is still a bed, so it satisfies the oath.

Core Idea
An accessory attached to the sworn object breaks the oath; a separate entity does not unless intended as extra cushioning.

Oaths concerning objects often raise questions about what counts as 'using' the object. This passage from Al-Jawhara examines two scenarios: sleeping on a mattress covered with a quilt versus sleeping on an additional mattress placed on top. The Hanafi masters differ on the second scenario, and the correct position for fatwa is identified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage gives two distinct cases: (1) sleeping on a mattress that has a quilt (qaram) on top, and (2) sleeping on another mattress placed on top of the sworn mattress. Determine the ruling in each.
2
Analyze the basis for differentiation
The quilt is considered an accessory (taba‘) to the mattress, so sleeping on it is sleeping on the mattress. For the second mattress, the key question is whether it is also an accessory.
Accessory status depends on whether the item is integral to the principal object.
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Muhammad holds that the second mattress is not an accessory, so the oath is not broken. Abu Yusuf argues that because it is placed for extra cushioning, the sleeper is effectively on the sworn mattress, breaking the oath.
4
Identify the relied-upon position (mufta bihi)
The text explicitly states 'wa huwa al-sahih' (it is correct), referring to Imam Muhammad's view. Thus, the fatwa is given according to Muhammad.
This is an example of tarjih within the school.

Oath on Wife's Exit: Permission Required Each Time
Ruling on whether one-time permission suffices for repeated exits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two oath phrasings regarding permission and state whether each repeated exit requires renewed permission.

Core Idea
One oath exception phrase 'except with permission' requires permission each time; 'until permission' is a limit.

This lesson examines a common fiqh scenario: a husband's oath restricting his wife's exit. The ruling hinges on the precise wording used, either a general exception or a limit. Understanding this distinction prevents unintended oath-breaking and ensures correct legal outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath phrasings
First phrasing: 'she will not go out except with my permission' (إلا بإذني). Second phrasing: 'you will not go out until I permit you' (حتى آذن لك). Note the difference between 'except' (general exception) and 'until' (limit).
2
Apply the Hanafi rule on exception vs limit
In the first, each exit is a new instance requiring a new permission; one-time permission does not cover subsequent exits. In the second, the word 'until' sets a limit, so one permission ends the oath entirely.
The ruling is based on the wording as stated; the swearer's intention does not affect the judicial ruling (qada'an) but may matter religiously (diyanatan).
3
Note the role of intention (niyyah)
If the swearer intended multiple permissions, that is accepted religiously but not in court. The external wording prevails judicially.
From Al-Hidayah: 'if he intended permission once, he is believed religiously not judicially'.
4
Determine the consequence (breaking oath or not)
In the first phrasing, each exit without permission breaks the oath. In the second, after one permission, subsequent exits do not break the oath.
The oath ends with the permission; further exits are not violations.

Core Idea
A permitted slave's property is legally distinct from his master's; an oath against the master's animal is not broken by riding the slave's animal.

Oaths are taken seriously in Islamic law. One common scenario is swearing not to ride a specific person's animal. This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).

Question-answer ruling on continuity of receipt in oaths.

Oath to Perform vs. Oath to Refrain

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi rulings for oaths to perform and oaths to refrain, and apply the conditions for barr (fulfillment) and hanth (breach).

Core Idea
An oath to do is fulfilled once; an oath not to do requires permanent abstention.

This passage, an admonition from a Hanafi commentary, clarifies two fundamental categories of oaths. Understanding the distinction between an oath to perform an act (yamin al-fi'l) and an oath to refrain (yamin al-tark) is crucial for determining when an oath is fulfilled (barr) or broken (hanth). The passage provides clear rules and an illustrative example involving riding someone's animal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath type
Determine whether the oath is to perform an act (e.g., 'I will do X') or to refrain (e.g., 'I will not do Y'). This initial classification dictates the entire ruling.
The Arabic phrasing often uses 'أفعل' (I will do) for action oaths and 'لا أفعل' (I will not do) for refrain oaths.

Analyze qualifications for action oaths
If the oath is to perform, check if it is qualified by time, place, or specific object. If unqualified, one act fulfills it permanently. If qualified, the act must match the qualification exactly.

3
Apply the rule for refrain oaths
If the oath is to refrain, the obligation is continuous for life. Any single act of commission (doing the forbidden thing) breaks the oath. There is no grace period or minimum number.
This is because refraining is a continuous state; any interruption violates it.

Commentary
This passage is a set of rulings and a major inter-madhhab dispute regarding the kaffarah (expiation) for breaking an oath. The text comes from the book al-Tashil al-Daruri Sharh al-Quduri. It begins with the types of kaffarah: feeding poor people, clothing them, or freeing a slave. For clothing, each poor person must receive a garment that covers the 'awra sufficiently for prayer. The minimum garment is discussed with an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: according to the sound narration from Muhammad, Abu Yusuf, and Abu Hanifa, it must cover most of the body, so a simple trouser is not enough because the wearer would still be considered naked in custom. Tanfil (Extra Reward) in Battle: Conditions and Permissibility
QA on tanfil conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the imam may grant extra reward (tanfil) and identify the correct timing and source for such grants.

Core Idea
Tanfil is permissible only before spoils are brought to Dar al-Islam; after that, it must come from the khums.

In jihad, the imam may encourage fighters by offering extra rewards (tanfil) beyond the standard share. This lesson explains the ruling and its two key conditions: timing (before removal to Dar al-Islam) and source (after removal, only from the khums). Understanding these ensures lawful distribution of booty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is tanfil (extra reward). Its ruling is permissible (ja'iz) for the imam. Recognize that it is not obligatory but a discretionary incentive.
2
Determine the permissible timing
Tanfil is only allowed before the spoils (ghanimah) are taken to Dar al-Islam. If granted after, it becomes invalid unless it comes from the khums.
The phrase 'before ighraz al-ghanimah ila Dar al-Islam' is key.
3
Identify the source after removal
Once spoils are brought to Dar al-Islam, tanfil can only be given from the khums (the imam's one-fifth share). The commentator clarifies that 'after the khums' means after deducting the khums, so the promised share (e.g., one-fourth) is from the remaining four-fifths.
The Hidayah and 'Ayni explanations confirm this.

Chapter on one who becomes Muslim in the Abode of War. The question: If a disbeliever becomes Muslim in Dar al-Harb, does he secure himself by virtue of Islam? The answer: He secures himself, his young children, and every property that is in his hands, and every deposit he has with a Muslim or dhimmi. The question: If a man among them becomes Muslim and he has real estate in Dar al-Harb, or a wife, or adult children, what is the ruling on these things when we conquer them? The answer: When we conquer Dar al-Harb, his real estate, his wife, her pregnancy, and his adult children are all fay (spoils). And whoever among his slaves fights (against Muslims) is also fay. The question: If a man becomes Muslim in Dar al-Harb and he has wrongfully taken property in the hands of a harbi, or in the hands of a Muslim or dhimmi, what is its ruling? The answer: Whatever of his property is in the hands of a harbi is fay, whether it is usurped or a deposit. And what is usurped in the hands of a Muslim or dhimmi is fay according to Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him. And Muhammad said: It is not fay. 'It is not fay': Thus Muhammad mentioned it, and the difference is recorded in al-Siyar al-Kabir. And they mentioned in Sharh al-Jami' al-Saghir the statement of Abu Yusuf along with Muhammad's statement, checked from al-Hidayah.
Source pages p. 514

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal status of a person who converts to Islam while still in Dar al-Harb (enemy territory). The first ruling: the convert secures himself (i.e., his life and person) from being treated as an enemy combatant. This immunity extends to his young children (who follow him in Islam) and any property in his immediate possession, as well as deposits he has given to Muslims or dhimmis (which are protected). However, if the Muslims later conquer that territory, the convert's real estate, wife (pregnant or not), and adult children (who did not convert) become fay—i.e., part of the spoils distributed among the Muslim army. Similarly, any of his slaves who fight against Muslims are also fay. A further dispute arises regarding property that was wrongfully taken (ghasb) from the convert and is now in the hands of a Muslim or dhimmi: Abu Hanifa holds that such property is also fay (lost to the convert as spoils), but Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that it is not fay—it remains the property of the convert. The text notes that this difference is recorded in al-Siyar al-Kabir, and Abu Yusuf's view aligns with Muhammad's according to some commentaries.

Key Terms
محرز (muhriz): One who secures or protects; here, the convert secures himself and certain assets from being spoils.
فيء (fay): Spoils acquired from the enemy without active fighting; includes property left behind after conquest.

Rulings on the Apostate: Male and Female
Ruling on apostate male (kill) and female (imprison forever)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on male and female apostates, and identify the procedural steps required before execution.

Core Idea
A male apostate is killed after due process; a female apostate is imprisoned forever.

This lesson presents the core Hanafi rulings on apostasy (riddah) as outlined in classical fiqh texts. The rulings distinguish between male and female apostates and specify the exact procedure to be followed before any punishment is carried out. Understanding these rulings is essential for correctly applying Islamic criminal law within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Note that the ruling distinguishes between a male apostate (murtaad) and a female apostate (murtaaddah). Each has a different final punishment.
2
State the procedure for the male apostate
The ruler must follow four steps: (1) present Islam to him, (2) clarify any doubt (shubha) he may have, (3) imprison him for three days to encourage repentance, (4) if he does not repent, execute him.
The three-day imprisonment is a wajib procedural step.
3
Explain the ruling if someone kills him before the procedure
If a person kills the apostate before the presentation of Islam, the act is makruh tanzihi (disliked but not sinful). However, no qisas or diya is owed because the apostate’s life is already forfeit once apostasy is confirmed.
This shows that the procedure is a condition for the execution, not for the forfeiture of life.
4
State the ruling for the female apostate
A female apostate is not executed. Instead, she is imprisoned indefinitely until she returns to Islam. This is based on the hadith 'لا يقتل مؤمنة' (a believing woman is not killed).
The Hanafi school extends this principle to apostasy; the Shafi'i school disagrees.
5
Note the inter-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that a female apostate is also executed. In practice, a Hanafi mufti must follow the Hanafi position.
This ikhtilaf is a legitimate difference rooted in different interpretations of the same hadith evidence.

If someone kills him before presenting Islam, that is disliked for him, but there is no qisas or blood-money upon him. If a woman apostatizes — Allah forbid — she is not killed, but is imprisoned forever until she becomes Muslim.
Source pages p. 524

Commentary
This passage states the Hanafi ruling on apostates. The passage type is a ruling statement. For a male apostate, the prescribed procedure is: first, present Islam to him; second, if he has any shubha (doubt), clarify it; third, imprison him for three days to give him time to reflect; fourth, if he repents, accept it; otherwise, execute him. If someone kills him before the presentation of Islam, that act is makruh (disliked) but no qisas or diya is incurred, because the apostate's life is forfeit (mubah al-dam) once apostasy is established, though the presentation is wajib. For a female apostate, the ruling differs: she is not executed regardless, but imprisoned indefinitely until she returns to Islam. This distinction is based on the Prophet's hadith 'لا يقتل مؤمنة' (a believer woman is not killed), interpreted here as applying to apostasy. The Hanafi school holds that women are not killed for apostasy, whereas the Shafi'i school holds they are killed. This passage does not include the proof; it is a straightforward ruling for memorization.

Key Terms
مرتد (murtadd): A person who leaves Islam; in this context, one who apostatizes and is subject to three-day imprisonment and then execution if male, indefinite imprisonment if female.
شبهة (shubha): A doubt or conceptual ambiguity that may have led to apostasy; the Imam should clarify it.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliatory punishment (eye for eye); here, not applicable when someone kills an apostate before presentation of Islam.
