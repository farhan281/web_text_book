Rulings on Delegated Divorce (Ikhtiyar and Tamlik)
Q&A on delegating divorce to wife and a third party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three types of delegated divorce (ikhtiyar, command, and agency) and apply the correct ruling based on the husband's wording and intention.

Core Idea
The wording of delegation determines the nature of the divorce: 'Choose yourself' yields ba'in; 'Divorce yourself' yields raj'i; agency is unrestricted unless qualified.

Delegated divorce (tamlik) is a special mechanism in Hanafi fiqh where the husband vests the right to divorce in the wife or a third party. The specific wording and conditions affect the outcome. This lesson covers three common scenarios from the matn: the wife's choice (ikhtiyar), the wife's command (talliqi), and appointment of an agent. Each scenario has distinct rules regarding the number of divorces, revocability, and location restrictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the delegation wording
Determine whether the husband said 'ikhtari nafsaki' (choose yourself), 'talliqi nafsaki' (divorce yourself), or appointed an agent. This determines the category of delegation.
Wording is dispositive; ambiguous phrases are interpreted by the jurists.
2
Note the presence of the word 'nafs' (self)
For ikhtiyar, the word 'self' must be mentioned by either the husband or the wife. If absent, no divorce occurs.
This condition applies specifically to ikhtiyar, not to the other scenarios.
3
Distinguish between ba'in and raj'i
Ikhtiyar results in a single ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, regardless of intention. The command 'talliqi' defaults to a single raj'i (revocable) divorce unless the husband intended three and she executed three.
The difference hinges on the nature of the delegation: ownership (tamlik) vs command (amr).
4
Consider the husband's intention
For 'talliqi', if the husband intended three divorces and the wife pronounced three, three occur. If he intended two and she is a free woman, it is invalid; but if she is a slave, two are valid.
Intention matters only when the wife's action matches the intended number.
5
Apply the rule for agency
If the husband appoints an agent with absolute authority ('Talaq imra'ati'), the agent may divorce at any time, not restricted to the gathering. If conditional ('in shi'ta'), the agent must act in the same gathering.
The restriction is based on the presence of an option (mashiyya).

Inheritance After Ba'in Divorce During Terminal Illness (Talaq al-Mareed)
Q&A on inheritance after divorce in illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance for a woman divorced with a ba'in divorce during the husband's terminal illness and identify the condition regarding the iddah.

Core Idea
A ba'in divorce during terminal illness does not cut off inheritance if the husband dies before her iddah expires.

In Hanafi fiqh, a terminal illness (marad al-mawt) triggers special rulings to prevent abuse of inheritance rights. One such case is divorce (talaq) pronounced during the husband's fatal illness. This lesson examines when a divorced woman still inherits from her deceased husband, focusing on the distinction between ba'in and raj'i divorce and the role of the iddah period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of divorce
Distinguish between ba'in (irrevocable) and raj'i (revocable) divorce. The ruling discussed primarily concerns ba'in divorce, as raj'i divorce already entitles the wife to inheritance during iddah regardless of the husband's health.
The text notes that al-Quduri limited his discussion to ba'in, implying the rule is specific to that type.
2
State the condition for inheritance
The wife inherits if (1) the divorce occurred during the husband's terminal illness, and (2) the husband dies before her iddah expires. If he dies after the iddah, she does not inherit.
The iddah for ba'in divorce is three menstrual cycles or three months; for a pregnant woman, it lasts until delivery.
3
Understand the rationale (hikmah)
The rule is known as 'mas'alah al-farr' (the escapee's case). If the husband divorces to deprive her of inheritance, the law 'punishes' him by allowing her to inherit within the iddah window.
This is a form of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to evil).
4
Apply to triple divorce (mughallaz)
Triple divorce (talaq mughallaz) is treated like ba'in for inheritance purposes.
No distinction is made between ba'in and mughallaz in this context.
5
Distinguish from divorce in health
If the husband was healthy at the time of divorce, the wife does not inherit even if she is in iddah. The special rule applies only to terminal illness.
The iddah after a healthy divorce does not preserve inheritance rights.

Number of Divorces Based on Wife's Status
First Q&A: ruling on number of divorces based on wife's status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the number of divorces a husband can give based on the wife's status (free or slave) and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
The wife's status determines the number of divorces, not the husband's: slave wife gets two, free wife gets three.

In Hanafi fiqh, the number of divorces a husband may pronounce is not tied to his own freedom or enslavement, but to that of his wife. This ruling addresses a common question: can a free man give three divorces to a slave wife? The text clarifies that the wife's status governs both the maximum number of divorces and the iddah period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The number of divorces allowed depends on the wife's status (free or slave), not the husband's.
2
Apply to free wife
A free wife can be divorced up to three times. After three, she becomes permanently prohibited.
3
Apply to slave wife
A slave wife can be divorced up to two times. After two, she becomes permanently prohibited.
4
Note the iddah difference
The iddah of a slave wife is two menstrual periods, while that of a free wife is three.
5
Understand the consequence
Two divorces for a slave wife are like three for a free woman—they create a severe prohibition (binding divorce).

Core Idea
If one spouse comes to own the other, marriage dissolves automatically – ownership and marriage cannot coexist.

This second miscellaneous question addresses a unique situation: what happens if a husband inherits his wife as a slave, or a wife inherits her husband? According to Hanafi fiqh, the marriage is dissolved immediately because one person owning another is incompatible with the marital relationship. This dissolution is called 'furqa' and occurs without a divorce.

1
Identify the trigger
Acquisition of ownership (full or partial) of one spouse by the other triggers automatic separation.
The acquisition can be through inheritance, purchase, or gift.
2
Note the direction
The ruling applies both ways: husband owning wife or wife owning husband.
3
Understand the ruling
Upon ownership, the marriage is automatically dissolved (furqa) without need for divorce.
4
Distinguish from talaq
This is not a divorce; it is a separation due to legal incompatibility of ownership and marriage.

Khul' – Divorce by Redemption
Q&A defining khul' and its legal effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions and legal effect of khul' according to the Hanafi school and distinguish it from a unilateral divorce.

Core Idea
Khul' is a wife-initiated divorce by compensation, resulting in one ba'in divorce.

This lesson defines khul', a form of marital separation initiated by the wife in exchange for payment to the husband. The Quranic basis is the fear of not upholding Allah's limits. We will examine its condition, mechanism, and legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Nature
Recognize that khul' is a separation initiated by the wife (not the husband) through a financial offer. It is a distinct type of dissolution, not a unilateral talaq.
The term 'khul' literally means 'to take off' as in removing a garment.
2
State the Condition for Validity
The condition is mutual discord (shiqaq) and fear that the spouses cannot uphold the limits of Allah. Without this condition, khul' may be discouraged or invalid.
The proof is the Quranic verse: 'If you fear that they will not maintain the limits of Allah, then there is no blame upon either of them concerning that by which she ransoms herself.'
3
Describe the Mechanism
The wife says, 'I divorce you for such wealth,' and the husband accepts. The offer and acceptance create the khul' contract.
The compensation (usually money) must be specified and agreed upon.
4
Identify the Legal Effect
A single ba'in (irrevocable) divorce occurs. The wife must pay the agreed compensation. The couple cannot remarry without a new marriage contract.
Ba'in means the husband has no right to take back the wife during iddah; he must remarry with her consent.
5
Distinguish from Regular Talaq
In regular talaq, the husband initiates and may revoke a raj'i divorce. In khul', the wife initiates and the result is ba'in with financial obligation.
Khul' is considered a type of divorce because it ends the marriage, but it is not a revocable divorce.

Disputes Over Revocation: Precedence of the Wife's Word

Core Idea
In iddah disputes, the wife's knowledge is decisive — her word is taken without oath.

When a husband claims he revoked his revocable divorce during the iddah, but the wife denies it or claims the iddah has already ended, a conflict arises. The Hanafi school resolves this by giving precedence to the wife's statement about her own iddah, reflecting the principle that the one who experiences the condition is best informed. Question: He divorced his wife three times, and she said: My iddah has expired, and I married another husband and he entered me, then he divorced me and my iddah has expired. Is it permissible for the first husband to rely on her statement and marry her? Answer: It is permissible for the first husband to believe her and marry her again if his predominant opinion is that she is truthful, provided that the time period is possible.
Source pages p. 364

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement about relying on a woman's testimony regarding her own iddah. In a situation where a woman claims that her iddah from the first divorce has expired, she married a second husband, consummated, and then her iddah from that divorce has also expired, the first husband is allowed to accept her word and remarry her. This is subject to two conditions: (1) he has a strong presumption (ghalib al-zann) that she is telling the truth, and (2) the time elapsed is sufficient for such events to have occurred. This ruling recognizes that in many cases, the husband cannot verify her claims externally, so he may rely on her if she appears credible and the timeline is plausible. It is not an absolute permission but a concession based on practicality.

Key Terms
غالب الظن (ghalib al-zann): Predominant belief or strong presumption; a degree of certainty short of absolute but sufficient for action.
