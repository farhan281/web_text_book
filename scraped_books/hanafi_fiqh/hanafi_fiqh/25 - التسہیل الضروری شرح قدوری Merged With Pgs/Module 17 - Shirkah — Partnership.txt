The Hanafi Definition of Shirkah and Its Condition
Definition of shirkah with two modes of capital mixing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shirkah and identify the two ways in which the mixing of capital can occur.

Core Idea
Shirkah in Hanafi fiqh requires physical mixing of capitals beyond discernibility.

This lesson introduces the Book of Partnership with a clear definition. Understanding what constitutes shirkah in the Hanafi school is foundational because it determines the conditions for valid partnership contracts. The key concept is the mixing of assets so that individual shares cannot be distinguished.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question-answer structure
The passage begins with a question 'What is shirkah?' followed by a direct answer. This is a typical format for defining key terms in Hanafi fiqh texts.
The answer is concise and legally precise.
2
Extract the core definition
The definition states: 'It is the mixing of two capitals such that one cannot be distinguished from the other.' Note that the condition is indistinguishability.
Mixing (ikhtilat) is the essential element.
3
Recognize the two causes of mixing
The passage explains two ways mixing can occur: (1) deliberate mixing (khalṭ) by both parties, and (2) accidental mixing without deliberate action, such as inheriting joint property, receiving a joint gift, or an accidental spill.
Both lead to the same legal result.
4
Note the result of mixing
After mixing, the capitals become indistinguishable. This physical condition is what constitutes a valid shirkah.
Without indistinguishability, no partnership exists.
5
Apply the definition to examples
Consider scenarios: inheriting a house together (accidental mixing) vs. each keeping separate cash but agreeing to share profits (no mixing). Only the former creates shirkah.
Use the two modes to test real situations.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Partnership (Kitab al-Shirkah) with a definition. According to Hanafi fiqh, partnership is not merely an agreement to share profits and losses, but requires an actual mixing (ikhtilat) of the capital property such that the shares become indistinguishable. This mixing can occur in two ways: (1) by deliberate mixing (khalṭ min al-jānibayn), where both parties physically combine their assets; or (2) by accidental mixing without deliberate action, such as when two people inherit the same property or receive a joint gift, or when oil from one vessel accidentally spills into another. The essential condition for a valid shirkah is this physical indistinguishability of the capital; without it, the partnership is not considered properly formed. This definition is the basis for subsequent rulings on partnership contracts in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
الشركة (ash-shirkah): Partnership; a contract or situation where the capital of two or more persons is mixed so as to be indistinguishable.
اختلاط المالين (ikhtilāṭ al-mālayn): Mixing of two properties such that they cannot be distinguished.
الخلط (al-khalṭ): Intentional mixing by one or both parties.

Definition and Types of Partnership in Hanafi Fiqh
Defining partnership and its categories with detailed conditions for Mufawadah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between property partnership and contract partnership, and state the key conditions for the validity of Mufawadah partnership in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Partnership is either by joint ownership or by contract; Mufawadah requires full equality in capital, actions, and debt.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi classification of partnership (shirkah). Understanding these categories is crucial because the legal implications differ drastically: in property partnership, each partner is like a stranger to the other's share, whereas in contract partnership, mutual agency and guaranty arise. The text gives special attention to Mufawadah, a full partnership with stringent equality conditions that is unique to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of partnership
From the passage, note that partnership is of two types: property partnership (shirkat al-amlak) and contract partnership (shirkat al-'uqud). Property partnership arises from joint ownership without a contract, while contract partnership is based on an agreement.
This division is fundamental in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand property partnership
Property partnership occurs through inheritance or co-purchase. Its key ruling is that each partner cannot dispose of the other's share without permission; each is like a stranger regarding the other's share.
Contrast this with contract partnership where mutual agency exists.
3
Recognize the four types of contract partnership
The passage lists: Mufawadah, 'Inan, artisans, and credit partnerships. This lesson focuses on Mufawadah; the others will be covered separately.
Mufawadah is the most comprehensive form.
4
List the conditions for Mufawadah
For Mufawadah, partners must be equal in capital (مال), actions (تصرف), and debt (دين). The partnership is founded on agency (وكالة) and suretyship (كفالة). Purchases are joint except for family necessities.
Equality is strict: all aspects must match.
5
Note the restrictions on who can form Mufawadah
Mufawadah is valid only between free, adult, sane Muslims. It is invalid between a free person and a slave, between a minor and an adult, and between a Muslim and a disbeliever.
These restrictions ensure full legal capacity and mutual trust.

Definition and Ruling of Partnership of Ownership (Shirkat Amlak)
Defines two partnership types and ruling of ownership partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shirkat amlak, distinguish it from shirkat uqud, and state the ruling regarding each partner's authority over the other's share.

Core Idea
Ownership partnership is co-ownership, not a contract; each partner is like a stranger to the other's share.

Before studying commercial partnerships, Hanafi fiqh first distinguishes between mere co-ownership and contractual partnership. This lesson introduces the first type, shirkat amlak, which arises from inheritance or joint purchase. The key ruling is simple: without permission, no partner may dispose of the other's share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions of partnership
The text opens by stating that partnership is of two types: shirkat amlak and shirkat uqud. Recognize this as the foundational classification in Hanafi partnership law.
Shirkat uqud will be covered in subsequent lessons.
2
Define shirkat amlak
The text gives the image: two people inherit or jointly purchase an asset, thereby sharing ownership. Note that no contract is needed; it arises from a common event.
This is a state, not a contractual relationship.
3
Determine the ruling
The ruling is that it is not permissible for one partner to dispose of the other's share without permission. Each partner is like a stranger regarding the other's portion.
This means no inherent agency; consent is mandatory.
4
Understand the legal consequence
Unauthorised disposal by a partner is invalid and haram. The co-ownership itself is mubah (permissible), but acting without permission is prohibited.
This distinction is critical for avoiding sin and legal disputes.

Four Types of Contractual Partnership
Enumeration of four partnership types with their Arabic names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four types of contractual partnership (shirkat al-uqud) and identify the distinguishing feature of each.

Core Idea
Hanafi law recognises four distinct partnership forms, each with unique rules on capital, labour, and liability.

The Mukhtasar al-Quduri opens the chapter on shirkah with this concise classification. Understanding these four names and their core features is the first step toward mastering the detailed rulings that follow. Each type serves a different commercial need.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic names
Learn the four terms: شركة مفاوضة, شركة عنان, شركة الصنائع, شركة الوجوه. Associate each with a key idea: equality (mufawada), limitation ('inan), labour (sana'i'), and credit (wujuh).
Use mnemonic: 'MELC' – Mufawada, 'Inan, Labour, Credit.
2
Identify the distinguishing feature of each
For each type, note what makes it unique: شركة مفاوضة requires equal capital and full mutual agency; شركة عنان allows unequal contributions and no suretyship; شركة الصنائع is based on skill-sharing; شركة الوجوه operates on reputation without capital.
These differences determine profit-sharing and liability.
3
Understand the overall permissibility
All four are permissible (mubah) in Hanafi fiqh, though each has specific conditions. This classification is well-established in Zahir al-Riwayah.
Some later schools may not accept شركة الوجوه or شركة الصنائع as independent forms.

Full equality in capital, actions, and debts is the essence of شركة مفاوضة.

Effect of Inheritance or Gift on شركة مفاوضة
Explains that if a partner inherits or receives a gift, the شركة مفاوضة dissolves into شركة عنان.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a partner in a شركة مفاوضة receives additional wealth and explain why the partnership transforms into شركة عنان.

Core Idea
A شركة مفاوضة dissolves automatically if one partner gains extra wealth — equality is its foundation.

In Hanafi partnership law, شركة مفاوضة requires perfect equality of capital and agency. If that equality is broken by one partner's inheritance or gift, the contract cannot continue as مفاوضة. This lesson examines the automatic transformation into شركة عنان, a more flexible partnership that does not demand equality. Understanding this rule clarifies the strict conditions of مفاوضة and the practical response to changed circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original contract
The partners formed a شركة مفاوضة, which requires equality in capital, profit, agency, and suretyship. Any material imbalance violates its core condition.
This equality is a shart (condition) for the validity of مفاوضة.
2
Identify the disrupting event
One partner inherits wealth or receives a gift that enters his possession, increasing his capital. This event breaks the equality.
The new wealth must be of the type that allows partnership (mal mutaqawwim).
3
Determine the legal consequence
The شركة مفاوضة becomes invalid (تبطل) in its specific form because a fundamental condition is lost. However, the partnership does not become void entirely.
تبطل here means the contract ceases to exist as مفاوضة, not that it becomes haram.
4
Recognize the new contract
The partnership automatically transforms into شركة عنان, which does not require equality. Profit sharing will now be proportional to capital contributions, and mutual suretyship ends.
The transformation is a legal consequence, not a new contract requiring fresh offer and acceptance.
5
Apply the practical effect
The partners must adjust their profit-sharing according to the new capital ratio. Any previous unlimited liability for the other's debts becomes limited.
If the partners wish to continue full equality, they must reconstitute a new مفاوضة after equalizing capital.

Core Idea
A partnership to collect ownerless goods is invalid because each keeps what he personally acquires.

This lesson addresses a specific type of partnership where two or more people agree to collectively gather firewood, grass, or hunt animals. While partnerships in capital and labor are common in fiqh, the Hanafi school rules that this arrangement is invalid. The key reason is that the items are ownerless (mubah) and become owned only through individual acquisition (ihraz). Thus, each partner's catch or collection belongs solely to him, and there is no joint ownership or profit sharing. This highlights a fundamental principle: a valid partnership requires a clear, shared subject matter that can be jointly owned from the start.

Profit Distribution in a Fasid Partnership
Ruling on profit division in a fasid partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for profit distribution in a fasid partnership and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
In a fasid partnership, profit follows capital; any unequal profit condition is void.

This lesson addresses a common question about invalid partnerships (shirkah fasidah). The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: when a partnership is defective, profit must be distributed according to each partner's capital investment, not according to any invalid stipulation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of partnership
The text refers to a 'shirkah fasidah'—an invalid partnership due to a defect in its conditions. Recognize that this is not a valid contract but one that needs corrective rules.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between fasid (invalid) and batil (void); fasid can be rectified, e.g., by adjusting profit shares.
2
Note that profit has occurred
The question assumes profit was earned. The ruling applies only after profit is realized.
3
Apply the rule: profit by capital proportion
The answer states the profit is divided according to the proportion of each partner's capital (رأس المال). This is the operative rule.
This ensures fairness based on investment, not on invalid terms.
4
Void any condition of differentiation
Any stipulation that would give one partner a larger share of profit despite equal or lesser capital is null (باطل).
The condition itself is void, not the entire partnership necessarily.

This lesson addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute regarding zakat in partnership. Since the contract terminates automatically, any actions taken after the event (e.g., trades) are invalid as the partnership no longer exists. No need for a court order or mutual agreement.
