Definition and Conditions of Ijarah (Hiring/Leasing)
Defines ijarah and outlines its validity conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah and identify its essential conditions for validity.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on benefits for compensation; both must be known.

This lesson introduces the foundational definition of ijarah (hiring/leasing) from the Hanafi perspective. The text clarifies that ijarah is not a sale of the asset itself but of its usufruct, and it sets out the key conditions that must be met for the contract to be valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of ijarah
Locate the core definition in the text: 'a contract on benefits for compensation.' Distinguish it from a sale of the asset.
2
List the conditions for validity
Extract the three conditions: benefits must be known, compensation must be known, and compensation must be permissible as a price in sale.
3
Examine how benefits become known
Identify the three methods: by time (duration), by work/specification, or by designation/indication.
4
Understand the requirement for compensation
Compensation must be known by number or weight, and must be something that can legally be a price in a sale.

Definition and Essential Condition of Ijarah
Defining ijarah and stating its essential condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah and identify its essential condition of known usufruct and known wage.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on usufructs for a known wage; both usufruct and wage must be known to avoid gharar.

This lesson introduces the foundational definition of ijarah (leasing/hiring) from the Hanafi matn. The text uses a question-and-answer format to clarify the essence of the contract and the key condition for its validity. Understanding this definition is critical because it distinguishes ijarah from sale and sets the parameters for permissible transactions.

Guided Path
1
Extract the core definition
Identify the key phrase: 'عقد على المنافع بعوض' – a contract upon usufructs for a consideration. Note each component: 'aqd (contract), manafi' (usufructs), 'iwad (consideration).
2
Distinguish ijarah from sale
Sale transfers ownership of a tangible asset; ijarah transfers the right to use (usufruct) for a period. This difference is fundamental in Hanafi fiqh.
Ijarah is a mu'awada (commutative) contract like sale, but the subject matter is manafi' rather than 'ayn.
3
Identify the essential condition
The text states that validity requires the usufruct and the wage to be 'ma'lum' (known). This condition prevents gharar (uncertainty).
Knowledge of usufruct includes specifying its type, duration, and extent; knowledge of wage includes amount and mode of payment.
4
Relate the condition to gharar
Gharar invalidates contracts in Hanafi law. If either the usufruct or the wage is unknown, the contract is fasid (corrupt).
This links to the broader Hanafi principle that exchange contracts require certainty of countervalues.
5
Apply the condition to the wage
The text specifies that any consideration permissible as a price in a sale is permissible as a wage in ijarah. Thus, money, commodities, or services that are valid in sale can be used as ujrah.
The wage must be known by number, weight, or measure; otherwise it is invalid.

Three Methods to Make Usufruct Known in Ijarah
Explaining three methods to make usufruct known in ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three methods by which usufructs are rendered known in Hanafi ijarah and apply each method to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Usufructs in ijarah are known by period, work specification, or direct reference—each eliminates gharar.

The usufruct (manfa'ah) in an ijarah contract must be known to ensure validity and prevent disputes. But usufructs are intangible—how can they be specified? The Hanafi answer provides three clear methods, each suited to different types of contracts. This lesson unpacks those methods with practical examples.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Problem
The consideration (ujrah) is known by number or weight, but usufructs are not measurable that way. Why must the usufruct be known? To avoid gharar (uncertainty).
2
Method 1: By Period (Muddah)
For time-based usufructs (e.g., renting a house), any clearly defined period suffices—a month, a year, etc. The contract is valid for that duration.
No requirement for continuity; the period must be unambiguous.
3
Method 2: By Work and Naming (Amal wa Tasmiyah)
For hiring laborers or animals, specify the type of work (e.g., sewing, dyeing) and the object (e.g., this garment). Also specify the quantity/distance for transport or riding.
Vague descriptions like 'do some work' are insufficient.
4
Method 3: By Specification and Pointing (Ta'yin wa Isharah)
When the subject matter is physically present (e.g., this food), pointing to it makes the usufruct known. The destination must still be known.
Used for specific movable assets.
5
Apply to Examples
Given a scenario, identify which method applies and confirm the usufruct is known. If none applies, the contract would be fasid.

This passage elaborates on the condition that the usufruct (manfa'ah) in an ijarah contract must be known (ma'lumah). The question acknowledges that the consideration (ujrah) is known by standard measures, but usufructs are intangible and require a different method. The answer provides three distinct ways usufructs become known: first, by time duration (muddah), such as renting a house for a month or a year, or land for farming for a season; any clear time period suffices. This classification is a key feature of Hanafi ijarah law.

Renting Houses and Shops: Permissible Uses and Restrictions
Ruling on renting houses and shops with lessee's usage restrictions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default permissible uses of rented houses and shops and identify the three trades that require the lessor's permission.

Core Idea
A lessee may use the property freely except for blacksmithing, fulling, and grinding — these need the lessor's consent.

This lesson addresses a common practical question: when you rent a house or shop, what are you allowed to do inside it? The Hanafi school gives broad freedom to the lessee, but places clear restrictions on trades that physically harm the property. Understanding these limits protects both the lessor's asset and the lessee's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object and purpose of the rental
Distinguish between houses (dur, for residence) and shops (hawanit, for trade/industry). Note the default use for each.
Houses are presumed for residence; shops for business.
2
State the general principle of lessee's freedom
The lessee may do whatever lawful act he wishes within the property, as long as it does not damage the asset.
This is the default – broad permission.
3
Memorize the three prohibited trades
Blacksmithing (al-hidadah), fulling (al-qassarah), and grinding (al-tahn) are not permissible without explicit permission.
These trades cause excessive damage (fire, water, vibration).
4
Understand the rationale and exception
The prohibition is based on the inherent damage these activities cause. If the lessor specifically permits them, they become allowed.
Permission overrides the default restriction.

Validity Conditions for Renting Land and Courtyard
Validity conditions for renting land and courtyard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid lease of agricultural land or courtyard and identify the implied rights of irrigation and access.

Core Idea
A lease of land for agriculture is valid only if the crop is specified or a general stipulation is made.

In this lesson, we examine the conditions for renting agricultural land and a courtyard (saha) for building or planting. The Hanafi school requires that the usufruct (manfa'a) be known; thus, for agricultural land, the lessee must either name the crop to be grown or stipulate a general option to plant anything. The rights of irrigation and access are automatically included as they are essential for use. Understanding these conditions prevents invalid contracts and disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of leases
The passage discusses two separate cases: renting agricultural land for farming and renting a courtyard (saha) for building or planting trees. Recognize that different conditions may apply.
Agricultural land and courtyard are both real property but used for different purposes.
2
Extract the condition for agricultural land
The contract for agricultural land is not valid (sahih) unless the lessee names the specific crop (e.g., wheat) or stipulates that he may plant whatever he wishes. This condition ensures the usufruct is determined and avoids gharar (uncertainty).
If the crop is not specified and no general stipulation is made, the contract is fasid (invalid).
3
Note the implied adjuncts
The rights of irrigation (shurb) and access (tariq) are automatically included in the lease even if not explicitly mentioned. They are essential for the use of agricultural land.
This is because the lease would be impossible without them, so they are considered part of the contract by necessity.
4
State the ruling for courtyard
Renting a courtyard for building or planting trees is permissible (ja'iz). The passage does not specify additional conditions for validity beyond the general requirement of determining the usufruct (which is met by specifying the purpose).
The same implied rights may apply depending on context, but not explicitly stated here.
5
Summarize the validity conditions
For agricultural land: either specify crop or stipulate general planting option. For courtyard: permissible for building/planting. In both, the lease is valid if the usufruct is known.
These rules are from the Hanafi school, as presented in the text.

End-of-Term Obligations for Structures and Plants
Obligations at lease termination for structures and plants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the lessee's default obligation to remove buildings and plants at lease end and explain the landowner's alternative options.

Core Idea
The lessee must remove structures and plants unless the landowner chooses to pay for materials or allow them to remain.

When a lease of a courtyard (saha) for building or planting ends, the land must be returned. The Hanafi school imposes a clear duty on the lessee to remove any additions and hand over the land empty. However, the landowner has the right to retain the structures/plants by paying their salvage value or to allow them to remain, with ownership separating. This ruling balances the rights of both parties and prevents unjust enrichment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default obligation
The passage says: 'يلزمه أن يقلع البناء والغرس ويسلمها فارغة' – he is obligated to remove the building and planting and hand it over empty. This is the default rule: the lessee must dismantle and clear the land.
The obligation (lazim) indicates it is mandatory.
2
Analyze the landowner's first option
The landowner may choose to pay the lessee the value of the materials 'as removed' (i.e., the salvage value) and take ownership of the structures and plants. This allows the landowner to keep the additions without them being dismantled.
The value is not the construction cost but the worth of the materials when detached.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of rulings from the Hanafi school on renting real estate (houses, shops, lands, courtyards). The general principle is that renting is permissible for permissible uses, with specific restrictions on certain trades (blacksmithing, bleaching, grinding) unless the lessor permits. Subletting and Conditional Rent (The Attar-Blacksmith Dispute)
Rulings on subletting and conditional rent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling on subletting and conditional rent, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding conditional rent.

Core Idea
Subletting is generally allowed; conditional rent (different rates for different tenants) is valid per Abu Hanifa but invalid per the two companions.

When a lessor stipulates that only a specific person may occupy the property, can the tenant sublet to someone else? The Hanafi school allows subletting because property does not change with the user. More controversially, if the rent is conditioned on the type of tenant (e.g., attar vs. blacksmith), Abu Hanifa holds the contract valid, while his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it invalid due to uncertainty. This lesson explores both issues.

Guided Path
1
Understand the ruling on subletting
Even if the lessor stipulates that a specific person must dwell, the tenant may sublet to someone else because property does not vary with the user.
This applies to real estate, not to services.
2
Understand conditional rent scenario
The lessor says: 'If you put an attar (perfume seller), rent is 1 dirham; if a blacksmith, 2 dirhams per month.'
3
State the position of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa: The contract is valid. Whichever use occurs, the named rent is due.
He sees the condition as a valid regulation tied to actual use.
4
State the position of the two companions
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: The lease is invalid (fasid) because the rent is uncertain (gharar) – it depends on the tenant's choice.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, tenant pays the named rent. Under Sahibayn, the contract is void, so the tenant must pay the market rate (ujrat al-mithl).
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

Rent Liability: Possession, Usurpation, and Termination
Rulings on rent liability and termination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules regarding rent liability upon possession, the effect of usurpation, defects, and automatic dissolution of the lease.

Core Idea
Rent is due upon possession even if unused; usurpation before use drops rent; serious defects or destruction dissolve the lease automatically.

Once a tenant takes possession (qabd) of the rented property, the rent is due regardless of actual use. However, if a third party usurps (ghasb) the property before the tenant uses it, the rent is dropped. If the property is destroyed or a vital utility (like water) is cut off, the lease dissolves automatically without need for rescission. This lesson covers these scenarios and the right to rescind for defects.

Guided Path
1
Understand rent liability upon possession
The tenant owes rent once he takes possession, even if he never dwells, because he is able to benefit (tamakkun min al-intifa').
This is based on the idea that the lessor has made the benefit available.
2
Understand the effect of usurpation
If a usurper (ghasib) seizes the property before the tenant uses it, all rent is dropped. If the tenant used it briefly, rent is due for that period and drops for the remainder.
Usurpation prevents the tenant from benefiting, so rent is abated.
3
Understand the right to rescind for defects
If a defect harms the intended use (e.g., a broken sewer), the tenant may rescind (faskh) the lease.
This is an option (khiyar al-ayb) similar to sale.
4
Understand automatic dissolution
If the house collapses, or water supply to the land/mill is cut off, the lease dissolves automatically without need for rescission.
These are cases where the subject matter is destroyed or the benefit is completely lost.

The original position of Abu Hanifa is that it is not allowed, but later scholars faced a pressing need to preserve the Quran and other religious duties. They developed a nuanced exception that allows hiring for teaching Quran, fiqh, adhan, and imamate, but not for every act of worship. We will trace the legal reasoning and the limits of this exception.

Guided Path
1
State the original Hanafi principle
Recall that according to al-Hidayah, any act of worship that is specifically incumbent on a particular Muslim (ta'ah yakhtassu biha al-muslim) cannot be hired for. This is the starting point.
Examples: adhan of a specific mu'adhdhin, imamate of a specific imam, teaching Quran for a specific teacher.
2
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i allows hiring for any act that is not specifically required of the hired person. Understand the difference: Hanafis focus on the specificity of the obligation, Shafi'is focus on the generality of the act.
Later Hanafi scholars adopted the Shafi'i view for certain cases out of necessity.
3
Identify the necessity that prompted the exception
The later scholars from Balkh observed that huffaz (memorizers of Quran) were scarce, people were negligent, and without allowing wages for teaching, the Quran might be lost. Thus they deemed it good (istihsan) to permit hiring for teaching Quran.
This is a clear example of istihsan overriding qiyas.
4
Trace the expansion to other acts
The exception was extended to teaching fiqh, adhan, and imamate, as recorded in al-Nihayah and Rawdat al-Zindawisi. The rationale is similar: these are essential communal obligations (fard kifayah) that would be neglected without financial incentive.
The extension is not automatic; each case must be evaluated for necessity.
5
Apply the limit: what remains excluded
The later fatwa explicitly does not permit hiring for other acts of worship, such as reciting Quran in tarawih. Shaykh Rashid Ahmad al-Kanqohi ruled that hiring for 'isma' (making heard) in tarawih is haram, because there is no necessity comparable to that for teaching or adhan.
The exception is limited to preserving core religious knowledge and essential communal functions.

Liability of the Common Hired Worker – Dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Question-answer on the division of hired workers and the dispute on liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the common hired worker (ajir mushtarak) and the specific worker, and state the differing rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding liability for lost property.

Core Idea
The common hired worker is a trustee (amin) under Abu Hanifa, not liable for loss without transgression; the Sahibayn hold him liable as a guarantor.

This lesson focuses on the liability (daman) of the hired worker (ajir). The jurists divide workers into two types: common (ajir mushtarak) and specific (ajir khas). The key dispute is whether the common worker is a trustee (amanah) or a guarantor (damin) for property in his possession. Imam Abu Hanifa considers the worker a trustee, while his two companions hold him liable. We will examine the implications of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of ajir
The common worker (ajir mushtarak) works for multiple people and is not exclusive (e.g., dyer, washerman). The specific worker (ajir khas) is hired exclusively by one person, like a personal driver.
The classification affects liability for property in the worker's possession.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position on the common worker
Abu Hanifa rules that property in the hands of a common worker is a trust (amanah). If it is destroyed without his fault (transgression or negligence), he is not liable.
This is based on the idea that the worker is not a usurper; he holds the property for the benefit of the owner.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the common worker is liable (daman) for the property, even if it is destroyed without his fault. They view him as a guarantor because he benefits from the work.
This is a stricter view to protect the owner's property.
4
Identify the legal basis for each view
Abu Hanifa draws analogy with a trustee (amin) who is not liable unless negligent. The Sahibayn analogize to a borrower (musta'ir) who is liable for loss.
The difference stems from whether the worker is seen as benefiting the owner (trust) or benefiting himself (liability).
5
Determine the practical consequence
If a dyer loses or damages a garment without fault, under Abu Hanifa the owner bears the loss; under the Sahibayn, the dyer must compensate.
The mufta bihi today is often the view of the Sahibayn for the common worker, to protect owners.

Ruling on whether an artisan may delegate work when stipulated or unspecified

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A condition binds; without it, delegation is the default.

This lesson addresses a common question in hire contracts for services: when can an artisan (tailor, carpenter, etc.) delegate the work to another? The answer depends on whether the hiring party stipulated personal performance. We will examine two clear rulings from the text that hinge on the presence or absence of such a stipulation.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios
The text presents two cases: one with an explicit stipulation that the artisan must work himself, and one where the contract is absolute with no such condition.
The questions are structured as Q&A, each with a distinct answer.
2
Extract the ruling for each scenario
For the stipulated case, the artisan may NOT delegate ("ليس له ذلك"). For the absolute case, delegation IS permissible ("جاز للصانع").
The ruling changes completely based on the stipulation.
3
Identify the decisive condition
The presence or absence of a stipulation that the artisan work personally is the sole factor that changes the ruling. The condition creates a contractual obligation.
Hanafis consider valid conditions binding (وفاء بالشرط).
4
Generalize the legal principle
When a valid condition is agreed upon, it must be honored. In its absence, the default rule of permissibility applies, allowing the artisan to delegate because the obligation is to deliver the result, not personal service.
This aligns with the Hanafi maxim: العبرة في العقود بالمقاصد والمعاني.
5
Apply to variations
If the contract is for a specific artisan renowned for his skill (ajir khas), the stipulation might be implied even if not explicit. However, this passage focuses on explicit stipulation.
Other schools may differ; Hanafis strictly enforce the stipulation.

Alternative Wages Based on Time: Dispute of Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn
Hanafi internal dispute on validity of wages varying by time of completion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on time-based wage alternatives in ijarah, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Core Idea
When a wage depends on today vs. tomorrow, Abu Hanifa voids the lower wage; the Sahibayn enforce both conditions.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about a contract where the wage changes based on whether the work is done today or tomorrow. Abu Hanifa considers the lower wage for tomorrow invalid, while the Sahibayn uphold both as valid. Understanding their reasoning and the practical outcome is central to grasping Hanafi contract law.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Identify the contractual terms: wage of 1 dirham if sewn today, 1/2 dirham if sewn tomorrow.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
If the work is done today, he gets 1 dirham. If tomorrow, he gets ujrat mithl (fair wage) capped at 1/2 dirham.
Abu Hanifa deems the 'tomorrow' condition fasid (corrupt).
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position
Both conditions are valid (ja'izan). The worker gets exactly 1 dirham for today, 1/2 dirham for tomorrow.
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold the contract fully effective.
4
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Hanifa likely sees the lower wage as creating gharar (uncertainty) or a penalty for delay. The Sahibayn emphasize party autonomy and known wages per option.
Hadith 'al-muslimun 'ala shurutihim' (Muslims are bound by their conditions) supports the Sahibayn.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, worker gets ujrat mithl (max 1/2) for tomorrow; under Sahibayn, exactly 1/2.
The difference is capped vs. exact lower wage.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a contract where the wage differs based on whether the work is completed today or tomorrow. The hirer offers a higher wage for same-day completion and a lower wage for next-day completion. Abu Hanifa's position: If the tailor sews it today, he gets the stipulated dirham; if he delays to tomorrow, he is not bound by the lower stipulated wage but instead receives the 'ujrat mithl' (fair market wage for that work), which cannot exceed half a dirham. Abu Hanifa considers the condition of paying half a dirham for tomorrow invalid because it encourages delay, resembles a penalty, or creates uncertainty that is akin to gharar. His view ensures the tailor does not lose out unfairly. Both time frames are known, so there is no prohibited gharar. The contract is fully effective, and the tailor receives the wage according to whichever condition is fulfilled. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school is the view of Abu Hanifa according to some later authorities, but the Sahibayn's view is also strong and adopted by scholars like al-Karkhi. The text presents the dispute without tarjih, so students should know both positions.

Who Is Believed in an Owner-Artisan Dispute?
Ruling on owner-artisan dispute over instructions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling in a dispute between owner and artisan over instructions and apply the two compensation options.

Core Idea
The owner's word is taken with oath over the artisan's in a dispute about instructions.

When you give cloth to a tailor or dyer and later disagree about what you ordered, whose word counts? This lesson unpacks a clear Hanafi rule: the owner's claim is preferred, but only with an oath. We'll explore why this protects the owner and what options arise if the artisan deviated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
The owner gives cloth to an artisan (tailor/dyer). After work, they disagree: owner says specific instructions were given (e.g., qaba or red), artisan says he followed orders. Isolate the exact conflict.
The dispute is about the content of the instruction, not the quality of work.
2
Apply the procedural rule: owner's word with oath
The Hanafi position: the owner's statement is accepted, but he must take an oath. This shifts liability to the artisan if he deviated.
Oath is required; without it, the owner's claim is not automatically accepted.
3
Analyze the consequence: artisan's liability (daman)
Once the owner swears, the artisan is liable for violating orders. The owner has two choices: (a) take the value of the unworked cloth (no wage) or (b) take the altered cloth and pay fair wage (ujrat mithl), capped at the agreed wage.
The fair wage cannot exceed the originally agreed wage to protect the artisan from inflated demands.
4
Understand the rationale
The owner is the principal (amir) and knows his own instruction; the artisan is the agent and could be suspected of deviating. Thus the owner's account is given precedence.
This is specific to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may differ.

The legal reasoning is that the owner is the principal (amir) and the artisan is the agent (ma'mur); the principal's account of his own instruction is given precedence because the owner knows what he commanded. Additionally, the artisan is the one performing the work and could be suspected of deviating, so the burden of proof falls on him to show he followed orders, but since he cannot prove it, the owner's oath suffices. This protects the owner from being forced to pay for unwanted work while also preventing the artisan from being completely uncompensated if the owner chooses the second option. The ruling applies to cases where the artisan clearly deviates from orders; if there is ambiguity, the owner's oath is decisive.

Dispute Over Whether Wage Was Agreed
Intra-Hanafi dispute on who is believed when wage is denied

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions regarding a wage dispute between an owner and an artisan, and apply the relevant position based on custom or profession.

Core Idea
When no wage is agreed, who is believed? Abu Hanifa trusts the owner; the Sahibayn consider custom.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: the owner of a cloth claims he gave it for work without a wage, while the artisan claims a wage was agreed. The three imams differ on who should be believed. This lesson will walk you through each position and the legal reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the dispute scenario
Identify the parties: owner (صاحب الثوب) and artisan (صانع). The conflict is over the existence of a wage agreement.
No external evidence is mentioned; only the conflicting claims.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the owner's statement, supported by his oath, is accepted. The artisan must prove the wage agreement. This follows the general principle that the defendant's oath prevails (البينة على المدعي واليمين على من أنكر).
The artisan is the claimant (مُدَّعٍ), the owner is the defendant (مُنْكِر).
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf considers the custom (عادة) of the owner. If it is customary for this owner to pay wages for such work, then the artisan is entitled to the wage; otherwise not. He introduces 'urf as a tie-breaker.
Focus is on the owner's habitual behavior.
4
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad al-Shaybani looks at the artisan's profession (ابتذال). If the artisan is a professional who commonly works for wages (مبتذل), his claim is more plausible, and his word with oath is accepted.
Focus is on the artisan's standing—if he works only for pay, his claim aligns with his norm.
5
Contrast the practical outcome (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, owner wins with oath; under Abu Yusuf, result depends on owner's custom; under Muhammad, if artisan is professional, artisan wins with oath. Later authorities often follow Abu Hanifa, but custom may shift the weight.
The dispute shows how different presumptions lead to different results.

Death of a Party and Dissolution of Ijarah
Ruling on dissolution of ijarah upon death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for dissolution of ijarah upon death of a party and distinguish between contracts made for oneself and for another.

Core Idea
Ijarah dissolves upon death of a party who contracted for himself, but continues if contracted for another's benefit.

When one party to an ijarah contract dies, the effect on the contract depends on whose benefit the contract was made. The classical Hanafi ruling differentiates between a personal contract and one made on behalf of another, such as by a guardian or agent. This distinction protects the rights of the ultimate beneficiary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the event
The act is a valid ijarah contract, and the event is the death of one of the two contracting parties (lessor or lessee).
Assume the contract was valid and ongoing at the time of death.
2
Determine for whom the contract was made
Ask whether the deceased party contracted for his own benefit (li nafsih) or for the benefit of another person (li ghayrihi).
Examples: agent for principal, guardian for minor, father for child.
3
Apply the rule
If for himself, the ijarah dissolves automatically (infaskhat). If for another, the contract continues.
Dissolution is immediate; the contract is not inherited by the heirs.
4
Understand the rationale
Contracts for oneself are tied to the individual's capacity; contracts for others transfer the benefit to the surviving beneficiary, so they persist.
This protects the rights of the dependent party.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
السؤال: إذا مات أحد المتعاقدين، فهل يبقى عقد الإجارة على ما كان؟ الجواب: ينظر فيه: إن كان عقدها لنفسه انفسخت الإجارة بموته، وإن كان عقدها لغيره كالوكيل لموكله وكالولي للصبي، أو الوالد لولده لم تنفسخ.

Commentary
This passage addresses the effect of death on an ijarah contract. The ruling distinguishes between who the contract is made for. If the deceased party (either lessor or lessee) entered into the contract for his own personal benefit (li nafsih), the ijarah is dissolved (munfasikh) upon his death. The reason is that ijarah is a contract that creates mutual obligations over time, and death terminates the personal capacity to fulfill or receive the benefit. For example, if a person rents a house for himself and dies, his heirs are not bound by the remainder of the lease. However, if the contract was made on behalf of another person, such as an agent (wakil) for his principal (muwakkil), a guardian (wali) for a minor (sabi), or a father for his child (walad), then death of the agent/guardian/father does not dissolve the contract because the contract is for the benefit of the principal/minor/child, and that beneficiary survives. The ruling ensures that the rights of the beneficiary are not cut off by the death of the intermediary. The key is the intention and capacity: contracts made for oneself are tied to the individual; contracts made for others transfer the benefit to the other party, so they continue.

Key Terms
انفسخت (infaskhat): The ijarah contract becomes automatically dissolved (munfasikh) upon the death of a party when contracted for oneself.
الوكيل (al-wakil): An agent who contracts on behalf of another (muwakkil).
الولي (al-wali): A guardian who contracts on behalf of a minor or ward.

Excuses for Rescission of Ijarah
Question-answer style presenting excuses for ijarah rescission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on rescission of ijarah due to valid excuses and apply it to scenarios involving loss of capital or bankruptcy.

Core Idea
Ijarah can be rescinded when a valid excuse makes performance impossible or harmful.

In Hanafi fiqh, ijarah (lease) contracts are normally binding, but the law recognizes that unforeseen circumstances can make continuation unjust or impossible. This passage presents two classic examples: the lessee who loses his capital, and the lessor who becomes bankrupt. Understanding these excuses helps you see how the Shariah balances contractual obligations with preventing hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage asks whether ijarah can be rescinded due to excuses (a'dhar). The answer is yes. Focus on the general principle: valid excuses permit dissolution.
The ruling is not absolute – it requires a genuine excuse that makes performance extremely difficult.
2
Extract the two examples
First example: a lessee rents a shop to trade, then loses his capital (mal). Second example: a lessor leases property, then becomes bankrupt with debts that can only be paid by selling the property.
Both involve financial loss, but from different sides of the contract.
3
Analyze why each is an excuse
In the first, the lessee's purpose (trade) becomes impossible without capital; continuing would be wasteful. In the second, the lessor's creditors have a superior claim; the lease would block sale of the asset.
The excuse must defeat the purpose or cause severe hardship that outweighs the contract's binding nature.
4
Note the judicial role
In the bankruptcy scenario, the judge rescinds the contract and sells the property. This shows that rescission may require judicial authority when third-party rights (creditors) are involved.
The first example does not mention a judge, implying the lessee may rescind directly.
5
Formulate the general rule
An ijarah contract may be rescinded if a valid excuse arises that makes continuation extremely difficult or impossible, provided the excuse is genuine and unforeseeable.
The list of excuses is not exhaustive; other valid excuses (e.g., illness, transfer) are covered in longer fiqh works.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of ijarah being dissolved (munfaskhah) due to valid excuses (a'dhar). The Hanafi school allows termination of an ijarah contract under certain extraordinary circumstances that make continued performance harmful or impossible. Two examples are given: (1) A person rents a shop for trade, but then loses all his capital (mal). Without capital, the purpose of the rental (trade) is defeated, and the lease becomes burdensome without benefit. This constitutes an excuse that allows the lessee to rescind the contract. (2) A person who has leased out his property (as lessor) becomes bankrupt (muflis) and has debts that cannot be paid except by selling that property. In such a case, the judge (qadi) may rescind the ijarah contract and sell the property to satisfy the debts. This overrides the lessee's right to continue the lease because the creditors' rights take precedence. In both cases, the rescission is by judicial order to prevent hardship. The ruling protects individuals from being locked into contracts that become impossible or unjust due to unforeseen circumstances. The list of excuses is not exhaustive; other valid excuses may include illness, transfer of employment, etc., as detailed in fiqh works.
