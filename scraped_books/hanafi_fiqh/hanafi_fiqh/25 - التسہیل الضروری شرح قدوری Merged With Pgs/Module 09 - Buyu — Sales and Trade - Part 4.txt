Core Idea
When part of a purchased item is reclaimed, the buyer may either take the remainder at a proportional price or cancel the sale.

When a buyer purchases an item and later discovers that part of it rightfully belongs to another, the sale is partially invalidated. The Hanafi school provides the buyer with a protective option (khiyar) to avoid loss. This short ruling from the Quduri matn outlines the two clear choices the buyer has.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A third party reclaims part of a vessel that was sold. The buyer has paid part of the price but not the full amount.
The key elements: partial reclamation, partial payment.
2
Locate the ruling
The Hanafi ruling gives the buyer a choice (khiyar) – he is not forced to accept the defective contract.
3
Parse the two options
Option A: Take the remaining part for his share of the price (proportional reduction). Option B: Rescind the entire sale and get a full refund of what was paid.
The price adjustment is based on the proportion of the reclaimed part to the whole.
4
Understand the rationale
The khiyar protects the buyer from loss due to a defect in title. The seller could only transfer ownership of the part he actually owned.
This is a form of khiyar al-'ayb (option for defect) applied to a title defect.

No option when part of a uniform object is reclaimed; the sale continues proportionally.

The buyer has no option (khiyar) to cancel the sale. The sale remains valid for the remaining portion.

The buyer must accept the remaining silver at a proportionally reduced price. For example, if half is reclaimed, he pays half the original price and takes half the lump.
The price reduction is based on the share of the reclaimed portion.

Core Idea
In mixed currency exchanges, pair each species with its own kind — the excess between different species is permissible.

This lesson examines a specific case of currency exchange (sarf) where two types of money appear on both sides of a transaction. The Hanafi school uses a systematic pairing method to validate such exchanges while ensuring the condition of hand-to-hand delivery (taqabud) is met. Understanding this ruling will sharpen your ability to analyse complex sales involving multiple currencies.

Identify the transaction structure

2
Apply the pairing method
Match dirhams with dirhams and dinars with dinars. The dirhams on one side are paired with dirhams on the other, and similarly for dinars.
This is the key Hanafi technique: each species is treated as exchanged for its own kind, regardless of quantity.
3
Verify the condition of hand-to-hand delivery
Since the species involved differ (dirham vs dinar), the excess in each pairing is not riba al-fadl. However, the entire exchange must be immediate and hand-to-hand.
Hand-to-hand delivery is a condition for all sarf transactions.
4
State the ruling
The sale is permissible (ja'iz). Each species is considered as substituted for the other species, making the transaction valid.
The ruling is based on the principle that excess is permissible between different ribawi items.

Core Idea
Exchanging different quality dirhams is valid if total quantity is equal.

In this lesson, we examine a simple transaction: selling two sound dirhams and one bad dirham for one sound and two bad dirhams. The Hanafi ruling is clear: it is valid. But why? The key is that the species is the same (silver) and the total quantity (three dirhams each side) is equal, so no prohibited excess exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items exchanged
List the two sides: Seller gives: 2 sound dirhams (ṣaḥīḥ) + 1 bad dirham (ghillah). Buyer gives: 1 sound dirham + 2 bad dirhams.
Sound = good quality, bad = degraded.
2
Classify the dirham types
Both sound and bad dirhams belong to the same species (silver). Quality difference does not change the species.
In Hanafi fiqh, riba al-fadl applies only when exchanging same species; quality difference is irrelevant if weight/number is equal.
3
Check equality of quantity
Total dirhams on each side: 3 dirhams. The total number (and presumably weight) is equal. No excess exists.
Equality of weight is required for same-quality dirhams, but when quality differs, equality of quantity suffices.
4
Apply the Hanafi ruling
Since the species is the same and the total is equal, the exchange is valid and permissible. Hand-to-hand transfer is essential.
The sale is ṣaḥīḥ (valid). No riba because no excess.

Ruling on Mixed Coins: Majority Content Determines Legal Classification
Ruling on selling coins containing alloy for pure coins of the same species

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the majority-content test to classify mixed coins (dirhams/dinars) and state the ruling when sold for their own kind with excess.

Core Idea
Coins are ruled by what dominates their content — majority silver/gold keeps the riba ruling; majority alloy makes them commodities.

In this lesson, we examine a classic Hanafi ruling on coins that contain alloy (ghish). The key is the concept of 'ghalabah' (majority). The answer distinguishes two scenarios: when the pure metal dominates, the coin retains its ribawi status; when the alloy dominates, it becomes a commodity ('urūḍ), allowing sale with excess. This ruling is essential for understanding how physical composition affects monetary exchanges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The question deals with dirhams (silver) or dinars (gold) that contain an alloy (ghish). The sale is considered with their own kind (e.g., mixed dirhams for mixed dirhams) with excess (tafadul).
This is a type of riba al-fadl case.
2
Determine the majority content
The ruling hinges on whether the majority of the coin's mass is the pure metal (silver/gold) or the alloy. This is the critical condition.
'Ghalabah' means more than half by weight.
3
Apply the ruling based on majority
If the majority is pure metal, the coin is treated like pure silver/gold: selling it for its own kind with excess is prohibited (riba). If the majority is alloy, it is treated as a commodity: selling with excess is permissible, but must be structured as an exchange of different species.
The permissibility requires converting the species, i.e., treating the pure portion as one species and the alloy as another.
4
Understand the practical condition
When the coin is a commodity, the transaction must involve hand-to-hand transfer (taqabud) and the exchange is effectively between the pure part and the alloy part, making it permissible.
This avoids riba by ensuring it is not a sale of like for like with excess.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on effect of currency obsolescence before payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on the effect of currency obsolescence before payment, and identify which position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
When alloyed coins become obsolete before payment, Abu Hanifa voids the sale; the Sahibayn impose value payment.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a sale concluded with alloyed coins that become obsolete before the seller receives payment. The core question is whether the contract survives and, if so, how to compensate the seller. The three great Hanafi imams offer different solutions, reflecting their views on the nature of currency and the point at which value is determined.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and identify the key legal issue
A buyer purchases goods using alloyed coins that become obsolete (kasad) before the seller takes possession of the payment. The key issue: is the contract still valid, and what does the buyer owe?
Kasad means the coins are no longer traded as currency.
2
Identify the three Hanafi positions and their rulings
Abu Hanifa: sale is void (batil). Abu Yusuf: sale valid, buyer pays value at sale date. Muhammad: sale valid, buyer pays value at obsolescence date.
The Sahibayn agree the contract stands but differ on the valuation date.
3
Compare the underlying reasoning
Abu Hanifa sees the currency as essential consideration that has failed, so the contract cannot stand. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad treat the contract as binding and substitute a value payment to preserve it.
Abu Hanifa's view is stricter; the Sahibayn are more pragmatic.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its rationale
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Kasani, al-Marghinani) prefer Abu Yusuf's view because it provides a fixed valuation point and avoids nullifying contracts.
The mufta bihi is the value at the date of sale.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Salam requires a known capital, place of delivery, and deferred term—but the Sahibayn relax the first two if the capital is a specific item.

Salam is a forward sale with strict conditions to avoid gharar. This lesson focuses on the essential conditions and a key intra-madhhab disagreement that affects how those conditions are applied in practice.

This dispute only arises when the capital is a 'known specific item' (mu'ayyan).

Determine the mufta bihi
Though the text does not explicitly state the relied-upon view, the madhhab adopts Abu Hanifa's stricter position to minimize risk.
Mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as per later Hanafi sources.

Permissible and Impermissible Commodities in Salam
Lists permissible and impermissible subject matters with required specifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine which commodities are valid for salam based on the required specifications and explain why jewels and beads are excluded.

Core Idea
Salam is valid for goods that can be precisely described (like cloth and bricks) but invalid for items like jewels where specification is impossible.

Not every commodity qualifies for a salam contract. The passage provides examples: clothes, bricks, silk are permissible if detailed; jewels and beads are not. This lesson examines the logic behind these rulings.

Identify permissible commodities and their required specifications

The more variables, the more specification required.
2
Identify impermissible commodities
Jewels (jawahir) and beads (khurz) are not permissible for salam. Reason: they vary greatly and cannot be adequately described to eliminate gharar.
This is a ruling from the passage.
3
Understand the rationale
Goods that can be standardized (like bricks) are salam-worthy; goods that are unique (like jewels) are not.
The key is 'mamqur al-taslim' — deliverable in a standard form.

Additional Restrictions: Disposition, Market Availability, and Invalid Specifications
States additional restrictions on salam: no disposition before possession, condition of market availability, and invalid salam in personal measures or specific produce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the restrictions on disposing of capital and subject matter before possession, the condition that the subject matter must be continuously available, and the invalidity of salam in specific personal measures or produce.

Core Idea
Salam contracts forbid pre-possession disposition, require market availability throughout the term, and reject specifications linked to specific individuals or locations.

Beyond the core conditions, salam has additional rules to protect both parties. This lesson covers three important restrictions: no disposal before possession, the requirement that the goods remain available in the market, and the invalidity of salam tied to specific personal measures or specific sources.

Guided Path
1
Understand the restriction on disposition before possession
Neither the capital nor the subject matter can be used by the other party until possession is taken. This includes partnerships and assignments.
This prevents riba-like exchanges and ensures the subject matter is actually in the market.
2
Understand the condition of continuous market availability
The subject matter must exist and be available in the market from the time of contract until the term expires. It should not become extinct.
If the goods disappear from the market, the contract is void as delivery becomes uncertain.
3
Understand invalid specifications: personal measures and specific produce
Salam in the measure of a specific person's arm or basket is invalid because it is not standardized. Similarly, salam in the fruit of a specific tree or food of a specific village is invalid due to uncertainty of yield.
The subject matter must be determinable by general market standards.

This passage explains the procedural stages of shuf‘a and includes an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of delay after the right has been fixed.

First, the text explains three distinct stages: 
1. Thubut (establishment): The right of shuf‘a arises immediately upon the conclusion of the sale contract. At this point, the right exists but is not yet 'fixed'.
2. Istiqrar (fixing): The right becomes fixed (i.e., binding) once the pre-emptor performs 'ishhad' — formally calling two witnesses to testify that he claims his right. This is done in two parts:

Core Idea
Preemption is lost if you delay testimony or testify incompletely.

The right of preemption (shuf'a) allows a co-owner or neighbor to step into a sale. But this right is fragile: it requires immediate and proper testimony. This lesson examines two specific failures that cause the right to become void, as explained in the classical Hanafi text.

Commentary
This passage provides two rulings concerning the loss of preemption (shuf'a) due to failure of testimony (ishhad). The first ruling states that if the preemptor knows of the sale and is able to produce witnesses but deliberately refrains from testifying, his right of preemption is void. The second ruling states that even if he does testify in the gathering (majlis) but fails to testify specifically against either the buyer or the seller, or fails to testify at the location of the property, the preemption is also void. The key condition is that the testimony must be immediate and properly directed.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf‘a): The right of preemption, allowing a co-owner or neighbor to purchase a sold property by substituting the buyer.
الإشهاد (al-ishhād): The act of having witnesses testify to one's claim of preemption immediately upon learning of the sale.

Key Takeaway
Preemption is lost if the preemptor fails to testify immediately upon learning of the sale, or if his testimony is not directed at the contracting parties or the property.
