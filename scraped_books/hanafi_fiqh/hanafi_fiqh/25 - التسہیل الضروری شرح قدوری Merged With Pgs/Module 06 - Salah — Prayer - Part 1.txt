The Ruling of Salah as an Individual Obligation
Question and answer establishing the obligation of salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that salah is fard al-ayn (individual obligation) and explain the consequence of denying its obligation.

Core Idea
Salah is an individual obligation (fard); denying it removes one from the fold of Islam.

This passage is the opening of the Book of Salah, establishing its supreme importance. It answers a fundamental question: what is the ruling of salah? The answer is clear and emphatic — salah is fard, an obligation, and its denial leads to apostasy. This lesson anchors the foundational ruling upon which all further details of salah (conditions, pillars, etc.) are built.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Focus on the answer: salah is the greatest pillar after the Shahadah. The ruling is fard (obligatory).
Note that the passage explicitly uses the term 'fard'.
2
Recognize the scope of obligation
The obligation applies to every adult, sane person, male and female. This is fard al-ayn (individual obligation), not fard kifayah (community obligation).
The qualification 'mukallaf' (accountable) includes only those who are adult and sane.
3
Understand the consequence of denial
Denying the obligation (fardiyyah) removes one from the religion of Islam. This is ijma', as salah is a necessarily known part of the religion.
Hanafi: fard is established by definitive evidence (qat'i), so denial is kufr.

Prayer Times and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Zuhr End
Defines the five prayer times with intra-Hanafi dispute on Dhuhr end time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five prayer times and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the end time of Zuhr prayer, including its practical consequence.

Core Idea
Zuhr end time: shadow equal (Sahibayn) or twice (Abu Hanifa) — a difference that shifts Asr start.

This passage from a classical Hanafi text defines the five daily prayer times and introduces a key internal disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on when Zuhr time ends. Understanding this dispute is essential for correctly calculating prayer schedules in the Hanafi school and recognizing the flexibility within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal significance of prayer times
Prayer times are a condition (shart) for the validity of the obligatory salah. If a prayer is performed outside its prescribed time, it is not accepted. The text lists the five times with their beginnings and ends.
This is a shart of wujub and ada (condition of obligation and validity).
2
Locate the definition of Zuhr end time in the text
The text gives two definitions: Abu Hanifa says Zuhr ends when the shadow of everything becomes twice its length (excluding fay' al-zawal); Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) say it ends when the shadow becomes equal to its length (excluding fay' al-zawal).
Pay attention to 'سوى فيء الزوال' — both views exclude the midday shadow.
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school between the founder and his two main students. The dispute is recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah and is based on different interpretations of the same hadith evidence.
The Sahibayn's view is often considered more practical and is adopted in later fatwa.
4
Work out the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If Zuhr ends later (Abu Hanifa's view), Asr begins later. This means a follower of Abu Hanifa can pray Zuhr at a later time compared to a follower of the Sahibayn. The actual prayer schedule changes accordingly.
The difference can be up to the time it takes for the shadow to double from equal length.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text does not explicitly state which view is relied upon. However, in later Hanafi practice, the view of the Sahibayn (shadow equal) is generally preferred for ease and because it aligns with other schools.
Always check local Hanafi practice; some regions may follow Abu Hanifa's view.

Times of the Five Prayers and the Shafaq Dispute
Defines prayer times with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on shafaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the times of the five daily prayers according to the Hanafi school and explain why Abu Hanifa's definition of shafaq (whiteness) is the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The end of Maghrib and start of Isha depend on whether shafaq is redness or whiteness; the Hanafi school relies on whiteness.

The five daily prayers are bound by celestial signs marking their beginning and end. This passage from a Hanafi commentary details each time window and reveals a key intra-madhhab disagreement: what exactly is shafaq (the afterglow after sunset)? The answer determines whether Maghrib ends with the disappearance of redness or later with the whiteness. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the school's relied-upon ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five prayer time windows
List each prayer (Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha, Fajr) and note their defined beginnings and ends from the text.
Focus on the celestial signs: sun position, shadow lengths, dawn and sunset.
2
Analyze the Zuhr/Asr boundary dispute
Note that Abu Hanifa defines Zuhr end when shadows are twice object height, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say when shadows equal object height. This affects when Asr begins.
Both positions are valid within the Hanafi school; the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
3
Understand the shafaq dispute
Identify the two definitions: Abu Hanifa says shafaq is the whiteness after sunset; the two disciples say it is the redness. This directly sets the end of Maghrib and start of Isha.
The practical difference: Maghrib time is longer under Abu Hanifa's view.
4
Trace the tarjih (preference) for whiteness
Examine the evidence cited: practices of Companions (Abu Bakr, Umar, Mu'adh, Aisha), narrations, and the reasoning that the red shafaq is not definitively established.
The later Hanafi masters (e.g., author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) adopted Abu Hanifa's position as the mufta bihi.
5
Apply the ruling to determine Maghrib and Isha times
Given that shafaq is whiteness, Maghrib ends and Isha begins only when the white afterglow disappears from the western horizon.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the times of the five daily prayers from a Hanafi commentary. It begins by stating the obligation of five daily prayers, each with a defined beginning and end. It then enumerates each prayer's time, noting the intra-Hanafi dispute on the end of Zuhr: Abu Hanifa holds that Zuhr ends when every object's shadow reaches twice its length (excluding the shadow at noon), while the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it ends when the shadow equals the object's height. This difference affects the start of Asr. For Asr, it begins when Zuhr time ends according to either opinion, and ends at sunset. Maghrib begins at sunset and ends with the disappearance of shafaq; Isha begins after shafaq disappears and ends at second dawn; Fajr begins at second dawn and ends at sunrise. The passage then defines fay' al-zawal and elaborates on shafaq, explaining that linguistically it includes both the red and white afterglows. The key ikhtilaf is that Abu Hanifa considers shafaq to be the whiteness, while the Sahibayn consider it the redness. This has a practical effect: according to Abu Hanifa, Maghrib ends later (when whiteness fades), and according to the disciples, it ends earlier (when redness fades). The commentary then supports Abu Hanifa's view by citing the practice of prominent Companions (Abu Bakr, Umar, Mu'adh, Aisha) and narrations, and quotes Ibn Khuzaymah who says that if red disappears but white remains, entry of Isha is doubtful due to disagreement, and that the red shafaq is not definitively established. The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school is that shafaq is the whiteness, making Maghrib time longer. Finally, it defines the second dawn (true dawn) as the horizontal spreading light, distinguishing it from the false dawn (vertical light).

Core Idea
When qibla is unclear, ijtihad is wajib; pray according to your strongest guess.

This lesson covers the second ruling: when a person is confused about the qibla and has no one to ask, he must exert his own reasoning (ijtihad) to estimate the direction. He then prays towards the direction his mind strongly inclines to. This is called taharrī, and it is a wajib duty when means of knowledge are absent.

1
Confirmation of confusion
The person must genuinely not know the qibla direction and have no reliable source to ask.
If there is a knowledgeable person nearby, he must ask.
2
Perform ijtihad (taharrī)
Use available signs: sun, moon, stars, mosque direction, compass, etc. One must exert effort to reach a conclusion.
This is wajib; one cannot just choose randomly.
3
Pray based on dominant opinion
After ijtihad, if one's predominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) points to a direction, he prays facing that direction.
If two directions are equally likely, one should delay if time permits, otherwise choose one.
4
Note the obligation
Ijtihad is obligatory (wajib) in this situation. Neglecting it without excuse results in invalid prayer.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the six fara'id
Learn the six obligatory acts: Takbirat al-ihram, standing, recitation, bowing, prostration, and final sitting. These are the pillars (arkan) of prayer.
These are the minimum essential acts; additional acts are either wajib or sunnah.
2
Understand the ruling on omission
If any fard is omitted, whether intentionally or forgetfully, the prayer is invalid. It must be repeated in full.
This contrasts with wajib acts, where sujud al-sahw is sufficient.
3
Differentiate from wajib
Wajib acts are also obligatory but their omission can be compensated by sujud al-sahw. Fard acts cannot be compensated; the prayer is nullified.
This is a key Hanafi principle.

Question: Explain the fara'id of prayer. Answer: Its fara'id are six: 1. The takbirat al-ihram, 2. Standing, 3. Recitation even if one verse, 4. Bowing, 5. Prostration, 6. The last sitting to the extent of the tashahhud. Question: What is the ruling of the fara'id? Answer: It is necessary to perform each fard. If one omits any of them, intentionally or forgetfully, his prayer is not valid. He must repeat it then. Omitting a fard is not compensated by the prostration of forgetfulness (sajdah al-sahw).

Commentary
This passage lists the six fara'id (obligatory acts) of prayer according to the Hanafi school. They are: (1) Takbirat al-ihram (the opening 'Allahu Akbar'), (2) Qiyam (standing) during the fard prayer for those able, (3) Qira'ah (recitation of at least one verse of the Quran), (4) Ruku' (bowing), (5) Sujud (prostration), and (6) Al-qa'd al-akhir (the final sitting) sufficient for reciting the tashahhud. The ruling is that each of these is fard; omitting any of them, whether intentionally or forgetfully, invalidates the prayer. The prayer must be repeated. Importantly, omission of a fard cannot be rectified by sujud al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness), which only compensates for missed wajib acts. This is a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: fard acts are integral to the prayer's validity, while wajib acts are necessary but omission can be compensated by sujud al-sahw.

Key Terms
التحريمة (tahrimah): The opening takbir ('Allahu Akbar') that initiates the prayer and makes it prohibited to do worldly actions.
القعود الأخير (al-qa'd al-akhir): The final sitting in prayer, after the last rak'ah, sufficient to recite the tashahhud.
سجود السهو (sujud al-sahw): Two extra prostrations performed to compensate for the omission or delay of a wajib act in prayer.
