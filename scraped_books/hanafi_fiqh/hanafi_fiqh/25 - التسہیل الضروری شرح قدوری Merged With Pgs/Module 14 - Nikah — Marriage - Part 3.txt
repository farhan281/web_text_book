Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's marriage where the mahr is his service, and identify the condition of the master's permission.

Core Idea
A slave's service can be valid mahr with master's permission, making the marriage sahih.

This lesson addresses a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: a slave marrying a free woman with the mahr being his service for a year. Unlike a free man, a slave's labor is owned by his master, so the master must consent. The ruling clarifies the validity of such a marriage and the rights of the wife to the slave's service.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the mahr
The parties are a slave and a free woman. The mahr is specified as the slave's service for one year.
2
Determine the condition of validity
The master's permission is essential because the slave's labor is owned by the master.
Without permission, the marriage is suspended (mawquf).
3
State the ruling
The marriage is valid (sahih) and the wife is entitled to the service.
4
Note the basis
The slave's labor is considered property (mal) that can be transferred with the master's authorization.
5
Contrast with a free man
A free man's labor is not owned by anyone, so he can also offer service as mahr if specified, but without the need for permission.
The key distinction is the ownership of labor.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario in marriage contracts: the wali (guardian) stepping forward to guarantee the mahr. The Hanafi school clarifies the legal effect of this guarantee. Identify the act and parties
The act is the wali (guardian) guaranteeing the mahr (dower) for the wife. The parties are the wali (guarantor), the husband (principal debtor), and the wife (creditor).

State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling is that the guarantee is valid (sahih). The Hanafi school permits a third party to guarantee a future debt if the subject is known.

Due to the valid guarantee, the wife has the option to demand payment of the mahr from either her husband or her wali. This dual recourse provides additional security.

Rulings on Unspecified Mahr: Animal vs. Cloth
Rulings and commentary on mahr being unspecified animal or cloth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid and invalid mahr specifications when the mahr is an animal or cloth, and explain the resulting obligations.

Core Idea
An unspecified animal mahr is valid with the average; an unspecified cloth mahr is invalid and triggers mahr al-mithl.

In Hanafi marriage contracts, the mahr must be specified with sufficient clarity. However, the school differentiates between types of unspecified mahr. This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that the key factor is whether the genus (jins) is known, leading to different rulings for animals and cloth. Understanding this distinction prevents disputes and ensures correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two scenarios: mahr specified as an animal without description, and mahr specified as a cloth without description. Note that both are cases of unspecified mahr but yield different rulings.
The animal case is valid; the cloth case is invalid.
2
Analyze the animal ruling
When the mahr is an animal of a specified genus (e.g., horse, donkey) without further description, the specification is valid. The woman is entitled to the average (wasat) of that animal type. The husband has the choice to give the average animal or its cash value.
This is sahih according to Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the cloth ruling
When the mahr is a cloth without specifying its type (e.g., just 'thawb'), the specification is invalid due to ignorance of the genus (jahalat al-jins). Consequently, the woman is entitled to mahr al-mithl. If the husband specifies a type (e.g., Herawi cloth), it becomes valid and he has a choice.
This is batil; mahr al-mithl is wajib.
4
Understand the illah (legal cause)
The difference lies in the nature of the items. Animals of a known genus (like horse or donkey) are considered a single genus; only the description is unknown. Cloths, however, are multiple genera (e.g., Herawi, Yamani), so not specifying the type causes ignorance of the genus itself, which invalidates the specification.
Jahalat al-jins invalidates; jahalat al-wasf does not.
5
Apply to practice
When drafting a mahr, ensure that the genus is clearly stated. For any item that encompasses multiple genera, specify the type. For items with a known genus, description is optional but recommended to avoid disputes.
This prevents unnecessary litigation over mahr.

The second case: the mahr is an unspecified cloth. Here the ruling is different: mahr al-mithl is obligatory because cloths are diverse genera; not specifying leads to ignorance of the genus. Marriage without Mahr: Later Agreement and Entitlement
Ruling on marriage without mahr and later mutual agreement on mahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a marriage contracted without mahr when spouses later agree on a mahr, and distinguish the wife's entitlement based on whether consummation occurred.

Core Idea
A marriage without mahr is valid; later agreement on mahr is binding but changes at divorce before consummation.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a marriage is validly contracted without specifying a mahr, and later the spouses agree on a specific amount. The Hanafi ruling clarifies that the mutual agreement is valid and governs the mahr, but the timing of divorce alters the wife's entitlement. Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the rules of mahr correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Initial Condition
Recognize that a marriage contracted without specifying a mahr is valid in the Hanafi school. Mahr is not a condition of validity.
2
Effect of Later Mutual Agreement
If the parties later agree on a specific mahr, that agreement is valid and binding. It becomes the mahr owed.
3
Consummation or Death Scenario
If the husband has intercourse with her, or valid seclusion occurs, or the husband dies, the wife is entitled to the full agreed mahr.
4
Divorce Before Consummation Scenario
If the husband divorces her before any intercourse or valid seclusion, she does not get the agreed mahr. Instead, she receives mut'ah (a consolatory gift).
Mut'ah is prescribed in Quran 2:236 for divorced women before consummation when no mahr was specified.

If the husband divorces her before consummation, she is not entitled to the agreed mahr; instead, she receives mut'ah, which is a consolatory gift prescribed by the Quran for divorced women before consummation.

Ruling on Mahr in a Void Marriage After Judicial Separation
Mahr ruling for void marriage after judicial separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the mahr obligation in a void marriage (nikah fasid) when the judge separates the couple, distinguishing between scenarios before and after consummation.

Core Idea
In a void marriage, mahr is only due after actual intercourse, and it is capped at the named mahr.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on mahr when a void marriage is dissolved by a judge. The key factor is whether consummation occurred. Understanding this helps in resolving disputes over financial rights in invalid marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of marriage
Recognize that the marriage is fasid (void) due to a defect, not batil (invalid). The judge separates the couple, which triggers the rules.
2
Determine if intercourse occurred
The ruling depends on whether there was actual intercourse (dukhul) or only seclusion (khalwa sahiha). In a void marriage, khalwa does not trigger mahr.
3
Apply the mahr rule
If separation before intercourse: no mahr. If separation after seclusion but no intercourse: no mahr. If after intercourse: mahr al-mithl is due but cannot exceed the named mahr.
4
Note the cap
The mahr al-mithl is capped at the named amount to prevent the woman from profiting from the void marriage.
