Conditions for Validity of the Kitabah Contract
Rulings on validity of kitabah based on price clarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid kitabah contract and apply them to determine validity in specific scenarios.

Core Idea
For a kitabah to be valid, the price must be known in species and description — uncertainty invalidates.

This lesson examines the foundational rulings on kitabah validity. The text answers key questions: what happens if the slave sets his own price, or if the kitabah is made for an unspecified animal or garment? Understanding these conditions is crucial to grasping the Hanafi approach to contract clarity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Look at each question and answer to determine whether the kitabah contract is declared valid (sahih) or invalid (fasid).
For example, kitabah on the slave's own value is fasid, but on an unspecified animal is sahih.
2
Extract the condition violated
In invalid cases, identify which element of the price (jins, qadr, wasf) is unknown.
If the jins is not named, it's invalid; if jins is named but wasf not, it's valid.
3
Apply the condition to new cases
Given a new scenario, test whether the price is known enough.
For instance, 'one thousand dirhams' is valid; 'one animal' without species is invalid.
4
Note the exception
Even an invalid kitabah can lead to freedom if the slave actually pays the agreed amount.
This is because payment substitutes for the needed clarity.

Book of Mukatab
Rulings of the Mukatab
Question: He contracted kitabah on his own value (i.e., the slave sets his own price), what is the ruling of this kitabah?
Answer: This kitabah is also invalid due to ignorance of the amount, type, and description of the value. Nevertheless, if he pays the value, he becomes free.
Question: The slave contracted kitabah on an unspecified animal, or on a garment whose type was not named, what is the ruling of this kitabah?
Answer: The kitabah is valid in the first case, and invalid in the second case.
Question: He contracted kitabah on his two slaves in one contract for a thousand dirhams, is this kitabah valid?
Answer: Yes, the kitabah is valid. So when they both pay what is upon them, they both become free; and if they become incapable, they are returned to slavery.
Question: He contracted kitabah on his two slaves on condition that each is guarantor for the other, what is the ruling of this kitabah?
Answer: This kitabah is valid. Whichever of them pays the entire kitabah amount, he and his companion become free, and the one who paid can claim from his companion half of what he paid.
Question: He contracted kitabah on his slave, then freed him immediately, what is its ruling?
Answer: He becomes free by that freeing, and the kitabah amount is dropped from him.
Question: He contracted kitabah on his slave, then he (the master) died, does the kitabah become void by that?
Answer: It does not become void, and it is said to the mukatab: Pay the money to the heirs of the deceased master according to its installments.
Question: If one of the heirs frees him, is his freeing effective?
Answer: If one of them frees him, his freeing is not effective. But if they all free him, he becomes free, and the kitabah amount is dropped from him.
Question: Is it permissible for the master to contract kitabah with his umm walad (slave-concubine who bore him a child)?
He becomes free: because it is the substitute in meaning from 'al-Hidayah'. The first case means that he specifies the genus but does not specify the type and description, and it refers to the middle (average), and the master is compelled to accept the value. But if he does not specify the genus, like saying 'beast', it is not valid because it includes different genera and extreme ignorance arises. From 'al-Hidayah'.

Question: This is permissible. But if the master dies before payment of the kitabah amount, the amount is dropped from her because she becomes free then due to her being an umm walad for him.
Question: He contracted kitabah on his female slave, then he had intercourse with her and she bore him a child, then how should this female slave act?
Answer: She has a choice: if she wishes, she continues on the kitabah; then when she pays the money, she becomes free. And if she wishes, she declares herself incapable, and she is an umm walad for him, so she waits for his death to become free.
Question: If he contracted kitabah on his mudabbara (slave promised freedom after his death), what is its ruling?
Answer: This kitabah is valid. If the master dies and he has no property other than her, she has a choice between striving (by working) for two-thirds of her value or striving for the entire kitabah amount.
Question: A man contracted kitabah on his female slave first, then made her mudabbara, what is the ruling of this tadbir? And what should the female slave do then?
Answer: This tadbir is valid, and she has a choice: if she wishes, she continues on the kitabah, and if she wishes, she declares herself incapable, and she is mudabbara, and then the rulings of tadbir apply to her.
Question: If she continues on her kitabah, then the master dies, does she become free because she is mudabbara?
Answer: If the master dies after she chose to continue on the kitabah and he has no property other than her, she has a choice: if she wishes, she strives for two-thirds of the kitabah amount, or two-thirds of her value according to Abu Hanifah.
Question: A mukatab slave bought a slave from the kitabah money, then freed this purchased slave in exchange for money, is that permissible for him?
Answer: It is not permissible.
Question: If the mukatab gives this purchased slave as a gift in exchange for compensation, what is its ruling?
Answer: This gift is not valid.
According to Abu Hanifah, and the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: she strives for the lesser of the two. From 'al-Jawharah'.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of rulings (ahkam) on the kitabah contract, presented in a question-and-answer format. The author addresses various scenarios regarding the validity, conditions, and consequences of kitabah.

First ruling: If a slave contracts kitabah on his own value (i.e., he sets the price himself), the contract is invalid (fasid) because the value is unknown in amount, type, and description. However, if he actually pays that value, he becomes free by operation of law, because payment fulfills the intended compensation.

Second ruling: If a slave contracts to pay an unspecified animal or an unspecified garment, the first (animal) is valid if only the type is left unspecified but the genus is known, while the second (garment) is invalid if even the genus is not named. The reasoning: specifying the genus allows reference to an average or middle value, whereas complete ignorance of genus leads to extreme ignorance.

Third ruling: Joint kitabah (one contract for two slaves on a single amount) is valid. If both pay, both are freed; if both fail, they return to slavery.

Fourth ruling: If joint kitabah includes a mutual guarantee clause, it is valid. If one slave pays the entire amount, both are freed; the paying slave can recover half from the other.

Fifth ruling: If a master contracts kitabah then later frees the slave immediately (munjizan), the freeing takes effect and the kitabah debt is waived.

Sixth ruling: If the master dies, the kitabah does not dissolve; the mukatab must pay the installments to the master's heirs.

Seventh ruling: If one heir attempts to free the mukatab, it is ineffective unless all heirs do so collectively; then the slave is freed and the debt waived.

Eighth ruling: Kitabah with an umm walad is valid, but if the master dies before payment, the debt is dropped and she becomes free by being an umm walad.

Ninth ruling: If the master has intercourse with a mukatab slave-concubine and she bears a child, she may choose to continue the kitabah or declare incapacity; if she chooses the latter, she becomes an umm walad and waits for the master's death to be freed.

Tenth ruling: Kitabah with a mudabbara (slave promised freedom after death) is valid. Upon the master's death with no other property, the slave may choose to work for two-thirds of her value (the portion of freedom) or the full kitabah amount.

Eleventh ruling: If a slave is first made a mukatab, then later declared mudabbar, the tadbir is valid, and she has the choice to continue on kitabah or become mudabbara. If she continues on kitabah and the master dies, according to Abu Hanifah she must work for two-thirds of either the kitabah amount or her value, while the two companions say she works for the lesser of the two.

Twelfth ruling: A mukatab cannot buy another slave from kitabah money and then free him for money; such a transaction is invalid.

Thirteenth ruling: A mukatab cannot gift a purchased slave in exchange for compensation; the gift is invalid.

These rulings illustrate the Hanafi position that kitabah is a flexible contract but requires clarity in terms, and that the mukatab retains certain rights and obligations even in special circumstances like death, birth of a child, or subsequent tadbir.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who enters into a kitabah contract.
كتابة (kitabah): Contract of gradual manumission.
جهالة (jahala): Ignorance of a essential term leading to invalidity.

Joint Kitabah and Mutual Guarantee
Rulings on joint kitabah with and without guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rulings on joint kitabah with and without a guarantee clause.

Core Idea
Two slaves can jointly contract kitabah; if one pays all, both are freed and the paying slave can recover half.

This lesson covers two related rulings: joint kitabah (one contract for two slaves) and joint kitabah with a mutual guarantee clause. The text provides clear answers on validity and consequences, including how the slaves are freed and what happens upon incapacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Determine if the kitabah is for a single slave or multiple, and if a guarantee clause exists.
Joint kitabah: one contract, two slaves, one price.
2
Apply the default joint kitabah ruling
If no guarantee, each slave is liable for his portion (half the total). Both must pay to be freed.
If both pay, both freed; if both fail, both return to slavery.
3
Apply the guarantee ruling
If each guarantees the other, they are jointly and severally liable.
Whichever pays the full amount frees both; the paying slave can recover half from the other.
4
Analyze the thamarah
Note the practical difference: with guarantee, one slave can free both by paying the full sum.
Without guarantee, partial payment does not free anyone.

Wala' al-Muwalat: Agreement-Based Clientage and Its Distinctions
Q&A explaining wala' al-muwalat and its three differences from wala' al-itaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three key differences between wala' al-muwalat and wala' al-itaqah, and apply the rule that wala' al-muwalat is revocable and subordinate to dhawi al-arham.

Core Idea
Wala' al-muwalat is a revocable agreement for mutual inheritance, weaker than wala' al-itaqah – it can be revoked and comes after distant kin.

This lesson focuses on wala' al-muwalat – a clientage relationship established by mutual agreement, not by manumission. The passage from al-Jawharah highlights three critical differences from wala' al-itaqah: inheritance by mutual consent, revocability, and priority relative to distant kin. Understanding these distinctions helps clarify the relative strength of each type of wala'.

Guided Path
1
Define wala' al-muwalat
It is a contract between two Muslims (often a non-lineal convert and an established Muslim) agreeing to inherit each other and pay blood-wit on each other's behalf.
It requires mutual consent and is not tied to manumission.
2
Identify the first difference: inheritance by agreement
In wala' al-muwalat, the two parties can agree to inherit each other. In wala' al-itaqah, inheritance is automatic and unilateral (master inherits from freed slave, not vice versa).
The passage says: 'يتوارثان إذا اتفقا على ذلك' – they inherit from each other if they agree.
3
Identify the second difference: revocability
Wala' al-muwalat can be revoked by either party at any time. Wala' al-itaqah is permanent and cannot be nullified.
The passage says: 'يحتمل النقض' – it can be revoked.
4
Identify the third difference: priority over distant kin
Wala' al-muwalat is after dhawi al-arham (distant kin), meaning distant blood relatives inherit before the muwali. In wala' al-itaqah, the master comes before dhawi al-arham.
The passage says: 'مؤخر عن ذوي الأرحام' – deferred behind distant kin.

Meaning of Ibaq and Reward for Returning a Runaway Slave
Definition and ruling on ja'alah for returning a fugitive slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ibaq and explain the reward (ju'l) for returning a runaway slave, including the conditions and amounts.

Core Idea
A runaway slave’s return entitles the finder to a fixed reward of forty dirhams from a three-day journey, prorated for shorter distances.

This passage from Kitab al-Ibaq defines the act of a slave running away (ibaq) and then addresses the reward for a person who returns such a slave. The Hanafi school sets specific rules to incentivize return while preventing excessive profit. The ruling applies to both male and female slaves and is based on the principle of ja'alah (reward for a service).

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of ibaq
Ibaq is defined as the deliberate rebellion and flight of a slave from the master. Note that the Hanafi definition emphasizes intentional escape.
This distinguishes it from mere absence or being lost.
2
Understand the condition for the reward
The reward (ju'l) becomes due when the slave is returned from a distance of three days journey or more. If less, the reward is prorated proportionally.
The distance is measured by a standard travel day.
3
Calculate the reward amount
For a three-day journey or more, the finder receives forty dirhams. For shorter distances, the reward is calculated proportionally (e.g., two days yields 2/3 of forty).
Forty dirhams was a common fixed amount in Hanafi fiqh for such rewards.
4
Apply the cap if the slave's value is less
If the market value of the slave is less than forty dirhams, the reward is reduced to the value minus one dirham. This prevents the finder from profiting more than the slave's worth.
The one dirham deduction ensures the reward is never equal to the full value.
5
Recognize the legal basis
The ruling is based on the principle of ja'alah (contract of reward). The master implicitly promises a reward for the return of the slave, and upon fulfillment, the reward becomes wajib (obligatory).
This is not a sale but a unilateral promise to pay for a service.

Definition of 'Aqila: Who Pays the Diya?
Defines 'aqila and specifies Hanafi ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'aqila according to Abu Hanifa, identify the condition that triggers their obligation, and distinguish this definition from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
The 'aqila are the people of the diwan, not relatives—registration determines responsibility for diya.

This passage introduces the crucial concept of 'aqila—the group that pays diya (blood-money) on behalf of a killer. The Hanafi school defines 'aqila uniquely based on the historical practice of the early caliphs: those registered in the military diwan (payroll) bear the diya for a killer among them. This lesson unpacks the definition, its conditions, and its practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term and its linguistic root
The term 'aqila derives from 'aql (payment of blood-money). Recognize that the word refers to those who 'pay' (ya'qilun) the diya on behalf of the killer.
The root 'aql also means 'to bind' or 'to restrain'—the diya restrains retaliation.
2
State the general rule for when 'aqila is responsible
The passage says: 'Every diya that is obligated by reason of the killing itself is binding on the 'aqila.' This means the diya must be a direct consequence of the killing (e.g., intentional killing where diya is accepted, or accidental killing), not a compromise settlement (sulh).
The phrase 'bi nafs al-qatl' excludes diya agreed upon by compromise.
3
Define 'aqila according to Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa defines the 'aqila as 'ahl al-diwan' (the people of the military register) if the killer is among them. The diya is taken from their stipends (ata'ya).
This is a Hanafi-specific view based on Umar's establishment of the diwan.
4
Apply the definition to a scenario
Consider a soldier registered in the state payroll who accidentally kills someone. Under Abu Hanifa, the diya is paid by all registered members of the diwan proportionally from their stipends. If the killer is not registered, the 'aqila do not pay; instead, the killer's own assets or other groups may be responsible.
Later Hanafi fatwa adapts this principle when no official diwan exists.

Commentary
This passage addresses intoxicating substances beyond grape-based wine. It explicitly rules that bang (a narcotic), hashish, hemp leaves, and opium are haram because they corrupt the intellect and distract from worship. However, their prohibition (hurma) is of a lesser degree than that of wine (khamr). The practical consequence is that if one consumes these substances and becomes intoxicated, the hadd punishment (80 lashes for wine drinking) is not applied; instead, the judge inflicts a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) that is less than the hadd. The passage then extends the ruling to a specific tree drink called 'tar' in eastern India, which is also drunk for amusement. This distinction in punishment reflects the Hanafi view that hadd is specifically for khamr (wine) and not for other intoxicants, even though they are also prohibited. The reasoning is based on the general principle that anything corrupting the mind is haram, but the specific punishment for wine is derived from the explicit hadd texts.

The Four Vessels of the Hadith: Lexical Definitions
Lexical definitions of four vessel types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the four vessel types (al-hantam, al-dubba, al-naqir, al-muzaffat) and explain their significance in the Hanafi ruling on nabidh.

Core Idea
The four prohibited vessels are defined by their material and shape, not their substance.

This passage from Fath al-Bari provides the lexical definitions of the four vessel types mentioned in the hadith that initially prohibited nabidh in certain containers. Understanding these terms is crucial for grasping the subsequent Hanafi ruling that later abrogated the prohibition based on a shift in the effective cause ('illa).

Commentary
This passage provides lexical explanations of the four vessel types from the hadith. These definitions are essential for understanding the earlier rulings. Al-hantam refers to green jars, as explained by Ibn Umar. Al-dubba means gourd, specifically dried gourd according to al-Nawawi. Al-naqir is a hollowed palm stump.
