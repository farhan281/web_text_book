Definition of Mudarabah (Qirad)
Linguistic and legal definition of mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of mudarabah in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish it from other contracts such as loans.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is a profit-sharing partnership: capital from one, work from the other, profit shared as agreed.

This passage introduces the term Mudarabah (also known as Qirad) and provides both its linguistic origin and its legal meaning. You will learn that linguistically it derives from 'traveling' (darb), and legally it is a contract for partnership in profit. This foundational definition is essential for understanding the rules of Mudarabah transactions in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root
The term 'mudarabah' is derived from 'al-darb', meaning traveling in the land. This indicates that the worker (mudarib) typically travels for trade.
Linguistic connection to trade journeys.
2
Understand the legal definition
Legally, mudarabah is a contract for partnership in profit ('aqd 'ala al-shirkah fi al-ribh).
Key: profit-sharing, not fixed return.
3
Identify the parties and their contributions
One party (rabb al-mal) provides capital, the other (mudarib) provides work (labor).
Capital and labor are distinct.
4
Recognize the profit-sharing element
Profits are shared according to an agreed ratio; losses are borne solely by the capital provider.
Profit ratio must be agreed upon; loss allocation is default in Hanafi fiqh.

Mudarib Cannot Delegate Without Permission
General ruling on delegation in mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mudarib's delegation and identify the condition that makes it permissible.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot delegate trust without the owner's explicit permission or discretion.

Mudarabah is a partnership based on trust. The mudarib acts as an agent, and agency is personal. This lesson clarifies when the mudarib may lawfully transfer the capital to another mudarib — only with the owner's authorization or a broad statement like 'i'mal bi ra'yik' (act on your own judgment).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is the mudarib giving the capital as mudarabah to another person. The base ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuuz).
This is the starting point: prohibition.
2
Identify the Exception Conditions
Two conditions make it permissible: (1) the capital owner explicitly permits it, or (2) the owner says 'i'mal bi ra'yik', which grants the mudarib full discretion.
The phrase 'i'mal bi ra'yik' is a blanket permission.
3
Distinguish the Levels of Permission
Permission can be specific ('you may delegate to X') or general ('act as you see fit'). The Hanafi school treats the general phrase as encompassing delegation.
This distinction affects liability.

Liability for Unauthorized Delegation in Mudarabah
Q&A on mudarib's liability for unauthorized delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a mudarib becomes liable for unauthorized delegation and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i.

Core Idea
The first mudarib guarantees the capital only if the second mudarib profits — profit triggers liability, not delegation alone.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a key question in mudarabah: what happens when the mudarib (agent) hands the capital to someone else without the owner's permission? The Hanafi ruling ties liability (daman) to a specific condition. We'll walk through the rule, its rationale, and the difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the action
The first mudarib (agent) gives the capital he received from the owner to a second mudarib without the owner's permission. This is an unauthorized delegation.
2
State the general ruling
The first mudarib guarantees the capital to the owner, but this guarantee is not absolute. It is not automatic upon delegation.
3
Identify the condition for liability
The guarantee arises only if the second mudarib makes a profit. The passage states: 'if the second mudarib profits, the first mudarib guarantees the capital to the owner.'
4
Understand the rationale
Profit indicates that the capital was actually used in trade, realizing the unauthorized act. If no profit is made, the capital may not have been used, so no liability.
5
Note the effect of owner's ratification
If the owner later ratifies (by guaranteeing the first mudarib), the sub-mudarabah between the two mudaribs becomes valid, and they share profit as per their agreement.

السؤال: فلو دفع المضارب المال مضاربة إلى غيره ولم يأذن له رب المال في ذلك، هل يضمن المضارب الأول مال رب المال؟ الجواب: يضمن لكن لا بنفس العقد، ولا بتصرف المضارب الثاني، بل يضمن المضارب الأول المال لرب المال إذا ربح المضارب الثاني، فإذا ربح المضارب الثاني ضمن المضارب الأول المال لرب المال. لرب المال : ثم إن ضمن رب المال المضارب الأول صحت المضاربة بين المضارب الأول وبين المضارب الثاني، وكان الربح بينهما على ما شرطا من "الهداية".
Question: If the mudarib gives the capital as mudarabah to another without the capital owner's permission, does the first mudarib guarantee the capital? Answer: He guarantees, but not because of the first contract, nor due to the actions of the second mudarib. Rather, the first mudarib guarantees the capital to the owner if the second mudarib makes a profit. So if the second mudarib profits, the first mudarib guarantees the capital to the owner. (Then) if the owner confirms (guarantees) the first mudarib, the mudarabah between the first and second mudarib becomes valid, and the profit between them is as they stipulated - from Al-Hidayah.
Source pages p. 267

Commentary
This passage explains the liability (daman) of the first mudarib when he delegates without permission. The guarantee arises only if the second mudarib makes a profit (arbaha). This is a unique Hanafi rule: the first mudarib does not guarantee for the contract or the second mudarib's actions per se, but because the profit made shows that the capital was used, and since the owner didn't authorize delegation, the first mudarib must compensate. If the owner later ratifies (yadman, i.e., guarantees to bear any loss), then the mudarabah between the two mudaribs becomes valid (sahha), and they share profits as they agreed. The reference to 'Al-Hidayah' indicates the source.

Key Terms
يضمن (yadman): He becomes liable to pay compensation (i.e., guarantee the capital).

State the ruling

Profit Distribution: Delegation in Mudarabah
Profit distribution when first mudarib delegates with half share.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate profit distribution in a two-tier mudarabah where the first mudarib delegates his entire share to a second mudarib.

Core Idea
A mudarib who delegates his entire role receives nothing.

Mudarabah often involves delegation. When the first mudarib gives the entire capital to a second worker, his share shifts to the second. This lesson breaks down the exact calculation and the rule that leaves the first mudarib with zero profit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their shares
List the capital owner, the first mudarib, and the second mudarib. Note owner's share (1/2) and first mudarib's original share (1/2 of profit).
2
Trace the delegation
The first mudarib delegates the entire mudarabah to a second mudarib, agreeing to give the second 1/2 of the profit (i.e., the first mudarib's entire share).
This transfers the entire worker's share to the second mudarib.
3
Calculate the profit distribution
Total profit = 1. Owner gets 1/2. Remaining 1/2 goes to the second mudarib. First mudarib gets 0.
The first mudarib gave away his share entirely.
4
State the final ruling
The division is valid (sahih). First mudarib receives nothing.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

In this scenario, the first mudarib delegates to a second mudarib with the second getting half the profit. The original agreement between owner and first is half for owner, half for first. The remaining half goes to the second mudarib (since the first mudarib has delegated his role and the second is now entitled to the worker's share). The first mudarib gets nothing (la shay') because he has given away his share entirely to the second. This is based on the principle that the first mudarib's share is consumed by the delegation when he hands over the whole operation to the second for a half share.

Third profit distribution scenario with mudarib guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the profit distribution when a mudarib stipulates a share exceeding available profit, and state that the first mudarib must cover the shortfall from his own wealth.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot promise more than existing profit; any shortfall is his personal liability.

This lesson analyzes a third scenario in mudarabah profit distribution. The second mudarib stipulates two-thirds of the profit, but the owner retains a fixed half. Only half remains for the mudaribs. The first mudarib must personally guarantee the missing one-sixth to the second mudarib, revealing that promises beyond available profit create personal liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and initial shares
Recognize three parties: capital owner, first mudarib, second mudarib. The owner stipulates half profit for himself.
2
Note the second mudarib's stipulation
The second mudarib demands two-thirds of the total profit, not just of the remaining half. The first mudarib agrees.
3
Calculate actual available profit for mudaribs
Owner's half is fixed, so mudaribs share only the remaining half total profit.
1/2 of total profit is shared between the two mudaribs.
4
Determine the shortfall
Second mudarib expected 2/3 total profit; he receives 1/2 total profit from the shared pool. Shortfall = 2/3 - 1/2 = 1/6 total profit.
5
Identify the first mudarib's guarantee
The first mudarib must pay the shortfall of 1/6 total profit from his own wealth to the second mudarib, so the second mudarib's total becomes 2/3.
This means the first mudarib loses his own share and pays extra from his pocket.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Profit in mudarabah is distributed according to the conditions agreed by each party, even with subcontracting.

This lesson explores the Hanafi position on a mudarib subcontracting the mudarabah funds to another mudarib with the capital owner's permission. Several scenarios illustrate how profit is divided among the capital owner, the first mudarib, and the second mudarib based on the profit-sharing stipulations each party imposes. Understanding these calculations is essential for correctly applying mudarabah contracts in practice.

1
Identify the parties and their roles
Determine who is the capital owner (rabb al-mal), the first mudarib, and the second mudarib. Note the profit-sharing conditions each stipulates.
The first mudarib is the original working partner; the second mudarib is the subcontracted manager.

Analyze the profit-sharing stipulations
Read each scenario and record the share each party claims: capital owner's share, first mudarib's share with the second, and any conditions on the second mudarib's profit.
Stipulations bind the parties unless contrary to the contract's essence.

Core Idea
Distributed profit is provisional; it must be returned if later loss impairs capital.

In a mudarabah, profit is often distributed before the partnership ends. But what if the capital is later lost? Does the mudarib keep his share? This lesson presents a clear Hanafi ruling: profit must be returned to prioritize the capital. Understanding this protects the investor's rights and clarifies the provisional nature of profit.

Guided Path

The parties must return the profit (yataraddan) to each other until the rabb al-mal recovers the original capital. The capital is prioritized over any profit distribution.

After restoring the capital, any surplus is shared as profit according to the original mudarabah agreement. If the capital is still deficient, the mudarib bears no liability.
The mudarib is not a guarantor of capital; only the rabb al-mal bears the loss after profit is reclaimed.
