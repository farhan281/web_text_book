The Lawsuit Falls When Both Claimants Lack Evidence
Q&A on a qada procedural ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two litigants each refer to the same absent third party without evidence and explain why the lawsuit is dropped.

Core Idea
Without evidence, a lawsuit between two claimants referencing an absent third party is dropped — possession prevails by default.

This lesson examines a specific procedural ruling in Hanafi qada: when both the possessor and an outsider claim rights to an item through the same absentee, but neither can prove it, the lawsuit falls. This illustrates the importance of evidence (bayyinah) and the default presumption of lawful possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The possessor (صاحب اليد) claims the item is a deposit (وديعة) from an absent person. The outsider (another man) claims he bought the same item from that same absent person.
Both claims are unsubstantiated.
2
Recognise the common absent third party
Both claims refer to the same absent person (فلان). Neither party brings evidence to support their claim.
3
Apply the rule: lawsuit falls
Since no evidence is presented, the case cannot proceed; the lawsuit is dropped (تسقط الخصومة). The item remains with the possessor under the principle that possession is presumed lawful until proven otherwise.
This ruling prevents baseless litigation and maintains stability.

Commentary
Here, the possessor claims that the disputed property is a deposit from an absent third party (فلان). The other claimant (outside) claims he bought it from the same absent person. Both refer to the same third party, but neither brings evidence (بينة). The ruling is that the lawsuit is dropped (تسقط الخصومة) between them. Why? Because without evidence, neither party can prove their claim. The possessor's claim of deposit does not require him to be an owner, so the outsider cannot hold him liable without proof. Similarly, the outsider's purchase claim is unsubstantiated. The property is left with the possessor under the original presumption that his possession is lawful until proven otherwise.

Ruling on proof of absent third-party ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a plaintiff's lawsuit is dismissed if the defendant proves the claimed property belongs to an absent third party through a lawful transaction or usurpation.

Core Idea
Proven possession from an absent third party ends the plaintiff's case against the defendant.

In litigation, a defendant may admit possessing a claimed item but assert it belongs to another person who is not present. The following ruling clarifies when such a claim, backed by evidence, extinguishes the lawsuit against the defendant. Understanding this principle prevents prosecuting the wrong party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defendant's claims
The defendant may state that the item was deposited (wadī‘a), pledged (rahn), or even usurped (ghaṣb) from an absent third party.
All three scenarios involve the defendant admitting possession but denying ownership vis-à-vis the plaintiff.
2
Apply the condition: evidence (bayyinah)
The ruling only applies if the defendant presents credible evidence (witnesses or documents) supporting his claim about the absent person.
Without evidence, the lawsuit continues against the defendant.
3
Determine the legal consequence
If the defendant proves his claim, there is no lawsuit (lā khuṣūma) between him and the plaintiff. The plaintiff must instead sue the absent person.
The defendant is considered a mere holder (yad amāna or yad damān depending on the claim) vis-à-vis the plaintiff.
4
Understand the rationale
The plaintiff's claim is against the person who allegedly wronged him, not against a third-party holder. The defendant's possession is not hostile to the plaintiff's claim if it is derived from another.
This prevents multiplicity of suits and protects innocent bailees.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa divides 3/4 and 1/4; the Sahibayn divide 2/3 and 1/3 — a difference rooted in how they treat the overlapping claim.

When two parties present valid evidence for ownership of the same property, but their claims differ (one claims the whole, the other half), the Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on how to allocate shares. This passage from a classical fiqh text presents the views of Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, along with a crucial marginal note explaining the nature of the judgment. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to reason about how evidence and claims interact in Islamic property law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the parties
Note that the house is in the hand of a third person (not a claimant). Two claimants come with evidence: one claims the entire house, the other claims half. Both have valid evidence.
This is not a case of two possessors; it's about competing evidence of ownership.
2
Work through Abu Hanifa's allocation
Abu Hanifa says the house is divided into quarters: the full claimant gets 3/4, the half claimant gets 1/4. Think: the full claimant's evidence proves half definitively (since the half claim is included), leaving the remaining half contested; each gets half of that contested half, so 1/2 + 1/4 = 3/4 for the full claimant, 1/4 for the half claimant.
He prioritizes the 'fuller' evidence in the overlapping part.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the house is divided into thirds: 2/3 to the full claimant, 1/3 to the half claimant. The practical difference is the numerical shares: 3/4 vs 2/3 for the full claimant. This affects how much property each party actually receives.
The dispute is about the method of allocation when evidence overlaps.

The text does not explicitly state which view is preferred. However, in the Hanafi school, the fatwa generally follows Abu Hanifa's position, but both views are transmitted in the zahir al-riwayah.

Resolving Animal Disputes by Age Matching
Ruling on animal dispute resolution by age matching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for resolving an animal possession dispute when each claimant claims birth in his possession with different dates, and determine when the animal is awarded to one claimant versus shared.

Core Idea
Award the animal to the claimant whose birth date matches the animal's age; if unclear, share it equally.

When two men each claim that an animal was born in their possession and present evidence, but differ on the date of birth, how should a judge decide? This ruling from the Hanafi school provides a practical method: compare the claimed dates to the animal's actual age, determined by physical signs. If one date fits the age, that claimant wins; otherwise, the animal is divided equally, as if no dates were given.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Both claimants present evidence (bayanah) that the animal was born in their possession and each states a date. The judge initially accepts both as equally strong.
The dates are the only difference; without them, the animal would be shared from the start.
2
Apply the age test
The judge calls an expert to determine the animal's age (sinn al-dābbah) by examining its teeth and other characteristics. Then compare each claimed date against this age.
The age must be consistent with the date (e.g., a 2-year-old animal cannot have been born last month).
3
Determine the outcome
If exactly one claimant's date matches the age, the animal is awarded to him. If neither or both dates match (ambiguous), the timing falls away (saqaṭa al-tawqīt) and the animal is shared equally between them.
The marginal note explains: when dates are ambiguous, it is as if they were never mentioned.

The Rider's Priority in Animal Disputes
Ruling on priority between rider and halter holder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on priority between a rider and a halter holder in an animal dispute and explain why the rider is considered more entitled.

Core Idea
In a dispute, the rider is entitled over the halter holder because actual possession trumps partial control.

Disputes over possession of movable property arise in many forms. This lesson addresses a classic scenario: two people claim an animal, one riding it and the other holding its halter. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule based on the strength of possession. Understanding this ruling will help you apply the principle of actual control (yad) in property disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the object
The dispute involves an animal (dābba). One person is riding it (rākib), the other is holding its halter (mutaʿalliq bi-lijāmihī). The object is the animal itself.
The halter (lijām) is the headgear used to lead the animal.
2
State the ruling
The answer is straightforward: al-rākib awlā bihā – the rider is more entitled to the animal.
This means the judge should award possession to the rider pending evidence.
3
Identify the basis (ʿilla)
The rider has actual physical control (yad) over the animal, while the halter holder only has a grip on the rope, which is partial control. In Hanafi fiqh, stronger possession gives stronger entitlement.
This is not a final ownership decision; it is about interim possession.
4
Note the caveat
This ruling is about who keeps the animal until ownership is proved. If the halter holder later provides evidence of ownership, the animal is returned to him.
The ruling prevents disruption of actual possession without clear proof.

Book of Judge to Judge. Question: It may be necessary for a judge to write a letter to another judge; is this letter accepted? Answer: A judge's letter to a judge is accepted in matters of rights (ḥuqūq), if witnesses testify to it before him, and it is not accepted in ḥudūd and qiṣāṣ. If they testify in the presence of the opponent, he judges based on the testimony and writes his judgment; if they testify without the presence of the opponent, he does not judge and writes the testimony so that the addressee can judge by it. And he must read the letter to the witnesses so that they know what is in it, then he seals it and hands it over to them.

Commentary
This lengthy ruling-statement details the procedure for a judge (qāḍī) sending a written judgment (kitāb) to another judge. The Hanafi school accepts such written communication in civil cases (ḥuqūq, i.e., rights of individuals) but not in criminal cases (ḥudūd and qiṣāṣ). The letter must be witnessed by two witnesses who can verify its contents. The witnesses must testify before the first judge; if the opposing party is present, the judge can issue a ruling directly; if absent, the judge writes the testimony for the second judge to rule. The letter must be read to witnesses, sealed, and handed to them for delivery.

Key Terms
الحقوق (al-ḥuqūq): Civil rights and financial claims, as opposed to ḥudūd and qiṣāṣ.
الحدود (al-ḥudūd): Fixed criminal punishments prescribed by Islamic law, such as for theft or adultery.
القصاص (al-qiṣāṣ): Retaliation or retribution for bodily harm or murder, where the victim's family has the right to demand equivalent punishment.

Conditions for Accepting a Judge's Written Judgment
Question and answer on conditions for accepting a written judgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions required for a Hanafi judge to accept and enforce a written judgment from another judge and apply them to authenticate such a document.

Core Idea
A judge's written judgment requires witness testimony and the opponent's presence to be enforceable.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, a written judgment sent from one judge to another is not automatically accepted. This passage from al-Tashil al-Durari explains the safeguards: the receiving judge must have two male witnesses (or one man and two women) testify to the letter's authenticity, and the opponent must be present for enforcement. These conditions ensure the rights of the litigant are protected and that the document is properly verified before any action is taken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Accepting a written judgment from another judge. The ruling: it is accepted only under specific conditions.
The act is 'accepting' (قبول) which here implies enforcing the judgment.
2
List the conditions
The passage states two conditions: witness testimony (two men or one man and two women) and the presence of the opponent (الخصم).
These are conjunctive — both must be satisfied.
3
Describe the witness testimony procedure
The witnesses must have received the letter from the original judge in his court, heard it read, and seen it sealed. Then they hand it to the receiving judge.
The testimony authenticates both the content and the seal.
4
Explain the enforcement after conditions are met
The receiving judge inspects the seal, opens the letter, reads it to the opponent, and binds him with its contents.
The opponent's presence is required for enforcement.
5
Note cross-madhhab considerations
Other schools may have different witness requirements; within Hanafi, this is the relied-upon position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
Shafi'is, for example, may accept one male witness or two females.

Commentary
This ruling-statement completes the procedure for transmitting a written judgment between judges. The receiving judge cannot accept the letter without two witnesses (or one man and two women) testifying to its authenticity.
