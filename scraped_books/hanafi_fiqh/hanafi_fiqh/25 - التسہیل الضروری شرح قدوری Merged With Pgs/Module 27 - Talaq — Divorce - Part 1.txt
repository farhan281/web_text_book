The Three Types of Divorce: Ahsan, Sunnah, and Bid'ah
Defines the three types of divorce and their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of divorce (Ahsan, Sunnah, Bid'ah) and state the conditions for each according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Divorce is classified into three types: Ahsan, Sunnah, and Bid'ah; only Ahsan and Sunnah are praiseworthy.

This passage introduces the classification of divorce. Understanding these categories is essential for knowing which divorces are in accordance with the Sunnah and which are blameworthy innovations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Divorce is permissible as a last resort when marital life cannot continue. The text presents it as a form of deliverance (khalas).
Divorce is not recommended but allowed.
2
Name the three types and their characteristics
The text lists Ahsan, Sunnah (also called Hasan), and Bid'ah. For each, note the number of pronouncements and the timing conditions.
Ahsan: one divorce in a clean period; Sunnah: three divorces over three clean periods; Bid'ah: three divorces at once or in one clean period.
3
Differentiate between Ahsan and Sunnah regarding timing and number
Ahsan is only one pronouncement; Sunnah is three. Both require that each pronouncement be in a clean period free from intercourse, but Ahsan requires waiting the full 'iddah without revoking, while Sunnah allows revocation between pronouncements.
The description emphasizes that for a consummated wife, Sunnah timing applies; for an unconsummated wife, only the number matters.
4
Define Bid'ah and note its disapproval
Bid'ah divorce violates the Sunnah pattern either by combining three pronouncements in one utterance or by giving multiple divorces in a single clean period. The text states it is 'bid'ah' (innovation), which implies it is disapproved but still effective.
Hanafi school holds that Bid'ah divorce is valid but sinful.

Commentary
This is a definition and classification of divorce (talaq) into three categories. It begins with a general introduction: if marital life becomes impossible, Islam permits divorce as a solution. The three types are: 1) Ahsan (most excellent): a single divorce in a pure period without intercourse, then waiting until the 'iddah ends without revoking. 2) Sunnah (recommended): three divorces given over three pure periods, each without intercourse, for a consummated wife. For an unconsummated wife, the Sunnah in number is three, but timing is not required. 3) Bid'ah (innovated): three divorces pronounced together in one sentence or in one pure period, or two divorces in one pure period. The text then mentions a dispute regarding a single irrevocable divorce (al-wahida al-ba'ina). According to one narration from al-Asl, pronouncing a single ba'in divorce is against the Sunnah because it adds an unnecessary element of irrevocability. According to another narration from al-Ziyadat, it is not disliked (makruh) because the husband may need an immediate and final separation. The relied-upon position is that a single ba'in divorce is permissible but not preferred. The passage ends with a definition from Ibn al-Humam: Bid'ah talaq is any divorce that differs from the two Sunnah types (Ahsan and Hasan/Sunnah).

طلاق البدعة (talaq al-bid'ah): Innovated divorce: three divorces in one utterance or in one clean period.

The Ruling on a Single Irrevocable Divorce (al-Wahida al-Ba'ina)
Discusses the internal Hanafi dispute on a single ba'in divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi narrations regarding the single ba'in divorce and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A single irrevocable divorce is permissible when needed, but the preferred form is revocable.

The text records a difference within the Hanafi school about whether pronouncing a single ba'in divorce is against the Sunnah or not. This lesson clarifies the debate and the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act under dispute
The act is pronouncing a single divorce that is made irrevocable (ba'in) immediately, meaning it cannot be revoked during the 'iddah.
2
State the first narration (from al-Asl)
According to al-Asl, this practice is against the Sunnah (akhta' al-sunnah) because there is no need to add the extra quality of irrevocability to the deliverance.
This view deems it makruh.
3
State the second narration (from al-Ziyadat)
According to al-Ziyadat, it is not disliked (la yukrah) because the husband may have a genuine need for an immediate final separation (al-hajah ila al-khalas najizan).
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
Later Hanafi authorities, including al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir, prefer the view of al-Ziyadat. Thus, a single ba'in divorce is permissible when there is a need, though it is better to avoid it.
The tarjih is based on contemporary needs (li al-hajah).
5
Recognize the practical consequence
A husband can give a single ba'in divorce without incurring sin according to the preferred view, but the revocable (raj'i) divorce remains the Sunnah ideal.
If given, the wife immediately enters 'iddah and cannot be taken back without a new marriage.

Revocable vs Irrevocable Divorce and Bid'ah Divorce
Explains revocability and bid'ah divorce ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between revocable and irrevocable divorce and state the ruling on bid'ah divorce.

Core Idea
A single divorce of a consummated wife is revocable; for a non-consummated wife, it is irrevocable. Bid'ah divorce is valid but sinful.

Divorce law depends crucially on whether the marriage was consummated. This lesson covers the basic distinction and explains that divorcing in a manner contrary to the Sunnah still takes effect, though the husband incurs sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify wife type
Determine if the wife is madkhul biha (consummated) or ghayr madkhul biha.
2
Apply revocability rule
For a consummated wife, one divorce is revocable (talaq raj'i); for a non-consummated, it is irrevocable (talaq ba'in).
3
Understand bid'ah divorce
Bid'ah divorce (e.g., three at once) is valid but the husband is sinful for violating the Sunnah.
4
Apply to scenarios
Given a scenario, determine the type of divorce and its effect.

Commentary
This passage provides practical rulings on divorce. First, it distinguishes between consummated and non-consummated marriages: a single divorce for a consummated wife is revocable (he can take her back during 'iddah), but for a non-consummated wife it is irrevocable immediately. Bid'ah divorce (e.g., three at once) is valid but sinful. Divorce during menstruation is also valid for a consummated wife, but prohibited; he must take her back and then divorce properly in a tuhr. For a non-consummated wife, divorce during hayd is permissible. Divorce during pregnancy is allowed even if immediately after intercourse. The Sunnah method for three divorces for a consummated wife is: one divorce in each of three separate tuhr periods without intercourse. For a woman who does not menstruate (e.g., due to age or amenorrhea), the separation is based on months: one divorce per month for three months. The passage ends by contrasting these sunnah methods with the bid'ah methods (three in one word, etc.), which also effect divorce but the husband is sinful. The cross-reference indicates that details of taking back (رجعة) will be covered elsewhere.

Divorce During Menstruation and Pregnancy
Explains rulings on divorce during hayd and pregnancy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on divorce during menstruation and pregnancy and explain the required action for a consummated wife.

Core Idea
Divorce during menstruation is valid but prohibited; the husband must take her back. Divorce during pregnancy is valid even immediately after intercourse.

This lesson clarifies the permissibility of divorce during a woman's menstrual period and during pregnancy, addressing common misconceptions about validity and sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wife's state
Is she madkhul biha? Is she menstruating or pregnant?
2
Apply rule for hayd
For a consummated wife, divorce during hayd is valid but sinful; he must take her back. For a non-consummated wife, it is permissible.
3
Apply rule for pregnancy
Divorce during pregnancy is permissible and not sinful, even if immediately after intercourse.
4
Understand the required action

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Divorce presents rulings on the validity and method of divorce in the Hanafi school. It opens by distinguishing between consensual and non-consummated marriages: for a consummated wife, one revocable divorce does not end the marriage until the 'iddah expires, allowing the husband to take her back. For a non-consummated wife, one divorce is immediately irrevocable. The text then addresses innovations (bid'ah) in divorce: a divorce that violates the Sunnah (e.g., during menstruation) is still effective, but the husband is sinful. The Hanafi school holds that divorce during menstruation is prohibited but valid; the husband must take her back and divorce properly when she is pure. Similarly, divorce during pregnancy is permissible even if immediately after intercourse. The proper sunni method for three divorces of a consummated wife is: one divorce in each of three consecutive pure states (tuhr) without intercourse. For a non-menstruating woman (e.g., old or young), the separation is by months: one divorce per month. The passage also notes that if one divorces all three together or in one tuhr, it is effective but sinful. For a pregnant woman, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf prescribe a month between divorces, while Muhammad al-Shaybani recommends only one divorce in accordance with the Sunnah.

Key Terms
طلاق البدعة (talaq al-bid‘ah): A divorce that violates the Sunnah, e.g., during menstruation or in a pure state after intercourse.
حيض (hayd): Menstruation; a state of ritual impurity that affects certain acts like prayer but not divorce validity.
رجعة (raj‘ah): Revocation of divorce by husband during 'iddah.

Proper Sunni Method for Three Divorces and the Case of Pregnancy
Rules for sunni divorce and intra-Hanafi difference on pregnant wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the sunni method for three divorces to both menstruating and non-menstruating women, and identify the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the pregnant wife.

Core Idea
The sunni method for three divorces requires spacing: three tuhrs for menstruating women, three months for non-menstruating; for pregnant women, there is ikhtilaf.

Divorcing according to the Sunnah protects the woman's rights and allows for reconciliation between each pronouncement. The Hanafi school mandates a specific interval between divorces to avoid hasty separation. This lesson explains the method for regular menstruating women, those who do not menstruate, and pregnant women—including the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf against Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wife's type: menstruating, non-menstruating, or pregnant
The sunni method depends on the woman's reproductive status. The text gives separate rules for each.
Non-menstruating women include those who are too young, too old, or have amenorrhea.
2
Apply method for menstruating woman (madkhul biha)
Pronounce one divorce in a pure state (tuhr) without intercourse. Wait for the next tuhr (after the next menstruation) for the second, and again for the third.
This ensures the woman is not pregnant from the previous cycle and allows time for reconciliation.
3
Apply method for non-menstruating woman
Pronounce one divorce per month, with a full month's interval. This substitutes for the tuhr period.
The interval is approximately 30 days; the text says 'shahr' (month).
4
Apply method for pregnant woman (ikhtilaf)
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: separate each divorce by a month. Muhammad: only one divorce is sunnah; three in pregnancy is not sunnah.
The mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, allowing three with monthly intervals.
5
Recognize that non-sunni methods are valid but sinful
The text states that if one divorces all three together or in one tuhr, the divorce occurs but the husband is sinful.
This reinforces the principle from the previous lesson: sin does not nullify divorce.

Divorce of Intoxicated and Coerced Persons: The Hanafi Deterrence Principle and Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Detailed ruling on divorce by intoxicated/coerced persons with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the divorce of intoxicated and coerced persons, explain the deterrence principle (zajr), and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding non-grape intoxicants.

Core Idea
The drunkard's divorce is effective as a deterrent, even if his mind is gone, because the cause was sinful.

In this lesson, we examine a detailed Hanafi ruling on the validity of divorce by an intoxicated or coerced person. The passage explains the general principle that sanity and adulthood are required, but then addresses the exceptional cases of intoxication and coercion. The key legal reasoning (ta'lil) is based on deterrence (zajr) and the consideration of the cause (sabab) being sinful. An intra-Hanafi dispute over intoxicants from non-grape sources is also resolved by later fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the baseline ruling
Identify the general rule: divorce is valid only from a sane adult husband. Invalid from a minor, insane, or sleeping person.
This establishes the default requirement of sanity and adulthood.
2
Identify the exceptional cases
Note that the divorce of an intoxicated (sakran) and coerced (mukrah) person is valid. These are exceptions to the sanity and willingness requirements.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Understand the deterrence principle (zajr): because the loss of mind is due to a sinful act (drinking intoxicant), the divorce is effective to deter the person from the sin. The law treats him as legally sane to increase accountability.
The source text quotes al-Hidayah and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq on this point.
4
Examine the distinction based on cause
If the loss of mind is due to a non-sinful cause (e.g., headache from drinking), the divorce does not occur because the cause is not sinful.
This distinction is crucial: the ruling depends on whether the intoxication is sinful or not.
5
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
Identify the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (divorce occurs for any intoxicant) and Abu Yusuf (only for grape wine). Note that the later fatwa follows Muhammad's position, as stated in Fath al-Qadir.
This dispute is specifically about non-grape intoxicants (e.g., from grains, honey).

Question: Does the divorce of every husband occur? Answer: The divorce of every husband occurs if he is sane and adult. The divorce of a boy, an insane person, and a sleeping person does not occur. Question: What is the ruling of the divorce of an intoxicated person and one who is coerced? Answer: Their divorce occurs. The commentator: Their divorce occurs this if he drank an intoxicant, and his mind faded due to a cause that is a sin, so it is considered remaining legally as a deterrent for him. So if he drank and got a headache, and his mind faded due to the headache, we say: his divorce does not occur, from al-Hidayah. And it is said in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (3/266): Because when He addressed him in his state of intoxication with commands and prohibitions regarding subsidiary rulings, we know that He considered him like one with sound mind, as a severity upon him in subsidiary rulings. And they interpreted here according to the madhhab of Abu Hanifa, which is: he who does not distinguish between a man and a woman, nor between the sky and the earth, then if there is with him enough mind to fulfill religious obligation, he is like one sober. He made it general so it includes one who became intoxicated under coercion or necessity and divorced. And it was definitively stated in al-Khulasa that it occurs, reasoned that the loss of mind was by an act that is originally prohibited, even if it became permissible due to coercion, but the reason prompting the prohibition remains, so the effect of the existence of the reason applies regarding divorce. It also includes one who became intoxicated from drinks made from grains and honey: this is the statement of Muhammad, and the Second Imam said it does not occur. It is said in Fath al-Qadir: Fatwa is given according to Muhammad's statement because intoxication from every prohibited drink [is included]. It also includes one whose mind disappeared by eating hashish, and he divorced, which is called the leaves of cannabis. It has been agreed upon the occurrence of his divorce by the fatwa of the scholars of both madhhabs, Shafi'i and Hanafi, because of their fatwa on its prohibition and disciplining its users, until they said: Whoever says it is permissible is a heretic. This is in al-Mubtagha with a jīm, and followed by the muhaqqiq Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir. Delegation of Talaq (Tafwid al-Talaq)
Question-answer rulings on delegating talaq to the wife or a third party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the conditions for valid delegation of talaq and IDENTIFY the type of talaq that results from different forms of delegation.

Core Idea
The authority to divorce expires when the session ends unless the husband says 'whenever you wish.'

The Hanafi school allows a husband to delegate the pronouncement of talaq to his wife or a third party. This delegation, however, is governed by strict conditions: the wife's authority is usually confined to the sitting (majlis) unless otherwise stated. The wording also determines whether the resulting divorce is ba'in or raj'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The passage covers delegation to the wife (with two wording variations) and delegation to a third party agent.
Each scenario has different rules.
2
Distinguish 'choose yourself' from 'divorce yourself'
When the husband says 'ikhari nafsaki' (choose yourself), the wife's choice results in one ba'in talaq. When he says 'talliqi nafsaki' (divorce yourself), it is one raj'i talaq unless the husband intended three and she said three.
The count of talaq is not automatically three.
3
Note the temporal scope of authority
For both phrases, the wife must act in the same sitting unless the husband adds 'mata shi'ti' (whenever you wish), which gives ongoing authority.
Leaving the sitting ends the authority.
4
Apply the condition of mentioning 'self'
The word 'nafs' (self) must appear in either the husband's or wife's speech for the talaq to take effect.
This is a necessary condition.
5
Contrast delegation to a third party
If the husband appoints a male agent unconditionally, the agent can divorce the wife anytime, not limited to the sitting. But if he says 'if you wish', the agent must act in the sitting.
The agent's authority is broader unless restricted.

Core Idea
A ba'in divorce during terminal illness grants inheritance only if death occurs within 'iddah; raj'i does not need this rule.

This passage deals with a protective measure in Hanafi law: a husband may divorce his wife while terminally ill to deprive her of inheritance. To prevent such abuse, the school holds that if the divorce is ba'in (irrevocable) and he dies within her 'iddah, she still inherits. However, there is an internal difference: should this rule also apply to raj'i (revocable) divorce?
