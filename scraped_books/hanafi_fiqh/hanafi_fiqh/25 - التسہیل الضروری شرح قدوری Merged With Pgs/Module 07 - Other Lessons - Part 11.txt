Performing Kaffarah Before Breaking an Oath: Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on kaffarah before hinth with Shafi'i disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on performing kaffarah before breaking an oath and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Performing kaffarah before breaking an oath does not suffice; it must be repeated after the violation.

This lesson examines a practical scenario: a person swears an oath, then performs the expiation before actually breaking it. Does this fulfill the obligation? The passage provides the Hanafi ruling and notes a disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly discharging kaffarah obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the issue
The act is performing kaffarah before breaking the oath (hinth). The issue is whether this fulfills the obligation.
2
State the Hanafi position
It does not suffice; the kaffarah must be performed again after hinth.
3
Note the inter-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i generally allows it, except if the kaffarah is by fasting.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
A Hanafi must repeat the kaffarah in all cases; a Shafi'i only repeats if the kaffarah was fasting.
5
Recognize the source
The ruling is from Al-Jawharah, a Hanafi text.

This passage presents a ruling with an inter-madhhab dispute. For a Hanafi, therefore, one must repeat the kaffarah after breaking the oath, regardless of the method.

Core Idea
When the public treasury is empty, the qasim's fee is borne by the partners, and he must be just, trustworthy, and knowledgeable.

In Hanafi law, the state normally provides a salaried qasim for property division. However, when the Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) is insufficient, the qadi (judge) may appoint a fee-based qasim whose compensation comes from the partners (shuraka'). This passage outlines the essential qualities the qasim must possess and additional rules to prevent abuse, such as not forcing a single qasim upon the parties and prohibiting cartels among qasims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the ruling addresses a situation where the Bayt al-Mal has no funds to provide a salaried qasim. This triggers the need for an alternative arrangement.
2
Determine the Solution
The qadi appoints a qasim who will divide the property for a fee (ujrah). This fee becomes an expense on the partners, not on the state.
3
List the Required Qualities
The qasim must be just (adl), trustworthy (mamun), and knowledgeable about division (alim bi al-qismah). These are conditions for validity.
These qualities ensure fairness and accuracy in distribution.
4
Note the Prohibitions
The qadi cannot force the partners to use a single specific qasim; they may choose any qualified one. Also, qasims are not allowed to form a cartel (yushtarikun) to fix prices or monopolize the work.
This prevents exploitation and maintains free choice.

Distribution of Qasim's Fee: Per Capita or Proportionate
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fee distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on dividing the qasim's fee and identify their underlying principles.

Core Idea
Partners share the divider's fee: per capita or by share? The Hanafi school holds two views based on benefit principle.

This passage addresses a practical question in partnership: when a qasim (property divider) is hired, how should his fee be split among the partners? Abu Hanifa and his two companions differ on the basis of apportionment. Understanding this dispute reveals the Hanafi method of balancing equality against proportionality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed element
The act is hiring a qasim to divide jointly owned property. The dispute is about how to distribute his fee among the partners: equally per capita or proportionally to their shares.
The fee itself is permissible (mubah); only its allocation is contested.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that the fee is divided equally among the partners regardless of share size. The basis is that the service of division benefits all partners equally—it resolves the common ownership for everyone.
Key phrase: 'ala 'adad ru'us al-shuraka' (per capita).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the fee is proportional to each partner's share. Their basis is that the benefit of clear title is proportional to one's ownership—a larger share gains more.
Key phrase: 'ala qadr ansibaihim' (proportional to shares).
4
Recognize the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
When shares are unequal, the fee per partner differs dramatically. For example, if A owns 2/3 and B owns 1/3, under Abu Hanifa they pay equally; under Sahibayn, A pays twice as much.
This ikhtilaf has real financial impact.
5
Note the mufta bihi
The text does not specify which view is relied upon. Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., from the Ottoman period) often prefer the Sahibayn's view, considering it more equitable.
When giving fatwa, one should check the preferred view in later sources.

This is based on the idea that the service of division benefits all partners equally, as it resolves the common ownership. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the fee is apportioned according to each partner's share in the property, since the benefit received is proportional to one's share. Division of Inherited Real Estate: Evidence or Admission?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on evidence for inherited real estate division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on requiring evidence for division of inherited real estate, and explain the practical consequence of the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires proof of death and heirs for dividing inherited real estate; the Sahibayn accept the partners' mutual acknowledgment.

When partners claim to have inherited a house or land, can the qadi simply divide it based on their word? Abu Hanifa insists on independent evidence to protect potential absent heirs and creditors. The Sahibayn find the partners' own admission sufficient, as they are the stakeholders. This dispute, however, applies only to real estate—for other joint property, all agree that admission suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural scenario
Recognize the situation: partners appear before the qadi with a property they claim is inherited from a named deceased. The issue is whether the qadi must verify the inheritance or can accept their claim.
The property is real estate ('iqaar) like a house or land.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that division is not permitted until the partners produce evidence (bayyinah) of two facts: the death of the alleged ancestor, and the exact number of legal heirs.
The evidence must be external, not just the partners' own statements.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) rule that the qadi may divide based on the partners' acknowledgment (i'tiraf), without requiring further proof.
They consider the partners' mutual admission sufficient to establish their respective shares.
4
Note the scope of the dispute
The difference is limited to real estate. For movable or other joint property (e.g., animals, goods), all three imams agree that division based on their claim is valid.
The text explicitly says: 'hadha al-ikhtilaf innama huwa fi al-'iqaar'.
5
Understand the rationale behind the difference
Abu Hanifa's caution arises because real estate is often substantial and may involve absent heirs or creditors whose rights could be overlooked. The Sahibayn emphasize that the partners own the property and their admission binds them, so external evidence is unnecessary.
This distinction reflects differing weight given to potential third-party rights versus the parties' autonomy.

Partition of Real Estate on Claim of Purchase Without Proof
Ruling on division when partners claim purchase without specifying mode

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dividing real estate when partners claim purchase or ownership without specifying the mode of acquisition, and identify the condition that triggers division based on acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Mutual acknowledgment of purchase or ownership suffices for the qadi to divide real estate – no evidence required.

In the previous lesson, we learned the ruling when partners inherit real estate. Now we turn to a different scenario: partners who claim they purchased the property or simply claim ownership without detailing how they acquired it. The Hanafi school treats this situation more leniently, allowing the qadi to divide based solely on the parties' mutual acknowledgment. This lesson explains the reasoning and the conditions for this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of claim
Determine whether the partners are claiming inheritance, purchase, or general ownership. The ruling changes based on the category.
Inheritance requires proof; purchase and general ownership do not.
2
Distinguish from inheritance
Recall that inheritance claims require specification of shares and proof of heirship. Here, purchase is a consensual transaction, and the parties' statements suffice to establish mutual rights.
The key difference is the absence of third-party rights (other heirs).
3
Apply the ruling of division based on acknowledgment
Once it is established that the claim is for purchase or general ownership, the qadi must divide the property according to the partners' own acknowledgments of their shares.
The text states: 'yuqasimuhu baynahum bi i'tirafihim' – he divides it among them based on their acknowledgment.
4
Note the condition of no conflicting claim
This ruling applies only when there is no third party disputing the claim. If an outsider challenges ownership, the partners would need to provide evidence.
Conflicting claims would transform the case into a disputed ownership, requiring proof.

This ruling is consistent with the Sahibayn's view on inherited real estate, but here it is the unanimous position (according to the text) for non-inheritance claims.

Division with an Absent Heir: Inherited vs. Purchased Property
Rulings on division when one partner is absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a qadi may divide inherited property in the absence of one heir and explain why purchased property is treated differently.

Core Idea
Inherited property can be divided despite an absent heir; purchased property cannot.

When co-heirs inherit property, their shares are fixed by law. But what if one heir is absent and the others want to divide? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule based on how the property was acquired – inherited or purchased – and who holds possession. This lesson unpacks two scenarios from the matn, showing the qadi's authority and limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the property type
Read the first question: present heirs, proof of death, property in their possession, one heir absent. Note the property is inherited (موروثة). The second scenario involves purchased property. Distinguish them.
2
State the qadi's action for inherited property
The qadi divides at the request of the present heirs and appoints an agent (وكيلا) to receive the absent heir's share. This is allowed because inheritance rights are established and the absent heir's share is protected by the agent.
3
Contrast with purchased property
If the property was purchased (not inherited), division cannot proceed without the absent partner's presence or consent, even if purchase is proven. The rights arise from contract, requiring mutual agreement or presence.
4
Consider the effect of possession by the absent heir
The second question: the absent heir has possession of the property (or part of it). Even for inherited property, the qadi does not divide because the present heirs cannot compel partition against someone in possession without his consent.
5
Synthesize the rule
Formulate the complete rule: For inherited property held by present heirs, the qadi may divide and appoint an agent for the absent heir. For purchased property, or if the absent heir holds possession of any property (inherited or not), division requires the absent party's consent or presence.
This is a memorization anchor – the distinction hinges on the source of ownership and possession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a contract made under duress, identify the conditions for the option to rescind, and apply the distinction between receipt as trust (amāna) and liability (ḍamān).

Core Idea
A coerced contract is valid but voidable; the coerced party has the choice to rescind unless he ratifies voluntarily.

In Hanafi law, duress (ikrāh) does not automatically invalidate a contract; rather, it gives the coerced party an option to rescind. This lesson explores the rulings on duress in sales, purchases, leases, and other obligations, including the liability of the parties and the status of the price as a trust when received under duress.

Guided Path
1

Lesser threats may not trigger the same option.
2
Understand the coerced party's option
After duress ends, the coerced person can either affirm (imḍā') or rescind (faskh) the contract. This option exists only if he did not voluntarily accept the price or deliver the goods.

If the coerced seller receives the price under duress, he holds it as a trust (amāna). He must return it if it still exists. If it perishes without his fault, he is not liable. In contrast, voluntary receipt makes the contract final.
The price is not owned; it is merely custody.

If the buyer (not coerced) receives the sold item and it perishes, he must guarantee its value to the seller. The coerced seller may also claim compensation from the coercer for any loss.
The coercer is liable even if the price is destroyed, but the seller cannot recover from the coercer if the price perishes as amāna.

Core Idea
Only genuine fear for life or limb makes the prohibited permissible; the heart must remain firm in faith.

Coercion can excuse otherwise sinful acts, but only within strict boundaries. This lesson from the Hanafi commentary details when eating carrion, drinking wine, or even uttering words of disbelief become permissible under duress. Assess the threat level

Apply the ruling for uttering disbelief

The coerced person is excused but may still be liable in some cases; the text gives the owner the right to claim from the coercer.

If someone endures the threat and is killed rather than committing the forbidden act, they are rewarded and not sinful. This underscores that while coercion permits the act, steadfastness is praiseworthy.

Core Idea

This lesson clarifies a fundamental classification in Islamic public finance: the distinction between fay' (wealth acquired without battle) and ghanimah (booty taken by force). The Shafi'i school does not recognize the exception for Arab Christians; they argue jizya is obligatory from all non-Muslims. This difference stems from their approach to ijmā' and textual evidence.

Four rulings on ownership transfer by overpowering

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four Hanafi rulings on property captured by disbelievers and apply the distinction between recovery before and after distribution.

Core Idea
Overpowering (ghalaba) by disbelievers in dar al-harb transfers ownership; pre-distribution recovery restores full ownership without payment.

When Muslims lose property to enemy forces, the question arises: who owns it if it is recaptured? Hanafi fiqh provides clear rules based on whether the property has been distributed as booty. This lesson presents four scenarios that every student of siyar must know.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four cases
Read the passage carefully and list the four distinct scenarios: (1) disbelievers overpowering other disbelievers, (2) disbelievers overpowering Muslims and then recovery before/after distribution, (3) merchant buying such property, (4) a stray camel fleeing to disbelievers.
Each case hinges on the concept of ownership transfer (tamlik) through istila (overpowering).
2
Understand the key principle: ownership by overpowering
According to the Hanafi school, when disbelievers overpower Muslim property and take it into dar al-harb, they become the owners. This is a necessary consequence of the lack of protection for Muslim property in enemy territory.
This principle differs from the Shafi'i school, which requires ihraz (taking into their territory) for ownership.
3
Distinguish pre-distribution and post-distribution recovery
If Muslims recapture the property before the spoils are divided (qismah), the original owners take it back free. If after distribution, they must pay its value to the Muslim who received it as his share.
Before distribution, the property is still considered owned by the original Muslim; after distribution, it becomes part of the booty owned by the recipient.
4
Analyze the merchant scenario
A merchant who buys such property from dar al-harb and brings it to dar al-Islam holds valid ownership. The original owner has the option to buy it at the merchant's cost or leave it.
The merchant's purchase is valid only if the property had been legitimately owned by the disbelievers through overpowering.
5
Apply to the stray camel case
If a Muslim's camel escapes to enemy territory and is taken by them, they own it. This is a specific application of the general rule of ownership by overpowering.
This rule applies to any movable property that falls into enemy hands without a fight.

Commentary

الجواب: يجوز له أن ينظر إلى موضع القرحة فقط، ولا ينظر إلى سائر الفخذ.
السؤال: وما حكم نظر الرجل إلى الرجل، ونظر المرأة إلى المرأة؟
الجواب: ينظر الرجل من الرجل إلى جميع بدنه إلا ما بين سرته إلى ركبتيه، وكذلك تنظر المرأة من المرأة إلى ما يجوز للرجل أن ينظر إليه من الرجل.
السؤال: وما يجوز للمرأة أن تنظر من الرجل؟
الجواب: يجوز لها أن تنظر من الرجل إلى ما يجوز للرجل أن ينظر من الرجل.
قال العبد الضعيف عفا الله تعالى عنه لما كان الأمن من الشهوة شرطا لجواز النظر إلى وجه الأجنبية، بل هو مشروط لجواز النظر إلى وجه المحارم أيضا لم يكن من الدين أن تخرج النساء سافرات كاشفات وجوههن في الأسواق والسكك، طائفات في الحدائق والمنتزهات؛ لأن الزمان زمان فسوق وعصيان وكيف يؤمن على هؤلاء الفسقة أنهم ينظرون إلى وجوه الأجنبيات من غير شهوة في النفس ولذة في النظر.
و لم ينتبه بعض الناس إلى هذا، فجوز خروج النساء سافرات من بيوتهن، وقال: إن الفقهاء ذكروا أن الوجه والكفين جاز كشفهما أمام الأجانب، فلما علمت النساء بهذه الفتيا التي صدرت من هؤلاء خرجن متبرجات سافرات كاشفات وجوههن ورؤوسهن مبديات صدورهن كما يرى في الأسواق والحارات، ولم يعرف هذا المجتهد طبيعة النساء وميلهن إلى التبرج والبروز أمام الرجال؛ إظهارا للزينة، ولم ينتبه أنهم لا يكتفين بكشف الوجه واليدين فقط، فزل وضل وأضل ور الله تعالى أصحاب التقى من أهل الفتيا ورحم حيث ألزموا المرأة أن لا تخرج إلا محتجبة حجابا كاملا متجلببة بجلباب كبير يغطي رؤوسهن ووجوههن وصدورهن، والله يوفق الجميع لما يحب ويرضاه.
السؤال: وما حكم نظر الرجل من أمته وزوجته؟
الجواب: جاز له أن ينظر من أمته التي تحل له، وزوجته إلى جميع البدن.
السؤال : بقي حكم نظر الرجل إلى ذوات محارمه فبينوه.
الجواب: جاز للرجل النظر من ذوات محرمه إلى الوجه والرأس والصدر والساقين، ولا ينظر إلى ظهرها وبطنها وفخذها، ولا بأس بأن يمس ما جاز له أن ينظر إليه منهن.

Chapter on Intercourse, Looking, and Touching. He should not look at her face except out of need, and it is not permissible for him to touch her face or her hands, even if he feels safe from desire. Question: What need necessitates looking at her face? Answer: The need arises for a witness if he wants to testify against her, and for a judge if he wants to pass judgment against her, so it is permissible for them to look at the face of a non-mahram woman, even if they fear desire. Question: A sore on a woman's thigh and a need arises for a doctor to look at it; what should he do? Answer: It is permissible for him to look at the site of the sore only, and not at the rest of the thigh. Question: What is the ruling on a man looking at a man, and a woman looking at a woman? Answer: A man may look at another man's entire body except what is between his navel and his knees; likewise, a woman may look at another woman's body to what is permissible for a man to look at from a man. Question: What is permissible for a woman to look at from a man? Answer: It is permissible for her to look at from a man what is permissible for a man to look at from a man. The weak servant, may Allah pardon him, said: Since safety from desire is a condition for the permissibility of looking at the face of a non-mahram woman—rather it is also a condition for looking at the faces of mahram women—it is not from the religion for women to go out unveiling, revealing their faces in markets and streets, wandering in gardens and parks, because the time is a time of sin and disobedience. How can it be trusted that these sinners look at the faces of non-mahram women without desire in the soul and pleasure in the gaze? And some people have not paid attention to this, so they permitted women to go out unveiling from their homes, saying: 'The jurists mentioned that the face and hands may be uncovered before non-mahram men.' When women learned of this fatwa issued by these people, they went out ornamented, unveiling, revealing their faces, heads, and chests as seen in markets and alleys. Answer: It is permissible for him to look at his female slave whom he may have intercourse with, and his wife, at their entire body. Question: The ruling on a man looking at his mahram women remains; explain it. Answer: It is permissible for a man to look at his mahram women's face, head, chest, and legs; he should not look at their back, belly, or thigh. Commentary
This passage from 'Al-Tashil al-Daruri Sharh al-Quduri' presents a series of question-and-answer rulings on the permissibility of looking and touching between different categories of individuals. It opens with the general prohibition: a man may not look at a non-mahram woman's face except for a legitimate need, and he may not touch her face or hands even if he feels safe from desire. The author then clarifies exceptions: a witness or judge may look at her face for legal proceedings, and a doctor may look only at the injured area. For same-gender looking, the 'awrah (private area) is defined as between navel and knee. A woman may look at a man to the same extent. The author then offers a lengthy societal commentary, arguing that the classical ruling allowing face and hands to be uncovered was conditioned on safety from fitna (temptation), and in corrupt times women must cover fully. The rulings then cover mahram women: a man may look at their face, head, chest, and legs, but not back, belly, or thigh. He may look at his wife and female slave he may have intercourse with entirely. Looking at another's female slave is like looking at mahram. A male slave may not look at his mistress beyond what is allowed for an ajnabi. The marginal notes from al-Durr al-Mukhtar clarify that 'whom he may have intercourse with' excludes certain categories of slaves, and that the permissibility of looking at legs and touching mahram is conditioned on safety from desire.

Key Terms
أجنبية (ajnabiyyah): A woman who is not a mahram to a man, and not his wife or female slave.
محارم (mahārim): Relatives who are permanently prohibited for marriage due to blood, fosterage, or affinity.
حاجة (hājah): A legitimate necessity that justifies an otherwise prohibited act, such as witnessing or medical treatment.
