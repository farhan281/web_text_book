Tawkeel for Litigation: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
States the condition of opponent's consent for litigation agency and the intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on agency for litigation and identify the exceptional circumstances under which Abu Hanifa allows it without opponent consent.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, agency for litigation generally requires opponent consent unless the principal is sick or traveling three days or more.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of appointing an agent for litigation (tawkeel bil-khusumah). The core question is whether the opponent's consent is a condition for such agency. We will explore the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and understand the practical difference in their rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling question
The act is appointing an agent for litigation. The key question: does it require the opponent's consent to be valid?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that agency for litigation is not valid without the opponent's consent, except when the principal is sick or absent for a distance of three days or more.
The exception is for necessity—when the principal cannot attend personally.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that agency for litigation is valid even without the opponent's consent in all cases.
They consider the contract of agency as generally permissible without such condition.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa's view, a healthy present principal cannot delegate without opponent agreement; under the Sahibayn, he can. This affects the ease of conducting litigation.
Practical consequence: the opponent can obstruct representation under Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Consider the tarjih
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn's view for ease of transactions.
This is an example of later preference (tarjih) within the school based on practical need.

Agent for Litigation: Does He Also Receive?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on implied authority to receive after litigation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority position that a litigation agent may receive the proceeds, identify Imam Zufar's dissenting view, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
The agent for litigation is also agent for receipt — majority extends agency to avoid multiplicity.

A common question in agency law: if you appoint someone to represent you in court, can they also take possession of the property or money awarded? The Hanafi school addresses this directly. The majority of the founding imams hold that the litigation agency includes the power to receive, while Imam Zufar disagrees. Understanding this difference clarifies how agency authority is constructed and what a principal must specify.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
The source asks whether an agent appointed for litigation (khusumah) automatically has the right to receive what is obtained through that litigation.
This is about implied authority, not explicit permission.
2
State the Majority Position
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad all hold that the agent for litigation is also an agent for receipt. This is the zahir al-riwayah position.
The reasoning: receipt is a necessary completion of the litigation.
3
Identify the Dissenter
Imam Zufar held that the litigation agent does not have authority to receive; receipt is a separate act requiring separate agency.
Zufar's view is a minority intra-Hanafi opinion.
4
Analyze the Practical Difference (Thamarah)
Under the majority, the agent can validly take possession of the judgment award. Under Zufar, the principal must collect personally or appoint a separate agent.
This affects the validity of the agent's receipt.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
The majority view is the relied-upon position for fatwa, as it is the established view of the three leading imams.
Zufar's view is considered weak (marjuh) in practice.

When Does an Agent's Acknowledgment Bind the Principal?
Intra-Hanafi ruling on agent's acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority position on the validity of an agent's acknowledgment and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
An agent's acknowledgment binds the principal only if made in court; outside court it is invalid per the majority.

In litigation, an agent (wakil) may sometimes make a statement that admits a right against his principal. Does this acknowledgment bind the principal? The Hanafi school has an important internal difference. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its context
The passage discusses an 'iqrar' (acknowledgment) made by an agent for litigation (wakil bi al-khusumah) against his principal (muwakkil). Determine the act and the parties involved.
The acknowledgment admits something harmful to the principal.
2
State the majority Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad)
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the acknowledgment is valid and binding only if made before the judge (qadi). If made elsewhere, it is not valid.
They also rule that the agent is removed from the litigation after such an acknowledgment.
3
Identify the opposing view (Abu Yusuf)
Abu Yusuf holds that the acknowledgment is valid even outside court, thus binding the principal in all circumstances.
No explicit proof is cited in the passage; the difference rests on the scope of the agent's authority.
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under the majority view, the principal is only bound by court admissions; under Abu Yusuf, any admission by the agent binds the principal.
This affects whether the principal can contest the admission later.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
Later Hanafi texts prefer the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) as the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Thus, in practice, an agent's out-of-court acknowledgment does not bind the principal.

Definition and Types of Sulh
Defines sulh and classifies it into three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sulh and list its three types according to the Hanafi school, distinguishing each based on the defendant's stance.

Core Idea
Sulh is a contract to end disputes, valid even if the defendant denies the claim.

This passage opens the Book of Sulh (Settlement) in the Hanafi fiqh manual. It defines sulh and classifies it into three types depending on whether the defendant admits, denies, or remains silent about the claim. Understanding this classification is essential because each type carries different legal consequences regarding the nature of the transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of sulh
Read the definition: it is a mutual agreement where disputing parties take some of the claimed right and leave some, thereby ending the dispute.
The phrase 'wa al-sulhu khayr' (and sulh is better) indicates its praiseworthiness.
2
List the three types of sulh
The text states sulh is of three types: (1) with iqrar (admission), (2) with sukoon (silence), and (3) with inkar (denial).
These are based on the defendant's response to the claim.
3
Explain sulh with iqrar
The defendant admits the right but agrees to settle for something less or different.
This is treated as a sale (bay') if the consideration is money, or hire (ijara) if it is a usufruct.
4
Explain sulh with sukoon
The defendant neither admits nor denies but settles to avoid litigation.
Hanafi law treats this like sulh with denial (iftida'), meaning the defendant pays to end the claim.
5
Explain sulh with inkar
The defendant denies any liability but still agrees to pay something to remove the dispute.
From the defendant's view, it is an indemnity (iftida'); from the plaintiff's view, it is a sale of the disputed right.

Rulings of Sulh for Each Type
States legal rulings for two types of sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings of sulh with admission from sulh with denial and state the perspective-based legal nature of each.

Core Idea
Sulh with admission is a sale or lease; sulh with denial is a ransom for the defendant and a sale for the plaintiff.

Sulh (settlement) is a contract to end a dispute. Its legal nature changes depending on whether the defendant admits or denies the claim. This lesson unpacks the two scenarios, showing how the same payment can be a commutative transaction for one party and a ransom for the other.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of sulh
Note that sulh is classified based on the defendant's stance: iqrar (admission) or sukoon/inkar (silence/denial). Each has different rules.
The passage treats sukoon and inkar together.
2
Apply sale/lease rules to sulh with iqrar
If the defendant admits the claim, the settlement is treated as a sale (if property-for-property) or lease (if property-for-usufruct). Conditions of those contracts (offer, acceptance, etc.) must be satisfied.
3
Analyze the dual nature of sulh with sukoon/inkar
From the defendant's perspective, it is a ransom from the oath (iftida' al-yamin) and termination of litigation. From the plaintiff's perspective, it is a commutative transaction (mu'awada).
This duality arises because the defendant denies ownership of the claimed right.
4
Understand the practical implications
The different legal natures affect rights like the option of defect (khiyar al-'ayb). The plaintiff, viewing it as a sale, can return the item for a defect; the defendant cannot because he sees it as a ransom.
The text from al-Jawharah reinforces this asymmetry.

Commentary
This passage provides the legal rulings for the two broad categories of sulh: (1) Sulh with iqrar (admission) and (2) Sulh with sukoon (silence) and inkar (denial) combined. For sulh with iqrar, the ruling depends on the nature of the consideration: if property (mal) is exchanged for property, it is treated like a sale (bay'), and all conditions of a valid sale apply (offer, acceptance, possession, etc.). If property is exchanged for usufructs (manafi'), it is treated like a lease (ijara), and the rules of ijara apply. For sulh with sukoon and inkar, the ruling is dual: from the perspective of the defendant (al-mudda'a alayh), it is considered an iftida' (ransom) from having to take an oath (yamin) and to end the litigation. It is not a mu'awada because the defendant claims the property is his own, so what he pays cannot be a price for it; it is a payment to free himself from the dispute. From the perspective of the plaintiff (al-mudda'i), it is considered a mu'awada (commutative transaction) because he believes the claimed right is his, and what he receives is compensation for that right. This dual nature has practical implications: for example, the plaintiff can return the received amount if a defect is found, etc.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The judge must protect the missing person's wealth and support his family.

When a man disappears with no knowledge of his whereabouts or status, his property and family become vulnerable. Islamic law assigns the judge the duty to appoint a trustworthy guardian to preserve his wealth, collect his rights, and provide for his wife and minor children. This ruling ensures justice and prevents loss until the missing person's fate is known.

The judge's appointment and the guardian's actions are obligatory (wajib) to prevent harm and fulfill the needs of the dependents. The text uses an imperative (ينصب) indicating a duty.

This lesson covers a straightforward but important ruling in Islamic criminal law: when the killer and the heirs of the victim agree on a financial settlement instead of qisas, the right to retaliation is dropped and the money becomes an obligation. The amount is not restricted to the standard diyah; any sum agreed upon is binding.

One heir's forgiveness drops qisas entirely; remaining heirs claim diyah proportionally.

When multiple heirs are entitled to qisas, the Hanafi school holds a unique position: if any single heir forgives or settles, the entire right of qisas falls. Core Idea
A single execution suffices for multiple victims; one life cannot be taken more than once.

In criminal law, a single murderer may kill several people. This raises the question: should he be executed multiple times? The Hanafi school addresses this with a clear principle based on the nature of qisas.

Identify the scenario

State the ruling
The killer is executed once for all the victims collectively.
3
Explain the rationale
A person cannot be physically killed more than once; one death expiates all blood claims.
4
Apply the consequence for heirs
If one heir executes qisas, the rights of all other heirs drop immediately.
5
Contrast with other views
The Hanafi position is shared by the Shafi'i school; some Hanbali scholars differ but the relied-upon view is one execution.

When a group kills one person, all are executed – each is a killer.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When the killer dies, the right of qisas is extinguished — no claim on his estate.

This lesson addresses a critical boundary in criminal liability: what happens to the right of retaliatory execution (qisas) when the offender dies before the sentence is carried out. The Hanafi school holds that qisas, being a personal punishment, cannot be transferred to heirs or the estate. Therefore, the right drops entirely, and no financial compensation is due from the deceased's wealth.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the trigger condition
The ruling applies when a person has become liable for qisas (e.g., convicted of murder) but dies before the execution is carried out.
Death could be natural or otherwise; the key is that the sentence was not yet executed.
2
State the primary ruling
Qisas drops (saaqit). The obligation is extinguished.
This is the core ruling.
3
Identify the secondary consequence
Nothing is obligatory on the deceased's wealth — the estate bears no diyah or other compensation.
This contrasts with some other schools.
4
Understand the legal basis
Qisas is a personal physical punishment. It attaches to the body, not to property. Once the body is gone, the right to execute it is lost.
The victim's heirs cannot substitute qisas with a claim on the estate.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Islamic criminal law where one act results in two deaths with different degrees of intent. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying qisas and diyah. The ruling demonstrates how the Hanafi school treats unintended consequences of an intentional act.

Guided Path

Identify the two victims
In the scenario, there are two victims: the first is the direct target of the arrow, the second is the person hit after the arrow passes through the first.

Qisas is retributive; diyah is compensatory.

The diyah for accidental killing falls on the 'aqila, not the shooter personally, based on the principle of shared responsibility.
This is a Hanafi position; some schools differ on who pays.

Core Idea
A claim (da'wa) is attributing a right to oneself in a dispute.

Understanding the precise definition of da'wa is foundational for Islamic legal procedure. This lesson unpacks the term both linguistically and in Shariah, clarifying the roles of the parties involved and the grammatical nuances that distinguish it from ordinary speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term
Locate the word الدعوى (da'wa) in the passage. Note that it is the subject of the definition.
2
Separate linguistic from legal
Read the linguistic definition: 'attributing something to oneself' (إضافة الشيء إلى نفسه). Then read the legal definition: 'attributing something to oneself in a dispute' (إضافة الشيء إلى نفسه عند المنازعة). Compare the two to see the added condition of dispute.
The dispute (munaza'ah) is what gives the term its legal character.

From the example: 'Zaid claimed property from Amr' — identify Zaid as claimant (مدع), Amr as defendant (مدعى عليه), and the property as the subject of claim (مدعى به).

Observe that الدعوى is feminine (alif for femininity) and does not take tanwin. Its plural is دعاوى (da'awa) with fatha on the waw. Also note the verbal noun ادعاء (id'di'a') from افتعال.
Grammatical precision avoids confusion in legal documents.

When the Defendant Claims Property Belongs to an Absent Party
Q&A on defendant's claim that disputed property is a deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a defendant in a property claim proves that the item belongs to an absent third party.

Core Idea
If the defendant proves the property belongs to an absent party, the claimant cannot sue the defendant.

In a property dispute, the defendant may admit possession but claim the item belongs to an absent third party. This lesson examines the legal consequence of such a claim when supported by evidence. You will learn the conditions under which the defendant is discharged from the lawsuit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
A claimant (plaintiff) sues the defendant for a specific item. The defendant responds by stating that the item is not his own but is a deposit, pawn, or usurpation from an absent person. Understand that the defendant is not denying possession but denying ownership.
The defendant's claim does not exculpate him from possession, but it changes the legal relationship.
2
Recognize the three possible claims
The defendant may say it is a deposit (وديعة), a pawn (رهن), or that he usurped it (غصب) from an absent person. Each implies a non-ownership relationship.
These claims all point to a third party as the true owner.
3
Understand the condition for the ruling
The defendant must provide evidence (بينة) of his claim. Without proof, the claim is not accepted and the defendant remains liable as the possessor.
Evidence can include witnesses, documents, or other proof accepted by the court.
4
Apply the ruling
If the defendant brings evidence, the judge declares la khusumah (no lawsuit) between the claimant and the defendant. The case is dismissed between them.
The claimant is not barred from suing the absent party or bringing additional evidence against the defendant.
5
Identify the practical consequence
The claimant must pursue the absent third party to recover the property or prove that the defendant's claim is false. The defendant is not liable to the claimant as an owner.
This protects custodians who hold property on behalf of others.

When a Defendant Becomes a Proper Opponent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a defendant claiming purchase becomes a proper opponent (khasm) and distinguish it from cases where he merely holds the property for another.

Core Idea
A defendant who claims ownership by purchase is a proper opponent; one who merely holds for another is not.

In the previous ruling, a defendant who merely holds property for an absent party (e.g., as a deposit) was not considered a proper opponent. This passage clarifies the key distinction: if the defendant claims he bought the property from the absent party, he asserts ownership and becomes a proper litigant. Apply the rule
The Hanafi rule: A defendant who claims ownership (e.g., by purchase) becomes a proper opponent (khasm) to the claimant. The claimant can now proceed against him directly without needing the absent third party.

Conflicting Claims: Theft vs. Deposit – Evidence Does Not Dismiss
Q&A on conflicting claims with evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when both parties present evidence in a case of conflicting claims (theft vs. deposit) and explain why the lawsuit is not repelled.

Core Idea
When claims contradict, evidence alone does not dismiss the case; the judge must adjudicate.

In this Q&A, we examine a scenario where the claimant asserts theft and the possessor asserts deposit, both with evidence. The question is whether the lawsuit is dismissed. The answer is no—because the claims directly conflict, the judge must hear the case and decide. This ruling highlights a key principle in judicial procedure: conflicting evidence requires adjudication, not dismissal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
There is a possessor (defendant) holding an item. A third party (claimant) asserts that the item was stolen from him. The possessor counter-claims that a different person deposited it with him.
2
Note the evidence from both sides
Both parties bring evidence (witnesses) supporting their respective claims. The claimant's evidence supports theft; the possessor's evidence supports deposit.
3
Analyze the contradiction
The two claims are mutually exclusive: if it was stolen, then the possessor cannot have a valid deposit from a third party (since theft implies wrongful taking). If it is a valid deposit, theft is negated.
This contradicts the earlier case where the defendant merely claimed deposit without the claimant alleging theft—there, no direct conflict arises because the claimant does not dispute the defendant's right to hold.
4
Apply the ruling
Because the claims contradict, the lawsuit is not repelled. The judge must hear both sides and evaluate the evidence to determine which claim is stronger.
The ruling 'la tandafi' (not repelled) means the case proceeds to trial.
5
Distinguish from a case without direct contradiction
If the claimant merely said 'this item is mine' without alleging theft, and the defendant said 'it is a deposit,' the claims do not necessarily contradict; the case may be dismissed if the defendant's evidence is sufficient. But here, the theft allegation creates a direct conflict.
This distinction is crucial for legal practice.
