Obligation of Fasting Ramadan
Q&A establishing obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that fasting Ramadan is fard 'ayn upon every mature Muslim and cite the Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
Fasting Ramadan is fard 'ayn — a personal obligation proven by the Quran's command.

This lesson establishes the foundational ruling of fasting Ramadan. You will learn not only the obligation itself but also who it applies to and the Quranic proof that makes it binding. The passage uses a simple Q&A format to make the ruling clear and memorable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is fasting the month of Ramadan. The ruling is fard (obligation). In the Hanafi school, 'fard' means an obligation proven by definitive evidence (qat'i).
2
Identify the conditions of obligation
The obligation applies to every mature (baligh) Muslim, male or female. Conditions: being Muslim, having reached puberty, and being sane.
3
Locate the Quranic proof
The author cites Surah Al-Baqarah 2:183, which begins 'O you who have believed, fasting is prescribed for you...'
4
Understand the evidence: wajh al-dalalah
The word 'kutiba' (prescribed) indicates an obligation. In Hanafi usul, a command expressed in the past tense is still binding. The verse directly addresses believers, making it a personal duty.

Definition and Timing of Fasting
Defines fasting linguistically and legally with time limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of sawm and identify its time limits and essential condition according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Fasting is abstaining from food, drink, and marital relations from true dawn to sunset with intention.

This foundational passage from the matn clarifies the precise meaning of sawm. Understanding this definition is essential for all subsequent rulings on fasting, as it sets the boundaries of what constitutes a valid fast in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish linguistic and legal meanings
Identify that linguistically, sawm means any refraining, while legally it is restricted to refraining from specific acts with intention and within a specific time.
The linguistic meaning is broader; the legal meaning is a customised definition.
2
List the three abstentions
Note that the legal definition requires abstaining from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse. These are the only acts that break the fast.
Other acts like backbiting are sinful but do not break the fast.
3
Identify the time frame
The fast begins at the second dawn (true dawn) and ends at sunset. The first dawn (false dawn) is not considered.
The second dawn is when the light spreads horizontally across the horizon.
4
Recognize the condition of intention
Intention (niyyah) is a required condition for the fast to be valid. Without it, the abstention does not count as worship.
Intention must be made before the time of fasting begins; it can be made at night or before dawn.

Rukhsah for Traveler and Sick in Ramadan
Rulings on concession for traveler and sick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a traveler or sick person may break the fast in Ramadan and identify the obligation of qada upon recovery.

Core Idea
A traveler or sick person may break the fast but must make up missed days if the excuse is removed.

Fasting in Ramadan is obligatory, but the Hanafi school grants a concession (rukhsah) for those facing genuine hardship. This lesson examines the precise conditions that permit breaking the fast and the subsequent obligation to make up missed days. The passage clarifies two categories—traveler and sick—and addresses what happens if death occurs during the state of excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the first question-answer to list the two types of people granted rukhsah: the traveler (undertaking qasr distance) and the sick person (fearing increased illness).
Traveler must be traveling masafat al-qasr; sick must fear worsening.
2
Note the condition for each
The traveler's condition is the distance; the sick's condition is the fear of harm (not just any illness). Understand that these are permissions, not obligations.
Fasting is better for the traveler if no harm.
3
Understand the ruling upon death
If either dies while still in the state of travel or illness, no qada or fidyah is due. The concession covers them completely.
This is a unique Hanafi position.
4
Understand the ruling upon recovery/residence
If the sick recovers or the traveler returns to residence, qada of missed fasts becomes wajib (obligatory).
Residence means original homeland or watan al-iqamah.
5
Distinguish permission vs. better
The traveler is allowed to break but fasting is afdal if he can do so without harm. This emphasizes that rukhsah is not a license to abandon worship.
The school encourages spiritual excellence.

Fasting Rulings for Pregnant, Breastfeeding, and Menstruating Women
Provides rulings for two categories of women regarding fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the concession for pregnant/breastfeeding women and the prohibition for menstruating/postpartum women regarding fasting in Ramadan, and state the obligation of qada for both groups.

Core Idea
Pregnant and breastfeeding women may break if fearful; menstruating and postpartum women must not fast — both must make up missed days.

This passage from the Quduri matn clarifies two distinct rulings for women in Ramadan. While both categories—pregnant/breastfeeding and menstruating/postpartum—share the obligation to make up missed fasts, the permissibility of breaking the fast differs: one is a rukhsah (concession), the other a prohibition. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of women
The passage addresses two groups: pregnant/breastfeeding and menstruating/postpartum. Separate their rulings.
2
State the ruling for each category
For pregnant/breastfeeding: it is permissible (ja'iz) to break if fear of harm. For menstruating/postpartum: it is obligatory (wajib) not to fast, i.e., haram to fast.
The first is a rukhsah, the second is an original prohibition.
3
Note the shared consequence
Both groups must make up (qada) the missed days after Ramadan.
4
Distinguish the basis
The pregnant/breastfeeding ruling is based on fear of harm (rukhsah); the menstruating/postpartum ruling is because menstruation and postpartum bleeding are incompatible with fasting (manafi li al-sawm).
This explains why one is permissible and the other is obligatory to break.

Elderly Incapable of Fasting: Fidyah
Defines the elderly who cannot fast and their obligation to pay fidyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a shaykh fani regarding fasting and define fidyah with its conditions.

Core Idea
An elderly person who can never fast must pay fidyah, not qada.

Fasting Ramadan is obligatory for every capable Muslim. But what if someone is so old or ill that they can never fast, even after Ramadan? The Quran allows a compensation. In this lesson, we define who qualifies and what they must pay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the special category
Note the term 'shaykh fani' – a person who cannot fast permanently, neither in Ramadan nor later. Understand that this excludes temporary incapacity.
Shaykh fani: man or woman.
2
Determine the ruling for Ramadan
The text says 'jaza lahu an la yasuma' – it is permitted not to fast. This is a rukhsah (concession). The obligation is lifted but replaced by fidyah.
Not a license to skip without compensation.
3
Understand the dropped qada
Qada is not obligatory because the incapacity extends to the end of life. Impossibility removes the duty of qada.
Contrast with temporary illness where qada remains.
4
Define fidyah
Fidyah is feeding one poor person per missed day, similar to kaffarah. Its value follows the same rules as expiatory feeding.
Hanafi: half sa' wheat or one sa' dates/barley.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools may require qada or combine fidyah and qada. The Hanafi position is based on impossibility (al-mashaqqah), not postponement.
Importance of understanding the basis.

Ruling for Delayed Qada of Fasts Until Death
Ruling for one who postponed making up missed fasts and died

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a person who delays qada of missed Ramadan fasts until death and identify the two required actions: tawbah and wasiyyah for fidyah.

Core Idea
Repentance and a will for fidyah: the Hanafi solution for unrecovered fasting debts.

This passage addresses a common situation: a Muslim who missed Ramadan fasts due to a valid excuse (travel/illness) later becomes able to make them up but procrastinates and dies without doing so. The Hanafi school provides a clear teaching: combine spiritual repentance with a financial provision from the estate. Understanding this ruling protects one from a lingering obligation and ensures the heir knows their duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Determine the person's situation: he had a valid excuse (safar or marad) that prevented fasting during Ramadan. Later, the excuse ended, and he had sufficient days before death to perform qada, but he did not. Death then approached.
The key condition: he had the ability to make up (قدرة على القضاء) but neglected it.
2
State the Spiritual Duty
The person must first turn to Allah in sincere repentance (tawbah) and seek forgiveness. This is because delaying an obligatory act (qada) without reason is sinful.
Repentance (الاستغفار والتوبة) is wajib for the sin of procrastination.
3
Define the Financial Provision
In addition to tawbah, he must make a will (wasiyyah) that his guardian (wali) give fidyah from his estate for each missed day: half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates or barley.
A sa' is approximately 2.5 kg (Hanafi). The type of staple is specified.
4
Clarify the Condition of the Will
The fidyah is binding on the estate only if the deceased made a will. Without a will, the heirs have no obligation to give fidyah from the estate (though they may do so voluntarily).
This distinguishes Hanafi from schools that require qada by proxy.

Fidyah Rulings: Amount, Manner, and Guardian's Liability
Expands on fidyah rulings from multiple Hanafi sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fidyah amount for hardship break, explain the distinction from sadaqat al-fitr, and apply the ruling on guardian's liability for the deceased's fidyah from a will.

Core Idea
Fidyah for hardship break requires two filling meals per day, unlike sadaqat al-fitr; guardian must fulfill will for fidyah from one-third of estate.

This lesson draws from multiple authoritative Hanafi sources to clarify two connected rulings: the fidyah due for breaking a fast due to hardship, and the obligation of the guardian to execute a will for fidyah of the deceased. Understanding the precise amount, manner, and legal implications ensures correct practice in matters of compensation and estate management.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and ruling
Determine why fidyah is given instead of qada – the excuse (hardship) is not temporary, so compensation is immediate. Note the ruling: fidyah is obligatory (wajib) per day.
This contrasts with a temporary illness where qada is delayed.
2
Determine the amount and type of food
Identify the quantity: half a sa' of wheat or raisins, or one sa' of dates or barley – same as sadaqat al-fitr in measure. However, the manner is two filling meals per day (ta'am al-ibahah).
The term 'sa'' is a measure roughly 2.5 kg for wheat, but varies by region.
3
Distinguish from sadaqat al-fitr
Note the key difference: sadaqat al-fitr is given once per person, while fidyah is given as two meals per day. The quantity per meal is similar, but the distribution differs.
This nuance prevents confusion in application.
4
Apply the will ruling for the deceased
If the deceased made a will for fidyah, the guardian must pay from one-third of the estate. If no will, it is permissible but not obligatory. If the heir volunteers, it is accepted.
The 'guardian' is the executor or heir managing the estate.

Fidya for the Elderly and the Deceased: Compensation for Missed Fasts
Q&A on fidya and qada for elderly and deceased

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on fidya for the permanently incapacitated elderly and for the deceased with unfulfilled fasts, and apply the conditions that trigger obligation versus merely recommended payment.

Core Idea
The permanently incapable elderly pay fidya; the deceased who could have made up must bequeath fidya from one-third of the estate.

Fasting is a pillar of Islam, but Allah grants concessions. One such concession is the permission to break the fast in Ramadan due to permanent incapacity (old age) or temporary excuses (travel/illness). This lesson examines two situations: the elderly who cannot fast at all and the deceased who had the chance to make up but did not. In both, fidya (compensation) becomes due, but the rules differ in obligation and source of payment. Understanding these distinctions is vital for correct application in daily life and estate planning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The lesson covers two distinct cases: (1) the living elderly (shaykh fani) who is permanently unable to fast and cannot perform qada, and (2) a person who died after having missed fasts due to a temporary excuse and having the ability to make up but failing to do so.
The first is a living person, the second is a deceased person – different rulings apply.
2
Determine the ruling for the living elderly
For the shaykh fani, fasting is not obligatory (he is excused), but he must pay fidya for each missed day. This is a wajib substitute because qada is impossible.
Fidya is paid during life, not left as a bequest.
3
Determine the ruling for the deceased
If a person had a temporary excuse (travel/illness) and was able to make up the fasts but did not, and then dies, he must repent and bequeath that fidya be paid from his estate. The bequest is limited to one-third of the estate; if no bequest, the heir is not obligated (but may volunteer).
The bequest is wajib if the person had the ability to make up and missed it.
4
Apply the fidya amount and type
The fidya per missed day is half a sa' of wheat or a sa' of dates/barley (or equivalent value). This is similar to sadaqat al-fitr in quantity but differs in that ownership (tamlik) is not required; mere permission (ibaha) suffices.
Later authorities (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, al-Durr al-Mukhtar) confirm this.
5
Distinguish the obligation of the guardian
The guardian (heir or executor) is obligated to execute the bequest from the deceased's wealth (if bequest was made). If no bequest, it is mustahabb for the guardian to pay voluntarily, but not wajib.
The term wali includes both heir and executor.

Rulings on Those Who Start Fasting Mid-Day in Ramadan
Q&A on partial-day fasting obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for five categories of people who become obligated to fast mid-day in Ramadan and identify when imsak is required and when qada is not required.

Core Idea
Partial-day obligation requires imsak but not qada of that day for new obligants.

In the month of Ramadan, not everyone begins the day already obligated to fast. A minor may reach puberty, a disbeliever may accept Islam, an unconscious person may regain awareness, an insane person may recover sanity, a traveler may arrive home, or a menstruating woman may become pure. Each of these cases raises the question: what must they do for the remainder of that day? And must they make up that partial day later? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position based on the text of Sharh al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five categories
List the five types of people mentioned in the passage: minor reaching puberty, disbeliever accepting Islam, unconscious person, insane person recovering, traveler arriving (or menstruating woman becoming pure).
The traveler and menstruating woman are grouped together in the last question.
2
State the common ruling for the remainder of the day
For all categories, the ruling is: they must refrain (imsak) from fast-breakers (eating, drinking, sexual intercourse) for the rest of that day out of respect for the month of Ramadan.
This is wajib, but NOT a valid fast, so if they break it, no qada is due.
3
Distinguish imsak from actual fasting
Imsak is merely abstaining from these three things; it does not constitute a valid fast that would require a missed-day makeup if broken. The text explicitly says no qada is required for that partial day.
For the unconscious person who had intended before losing consciousness, the day is counted as a valid fast and no qada for that day.
4
Identify exceptions: unconscious person with prior intention
If a person intended to fast before becoming unconscious, that day's fast is valid and no qada is required. But any full days of unconsciousness must be made up.
This highlights the importance of intention at the beginning of the day.
5
Apply to the insane person's makeup requirement
The insane person must make up all days missed during insanity, and fast the remaining days of Ramadan. This differs from the partial-day cases where no qada for the day of recovery.
The insane person's state covered full days, so qada is required for those full days.

Establishing Ramadan and Eid Crescents in Hanafi Fiqh
Rulings on establishing Ramadan and Eid crescents with testimony conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for accepting crescent testimony for Ramadan and Eid, and explain the ruling for an individual whose testimony is rejected.

Core Idea
For Ramadan, one witness suffices when cloudy; for Eid, two; the community's ruling prevails over private sighting.

The beginning and end of Ramadan depend on moon sighting. The Prophet's hadith provides the framework, and Hanafi fiqh details the conditions for accepting testimony. This passage from al-Quduri clarifies the rules for both Ramadan and Eid, as well as the individual's duty when his testimony is not accepted. Understanding these rulings ensures proper observance of the fast in accordance with the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary evidence
Start with the hadith quoted in the text: 'Fast upon seeing it and break your fast upon seeing it...' This is the foundational dalil.
The hadith is reported by Bukhari and Muslim.
2
Analyze the default rule and exception
The default is that the crescent must be seen by a large group for certain knowledge. The exception is when the sky is cloudy (ghaym or 'illah), in which case the testimony of one just Muslim suffices for Ramadan.
This exception is derived from the phrase 'فإن غُمَّ عليكم'.
3
Contrast clear vs cloudy for Ramadan
In clear skies, mass testimony is needed; in cloudy, one witness is accepted. Understand the wisdom: facilitating the start of Ramadan.
4
Examine the stricter requirements for Eid
For Eid al-Fitr, even with clouds, two men or one man and two women are required; without clouds, mass testimony. Note the asymmetry with Ramadan.
The stricter requirement for Eid avoids a premature end of Ramadan.
5
Understand the individual's duty when testimony is rejected
If a person sees the crescent but the imam does not accept, for Ramadan he fasts alone (privately), but for Eid he fasts with the people (does not break fast). This maintains community unity.
The ruling balances personal certainty with public order.

Commentary
This passage explains how the month of Ramadan is established (thubut), based on the hadith of the Prophet. The key principle is that fasting begins upon sighting the crescent (ru'ya) of Ramadan. This ensures unity and avoids public confusion. The hadith is cited as proof, with reference to Bukhari and Muslim.

العلة (al-'illah): An atmospheric obstruction such as clouds, dust, or fog that prevents clear sighting of the crescent.

The Definition of Intention (Niyyah) in Fasting
Definition of niyyah and beginning of classification of types of fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of niyyah for fasting and explain why it must be made in the heart, not verbally.

Core Idea
Intention is the resolve in the heart — not spoken — and it must specify which fast you are observing.

This passage establishes a foundational principle: fasting requires a specific intention (niyyah) made in the heart. The author defines niyyah as 'al-ʿaqd bil-qalb' — a binding resolve — to fast a particular fast for Allah. This definition carries practical rules about timing and specification, which we will explore.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Read the passage and find the exact Arabic phrase that defines niyyah. The definition follows the question 'fama ma'na al-niyyah?' (what is the meaning of intention?).
The definition is concise: 'al-niyyah hiya al-ʿaqd bil-qalb'.
2
Identify the operative words
The definition uses 'al-ʿaqd bil-qalb' (resolve in the heart). Note that 'al-ʿaqd' implies a firm contract, not a mere thought. 'Bil-qalb' emphasizes the internal nature — not verbal.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes intention from mere awareness; it is an active resolve.
3
Note the object of intention
The intention is for 'al-sawm al-fulani' (a specific fast). This means the intention must specify which fast (e.g., Ramadan, a vow, voluntary) and for whose sake (Allah).
Specifying the type of fast is a condition of validity.
4
Mark the condition
The text states that intention is a condition (shart) for fasting: 'qayyadtum al-sawm bi-al-niyyah.' Without it, the act of abstaining does not count as worship.
This condition is derived from the hadith 'Innama al-a'mal bil-niyyat'.
5
Recognize the classification
The passage begins to list types of fasting ('thumma al-sawm 'ala duroob'). Though it cuts off, anticipate categories like obligatory (Ramadan, kaffarah) and voluntary (nafl).
The types will determine the specific rules for niyyah timing (night vs. after dawn).

Night Intention for Tamattu' and Qiran Fasts
Q&A on necessity of night intention for compensatory fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the fasts of tamattu' and qiran require intention from the night and explain why they are treated like other expiatory fasts.

Core Idea
Tamattu' and qiran fasts require night intention because they are obligatory expiations, not voluntary.

This short Q&A addresses a specific case: pilgrims performing tamattu' or qiran Hajj who cannot afford the sacrificial animal must fast. The ruling clarifies that these fasts are akin to other expiatory fasts (kaffarat) and thus require the intention to be made during the night before dawn. Understanding this condition is essential for the validity of these compensatory fasts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act is the fast observed by a mutamatti' or qarin as compensation when unable to offer the sacrificial animal (hadi). This falls under obligatory fasts (wajib), specifically expiatory fasts.
2
Identify the hukm level of the condition
The passage states that 'it is required' (yushtarat) that they intend from the night. This makes night intention a condition (shart) for the validity of the fast.
In Hanafi fiqh, conditions for obligatory fasts are stricter than for voluntary fasts.
3
Locate the analogy (qiyas)
The author compares these fasts to 'the fasts of the rest of the expiations' (kaffarat). This indicates the ruling is derived by analogy: since all obligatory expiatory fasts require night intention, these do too.
The basis is the general hadith: 'Whoever does not intend the fast before dawn has no fast.' (Abu Dawud)
4
Determine the consequence of omission
If the pilgrim fails to make the intention during the night, the fast is invalid (batil) and must be made up (qada') with the correct intention.
Contrast with voluntary fasts where intention can be made before midday in the Hanafi school.

Ruling on Qada for Broken Voluntary Fast
Explanation that breaking a voluntary fast requires qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that breaking a voluntary fast requires qada and explain the principle that a voluntary act becomes binding once started.

Core Idea
Once you begin a voluntary act, it becomes wajib to complete; breaking it requires qada.

This lesson addresses a common point of confusion: whether a voluntary act of worship, once started, can be abandoned without consequence. The Hanafi school holds that the volunteer is free before starting, but upon commencement, completion becomes obligatory. If the act is corrupted, qada (makeup) becomes necessary. This principle applies across voluntary prayers, fasting, Hajj, and Umrah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial freedom of the volunteer
Before starting, the volunteer (mutatawwi') is the commander of himself — he is free to begin or not.
This is the general principle for all nafl acts.
2
Recognize the obligation upon starting
Once the act is commenced, completion becomes wajib (obligatory). This is based on the Quranic command and hadith.
3
Determine the consequence of breaking
If the voluntary act is broken (e.g., a fast is intentionally nullified), qada becomes wajib.
4
Apply the rule to other acts
The same ruling applies to voluntary prayer, Hajj, Umrah — all voluntary acts become binding once started.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) do not require qada for broken voluntary fasts. Understand the basis of the Hanafi position.

Invalidators of Fasting That Only Require Qada
List of actions that invalidate fast and necessitate only makeup

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the nine actions that invalidate the fast and identify which require only qada (not kaffarah) in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Nine actions invalidate the fast but only require qada — no kaffarah.

In the Hanafi school, fasting in Ramadan is a pillar of Islam. While some violations, such as deliberate intercourse or eating, require both qada (makeup) and kaffarah (expiation), many other actions that break the fast only necessitate qada. This passage, structured as a question and answer, enumerates those actions. Understanding this list is essential for distinguishing between major and minor invalidators.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invalidators
List each action mentioned in the passage: emission from kiss/touch, swallowing a pebble/iron/date stone, incomplete intercourse with emission, enema, snuff, ear drops, wound treatment, mistaken suhur, mistaken iftar.
There are nine distinct invalidators.
2
Categorize by cause
Group them into: sexual/emission, swallowing non-food items, medical interventions (enema, snuff, ear drops, wound), and mistakes in timing.
This helps in memorisation and application.
3
Note the common consequence
All these invalidators require only qada (makeup). Contrast with deliberate intercourse or eating which require kaffarah as well.
Kaffarah is only for major violations in Ramadan.
4
Apply the rule
Given a new scenario (e.g., swallowing a vitamin tablet), decide if it fits this list or if it requires kaffarah.

This passage lists the actions that invalidate a fast (mufsidat al-sawm) but do not require kaffarah (expiation) — only qada (makeup) is obligatory. In all these, the fast is invalid and only qada is required because the action does not fulfill the description of a major violation that warrants kaffarah (such as deliberate intercourse or eating).

Instilling Medicine into the Urethra: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Fasting Ruling
Intra-Hanafi dispute on a specific nullifier of fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on this issue and apply the relied-upon position in fatwa.

Core Idea
Medicine instilled into the urethra does not invalidate the fast according to the majority of Hanafi imams.

A straightforward action—dripping medicine into the urethra—generates an internal Hanafi disagreement. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold the fast remains valid, while Abu Yusuf considers it broken. Which view is relied upon for fatwa, and why? This lesson unpacks the rationale and practical consequence of this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act in question and the legal context
The act is instilling medicine drop by drop into the urethra (iqtar fi al-ihlil) while fasting. Determine the general principle: anything that enters the body through an open passage may invalidate the fast.
The urethra is a specific passage that the Hanafi school analyzes separately.
2
State the positions of the three Hanafi imams
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the fast remains valid (no invalidation). Abu Yusuf: the fast is invalidated. Memorize this as an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The passage explicitly gives the answer: 'la yufsidu bi-dhalika sawmuhu' (his fast is not invalidated) for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
3
Analyse the apparent basis of each position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad likely consider that the urethra is not an open passage (manfadh maftuh) that reaches the interior (jauf). Abu Yusuf may analogize (qiyas) it to the ear or nose, where medicine reaches the stomach.
The passage does not give the proofs, but this reasoning is well-known in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and explain the tarjih
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as it is the majority view and the basis of fatwa in the school.
This tarjih is based on the principle of akhdh bi qawl al-jumhur (taking the majority opinion) within the madhhab.
5

Basic Ruling on Vomiting and Kaffarah in Fasting
Basic rulings on invalidation of fast by vomiting and kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on vomiting in fasting and identify when qada and/or kaffarah are required.

Core Idea
Involuntary vomiting does not break the fast; deliberate vomiting of a mouthful requires qada; kaffarah is only for Ramadan violations.

Many people assume any vomiting invalidates a fast, but Hanafi fiqh makes a crucial distinction between involuntary and deliberate vomiting. This lesson establishes the basic rulings from al-Quduri's matn, including when qada or kaffarah apply.

1. Identify the act
Determine whether the person vomited involuntarily (dhara'ahu al-qay') or deliberately induced vomiting (istiqa').

If deliberate, is it a mouthful (mil' al-fam) or less? The threshold for invalidation is a mouthful.

Whoever deliberately breaks a Ramadan fast by intercourse or eating/drinking must perform qada and kaffarah (free a slave, fast two months, or feed 60 poor).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani to scenarios involving return or re-swallowing of vomit and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on whether return or re-swallowing of vomit breaks the fast based on the principle of ‘legally recognized exit’ vs ‘action’.

The basic ruling on vomiting is straightforward, but the Hanafi school contains a sophisticated internal dispute over cases where vomit returns by itself or is re-swallowed. This lesson unpacks the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani as reported in Fath al-Qadeer, revealing how distinct usul principles lead to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the three variables
Determine: (a) type of vomiting (overcome or induced), (b) quantity (mouthful or less), (c) whether the vomit exits, returns by itself, or is re-swallowed.
The dispute is about scenarios where vomit comes out and then either returns (awd) or is re-swallowed (i'adah).
2
2. State Abu Yusuf's principle
Abu Yusuf considers the legal exit (khuruj shar'i): if a mouthful exits, it nullifies ablution; thus, if it re-enters, it breaks the fast. For less than mouthful, no legal exit, so re-entry does not break.
His focus is on the exit as the operative cause (illah).
3
3. State Muhammad's principle
Muhammad considers the action (sunn'): re-swallowing, regardless of quantity, is a deliberate act that breaks the fast. However, if vomit returns by itself (no action), it does not break.
His focus is on the voluntary act of swallowing.
4
4. Apply to the four scenarios
Work through: mouthful returns by itself, mouthful re-swallowed, less than mouthful returns, less than mouthful re-swallowed. Each has a different combination of rulings.
Consensus: re-swallowing a mouthful breaks fast for both.
5
5. Identify the mufta bihi
The apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) often follows Muhammad, but some later scholars preferred Abu Yusuf's view in certain cases. Zufar agrees with Muhammad.
The text indicates the 'chosen view' (mukhtar) is often Abu Yusuf's for less-than-mouthful re-swallowing.

Rulings of I'tikaf: Definition, Types, and Conditions
Q&A on i'tikaf: definition, ruling, types, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of i'tikaf, differentiate its types (sunnah mu'akkadah kifayah, mustahabb, wajib), and identify the conditions for a vowed i'tikaf according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is staying in the mosque with intention; its ruling ranges from sunnah to wajib, and fasting is a condition for a vowed i'tikaf.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rulings on i'tikaf (spiritual retreat). You will learn its essential definition, the various legal categories it falls into, and the specific conditions that apply when it is undertaken as a vow. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying i'tikaf in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and essential elements
Extract the definition from the passage: i'tikaf is 'staying in the mosque with intention' (al-lubth fi al-masjid bi al-niyyah). Note that both the physical act of staying and the intention are required.
Intention differentiates i'tikaf from mere lingering in the mosque.
2
Classify the primary ruling (sunnah mu'akkadah kifayah)
The passage states that i'tikaf in the last ten days of Ramadan is an emphatic sunnah that is communal obligation (sunnah mu'akkadah 'ala al-kifayah). If one person from the locality performs it, the obligation is lifted; otherwise all are sinful.
This is the default recommended i'tikaf.
3
Distinguish other types: mustahabb and wajib
I'tikaf outside Ramadan is mustahabb (recommended), with a minimum duration of one hour. It becomes wajib (obligatory) when one makes a vow (nadhr) to perform it, and the obligation is according to the vow.
The vow makes the act binding.
4
Analyse conditions for a vowed i'tikaf
For a vowed i'tikaf, the passage specifies: (1) nights are included and must be consecutive even if not stipulated; (2) fasting is a condition (shart) for the i'tikaf to be valid. If one vows i'tikaf without fasting, he must still fast because the fast is inherent to the vow.
This differs from the Shafi'i school which does not require fasting.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that i'tikaf is sunnah but fasting is not a condition for its validity, even if vowed. The Hanafi school relies on the principle that a vow must fulfill its requirements as intended, and fasting is an implied part of i'tikaf based on practice and hadith.
This ikhtilaf affects how vows of i'tikaf are executed.

Strict Consecutiveness in Kaffarah Fast
Ruling on breaking the kaffarah fast for any reason

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking the kaffarah fast and explain why any break, even with an excuse, invalidates consecutiveness.

Core Idea
Any break in the two-month kaffarah fast, excused or not, destroys consecutiveness and requires restarting.

The kaffarah fast for zihar requires two consecutive months. A common misunderstanding is that a valid excuse like travel might allow continuation. This lesson clarifies that the condition of tawatur is absolute: any break, regardless of reason, nullifies the consecutiveness and forces a fresh start.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the kaffarah fast (two consecutive months) for zihar. The ruling: if any day is missed for any reason, the fast must be restarted from the beginning.
The text says 'يستأنف' (restart).
2
Identify the condition
The key condition is tawatur (consecutiveness): the two months must be fasted without any break.
Tawatur is a shart (condition) for validity.
3
Recognize the consequence of breaking the condition
If tawatur is broken, the entire sequence becomes invalid; the person cannot merely make up the missed day but must restart the two-month period.
This applies whether the break was with a valid excuse (like illness) or without (intentional).
4
Note the absoluteness of the ruling
The ruling does not distinguish between excuses. The moment consecutiveness is lost, the obligation to start over is triggered.
No qada of individual days; full restart.
