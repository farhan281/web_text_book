Breastfeeding Between Co-Wives: Prohibition and Mahr
Ruling on breastfeeding between co-wives and mahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when an older wife breastfeeds a younger wife and explain the mahr distribution based on the older wife's intent.

Core Idea
Breastfeeding between co-wives invalidates both marriages and shifts mahr liability to the intentional actor.

In this lesson, we examine a specific case from the Hanafi fiqh of breastfeeding (rida'): a man marries an older woman and a young girl who is still being breastfed. The older wife then breastfeeds the younger wife. What happens to the marriages and the dower (mahr)? The ruling is clear: both women become forbidden to the husband, and the mahr consequences depend on the older wife's intent. Let's walk through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal effect
The older wife breastfeeds the younger wife. Determine the ruling on the marriage: does it remain valid or become invalid?
Breastfeeding creates a foster-mother relationship, making the younger wife a mahram to the older wife's husband.
2
State the prohibition
Both wives become forbidden (haram) to the husband. The marriage is dissolved because the older wife becomes the mother (by fosterage) of the younger wife.
This ruling applies regardless of the intent behind the breastfeeding.
3
Analyze the mahr for the older wife
If the husband had not consummated the marriage with the older wife, she receives no mahr because she caused the dissolution by her own act.
If consummation had occurred, the rule might differ, but here it is before consummation.
4
Analyze the mahr for the younger wife
The younger wife is entitled to half the mahr (standard for dissolution before consummation). The husband must pay this, but may recover it from the older wife if she intentionally caused the corruption (ta'ammud al-fasad).
Intent is key: if the older wife deliberately breastfed to break the marriage, she must reimburse the husband.
5
Consider the role of intent
If the older wife did not intend to cause corruption, she is not liable for reimbursement. The husband bears the half-mahr himself.
The Hanafi school distinguishes between deliberate and accidental acts affecting marital rights.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mixing milk with food: Abu Hanifa says no prohibition; Sahibayn say yes if milk dominates. The relied-upon view is the Sahibayn's.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between the school's founder and his two foremost students. The issue is whether breast milk that has been mixed with solid food can still establish the foster relationship (rada') that creates mahram prohibitions. Understanding this dispute reveals how later Hanafi authorities weighted the opinions of the Sahibayn over the Imam's own view in matters of precaution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the point of disagreement
Locate the exact issue: does mixing breast milk with food nullify its effect for establishing foster relationships, even if the milk is the majority?
The dispute is only about cases where milk is the majority; if milk is minor, all agree no prohibition.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa holds that no prohibition attaches, even if the milk is dominant. For him, mixing with food changes the legal identity of the milk.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that prohibition attaches if the milk is the dominant component. They consider the substance as milk by preponderance.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a child eating milk-mixed food does not become a mahram; under the Sahibayn, the child becomes a foster-child and marriage prohibitions apply.
5
Recognize the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi manuals (e.g., al-Hidayah) prefer the view of the Sahibayn. The operative ruling for fatwa is that prohibition attaches when milk dominates.
This is due to the principle of ihtiyat (legal caution).

Establishing Lineage from Husband: The Six-Month Minimum
Ruling on lineage attribution based on birth timing

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A child born six lunar months or more after marriage is attributed to the husband if he acknowledges or stays silent.

Lineage (nasab) is a fundamental legal right in Islamic family law. This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on when a child's lineage is established from the husband based on the timing of birth relative to the marriage contract. The key principle is the minimum pregnancy period of six lunar months—a threshold that determines whether the child is legally attributed to the husband or not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize the context: a man marries a woman, and she gives birth to a child. The question is whether that child's lineage is legally attributed to the husband.
The scenario assumes a valid marriage contract.
2
Apply the Time Condition
Determine if the birth occurred at six lunar months or more from the day of marriage. If yes, proceed to step 3. If less than six months, lineage is not established—no further action needed.
Six lunar months = approximately 177 days.

Consider the Husband's Response
If the time condition is met, lineage is established if the husband explicitly acknowledges the child or remains silent (silence is considered acceptance). If he denies the child, lineage is not established and he must perform li'an (mutual cursing) to sever the attribution.
The text only mentions acknowledgment and silence; denial via li'an is covered elsewhere.

Spousal Disputes on Mahr and Household Goods
Procedural rules for judges in spousal claims over mahr and household goods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for adjudicating spousal disputes over mahr amount and household goods ownership.

Core Idea
Evidence and customary presumptions guide the judge in spousal financial disputes.

Marital disputes over financial matters can lead to conflicting claims. This passage from the Book of Claims (Kitab al-Da‘wa) provides clear rules for a judge to resolve two common scenarios: disagreement over the amount of mahr (dowry) and ownership of household items. These rulings are based on presumptions of customary practice and the availability of evidence. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone studying Hanafi fiqh, as they illustrate how the school balances textual proof (evidence) with customary practice (urf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of dispute
First, determine whether the dispute is about the mahr amount or ownership of household goods (matā‘ al-bayt). The legal procedure differs for each.
The two scenarios are independent; each has its own rules.
2
Apply the evidence hierarchy for mahr
If one spouse produces evidence (bayyinah), their claim is accepted. If both produce evidence, the woman's evidence is preferred. If neither has evidence, they take mutual oaths (tahāluf) and the judge determines mahr al-mithl.
The marriage is not annulled despite the dispute.
3
Determine the mahr amount using mahr al-mithl
When no evidence exists, compare mahr al-mithl with the spouses' claims. If mahr al-mithl equals or is less than the husband's admission, his claim prevails. If it equals or exceeds the wife's claim, her claim prevails. If it falls in between, mahr al-mithl is awarded.
This ensures fairness without penalizing either party.
4
Classify household goods by gender suitability
For household goods, categorize each item: exclusively male (e.g., turban, sword) → husband; exclusively female (e.g., anklet, silk garments) → wife; suitable for both (e.g., bed, mat, vessels) → husband (per Abu Hanifa based on customary purchase).
This presumption can be rebutted by evidence.
5
Account for intra-Hanafi differences and death scenarios
Abu Yusuf adds that the wife receives her customary outfit and the husband gets the rest with his oath. He treats divorce and death equally. Muhammad agrees with Abu Hanifa but adds that if one spouse dies, items suitable for both go to the husband's heirs.
The text does not specify a single mufta bihi, but Abu Hanifa's view is generally followed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A man may look at the entire body of his wife and lawful slave woman without restriction.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi ruling on a man's gaze at his wife and his female slave. While the general rule is permissibility, there are important exceptions based on the woman's legal status. We will examine the conditions that restrict this permission, especially when desire is involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Ruling
The passage states that it is permissible (jā'iz) for a man to look at the entire body of his wife and his lawful slave woman.
This is the base ruling.
2

The phrase 'who is lawful for him' excludes certain categories: a Magian slave woman, a mukatabah, a slave married to another, and one prohibited by fosterage or marriage.

For looking at the legs (and by extension, for any mahram woman), the ruling is conditional upon safety from desire—both the man's and the woman's.
If desire is feared, looking is not permissible.

Any woman who is not lawful for intercourse (e.g., those listed) is governed by the same ruling as a non-mahram woman.

A male slave is legally equivalent to a non-mahram man (ajnabi) for the rules of looking at his female owner.

This short passage clarifies an important aspect of the law of looking (nazar) within the master-slave relationship.
