Hanafi Ruling on Ila' (Oath of Abstinence)
Defines Ila' and explains its rulings with Quranic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Ila' and apply it to scenarios of fixed-term and perpetual oaths, including the consequences of fay' and failure to return.

Core Idea
Ila' obliges the husband to either return to his wife within four months or face a divorce.

This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on Ila'—an oath by a husband to abstain from intercourse. The source passage presents two forms: a fixed four-month oath and a perpetual oath, clarifying the legal consequences of returning (fay') or failing to return, all grounded in Quranic evidence. By mastering this topic, you will understand how the Hanafi school balances marital rights and contractual obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what Ila' is and its legal category. Ila' is an oath to abstain from intercourse for four months or more. It is not inherently haram but carries specific consequences.
2
Distinguish the two forms of Ila'
Separate the fixed-term oath (for exactly four months) from the perpetual oath (using 'forever' or indefinite). Each has different implications for the continuity of the oath.
3
Analyze the consequences of fay' (return)
If the husband has intercourse within four months, he breaks the oath, must pay kaffarah, and the Ila' is nullified, allowing future relations without penalty.
4
Analyze the consequences of failing to return
If the husband does not have intercourse within four months, the wife becomes separated by one ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, and the oath is dropped (for fixed-term) or remains (for perpetual).
For perpetual oath, the oath persists, so if the couple remarries, the Ila' returns and the cycle repeats up to three divorces.
5
Locate the dalil and its application
Examine the Quranic verses (2:226-227) and how they establish the four-month waiting period, the option of fay' (return) with forgiveness, and the alternative of divorce.
The imperative 'tarabbusu' indicates obligation; the two paths are explicitly set.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the Hanafi ruling on Ila' (an oath of abstinence from intercourse). The author first defines the basic scenario: a man swears by Allah not to approach his wife for four months. If he breaks his oath by having intercourse within the four months, he must pay kaffarah (expiation) for breaking the oath, and the Ila' is nullified, meaning he may approach her thereafter without consequence. This act of intercourse is called fay' (return). If he does not have intercourse until the four months expire, his wife becomes separated by one ba'in (irrevocable) divorce. If the man subsequently remarries her after the first divorce, the Ila' returns, and the same sequence repeats up to a maximum of three divorces. After three divorces, if she marries another husband and then returns to him, no further divorce occurs from the original Ila'; the oath remains but only requires expiation if broken. The legal basis is the Quranic verses (2:226-227), which establish the four-month waiting period and the two options: fay' (return) or 'azm al-talaq (resolve on divorce). The passage also clarifies that the perpetual oath does not produce a divorce after a third marriage because the right to divorce has been exhausted by the three divorces. The final note cites 'Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq' emphasizing that the oath remains in effect for expiation purposes even after the marital bond is severed.

الكفارة (al-kaffārah): The expiation required for breaking an oath, which in this context is freeing a slave, feeding or clothing ten poor persons, or fasting three days.

Ila' from a Revocably vs Irrevocably Divorced Woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between revocable and irrevocable divorce in the context of Ila', and state when an oath of abstinence constitutes Ila'.

Core Idea
Ila' applies only if the woman is still a wife—revocable divorce preserves the marriage bond.

This lesson examines a specific condition for the validity of Ila' (swearing abstinence from one's wife). The key factor is whether the divorced woman is still considered a wife during her waiting period. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rulings of Ila'.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of divorced women
Recall that a revocably divorced woman (rajʿiyyah) remains in marriage during her 'iddah, while an irrevocably divorced woman (bā'inah) is completely separated.
This distinction determines marital status.
2
Analyze the condition for Ila'
Ila' requires a valid marital relationship. Since the revocably divorced woman is still a wife during 'iddah, the oath binds her. The irrevocably divorced woman is not a wife, so no Ila'.
The condition is the existence of a marriage bond.
3
Apply to the given ruling
The passage states: 'If he swears abstention from a revocably divorced woman, he is a Muli; if from an irrevocably divorced woman, he is not a Muli.' Confirm the logic.
This is a clear conditional ruling.

Ruling when a husband swears to abstain for less than four months.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An oath of abstinence under four months is not Ila' — no divorce, only a broken oath requiring kaffarah.

This lesson addresses a frequent scenario: a husband swears not to approach his wife for a period less than four months. Many mistakenly believe this automatically triggers Ila' and eventual divorce. However, the Hanafi school strictly requires a minimum four-month period for Ila' to take effect. Without it, the oath is treated as an ordinary solemn promise, with its own consequences.

Classify the legal nature
Determine that this does not fall under the definition of Ila' because the period is below the four-month threshold.
Ila' requires a minimum of four months (Quran 2:226).
3
Apply the ruling for ordinary oaths
If the husband approaches his wife within the stated period, he breaks his oath and must perform kaffarah. If he refrains, no divorce occurs.
Kaffarah is expiation for breaking a solemn oath.
4
Summarize the practical outcome
No separation (talaq) occurs; only potential kaffarah for oath-breaking.
The marriage remains valid.

Ila' Period for a Slave Wife
Question-answer specifying Ila' period for a slave wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Ila' period for a slave wife and explain why it differs from that of a free woman in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
The Ila' period for a slave wife is two months, half that of a free woman.

This lesson addresses a specific case: when a husband swears Ila' (an oath to abstain from marital relations) from his slave wife. The Hanafi school reduces the waiting period to two months, reflecting the principle that a slave's rights in matters of divorce and 'iddah are half of a free woman's. Understanding this ruling helps illustrate broader patterns in Hanafi family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the wife's status
The act is Ila' (an oath to abstain from intercourse). The wife is a slave woman (amah). Recognize that the ruling applies specifically to a slave wife, not a free wife.
The source explicitly says 'amah' (slave woman).
2
Determine the applicable period
Read the answer: the period of Ila' with respect to her is two months. Memorize this as the key rule.
Two months is less than the standard four months for a free woman.
3
Compare with the standard Ila' period for a free woman
Recall that for a free wife, the Ila' period is four months. Here it is two months. Understand the half-principle: slave women have half the rights in divorce and 'iddah matters.
This is consistent with other half-rulings, e.g., 'iddah of a slave is two months instead of four.
4
Identify the consequence of non-approach
If the husband does not approach her within two months, she becomes separated from him by one irrevocable divorce (ba'in). This is the same legal effect as for a free woman, but with a shorter period.
The divorce is ba'in (irrevocable), not raj'i (revocable).

Verbal Fay' When Intercourse is Impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on verbal fay' when physical fay' is impossible due to valid excuses.

Core Idea
When intercourse is impossible, a mere verbal declaration suffices to nullify Ila'.

The husband who regrets Ila' must return to marital relations within four months to avoid divorce. But what if he is physically unable? Hanafi fiqh provides leniency: if the husband or wife is ill, or the wife is a minor or inaccessible, verbal fay' is sufficient. This lesson explains the conditions and validity of such verbal fay'.

Apply the ruling
The husband may say with his tongue: 'F'tu ilayhā' (I have returned to her). This verbal fay' nullifies the Ila'.
No specific wording is required as long as meaning is clear.
4

Once declared, the Ila' is dropped, and no divorce occurs.
The verbal fay' is legally valid and sufficient.

الإعتاق (al-i‘tāq): Manumission: freeing a slave by the master's declaration.
المهر (al-mahr): Dower: the mandatory payment from husband to wife at marriage.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
If a man is coerced to divorce his wife and does so before consummation, what compensation does the coerced receive from the coercer?
A) Full mahr
B) Half the mahr
C) Nothing
D) The value of the slave

Key Takeaway
Coerced divorce and emancipation are valid in Hanafi fiqh, and the coercer must compensate for the slave's value and, if before consummation, half the dower.
