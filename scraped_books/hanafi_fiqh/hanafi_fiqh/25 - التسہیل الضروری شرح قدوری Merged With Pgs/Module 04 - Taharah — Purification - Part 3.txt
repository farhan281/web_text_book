The Sahibayn's Ruling on Interdiction of the Spendthrift
Ruling on interdiction of the spendthrift by the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sahibayn's ruling on the interdiction of a spendthrift and explain the conditions under which his transactions may be ratified or invalidated.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn interdict the prodigal, void his transactions unless the judge ratifies a beneficial one, and never hand over his wealth until prudence appears.

This passage presents the view of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the legal interdiction (ḥajr) of a person who is a spendthrift (safīh). Their ruling is precautionary: the prodigal is prevented from disposing of his property, and any sale he makes is not binding unless a judge finds it beneficial and ratifies it. Moreover, even after reaching puberty, if he lacks prudence, his wealth is not given to him indefinitely. This lesson unpacks the key elements of this ruling and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
Determine who is being discussed: a safīh (spendthrift) – a person who wastes money and lacks financial prudence.
The term safīh is defined by the jurists as one who spends wealth in ways contrary to what is customary among people of sound judgment.
2
State the primary ruling
The Sahibayn say: 'يحجر على السفيه ويُمنع من التصرف في ماله' – He is interdicted and prevented from disposing of his money. This means a legal restriction is placed on him.
Interdiction (ḥajr) does not remove ownership but restricts the power to transact.
3
Examine the exceptions for transactions
If the safīh sells something, the sale is not executed automatically. However, if the judge finds the transaction beneficial, he may ratify it, making it valid.
The judge's ratification is a discretionary act based on maslahah (public interest).
4
Note the condition for handing over wealth
Even after reaching puberty (bulūgh), if the person is not prudent (ghayr rashīd), his wealth is not handed over to him according to the Sahibayn indefinitely – until rushd (prudence) is observed. All his financial disposals are invalid.
Rushd requires sound judgment in financial matters; it is a separate condition from physical maturity.
5
Identify obligations from his wealth
Despite interdiction, certain obligations are fulfilled from his wealth: zakāt is taken, and maintenance (nafaqah) is provided for his children, wife, and relatives whose maintenance is obligatory upon him.
These obligations take priority over the restriction because they are debts owed to Allah or to dependents.

Core Idea
Deliberate mixing or spending from a deposit creates liability; accidental mixing creates partnership.

This Q&A passage addresses common scenarios that arise when a depositary (person holding a trust) either mixes the deposit with his own property or spends from it. Understanding these rulings is essential for safeguarding trusts and knowing when liability attaches.

Any deliberate act of mixing or spending that uses or confuses the deposit makes the depositary liable. Accidental mixing creates partnership.
The core distinction is between deliberate transgression (ta'addī) and accidental occurrence.

Question about liability after removing transgression

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether removing transgression after a deposit misuse restores the trust status and lifts liability.

Core Idea
Full removal of transgression restores the deposit to a trust — liability does not persist.

This passage poses a critical question in the law of deposits (wadi'ah): if a depositary misuses the deposit but then undoes the transgression, does the special liability (daman) continue? The answer is not given in the text, but Hanafi fiqh provides a clear ruling based on the nature of trust and the effect of restoration. Understanding this ruling is essential for distinguishing between temporary and permanent liability in bailments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of transgression
The passage lists examples: riding an animal, wearing a garment, employing a slave, redepositing with another. Recognize these as misuse that transforms the trust (amanah) into a guarantee (daman).
2
Recognise the removal of transgression
The phrase 'azala al-ta'addi' means the depositary ceased the wrongful use and returned the deposit to his custody. Understand that this is a complete cessation of the wrongful act.
3
Understand the legal question
The question is: after the transgression is removed, does the ruling of liability (hukm al-daman) remain as it was? This is the core issue: does the liability attach permanently once triggered?
4
State the Hanafi position
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is that once the transgression is fully removed and the deposit is restored to its original condition and place, the liability is lifted, and the deposit reverts to being an amanah (trust). The depositary is no longer a guarantor.
Some scholars (e.g., certain Shafi'i views) hold that liability remains. This difference is explained in the madhhab dispute map.
5
Note the practical effect
If the deposit is lost after the removal, the depositary is not liable unless a new transgression occurs. This distinguishes Hanafi law from stricter views.

Mut'ah: Composition and When It Is Obligatory or Recommended
States the composition and obligation of mut'ah for divorced women.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the composition of mut'ah and identify the exact conditions under which it becomes obligatory versus recommended in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Mut'ah is wajib only for a woman divorced before consummation with no dower specified.

In this passage, the commentator answers two key questions about mut'ah (the consolatory gift for divorced women): first, what does it consist of, and second, for whom is it obligatory or recommended? Understanding this ruling helps clarify the Hanafi position on post-divorce financial obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composition of mut'ah
The passage states mut'ah consists of three garments: a shift (dir'), a head covering (khimar), and a wrap (milhafah). Note that it must be 'similar to her clothing' meaning appropriate to her status.
2
Determine the ruling and its conditions
Mut'ah is wajib (obligatory) only for a woman divorced before consummation with no dower specified. It is mustahabb (recommended) for all other divorced women, except the one divorced before consummation with a specified dower—for her, it is not prescribed.
The exception is important: if dower was specified before consummation, mut'ah is not given; she takes half the dower instead.
3
Apply the ruling to the four possible cases
Consider the four combinations: (1) divorced before consummation, no dower → wajib; (2) divorced before consummation, dower specified → no mut'ah; (3) divorced after consummation, any dower → mustahabb; (4) divorced after consummation, no dower → mustahabb. The passage only explicitly states (1) and (2); the recommendation for others is a general rule.
This step helps you see the logic behind the Hanafi distinction.

Rulings on Cooked Grape Juice and Vinegar
Q&A on grape juice and vinegar rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on grape juice cooked until two-thirds evaporate and explain the permissibility of vinegar derived from wine.

Core Idea
Cooking grape juice removes its prohibition; wine turned to vinegar becomes halal.

We begin with several Q&A rulings on intoxicating beverages. The first addresses grape juice that has been cooked, and the next two address the transformation of wine into vinegar. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi principle that substantial change of substance (istihala) can render a previously prohibited item permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the substance and its transformation
For each Q&A, note the starting substance (grape juice or wine) and the process applied (cooking or acetification).

2
State the ruling
For grape juice cooked until two-thirds evaporate: halal even if it becomes strong. For wine that turns into vinegar: halal, regardless of how it became vinegar.
The ruling hinges on the removal of intoxicating potential or essence.
3
Identify the underlying legal basis
The rulings rely on istihala (transformation) and the principle that the prohibition of wine is tied to its intoxicating quality; once lost, the substance is permissible.
This is consistent with the Hanafi position that the ruling follows the 'illa (effective cause).

Under overwhelming coercion (ikrah mulji') threatening life or limb, consuming ordinarily forbidden items becomes obligatory; refusal results in sin.
