Homicide categories in Hanafi fiqh: intentional (qisas), semi-intentional (diyah+kaffarah), mistaken (diyah+kaffarah), and by cause (diyah only).

Commentary
This passage from al-Hidayah defines homicide categories in Hanafi fiqh. It begins with the linguistic and technical definition of 'jinaayah' (criminal offense), which covers both murder and bodily harm. The main focus is the fivefold division of killing: (1) 'amd (intentional) – using a weapon or lethal instrument; its punishment is retaliation (qisaas) unless forgiven, and the killer is deprived of inheritance; no expiation. The passage emphasizes that in mistake killing, though there is no sin of murder, the act still carries a degree of sin due to lack of caution; expiation is a sign of this. Inheritance is only barred in cases involving sin. The rulings are drawn from the Hanafi tradition, particularly al-Hidayah.

Definition of Hadd
Defines the term 'hadd' linguistically and legally

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic meaning of hadd, define its legal meaning, and identify its key characteristics that distinguish it from other punishments.

Core Idea
Hadd is a fixed punishment that prevents crime, prescribed by Allah and non-pardonable.

The term 'hadd' (plural hudud) is a cornerstone of Islamic penal law. Understanding its precise definition is essential to distinguish it from other penalties like ta'zir and qisas. This lesson unpacks the linguistic and legal meanings from a classical Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic meaning
Recognize that hadd linguistically means 'prevention' (al-man'). This helps explain why these punishments are called hudud: they deter people from committing certain sins.
2
Define the legal meaning
Legally, hadd is a prescribed punishment (uqubah muqaddarah) that fulfills a right of Allah (haqq Allah). The phrase 'prescribed' means the amount is fixed by Shari'ah, not left to discretion.
This distinguishes hadd from ta'zir (discretionary punishment) and qisas (retribution).
3
Identify the implications of 'right of Allah'
When a punishment is a right of Allah, it cannot be waived or pardoned by individuals after the crime is proven. The state must enforce it if the conditions are met.
Contrast with qisas, which can be pardoned by the victim's family.

Proving Zina through Four Witnesses (Bayyinah)
Explains the first method of proving zina: witness testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for establishing zina through witnesses and explain the consequence of insufficient witnesses.

Core Idea
Zina is proven by four male eyewitnesses; fewer or unreliable witnesses leads to the accusers being punished for qadhf.

In Islamic criminal law, the hadd punishment for zina requires an extraordinarily high standard of proof. This lesson examines the first method: presentation of four male witnesses who directly observed the act. The judge must verify their integrity ('adala) and the explicit details of the act. Failure to meet this standard results in punishment for the accusers (qadhf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the first method of proof
The text states that zina is established by bayyinah (evidence). Bayyinah here specifically means four male witnesses testifying against a man or woman.
Bayyinah in zina is restricted to this definition.
2
List the conditions for the witnesses
The witnesses must be four in number, male (by implication), and must describe the act in explicit detail: 'like a probe in a kohl container.' They must also be 'adil (upright) in private and public.
The graphic description ensures certainty of the act.
3
Understand the judge's procedure
The judge (Imam) questions the witnesses about what, how, where, when, and with whom the zina occurred. He also investigates their integrity.
This thorough questioning aims to eliminate any doubt.
4
Recognize the consequence of insufficient witnesses
If the number of witnesses falls short of four, or if any are found to be fasiq (transgressors), then the accusers themselves are subject to the hadd of qadhf (false accusation, 80 lashes).
This strict rule protects innocent people from false accusations.

His view is not leniency; he still allows severe punishment, including imprisonment until death or execution for habitual offenders, but as siyasa, not hadd.

The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a first-time offender gets tazir (imprisonment or flogging) and may be killed only if habitual (siyasa). Under Sahibayn, the first offense triggers the fixed hadd (jald/rajm).
This affects the judge's discretion and the finality of punishment.

Historical Precedents and Rationale for the Mufta Bihi on Liwat
Historical precedents and rationale for the mufta bihi on liwat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rationale behind the mufta bihi position for liwat and cite the key historical precedents from the Companions that support it.

Core Idea
The mufta bihi view imposes discretionary killing for habitual sodomy, backed by Companion actions and the principle that fixed hadd is avoided where textual evidence is lacking.

This lesson dives into the later Hanafi tarjih (preferred view) on the punishment for liwat, as derived from the passage. We will see why the absence of a fixed hadd does not imply leniency, and how the ruler's discretionary power (siyasa) is used to impose severe deterrent punishments, especially for repeat offenders.

Guided Path

Identify the mufta bihi view
Later scholars (Ibn Abidin, al-Ashbah, al-Bayri) rule that the sodomite is not given hadd unless he repeats, then he is killed as a siyasa punishment. This is the operative position for fatwa.
This is a compromise between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn, incorporating severe punishment without calling it hadd.

Examine the Companion precedents
The passage reports three precedents: (1) Khalid ibn al-Walid found a man practicing sodomy; Abu Bakr consulted the Companions; Ali recommended burning, and they agreed. This distinction is crucial for understanding why the punishment is not called 'hadd'.

Hadd al-Qadhf: Punishment, Conditions, and Proof
Q&A rulings on hadd al-qadhf with proof and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadd punishment for qadhf, list the five conditions of ihsan for the victim, and explain the Quranic proof for the 80 lashes.

Core Idea
Lashing 80 times is the fixed penalty for falsely accusing a chaste person of zina.

This lesson presents the core rulings on hadd al-qadhf (false accusation of zina) from the Hanafi school. The punishment is established by the Quranic command in Surah An-Nur:4, but its application depends on the victim meeting the conditions of ihsan (chastity). Through a series of Q&A, the text clarifies the punishment, the definition of ihsan, and key exceptions. We will work through the proof, conditions, and selected applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling and its proof
The text opens with the hadd for a free accuser: 80 lashes. The proof is the Quranic verse (An-Nur:4) with the imperative 'fajlidoohum'. According to Hanafi usul, the imperative indicates obligation (wujub). Note the method of lashing: distributed over limbs, heavy garments removed.
The verse also includes perpetual rejection of testimony, but that is beyond this lesson.
2
Define the conditions of ihsan for the victim
The text lists five qualities: free, adult, sane, Muslim, and chaste from zina. If the victim lacks any of these, the accuser is not subject to hadd, but may receive ta'zir (discretionary punishment). This ihsan is specific to qadhf, distinct from ihsan for zina (which requires marriage).
Memorize these five: hurr, baligh, 'aqil, muslim, 'afif.
3
Apply the ruling to a slave accuser
If the accuser is a slave, the hadd is halved to 40 lashes. This follows the general principle that punishments for slaves are half of those for free persons, analogous to the half punishment for slave women in zina (Surah An-Nur:2).
No explicit proof given here; it is derived by qiyas.
4
Distinguish between hadd and ta'zir in various accusations
The text examines several statements: 'you are not your father's son' is qadhf; 'O Nabati' or 'O son of rainwater' is not; attributing someone to a relative other than father is not. Accusation against a deceased person: hadd applies only if lineage is impugned (father or son may demand). Accusation against a fornicator, slave, or disbeliever does not incur hadd but ta'zir. Insults like 'fasiq' or 'donkey' may incur ta'zir depending on custom and status.
Ta'zir is discretionary, unlike fixed hadd.
5
Handle special cases and edge rulings
The text covers: retraction of confession is not accepted; li'an scenarios where subsequent accusation may or may not incur hadd depending on the reason for li'an; a slave cannot demand hadd against his master for accusing his mother; death from properly administered hadd or ta'zir incurs no blood money. These illustrate the boundaries and procedural aspects of the hadd.
The mufta bihi is as stated; no intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf noted.

Qadhf After Li'an and the Effect on Hadd
Ruling on qadhf after li'an

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qadhf of a woman after li'an and distinguish between different li'an scenarios.

Core Idea
A woman who has undergone li'an for denial of paternity loses her muhsan status for future qadhf, but not if li'an was only for zina accusation.

Li'an is a special procedure when a husband accuses his wife of zina without witnesses. The Hanafi school treats the resulting separation and negation of paternity as affecting the wife's status. In this lesson, we examine a specific question: if a third party later accuses that same woman of zina, does he receive hadd? The answer depends on the reason for the original li'an.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of li'an
Li'an can be due to denial of paternity (the husband denies the child is his) or due to accusation of zina without a child.
In the first case, the child's lineage is negated; in the second, only the accusation is resolved.
2
Identify the effect on the wife's muhsan status
After li'an for paternity denial, the wife is considered to have committed zina (since the child is not from the husband), so she loses her chastity (ihsan). She is no longer muhsan.
If li'an was only for zina accusation without a child, her chastity remains intact unless proven.
3
Apply the rule for third-party qadhf
If she loses muhsan status, a third-party accuser does not receive hadd (only ta'zir). If she remains muhsan, the accuser is subject to hadd.
The hadd is only for accusing a muhsan.

The Hadd for Theft: Amputation and Its Conditions
Primary rulings on theft and amputation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for hand amputation for theft in the Hanafi school and identify which factors waive the punishment.

Core Idea
Amputation for theft requires nisab, hirz, and victim demand; missing limbs waive the hadd.

This lesson covers the primary rulings on the hadd punishment for theft (sariqa) according to the Hanafi school. We will learn the amputations for first, second, and third offences, the conditions that must be met, and the circumstances that prevent the punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three stages of punishment
The first theft leads to amputation of the right hand, the second to amputation of the left foot, and the third to permanent imprisonment.
No amputation beyond the second offence.
2
Determine the conditions for amputation
Amputation requires a minimum value of ten dirhams (nisab) and theft from a protected place (hirz).
Hirz can be by location or by the owner's presence.
3
Recognize the effect of missing limbs
If the left hand or right foot is missing or paralyzed, the hadd is not applied because the prescribed limb cannot be amputated.
This is based on the principle of exacting the prescribed punishment.
4
Understand the requirement of victim's demand and its waiver
The victim must be present and demand the punishment. If the victim gifts or sells the stolen item to the thief, or if its value drops below nisab, amputation is waived.
This shows the victim's right to waive the hadd.

The verse continues (5:34) with an exception: those who repent before being captured are not subject to these punishments. Repentance waives the hadd.
This exception applies only if repentance occurs before the authorities gain power over the robber.
5
Analyze the legal weight
Since this text is from the Quran with qat'i thubut and dalala, its rulings are binding and cannot be overridden by istihsan or other secondary sources.
The Hanafi school treats these punishments as fixed obligations (fard ayn) on the state to implement when conditions are met.

Highway Robbery: Imprisonment and Cross-Amputation
First two scenarios of highway robbery punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for highway robbers who are caught before committing violence or who take property, and identify the conditions for imprisonment and cross-amputation.

Core Idea
Highway robbery has fixed punishments with precise conditions: imprisonment until repentance or cross-amputation for theft above nisab.

This lesson covers the first two scenarios of highway robbery from the Hanafi fiqh text. Understanding these rulings requires knowing the conditions that trigger each punishment—whether the robbers are caught before any harm or after taking property up to the nisab threshold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial condition
Note that the law applies to a group of resistant people or a single person capable of resistance who set out to cut the road (highway robbery).
The capability to resist distinguishes highway robbery from ordinary theft.
2
Scenario without property or killing
If they are caught before taking property or killing anyone, the imam imprisons them until they repent. This is a disciplinary measure, not a fixed hadd.
Repentance is a condition for release; the state monitors their reformation.
3
Scenario with property taken
If they take property from a Muslim or dhimmi, and when divided equally each robber's share reaches ten dirhams or its value, the imam amputates their hands and feet.
Ten dirhams is the nisab for this hadd, similar to the nisab for theft.
4
Method of amputation
The amputation is 'from opposite sides' (min khilaf): meaning right hand and left foot are cut, or left hand and right foot, to maximize disfigurement and deterrence.
This is unique to highway robbery; for simple theft only the right hand is cut.
5
Note the fixed nature
These are hadd punishments—they cannot be reduced or forgiven by the imam or victims. The imam must apply them when conditions are met.
The only waiving here is the first scenario where repentance ends imprisonment.

So if a group of resistant people or a single person capable of resistance go out to cut the road, and they are caught before they take any property and before they kill any person, the imam imprisons them until they repent. If they take property of a Muslim or a dhimmi – and the property, when divided among the group, comes to ten dirhams or more per person, or what reaches that value – the imam cuts their hands and feet from opposite sides. If they kill a person and do not take property, the imam kills them as a hadd, and if the heirs forgive them, their forgiveness is not considered. Commentary
This passage details the three scenarios of highway robbery and their corresponding fixed punishments (hadd). Scenario 1: If the robbers are caught before taking any property or killing anyone, they are imprisoned until they show repentance. Scenario 2: If they take property (each robber's share reaches the nisab of ten dirhams), their hands and feet are amputated crosswise. Scenario 3: If they kill but take no property, they are executed as a hadd, and the victim's family's forgiveness does not waive the punishment because it is a hadd of Allah. Scenario 4: If they both kill and take property, the imam has a choice among three punishments: cross-amputation followed by execution or crucifixion, or execution alone, or crucifixion alone. These details are derived from the Quranic verse and Hanafi jurisprudence.

Key Terms
من خلاف (min khilaf): Cross-wise; meaning the right hand and left foot are amputated (or left hand and right foot).
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment ordained by Allah, which cannot be altered or forgiven by human authority.
