Women's Iddah: Definition and Duration
Definition of iddah and its basic periods for a free woman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of iddah and identify the correct iddah period for a free woman who menstruates and one who does not.

Core Idea
A divorced or widowed woman must wait a prescribed period before remarriage — this is iddah.

The iddah is a foundational concept in Islamic family law, ensuring clarity of lineage and respect for the marriage bond. In this lesson, we examine a concise text from the classical Hanafi tradition that defines iddah and spells out the two basic durations for a free woman. The source uses a question-answer format to make these rules easy to memorize.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term ‘iddah’
The Arabic root is ‘add, meaning to count. The woman counts her menstrual cycles or months. The term ‘iddah is derived from this counting.
2
Identify when iddah is required
Iddah becomes obligatory upon divorce (any type: revocable, irrevocable, severe), separation (e.g., faskh, khul‘), or the death of the husband.
3
Memorize the basic durations for a free woman
For a menstruating free woman: three complete menstrual cycles (ثلاث حيض كوامل). For a non-menstruating free woman (due to youth or old age): three months.
In the Hanafi school, ‘qur’’ means menstruation, so three cycles means three menstrual periods plus the intervening intervals of purity.

Ruling on Divorce Count After Tahlil Marriage
Provides a ruling on the number of divorces remaining after remarriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the number of divorces a woman returns with after a valid tahlil marriage and identify the conditions for the annulment of previous divorces.

Core Idea
A valid intervening marriage annuls all previous divorces; the first husband regains full three divorces.

This lesson clarifies a key ruling in Hanafi divorce law. When a man divorces his wife three times, she becomes unlawful for him until she marries another man, consummates the marriage, and then becomes separated. The question arises: if she remarries the first husband, how many divorces does he have left? The Hanafi school, based on consensus (ijma) of the three founding imams, rules that the second marriage completely annuls the earlier three divorces, so the first husband starts anew with three divorces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand the case: a triple divorce followed by a valid intervening marriage (with consummation) and then return to the first husband.
2
Understand the term 'hadam'
Recognize that 'hadam' (annulment) means the previous divorces are completely erased, as if they never occurred.
This is the core principle of the ruling.
3
Note the consensus (ijma)
The ruling is based on the consensus among Imam Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
Ijma gives the ruling strong authority.
4
Apply the ruling
Thus, after a valid tahlil marriage, the first husband has three divorces at his disposal again.
He can divorce her up to three times before she becomes permanently unlawful.

Iddah Durations for Free and Slave Women
Lists the specific durations of 'iddah based on the woman's status and circumstances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct iddah duration for any given woman (free or slave) based on her circumstances: menstruating, non-menstruating, pregnant, divorced, or widowed.

Core Idea
Iddah varies: free women have longer periods; slave women get half except for pregnancy.

The iddah (waiting period) after divorce or death is a legal obligation with specific durations based on the woman's status and condition. This lesson presents a concise list that you must memorize for accurate application. Understanding these distinctions is essential to avoid violating sacred marital laws.

Guided Path
1
Identify the woman's status
First, determine if the woman is free (حرة) or a slave (أمة). This is the primary factor that splits the rulings.
Slavery is not practiced today, but the principle remains for historical understanding.
2
Check menstruation and pregnancy
Ask: does she menstruate? Is she pregnant? These two conditions override other general rules, especially pregnancy, which sets the iddah until delivery regardless of status.
If she is pregnant, the iddah ends with childbirth for both free and slave women.
3
Apply the free woman's rules
For free women: if menstruating → three complete periods; if not menstruating (due to youth or menopause) → three months; if widowed and not pregnant → four months and ten days; if pregnant → until delivery.
Memorize these four categories.
4
Apply the slave woman's rules
For slave women: all durations are halved compared to free women, except for pregnancy (still until delivery). Thus: menstruating → two periods; non-menstruating → one and a half months; widowed → two months and five days; pregnant → until delivery.
The halving principle applies to the duration, not to the nature of the waiting period.
5
Summarize the differences
Create a mental table comparing free vs. slave for each scenario. This solidifies memorization and highlights the consistent half rule.
Write it down to reinforce learning.

Counting 'Iddah Periods After Divorce During Menstruation
Clarifies that the menstrual period in which divorce occurs is not counted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a menstrual period during which divorce occurs counts toward 'iddah, and identify the number of complete periods required.

Core Idea
A period already in progress at divorce does not count; three complete periods after it are required.

This lesson addresses a common practical question: if a husband divorces his wife while she is menstruating, does that menstrual period count as one of the three required for 'iddah? The answer from the Hanafi school is clear: it does not. The 'iddah begins after the divorce, so the ongoing period is not part of it. Instead, the wife must complete that period and then observe three full menstrual cycles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the ruling applies when a husband pronounces divorce while his wife is experiencing menstruation.
This is a specific scenario; different rules apply if divorce occurs during a period of purity (tuhr).
2
State the Core Ruling
Memorize that the menstrual period in which the divorce takes place is not counted toward the three periods of 'iddah.
The Arabic phrase 'la tuhtasab' (لا تحتسب) means 'it is not counted.'
3
Determine the Required Number
The wife must observe three complete periods after that initial period. The word 'kawāmil' (كوامل) emphasizes that each period must be a full cycle.
A complete period includes the bleeding and the subsequent clean interval (tuhr) until the next bleeding.
4
Understand the Legal Rationale
The 'iddah begins at the moment of divorce, so a period that started before that moment is not part of the waiting period. This ensures the wife fulfills the full waiting period.
This is a protective measure to avoid an incomplete 'iddah.

The Longer Iddah When Divorced in Fatal Illness
Presents the Hanafi ruling and disagreement on which iddah applies when a woman is divorced during fatal illness and her husband dies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the iddah of a woman divorced during her husband's fatal illness, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifah/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A woman divorced during her husband's fatal illness observes the longer of the two iddahs to preserve her inheritance right.

This lesson addresses a nuanced scenario encountered in both family law and inheritance: a woman is divorced by her husband during his fatal illness, and then he dies while she is still in her iddah. Which iddah should she complete? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on this matter, with the majority position of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad al-Shaybani requiring the longer period, while Abu Yusuf holds a different view for irrevocable divorces. Understanding this ruling is crucial for correctly determining both the waiting period and inheritance rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognise the key facts: the husband divorces his wife while terminally ill, then dies before her iddah from divorce ends. This triggers both a divorce iddah and a death iddah.
The husband's fatal illness is a necessary condition for the inheritance rule.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad
According to these two imams, the woman must observe the longer of the two iddahs (ab'ad al-ajalayn). Compare the duration of the death iddah (4 months 10 days for a free woman) and the divorce iddah (3 periods or 3 months) and take the longer.
The death iddah is usually longer, but if the woman is pregnant, the divorce iddah (until delivery) could be longer.
3
State the position of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf holds that if the divorce is irrevocable (bā'in) or triple, the iddah is only three menstrual periods (the divorce iddah). However, if the divorce is revocable (raj'ī), he agrees that the death iddah applies by consensus.
This is a minority opinion within the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the rationale
The benefit (fā'idah) of the longer period is that the woman retains the right to inherit from her husband as long as she is in iddah. Extending the iddah preserves her financial rights.
The purpose of iddah here is not only to avoid pregnancy confusion but also to allow mourning and inheritance.
5
Apply the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the majority view, the woman may have a longer waiting period but can inherit from her husband's estate. Under Abu Yusuf's view, she may have a shorter waiting period but loses inheritance if the death iddah is not completed.
The mufta bihi (relied upon) position is that of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, as reflected in al-Hidayah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the time limits for establishing lineage of a child born to a widow or woman in 'iddah, and identify and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the sahibayn regarding the number of witnesses required for birth testimony.

Core Idea
A child born to a widow within two years links to the deceased; a child born to a woman in 'iddah within six months of her claim links to the former husband; but proving the birth itself splits the Hanafi school.

This lesson delves into a nuanced area of Hanafi family law: how lineage (nasab) is established for children born to widows and women in 'iddah. While the time limits are clear—two years from the husband's death for a widow, six months from the claim of completed 'iddah—the evidentiary requirement for proving the birth itself is a matter of internal Hanafi disagreement. Abu Hanifa insists on standard testimony (two men or a man and two women), while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, accept the testimony of a single woman. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how the Hanafi school balances evidentiary caution with practical facilitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Time-Limit Rulings
Read the source passage and identify the two explicit time limits: (1) for a widow, lineage is established up to two complete years from death; (2) for a woman in 'iddah who acknowledges completion, lineage is established if she gives birth before six months, but not if at six months or later.
The six-month minimum pregnancy duration is key to understanding the logic.
2
Recognize the Evidentiary Question
The passage then poses a question: beyond time, what condition is needed? Abu Hanifa requires testimony of two men or a man and two women to the birth, unless the pregnancy is obvious or the husband admits. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say one woman suffices in all cases.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute on a procedural matter.
3
Apply the Ruling to a Hypothetical Scenario
Consider a widow who gives birth 18 months after her husband's death. The child's lineage is established. But if the widow lives alone and no witnesses saw the birth, Abu Hanifa would not accept the child's lineage based on the widow's claim alone unless she produces two men or one man and two women. The sahibayn would accept her own testimony or any one woman's.
This highlights the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).
4
Reflect on the Evidentiary Principle Behind Each Position
Abu Hanifa analogizes birth testimony to other legal testimonies requiring two witnesses. The sahibayn consider childbirth a matter primarily known to women and therefore relax the requirement to a single woman, following the practice of the Companions.
This reflects the Hanafi usul principle of istihsan (juristic preference) applied by the sahibayn.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi (Operative Position)
Later Hanafi authorities prefer the sahibayn's view because it is more facilitative and aligns with ease in family matters. Hence, in fatwa, the testimony of one woman is sufficient.
Always be aware which position is relied upon in practice.

Li'an: Definition, Procedure, and Conditions
Defines li'an, procedure, consequences, and conditions with a tasdiq clarification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of li'an, describe its procedure, list its conditions, and explain why the wife's confirmation (tasdiq) does not incur hadd for zina.

Core Idea
Li'an replaces hadd penalties with mutual oaths, but the wife's confirmation only averts oaths, not a confession.

This passage from a Hanafi manual defines li'an—a unique procedure where spouses exchange oaths and curses to resolve accusations of zina or paternity denial. It details the step-by-step oath-swearing, the consequences of refusal, and a critical distinction: the wife's confirmation (tasdiq) of the accusation does not amount to a confession to zina, so no hadd penalty follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Li'an is wajib (obligatory) when a husband accuses his wife of zina or denies paternity and the wife demands its consequence. Note the hukm level.
Wajib means required, not fard; refusal leads to imprisonment and hadd for qadhf.
2
Describe the procedure
The husband swears four times that he is truthful, then a fifth oath invoking Allah's curse if he lies. The wife swears four times that he is lying, then a fifth invoking Allah's wrath if he is truthful. Both point to each other during oaths.
This is the formal substitution for hadd punishment.
3
Understand consequences of refusal
If the husband refuses li'an, the judge imprisons him until he performs or retracts (then gets hadd for qadhf). If the wife refuses, she is imprisoned until she performs li'an or confirms her husband.
Imprisonment is a coercive tool, not punishment itself.
4
Analyze the tasdiq issue
The wife's confirmation (tasdiq) averts li'an, but it is not an intentional confession (iqrar). Therefore, it does not incur hadd for zina. This corrects a variant in some copies of al-Quduri.
The text explicitly rejects the idea that tasdiq equals iqrar.
5
List conditions for li'an
Both spouses must be ahl al-shahada (free, Muslim, just, not previously punished for qadhf). The wife must be muhsan (chaste, free, Muslim, married validly). Her demand is a condition.
If the husband cannot perform li'an due to disqualification, he receives ta'zir instead of hadd.

Commentary
This passage forms a comprehensive treatment of li'an (mutual imprecation) from the Hanafi perspective. It opens by defining li'an as the procedure required when a husband accuses his wife of zina or denies paternity of her child, provided both are legally competent to testify (ahl al-shahada) and the wife is someone whose false accuser would face the hadd penalty of qadhf (i.e., she is chaste/muhsan). The wife then swears four times that he is lying, adding a fifth oath invoking Allah's wrath if he is truthful. This elaborate oath-swapping replaces the hadd penalty for both parties. The second question addresses the scenario where the husband is willing but the wife refuses: she is imprisoned until she either performs li'an or confirms (tasdiq) the husband's accusation. The marginal note from al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies that such confirmation does not constitute a confession (iqrar) to zina, so it does not incur the hadd penalty for zina; it merely averts the li'an. The note also corrects a variant in some copies of al-Quduri that suggested the wife would be punished for zina upon confirmation—this is rejected because hadd cannot be established by a mere confirmation, which lacks the intent of confession. The passage then details conditions: both must be ahl al-shahada (free, Muslim, not previously punished for qadhf); the wife must be muhsan (chaste, free, Muslim, married in a valid marriage). The husband's denial of paternity is treated as an implicit accusation (qadhf) because the default is that a child born in a valid marriage is attributed to the husband. The demand of the wife is a necessary condition for li'an, as it is her right. Finally, if the husband cannot perform li'an due to disqualifications (e.g., being a slave, non-Muslim, or previously punished for qadhf), he is subject to ta'zir (discretionary punishment) rather than hadd, because he has harmed her reputation.

Core Idea
If the wife refuses li'an, she is imprisoned until she performs it or confirms her husband's accusation.

Conditions for Li'an and Effect of Tasdiq
Second Q&A: Conditions of eligibility and effect of wife's confirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two key conditions for li'an (ahl al-shahada and wife's ihsan) and explain why the wife's confirmation (tasdiq) does not impose hadd.

Core Idea
Li'an requires both spouses to be eligible witnesses and the wife to be muhsana; tasdiq repels li'an but is not an admission of guilt.

The previous lesson established that a wife who refuses li'an may be imprisoned. But li'an does not apply to all couples. In this lesson, we examine two crucial conditions: both spouses must be ahl al-shahada (eligible to give testimony), and the wife must be one whose slanderer would be punished (i.e., muhsana). We also correct a common misunderstanding: if the wife confirms her husband's accusation, this does not make her liable for hadd of zina, because confirmation is not an intentional admission.

1
State the conditions for li'an

If either condition is missing, the husband receives hadd of qadhf, and no li'an occurs.
2
Analyze the effect of missing conditions on the husband
If the husband is not ahl al-shahada (e.g., slave, kafir, or previously punished for qadhf), he receives the hadd of qadhf for slandering his wife, and no li'an.
The wife's condition matters: if she fails the condition, the husband has no hadd and no li'an.

Understand the effect of tasdiq (confirmation)

The Quranic basis is Surah Al-Baqarah 2:226-227, which establishes the four-month waiting period and the choice between return (fay') or divorce.

Key Terms
الإيلاء (al-īlā'): A husband's oath to abstain from sexual relations with his wife for a period of four months or more.
المول (al-mūlī): The husband who takes an oath of ila.
الفيء (al-fay'): Return to marital relations, meaning the husband has intercourse within the four months, which nullifies the ila and requires expiation.
كفارة الحنث (kaffārat al-ḥinth): Expiation for breaking an oath: feeding or clothing ten poor persons, or freeing a slave.

Checkpoint
In the case of an indefinite ila ('I will never approach you'), after the first four months without intercourse, what is the status of the oath?
A) The oath drops completely.
B) The oath remains in effect for future marriages.
C) The oath remains and continues to cause talaq every four months indefinitely.
D) The oath remains but only requires expiation if broken.

Key Takeaway
Ila is a husband's oath to abstain from intercourse; if he returns within four months, expiation is due and ila ends; if not, one talaq occurs; indefinite oaths remain active after divorce.
