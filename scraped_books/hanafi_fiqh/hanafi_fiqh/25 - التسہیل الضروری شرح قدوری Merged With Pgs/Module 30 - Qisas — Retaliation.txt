Learning Objective

Core Idea
Killing is not one crime — five distinct categories determine the legal consequence.

This lesson opens the Book of Crimes. Before studying specific rulings, we first need precise terminology. The passage defines jinaaya and then presents a fivefold classification of murder, each with its own punishment regime. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly distinguishing between qisas, diyat, and kaffarah.

Settlement and Pardoning in Qisas for Homicide
Explanation of sulh and 'afw in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on sulh and 'afw in qisas for homicide and apply them to scenarios involving multiple heirs.

Core Idea
A settlement or pardon by any heir drops the qisas but preserves the financial right of other heirs.

In cases of intentional homicide, the heirs of the victim have the right to demand qisas or to accept a settlement. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules on how these choices affect the killer's liability and the rights of multiple heirs. This lesson examines the rulings on sulh and 'afw as stated in the classical fiqh text, supported by a Quranic proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (sulh between killer and heirs) and state the legal effect: qisas drops and the agreed compensation becomes wajib.
The compensation is wajib regardless of amount (little or much).
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites Quran 2:178 as proof. Identify the specific phrase used: 'faman `ufiya lahu min akhihi shay'.
The dalil is naqli (Quran), qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the verse indicates that after a pardon, financial compensation becomes obligatory. The phrase 'min akhihi shay' implies that something (the qisas) is remitted, and the commands 'ittiba' bil-ma'ruf' and 'ada' bi-ihsan' refer to the financial follow-up.
This establishes the validity of replacing qisas with diyah.
4
Analyze the effect of multiple heirs
When one heir pardons or settles for his share, qisas drops for all because qisas is indivisible. The remaining heirs retain their proportional share of diyah.
They may demand their share, settle individually, or pardon.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a case with three heirs: if one pardons, qisas falls, and the two others share the diyah proportionally. The killer must pay what he committed with kindness.
The text emphasizes mutual consideration: killer should not cause hardship, heirs should not be exorbitant.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on jinayat explains two related rulings on settlement and pardoning in qisas for homicide. First, if the killer and all the heirs agree on a financial settlement (sulh) in exchange for dropping qisas, the ruling is that qisas drops and the agreed sum becomes obligatory (wajib) on the killer, regardless of whether the amount is large or small. Second, if there are multiple heirs (shuraka') and only one of them pardons or settles for his share, the effect is that qisas drops for all because retaliation is indivisible. However, the remaining heirs retain their proportional share of the diyah (blood-money). They may either demand their share from the killer or individually settle or pardon. The killer is obligated to pay what he has committed to with fairness and ease, and the heirs should not unreasonably burden him. The passage then cites the Quranic verse (2:178) which commands that after a pardon, the follow-up should be with fairness and payment with kindness. This verse is used as a proof (dalil) that financial compensation is a valid alternative to retribution in cases of murder, encouraging peaceful resolution.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind, specifically execution for murder or amputation for injury.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood-money, financial compensation paid to heirs of the victim.
العفو (al-`afw): Pardoning, dropping the right of qisas without compensation.
الصلح (al-sulh): Settlement, agreement to drop qisas in exchange for financial compensation.

Qisas for One Killing Many and Many Killing One
Rulings on multiple victims and multiple perpetrators in qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas when one kills multiple victims and when multiple kill one, and apply the principle to novel scenarios.

Core Idea
A single killer is executed once for all victims; multiple killers are all executed for one victim.

This lesson covers two opposite but equally important qisas scenarios: a single murderer with multiple victims, and a group of murderers with a single victim. Understanding these rulings clarifies how Hanafi law balances justice — the life of the killer is taken once regardless of victim count, but every participant in a killing is individually liable.

Special Cases in Qisas: Death, Slave's Confession, and Shooting Through
Rulings on three exceptional scenarios affecting qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for three exceptional scenarios in qisas: death of the offender, confession of a slave, and an arrow that kills two persons.

Core Idea
Qisas is a personal penal right that drops upon death but applies on a slave's confession; unintended killing shifts diyah to the 'aqila.

Qisas (retaliation) is normally due on the killer, but special circumstances alter the ruling. What if the killer dies before execution? What if the killer is a slave? What if an intended shot also kills an unintended victim? This lesson covers these three distinct cases as stated by Imam al-Quduri, clarifying how the Hanafi school applies qisas and diyah.

Guided Path

Extract the three scenarios
Read the Arabic text and list the three distinct cases: death of the offender before qisas, a slave's confession to intentional killing, and an arrow that goes through the intended target killing another person as well.
2

For each scenario, note the hukm (ruling): first, qisas drops and nothing is due from the estate; second, qisas is binding on the slave; third, qisas for the intended victim and diyah on the 'aqila for the unintended victim.
3
Identify the legal reasoning
Understand the principle behind each ruling: qisas is a personal right that does not transfer to the estate upon death; a slave's confession regarding his own person is valid despite his property status; the unintended killing is treated like manslaughter with diyah on the 'aqila.
4
Note the role of the 'aqila
In the third scenario, the diyah for the unintended victim is payable by the killer's 'aqila (paternal male relatives), not by the killer himself, because the killing was unintentional in relation to that victim.

Qisas for a Killed Mukatib: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on who executes qisas for a mukatib, including a dispute between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a master has qisas for a killed mukatib, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scenario where the mukatib leaves sufficient estate.

Core Idea
The master's right to qisas for his mukatib depends on the absence of other heirs and the insufficiency of the mukatib's estate.

This lesson examines the nuanced ruling on qisas when a mukatib (a slave on a contract of manumission) is killed. The general principle is that the right to demand qisas belongs to the heir, but for a mukatib, the master may be the heir only if the mukatib dies without sufficient assets to complete his freedom. The passage from Al-Hidayah and Al-Jawhara presents a scenario that splits the Hanafi school: when the mukatib leaves enough property but has no other heir, do the master's rights remain? We will walk through the conditions and the dispute between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal condition
Determine the status of the mukatib – his death before or after paying the kitaba – and whether he left sufficient property (wafa').
The mukatib's estate is key: if he left wafa', he could have paid for freedom; if not, he remains a slave in effect.
2
Distinguish the three scenarios
The passage lists three cases: (a) no wafa', no other heir → master has qisas; (b) wafa' and other heir → no qisas for master; (c) both master and other heirs → ambiguity (ishtibah).
3
Isolate the disputed scenario
The key dispute arises in the fourth scenario: mukatib killed intentionally, no heir except master, but leaves wafa'.
This scenario is not explicitly covered in the first three, hence the ikhtilaf.
4
State the Hanafi majority position
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that qisas is wajib and the master executes it.
This is the mufta bihi view.
5
Contrast with Imam Muhammad's view
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that there is no qisas; only diyah.
His basis: doubt about the master's right due to potential freedom.
6
Understand the basis of disagreement
The disagreement centers on whether the wafa' effectively makes the mukatib free, thereby removing the master's heirship. The majority see the master as still the legal heir.
Both positions have internal logic within Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This lesson addresses the complex case of a mukatib (a slave who has contracted to buy his freedom) who is intentionally killed. The general ruling is that the right to demand qisas belongs to the heir of the victim. The text clarifies three scenarios: (1) If the mukatib has no heir except the master and he did not leave enough property to pay the kitaba, the master has qisas. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is typically the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as it is the majority.

Commentary
This passage presents a comprehensive list of rulings on qisas for injuries to limbs and other body parts. The overall principle is that qisas (retaliation in kind) is only valid when exact equality (mumathalah) is possible. For injuries where equality cannot be ensured, such as bones (except teeth), internal organs, or where the victim's wound cannot be matched precisely due to anatomical differences, qisas is not applied; instead, blood money (arsh/diyah) is due. Key points: (1) Amputation at joints (wrist, ankle, nose tip, ear) allows qisas because the cut is precise. (2) For the eye, if it is gouged out, no qisas because exact retaliation may cause excess; but if the eye remains but vision is lost, qisas is applied by using a heated mirror and cotton to similarly destroy vision without removing the eye. (3) Tooth is considered like a joint, so qisas applies, but other bones do not allow qisas. (4) Head wounds (shajjah) allow qisas if the wound can be matched precisely. (5) Tongue and penis: no qisas unless cut at the glans (hashafah) for penis, because otherwise exact measurement is impossible; Abu Yusuf differed on tongue and penis. (6) No qisas between a man and woman, free and slave, or two slaves for injuries below the life (i.e., non-fatal), because of lack of equality in value. (7) Qisas for limbs applies between Muslim and dhimmi (non-Muslim citizen) – equality in rights. (8) If the victim heals completely from an injury that could not be exactly retaliated (e.g., half-forearm cut, deep wound), no qisas, only arsh. (9) If the assailant has a defective hand, the victim may choose to take the defective hand or demand full blood money. (10) For head wounds of different sizes, the victim may exact qisas only to the extent of his injury. The explanations from al-Jawharah reinforce that qisas is not applied where exact equivalence is impossible, and arsh is obligatory instead.

المرآة (al-mir'ah): A mirror heated to reflect heat onto the eye; used in the procedure to remove vision as qisas for blinding.
شجة (shajjah): A head wound that reaches the bone or beyond; specific classifications exist (e.g., simple, penetrating).
جائفة (ja'ifah): A penetrating wound that reaches the body cavity (e.g., abdomen or chest).
حشفة (hashafah): The glans of the penis; the only part of the penis where qisas applies if cut.
أرش (arsh): Blood money for injuries; fixed compensation when qisas is not possible.

When multiple offenders injure a single limb, none face qisas but share half the life-diyah; when one offender injures multiple victims, each may claim qisas or share compensation.

Types of Head Wounds (Shijaj) and Their Rulings
Defines ten head wounds and prescribes diyat or qisas for each, with a dispute on qisas for lower wounds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the ten types of shijaj, state the prescribed diyat for each, and explain the Hanafi position on qisas for these wounds.

Core Idea
Only the mudihah (bone-baring wound) may entail qisas; other head wounds carry fixed diyat or just compensation.

Head wounds require precise classification to determine compensation. This passage from Al-Hidayah enumerates ten types of shijaj with their definitions and assigns specific blood money or, in the case of intentional mudihah, retaliation. Understanding these categories is essential for correct legal application.

Guided Path
1
List the ten shijaj
Memorize the sequence: harisah, damiyah, badi'ah, mutalahimah, simhaq, mudihah, hashimah, munaqqilah, ammah, ja'ifah.
Order is based on increasing severity from skin scratch to cavity wound.
2
Match each to definition
For each type, recall the depth or effect: e.g., mudihah lays bare the bone; hashimah breaks bone.
Definitions are given in the text.
3
Assign the ruling
Note which types have fixed diyat (hashimah, munaqqilah, ammah, ja'ifah), which have hukumat al-adl (below mudihah), and which may have qisas (only intentional mudihah).
Mudihah is the only type where qisas is possible.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa said no qisas for any shijaj; Imam Muhammad said qisas for wounds below mudihah (i.e., up to simhaq). Al-Hidayah presents Muhammad's view as zahir al-riwayah.
The mufta bihi is the view of Imam Muhammad.
5
Apply the inclusion rule
If a mudihah also causes loss of intellect or hair, only one diyat is due; the lesser injury is included. This is because loss of intellect is like death and hair loss is encompassed by the wound's arsh.
This prevents double compensation for related harms.

Coercion to Kill an Innocent Muslim: Ruling and Liability
Question-answer on coercion to murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing an innocent Muslim under coercion, explain why qisas falls on the coercer, and apply the principle that no necessity justifies taking an innocent life.

Core Idea
One may not kill an innocent Muslim to save one's own life; qisas rests on the coercer.

This lesson examines a severe case of coercion (ikrāh): when a person is threatened with death unless he kills an innocent Muslim. The Hanafi school holds that one must endure death rather than kill an innocent. Even if the coerced person kills, the blood-liability (qisas) falls on the coercer, not the coerced. This ruling underscores the absolute sanctity of a Muslim's life, which cannot be sacrificed even under existential duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario and the core legal question
Read the first question: a man is coerced to kill a Muslim on threat of his own death. The central issue: is he permitted to kill to save himself?
The scenario is one of full duress (ikrāh muljiʼ).
2
Extract the first ruling and its basis
The answer: لا يسعه ذلك، بل يصبر ولو قتل. He is not permitted; he must endure death. The basis is the absolute sanctity of innocent life.
Compare with property destruction under coercion, which is allowed.
3
Extract the second ruling and its implication
If he kills, he is sinful (آثم). Coercion does not remove the prohibition of killing an innocent.
Sin attaches despite duress.
4
Extract the third ruling and its legal consequence
Qisas is obligatory upon the coercer, not the coerced. The coercer is the direct cause (sabab); the coerced is a tool (ālah).
This is a key Hanafi distinction.
5
Summarize the underlying principle from Al-Hidayah
The marginal note states: قتل المسلم لا يستباح لضرورة ما. Killing a Muslim is not permissible for any necessity.
This principle overrides even the threat of one's own death.
