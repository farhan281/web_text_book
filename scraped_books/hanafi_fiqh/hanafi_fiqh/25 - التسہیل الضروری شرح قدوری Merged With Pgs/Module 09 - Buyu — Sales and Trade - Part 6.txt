Appointing an Agent and Breaking an Oath
Ruling on whether appointing an agent breaks an oath not to transact

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the basic ruling that appointing an agent does not break an oath not to transact, and identify when intention or authority causes the oath to be broken.

Core Idea
An oath is interpreted by its literal scope; agency does not break it unless intention or authority extends it.

When someone swears not to sell, buy, or hire, then appoints an agent to do that for them, does this violate their oath? The Hanafi ruling distinguishes between the literal wording of the oath and the swearer's intention or status. This lesson unpacks the basic rule and its two critical exceptions from Al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the action
The swearer says: 'I will not sell, or buy, or hire.' Then he appoints an agent to do one of these. Determine whether the agent's action fulfills the literal meaning of the oath.
The oath is a personal promise; agency introduces another person.
2
State the basic ruling
The text rules: 'He does not break it.' The oath is not violated because the contract is performed by the agent, not the swearer personally.
Reason: The obligations (rights) fall on the agent, not the principal.
3
Understand the default interpretation
The oath is interpreted according to its literal directive. Unless specified otherwise, 'I will not sell' means 'I will not personally sell.'
This follows the Hanafi principle of al-haqiqah (literal meaning) in oaths.
4
Identify the first exception: inclusive intention
If the swearer intended his oath to cover both personal action and delegation (i.e., he made his oath general), then the agent's action breaks the oath.
The text: 'if he intended that, he breaks it' – intention broadens the scope.
5
Identify the second exception: person of authority
If the swearer is a person of authority (e.g., a ruler) who normally does not contract personally, then appointing an agent effectively counts as his own action, breaking the oath.
Because 'he does not undertake the contract himself' – his norm is agency.

Oath on Repaying Debt with Defective Coins
Fatwa on oath fulfillment with defective currency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether paying a debt with defective coins (zuyuf, bannahrajah) fulfills an oath to repay and explain the legal principle that defects do not negate the genus.

Core Idea
Paying a debt with defective coins fulfills an oath because defects do not negate the genus of the currency.

In this lesson, we analyze a fatwa from al-Hidayah concerning oaths and debt repayment. A person swears to repay a debt on a specific day, pays with coins that later turn out to be counterfeit (zuyuf), of inferior quality (bannahrajah), or claimed by a third party (mustahaqqah). Does he break his oath? The answer reveals an important Hanafi principle about the effect of defects on the genus of money.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Question
Read the passage: a man swears to repay a debt today, then pays. The creditor later finds some coins defective. Does the debtor fulfill his oath?
Focus on the legal question, not the factual details.
2
Recognize the Key Terms
Note the three types of defective coins mentioned: زيوف (counterfeit but circulating), بنهرجة (poor quality, often rejected), and مستحقة (claimed by another). Understand that these are all substandard but still considered currency of the same genus.
The definitions are provided in the commentary for clarity.
3
Analyze the Answer and Reasoning
The fatwa states: yes, he fulfills the oath. The reason is that counterfeitness is a defect, and a defect does not eradicate the genus. Thus, the coins are still of the same type as the debt owed.
The principle is that defects in quality do not change the essential nature (jins) of the currency.
4
Consider the Implications of Acceptance and Third-Party Claims
If the creditor accepts the defective coins, the debt is considered paid. Even if the money is later claimed by a rightful owner, the oath fulfillment is not undone because receipt of claimed property is valid.
The fulfillment is based on the debtor's act of payment, not on the ultimate validity of the coins.

Two Claimants for Purchase of the Same Slave: Rulings on Date and Possession
Rulings on competing claims of purchase with or without dates and possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for competing purchase claims, distinguishing outcomes based on presence of purchase dates or possession.

Core Idea
When two claimants prove purchase, the earlier date or possession gives priority; otherwise they divide the slave.

Disputes over ownership of a purchased item can arise when two buyers present evidence of sale from the same seller. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: if both have only evidence without dates or possession, each may take half; but if one has an earlier purchase date or possession, that claimant prevails. This lesson unpacks these rules from the classical text al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and their evidence
Two persons each claim to have bought the same slave from the same seller. Both produce valid evidence (bayyinah) of the sale.
The evidence is presumed equal in probative value at this stage.
2
Default ruling: equal division with option
Since both claims are equally supported, the judge gives each the option to take half the slave for half the price. If both agree, the slave is divided.
This effectively annuls the original sales and creates a new sale of half-shares.
3
Effect of rescission
If one claimant refuses the half and chooses faskh (rescission), the other cannot take the whole slave; the sale is fully void.
The judge's division is contingent on both accepting.
4
When purchase dates are mentioned
If both claimants provide dates of purchase, the slave goes to the one with the earlier date. The earlier date proves prior ownership.
This rule gives priority to the contractual timeline.
5
When dates are absent and possession is present
If no dates are mentioned, but one claimant has physical possession (qabd), that claimant is more entitled to the slave.
Possession is a strong indicator of ownership when documentary evidence is silent.
6
When only one claimant has a date
If only one claimant has a specified date, the slave is awarded to that claimant, regardless of possession.
A specified date overrides lack of possession.

Resolving Conflicting Purchase Claims from the Same Seller
Ruling on conflicting purchase claims with and without dates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving conflicting purchase claims from the same seller, distinguishing between scenarios with and without dates, and with or without possession.

Core Idea
Priority: earlier date > possession > no date/possession leads to equal sharing with option.

When two claimants each produce evidence of purchasing the same item from the same seller, how does a judge decide? The Hanafi school provides clear, hierarchical rules based on the presence of dates and possession. This lesson unpacks the classical text from al-Hidayah, covering all possible scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and the object
Both claim to have bought the same slave from the same seller. Each brings evidence (bayyinah). The judge must determine who is entitled.
The seller is not a party to the dispute; the claims are between the two buyers.
2
Check for dates on the contracts
If both contracts have dates, compare them. The earlier date gives priority to that claimant. The slave is awarded to the one with the earlier date.
If only one has a date, that one is preferred regardless of possession.
3
If no dates, check possession
If neither contract has a date but one claimant has taken possession (qabd), that claimant is preferred.
Possession is a strong indicator of ownership when dates are absent.
4
If no dates and no possession, equal sharing with option
When neither has a date nor possession, each claimant has the option (khiyar) to take half the slave for half the price or to rescind (faskh) the claim entirely.
If one chooses rescission, the other does not automatically get the whole; the case remains open.
5
Apply the hierarchy consistently
The judge follows this order: (1) earlier date, (2) possession if no dates, (3) equal sharing with option if neither dates nor possession. This ensures objective and predictable rulings.

Ruling on equal claims from different sellers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two claimants each prove purchase from different sellers with dates and explain why dates do not create preference.

Core Idea
Different sellers mean no date priority; claimants are equal.

When two claimants independently prove purchase of the same item from different sellers, even if they bring dated contracts, the claims do not conflict over the same source. The Hanafi school rules that they are equal, regardless of date. This lesson explains the logic and the practical outcome: the item is divided equally between them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two claimants each claim to have purchased the same item. Importantly, they name different sellers and produce evidence (e.g., witnesses, documents) with dates.
2
Recognize the key fact: different sellers
Because the sellers are different, each claimant's purchase is from a separate legal origin. The date does not resolve who has a better right because the dates refer to distinct transactions.
3
Apply the ruling: no tarjih
The judge does not prefer one over the other. Both claims are equally valid in terms of proof. The item is treated as jointly owned by them, and the judge orders equal division.
This contrasts with claims against the same seller, where the earlier date would give priority.

Disputes Between Buyer and Seller on Price and Item
Rules for adjudicating buyer-seller disputes on price and item.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedural rules when buyer and seller dispute the price or item, by identifying which scenario applies and stating the ruling.

Core Idea
When buyer and seller dispute price or item, evidence resolves; if none, mutual oaths annul the sale.

This lesson covers the Hanafi rules for adjudicating disputes between buyer and seller when they disagree on the price, the quantity of the item, or conditions like credit term or option. These rules ensure that disputes are resolved fairly, with priority given to evidence and a structured procedure when evidence is absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of dispute
Determine whether the dispute is about the price, the quantity of the item, a credit term, an option, or the receipt of part of the price. This determines which rule applies.
The text lists several possible subjects: price, item quantity, term, option, partial payment.
2
Check for evidence (bayyinah)
See if either party has witnesses or other proof. If one party has evidence, judgment is in their favor. If both have evidence, the evidence supporting the higher claim (higher price or larger quantity) is preferred.
Evidence here means two male witnesses or one male and two females, per Hanafi law.
3
Apply the no-evidence procedure
If neither has evidence, the judge first asks the buyer to accept the seller's claimed price, or the seller to accept the buyer's claimed quantity. If they refuse, the judge administers mutual oaths (tahāluf), starting with the buyer.
If one party refuses to swear, the other's claim becomes binding. If both swear, the sale is annulled.
4
Handle special scenarios: perished item
If the sold item perishes after delivery and the price is disputed, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that there is no mutual oath; the buyer's word on price is taken with his oath. Muhammad rules for mutual oaths and annulment on the value of the perished item.
Similarly, if one of two slaves perishes, Abu Hanifa requires no mutual oath unless the seller forgoes the share; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad order mutual oaths.
5
Differentiate disputes on term and option
If the dispute is about a credit term or a condition of option, there is no mutual oath. The word is given to the person who denies the existence of the term or option, with his oath.
This is different from disputes on price or quantity, where mutual oaths apply.

Paternity of a Slave-Girl's Child After Sale
Determining paternity of a child born to a sold slave-girl within six months

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paternity of a child born to a sold slave-girl within six months and apply it to scenarios involving competing claims.

Core Idea
A child born within six months of sale belongs to the seller — the minimum gestation decides paternity.

In classical Hanafi jurisprudence, the sale of a slave-girl may be rescinded if she gives birth shortly after the sale, revealing that the child was conceived before the transfer. This lesson explains how paternity is determined and the consequences for the sale contract, including the status of the slave-girl as umm walad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the temporal condition
The passage specifies a six-month window from the sale date. Hanafi law considers six months as the minimum pregnancy duration; any birth within that period indicates conception before the sale.
This condition is the key to the ruling.
2
Apply the condition to the seller's claim
If the child is born less than six months after the sale, the seller's claim of paternity is accepted. The child is attributed to him, and the slave-girl becomes umm walad (mother of his child, freed upon his death).
The seller does not need to prove actual paternity; the timeframe suffices.
3
Determine the effect on the sale contract
Because the child was conceived before the sale, the slave-girl was effectively an umm walad at the time of sale, making the sale contract voidable (fasid). The sale is rescinded (faskh), and the price is returned to the buyer.
Rescission returns both parties to their pre-sale positions.
4
Consider the buyer's competing claim
Even if the buyer also claims the child, the six-month rule still assigns the child to the seller. The buyer's claim does not change the outcome because the child's conception clearly predates the sale.
The buyer cannot override biological fact.

Hoarding essential food is prohibited, but price-fixing by the ruler is only allowed if sellers grossly overcharge, and even then it is not obligatory.

This lesson examines two interrelated commercial regulations: hoarding (ihtikar) and price-fixing (tas'ir). While both schools agree that hoarding essential foodstuffs to the detriment of the community is prohibited, they diverge sharply on the ruler's role in setting prices. The Hanafi school generally discourages price-fixing as an infringement on market freedom, permitting it only when sellers engage in gross exploitation. In contrast, the Maliki school obligates the ruler to fix prices during inflation, based on public interest. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the basis of modern Islamic economic policy debates.

The Hanafi response, via al-Shami, notes that the Hanafi school does not condition gross excess; rather, the ruling is based on necessity (darura). The distinction between the two schools is that Hanafi allows price-fixing only in extreme cases, while Maliki obligates it generally during inflation.
Al-Shami's commentary highlights the different conditions.

In Hanafi school, rulers rarely intervene in pricing unless there is gross exploitation. In Maliki, rulers are required to fix prices during times of high prices, which could happen more frequently. This affects daily market regulation.

Ruling on Price-Fixing (Tas'ir) and Hoarding (Ihtikar)
Ruling on whether the ruler should fix prices and the conditions for permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on price-fixing, identify the condition that permits it, and explain the difference between the Hanafi and Maliki positions.

Core Idea
The ruler should not fix prices; only when sellers excessively raise prices may he intervene, and only after consultation.

This passage addresses a common concern in Islamic markets: should the ruler set prices to prevent exploitation? The Hanafi school takes a cautious approach, generally leaving prices to the market, but allows intervention in cases of clear abuse. This lesson explains the default ruling, the exception, and how the Hanafi view differs from the Maliki school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The text begins with the default: the ruler should not fix prices – it is not befitting (لا ينبغي).
This indicates the general principle of free trade.
2
Note the condition for exception
Al-Durr al-Mukhtar states that the ruler only fixes prices when owners exceed the price excessively (fahish).
Excessive overpricing is the key condition.
3
Understand the procedural requirement
Even when the condition is met, the ruler must act with consultation (بمشورة أهل الرأي).
This ensures fairness and expertise.
4
Compare with Maliki position
Imam Malik holds that the ruler must fix prices in times of high prices without the condition of excessive transgression.
This shows the practical difference between the schools.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a shortage where sellers double prices: Hanafi allows intervention only if increase is excessive and after consultation; Maliki would oblige it.
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear in policy.

الاحتكار والتسعير
الجواب: لا ينبغي له أن يُسعر عليهم، بل يتركهم يبايعون أموالهم حسب ما أرادوا.
قال في الدر المختار ولا يسعر الحاكم إلا إذا تعدى الأرباب عن القيمة فاحشا، فيسعر بمشورة أهل الرأي. وقال مالك: على الوالي التسعير عام الغلاء. قال الشامي في الحاشية أي يجب عليه ذلك، كما في "غاية البيان"، وأيضا لم يشترط التعدي الفاحش كما ذكره ابن الكمال، وبه ظهر الفرق بين المذهبين (٢٥٦/٥).
Hoarding and Price-Fixing. Answer: It is not befitting for him (the ruler) to fix prices for them, rather he should leave them to trade their goods as they wish. It is stated in al-Durr al-Mukhtar: The ruler does not fix prices unless the owners exceed the price excessively; then he fixes with the consultation of people of opinion. Malik said: It is upon the ruler to fix prices in times of high prices. Al-Shami said in the hashiyah: i.e., it is obligatory upon him, as in Ghayat al-Bayan; also he did not stipulate excessive transgression as Ibn al-Kamal mentioned, and thereby the difference between the two schools becomes apparent (5/256).

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on price-fixing (tas'ir) and hoarding (ihtikar). The core ruling is that the ruler should not set prices for merchants, allowing free trade. However, the commentary from al-Durr al-Mukhtar and al-Shami clarifies that if sellers exploit by charging excessively (fahish), the ruler may fix prices with expert consultation. A comparison is made with Imam Malik's school, which mandates price control in times of scarcity without the condition of excessive exploitation, highlighting the difference between the two madhhabs.

Key Terms
تسعير (tas'ir): Setting prices by the ruler or authority.
احتكار (ihtikar): Hoarding goods to drive up prices.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school permits price-fixing only when sellers charge an excessive price and after consultation, differing from the Maliki school which mandates it during high prices.
