Hawala: Definition and Conditions for Validity
Definition of hawala and conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of hawala and identify the three parties whose consent is required for its validity.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers a debt from one liability to another—it only works for debts, not objects, and all three parties must consent.

This lesson introduces the basic contract of hawala, a transfer of debt that is commonly used in Islamic finance and daily transactions. We begin with a clear definition and then examine the essential conditions that make it valid. Understanding these fundamentals will prevent common errors when structuring hawala agreements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the first part of the passage: hawala is the transfer of a debt from one dhimma to another. Note that it is only for debts (duyun), not for specific objects (a'yan) or rights (huquq).
The word 'dhimma' means legal liability or capacity.
2
Recognize the scope limitation
Hawala is permissible only for debts. Physical objects require delivery, and rights are not transferable through hawala. This is a key restriction.
3
List the three parties
Identify the muhil (original debtor), muhtal (creditor), and muhtal 'alayh (new debtor). Memorize their roles.
Each party has a specific Arabic term.
4
State the consent requirement
For validity, all three parties must consent. Without any one's consent, the hawala is invalid. Understand why: each party's interests are affected.
The Hanafi school emphasizes consent of the new debtor because he takes on liability.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hawala (Transfer of Debt) provides a definition and the fundamental conditions for its validity. First, the definition: hawala is the transfer of a debt from one person's liability (dhimma) to another. This means that the original debtor (muhil) transfers the obligation to pay a debt to a third party (muhtal 'alayh), so that the creditor (muhtal) now claims from the new debtor. The text notes that hawala is permissible only for debts (duyun), not for specific physical objects (a'yan) or intangibles like rights (huquq). This is because a specific object cannot be transferred in this manner without delivery, and rights are not debts. Second, the conditions for validity: the consent (rida) of all three parties is required: (1) the muhil (the original debtor), (2) the muhtal (the creditor), and (3) the muhtal 'alayh (the new debtor). Without the agreement of any one of them, the hawala is not valid. This is because it is a contract that involves transferring obligation, and each party's interests are affected.

Key Terms
حوالة (hawala): Transfer of a debt from one person's liability to another
ذمة (dhimma): Legal capacity or liability; the abstract entity that bears obligations
محيل (muhil): The original debtor who transfers the debt
محتال (muhtal): The creditor, the one to whom the debt is owed
محتال عليه (muhtal 'alayh): The new debtor, the person to whom the debt is transferred

Right of Recourse in Hawala and Definition of Tawa
Ruling on finality of hawala and definition of tawa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the effect of a completed hawala on the original debtor and identify the exception of tawa.

Core Idea
Once hawala is complete, the original debtor is free unless the creditor's right is lost (tawa).

This lesson explains the finality of hawala. Upon transfer, the original debtor is discharged; the creditor cannot revert except in one case: when the debt is lost due to the new debtor's inability to pay. This exception is called tawa, and it protects the creditor from total loss.

Guided Path
1
State the general ruling of hawala
Identify what happens upon completion: the muhil (original debtor) is bari' (discharged) and the muhtal (creditor) cannot return to him.
This is the default position.
2
Identify the exception
Find the condition that allows recourse: only when there is tawa (loss of the right).
Tawa is a technical term.
3
Define tawa
Understand that tawa means the creditor's right is lost, typically due to the new debtor's insolvency or death without leaving assets.
The text does not complete the answer, but classical sources define it as such.

Commentary
This passage completes the discussion of hawala by addressing the effect once the transfer is finalized. It states that upon the completion of a valid hawala (the transfer of debt), the original debtor (muhil) is discharged (bari') from the debt. Consequently, the creditor (muhtal) cannot revert to the original debtor to claim payment. There is, however, one exception: if the creditor's right is 'lost' (tawa). The term 'tawa' (also spelled 'tawan') refers to a situation where the new debtor (muhtal 'alayh) becomes insolvent or defaults to the extent that the creditor cannot recover the debt from him. In such a case, the creditor may return to the original debtor to claim payment. The second question asks for the definition of tawa and how it materializes, but the answer is not given in the surviving text (it likely continues on the next page). This doctrine protects the creditor from complete loss due to the new debtor's inability to pay, ensuring that hawala does not become a means of injustice.

Key Terms
توى (tawa): Loss of the creditor's right due to the new debtor's insolvency or inability to pay

The Definition of Taway in Hawala and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Its Conditions
Defines taway and presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on its conditions, followed by two applied scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of taway, identify the conditions for its realization according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the ruling to two specific hawala dispute scenarios.

Core Idea
Taway in hawala occurs under specific conditions; Abu Hanifa requires two, the Sahibayn add a third: judicial insolvency.

Hawala (transfer of debt) is a common transaction where a debtor (muheel) transfers his debt to a third party (muhtal alayh). If the assignee cannot pay, the assignor's liability may revive, but only when taway (loss) is established. The Hanafi school debates the precise conditions that trigger taway, and this passage clarifies the positions with two practical examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of hawala
Recognise that in hawala, the muheel's debt is transferred to the muhtal alayh. If the muhtal alayh fails to pay, the muhtal (creditor) may revert to the muheel only if taway (loss) occurs. Taway is the event that re-establishes the muheel's liability.
2
Memorise the two conditions of Abu Hanifa
According to Abu Hanifa, taway is realised by either: (1) the muhtal alayh denies the hawala and takes an oath, with no evidence for the muhtal; or (2) the muhtal alayh dies insolvent.
Note that these are two separate events, each sufficient.
3
Add the third condition of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad add a third condition: the judge declares the muhtal alayh insolvent during his lifetime. Thus, they consider taway to be established by any one of three events.
4
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference matters when a judge declares insolvency while the muhtal alayh is still alive but bankrupt. Under Abu Hanifa, this alone does not constitute taway; under the Sahibayn, it does, and the muheel becomes liable.
5
Apply the rulings to the two qa scenarios
Read the two questions: (1) If muhtal alayh demands payment from muheel, and muheel claims he assigned for a debt, the muheel's claim is not accepted; he must pay. (2) If muheel demands from muhtal and claims assignment was for collection, the muheel's statement is taken with oath. Understand that these are separate from the taway conditions but illustrate related hawala disputes.

Commentary
This passage addresses the concept of taway (loss) in the context of hawala (transfer of debt). It begins by defining taway as loss (talf). The passage then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, taway is established by either the muhtal alayh (assignee) denying the hawala and taking an oath (with no evidence from the muhtal against him), or the muhtal alayh dying insolvent. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad), taway is established by one of three matters: the two mentioned by Abu Hanifa, plus a third: the judge declaring the muhtal alayh insolvent during his lifetime. The practical consequence is that if taway occurs, the duty of the muheel (assignor) to pay the debt is affected. The passage then addresses two further questions about hawala: first, if the muhtal alayh demands payment from the muheel, and the muheel claims he had assigned due to a debt owed to him, the muheel's claim is not accepted; he must pay. Second, if the muheel demands from the muhtal and claims the assignment was for collection, while the muhtal claims it was for a debt, then the muheel's statement is taken with his oath.

Key Terms
التوى (al-taway): In this context, the loss that falls upon the muhtal (creditor) such that he cannot recover from the muhtal alayh, triggering the muheel's liability.
المحتال عليه (al-muhtal alayh): The person on whom the hawala (transfer) is made – the assignee who owes a debt to the muheel and now owes the muhtal.
المحيل (al-muheel): The person who makes the hawala – the assignor who transfers his debt to the assignee.
