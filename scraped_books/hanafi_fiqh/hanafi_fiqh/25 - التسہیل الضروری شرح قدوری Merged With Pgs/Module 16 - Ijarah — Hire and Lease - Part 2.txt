Dispute Resolution When Worker Violates Instructions: Owner's Options
Ruling on dispute resolution in ijarah with worker violation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dispute resolution when a worker violates instructions and apply the owner's two options.

Core Idea
If a worker violates orders, the owner's word with oath is accepted; he may claim unworked value or take the garment and pay fair wage.

In an ijarah contract, if the worker (tailor or dyer) exceeds or violates the owner's instructions, a dispute may arise over liability. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling on whose word is taken and how the owner can be compensated. This passage from al-Tashil al-Daruri explains the rule, which balances the rights of both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
The owner gives his garment to a tailor or dyer who then violates his specific instructions (e.g., cuts it differently or dyes it wrong). A dispute arises: the owner claims the worker acted contrary to orders, while the worker may deny it.
2
Apply the rule of evidence
The ruling states that the owner's statement (with an oath) is accepted over the worker's. This is the default rule when the worker is the one who performed the act and the owner disputes it.
The owner must swear an oath; if he refuses or his oath is insufficient, the worker's statement may be accepted.
3
Determine liability
Once the owner swears and the violation is established, each worker (if multiple) is liable for the damage caused by the violation. They must compensate the owner.
Joint liability: each guarantees according to their share of the violation.
4
Outline the owner's options
The owner has two choices: (1) claim the full value of the garment in its unworked state (i.e., the original cloth value) without paying any wage, or (2) take the garment as it is and pay the worker a fair wage (ujrat al-mithl) but not exceeding the originally named wage.
The fair wage is the market rate for similar work, capped at the agreed wage.

Invalidating Conditions in Ijarah
Conditions that invalidate lease and wage ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which conditions invalidate a lease contract and explain the wage ruling when an invalid lease is performed.

Core Idea
Conditions not essential to the lease make it void; if work is done, the worker gets fair wage capped at the agreed amount.

Not every condition added to a lease is acceptable. The Hanafi school specifies that conditions extraneous to the contract's nature render it invalid, but if the work is already performed, the worker is entitled to fair compensation. This lesson unpacks these rulings through the Q&A format of the source text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
Read the first question: 'Does the lease become invalid due to conditions?' Identify the act (lease) and the type of condition (those not required by the contract).
Extraneous conditions are those that add obligations or benefits not inherent in the lease.
2
Determine the ruling
The answer states that such conditions invalidate the lease, just as they invalidate a sale. Note the hukm level: the contract becomes fasid (invalid).
3
Understand the consequence if work is done
The second question: what happens if the invalid lease is executed? The lessee must pay ujrat al-mithl (similar wage), capped at the named wage. Apply this to a scenario.
This cap protects the lessee from exceeding the agreed amount.
4
Apply the principle
Test a condition: e.g., 'tenant must paint the house after lease end.' This is extraneous, so the lease is fasid. If the tenant uses the property, he owes ujrat al-mithl not exceeding the rent.
Contrast with inherent conditions like payment terms.
5
Recognize the analogy to sale
The ruling explicitly parallels sale (kama yafsad al-bay'). This shows the Hanafi principle that invalidating conditions in exchange contracts are treated similarly.
This analogy is core to understanding the structure.

Commentary
This passage covers two topics. First, invalidating conditions: a lease contract is invalidated (fasid) by conditions that are not inherent in the contract (e.g., a condition that the lessee paint the house after lease end). If an invalid lease is executed, the worker is entitled to the fair wage (ujrat al-mithl) not exceeding the named wage. Second, the option clause (khiyar al-shart): just as in sale, it is valid in lease. This allows parties to stipulate a period to rescind the lease. This is consistent with the Hanafi view that khiyar is permissible in contracts of exchange.

Option Clause in Ijarah
Validity of option clause in Ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the option clause (khiyar al-shart) is valid in lease contracts and justify it by analogy to sale.

Core Idea
Just as in sale, parties may stipulate an option to rescind a lease within a specified period.

Many assume the option to cancel (khiyar al-shart) applies only to sale, but the Hanafi school extends it to lease contracts. This lesson covers the validity of such a condition, drawn from the same source text.

Guided Path
1
Recall the ruling on option in sale
Remember that in sale, khiyar al-shart (option to rescind within a specified period) is valid. This is a well-known principle.
This analogy is explicitly made in the text: 'kama yasihhu fi al-bay'.
2
Analogize to lease
Lease is also a contract of exchange (mu'awada), like sale. Therefore, the same validity applies unless there is specific evidence to exclude it.
The Hanafi school treats exchange contracts similarly for option clauses.
3
Recognize that option clause is inherent
Unlike extraneous conditions (e.g., painting), an option clause relates to the binding nature of the contract and does not add an extra obligation. It is considered inherent.
This distinguishes it from invalidating conditions.
4
Apply the ruling
If a lessee stipulates the right to cancel within three days, the lease is valid with that option. Both parties must abide by the option period.
If the option expires without cancellation, the contract becomes binding.
5
Contrast with invalid conditions
An option clause is valid because it does not impose an additional duty; it only grants a right to rescind. This is why it is allowed.
This contrast reinforces the principle: conditions that alter the core exchange (like additional services) invalidate.

Disputes in Lease Contracts: Before, After, and Partial Utilization
Rules for disputes in lease contracts before, after, and partial performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for disputes between lessor and lessee in three scenarios: before utilization, after full utilization, and after partial utilization.

Core Idea
The timing of the dispute determines whether mutual oaths are needed and who gets the benefit of the doubt.

In lease contracts, when the lessor and lessee disagree about the rent or terms, the court must have a clear procedure. The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on how much of the lease period has been utilized. This lesson explores three distinct cases: before any use, after full use, and after partial use. Mastering these distinctions is essential for correctly resolving ijara disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the timing
Determine whether the dispute occurs before, after full, or after partial utilization (istīfā').
2
Apply the ruling for no utilization
If before any use, both parties take mutual oaths (tahāluf) and rescind the contract.
3
Apply the ruling for full utilization
If after full use, no mutual oaths; the lessee's word is taken with his oath.
4
Apply the ruling for partial utilization
If after partial use, mutual oaths for the remainder, and the lessee's word for the past period with his oath.
5
Understand the logic
The lessee is generally trusted for past utilization to avoid injustice, while mutual oaths reset the future.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
دعوى المؤجر والمستأجر السؤال: قد يقع الاختلاف بين المؤجر والمستأجر، فكيف يقضى بينهما؟ الجواب له أيضا صور مختلفة، فاحفظ أحكامها كما يلي: ١ - إذا اختلفا في الإجارة قبل استيفاء المعقود عليه، تحالفا وترادًا. ٢ - وإن اختلفا بعد الاستيفاء لم يتحالفا، وكان القول قول المستأجر مع يمينه. - وإن اختلفا بعد استيفاء بعض المعقود عليه، تحالفا وفسخ العقد فيما بقي، وكان القول فيما مضى قول المستأجر مع يمينه.
Claim of the lessor and lessee. Question: Disagreement may occur between the lessor and lessee, so how is judgment passed between them? Answer: It also has different forms, so memorize their rulings as follows: 1. If they differ regarding the lease before the subject matter is fully utilized, they take mutual oaths and rescind the contract. 2. If they differ after full utilization, they do not take mutual oaths, and the word is of the lessee with his oath. 3. If they differ after partial utilization of the subject matter, they take mutual oaths and rescind the contract for the remainder, and regarding the past, the word is of the lessee with his oath.
Source pages p. 485

Commentary
This passage provides the Hanafi ruling for disputes between a lessor (landlord) and lessee (tenant) in a lease contract. The passage is a ruling statement with three distinct scenarios. The key factor is the timing of the dispute relative to the utilization of the leased asset. Before any utilization, both parties take oaths (tahāluf) and the contract is rescinded (taraaddā). After full utilization, the lessee's statement is taken with his oath, and no mutual oaths are needed. After partial utilization, the contract is rescinded for the remaining period with mutual oaths, but for the period already utilized, the lessee's word is taken. This protects the lessee's claim about the rent already paid while allowing clean termination for the future.

Key Terms
الاستيفاء (al-istīfā'): Full utilization or completion of the leased service/asset.
ترادا (tarāddā): Mutual rescission of the contract, each returning what they received.
