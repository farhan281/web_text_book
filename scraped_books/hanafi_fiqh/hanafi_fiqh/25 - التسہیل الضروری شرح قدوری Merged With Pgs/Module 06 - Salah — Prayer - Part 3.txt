Making Up a Spoiled Voluntary Prayer: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Qada Units
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on qada of spoiled nafl prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi ruling that a spoiled voluntary prayer must be made up, and differentiate the positions of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (make up only the uncompleted portion) from Abu Yusuf (make up the entire prayer).

Core Idea
A commenced voluntary prayer is binding; completed units stand, only the spoiled part requires qada.

Prayer that is begun voluntarily becomes binding to complete. If you spoil it, you must make it up. But what if you only spoil part? The Hanafi school has a nuanced view: a completed unit stands alone. Let's examine the ruling and the intra-Hanafi dispute about how many rak'as to make up when a four-rak'a nafl is spoiled after completing the first two.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Recognize that starting a nafl prayer makes it binding. Spoiling it (e.g., breaking wudu) requires making up (qada) the spoiled portion.
2
Examine the specific scenario
Consider the case where a person intends four rak'as, completes the first two (sits and recites tashahhud), and then spoils the last two. The question is: what must be made up?
3
State the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the first two rak'as form a complete unit (shaf'). Therefore, only the spoiled portion (the last two) need to be made up. They rule: make up two rak'as.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
4
State Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf considers the entire four-rak'a prayer as one unit. Spoiling any part spoils the whole. He rules: make up all four rak'as.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: If someone starts four rak'as and spoils after two, Abu Hanifa requires two rak'as qada; Abu Yusuf requires four. The relied-upon position is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
This shows how a difference in conceptualizing the prayer's structure leads to different rulings.

Praying Nafl Sitting While Able to Stand
Ruling on permissibility of sitting in nafl despite ability to stand, with half reward.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for performing voluntary (nafl) prayer while sitting without an excuse and explain the effect on reward.

Core Idea
Sitting in nafl is permissible but halves the reward.

This lesson addresses a common question: Can one pray voluntary (nafl) prayers sitting down even if physically able to stand? The Hanafi ruling is clear—it is permissible, but doing so reduces the reward. Understanding this principle helps avoid confusion between voluntary and obligatory prayers, where sitting without excuse is not allowed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing voluntary (nafl) prayer while sitting despite being able to stand. The ruling is permissible (ja'iz). Confirming this is the first step in applying the principle.
Note that the ruling applies only to nafl, not fard.
2
Determine the effect on reward
The reward is halved (yatanaṣṣafu). This is derived from the hadith that the prayer of a sitting person is half that of a standing person. Recognizing this reduction is key to understanding the compromise between ease and optimal worship.
The halving is specific to nafl; for fard, sitting without excuse invalidates the prayer.
3
Distinguish from obligatory prayer
Contrast this with fard prayer: sitting without a valid excuse (e.g., illness) is impermissible and would invalidate the prayer. This distinction prevents misapplication of the ruling.
The hadith about half reward is understood in Hanafi school to apply solely to nafl.

It introduces the key term 'sahib al-tartib' — a person who has missed fewer than six prayers — and details the obligation to pray them in sequence. Exceptions to the Obligation of Tartīb
Three exceptions to the obligation of tartīb in missed prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions that nullify the obligation of arrangement (tartīb) in missed prayers and explain the rationale behind each exception.

Core Idea
The obligation to pray missed prayers in sequence drops when the number reaches six, the missed prayer is forgotten, or time is tight—due to hardship or necessity.

Arranging missed prayers in order (tartīb) is normally obligatory (wājib). However, the Hanafī school recognizes that strict adherence can become burdensome or impracticable. This lesson examines three specific exceptions where the requirement of tartīb is lifted, as recorded in the classical Hanafī texts, ensuring that the obligation does not lead to undue hardship or the loss of the current prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling
First, acknowledge that the general rule is that missed prayers must be made up in the order they were missed (tartīb). This is considered wājib in the Hanafī school.
Tartīb applies to all fard prayers, but not to witr (which is wājib).
2

The text specifies three cases where the obligation of tartīb falls away: (1) when missed prayers reach six, excluding witr; (2) forgetting a missed prayer; (3) tightness of time.
These exceptions are conditions that suspend the requirement.
3
Understand the first exception: six missed prayers
When the number of un-arranged missed prayers (excluding witr) becomes six or more, tartīb is no longer required. The rationale is that insisting on sequence with a large backlog would cause hardship (ḥaraj).
The missed prayers must be 'definitive' (iʿtiqādiyyah), meaning those whose obligation is certain (i.e., fard prayers that were definitely missed).

If a person forgets that they have a missed prayer, and they pray the current prayer, the current prayer is valid without having to repeat it. Forgetting is considered a valid excuse (ʿudhr).
This does not cancel the obligation to make up the missed prayer later, but it removes the requirement of arrangement for the current prayer.
5
Understand the third exception: tight time
When the time for the current prayer is so limited that making up the missed prayer first would cause the current prayer to be missed, one may pray the current prayer first and postpone the makeup.
The principle is to avoid losing the current prayer entirely.

Statements That Nullify Prayer (Al-Aqwal al-Mufsida)
Lists the statements that invalidate prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the verbal nullifiers of prayer and apply the Hanafi rule that any speech, even unintentional, invalidates the prayer.

Core Idea
Any speech, even a single word, nullifies prayer; responses, greetings, and even Quranic verses intended as answer invalidate.

In the Hanafi school, the prohibition on speech during prayer is absolute: any utterance meant to communicate or answer invalidates the salah. This includes a wide range of expressions, from groaning to reciting Quran in response to a question. Understanding these helps maintain the sanctity of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verbal nullifiers from the list
Read through the statement items (الأقوال) from the text. They include: speaking any word, greeting, returning a greeting, expressions of disgust (uff), groaning, moaning, crying due to worldly pain, responding to sneeze, answering a question about Allah, istirja, saying alhamdulillah upon good news, expressing wonder, any response/address, prompting a non-imam, and lahn that changes meaning.
The text includes 15 items; note some are actions? Actually all are verbal.
2
Understand the general principle: 'any speech intended as response or address'
The Hanafi principle is that speech during prayer is forbidden (haram) and nullifies the prayer if it is intended to communicate with someone else. Even a single letter if it conveys meaning.
The text says 'even one word, whether intentional, forgetful, or mistaken.'
3
Distinguish between permissible and impermissible recitation
Reciting Quran, dhikr, or du'a during prayer is permissible as part of the prayer. However, if the same words are used as a response to someone (e.g., saying 'Allahu Akbar' as a warning, or answering a question with Quranic verse), the prayer is nullified.
Example: saying 'La ilaha illallah' as a response to a question is nullifying; but reciting it as dhikr is fine.
4
Identify the exceptions: crying due to fear of Allah or Qiyamah
Crying due to the mention of Paradise or Hell is not nullifying; it is considered a natural expression of devotion. But crying due to personal pain or calamity nullifies.
This distinction shows that the intent matters: if the cry is a response to worldly concern, it nullifies.
5
Note the issue of lahn (grammatical mistakes) that change meaning
If a person mispronounces a word in such a way that the meaning changes (e.g., prolonging the hamza in 'Allahu Akbar' makes it interrogative 'AAllahu akbar?'), the prayer is nullified because it changes the intended meaning of takbir.
This is specific to recitation; minor lahn without meaning change may not nullify.

(2) A person who performed tayammum (due to water unavailability) can lead those who performed wudu; this is based on istihsan. (3) Similarly, one who wiped over his leather socks (khuffs) can lead those who washed their feet. (4) A healthy standing person can pray behind an imam who is sitting due to a valid excuse (illness). (6) A person praying a voluntary (nafl) prayer may follow an imam praying an obligatory (fard) prayer. (7) But the opposite (obligatory follower behind voluntary imam) is not allowed. (8) A person without a valid excuse (e.g., healthy) cannot follow an imam who has a chronic excuse that prevents proper purity, like urinary incontinence, since the imam's state is deficient.

Cutting or Completing Fajr and Maghrib When Congregation Begins
Rules for cutting or completing when iqamah is established during Fajr or Maghrib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on whether to cut or complete Fajr and Maghrib prayers when the congregational prayer is established, and explain the role of the maxim 'the majority has the ruling of the whole'.

Core Idea
Cut your individual Fajr or Maghrib prayer unless you have bound the second rak'ah with sajdah.

Imagine you are praying Fajr or Maghrib alone, and you hear the iqamah for the congregation. What should you do? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: if you have prayed only one rak'ah, you must cut your prayer and join the imam. If you have prayed two rak'ahs (for Maghrib), the key is whether you have bound the second rak'ah with sajdah. This lesson unpacks that ruling, including the legal maxim that treats the majority of a prayer like its entirety.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prayer and rak'ah count
Determine whether you are praying Fajr (2 rak'ahs) or Maghrib (3 rak'ahs) and how many rak'ahs you have completed.
Only Fajr and Maghrib are covered in this ruling.
2
Check the 'sajdah binding' of the second rak'ah
If you have prayed a second rak'ah, ask: Have you performed the sajdah (prostration) that binds that rak'ah? In Hanafi terminology, a rak'ah is bound with sajdah when you have performed the two prostrations of that rak'ah.
Binding with sajdah completes the rak'ah so that it counts as a full unit.
3
Apply the rule: Cut or complete
If you have prayed only one rak'ah of either prayer, cut and join the imam. If you have prayed two rak'ahs of Fajr or Maghrib, cut only if you have not bound the second rak'ah with sajdah; otherwise, complete your prayer and do not join.
For Fajr, two rak'ahs bound with sajdah means your prayer is finished, so you cannot join the congregation.
4
Understand the maxim for Maghrib
For Maghrib, if you have completed two rak'ahs (with sajdah), the majority of the prayer (2 out of 3) is done, so the prayer is treated as if it were complete. Therefore, you finish the third rak'ah alone and do not join the congregation.
The maxim 'li al-akthari hukm al-kull' (the majority has the ruling of the whole) justifies this.

Rulings on Hadath During Prayer
Q&A rulings on hadath during prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on minor and major hadath during prayer, including when to build upon the prayer and when to restart.

Core Idea
Bina is allowed for minor hadath but not for major hadath; speech after hadath invalidates completely.

This lesson explains the Hanafi rulings when a person experiences hadath (ritual impurity) during prayer. The general rule is that one may leave the prayer, renew wudu, and continue from where they stopped (bina). However, there are important exceptions: major hadath, speech after hadath, and specific scenarios for imams and the end of prayer. Understanding these distinctions prevents unnecessary invalidation and provides flexibility in worship.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule for minor hadath
Identify that the worshiper leaves the prayer, performs wudu, and builds upon what has been prayed. Note that restarting is considered better but not obligatory.
This is the default ruling for minor hadath (e.g., passing wind, urine).
2
Apply the imam and deputy rule
If the imam has hadath, he appoints a deputy. If the deputy is a masbuq (one who missed part of the prayer), he leads his remaining rak'ahs then appoints a mudrik to make taslim. If the deputy is a mudrik, he completes the prayer and makes taslim with the followers.
This ensures congregational prayer continues smoothly.
3
Identify the end-of-prayer exception
If hadath occurs after the worshiper has sat for the amount of tashahhud that includes taslim, he leaves, performs wudu, and then makes taslim without additional rak'ahs.
This is a concession for near-completion of prayer.
4
Contrast the effect of speech and movement
If the worshiper speaks after hadath, the prayer becomes invalid and building upon it is not allowed, regardless of intention or forgetfulness. However, walking to the place of wudu or turning from the qibla is excused and does not invalidate, even if it involves more than three steps.
Speech after hadath is treated like any other invalidating talk, but movement out of necessity is excused.
5
Recognize major hadath cases and their consequence
Major hadath (janabah from wet dream, insanity, fainting, loud laughter) invalidates the prayer completely. No building is allowed; the worshiper must restart after ghusl (for janabah) or after wudu (for the other cases).
These are considered greater impurities that break the prayer entirely.

Core Idea
Sujud al-sahw compensates for adding a similar action or omitting a wajib in prayer.

Sujud al-Sahw is a prescribed prostration to correct forgetfulness during prayer. This lesson outlines when it is required, how to perform it, and the rulings concerning the imam and follower, based on the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of forgetfulness
Determine whether the mistake involved adding an action of the same type (e.g., repeating a rukn or adding a rak'ah) or omitting a wajib (e.g., Fatihah, first sitting, qunut).
These are the only triggers for sujud al-sahw in the Hanafi school.
2
Learn the correct procedure
After the final tashahhud, perform two prostrations (sajdatayn), say salam, then repeat tashahhud and salam again.
The double salam is a distinctive Hanafi practice.
3
Understand the imam-follower relationship
If the imam forgets, both imam and follower must perform sujud al-sahw. If the imam does not, the follower also does not. The follower's own forgetfulness does not require sujud from either.
The follower's sujud is linked to the imam's forgetfulness, not his own.
4
Apply the ruling for the first sitting
If one forgets the first tashahhud and remembers before fully standing, return to sitting. If closer to standing (i.e., after straightening), continue and perform sujud al-sahw at the end.
The criterion is physical proximity to sitting or standing.
5
Apply the ruling for the last sitting
If one forgets the last sitting and stands for a fifth rak'ah in a four-rak'ah prayer, return to sitting as long as prostration for the fifth has not occurred, skip the fifth, and perform sujud al-sahw.
The extra rak'ah is abandoned, and sujud al-sahw compensates for the added action.

Prostration of Forgetfulness (Sujud al-Sahw)
Question: If a praying person forgets in his prayer, what should he do?
Answer: If a praying person forgets in his prayer, and adds an action of the same type, for example: repeating its bowing, or adding a rak'ah, or omits a wajib action, such as leaving the recitation of Surat al-Fatihah or a surah after it, or leaving the first sitting, or one of the two tashahhuds, or leaving the qunut in witr, or the takbirs of the two 'Eids, or the imam reciting aloud where he should be quiet, or quietly where he should recite aloud, he performs two prostrations for forgetfulness after the final tashahhud, then says salam, then recites tashahhud again, then says salam.
Question: Is sujud al-sahw obligatory upon the followers due to the forgetfulness of their imam? Answer: The forgetfulness of the imam makes sujud obligatory upon both the imam and the follower.
Question: If the imam does not perform sujud, what should the follower do? Answer: If the imam does not perform sujud, the follower also does not perform sujud.
Question: If the follower forgets, is sujud obligatory upon him? Answer: Sujud is not obligatory upon him nor upon his imam.
Question: If someone forgets the first sitting in a four-rak'ah or three-rak'ah prayer and then remembers, how should he act? Answer: He looks at his situation: if he is closer to sitting, he returns to sitting, recites tashahhud, completes his remaining prayer, and there is no sujud al-sahw upon him; if he is closer to standing, he does not return, continues his prayer, and performs sujud al-sahw after the final tashahhud and says salam.
Question: If he forgets the last sitting, what should he do? Answer: If he forgets the last sitting in a four-rak'ah prayer and stands for the fifth, he returns to sitting as long as he has not prostrated, leaves the fifth rak'ah, and performs sujud al-sahw.
Then he says salam: and he recites salawat upon the Prophet and supplication in the final sitting in the chosen view, and it is said: in both (sittings) as a precaution.

What Necessitates Sajdah al-Sahw and Its Performance
Lists causes and procedure of sajdah al-sahw

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the causes of sajdah al-sahw and describe its correct performance according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sajdah al-sahw compensates for unintentional additions or omissions of wajib acts in prayer.

In this lesson, we learn the cases when a worshiper must perform extra prostrations to compensate for forgetfulness during prayer. The Hanafi school details specific triggers—adding an act of the same type or omitting a wajib—and prescribes a precise sequence: two prostrations after the final tashahhud, followed by salam, a second tashahhud, and final salam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger
Determine whether the forgetfulness involved an addition (e.g., repeating a rukn, adding a rak'ah) or an omission of a wajib act (e.g., Fatiha, surah, first sitting, tashahhud, qunoot, Eid takbeers, imam's loud/silent recitation). Both triggers necessitate sajdah al-sahw.
The omission must be of a wajib, not a sunnah or fard. Omitting a fard invalidates the prayer entirely.
2
Confirm the error is unintentional
Sajdah al-sahw is only for unintentional forgetfulness (sahw). If the act is done intentionally, the ruling differs and may invalidate the prayer.
Intentional addition or omission without valid reason nullifies the prayer.
3
Execute the compensation
After completing the last tashahhud, perform two prostrations, then say salam. Then recite tashahhud a second time and say salam again. This sequence is wajib.
The second tashahhud and salam are necessary to correct the order disturbed by the extra prostrations after salam.
4
Recognize the wisdom
Sajdah al-sahw restores the prayer's completeness without requiring repetition, demonstrating the school's practical approach to human forgetfulness.

The follower’s sajdah depends on the imam’s forgetfulness, not his own.

This lesson presents three key rulings on sajdah al-sahw in congregational prayer according to the Hanafi school. Understanding these rules ensures that you correctly follow the imam and know when to perform the prostration of forgetfulness.
