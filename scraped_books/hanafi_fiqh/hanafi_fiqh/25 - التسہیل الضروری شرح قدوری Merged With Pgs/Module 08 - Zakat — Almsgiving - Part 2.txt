Core Idea
Zakat is on the nisab, not the surplus; loss of surplus does not reduce the zakat obligation.

When a person owns livestock that exceeds the nisab, the excess ('afw) is not subject to zakat. But what happens if some animals die after the year completes? Do you still pay zakat on the remaining nisab in full, or is it reduced proportionally? This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute between the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and the two imams (Muhammad and Zufar), with clear examples from camels and sheep.

Guided Path

What is the scenario? The nisab remains but the 'afw perishes after the year. The question: is zakat due on the remaining nisab in full or reduced proportionally?
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold zakat is on nisab only, so full zakat remains. Muhammad and Zufar hold zakat is on the entire herd, so it reduces proportionally.
3

9 camels (nisab 5, 'afw 4), 4 perish. According to Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf, zakat is a full sheep. According to Muhammad/Zufar, it is 5/9 of a sheep.

The practical difference is the amount of zakat due. The dispute hinges on whether zakat is attached to the nisab alone or distributed across the entire herd.

زكاة الزروع والثمار
السؤال: هل في الزروع والثمار زكاة؟
الجواب: نعم فيها زكاة، فيخرج عشر ما خرج، أو نصف العشر، حسب اختلاف المياه التي تسقى بها.
السؤال: هل في ذلك نصاب ؟
الجواب: لا نصاب في ذلك عند أبي حنيفة ، فكل ما أخرجته الأرض قليلا كان أو كثيرا تجب فيه الزكاة إلا الحطب والقصب والحشيش، فإنه لا زكاة فيها عنده وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد مما لا تجب الزكاة في الخارج من الأرض إلا فيما له ثمرة باقية، بشرط أن تبلغ خمسة أوسق، وليس في الخضروات عندهما صدقة.
السؤال: الوسق ما هو ؟
الجواب هو مكيال أهل المدينة وكان يسع ستين صاعا.
السؤال: ما التفصيل في وجوب العشر ونصفه ؟ الجواب: إذا سقي سيحا أو سقته السماء ففيه العشر، وإذا سقي بغرب أو دالية أو را سانية ففيه نصف العشر على القولين.
السؤال: إذا كان الخارج مما لا يوسق أي لا يكال بالأوسق كالزعفران والقطن، فما قول الصاحبين في وجوب الزكاة فيه؟
الجواب: قال أبو يوسف له : إذا بلغت قيمته قيمة خمسة أوسق من أدنى ما يدخل تحت الوسق، يجب فيه العشر.
وقال محمد ه: يجب العشر إذا بلغ الخارج خمسة أمثال من أعلى ما يقدر به نوعه، فاعتبر في القطن خمسة أحمال ، وفي الزعفران خمسة أَمْنَاء.
السؤال: هل يجب العشر في العسل؟
الجواب : إذا أخذ العسل من أرض العشر يجب فيه العشر قلّل أو كثر عند أبي حنيفة محله، وقال أبو يوسف : لا شيء فيه حتى تبلغ عشرة أزقاق، وقال محمد لله: لا شيء فيه حتى تبلغ خمسة أفراق.
السؤال: الفرق كم مقداره؟
الجواب: هو ستة وثلاثين رطلا بالعراقي.
السؤال: إذا خرج شيء من أرض الخراج، هل يجب فيه العشر؟
الجواب: لا يجب فيه العشر، بل يكتفى فيه بالخراج. السؤال: هل ترفع المؤن كأجرة العمال ونفقات البقر وغير ذلك قبل أداء العشر أو نصفه؟ الجواب: المؤن لا ترفع، بل يجب إخراج الواجب من جميع ما أخرجته الأرض، عشرا كان أو نصفه.

The Book of Zakat
Zakat of Crops and Fruits
(The completion of the year, so on the remainder there is a complete sheep according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, may they have reward, and according to them (the two) half a sheep, and if sixty of them perish, then according to the two Shaykhs half a sheep, and according to them a quarter of a sheep, so reflect.)
Zakat of Crops and Fruits
Question: Is there zakat on crops and fruits?
Answer: Yes, there is zakat on them. One-tenth of what comes out is paid, or half of one-tenth, according to the difference of the waters with which they are irrigated.
Question: Is there a nisab (minimum threshold) for that?
Answer: According to Abu Hanifa, there is no nisab for that. So whatever the earth produces, whether little or much, zakat is obligatory on it, except for firewood, reeds, and grass, for there is no zakat on them according to him. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: Zakat is not obligatory on what comes out of the earth except for that which has lasting fruit, on condition that it reaches five awsuq (plural of wasq), and there is no sadaqah on vegetables according to them.
Question: What is the wasq?
Answer: It is a measure of the people of Madinah, and it holds sixty sa'.
Question: What is the detail regarding the obligation of the tenth and half of it?
Answer: If it is irrigated by a channel (sayhan) or by rain (sama'), then there is the tenth. If it is irrigated by a bucket (gharb) or a waterwheel (daliyah) or a noria (ra'saniyah), then there is half of the tenth according to the two opinions.
Question: If the produce is not measured by awsuq, such as saffron and cotton, what is the statement of the two companions regarding the obligation of zakat on it?
Answer: Abu Yusuf said: When its value reaches the value of five awsuq of the lowest of what comes under the wasq, the tenth is obligatory on it. Muhammad said: The tenth is obligatory when the produce reaches five times of the highest of what is measured by its type. So he considered for cotton five loads (ahmal), and for saffron five amna' (plural of mann).
Question: Is the tenth obligatory on honey?
Answer: If honey is taken from the land of tithe (ard al-'ushr), the tenth is obligatory on it, whether little or much, according to Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him. Abu Yusuf said: Nothing is due on it until it reaches ten jars (azqiq). Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on him, said: Nothing is due on it until it reaches five afraq (plural of farq).
Question: What is the measure of a farq?
Answer: It is thirty-six ratls (pounds) by the Iraqi measure.
Question: If something comes out from kharaj land, is the tenth obligatory on it?
Answer: The tenth is not obligatory on it; rather the kharaj suffices for it.
Question: Are expenses such as wages of workers, expenditures on oxen, and others deducted before paying the tenth or half of it?
Answer: Expenses are not deducted; rather the obligatory amount must be paid from all that the earth produces, whether tenth or half of it.
Question: Explain the expenditures of zakat?
The Expenditures of Zakat
(Amna': it is the plural of mann - with a hamzated lam - not the plural of man. It is said in al-Qamus: al-mann is a known measure, or a scale, or two ratls, al-manna' (plural?) amna'. Amnan like al-manna and the plural and the plural of feminine: It is said in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (2/256) that the wages of workers, expenditure on oxen, digging of canals, wages of a guard, etc. are not deducted because the Prophet ruled the difference in the obligatory due according to the difference in expense.)

Commentary
This passage from the book of zakat covers the rulings on zakat of crops and fruits, including intra-Hanafi differences. The text begins with a note about a ruling on livestock (possibly a marginal gloss) but the main topic begins with the second heading. The Q&A format clarifies key issues. Abu Hanifa holds that there is no nisab for crops; any amount is subject to ushr except for firewood, reeds, and grass. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, require a nisab of five awsuq (approximately 653 kg) and limit zakat to produce with lasting fruit (excluding vegetables). The rate depends on irrigation: rain or channel water gives full ushr (10%), while artificial irrigation (bucket, waterwheel) gives half ushr (5%). For items not measured by volume (like cotton, saffron), Sahibayn differ: Abu Yusuf uses value equivalent to five awsuq of the cheapest item, while Muhammad measures by weight/load specific to the item. For honey from tithe land, Abu Hanifa again imposes ushr regardless of quantity; Sahibayn set thresholds of ten jars (Abu Yusuf) or five afraq (Muhammad). Kharaj land pays kharaj, not ushr. Crucially, production expenses (labor, oxen, irrigation costs) are not deductible before paying ushr, contrary to modern accounting. The passage ends with a brief and incomplete discussion of zakat expenditures, quoting al-Bahr al-Ra'iq that the Prophet's ruling on varying rates by irrigation method implies that expenses are not considered. The key takeaway is the significant difference between Abu Hanifa and his students on nisab and thresholds, which affects practical zakat calculations.

Key Terms
العشر (al-'ushr): One-tenth of the produce, obligatory as zakat on rain-fed or channel-irrigated crops.
نصاب (nisab): Minimum threshold for zakat obligation; for crops, Abu Hanifa says none, Sahibayn require 5 awsuq.
وسق (wasq): A measure of volume equal to 60 sa' (approximately 130 kg or 653 kg depending on item).
خراج (kharaj): Land tax paid on kharaj lands; it replaces ushr for crops grown on such land.

Expenses Not Deducted Before Zakat on Crops
Ruling that expenses are not subtracted before paying 'ushr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that farming expenses are not deducted before paying 'ushr (zakat on crops) and explain the Prophetic rationale cited in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Core Idea
Expenses do not reduce zakat on crops; 'ushr is owed on the entire harvest.

When calculating zakat on agricultural produce, a common question is whether costs like wages, irrigation, and feed can be subtracted. The Hanafi school firmly holds that no such deductions are allowed. This lesson examines the ruling, its textual basis, and its practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat on crops ('ushr or half-‘ushr). The ruling is that expenses (mu'un) are not deductible.
2
Understand the deduction issue
Expenses include wages of workers, cattle feed, digging canals, and guard wages. The question is whether these reduce the gharable produce.
3
State the Hanafi position
The answer is clear: expenses are not deducted; zakat is obligatory on the entire produce harvested.
4
Note the rationale from the Sunnah
The Prophet ﷺ made the rate vary with the mode of irrigation, not with costs. This implies that expenses are already considered in the rate difference, so separate deduction is not allowed.
Reference from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 'because the Prophet ruled that the wajib varies with the expense'.
5
Recognize the authority
This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school, as stated in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and other major works.

Many Muslims assume zakat is only for the poor, but the Quran specifies eight categories. This lesson unpacks each category from the verse and provides clear Hanafi definitions. Mastering these categories is essential for correct zakat disbursement.

Giving Zakat to Spouse: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on giving zakat to spouse with intra-Hanafi disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on giving zakat to one's spouse and identify the operative ruling (mufta bihi) for each direction.

Core Idea
Zakat to a spouse: Abu Hanifa prohibits both ways; Sahibayn allow wife to husband but not vice versa.

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the permissibility of giving zakat to one's spouse. While the classical Hanafi position according to Imam Abu Hanifa forbids zakat to a spouse in either direction, his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, made a distinction: they permit a wife to give zakat to her husband but maintain the prohibition for a husband giving to his wife. Understanding the underlying reasoning (illa) of maintenance obligations will help you navigate this nuanced ruling and apply the mufta bihi (operative fatwa) in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa
Identify the general rule: Abu Hanifa holds that giving zakat to one's spouse (husband to wife or wife to husband) does not discharge zakat. This is because spouses benefit from each other's wealth, so giving zakat to a spouse is like giving to oneself.
The illa is the mutual maintenance right (nafaqah al-zawjiyah).
2
State the ruling of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani distinguish between directions: a wife giving zakat to her husband is valid, but a husband giving to his wife is invalid. Their reasoning is that a wife is not obligated to spend on her husband, so there is no circular benefit.
The opposing school here is within the Hanafi school—the Sahibayn.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (illa)
Understand why the rulings differ: Abu Hanifa sees spousal benefit as mutual and reciprocal, while the Sahibayn see it as asymmetrical (the husband is required to support the wife, not the other way around). This leads to different conclusions.
The illa for Abu Hanifa is the general spousal benefit; for Sahibayn it is the specific obligation of maintenance.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is that in the Hanafi school, a woman may give her zakat to her poor husband (per Sahibayn), while a man may not give zakat to his poor wife (per Abu Hanifa).
The mufta bihi often follows Sahibayn for the wife's case, and Abu Hanifa for the husband's case.
5
Determine the mufta bihi for each scenario
Later Hanafi jurists generally follow Abu Hanifa for the husband's case (invalid) and adopt the Sahibayn's view for the wife's case (valid) as the operative fatwa. This is the position for practice.
Refer to works like al-Kasani's Bada'i' al-Sana'i' and Ibn Abidin's Radd al-Muhtar.

Ruling on permissible and impermissible game death due to secondary causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game that dies from a secondary cause after being shot, and apply the condition that direct death from the shot is required for halal.

Core Idea
If game dies from drowning or fall, it is haram; only direct death from the shot is halal.

This passage presents two critical scenarios that every hunter must know: when does a shot animal become unlawful to eat? The answer hinges on the cause of death. The Hanafi school draws a clear line: if the animal dies from a secondary cause like water or a fall, the meat is forbidden. This lesson will help you internalize this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two situations: (1) game falls into water and is found dead, or falls from a height and dies, and (2) game falls directly to the ground after being shot and dies.
2
State the ruling for each
For scenario (1), the answer is haram. For scenario (2), the answer is halal.
3
Determine the underlying condition
The decisive factor is whether the death was caused by the hunter's shot or by a subsequent accident. If the shot itself caused death (direct hit, animal dies on the spot), it is halal. If an intervening cause (water, fall) caused death, it is haram.
This is a condition (shart) for halal consumption.
4
Apply the rule to new cases

Ruling on a Vow to Give All Wealth
Ruling with proof using qiyas and istihsan on a vow to give all wealth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a vow to give all one's wealth and distinguish when only zakatable assets are required versus all wealth.

Core Idea
A vow to give all wealth is restricted to zakatable assets unless the vow explicitly intends everything.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person vows to give charity with all his wealth. The initial analogy (qiyas) would suggest he must give everything, but the Hanafi school employs istihsan (juristic preference) to limit the obligation to zakatable categories. However, if he explicitly intends all his wealth, his intention is honoured, and the entire wealth becomes wajib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the initial ruling
The passage deals with a vow (nadhr) to give charity with 'my wealth' (bimalihi). The initial answer states he must give the type of wealth on which zakat is obligatory (jins ma tajibu fihi al-zakat).
2
Understand the qiyas argument
By strict analogy (qiyas), one would think he must give all his wealth, because 'mal' literally means everything he owns. This is the apparent implication of the wording.
3
Apply istihsan to override qiyas
Istihsan (juristic preference) overrides qiyas: vows are interpreted in light of their underlying purpose, which is similar to the obligatory charity (zakat). Zakat is only due on specific categories and amounts, so the vow is limited to those categories: gold, silver, trade goods, and grazing livestock.
The rule is: al-nudhur tuhmalu 'ala usuliha fi al-furud (vows are interpreted according to their origins in obligations).
4
Note the exception for explicit intention
If the person, by his vow, intended all his wealth (e.g., by saying 'all my mal' or explicitly intending everything), then all of it becomes wajib because he has voluntarily intensified the obligation (shaddada 'ala nafsih).
This is an important practical nuance: the default is limited, but intention can expand the obligation.

Classification of Ushri and Kharaji Land and Fixed Kharaj Rates
Classification and fixed rates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify land as ushri or kharaji based on water source and state the standard kharaj rates set by Caliph Umar for the Sawad region.

Core Idea
Land irrigated by public rivers is ushri; land irrigated by Persian canals is kharaji—two distinct categories with fixed tax rates.

The Hanafi school distinguishes land by its water source: land watered by rivers no one owns (like the Tigris and Euphrates) is ushri, while land revived using man-made Persian canals is kharaji. Caliph Umar fixed kharaj rates per jarib for different crops, providing a clear basis for the land tax system.

Guided Path
1
Identify the water source
Determine whether the irrigation comes from a natural river (public) or a man-made canal (dug by Persians). This classifies the land as ushri or kharaji.
The source text mentions 'great rivers no one owns' and 'rivers dug by Persians like Nahr al-Malik'.
2
Recall the Umaric kharaj rates
For arable land reached by water: 1 qafiz Hashimi (one sa' and one dirham) per jarib. For alfalfa: 5 dirhams per jarib. For continuous grapevines or date palms: 10 dirhams per jarib. Other crops are assessed by capacity.
These rates are from the practice of Caliph Umar on the Sawad lands.
3
Apply the reduction rule
If the land cannot bear the imposed kharaj, the ruler (Imam) may reduce it to a suitable amount.
This is based on the principle of 'capacity' (taqah).

Rules of Kharaj: Continuity, Drop, and Non-Combination with Ushr
Continuity and conditions of kharaj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when kharaj drops, when it continues despite ownership change, and why it does not combine with ushr.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a fixed annual land tax: it drops with natural disaster, remains due on intentional idleness, and never combines with ushr on the same land.

The Hanafi school provides clear rules on how kharaj behaves under various circumstances. Natural disasters cancel kharaj for that year, but deliberate neglect does not. Conversion to Islam or sale to a Muslim does not change the land's tax status. Crucially, ushr and kharaj cannot both be levied on the same land—a point of difference with the Shafi'i school. Additionally, kharaj is not repeated with multiple harvests in a single year.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario for kharaj drop
Kharaj drops if the land is overwhelmed by water, or water is cut off, or a blight destroys the crop. These are acts of God.
The source mentions 'water overwhelmed', 'water cut off', and 'blight' as examples.
2
Contrast with deliberate idleness
If the owner intentionally leaves the land idle and it does not grow, kharaj remains due. This prevents tax evasion.
The question explicitly asks about 'idling' (ta'teel).
3
Apply the rule on ownership change
Whether the owner converts to Islam or sells to a Muslim, the land remains kharaji. The tax continues unchanged.
This shows kharaj is attached to the land, not the owner's faith.
4
Apply the non-combination rule
No ushr is due on produce from kharaji land. The two taxes cannot coexist on the same land.
The text explicitly says 'la jam'a bayna al-wazifatayn'.
5
Recall the non-repetition rule
Kharaj is not repeated with each harvest in the same year; it is a fixed annual tax. Ushr, however, applies to every harvest.
The reason given: Umar did not impose it repeatedly.

A kharaji landowner's crop is destroyed by a locust plague. Which of the following is correct according to the Hanafi school?
A) Kharaj is still due because the owner could have prevented it.
B) Kharaj is dropped for that year because it is a natural disaster.
C) Kharaj must be paid from other income of the owner.
D) The land becomes ushri due to the loss.

Key Takeaway
Kharaj drops with natural disasters but remains due on deliberate idleness; conversion to Islam does not change the land's tax status; and kharaj and ushr never combine on the same land.
