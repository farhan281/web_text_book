The base number is the LCM of the share denominators, ensuring all shares become whole-number fractions.

In Hanafi inheritance, the estate is divided into portions based on fixed shares (fara'id) and residue (asabah). The first step is to determine the base number (asl al-mas'alah), which is the least common multiple of the denominators of all fixed shares present. This lesson walks you through the standard combinations and their corresponding base numbers, as laid out by the classical jurists.

Find the least common multiple of the denominators. This is your base number.
For common pairs like 1/2+1/2, LCM=2; 1/2+1/3, LCM=6; etc.
3

The Doctrine of 'Awl: Adjusting the Base When Shares Exceed the Estate
Definition and classification of 'awl in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'awl, list the seven inheritance base numbers, identify which bases undergo 'awl, and state the possible 'awl values.

Core Idea
When fixed shares exceed the total estate, 'awl increases the denominator to proportionally reduce each share.

In inheritance calculations, the Quranic shares (furus) are fractions of the estate. Sometimes the sum of these fractions exceeds 1 (the whole estate). The Hanafi school, following the practice of Caliph 'Umar (RA), introduced the principle of 'awl (increase) to adjust the denominator so that the estate can be distributed fairly. This lesson explains the seven possible denominators and which ones can undergo 'awl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
When the sum of prescribed shares (furus) is greater than 1, the base number (makhraj) is too small to allocate full shares. The estate must be divided proportionally.
This is called a 'a'ilah (deficient) problem.
2
Define 'awl
'Awl means increasing the denominator to a larger number while keeping the numerators unchanged. The shares become smaller fractions of the estate, but each heir receives their prescribed proportion.
The term 'awl literally means 'to rise' or 'increase'.
3
Recall the seven denominators
The possible denominators in inheritance problems are derived from the Quranic shares: 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24.
These are the bases that appear from the fractions 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8, 1/12, and 1/24.
4
Distinguish bases that 'awl from those that do not
Bases 2, 3, 4, and 8 never require 'awl because their complements (the remaining shares) always fit. Bases 6, 12, and 24 can be insufficient and thus undergo 'awl.
The rule is empirical: combinations of shares from these bases may exceed 1.
5
Memorize the possible 'awl values
Base 6 can 'awl to 7, 8, 9, or 10. Base 12 can 'awl to 13, 15, or 17. Base 24 can only 'awl to 27.
These specific numbers are derived from practice; they cover all possible combinations of shares.

Correcting the Base Number in Inheritance (Tashih al-Mas'alah)
Procedural explanation of arithmetic correction in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four arithmetic relations (tamathul, tadakhul, tawafuq, tabayun) to determine the multiplier and compute the corrected base number for both non-‘awl and ‘awl cases.

Core Idea
Multiply heads (or their wafq) by the base to make shares integers.

This passage from the Faraid chapter introduces the essential procedure of tashih al-mas'alah – correcting the base number when inheritance shares of a group do not distribute as whole numbers. Mastering the four arithmetic relations between the number of heads and their shares is the key to correctly adjusting the base, ensuring that each heir receives an integer share.

Guided Path
1
Learn the four relations
Memorize tamathul (equality), tadakhul (overlap), tawafuq (compatibility), and tabayun (disparity). These determine the multiplication factor.
The relation is between the number of heads in a group and the number of shares assigned to that group.
2
Identify the relation in a given case
Compute the relation between heads and shares. For example, 2 heads and 3 shares: 2 and 3 are tabayun (coprime). 6 heads and 3 shares: 6 and 3 have tadakhul (6 divisible by 3) or tawafuq, depending on interpretation – the author uses tawafuq when a common divisor exists.
In Hanafi faraid, the relation is categorized strictly: tadakhul when one divides the other completely; tawafuq when both share a divisor but neither divides the other.
3
Choose the multiplier
If tabayun, multiply the full number of heads by the base (or its ‘awl). If tawafuq, multiply only the wafq (the reduced number: heads divided by the common divisor). If tadakhul, the larger is a multiple of the smaller; treat as tabayun with the smaller? Actually tadakhul is not used for correction; it appears when heads and shares are in tadakhul, the shares divide evenly? The passage does not use tadakhul in correction examples; only tabayun and tawafuq are mentioned. So focus on those.
For tadakhul, the shares already divide? Not explicitly discussed. The examples only cover tabayun and tawafuq.
4
Multiply and distribute
Multiply the chosen multiplier (heads or wafq) by the original base (or its ‘awl if it increased). The product is the new base (sahih al-mas'alah). Assign shares accordingly.
In the wife+two brothers example, tabayun: multiply 2 (heads) by base 4 → new base 8. Wife gets 2, brothers get 6.
5
Verify the result
Check that all shares become integers. If not, re-evaluate the relation or the multiplier.
The corrected base ensures each heir receives a whole number of shares.

A woman and six brothers: the woman gets a quarter, the brothers get three-quarters – i.e., three shares, which are not divisible among them, so multiplication is needed. If you multiply one-third of their number, i.e., two (which we call the agreement (wafq) of their number), by the base number (four), you get eight, and the problem becomes correct: the woman takes two, and the six brothers take one each. This example clarifies correction of a non-aul case. As for when the problem is aul (the sum of shares exceeds the base), an example of tabayun (mutual primality) is when the estate is divided among a husband and three full sisters: the base is six and it auls to seven, and the problem becomes correct from twenty-one. As for when the problem is aul and involves tawafuq (common factor) and needs multiplication by the aul, an example: a woman dies leaving a husband, parents, and six daughters. The base is twelve: the husband gets a quarter, i.e., three; the parents get two sixths, i.e., four; the six daughters get two-thirds, i.e., eight. The sum exceeds the base, and it auls to fifteen. The daughters' shares (eight) break on their head count. Between the head count and shares there is a common factor of half, so we reduce the head count by half to three, then multiply it by the aul (fifteen), resulting in forty-five. From that the problem becomes correct: the husband takes nine shares, the parents take twelve, and the daughters take twenty-four (four for each). Thus the distribution is sound. Question: What you have mentioned teaches us the cases of correction when shares break on one group. If they break on two or more groups, how is the knot unraveled? Answer: If shares break on two or more groups, multiply one group's head count by the other, then multiply the product by the third group's head count, then multiply that by the base number. This is when the head counts are mutually prime (tabayun), as when a man dies leaving two wives, five grandmothers, three uterine brothers, and a paternal uncle: the base is twelve – the two wives get a quarter (three), the grandmothers get a sixth (two), the uterine brothers get a third (four), and the uncle gets the remainder (three). Shares break on the two wives, the grandmothers, and the brothers. Multiply the number of wives (two) by the number of grandmothers (five), giving ten; then multiply ten by the number of brothers (three), giving thirty; then multiply thirty by the base (twelve), giving three hundred sixty. From that the problem becomes correct. If the numbers are equal (tamathul), one suffices for the other, as when someone leaves two wives and two brothers: the base is four – the two wives get a quarter (one share), and the two brothers take the remaining three. Shares break on each group. If you want to correct the problem, multiply two (the number of one group) by the base (four), giving eight. From that the problem becomes correct: the two wives take a quarter, i.e., two (one each), and the two brothers take six (three each). If one number is a part of the other (tadakhul), i.e., the smaller is included in the larger, as with four wives and two brothers – multiplying the four suffices for the other. If one number agrees with the other (tawafuq), you multiply the agreement fraction (wafq) of one by the whole of the other, then the product by the base. For example, four wives, a sister, and six uncles: six and four agree by half, so multiply half of one by the whole of the other, then the product by the base.

Key Terms
تصحيح المسألة (tas-hih al-mas'ala): The process of adjusting the base number so that shares become integers for all heirs.
وفق (wafq): The number obtained by dividing the head count by the common factor between heads and shares (or between two head counts).
عول (awl): Increase of the base number when the sum of prescribed shares exceeds the original base.

Tas-hih al-Mas'ala: Multiple Groups
Second part answering the question about multiple groups, explaining the combining rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of tas-hih when shares break on two or more groups, including handling all four numerical relationships: tabayun, tamathul, tadakhul, and tawafuq.

Core Idea
For multiple groups, combine head counts using the appropriate relationship rules before multiplying by the base.

When more than one group of heirs has indivisible shares, the correction process becomes more complex. This lesson explains the general rule given in the text: multiply the head counts of the groups together, but first reduce them using the relationship types (tabayun, tamathul, tadakhul, tawafuq) to avoid overcorrection. The method ensures a single corrected base that works for all groups.

Guided Path
1
Identify all groups with broken shares
List each group where the shares assigned to that group are not divisible by the head count. Note the head count for each group.
e.g., in the example: wives (2 heads), grandmothers (5), uterine brothers (3).
2
Determine the relationship between each pair of head counts
For multiple groups, combine their head counts sequentially. Check each pair for: tabayun (MP), tamathul (equal), tadakhul (one divides the other), or tawafuq (common factor).
In the example, all pairs are tabayun: 2-5, 2-3, 5-3.
3
Combine head counts according to their relationship
If tabayun: multiply the two numbers. If tamathul: use one of them. If tadakhul: use the larger. If tawafuq: multiply the wafq of one by the whole of the other (or vice versa).
Use these rules sequentially for each group added. Example: 2×5=10, then 10×3=30.
4
Multiply the combined head count by the base (or aul)
Take the final product from step 3 and multiply it by the original base (or the aul if the case is aul) to get the corrected base.
Example: 30 × 12 = 360.
5
Distribute using the new base
Assign each group their original fraction of the new base, then divide each group's total within the group by its head count to get integer shares per head.
Wives: 3/12 of 360 = 90, per wife 45; grandmothers: 2/12 of 360 = 60, per grandmother 12; brothers: 4/12 of 360 = 120, per brother 40; uncle: 60.

Munasakha Calculation: From Shares to Dirhams
Faraid calculation example with conversion method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the final shares for heirs in a munasakha case by combining estates and converting to monetary amounts.

Core Idea
Munasakha combines estates; after correct division, divide by base to get each heir's share value.

This lesson walks through a munasakha example where shares from two deceased persons are combined. The passage gives specific numbers (8, 4, 3, 6, 3) that sum to 48, and then explains how to compute each heir's portion in real currency. Focus is on the computational step: divide the total solved estate by the common base (48) to get the value per share.

Guided Path
1
Identify all heirs from the two deaths
Note each heir mentioned: an unlabeled heir with 8 shares, the brother of the second wife with 4, the second living wife with 3, the father of the second deceased with 6, and his mother with 3. These are post-munasakha shares.
The unlabeled heir (8 shares) may be a son from the first marriage.
2
Recognize that the shares sum to a common base
Add the shares: 8 + 4 + 3 + 6 + 3 = 48. This is the base number (asl al-mas'alah) for the final estate distribution.
Ensure no additional heirs are missing; the total must match the estate's solved base.
3
Convert shares to dirhams using the base
After the munasakha is solved, to find each heir's monetary portion, divide the total estate value (in dirhams) by 48, then multiply by each heir's share count.
The text says 'divide the solved case’s total by forty-eight; whatever comes out, you take the dirhams' – meaning each share of 48 corresponds to 1/48 of the estate.
4
Apply the method to any munasakha
Generalize: identify heirs, assign shares from each deceased, combine using a common base, then divide estate value by that base to get per-share value.
This method works for any correct munasakha solution.
