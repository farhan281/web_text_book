Hanafi Legal Maxims on Qisas, Diyat, and Liability
Compilation of Hanafi fiqh maxims on damages, qisas, and diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key Hanafi legal maxims governing qisas, diyat, and liability, and identify their application in criminal and tort cases.

Core Idea
Hanafi maxims on damages and retaliation: qisas requires equivalence, liability follows causation, and fixed amounts are divinely set.

This passage from the Hidayah compiles over twenty legal maxims from the chapters on offenses (jinayat) and diyat. Each maxim encapsulates a ruling without proof, forming the foundation for Hanafi criminal and tort law. Understanding these qawa'id is essential for consistent legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of each maxim
Group the maxims by topic: qisas, diyat, liability, evidence, or procedure. Note any chapter headings.
Maxims 43-49 deal primarily with qisas and diyat; 50-52 with evidence; 53-54 with fixed quantities; 55-56 with compensation and acknowledgment; 57-59 and 20-22 with liability principles.
2
State the ruling in plain language
Paraphrase each maxim to capture its legal effect. Focus on the operative condition or outcome.
E.g., maxim 45: 'Qisas requires that the victim and offender have equal legal protection (isma).'
3
Note any intra-Hanafi disagreement
For maxims 42 and 49, record the differing views of Zufar and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
Maxim 42: Zufar holds price decrease reduces debt; Abu Hanifa says no. Maxim 49: Abu Hanifa says qisas right belongs to caliph; companions say to heirs.
4
Consider the practical consequence
Apply each maxim to a hypothetical scenario to see its real-world effect.
E.g., maxim 43: If a person unintentionally kills another, the 'a'qila pays diyah, not the killer individually.

Commentary
This passage is a collection of legal maxims (qawa'id) from various chapters of the Hidayah, primarily from the Book of Jinayat (Offenses) and related topics. Each maxim states a ruling without explicit proof, as is typical of a matn. The numbering (42)-(62) indicates continuity from earlier pages. The first maxim (42) from the chapter on pledges (rahn) states that a decrease in the market price of a pledged item does not reduce the debt owed, contrary to Zufar. (43) establishes that diyah (blood-money) for killing is initially on the 'a'qila (the offender's tribal group). (44) treats the whole body as a single place regarding liability. (45) states that qisas (retaliation) requires equality in the protection of life (isma). (46) mandates qisas only when exact equivalence is possible. (47) says that if an act produces multiple effects, it is considered multiple acts. (48) requires combining wounds for retaliation if possible. (49) is a dispute: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the right to demand qisas belongs to the caliph (ruler), while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it belongs to the heirs. (50) equates testimony with direct observation for legal purposes. (51) notes that an absolute term differs from a restricted one. (52) says the state of the act at the time of commission is what matters. (53) asserts that fixed quantities in diyah are based on revelation (tawqif). (54) mandates full diyah if a limb's entire benefit or intended beauty is lost. (55) states that benefits (manafi') are not compensable except by contract or shubha. (56) limits acknowledgment to the acknowledger alone. (57) prohibits liability based on doubt. (58) permits everyone to perform devotional acts. (59) reminds that using a public road is permissible only if it does not cause harm. (20-22) are general principles of liability: permissible acts can cause liability if they harm others; causation with transgression leads to liability; voluntary acts are attributed to the doer, not others.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('a'qila): The group of male agnatic relatives (asaba) of the offender who are liable to pay diyah in cases of unintentional killing or injury.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional killing or injury, subject to strict conditions of equivalence.
دية (diyah): Blood-money or monetary compensation paid for killing or injury, either as substitute for qisas or in cases where qisas is not applicable.

The Maxim: 'All of the Body is a Single Place' – Application in Qisas
Explanation of a legal maxim with an application example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'jami' al-badan ka al-mahall al-wahid' and apply it to determine when qisas is obligatory for an intentional attack that causes death.

Core Idea
Intentional attack on any part of the body, even if missing the target, triggers qisas because the whole body is one legal place.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi maxim from al-Hidayah: the entire human body is treated as a single legal location. This principle has important consequences for homicide rulings: if a person intentionally attacks one part of the body but hits another and causes death, the killing is still considered intentional murder (qatl 'amd). We will explore how this maxim is used to establish the obligation of qisas (retributive justice) in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and its Literal Meaning
Begin by reading the Arabic maxim: 'جَمِيعُ الْبَدَنِ كَالْمَحَلِّ الْوَاحِدِ'. Understand that it means the entire body is like a single place. Note that this is a legal fiction for the purpose of unification of rulings.
The maxim is a qa'ida fiqhiyya (legal maxim) specific to the law of retaliation.
2
Understand the Rationale (Ta'lil)
The commentator explains that because the body is one place (mahall wahid), the ruling for the whole applies to every part. Thus, intentional killing (qatl 'amd) is established if the attacker intended to hit any part, regardless of which part was actually hit.
The underlying principle: 'in one place, the whole and the part share the same ruling.'
3
Apply the Maxim to the Example
Consider the scenario from the text: a person aims an arrow at another's neck but misses and hits the eye, causing death. Under the maxim, the intention to kill the whole body is present because the neck is part of the body. Therefore, the killing is intentional (qatl 'amd) and qisas is obligatory.
This contrasts with a case where the act is purely accidental (e.g., no intention to hit any person).
4
Derive the Ruling (Hukm)
Since intention to kill a part equals intention to kill the whole, the ruling of qisas applies. The killer is liable to qisas (retaliation) because the death resulted from an intentional act directed at the victim's body.
The same principle extends to other bodily harms: any intentional injury to a part is treated as injury to the whole for purposes of diyah or qisas.
5
Connect to the Broader Principles of Jinayat
The passage also references the obligation of diyah (blood money) and expiation (kaffara) in related contexts. Understand that the maxim operates alongside other rules, such as the distinction between intentional and mistaken killing.
The later part of the passage (from Al-Binayah) reiterates that diyah is initially on the 'aqilah unless exceptions apply.

Commentary
This passage introduces a important legal maxim: 'All of the body is like a single place.' The commentator explains that this means the entire human body is treated as a single location for legal rulings. Consequently, the ruling that applies to the whole body (e.g., that intentional killing triggers qisas) also applies to any individual part of the body. The author of Hidayah uses this principle to address a scenario: if a person aims at one part of the body (like the neck) but hits another part (like the eye) and causes death, the killing is still considered intentional murder (qatl 'amd) because the intention was to target a part of the body, and the body is legally a single place. Thus, qisas becomes obligatory. The passage also includes a repetition of the earlier principle regarding diyah and expiation, likely as a cross-reference or a note from a commentary (Al-Binayah). This reinforces the idea that the initial obligation of diyah (when it arises from the act itself) lies on the 'aqilah, with exceptions as previously mentioned.

The Entire Body as a Single Legal Locus for Intent in Homicide
Maxim establishing the unity of the body for intent in homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that the whole body is one place to determine when qisas is wajib in cases of misaligned targeting.

Core Idea
Intent to kill any part of the body is intent to kill the whole — the entire body is one legal locus.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a foundational Hanafi principle for retaliation (qisas). It establishes that the human body is treated as a single unit for the purpose of intent. Understanding this maxim clarifies that if a killer misses the intended body part but still causes death, the killing remains intentional (qatl amd) and qisas is wajib. The commentary illustrates this with the example of aiming at the neck but hitting the eye, causing death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
State the Arabic maxim: 'جميع البدن كالمحل الواحد' (the entire body is like a single place). Understand its meaning: the whole body is one legal unit.
The maxim is from Al-Hidayah and is authoritative in the Hanafi school.
2
Examine the example
Consider the scenario: a person deliberately aims an arrow at another's neck but misses and hits the eye, causing death.
The key is that the act was intentional (qasd) and the victim died.
3
Apply the maxim to the example
Since the entire body is one place, intent to kill the neck is intent to kill the whole body. Therefore, hitting the eye is still intentional killing (qatl amd) because the eye is part of that single unit.
The maxim collapses the distinction between the intended part and the actual part.
4
Derive the ruling
Because qatl amd is established, qisas (retaliation) becomes wajib (obligatory). The ruling is the same as if the intended part had been hit.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that the whole and the part share the same ruling in one locus.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) differentiate between body parts; they may require that the specific intended part be hit for the killing to be considered amd. The Hanafi school rejects this distinction.
This illustrates a major practical difference in the application of qisas.

The Principle of Action Multiplying by Effect
States a legal maxim and applies it to homicide by a single arrow killing two

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-fi'l yata'addadu bi ta'addudi al-athar' to determine the legal rulings for multiple victims from a single act of killing.

Core Idea
A single act that kills two yields two rulings: qisas for the intended, diyah for the unintended.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim of the Hanafi school: the action multiplies by the multiplication of its effect. This maxim is not merely theoretical; it directly governs how we assign legal consequences to a single physical act that produces multiple distinct results. We will explore its meaning through the classical example from Al-Hidayah of a single arrow that passes through one victim and kills a second, each giving rise to a different legal ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its meaning
Read the principle: 'al-fi'l yata'addadu bi ta'addudi al-athar.' Understand that it means a single act is legally treated as multiple acts when its effects are multiple. The ruling on the act is based on each effect separately.
This is a qa'ida kulliya (universal maxim) in Hanafi fiqh, particularly applied in jinayat (homicide and injuries).
2
Recognize the scenario from Al-Hidayah
Consider the case: a man intentionally shoots an arrow at one person; the arrow passes through and kills a second person. Both die. One act of shooting, two deaths.
3
Differentiate intent for each victim
For the first victim, the shooting was intentional ('amd). For the second victim, the killing was unintended (khata') because the arrow passed through the first and hit the second without direct intent.
Intent is determined by the immediate target, not the ultimate trajectory.
4
Apply the maxim to derive two rulings
Since the act produces two effects (two deaths), and those effects differ in legal category (intentional vs. mistaken), the act is treated as two separate acts. For the first (intentional), the ruling is qisas (retaliation). For the second (mistaken), the ruling is diyah (blood money) payable by the 'aqilah (male agnates).
The maxim overrides the physical unity of the act in favor of legal multiplicity based on effects.
5
Conclude the legal consequence
Thus, the shooter is liable for qisas for the first victim and also liable for diyah for the second. The two liabilities coexist because the act is considered multiple in law.
Without this maxim, one might incorrectly argue only one crime occurred.

The Principle of Combining Wounds (Merging Jinayat)
States the rule for merging multiple wounds and lists four scenarios where merging is inapplicable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for merging multiple wounds (tadakhul al-jirahat) and apply it to scenarios where the acts differ in legal category.

Core Idea
Multiple wounds merge into one compensation only if all acts share the same legal category.

When multiple injuries are inflicted on the same person, Islamic law often seeks to combine them into a single obligation. This lesson introduces the Hanafi principle of combining wounds (al-jam' bayn al-jirahat). We will see when merging (tadakhul) is wajib and when it is not, as illustrated by four classic scenarios from al-Hidayah involving cutting off a hand and then killing the victim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Locate the rule: 'Combining wounds is obligatory as much as possible to complete the first.' Understand that merging is the default, but only under specific conditions.
The principle appears at the beginning of the passage.
2
Understand the Condition for Merging
Merging (tadakhul) requires that the acts be of the same legal category: both intentional ('amd) or both mistaken (khata'). If categories differ, merging is not possible.
The passage explicitly states this condition.
3
Examine the Four Scenarios
The text lists four logical combinations for cutting and killing: both mistaken, both intentional, killing mistaken and cutting intentional, or cutting mistaken and killing intentional.
These are derived by 'aqli division (qismah 'aqliyyah).
4
Determine Why Merging Fails
In the four scenarios discussed, the two acts always have different legal categories (one khata', one 'amd), so merging is not wajib. Separate compensation (daman) is due for each act.
The passage emphasizes the difference in ruling as the obstacle.
5
Identify the Practical Consequence
When merging fails, the offender must pay separate diyah (for mistaken harm) and qisas (for intentional killing), each according to its own rules.
This ensures justice for each distinct injury.

Combining Wounds in Hanafi Qisas: The Principle of al-Jam' bayn al-Jirahat
Stating the rule of combining multiple injuries when possible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of combining wounds (al-jam' bayn al-jirahat) and identify when combination is wajib versus when separate compensation is required, applying the conditions of same ruling and same place.

Core Idea
Combining wounds is wajib only when possible: same ruling and same place.

In Hanafi law of injuries (jinayat), when multiple injuries occur to the same person, the default is to combine them into a single compensation to complete the first. However, this principle applies only when the injuries share the same legal ruling (e.g., both intentional or both unintentional) and occur on the same limb. This passage from al-Hidayah states the rule and expands through detailed case classifications, showing how the principle operates across 16 possible scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The author opens with the rule: 'الجمع بين الجراحات واجب ما أمكن' — Combining wounds is obligatory as much as possible. The phrase 'ما أمكن' (as much as possible) is key: there is a condition of possibility.
The ruling is wajib, not fard, as it is based on a principle derived from the sources.
2
Understand the condition of possibility
Combination is possible only when two conditions are met: (1) the two acts have the same legal ruling (both 'amd (intentional) or both khata' (unintentional)), and (2) they affect the same place (mahall) on the body. When these conditions are absent, separate compensation is required.
The commentary explains that if the acts differ in ruling (e.g., one khata' and one 'amd), tadakhul (overlapping) is not possible.
3

The author lists cases: e.g., unintentionally cutting a hand then intentionally killing before healing; or after healing; or both intentional. In each, if the rulings differ, combination is not wajib; each act incurs its own daman.
The classification extends to eight aspects based on healing status, and to sixteen when considering one or two perpetrators.
4
Recognize the scope of the principle
The passage enumerates 16 possibilities by combining: type of act (intentional/unintentional) for cutting and killing, timing (before/after healing), and number of perpetrators (one or two). This shows the principle applies broadly but always under the condition of possibility.
The rule is summarized: if two acts are from different persons, each is dealt with separately; if from the same person, combination is considered.

Commentary
This passage discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute over the fundamental nature of the right to qisas (retaliation for murder or injury). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, qisas is established by way of khilafah (deputyship). This means that the guardian (wali) of the victim holds the right to demand qisas as a representative of the victim, but not as an inheritor. Consequently, one guardian cannot act as a deputy for another guardian. In contrast, the two disciples, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that qisas is established by way of wirathah (inheritance). This means that the right to qisas is first established for the victim (in abstract) and then transfers to the heirs as a property right. Therefore, one heir can represent all others, and a single heir can act as opponent (khasm) on behalf of all. The practical application is shown in a case where a victim has two sons: one present, one absent. If the present son proves qisas through testimony, Imam Abu Hanifa requires the absent son to re-present the evidence if he arrives later, because the present son was not his deputy. The Sahibayn say the absent son need not repeat the evidence, because the present son was acting on behalf of all heirs. The evidence for the Sahibayn is that qisas is a compensation for the victim's life, akin to property, which passes by inheritance. Abu Hanifa argues by analogy with a slave receiving a gift: ownership of a gift goes to the master directly, not to the slave, because the slave lacks capacity to own; similarly, the deceased lacks capacity to have qisas as an action, so the right vests directly in the guardians as deputies, not as inheritors. The text then quotes from Al-Hidayah and Al-Inayah, confirming the positions. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) for fatwa is not explicitly stated here, but in the Hanafi school, the view of Abu Hanifa is predominant in many qisas matters, though the Sahibayn's view is also considered strong.

وراثة (wirathah): Inheritance; the right to qisas passes from the deceased to the heirs as an inheritable right.

Commentary

Qasamah Only When There is a Sign of Murder
Stating the maxim that qasamah requires probability of murder, defined by visible traces.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for qasamah according to the Hanafi school and apply the maxim 'al-qasamah tattabi' ihtimal al-qatl' to distinguish cases where qasamah applies from those where it does not.

Core Idea
Qasamah is only established when visible traces of murder exist on the victim's body.

The principle 'Qasamah follows the probability of murder' is a foundational maxim in Hanafi criminal law. It establishes that the solemn procedure of qasamah—taking oaths to determine liability for homicide—cannot be invoked unless there is tangible evidence, known as athar, that suggests homicide. This passage from Usool al-Hidayah explains what constitutes athar and why it is necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
The maxim states that qasamah is contingent on the probability of murder. Understand that probability (ihtimal) is derived from visible signs, not mere suspicion.
2
Define the key term 'athar'
What counts as a trace? Wound, beating mark, strangulation, blood from orifices. Absence of all such signs means no probability.
These are the specific Hanafi indicators.
3
Apply the maxim to a scenario
If a body is found with no signs, the ruling is no qasamah and no diyah. The death is presumed natural (hatf anfih).
The burden of proof for homicide shifts; without athar, the case is not treated as murder for qasamah purposes.
4
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider strong circumstantial evidence (lawth) even without physical marks. Recognize the ikhtilaf.

The Rule of the First Found Part (al-Mawjud al-Awwal)
States the rule for qasamah based on the first found part of a body

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of al-mawjud al-awwal to determine whether qasamah is wajib when only part of a murdered body is found.

Core Idea
Qasamah applies to the first found part only if it constitutes the majority or half with the head.

When a murdered body is discovered in parts, the law must decide whether the locality is liable for blood money. The Hanafi school uses a hypothetical test: consider the missing part—if it would trigger qasamah, then the found part does not. This lesson unpacks that logic using the principle of al-mawjud al-awwal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional structure
The Arabic text presents a conditional: 'if the first found part is in such a state that if the remainder were found, qasamah would apply, then qasamah is not wajib on that part; otherwise it is wajib.' Identify the two clauses: condition (X) and result (Y).
The condition refers to the hypothetical finding of the missing part.
2
Translate the hypothetical into quantity
The condition 'if the remainder would make qasamah applicable' implies that the missing part is the majority of the body. Since qasamah applies only to a whole body or a majority, the missing part must be more than half. Consequently, the found part is less than half.
This is the key logical inference.
3
Apply the inverse case
Now consider the opposite: if the first part is such that the remainder would NOT trigger qasamah (i.e., the missing part is less than half), then qasamah IS wajib on the first part. This occurs when the found part is more than half or half with the head.
Thus the first part itself must be the majority or half with head to impose qasamah.
4
Confirm with examples from al-Hidayah
The text from al-Hidayah gives concrete cases: finding the whole body, more than half, or half with head imposes qasamah and diyah. Finding less than half (e.g., a hand, foot, or head alone) imposes nothing. These examples align with the principle derived above.
The examples resolve any apparent contradiction in the conditional phrasing.

Key Takeaway
The first found part of a murdered body triggers qasamah only if it is the majority or half with the head; otherwise, no liability.
