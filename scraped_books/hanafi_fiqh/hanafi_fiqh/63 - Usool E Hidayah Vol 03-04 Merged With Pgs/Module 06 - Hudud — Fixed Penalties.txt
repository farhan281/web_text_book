Penalty Does Not Allow Substitution
Maxim on non-substitutability in punishments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that punishment is non-delegable and apply it to distinguish between personal penalties and financial liabilities.

Core Idea
Punishment is personal — no one else can undergo it for you.

This maxim, "al-ʿuqūba lā tajrī fīhā al-niyāba," is a foundational rule in Hanafi criminal law. It declares that criminal penalties—whether fixed (ḥadd), retaliatory (qiṣāṣ), or discretionary (taʿzīr)—cannot be executed by a representative. Understanding this principle clarifies why punishments are inalienable rights of the state that must be inflicted exactly on the offender, unlike financial obligations which can be delegated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
Read the Arabic maxim: الْعُقُوبَةُ لَا تَجْرِي فِيْهَا النِّيَابَةُ. Break it into key terms: al-ʿuqūba (punishment), lā tajrī (does not operate), fīhā (in it), al-niyāba (substitution).
The term niyāba refers to agency or representation.
2
Determine the scope of 'punishment'
Identify which penalties are covered. According to Hanafi fiqh, this includes all criminal punishments: ḥadd (fixed penalties like lashing for zinā), qiṣāṣ (retaliation), and taʿzīr (discretionary punishment).
Financial penalties like diya (blood money) are compensatory, not punitive, and thus may be delegated.
3
Understand the legal rationale
Punishment is a personal liability (litizām fiʿlī) meant to deter and reform the offender. It cannot be transferred because the personal experience of pain or deterrence is lost if someone else bears it.
This aligns with the principle that ḥudūd are rights of Allah that require personal execution.
4
Contrast with permissible delegation
Distinguish this maxim from areas where substitution is allowed, such as financial debts (duyūn), where a representative can pay on behalf of the debtor. This is because financial obligations are not personal in the same way.
For example, paying a loan can be done by a third party, but receiving a fixed punishment cannot.
5
Apply to a concrete case
Consider a person convicted of false accusation (qadhf) and sentenced to 80 lashes. According to this maxim, he cannot send a friend to receive the lashes. The penalty must be inflicted on his person.
Even if the friend volunteers, the judge must reject the substitution.

Core Idea
Hudud and qisas are built on avoidance; evidence that increases doubt must be rejected.

This passage explains a critical Hanafi principle: hudud and qisas are founded on the concept of 'isqat' (dropping/averting) rather than establishment. This principle has a direct consequence: a judge's letter (kitab al-qadi) is not accepted as evidence in hudud and qisas cases. Let's examine why this ruling emerges from the foundational maxim.

Guided Path

The ruling is that a judge's letter (kitab al-qadi) is not accepted as evidence in hudud and qisas cases.
2
Identify the principle
The underlying principle is that hudud and qisas are based on isqat (dropping/averting). This means the law prefers to avoid punishments rather than impose them, especially when doubts arise.

The proof includes the hadith 'Avert the hudud as much as you can' (إِدْرَقُوا الْحُدُودَ مَا اسْتَطَعْتُم) and the general rule that hudud fall by doubts. The text also cites a narration from Ali (RA) about testimony upon testimony.

Apply the logic
Accepting a letter would seek to establish the punishment, which contradicts the foundation of avoidance. Additionally, letters carry a higher risk of alteration or falsehood, increasing doubt.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hudud and qisas are built on avoidance; they must be averted whenever possible.

This passage establishes a foundational maxim in Hanafi penal theory: hudud (prescribed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) are founded on the principle of isqat—they are to be dropped or avoided. This principle shapes how evidence is evaluated and which procedures are permissible. The passage then applies this maxim to the specific issue of accepting a judge's written communication (kitab al-qadi) in hudud and qisas cases, explaining why such writing is not accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
The passage states that hudud and qisas are based on isqat (dropping/avoidance). Understand what this means: the legal system prefers to avoid applying these severe punishments.
Isqat is a core Hanafi concept in penal law.
2
Cite the dalil
The hadith 'Avert the hudud as much as you can' (إِدْرَقُوا الْحُدُودَ مَا اسْتَطَعْتُم) is the textual proof. The imperative 'idraqu' indicates a command to avoid.
This hadith is from Ali ibn Abi Talib and is ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Understand the rationale
Because hudud are to be averted, anything that introduces doubt (shubha) or increased possibility of falsehood—like written testimony—is not accepted. The passage mentions 'shubha' due to potential falsehood or substitution.
The principle 'al-hudud tusqatu bi al-shubhat' (hudud are dropped by doubts) is a corollary.
4
Apply the maxim to kitab al-qadi
The passage explicitly states that a judge's written communication is not accepted in hudud and qisas because it would require establishing the punishment, contradicting the avoidance principle. Writing increases the chance of error or forgery.
This is distinct from cases involving property rights, where written testimony may be accepted.
5
Recognize broader significance
This principle affects many procedural rules: testimony upon testimony (shahada ‘ala al-shahada) is not accepted in hudud/qisas, and a judge's letter is similarly rejected.
It reflects the Shariah's emphasis on mercy and caution in severe punishments.

Prohibition of Taking Compensation for Another's Right
Stating the prohibition of settling claims involving rights of others (e.g., hadd)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking compensation for the right of another and explain why it is prohibited with reference to haqq al-ghayr.

Core Idea
You cannot buy or sell a right that does not belong to you.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle regarding settlements (sulh). When a claim involves a right that belongs to another party—especially the rights of Allah (huquq Allah) such as hadd punishments—it is not permissible to settle that claim by taking compensation. The right of Allah cannot be bargained away by individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled? Taking compensation (al-i'tiyad) for another's right (haqq al-ghayr). The ruling is that it is not permissible (la yajuz), i.e., haram.
2
Define 'right of another' (haqq al-ghayr)
Understand what constitutes haqq al-ghayr: it includes rights of Allah (e.g., hadd punishments) and rights of third parties. The contracting parties do not own these rights, so they cannot dispose of them.
This principle applies even if the parties agree.
3
Apply to the case of hadd punishments
Example: witnesses in a hadd case (zina, drinking) cannot accept money to drop their testimony. The hadd is haqq Allah, not the witnesses' right to forgive.
4
Distinguish from permissible sulh
Sulh is valid when the right being settled belongs exclusively to the parties, e.g., financial disputes. The prohibition only applies to rights not owned by the parties.
Thus, this principle does not invalidate all settlements.
5
Note the consensus among Hanafi imams
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani all agree on this ruling. There is no intra-Hanafi difference.
Authority weight: zahir al-riwayah.

The Prohibition of Taking Compensation for Another's Right
States the principle and applies it to hadd and paternity cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that taking compensation for another's right is impermissible and identify which types of sulh (compromise) are void and which are valid.

Core Idea
You cannot take compensation for a right that belongs to someone else, whether Allah's right (hadd) or a child's right (nasab).

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a foundational principle: al-i'tiyad min haqq al-ghayr la yajuz. It applies this to two critical cases—hadd punishments and paternity claims—and then explains how sulh is categorized based on its substance, treating it as a sale or lease accordingly. Understanding this principle is essential for distinguishing valid compromises from void ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
The principle states that taking compensation (i'tiyad) for a right that belongs to another is not permissible. This includes rights of Allah (hadd) and rights of third parties (e.g., a child's lineage).
The principle is derived from the general rule that one cannot dispose of another's property without permission.
2
Recognize that hadd is Allah's right
Hadd punishments are fixed rights of Allah. Therefore, a criminal cannot pay the accuser to drop the hadd case; such sulh is void.
The text gives examples of zina and drinking wine.
3
Recognize that nasab is the child's right
Lineage (nasab) is a right belonging to the child, not the mother. If a woman claims paternity and the man offers money to drop the claim, the sulh is void because she is taking compensation for another's right.
The scenario applies when the woman claims divorce and the man confirms it.
4
Distinguish valid sulh for one's own right
When sulh involves a person's own right (e.g., property claim), it is valid. If the sulh is for property in exchange for property, it is treated as a sale; if for property in exchange for usufruct, it is treated as a lease.
The text clarifies that consideration is given to the meaning (ma'na), not the wording.

Ghasb Cuts Off Transference of Liability
Explains that ghasb terminates transference of causal liability from one person to another

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the principle that ghasb cuts off transference (sarayat) of liability and apply it to determine who is liable when a usurped slave dies from a pre-existing wound.

Core Idea
Ghasb transfers liability from the original injurer to the usurper by cutting off causal transference.

This lesson explores a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: that ghasb (usurpation) severs the chain of causality for liability. The Hidayah presents a scenario where a slave's hand is cut, then the slave is usurped and dies from the cut. Who pays? The answer hinges on whether ghasb occurred before or after the injury and the principle that ghasb is a cause of ownership. Understanding this principle clarifies how liability transfers in property torts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, note the two cases presented: (1) The master cuts his own slave's hand, then a third party usurps the slave, and the slave dies from the cut in the usurper's possession. (2) The master cuts the slave's hand while the slave is already in the usurper's possession, and the slave dies from the cut.
The difference in timing of the cut relative to ghasb is crucial.
2
State the ruling for each case
For case (1): The usurper must pay the value of the slave as a one-handed person. For case (2): Nothing is owed by anyone (the master cannot be liable for harming his own property, and the usurper is not liable because the injury occurred after his possession? Actually the master cut while in usurper's hand, so the usurper didn't cause it, and the master harms his own property, so no liability).
The second case shows that if the cut occurs after ghasb, the usurper is not liable because the cut is new and not a pre-existing condition.
3
Understand the principle: ghasb cuts off sarayat
The Hidayah states: 'al-ghasb qati' li al-sarayah' (ghasb cuts off transference). The reasoning is that ghasb is a cause of ownership (sabab al-milk). When a usurper takes possession, the property is guaranteed under his liability, and any pre-existing causal link to the original owner is severed.
Sarayat refers to the transference of the effect of an injury (like a wound leading to death) from the original cause to a subsequent event.
4
Apply the principle to the first case
In case (1), the slave's death is attributable to the cut, but because ghasb occurred after the cut, the liability for the death transfers to the usurper. The usurper must pay the value because he became the effective owner (upon payment of guarantee) and the original cutter's causation is cut off.
The usurper pays the value as a one-handed slave, i.e., the value after the injury, not the full value before.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from Al-Hidayah on the principle that ghasb (usurpation) cuts off transference (sarayat) of liability. The scenario: A slave's hand is cut by the master (or another). Then a third party usurps (ghasb) the slave, and the slave dies from the wound while in the usurper's possession. The cutter (master) is not liable because ghasb cuts off the transference of the fatal wound's attribution from the cutter to the usurper. Conversely, if the master cuts the hand while the slave is already in the usurper's possession and then the slave dies in the usurper's hand, nothing is owed by anyone (the usurper is not liable because the injury was inflicted by the master, but the master cannot be liable for harming his own property). The difference hinges on ghasb as a cause of ownership: the usurper, by taking possession wrongfully, becomes liable for the slave, and this liability cuts off the chain of causation from the original injury.
