Principle of Qisas for Non-Lethal Injuries
Stating the principle of mumathalah for qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule of mumathalah for qisas in non-lethal injuries and apply it to determine whether qisas or financial compensation is wajib.

Core Idea
Qisas for injuries is wajib only when exact equivalence is possible; otherwise compensation.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a clear and rigorous principle for retaliation in wounds less than death. The author, al-Marghinani, builds on earlier rules for homicide and extends them to non-lethal injuries by requiring exact equivalence (mumathalah) between the injury inflicted and the retaliation. When that equivalence is impossible, the state must impose monetary compensation rather than bodily retaliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an intentional non-lethal wound (jarh) inflicted on another. The ruling is that qisas is wajib if exact equivalence is possible, otherwise it is not wajib and financial compensation (diya or hukuma) is due.
2
Locate the principle and its proof
The principle: 'Kullu ma amkana ri'ayatu al-mumathalati fihi yajibu fihi al-qisas wa illa fala.' (Every wound in which observing equivalence is possible, qisas is wajib; otherwise not.) Proof: Quranic verses 'Al-juruhatu qisas' (5:45) and 'Wa man qutila mazluman...' (17:33), which indicate general obligation.
Also supported by rational argument: considering differences beyond inviolability would lead to mutual destruction.
3
Apply to the example of cutting a hand at the joint
If someone intentionally cuts off another's hand at the wrist, equivalence is possible because both hands have the same benefit (manfa'ah) regardless of size. Therefore, the cutter's hand must be cut off in qisas, even if it is larger.
Size difference is irrelevant because the cause (illa) for qisas is the protected limb (ismah), not physical dimensions.
4
Apply to the example of breaking a bone
If someone breaks a bone (rib, collarbone, forearm), exact equivalence is impossible because bones cannot be broken in exactly the same way without risk of excess or error. Therefore no qisas; instead a fair assessment (hukumat al-adl) by the judge is due.
Hukuma is an estimate, not a fixed diya, because no fixed diya exists for such injuries.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i held that if the cutter's hand is larger, exact equivalence is not achieved, so qisas may be replaced by compensation. The Hanafi response: the illa is the protected benefit, not physical size, so equivalence is retained.
This difference shows the impact of defining the effective cause (illa) in qisas.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah discusses the principle of qisas (retaliation) for injuries less than death (ma duna al-nafs). The author, Imam al-Marghinani, first establishes a foundational rule from the earlier discussion: in cases of killing, the Hanafi school holds that a man can be killed in qisas for a woman, an adult for a child, a healthy person for a blind or chronically ill person, and so on, because the difference between them is not in the inviolability of life (isma) but in other attributes. Since qisas requires equality in the cause (al-illa) — that is, the protected life — these differences do not prevent qisas. This is based on general Quranic verses like al-Isra: 33 and other proofs.

The core principle for injuries is stated in Arabic: "Kullu ma amkana ri'ayatu al-mumathalati fihi yajibu fihi al-qisas wa illa fala." — Every wound in which observing equivalence is possible, retaliation is obligatory; otherwise not. This means that for non-lethal wounds, qisas is only valid when the injury can be exactly replicated on the offender. If exact replication is impossible, qisas cannot be carried out; instead, financial compensation (diya or hukuma) must be paid.

Two practical examples are given: 
1. If someone intentionally cuts off another's hand at the joint (from the wrist), the cutter's hand is also cut off at the joint in qisas, even if the cutter's hand is larger. The size difference does not matter because the benefit (manfa'ah) of the hand does not vary with size; both hands serve the same function. Thus, equivalence is achievable.
2. If someone breaks a bone (e.g., a rib, collarbone, or forearm), qisas is not possible because bones cannot be broken in exactly the same way without risk of error or excess. Instead, the judge awards a fair compensation (hukumat al-adl), which is an estimate of the loss, not a fixed diya.

This passage also notes that the Hanafi position differs from Imam al-Shafi'i, who held that certain differences (like gender or health status) prevent qisas in killing. The Hanafi response relies on the general import of the Quranic verses and the reasoning that maintaining equality beyond inviolability would lead to mutual destruction (taqatul wa tafani). The principle is supported by the Qur'anic verse "Al-juruhatu qisas" (And wounds are retaliation) from Surah al-Ma'idah.

The overall ruling: The Hanafi school makes qisas obligatory for any intentional injury where exact replication is feasible. Where it is not feasible, diya or hukuma is substituted. This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) and is consistent across the school's core texts.

Key Terms
مماثلة (mumathalah): Exact equivalence or similarity in the type and location of the injury for the purpose of qisas.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for homicide or injury, carried out under judicial supervision.
حكومة عدل (hukumat al-adl): A fair monetary assessment set by a judge when qisas is not possible and no fixed diya is prescribed.
عصمة (ismah): Inviolability or immunity from attack; the protected status of a person's life and limb.
