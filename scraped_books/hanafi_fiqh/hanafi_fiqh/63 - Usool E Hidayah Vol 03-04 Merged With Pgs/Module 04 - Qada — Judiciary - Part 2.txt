Applying Bara'at al-Dhimmah to an Ambiguous Confession
Applying default innocence to a linguistic ambiguity in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of bara'at al-dhimmah to resolve an ambiguous confession and identify the operative Hanafi position in the dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
When a confession is ambiguous, the presumption of innocence limits liability to the smallest possible obligation.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on interpreting the word 'fi' in a confession. The dispute illustrates how the foundational principle of bara'at al-dhimmah (presumption of freedom from liability) is applied to resolve linguistic ambiguity. We will explore the arguments of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, and understand why the relied-upon position adopts the lesser obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Confession and Its Ambiguity
Read the confession: 'a cloth in ten cloths.' The word 'fi' can mean containment (inside) or 'between.' Determine how each meaning affects the number of cloths owed.
Containment suggests the cloth is part of the ten, making eleven total; 'between' suggests one cloth among ten, so only one.
2
Examine the Linguistic Evidence
Abu Yusuf cites the Quranic verse 'fadkhuli fi ibadi' (89:29) where 'fi' means 'between.' This shows dual usage. Contrast with Muhammad's view that the primary meaning is containment.
The existence of two possible meanings creates doubt (shakk).
3
Apply the Principle of Bara'at al-Dhimmah
Since doubt exists, the original freedom from liability (al-asl bara'atu al-dhimmah) applies. No extra obligation can be imposed based on uncertainty.
The principle dictates that only the certain minimum is owed.
4
Compare the Rulings
Abu Yusuf: one cloth due. Muhammad: eleven cloths due. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
The difference is ten cloths.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The commentary (Usool E Hidayah) prefers Abu Yusuf's view, applying bara'ah. This is the operative position for fatwa.
Tarjih is based on the strength of the principle and the doubt.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the interpretation of a confession formula. The principle of bara'at al-dhimmah (presumption of innocence from liability) is invoked by Imam Abu Yusuf to limit the number of cloths owed to one, while Imam Muhammad interprets the word 'fi' as a container (zarfiyyah) implying eleven cloths. Abu Yusuf argues that 'fi' can also mean 'between' (bayn/wast), as in the Quranic verse 'Enter among My servants' (fadkhuli fi ibadi), where 'fi' means 'between.' Therefore, ambiguity arises, and since doubt cannot establish liability beyond the minimum, only one cloth is owed. The commentary aligns with Abu Yusuf's view, stating that the principle of bara'at al-dhimmah supports the lesser obligation. The passage includes references to al-Hidayah, al-Binayah, and Fath al-Qadir for further reading.

Key Terms

اقرار (iqrar): A legal confession or acknowledgment of a right or obligation, which is binding if clear and unconditional.
فی (fi): A preposition that can indicate containment (zarfiyyah) or 'between' (bayn/wast), leading to ambiguity in this context.

Preference of the Outsider's Evidence Over the Possessor's
Stating the rule of evidence preference after division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on evidence preference in division disputes and apply it to a scenario where both parties present witnesses.

Core Idea
The outsider's proof outweighs the possessor's possession when both bring evidence.

In property disputes after a division, one party may claim an item in the other's possession belongs to his share. When both produce witnesses, which evidence prevails? This lesson explains the Hanafi rule that the claimant's (outsider's) witnesses are given preference over the possessor's witnesses, even though the possessor's possession itself is a form of proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Positions
In the scenario after division, one partner (the claimant) asserts that a specific item in the other partner's (the possessor's) hands belongs to him. The claimant is the outsider (kharji) because he does not physically hold the item.
The division has already occurred; the dispute is over the identity of shares.
2
Recognize the Default Presumption of Possession
In Hanafi law, possession (yad) is itself a form of proof (hujjah). The possessor does not bear the burden of proof initially; the claimant must bring evidence.
This is why the possessor's witnesses are considered, but they are outweighed.
3
Understand the Rule of Preference
The rule states that if both parties bring evidence, the evidence of the outsider (claimant) is given preference over that of the possessor. This is called 'bayyinah al-kharji tutarrabahu 'ala bayyinah dhi al-yad'.
Preference is given to the claimant because he is the one who needs to prove, and his evidence is stronger than the mere presumption.
4
Apply the Rule to the Given Scenario
In the example from al-Hidayah, when one partner claims a room belongs to his share and both present witnesses, the judge must accept the claimant's evidence and rule in his favor.
The possessor's evidence is not completely rejected, but it is not sufficient to counter the claimant's evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with explanation regarding evidence in disputes after partition. The principle is that if after a division of jointly owned property, one partner claims that a specific item (e.g., a room) in the other's possession actually belongs to his share, and the other denies, the burden of proof is on the claimant. If both parties produce evidence (bayyinah), the evidence of the claimant—who is considered the 'outsider' (kharji) because he does not have possession of the disputed item—is given preference over the evidence of the current possessor (dhu al-yad). This is because the possessor's possession itself is a form of evidence (al-yad hujjah), but the Hanafi jurists hold that when both parties bring evidence, the outsider's evidence outweighs the possessor's mere possession. The rule is derived from the principle that the claimant (mudda'i) must prove his case, and if he does, his evidence is stronger than the defendant's evidence because the defendant's possession is already a type of presumption (zahir), but clear evidence overcomes that presumption. This applies in cases where the division has already taken place and a dispute arises over the identity of shares. The reference to al-Hidayah indicates this is the relied-upon position in the school.

خارج (kharji): An outsider, someone who is not in possession of the disputed property. In this context, the claimant who does not hold the property.
ذو الید (dhu al-yad): The person in possession of the property at the time of the dispute.

A Judge's Ruling Contrary to Ijma' Is Void
Applies the principle that a judge's ruling against ijmah is unenforceable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a judge's ruling contrary to ijma' is void and apply it to a case where ijma' exists on a fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling is void if it contradicts ijma', because consensus is a definitive proof (qat'i).

In this passage from al-Hidayah, Imam al-Marghinani establishes a fundamental principle: no judicial decision can override the consensus of the scholars. He demonstrates this through a specific case: the sale of an animal intentionally slaughtered without tasmiyah. Understanding this principle safeguards the supremacy of collective scholarly authority over individual judgment.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the ruling: A judge's ruling that opposes ijma' is not enforceable (la yanfudhu).
This is the core legal maxim.
2
Identify the proof
The proof is ijma' (consensus) of the mujtahids, which is a qat'i (definitive) source of law.
Ijma' is equivalent in authority to the Qur'an and mutawatir Sunnah in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
Ijma' is a definitive proof; any ruling opposing a definitive proof is invalid by default.
No individual ijtihad can contradict a collective consensus.
4
Apply to the example
If a judge permits the sale of an animal slaughtered without pronouncing Allah's name (tasmiyah), that ruling contradicts ijma' on its impermissibility, so it is void.
The animal remains non-halal; the sale is not permitted.
5
Consider limits and exceptions
This principle applies to any ijma' that is firmly established. If there is no ijma', a judge's ruling may stand unless it contradicts a stronger proof.
If the ruling contradicts a zanni (speculative) proof, it may be enforceable in some cases.

Qisas for Wounds: The Condition of Exact Similarity
Establishes the rule for qisas in injuries less than death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for qisas in wounds, apply it to distinguish when qisas is wajib versus diyah, and explain why the Hanafi school rejects additional conditions like faith and freedom.

Core Idea
Qisas for wounds is wajib only when exact similarity is possible; otherwise, diyah applies.

This lesson examines a key ruling from Al-Hidayah on qisas (retaliation) for wounds that fall short of murder. The central principle is simple but carries profound implications: qisas is only obligatory when the injury can be exactly replicated. When that is impossible, the law shifts to financial compensation (diyah). The passage also sets out the Hanafi stance against the Shafi'i requirement of equality in faith and freedom, arguing from Quranic generality and the prevention of social harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qisas for wounds (al-qisas fima duna an-nafs). The ruling is that it is wajib. Determine the condition that triggers wajib: the possibility of exact similarity (mumathalah).
If similarity is impossible, the ruling changes to diyah (compensation).
2
Locate the condition and its scope
The condition is 'imkan ri'ayat al-mumathalah' – the feasibility of observing identical retaliation. For example, cutting a hand at the wrist can be mirrored exactly; slashing a cheek that heals differently cannot.
This condition is specific to wounds; for murder, the Hanafi school does not require such similarity beyond 'ismah.
3
Identify the proof (dalil)
The proof cited is Quranic: 'And whoever is killed unjustly, We have given his heir authority' (al-Isra 17:33) and other general verses. The Hanafi usul interprets the word 'sultan' as general authority for retaliation, including wounds.
The proof is dalil naqli with qati thubut but zanni dalala (the general wording is interpreted broadly).
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Imam al-Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i requires additional conditions for qisas: equality in faith (Muslim for Muslim) and freedom (free for free). The Hanafi response is that the Quranic generalities do not restrict qisas to equal status, and considering extra differences would nullify qisas in many cases, leading to feuds.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi in this text.
5
Derive the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf is that in Hanafi law, a Muslim can face qisas for killing a dhimmi, and a free man for a slave. In Shafi'i law, such cross-status qisas is not allowed.
For wounds, the condition of similarity applies regardless of status, as long as 'ismah is equal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim equating testimony with eyewitness and apply it to determine when qisas is wajib based on testimony.

Core Idea
Testimony and direct observation have equal probative force in establishing legal facts.

This passage introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi judicial proof: testimony before a court carries the same legal weight as the judge's own direct observation. The principle is then applied to a qisas (retaliation) case to illustrate its practical implications, showing that if valid testimony proves an intentional killing with a sharp weapon, qisas becomes wajib.

1

Imam Abu Hanifa requires a sharp weapon for qisas; the Sahibayn accept other lethal means.
5
Confirm the Ruling
Because testimony is equivalent to eyewitness, the judge's ruling on qisas is the same as if he had witnessed the killing himself. Thus, testimony suffices for imposing qisas.

Mutlaq and Muqayyad: The Distinctness Principle
Defines the principle and applies it to testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that mutlaq and muqayyad are legally distinct and apply it to a case of discrepant testimony in homicide.

Core Idea
Mutlaq and muqayyad cannot be reconciled—they yield separate rulings, so testimony that mixes them is invalid.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a core usul principle: an absolute (mutlaq) statement and a restricted (muqayyad) statement are treated as legally distinct. The author immediately applies it to witness testimony in a homicide case. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating evidence and deriving correct rulings in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the first line: 'The absolute differs from the restricted.' Recognize that this is a legal maxim (qa'ida) of interpretation.
The principle is stated as a rule of usul al-fiqh.
2
Understand the basis
The commentary explains that the restricted contains a restriction while the absolute is free from it, so they are opposite. This means they cannot be reconciled but each stands on its own.
The term 'yughayiru' indicates essential difference.
3
Apply to testimony
The author gives an example: one witness specifies a stick (muqayyad), the other gives no specification (mutlaq). These are legally different because the restricted testimony would require diyah on the 'aqilah, while the absolute would require diyah from the killer's property.
The legal effect (diyah liability) differs based on the restriction.
4

In Hanafi fiqh, for rights of people (huquq al-ibad), liability is triggered by the act of harm, regardless of the actor's intention.

The Definition of an Oath in Hanafi Fiqh
Defining the oath and applying to qasamah eligibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of an oath and identify which persons are eligible to take an oath in qasamah.

Core Idea
An oath is a valid statement; only sane adults can make a valid oath.

This lesson examines a foundational principle from Al-Hidayah: al-yaminu qawlun sahihun. This definition is not merely semantic—it carries significant legal consequences, particularly in the chapter of qasamah (compurgation). By understanding the genus and differentia of this definition, we derive clear rules about who may participate in oath-taking procedures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the principle: 'al-yaminu qawlun sahihun' (the oath is a valid statement). This is the core legal definition.
This principle is from Al-Hidayah and is authoritative in the Hanafi school.
2
Break Down the Definition
Analyze the definition: genus = qawl (statement), differentia = sahih (valid). Understand that 'sahih' implies legal validity, not just truthfulness.
The differentia restricts the genus to statements that meet the criteria of legal capacity.
3
Understand Legal Capacity Requirement
A valid statement (qawl sahih) can only be produced by a sane adult (aqil baligh). Therefore, an oath as a valid statement requires the same capacity.
Children and insane persons lack the capacity to produce qawl sahih.
4
Apply to Qasamah
In the chapter of qasamah, the ruling explicitly states: 'No qasamah upon a child or an insane person.' This is derived directly from the definition.
The ruling is consistent across the Hanafi school (muttafaq alayh).
5
Recognize the Proof Type
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the nature of speech and legal capacity. No specific scriptural text is needed beyond the definition.
The proof is conceptual, relying on the meaning of 'qawl sahih'.

Commentary
This passage is a short commentary on a principle from Al-Hidayah regarding the definition of an oath (yamin). The principle states: 'The oath is a valid statement.' The author explains that an oath is only valid if it comes from a sane (aqil) adult (baligh), because a valid statement (qawl sahih) requires rationality and maturity. Therefore, a child and an insane person cannot take a valid oath. This is applied to the chapter of qasamah (compurgation or oath-taking in cases of homicide). The ruling quoted from Al-Quduri's text explicitly states that there is no qasamah upon a child or an insane person. The reasoning is purely definitional: since an oath is a type of statement, only those capable of making a valid statement can perform it. This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the nature of speech and legal capacity. The authority weight is from Zahir al-Riwayah as it comes from Al-Hidayah and Al-Quduri's matn.

Key Terms
الْيَمِين (al-yamin): An oath or sworn statement made in legal proceedings, especially in qasamah.
قَوْلٌ صَحِيحٌ (qawlun sahihun): A legally valid statement that is comprehensible and issued by a legally capable person.
قَسَامَة (qasamah): A collective oath taken by the accused's clan in cases of homicide to deny guilt or by the victim's heirs to claim blood money.
