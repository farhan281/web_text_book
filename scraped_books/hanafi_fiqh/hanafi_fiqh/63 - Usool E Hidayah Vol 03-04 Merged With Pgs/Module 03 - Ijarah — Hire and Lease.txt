Core Idea
A subsequent lease retroactively validates prior unauthorized acts as if agency existed.

This lesson introduces a concise Hanafi maxim that governs cases where a person initially acts without authority (e.g., rents out someone else's property) and later the owner leases the property to that person. Understanding this maxim helps determine the legal validity of earlier actions and ensures that the lessee is not treated as a usurper.

Work through the given example: A rents B's house without B's permission; later B leases the house to A. The subsequent lease retroactively validates A's initial act, making it as if A acted as B's agent from the start.

Sin Cannot Be Earned by Contract
A foundational Hanafi legal maxim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-ma'siyah la tastahiqqu bi al-'aqd' and identify when a contract is batil due to sinful subject matter.

Core Idea
A contract for a sinful act is void; sin cannot be made permissible by agreement.

This short maxim from Hanafi legal theory sets a fundamental limit on the power of contracts. No agreement, no matter how mutual, can transform a prohibited act into a permissible one. Understanding this principle is essential for evaluating the validity of contracts, especially in areas like ijara (hire) where the subject matter may involve sin.

Guided Path
1
Understand the maxim's literal meaning
The Arabic 'al-ma'siyah la tastahiqqu bi al-'aqd' means 'sin is not earned by contract.' That is, a contract cannot make an act of disobedience rightful or permissible. The contract itself becomes void.
2
Identify the type of contract affected
This maxim primarily applies to contracts where the subject matter or performance involves a sinful act, such as hiring someone to steal, commit murder, or engage in other prohibitions.
The focus is on the subject matter, not the form of the contract.
3
Distinguish between batil and fasid
In Hanafi law, a contract for a sinful act is batil (void ab initio) rather than fasid (irregular). This means it produces no legal effects: no obligation to pay wages, no entitlement to the subject matter, and no transfer of ownership.
A fasid contract can sometimes be corrected; a batil contract cannot.
4
Apply the maxim to examples
Test the maxim on scenarios like hiring a singer for sinful songs, renting a property for a gambling den, or paying someone to break another's property. In each case, the contract is void.
The maxim applies regardless of the mutual consent of the parties.

Commentary
This maxim asserts that a contract cannot make a sinful act permissible or give a right to commit it. For example, hiring someone to steal or commit murder is invalid because the act is sinful. The contract itself is void.

Hire Cannot Be for Intentional Destruction
Ruling that hiring to intentionally destroy property is invalid

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ijarah is for benefit, not destruction; a contract for damage is not hire.

This lesson presents a fundamental maxim of Hanafi contract law: you cannot validly hire someone to intentionally destroy an object. Even if you own the object, such an agreement falls outside the scope of ijarah. Understanding this rule clarifies the essential nature of ijarah as a contract for lawful benefit (manfa'ah), not for causing loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that the passage refers to ijarah (hire/lease). Ijarah is defined as a contract for the use of a benefit in exchange for payment.
2
Understand the prohibited subject matter
The phrase 'itlaf al-a'yan maqsudan' means intentional destruction of physical objects. This is the opposite of benefit; it is a harm.
Destruction is not a benefit (manfa'ah) in Islamic law.
3
Grasp the legal consequence
The contract 'la yan'aqidu' – it does not conclude. It is completely invalid (batil) from the start, not merely voidable.
No rights or obligations arise from such an agreement.
4
Apply the maxim to a concrete example
Consider hiring a worker to break your old furniture. This is not a valid ijarah; it would be treated as a contract of guarantee (damān) or as a tort if damage occurs.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: permission is always qualified by an implicit condition of safety. Permission is not absolute; it carries an implicit condition that the permitted action must be safe. The owner's consent does not waive liability for harm caused by negligence or abuse.

This maxim is rooted in the broader principle of avoiding harm (la darar wa la dirar). The condition of safety protects the owner's property rights.
4
Apply to a case

Undertaking What One Cannot Fulfill: The Hanafi Maxim on Capacity
A Hanafi maxim on capacity to fulfill obligations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on capacity and apply it to contracts where fulfillment is impossible.

Core Idea
An obligation impossible to fulfill is void from the start.

This maxim is a cornerstone of Hanafi contract law, preventing individuals from binding themselves to tasks beyond their capacity. It ensures that obligations are practical and enforceable, rooted in the principle that the Law does not require the impossible. Understanding this maxim is essential for evaluating the validity of contracts in Ijara (hire) and Buyu' (sale).

Guided Path
1
Recite the maxim
Memorize the Arabic wording: 'إِلْتِرَامُ مَا لَا يَقْدِرُ عَلَى الْوَفَاءِ بِهِ لَا يَجُوزُ'. Understand the meaning: 'Undertaking what one is not able to fulfill is not permissible.'
Focus on the key terms: iltizam (undertaking) and al-wafa' (fulfillment).
2
Define the scope
The maxim applies to any contractual obligation or promise where the subject matter is impossible to deliver or perform. It does not apply to mere difficulty or hardship.
Distinguish between actual impossibility (ghayr maqdur) and hardship (mashaqqah).
3
Identify the legal effect
An undertaking that is impossible to fulfill is impermissible (la yajuz) and therefore the contract is void (batil). No legal consequences arise from such an obligation.
The contract is void ab initio; no rights or duties are created.
4
Apply to specific contracts
In Ijara: hiring someone to paint a ceiling that is physically inaccessible. In Buyu': selling goods that do not exist and cannot be produced. Both contracts are void under this maxim.
These examples illustrate the maxim's practical relevance.
5
Distinguish from similar principles
The maxim is different from the concept of 'gharar' (uncertainty) or 'taghrir' (deception). It specifically targets complete incapacity, not risk or ambiguity.
Impossibility is absolute; uncertainty is relative.

Personal acts of worship cannot be hired because reward is inalienable.

Also excluded: teaching religious knowledge, leading prayer, calling adhan, because these involve worldly benefits.

Note school-specificity
The word 'indana' indicates this is the Hanafi position. Other schools may differ. For example, Shafi'is allow hiring for teaching Quran.

Principle: A Thing Does Not Contain What Is Above It – Application to Borrowing
Explaining the usul maxim and applying it to borrowing; defining 'ariyah as tamlik al-manafi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'a thing does not contain what is above it' to determine the permissibility and liability of sub-renting a borrowed item.

Core Idea
A borrower cannot sub-rent because a weaker contract (borrowing) cannot subsume a stronger one (leasing).

This lesson examines a powerful usul maxim that governs contractual hierarchies. The maxim 'a thing does not contain what is above it' is applied to the contract of borrowing ('ariyah) to prohibit sub-renting. Understanding this maxim illuminates the Hanafi distinction between compensatory and non-compensatory contracts and the resulting liability rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contractual hierarchy
Recognize that 'ariyah is a non-compensatory contract (weaker) while ijara is a compensatory contract (stronger). The passage states that a non-compensatory contract cannot include a compensatory one.
2
Apply the maxim
The maxim 'ash-shay' la yatadammun ma huwa fawqahu' means a weaker entity cannot subsume a stronger one. Therefore, 'ariyah cannot contain ijara.
3
Derive the ruling
Since sub-renting would require the borrower to grant a right stronger than his own, it is invalid. If he sub-rents and the item perishes, he becomes liable (damin).
4
Understand the definition of 'ariyah
Hanafi school defines 'ariyah as tamlik al-manafi' (ownership of benefits), not mere permission. This underpins the maxim's application because ownership of benefits is still a weaker right than ownership for compensation.
5
Contrast with the opposing school
The Shafi'i school defines 'ariyah as ibaha al-intifa' (permission to use), which may allow sub-renting with the owner's permission. The Hanafi definition leads to stricter liability.

Commentary
This passage from Usool E Hidayah presents two distinct rulings under the chapter of void ijarah (إجارة فاسدة). The first is a linguistic-legal principle: when the Arabic word 'kull' (meaning 'each' or 'every') is applied to something that has no finite limit, it is interpreted to refer to a single unit rather than a general multitude. This is because applying it to all possible units is impossible due to infinite regress, and any specified number would be arbitrary. The second part discusses a scenario where a hired animal is pulled by its bridle or struck and consequently dies. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the hirer is liable for the damage, even if the action was customary, because the permission to use the animal is implicitly conditioned on safety. Since the objective (driving) can be achieved without such forceful actions, the hirer exceeds the bounds of permission. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that customary actions are included in the general permission of the contract, so no liability arises. The reliable position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as it aligns with the principle of safeguarding the owner's property. The principle of 'kull' is then applied to a lease contract where the tenant says 'I rent this house for each month for a dirham' without specifying total months. The contract is valid only for the first month; subsequent months are void because 'month' is potentially infinite, and 'kull' is limited to one. To avoid this, the parties must specify a fixed total, e.g., ten months each month for a dirham.

Key Terms
كُلّ (kull): a word meaning 'each' or 'every'; when applied to something infinite, it is interpreted as a single unit in this context
إجارة فاسدة (ijārah fāsidah): a lease contract that is invalid due to some deficiency, e.g., uncertainty in the period or rent

Liability for Pulling a Hired Animal: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for customary acts in ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the hirer's liability for customary acts that cause harm, and evaluate the Hanafi response that permission is conditioned on safety.

Core Idea
Customary acts in ijarah are permitted, but Abu Hanifa conditions permission on safety when the objective can be achieved without the act.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a hirer who performs a customary action (like pulling the bridle to stop a horse) is liable if the animal dies. Abu Hanifa imposes liability because the permission is implicitly conditioned on safety, while the Sahibayn argue that customary actions are included in the general permission of the contract. Understanding this disagreement clarifies the limits of permission in lease contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed action
The action is 'كَبَحَ الدَّابَّةَ بِلِجَامِهَا' – pulling the animal by the bridle to stop it. If the animal is destroyed as a result, is the hirer liable?
This is a specific act of using the hired animal beyond normal driving.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the hirer is liable (يضمن) even if the action is customary. His proof: permission to use the animal is implicitly conditioned on safety (مقيد بشرط السلامة). Since the objective (driving) can be achieved without pulling/beating, the hirer exceeds the bounds of permission.
Abu Hanifa's ruling is mufta bihi in the school.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the hirer is not liable (لا يضمن) if the action is customary (فعلًا متعارفًا). Their proof: customary actions are included under the absolute contract (يدخل تحت مطلق العقد), so they occur with the owner's implicit permission.
They analogize to other customary uses.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa's argument concedes that the act occurs with permission but qualifies that permission: it is conditional on safety. Just as passing through a road is permitted only when safe, pulling the bridle is only for emphasis; the objective can be achieved without it, so safety is a condition.
The analogy (كالمرور في الطريق) strengthens the condition of safety.
5
Determine the practical outcome (thamarah)
If a hirer's customary action causes harm, Abu Hanifa imposes liability; Sahibayn do not. The reliable position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view, as supported by Al-Hidayah and later sources.
This affects compensation claims in ijarah disputes.

Commentary
This passage from Usool E Hidayah presents two distinct rulings under the chapter of void ijarah (إجارة فاسدة). The first is a linguistic-legal principle: when the Arabic word 'kull' (meaning 'each' or 'every') is applied to something that has no finite limit, it is interpreted to refer to a single unit rather than a general multitude. This is because applying it to all possible units is impossible due to infinite regress, and any specified number would be arbitrary. The second part discusses a scenario where a hired animal is pulled by its bridle or struck and consequently dies. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the hirer is liable for the damage, even if the action was customary, because the permission to use the animal is implicitly conditioned on safety. Since the objective (driving) can be achieved without such forceful actions, the hirer exceeds the bounds of permission. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that customary actions are included in the general permission of the contract, so no liability arises. The reliable position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as it aligns with the principle of safeguarding the owner's property. The principle of 'kull' is then applied to a lease contract where the tenant says 'I rent this house for each month for a dirham' without specifying total months. The contract is valid only for the first month; subsequent months are void because 'month' is potentially infinite, and 'kull' is limited to one. To avoid this, the parties must specify a fixed total, e.g., ten months each month for a dirham.

Renting with 'Each Month for a Dirham': The Hanafi Ruling
Explains ruling with proof from linguistic principle of 'kull'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a lease contract with a monthly rent but unspecified total duration, and explain the linguistic principle of 'kull' that underlies it.

Core Idea
If rent is specified 'each month' but total months unknown, the lease is valid only for one month; 'kull' is restricted to one.

When a lessor says 'I rent this house to you for a dirham each month' without stating how many months, an ambiguity arises. Is the contract valid for every month indefinitely? The Hanafi school applies a key linguistic rule: the word 'kull' (every) when attached to something that has no defined limit reverts to a single unit. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract scenario

2

Valid (sahih) for the first month, invalid (fasid) for the remaining months.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Locate the linguistic principle
The word 'kull' when entering upon something indefinite (لا نهاية له) is restricted to the singular.
This is an established Hanafi usul rule: إذا دخلت كل على ما لا نهاية له انصرفت إلى الواحد
4
Apply the principle to the case
Since the total months are unknown (مجهول), acting upon generality is impossible; so it is interpreted as one month.
Any other number would be preference without preponderator (ترجيح بلا مرجح).
5
Note the exception
If the lessor specifies a total number (e.g., ten months, each month for a dirham), then the contract is valid for all months.
Because the indefinite becomes defined.

Invalidity of Hiring for Unpredictable Acts (Stud Fee)
Proving that taking wages for animal mating is invalid due to inability to guarantee result

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking wages for animal mating, explain the underlying principle (inability to guarantee result), and distinguish the Hanafi position from that of Shafi'is/Hanbalis.

Core Idea
Hiring for an uncertain outcome is invalid because you cannot guarantee what is beyond your control.

In Hanafi fiqh, a contract of hire (ijara) requires that the service be precisely defined and within the hirer's power to deliver. This lesson explores a key application: the prohibition of taking a fee for mating animals (ajrat al-'asb). The ruling is derived from a general principle that one cannot undertake something one cannot fulfill. We will examine the rationale, the dispute with other schools, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is taking wages for mating a male animal with a female. The ruling in the Hanafi school is that it is not permissible (la yajuz).
The fee is called 'asb.
2
Explain the underlying principle
The principle is 'undertaking something one cannot fulfill is invalid.' The person charging the fee implicitly guarantees pregnancy, which is not in his control.
Pregnancy is an uncertain outcome; it depends on divine will.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): since the outcome is uncertain, the contract involves deception (khida') and taking wages without proper consideration.
The contract is fasid (invalid) because the object of hire is not known with certainty.
4
Compare with other schools
Some Shafi'is and Hanbalis allow stud fees because they view the physical service of mating as hireable. The Hanafi response is that the intended benefit (pregnancy) is not guaranteed.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; each school derives from its own usul.

Core Idea
Leasing an undivided share is invalid because delivery of the share alone is inconceivable.

This passage addresses a key limitation in Ijarah: the object must be deliverable. When property is jointly owned, an undivided share cannot be physically handed over without the partner. Abu Hanifa holds that leasing such a share to a non-partner is invalid (fasid), even if the share is known. The proof is rational: delivery is impossible, so the contract fails to fulfill a necessary condition.

This exception shows that the ruling is specific to non-partners.

Commentary
This passage from Usool e Hidayah explains two key principles of the Hanafi law of ijarah (hire/lease). The first principle (carried forward from a previous discussion) concerns leasing an undivided share (musha'). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is not permissible to lease an undivided share to a non-partner, regardless of whether the share is known or unknown, and regardless of whether the property is divisible (like a house) or indivisible (like a slave). The reason is that the lessor must be able to deliver the object of the lease. Delivering an undivided share alone is not practically possible, and what cannot be delivered cannot be validly leased because the purpose of ijarah—usufruct—is unattainable. This is the position of Abu Hanifa; it is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) ruling. The exception is when the lessee is a co-owner (partner), because delivery in that case is feasible. The second principle (numbered 127) states that a contract of ijarah is not valid when it involves the intentional destruction of the leased material object (ayn). Ijarah is a contract for the transfer of usufruct, not for the consumption of the object itself. Therefore, hiring a cow or goat to drink its milk is invalid because the milk (the object) is intentionally consumed. However, an exception exists for wet nurses (istijar al-zuhr). By strict qiyas, hiring a wet nurse would also be invalid because it involves consuming milk. But the Hanafi school permits it by istihsan, based on the Quranic verse (Al-Talaq 65:6) and because the contract is for the service of nursing (act of upbringing), not for the milk itself. The milk is consumed incidentally, not as the primary purpose. The commentator confirms that the first ruling (prohibition of intentional destruction) is closer to the principles of fiqh, but the exception for wet nurses is established by divine text and istihsan.

Key Terms
مشاع (musha'): An undivided share in a jointly owned property; ownership of an indivisible portion without physical demarcation.
إجارة (ijarah): A contract for the transfer of usufruct (manfa'at) of an object or service for a known compensation (ujrah).

Ijarah on Intentional Destruction of Objects and the Exception for Wet Nurses
Explains the invalidity of ijarah on intentional destruction and the istihsan for wet nurses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why ijarah for intentional destruction of objects is invalid, and why hiring a wet nurse is permitted by istihsan despite the general principle.

Core Idea
Ijarah is for usufruct, not consumption; wet nurses are allowed because the contract is for service, not for milk.

This lesson covers the second principle: ijarah cannot be concluded for the intentional destruction of material objects. Hiring a cow to drink its milk is invalid because the milk is consumed. However, hiring a wet nurse is an exception: although milk is consumed, the contract is for the service of nursing. This exception is established by the Quran and istihsan.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Ijarah is a contract for usufruct, not for consuming the object. Therefore, any contract that intentionally destroys the object is invalid.
2
Apply the principle to an example
Hiring a cow to drink its milk is invalid because the milk (a material object) is intentionally consumed.
3
Identify the exception
Hiring a wet nurse (istijar al-zuhr) is valid despite involving milk consumption. Why? Because the contract is for the service of nursing, not for the milk itself.
4
Understand the proof (dalil)
The Quranic verse (Al-Talaq 65:6) commands giving wages to wet nurses. This text overrides the general qiyas that would prohibit it.
5
Recognize the role of istihsan
Istihsan (juristic preference) is used here to depart from qiyas in favor of the stronger evidence (the Quran) and public benefit.

Commentary
This passage from Usool e Hidayah explains two key principles of the Hanafi law of ijarah (hire/lease). The first principle (carried forward from a previous discussion) concerns leasing an undivided share (musha'). According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is not permissible to lease an undivided share to a non-partner, regardless of whether the share is known or unknown, and regardless of whether the property is divisible (like a house) or indivisible (like a slave). The reason is that the lessor must be able to deliver the object of the lease. Delivering an undivided share alone is not practically possible, and what cannot be delivered cannot be validly leased because the purpose of ijarah—usufruct—is unattainable. This is the position of Abu Hanifa; it is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) ruling. The exception is when the lessee is a co-owner (partner), because delivery in that case is feasible. The second principle (numbered 127) states that a contract of ijarah is not valid when it involves the intentional destruction of the leased material object (ayn). Ijarah is a contract for the transfer of usufruct, not for the consumption of the object itself. Therefore, hiring a cow or goat to drink its milk is invalid because the milk (the object) is intentionally consumed. However, an exception exists for wet nurses (istijar al-zuhr). By strict qiyas, hiring a wet nurse would also be invalid because it involves consuming milk. But the Hanafi school permits it by istihsan, based on the Quranic verse (Al-Talaq 65:6) and because the contract is for the service of nursing (act of upbringing), not for the milk itself. The milk is consumed incidentally, not as the primary purpose. The commentator confirms that the first ruling (prohibition of intentional destruction) is closer to the principles of fiqh, but the exception for wet nurses is established by divine text and istihsan.

استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference that overrides qiyas for a stronger evidence or public good.
ظئر (zuhr (plural: zuhr)): A wet nurse; a woman hired to breastfeed an infant.

Ijarah Invalid on Intentional Destruction of 'Ayn: The Principle and Istihsan Exception
Explaining the principle and exception for ijarah on consumable objects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that ijarah is invalid on intentional destruction of the 'ayn, explain the qiyas against hiring a wet nurse, and apply the istihsan exception based on the Quran.

Core Idea
Ijarah is for usufruct, not destruction of the object; the wet nurse is an exception by Quranic istihsan.

In Hanafi fiqh, the contract of ijarah (hire) is fundamentally a transfer of usufruct (manfa'ah) while the 'ayn (object) remains intact. Therefore, any contract that intentionally consumes the 'ayn is not a valid ijarah. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains this principle and highlights a unique exception: hiring a wet nurse (dā'ī). Although qiyas would forbid it, the Quran explicitly permits it, leading to an istihsan ruling. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
State the general rule: ijarah cannot be on intentional destruction (itlāf) of the 'ayn. Ijarah only transfers usufruct while the object remains.
This is the default rule based on the nature of ijarah.
2
Apply the principle to a case
Consider hiring a cow to drink its milk. The milk is the 'ayn, and drinking it intentionally destroys it. Therefore, the contract is invalid.
The milk is not a usufruct; it is the substance itself.
3
Identify the exception
Hiring a wet nurse (dā'ī) is valid by istihsan, even though qiyas would disallow it. The contract is for the service of breastfeeding and childcare, and the milk is consumed incidentally.
The exception is based on a stronger evidence: the Quran.
4
Locate the proof
The Quranic verse (At-Talaq 65:6) commands giving wages to wet nurses. This text provides a direct basis for istihsan, overriding the qiyas analogy to hiring animals.
The imperative indicates permissibility and contractual validity.
5
Differentiate the two cases
In the cow case, the primary object is the milk (consumed intentionally). In the wet nurse case, the primary object is the act of rearing; milk consumption is secondary (taba'an).
This distinction (maqsūdan vs. taba'an) is crucial for ijarah.

Core Idea
Custom is legally valid only where no textual evidence exists — text always prevails over custom.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational legal maxim of the Hanafi school: custom (urf) is considered only in the absence of explicit textual evidence (nass). This principle is crucial for understanding how secondary sources operate within Hanafi methodology. The passage illustrates its application to the duties of a wet nurse in a hire contract, showing how custom fills gaps where the primary sources are silent.

Wage Cannot Be from the Product of the Worker's Labor
Prohibits making the wage a part of what the worker produces from his labor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying a worker with the product of his labor and identify the consequence of such an agreement.

Core Idea
A worker's wage cannot be a share of what he produces; such a contract is invalid and the worker receives the customary wage.

This lesson examines a key maxim in Hanafi contract law: the wage (ujr) must be something separate from the worker's own labor. The prohibition stems from the hadith forbidding 'qafiz al-tahhan' (the miller's measure). We will see how this rule applies to common examples like weaving and transportation, and learn the remedy when such a contract is made.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the core rule: 'Making the wage a part of what comes out of the worker's labor is not permissible.'
This is a general principle governing all ijara contracts.
2
Identify the basis
Recognize that the prohibition comes from the hadith forbidding qafiz al-tahhan (paying the miller with some of the flour he grinds).
The underlying reasoning is that compensation cannot be of the same type as the worker's labor (جزاء من جنس عمل الأجير لا يجوز).
3
Understand the examples
Analyze the given cases: a weaver given cotton to weave cloth with half the cloth as his wage, and a donkey hired to carry flour with the wage being some of that flour.
Both examples illustrate contracts where the wage is the very thing the worker produces or transports.
4
Derive the ruling
Conclude that such an ijara is fasid (invalid) and the worker is entitled to ujr al-mithl (customary wage) for his labor.
The contract is not completely void; the worker still receives fair compensation.

In such cases, the ijarah becomes fasid (defective) and the worker is entitled to ajr al-mithl (standard market wage) rather than the agreed amount. The passage also touches on wet nurses, noting that customary duties like washing clothes and preparing food are on the wet nurse even if not explicitly stipulated. The Arabic quotes from al-Lubab and al-Hidayah confirm these rulings and cite the authoritative sources.

Key Terms
قفیز الطحان (qafiz al-tahhan): A measure of grain given to a miller as payment for grinding, which is prohibited in hadith.
اجرت مثل (ajr al-mithl): Standard market wage for similar work, payable in a fasid ijarah.
اجارہ فاسد (ijarah fasidah): A defective lease/employment contract that is invalid due to an impermissible condition.

Key Takeaway
Wages cannot be from the worker's own output; such a contract is fasid and the worker is due ajr al-mithl.
