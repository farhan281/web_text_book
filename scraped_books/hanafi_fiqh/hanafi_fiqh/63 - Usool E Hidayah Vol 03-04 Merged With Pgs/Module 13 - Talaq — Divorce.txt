The Hanafi Maxim on Conditional Suspensions
A Hanafi maxim on conditional suspensions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on ta'liq bi al-shart and apply it to distinguish between valid and invalid conditional legal acts.

Core Idea
Ta'liq bi al-shart is valid only in pure waivers and oaths, not in contracts of exchange.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: ta'liq bi al-shart (suspension by condition) is restricted to pure waivers (isqatat mahdah) and oaths. Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly applying conditional language in family law, commercial transactions, and vows. While other schools may allow conditional contracts, the Hanafi school maintains that contracts require immediate effect.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
The maxim states: التعليق بالشرط يختص بالإسقاطات المحضة ويحلف بها. Meaning: Suspension by condition is specific to pure waivers and oaths.
This is the core statement.
2
Identify the two permitted categories
Pure waivers (isqatat mahdah) include divorce, manumission, and forgiving debts. Oaths (yamin) include conditional vows like 'If I do X, I will give charity.'
3
Recognize the excluded category
Contracts of exchange (mu'awadat) like sale, lease, and marriage cannot be suspended on a future condition. They must be immediate.
4
Apply the maxim
Given a scenario, determine if the conditional act falls under isqatat or yamin. If yes, it is valid; if not, it is invalid.
This requires knowing the definition of pure waivers.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement: a legal maxim. It means that suspending a legal effect on a future condition (ta'liq bil-shart) is valid only in two categories: (1) pure waivers (isqatat mahdah) such as divorce, manumission, or releasing a right, and (2) oaths (yamin) like conditional vows. In contracts like sales or leases, suspension is generally invalid because they require immediate effect. This is a distinctive Hanafi principle.

الإسقاطات المحضة (al-isqatat al-mahdah): Pure waivers or relinquishments of rights, such as divorce, freeing a slave, or forgiving a debt.

Confession of a Sick Person: When Does It Not Prove a Debt?
Ruling with proof on the priority of debts in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the confession of a terminally ill person and apply it to determine priority among debts.

Core Idea
A confession that nullifies another's right is not a valid proof.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: a confession (iqrar) is only binding on the confessor. When a terminally ill person confesses to debts that would harm existing creditors, the confession loses its probative value. The passage from Al-Hidayah establishes that debts from health and known causes take precedence over debts confessed during terminal illness. Understanding this rule is crucial for protecting creditors' rights in estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a confession (iqrar) by a terminally ill person. Ruling: it is not considered a proof if it nullifies another's right.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the principle that confession is invalid when it nullifies another's right. The proof includes analogy (qiyas) to mortgage/lease: one cannot dispose of mortgaged property, similarly the sick person cannot confess to new debts harming existing creditors.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The key phrase: 'إِذَا تَضَمَّنَ إِبْطَالَ حَقِّ الْغَيْرِ' (if it involves nullification of another's right). The word 'تضمن' indicates the mere presence of nullification negates evidentiary value.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
This is the Hanafi position; other schools may treat all debts equally. The Hanafi view is based on confession being a limited proof (hujjat qasirah).
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi is that debts of health and known causes are prioritized over debts confessed in terminal illness.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the confession of a sick person. The author begins by stating the general principle: a confession is not accepted as a proof if it involves the nullification of another's right. In Urdu, it is explained that a confession is a limited proof (hujjat qasirah), meaning it is only binding on the confessor and not on third parties. The specific application is when a terminally ill person confesses to debts while there are existing debts from his period of health or debts with known causes during his illness. In such a case, the earlier debts take precedence over the confessed debts. The proof is that the confession of the sick person would otherwise nullify the rights of the earlier creditors, whose rights are already attached to the property for satisfaction. This is analogous to other situations: if a person has mortgaged or leased an item, he cannot later dispose of it because that would nullify the rights of the mortgagee or lessee. Similarly, the sick person's confession to new debts would nullify the rights of existing creditors. The author cites Al-Inayah to support that the right of creditors from health is attached to the property, and hence the sick person is prohibited from making donations or favors that exceed one-third of his estate. Therefore, the confession of the sick person is not considered a valid proof against the earlier creditors.

Cause of the Forbidden is Forbidden
Uses maxim to prohibit dawa‘i (touching, kissing, looking) for muzahir before kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'sabab al-haram haram' and apply it to the case of zihar to determine that touching, kissing, and looking with desire before kaffarah are haram.

Core Idea
The cause of the forbidden is also forbidden: if an act necessarily leads to haram, it becomes haram.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi legal maxim derived from the Quran and applied in al-Hidayah to the case of zihar (a form of divorce-like oath). While the prohibition of intercourse before expiation is clear, the question arises whether preliminary acts such as touching, kissing, or looking with desire are also forbidden. The maxim 'sabab al-haram haram' resolves this by ruling that the cause of the forbidden is itself forbidden, thus making these motives (dawā‘ī) haram as well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its Quranic source
The maxim 'sabab al-haram haram' is derived from the verse 'Do not say Ra'ina' (al-Baqarah: 104), where a permissible word was prohibited because it could lead to harm. This principle is then applied to the case of zihar.
The maxim is a legal rule (qa‘ida fiqhiyya) that governs cases where a permissible act becomes a means to a forbidden end.
2
Understand the context of zihar
A muzahir (man who declares zihar) must pay kaffarah before resuming marital relations. Intercourse before kaffarah is explicitly forbidden by Quran (al-Mujadilah: 3).
The prohibition applies to intercourse itself; the question is whether it extends to its preliminaries.
3
Apply the maxim to the preliminaries (dawā‘ī)
Touching, kissing, and looking with desire are causes that naturally lead to intercourse. Since the end is haram, the means are also haram by application of the maxim. This is analogous to the rules of i‘tikaf, where both intercourse and its motives are prohibited.
The author explicitly compares to i‘tikaf to reinforce the reasoning.
4
Confirm the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah) is that these acts are haram before kaffarah. This is mukhtalaf fih; some scholars may consider them merely makruh, but the Hanafi view relies on the inevitability of leading to intercourse.
The ruling is based on Quran and qiyas (analogy) from i‘tikaf.

The Principle that the Cause of Haram is Haram
Explaining that permissible acts become haram if they lead to haram, using zihar and i'tikaf examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that the cause of haram is haram, explain its derivation from the Quranic verse, and apply it to the cases of zihar and i'tikaf.

Core Idea
A permissible act becomes haram if it inevitably leads to a haram act.

In this lesson, we explore a key Hanafi legal principle: sabab al-haram haram (the cause of the haram is haram). This principle extends the prohibition of a haram act to its necessary preludes, even if those preludes would otherwise be permissible. The passage from Al-Hidayah illustrates this using the example of a man who has declared zihar and is prohibited from intimate contact until he pays expiation, and draws an analogy with the state of i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its basis
The principle is that the cause of haram is haram. Its basis is the Quranic verse prohibiting saying 'ra'ina' because it could be misused.
The verse shows that even a permissible word becomes haram if it leads to harm.
2
Examine the application to zihar
The passage states that a muzahir (one who has done zihar) is prohibited from touching, kissing, or looking with desire before expiation, because these acts lead to the prohibited intercourse.
Intercourse before expiation is explicitly haram due to Quran 58:3.
3
Understand the analogy with i'tikaf
The author compares to i'tikaf, where intercourse and its preludes are also prohibited, showing consistency of the principle.
In i'tikaf, the prohibition of preludes is well-established.
4
Trace the dalil (Quran and Qiyas)
The Quranic verse (58:3) obligates expiation before touching, and the principle is derived from the general directive to avoid causes of haram.
The principle is further supported by qiyas (analogy) between zihar and i'tikaf.
5
Apply the principle to other contexts

Commentary

The author explains that if a permissible act becomes a means to achieve something haram, that act itself becomes haram. The author draws an analogy with i'tikaf, where intercourse and its preludes are also prohibited. The passage cites from Al-'Inayah and Al-Binayah to support the ruling. The key point is that the prohibition extends not only to the final haram act but also to its immediate causes.

Accepting a Trustworthy Person's Report on Husband's Death or Divorce
Explains conditions for accepting a single trustworthy report about marriage dissolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a woman may act on a single person's report about her husband's death or divorce, and distinguish it from cases requiring full testimony.

Core Idea
A newly occurring decisive event without a contradictor can be proven by a single trustworthy report.

This passage from Usool al-Hidayah addresses a practical scenario: a woman receives news of her husband's death or triple divorce from a single trustworthy person. The Hanafi school permits her to act on this report because the event is newly occurring and there is no one to contradict it. We will explore the underlying principle and its limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a woman acting on a report by observing iddah and remarrying. The ruling is that it is permissible (la ba'sa) according to the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the principle that a single report is accepted for a newly occurring decisive event (qati' tari') if there is no contradictor (munazi'). This is based on istihsan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The 'illa (effective cause) is that the event is tari' (newly occurring) and there is no munazi' (contradictor). The original marriage does not contradict because it does not indicate continuance.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools may require two witnesses for any dissolution. The Hanafi school holds this istihsan as stronger.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi is that a single trustworthy report suffices for tari' events without contradictor. This is the established position from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage from Usool al-Hidayah discusses the acceptance of a report from a single person regarding the dissolution of a marriage. The type is a ruling with proof, using the principle that a newly occurring decisive factor (like death or divorce) without any contradictor can be established by a single report. The text first states the principle: if the information concerns a newly occurring event and there is no one to contradict it, the report of one trustworthy person is accepted. If the reporter is not trustworthy, additional corroboration is needed. Then an objection is raised: one might argue that the husband's ownership (milk) over his wife is established, and established ownership cannot be overturned by a single report. The answer is that the husband's ownership is only proven by istishab (presumption of continuity), which is weaker than a trustworthy report. The passage then gives concrete examples: if a trustworthy person informs the woman that her husband died or gave her three divorces, she may begin her iddah and remarry because the event is newly occurring and there is no contradictor. However, if a man informs her that her original marriage was invalid (e.g., due to fosterage or apostasy), she cannot act on it because the husband claims the marriage is valid, creating a contradiction. In such a case, she needs two male witnesses or a man and two women. The Hanafi position is clearly stated: the report of a single trustworthy person is sufficient for newly occurring events without contradictors. This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah. The commentary follows the author's order: first the principle, then the objection and answer, then the examples.
