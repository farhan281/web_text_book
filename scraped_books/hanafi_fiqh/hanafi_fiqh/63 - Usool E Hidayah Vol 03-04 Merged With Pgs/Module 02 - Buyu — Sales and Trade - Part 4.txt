Condition for Shuf'a: Exchange of Property for Property
Stating the Hanafi condition that shuf'a is only established in exchange of property for property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for shuf'a (pre-emption) and determine whether a transaction qualifies based on the nature of the consideration.

Core Idea
Shuf'a requires that the price be property (mal); otherwise the right does not arise.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'a) allows a co-owner or neighbour to purchase property sold to a third party. In the Hanafi school, this right is not automatic for every sale; it depends on the nature of the transaction. This lesson examines a foundational condition: the consideration (price) must be 'property' (mal). Understanding this condition clarifies when shuf'a applies and when it does not, as illustrated by the rule of price reduction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognise that shuf'a is a right to take property by paying its sale price. Here, the ruling is that this right is wajib (obligatory) only conditionally.
The condition is the focus of this passage.
2
Parse the condition: mubadala al-mal bil mal
Break down the Arabic phrase: mubadala (exchange), mal (property), 'an exchange of property for property'. Both the sold object and the price must be mal according to Islamic law.
Mal includes valuable, permissible items; excludes non-valuable or prohibited things.
3
Understand what is excluded
If the price is not mal (e.g., services, a forbidden substance, or a mere promise), the transaction does not create shuf'a. This is the key limiting condition.
Contrast with schools that allow shuf'a for non-property considerations.
4
Apply to price reduction scenario
The passage links the condition to the rule of partial price reduction: since the price is mal, a reduction of part of it attaches to the contract, and the shafi' (pre-emptor) takes the property at the reduced price. A reduction of the entire price would make the contract no longer a property-for-property exchange, so it does not bind the shafi'.
This shows the practical consequence of the condition.
5
Verify cross-madhhab awareness
Note that other schools may have a broader definition of shuf'a. This condition is specific to the Hanafi school and based on zahir al-riwayah.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement establishing a fundamental condition for the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) in the Hanafi madhhab. The core principle is that shuf'a is only established when the transaction involves an exchange of property (mal) for property (mal). This means that both the sold property (the pre-empted property) and the consideration (price) must be considered 'mal' (property) according to Islamic law. If the price is not property—for example, if it is a non-monetary exchange like barter with services, or if the consideration is something that is not legally considered property (such as a prohibited substance)—then shuf'a does not apply. The commentary then links this principle to the earlier discussion of price reduction: the fact that shuf'a is about taking the property at the price (thaman) reinforces that the price must be mal. The author uses this to explain that partial reduction of price attaches to the contract (as seen in the previous lesson) precisely because the price is the consideration and its reduction is part of the exchange. The authority of this position is from Al-Hidayah, and it is a foundational rule in Hanafi shuf'a law.

Key Terms
مال (mal): Property: anything that has value and is legally permissible for use and trade according to Islamic law.
مبادلة (mubadala): Exchange: a transaction where one property is given in return for another, typically a sale.
شفعة (shuf'a): Pre-emption: the right to acquire property sold to a third party by paying the price, established in certain types of property exchanges.

Shuf'ah Requires Exchange of Property for Property
Stating the fundamental principle for validity of pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that shuf'ah only arises in property-for-property transactions to determine when pre-emption applies or does not apply, and explain the effect of price reduction.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only applies when both the sold property and its price are lawful property (mal); non-property considerations invalidate pre-emption.

This lesson examines a fundamental principle of Hanafi pre-emption law: shuf'ah (the right of a co-owner to step into a sale) is only established when the transaction is an exchange of property for property. The passage from Al-Hidayah states this rule and then applies it to two categories: price reduction by the seller, and transactions where the consideration is not property. Understanding this principle will enable you to identify when shuf'ah can be claimed and when it cannot.

Guided Path
1
State the fundamental rule
Identify the explicit statement: 'shuf'ah is only established in exchange of property for property.' Recognize that this is the core Hanafi position.
The Arabic phrase 'مبادلة المال بالمال' is key.
2
Apply the rule to price reduction
Consider two scenarios: (1) seller reduces part of the price → pre-emptor benefits; (2) seller forgives the entire price → pre-emptor still pays full price. Analyze why the rule produces these results.
Reduction of part is attached to the original contract; forgiveness of whole is not.
3
Apply the rule to excluded transactions
List the five examples given where shuf'ah does not apply: house as dower, khul', rent, settlement for homicide, exchange for freeing a slave. For each, explain why the consideration is not property (mal).
The pre-emptor cannot own or replicate these non-monetary considerations.
4
Identify the underlying reasoning (ta'lil)
Understand that the rule is founded on the condition that the pre-emptor must be able to take the property on the same terms as the buyer. If the consideration is not property, this condition cannot be met.
The text says 'because it is possible to observe the condition of the Law.'

Commentary
This passage presents a key ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) only arises in transactions where both the sold property and its price are property (mal). The author of Al-Hidayah first states this principle, then uses it to explain two related issues: (1) the effect of price reduction by the seller, and (2) cases where shuf'ah does not apply. For price reduction: if the seller reduces part of the price, the pre-emptor benefits from that reduction because it is considered part of the original contract; if the seller forgives the entire price, the pre-emptor is not excused from paying—he must pay the full price because forgiving the whole price is not part of the original contract. For non-applicability: shuf'ah does not apply when a house is given as dower (mahr), used for khul' (divorce initiated by wife with compensation), given as rent, used as settlement for intentional homicide (blood money), or in exchange for freeing a slave. The reason is that in these cases, the consideration is not property (mal) in the legal sense, or the pre-emptor cannot take the property on the same terms as the buyer. All these examples stem from the core rule: shuf'ah is only for money-for-property transactions.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf'ah): The right of pre-emption allowing a co-owner or partner to acquire the sold share at the price paid by the buyer.
مبادلة المال بالمال (mubadalat al-mal bi al-mal): Exchange of property for property, meaning the consideration must be a legal property (mal).
حط الثمن (hatt al-thaman): Reduction or forgiveness of part or all of the price by the seller.

Cases Where Shuf'ah Does Not Apply
Listing cases where shuf'ah is not established due to non-monetary consideration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the transactions where shuf'ah does not apply and explain the Hanafi principle of mubadalah al-mal bi al-mal.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only applies when both sides of the transaction are property (mal).

This lesson examines specific transactions where the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) does not arise, even though a house is transferred. The Hanafi school restricts shuf'ah to exchanges of property for property (mubadalah al-mal bi al-mal). Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying shuf'ah rules in real-life situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common factor
Review the list of transactions (dower, khul', rent, blood money settlement, manumission) and find what they share: the house is given as consideration for something other than money or property.
Each transaction involves a non-monetary countervalue.
2
State the Hanafi principle
The governing rule: 'Shuf'ah is only wajib in an exchange of property for property.' This is the key principle that excludes the listed cases.
This principle is derived from the basis that shuf'ah protects a co-owner/neighbour from an unwanted partner; it only applies when the buyer paid property (mal).
3
Explain why non-monetary considerations are not mal
Understand the Hanafi definition of mal: tangible property with market value. Dower (mahr), khul' payment, rent (usufruct), blood money (diya), and manumission (itq) are not considered mal in this context because they are personal obligations or services.
Thus, the pre-emptor cannot offer the same consideration.
4
Apply the principle to each case
For each given scenario, confirm that the consideration is not property, therefore the transaction is not a sale (or exchange of property) for shuf'ah purposes.
The list covers the major cases; the principle can be extended to analogous situations.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Contrast the Hanafi position with the Shafi'i view, which may allow shuf'ah if the non-monetary consideration has equivalent value.
This difference stems from different definitions of mal and the scope of shuf'ah.

How Khiyar Affects Shuf'ah: Seller vs Buyer Option
Ruling with proof: effect of seller/buyer option on pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for shuf'ah when the seller or buyer has a khiyar option and explain the underlying principle of ownership removal.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah requires full removal of seller's ownership; a seller's option blocks it, a buyer's option does not.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) is not automatic—it hinges on a precise condition: the seller must have completely divested ownership. When a sale includes an option (khiyar), the timing of that divestment depends on who holds the option. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, grounded in the principle of 'zawal al-milk' (removal of ownership), and shows how it applies to two common scenarios: seller's option and buyer's option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the master principle
The text states: 'Shuf'ah is based on the removal of ownership from the seller.' Recognize that this is the foundational rule from which all derivative rulings flow.
This principle is qat'i within the Hanafi school.
2
Apply the principle to seller's option
If the seller stipulates an option for himself, ownership has not yet left him. Therefore, shuf'ah does not arise immediately. The sale is suspended until the option is dropped or expires.
The option prevents removal of ownership (yamna' zawal al-milk).
3
Apply the principle to buyer's option
If the buyer stipulates an option, ownership still leaves the seller by consensus. Hence, shuf'ah is established immediately upon the contract.
The buyer's option does not affect the seller's divestment (la yamna' al-zawal).
4
Trace the proof (wajh al-dalalah)
The proof is rational (qiyas): ownership is a legal meaning that is either removed or not; the existence of an option in the seller's hands keeps it attached, while an option in the buyer's hands does not. This is clear from the nature of ownership and contract.
The dalil is 'aqli, not naqli, but its dalalah is qat'i in Hanafi usul.
5
Note the practical difference and ikhtilaf
The ruling differs if the option is later dropped: shuf'ah becomes obligatory from that point. Some non-Hanafi scholars may hold that shuf'ah is never established until the sale is final, but the Hanafi position is relied upon.
The cross-madhhab dispute is legitimate; Hanafi tarjih rests on the agreed-upon principle.

Commentary
This passage establishes a fundamental rule in Hanafi law of pre-emption: shuf'ah depends on the removal of ownership from the seller (zawal al-milk 'an al-ba'i'). The reason is that the pre-emptor's right is to step into the buyer's shoes, which only makes sense if the seller has fully divested himself. The commentary then applies this rule to sales with an option (khiyar) condition. If the seller stipulates an option for himself, the sale is conditional, and ownership does not fully transfer until the option is either exercised or dropped. Hence, no shuf'ah arises until the option is dropped. If the buyer has the option, ownership transfers immediately because by consensus the seller's ownership is removed upon conclusion of the sale, even though the buyer may later rescind. Therefore, shuf'ah is established immediately. The text also notes that if the seller's option is later dropped, shuf'ah becomes obligatory, and the pre-emptor must make his demand after that point.

Key Terms
زوال الملك (zawal al-milk): The removal of ownership from the seller; it is the basis upon which the right of pre-emption arises.
خيار (khiyar): Option or right to rescind a contract within a specified period.

Principles of Shuf'ah: Ownership Transfer and Absolute Exchange
States two foundational principles of shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for shuf'ah (transfer of ownership from seller and absolute exchange) and apply them to cases of khiyar and partition.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah requires definitive ownership transfer and absolute exchange; no pre-emption in partitions or seller's option sales.

This passage from al-Hidayah lays down two foundational principles that determine when the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) arises. Understanding these principles is crucial because they distinguish valid pre-emption claims from invalid ones in common transactional scenarios such as sales with options and property partitions. The author uses these principles to explain why shuf'ah is not established when the seller retains an option (khiyar) or when co-owners merely partition their property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first principle
The passage states that shuf'ah is based on the removal of ownership from the seller. This means that as long as the seller retains any ownership interest (e.g., through khiyar), the pre-emptor's right does not arise.
The key Arabic phrase: 'الشُّفْعَةُ تَبْتَنِي عَلَى زَوَالِ الْمِلْكِ عَنِ الْبَائِعِ'.
2
Apply to seller's khiyar
If the seller sells with a condition of option for himself, ownership does not definitively transfer. Therefore, shuf'ah is not established. It becomes obligatory only when the option is dropped.
The text: 'فَإِنْ أُسْقِطَ الْخِيَارُ وَجَبَتِ الشُّفَعَةُ'.
3
Apply to buyer's khiyar
If the buyer has the option, ownership leaves the seller immediately by consensus, so shuf'ah is established right away.
Contrast with seller's khiyar: ownership transfer is prevented only in seller's option.
4
Identify the second principle
Shuf'ah is only legislated in absolute exchange (mubadalah mutlaqah), i.e., a full commutative transaction where property is exchanged for property.
The Arabic: 'الشُّفْعَةُ مَا شُرِعَتْ إِلَّا فِي مُبَادَلَةِ الْمُطْلَقَةِ'.
5
Apply to partition (qismah)
Partition among co-owners is a separation (ifraz) of rights, not an exchange. Since it lacks the element of absolute exchange, shuf'ah does not apply to a neighbor who is not a party to the partition.
The judge can enforce partition, confirming it is not a voluntary exchange.

Commentary
This passage from Usool-e-Hidayah presents two fundamental principles of Hanafi pre-emption law. The first principle establishes that the right of shuf'ah depends entirely on the transfer of ownership away from the seller. As long as the seller retains any ownership interest, the pre-emptor's right does not arise. This is illustrated by the case of a sale with a condition of option (khiyar) for the seller: since the seller can still cancel the sale, ownership has not definitively transferred, so no shuf'ah. Conversely, if the option is for the buyer, ownership leaves the seller immediately by consensus, so shuf'ah is established. However, if the seller's option is later dropped, the transfer becomes complete, and shuf'ah arises, with the demand (talab) required at that moment. The second principle limits shuf'ah to truly commutative transactions (mubadalah mutlaqah) — exchanges where property is swapped for property in a full bilateral sense. This excludes mere partitions (qismah) among co-owners, which are legally categorized as separation (ifraz) rather than exchange. Since the judge can compel a co-owner to partition, it lacks the voluntary exchange element essential for shuf'ah. Thus, despite the common misconception, a neighbor does not gain pre-emption rights from a simple division of jointly owned land.

مُبَادَلَةُ الْمُطْلَقَة (mubadalah al-mutlaqah): Absolute exchange; a fully commutative transaction where property is exchanged for property in every respect.

The Hanafi Maxim: Shuf'ah Is Not a Settled Right
Explaining the first maxim on shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that shuf'ah is not a settled right and apply it to explain why accepting compensation for it is invalid.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is merely a right to acquire, not a right in the property — it cannot be sold or exchanged.

The Hanafi school establishes a foundational principle for the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah): it is not a settled right in the property itself, but only a personal right to acquire ownership. This distinction carries a critical legal consequence — the pre-emptor cannot trade his right for money or other consideration. In this lesson, we will examine this maxim from al-Hidayah and see how it invalidates any compromise (ṣulḥ) made for a consideration in exchange for shuf'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
The maxim is: حَقُّ الشُّفَعَةِ لَيْسَ بِحَقِّ مُتَقَرَّرٍ فِى الْمَحَلّ. It means the right of shuf'ah is not a settled right in the property.
This maxim is the foundation for rulings on shuf'ah compensation.
2
Understand the distinction
A settled right (like ownership) is fixed in the property and can be compensated. Shuf'ah is merely a right to acquire ownership (ḥaqq al-tamalluk); it does not change before or after taking consideration.
If a right changes with consideration, it is not settled.
3

Since shuf'ah is not a settled right, taking compensation for it is invalid. Therefore, if a pre-emptor makes a compromise (ṣulḥ) with a third party for a consideration in exchange for his shuf'ah, the shuf'ah becomes void and the consideration must be returned.

The Second Maxim on Shuf'ah: Abandonment Invalidates the Right
Explaining the second maxim on loss of shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding the loss of shuf'ah by the seller and his agent, and distinguish it from the buyer's retention.

Core Idea
Selling shows abandonment; shuf'ah, a weak right, is lost by abandonment but not by desire.

This lesson explains the second maxim in the chapter on shuf'ah from al-Hidayah. The maxim draws a critical distinction: the seller and his agent lose the right of pre-emption because their act of selling indicates they have turned away from the property, while the buyer and his agent retain it because their purchase shows desire. We will unpack the reasoning and see the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
The maxim states: 'Whoever sells, or for whom a sale is made, has no shuf'ah, unlike the buyer.' The seller and his agent lose shuf'ah; the buyer and his agent do not.
2
Understand the Reasoning (I'rad)
The seller's act of selling evidences i'rad (abandonment) of the property. Shuf'ah is a weak right (haqq da'if) that is invalidated by abandonment, not by desire.
The key term is i'rad – turning away from the property.
3
Apply to the Seller's Agent
If the seller's agent sells the property, even if he is also a shafi' (potential pre-emptor), he loses shuf'ah because his participation in the sale shows abandonment.
The agent's selling is attributed to the seller; the agent's own shuf'ah right is also void.
4
Contrast with the Buyer's Agent
The buyer's agent acts on behalf of the buyer, whose purchase shows raghbah (desire). Therefore, the buyer's agent retains the right to claim shuf'ah.
Desire strengthens the right; only abandonment weakens it.
5
State the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the seller and his agent lose shuf'ah, while the buyer and his agent retain it, based on the principle that shuf'ah is lost through i'rad.
This is consistently recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah sources like al-Hidayah.

The Principle of No Compulsion on Contracts and Its Applications
States the maxim 'no compulsion on contracts' and applies it to shuf'ah and qasim cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'la jabr ala al-uqud', explain its rational proof, and apply it to the cases of shuf'ah (pre-emption) and qasim (partitioner).

Core Idea
No compulsion on contracts: consent is essential; even a judge cannot force a contract or appoint a specific partitioner.

This lesson examines the foundational Hanafi principle that contracts require mutual consent. The author of al-Hidayah presents the maxim 'la jabr ala al-uqud' and then applies it to two practical scenarios—pre-emption and partition—showing that the judge has no power to compel parties in these contractual matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its legal consequence
Read the opening statement: 'لا جبر على العقود'. Understand that this means no compulsion is valid in any contract. The judge cannot force anyone into a contractual relationship.
This is a general principle, not limited to sale or hire.
2
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The maxim is proven by reason: contracts require mutual consent (taradi), and compulsion negates consent. Without consent, a contract is invalid. This is also supported by the Quranic verse 'an taradin minkum' (4:29).
The proof is considered qat'i (definitive) in transmission and meaning.
3
Apply to shuf'ah: agent for seller vs agent for buyer
If a person acts as agent for the seller while being the pre-emptor, he cannot claim shuf'ah because he was instrumental in completing the sale. Seeking to undo his own act contradicts the principle. Conversely, if he is agent for the buyer, he retains shuf'ah because the purchase is a new act of desire.
The key distinction is whether the person is trying to undo what he himself caused.
4
Apply to qasim: why the judge cannot fix a specific partitioner
The judge cannot compel co-owners to hire a particular qasim (partitioner) because that would be a forced contract. Additionally, fixing one qasim could lead to exploitation—he might demand excessive wages. The right to choose belongs to the parties.
The rationales are: no compulsion on contracts + prevention of harm (la darar).
5
Extract the broader implication
The maxim applies to all contracts and even to non-contractual appointments that require consent, such as selecting a qasim. The judge's role is to facilitate, not coerce.
This principle upholds individual autonomy in financial and personal dealings.

Judicial Division Requires Two Opposing Parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for judicial division in Hanafi law and explain why one heir alone cannot suffice even with proof.

Core Idea
One person cannot be both opponent and opposed; two parties must be present for judicial division.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a fundamental procedural requirement in Islamic estate division. You might think that if an heir produces clear evidence, the judge can proceed immediately. The text, however, shows that the presence of two adverse parties is a non-negotiable condition for the validity of judicial division. Understanding this principle protects absent heirs from having their rights adjudicated without representation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is judicial division (qismah) of an estate. The ruling is that the judge must not divide when only one heir is present, even if that heir brings proof (bayyinah).
The ruling is batil (invalid) if attempted without the condition.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a qiyas (analogy) drawn from the nature of litigation (khusumah). Just as a court requires two parties for a lawsuit, division is treated as a bilateral exchange requiring two opponents.
No direct Quranic or hadith text is cited; the reasoning is rational (dalil aqli).

Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning)
The text argues: if the sole heir acts for himself, there is no opponent for the deceased or absent heirs. If he acts for both himself and the absent, then no one stands to oppose him and challenge the evidence. Hence, one person cannot fulfill both roles.
The principle is that a single person cannot be both opponent and opposed (mukhasim wa mukhasam).

Some schools may permit division with one party if the absent heir's share is reserved. The Hanafi school disagrees, requiring two present parties.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.

Evaluate the tarjih (preferred position)
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi and is based on Zahir al-Riwayah. No intra-Hanafi difference is recorded here.
The ruling is authoritative for fatwa.

Core Idea
Qismah (physical division) overrides muhaya'ah because it finalizes ownership completely.

When two people co-own property, they can either physically divide it (qismah) or take turns using it (muhaya'ah). The Hanafi rule gives priority to physical division if one party demands it, because ownership of the physical asset is primary and more complete. The passage from al-Hidayah establishes this hierarchy with rational evidence.

The passage asserts that qismah is stronger (aqwa) than muhaya'ah. This is the core legal rule.
The word 'aqwa' in Arabic indicates precedence.
3
Understand the rational proof
The proof is based on the relationship between the physical asset ('ayn) and its usufruct (manfa'ah). The 'ayn is the origin and the manfa'ah is subsidiary. The origin is naturally stronger than its subsidiary.
This is a dalil aqli (rational evidence) – not a text.
4
Apply to a dispute
If one co-owner demands qismah and the other demands muhaya'ah, the judge must enforce qismah because it is the stronger and more complete partition.
The judge has no discretion; the ruling is mandatory.

The passage mentions that this issue is repeated in the chapter to serve as a basis for other rulings, showing its importance.
This indicates that the principle is foundational for multiple derivative cases.

Division is Stronger than Temporary Allocation
Maxim and application to co-owner disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that division (qismah) is stronger than temporary allocation (muhayah) to resolve conflicts between co-owners demanding different forms of partition.

Core Idea
Division transfers ownership; muhayah only grants use. Hence division prevails.

When co-owners disagree on how to end joint ownership, the Hanafi school gives priority to physical division over mere allocation of use. This lesson unpacks the maxim 'al-qismatu aqwa min al-muhayah' and its practical applications in property disputes. You will learn why division is considered legally stronger and how a judge must rule when partners demand different forms of partition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
Two co-owners disagree: one demands physical division (qismah) of the property, the other demands temporary allocation of use (muhayah). Which claim prevails?
The conflict is between two legitimate forms of partition.
2
State the maxim
The maxim: 'al-qismatu aqwa min al-muhayah' – division is stronger than temporary allocation. Understand why: division transfers ownership, while muhayah only transfers usufruct temporarily.
The original (division) is stronger than the subordinate (muhayah).
3
Apply the maxim to the dispute
The judge must order division because it is more effective (ablagh) in completing the separation of ownership. The demand for division takes priority over the demand for muhayah.
This is a procedural ruling: the judge is obliged to grant the request for division.
4
Recognize the exceptions
If the co-owners voluntarily agree to muhayah in a way that mirrors a permissible division (e.g., one takes the upper floor, the other the lower), it is valid as an acknowledgement. But if one later demands division, it voids the muhayah.
Exception: muhayah that resembles division is permissible, but it remains weaker.

Commentary
This passage from Usool al-Hidayah presents a key Hanafi legal principle regarding proof of defects (mufsidat) in contracts. The principle distinguishes between two types of defects: (1) tari (supervening), which arises after the contract's conclusion, and (2) muqarin (concomitant), which existed at the time of the contract. For a tari defect, the report of a single just ('adl) person is sufficient to establish it. For a muqarin defect, the standard requirement of two male witnesses or one male and two females (shahada) is necessary. The author applies this principle to marriage (nikah). If a woman receives news from a reliable person that her absent husband has died or divorced her (tari defect), she may act upon it and remarry after 'iddah. If the news concerns a defect present at the contract, such as the husband being an apostate or a foster brother (muqarin defect), formal testimony is needed. The passage then addresses a quote from Al-Hidayah and a potential objection: since the husband's ownership (milk) over the wife is established, how can it be nullified by a single reporter? The Hanafi response is that the husband's ownership is not established by a definitive proof (dalil mujib) but only by istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). Since khabar al-wahid (the report of one) is stronger than istishab, it suffices to override the presumption. This illustrates the Hanafi prioritization of evidence. The passage concludes by reinforcing that the rule applies only to supervening defects; for concomitant ones, the standard two-witness requirement stands.

مفسد (mufsid): That which corrupts or invalidates a contract; a defect or invalidating factor.
طارئ (tari'): Supervening, arising after the contract's conclusion, not present at the time of the contract.
مقارن (muqarin): Concomitant, present at the time of the contract from the beginning.
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): Report of a single person; used in a broader sense than shahadat, can be accepted if the reporter is trustworthy and the matter is supervening.
ثقة (thiqah): Trustworthy, reliable; a person whose report carries weight in legal matters.

Benefits Not Compensable Except by Contract or Shubha
Establishes a principle with rational proof and application to diyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that benefits are not compensable without a contract or shubha, and apply it to determine when compensation is due for lost or diminished benefits.

Core Idea
Benefits are accidents, so they are not property unless linked to a contract.

This lesson explains a foundational Hanafi principle: benefits (manafi') like the use of a house or labor are not inherently compensable. Compensation is only due when there is a valid contract (e.g., lease) or a semblance of a contract (e.g., a defective lease). The rationale is that benefits are accidents (a'rad) without permanence, so they cannot be treated as property. We will see how this principle applies to diyat (blood money) cases, such as the healing of a head wound.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
State the principle from the text: 'Benefits are not compensable except by a contract or a semblance of a contract.'
2
Understand the nature of benefits
Understand that benefits are considered accidents (a'rad) with no permanence, thus not property (mal).
3
Identify the exceptions
Identify the two exceptions: valid contract (e.g., ijarah sahihah) and semblance of a contract (e.g., ijarah fasidah).
4
Apply to the wound case
Apply the principle to the case: a wound heals and hair grows back; no indemnity for the benefit of recovery because no contract exists between the parties.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Recognize that other schools may treat benefits differently; Hanafi strictly limits compensability.

The Invalidity of Deferment in Specific Goods
Stating the legal maxim that deferment is invalid in specific tangible goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that deferment in 'ayan is invalid and apply it to sale and diyat scenarios.

Core Idea
Deferment is invalid for present goods ('ayan) because there is no necessity; only the price may be deferred.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi maxim governing deferred delivery in contracts. The principle distinguishes between price (thaman) and specific goods ('ayan). Understanding this rule helps you identify when a deferment condition is void under Hanafi fiqh and how it applies in practice, such as in the sale of a car or the handover of a slave as compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
State the legal maxim: 'التَّأْجِيلُ فِي الْأَعْيَانِ بَاطِلٌ' (Deferment in specific goods is invalid). This is the core ruling of the lesson.
The maxim is a general principle, not limited to sales but applicable to any transfer of 'ayan.
2
Understand the Rationale
Explain why deferment is invalid in 'ayan: necessity (zarurah) justifies deferring the price (because the buyer may lack funds), but 'ayan are already present, so there is no need for delay.
Necessity is the key illa (effective cause) that differentiates price from goods.
3
Apply to the Slave and Ransom Case
The Hidayah applies the maxim to a master who chooses to hand over a slave who committed homicide. The slave is an 'ayn, so deferment of his handover is invalid. Similarly, if ransom (fidyah) is given instead, it is a substitute for the slave and thus also cannot be deferred.
The principle extends to substitutes (na'ib) of 'ayan.
4
Distinguish from Deferment of Price
Contrast with deferring the price: the Hanafi school allows ta'jil in thaman because of the buyer's need for time to pay. This exception is based on necessity and does not apply to 'ayan.
The maxim is specific to 'ayan; deferment of debt (dayn) is treated differently.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement presenting a Hanafi legal maxim: 'Deferment in specific goods is invalid.' The commentary explains the rationale: deferment (ta'jil) is allowed in the price (thaman) due to the buyer's need for time to pay, but in the case of specific goods ('ayn), the goods are already present, so there is no need for deferment. Consequently, any substitute for specific goods (like ransom for a slave) also cannot be deferred. The Hidayah applies this rule to the case of a slave who commits homicide: if the master chooses to hand over the slave as compensation, it must be done immediately; deferment is not allowed. Similarly, if the master opts to pay ransom (fidyah) instead, that too must be immediate, as it stands in place of the slave.

Key Terms
اعیان (a'yan): Specific, tangible goods present at the time of contract; as opposed to dayn (debt) or thaman (price).
تأجیل (ta'jil): Deferment, postponement of delivery or payment to a future date.
فدیہ (fidyah): Ransom, compensation paid in lieu of handing over the slave.

The Maxim of Valid Exceptions in Contracts
Introduces a rule about valid exceptions in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding exceptions in contracts and apply it to determine whether a given exception is valid.

Core Idea
Only what can be sold alone can be excepted from a contract.

This lesson introduces a fundamental maxim in Hanafi contract law governing exceptions (istithna'). The principle is simple: if an item or right can validly be the sole object of a contract (e.g., sold by itself), then it can also be validly excluded from a larger contract. Conversely, anything that cannot be independently transacted cannot be validly excepted. Understanding this maxim is crucial for drafting valid sale agreements and wills.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its context
The act is making an exception (istithna) within a contract, typically a sale. The maxim provides a condition for its validity.
The exception is a statement like 'except X' that excludes part of the subject matter.
2
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Recite the maxim: 'ما يصح إفراده بالعقد يصح استثناؤه وما لا فلا' meaning 'What is valid to be singled out by contract, its exception is also valid, and what is not, then not.'
Memorizing this precise wording is key.
3
Analyze the logical structure
The maxim is a conditional: if something can be validly contracted alone (ifrad), then its exception is valid; otherwise, it is invalid.
The condition is about the independent contractability of the excepted item.
4
Apply the maxim to concrete examples
Consider a sale of land with an exception of the house on it. Since a house can be sold alone, the exception is valid. If the exception is for an undefined portion (e.g., 'some of the grain'), that cannot be contracted alone due to gharar, so the exception is invalid.
The excepted item must be specific, owned, and transferable.
5
Contrast with common misunderstandings
One might think any specified exception is valid, but the maxim restricts validity to items that are independently contracted. An exception of a non-existent or non-transferable right is invalid.
Always check the independent contractability of the excepted item.
