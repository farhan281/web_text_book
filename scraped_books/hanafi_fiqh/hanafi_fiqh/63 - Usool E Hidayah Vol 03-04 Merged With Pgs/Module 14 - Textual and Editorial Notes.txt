Principles of Division and Sharecropping
Legal maxims on division and sharecropping

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on division with unequal shares and explain when custom overrides qiyas in sharecropping.

Core Idea
Mutual consent validates unequal division, and established custom can override analogical reasoning.

This lesson introduces the first set of legal maxims from Usool E Hidayah, covering the chapters on division of property (qismah) and sharecropping (muzāra‘ah). These maxims establish foundational rules about consent, evidence, and the role of custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three maxims on division and their conditions.
Read maxims 11-13 and note that unequal division is permissible with mutual consent, evidence of an outsider takes precedence over the possessor's, and division is stronger than sharing by turns.
Focus on the conditions of validity for each ruling.
2
Understand the priority of evidence in division disputes.
Maxim 12: an outsider's evidence is given precedence over the current possessor's evidence in cases of ownership claims after division.
This is a specific evidentiary rule.
3
Recognize when custom can override qiyas in sharecropping.
Maxim 14 states that qiyas is abandoned due to established custom, meaning that customary practices in sharecropping contracts are upheld even if they differ from analogical reasoning.
Custom must be widespread and consistent.
4
Note the consequences of stipulating conditions that end partnership.
Maxim 15: a condition that terminates the partnership invalidates the muzāra‘ah contract. Maxim 16: delaying a solution is easier than annulling the contract entirely.
These avoid unnecessary nullification.

Commentary
This passage presents a collection of 24 legal maxims (al-qawā‘id al-fiqhiyyah) extracted from the Hanafi work Usool E Hidayah, which itself compiles the principles underlying the rulings of Al-Hidayah. Each maxim is numbered and often preceded by the chapter heading (e.g., Kitāb al-Muzāra‘ah, Kitāb al-Musāqāh). These maxims serve as foundational rules that Hanafi jurists use to derive and apply rulings consistently. For example, principle (11) states that a division of joint property where shares are unequal is valid if both parties agree, highlighting the importance of mutual consent. Principle (12) gives precedence to the evidence of an outsider claiming ownership over the evidence of the current possessor in cases of disputed division. The maxims span various fiqh topics including partnership, agriculture, worship, slaughter, sales, and land revival. The commentator (likely following the method of Usool E Hidayah) presents these principles without detailed proofs, as they are intended for memorization and serve as anchors for legal reasoning. The final maxim (34) refers to the concept that water's natural force supersedes any artificial force, a principle from the chapter on reviving dead lands.

Key Terms
قسمة (qismah): Division of jointly owned property among co-owners
مزارعة (muzāra‘ah): Sharecropping contract between landowner and cultivator
مهاياة (muhāyāh): Sharing by turns the use of joint property without division

Principles of Watering and Slaughter
Legal maxims on watering and slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle that texts are caused, and state the rulings on judge's verdicts and slaughter under necessity.

Core Idea
Legal texts have underlying causes, and a judge's verdict against consensus is void.

This lesson covers maxims from the chapters on watering (musāqāh) and slaughter (dhabā’ih). The first maxim highlights that all textual rulings have underlying reasons; the subsequent maxims address judicial authority and the rules of slaughtering animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle about texts.
Maxim 17: 'The principle regarding texts is that they are caused (i.e., have underlying reasons).' This means that every legal ruling from the Quran and Sunnah has an effective cause (illah).
This relates to usul al-fiqh and qiyas.
2
Understand the rule on a judge's verdict contrary to consensus.
Maxim 18: A judge's verdict that contradicts ijma' is unenforceable. This shows that ijma' is binding on judges.
Ijma' here refers to consensus of the Companions or scholars.
3
Examine the principle of achieving the objective.
Maxim 19: 'Disparity of cause is not considered when the objective is attained.' If the intended result is achieved, differences in the means do not affect the ruling.
Applied in slaughter: different methods can be valid if the goal (halal meat) is obtained.
4
Learn the rules of slaughter.
Maxims 20-21: Unnecessary extra pain is disliked, and slaughter under necessity is only allowed when normal slaughter is impossible.
These regulate the method and timing of slaughter.

Principles of Sacrifice (Udhiyah)
Legal maxims on sacrifice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rules on defects in sacrificial animals and the relation between substitute and original.

Core Idea
Acts of worship are not obligatory on one person due to another, and reason cannot determine quantities.

This lesson covers five maxims from the chapter on sacrifice (uḍḥiyah). They address the nature of worship, the role of reason in determining quantities, the impact of defects, and the ruling of substitutes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle regarding acts of worship.
Maxim 22: 'The principle regarding acts of worship is that they are not obligatory on one person due to another.' This means that worship obligations (e.g., sacrifice) are personal; one person cannot be compelled to perform them on behalf of another without consent.
This relates to personal liability in ibadat.
2
Understand the limitation of reason.
Maxim 23: 'Reason cannot determine quantities.' This means that the specific amounts prescribed in Shariah (e.g., time frames, numbers, measures) cannot be changed by human reasoning.
Applies to nisab, prescribed punishments, etc.
3
Learn the rule on defects.
Maxim 24: A severe defect prevents the sacrifice from being valid, while a minor one does not. This requires distinguishing between major and minor defects.
Hanafi fiqh has detailed lists of defects (e.g., blind, lame).
4
Examine the attachment of preliminaries.
Maxim 25: 'The state of slaughter and its preliminaries are attached to slaughter.' This means that actions leading up to the sacrifice (e.g., tying the animal) are considered part of the slaughter process.
5
Understand the ruling of substitutes.
Maxim 26: 'The ruling of a substitute is the same as that of the original.' If an animal is sacrificed on behalf of another person, it takes the same rulings as if that person had performed it personally.

Principles of Disliked Acts and Transactions
Legal maxims on disliked acts and sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rulings on imitating disbelievers, subsidiary matters, and the testimony rules in transactions.

Core Idea
Imitating polytheists in dress is forbidden, and subsidiary matters are not considered in legal rulings.

This lesson presents maxims from the chapter on disliked acts (karāhah) and sections on istibra' and sales. They cover dress, subsidiary issues, statements of disbelievers, and rules for establishing corrupting factors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on imitating polytheists.
Maxim 27: 'Imitating the dress of polytheists is forbidden.' This reflects the principle of non-resemblance to non-believers in external appearance.
This applies to distinctive religious attire.
2
Understand that subsidiary matters are not considered.
Maxim 28: 'Matters of a subsidiary nature are not considered.' This means that minor or secondary aspects do not affect the primary ruling.
Example: a small defect in a garment does not change the ruling of prayer.
3
Learn that a disbeliever's statement is accepted in transactions.
Maxim 29: 'The statement of a disbeliever is accepted in transactions.' In matters of sales, gifts, etc., their word is taken as valid if no reason to doubt.
This facilitates trade.
4
Examine the attribution of rulings to the complete cause.
Maxim 30: 'The ruling is attributed to the complete cause.' A cause that is complete triggers the rule; incomplete causes do not.
5
Understand the cause of prohibition.
Maxim 31: 'The cause of a prohibited thing is itself prohibited.' If a means necessarily leads to haram, that means is also haram.
6
Master the rules of evidence for corrupting factors in sales.
Maxim 32: A corrupting factor (mufsid) that arises later is established by one just witness; if concurrent, it requires two.

The Principle of Prohibition to the Extent of the Obstacle in Hibah Revocation
Ruling on donor's revocation when donee sells part of undivided gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-imtina' bi qadr al-mani'' to determine the donor's right of revocation when the donee has disposed of part of the gifted property.

Core Idea
Revocation is barred only to the extent of the obstacle; the donor retains the right to the unobstructed portion.

In Hanafi fiqh, a donor has the right to revoke a gift (ruju') even after delivery, unless an obstacle prevents it. This lesson examines a specific ruling from Al-Hidayah where the donee sells part of an undivided gifted land. The key principle is that the prohibition of revocation is limited to the extent of the obstacle—so the donor can still revoke the unsold portion. This ruling also illustrates a broader legal maxim used across various chapters of fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the donor's revocation (ruju') of a hibah. The ruling is that it is mubah (permissible) as long as no obstacle (mani') prevents it. Obstacles include the donee disposing of the property (e.g., selling it) or the death of either party.
The default permission for revocation is a distinctive Hanafi position—other schools generally do not allow revocation after delivery.
2
Recognize the obstacle in the scenario
The donee sells half of the undivided gifted land. This sale transfers ownership of that half to a third party, creating an obstacle to revocation for that portion because the property is no longer in the donee's possession.
If the gift were divided or the sale were of a specific part, different rules might apply.
3
Apply the maxim: al-imtina' bi qadr al-mani'
The prohibition of revocation exists only to the extent of the obstacle. Since only half is sold, the obstacle covers only that half. Therefore, the donor may revoke the remaining unsold half.
This maxim is general in Hanafi usul and applies in many contexts beyond hibah.
4
Contrast with the no-sale scenario
If the donee sells nothing, the donor may revoke half of the entire gift. The reasoning: since the donor can revoke the whole gift, revoking part is valid by a primary method (al-tariq al-awwal).
The ability to revoke part when the whole is revocable is a logical extension.
