Istishab al-Hal: Only a Defensive Proof in Inheritance Claims
Ruling on wife's claim of conversion before husband's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that istishab al-hal is a defensive proof (hujjah dafi'ah) to resolve inheritance disputes involving conversion claims.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal preserves the original state; it cannot create a new right to inheritance.

This lesson examines a pivotal inheritance dispute: a Christian wife claims she converted before her Muslim husband's death, seeking inheritance. The heirs deny this. The Hanafi ruling turns on a key usul principle—istishab al-hal is only a defensive proof, not a positive one. By understanding this, you will see why the heirs' word prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting claims
The wife claims she embraced Islam before the husband's death, entitling her to inheritance. The heirs claim she converted after death, barring inheritance. The dispute is purely factual: timing of conversion.
Both sides have no external evidence; the case turns on legal presumption.
2
Locate the Hanafi principle of istishab al-hal
Istishab al-hal presumes that a state of affairs continues unchanged until proven otherwise. In Hanafi usul, this presumption is only a hujjah dafi'ah (defensive proof) used to repel claims, not a hujjah muthbitah (positive proof) to establish rights.
This distinguishes Hanafi views from other schools that may use istishab more expansively.
3
Apply the principle to the wife's claim
The wife uses her current state of being Muslim to argue she was Muslim at the time of death. This would require istishab to act as a positive proof, creating a right to inheritance. Since Hanafi law forbids this, her claim fails.
The wife is in the position of claiming (muthbitah), so istishab cannot assist her.
4
Apply the principle to the heirs' claim
The heirs are repelling (daf') the wife's claim. They rely on the original state: the wife was Christian before death, so she remains Christian at death per istishab. This defensive use is valid.
The heirs need only preserve the status quo, not create a new right.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools, like the Shafi'i, may accept istishab as evidence for both defense and entitlement. In practice, a Shafi'i court might accept the wife's claim if she is currently Muslim, absent counter-evidence.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether the wife inherits or not.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the scope of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) in inheritance cases. The issue is: a Muslim man dies leaving a Christian wife; she claims she converted to Islam before his death, thus seeking inheritance. The heirs claim she converted after his death, barring inheritance. The ruling is that the heirs' statement is accepted (al-qawl li al-warith), not the wife's. The reasoning is based on the Hanafi principle that istishab al-hal is only a 'hujjah dafi'ah' (proof for repelling) not a 'hujjah muthbitah' (proof for establishing rights). The wife is trying to establish a new right (inheritance), so istishab (her current being Muslim) cannot assist her because that would make it a positive proof. However, the heirs are repelling her claim, so they can rely on the original state of the wife being Christian before the death, since the husband died while she was still Christian in their claim. The commentary explains that istishab is a stronger principle than mere 'apparent state' (zahir), and it cannot be used to create entitlements; it only preserves the status quo. The Arabic quotes from al-Hidayah, Majma' al-Anhur, and al-'Inayah confirm that this is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Key Terms
استصحاب حال (istishab al-hal): Presumption that a state of affairs continues unchanged until evidence proves otherwise.
حجة دافعة (hujjah dafi'ah): A proof that repels a claim or prevents a change from the original state.
حجة مثبتة (hujjah muthbitah): A proof that establishes a right or claim.

Testimony for Heir Requires Proof of Death and Inheritance
States a rule about testimony for inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which an heir may be awarded property based on testimony and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Ownership for the deceased alone does not entitle the heir; death and inheritance must be proven.

This passage introduces a key principle in Hanafi testimony law regarding inheritance. It clarifies that when witnesses establish that the deceased owned a property, that alone is insufficient for the judge to award it to the heir. Additional testimony must confirm the death of the owner and that the property was left as inheritance. This prevents incomplete proof from unjustly transferring property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the first sentence: 'When ownership is established for the deceased, it is not adjudicated for the heir until the witnesses testify that he died and left it as an inheritance for him.' This is the core rule.
2
Break Down the Condition
The principle has two parts: (1) ownership proven for the deceased, and (2) additional testimony required: death and inheritance. The second part is the condition for the heir to succeed.
3
Analyze the Example
The commentary provides an example: someone claims a house belonged to his father, who gave it as a loan to the occupant. The testimony of the father's ownership does not give the son the house; he must also prove the father's death and that the house was part of the inheritance.
This illustrates why the condition exists: to prevent the heir from benefiting from incomplete evidence.
4
Generalize the Rule
Consider other scenarios where an heir claims property. The same principle applies: the heir must present testimony covering both the deceased's ownership and the inheritance chain.

No Bequest for the Heir (لا وصیة للوارث)
Concise ruling on bequests to heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to an heir and identify the condition under which it becomes valid.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir is void unless all other heirs consent.

This concise matn from Ibn Nujaym encapsulates a foundational Hanafi inheritance principle. Understanding this rule is critical for drafting valid Islamic wills and resolving estate disputes. The ruling is rooted in the Quranic ordering of bequest before shares and the Prophetic hadith prohibiting bequests to heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the explicit ruling from the matn: 'لا وصیة للوارث' (No bequest for the heir). This is the default Hanafi position.
The ruling is stated absolutely in the source.
2
Understand the rationale
Recognize that bequests to heirs would disrupt the fixed shares ordained by Allah in the Quran (4:11-12). The Prophet ﷺ said, 'Indeed Allah has given each rightful heir his share, so no bequest to an heir.'
The bequest is subordinate to the inheritance shares.
3
Recognize the exception
Note that the bequest becomes valid if the other heirs consent after the testator's death. This is the mufta bihi position – not stated in the matn but derived from later Hanafi works.
Consent must be given freely after death, not coerced.
4
Compare with other schools
Contrast with the Shafi'i view: a bequest to an heir is valid up to one-third of the estate without consent. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
Shafi'i relies on general permissibility of bequests; Hanafi restricts based on the hadith.

Debt Precedence Over Manumission: The Case of the Slave and the Creditor
Explanation of principle with applied dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that debt takes precedence over manumission and explain why the slave must work to pay his value when both claims are confirmed, applying the principle 'al-aqwa yadfa' al-adna'.

Core Idea
Debt is stronger than manumission because it is paid from the entire estate, so it overrides the slave's freedom and requires him to work to pay his value.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, the principle 'ad-daynu asbaqu' is introduced: the debts of a deceased must be paid before any other claims. The author then applies this to a specific case where a slave claims to have been freed in health, while a creditor claims a debt. The heir confirms both. What happens to the slave? Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the slave must work (saiyah) to pay his value to the creditor. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the governing principle
The passage begins with 'ad-daynu asbaqu' (debt has precedence). Understand that this means debts of the deceased are to be paid before any other claims, including bequests or manumission.
This principle is a general rule of inheritance and estate administration in Hanafi law.
2
Analyze the hypothetical scenario
Consider the case: a slave claims the deceased freed him in health; a creditor claims a debt of 1000 dirhams; the heir confirms both statements. The claims are simultaneous and the heir's confirmation gives them legal weight.
The scenario tests the interaction between debt and manumission.
3
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and its proof
Abu Hanifa rules that the slave must work (يسعى) to pay his value. His proof: the acknowledgment of debt is stronger because it binds all property, while manumission in illness is limited to one-third. The stronger repels the weaker (الأقوى يدفع الأدنى).
The strength of the debt claim overrides the manumission, necessitating saiyyah.
4
Contrast with the Sahibayn's ruling and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the slave is free without working. Their argument: both claims arose in health and were confirmed in one statement, so they are simultaneous. Manumission in health, even with debt, does not require saiyyah.
They treat both claims as equal and apply the rule that manumission in health is absolute.
5
Determine the tarjih and the mufta bihi
The text prefers Abu Hanifa's view, as the stronger claim must be given priority. Thus the operative ruling (mufta bihi) is that the slave works to pay his value. This is the established Hanafi position.
The tarjih is based on the hierarchy of claims: debt overrides manumission.

Principles of Hidayah (84) Debt is prior. Translation: Debt has precedence. Explanation: The meaning of the above principle is that the debt upon the deceased takes precedence over everything, i.e., debt is given priority in payment first, because debt is important and one begins with the important. The author of al-Hidayah, after mentioning the above principle, states that if a slave says to the heir: 'Your father freed me in health', and another person says: 'I have a thousand dirhams debt from your father', and the heir confirms both, then debt takes precedence over manumission, because there is no impediment to attributing [the debt] to the state of health, and it is not possible to attribute the manumission to that state, because debt prevents gratuitous manumission in illness. And therefore the slave must work to pay his value according to Abu Hanifa, and the two [Abu Yusuf and Muhammad] said: he is free and does not work to pay anything, because the debt and manumission in health appeared from him with the confirmation of the heir in one statement, so it is as if they were together. And manumission in health does not necessitate saiyah even if the manumitter had debt. And (to him), i.e., to Abu Hanifa, that the acknowledgment of debt is stronger, because it is considered from all the property, and the acknowledgment of manumission in illness is considered from one-third, and the stronger repels the weaker. So its effect, i.e., the effect of what is mentioned, is that the slave works to pay his value. (al-Binaya: Kitab al-Wasaya, Bab al-Itq fi Marad al-Mawt, vol. 13 p. 452-453) And whoever leaves a slave and he says to the heir: 'Your father freed me in health', and a man says: 'I have a thousand dirhams owed by your father', then the slave works to pay his value according to Abu Hanifa. (Hashiyat al-Shurunbulali ala Durar al-Hukkam, Kitab al-Wasaya, Bab al-Wasiyyah lil-Aqarib wa Ghayrihim, vol. 2 p. 440) al-Hidayah: Kitab al-Wasaya, Bab al-Itq fi Marad al-Mawt, vol. 4 p. 656. Commentary
This passage opens with a principle from al-Hidayah: debt has precedence. The author explains that when a person dies, their debts must be paid before any other claims, including bequests. He then provides a specific case: a slave claims the deceased freed him in health, and another creditor claims a debt. The heir confirms both. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the slave must work to pay his value (saiyyah) because the debt claim is stronger. The debt is considered from the entire estate, while manumission in illness is from one-third. Therefore the stronger (debt) overrides the weaker (manumission). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they say the slave is free without working because both claims arose in health, so they are simultaneous, and manumission in health does not entail saiyyah even if there is debt. The Hanafi position is that the slave must work to pay his value. This is the relied-upon ruling.

Key Terms
الدَّيْنُ أَسْبَقُ (ad-daynu asbaqu): Debt has precedence; meaning debt of the deceased takes priority over other claims like bequests or manumission.
السَّعَايَة (as-saiyah): The work a slave must perform to earn money to pay his value when he is partially free or manumitted but subject to a debt.
