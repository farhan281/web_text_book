Kafala is Based on Generosity: The Founding Maxim
Maxim on the foundational basis of kafala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the founding maxim of kafāla and explain its legal consequence of treating kafāla as a non-compensatory contract.

Core Idea
Kafāla is grounded in generosity, not commerce, so it is interpreted liberally to uphold the guarantee.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim from the Hanafi school: 'The foundation of kafāla (guarantee) is based on voluntary generosity (tawarru’).' This seemingly simple statement has profound implications for how guarantees are structured and enforced in Islamic law. Understanding this maxim helps distinguish kafāla from commercial contracts and clarifies when a guarantor may or may not demand compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
Find the exact Arabic phrasing: إِنَّ مَبْنَى الْكَفَالَةِ عَلَى التَّوَرُّع. Understand its literal meaning: 'Indeed, the foundation of kafāla is upon voluntary generosity.'
2
Understand the key term
Tawarru‘ means voluntary piety or generosity—doing good without expecting material return. Contrast this with commercial exchange where consideration is essential.
In Hanafi fiqh, tawarru‘ is a non-compensatory basis.
3
Derive the legal consequence
Because kafāla is based on generosity, it is valid even without consideration (e.g., the guarantor receives no fee). This differs from sales or hire contracts where consideration is a pillar.
The contract is upheld liberally to effect the guarantee.
4
Apply to a concrete example
Consider: A guarantees B’s loan from a bank without charging any fee. According to the maxim, this is a valid kafāla. If a fee were charged, it might raise questions about the contract’s nature.
No consideration is required for validity.
5
Recognise implications
The maxim also means that strict conditions of sale (like certainty of price, delivery) do not apply to kafāla. The contract is interpreted generously to achieve the guarantee’s purpose.
This liberal interpretation is a key feature of Hanafi kafāla.

Kafala Can Be Suspended on a Suitable Condition
Maxim on permissibility of conditional kafala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on attaching conditions to a kafala contract and identify whether a given condition is suitable (mulā'im) or unsuitable.

Core Idea
A kafala contract can be conditional, but only if the condition is suitable to its gratuitous nature.

The Hanafi school permits a guarantee (kafala) to be suspended on a future condition, but not all conditions are acceptable. This maxim distinguishes between suitable conditions that do not contradict the nature of the contract and unsuitable ones that would invalidate it. Understanding this rule helps in drafting valid conditional guarantees in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The maxim refers to kafala, a gratuitous (tabarru') contract where a guarantor assumes liability for a debt or obligation of another.
Gratuitous contracts are more flexible in Hanafi fiqh regarding conditions.
2
State the ruling
It is valid (sahih) to suspend (ta'līq) the kafala contract upon the occurrence of a future condition.
Suspension means the contract becomes effective only when the condition is fulfilled.
3
Identify the type of condition allowed
The condition must be suitable (mulā'im) – i.e., congruent with the nature of kafala. Unsuitable conditions (e.g., stipulating a fee or making it compensable) are invalid.
Suitable conditions are those that do not alter the gratuitous nature of the contract.
4
Apply to an example
If A says to B: 'I guarantee your debt if you do not pay by the due date,' this suspension is valid because the condition is suitable.
The condition relates to the debtor's default, which is relevant to the guarantee's purpose.

What Can Be Suspended on Condition Does Not Void by Invalid Conditions
Maxim on effect of invalid conditions in conditional contracts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on invalid conditions in suspendable contracts and apply it to kafāla and similar contracts.

Core Idea
Invalid conditions in suspendable contracts are voided, not the contract itself.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi maxim protecting certain conditional contracts. Knowing when a condition voids a contract versus when it is simply discarded is crucial for understanding the Hanafi approach to contracts like kafāla (guarantee). We will examine the maxim's precise scope and apply it to a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Determine if the contract is of a type that can be validly suspended on a condition (e.g., kafāla, waqf, wasiyya).
Not all contracts are suspendable; e.g., sale and marriage cannot be conditional.
2
Assess the condition
Classify the condition as valid (sharṭ ṣaḥīḥ) or invalid (sharṭ fāsid) according to the shari'a.
An invalid condition is one that contravenes the contract's nature or involves a prohibited element.
3
Apply the maxim
If the contract is validly suspendable and the condition is invalid, the maxim holds: the contract remains valid and the condition is void (lā yabṭulu).
The invalid condition is ignored; it does not affect the core contract.
4
Note the exceptions
Contracts that cannot be suspended (e.g., a sale with a suspensive condition) become void if any condition is attached, even if the condition is valid.
This maxim applies only where suspension is permissible.

Discharge of Principal Requires Discharge of Guarantor
Maxim on release of principal leading to release of guarantor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding discharge of the guarantor upon discharge of the principal debtor, and apply it to a concrete scenario.

Core Idea
Discharge of the principal debtor automatically discharges the guarantor.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in the chapter of kafalah (suretyship). The guarantor's obligation is accessory to the principal debtor's debt. Therefore, if the creditor releases the principal debtor, the guarantor is also released ipso facto. This maxim governs many practical transactions where guarantees are involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The maxim involves three parties: creditor, principal debtor (aṣīl), and guarantor (kafīl). Recognize that the principal debtor is the primary obligor, and the guarantor is a secondary obligor.
2
Understand the relationship
The guarantor's liability is contingent on the principal's debt. It is not independent; it derives from the principal's obligation.
This is why discharge of the principal removes the basis for the guarantee.
3
State the effect
When the creditor discharges the principal debtor (full and unconditional release), the guarantor is also discharged from any obligation under the guarantee.
No separate act of release is needed for the guarantor.
4
Note the condition
The discharge must be full and unconditional. If the release is partial or conditional, the guarantor may not be fully released.
Partial release may reduce the guarantor's liability proportionally.

The discharge of the principal (debtor) necessitates the discharge of the guarantor.
Source pages p. 6

Commentary
This maxim states that if the creditor releases the main debtor (aṣīl) from the debt, the guarantor (kafīl) is also automatically released. This is because the guarantor's obligation is secondary to the principal's. For example, if the creditor says 'I forgive the debt of A', and A had a guarantor B, B is also free from the guarantee.

Kafala of Specific Items is Invalid
Maxim on invalidity of guaranteeing specific items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that kafala of specific objects is invalid and explain why using the Hanafi condition that the subject must be a liability.

Core Idea
Guaranteeing a physical object is not valid kafala; only debts can be guaranteed.

In Hanafi law, kafala is a contract of guarantee that attaches to a liability (dayn), not to a physical object. This maxim draws a sharp line: if you try to guarantee the return of a specific item, the contract is void as kafala, though it may be treated differently under another contract type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is kafala (guarantee). The ruling is that kafala of specific objects is invalid (batil).
Invalid means the contract does not produce its intended legal effects.
2
Understand the key terms
Al-a'yān al-maḍmūna: specific physical objects that are guaranteed. Here, 'guaranteed by other than themselves' means they are not themselves debts but are objects whose return is being guaranteed.
The phrase 'bighayriha' indicates the object is not the liability itself.
3
Distinguish dayn from ayn
Kafala is valid for dayn (debt/liability) but not for ayn (specific object). A dayn is a financial obligation measurable and transferable; an ayn is a physical item.
Example: a loan of 100 dirhams is a dayn; a specific car is an ayn.
4
Apply the maxim to a scenario
If someone says 'I guarantee the return of your car', it is invalid as kafala. If they guarantee its value, it is valid as kafala of a debt.
The value becomes a dayn once determined.

Source Text

Kafāla of specific objects that are guaranteed by other than themselves is not valid.
Source pages p. 6

Commentary
This maxim clarifies that a guarantee (kafāla) cannot be contracted over specific objects ('ayn) that are not of the type that can be guaranteed via kafāla. Core Idea
Two guarantors share joint liability — this chapter defines their mutual rights and obligations.

This lesson opens the door to a specific branch of Hanafi fiqh: suretyship involving two persons. While a single guarantor's rules are straightforward, joint kafala raises questions about liability distribution, recourse, and creditor's options. Understanding this chapter heading sets the stage for the detailed rulings ahead.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the term 'Kafala'
Kafala is a contract of guarantee where a person (the guarantor) assumes responsibility for a debt or obligation of another (the principal debtor). In this chapter, the focus is on two guarantors.

2
Understand the scenario 'of two men'
The chapter specifically deals with two men who jointly guarantee a single debt. The key question: does each become liable for half, or is each fully liable? The Hanafi position is that each is liable for the whole debt, but if one pays, he can seek contribution from the other.
This differs from some other schools, making it a distinctive Hanafi ruling.
3
Expect the rulings on liability and recourse
The chapter will cover: creditor's right to demand full payment from either guarantor, the paying guarantor's right to reclaim from the co-guarantor, and any conditions that affect liability.
These rules derive from principles of joint obligation (iltizam).

The guarantor's release is independent; the principal's debt remains untouched.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi maxim in guaranty (kafālah). The previous maxim stated that discharging the principal automatically discharges the guarantor. Who is the kafīl (guarantor) and who is the aṣīl (principal)? Understand their roles.

The principal remains fully liable.
4
Contrast with the prior maxim
Compare: barā'at al-aṣīl causes barā'at al-kafīl, but not vice versa. Why? Because the guarantor's obligation is accessory to the principal's, but the principal's is primary.

Maxim comparing legal strength of two contract types

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A guarantee binds irrevocably; a gift can be revoked.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi contract law: guarantee contracts (such as suretyship) are stronger than gratuitous contracts (such as gifts or loans for use). Understanding this distinction is crucial for knowing when obligations become binding and irrevocable. We will explore the legal reasoning behind this maxim and its practical implications.

Guided Path

Identify the two contract types
First, recognize the two contracts being compared: 'aqd al-ḍamān (guarantee/suretyship) and 'aqd al-tabarru' (gratuitous donation). The former involves assuming another's liability; the latter is a voluntary transfer without compensation.
In Hanafi fiqh, a guarantee (kafalah) is a binding contract upon acceptance, while a gratuitous contract (e.g., hibah) is generally not binding until delivery.
2
Understand the basis of strength
Explore why a guarantee is considered stronger: it creates a strict liability on the guarantor that cannot be unilaterally revoked once accepted. In contrast, a gratuitous contract allows the donor to revoke before delivery in many cases.
The strength arises from the element of responsibility (daman) which is absent in pure generosity.
3
Apply to concrete cases
Consider examples: A guarantor for a debt cannot withdraw after the creditor accepts. A gift-giver can take back the gift before the recipient takes possession. This illustrates the maxim's practical effect.
Refer to 'aqd al-ḍamān as a binding commitment akin to a debt obligation.

Core Idea
Kafalah, built on expansion, tolerates minor ignorance; sale, built on restriction, does not.

This lesson explores the foundational principle that kafalah (suretyship) is a contract of beneficence, built on ease and expansion. Unlike sale, which requires full specification to avoid disputes, kafalah allows a degree of ignorance about the subject matter. This principle, affirmed by the author of al-Hidayah, has practical implications for the validity of guarantees where the amount is unspecified.

Recognize that kafalah is a gratuitous contract (tabarru') and not a commutative one. This understanding sets the stage for the leniency in its conditions.
Kafalah is a voluntary act of suretyship.
2

Explain that the foundation of kafalah is expansion and ease, which means its rulings are flexible compared to contracts of exchange.

This contrast clarifies the rationale behind the principle.

Commentary
This passage from Usool al-Hidayah presents principle 43: 'Indeed, the foundation of kafalah is upon expansion' (Inna mabna al-kafalah 'ala al-tawassu'). The author of al-Hidayah clarifies the application: in kafalah bil-mal (suretyship for property), the contract is valid whether the property is specified (e.g., 'one thousand') or unspecified (e.g., 'property'), because the underlying basis is expansion (tawassu'). The passage contrasts this with sale (bay'), which is built on restriction (diq) and therefore requires full specification to avoid dispute. The reasoning is rational (dalil 'aqli): since kafalah is a donation (tabarru'), it is treated like a gift, where generosity is encouraged and minor ambiguity is tolerated. The passage also notes that even with ignorance, the subject matter can often be identified through indirect means. This principle is relied upon in the Hanafi madhhab for contracts of suretyship.

Kafalah Suspended on a Suitable Condition is Valid
States the principle and provides rational proof for condition suitability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that kafalah may be suspended on a suitable condition and explain why kafalah tolerates uncertainty (jahala) while sale does not.

Core Idea
Kafalah's foundation of leniency permits conditions that would make a sale invalid due to uncertainty.

The Hanafi school holds that a contract of kafalah (suretyship) can be made conditional on a future event, provided the condition is suitable to the contract's purpose. This lesson unpacks the key principle: what makes a condition 'suitable' and why kafalah is uniquely tolerant of uncertainty. The ruling is supported by a rational proof comparing kafalah to sale, highlighting the different legal foundations (mabna) of the two contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its scope
Locate the core statement: 'Al-kafalahu yasihhu ta'liquha bi-shartin mula'in laha.' Understand that the ruling is about suspending (ta'liq) the contract on a condition, not about conditions that are part of the contract itself.
This principle is from Al-Hidayah and represents the relied-upon Hanafi position.
2
Define 'suitable condition' (shart mula'im)
A suitable condition is one that relates to the purpose of kafalah, such as the debtor's disappearance or default. Unsuitable conditions are unrelated events like wind or rain.
The suitability is determined by the contract's typical function: securing the creditor's right.
3
Contrast the basis of kafalah with sale
The text explains that sale is built on strictness (mudayaqa), so even a little jahala (uncertainty) leads to dispute and invalidates the contract. Kafalah is built on leniency (musamaha), so jahala does not lead to dispute because the creditor can still collect from the original debtor.
This talil (rational proof) is from Bada'i' al-Sana'i' and is key to understanding the difference.
4
Apply to examples
Work through the examples: suitable condition (e.g., 'If the debtor leaves the city, I will pay') vs. unsuitable condition (e.g., 'If it rains'). Confirm that unsuitable conditions are disregarded; the kafalah remains valid but the condition is void.
The Urdu explanation in the source gives these examples.

Contracts Suspended on Corrupt Conditions Remain Valid
States the principle that contracts capable of suspension are not invalidated by corrupt conditions, with application to kafalah and a dispute with Shafi'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a contract validly suspendable on a condition is not invalidated by a corrupt condition, and distinguish between the rulings for kafalah and agency regarding customary conditions.

Core Idea
A corrupt condition is void, but the contract itself remains valid if it can be suspended on a condition.

This passage from the Hidayah articulates a key Hanafi principle: not every corrupt condition destroys a contract. If the contract is of a type that can be suspended on a condition (like talaq, itaq, and kafalah), then even if the condition is invalid, the contract endures. The author uses this principle to explain rulings on suretyship (kafalah) and to address a disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i over suretyship for the person. Let's unpack the principle and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its scope
The passage states: 'Whatever can be suspended by a condition is not invalidated by corrupt conditions.' This applies to contracts that can be mu'allaq (suspended), like talaq (divorce), itaq (manumission), and kafalah (suretyship).
The principle is a general usul ruling: the contract remains valid, and the corrupt condition is void.
2
Apply the principle to kafalah
If a guarantor suspends kafalah on an unsuitable condition (e.g., 'if the wind blows'), the condition is corrupt and void, but the kafalah contract remains valid and immediately binding.
This shows the principle in action: the suspension is ineffective, but the guarantee becomes effective immediately.
3
Distinguish types of conditions in kafalah
The passage notes that suspended kafalah is valid only if the condition is customary (muta'arif); with an uncustomary condition, the suspended contract is not valid. This is a specific rule for kafalah, unlike agency (wakalah) where any condition is acceptable.
Customary conditions are those suitable to the contract's purpose, e.g., related to the debt's obligation or recovery.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
The passage states that Hanafis validate both kafalah bil-nafs (personal suretyship) and kafalah bil-mal (financial surety), while Imam al-Shafi'i considers kafalah bil-nafs invalid. The practical consequence: in Hanafi law, a person can be guaranteed to appear; in Shafi'i law, such a guarantee is unenforceable.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; both positions are based on different interpretations of the nature of suretyship.

Invalid Conditions Do Not Nullify Validly Suspended Contracts
Explaining the principle that valid suspension survives invalid conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that validly suspended contracts are not invalidated by invalid conditions, and apply it to kafalah contracts, including the distinction between kafalah bi al-nafs and kafalah bi al-mal and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Valid contracts survive invalid conditions — the condition falls, the contract stands.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every invalid condition destroys the contract. The key is whether the contract itself can be validly suspended on a condition. If so, adding an invalid condition makes the condition void but the contract remains valid and takes effect immediately. This principle, established by analogy to talaq and itq, is central to kafalah (guarantee). This lesson unpacks the rule, its application to kafalah, and the divergent view of Imam al-Shafi'i on kafalah bi al-nafs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act under discussion is the contract of kafalah (guarantee) which can be immediate or suspended on a condition. The ruling: kafalah is permissible (ja'iz) in all forms, including kafalah bi al-nafs and kafalah bi al-mal.
Kafalah bi al-nafs means undertaking to produce the person of the debtor; kafalah bi al-mal means financial liability.
2
State the Legal Principle
The central qa'idah: 'Every contract that can be validly suspended on a condition is not invalidated by invalid conditions.' This is the foundation for the analysis.
The principle is general, applying to contracts like talaq and itq as well.
3
Locate the Proof (Dalil)
The proof is qiyas (analogy) on talaq and itq. In those acts, if a person says 'I divorce you when the pilgrims arrive' (an unknown condition), the divorce takes effect immediately because the condition is invalid but the act is validly suspendable. Similarly, in kafalah, an unsuitable condition (e.g., 'if wind blows') is void, but the kafalah remains valid and the obligation becomes due immediately.
The illa (effective cause) is that the contract can be validly suspended on a condition; the hukm is that invalid conditions do not nullify it.
4
Apply the Principle to Kafalah Examples
The text gives the example: 'If the wind blows or rain comes, I am a guarantor.' This condition is unsuitable and thus void, but the kafalah is valid and the money becomes due immediately. The principle holds regardless of whether the condition is customary or uncustomary.
Note the distinction: suitable conditions (e.g., 'if the sold item is reclaimed') make the suspension valid; unsuitable conditions do not affect the contract's validity.
5
Note the Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that kafalah bi al-nafs (guarantee of the person) is invalid (batil). The Hanafi school holds it valid (ja'iz). The practical consequence: in Hanafi courts, such guarantees are enforceable; in Shafi'i courts, they are void.
The thamarah al-khilaf: enforceability of personal guarantees. This dispute is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the tradition.

The Exoneration of the Principal Exonerates the Guarantor
States a Hanafi maxim on guaranty discharge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on guarantor discharge and apply it to determine when a guarantor is freed from liability.

Core Idea
Discharge of the principal debtor automatically frees the guarantor; the reverse is not true.

This lesson examines a core principle of Hanafi guaranty law: the maxim 'Bara'at al-asil tujibu bara'at al-kafil'. Understanding this maxim is crucial for determining the legal relationship between principal debtor and guarantor. The passage from Al-Hidayah explains the logic and cites supporting sources. By the end, you will be able to apply this rule to concrete scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal relationship
In kafalah, the principal debtor (asil) has the primary obligation; the guarantor (kafil) is subsidiary and dependent.
2
State the maxim
Articulate: 'The exoneration of the principal necessitates the exoneration of the guarantor.'
Memorize the Arabic: بَرَاءَةُ الْأَصِيلِ تُوْجِبُ بَرَاءَةَ الْكَفِيلِ
3
Understand the reasoning
The debt is owed by the principal; the guarantor is merely a surety. When the principal is free, the surety is automatically freed a fortiori.
The guarantor's obligation is derivative, not original.
4
Apply the converse
Note that the reverse does not hold: freeing the guarantor does not free the principal. The principal is the original obligor.
The passage explicitly quotes: 'exoneration of the guarantor does not necessitate exoneration of the principal.'
5
Work through an example
If the creditor forgives the debtor's debt or collects it, the guarantor is also discharged, even without explicit release.
This is the practical consequence of the maxim.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi legal maxim (qa'idah) from the chapter of kafalah (guaranty). The maxim states: 'The exoneration of the principal (asil) necessitates the exoneration of the guarantor (kafil).' The author explains that the guarantor's obligation is subsidiary to the principal's. When the principal debtor is released from the debt (either by payment or by the creditor's remission), the guarantor is automatically released. Penalties Do Not Admit Substitution
A maxim with explanatory commentary on kafalah for hudud and qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim that penalties do not admit substitution, explain its application to hudud and qisas, and determine the validity of kafalah for such penalties.

Core Idea
Penalties are personal; you cannot punish an innocent substitute.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi principle: criminal penalties, such as hudud and qisas, cannot be transferred to another person. This maxim has direct implications for the contract of guarantee (kafalah). Understanding why substitution is impermissible will clarify why kafalah for hudud and qisas is invalid, and how this differs from financial guarantees.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
The maxim is: 'Al-uqubah la tajri fiha al-niyabah' (Penalties do not admit substitution). Write it in Arabic and English.
Memorize this maxim as it appears in Al-Hidayah.
2
Identify the scope
The maxim applies to 'uqubat', specifically hudud (fixed Quranic punishments) and qisas (retribution). List examples: hadd for zina, theft, qisas for murder.
Financial penalties like diyah are not included.
3
Grasp the rationale
The purpose of punishment is deterrence (zajr) of the criminal. Substitution fails because the substitute is not the criminal, so the deterrent effect is lost.
Read the commentary: 'al-maqsud huwa al-zajr wa huwa la yatahaqqaq bi al-na'ib'.
4
Apply to kafalah
Since penalties cannot be exacted from a substitute, a guarantee (kafalah) for hudud or qisas is invalid. It is impossible to enforce the penalty on the guarantor.
Contrast with financial guarantees where the debt can be collected from the guarantor.
5
Test with an example
Consider a person who commits theft; another person cannot guarantee to receive the hand-cutting hadd on his behalf. Such a kafalah is void.
The example from the text: hadd or qisas cannot be imposed on the guarantor.

This lesson explores a key principle of Hanafi fiqh: the non-delegability of criminal punishments. The proof is that punishments ('uqubat) do not allow deputyship (niyabah). The 'illa is that the objective of punishment is deterrence (zajr), which cannot be achieved through a deputy.

Kafalah for Specific Items: Inherently Liable vs. Liable Through Another
Explains when kafalah is valid based on type of liability (daman).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between inherently liable items (madhmun binafsihi) and items liable through another (madhmun bighayrihi), and apply the Hanafi rule on the validity of kafalah for each.

Core Idea
Kafalah for a specific item is valid only if the item is inherently liable and cannot be replaced by payment.

You might think a guarantor can only guarantee debts, not physical objects. But the Hanafi school allows suretyship for certain items—those that are inherently liable on the principal. This lesson will clarify the distinction between items that are inherently liable and those that are liable through another obligation, and why only the first type qualifies for valid kafalah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that items subject to kafalah fall into two types: inherently liable (madhmun binafsihi) and liable through another (madhmun bighayrihi). The first type obligates the principal to return the item or its value; the second types liability is tied to a separate obligation like a price or debt.
Examples of inherently liable: usurped goods, items taken on trial for purchase, items sold in a corrupt sale. Examples of liable through another: sold item before delivery (liable through price), pawned item (liable through debt).
2
State the condition for valid kafalah
The condition is that the principal cannot be released from liability except by delivering the item or its equivalent. This condition holds for inherently liable items but not for those liable through another.
For items liable through another, the principal can exit by paying the price or debt, so the condition is not met.
3
Apply the condition to examples
Test the condition on examples: usurped item requires return of the item or its value, so kafalah is valid. Sold item before delivery can be settled by paying the price, so kafalah is invalid.
Similarly, pawned item: pawner can repay debt and retrieve item, so kafalah is invalid.
4
Compare with Shafi'i position
Understand that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that kafalah for any specific item is invalid, regardless of liability type, because suretyship is only for debts. The Hanafi view is more nuanced and is the relied-upon position in the madhhab.
The practical difference: Hanafis allow a guarantor for the return of a usurped item; Shafi'is do not.

Shariah Rulings Follow Linguistic Meanings: Hawalah and Kafalah
Defining the principle and applying it to two contracts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that shariah rulings follow linguistic meanings and apply it to distinguish the legal effects of hawalah (transfer of debt) and kafalah (suretyship).

Core Idea
Literal meaning determines legal effect: hawalah transfers debt freeing the debtor; kafalah joins liability without release.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: the legal consequence of a contract derives from the literal (lughawi) meaning of its name. The author applies this to two common financial transactions: hawalah (transfer of debt) and kafalah (suretyship). By understanding that hawalah literally means 'transfer' and kafalah means 'joining', we can predict whether the original debtor is released or remains liable. This principle is central to Hanafi legal reasoning and illustrates how language shapes fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle

The principle is a general rule applied throughout Hanafi fiqh.

Hawalah literally means 'transfer' (naql). Legally, it is the transfer of a debt from the liability of the original debtor (muhil) to another person (muhal 'alayh).
In Hanafi law, this results in the complete release of the muhil.
3
Define Kafalah Linguistically and Legally
Kafalah literally means 'joining' (damm). Legally, it is a contract where a guarantor (kafil) joins his liability to that of the original debtor (aseel) without releasing the debtor.
The aseel remains liable; the kafil adds an additional obligation.
4
Apply the Principle
Since hawalah means transfer, the debt must move entirely from the muhil; thus the muhil is free. Since kafalah means joining, the original debt must remain on the aseel for the joining to be meaningful; thus the aseel stays liable.
This is a direct application of the linguistic principle.

These texts illustrate the practical implications of the principle.

Key Terms
كفالة بالنفس (kafalah bi al-nafs): Suretyship for the person; the guarantor (kafil) undertakes to produce the person of the debtor/defendant when required.
كفيل (kafil): The person who provides the surety; the guarantor.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference overriding qiyas; a source of law in cases where strict analogy would lead to hardship.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
According to the Hanafi school, which of the following is true about kafalah bi al-nafs?
A) It is invalid because it involves restricting a free person.
B) It is permissible and valid, based on the hadith 'al-za'im gharim' and istihsan.
C) It is only permissible for property, not for the person.
D) It is mandatory for every plaintiff.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school validates kafalah bi al-nafs based on the general hadith and istihsan, enabling judges to order personal suretyship to protect the plaintiff's right.
