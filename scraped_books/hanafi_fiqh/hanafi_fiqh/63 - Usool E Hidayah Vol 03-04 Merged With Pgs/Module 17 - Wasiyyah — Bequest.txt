No Will for the Killer
List of legal maxims under the chapter on will description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that any unlawful killing disqualifies the killer from benefiting under the victim's will.

Core Idea
A will for the killer is void – even accidental killing blocks inheritance by will.

The first maxim in our chapter declares that a person who kills the testator cannot receive anything by will. This rule is specific to the Hanafi school and extends beyond intentional murder. Understanding this maxim helps you see how the law prevents benefiting from wrongdoing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The maxim 'la wasiyyata lil-qatil' states a clear prohibition: the killer has no right to a will from the victim. Determine what type of killing triggers this rule according to the Hanafi school.
The text says 'indana' (according to us), indicating it is the Hanafi position.
2
Distinguish the scope
Note that Hanafis apply this to any unlawful killing, whether intentional or accidental. Compare with other schools that may restrict it to intentional killing only.
This difference affects cases of manslaughter or accidental death.
3
Apply to a scenario
Given a case where a beneficiary accidentally causes the testator's death, determine whether the will is valid under Hanafi law.
The ruling is that the will is void.

No Will for the Heir
List of legal maxims under the chapter on will description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a will in favor of an heir is invalid unless the other heirs consent after the testator's death.

Core Idea
An heir cannot receive extra via will; it is invalid without other heirs' permission.

The second maxim prohibits a will for an existing heir. This prevents unfair distribution that would override the fixed shares of inheritance. The rule has an important exception: if the other heirs agree after death, the will can be enforced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition
The maxim states 'la wasiyyata lil-warith' – no will for an heir. This means a testator cannot bequeath additional property to someone who already inherits by law.
This is based on the hadith: 'No will for an heir' (la wasiyyata li-warith).
2
Understand the exception
After the testator's death, if the other heirs give permission, the will can be valid. The ban is to protect their shares, so their consent lifts it.
The maxim itself does not mention the exception; it is derived from fiqh works.
3
Apply to a scenario
Given a testator with a son and daughter, can he will the house to his son who already inherits as a residuary heir?
Initially no; if the daughter consents, then yes.

Ownership by Acceptance
List of legal maxims under the chapter on will description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that ownership of a bequeathed item transfers to the legatee only upon acceptance after the testator's death, not automatically.

Core Idea
The legatee must accept the bequest; it is not owned until acceptance.

The third maxim clarifies the nature of a bequest: unlike a gift (hiba) which is completed by offer and acceptance, a will (wasiyyah) transfers ownership only when the beneficiary accepts it after the testator's death. The beneficiary may also reject it.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle
The maxim 'al-musa bihi yamliku bi'l-qabul' means the bequeathed item is owned by acceptance. The legatee does not own it immediately upon the testator's death; ownership depends on the legatee's acceptance.
This is different from inheritance which transfers automatically.
2
Compare with gift
In a gift (hiba), ownership transfers at the time of the contract when the donee accepts in the donor's lifetime. In a will, acceptance can only occur after the testator's death, and until then the legatee has no ownership.
The will is a suspended contract (mawquf).
3
Apply to a scenario
The testator bequeaths a car to Zaid. Zaid may accept or reject. If he rejects, the car remains part of the estate and is distributed among heirs.
Acceptance can be explicit or implied (e.g., taking possession).

Begin with the Most Important
List of legal maxims under the chapter on will description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of priority when multiple bequests or debts compete, starting with the most important (e.g., debts before bequests).

Core Idea
Debts take priority over bequests; among bequests, the more important comes first.

The fourth maxim lays down a priority rule: when the testator's estate is insufficient to cover debts and multiple bequests, the most important obligations (e.g., funeral expenses, then debts, then bequests) are settled first. This principle is derived from the general legal maxim of 'tasawwuf' (preference).

Guided Path
1
Identify the priority hierarchy
The maxim says 'begin with the most important, then the next important'. In context, this means funeral expenses first, then debts, then bequests, especially when the estate is insufficient.
This is also a general rule in mu'amalat: important obligations take precedence.
2
Apply to competing bequests
If there are multiple bequests that together exceed the one-third of the estate, they are executed proportionally, but the maxim suggests that bequests to closer relatives might be given priority (though Hanafi law often applies proportional reduction).
The maxim here is broader; it is applied in various fiqh areas.
3
Use in estate distribution
When distributing the estate, first pay debts, then execute bequests up to one-third. If debts exceed assets, bequests are void.
Debts are more important because they are obligations to people (huquq al-'ibad).

Validity of Exceptions in Contracts
List of legal maxims under the chapter on will description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that an exception (istithna') in a contract is valid only if the excepted item can validly be the subject of an independent contract.

Core Idea
Exceptions are valid only for items that could be contracted separately; otherwise they are void.

The fifth maxim deals with exceptions in contracts, such as selling a house except the door. The rule is: if the excluded item can be sold separately (e.g., the door is a separate entity), the exception is valid. If cannot (e.g., selling a tree except its fruit), the exception is invalid and the whole contract may be affected.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition
The maxim: 'What is valid to be made exclusive by contract is valid to be excluded by exception.' This means if you can validly sell an item separately, you can exclude it from a sale.
Example: selling a house excluding the front door is valid because you can sell the door separately.
2
Identify invalid exceptions
If the item cannot be the independent subject of a contract (e.g., it is not separately owned or is inseparable), the exception is void, and the contract may be either void or subject to interpretation.
Example: selling a sheep excluding its wool (wool not yet shorn) – invalid because wool is not a separate entity at the time.
3
Apply in wills
In wills, this principle matters when a testator makes a bequest with an exception (e.g., 'I give my house to X except the garden'). The exception is valid only if the garden can be separately bequeathed.
This maxim is also used in other contracts like sale and gift.

Ignorance Does Not Invalidate a Will
Maxim on the effect of ignorance on wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that ignorance (jahala) does not prevent the validity of a will and distinguish it from the rule for sale contracts.

Core Idea
A will is valid even if the beneficiary or amount is unknown – charity tolerates uncertainty.

Islamic inheritance law distinguishes between commercial contracts and gratuitous dispositions. In sales, uncertainty (gharar) can render a contract void. But a will is an act of benevolence, so the strict requirements of certainty are relaxed. This lesson explores the first maxim in this chapter, which states that ignorance does not invalidate a will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making a will (wasiyyah). The ruling: it is valid despite ignorance (jahala) about details.
2
Understand the rationale
Because a will is a gratuitous transfer (tabarru'), not a commercial exchange. Charitable acts do not require the same precision.
3
Contrast with sales
In a sale, jahala leads to gharar and invalidates the contract (fasid). In a will, it is forgiven.
4
Apply to a scenario
Example: A testator says, 'I leave one-third of my estate to my poor relatives' without naming them. The will is valid; the executor distributes to the poor.
5
Memorize the maxim
Repeat the Arabic: الْجَهَالَةُ لَا تَمْنَعُ صِحَّةَ الْوَصِيَّةِ.

Repeated Description Refers to the Same Thing
Maxim on repetition of description in wills

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a testator describes a bequest using two similar expressions, the second refers to the same entity as the first.

Core Idea
If a testator says 'my white horse, the one I ride', both describe the same horse.

Wills sometimes contain multiple descriptions of the same property. This maxim resolves the potential ambiguity: the second mention is not a separate bequest but a confirmation of the first. This avoids double bequest and confusion. This rule is a principle of interpretation specific to wills, but similar ideas appear in other areas of law.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
The maxim is about interpreting a will when the testator describes the bequest twice.
2
Identify the rule
When knowledge is repeated, the second mention is intended to refer to the same thing as the first.
3
Apply to a scenario
Example: Testator says, 'I give my white horse, the one I ride.' Both phrases refer to the same horse. The will gives one horse, not two.
4
Contrast with a different phrasing
If the testator says 'my white horse and the one I ride', that might be different (if 'and' is used).
5
Memorize the maxim

Maxims of Bequests in Death Illness
List of legal maxims under the chapter on manumission in death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi legal maxims governing bequests in a death illness and apply them to determine distribution priorities.

Core Idea
Bequests share proportionally within one-third; debts and stronger claims override weaker ones.

This lesson presents three foundational maxims from the chapter on manumission in death illness. They structure how bequests are handled when a person makes them in a terminal illness. Understanding these principles is essential for correctly distributing the estate after debts and before inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Recite the three maxims

This maxim recurs across many fiqh chapters.
4

Debts of the deceased must be paid in full before any bequest is executed. The one-third limit for bequests is calculated on the gross estate before debt deduction.
This is a specific application of maxim (83).

In practice: first, pay all debts from the estate. Second, calculate one-third of the gross estate. Third, distribute bequests from that one-third, reducing proportionally if they exceed the limit. Finally, the residue goes to legal heirs.
Always prioritize debts over bequests.

When Ijma' Overrides the Apparent Meaning
Maxim on the precedence of consensus over apparent meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that the apparent meaning (ẓāhir) is abandoned after a consensus (ijma') to disregard it, and apply this to a legal text.

Core Idea
Consensus (ijma') can set aside the apparent literal meaning of a text.

In Hanafi usul, the apparent meaning of a word (ẓāhir al-lafẓ) is generally binding. However, if there is a consensus of scholars (ijma') that this apparent meaning is not intended, then the consensus overrides it. This maxim is a crucial principle of interpretation, especially when dealing with texts that might be understood literally but are understood unanimously in a different sense.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The maxim states that ẓāhir al-lafẓ (apparent meaning) 'lā yuʻtabaru' (is not considered) after there is a firm consensus (al-ʻiqd al-ijmāʻ) to abandon it.
Note the word 'ʻiqd' implies a binding agreement, not just common practice.
2
Understand the Condition
The condition for ignoring the apparent meaning is the existence of ijmaʻ (consensus) on leaving it. Without ijmaʻ, the ẓāhir remains operative.
This is a limit: mere difference of opinion does not override ẓāhir; only consensus does.
3
Apply to a Classical Example
Consider the Quranic verse 'and eat and drink until the white thread becomes clear to you from the black thread' (2:187). Literally, it mentions threads, but ijmaʻ holds that the intended meaning is the true dawn, not actual threads.
The ẓāhir would suggest literal threads, but ijmaʻ overrides this.
4
Recognize the Hanafi Position
Hanafis prioritize ẓāhir as a primary source of meaning (ḥujja), but they acknowledge ijmaʻ as a superordinate source that can amend interpretation.
This balances textual fidelity with the collective authority of scholars.

Executorship is Vicegerency: The Hanafi Principle of Wasiyyah as Khilafah
Definition of wasiyyah as khilafah with ruling on executor's actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an executor's action is valid without knowledge of appointment, and distinguish executorship from agency in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Executorship is vicegerency (khilafah), not agency; the executor's authority begins at death and does not require knowledge.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a foundational principle: the executor is a caliph of the deceased, not merely a deputy. This distinction has a practical consequence: the executor's actions on the estate are valid even if he was unaware of his appointment. Why? Because after death, the testator's authority ends, and there is an urgent need for someone to manage the estate. Unlike an agent, the executor does not require knowledge because his role is that of a successor, not a representative of a living person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Read the statement 'Executorship is vicegerency' (الوصاية خلافة). Understand that the author defines the legal nature of an executor as a successive authority (caliph) rather than a deputy.
The term 'khilafah' implies authority that takes effect after the original authority has ceased.
2
Derive the ruling from the principle
Note the practical ruling: if an executor sells something from the estate before learning of his appointment, the sale is valid. This follows from the principle that vicegerency does not require the executor's knowledge.
The ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) due to the need for estate management after death.
3
Contrast with agency (wakalah)
Compare with agency: an agent must know of his appointment because the principal is alive and capable of acting; agency is deputyship (niyabah), not vicegerency.
Agency requires knowledge because the principal can revoke or supervise; in executorship, the principal is dead, so no such possibility exists.
4
Recognize the role of istihsan
Understand that the validity of the executor's action is established by istihsan, which overrides the qiyas (analogy) that might require knowledge. The need (hajah) after death necessitates immediate authority.
Istihsan here is based on the general need to avoid disruption of the estate.

Guardianship as Caliphate: Wasi Acts Without Knowledge
Defining wasiyyah as khilafah with proof from istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle 'al-wisayatu khilafah' to determine the validity of a wasi's disposition without knowledge and distinguish it from agency.

Core Idea
Guardianship is caliphate, not agency; knowledge of appointment is not required for validity.

In Hanafi fiqh, the position of a guardian (wasi) is fundamentally different from that of an agent (wakil). While an agent must know of his agency to act, a guardian derives authority from the testator's death itself, not from personal knowledge. This lesson unpacks the principle 'al-wisayatu khilafah' and its practical effect on estate sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act in question? It is a sale by a wasi (guardian) of estate property before he knows of his appointment. What is the Hanafi ruling? It is valid (sahih).
2
Locate the dalil and its type
The proof is the principle 'al-wisayatu khilafah' supported by istihsan (juristic preference). Identify the reasoning (talil) used: death ends the testator's capacity, so appointment of a deputy is impossible; thus guardianship must be succession, not agency.
The primary evidence is istihsan, not a direct nass.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the principle prove validity without knowledge? Because caliphate (khilafah) requires only the fact of succession, which occurs at death; knowledge is irrelevant. In contrast, agency (niyabah) requires knowledge because the principal is alive and can communicate.
The need (hajah) to manage estates after death justifies the istihsan ruling.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school disagrees: they treat the wasi as an agent who must have knowledge. Understand their qiyas on wakalah.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah adopts this as the mufta bihi position. The tarjih is based on istihsan and the nature of death preventing normal agency.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

Core Idea
A bequest is not owned until the legatee accepts it; acceptance is the key.

This lesson presents a foundational principle of Hanafi wasiyyah (bequests). Unlike inheritance, which transfers automatically upon death, a bequest requires the legatee's explicit or implied acceptance for ownership to transfer. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the law of wills.

If the legatee rejects the bequest, the property remains part of the testator's estate and is distributed among the heirs.
Rejection may occur after the testator's death; it voids the bequest.

Inheritance transfers ownership automatically at death, but a bequest requires the legatee's consent. This is a key difference in Hanafi fiqh.

No Bequest to an Heir: The Prohibition and Its Rationale
Explains the principle that a bequest to an heir is invalid, with proof from hadith and rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a bequest to an heir, cite the primary hadith proof, and apply the rule to analogous cases like hibah in terminal illness.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir is invalid: the Prophet prohibited it absolutely.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in Islamic inheritance law: a testator cannot make a bequest (wasiyyah) in favour of an heir. The Hanafi school holds this prohibition as absolute, based on the prophetic hadith 'لا وصية لوارث' and the wisdom of preventing harm and favouritism among heirs. We will explore the primary textual evidence, the rational justification, and how this rule extends to gifts made during terminal illness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its central ruling
The act is making a bequest to one's own heir. Determine the hukm level: permissible or impermissible? According to the Hanafi school, it is invalid (ghayr ja'iz) and impermissible.
The ruling applies to all bequests to heirs, regardless of consent from other heirs.
2
Locate and analyze the primary dalil
The primary proof is the hadith: 'أَلَا لَا وَصِيَّةَ لِوَارِثِ'. Recognize its chain (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning (dalala) is qat'i – a clear prohibition. In Hanafi usul, a prohibition (nahy) indicates tahrim (impermissibility).
Hadith narrated by multiple Companions, strengthening its authenticity.
3
Understand the rational (ma'qul) reason
The text gives a second reason: bequest to an heir causes harm (idha) to other heirs and leads to cutting family ties (qati'ah al-rahim). This supports the prohibition and prevents injustice.
This rationale also applies to gifts in terminal illness to an heir.
4
Apply the rule to analogous cases
The Hidayah author extends the ruling to a gift (hibah) made by a terminally ill person (marad al-mawt) to an heir. Such a gift is treated like a bequest because the illness signals impending death, making the disposition similar to a will.
Thus, hibah to an heir during terminal illness is also invalid.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) permit a bequest to an heir if the other heirs consent after death. The Hanafi position is stricter: the hadith is absolute, and no exception is made.
Know the thamarah al-khilaf: in practice, Hanafi jurists reject such bequests outright.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why acceptance is necessary for ownership of a bequest in the Hanafi school and compare it to the view of Imam Zufar.

Core Idea
A bequest transfers ownership only upon the legatee's acceptance, because it is a new ownership, not inheritance.

A bequest (wasiyyah) is a voluntary transfer of property to a non-heir, effective after the testator's death. But when does the legatee actually become the owner? This lesson explores the Hanafi principle that ownership vests only upon the legatee's acceptance, distinguishing bequest from inheritance, and addresses an intra-madhhab disagreement with Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Identify the act being ruled upon: the bequeathed property (musabih bihi) and the legatee (musabih lahu). The ruling: ownership is contingent on acceptance.
The ruling is not that the bequest is invalid without acceptance, but that ownership does not vest.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a rational (aqli) argument: a bequest establishes a new ownership; any new ownership requires the recipient's consent (acceptance). This is analogous to contracts of sale or gift.
No explicit Quran or hadith is cited; it is a qiyas-based inference.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reasoning: since a bequest creates a new ownership (malikiyyah jadidah), it requires the legatee's acceptance as a condition (shart). Inheritance, by contrast, is a substitution (khilafah) that transfers automatically.
The text explicitly contrasts with inheritance to strengthen the proof.
4
Note the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar (within the Hanafi school) holds that acceptance is not required; ownership transfers automatically upon the testator's death, akin to inheritance.
The majority of Hanafi scholars, including the three primary imams, reject Zufar's view.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The majority view is preferred and is mufta bihi. The practical consequence: if a legatee dies before accepting, the bequest returns to the testator's estate (according to the majority), not to the legatee's heirs.
Al-Hidayah and al-Inayah affirm the majority position.

The Principle al-Aqwa Yadfa' al-Adna: Debt vs. Freedom Claims
Explains the principle and applies it to a case of conflicting claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle 'al-aqwa yadfa' al-adna', apply it to conflicting claims in an estate, and distinguish Imam Abu Hanifa's position from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The stronger claim prevails: debt over freedom, but freedom remains in form with a compensatory obligation.

In Hanafi fiqh, when two valid claims conflict, the stronger (aqwa) repels the weaker (adna). This lesson unpacks this principle through a concrete case: a deceased person's slave claims freedom in health, while a creditor demands payment of a debt. The heir confirms both. How does the law resolve this tension? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Sahibayn) offer differing solutions, rooted in the same principle but with different outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting claims
Note the two rights: the slave's right to freedom (based on the deceased's alleged manumission in health) and the creditor's right to repayment of a debt. Both are confirmed by the heir (executor).
Freedom in health is immediate and unlimited; in fatal illness it is limited to one-third of the estate.
2
Determine which claim is stronger (aqwa)
Apply the criterion: the claim that applies to a greater portion of the estate is stronger. Debt applies to the entire estate; freedom in illness applies only to one-third. Therefore, debt is stronger.
The principle 'al-aqwa yadfa' al-adna' is rational (dalil aqli) based on the scope of each claim.
3
Apply the principle: stronger repels weaker
Since the debt is stronger, it should prevail. However, the freedom, once established, cannot be physically annulled. So the effect is achieved by requiring the slave to earn the amount of the debt (his own value) to pay it off.
This preserves the form of freedom but nullifies its benefit in meaning.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Abu Hanifa holds the slave must earn (wajib). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold the slave is free without any earning obligation. They either give equal weight to both or prioritize freedom.
The dispute is over the subsidiary consequence, not the principle itself.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Imam's view, the slave is free but must work to pay the debt. Under Sahibayn, the slave is immediately and fully free. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) in al-Hidayah follows Imam's view.
The principle itself remains authoritative; the dispute is about its application to this scenario.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A relative in custom is one connected through an intermediary; parents and children are not 'aqriba' for bequests.

In the chapter on bequests, the Hanafi school defines the term 'relative' (qarib) based on linguistic custom. This definition has practical consequences: if a testator bequeaths to his 'relatives', the bequest does not include parents or children because they are directly related without an intermediary. This lesson unpacks that definition and its application.

This is a lexical definition based on custom ('urf).

Parents and children have no intermediary between them and the testator.

Explicitly naming individuals overrides this definition.

Consensus Abandoning Apparent Meaning: The Hanafi Principle in Wills
Usul principle embedded in a fiqh ruling: consensus abandons apparent meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the apparent meaning of a word is abandoned when there is consensus to do so, using the will for relatives as an example.

Core Idea
Consensus (ijma) can outweigh the literal meaning: 'aqarib' is interpreted by purpose, not dictionary.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul principle: when there is consensus to depart from the apparent meaning of a word, that meaning is no longer authoritative. The example of 'aqarib' in wills illustrates this perfectly, as the scholars agreed to restrict its meaning based on the obligation of silat al-rahim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The text states: 'Zahir al-lafz la yu'tabar ba'd al-ijma' 'ala tarkihi'. This means the apparent meaning of a word is not considered after consensus to abandon it.
This is a usul principle.
2
Recognize the consensus
There is consensus that maintaining ties with mahram relatives is wajib, which forms the basis for interpreting 'aqarib' narrowly.
The consensus is on the obligation of sila, not directly on the word.
3
Apply the principle to the will
The literal meaning of 'aqarib' would include all blood relatives, but due to consensus on the purpose (sila), the word is restricted to mahram relatives.
This shows how usul principles guide fiqh rulings.
4
Conclude the legal effect
Therefore, the will is valid and must be distributed to the nearest mahram relatives, not to parents/children.
This is Abu Hanifa's view.

Literal Meaning Overridden by Consensus: Wasiyya for Relatives
Usul maxim with fiqh application in wasiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whom a will for 'aqarib' includes and explain how ijma overrides the literal meaning.

Core Idea
Ijma overrides literal meaning: 'aqarib' in wills excludes parents and children.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul principle: the literal meaning of a word is abandoned when consensus (ijma) has established a different interpretation. We apply this to the chapter of wills (wasiyya): when a testator bequeaths to his 'aqarib' (relatives), the Hanafi school—following Abu Hanifa—interprets this as the nearest mahram blood relatives, excluding parents and children, based on the consensus of the Companions. By understanding this principle, you will grasp how external evidence can restrict general terms in Sharīʿah.

Guided Path
1
Understand the usul principle
Grasp that the zahir (apparent meaning) of a word is normally binding, but if consensus (ijma) agrees to abandon that meaning, the consensus takes precedence.
This principle appears in Hanafi usul texts; here it is applied to fiqh.
2
Identify the ruling on wasiyya for aqarib
According to Abu Hanifa, when a person makes a will for his 'aqarib', the bequest is distributed among two or more of the nearest mahram blood relatives, excluding parents and children.
The condition is that these relatives must not be heirs (i.e., not entitled to fixed shares).
3
Analyze the rationale (sabab)
The purpose of such a will is to compensate for negligence in the obligatory duty of maintaining family ties (silat al-rahim), which is only wajib for mahram relatives.
Hence, the will is restricted to those for whom maintaining ties is obligatory.
4
Verify the role of ijma
The consensus of the Companions established that 'aqarib' in the context of wills does not include parents and children, thus overriding the literal linguistic meaning.
This is a classic example of takhsīs al-ʿāmm bi al-ijmāʿ (specification of a general term by consensus).
5
Note the practical outcome (thamarah)
The executor must identify the nearest mahram relatives (e.g., siblings, uncles, aunts) and distribute the bequest equally among them (two or more).
If there is only one such relative, the will is still valid? The passage says 'two or more', implying at least two recipients; further details may be in other texts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A will for 'ahl' is for the wife only because the absolute term reverts to its real meaning: wife.

In wills, testators often use general terms like 'ahl' (family). The Hanafi school strictly interprets such terms in their literal sense unless context indicates otherwise. This lesson explains why a bequest 'for the ahl of so-and-so' is exclusively for the wife, drawing on the principle that an absolute term (mutlaq) is taken in its real meaning (haqiqah) and on the Quranic verse that uses 'ahl' to mean wife.

The word 'ahl' is uttered as an absolute term (mutlaq) without qualification. The usul al-fiqh principle is that an absolute term is interpreted in its real (haqiqi) meaning unless a qarinah (contextual clue) indicates otherwise.

Legatee of Container Does Not Share with Legatee of Contents
Explains the Hanafi maxim separating container and contents in bequests.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that a legatee of a container does not share in the contents, and apply it to cases involving bequests of slave-girls and their unborn children, rings and gems, and baskets and dates.

Core Idea
A bequest of a container does not include its contents; each legatee receives only what was explicitly given.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle in the law of bequest (wasiyya): the separation between container and contents. When a testator bequeaths a container to one person and its contents to another, each takes only what was specified. We will explore the rationale and apply it to concrete examples from the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
The maxim states: 'The legatee of the container does not share with the legatee of the contents.' This is the core rule.
The maxim is found in Al-Hidayah and other Hanafi works.
2
Understand the rationale
The container and contents are distinct legal entities; a bequest of one does not imply the other. The bequest transfers only what is explicitly stated.
This is based on the general principle that bequests are construed strictly.
3
Review the examples
Three examples are given: (1) slave-girl and her unborn child, (2) ring and its gem, (3) basket of dates and the dates themselves. In each, container and contents are bequeathed to different persons.
The examples illustrate the maxim across different types of property.
4
Apply the maxim
In each example, the legatee of the container (slave-girl, ring, basket) gets only that container, and the legatee of the contents (child, gem, dates) gets only the contents. Neither shares in the other's bequest.
The bequests are all valid (sahih) as long as the items are distinct and identifiable.
5
Note the generality
The rule applies to any container-contents relationship where the items are separable and the bequests are explicit. It does not apply if the testator indicates otherwise.
This is a default rule; the testator may always specify a different intention.

General Term Equivalent to Specific: Bequest of Slave-Girl and Unborn Child
Application of 'aam muhit principle to bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a comprehensive general term ('aam muhit) is treated like a specific term (khaas) in certainty to determine the distribution in a bequest when the testator's offers are connected or separated.

Core Idea
A comprehensive general term is as certain as a specific term.

In Hanafi usul, not all general terms are treated as uncertain (zanni). When a general term encompasses all members comprehensively (ihata), it becomes equivalent to a specific term in certainty (qat'i). This lesson applies that principle to a classic case from Kitab al-Wasaya: a testator bequeaths a slave-girl and her unborn child. The ruling depends on whether the two bequests are made in one connected statement or separately. Understanding this nuance clarifies how Hanafis handle general terms in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two offers
The testator makes two bequests: one for the slave-girl and one for her unborn child. Identify each offer separately.
2
Determine connection or separation
Check if the testator utters both bequests in one connected statement (e.g., 'I give my slave-girl and what is in her womb to Zaid') or separates them (e.g., first bequeaths the slave-girl, then later bequeaths the child to another).
Connection means the two offers are made together without interruption.
3
Apply the principle of comprehensive general term
If the offers are connected, the word 'slave-girl' in the general sense comprehensively includes the unborn child (since both are mentioned together). This general term becomes like a specific term, so the entire bequest goes to the same legatee. If separated, the general term loses its comprehensive nature, and the terms 'slave-girl' and 'child' are treated as separate specific terms.
The principle: 'A comprehensive general term is like a specific term' (al-‘aam al-muhit bi-manzilat al-khaas).
4
Derive the ruling for each scenario
For connected offers: both items go to the same legatee. For separated offers: the slave-girl goes to the first legatee, and the unborn child is shared between the two legatees (since both terms are now qat'i and conflict).
The ruling is based on the certainty of the terms: when both are qat'i, they must be harmonized by giving each a share.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the validity of a dhimmi's will to convert his house into a church. The general principle is stated first: a will for a sin (ma'siyah) is invalid. The Urdu commentary explains that executing such a will would necessitate establishing the sin, which is impermissible. The specific case: a dhimmi makes a will for an unspecified, unconfined group to demolish his house and build a church. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), this will is void because building a church in dar al-Islam is a sin, even if the dhimmi considers it a good deed. Abu Hanifa, however, holds the will to be valid. The passage then quotes from two Hanafi commentaries: Al-Inayah and Al-Binayah, both confirming the disagreement. The Sahibayn's reasoning is that the act is a real sin (ma'siyah haqiqiyyah) and a will for sin is invalid because executing it establishes the sin (taqrir al-ma'siyah). The three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) agree with the Sahibayn. The passage does not provide Abu Hanifa's explicit proof, but his view is recorded as permitting the will.

Validity of Gestures Only When Customary
Establishes the principle that gestures are valid only if customary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a chronic mute (akhras) and a temporary mute (muttasil al-lisan) and apply the Hanafi rule on the validity of gestures in legal acts.

Core Idea
Gestures are valid only when they are customary and understood.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle: gestures can substitute for speech in legal transactions, but only if they are conventionally understood. The Hidayah presents a ruling on wills made by gesture, contrasting the chronic mute whose gestures are customary with the temporary mute whose gestures are not. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being ruled upon? A will (wasiyyah) expressed through gestures. The ruling: it is valid (sahih) if gestures are customary.
2
Distinguish the two cases
Chronic mute (akhras): gestures are customary → valid. Temporary mute (muttasil al-lisan): gestures are not customary → invalid.
The key is custom (urf), not the disability itself.
3
Understand the proof (dalil)
The proof is the principle itself: gestures are only considered when customary. This is a rational argument based on the need for clear communication.
4
Recognize the inter-madhhab dispute
The text states this principle is a proof against Imam al-Shafi'i, implying disagreement. The Shafi'i school may accept gestures even when not customary.
The text does not detail the Shafi'i position; it only presents the Hanafi proof.

300 (93) Gestures are only considered when they have become customary and known. Translation: A gesture is only considered valid when it is customary and known. Explanation: The meaning of this principle is that the gestures of a person are valid if his gestures are customary and familiar, i.e., people understand something from these gestures, for example, the gesture of a mute (akhras). And whoever's gestures are not familiar, they will not be considered valid, meaning rulings will not be attached to them, like the gestures of a person with a temporary speech impediment (muttasil al-lisan), i.e., a person whose tongue has temporarily become disabled due to illness. The author of Hidayah mentions this principle and explains that if some person made a will for someone and had the will written, and some people said to him, 'We will be witnesses to this will,' and he nodded in affirmation or wrote something indicating affirmation and consent, or something issued from him that indicated affirmation and acknowledgment, then this is valid and the will will be realized, because this stands in place of speech for him. But if a person is not truly mute but his tongue is temporarily disabled, then his gesture will not be considered valid, because the gestures of a chronic mute are familiar, and whoever's gestures are familiar, they are considered valid. Therefore, the gestures of a chronic mute are valid, while the gestures of a person with a temporary speech impediment are not familiar because it is possible that when the illness ends, he will speak again. Hence, his gesture and writing do not stand in place of speech. And whoever's gestures are not familiar, they are not considered valid. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof that establishes a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: gestures (isharah) are only legally valid when they are customary (ma'hudah ma'lumah). The text differentiates between two types of speech-impaired individuals: the akhras (chronically mute) whose gestures are conventionally understood by people, and the muttasil al-lisan (person with a temporary speech impediment) whose gestures are not customary because they can potentially speak after recovery. The ruling is applied to contracts and wills: a chronic mute's gesture of affirmation or writing can substitute for spoken consent, making a will valid. In contrast, a temporary mute's gestures are not valid substitutes. The passage concludes by noting that this principle serves as a Hanafi proof against Imam al-Shafi'i, implying a disagreement with the Shafi'i school on this point. The passage does not detail the Shafi'i position but states the Hanafi argument. The citation from al-Hidayah and al-Jami' al-Saghir reinforces the authority of this ruling.
