Slaughtering Tame vs. Wild Animals
Ruling with proof on voluntary vs. emergency slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between voluntary and emergency slaughter and apply the correct ruling based on whether the animal is tame or wild and the owner's ability.

Core Idea
Voluntary slaughter is the rule; emergency slaughter is only a substitute when voluntary is impossible.

This lesson explores a key distinction in Hanafi slaughter law: the method depends on whether the animal is tame or wild, and critically, on whether voluntary slaughter is possible. The passage establishes that emergency slaughter (wounding any part) is only a substitute for voluntary throat cutting and cannot be used when one has control over the animal. This principle is derived by analogy to sheep and supported by scholarly consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of slaughter
First, understand the two categories: voluntary (dhabh ikhtiyari) – cutting the throat between the collarbone and jaws – and emergency (dhabh idtirari) – killing by any wound that causes blood to flow.
Voluntary is the default for controllable animals.
2
Grasp the legal principle
The passage states: when you are able to perform voluntary slaughter, emergency slaughter is not permitted. Emergency is a substitute (badal) that only becomes valid when the primary method is impossible.
This is a key maxim in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Apply to tame vs wild animals
Tame animals (ma'nus) are controllable, so they require voluntary slaughter. Wild animals (wahshi) are generally uncontrollable, so emergency slaughter suffices. Similarly, tame animals that become uncontrollable (e.g., fall into a well) also become eligible for emergency slaughter.
The same ruling applies to domesticated cattle that run wild.
4
Examine the proof
The proof is based on qiyas (analogy): tame game is like a sheep – both are controllable – so their slaughter must be voluntary. The consensus of scholars is cited, reinforcing the principle.
This is an example of qiyas jali (clear analogy).
5
Note the Hanafi position and cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school relies on this distinction. Some Shafi'is may require throat cutting for all animals, but Hanafis allow emergency methods when voluntary is impossible.
The practical effect is seen in cases of runaway livestock.

When a Magian Spurs a Muslim's Hunting Dog
Provides ruling and reasoning for permissibility when a Magian spurs a Muslim's dog

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of causal hierarchy (tahaluf al-asbab) to determine the permissibility of prey when multiple agents influence a hunting dog.

Core Idea
A Muslim's initial sending of a hunting dog prevails over any subsequent spurring by a Magian, making the prey halal.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced scenario in hunting law: what happens when a Muslim sends his dog but a Magian spurs it on? The Hanafi ruling clarifies that the stronger legal cause (irsal) overrides the weaker (zajr), preserving the permissibility of the prey. In this lesson, you will unpack the legal reasoning behind this ruling and learn to apply it to similar situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal elements
Recognize that hunting with a dog involves two distinct causal acts: the initial sending (irsal) and any subsequent incitement (zajr). The ruling in this passage is that eating the prey is permissible (halal).
The ruling is explicitly stated in the source.
2
Distinguish the strengths of the causal acts
Understand that in Hanafi legal theory, irsal (sending) is a stronger legal cause than zajr (spurring). The passage argues that an action is nullified only by a stronger or equal action, and since zajr is weaker, it does not nullify the original irsal.
This hierarchy is based on the principle that the initiating act carries greater legal weight.
3
Apply the rule of nullification by stronger/equal acts
Take the general rule that a fi'l (action) is only nullified by an action that is superior or equal to it. Here, because the Muslim's irsal is stronger than the Magian's zajr, the irsal remains effective.
The rule is mentioned in the Arabic text: 'فعل اپنے سے اعلی یا اپنے مساوی فعل سے ختم ہوتا ہے'.
4
Derive the ruling for the scenario
Conclude that the permissibility established by the Muslim's lawful act of sending persists, so the prey is halal. The Magian's contribution does not introduce a sufficient cause to render the meat unlawful.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Hanafi school regarding hunting with dogs. It addresses a scenario where a Muslim sends his dog, but a Magian (fire-worshipper) subsequently spurs it on, causing the dog to become more excited and kill the prey. The ruling is that the prey is permissible to eat. The legal reasoning (ta'lil) is based on the hierarchy of actions in Islamic law: an action (fi'l) is only nullified by an action that is superior or equal to it. Here, the initial act of sending (irsal) by the Muslim is a stronger legal cause than the subsequent spurring (zajr) by the Magian. Since spurring is lower in rank, it does not cancel out the original sending. Therefore, the permissibility (halal) established by the Muslim's lawful action remains intact. This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi principle that the stronger cause prevails. Note that the opposite scenario—if the Magian sends first and the Muslim spurs—would yield a different ruling, as per the Arabic text cited from Al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
ارسال (irsal): The act of sending a hunting dog to pursue prey, legally considered the primary cause of capture.
زجر (zajr): The act of spurring or inciting a dog by shouting or gestures after it has been sent.

The Precautionary Preference of Prohibition when Causes Combine
States the legal maxim for combined causes and applies it to hunting scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi principle when causes of halal and haram combine in hunting, and determine the ruling based on avoidability of the prohibition cause.

Core Idea
When halal and haram causes combine and haram can be avoided, prohibition prevails as precaution.

Hunting often involves scenarios where the prey dies from multiple factors—a permissible one (like an arrow) and a potentially prohibitive one (like drowning). How does the Hanafi school resolve such conflicts? This lesson presents a key legal maxim from Al-Hidayah that governs this situation, establishing a clear hierarchy based on the possibility of avoiding the prohibitive cause. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly assessing the permissibility of hunted game.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
Read the opening Arabic: 'سَبَبُ الْحُرُمَةِ وَالْحِلَّ إِذَا اجْتَمَعَا...'. This states that when both causes are present, prohibition is given preference if avoidance of the cause of prohibition is possible.
The term 'احتياطا' signals the precautionary nature of this preference.
2
Define the condition of avoidability
The maxim distinguishes between avoidable and unavoidable causes of prohibition. If the cause can be avoided (e.g., retrieving the prey quickly), the prohibition applies. If impossible to avoid (e.g., the prey inevitably falls to the ground), the cause is ignored.
Avoidability is determined under normal circumstances, not exceptional ones.
3
Apply to the hunting scenario
The text applies the maxim: if a hunter shoots an arrow and the prey falls into water, the death is partly from the arrow (halal) and partly from drowning (haram). Since the hunter could have avoided the drowning (by retrieving quickly), the haram cause prevails. Conversely, if the prey falls on solid ground, the fall is unavoidable, so the halal cause prevails.
The ruling is qualified: if the arrow wound was instantly fatal, the drowning is irrelevant because the prey was already dead—thus avoidance is impossible and the halal cause prevails.
4
Note the qualification from Al-Inayah
The commentary mentions that the ruling is conditioned on the wound not being instantly fatal. If the arrow killed instantly, the subsequent fall into water is not considered a separate cause of death, so the meat is halal.
This shows the principle's nuance: 'impossibility of avoidance' includes cases where the prohibitive cause occurs after death.

Commentary
This passage is from the 'Fasl fi al-Ramy' (Section on Shooting) and contains a fundamental legal principle of the Hanafi school regarding the combination of causes of permissibility (halal) and prohibition (haram). The principle states: When both a cause of permissibility and a cause of prohibition are present in a situation, if it is possible to avoid the cause of prohibition, then prohibition is given precedence as a precaution (ihtiyatan). If it is impossible to avoid the cause of prohibition, then that cause is disregarded as if it did not exist. The author then applies this principle to two scenarios: (1) If a hunter shoots an arrow at an animal, and the animal falls into water and dies, it is not permissible to eat because the cause of prohibition (death by drowning) could have been avoided (the hunter could have retrieved it quickly). The key takeaway is the method of preferring prohibition when possible, and ignoring it when necessary.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding hunting with various projectiles. The commentator explains the principle that when a halal and haram element combine, the haram side prevails as a precaution (ihtiyat). The central point is the condition of wounding (jarh) for halal hunting. The text examines several scenarios: shooting with slingshots, guns, heavy stones, light sharp stones (marwah), sticks, etc. The ruling depends on whether death is definitely from a wound (halal) or from impact/weight (haram). If there is doubt, the animal is considered haram based on the principle. The Arabic portion includes the original text from Al-Hidayah and commentary from Al-Binayah, confirming that the condition for halal is that the projectile must cut (yajruh) the animal, and if it kills by crushing or weight without cutting, it is not permissible. The commentator also clarifies that if a heavy stone with an edge is used, and the animal dies without a wound, or if the veins are cut but death may have occurred from weight, it remains haram due to doubt. Only when death is certainly from the wound (e.g., a light sharp stone or arrow that cuts) is it halal. The passage also mentions the rule about a part cut from a living animal: if the animal is actually alive, the cut part is not halal; but if the animal is considered alive only in form (e.g., slaughtered by an illegal method), the part may be halal. This commentary uses the Hanafi hierarchy: al-Quduri's text is the base, Muhammad al-Shaybani's original statement is interpreted by later scholars (mashayikh), and the relied-upon position is that wounding is necessary, with doubt leading to haram.

Ruling on a Limb Cut from a Live Hunter Animal
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute on cut limb from hunted animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a limb cut from a live hunted animal is haram, identify the key hadith and distinction (haqiqatan/hukman), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on dhabh al-darurah.

Core Idea
A limb cut from a still-living hunted animal is carrion, not halal—even if the animal itself is halal.

When a hunter's arrow severs a limb from a game animal, the animal itself is halal by unanimous agreement, but what about the severed limb? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position that the limb is haram (carrion), based on the prophetic hadith and a nuanced distinction between real and legal life. We will also examine the Shafi'i view that the limb becomes halal if the animal dies from the shot, and understand why the Hanafis give priority to the general evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the severed limb is not eaten (haram). Note that the animal itself is halal by agreement.
The ruling applies only when the animal is alive both in reality (haqiqatan) and legally (hukman).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that al-Shafi'i and Ibn Abi Layla hold that if the animal dies from the shooting, the limb is halal, because it is severed by dhabh al-darurah (compulsory slaughter).
Their analogy compares cutting any limb in dhabh al-darurah to cutting the head in ordinary slaughter.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand their reasoning: In dhabh al-ikhtiyar (normal slaughter), cutting the head makes the head edible. They extend this to dhabh al-darurah, arguing that any limb cut off by the lethal wound should be edible.
They rely on qiyas (analogy) between the head in normal slaughter and the limb in compulsory slaughter.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis reject the analogy because the hadith 'Whatever is cut from a living animal is dead' (carrion) is general and overrides the analogy. Additionally, cutting the head in ordinary slaughter is uniquely exempted; the same exemption does not apply to limbs.
The Hanafis interpret 'living' in the hadith as fully alive (haqiqatan wa hukman), i.e., with both real and legal life.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis do not consume the severed limb, while Shafi'is consume it if the animal dies from the shot. The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the prohibition.
The disagreement is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different interpretive methodologies.
