The Principle of Tawqif and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Diya for an Apostate Target
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diya for apostate target

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions in the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding diya for an apostate target and explain the legal reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's view.

Core Idea
Liability is fixed at the time of the act; subsequent apostasy does not erase it.

This lesson examines a classical intra-Hanafi dispute that tests the limits of liability in homicide. At its heart is the principle that fixed measures (like diya amounts) are established by divine decree (tawqif), not human reasoning. The case involves a shooter whose target apostatizes between the act of shooting and the arrow striking. The dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (the Sāḥibayn) reveals deep differences about when liability crystallizes and whether a change in the victim's status can discharge an already incurred obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the parties
Understand the factual scenario: A Muslim shoots an arrow at a Muslim target. Before the arrow hits, the target apostatizes. The arrow then strikes the apostate, resulting in death. Identify the two positions: Imam Abu Hanifa holds the shooter liable for diya; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sāḥibayn) hold no liability.
The scenario is clearly stated in the source text.
2
Examine the Sāḥibayn's reasoning
The Sāḥibayn argue that apostasy nullifies the inviolability (isam) of the target's life. Since the life lost value at the moment of death, the shooter's obligation should be discharged. They liken it to destroying a slave who apostatizes after the act but before the destruction.
Their proof relies on the status of the victim at the time of the injury/death.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof (qiyas to ghasp)
Abu Hanifa argues that liability arises from the act of shooting, which was a direct cause when the target was still a Muslim. He analogizes to usurpation (ghasp): if one seizes property when it is owned, liability for loss is fixed at that moment, regardless of later changes. Similarly, the shooter's guarantee is fixed at the time of the shooting act.
This is a qiyas based on the principle that liability is attached to the act, not the final outcome alone.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical difference is clear: under Abu Hanifa's view, the shooter pays diya to the apostate's heirs; under the Sāḥibayn's view, no payment is made. This affects how estates handle such cases.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's, as presented in the major texts of the madhhab.
5
Reflect on the usul principle
The passage begins with the rule 'al-maqādīr tu'rafu bi al-tawqīf' — fixed measures are known by divine decree. This principle underpins the entire discussion: the amount of diya is fixed by revelation, but whether it is owed in this case is derived from legal reasoning (qiyas and i'tibār).
Understanding this principle helps avoid unwarranted extension of fixed penalties.

The Principle of Permission for Devotional Acts: No Liability for Unintended Harm
Principle that anyone may perform good deeds without liability for unintended harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that permission for devotional acts negates liability for unintended harm and apply it to scenarios such as mosque maintenance.

Core Idea
Performing a devotional act does not make you liable for unintended harm that results from it.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi principle: everyone is permitted to perform acts of devotion (qurbat), and if such acts inadvertently cause harm, the doer is not liable. We will examine the ruling, its application in cases like mosque maintenance, and the rationale of doubt in causation.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the principle
The text states that everyone is permitted to perform acts of devotion (qurbat) without liability for incidental harm. The principle is explicit: 'Kullu ahadin ma'dhunun fi iqamati al-qurbat'.
This is a general rule covering all devotional acts.
2
Apply to the mosque example
If a person beautifies a mosque (e.g., lays a mat or installs a lantern) and someone accidentally dies as a result, the doer is not liable. The text says this is by consensus (bil-ittifaq).
The act of beautifying the mosque is a qurbah.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason liability is negated is that the harm is often uncertain. The death might be caused by the act or by another factor. The text gives an example from fetal death where doubt about causation prevents compensation.
Doubt in causation (shakk) prohibits imposing liability.
4
Identify exceptions
If the act is clearly negligent or intentional, liability may arise. The principle applies only to inadvertent outcomes from permissible devotional acts.
For example, knowingly leaving a hazardous object would not be protected.

Principle of Sirayah: Transmission in Legal Descriptions Only
States the maxim on transmission in legal descriptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim of sirayah, distinguish between legal descriptions (awsaf shar'iyyah) and real descriptions (awsaf haqiqiyyah), and apply it to a fiqhi example.

Core Idea
Sirayah applies only to legal descriptions, not to physical ones.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim that governs how legal effects extend from one part to another. Understanding this maxim is critical for correctly applying rulings in areas like slavery, contracts, and torts, where the distinction between legal and physical attributes determines whether a ruling spreads.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Read the Arabic maxim and its translation. Memorize it as a precise legal formula.
The maxim is: 'السراية في الأوصاف الشرعية دون الأوصاف الحقيقية'.
2
Define the two categories of descriptions
Distinguish between legal descriptions (awsaf shar'iyyah) – attributes recognized by Shariah that carry legal consequences – and real descriptions (awsaf haqiqiyyah) – physical attributes like color or injury.
Legal descriptions include statuses like slavery, freedom, or contractual capacity.
3
Apply the classic example of partial freeing
If a master partially frees a slave, the freedom sirayah (transmits) to the entire slave because the description 'free' is a legal description. The slave becomes fully free.
This is a well-known application in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Explain why physical descriptions do not transmit
If an animal injures itself, the injury does not sirayah to other parts because it is a physical description. Each part is treated separately.
This is relevant in diyat (blood money) and liability cases.
5
Consider an application in transactions
In a sale, if a defect (ayb) sirayah to the entire commodity depends on whether the defect is considered a legal description (e.g., spoilage affecting the contract) or a physical one.
Hanafi jurists limit sirayah to legal descriptions to avoid unjust extensions.

Commentary
This is a concise legal maxim (qawida) from the Hanafi school. It states that the effect of an action or ruling (sirayah) applies only to legal descriptions (awsaf shar'iyyah) such as the status of being a slave or free, or the hukm of a contract, not to physical descriptions (awsaf haqiqiyyah) like color or weight. For example, if a slave is partially freed, the freedom sirayah (transmits) to the whole because it is a legal description; but if an animal injures itself, the injury does not transmit to its other parts because it is a physical description. This principle is used in various chapters, particularly in diyat and transactions.

Transference of Shar'i vs Haqiqi Attributes to Offspring
Ruling with proof: transference of debt vs crime to child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shar'i and haqiqi attributes and apply the Hanafi rule that only shar'i attributes (e.g., debt) transfer from a slave mother to her child, while haqiqi attributes (e.g., crime) do not.

Core Idea
Debt transfers from slave mother to child; crime compensation does not — shar'i attributes are transferable, haqiqi are not.

In this lesson, we examine a key Hanafi principle from Al-Hidayah: the transference (sarayat) of attributes from a slave mother to her child. The text distinguishes between shar'i attributes (like debt) that attach to the slave's person (raqabah) and transfer to the child, and haqiqi attributes (like crime) that are inherent in the mother and do not transfer. Understanding this principle clarifies why a child born to a debt-incurring slave is sold with her, but a child born after a crime is not subject to compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two cases: (1) a slave girl permitted in trade borrows and then gives birth; (2) she commits a crime and then gives birth. The ruling differs: in case (1) the child is sold with her; in case (2) the child is not given in compensation.
Focus on the legal consequence for the child.
2
Classify the attribute involved
Classify debt as a shar'i (legal) attribute because it is established by Shari'ah in the liability (dhimma) and is attached to the person (raqabah). Classify crime (jinayat) as a haqiqi (physical) attribute because it involves actual harm and its compensation is in the master's liability, not the slave's person.
The passage states: 'debt is a hukmi attribute obligatory in her liability, attached to her person'; crime compensation is 'in the liability of the master'.
3
Apply the principle of sarayat
According to Hanafi principle, shar'i attributes 'transfer' (sarayat) from the mother to the child because they are legal constructs that can move. Haqiqi attributes do not transfer because they are fixed in the original subject.
The text says: 'the shar'i attribute is a legal matter that changes with its change; the haqiqi attribute in a place cannot transfer to another.'
4
Note the analogy
The text draws analogy with kitabah (contract of manumission), tadbir (declaration of freedom after death), and rahn (mortgage) — all shar'i attributes that transfer to the child. This confirms the consistency of the principle.
This is a qiyas (analogical reasoning) that strengthens the ruling.
5
Conclude the practical ruling
Thus, for a slave girl who incurs debt, the child is sold with her to satisfy the debt. For a slave girl who commits a crime, the child is not subject to the compensation; the master bears the liability.
This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.

And his statement: 'And transference is in shar'i attributes, not haqiqi attributes' is based on the fact that the haqiqi attribute in a place cannot transfer to another, while the shar'i attribute is a legal matter that changes with its change. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Al-Hidayah on the principle of transference (sarayat) of attributes from a mother slave to her child. The principle distinguishes between shar'i (legal) attributes and haqiqi (physical/real) attributes. The ruling: If a slave girl who is permitted to trade (ma'dhun) takes a loan exceeding her value and then gives birth, the child is sold along with her to satisfy the debt. Core Idea
A repeated definite bequest is one bequest, not two.

The rule is straightforward: if a definite noun is repeated, the second occurrence is a reference to the first, not a new item. Understanding this rule prevents multiplication of bequests based on mere repetition.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Will to Convert a House into a Church
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of will for a church

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions (Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn) on a will to build a church and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of the dispute.

Core Idea
A will for what is objectively a sin is invalid, even if the beneficiaries consider it good.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about a will to convert a house into a church for unspecified people. Abu Hanifa holds the will valid based on the beneficiaries' perspective, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani rule it invalid because building a church is a sin in Islamic law. We will explore the reasoning of each side and determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the two opinions.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa holds: the will is valid. Why? Because the testator's intention is charitable and the beneficiaries consider it a good deed.
Abu Hanifa focuses on the subjective perspective of the beneficiaries.
2
Identify the opposing view
State the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) and the three other imams: the will is invalid. Why? Because the act of building a church is objectively a sin (ma'siyah) in Islam.
The Sahibayn prioritize the objective nature of the act over the beneficiaries' belief.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's dalil: a will for a sin is invalid, and executing it would confirm and perpetuate that sin.
They argue that the will itself is a wasiyyah bil-ma'siyah, which is void by scholarly consensus.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa's reasoning is that the testator's act is not sinful from the testator's perspective; it is a charitable donation to a community. The will is for a purpose that the beneficiaries consider good.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the will is valid (Abu Hanifa), the house becomes a church and the beneficiaries own it. If invalid (Sahibayn), the will is void and the house remains in the estate, distributed according to inheritance rules.
The practical consequence is whether the house is transferred or not.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of a will made by a Muslim (or possibly a dhimmi) to convert his house into a church for an unspecified group of people. The analysis follows the order of the text. First, Abu Hanifa holds that such a will is valid, presumably because the testator's intention is to give charity to a community, and the act of building a place of worship, even if for another religion, is not inherently sinful from his perspective. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) argue that the will is invalid because building a church is considered a sin in Islamic law (ma'siyah), regardless of the belief of the beneficiaries. They apply the general principle that a will for a sinful purpose is void. They further state that executing the will would entail affirming and perpetuating that sin, which is not permissible. The text then notes that the three other imams (al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad) also hold the view of invalidity. The commentary from Al-Inayah and Al-Binayah is incorporated to support this reasoning. Note that the authority_weight here is from the classical commentaries, not from the founding texts. The mufta bihi position later may follow the majority view of the Sahibayn and the three imams, but Abu Hanifa's position is recorded as a minority opinion.

When Gestures Substitute Speech: The Permanent Mute
Establishes rule for when gestures substitute speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi ruling on gestures of a permanent mute versus a temporary mute, and explain the underlying custom-based rationale.

Core Idea
Customary gestures of a permanently mute person stand in place of speech; temporary mute's gestures do not.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle that determines when non-verbal communication—gestures and writing—can replace spoken words in legal acts. The school distinguishes sharply between a permanently mute person (akhras) and someone with a temporary speech impediment (mu'taqal al-lisan). The decisive factor is whether the gestures are 'customary and known' (ma'hudah ma'lumah). This distinction not only resolves practical cases like wills and contracts but also forms part of the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i school's stricter stance on speech requirements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Condition
State the Hanafi ruling: gestures and writing of a permanently mute person (akhras) are valid for legal acts. Note the essential condition: their gestures must be customary (muttaraf) and known (ma'lum) to people.
This condition is derived from the principle 'al-isharat innama tu'tabaru idha sarat ma'hudatan ma'lumatan'.
2
Distinguish the Two Categories
Contrast the akhras (permanent mute) with the mu'taqal al-lisan (temporary speech impediment). For the temporary mute, gestures are not valid because recovery is possible, so their gestures are not reliably customary.
The key difference is permanence: the akhras has no hope of speech, while the mu'taqal may regain speech.
3
Trace the Proof from Imam Muhammad
Examine the proof from al-Jami' al-Saghir: a deaf-mute's nod or writing 'yes' in response to a will reading makes the will valid. This is the evidence that customary gestures substitute speech.
The proof is a sahih riwayah from Zahir al-Riwayah, though not mutawatir.
4
Analyze the Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Understand that this principle is the Hanafi response to Imam al-Shafi'i's position, who likely requires explicit speech for contracts. The Hanafis argue that custom ('urf) makes gestures equivalent to speech.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis allow marriage, divorce, etc. via gesture; Shafi'is may not.
5
Apply the Ruling to New Scenarios
Given the principle, determine the validity of a mute person's gesture for buying, selling, marrying, or divorcing. The ruling is sahih (valid) for the permanent mute if the gesture is customary.
For a temporary mute, the act would be invalid (ghayr sahih) until speech returns.

Explanation: The meaning of the aforementioned principle is that the gestures of a person are valid when his gestures are customary and known, i.e., people understand something from these gestures, for example the gesture of a deaf-mute (akhras). The author of Hidayah, after mentioning the principle, states that if a person made a will for someone and wrote a will document, and some people said to him, 'We will be witnesses to this will document,' and he nodded in affirmation or wrote 'yes' or something occurred that indicated affirmation and acknowledgment, then it is permissible and the will is effected, because that stands in place of speech for him. But if he is not actually mute but his tongue is temporarily closed, his gesture is not considered, because the gestures of a deaf-mute are customary, and whoever's gestures are customary is considered valid. Therefore, the gestures of a deaf-mute are valid, and the gestures of a mu'taqal al-lisan are not customary because after the illness departs, his speaking again is quite possible. Hence, his gesture and writing do not stand in place of expression, and whoever's gestures are not customary are not considered valid. So the gestures of a mu'taqal al-lisan are also not valid. Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i. The ruling concerns the validity of gestures and writing for a person who cannot speak. The Hanafi position, derived from al-Hidayah and al-Binayah, distinguishes between a permanently mute person (akhras) and a temporarily speech-impaired person (mu'taqal al-lisan). The general principle is: a gesture or writing is considered a valid substitute for speech only if it is customary and understood by people. For the permanently mute, their gestures and writings are customary and thus valid in matters like wills, marriage, divorce, and manumission. For the temporarily impaired, because their condition may be cured and they may regain speech, their gestures are not considered a reliable substitute. The text cites Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir describing that if a will is read to a deaf-mute and he nods or writes 'yes', the will is valid. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position. The Hanafis use this principle to counter Imam Shafi'i's likely objection that gestures and writing are insufficient for legal acts. The underlying reasoning (ta'lil) is based on the custom ('urf) and the possibility of recovery for the temporary mute.

Key Terms
أخرس (akhras): A person who is permanently unable to speak, a deaf-mute.
معتقل اللسان (mu'taqal al-lisan): A person whose tongue is temporarily tied due to illness, not permanent.
معهودة معلومة (ma'hudah ma'lumah): Customary and known; gestures that people understand.
