Division Negates Partnership – A Foundational Hanafi Maxim
States a maxim about division and partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim, explain its meaning, and apply it to scenarios of co-ownership to determine when partnership ends.

Core Idea
Division ends co-ownership; each party becomes sole owner of their portion.

This brief maxim (qa'ida) from the classical Hanafi legal tradition encapsulates a key principle: when two or more persons divide a jointly owned property, the state of partnership (shirkah) terminates. It is a cornerstone rule for understanding the end of co-ownership and the transition to exclusive ownership.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the maxim's terms
Identify the key words: al-qismah (division/partition) and al-sharikah (partnership/co-ownership). The verb tunafi means 'negates' or 'is incompatible with'.
Al-sharikah here refers to shirkat al-milk (co-ownership), not contractual partnership.
2
Understand the legal effect
The maxim declares that division and partnership cannot coexist. Once a mutually owned property is partitioned by mutual agreement or court order, the legal relationship of co-ownership dissolves.
This applies to both movable and immovable property.
3
Distinguish between types of shirkah
Remember that shirkat al-milk is passive co-ownership (e.g., inheritance), while shirkat al-aqd is a contractual partnership for profit. The maxim directly applies to the former; for the latter, division of assets may also dissolve the contractual partnership, but additional rules apply.
Hanafi fiqh treats each type separately.
4
Apply to a simple scenario
Assume A and B inherit a house as co-owners. They agree to physically divide it into two apartments. After the division, each owns their apartment exclusively. The maxim confirms that the original partnership (shirkat al-milk) is negated.
The division must be lawful and meet conditions of partition.

Rights Vest According to Contract Attribution in Agency
Principle of contract attribution in agency with sub-rule on guarantee of benefits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of contract attribution in agency to determine which party (agent or principal) bears the rights and obligations, and explain the Hanafi position that benefits are not guaranteed.

Core Idea
The rights of a contract vest in the one to whom the contract is attributed — agent or principal.

When an agent enters into a contract, the legal consequences depend on to whom the agent attributes the contract. Imam al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah lays down a clear rule: if the agent attributes the contract to himself, all rights (like receiving price, delivering goods) attach to him; if he attributes it to the principal, the rights attach to the principal. This lesson unpacks that rule and its application to purchase agency, including the distinctively Hanafi view that benefits (manafi‘) arising from such contracts are not guaranteed (ghayr madmoonah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract attribution
Determine whether the agent, at the time of the contract, expressed the contract as his own (e.g., 'I sell' or 'I buy') or on behalf of the principal (e.g., 'I marry X on behalf of Zaid').
The attribution must be valid according to the nature of the contract.
2
Determine the vesting of rights
Apply the rule: if attribution to self → rights vest in agent; if attribution to principal → rights vest in principal.
Rights include obligations like delivery and payment.
3
Apply to purchase agency (wakalah bil-shira’)
In a purchase agency, the agent typically attributes the purchase to himself, so he is responsible for handing over the sold item and paying the price.
The principal is not directly liable to the seller.
4
Examine the sub-rule on guarantee of benefits
When the agent attributes to himself, the rights are 'not guaranteed by destruction' (ghayr madmoonah bil-itlaf). This means the agent is not liable for loss of benefits (manafi‘) because guarantee requires equivalence, and benefits have no equivalent.
This contrasts with the Shafi‘i position and applies to benefits, not necessarily to the physical object (‘ayn).
5
Recognize the practical consequence
If a benefit (e.g., use of a leased property) is destroyed while in the agent's custody, the agent owes no compensation. The principal bears the loss of the benefit.
The physical object is treated separately under general rules of guarantee.

Commentary
This passage introduces a key principle from the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wikalah) in the Hidayah. The rule distinguishes contracts based on to whom the agent attributes the contract at the time of its formation. If the agent attributes the contract to himself (e.g., in sale, purchase, lease), then all rights and obligations arising from that contract (like delivery of the object, payment of price, receiving the sold item) vest in the agent, not the principal. Conversely, if the agent attributes the contract to the principal (e.g., in marriage, divorce), the rights (like dower) relate to the principal. The commentary then applies this to purchase agency, stating that the agent is responsible for the transaction. Interestingly, the passage also connects this agency principle to the earlier topic of guarantee (daman). This is a distinctively Hanafi position, contrasting with al-Shafi'i who would impose liability. The passage ends with a reference to the Hidayah's original text on agency.

Division vs. Alternate Use in Shared Property
Ruling on division vs. alternate use in co-ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one co-owner demands division and the other demands alternate use, and explain why division is preferred.

Core Idea
If one co-owner demands division, the judge enforces it over alternate use because it ends disputes more permanently.

Co-ownership can lead to disagreements on how to share the property. The Hanafi school addresses this by giving priority to physical division when one party demands it, even if the other prefers alternate use (muhaya'ah). This lesson explores the ruling from Al-Bahr al-Raiq and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two demands
Recognize that the passage contrasts two scenarios: one co-owner demands qismah (division) while the other demands muhaya'ah (alternate use).
Both are valid ways to share property, but they have different legal effects.
2
Apply the rule when conflicting
When demands conflict, the judge enforces division because it is 'ablagh' (more effective) in settling the co-ownership and preventing future disputes.
This applies even if the property is already being used alternately by mutual agreement.
3
Distinguish mutual agreement scenario
If both co-owners voluntarily agree to alternate use (e.g., each occupying certain rooms or floors), it is permissible and valid. The judge does not intervene.
The key difference is the presence or absence of a dispute.
4
Understand the rationale
Division is permanent and removes co-ownership, while alternate use is temporary and may lead to future conflict. Thus, when one party insists on division, it is enforced to achieve finality.
The term 'ablagh' indicates a preference for the more decisive solution.

Commentary
This passage is a quote from Al-Bahr al-Raiq, a Hanafi fiqh work, providing a ruling on the conflict between division (qismah) and alternate use (muhaya'ah) in shared property. The ruling is that if one co-owner demands physical division of the property and the other demands alternate use (muhaya'ah), the judge must enforce division because it is more effective (ablagh) in ending the co-ownership. However, if they mutually agree to alternate use in a single house, such as each taking certain rooms or floors, it is valid because such division (if done formally) would also be permissible. Alternate use is treated as a form of affirmation (iqrar) of the shares. This demonstrates the Hanafi preference for permanent division over temporary arrangements when there is conflict, to avoid future disputes.

In Hanafi fiqh, when co-owners disagree on the mode of sharing, physical division is enforced over alternate use because it is more effective in ending disputes.
