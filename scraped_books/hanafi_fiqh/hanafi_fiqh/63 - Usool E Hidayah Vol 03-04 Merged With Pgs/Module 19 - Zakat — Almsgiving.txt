السفه لا يبطل حقوق الناس: Foolishness Does Not Nullify Rights
Stating the principle with rational proof and application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that safah does not nullify huquq al-nas and huquq Allah, apply it to zakat and nafaqah, and explain the istihsan-based proof.

Core Idea
Safah does not nullify any rights – interdiction is a greater harm than squandering.

You might think a spendthrift should have his finances restricted. But the Hanafi school holds that restricting a free adult’s agency is a greater harm than allowing his squandering. This lesson unpacks the principle that safah does not void obligations like zakat and nafaqah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The core ruling is that a safih (foolish/spendthrift) is NOT interdicted (hajr) and his financial transactions are valid.
This applies to free, sane, adult Muslims.
2
Extract the Principle
From the ruling, derive the principle: safah does not nullify any pre-existing rights – neither rights of Allah (huquq Allah) nor rights of people (huquq al-nas).
The principle is stated explicitly: السفه لا يبطل حقوق الناس.
3
Analyze the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is istihsan (juristic preference). Weighing harms: interdiction causes greater harm (loss of humanity) than squandering. Greater harm is not borne to repel lesser harm.
This is a rational (aqli) argument, not textual.
4
Apply to Specific Obligations
Zakat (huquq Allah) and nafaqah for dependents (huquq al-nas) remain wajib on the safih. They are not dropped due to his foolishness.
The passage explicitly mentions zakat and nafaqah.
5
Note Cross-Madhhab Difference
Other schools may allow interdiction for prodigality. The Hanafi position protects human dignity and autonomy.
This difference is legitimate ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement with accompanying proof from the Hanafi school. It establishes the principle that safah (foolishness or prodigality) does not nullify the rights of people (huquq al-nas) nor the rights of Allah (huquq Allah). The immediate context is the discussion of interdiction (hajr) for a safih (spendthrift). The author, explaining the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, argues that declaring a safih interdicted would cause greater harm by stripping him of his humanity and equating him to animals, while the harm of his squandering is lesser. Therefore, the safih is not interdicted, and his financial transactions are valid. Despite his safah, he remains obligated to fulfill all rights: zakat (a right of Allah) and nafaqah (maintenance) for family members (a right of people). The text cites al-‘Inayah and al-Binayah to support this istihsan-based reasoning, emphasizing that greater harm (interdiction) is not borne to repel a lesser harm (squandering). The ruling underscores that safah does not nullify any pre-existing obligations—the safih must pay zakat from his wealth and provide for those dependent on him. This is a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh relevant to chapters on hajr (interdiction), zakat, and nafaqah.

The Substitute Follows the Original: Udhiyah Skin
Explaining the legal principle of substitute inheriting original's ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the substitute follows the original's ruling and apply it to the case of udhiyah skin to determine permissible uses.

Core Idea
The substitute takes the ruling of the original: udhiyah skin can be used personally, and its price can buy durable items.

In the previous lesson, we saw that the skin of a sacrificed animal can be used personally or given in charity. Here, the author of Al-Hidayah deepens the discussion by introducing a broader principle: the legal ruling of a substitute (badal) mirrors that of the original (mubdal). This principle is then applied to the permissibility of selling the skin and using its proceeds. Understanding this principle is key to navigating many similar fiqh scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original item and its ruling
The original (mubdal) is the skin of the sacrificed animal. Its ruling is that it can be used personally (e.g., as a prayer mat) or given in charity. Giving in charity is better, but personal use is permissible.
This ruling is derived from qiyas on meat: since meat can be benefited from, the skin, being of lesser value, is also permissible.
2
Recognize the substitute and the principle
When the skin is sold, the price (or item purchased with it) becomes the substitute (badal). The principle states that the substitute has the same ruling as the original—here, the skin's permissibility transfers to the substitute.
The principle is expressed as 'badal takes hukm al-mubdal'.
3
Distinguish between durable and consumable substitutes
The principle applies fully when the substitute is a durable item (e.g., a prayer mat) from which benefit is derived while the item remains intact. For consumable items (e.g., vinegar), the benefit destroys the item, so the purchase is not valid because the substitute does not preserve the same nature of benefit.
This distinction is crucial: the substitute must be similar in the manner of benefit (with remnant of the item).
4
Understand the role of istihsan
Strict qiyas might forbid any sale of the skin, but the Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to allow selling it for durable goods. This is because the substitute (price) is treated as a continuation of the original's permissibility.
Istihsan overrides strict analogy when a more suitable ruling is needed.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal principle from the Hanafi school: the substitute (badal) takes the ruling of the original (mubdal). It is an expansion on a ruling regarding the skin of a sacrificed animal (udhiyah). The principle is applied here to the permissibility of using the skin or its price to buy durable household items. The reasoning is that since the meat itself is permissible to benefit from, the skin—being of lesser value—is also permissible to use. By istihsan (juristic preference), if the skin is sold and with its price one buys an item that remains intact while being used (e.g., a prayer mat), that purchase and use are valid. This is because the substitute (price) takes the same ruling as the original (skin). However, if the skin is sold for dirhams or for consumable items (like vinegar), this is not allowed because the substitute (price) is not being used for something with the same ruling as the original (skin cannot be sold for cash; cash does not remain in the same category as skin). The authority for this is al-Binayah, and the ruling is based on istihsan, not qiyas.

Key Terms
بدل (badal): Substitute; here it refers to the price or item obtained from selling the skin of the sacrificed animal.
مبدل (mubdal): The original; here it refers to the skin of the sacrificed animal itself.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; a method of reasoning that departs from strict qiyas in favor of a more suitable ruling based on maslaha or custom.
