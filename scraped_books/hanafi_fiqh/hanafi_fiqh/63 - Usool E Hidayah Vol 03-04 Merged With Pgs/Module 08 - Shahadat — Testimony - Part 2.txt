Testimony Equivalent to Eyewitness Observation in Qisas
Ruling and proof that testimony equals eyewitness observation for qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that judicial testimony establishes facts as if directly observed and apply it to determine when qisas (retaliation) becomes wajib.

Core Idea
Testimony establishes facts as if seen; qisas is wajib when intentional killing with a sharp instrument is proven.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi criminal law: what is established through valid testimony is treated legally as if the judge himself witnessed it. This principle is crucial for qisas (retaliation) cases, where direct eyewitnesses may be unavailable. The passage from Al-Hidayah applies this maxim to a specific scenario: witnesses testify that the accused struck the victim with a sharp instrument, and the victim died after being bedridden. Understanding this principle clarifies the evidentiary power of testimony in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the legal maxim
Identify the maxim: 'Al-thabit bil-shahadati ka al-thabit mu'ayanatan' (What is established by testimony is like what is established by direct observation). This is the core ruling.
2
Identify the case and the ruling
The case: witnesses testify that Zaid struck Amr intentionally with a sharp instrument (shay' jarih), and Amr remained bedridden until death. The ruling: qisas (qawad) is wajib on Zaid.
The maxim makes testimony legally equal to seeing the act.
3
Note the condition from Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa requires the instrument to be jarih (cutting/piercing). If the instrument is not sharp, qisas is not wajib at the outset; it may be shibh al-'amd (quasi-intentional).
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) do not require this condition.
4
Understand the proof
The proof is the maxim itself, which is a legal principle derived from the Sharia's treatment of testimony as a substitute for direct observation. It is a form of qiyas based on the uniform acceptance of testimony in courts.
The maxim is from Al-Hidayah and Al-Jami' al-Saghir (Zahir al-Riwayah).
5
Recognize the practical consequence
The principle ensures that criminals cannot evade qisas simply because no one saw the act. Valid testimony establishes the fact fully, allowing the judge to impose the same punishment as if he saw it.
This applies across jinayat, hudud, and other areas where testimony is accepted.

Commentary
This passage presents a foundational legal principle (qawida fiqhiyya): 'What is established by testimony is like what is established by direct observation.' The author of Al-Hidayah uses this principle to justify the application of qisas (retaliation) in cases where death results from a beating that was witnessed only through testimony. The principle means that once testimony is accepted according to shar'i conditions, the fact is treated as if the judge himself saw it. The specific case: witnesses testify that Zaid struck Amr with a sharp instrument (shay' jarih) intentionally, and that Amr remained bedridden until death. The ruling: qisas (qawad) is obligatory on Zaid, because the testimony establishes the act of intentional striking with a lethal instrument, just as if it were seen. The condition that the instrument be jarih (cutting/piercing) is from Abu Hanifa; otherwise, if struck with a non-sharp object, qisas may not be required. The reference to Al-Jami' al-Saghir by Imam Muhammad provides the fact pattern: the victim 'remained bedridden until he died,' establishing causation. This principle ensures that judicial proof through testimony has full evidentiary value, allowing the judge to impose hadd or qisas based on testimony alone, without requiring physical evidence or confession.

Key Terms
ثابت بالشهادة (thabit bil-shahadah): A fact established through the testimony of qualified witnesses in a shari court.
آلة جارحة (alah jarihah): A sharp or cutting instrument used to wound, capable of causing death by cutting or piercing.
قود (qawad): Retaliation, specifically the execution of qisas by the state or heir.

Testimony of a Former Opponent: The Principle and Its Application
Ruling on rejection of testimony of a former opponent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a former opponent's testimony is rejected and explain the rationale of tuhmah.

Core Idea
Once a khasm, always tainted: even after leaving opposition, tuhmah bars testimony by consensus.

This lesson introduces a key rule in Hanafi testimony law: a person who was once an opponent (khasm) in a case cannot later testify in that same case, even if they are no longer a party. The principle is based on the concept of tuhmah (suspicion of bias), which persists despite the change in status. This ruling is established by consensus across the schools, making it a firm and widely accepted principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the Arabic text: 'Whoever becomes an opponent in an incident, then leaves being an opponent, his testimony in that incident is not accepted by consensus.' Note that the ruling applies to the same incident (ḥādithah), not to unrelated cases.
The ruling is absolute: no distinction is made between civil and criminal matters.
2
Understand the condition of being a khasm
A khasm is a litigant or someone who has a direct legal interest in the outcome. This includes parties, but also those who might be implicated, such as inhabitants of a locality in a qasamah case.
The commentary gives an example: two residents of a locality become khasm when a body is found, because they could be accused.
3
Grasp the concept of tuhmah
Tuhmah is a legal suspicion of partiality that renders testimony inadmissible. Even if the person is no longer an opponent, the taint of self-interest remains.
Hanafis consider tuhmah sufficient to reject testimony; other schools may require proof of actual bias.
4
See the consensus and its implications
The text explicitly states 'by consensus' (bil-ijmāʿ). This means the ruling is agreed upon by all madhāhib, strengthening its authority.
There is no ikhtilāf among the four schools on this principle.
5
Apply to the qasamah example
In the example, two locals testify that an outsider killed someone. Initially, they become khasm because they could be liable for qasamah. Later, the guardian claims against the outsider, and they cease to be opponents. Yet their testimony is still rejected due to the original tuhmah.
This shows the principle in action, even in complex criminal procedure.

Commentary
This is a ruling passage about the rejection of testimony from a former opponent. The principle: if a person becomes a khasm (litigant or opponent) in a case, even if they later cease to be an opponent, their testimony in that same case is not accepted, and this is by consensus. The reason is tuhmah (accusation of bias). The commentator provides an example: two residents of a locality testify that an outsider killed someone. Initially, these witnesses become opponents because they are suspecting themselves (as potential accuseds in qasamah). When the guardian of the victim claims against the outsider, they cease to be opponents, but their testimony is still rejected because the tuhmah remains that they are trying to shift blame. The Arabic quote at the end appears to be a reference to an earlier principle (al-mawjud al-awwal) from the same source, possibly a cross-reference by the author, but it is not directly related to the khasm principle. The lesson emphasizes that even after the change of status, the taint of self-interest invalidates testimony.

Key Terms
خصم (khasm): An opponent or litigant in a legal proceeding; someone who has a direct interest in the outcome.
تہمت (tuhmah): Accusation or suspicion of partiality or bias that prevents acceptance of testimony.

Testimony of a Former Litigant Rejected
Principle on rejection of testimony after exiting litigation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that testimony of a former litigant is permanently rejected, and apply it to the qasamah and guardianship examples.

Core Idea
Once disqualified by litigation, testimony remains rejected even after the witness ceases to be a litigant.

This lesson examines a foundational rule of shahadah: once a person becomes a party to a case, their testimony is forever blocked by the suspicion of bias (tuhmah), even after they exit the dispute. The passage from al-Hidayah establishes this principle by consensus and illustrates it through two key scenarios: residents testifying in a qasamah case, and a former guardian testifying regarding the ward. Understanding this rule is essential for grasping the strictness of Hanafi witness conditioning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its consensus basis
Read the first sentence of the Arabic text: 'Whoever becomes a litigant in a case, then leaves being a litigant, his testimony in that case is not accepted by consensus.' Pinpoint that this ruling is muttafaq alayh – agreed upon by all schools.
The consensus (ijma) gives the ruling its binding force.
2
Understand the rationale of tuhmah
The commentary explains the reason: 'because of suspicion (tuhmah)'. A former litigant may still harbour bias or be suspected of partiality. Recognise that tuhmah is a permanent bar for that specific case.
Even after leaving the litigation, the taint of potential self-interest remains.
3
Apply to the qasamah context
Consider the example: Two residents of a locality testify against a stranger for the murder of a body found in their locality. Under qasamah, residents would be liable for diyah, so they are effectively litigants – their testimony is rejected even if the victim's guardian later claims against another.
The residents are considered 'in the position of killers' due to potential liability.
4
Apply to the guardianship context
A person was a guardian (wasi) of a minor and thus could not testify in matters concerning the minor. After the minor reaches adulthood, the guardianship ends. Yet the former guardian's testimony in those same matters is still not accepted.
The disqualification persists even after the legal relationship ends.
5
Recognise the permanence of disqualification
Synthesise both examples: the bar arises from the initial involvement as a litigant (or its equivalent), and it does not lift when that involvement ends. This is a strict rule to protect the integrity of testimony.
The principle is not temporary but perpetual for that specific case.

Testimony Accepted When Potential Litigancy Ceases
Principle on acceptance of testimony after potential litigancy ceases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that testimony is accepted when the potential to become a litigant (aradiyyah) ceases, and distinguish it from cases of actual litigancy.

Core Idea
Testimony is accepted if the potential to be a litigant ends before the testimony.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi rule: when a person only had a theoretical possibility of being sued (aradiyyah) and that possibility vanishes before he testifies, his testimony is accepted by consensus. This contrasts with the previous principle where a person who was actually a litigant remains permanently tainted. Understanding aradiyyah is vital for judges evaluating witness eligibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
State the ruling: testimony is accepted. Identify the condition: the witness had a potential (aradiyyah) to become a litigant, but that potential has ceased before testifying.
The cessation must occur before the testimony is given.
2
Understand the concept of aradiyyah
Aradiyyah means a theoretical possibility, not an actual state. It is a weaker taint than actual litigancy. Examples: someone who could have been sued but the claim was dropped, or a potential guardian who never acted.
Distinguish from actual litigancy where the person was directly involved.
3
Recognize the evidence: ijma’
The ruling is established by consensus (ijma’) of the scholars. There is no dispute among the Hanafi school on this point.
Ijm’ gives the ruling qat’i (definitive) authority.
4
Apply to examples from qasamah and guardianship
In qasamah, witnesses from the victim’s locality are not accepted because they are actual litigants. But if they only had potential and it ceased, they are accepted. Similarly, a former guardian who was actually a litigant remains tainted, but one with only potential (the possibility of being appointed) and that potential ends is accepted.
These examples illustrate the boundary between actual and potential.

Testimony of Two Neighborhood Witnesses Against a Third Party: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness admissibility in qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the acceptance of testimony from neighborhood witnesses when the plaintiff sues another party, and apply the analogy with a dismissed agent.

Core Idea
Neighborhood witnesses are not automatically disqualified; their admissibility depends on whether they remain potential opponents.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about the admissibility of testimony from two residents of a neighborhood against a third party in a murder case. The key issue is whether the witnesses were potential opponents (عرضية الخصومة) and whether that status ends when the plaintiff sues someone else. Abu Hanifa holds that the testimony is not accepted, while his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), argue that it is accepted by analogy with a dismissed agent. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the Hanafi approach to witness impartiality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The case involves two witnesses from the same neighborhood as the victim testifying against a person from outside the neighborhood. The guardian of the victim has already sued a third party for the murder. The question is: can these two neighbors testify against the accused?
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa: testimony is not accepted because the witnesses were potential opponents (they could have been accused themselves). The Sahibayn: testimony is accepted because the potential opponent status was removed when the plaintiff sued another person.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's analogy
The Sahibayn draw an analogy (qiyas) with a legal agent (وكيل بالخصومة) who is dismissed before litigation. Just as a dismissed agent can testify after dismissal, the neighbors can testify once the plaintiff's claim against them is no longer present.
The analogy highlights the removal of adversarial interest.
4
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: in a murder case, if the guardian sues a specific person, neighborhood witnesses can testify against that person. If no specific person is sued, they cannot testify because they themselves could be accused.
The Sahibayn's view allows more testimony to be admitted.
