Agency Continues as Long as the Matter Exists
States a maxim about the continuance of agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim about agency continuance and apply it to determine when an agency automatically ends.

Core Idea
Agency ends automatically when the task is complete.

This passage introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi agency law: the agency (wakalah) remains in effect only as long as the matter (amr) for which it was created continues. Once that matter ceases, the agency terminates without the need for explicit revocation. This principle distinguishes wakalah from contracts that require formal dissolution and provides clarity on when an agent's authority expires.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Read the maxim carefully. In Arabic: 'بَقَاءُ الْوَكَالَةِ يَعْتَمِدُ قِيَامَ الْأَمْرِ'. In English: 'The continuance of agency depends on the existence of the matter.'
Memorize this as a key legal maxim.
2
Identify the key condition
The word 'قيام الأمر' (existence of the matter) is the condition. The agency continues only as long as the matter exists. If the matter ends, the agency ends.
Contrast with other conditions like the agent's consent or principal's life.
3
Apply the maxim to a scenario
Given a scenario, ask: Does the matter still exist? If yes, agency continues; if no, agency ends automatically.
Practice with examples from everyday life.

Chapter of Dismissal of the Agent. (81) The continuance of agency depends on the existence of the matter.

This maxim, under the chapter on dismissal of an agent, states that an agency (wakalah) remains valid only as long as the task for which the agent was appointed still exists. If the task is completed or the subject matter ceases, the agency automatically ends.

Constructive Dismissal and Agent's Knowledge
States a maxim about dismissal of an agent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim of constructive dismissal and apply it to determine the validity of an agent's actions after termination.

Core Idea
Constructive dismissal ends agency immediately, even if the agent doesn't know.

This lesson introduces a key maxim regarding agency termination. While explicit revocation requires notification, constructive dismissal—such as death or insanity—takes effect automatically. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining the validity of an agent's post-termination actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
Read the Arabic maxim and its English translation. Note the key term 'al-azl al-hukmi' (constructive dismissal).
2
Define Constructive Dismissal
Constructive dismissal includes causes like death, insanity, or apostasy of either party. These are events that terminate agency by operation of law.
Hanafi fiqh lists these as automatic termination events.
3
Contrast with Explicit Dismissal
Explicit dismissal (al-azl al-sarih) requires communication to the agent to be effective. Constructive dismissal does not.
The maxim specifically addresses this contrast.
4
Apply the Maxim
Consider a scenario: principal dies, agent acts without knowing. Under this maxim, the agent's actions are invalid because the agency terminated at death.
The maxim provides certainty: no need to inquire about the agent's knowledge.
5
Memorize the Arabic
Commit the Arabic maxim to memory: 'اَلْعَزْلُ الْحُكْمِيَّ لَا يَتَوَقَّفُ عَلَى عِلْمِ الْوَكِيلِ'.
This is a foundational maxim in Hanafi agency law.

Ignorance of the Confessed Item Does Not Prevent Valid Iqrar
States a rule about confession (iqrar) of unknown items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that ignorance of the confessed item does not invalidate iqrar, and apply it to scenarios of unspecified confession.

Core Idea
A confession of an unknown item is valid; ignorance only affects enforceability until specification.

This lesson examines a key maxim from the Hanafi book of iqrar (judicial confession). The maxim states that the confessor’s lack of precise knowledge about the item confessed does not render the confession void. This rule might seem counterintuitive, but it reflects the principle that iqrar is a strong proof against oneself, and details can be filled in later.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What does the maxim say about the effect of jahalah on iqrar? It states that ignorance of the confessed item does not prevent validity of the confession.
2
Distinguish types of ignorance
Not all ignorance is the same. The ignorance here refers to lack of specification (e.g., unknown amount), not total ambiguity about the existence of the right. The confession must be of a right that can be specified later.
3
Apply the maxim to a scenario
Consider a person who says 'I owe you a debt' without specifying the amount. According to this maxim, the iqrar is valid. The amount must be clarified later, but the confession itself is enforceable once specification is provided.

Exception is a Disposition in the Uttered Words
Defines the nature of exception in speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istithna (exception) as a disposition in the uttered words and state its consequence for interpreting legal statements.

Core Idea
An exception modifies the spoken words, not the hidden intention.

This short maxim establishes a key principle for interpreting statements that include exceptions. Understanding whether an exception affects the utterance or the speaker's intention has direct consequences for contracts, oaths, and other legal declarations. The Hanafi school holds that istithna operates on the literal wording, not the unexpressed intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term
Recognize that 'istithna' means exception, a phrase that excludes part of a general statement.
2
Parse the phrase 'disposition in the uttered'
The Arabic 'تصرف في الملفوظ' means the exception acts on the spoken words themselves, not on the speaker's unspoken intention.
3
Contrast with alternative views
Some might argue that intention matters; the Hanafi maxim insists on the literal wording to avoid subjective disputes.
This is a key point in Hanafi usul.
4
Apply to a concrete example
When someone says, 'I owe you 100 dirhams except 10,' the exception modifies the utterance, so the debt is 90 dirhams, regardless of inner intention.
This example shows practical application.

Commentary
This maxim defines istithna (exception, such as saying 'except' in a statement) as a modification of the uttered words rather than a change in the speaker's unexpressed intention. In legal contexts, an exception is understood to qualify the preceding statement literally.

The Limit on Confession: Not Harming Others' Rights
States a limit on the validity of confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a confession loses its evidentiary value and apply it to cases involving third-party rights.

Core Idea
Confession that harms a third party's right is not considered legal evidence.

This lesson introduces a crucial limit on the general principle that confession is decisive evidence. While a person's admission is normally binding, Hanafi fiqh protects the rights of others by rendering such a confession ineffective if it would damage a third party's legitimate claim. This rule is especially relevant in cases of terminal illness, where confessions could be used to circumvent inheritance or creditor rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Default Ruling
The act in focus is confession (iqrar). By default, it is considered strong evidence (daleel qati) against the confessor. Understand this principle first.
In Hanafi law, a person's confession is binding on themselves and their property.
2
State the Exception
The maxim says: 'la yu'tabar daleelan idha kana fihi ibtal haqq al-ghayr' — it is not considered evidence when it involves nullifying another's right. This is a restriction on the general rule.
The exception applies whether the confessor is healthy or sick, but is especially emphasized in the chapter on the sick person's confession (bāb iqrār al-marīḍ).
3
Understand the Rationale
The rationale is protecting third parties from harm. A confession that would eliminate or reduce the lawful rights of creditors, heirs, or others is not accepted, preventing fraud or preferential treatment.
This aligns with the maqasid (objectives) of protecting property and justice.
4
Apply the Ruling to a Scenario
When evaluating a confession, first check whether it harms another person's established right. If it does, the confession is not valid as evidence regarding that right. The affected party can challenge it.
The burden is on the one asserting the confession to show it does not harm others, or the confession is struck down.

Chapter of the Confession of the Sick. (103) Confession is not considered evidence when in it there is nullification of the right of another.
Source pages p. 10

Commentary
Under the chapter of a sick person's confession, this maxim states that if a confession would invalidate the rights of a third party (e.g., creditors), it is not accepted as evidence. This protects the interests of others, especially in cases of terminal illness where confessions may be suspect.

Key Terms
إِبْطَالُ حَقَّ الْغَيْرِ (ibtal haqq al-ghayr): Invalidation of the right of another person.

Consideration in Contracts is for Meanings, Not Words
States a principle of contract interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-I'tibar fi al-'Uqud li al-Ma'ani' and apply it to determine the validity of a contract based on the parties' intention.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the true intent behind a contract determines its validity, not just the words used.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi contract law: that the substantive meaning (ma'na) and intention of the parties override mere verbal expressions. This maxim, recorded in the chapter of Settlement (Sulh), guides judges and jurists in interpreting ambiguous contracts. Understanding this principle is key to appreciating the Hanafi emphasis on niyyah (intention) in all transactions.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Learn the exact Arabic wording: الْإِعْتِبَارُ فِي الْعُقُودِ لِلْمَعَانِي. Note the key term 'al-ma'ani' (meanings/intentions).
This maxim is also cited in other Hanafi works like al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir.
2
Understand the key term 'al-ma'ani'
Al-ma'ani refers to the intended meanings and purposes of the contracting parties, not the literal dictionary definitions of words.
It reflects the Hanafi focus on intention (niyyah) in all acts.
3
Apply the maxim to a contract scenario
When the literal wording of a contract conflicts with the clear intention of the parties, the intended meaning governs the contract's validity and interpretation.
Example: If words suggest a sale but both parties intended a gift, the contract is a valid gift.
4
Recognize the limits of the maxim
The maxim does not discard words entirely; words remain primary evidence of intention. Only when intention is clearly established may words be overridden.
If intention is ambiguous, the literal wording is relied upon.

Source pages p. 10

Commentary
This maxim states that when interpreting contracts, the focus is on the intended meaning and purpose, not merely the literal wording. If the words appear ambiguous but the intention is clear, the contract is valid according to the intended meaning.

Compensation for Another's Right is Prohibited
States a clear legal prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi prohibition on compensating for another's right and apply it to identify invalid transactions.

Core Idea
You cannot buy or sell a right that belongs to someone else.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi maxim: no one may receive compensation in exchange for giving up a right that belongs to another person. Only the actual right holder can transact on their own right. This rule safeguards personal rights from being traded by outsiders.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Maxim
Learn the Arabic text and its literal meaning: 'Compensation from the right of another is not permissible.' This is a concise, standalone ruling.
The maxim uses 'al-i'tyad' (receiving compensation) and 'haqq al-ghayr' (another's right).
2
Identify the Ruling
The ruling is a prohibition (haram). Any attempt to give or take compensation for a right that does not belong to you is legally invalid.
The word 'la yajuz' indicates impermissibility.
3
Recognize the Condition
The key condition is that the right in question belongs to another person. If the person has the right themselves, compensation is permitted.
Distinguish between one's own right and another's right.
4
Apply to Examples
Practice by applying the maxim to realistic scenarios: paying a creditor's friend to forgive a debt, or offering money to a neighbor to drop someone else's complaint.
The transaction is void (batil) if it involves another's right.

Preference by Strength, Not Number
Maxim on legal preference based on strength of reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the meaning of the Hanafi maxim on tarjīḥ and apply it to distinguish between strong and weak legal causes.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, quality of evidence outweighs quantity when preferring one ruling over another.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh: legal preference (tarjīḥ) is determined by the strength of the underlying causes (ʿilal), not by their number. This principle guides a jurist when conflicting evidences appear to have equal apparent weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim's core claim
Read the Arabic maxim and its translation. Note that it negates preference by multiplicity and affirms preference by strength.
2
Define key terms: tarjīḥ and ʿillah
Understand that tarjīḥ means giving preference, and ʿillah is the legal cause or ratio legis behind a ruling.
3
Distinguish number vs. strength
Recognise that a single strong cause (e.g., a clear qiyās jaliyy) can outweigh multiple weak causes. The maxim emphasizes quality over quantity.
This does not reject collective evidence; it prioritises internal strength.
4
Apply the maxim to a scenario
Consider two qiyās arguments for a ruling: one has a clear and directly relevant ʿillah, the other has many but weak analogies. The maxim guides preference to the strong single cause.

Source Text

التَّرْجِيْحُ لَا يَقَعُ بِكَثْرَةِ الْعِلَلِ بَلْ بِقُوَّةٍ فِيهَا
Preference does not occur by the multitude of causes, but by strength in them.
Source pages p. 10

Commentary
This maxim addresses the methodology of tarjīḥ (giving preference) when there are conflicting evidences or opinions. It means that when weighing legal proofs (ʿilal, sing. ʿillah), the number of arguments is not decisive; rather, one must look at the strength (quwwah) of each cause. For example, a single strong analogy (qiyās jaliyy) may outweigh several weak analogies. This is a principle of uṣūl al-fiqh applied in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Key Terms
ترجيح (tarjīḥ): The process of preferring one legal opinion or evidence over another.
علة (ʿillah): A legal cause or rationale that forms the basis for a ruling.

Key Terms from Source Passage

In Hanafi tarjīḥ, the quality of a legal cause (ʿillah) is decisive, not the quantity of supporting arguments.

Acknowledgment Cannot Be Conditional
Maxim on unconditional nature of iqrār

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that acknowledgment cannot be conditional and apply it to distinguish valid iqrār from invalid ones.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment must be immediate and unconditional; a conditional iqrār is void.

This lesson explains a fundamental Hanafi maxim: acknowledgment (iqrār) cannot be suspended on a condition. This rule is critical for distinguishing a binding admission of debt or right from a mere promise. The maxim ensures legal certainty in obligations.

Guided Path
1
Define iqrār
Iqrār is a declaration by which a person affirms a right or liability against themselves. It is a form of testimony against oneself.
Iqrār is one of the causes of obligation in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand ta'līq
Ta'līq means attaching the effect of a legal act to a future condition or event. In iqrār, this is impermissible.
Examples: 'If you cook dinner, I owe you $10' – this is ta'līq.
3
Apply the rule
When presented with a statement, check if it contains a condition. If it does, the iqrār is invalid (bāṭil).
The iqrār must be absolute and immediate.
4
Distinguish from contracts
Note that ta'līq is generally valid in contracts (e.g., sales, marriage) but not in iqrār. This is because iqrār is a form of testimony, which requires certainty.
This distinction is crucial for Hanafi fiqh understanding.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
الْإِقْرَارُ لَا يَحْتَمِلُ التَّعْلِيقَ بِالشَّرْطِ
Acknowledgment does not admit of suspension by a condition.
Source pages p. 10

Commentary
This maxim means that iqrār (acknowledgment of a right) cannot be made conditional upon a future event. For example, saying 'I owe you $100 if I pass the exam' is not a valid acknowledgment because it is suspended (ta'līq). The acknowledgment must be immediate and unconditional. This is because iqrār is a type of testimony against oneself, and such testimony must be definite.

Key Terms

تعليق (ta'līq): Suspending the effect of a legal act on a future condition.

A Thing Does Not Include Its Equal: The Maxim of Taḍammun
Maxim on implication of equal things

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-shay' la yataḍamman mithlahu' to distinguish between independent legal rights and identify cases where inclusion is impossible due to equal strength.

Core Idea
An equal thing does not contain its equal; independence of legal rights prevents automatic implication.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi legal maxim about implication (taḍammun). It states that if two legal concepts are equal in strength (quwwah), one cannot be said to include the other. This principle is crucial in distinguishing between rights that are independent, such as ownership of usufruct versus substance. Understanding this maxim helps avoid erroneous expansions of rights in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Begin by memorizing the Arabic text: الشَّيْءُ لَا يَتَضَمَّنُ مِثْلَهُ لِتَسَاوِيُهِمَا فِي الْقُوَّةِ. Its literal translation: 'A thing does not include its like because of their equal strength.'
Focus on the key term 'yataḍamman' (includes/implicates).
2
Identify the key term: taḍammun
Taḍammun means inclusion or implication: when one thing legally contains or implies another. Here, it is denied because both are equal in strength.
In Hanafi usul, taḍammun is valid only if one is stronger or includes the other.
3
Understand the condition 'equal strength'
Equality in strength (quwwah) means both legal concepts have the same level of authority or independence. For example, the right to usufruct and the right to ownership of substance are both independent rights, so neither implies the other.
If one right is stronger, inclusion may be possible (e.g., ownership implies right to use, but not vice versa).
4
Apply to a Hanafi fiqh example
Consider a lease: the lessee owns the usufruct (manfa'ah) for the term. According to this maxim, this ownership does not imply ownership of the substance (raqabah) because both rights are equal in strength—they are independent. Thus, the lessee cannot claim the property itself.
This distinction prevents automatic transfer of ownership.

Core Idea
Death of either party dissolves the agency — it is a personal contract that ends with the person.

This lesson introduces a fundamental maxim in Hanafi contract law: agency (wakālah) is automatically terminated upon the death of either the principal or the agent. Understanding this rule is essential for knowing when an agent's authority ends and why the contract cannot be inherited.

The effect is immediate and automatic.

No distinction is made between principal and agent.

Agency is a contract of trust (amānah) founded on the personal capacity of the parties. It does not transfer to heirs.
The rationale also applies to loss of legal capacity (e.g. insanity), though not mentioned here.
5
Distinguish from other terminations
Unlike revocation, which is a deliberate act, death is an involuntary cause that automatically ends the contract.
Actions performed by the agent before death of either party remain valid.

Core Idea
Type of possession determines liability: trustee is innocent unless negligence; usurper is liable even for accidents.

This short maxim from the Hanafi tradition packs a powerful legal principle: not all possessions are equal in trustworthiness. While someone holding property as a trust (amānah) is only liable for negligence, a usurper (ghāṣib) bears full responsibility even if the property is destroyed without fault. Understanding this classification is key to resolving disputes over damaged goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its scope
The maxim 'al-aydi takhtalif fi al-amānah' states that legal possession (yad) varies in the degree of trust (amānah) attached to it. This is a general principle governing liability for property in one's control.
Memorize the Arabic: الأيدي تختلف في الأمانة.
2
Distinguish trust hand (yad amānah) from guarantee hand (yad ḍamān)
A trust hand (e.g., depositee) is only liable for loss caused by negligence or transgression. A guarantee hand (e.g., usurper, buyer after delivery) is bound to compensate for loss even if not due to fault.
Key difference: yad amānah = no liability unless ta'dī (wrongful act); yad ḍamān = strict liability.
3
List examples of each type
Examples: trustee (wadī'), partner (sharīk), agent (wakīl) – usually yad amānah. Usurper (ghāṣib), borrower (musta'īr), buyer after delivery – yad ḍamān.
The classification depends on the legal relationship, not the person's character.
4
Apply to a scenario to determine liability
Given a scenario (e.g., borrowed item destroyed without fault), determine whether the possessor must compensate. If yad amānah, no; if yad ḍamān, yes.
Practice with examples from daily transactions.

The Maxim 'Al-Imtina' bi Qadr al-Mani''
A Hanafi legal maxim stating proportionality of prevention.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prevention is proportional to the preventer — the invalidating effect is limited to the specific defect.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that governs the scope of prohibition or invalidity. The maxim states that when an act is prevented due to a specific legal hindrance (mani'), the prevention is limited to the extent of that hindrance. In practice, this means that a defect in a contract only nullifies the defective part, not the entire contract, as long as the defect is severable. Understanding this maxim helps in correctly applying rulings in sales, leases, and other transactions.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its literal meaning
Recite the Arabic: الْاِمْتِنَاعُ بِقَدْرِ الْمَانِعِ. Understand that 'al-imtina'' means prevention/prohibition and 'al-mani'' means the preventer. Literally: 'Prevention is according to the extent of the preventer.'
Memorize the Arabic wording as it is a core Hanafi legal maxim.
2
Identify the key terms
Analyze the two terms: al-imtina' refers to the legal prevention of an act's validity or effect, and al-mani' is the specific legal cause that triggers this prevention. In Hanafi usul, a mani' is a circumstantial hindrance that blocks the operation of a ruling.

The maxim teaches that the invalidating effect of a preventer is confined to its own scope. If a contract has a defect that can be separated without affecting the core, only the defective part is void, and the rest remains valid.

If the defect is integral to the contract (e.g., the subject matter is legally impermissible), then the entire contract is void because the preventer affects the essence. The maxim still applies: the prevention is proportional to the preventer, but here the preventer is the entire subject matter.

Ghasb is the wrongful taking of physical control by displacing the owner's possession through an act on the property.

Guided Path

Identify the subject

Once ghasb is established, the usurper (ghāṣib) must return the property in its original condition if possible, and must compensate for any loss or destruction (ḍamān). This obligation arises regardless of whether the usurper benefitted.

Hanafi fiqh treats ghasb and ghalaba (unlawful occupation) similarly.

Three consecutive legal maxims

This lesson covers three foundational maxims from the chapter on sacrifice (udhiyah) and slaughter. They address animal defects, the continuity of the slaughter act, and the legal effect of substitutions. These rules ensure that the ritual sacrifice is performed with a sound animal and proper procedure.

Maxim on Sales: Corrupting Factor (32)
Single maxim on proof of corrupting factors in sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the maxim on corrupting factors in sales, distinguishing between subsequent and concurrent defects.

Core Idea
A subsequent defect is established by one witness; a concurrent defect requires two.

This lesson presents a single maxim from the sales chapter regarding how corrupting factors (al-mufsid) are proven. It distinguishes between defects that occur after the contract (tari) and those present at the time (muqarin).

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of corrupting factors
A corrupting factor can be tari (arising after the contract) or muqarin (existing at the time of contract). The proof requirements differ.

2
Apply the proof rule for tari
If the factor is subsequent, the testimony of one just male is sufficient to establish it.
The rationale is that the initial contract was sound.
3
Apply the proof rule for muqarin
If the factor is concurrent, two just witnesses are required because the validity of the contract itself is in question from the start.
This is a stricter requirement.

Core Idea
Accepted general rulings prevail over disputed specific ones; water’s force overcomes other forces; an act is removed by a superior or similar act.

This lesson captures three maxims from the chapter on reviving dead lands (ihya' al-mawat). They guide how to prioritize rules when there is disagreement, how to resolve conflicts between forces (especially water rights), and how certain actions can nullify prior actions.

Maxims on Pledge (39-41)
Three maxims on pledge (rahn)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three maxims on pledge (rahn) and apply them to determine liability for trusts, responsibility for maintenance and preservation, and the rule when a pledged item is connected to a non-pledged item.

Core Idea
Trusts become liable with transgression; maintenance costs are on the pledgor, preservation costs on the pledgee; a pledged item cannot be connected to non-pledged property.

This lesson presents three maxims from the chapter on pledge (rahn). They determine when a trustee (pledgee) is liable for loss, who bears the costs of maintenance and preservation, and the invalidity of a pledge that is physically attached to non-pledged items.

Guided Path
1
Understand Maxim 39
Maxim 39: الأمانات تضمن بالتعدي – Trusts are liable in case of transgression. The pledgee holds the pledged item as an amanah (trust), so he is not liable for loss unless he transgresses (e.g., uses it).
Transgression makes him a guarantor.
2
Understand Maxim 40
Maxim 40: What is needed for the maintenance and preservation of the pledge is on the pledgor; what is for its protection or to return it to the pledgee is on the pledgee.
Maintenance (e.g., feeding an animal) is pledgor's duty; preservation (e.g., guarding) is pledgee's duty.
3
Understand Maxim 41
Maxim 41: If the pledged item is connected to something not pledged, the pledge is not valid.
The pledge must be a distinct property; sharing with non-pledged parts makes the contract invalid.

Nullifying an Action by a Superior or Equivalent Action
Legal maxim on nullification of actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim on nullification of actions and apply it to identify when a subsequent action can nullify a prior act of worship or transaction.

Core Idea
An action is nullified only by an action of higher or equal legal status.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that governs the nullification of actions. The maxim states that an act (such as a prayer or contract) can be nullified by a subsequent act that is either legally superior (fawq) or equivalent (mithl). For example, a voluntary prayer (nafl) may be nullified by the obligation to perform a fard prayer, because fard is above nafl. Understanding this principle is essential for determining the validity of breaking one act for another and for resolving conflicts between overlapping devotional or contractual obligations.

Guided Path
1
Define the Key Terms
Understand 'fi'l' (action) and 'raf'' (nullification). Fi'l includes any voluntary act subject to legal rules; raf' means lifting the legal effect of an action by a subsequent action.
The Hanafi school distinguishes raf' from ibtal (invalidation).
2
Identify the Two Conditions for Valid Nullification
The nullifying action must be either legally above (fawq) the original action or equal (mithl) to it. Inferior actions cannot nullify.
Legal status is determined by the shariah classification (fard, wajib, sunnah, nafl, etc.).
3
Apply the Maxim to a Concrete Example
Consider a person performing a voluntary prayer (nafl). When the iqamah for the obligatory prayer (fard) is called, the nafl prayer may be nullified because fard is above nafl.
This is a common practice in the Hanafi school.
4
Distinguish Nullification from Invalidation
This maxim deals with raf' (lifting the legal effect) not ibtal (rendering an act invalid). A valid act can be nullified, but an invalid act has no legal effect to begin with.
The maxim does not allow arbitrary cancellation; it only applies when the subsequent act has a higher or equal claim.

Legal maxim on resolving conflict between halal and haram causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim on conflicting causes of halal and haram and apply it to scenarios where avoidance is possible or not.

Core Idea
When halal and haram causes coincide, the haram side prevails if avoidable; if unavoidable, the haram is ignored.

This lesson introduces a core Hanafi legal maxim that resolves conflicts between causes of permissibility and prohibition. Understanding this maxim is crucial for deriving rulings in doubtful situations. The maxim balances precaution (iḥtiyāṭ) with practical necessity, reflecting the school's methodological nuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two causes
Recognize that the scenario involves both a cause (sabab) making something halal and a cause making it haram. The maxim provides a rule for when they coincide.
The maxim assumes both causes are equally present in the same object or situation.
2
Assess avoidability
Determine whether it is possible (amkana al-taḥarruz) to avoid the cause of prohibition. If yes, proceed to step 3; if not, go to step 4.
'Avoidance' means physically staying away from the prohibited element without undue hardship.
3
Apply precaution (iḥtiyāṭ)
If the haram cause is avoidable, the ruling of prohibition is given preponderance (turajjah jihat al-ḥurma). This is a precautionary measure to safeguard against sin.
This does not mean the halal cause is invalid; rather, the stronger obligation is to avoid the prohibited.
4
Treat haram cause as non-existent
If avoidance is impossible, the existence of the haram cause is disregarded (yarā wujūduhu majrā 'adamihi), and the ruling of permissibility applies.
This prevents undue hardship and keeps the matter permissible when prohibition cannot be avoided.

Combining Wounds in Qisas: The Hanafi Maxim
Legal maxim on combining wounds for qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on combining wounds in qisas and apply it to determine whether a single retaliatory act suffices for multiple wounds.

Core Idea
Combine multiple wounds into one qisas act when possible to avoid excess.

This lesson introduces a core Hanafi principle in the law of qisas (retaliation). When a victim suffers several wounds, the Shariah commands proportionality and mercy even in punishment. The maxim 'al-jam'u bayna al-jirahati wajibun ma amkana' (combining wounds is obligatory when possible) ensures that the retribution is not multiplied unnecessarily. Understanding this rule requires grasping its rationale: tatmiman lil-awwal (completion of the first wound). We will walk through the conditions and application of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The maxim states that combining multiple wounds during qisas is obligatory (wajib) when possible. Identify the act (combining wounds) and its legal classification (obligatory).
This is not a recommendation but a binding rule within the limits of feasibility.
2
Understand the rationale: tatmim lil-awwal
The reason for combining is to complete the retribution for the first wound. If you inflict a separate act for each wound, you might exceed the proportionate punishment. Combining ensures the first wound is fully retaliated and additional wounds are covered by the same act.
The phrase 'tatmiman lil-awwal' is key to understanding the Hanafi perspective.
3
Determine when combining is possible
Assess the nature of the wounds: are they on the same limb or area? Can one matching cut or injury encompass all wounds without causing excessive harm? If yes, combining is obligatory. If not (e.g., wounds on different limbs), then separate qisas must be performed.
Physical possibility is the criterion—not convenience or discretion.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario with multiple wounds, decide whether combining is feasible. For example, if the victim has three cuts on the forearm, the judge should aim for a single cut that covers all three sites. If the cuts are on the arm and leg, separate retaliation is needed.
Always consider proportionality: the combined act must not be more severe than the sum of the wounds.

Commentary
Ruling statement: This maxim pertains to the application of qisās for multiple wounds. When a victim has multiple wounds, the principle is to try to apply a single act of retribution that covers all wounds simultaneously, if possible, as a way of completing the retribution for the first wound. For example, if the victim has cuts on different parts of the body, the judge should try to execute one matching cut that encompasses all the wounds, to avoid excessive punishment. If that is not possible, then separate qisās for each wound may be considered. This ensures proportionality and mercy in punishment.

Key Terms
جمع (jam'): Combining or unifying separate acts into one.
تتميم (tatmīm): Completion; making whole or finishing the retribution for the first wound.
