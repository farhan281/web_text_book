The Qawida of Binding Transactions and Its Application to the Ma'dhun
Explains a qawida and its application to the ma'dhun

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi qawida on binding vs non-binding transactions, explain its proof via qiyas, and apply it to determine when a ma'dhun slave becomes restricted.

Core Idea
A non-binding transaction needs capacity at its inception and throughout; a binding one only at inception.

This lesson unpacks a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that distinguishes binding (lazim) from non-binding (ghayr lazim) transactions. The maxim dictates whether the capacity (ahliyyah) of the acting party must persist for the transaction to remain valid. We will explore the rationale behind this principle and see it applied to the classic case of a ma'dhun slave whose master loses capacity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Qawida
Read the Arabic maxim: 'ما يكون لازما من التصرف يعطى لدوامه حكم الابتداء'. Understand that for a binding transaction (lazim), continuation does not require the same capacity as inception; for a non-binding transaction (ghayr lazim), it does.
The key distinction: lazim vs ghayr lazim.
2
Identify the Two Categories
Binding transactions (e.g., sale after acceptance) are valid with capacity at inception only. Non-binding transactions (e.g., a master's permission to a slave to trade) require capacity throughout.
The ma'dhun permission is ghayr lazim.
3
Examine the Proof
The proof is qiyas: just as capacity is needed at inception for any transaction, a non-binding one requires it for continuation because its continuation is akin to a new inception.
The dalil is a rational analogy, not a direct text.
4
Apply to the Ma'dhun Case
When the master dies (death ceases ownership), becomes insane (ceases capacity), or apostatizes and joins dar al-harb (ownership transfers), the permission becomes void. The slave becomes mahjur (restricted).
The master's permission is non-binding, so its continuation needs his capacity.
5
Contrast with Binding Obligations
Earlier in the passage, the safih (fool) is still obligated to pay zakat and maintenance because those are binding obligations (rights of Allah and people) and do not require capacity for continuation.
Binding obligations are not transactions but duties.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, explaining a key Hanafi qawida (legal maxim) and its application. The maxim states: 'A non-binding transaction requires capacity at its continuation just as at its inception.' The author begins by discussing the case of a safih (foolish person) in relation to zakat and maintenance, showing that safah does not exempt one from obligations of Allah or rights of people. Then, under the heading 'Kitab al-Ma'dhun', the principle is stated: for a binding transaction, its continuation does not require the capacity needed at inception, but for a non-binding transaction, capacity is required throughout. This is applied to the case of a ma'dhun slave (a slave permitted to trade). If the master dies, becomes insane, or apostatizes and joins dar al-harb, the slave becomes restricted (mahjur). The reason is that the master's permission is a non-binding transaction, so its continuation requires the master's capacity. Death and apostasy remove ownership, insanity removes legal capacity, so the permission becomes void. The author quotes from Al-Hidayah, Al-Inayah, and Al-Ikhtiyar to support this ruling. The quote from Al-Hidayah Kitab al-Ghasb at the end is actually about the same ma'dhun case, demonstrating that this principle applies across chapters.

Key Terms
تصرف لازم (tasarruf lazim): A binding transaction that does not require capacity for its continuation, e.g., sale that is binding upon both parties.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave permitted by his master to engage in trade.
محجور (mahjur): A restricted person, here a slave whose permission to trade is revoked due to loss of capacity of the master.

Unnecessary Pain in Slaughter is Makruh
Explains the principle of unnecessary pain in slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting the spinal cord or head during slaughter and explain why it is makruh but the meat remains halal.

Core Idea
Causing extra pain in slaughter is disliked, not forbidden.

In this lesson, we examine a key principle from al-Hidayah: any act causing unnecessary pain during slaughter is makruh. The author applies this to cutting the spinal cord (nakh') or severing the head. You will learn the proof and why the slaughter remains valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Cutting the spinal cord or head during slaughter. The ruling: makruh (disliked).
Makruh here is tanzihi, not sinful.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the report of 'Umar forbidding nakh' and hastening death. Al-Bayhaqi attributes the view of karahah to al-Shafi'i as well.
The dalil is ahad, zanni al-thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition indicates dislike because the act does not invalidate the slaughter; the essential requirement (cutting throat) is fulfilled.
The imperative of nahy is interpreted as tanzih here.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
No significant ikhtilaf: both Hanafi and Shafi'i consider it makruh. No one says haram.
Imam al-Shafi'i also holds it makruh per al-Bayhaqi.

Emergency Slaughter Only When Normal Slaughter is Impossible
Defines the condition for resorting to emergency slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid emergency slaughter in Hanafi fiqh and apply it to determine whether emergency slaughter is permissible in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Emergency slaughter is only valid when normal slaughter is impossible — it is a substitute, not an alternative.

This lesson explains a key principle from al-Hidayah: emergency slaughter (dhabh al-idtirar) is a concession allowed only when normal slaughter (dhabh al-ikhtiyar) is impossible. The Hanafi school emphasizes that normal slaughter is the primary method for domesticated animals; emergency methods like shooting or stabbing are only backups. Understanding this hierarchy ensures that meat is halal by the correct method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of slaughter
First, recognize that dhabh al-idtirar (emergency slaughter) and dhabh al-ikhtiyar (normal slaughter) are distinct categories. Normal slaughter is the prescribed method; emergency slaughter is a concession.
Normal slaughter requires cutting the throat while the animal is under control; emergency slaughter includes any means that causes death when normal slaughter is impossible.
2
Understand the hierarchy
The principle establishes a hierarchy: normal slaughter is the original (asl), emergency slaughter is a substitute (na'ib). The substitute is only valid when the original is impossible.
This is a logical and legal order: the primary method must be attempted first.
3
Recognize the condition (shart)
The condition for emergency slaughter is inability (al-ajz) to perform normal slaughter. If one is capable, emergency slaughter is not permissible.
The condition is a negative one: inability. It must be actual, not just difficulty.
4
Apply the condition to a scenario
Given a scenario, assess whether the slaughterer can physically and practically perform normal slaughter. If yes, emergency slaughter is invalid; if no, it is valid.
Consider factors like the animal's location, controllability, and available tools.
5
Note the wisdom behind the ruling
The ruling ensures that the prescribed method is prioritized, preserving the integrity of the act and avoiding unnecessary deviations.
Also protects the animal from prolonged suffering when possible.

The Principle of Subordinates in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains the principle and its application to gold/silver plating, with intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi legal principle 'lā mu‘tabara bi al-tawābi‘' and apply it to determine the ruling on using items with gold/silver subordinate parts, including the intra-madhab difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Subordinate elements do not alter the ruling; gold/silver plating on edges is permissible if avoided, per Abu Hanifa.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every element of an object carries independent legal weight. The maxim 'lā mu‘tabara bi al-tawābi‘' teaches us that subordinate parts (tawābi‘) follow the ruling of the original. In this lesson, we see how this principle applies to vessels and other items with gold or silver plating, and we explore a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Maxim
Read the principle 'lā mu‘tabara bi al-tawābi‘' and grasp that it means subordinates are not independently considered in rulings; the ruling attaches to the original.
This is a key qā‘ida in Hanafi fiqh for mixed materials.
2
Identify the Scenario
Recognise that the scenario involves vessels, saddles, beds, etc., where gold or silver is attached only on edges or as inlay – not as the main material.
The metal must be truly subordinate (tābi‘), not the intended substance.
3
Apply the Principle
Trace how Abu Hanifa applies the maxim: because the gold/silver is subordinate, the object retains the ruling of its original material (e.g., wood, ordinary metal). Hence, using it is permissible (mubah) if one avoids direct contact with the precious metal.
The condition of avoidance (ittiqā’) is crucial.
4
Analyse the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Note that Abu Yusuf considers such use disliked (makruh), and Muhammad has two narrations. Understand their dalil: general prohibitions on using gold/silver vessels override the subordinate consideration.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
5
Note the Practical Consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: per Abu Hanifa, Muslims may use such items with caution; per Sahibayn, they should avoid them. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
The dispute extends to many items: vessels, chairs, ceilings, mosque decorations, mirror frames, etc.

Commentary

Ruling Attributed to Complete Cause
Explains a legal maxim with istibra' example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim "al-hukm yudāfu ilā tamām al-illah" to determine when istibra' becomes wājib upon a buyer of a slave girl.

Core Idea
A ruling attaches only when its cause is fully complete; partial cause yields no obligation.

This lesson unpacks a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim that governs the timing of obligations. Using the specific case of istibra' (waiting period for a slave girl), the author of Al-Hidāyah demonstrates that the ruling of wujūb only arises when the cause—full ownership (milk al-raqabah)—is completely realized. Partial ownership does not trigger the ruling.
