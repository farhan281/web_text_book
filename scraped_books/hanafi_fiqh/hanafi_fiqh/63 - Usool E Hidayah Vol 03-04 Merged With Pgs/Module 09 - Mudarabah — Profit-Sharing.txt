The Principle in Mudarabah is Generality
States a legal maxim concerning mudarabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling in mudarabah and apply the principle of generality to determine permissibility of conditions in such contracts.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, all conditions and practices are permissible unless specifically prohibited.

This brief maxim from Hanafi jurists establishes a foundational principle for the mudarabah contract. It teaches us that the default attitude in mudarabah is not restriction but permissibility. Understanding this helps us navigate the many conditions that can be agreed upon between capital provider and entrepreneur.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Learn the exact wording: 'الْأَصْلُ فِي الْمُضَارَبَةِ الْعُمُومُ'.
2
Understand the key term
'al-umum' means generality, i.e., default permissibility. All acts and conditions in mudarabah are allowed unless a specific prohibition exists.
3
Identify the scope
This maxim applies to the mudarabah contract of Islamic finance, covering all aspects including conditions, practices, and profit-sharing arrangements.
4
Contrast with other contracts
In sale (bay'), the default is prohibition (al-asl fi al-buyu' al-hurmah). In mudarabah, the opposite: permissibility is the baseline.
5
Apply the principle
Given a scenario, determine permissibility based on whether there is a specific prohibition. Default: permissible unless proven otherwise.

Ignorance in Profit Share Invalidates Mudarabah
Maxim on vitiating mudarabah by uncertainty in profit share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on profit share uncertainty in mudarabah and explain why it invalidates the contract.

Core Idea
Uncertainty in profit share voids the mudarabah; a known ratio is essential.

Mudarabah is a fiduciary contract where one party provides capital and the other provides labor. For the contract to be valid, the profit share of each party must be clearly specified from the outset. This lesson examines the maxim that any ignorance (jahālah) in the profit share corrupts the contract, making it fāsid (voidable).

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that this maxim applies to mudarabah, a partnership where capital and labor are combined for profit sharing.
2
Identify the vitiating element
The element that invalidates the contract is jahālah (ignorance) specifically in the profit share (ribḥ).
Uncertainty about the capital amount is a separate issue.
3
State the ruling
The Hanafi ruling: any arrangement that introduces uncertainty into how profits are divided makes the mudarabah fāsid (corrupt).
The maxim uses the verb 'yufsid' (corrupts), not 'yubṭil' (renders void), but the contract can be corrected if the parties agree to a known ratio later.
4
Explain the reasoning
Mudarabah is a contract of amānah (trust) and requires clarity to avoid disputes. Uncertainty in profit share leads to potential conflict and is prohibited.
5
Apply to an example
If A gives B capital and says 'I will give you some of the profit', the share is unknown. The contract is fāsid because the profit share is not clearly defined.

This is because mudarabah requires a known profit-sharing ratio from the outset.

The Default Generality (al-umum) in Mudarabah
States the principle of generality in mudarabah and its application to disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that mudarabah is unrestricted by default and apply it to resolve disputes over restrictions.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the default is unrestrictedness – any restriction must be explicitly stated by the rabb al-mal.

This lesson examines a key maxim in Hanafi fiqh: 'The principle in mudarabah is generality' (الأصل في المضاربة العموم). This maxim determines the burden of proof when a dispute arises between the investor and the worker. The passage from al-Hidayah and its commentaries establishes that the mudarib's word is taken when he claims the contract was unrestricted, while the rabb al-mal must prove an explicit restriction. The proof is that mentioning the word 'mudarabah' itself gives the mudarib full authority to transact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal maxim
State the Arabic maxim and its English meaning: 'al-aslu fi al-mudarabati al-umum' – the principle in mudarabah is generality (unrestrictedness).
This maxim is the foundation of the ruling.
2
Understand the dispute scenario
The rabb al-mal claims a specific restriction (e.g., cash-only, trade in Basra). The mudarib claims the contract was unrestricted (itlaq).
This is the typical ikhtilaf that triggers the maxim.
3
Apply the maxim to resolve the dispute
The mudarib's claim of unrestrictedness is accepted because the default in mudarabah is generality. The rabb al-mal must prove he explicitly imposed a restriction.
Burden of proof is on the one alleging a deviation from the default.
4
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The author argues: 'Do you not see that he owns disposition by the mere mention of the word mudarabah?' This shows that unrestricted authority is inherent in the contract's name.
The proof is based on the linguistic implication of the term mudarabah.
5
Cross-reference with wakalah
The passage draws an analogy to agency (wakalah), where minor ambiguity is tolerated by istihsan. This reinforces the leniency in mudarabah as well.
This is an expansion probe – the author uses a parallel to support the ruling.

It then applies this maxim to resolve a dispute between the rabb al-mal (investor) and the mudarib (worker) regarding whether the mudarabah was restricted (e.g., to cash transactions only) or unrestricted. This principle ensures that the default in a mudarabah contract is flexibility, and any restriction must be explicitly stated by the rabb al-mal.

Whose Word Prevails in a Mudarabah Scope Dispute
Hanafi ruling on whose statement prevails in mudarabah scope dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule when the mudarib and rabb al-mal dispute the scope of authority, and explain why the mudarib's claim is accepted.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the default is unrestricted authority; the mudarib's claim of generality prevails unless the rabb al-mal proves a restriction.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling on disputes in mudarabah. When the manager (mudarib) and investor (rabb al-mal) disagree about whether the manager's authority was restricted or unrestricted, whose word is accepted? The Hanafi school answers: the mudarib's. Why? Because the very nature of a mudarabah contract implies broad trading authority. Let us explore the legal reasoning behind this position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The rabb al-mal claims he imposed a restriction (e.g., trade only in land or only for cash). The mudarib claims the contract was unrestricted (iṭlāq). Understand the exact dispute.
The restriction could be on place, time, type of goods, or mode of sale.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi school rules that the mudarib's statement is accepted. The burden of proof falls on the rabb al-mal to show the restriction.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Locate the reasoning (talīl)
The default in mudarabah is generality (al-asl fi al-mudarabah al-umum). The word 'mudarabah' itself grants the mudarib general permission to trade. Therefore, the mudarib's claim of unrestrictedness aligns with the contract's inherent nature.
The proof is not a text but a rational principle derived from the contract's definition.
4
Consider the implication for the rabb al-mal
If the rabb al-mal wanted a restriction, he must have explicitly stated it at the time of contract. Silence implies generality.
This protects the mudarib from unexpected limitations.
5
Note the authority and weight
The ruling is established in the Hanafi school from early authorities and is considered mufta bihi. It is found in Al-'Inayah, Al-Binayah, and Fatawa Qadikhan.
Cross-madhhab: some other schools may differ, but the Hanafi position is clear.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Any condition that makes the profit share unknown invalidates the mudarabah contract.

The Hidayah presents a concise yet critical rule governing mudarabah: any condition that causes uncertainty (jahalah) about the profit share renders the contract invalid. This lesson unpacks that principle and explores its application through a concrete example. Understanding this rule is essential for ensuring that profit-sharing arrangements remain free from ambiguities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a mudarabah contract. The ruling is that it becomes fasid (invalid) if a condition causes jahalah in profit.
Fasid means voidable; the contract is not completely void but lacks legal effect until rectified.
2
Understand key terms
Jahalah means ignorance/uncertainty about the profit share. Shart is a stipulation by either party. The profit is the object of the contract, so it must be known.
Knowledge of the object is a general requirement in commutative contracts.
3
Recognize the rationale
The profit share (ribh) is the ma'qud alayh (object of exchange). If it is unknown, the contract resembles a gamble and leads to disputes. Hence any condition causing jahalah invalidates.
This rationale is based on the need for clarity to prevent conflict.
4
Apply to the example
If the capital provider stipulates a fixed amount (e.g., 2000) for himself and the remainder to the worker, the profit share is unknown because total profit is unknown. This constitutes jahalah, so the contract is fasid.
The same applies to any condition that makes the share indeterminate.
5
Note the universality
The principle covers any condition, not just the fixed amount example. Any stipulation that leaves the profit share uncertain invalidates the mudarabah.
This is the established Hanafi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mudarabah upon the death of either party and explain the qiyas (analogy) to wakalah that supports it.

Core Idea
Death of mudarib or rabb al-mal voids mudarabah because it is a form of agency, which ends with death.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from the Hidayah: the death of either the investor (rabb al-mal) or the manager (mudarib) in a mudarabah partnership invalidates the contract. The Hanafi school derives this by analogy to agency (wakalah), where death likewise terminates the relationship. Understanding this qiyas reveals how Hanafi jurists extend established rules to new contexts while maintaining internal coherence.

The source text quotes from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira and Al-Lubab.

The practical result is that upon death, the mudarabah contract is void immediately; any profits after death are not subject to the original profit-sharing agreement.
This ruling is undisputed (muttafaq alayh) in the Hanafi school.

The Possessor's Statement in Mudarabah Disputes
Establishment of the legal maxim that the possessor's word is taken regarding the amount received

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim governing disputes over received property and explain why the mudarib's statement is accepted despite the initial contrary view.

Core Idea
In disputes over received property, the possessor's word prevails, even over a denier's claim.

This lesson examines a foundational Hanafi maxim from the chapter of mudarabah: when the mudarib and capital owner disagree about the amount of capital given, whose statement is accepted? The passage records an intra-Hanafi dispute where Imam Abu Hanifa initially held the owner's statement, then retracted in favor of the mudarib. Understanding this shift and its reasoning is key to grasping the school's commitment to contextual analysis over rigid rules.

Guided Path
1
State the legal maxim
Identify the principle: 'al-qawl qawl al-qābiḍ' – the statement of the possessor is accepted regarding the amount received. Note that it applies to both guarantors (ḍāmin) and trustees (amīn).
This maxim is not limited to mudarabah; it applies broadly to any possession dispute.
2
Apply the maxim to the mudarabah scenario
Consider the case: the mudarib has 2000 dirhams, claims capital was 1000 and profit 1000; owner claims capital was 2000. Under the maxim, the mudarib's statement is accepted because he is the possessor of the amount in question.
The financial consequence: the mudarib owes only 1000 (the lesser amount) after swearing an oath.
3
Identify the initial disagreement
Note that Abu Hanifa initially agreed with Zufar that the owner's statement should be accepted, because the owner is the denier (munkir) of the claimed partnership, and the default rule favors the denier.
This shows the tension between the maxim of the denier and the maxim of the possessor.
4
Trace Abu Hanifa's retraction and its reasoning
Abu Hanifa later retracted (raja'a) and adopted the mudarib's statement. The reason: the real dispute is about the amount received, not the partnership itself. The possessor is more knowledgeable of what he received.
This retraction demonstrates istiḥsān (juristic preference) overriding the initial qiyas.
5

The final Hanafi fatwa is that the mudarib's statement is accepted. Zufar's view is marjūḥ (non-operative).
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is valid, but the school follows Abu Hanifa's final view.

Explaining the rule that the possessor's statement is accepted in disputes over capital amount.

In disputes over the amount of capital possessed, the possessor's statement is accepted, not the denier's.
