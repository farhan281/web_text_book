Action Lifted by What is Above or Equal
States a principle and applies it to hunting with dogs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle 'al-fi‘l yurfa‘u bima huwa fawqahu aw mithlahu' and apply it to determine whether a secondary act (zajr) cancels the primary act (irsal) in hunting.

Core Idea
An action is only canceled by a stronger or equal action—the weaker never overrides.

In Hanafi fiqh, the concept of 'raf‘' (lifting/canceling) is crucial when multiple actions interact. This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces a foundational rule: a later action lifts a prior action only if it is superior or equal. The rule is illustrated in the context of hunting with dogs: a Muslim sends his dog (irsal), then a Magian excites it (zajr). Does the Magian's act nullify the Muslim's? The principle provides a clear answer.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Write down al-fi‘l yurfa‘u bima huwa fawqahu aw mithlahu. Note that raf‘ means lifting/canceling an action's legal effect.
The principle applies across fiqh, not just hunting.
2
Identify the two actions
Identify irsal (sending the dog) by the Muslim, and zajr (exciting the dog) by the Magian.
Irsal is the primary act; zajr is secondary.
3
Compare their strength
Determine whether zajr is above (fawqa), equal (mithl), or below (duna) irsal. According to the principle, if zajr is weaker, it cannot lift irsal.
The Hanafi position holds that zajr is weaker because it is a mere incitement, not the release.
4
Derive the ruling
Since zajr is not above or equal to irsal, the act of the Muslim remains effective, and the prey is halal.
Thus, the Magian's involvement does not render the prey haram.
5
Note the ruling's consistency
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi position.
The presence of a non-Muslim in a supporting role does not override the Muslim's primary act.

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental Hanafi legal principle: an action is only canceled or superseded by an action that is either stronger (fawqahu) or equal (mithlahu). The principle is illustrated in the context of hunting with dogs. If a Muslim sends his dog (irsal = the act of releasing the dog), and then a Magian (Majusi) excites the dog (zajr = shouting or inciting), the prey remains permissible (halal) because the Muslim's act is stronger or equal to the Magian's act. The Magian's excitation is considered weaker; hence, it does not nullify the effect of the Muslim's sending. The passage also includes an extended quote from Al-Binayah about reviving dead lands (Ihya al-Mawat) as another example of the same principle: the Imam's permission is required for reviving land because the land is under his control (qahr), and his permission is a stronger authority that must be present. This interjection appears to be a parenthetical illustration, but the primary focus remains the hunting scenario. The Hanafi position is clear: the permissibility of the prey depends on who performed the dominant act.

Key Terms
فعل (fi‘l): Action or act, here referring to the act of sending a dog (irsal) or exciting it (zajr).
رفع (raf‘): Lifting, canceling, or superseding; the act of removing the effect of a prior action.
زجر (zajr): Exciting or inciting an animal, e.g., shouting to make a dog more eager in hunting.
ارسال (irsal): Releasing or sending a dog for hunting; the primary act of the hunter.

Combining Causes of Prohibition and Permissibility in Hunting
Introduces a principle for when causes of prohibition and permission conflict in hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle for when causes of prohibition and permissibility conflict, and apply it to the two hunting scenarios involving a Magian and a Muslim.

Core Idea
When prohibition and permission causes combine, the stronger cause prevails—the sending (irsal) dominates over mere excitation (zajr).

In the previous lesson, we learned that when a Muslim sends his dog and a Magian excites it, the prey is halal. Now we examine the reverse: if a Magian sends the dog and a Muslim excites it, what is the ruling? The key is to identify which cause is stronger. This lesson introduces a general principle for resolving such conflicts: when the stronger cause of prohibition is present and can be avoided, the prohibition side is preferred as a precaution.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
The passage gives a rule: when causes of prohibition and permission combine and the prohibition cause can be avoided, prefer prohibition as a precaution. If it cannot be avoided, treat it as non-existent.
2
Identify the two scenarios
(a) Magian sends, Muslim excites: prey is haram. (b) Muslim sends, Magian excites: prey is halal (from previous lesson).
3
Explain why the stronger cause prevails
The excitation (zajr) is weaker than sending (irsal). The prohibition cause from the Magian's sending dominates, so the prey is haram.
The term 'shubhat al-hurma' indicates a weak prohibition that may influence the ruling.
4
Understand the term shubhat al-hurma
The passage mentions 'shubhat al-hurma' (suspicion of prohibition) in the context of the first scenario, indicating that even a weak prohibition can affect the ruling when the sending is by a non-Muslim.
5
Apply the principle to similar cases
Extend: if both acts are by a non-Muslim, the prey is haram; if both by Muslim, halal; if mixed, the stronger cause determines.
This principle is a memorization anchor for taharri (preference).

Commentary
This passage introduces another key principle: when two conflicting causes (sabab al-hurma and sabab al-hill) are present, and it is possible to avoid the cause of prohibition, the prohibition side is given preference as a precaution (ihtiyatan). If avoidance is impossible, the prohibition cause is treated as non-existent. Two scenarios are presented: (1) If a Magian sends the dog and a Muslim excites it, the prey is not eaten (haram) because the Magian's act (irsal) is the stronger cause, and the Muslim's excitation is weaker and cannot override it. (2) If a Muslim sends the dog and a Magian excites it (already discussed under principle 35), the prey is permissible because the Muslim's irsal is stronger. The passage further clarifies that the principle of 'action lifted by superior/equal' (principle 35) is the underlying rationale. The term 'shubhat al-hurma' (suspicion of prohibition) is mentioned: in the first scenario (Magian sends, Muslim excites), the prohibition is stronger because it is established more quickly. The Hanafi position is that the action with greater strength prevails. The quotes from Al-Inayah and Al-Binayah confirm these rulings as standard Hanafi positions.

Key Terms
سبب الحرمة (sabab al-hurma): The cause or factor that makes something prohibited (e.g., the dog being sent by a non-Muslim).
سبب الحل (sabab al-hill): The cause or factor that makes something permissible (e.g., the dog being sent by a Muslim).
شبهة الحرمة (shubhat al-hurma): A suspicion or doubt of prohibition; a lesser degree of prohibition that may affect the ruling.

The Principle of the Severed Limb and Conditions for Hunting Tools
Establishing a principle and applying it to hunting scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a limb cut from a living animal and apply the condition of wounding to classify hunting tools as permissible or not.

Core Idea
A limb severed from an animal that could survive is carrion; the tool must cut, not crush.

This lesson addresses a foundational principle in Hanafi hunting fiqh: the ruling on a limb that becomes separated from an animal while it is still alive. The key distinction is whether the animal was legally alive (i.e., could survive the wound) or only physically alive (i.e., death was inevitable). The source also applies this principle to various hunting tools, requiring that they cut by wounding rather than kill by weight.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Identify the two categories of 'living' (ḥaqīqatan wa ḥukman vs. ṣūratan lā ḥukman) and the ruling on the limb.
The principle is axiomatic: a limb from a legally alive animal is haram.
2
Identify the condition for a hunting tool
The tool must cause a wound (jarḥ). If it kills by weight (thiqal) or crushing (daqq), it is invalid.
Wounding is a condition (shart) for the permissibility of the game.
3
Apply to the stone example
A heavy stone with sharpness kills by weight, not wound; a light sharp stone kills by wound and is permissible.
The distinction hinges on whether the tool's primary effect is cutting or crushing.
4
Apply to the flint and stick
A flint that does not cut properly kills by crushing (like a club); a stick kills by weight; both are invalid.
The absence of a clean wound renders the game non-halal.

اصول ہدایہ ۲۹۹ جائے گا، کسی ایسے حکم کا فیصلہ نہ کیا جائے گا کہ اس کے ثبوت میں شک واقع ہو۔ صاحب ہدایہ مذکورہ اصول ذکر کر کے یہ بتلا رہے ہیں کہ جب خنثیٰ مشکل امام کے پیچھے کھڑا ہوگا تو مرد اور عورتوں کی صف کے درمیان کھڑا ہو گا اور مرد یا عورتوں کی صف کے ساتھ کھڑا نہ ہو، کیونکہ ہوسکتا ہے کہ وہ عورت ہو اور مردوں کی صف میں کھڑا ہو جائے تو اس کے دائیں بائیں اور پیچھے نمازیوں کی نماز فاسد ہو جائے گی اور یہ بھی ممکن ہے کہ وہ مرد ہو تو عورتوں کی صف میں کھڑا ہونے سے اس کی اپنی نماز فاسد ہو جائے گی، اس لیے خنثی مشکل کے متعلق امور دین میں احوط پر عمل ہوگا اور احوط یہی ہے کہ وہ مرد اور عورتوں کی صف کے درمیان کھڑا ہو ۔ قال رَضِيَ اللهُ عَنْهُ الأصل فى الخنثى المشكل) وتذكير الضمير فيه للتغليب المذكور، لأن فيه جهة الذكورة وجهة الأنوثة، الأصل في الخنثى المشكل أن يؤخذ فيه بالأحوط والأوثق في أمور الدين، وأن لا يحكم بثبوت حكم وقع الشك في ثبوته) قال المشكل ولم يقل المشكلة لأنه لما لم يعلم تذكيره وتأنيثه والأصل هو الذكر؛ لأن حواء عَلَيْهَا السَّلَامُ خلقت من ضلع آدم عَلَيْهِ السَّلَامُ. (قال) أى القدورى: (إذا وقف خلف الإمام قام بين صف الرجال والنساء، لاحتمال أنه امرأة، فلا يتخلل الرجال كي لا يفسد صلاتهم، ولا النساء لاحتمال أنه رجل فتفسد صلاته). (البناية: كتاب الخنثى، فصل في أحكام الخنثى، ج ۱۳ ص ۵۳۱) والأصل في ذلك أن الخنثى المشكل يؤخذ له فى جميع أموره بالأحوط في أمور الدين فإذا ثبت هذا قلنا يقف بين صف الرجال والنساء لأنه يحتمل أن يكون امرأة فإذا وقف في صف الرجال أفسد عليهم ويحتمل أن يكون رجلا فإذا وقف في صف النساء أفسدن عليه فأمر بالوقوف بين ذلك ليأمن الأمرين. Principles of Hidayah 299. ... A ruling in which there is doubt about its proof will not be decided. The author of Hidayah mentions this principle and explains that when a hermaphrodite with uncertain gender stands behind the imam, he will stand between the row of men and women, and not with either row. Because it is possible that he is a woman, and if he stands in the row of men, the prayers of those to his right, left, and behind will be invalidated. It is also possible that he is a man, then if he stands in the row of women, his own prayer will be invalidated. Therefore, in matters of religion, the precautionary approach is taken regarding the hermaphrodite, and the precaution is that he stands between the rows. He (the author) said, may Allah be pleased with him: The principle regarding the hermaphrodite with uncertain gender is that the precautionary and most trustworthy approach is taken in religious matters, and that no ruling regarding which doubt exists about its proof is established. He said 'al-mushkil' (masculine) and not 'al-mushkila' (feminine) because his gender is not known, and the default is masculine, since Hawwa (peace be upon her) was created from the rib of Adam (peace be upon him). Al-Quduri said: When he stands behind the imam, he stands between the row of men and women, due to the possibility that he is a woman, so he does not intermix with men lest he invalidate their prayer, and not with women due to the possibility that he is a man, so his own prayer would be invalidated. (Al-Binayah, Book of the Hermaphrodite, Chapter on Rulings of the Hermaphrodite, vol. 13 p. 531) The principle in this is that the hermaphrodite is treated with precaution in all his affairs. When this is established, we say he stands between the rows of men and women, because it is possible he is a woman, so if he stands in the row of men he invalidates their prayer, and it is possible he is a man, so if he stands in the row of women they invalidate his prayer. Thus he is commanded to stand between them to be safe from both issues. (Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, Book of the Hermaphrodite, vol. 1 p. 358)

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with supporting reasoning. It addresses the case of a khuntha mushkil — a person whose biological sex is ambiguous and cannot be determined. The core principle applied here is al-ahwat (precaution) in religious matters. Because there is a genuine doubt whether the person is male or female, placing them in either the men's row or women's row in congregational prayer risks invalidating the prayer of others or their own prayer. Therefore, the ruling is that they must stand between the two rows. The text mentions that the base matn from al-Quduri states this, and the commentators expand on the reasoning: the default grammatical gender is masculine because Hawwa was created from Adam, but the ruling is based on precaution. The citation from al-Binayah and al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah reinforces that this is the established Hanafi position. The passage does not discuss any intra-madhhab differences; it presents the relied-upon view.

Key Terms
خنثى مشكل (khuntha mushkil): A person whose gender (male or female) is uncertain and cannot be determined by external signs.
الأحوط (al-ahwat): The precautionary approach; choosing the option that avoids potential harm or sin when a ruling is uncertain.
