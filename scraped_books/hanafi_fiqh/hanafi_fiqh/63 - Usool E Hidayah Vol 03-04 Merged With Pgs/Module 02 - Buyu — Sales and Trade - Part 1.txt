Key Hanafi Legal Maxims on Sales and Contracts
List of Hanafi legal maxims from various chapters on sales and contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and explain the 39 key Hanafi legal maxims from Usool al-Hidayah governing sales and contracts, and apply them to basic transactional scenarios.

Core Idea
Maxims like 'sale with gharar is invalid' and 'custom is like a condition' form the backbone of Hanafi contract law.

This lesson presents a comprehensive list of legal maxims from the Hanafi school, compiled from the Usool al-Hidayah. These maxims encapsulate the principles underlying sales (buyu), options (khiyar), invalid sales, and other contractual matters. Mastery of these maxims is essential for understanding and applying Hanafi fiqh in transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter headings
Scan the list and note the chapter headings: general maxims (13-15), khiyar ar-ru'yah (16-18), khiyar al-'ayb (19-20), bay' fasid (21-26), murabaha/tawliya (27), miscellaneous (28-31), istihqaq (32-33), bay' fuduli (34), salam (35-37), scattered issues (38-39). This gives the structure.
The numbering is from the original text; some numbers repeat.
2
Read each maxim with its translation
Read the Arabic and English translation for each maxim. Focus on understanding the core legal concept. Pay attention to key terms like badalain, gharar, khiyar.
Use the source_translation as your guide.
3
Group maxims by topic
Group the maxims under their chapter headings. For example, maxims 21-26 all relate to invalid sales. This aids memorization and contextual understanding.
Create mental categories: options, invalid sales, possession, etc.
4
Memorize key terms and phrases
Memorize the Arabic phrasing of the most important maxims, such as (22) بَيْعُ مَا فِيهِ غَرَرٌ لَا يَجُوزُ and (28) الْمَعْرُوفَ كَالْمَشْرُوطِ. These are frequently cited in Hanafi fiqh.
Focus on short, impactful maxims first.
5
Practice applying maxims to simple cases
Take a simple transaction scenario and identify which maxims apply. For example, a sight-unseen purchase involves khiyar ar-ru'yah (16-18) and possibly defect (19). Write out the reasoning.
The practice tasks below will help reinforce this.

Commentary
This passage presents a list of the fundamental legal maxims from the Hanafi school, specifically from the Usool al-Hidayah, which collects the principles underlying the rulings of al-Hidayah. The passage begins with three general maxims: (13) The two considerations in a sale (the price and the commodity) cannot be owned by the same person simultaneously, meaning each must be owned by different parties at the time of contract. (14) Inheritance only applies to wealth that is transferable, so non-transferable rights (like the right to custody) are not inherited. (15) Stipulating a condition of choice for a third party is valid by istihsan (juristic preference), even though by qiyas it might not be. Then comes the chapter on the 'Option of Viewing' (Khiyar ar-Ru'yah), with three maxims: (16) It is not required that the buyer has seen the entire item before the sale; seeing part is sufficient if it represents the whole. (17) If direct viewing is impossible, a detailed description (tashbih) acts as a substitute. (18) The agent's viewing of the item is considered like the principal's viewing. Next is the chapter on the 'Option of Defect' (Khiyar al-'Ayb): (19) In a dispute over the quantity of goods received by the buyer, the buyer's statement is accepted (unless proven otherwise). (20) The existence of a preemption right does not prevent the sale from being complete; the sale is valid even if the preemptor later claims. Then the chapter on 'Invalid Sale' (al-Bay' al-Fasid): (21) Selling something that cannot be delivered (e.g., a bird in the air) is invalid. (22) Sales involving excessive uncertainty (gharar) are invalid. (23) Selling a subsidiary item independently without the principal is invalid (e.g., selling the fruit while retaining the tree). (24) Taking possession of an item that is already in one's hands as a trust does not count as taking possession for a new sale. (25) Taking possession through an appointed agent suffices for the sale. (26) Only items that can be stored (i.e., tangible property) are considered 'mal' (wealth). Then the chapter on 'Murabaha and Tawliya' (sale at cost with profit sharing): (27) In murabaha, if there is a doubt about a defect, it is treated as certainty as a precaution, meaning the seller must disclose it. On the next page, we have more general maxims: (28) Customary practice is like a stipulated condition; what is known by custom is binding. (29) Minor differences in description that do not affect value are ignored. (30) Equality is determined by standard measures, not by estimation. (31) The explicit text (of the Quran or Hadith) takes precedence over custom. Then a brief heading 'Chapter of Riba' (interest) but no maxims under it, followed by 'Chapter of Preemption' (Istiḥqāq): (32) Evidence (bayyina) is binding on the judge and creates a binding ruling, while a confession (iqrar) is binding only on the one who confesses. (33) A settlement (sulh) over an unknown item for a known compensation is valid. Then a 'Section on Sale by an Unauthorized Person' (Fasl fi Bay' al-Fuduli): (34) If someone leases an item and later the owner sells it, the lease has the status of a prior agency that must be honored. Then 'Chapter of Salam' (advance payment sale): (35) In salam, the imagined description of the item is treated like actual existence, so the contract is valid on described attributes. (36) Salam is permissible for anything whose quality and quantity can be precisely specified; if not, it is not. (37) The non-existent (future goods) may be considered existent by legal concession for salam. Finally, 'Scattered Issues' (Masa'il Manthura): (38) According to the Hanafis, it is permissible for the seller or buyer to voluntarily increase the price or the commodity after the contract, unlike some other schools. (39) Ownerless property (mubah) belongs to the one who first takes possession of it, as per the hadith 'Whoever takes possession of a mubah item first, it is his.'

Key Terms
بَدَلَيْنِ (badalain): The two considerations in a sale: the price (thaman) and the commodity (muthman).
خِيَارُ الرُّؤْيَةِ (khiyar ar-ru'yah): The option for the buyer to confirm or cancel the sale after seeing the item if it was bought sight unseen.
غَرَرْ (gharar): Excessive uncertainty or risk in a transaction, making it invalid.

Known Custom is Like Stipulation
A legal maxim equating custom to explicit stipulation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim al-ma'rūfu ka al-mashrūṭi and apply it to resolve a silent contract term.

Core Idea
Customary practice binds as if written into the contract.

This short maxim is a pillar of Hanafi transactional law. It establishes that well-known customs have the same legal effect as explicit stipulations, preventing disputes over unspoken terms. However, it only applies when the custom does not contradict the Shari'a or the parties' express agreement.

Guided Path
1
Read the maxim exactly as written
Internalize the Arabic phrase: الْمَعْرُوفَ كَالْمَشْرُوطِ. Note that 'al-ma'rūf' refers to what is commonly recognized and not rejected by the Law.
The maxim is a qā'ida fiqhiyya, not a verse or hadith.
2
Understand its legal scope
This maxim applies primarily in mu'āmalāt (transactions and contracts). It does not apply to acts of worship (ibādāt) where custom plays a different role.
Hanafis give heavy weight to 'urf as a subsidiary source.
3
Identify the condition of non-contradiction
The custom must not contradict an explicit stipulation or a clear shari'a ruling. If it does, the explicit term or ruling prevails.
For example, a custom of usury would be invalid.
4
Apply the maxim to a silent contract
When a contract is silent on a point that is covered by established custom, that custom is treated as if it were written in the contract.
This fills gaps without needing written terms.

The maxim is used to resolve disputes when the contract is silent.

Disparity in Description is Negligible
Maxim stating minor descriptive variances have no legal effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-tafāwut fī al-wasfi hadr', explain its meaning, and apply it to a sale scenario involving minor descriptive variation.

Core Idea
Minor differences in description do not invalidate a contract; only gross disparity matters.

This short maxim is a cornerstone of Hanafi sale law. It prevents contracts from being overturned over trivial discrepancies, ensuring commercial stability. We will unpack the maxim word by word and see its practical impact.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Commit the exact wording to memory: 'al-tafāwut fī al-wasfi hadr'. This is a memorization anchor for the course.
The maxim is composed of three words. Understand each: al-tafāwut = disparity, fī al-wasfi = in description, hadr = disregarded.
2
Understand the legal meaning
The maxim means that when a sold item's actual qualities differ slightly from what was described or expected, the contract remains valid. The law disregards insignificant variation.
This applies to attributes like size, weight, color shade, or minor defects that do not change the type or purpose of the item.
3
Apply to a concrete scenario
Consider: A buyer purchases a 'fat goat' but receives a slightly thinner goat. The maxim rules the sale is valid because the description 'fat' is not precise; thinness here is a minor variation.
If the goat were a different species (e.g., a sheep), that would be gross disparity (tafāwut fāḥish).
4
Identify the limit: gross disparity
The maxim only protects minor differences. If the variation is substantial — e.g., buying a 'red car' but receiving a 'blue car' — the contract may be invalid or the buyer has an option (khiyār).
Hanafi jurists define gross disparity as something that changes the essential nature or significantly reduces the item's value.

The maxim prevents nullification due to trivial variances.

Equality by Standard Measurement
Maxim that equality is determined by standard measures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-musāwāh bil-mi'yār' and apply it to determine when equality is required in a ribawi transaction.

Core Idea
Equality in ribawi exchange is determined by standard measure, not by guesswork.

The maxim 'al-musāwāh bil-mi'yār' is a foundational rule in Hanafi sales law. It ensures that in transactions involving ribawi goods, equality is established using precise, standardized measuring tools. This principle prevents ambiguity (gharar) and protects against usury.

Guided Path
1
Recite the maxim
Learn the Arabic phrase 'الْمُسَاوَاةُ بِالْمِعْيَارِ' and its literal meaning: 'Equality is by (standard) measure.'
Memorize it as a concise legal principle.
2
Identify the context
The maxim applies to ribawi transactions (e.g., gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) where Shariah demands exact equality in exchange.
Not all sales require equality; only those involving ribawi items of the same genus.
3
Understand the requirement
Equality must be by standard weight, volume, or length — not by estimation, number of pieces, or visual approximation.
The mi'yār (standard measure) is what the law recognizes as reliable.
4
Apply to a case
In a gold-for-gold sale, if one party gives more by weight (even slightly), the transaction becomes ribawi and invalid.
The excess is considered riba al-fadl.
5
Note the scope
The maxim operates only when equality is legally required. It does not apply to non-ribawi transactions or to exchanges of different genera (e.g., gold for silver).
In a gold-for-silver sale, equality of weight is not required; only hand-to-hand delivery is needed.

Text is Stronger than Custom
Maxim prioritizing explicit legal text over customary practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-naṣṣu aqwā min al-‘urf', explain its meaning, and apply it to a transaction from the chapter of buyu' to determine which ruling prevails.

Core Idea
A clear text from Quran or Sunnah always overrides customary practice.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: 'al-naṣṣu aqwā min al-‘urf' (the text is stronger than custom). While customary practice ('urf) is a recognized secondary source in the Hanafi school, it cannot contradict an explicit text. This maxim serves as a hierarchy rule when apparent conflict arises between custom and a clear textual ruling.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim in Arabic
Recite 'al-nassu aqwa min al-urfi' and understand its literal meaning: the text is stronger than custom.
2
Define the two key terms
Define 'nass' as explicit textual evidence from Quran or Sunnah, and 'urf' as customary practice that is widespread and not contradictory to shari'a.
Hanafis distinguish between zahir and nass; nass is unambiguous.
3
Apply to a buyu' scenario
Consider a scenario where a local custom permits a sale with gharar (uncertainty) but a hadith prohibits gharar. The text overrides the custom, making the sale invalid.
4
Recognize the limitation
Custom remains valid when no nass exists or when the text is ambiguous. This maxim does not discard 'urf entirely.

This maxim establishes the hierarchy of sources: a clear textual evidence (naṣṣ) from the Quran or Sunnah takes precedence over customary practice ('urf). Hanafi usul al-fiqh recognizes 'urf as a subsidiary source, but it cannot override a definitive text. For example, even if a custom allows a transaction that involves riba, the text prohibiting riba prevails.

Key Terms

الْعُرْفِ (al-'urf): Customary practice that is widespread and not contradictory to shari'a.

Chapter on Istihqaq: Understanding Rightful Claims
Chapter heading for rightful claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the meaning of istihqaq and identify the types of legal claims it covers in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Istihqaq is the legal process of claiming property from a wrongful possessor.

This chapter opening introduces one of the most practically important sections of the book of sales (buyu'). The concept of istihqaq governs cases where a true owner appears after a sale or transfer has taken place. Understanding this chapter's scope will help you later analyze complex ownership disputes.

Guided Path

Cancellation May Occur by Implication
Maxim that cancellation can be established by implication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a contract is rescinded by implication (iqtiḍā') and apply the maxim to practical scenarios in sales law.

Core Idea
A contract can be rescinded by clear implied conduct, not only by explicit words.

In Hanafi sales law, rescission (faskh) does not always require explicit verbal cancellation. This maxim establishes that certain actions or circumstances can imply rescission, especially in options (khiyar) and defects. Understanding this maxim is crucial for determining contractual rights and obligations.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
Learn the Arabic and English versions of the maxim. This is the foundation for all applications.
The key terms are al-faskh (rescission) and iqtiḍā' (implication).
2
Define the key terms
Al-faskh is the annulment of a contract. Iqtiḍā' refers to an implied meaning that is necessarily understood from context or conduct.
In Hanafi usul, iqtiḍā' is a type of implicit dalālah.
3
Identify the legal implication
The maxim means that rescission can be inferred from actions that clearly indicate an intention to cancel, even without words. The implication must be unambiguous.
Example: returning defective goods and the seller accepting them.
4
Apply to practical examples
Consider the given example: buyer returns defective goods, seller accepts. The contract is rescinded by implication. Also apply to khiyar al-ayb (option of defect) and khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation).
Not every action implies rescission; it must be clear and mutual.

Commentary
This maxim indicates that the rescission (faskh) of a contract can be inferred from the parties' actions or circumstances, even without explicit verbal cancellation. For example, if a buyer returns defective goods and the seller accepts them, the contract is rescinded by implication. This is particularly relevant in options (khiyār) and defective merchandise (ayb).

Key Terms

الْاِقْتِضَاءِ (al-iqtiḍā'): Implication or necessary consequence.

Legal Rulings Accord with Linguistic Meanings
Maxim on correspondence between legal and linguistic meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that legal rulings accord with linguistic meanings and apply it to a fiqh example from the chapter of buyu'.

Core Idea
Legal rulings generally follow the common Arabic meaning of words unless a specific shar'i definition exists.

This maxim is a foundational principle of Hanafi interpretation: when the Lawgiver uses a word, it is understood according to its customary linguistic sense unless a technical meaning is explicitly given. For instance, the word 'sale' (bay') is interpreted in its common meaning of exchange of property. Understanding this maxim helps you see how Hanafi jurists approach textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Memorize the Arabic and its translation: 'al-aḥkām al-shar'iyya 'alā wifāq al-ma'ānī al-lughawiyya' – Legal rulings are in accordance with linguistic meanings.
This is a general rule, not absolute.
2
Identify the scope
This maxim applies to all shar'i rulings derived from language, except where the Lawgiver has assigned a specific technical meaning (e.g., ṣalāh means ritual prayer, not just supplication).
3
Apply to a fiqh example
Take the word bay' (sale) from the Quran (e.g., 2:275). Under this maxim, the term is understood linguistically as an exchange of property. Therefore, the fiqh ruling on the contract of sale derives its validity from the linguistic concept of exchange, requiring offer and acceptance (ījāb wa qabūl).
This example is from the chapter of buyu'.
4
Note the exception
Some terms have established shar'i meanings that override the linguistic sense. For instance, ṣalāh linguistically means 'supplication' but technically means the prescribed ritual prayer. The maxim holds unless there is evidence of a technical convention.

The legal rulings (shar'iyya) are in accordance with the linguistic meanings.

Commentary
This maxim is a principle of interpretation: shari'a terms are generally understood according to their customary Arabic meanings unless the Lawgiver has given a technical definition. For example, the word 'sale' (bay') in fiqh is interpreted based on its common meaning of exchange of property.

Key Terms
الْأَحْكَامُ الشَّرْعِيَّةُ (al-aḥkām al-shar'iyya): Rulings derived from the Qur'an, Sunnah, and ijtihad.
الْمَعَانِي اللُّغَوِيَّةِ (al-ma'ānī al-lughawiyya): The literal or common linguistic meanings of words in Arabic.

Legal Maxims from Currency Exchange and Transfer of Debt
List of two legal maxims from sarf and hawala chapters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the meaning of the maxims in currency exchange and hawala and apply them to simple contractual scenarios.

Core Idea
Absolute exchange implies exchange of individual items; legal rulings follow linguistic meanings.

These two maxims from Usool al-Hidayah provide foundational principles for contracts of currency exchange and transfer of debt. They are concise but powerful tools for ensuring valid transactions.

Guided Path
1
Interpret the First Maxim
The maxim from the chapter of sarf states that an absolute exchange (muqabalah mutlaqah) implies the exchange of individual units (fard bil fard). Consider what 'absolute' means and how it affects the contract.
Absolute here means unrestricted as to type or quantity.
2
Interpret the Second Maxim
The maxim from hawala states that legal rulings are in accordance with linguistic meanings. This means when a term is used in a contract, its linguistic definition takes precedence over customary or technical meanings unless specified.
Hanafis rely on the Arabic language as a primary source.
3
Apply Both to a Scenario
Take a case of currency exchange: A and B exchange two different currencies without specifying the rate. According to the first maxim, each unit is exchanged for a unit, implying equality. The second maxim clarifies that 'exchange' linguistically means swapping items, so the contract is valid if the items are equivalent in type and amount.

Commentary
This passage is a compilation of legal maxims (qawa'id fiqhiyya) from various chapters of Hanafi fiqh, drawn from the matn of Usool al-Hidayah. These maxims serve as foundational principles for deriving rulings in specific cases. The text begins with the chapter on currency exchange (sarf), where maxim (40) states that in an absolute exchange contract (unrestricted as to type), it implies exchange of individual items (i.e., each item for its counterpart). Then the chapter on guarantee (kafalah) covers multiple maxims: (41) about annulment being inferred, (42) about suspicion from partial testimony, (43) that guarantee is based on voluntary piety (tawarru'), (44) validity of conditional guarantee when condition is suitable, (45) that valid conditional acts are not spoiled by invalid conditions, (46) equality of proof and direct observation, (37) that discharging the principal discharges the guarantor (note numbering out of sequence), (48) punishment not delegable, (49) guarantee of specific items already guaranteed by others is invalid, and (50) that discharging a guarantor does not discharge the principal. The section on transfer of debt (hawalah) states that legal rulings follow linguistic meanings. The chapter on judge's conduct (adab al-qadi) derives judicial rulings from testimony. Subsequent sections cover judge's letter to another judge, arbitration, and testimony. Maxim (53) prohibits judgment against an absent party, (56) allows enforcement of judgment in ijtihad matters, (57) that external appearances are not proof of entitlement, (58) that executorship is a form of deputyship, (59) that no liability attaches to judge or trustee, (20) that no oath is on the judge. In the testimony chapter, (61) establishes judgment on proof, (62) analogy of favors, (63) testimony not obligatory, (64) writing analogy, (25) possession as indicator of ownership, (26) disallowing testimony when self-interested. Each maxim is a condensed ruling, and students should memorize them as they form the basis of legal reasoning in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
صرف (sarf): Currency exchange contract; sale of gold/silver or currencies with immediate delivery.
حوالة (hawala): Transfer of debt from one person to another; the transferor assigns the debt to the transferee.
كفالة (kafalah): Guarantee contract where one person (kafil) assumes liability for another's debt or obligation.
شهادة (shahadah): Testimony given in court, consisting of number and integrity of witnesses.
قضاء (qada'): Judicial verdict or judgment; the process of adjudication.

Legal Maxims from the Chapter of Guarantee (Kafalah)
List of legal maxims from the chapter of guarantee (kafalah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key maxims of guarantee (kafalah) and apply them to distinguish valid from invalid guarantee contracts.

Core Idea
Guarantee is based on piety and can be conditional; discharge of the principal discharges the guarantor, but not vice versa.

The chapter on guarantee in Usool al-Hidayah contains multiple maxims that define the nature and limits of the kafalah contract. These maxims clarify issues like annulment, suspicion, delegation, and the relationship between principal and guarantor.

Guided Path
1
List the Key Maxims
Identify each maxim by number and subject. The text covers annulment by implication (41), suspicion from partial testimony (42), foundation on piety (43), conditional validity (44), preservation despite invalid conditions (45), equality of proof and sight (46), discharge of principal (37), non-delegation of punishment (48), invalidity of guaranteeing already-guaranteed items (49), and non-discharge of principal by discharging guarantor (50).
Numbering in the text is not sequential; (37) appears after (46).
2
Categorize by Theme
Group maxims into: (a) nature of kafalah (piety, conditional), (b) effects of discharge, (c) limits of liability, (d) relationship to testimony.
3
Apply to a Hypothetical
Take a scenario: A guarantees B's debt, then B is discharged. According to maxim (37), the guarantor is also discharged. If instead only the guarantor is discharged, maxim (50) says the principal remains liable.

Two Maxims on Agency: Attribution and Tolerance of Ignorance
Two maxims on agency: attribution of contract rights and minor ignorance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two maxims of agency regarding attribution of contract rights and minor ambiguity, and apply them to identify whether the agent or principal bears rights.

Core Idea
When an agent contracts in his own name, rights belong to him; minor uncertainty in the subject is tolerated in agency.

In Hanafi law of agency, two fundamental maxims govern how the agent's actions bind parties. The first determines whose rights and obligations arise from the contract based on how the agent attributes it. The second allows a degree of flexibility in describing the subject of agency, unlike sales which require precise specification. This lesson anchors these two rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal maxims
Read the Arabic text and locate the two numbered statements: Maxim (78) on attribution and Maxim (79) on minor ignorance.
The maxims are each a complete legal principle.
2
Analyze Maxim (78): Attribution of contract rights
Focus on the first maxim. Understand that the key is how the agent attributes the contract. If he attributes it to himself, rights and obligations are his; if to the principal, they go to the principal.
Consider scenarios: agent says 'I buy for myself' vs. 'I buy for so-and-so'.
3
Analyze Maxim (79): Tolerance of minor ignorance
The second maxim allows a small amount of ambiguity (jahala yasira) in the object of agency. For example, 'buy fabric' without specifying exact type is acceptable, unlike in a direct sale where such ambiguity could invalidate the contract.
Excessive ambiguity (jahala fahisha) may invalidate the agency.
4
Apply both maxims to a simple case
Given a scenario where an agent buys an item without naming the principal, determine who bears the contractual rights. Then consider if the agent had vague instructions, does the agency remain valid?
Combine both maxims: attribution determines rights, and minor ignorance is allowable.

This lesson unpacks the elegant principle that makes this possible: the session (majlis) is a legal unit that gathers together all its moments into one. The Maxim of Ignorance Leading to Dispute in Sales
States the maxim with explanation and rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim regarding jahalah in sales and apply it to distinguish between invalidating and non-invalidating ignorance.

Core Idea
Only ignorance that leads to dispute invalidates a sale; harmless ignorance is overlooked.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all uncertainty in a sale contract is fatal. The key principle is whether that uncertainty (jahalah) is likely to lead to a dispute between the parties. This lesson examines the maxim: 'Ignorance that leads to dispute prevents the validity of sale.' Understanding this condition is essential for determining when a contract is valid (sahih) or invalid (fasid).

Guided Path
1
Identify the subjects involved
The passage discusses two key concepts: jahalah (ignorance/uncertainty) and munaza'ah (dispute). Note that the ruling is about the sale contract (al-bay').
2
Distinguish the two categories of jahalah
The maxim specifies that only jahalah that is 'mufdiyah ila al-munaza'ah' (leading to dispute) prevents validity. Other jahalah does not affect the contract.
Example: uncertainly about a minor detail like the exact shade of a car's color may be overlooked; unsure about the total price is fatal.
3
Understand the rational behind the maxim
The author explains why such ignorance voids the contract: it prevents delivery and receipt (taslim wa tasallum), the contract's intended purpose. Without the ability to peacefully exchange goods and payment, the contract becomes futile ('abath) and lacks a praiseworthy outcome.
This is a teleological argument: the aim of sale is mutual benefit through exchange. Dispute blocks that aim.
4
Apply the maxim to a concrete example
Consider a sale where the price is left unspecified ('I sell you this for whatever you think fair'). This creates jahalah that will likely lead to a dispute. Hence, the contract is invalid (fasid). In contrast, if the price is clearly stated but the buyer does not know the seller's name, that ignorance does not cause dispute and does not affect validity.
Always evaluate the potential for future conflict.

Principle of Specified Quantity in Sale
States and explains the principle from Al-Hidayah regarding specified quantity in sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a sale contract covers only the specified quantity and determine the ownership of any excess found after delivery.

Core Idea
A sale on a specified quantity excludes any excess — only the quantity named transfers to the buyer.

This lesson explores a fundamental Hanafi principle in sales law: a contract that fixes a specific quantity does not automatically include any increase. Derived from Al-Hidayah, this rule protects clarity in transactions and prevents disputes over unexpected surplus. We will examine the rationale, the exception for descriptions, and its application to concrete cases.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Write the maxim: 'Every contract that occurs on a specified quantity does not include other than it, unless it is a description.' Commit this to memory.

2
Apply to a concrete case
Consider the example of a heap of wheat sold as 100 qafiz for 100 dirhams. If the buyer finds 110 qafiz, determine who owns the extra 10 qafiz.
The principle directly answers that the extra belongs to the seller.

Identify the rationale
The price is given in exchange for the essence (quantity), not for descriptions. The contract was limited to the specified quantity, so any excess is outside the contract.
Quantity is the essence (أصل), description is an attribute (وصف).

If the excess is a description (e.g., cloth length), it is included without extra charge because descriptions do not correspond to a separate part of the price.
Example: cloth sold as '10 yards' but found to be 11 yards – the extra yard is part of the sale.
5
Connect to broader rules
This principle aligns with the requirement that quantity and price must be known to avoid disputes, as quoted from Al-Bahr al-Raiq.
Ignorance of quantity invalidates the sale.

Descriptions Do Not Allocate the Price
Explains the principle that descriptions do not allocate price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that descriptions do not correspond to any part of the price and determine the buyer's options when a described attribute is deficient.

Core Idea
The price is exchanged for the essence, not for descriptions.

In Hanafi sales law, a key principle governs how price relates to the attributes of a sold item. The source passage establishes that descriptions such as length, color, or quality do not allocate any portion of the price. Instead, the price is given for the essence (asl) of the commodity. Understanding this principle is essential to determine the legal remedies when a described attribute falls short of expectation.

Guided Path
1
State the Principle
Identify the core ruling: 'al-wasf la yuqabiluhu shay'un min al-thaman' – descriptions are not countered by anything from the price. This means the price corresponds exclusively to the essence, not to its attributes.
The principle applies to any description (length, weight, colour) in a sale contract.
2
Understand the Rationale
The reason given: a thing is matched by its like. The price is for the quantity/essence; descriptions are not a counterpart. Therefore, a deficiency in description does not entitle the buyer to a proportional refund.
This reasoning distinguishes Hanafi law from systems that attribute value to each quality.
3
Apply to the Cloth Example
If a cloth is sold as '10 cubits for 10 dirhams' and found to be only 9, the buyer cannot claim 1 dirham back. Instead, he has khiyar: either accept the cloth at full price or rescind the sale.
The khiyar is due to the failure of a condition (description), not because of price allocation.
4
Distinguish from Specified Quantity
When the contract specifies a precise quantity (e.g., 100 qafiz of wheat) and more is found, the excess belongs to the seller and the sale covers only the specified amount. This is because the quantity is the essence, not a description.
Excess over a specified quantity is not a description; it is an additional essence.

Description Is Not Compensated by Price: Al-Wasf La Yuqabiluhu Shay' min al-Thaman
Explaining the rule that price compensates essence, not description, in sales.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that description is not compensated by price, apply it to the case of cloth sold by the yard, and distinguish between description (wasf) and quantity (qadr).

Core Idea
Price is for the essence, not the description; deficiency in description gives the option to rescind, not reduce price.

In Hanafi sales law, the price you pay is in exchange for the substance of the item, not for its attributes. This lesson unpacks the rule 'al-wasf la yuqabiluhu shay' min al-thaman' and its application to common transactions like buying cloth or grain. Understanding this distinction prevents common mistakes in sale contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its scope
State the maxim 'al-wasf la yuqabiluhu shay' min al-thaman' and understand that price compensates the essence (asl), not descriptions (wasf) like length, color, or brand.
This principle is from the zahir al-riwayah of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Apply to the cloth example: deficiency and excess
Consider a cloth sold as 10 cubits for 10 dirhams. If found to be 8 cubits (deficiency), the buyer cannot reduce the price; he has the option (khiyar) to take it for the full price or rescind. If found to be 12 cubits (excess), the extra cubits belong to the seller.
The cubit is a description (wasf) in cloth, thus not compensated.
3
Contrast with grain by measure
In a sale of a pile of grain specified as 100 qafiz, if the pile is less, the buyer can reduce the price proportionally because quantity (qadr) is part of the essence. If more, the excess belongs to the seller.
Quantity in grain is part of the asl, not a mere wasf.
4
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The cubit in cloth is analogous to the limbs of an animal: both are descriptions not compensated by price. The effective cause (illa) is that they are attributes not defining the core identity.
Qiyas jaliy: clear analogy from the case of animal limbs.
5
Distinguish wasf from qadr
Description (wasf) is an attribute that does not change the essence; quantity (qadr) can be part of the essence if it defines the substance (like grain by measure). The determination depends on custom and the nature of the item.
This distinction is crucial for the correct application of the maxim.

The passage uses qiyas (analogy) to compare the cubit in cloth to the limbs of an animal, which are descriptions not compensated by the price. The distinction between wasf (description) and qadr (quantity) is critical: quantity in grain is part of the essence, while length in cloth is merely a description. The ruling gives the buyer khiyar (option) when the description is deficient, but no price adjustment.

Key Terms
الوصف (al-wasf): Description or attribute of the sold item that is not part of its essence, e.g., length, color, or brand.
الثمن (al-thaman): The price paid in exchange for the sold item.
القدر (al-qadr): Quantity or measure, which in the context of grain is considered part of the essence (asl) of the sale.
الخيار (al-khiyar): The option or right to confirm or rescind a contract, granted to the buyer when the described attribute is deficient.

The Rule of Independent Description in Sale Contracts
Explaining the exception when description is independently mentioned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a description (wasf) is independently mentioned in a sale and distinguish it from the general rule for quantity.

Core Idea
When a description is independently stated, it is treated as part of the essence—excess belongs to the buyer.

In the previous lesson, we learned that descriptions generally are not compensated by price. However, there is an important exception: when a description is given independent mention (ifrad bi al-dhikr) in the contract, it is elevated to the status of essence. This changes the ownership of any excess in that attribute. Let's explore this rule and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The passage states: 'al-wasf qad yakunu bi manzilati al-asli bi ifradihi bi al-dhikr' — the description becomes like the essence by being mentioned independently. This is the second key principle of the chapter.
Independent mention means the description is explicitly stated as a term of the contract, not merely incidental.
2
Understand the Distinction
The text contrasts the cubit (dhira') in cloth (a description) with quantity (qadr) in a grain pile (an essential attribute). The cubit is a description because it expresses length and width, whereas quantity in grain is part of the essence and directly compensated by price.
The distinction determines who owns any excess when the delivered item exceeds the specified measure.
3
Apply to Scenarios
If the cloth is less than specified, the buyer has the option (khiyar) to take it for the full price or leave it. If it is more, the excess belongs to the buyer, and the seller has no option. This is because the description is independent.
Contrast with the previous grain pile example: there, excess belongs to the seller (since quantity is part of essence, and sale is on a specific quantity).
4
Note Consequence for the Seller
When the description is independently mentioned, the seller cannot demand extra payment for the excess, nor can he cancel the sale. The buyer gets the benefit of the excess without additional cost.

Commentary
This passage presents a second principle: when a description (wasf) is given independent mention in the contract (ifrad bi al-dhikr), it is treated like the essence (asl) in terms of price compensation. This is an exception to the general rule from the previous lesson. The text then clarifies that the cubit (dhira') in cloth is a description, and therefore, if the cloth is found to be less, the buyer has the option to take it for the full price or leave it; he cannot reduce the price. However, if it is found to be more, the excess belongs to the buyer (contrary to the grain pile case where excess belongs to seller). This is because when a description is specifically mentioned, the sale encompasses it, so any excess in that described attribute goes to the buyer. The seller has no option. The passage references al-Jawharah al-Nirah and al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Kitab, both Hanafi commentaries. It distinguishes between the cubit in cloth (a description) and quantity in grain (a part of essence). The key takeaway: independent mention of a description elevates it to the status of essence for the purpose of ownership of excess.
