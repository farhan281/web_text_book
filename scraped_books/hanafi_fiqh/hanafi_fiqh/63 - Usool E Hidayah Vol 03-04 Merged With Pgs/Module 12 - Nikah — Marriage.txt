Proving Marriage by Mutual Confirmation
States a rule about proving marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that mutual confirmation (tasaduq) between spouses is sufficient to prove a marriage, and identify the condition that no third party rights are affected.

Core Idea
Marriage can be proven by the spouses' mutual agreement alone, as long as no third party rights are harmed.

In Hanafi law, the validity of a marriage may be proven by the joint testimony of the spouses themselves, even without external witnesses or documentation. This maxim, stated by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah, establishes the evidentiary weight of mutual confirmation (tasaduq). Understanding this rule clarifies when a judge can rule on the existence of a marriage based solely on the spouses' own statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
What is being ruled upon? Here it is 'marriage' (al-nikah) – specifically, the proof or establishment of a marriage in a legal dispute.
2
Understand the method of proof
The ruling says 'by mutual confirmation of the spouses' (bi-tasaduq al-zawjayn). Tasaduq means both spouses agree that a marriage exists between them.
Tasaduq is distinct from witnesses or written evidence.
3
Apply the condition
This rule applies only when no third party rights (e.g., prior spouse, heirs) are affected. If a third party has a claim, additional evidence may be needed.
The common misunderstanding is that tasaduq is absolute; in reality it is limited when third parties are involved.

The Maxim: Durability Follows Initiation
A Hanafi maxim on duration and initial validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim and apply it to determine whether a binding contract remains valid when a condition later appears that would have invalidated it at inception.

Core Idea
A binding contract's ongoing validity follows its initial formation.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi legal maxim: 'Whatever is binding from a transaction is given, for its continuance, the ruling of its initiation.' This principle provides stability to long-term contracts like marriage (nikah) and lease (ijara) by ensuring that once a contract is valid at inception, it is not retroactively invalidated by defects that emerge later. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly assessing the validity of ongoing obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its binding nature
Determine whether the transaction is classified as lazim (binding) in Hanafi fiqh. Binding contracts cannot be unilaterally revoked without cause.
Examples: marriage (nikah) and lease (ijara) are lazim once properly formed.
2
State the maxim clearly
Memorise the maxim: 'Ma yakunu lazimam min al-tasarrufi yu'ta li dawamihi hukmu al-ibtida'. Understand that it applies only to lazim contracts.
The maxim does not apply to non-binding (ghayr lazim) contracts like partnership (shirkah) or agency (wakalah) which are revocable.
3
Apply to marriage (nikah)
If a marriage was valid at the time of contract, it remains valid even if later it is discovered that the wife's guardian had a defect in his legal capacity. The initial validity carries forward.
Contrast: if the marriage was invalid (batil) from the start, the maxim cannot cure it.
4
Apply to lease (ijara)
If a lease contract was valid at inception, it continues to be valid for the entire term, even if a later event would have prevented the contract from being formed initially (e.g., a legal restriction on the property that arises later).
However, if the leased property is destroyed, the contract terminates due to impossibility, not due to a defect in inception.
5
Distinguish from supervening impossibility
The maxim applies only to defects that existed at inception but are discovered later. It does not cover events that make performance impossible after the contract is formed.
Example: destruction of subject matter, death of a party, or a change in law that prohibits performance.

For example, if a contract of marriage is valid at inception, it continues to be considered valid even if subsequent events would have made it invalid at inception. This is applied in long-term contracts like marriage and leases.

Marriage Judged by Spousal Confirmation
Explains the legal maxim that marriage is judged by mutual confirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi procedural rule that marriage is adjudicated based on spousal mutual confirmation in cases of disputed marriage claims.

Core Idea
The spouses' mutual acknowledgement (tasaduq) can establish marriage in court when no date evidence decides.

In Hanafi qada, marriage disputes have unique procedural rules. This lesson explores a key maxim: marriage can be established by the mutual confirmation of the spouses. We'll see how this applies when two men both claim a woman as wife.

Guided Path
1
Understand the maxim
Grasp the core statement: 'Marriage is among matters that are judged by the mutual confirmation of the spouses.' This means that if both parties acknowledge a marriage, the judge accepts it, even without other evidence.
The maxim is from al-Hidayah and is relied upon in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the scenario
Two men each claim a woman as their wife, both produce witnesses, but neither gives a date of marriage. How does the judge decide?
The woman's confirmation becomes the decisive factor.
3
Apply the rule
The judge asks the woman whom she confirms as her husband. Whichever man she acknowledges, he is declared her husband because of their mutual confirmation (tasaduq).
This overrides the equal weight of the witnesses.
4
Note the exception
If one of the claimants gives a specific earlier date of marriage, that claimant prevails, as date evidence outweighs confirmation.
The earlier date indicates a prior marriage.
5
Consider additional factors
According to some Hanafi authorities (Rukn al-Islam Ali al-Sughdi), other factors like possession or consummation also give priority, unless the other party proves an earlier marriage.
These factors help resolve conflicts when confirmation is not clear.

When the Master Loses Capacity: The Ma'dhun Slave's Authorization Ceases
Explains the effect of master's death, insanity, or apostasy on the slave's trading authorization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the cessation of a ma'dhun slave's authorization when the master dies, becomes insane, or apostatizes, and state the rational proof based on the principle that a non-binding disposition requires capacity for its duration.

Core Idea
A non-binding authorization needs capacity at every moment; lose it, and the slave becomes restricted.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the Hanafi fiqh of mu'amalat: the status of a slave authorized to trade (ma'dhun) when the master's legal capacity or ownership ends. The Hidayah explains that the authorization automatically ceases upon the master's death, insanity, or apostasy joining dar al-harb. The reasoning rests on a key Hanafi legal principle: a non-binding disposition (ghayr lazim) requires capacity for its continuation just as for its initiation. We will explore the proof and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act is the authorization (idhn) granted to a ma'dhun slave. The ruling is that it becomes null and the slave becomes mahjur (restricted) upon the master's death, insanity, or apostasy.
The ruling is a legal consequence (hukm wad'i), not a taklifi obligation.
2
Locate the dalil
What is the proof? The author cites a rational principle: a non-binding contract (ghayr lazim) requires capacity for its duration, just as for its inception. This is derived from the nature of contracts (aqd).
The dalil is aqli (rational) based on qiyas: continuation is analogous to initiation.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the principle apply? Since idhn is a non-binding contract, its continuation depends on the master's capacity (ahliyyah) and ownership. Death and apostasy remove ownership; insanity removes capacity. Hence the authorization ceases.
The text explicitly says: 'اعتبارًا بالابتداء' (by analogy with initiation).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there any disagreement? Within the Hanafi school, this ruling is agreed upon. Other schools may have different views on the nature of the authorization, but this lesson focuses on the Hanafi position.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih generally, but within Hanafi it is settled.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon? The mufta bihi is that the slave becomes mahjur immediately. No additional revocation is needed. The authority is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
Imam Muhammad's reports in the six core books confirm this.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school regarding the status of a ma'dhun slave (a slave authorized by his master to engage in trade). The passage explains that if the master dies, becomes insane, or apostatizes and joins dar al-harb, the slave's authorization automatically ceases and he becomes mahjur (restricted from trading). The reasoning is based on a key Hanafi legal principle: a non-binding (ghayr lazim) disposition requires capacity (ahliyyah) for its continuation just as it requires capacity for its initiation. The authorization (idhn) for trade is a non-binding contract. When the master dies, his ownership of the slave ends and transfers to heirs, so the contract ends. When he becomes insane, his capacity (ahliyyah) is removed, so the authorization is invalid by analogy with initiatory insanity. When he apostatizes and joins dar al-harb, his ownership over the slave is also lost (his property becomes war booty), so the contract ends. The commentary cites Al-Ikhtiyar and Al-Inayah to confirm this ruling. The proof is aqli (rational) based on the nature of non-binding contracts and the necessity of capacity for their validity.

Key Terms
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave whom the master has authorized to engage in trade, with limited liability.
محجور (mahjur): Restricted from trading; the state where the slave's authorization is revoked.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a legal maxim from the Hanafi school: 'Writing from a distant person is equivalent to speech from a near person.' The author first translates and explains the maxim: it means that the legal effect of a written statement from an absent person is the same as that of an oral statement from one who is present. The proof is from the practice of the Prophet ﷺ, who sent written letters to invite distant rulers to Islam. The author of Al-Hidayah uses this principle to validate the legal actions of a mute person (akhras) who can write or make recognizable gestures. The reasoning is a fortiori (qiyas auli): if writing substitutes for speech for a distant person due to his temporary inability to speak, then it is even more valid for a mute person whose inability is permanent. The passage then quotes from Al-Jami' al-Saghir: a mute person's marriage, divorce, emancipation, sale, and purchase are all valid through writing or gesture. Additionally, retaliation is in effect for him, and the slander hadd is not applied if he slanders (since he cannot speak). The source also distinguishes between a congenital mute and one with a temporary speech impediment (mu'taqil lisanahu). For the latter, gesture or writing does not substitute because his condition may improve. This shows the Hanafi position that the validity of actions via writing depends on the inability to speak being established and likely permanent.

الْكِتَابَةُ (al-kitābah): Writing by a person who is distant or unable to speak, which substitutes for oral speech in legal transactions.
الأَخْرَس (al-akhras): A mute person who is naturally unable to speak, as opposed to one with a temporary speech impediment.
الإِيمَاء (al-īmā’): A recognizable gesture, such as nodding, that conveys consent or intention.

Ruling on the Deaf-Mute's Legal Acts via Writing or Gestures
Explains that a deaf-mute's writing or gestures are valid for legal acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the legal validity of a deaf-mute's written or gestured acts and explain the qiyas auli argument used to derive it.

Core Idea
A deaf-mute's writing is even more valid than an absent person's—permanent inability makes writing the primary expression.

This lesson examines the legal capacity of a deaf-mute (akhras) in Hanafi fiqh. The passage from Al-Binayah clarifies that writing or clear gestures substitute for speech, making marriage, divorce, and other acts valid. The proof is a fortiori analogy (qiyas auli) from the absent person, whose writing is accepted due to temporary inability to speak, while the deaf-mute's inability is permanent, making the case for acceptance even stronger.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling
The subject is the deaf-mute (akhras). The ruling states that his legal acts (marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase, qisas, and exemption from hadd for qadhf) are valid if performed through writing or clear gestures.
2
Understand the analogy (qiyas auli)
The proof is a comparison with an absent person (gha'ib). For the absent person, writing substitutes for speech because distance prevents oral communication. For the deaf-mute, the inability is permanent and more binding, so the validity of writing applies a fortiori (بطريق أولى).
The term 'bitariq awla' indicates a stronger application.
3
Extract the illa (effective cause)
The common cause is inability to speak (al-'ajz 'an al-nutq). In the absent person it is temporary due to distance; in the deaf-mute it is permanent. The permanence makes the ruling more entrenched.
The Hanafi method here is tanqih al-manat.
4
Confirm the conditions for validity
The writing must be clear (كتابا) or the gesture must be understood (إيماء يعرف به). Ambiguous or unclear communication does not suffice.
This ensures the intent is unmistakable.
5
Apply the ruling to specific acts
Marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase, qisas, and exemption from hadd for qadhf are specifically mentioned. All are valid under the same principle.
Note that if the deaf-mute is the killer, qisas (retaliation) is applied; if he is the slandered victim, the accuser does not receive hadd.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Binayah, commenting on Al-Hidayah, presents a ruling from Al-Jami' al-Saghir of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. It addresses the legal capacity of the deaf-mute (akhras) in performing various legal acts. The ruling states that if a deaf-mute writes a document or gestures clearly, his marriage, divorce, manumission, sale, purchase, and even retaliation (qisas) if he kills are valid; also, if he is the victim of false accusation (qadhf), the hadd penalty is not imposed on the accuser. The proof is a qiyas auli (a fortiori analogy) comparing the deaf-mute to an absent person. For an absent person, writing substitutes for speech because distance prevents oral communication; for the deaf-mute, the inability to speak is permanent, making writing even more necessary. Thus, by primary analogy (بطریق اولی), his written or gestured acts are valid. The passage confirms the Zahir al-Riwayah position that the deaf-mute is legally competent for these acts through writing or intelligible gestures.
