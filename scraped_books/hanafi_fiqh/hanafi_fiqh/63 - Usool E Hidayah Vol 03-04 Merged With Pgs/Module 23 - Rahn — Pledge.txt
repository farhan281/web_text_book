Trusts Become Guaranteed Due to Transgression
Statement of a legal principle with application to mortgage transgression.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle 'Trusts are guaranteed due to transgression' and apply it to the case of a mortgagee's transgression, identifying the conditions for liability.

Core Idea
Transgression removes trust protection and imposes full liability.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle governing trusts: al-amānātu tuḍmanu bi al-taʿaddī. Normally, a trustee is not liable for loss unless negligent. However, once he transgresses, he becomes a guarantor. We will parse this principle and see how it applies to a mortgage (rahn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The principle is 'Trusts become guaranteed due to transgression'. Recognize that this is a legal maxim governing all amanah contracts.
2
Identify the default rule
Normally, amanah (trust) is not guaranteed; the trustee is not liable for loss unless negligence is proven. This is the baseline.
3
Identify the exception
Transgression (ta'addi) removes the trust status and imposes liability. The trustee becomes a guarantor (damin) and must pay full value.
4
Apply to mortgage (rahn)
The mortgagee holds the pledged item as a trust. If he transgresses (e.g., uses the item without permission), he becomes liable for the full value of the item, like a usurper (ghasib).
5
State the ruling
The mortgagee must pay the full value of the pledge, not just the debt amount, because the transgression triggers the guarantee.

The Principle: Trusts Become Liable Upon Transgression (Al-Amanat Tudmanu bi al-Ta'addi)
Maxim with application and proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that trusts become liable upon transgression and apply it to the case of a mortgagee who transgresses on a pawn, identifying the resulting liability.

Core Idea
Trusts are not liable unless the trustee transgresses; upon ta'addi, full liability arises as in ghasb.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi principle: amanat (trusts) do not carry liability unless the trustee commits ta'addi (transgression). The author of al-Hidayah applies this to the pawn contract (rahn): the mortgagee holds the pawn as an amanah for the surplus above the debt. If the mortgagee transgresses, he becomes liable for the full value of the pawn, not just the surplus. A hadith from the Book of Hunting is used as an analogy to illustrate how a part separated from a living entity is treated legally, reinforcing the separation of the surplus from the pawn.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Principle
The passage states ''Al-amanatu tudmanu bi al-ta'addi.'' What does this mean? Trusts are not liable originally, but if the trustee commits a wrongful act (ta'addi), he becomes guarantor. This is a general rule for all amanat.
Memorize the maxim: الأمانات تضمن بالتعدي.
2
Apply to Pawn
The mortgagee holds the pawn. The surplus above the debt is an amanah. If the mortgagee commits ta'addi (e.g., using, damaging, or misplacing the pawn without permission), he becomes liable for the full value of the pawn as if he had usurped it (ghasb). Why full value? Because the transgression changes the nature of the entire pawn from amanah to mamluk (like a usurped item).
The surplus is the excess value over the debt.
3
Analyze the Proof
The passage cites a hadith: ''What is cut from a living animal is dead'' (ما أبين من الحي فهو ميت). From the Book of Hunting, this shows that a limb severed from a living animal is legally dead and not edible, while the animal remains alive. This illustrates that a part separated from a whole is treated as a distinct legal entity. Analogously, the surplus of the pawn (the part above the debt) is a separate trust distinct from the debt obligation. This supports the idea that the surplus is an amanah, and ta'addi on it triggers liability that extends to the entire pawn.
The limb example is a dalil via analogy (qiyas), showing how a separated part gains its own legal status.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that only the surplus should be liable upon transgression, not the entire pawn. The Hanafi position is that full liability applies because the mortgagee's transgression corrupts the entire trust relationship, making the whole pawn like a usurped item. The practical difference is the amount of compensation: full value vs. surplus only.
The Hanafi view is mufta bihi in the Hidayah.

Principle 40: Expenses for Pawn's Preservation and Return
Distinguishing expenses for the pawn's benefit from expenses for its custody and return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between expenses for the pawn's preservation (on mortgagor) and expenses for its protection/return (on mortgagee) and apply the rule to specific cases.

Core Idea
Preserving the pawn's value is the mortgagor's cost; protecting and returning the pawn is the mortgagee's.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a clear division of expenses in a pawn (rahn) contract. Understanding who bears which costs is essential for both parties to avoid disputes. The principle distinguishes between expenses for the pawn's benefit (improvement, feeding) and expenses for its custody and return (storage, guarding).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of expenses
The passage divides expenses into two types: those for the pawn's preservation (ibqa) and those for its protection (hifz) and return. Preservation expenses benefit the pawn itself; protection expenses are for the mortgagee's duty as custodian.
2
Determine who bears preservation expenses
Expenses for the pawn's improvement and continued existence (e.g., feeding, medical care, repairs) are on the mortgagor (rahin) because he is the owner and benefits from the pawn's value.
3
Determine who bears protection and return expenses
Costs for guarding, storage, and returning the pawn to the mortgagee (or any part) are on the mortgagee (murtahin) because he is the custodian responsible for safekeeping.
Example: rent for a storage house and wages of a guard are on the mortgagee.
4
Apply to concrete examples from the text
The passage gives examples: the wage of a shepherd (for feeding) is on the mortgagor; the wage of a guard and rent of a storage house are on the mortgagee. Use these to test understanding.
5
Note the ta'addi (transgression) exception
The passage also references that if the mortgagee transgresses (ta'addi), he becomes liable for the full value of the pawn (principle 39). This is a separate but related rule about breach of trust.
This reinforces that the mortgagee's duty to protect is a trust.

Expenses for Pawned Property: Preservation vs. Maintenance
Ruling with proof (talīl) on pawn expenses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between preservation and maintenance expenses on pawned property and apply the Hanafi rule assigning each to the correct party.

Core Idea
Maintenance of the pawn is on its owner; preservation of the pawn is on its holder.

When property is pawned, who pays for its upkeep? The Hanafi school draws a clear line: expenses that sustain or grow the item (like fodder, clothing, medical care) fall on the mortgagor (owner), while expenses for its safety and return (like rent for storage, guard wages) fall on the mortgagee (holder). This separation rests on the principle that ownership triggers maintenance costs, while possession entails a duty of protection. In this lesson, we unpack the ruling, its proof by analogy to trust (wadi‘ah), and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Read the first part of the source: what activity is being ruled upon? The text distinguishes two categories: (1) what is needed for the maintenance (islah) and preservation (tabqiyah) of the pawn, and (2) what is for its protection (hifz) or return to the mortgagee. For the first category, the expense is on the mortgagor; for the second, on the mortgagee.
The key is to note that 'maintenance' (mā yaḥtāju ilayhi li-maṣlaḥat al-rahn wa-tabqiyatihi) is different from 'preservation' (mā kāna li-ḥifẓihi).
2
Locate the proof (dalīl)
The text provides a reasoning (ta‘līl): 'because the pawned item remains the property of the mortgagor, and its benefits belong to him, so its maintenance and preservation are upon him as a consequence of ownership (mu’unat al-milk).' The operative part is 'li-anna al-‘ayn … bāqin ‘alā milkihī' – because the thing remains his property. In Hanafi usul, the general rule is that expenses of an owned item fall on the owner. This is explicit. For preservation, the mortgagee must bear costs because he is the one obligated to return the item safely, so any cost to maintain possession (like rent for the house) is on him.
The text also quotes from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah that 'preservation (ḥifẓ) is obligatory upon the mortgagee whether the pawn is sufficient to cover the debt or not.'

Apportioning Expenses of the Pledged Item
Explaining which party bears costs of preservation and custody

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for apportioning expenses of a pledged item by distinguishing between preservation costs (on rahin) and custody costs (on murtahin).

Core Idea
Preservation costs fall on the owner; custody and return costs fall on the pledgee.

When an item is pledged as security, questions arise about who pays for its upkeep and safekeeping. This lesson lays out the Hanafi framework, which distinguishes between expenses that maintain the asset itself and those that secure the pledgee's right of possession. The ruling is based on the principle that ownership entails maintenance costs, while the pledgee's right of detention makes him responsible for custody and return expenses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of expenses
Distinguish between expenses for preservation/improvement (e.g., food, clothing, shepherding) and expenses for custody/return (e.g., guard fee, storage rent, recovering a runaway slave).
The general principle hinges on whether the expense benefits the pledge itself or serves the pledgee's right to hold the pledge.
2
State the general Hanafi rule
Recall: 'Whatever is needed for the benefit and preservation of the pledge is on the rahin; whatever is for its preservation or to return it to the murtahin is on the murtahin.'
This is stated explicitly in the text from Al-Binayah.
3

Go through the examples: shepherd's fee is on rahin (maintenance of animal); storage rent, guard fee, veterinarian fee are on murtahin (custody).

4

Ownership of the pledge remains with the rahin, so preservation costs are on him (like in a trust). The murtahin has the right to detain the pledge, so custody costs are on him.
Qiyas (analogy) is made to wadi'ah (deposit) where the depositor bears preservation costs.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If a dispute arises, the judge will assign costs according to this principle. For example, if the murtahin claims the rahin must pay for storage, the judge will rule against him.
This distinction prevents unjust enrichment and clarifies liability.

Pledging Attached Items: The Condition of Separable Possession
Stating the rule that a pledged item must be separable for possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a pledged item must be separable for possession and apply it to identify valid and invalid pledges in cases of attached items.

Core Idea
A pledge fails if the item is inseparably attached to a non‑pledged item – exclusive possession must be possible.

This lesson examines a key condition for the validity of a pledge (rahn): the pledged property must be capable of being taken into exclusive possession by the pledgee. If the item is attached or mixed with another item that is not part of the pledge, the contract cannot be completed because the pledgee cannot hold the pledged item alone. The text gives classic examples and explains the underlying reasoning through analogy with undivided shares (musha').

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The text states: If the pledged item (marhun) is attached to a non‑pledged item such that independent possession is impossible, the pledge is not permissible.
The hukm is batil (invalid) from the outset.
2
Understand the condition of qabd
In Hanafi law, qabd (possession) is a condition for the validity of rahn. If the pledgee cannot physically or meaningfully take the marhun alone, the condition is unfulfilled.
Qabd is not merely delivery but actual ability to hold separately.
3
Apply to the given examples
The text lists: fruit on a tree without the tree, crops in land without the land, land without its trees. In each case, the marhun cannot be possessed alone because it is attached by creation.
The attachment is natural, not temporary.
4
Connect to the parallel rule for musha'
The author analogizes this to the prohibition of pledging an undivided share (musha') in common property. The effective cause (illa) is the same: inability to take exclusive possession.
This is an explicit qiyas.
5
Recognize the illa and its limits
The illa is the inability to isolate the marhun from a non‑marhun item. If the attachment is temporary or removable, the rule may not apply. The key is whether independent qabd is possible at the time of contract.
This is a talil (reasoning by cause).

Here also the destruction of the slave occurred, so the debt will fall. He said: 'Whoever pawns a slave worth one thousand for one thousand (debt) to a term, and the price decreases until its value becomes one hundred, then a man kills him by mistake, and he pays its value one hundred, then the term expires: the mortgagee (murtahin) takes the one hundred as payment of his right, and does not claim anything from the pawner (rahin).' (Majma' al-Damanat: Kitab al-Rahn, vol. 1 p. 114)
Source pages p. 241

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on pawn (rahn). It starts by explaining that if a pawned slave dies in the lender's possession, the debt is extinguished regardless of who caused the death. This is contrasted with a mere decrease in value (e.g., price fluctuation), which does not affect the debt. The text then quotes from al-'Inayah: a decrease in market value after seizure is not considered, so it does not cause the debt to fall. A practical case from Majma' al-Damanat follows: A slave worth 1000 is pawned for a 1000 debt. The market value drops to 100, then the slave is accidentally killed; the killer pays 100 as compensation. When the debt term matures, the lender takes that 100 as payment of the debt and has no claim on the pawner for the remaining 900. This illustrates that the debt is considered satisfied by the actual loss of the pawn, even if the original value was higher. The underlying principle: in pawn, the debt is secured by the pawn; if the pawn perishes, the debt is extinguished to the extent of the pawn's value at the time of loss, but market fluctuations are ignored.

Key Terms
مرتهن (murtahin): The lender/mortgagee who holds the pawn as security
راہن (rahin): The pawner/debtor who gives the pawn
دین (dayn): The debt owed by the pawner to the lender
ہلاکت (halakat): Destruction or death of the pawned item
قبض (qabd): Possession of the pawn by the lender

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, a drop in market value of a pawned item does not reduce the debt; only the physical destruction of the pawn extinguishes the debt to the extent of its value.
