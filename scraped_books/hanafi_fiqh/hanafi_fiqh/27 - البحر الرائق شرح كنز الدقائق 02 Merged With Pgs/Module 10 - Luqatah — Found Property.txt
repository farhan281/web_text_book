Rulings on Property Found in Dar al-Harb
Rulings on property found in enemy territory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on property discovered in enemy territory based on the mode of entry (with or without aman) and identify when such property becomes ghanimah subject to khums.

Core Idea
Property taken without aman is lawful; with aman it must be returned or given in charity.

This part of the passage clarifies the fiqh of finding property in Dar al-Harb. The ruling hinges on whether the Muslim entered with a guarantee of safety (aman) or not. Additionally, if a group takes treasure by force, it becomes war booty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the entry status
Determine whether the finder entered Dar al-Harb with aman (safe conduct) or without.
Aman can be a visa or treaty.
2
Apply the ruling accordingly
With aman: must return the property to its owner; if not returned, the finder owns it but must give its value in charity. Without aman: the property is lawful (halal) for the finder.
Selling it when owned with aman is valid but not lawful for the buyer.
3
Recognize the exception for group force
If a group of people with power enters and captures property by force, it becomes ghanimah (war booty) and khums is obligatory.
This overrides the general ruling.
4
Understand the consequences of sale
If the finder sells the property (after owning it with aman), the sale is valid but the buyer does not acquire lawful ownership.
The finder should have given it in charity instead.

في المباح، وإن كانت مملوكة لبعضهم فإن دخل بأمان رده إلى صاحبها لحرمة أموالهم عليه بغير الرضا، فإن لم يرده إليه ملكه ملكاً خبيثاً فسبيله التصدق به، فلو باعه صح لقيامه ملكه لكن لا يطيب للمشتري بخلاف بيع المشتري شراء فاسداً لأن الفساد يرتفع ببيعه لامتناع فسخه حينئذ، وإن دخل بغير أمان حل له ويستثنى من إطلاق المصنف ما إذا دخل جماعة ذوو منعه دار الحرب وظفروا بشيء من كنوزهم فإنه يجب فيه الخمس لكونه غنيمة لحصول الأخذ على طريق القهر والغلبة قوله : ( وفيروزج ولؤلؤ وعنبر) أي لا تخمس هذه الأشياء، أما الأول فلأنه حجر مضيء يوجد في الجبال وقد ورد في الحديث لا خمس في الحجر» ونحوه الياقوت والجواهر كما قدمناه من كل جامع لا ينطبع أطلقه وهو مقيد بما إذا أخذها من معدنها، أما إذا وجدت كنزاً وهي دفين الجاهلية ففيه الخمس لأنه لا يشترط في الكنز إلا المالية لكونه غنيمة . وأما الثاني فالمراد به كل حلية تستخرج من البحر حتى الذهب والفضة فيه بأن كانت كنزاً في قعر البحر وهذا عندهما. وقال أبو يوسف: يجب في جميع ما يخرج من البحر لأنه مما تحويه يد الملوك . ولهما : إن قعر البحر لا يرد عليه قهر أحد فانعدمت اليد وهي شرط الوجوب. فالحاصل أن الكنز لا تفصيل فيه بل يجب فيه الخمس كيفما كان، الأرض أو لم يكن بعد أن كان مالاً متقوماً، وأما المعدن فثلاثة أنواع كما من جنس قدمناه أول الباب واللؤلؤ مطر الربيع يقع في الصدف فيصير لؤلؤاً، والصدف حيوان يخلق فيه اللؤلؤ والعنبر حشيش ينبت في البحر أو خثي دابة في البحر والله سبحانه أعلم.
Regarding permissible (property): if it is owned by some of them (the people of Dar al-Harb) and he entered with aman (safe conduct), he must return it to its owner due to the sanctity of their wealth without consent. If he does not return it to him, he owns it as a filthy ownership, so his way is to give it in charity. If he sells it, the sale is valid because his ownership is established, but it is not lawful (halal) for the buyer, unlike the sale of a buyer of a void purchase because the voidness is removed by his sale due to the impossibility of rescission then. If he entered without aman, it is lawful for him. An exception to the author's general statement is when a group of people with power enter Dar al-Harb and capture something from their treasures, then khums (one-fifth) is obligatory on it because it is war booty due to the taking being by means of force and domination. He said: (and firuzaj (turquoise), pearl, and ambergris) i.e., these things are not subject to khums. As for the first, it is a luminous stone found in mountains, and it has been narrated in the hadith: 'There is no khums on stones', and similarly ruby and jewels, as we have mentioned regarding any solid that does not melt. This is unrestricted but is restricted to when taken from its mine. If it is found as a treasure, i.e., a pagan burial, then khums is due on it because nothing is required for treasure except that it be valuable property, being booty. As for the second (pearl), by it is meant every ornament extracted from the sea, even gold and silver in it, if it is a treasure at the bottom of the sea. This is according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Abu Yusuf said: Khums is obligatory on everything extracted from the sea because it is what the hands of kings contain. For them (the two): the bottom of the sea is not subject to the force of anyone, so the hand (possession) is absent, which is a condition for obligation. The conclusion is that there is no detail regarding treasure; rather, khums is obligatory on it however it is, whether in land or not, provided it is valuable property. As for mines, they are of three types as we have mentioned at the beginning of the chapter. Pearl is the spring rain that falls into the shell and becomes a pearl, and the shell is an animal in which the pearl is created. Ambergris is a herb that grows in the sea or the dung of an animal in the sea. And Allah Most High knows best.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of rulings from the base text (Kanz al-Daqaiq) regarding property found in enemy territory (Dar al-Harb). It addresses two scenarios: entering with aman (safe conduct) and without. With aman, the finder must return the property to its owner; if he keeps it, he owns it but must give it in charity. Selling it is valid but not lawful for the buyer. Without aman, the property is lawful for the finder. An exception is made for treasures taken by force, which become ghanimah (war booty) and require khums (one-fifth). The passage then discusses specific items: turquoise, pearls, and ambergris. Turquoise is a stone from mines and is exempt from khums based on a hadith. Pearls and ambergris from the sea are also exempt according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, because they are not subject to forceful acquisition like land treasures. Abu Yusuf dissents, arguing that anything from the sea is akin to treasures of kings. The relied-upon position (cadhā) is that khums is not due on sea treasures unless they are found as a buried treasure (kanz) with the conditions of value and property. The passage concludes by clarifying the origin of pearls and ambergris.

Key Terms
كنز (kanz): Buried treasure, specifically from the pre-Islamic era, that has value and is subject to khums.
خمس (khums): One-fifth tax on war booty and treasure (kanz).
غنيمة (ghanimah): War booty taken by force from combatants, subject to khums.

Entering with aman protects enemy property; without aman, it is lawful for the finder.

Khums on Pearls and Amber
Khums on sea treasures and the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on khums for pearls and amber, identify the relied-upon position (the two), and compare it with Abu Yusuf's view.

Core Idea
No khums on sea pearls and amber unless they are found as buried treasure; the condition of forceful acquisition is absent.

This section addresses the intra-Hanafi dispute over khums on sea treasures. It distinguishes between minerals from mines, which are exempt, and treasure (kanz), which requires khums. The relied-upon position exempts pearls and amber unless they are found as a pagan burial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items discussed
Turquoise (a stone), pearls, and amber. All are natural products from sea or mountains.
2
Apply the hadith 'la khums fi al-hajar'
Stones from mines are exempt from khums based on this hadith. Pearls and amber are analogized to stones.
The hadith is weak (zanni) but relied upon.
3
Distinguish between mine extraction and treasure (kanz)
If taken from a mine, no khums. If found as a buried treasure (kanz) in land or sea, khums is due.
Kanz requires only that it be valuable property.
4
Understand the condition for khums on sea items
The relied-upon position (the two) requires khums on sea items only if they are found as a buried treasure (kanz) at the bottom of the sea.
If found floating or extracted normally, no khums.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf holds khums on all sea extractions because they resemble royal treasures. The two respond that the sea bottom is not subject to forceful acquisition, so the condition for khums is absent.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
6
Determine the mufta bihi
The position of the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is followed: no khums on sea pearls and amber unless kanz.
This is the zahir al-riwayah.
