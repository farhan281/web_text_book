Rulings on the Martyr's Shroud
Rulings on the martyr's shroud and burial attire.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for the martyr's shroud and identify items that are not part of the kafan.

Core Idea
The martyr is buried in his own bloodied clothes; only non-shroud items like weapons and fur are removed.

The martyr's burial rites differ from ordinary Muslims: he is not washed and is buried in the clothes he died in, with his blood. However, certain items attached to his body are not considered part of the shroud and must be removed. This lesson explains the Hanafi position on what is removed and how the shroud may be adjusted to match the sunna.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The martyr is buried in his own clothes, with his blood, without washing. This is the default ruling.
2
List the items not part of the kafan
Items not from the shroud (laysa min al-kafan) include: fur (farrow), padding (hashw), cap (qalansuwa), weapons (silah), and khuffs (al-khuff). These must be removed.
The term 'kafan' refers to the shroud; the martyr's existing clothes serve as his shroud.
3
Apply the increase/decrease principle
If the martyr's clothing is less than the sunna shroud (i.e., does not cover adequately), additional cloth should be added. If it exceeds the sunna, it should be reduced.
There is a difference of opinion: some scholars say increase is allowed up to three layers, while others say it is generally permissible.
4
Note the ruling on hanut
Perfume (hanut) is applied to the martyr like an ordinary deceased person, even though he is not washed.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the rulings regarding the martyr's burial. The author explains that the martyr is buried in his blood and clothes, but certain items are removed (like fur, padding, cap, weapons, khuffs). The shroud may be increased or decreased to match the sunna. The main dispute concerns washing: Abu Hanifa holds that the martyr is not washed even if in janabah, because martyrdom prevents impure state without removing it. The Two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that he must be washed, citing the hadith of Hanzala washed by angels. The commentary reconciles these views. For a child martyr, similar difference: Abu Hanifa says no washing (sword suffices for purification), while the Two require washing. 'Irtithath' means the martyr shows signs of life after battle (eats, drinks, sleeps, treats wounds, etc.), which nullifies the worldly status of martyrdom (he is washed), but he remains a martyr in the afterlife. The text also discusses menstruating women, postpartum, and madmen by analogy.

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahīd): A person killed in battle against disbelievers, with specific legal rulings: not washed, buried in bloodied clothes.
الكفن (al-kafan): The shroud for the deceased; for a martyr, it is limited to covering necessary parts; items not from the shroud (like weapons) are removed.
السلاح (al-silāḥ): Weapons; not part of the martyr's shroud, must be removed before burial.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Washing a Martyr in Janabah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on washing the martyr in janabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding washing a martyr killed in a state of janabah, and state the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: martyr in janabah is not washed; the Two: he must be washed. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

One of the most debated issues within the Hanafi school is the case of a martyr who was in a state of janabah before being killed. Abu Hanifa held that martyrdom prevents the obligation of ghusl, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, argued that ghusl is still required. This lesson explores the evidence and reasoning behind each position, and explains why the school eventually adopted Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
State the positions
Abu Hanifa: martyr not washed. Sahibayn: martyr must be washed if in janabah. Both agree on the hadith of Hanzala.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa uses the hadith of Hanzala: the Prophet did not order washing, and the angels washed him, indicating no human obligation. Also qiyas: the sword purifies the martyr. He distinguishes between hadath asghar and akbar; only minor impurity is removed by martyrdom.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's dalil
They argue that the angels washing Hanzala shows the necessity of washing, and janabah requires removal. The Prophet's silence is not evidence of permissibility; rather the angels' act shows it is wajib.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa's response: the angels' washing was a miracle (karama), not a normative act. Martyrdom is a mani' (preventer) of the obligation, not a rafi' (remover). Thus, the martyr remains free from the requirement.
This is the key distinction: martyrdom prevents the ruling from applying, rather than removing an existing impurity.
5
Determine the thamarah and tarjih
Practical difference: Abu Hanifa buries without ghusl; the Two require ghusl. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as chosen by later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani and the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the rulings regarding the martyr's burial. The author explains that the martyr is buried in his blood and clothes, but certain items are removed (like fur, padding, cap, weapons, khuffs). The shroud may be increased or decreased to match the sunna. The main dispute concerns washing: Abu Hanifa holds that the martyr is not washed even if in janabah, because martyrdom prevents impure state without removing it. The Two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that he must be washed, citing the hadith of Hanzala washed by angels. The commentary reconciles these views. For a child martyr, similar difference: Abu Hanifa says no washing (sword suffices for purification), while the Two require washing. 'Irtithath' means the martyr shows signs of life after battle (eats, drinks, sleeps, treats wounds, etc.), which nullifies the worldly status of martyrdom (he is washed), but he remains a martyr in the afterlife. The text also discusses menstruating women, postpartum, and madmen by analogy.

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahīd): A person killed in battle against disbelievers, with specific legal rulings: not washed, buried in bloodied clothes.
الجنابة (al-janābah): State of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl.
المانع (al-māni'): A preventer; in this context, martyrdom prevents the obligation of ghusl from arising, rather than removing an existing impurity.
