Ruling on Not Combing or Cutting the Deceased's Hair and Nails
Explains the ruling and supporting opinions on leaving the deceased's hair and nails untouched.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on combing the deceased's hair and cutting nails, explain the underlying rationale, and identify exceptions.

Core Idea
Combing hair or cutting nails of the deceased is prohibited because these acts are for adornment, which the deceased no longer needs.

This lesson examines a brief but important rule from the Hanafi school: one must not comb the deceased's hair or beard, nor cut their nails or hair. The rationale is that these actions are acts of adornment (zina), and after death, the person has no need for adornment. The commentary also provides nuances, such as the permissibility of kissing the deceased, applying perfume, and removing a broken nail. Understanding this ruling helps us respect the deceased's state and avoid unnecessary actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is combing hair, beard, and cutting nails of the deceased. The ruling is la yajuz (not permissible), which in Hanafi fiqh indicates a prohibition (makruh tahrimi).
The passage uses the phrase 'لا يسرح شعره ولحيته ولا يقص ظفره وشعره'.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given: because these are acts of adornment (zina) and the deceased is independent of adornment. So the prohibition is based on the cessation of need for beautification.
The text says 'لأنها للزينة وقد استغنى عنها'.
3
Recognize the exceptions
The passage notes that perfume is permissible, kissing the deceased is permissible, and if a nail breaks, it may be removed. These are not acts of adornment but rather care or expression of love.
Exceptions are found in citations from al-Qunyah, al-Mujtaba, and al-Zahiriyya.
4
Note the authoritative sources
The ruling is supported by citations from al-Qunyah, al-Mujtaba, and al-Zahiriyya, showing it is well-established in the Hanafi school.
This is commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq, so it relies on earlier authoritative texts.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn's statement that one does not comb the deceased's hair or beard, nor cut his nails or hair. The commentator affirms that this action is not permissible, as these acts are for adornment in life, and after death the deceased no longer needs them. Supporting citations from al-Qunyah state that beautification, combing, and cutting hair after death are impermissible, though applying perfume is allowed. Al-Mujtaba confirms that kissing the deceased is permissible. The mention of the beard alongside hair is for emphasis, not a repetition. Finally, al-Zahiriyya reports that if a nail breaks, it may be removed, based on a narration from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Key Terms
الزينة (al-zina): Adornment or beautification, which is the purpose of hair and nail care in life.
لا يجوز (la yajuz): Not permissible; a legal ruling of prohibition (haraam or makruh tahrimi).

Linguistic Analysis of 'Iqtar' and 'Qattara'
Correcting the reading of 'iqtar' based on Arabic grammar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the linguistic debate about the verb 'aqtara' in the context of ear drops during fasting, and identify the preferred reading according to Hanafi linguistic analysis.

Core Idea
A grammatical refinement does not alter the fiqh ruling; precise language protects legal consistency.

In Hanafi fiqh, the wording of a text is crucial for deriving rulings. This passage delves into the Arabic verb used for 'putting drops' in the ear, addressing a common misreading. The commentator refines the understanding of 'aqtara' by distinguishing between transitive and intransitive uses, ultimately affirming that the legal meaning remains unchanged regardless of vocalization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic dispute
Recognize that some scholars read 'aqtara' as passive voice (مبني للمفعول). The commentator argues this is grammatically problematic because 'aqtara' is intransitive in its base form.
The dispute is purely grammatical; the fiqh ruling is not in question.
2
Analyze the argument against passive reading
The commentator cites that 'aqtara' originally means 'its time to drip has come' (lazim), not 'to cause to drip'. It lacks transitive usage in classical lexicons like Al-Jawhari.
If 'aqtara' were transitive, the passive would be plausible, but it is not.
3
Consider the counter-evidence from Al-Maghrib
The commentator then presents a language variant (lugha) where 'qatara', 'qattara', and 'aqtara' all mean 'to pour drop by drop' (transitive). This rescues the possibility of a passive reading.
This shows linguistic diversity; the earlier critique was based on a narrower view.
4
Determine the preferred reading
Given the variant, both active and passive readings are possible, but the active is preferred for consistency with surrounding verbs. However, the meaning remains the same: 'putting drops'.
The fiqh ruling does not change; the analysis is about textual correctness.
5
Apply to the fiqh context
In practice, whether one reads 'iqtar' as active or passive, the legal effect is identical: it refers to inserting drops. The linguistic debate is internal to the text's transmission.
This reinforces that qira'at (readings) do not create new rulings unless they indicate a different legal meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a linguistic digression by the commentator, criticizing the reading of 'iqtar' (drops) in the matn. The claim was that some read 'aqtara' as passive (mabni lil-maf'ul). The commentator argues that 'aqtara' is originally intransitive (meaning 'it is time to drip'), not transitive. However, he offers a resolution: in the dictionary Al-Maghrib, 'qatara', 'qattara', and 'aqtara' are all valid transitive verbs meaning 'to pour drop by drop'. Therefore, the reading can be active or passive, but active is preferable for consistency with other verbs in the text. The point is that the word 'iqtar' in the ruling should be understood as 'drops'—the act of putting drops into the ear—and whether it is read as active or passive does not change the legal meaning. This demonstrates the importance of precise linguistic analysis in fiqh texts.

إقطار (iqtar): Verbal noun of 'aqtara' meaning 'the act of dripping' or 'instilling drops'.

Textual Criticism: 'Majnuna' vs 'Majbura' in the Fast of an Insane Woman
Critical refinement of a textual variant in a fiqh ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the textual issue regarding the word 'majnuna' in the passage on the fast of an insane woman and state how Hanafi scholars resolved the apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
A suspected scribal error does not invalidate a text if a valid interpretation exists.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into a textual puzzle concerning a case of a woman who fasts, becomes insane, and is then raped. The word 'majnuna' (insane) seems contradictory because an insane person cannot consent. Some scholars suggested the original read 'majbura' (coerced). The commentator demonstrates how the Hanafi tradition handles such textual issues without discarding the received text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
The text states that a woman who intended to fast then became insane and was raped incurs qada' and kaffarah. The problem: how can she be considered a fasting person while insane?
The word 'majnuna' seems to contradict the validity of the fast.
2
Understand the alternative reading
Some mashayikh proposed the original word was 'majbura' (coerced), which would make sense because a coerced woman is not liable for kaffarah.
This theory is based on a scribal error (tashif).
3
Examine the interpretation offered by most scholars
They said the woman was sane at the beginning of the day (thus she intended the fast) and became insane later; then intercourse occurred while she was insane, but she later recovered. Thus the fast was valid initially.
This interpretation allows the word 'majnuna' to stand.
4
Analyze the commentator's resolution
The commentator shows that both readings can be accepted: the scribal error theory does not preclude a valid interpretation, and Imam Muhammad left the text unchanged because a plausible ta'wil exists.
The objection is repelled by allowing multiple perspectives.

قوله : (وفي المجنونة بأن نوت الخ) قال في العناية تبعاً للنهاية وغيرها : قد تكلموا في صحة لأنها لا تجامع الجنون، وحكي عن أبي سليمان الجورجاني قال : لما قرأت على محمد هذه المسألة قلت له : كيف تكون صائمة وهي مجنونة ؟ فقال : دع هذا فإن انتشر في الأفق. فمن المشايخ من قال : كأنه كتب في الأصل مجبورة وظن الكاتب مجنونة ولهذا قال : دع فإنه انتشر في الأفق. وأكثرهم قالوا : تأويله أنها كانت عاقلة بالغة في أول النهار ثم جنت فجامعها زوجها ثم أفاقت وعلمت بما فعل قال في النهر : وهذا يقتضي عدم تصحيفها، وجزم في الفتح بأنها مصحفة من الكاتب قال : وتركها محمد بعد التصحيف لإمكان توجيهها ا.هـ. وهذا يفيد رفع الخلاف السابق إذ لا تنافي بين تصحيفها وتأويلها، و به اندفع دفع المؤلف لكن لا يخفى أن ما عن أبي سليمان ليس نصاً في أن الكاتب صحفها بل وقعت عن محمد كذلك غير أنه لم يصلحها لانتشارها وإمكان تأويلها وأيضاً استعماله مجبورة بمعنى ضعيف .
His saying: (And regarding the insane woman, that she intended the fast etc.) said in al-‘Inayah following al-Nihayah etc.: 'They have discussed its correctness because she cannot engage in intercourse while insane. It is narrated from Abu Sulayman al-Jurjani: When I recited this question to Muhammad, I said to him: How can she be fasting while insane? He said: Leave this, for it has spread in the horizon. So some mashayikh said: It is as if in the original it was written 'majbura' (coerced) and the scribe thought it was 'majnuna' (insane). And most of them said: Its interpretation is that she was sane and adult at the beginning of the day, then became insane, and her husband had intercourse with her, then she recovered and knew what he did.' He said in al-Nahr: 'This implies that it is not a scribal error.' And he decisively stated in al-Fath that it is a scribal error by the copyist, and said: 'Muhammad left it after the scribal error due to the possibility of interpreting it.' End. This benefits removing the previous disagreement, as there is no contradiction between it being a scribal error and its interpretation. And with this, the author's objection is repelled, but it is not hidden that what is from Abu Sulayman is not explicit that the copyist made an error; rather it came from Muhammad in that way, but he did not correct it due to its spread and the possibility of interpretation, and also his using 'majbura' in a weak sense.'

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hishiya) from the commentator on a potential scribal error in the original text. The earlier passage had used the word 'majnuna' (insane woman) in the case of a woman who intended to fast then became insane and was raped. Several later scholars noted a logical problem: an insane woman cannot consent to intercourse, but the text says her husband 'jama'aha' (had intercourse with her), which implies she was able to engage. Abu Sulayman al-Jurjani directly asked Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani) about this, and Muhammad dismissed the question saying 'it has spread in the horizon', meaning the text is well known and one can interpret it. Some commentators suggested the original text read 'majbura' (coerced) but was miswritten as 'majnuna'. Others, like the author of al-Nahr, argued that the word 'majnuna' is deliberate and the scenario is that she became insane during the day, so she was sane when she intended the fast but insane when the intercourse occurred. This removes the conflict. The author of al-Fath opined it is a scribal error, but Imam Muhammad left it unchanged because a valid interpretation exists. The commentator concludes that both possibilities (scribal error and valid interpretation) can coexist, and the objection is resolved. However, the evidence for scribal error is not definitive.

Key Terms
المجنونة (al-majnūnah): A woman afflicted with insanity (junnun).
المجبورة (al-majbūrah): A woman who is coerced (into intercourse) against her will.
التصحيف (al-taṣḥīf): Scribal error or corruption of the written text.
