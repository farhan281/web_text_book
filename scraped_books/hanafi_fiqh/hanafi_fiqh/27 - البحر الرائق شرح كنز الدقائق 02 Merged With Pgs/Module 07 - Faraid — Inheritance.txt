Washing the Deceased is a Communal Obligation
States the obligation and related scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that washing the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah) and apply the rules regarding prayer before washing and exhumation after burial.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a collective duty; prayer before washing must be repeated, and exhumation is prohibited after burial.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq by clarifying the obligation of washing the deceased. The primary ruling is that washing is a communal obligation (fard kifayah), like the funeral prayer and burial. The passage also addresses what happens if the prayer is offered before washing, and the rules regarding exhumation after burial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal classification
The text explicitly states that washing the deceased is one of the communal obligations (furuḍ al-kifayah), similar to praying over him, preparing him, and burying him. If all residents of a town abandon it, they are fought.
Fard kifayah means if some perform it, the obligation is lifted from the rest.
2
Note the consequence of prayer before washing
If the funeral prayer is performed before washing, it must be repeated. This indicates that washing is a condition for the validity of the prayer.
Repeating the prayer is required; it is not simply invalid.
3
Distinguish pre-burial vs post-burial discovery
If they remember that the deceased was not washed before the soil is thrown, the bricks are removed and the body is taken out, washed, and prayed over. Once the soil has been thrown (i.e., burial is complete), the body is not exhumed and the prayer is not repeated.
This shows the finality of burial.
4
Understand the ruling on remaining limbs
If a limb remains after washing, shrouding, and prayer, it is washed and the prayer is repeated. However, if only a small part like a finger remains after shrouding, it is not washed according to the most authoritative view. Muhammad holds that it should be washed in any case.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference; the relied-upon view is not to wash small parts.

And if they prayed over him before washing, they repeat the prayer. Likewise, if they remember before the soil is thrown on him, they remove the bricks, take him out, wash him, and pray over him. Muhammad said: It is washed in any case, as in al-Mujtaba. And in al-Qunyah: If a human head is found, it is not washed nor prayed over; if it were washed, the water would become used. And if he dies in his house and the heirs say we do not consent to his washing in it, they do not have that right because washing him in his house is among his needs, and they take precedence over the heirs. And in al-Zahiriya: It is better to wash the deceased for free; if the washer seeks payment, it is of two types: if there is someone else, it is permissible to take payment; otherwise not. And they differed about hiring a tailor to sew the shroud and the wages of the carriers, digger, and burier from the capital. And in al-Khaniya: If water runs over the deceased or rain falls on him, according to Abu Yusuf it does not substitute for washing because we are commanded to wash, and the flowing of water and falling of rain is not washing. And the drowned person is washed three times according to Abu Yusuf. And from Muhammad: If he intended the washing when pulling him out of the water, he is washed twice; if he did not intend, he is washed three times; and in one narration, he is washed once. And in Fath al-Qadir: Apparently the intention is required in it to remove the obligation from the obligated person, not to achieve his purification, and it is a condition for the validity of the prayer over him. And in the Fatawa of Qadi Khan: A deceased washed by his family without intention suffices them. And al-Ghaya chose this.

The first ruling is that washing is a communal obligation (fard kifayah), like the funeral prayer, preparation, and burial. If all residents abandon it, they are fought. If the prayer is performed before washing, it must be repeated. If a limb is found after prayer and shrouding, it is washed and the prayer repeated, except for a small part like a finger. Key Terms

غسل (ghusl): The ritual washing of the deceased's body.

Rulings on Found Remains, Heirs' Consent, and Payment for Services
Provides rulings on found head, heirs, and payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on: a found human head (not washed, not prayed), the heirs' inability to prevent washing in the deceased's home, and the permissibility of paying the washer and service providers from the estate.

Core Idea
A detached human head is not given funeral rites; heirs cannot forbid washing in the home; free washing is best but paid washing is allowed if no volunteer exists.

This section of the commentary addresses secondary but practical issues: the status of a found human head, the rights of heirs to prevent washing in the house, and the financial aspects of hiring a washer or burial workers. The rulings are derived from the principle that the deceased's needs take precedence over heirs' wishes and that communal obligations should be performed without burden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for a found human head
The text states that if a human head is found (detached from a body), it is not washed nor is the funeral prayer performed over it. If it is washed, the water becomes hashar (used).
This is because it is not considered a complete deceased person requiring ritual washing.
2
Analyze the heirs' right to object to washing in the house
If the deceased dies in his own house and the heirs say they do not consent to washing there, they have no right to prevent it. Washing in his house is considered a need (hājah) of the deceased, and his needs take precedence over the heirs' wishes.
The ruling emphasizes that the deceased's needs are prioritized over the living heirs' preferences.
3
Differentiate between free and paid washing
It is better (afḍal) to wash the deceased for free (majjānan). If the washer seeks payment, two scenarios exist: if there is someone else who can wash for free, the paid washing is impermissible; otherwise, it is permissible to take payment.
This ensures the obligation is fulfilled without unnecessary expense when possible.
4
Understand the dispute on hiring costs
The passage mentions a difference of opinion (ikhtilāf) regarding hiring a tailor to sew the shroud and paying the wages of carriers, diggers, and buriers from the deceased's capital (estate). The dispute is left unresolved in this passage.
This indicates that there is no single authoritative position on these costs; later sources may provide guidance.

Rulings on Water, Drowning, and Intention in Washing
Discusses water, drowning, and intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on: whether rainwater substitutes for ritual washing, the number of washes for a drowned person based on intention, and the requirement of intention for washing the deceased.

Core Idea
Rainwater does not count as ritual washing; a drowned person's washing depends on intention; intention for washing is required to lift the obligation, but family washing without intention suffices.

This final section addresses specific cases: whether accidental water (rain, immersion) suffices for washing, how to wash a drowned person, and the critical dispute over the necessity of intention. The passage presents views from Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Fath al-Qadir, and Qadi Khan, with al-Ghaya preferring the latter's opinion.

Guided Path
1
Examine the ruling on rainwater/water flow
According to Abu Yusuf, if water runs over the deceased or rain falls on him, it does not substitute for washing. The reason is that we are commanded to perform an intentional washing (ghusl), and mere water flow is not considered ghusl.
The act of washing requires intentional action, not just incidental contact with water.
2
Analyze the washing of a drowned person
Abu Yusuf says the drowned person is washed three times. Muhammad distinguishes based on intention: if the rescuer intended the washing when pulling the body out of water, it is washed twice; if not, three times. Another narration from Muhammad says only once.
The number of washes varies with intention, showing its importance.
3
Understand the role of intention (niyyah)
According to Fath al-Qadir, intention is apparently required in washing the deceased to remove the obligation from the community (not to purify the deceased), and it is a condition for the validity of the funeral prayer over him.
Intention here is for the obligation, not for the deceased's ritual purity.
4
Contrast with Qadi Khan's view
In the Fatawa of Qadi Khan, if a deceased is washed by his family without intention, it suffices them (i.e., the obligation is fulfilled). Al-Ghaya (al-Ghaya) chose this opinion as the preferred one.
This is the mufta bihi position according to al-Ghaya.

Inheritance of a Fetus Born Dead Due to Assault
Corrects the absolute ruling that a stillborn does not inherit, noting an exception for assault.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exception to the rule that a stillborn does not inherit and explain the legal proof (ghurrah) that establishes the fetus's life in cases of assault.

Core Idea
A stillborn from assault inherits because the ghurrah proves the fetus was alive at the time of injury.

You have likely learned that a stillborn child does not inherit and is not inherited from. That general rule, however, has a critical exception. When the stillbirth results from an assault—such as a blow to the mother's abdomen—the fetus is considered legally alive for inheritance purposes. This lesson unpacks that exception and the proof behind it, drawn from the obligation of ghurrah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule and Its Qualification
Note the statement that the rule 'a stillborn does not inherit and is not inherited from' is not absolute. It applies only when the fetus separates spontaneously.
The key word is 'not absolute' – an exception exists.
2
Recognize the Exception: Assault as Cause of Death
The exception is when the fetus is separated due to an assault. In that case, the fetus is among the heirs.
The text says 'إذا فصل فهو من جملة الورثة' – if it is separated (by assault), it is among the heirs.
3
Understand the Proof: Obligation of Ghurrah
The Lawgiver obligated the assailant to pay ghurrah (compensation). Ghurrah is only due for injury to a living being, not a dead one. Therefore, the fetus must have been alive at the moment of assault.
Ghurrah is a slave or its value (currently 500 dirhams). This is a strong indication of life.
4
Apply the Qiyas: Life Implies Inheritance
Since the fetus is ruled to have been alive, it qualifies as an heir just as any other living person. Consequently, it inherits from the deceased and its own share is inherited by its heirs.
The ruling is based on qiyas: ghurrah → life → inheritance.
5
Derive the Ruling and Note Source Authority
The mufta bihi is that the stillborn from assault is an heir. This ruling is from al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah and al-Mabsut, which are Zahir al-Riwayah sources.
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position, with no intra-madhhab dispute mentioned.

The Taba'iyyah of Captive Children: Abode vs Hand
Intra-Hanafi dispute on taba'iyyah of captive children

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three sources of taba'iyyah for captive children and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over the priority between the hand of the captor and the abode.

Core Idea
For captive children, the abode prevails over the hand in determining Islamic status.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal following (taba'iyyah) of children taken captive in war. While the school agrees on three sources of following—parents, the hand of the captor, and the abode—scholars differ on which takes precedence. The practical consequence affects whether the funeral prayer is performed for a child who dies in Dar al-Harb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sources of taba'iyyah
From the passage, list the three ways a captive child's legal status is determined: by parents, by the hand of the captor, and by the abode (Dar al-Islam).
2
Understand the disputed priority
The core ikhtilaf is between the hand (yad) and the abode (dar). Note which scholars support each view.
Al-Hidayah and Qadi Khan prefer the abode; al-Muhit prefers the hand when one parent is absent.
3
Examine the evidence for abode priority
Kashf al-Asrar reports a case where a dhimmi steals a child and brings him to Dar al-Islam: the child becomes Muslim by abode, regardless of the captor's hand.
4
Examine the evidence for hand priority
Al-Muhit argues that when one parent is absent, the hand of the captor is like ownership over property, so the child follows the captor.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical difference: if the child dies in Dar al-Harb, the hand-priority view leads to funeral prayer; the abode-priority view does not. The mufta bihi is that the abode takes precedence.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute over the sources of tawba (following) for captive children. The central issue is whether a child captured in Dar al-Harb follows the abode (dar) or the hand (yad) of the captor for legal status, particularly regarding being considered Muslim for funeral prayer. The text presents multiple positions: al-Hidayah and Qadi Khan prioritize the abode, while al-Muhit prioritizes the hand when one parent is absent. The commentator sides with the abode priority, citing a case where a dhimmi steals a child and brings him to Dar al-Islam, where the child becomes Muslim by abode. There is also discussion on whether the child must be rational; Ibn al-Humam restricts the ruling to non-rational children, as rational children can independently choose Islam. The passage concludes that tawba only applies to worldly rulings, and the ultimate fate in the afterlife is a matter of ikhtilaf. The ruling is Zahir al-Riwayah where agreement exists, but the order of priority between dar and yad is a later ikhtilaf with the mufta bihi being the abode priority.

تبعية (taba'iyyah): The legal following of a child to a parent, captor, or abode for determining his or her religious status in worldly matters.
سبي (sabi): A captive or the act of taking captives; here specifically a child taken as a captive of war.
