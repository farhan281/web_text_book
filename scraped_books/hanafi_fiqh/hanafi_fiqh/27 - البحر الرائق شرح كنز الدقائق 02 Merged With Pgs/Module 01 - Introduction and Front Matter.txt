Introduction to Al-Bahr al-Raiq: The Three Layers of Hanafi Fiqh Commentary
Bibliographic introduction to the three-layer commentary structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three layers of text in a standard Hanafi fiqh commentary (matn, sharh, hashiya) and state the roles of each author.

Core Idea
A Hanafi fiqh text often has three layers: the matn (core), the sharh (commentary), and the hashiya (marginal refinements).

When you open a volume of Al-Bahr al-Raiq, you encounter not one but three texts stacked on the page. This layered structure is a hallmark of the Hanafi scholarly tradition. Understanding who wrote each layer and how they relate to each other is your first step into the deeper study of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn
Find the core text (matn) at the top of the page. In this text, the matn is 'Kanz al-Daqaiq' by Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi.
The matn is a concise legal manual.
2
Identify the sharh
Directly below the matn is the sharh (commentary). Here it is 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq' by Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym.
The sharh explains and expands on the matn.
3
Identify the hashiya
In the margins are the hashiya (marginal notes). These are 'Minhat al-Khaliq' by Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin.
The hashiya provides critical refinements and later scholarly updates.
4
Understand the layout
The editor places the matn at the top, the sharh below it, and the hashiya in the margins. This arrangement allows simultaneous reading of all three layers.
This layout is typical for printed editions of Hanafi commentaries.
5
Recognize chronological order
The authors lived at different times: al-Nasafi d.710 AH, Ibn Nujaym d.970 AH, and Ibn Abidin d.1252 AH. The hashiya often incorporates the final settled position of the school.
Later layers (hashiya) can override earlier ones in fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the book 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq Sharh Kanz al-Daqaiq', a foundational Hanafi fiqh commentary. It identifies three layers of text: the matn (core text) 'Kanz al-Daqaiq' by Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi (d. 710 AH), the sharh (commentary) 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq' by Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym (d. 970 AH), and the hashiya (marginal notes) 'Minhat al-Khaliq' by Ibn Abidin (d. 1252 AH). The copyright and publication details show that this edition was published by Dar al-Kutub al-Ilmiyyah in Beirut in 1997.

Key Terms
متن (matn): The core text, here 'Kanz al-Daqaiq', upon which the commentary is based.
شرح (sharh): An explanation or commentary on the matn, here 'Al-Bahr al-Raiq'.
حاشية (hāshiya): Marginal notes or supercommentary, here 'Minhat al-Khaliq' by Ibn Abidin.

Navigating the Table of Contents: Your Roadmap to Fiqh
Table of contents listing chapters on salat, janazah, and zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to navigate the table of contents of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq to locate specific fiqh chapters on salat and zakat.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap to the fiqh curriculum — learn to read it to find rulings quickly.

This lesson introduces you to the table of contents from volume 2 of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. Although it may seem like simple front matter, the TOC reveals the organization of the Hanafi fiqh tradition: first prayer, then funerals, then zakat. Understanding this structure helps you navigate the text and see the logical progression of topics. Let's explore the TOC together and learn how to use it as a study tool.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major books (kutub)
Scan the TOC for the word 'Kitab' (book). You will find three major books: Kitab al-Salah (Book of Prayer), Kitab al-Jana'iz (Book of Funerals), and Kitab al-Zakah (Book of Zakat). These are the main divisions of the volume.
The TOC may list some books twice, indicating separate sections or a scribal duplication.
2
List the chapters (abwab) under each book
Under each Kitab, note the chapters (abwab) that follow. For example, under Kitab al-Salah you see chapters on invalidators, witr, catching the prayer, making up missed prayers, etc.
The chapters are ordered from general to specific, beginning with invalidators and ending with prayer in the Kaaba.
3
Observe the logical sequence of topics
Notice how the topics progress: first the essential rules of prayer, then specific types (Friday, Eid, eclipse, fear), then funerals, then zakat. This reflects the typical Hanafi curriculum order.
Understanding this sequence helps you anticipate what comes next and see connections between chapters.
4
Check for repeated entries
You may see 'Kitab al-Jana'iz' and 'Kitab al-Zakah' listed twice. This could indicate that the TOC covers more than one volume or that there are separate sections on these topics.
Repeated entries may also be a scribal or printing error; cross-check with the actual text.
5
Apply the TOC as a navigation tool
Use the TOC to quickly find a specific ruling. For instance, if you need the chapter on Friday prayer, look under Kitab al-Salah after 'Bāb al-musāfir'. The TOC tells you the order.
Practice by asking: 'Where is the chapter on zakat on cows?' Answer under Kitab al-Zakah, near the end.
