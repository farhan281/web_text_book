No Qada for Breaking a Fast on Prohibited Days
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qada for prohibited-day fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on qada for breaking a fast on prohibited days and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
No qada is required for breaking a fast started on a prohibited day according to the relied-upon Hanafi view.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about whether qada (make-up) is required when someone starts fasting on a prohibited day (like Eid) and then breaks it. The disagreement hinges on the legal significance of the mere start of the fast versus the completion of an act, with the relied-upon position from Zahir al-Riwayah holding that no qada is needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is starting a fast on a prohibited day (e.g., Eid) and then breaking it. The dispute is whether qada (make-up) is required.
The ruling is specific to prohibited days; not applicable to regular optional fasts.
2
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa) and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that no qada is required. The basis is that the mere start of the fast makes one a 'sa'im' (faster), and since the day is prohibited, the act is invalid and must be ended, with no protection owed.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
State the opposing Sahibayn position and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (in al-Nawadir) hold that qada is required. They analogize to starting a prayer in a disliked time, where qada is required if broken, and see the start as binding like a vow.
This is a weaker transmission (nawadir).
4
Analyze the key distinction between fasting and prayer
The distinction is that in fasting, the mere start constitutes being a faster, thus violating the prohibition immediately. In prayer, one is not considered a 'musalli' until a rak'ah is completed, so the act is protected until then.
This distinction leads to different rulings on qada.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position and tarjih authority
The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's (no qada), as preferred by Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah. The opposing view is from nawadir and not given equal weight.
Tarjih is based on authority of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This is a commentary on the matn statement that if one starts a fast on a prohibited day (e.g., Eid) and then breaks it, no qada (make-up) is required. The Zahir al-Riwayah (the relied-upon transmission) supports this view of Abu Hanifa. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, in the Nawadir (rare transmissions), hold that qada is required because the start of the fast creates a binding obligation similar to a vow, analogous to starting a prayer in a disliked time. The difference is that in fasting, the mere start makes one a 'sa'im' (faster), thus violating the prohibition immediately, so it should be canceled and no qada is needed. In prayer, one is not considered a 'musalli' until completing at least one rak'ah, so the prayer is protected and qada is required if broken. The text also notes that Abu Hanifa has another view that no qada is required in prayer as well, but the first view (no qada in fasting) is the preferred one. Later commentators like Fath al-Qadir and al-Tahrir object that this reasoning implies that no qada is needed even if one cuts after the prostration, but the answer is absolute in obligation. The passage is cut off but concludes that the start is not valid because it has no benefit.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad in the six core books; considered the primary authority in the Hanafi school.
النوادر (al-nawadir): Rare or less authoritative transmissions within the Hanafi school, often from Abu Yusuf or Muhammad that contradict the Zahir al-Riwayah.

No Qada' for Breaking a Fast on Prohibited Days: The Hanafi Position
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qada' for breaking fast on prohibited days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada' when breaking a fast on prohibited days, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When fasting on a prohibited day, the prohibition cancels the obligation to make up the fast.

When a person begins a fast on a day that is prohibited for fasting (such as the two Eids), and then breaks it, is he required to make up that fast? There is a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school itself. The relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwayah) says no qada' is needed, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) in the Nawadir say it is. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind the dispute and clarifies the operative ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Zahir al-Riwayah)
Identify the ruling from the core books: no qada' is required when one begins a fast on a prohibited day and then breaks it.
This is the view of Abu Hanifa and is the relied-upon transmission.
2
Identify the opposing view and its proof
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) in al-Nawadir argue that qada' is required because commencement (shuru') is binding like a vow, analogous to commencing prayer in a disliked time.
3
Follow the opponent's analogy
Understand the qiyas: just as starting a prayer in a disliked time requires qada' if invalidated, so too starting a fast on a prohibited day should require qada'.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response and distinction
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: in fasting, one becomes a fasting person immediately upon intention, thus violating the prohibition instantly. Therefore, the fast must be invalidated and preservation is not required. In prayer, one does not become a performer until completing a rak'ah, so preservation is needed.
5
Determine the practical consequence and tarjih
The thamarah al-khilaf: if someone fasts on Eid and then breaks it, no qada' according to Abu Hanifa, but qada' according to the Sahibayn. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view as it is the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding qada' when one begins a fast on a day that is prohibited for fasting (like the two Eids) and then breaks it. The base text from Kanz al-Daqa'iq states that there is no qada'. The commentator expands by narrating from Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in al-Nawadir that qada' is obligatory because the commencement of the fast is binding like a vow, analogous to commencing a prayer in a disliked time. Abu Hanifa distinguishes: by commencing the fast, one becomes a fasting person and thus violates the prohibition, so the fast must be invalidated and no preservation is required; hence qada' is not built upon it. In contrast, for prayer, one does not become a prayer-performer until after one rak'ah, so preservation is obligatory and qada' is required if invalidated. The Zahir al-Riwayah (the relied-upon transmission) is Abu Hanifa's view that no qada' is required. The passage then addresses an objection from later scholars: if the prohibition is tahrimi, then there is a conflict between the obligation to continue (based on the Qur'anic verse prohibiting invalidation of deeds) and the prohibition of continuing. The resolution is that the prohibition of breaking takes precedence, so one must break the fast. The commentator concludes that the correct view is that the fast is invalid and no qada' is due, but the dislike is considered tanzihi in the end.

Key Terms
النوادر (al-Nawadir): Rare transmissions from the Hanafi imams, as opposed to the six core books of Zahir al-Riwayah.
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Mabsut, al-Jami' al-Saghir, etc.) which contain the established rulings of the madhhab.
الشروع ملزوم (al-shuru' malzum): The commencement of an act makes it binding, like a vow that must be fulfilled.

Fasting as a Condition for Vowed I'tikaf Only
Explains that fasting is a condition specifically for nadhr i'tikaf, not for sunnah or nafl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that fasting is a condition only for nadhr (vowed) i'tikaf, distinguish it from sunnah and nafl i'tikaf, and apply the ruling to vow scenarios.

Core Idea
Fasting is a condition only for vowed (nadhr) i'tikaf, not for sunnah or nafl i'tikaf.

The Hanafi school holds that fasting is required as a condition for the validity of one who has vowed to perform i'tikaf. However, for the sunnah i'tikaf of the last ten days of Ramadan or voluntary i'tikaf, fasting is not a legal condition, though it is customary. This distinction is crucial for understanding the limits of obligation in acts of worship and avoiding overburdening the believer beyond what the Shari'ah requires.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its specific ruling
Determine which type of i'tikaf is being discussed: nadhr (vowed), sunnah (last ten days of Ramadan), or nafl (voluntary). The text establishes that fasting is a condition (shart) only for nadhr i'tikaf.
The matn initially lists fasting as a condition, but the clarification shows it applies only to nadhr.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites a hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) made up his i'tikaf in Shawwal, outside Ramadan, implying that i'tikaf can be performed without fasting.
This is a da'i (weak) hadith according to some, but used by Hanafis as a support.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The fact that the Prophet performed i'tikaf in Shawwal without fasting indicates that fasting is not an inherent condition for i'tikaf. Combined with explicit statements of the scholars, it is confined to nadhr.
The hadith also shows that the Prophet abandoned his i'tikaf due to an excuse, not because of a ruling change.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (dispute)
The Shafi'i school holds that fasting is a condition for all types of i'tikaf. Understand their proof based on general commands and the practice of the Prophet.
The Hanafi response relies on the distinction between nadhr and other types, supported by the hadith and scholarly consensus.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preponderance)
The majority of Hanafi authorities (al-Sarakhsi, al-Tahawi, Qadikhan, and the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) affirm that fasting is a condition only for nadhr i'tikaf. This is the mufta bihi position.
The intra-Hanafi difference on whether the sunnah i'tikaf requires fasting is resolved by noting that it is habitual but not legally required.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author begins by citing a hadith about the Prophet's i'tikaf and his anger at the tents, which shows that he abandoned his i'tikaf due to an excuse and made it up in Shawwal. He then discusses the conditions of i'tikaf mentioned in the matn: staying (lubth) is the pillar, while mosque, fasting, and intention are conditions. However, he notes that including fasting as a condition is problematic because the matn also states that the minimum of voluntary i'tikaf is an hour, making it impossible for fasting to be a condition since fasting requires a full day. To resolve this, he explains that the matn's reference to fasting as a condition applies only to vowed (nadhr) i'tikaf, not to sunnah or nafl. He provides supporting evidence: scholars explicitly state that fasting is a condition only for nadhr, and they derive rulings such as the invalidity of vowing i'tikaf for a night (since fasting cannot occur at night). He also cites a difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding vowing a night with its day. Further branches include: if one vows i'tikaf for a day after having eaten, it is invalid; and if one is already fasting voluntarily and then vows i'tikaf for that day, it is invalid because the day is not completed. The author then addresses a potential objection: could the matn be referring to the sunnah i'tikaf of the last ten days, which always includes fasting? He responds that the scholars explicitly limit the condition of fasting to nadhr only, and the sunnah i'tikaf is habitually done with fasting but not legally required. He cites al-Durar's tripartite division of i'tikaf into wajib, sunnah, and mustahabb, stating that fasting is a condition only for the first. Finally, he discusses the case of vowing i'tikaf for a month: if one vows the month of Ramadan, the Ramadan fast suffices; if he fails to perform i'tikaf, he must make up a month with intended fasting. The passage clarifies the Hanafi position that fasting is a condition solely for nadhr i'tikaf.

Minimum Duration of Voluntary I'tikaf and the Debate on Fasting
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fasting condition for nafl i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum duration of voluntary i'tikaf, explain the Hanafi deduction that fasting is not a condition, and evaluate al-Kamal ibn al-Humam's rational objection.

Core Idea
Voluntary i'tikaf requires no fasting – its minimum is an hour, based on Imam Muhammad's explicit text, not rational speculation.

The Hanafi school specifies a minimum of one hour for voluntary (nafl) i'tikaf, derived from Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Asl. However, a later Hanafi authority, al-Kamal ibn al-Humam, challenged the deduction that fasting is not a condition, arguing that reason permits a condition longer than the conditioned. This lesson unpacks the proof, the objection, and why the majority view remains the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
Identify the minimum duration of voluntary i'tikaf and the school's stance on fasting as a condition. Note the source: Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Asl.
The minimum is 'an hour' (saa'ah) – a period of time, not necessarily sixty minutes.
2
Analyze the Deduction (Istinbat) of the Mashayikh
Understand how the early scholars deduced that fasting is not a condition. They relied on the text: 'If he enters the mosque with the intention of i'tikaf, he is in i'tikaf as long as he stays' – no mention of fasting. Also, nafl acts are based on leniency.
An example of leniency: permitting prayer while riding despite ability to dismount.
3
Examine al-Kamal's Rational Objection
Al-Kamal argues that reason does not forbid a condition (fasting) being longer than the conditioned (short i'tikaf). He claims the deduction is invalid because it relies on a non-sequitur.
His argument is aqli (rational) not naqli (textual).
4
Evaluate the Hanafi Response
The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq responds that the rational possibility does not override the explicit text and the consensus of the reliable mashayikh who affirm that the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah) does not require fasting.
Authoritative works like al-Mabsut, Sharh al-Tahawi, etc., support this.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf and Tarjih
The practical difference: if one performs nafl i'tikaf without fasting, it is valid per majority, invalid per al-Kamal. The mufta bihi is the majority view.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both are valid scholarly positions, but the school relies on the majority.

Ruling on a Mu'takif Leaving for Hajj or Human Needs
Intra-Hanafi dispute on exiting i'tikaf for needs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a mu'takif leaving the mosque during obligatory i'tikaf and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on post-need delays.

Core Idea
Obligatory i'tikaf prohibits all exits except for dire need or Hajj; even a small delay after a need invalidates i'tikaf according to Abu Hanifa, but not according to the Sahibayn.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the boundaries of a mu'takif's movement. It establishes that the prohibition of exiting applies only to obligatory i'tikaf, then discusses two key exceptions: assuming ihram for Hajj/Umrah, and exiting for a human need. The most nuanced part is the intra-Hanafi dispute over how long one may stay outside after fulfilling the need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Determine the baseline ruling: during obligatory i'tikaf, exiting the mosque is haram except for valid excuses.
Valid excuses include necessary human needs and, with conditions, Hajj/Umrah.
2
Analyze the Hajj exception
Understand the scenario where a mu'takif assumes ihram: he may complete i'tikaf first, but if Hajj is at risk, he abandons i'tikaf and restarts it later.
Hajj takes priority over i'tikaf because it is time-sensitive.
3
Examine the human need exception
Exiting purely for a human need (e.g., using the restroom) is allowed. The dispute arises over what happens after the need is fulfilled.
The text distinguishes between exiting for the need itself and staying after for other purposes.
4
Compare the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa holds that any delay after finishing the need invalidates i'tikaf. The Sahibayn hold that it does not invalidate unless the delay exceeds half a day.
This ikhtilaf is based on differing views on necessity (darura) and the contract of i'tikaf.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf: in a case where a mu'takif exits for a need and then briefly visits a sick person, Abu Hanifa declares i'tikaf invalid, while the Sahibayn deem it valid if the total time away is less than half a day.
The mufta bihi position varies; some later authorities prefer the Sahibayn's view for ease.

The Invalidation of I'tikaf by Exit Without Excuse: Rulings and Exceptions
Intra-Hanafi ruling on exit invalidating i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on when exiting i'tikaf invalidates it, distinguish between excuse removing sin and preventing invalidation, and apply the exceptions (minaret, necessities).

Core Idea
Any exit without excuse invalidates i'tikaf, even if the excuse removes sin.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on the basic rule that leaving i'tikaf without a valid excuse invalidates it. It presents the well-known dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (the Sahibayn) regarding the duration of exit, and then clarifies that many commonly perceived 'excuses' (such as attending a funeral, giving testimony, or escaping danger) do not prevent invalidation—they only remove sin. The text also addresses specific exceptions like climbing the minaret for the adhan and exiting for defecation or Jumu'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and the dispute
Read the opening sentence: 'If he goes out for an hour without an excuse, it becomes invalid.' Note that this is absolute—any duration. Then see the disagreement: Abu Hanifa holds any exit invalidates; the Sahibayn say only if more than half a day.
The author in al-Hidayah implies preference for the Sahibayn, but the muhaqqiq in Fath al-Qadir prefers Abu Hanifa.
2
Understand the concept of 'excuse'
The text defines 'excuse' as what commonly occurs (like the earlier mentioned places—defecation, urination, Jumu'ah). It explains that even valid excuses (e.g., forgetfulness, coercion) do not save the i'tikaf from invalidation; they only remove sin.
This is a key distinction: an excuse in fiqh may lift culpability but not the legal effect of invalidating the act.
3
Analyze the list of scenarios that still invalidate
The passage lists: exiting for collapse of mosque, dispersal of family, coercion by a tyrant, fear for property, attending a funeral (even if personally obligatory), general mobilization, giving testimony, illness, rescuing a drowning person or fire. All these are excuses that remove sin but still invalidate i'tikaf.
Contrary to the commentator al-Zayla'i and the author of al-Bada'i who differentiated, the preferred view holds all these as invalidating.
4
Examine the exception for minaret climbing
If the mu'adhdhin climbs the minaret whose door is inside the mosque, it does not invalidate. Even if the door is outside, the apparent riwaya says it does not invalidate. For a non-mu'adhdhin, the correct view is that it also does not invalidate because the exit is to establish a sunnah (the adhan) and the sunnah is performed in its place.
This is a nuanced exception based on the purpose of the exit.
5
Consolidate the practical rule
The mufta bihi (approved view) according to Fath al-Qadir is Abu Hanifa's position: any exit without a legally recognized exception invalidates i'tikaf. Excuses only mitigate sin. Therefore, a mu'takif should avoid all unnecessary exits.
The exceptions are limited: exits for defecation, urination, Jumu'ah, and climbing the minaret for adhan (especially for mu'adhdhin) do not invalidate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on exiting i'tikaf without excuse, apply the definition of 'udhr, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, any intentional exit without excuse invalidates i'tikaf; Sahibayn require more than half a day.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does a short, intentional exit from the mosque without a valid excuse break one's i'tikaf? The base text in Kanz al-Daqa'iq states that any exit of an hour invalidates, but the commentator of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the disagreement. Abu Hanifa holds the strict view, while his two disciples rely on istihsan to allow brief, necessary exits. We will trace the arguments, the definition of 'udhr, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the points of the ikhtilaf
List the two positions: Abu Hanifa invalidates any intentional exit without excuse; the Sahibayn require the exit to exceed half a day.
Note that the Sahibayn use istihsan as their basis.
2
Define 'udhr (excuse)
From the passage, determine what qualifies as 'udhr. It means matters that commonly occur, like using the restroom or Friday prayer. Not all needs are excuses.
The commentator explicitly restricts 'udhr to prevalent necessities.
3
Analyze the tarjih (preponderance)
Examine why the author of Fath al-Qadir prefers Abu Hanifa: necessity for leniency must be necessary or prevalent, which short exits are not.
Al-Hidayah suggests Sahibayn's view is stronger, but the muhaqqiq prefers Abu Hanifa.
4
Apply the ruling to specific cases
List the scenarios mentioned: collapse of mosque, family dispersal, tyrant forcing exit, fear for property, funeral, jihād, testimony, illness, saving drowning/fire. Determine which are excusable according to the correct position.
According to the preferred view, all these excuses remove sin but do they save i'tikaf? The passage says they are excuses that remove sin, but i'tikaf is invalidated unless the exit is for the specific excuses (urination/defecation/Friday).
5
Summarize the thamarah al-khilaf
State practical difference: a short exit for a permissible need (e.g., fetching water) would invalidate i'tikaf under Abu Hanifa but remain valid under Sahibayn.
This affects fatwa, as the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's position.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on leaving the mosque during i'tikaf without a valid excuse. The base text states that any exit of an hour without excuse invalidates i'tikaf. The commentator explains that this ruling is from Abu Hanifa, who considers any intentional exit invalid, regardless of duration. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), differ: they hold that i'tikaf is only invalidated if the exit exceeds half a day, based on istihsan (juristic preference) due to necessity in short exits. The text notes that al-Hidayah suggests the Sahibayn's view is preferred, but the author of Fath al-Qadir favors Abu Hanifa's position, arguing that the necessity relied upon for leniency must be either constant or predominant, which is not the case here. The commentator then clarifies that 'excuse' here refers to matters that commonly occur, such as using the restroom or attending Friday prayer. If 'excuse' were taken broadly, even forgetfulness or coercion would be considered excuses, but they are not; they invalidate i'tikaf. The passage then lists various scenarios where exiting is disputed: if the mosque collapses, family disperses, a tyrant forces exit, fear for property, attending a funeral (even if obligatory), a general call to arms, giving testimony, illness, rescuing someone from drowning or fire. The commentator criticizes the commentator al-Zayla'i for distinguishing between these cases, following al-Bada'i, but insists that all are valid excuses that remove sin. In some cases, it may even be obligatory to break i'tikaf, such as when a funeral prayer becomes personally obligatory. The passage concludes by citing al-Hakim's statement in al-Kafi that according to Abu Hanifa, only exiting for defecation, urination, or Friday prayer negates invalidity, which limits the scope of the excuse.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference used by the Sahibayn to allow short exits due to necessity.
عذر (udhr): An excuse that commonly occurs, like using the restroom or attending Friday prayer; it prevents invalidation.
فساد (fasād): Invalidity; the i'tikaf becomes void and must be restarted.

Climbing the Minaret for Adhan: An Excused Exit from I'tikaf
Ruling on climbing the minaret for adhan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on climbing the minaret for adhan during i'tikaf and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether it applies only to the mu'adhdhin.

Core Idea
Climbing the minaret for adhan does not invalidate i'tikaf for anyone who goes out to establish the sunna of prayer.

This lesson focuses on a specific practical situation: a person in i'tikaf who wishes to climb the minaret to give the call to prayer. The Hanafi school considers this a valid excuse that does not break i'tikaf, but there is an internal difference as to whether this applies only to the appointed mu'adhdhin or to anyone. Understanding this ruling is crucial for those performing i'tikaf, especially in mosques where minaret access is needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act in question
The act is climbing the minaret for the purpose of giving adhan. The key question is whether this constitutes an exit (khuruj) that invalidates i'tikaf.
2
State the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya)
According to the apparent narration, climbing the minaret does not invalidate i'tikaf regardless of whether the minaret door is inside or outside the mosque.
This is from Fatawa Qadi Khan and al-Walwaliji.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some earlier Hanafi scholars restricted this exemption to the mu'adhdhin alone, arguing that his exit for adhan is exempted by necessity. The correct view, however, extends it to everyone.
The passage states: 'The correct view is that this is the statement of everyone for everyone.'
4
Explain the tarjih (preferred view) and its basis
The preferred view is that anyone climbing the minaret to establish the sunna of prayer does not invalidate i'tikaf, because the sunna is performed in its place (the mosque context), so it is not considered a true exit.
The reasoning: 'because he goes out to establish the sunna of prayer, and its sunna is performed in its place, so it is not considered as going out.'

Commentary
This passage covers several distinct rulings related to what invalidates i'tikaf. First, it discusses climbing the minaret for the call to prayer (adhan). The relied-upon view is that whether the minaret door is inside or outside the mosque, going up for adhan does not invalidate i'tikaf, because the caller is fulfilling a sunna of prayer, and that sunna is performed in its place, so it is not considered a technical 'exit' that breaks i'tikaf. The same applies to anyone, not just the mu'adhdhin, as long as the purpose is to establish the sunna prayer.

The passage then mentions that there was an objection by al-Maqdisi regarding some earlier text about Friday prayer, but the details are not fully clear.

Next, the text explains that invalidity of i'tikaf only applies to obligatory i'tikaf (vowed or vowed similar to Ramadan). When i'tikaf becomes invalid, the person must make it up (qada) by fasting, unless the invalidation was due to apostasy. If the vow was for a specific month, only the invalidated days need to be made up, and the person does not need to restart the entire month. If the vow was for an unspecified month, the person must restart to maintain consecutiveness. The invalidators are categorized into three types: (1) actions without excuse (exit, intercourse, eating/drinking during day – except apostasy), (2) actions with excuse (illness requiring exit), and (3) no action by the person (menstruation, insanity, prolonged unconsciousness). For prolonged insanity, analogy would drop the requirement to make up, but istihsan requires it because there is no hardship in making up i'tikaf. The passage also clarifies that insulting, arguing, or intoxication at night do not invalidate i'tikaf.

Finally, the text addresses a specific case: a woman in i'tikaf who gets divorced. She may leave the mosque and return to her home to continue her i'tikaf there, according to one view. However, the judge (al-Qadi) suggests that this should invalidate her i'tikaf because the occurrence of divorce is not a common excuse. The passage also defines 'exit' technically: it is the separation of both feet from the mosque; merely sticking one's head out does not constitute exit, and does not violate an oath of not leaving the house.

Vowing a Month of Fasting: Consecutiveness Requirements
Ruling on vow of fasting a specified vs unspecified month and obligation of consecutiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the requirement of consecutiveness in fasting vows for specified vs unspecified months and for i'tikaf, and apply the Hanafi principle that the servant's vow is analogized to God's obligations.

Core Idea
Consecutiveness in fasting vows is not automatic; it depends on specification or explicit stipulation, whereas i'tikaf always requires it by nature.

When someone makes a vow (nadhr) to fast a month, the requirement of consecutiveness (tawābuʿ) hinges on two factors: whether the month is specified, and whether consecutiveness is explicitly stated. This lesson unpacks these distinctions and contrasts them with i'tikaf, where consecutiveness is inherent. The underlying reasoning, explained in al-Mabsut, analogizes a servant's vow to obligations imposed by Allah and considers the natural continuity of the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow and its types
Look at the wording of the vow. The two main types are: (1) specifying a particular month (e.g., Rajab), and (2) not specifying a month (absolute). The ruling on consecutiveness differs for each.
2
Determine the ruling on consecutiveness
For a specified month, consecutiveness is wajib (obligatory). For an unspecified month, consecutiveness is not required unless the person explicitly says 'consecutively' (متتابعاً).
This is a key distinction: the default is no consecutiveness in an absolute vow.
3
Analyze the consequence of breaking a day
If a day is broken in a specified-month vow, no restart (istiqbal) is needed; only qada (make-up) is required. But if the vow was unspecified with an explicit condition of consecutiveness, breaking a day requires restarting the entire month.
This mirrors the rule for God-imposed obligations like zihar and kaffarah.
4
Compare with i'tikaf
I'tikaf (spiritual retreat) always requires consecutiveness, regardless of whether the month is specified or not. Breaking it necessitates restarting.
The reason is that i'tikaf is continuous by nature (day and night), unlike fasting which only occurs during daylight.
5
Understand the legal reasoning (talil) from al-Mabsut
The servant's obligation (vow) is analogized to Allah's obligations. What Allah made consecutive requires restart if broken (e.g., zihar fast). The absolute in i'tikaf implies consecutiveness because i'tikaf is continuous; fasting is naturally discontinuous (no night fasting) so consecutiveness must be explicitly stated.
This qiyas (analogy) uses the illa (effective cause) of natural continuity vs discontinuity.

Vows for I'tikaf: Eid, Early Performance, Location, and Death
Expansion of rulings on nathr al-i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on vowing i'tikaf, including cases of Eid timing, early performance, location specification, and death after partial fulfillment.

Core Idea
A valid i'tikaf vow must be fulfilled with permissible fasting; location specification is irrelevant.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq expands on rulings from Kanz al-Daqa’iq regarding vows of i’tikaf. It addresses four key issues: vowing on Eid, performing i’tikaf before the appointed time, specifying a particular mosque, and the case of death after partial fulfillment. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi approach to nathr (vows) and the conditions for i’tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine whether the vow (nathr) is valid (mun'aqid). For i'tikaf on Eid, the vow is valid but fasting is haram, so it must be fulfilled on another day.
2
Distinguish between nathr and yamin
If the vow was intended as an oath (yamin), a kaffarah (expiation) may be required instead of makeup. The text says: 'and expiate for his oath if he intended an oath'.
3
Understand the effect of time specification
If one vows i'tikaf on a specific day or month, performing it before that time is permissible (ta'jil) because the cause (the vow) has already existed.
The commentator states there should be no disagreement on this.
4
Recognize location specification as irrelevant
Specifying a particular mosque (e.g., al-Masjid al-Haram) does not bind the fulfillment; i'tikaf in any mosque suffices.
5
Handle death after partial fulfillment
If one dies after performing part of a vowed month of i'tikaf, compensation (feeding the poor) is given on his behalf for the entire month, as per al-Fatawa al-Zahiriyyah.
