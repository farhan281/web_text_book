Ruling on Moving a Deceased Body (Naql al-Mayyit)
Expansion on the ruling of moving a deceased body with proofs from the Salaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on moving a deceased body for burial, distinguishing between short and long distances, and identify the evidentiary basis from the Salaf.

Core Idea
Moving a body long distances is makruh tahrimi; short moves are permissible.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on transporting a deceased body (naql al-mayyit) from its place of death to another location for burial. The passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a key narration from Aisha (RA) and precedents from the Prophets to establish a nuanced position: short-distance moves are permissible, while long-distance moves are sinful. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between the evidence for permissibility and the evidence for dislike.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being discussed? What is the hukm level (permissible, disliked, etc.) according to the Hanafi school? The passage addresses two scenarios: short-distance moves (mile or two) and long-distance moves (from one city to another).
2
Locate the dalil
What evidence does the author cite? Look at the narration from Aisha (RA) and the actions of Ya'qub, Musa, Yusuf (peace be upon them) and Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas. These form the proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does each piece of evidence support the ruling? Aisha's statement indicates dislike for moving a body; the actions of the Prophets indicate permissibility. The author reconciles by differentiating distance.
Aisha's statement is a qawl sahabi, which is a source of law in Hanafi usul, while the actions of previous prophets are cited as precedents but are not binding on this ummah.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Is there disagreement among scholars? The text implies that some early Hanafis considered moving a body absolutely permissible, while others disliked it. The author's position is that long-distance moves are sinful.
The ikhtilaf is intra-Hanafi; the position of the madhhab as mufta bihi is the one distinguishing distance.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is relied upon for fatwa? The author of al-Bahr al-Raiq inclines toward permissibility with sin for long distances, following the proofs. The operative ruling for fatwa today is that moving a body from one city to another is makruh tahrimi.
The tarjih authority is the later Hanafi master Ibn Abidin (al-Bahr al-Raiq).

Defining the Martyr (Shahid) in Hanafi Law: Worldly Ruling vs. Afterlife Reward
Definition and conditions of a shahid (martyr) in worldly rulings versus hereafter reward

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a shahid in the worldly sense (ahkam al-dunya) and a shahid in the afterlife reward, and apply the conditions to classify a given death scenario according to Hanafi law.

Core Idea
A shahid in worldly rulings is only one killed in battle or unjustly without diyah; others may get reward but not special burial.

The term 'shahid' carries both a legal ruling in this world and a promise of reward in the hereafter. In the Hanafi school, these two aspects are not identical. This lesson unpacks the precise conditions for a person to be considered a shahid in the worldly sense (exempt from washing, buried in his clothes) versus those who receive the reward of martyrdom but are treated like ordinary deceased. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion when applying funeral rites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Read the first line of the passage: 'He is the one whom the people of war... kill, or who is found in a battlefield with evidence, or whom a Muslim kills unjustly without blood money.' This is the legal condition for worldly shahid.
2
Understand the exclusion
Note the restriction: natural death, fall, burning, drowning, etc. do not make one a worldly shahid. The author explicitly states 'he is not a martyr in the worldly ruling'.
3
Contrast with hereafter shahid
The Prophet (SAW) testified that those who drown, burn, die of stomach disease, or die as strangers are martyrs in the sense of reward. This shows the two categories: worldly shahid (special burial) and hereafter shahid (reward but normal burial).
The hadith is cited as evidence for the broader category.
4
Apply to an edge case
Examine the case from al-Tajnīs: a fighter who accidentally kills himself is washed (not a worldly shahid) because the death is not attributed to the enemy, but he gets the reward because he intended to strike the enemy. This illustrates the principle that intention and causation matter.

Two Muslim Armies Fighting Mistakenly
Ruling on two Muslim groups fighting each other mistakenly

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing the dead of two Muslim groups fighting each other by mistake and explain the reason (illah) that distinguishes them from martyrs.

Core Idea
Dead mistakenly killed by fellow Muslims are washed because their killers did not wrong them — unlike martyrs.

This lesson examines a specific case where two Muslim detachments mistakenly fight each other, each believing the other to be polytheists. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani provides the ruling: no blood money (diyah), no expiation (kaffarah), but washing the dead is obligatory (wajib). We will parse the reasoning behind each component and see how this case fits within the broader Hanafi janazah jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and sub-rulings

The text states 'he did not mention the ruling on washing' but then says 'they must be washed'—this is the commentator's inference.

Locate the reasoning for each sub-ruling

Self-defense negates liability; lack of wrongdoing prevents the dead from being treated as martyrs (who are not washed).
3
Understand the illah for washing
The illah (effective cause) for washing is that the killer did not commit an injustice (zulm). This contrasts with a martyr who is killed justly by an enemy; thus a martyr is not washed. In this case, the mistaken context removes the 'wrongfulness' of the killing.
The ruling underscores that washing is the default for deceased Muslims; the martyr exception is based on the killer's just act.
4

The Hanafi position is stated as authoritative. However, other schools may treat such cases differently. Recognize that this is a mukhtalaf fih issue, though the passage does not elaborate opposing views.
Within the Hanafi school, the ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi.
5
Apply to similar scenarios
Consider other mistaken killings among Muslims where both parties are at fault equally. The same reasoning applies: no diyah/kaffarah for the survivors, but the dead are washed. Distinguish from cases where one party is clearly aggressing.
This is a limited case; if the killer acted intentionally, the ruling would differ.

Conditions for Martyrdom and Mudafi' Case
Explaining and expanding conditions for being a martyr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six conditions for martyrdom in Hanafi law and apply them to determine whether a defender (mudafi') qualifies as a martyr.

Core Idea
A defender killed while protecting life or property is a martyr, even if struck by a stone.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into the precise criteria for martyrdom in Hanafi jurisprudence. It not only enumerates the six essential conditions but also introduces the defender (mudafi') as a distinct category, clarifying that the weapon used does not affect martyrdom status. The author also addresses a critical objection about blood money, reinforcing the Hanafi stance through analogy to highway robbers.

Guided Path
1
List the six conditions for martyrdom
From the text, extract the conditions mentioned in al-Mujtaba and al-Bada'i: sanity, puberty, killed wrongfully, no financial compensation due, purity from major impurity, and no irtithath.
These conditions determine whether a person is treated as a martyr in funeral rites.
2
Understand exceptions with blood money
Analyze the cases where blood money is due (e.g., settlement, father killing son) yet the victim is still a martyr because the killing itself carried the possibility of retaliation.
Financial compensation that arises after the fact does not negate martyrdom if the killing originally warranted qisas.
3
Recognize the defender as a fourth cause
Note that the text adds the defender (mudafi') as a separate category, martyred regardless of the weapon used, unlike a Muslim killed wrongfully who requires an iron instrument.
The defender's killer is akin to a highway robber, removing the blood money condition.
4

The Murtathth: When a Wounded Soldier Must Be Washed
Expansion of the ruling on murtathth with indicators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a wounded soldier is considered murtathth and requires ghusl, and explain the difference between Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad regarding the effect of a will.

Core Idea
A wounded soldier who shows signs of life after battle is washed; the content of his will determines if he dies a martyr.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on the Hanafi criteria for distinguishing between a martyr (shahid) who is not washed and a murtathth (mortally wounded) who must be washed. The key indicators include moving, buying/selling, and making a will, with a notable disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad over the nature of the will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the root ruling
Recall the general rule: a battlefield martyr dies immediately from his wounds and is not washed. The murtathth is an exception who shows signs of life after the wound.
The burden of proof is on establishing that the soldier did not die immediately.
2
Examine the indicators of life
The passage lists three indicators: moving to another place, engaging in a transaction (buy/sell), and making a will. Each suggests the person was alive after the wound.
These indicators are based on rational inference (dalil aqli).
3
Analyze the dispute on the will
Abu Yusuf holds that a will concerning worldly matters shows attachment to life, making the person murtathth. Muhammad holds that a will concerning the hereafter shows despair of life, so the person remains a martyr.
The author reconciles: each applies to different contexts; there is no real conflict.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the will is about worldly matters, ghusl is wajib; if about the hereafter, ghusl is not performed.
The mufta bihi position accepts both depending on the will's content.
5
Consider additional nuances
Moving from the battlefield for fear of being trampled does not give comfort, so it does not make one murtathth. Also, rulings differ before and after the war ends.
These details show the granularity of Hanafi fiqh.

Conditions for Martyrdom: Place, Weapon, and Knowledge of Killer
Legal analysis of shahid conditions and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi criteria for determining whether a deceased person qualifies as a shahid who is not washed, and distinguish the exceptions for robbers, hadd, qisas, and highway robbery.

Core Idea
A shahid is not washed only if killed unjustly with an edged weapon by a known enemy in jihad.

In the Hanafi school, not every unjust killing confers the status of martyrdom (shahada). This lesson unpacks three critical factors—place, weapon, and knowledge of the killer—that determine whether the victim qualifies as a shahid for whom ghusl and janazah prayer are waived. We will also examine special cases such as killing by robbers, and rulings for executions by hadd and qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is washing and praying over the deceased; for a shahid, this obligation (fard kifayah) is dropped. Determine who is a shahid based on the three factors.
The default is that all deceased Muslims should be washed unless proven otherwise.
2
Analyze the Place and Weapon Conditions
Distinguish between city (mṣir) and desert (mafāza). In a city, if killed with an iron weapon and killer unknown, he is not a shahid due to qasama/diya. In a desert, if any weapon used and killer unknown, he is a shahid.
The passage restricts 'city' to settled areas including villages.
3
Determine the Importance of Knowing the Killer
If the killer is known and the weapon is iron, qisas is wajib, so the victim is a shahid. If the killer is unknown or the weapon is not iron, diya is wajib, so the victim is not a shahid.
This is the core reasoning: qisas (= martyrdom) vs. diya (= no martyrdom).
4
Examine the Exception for Robber Attacks
Robbers killing in the city at night or highway robbers outside the city – the victim is a shahid regardless of weapon because no property is left, so no diya obligation.
This exception overrides the general 'city + unknown killer' rule because the killer is known collectively.
5
Differentiate from Hadd/Qisas and Baghi/Qat' al-Tariq
Those executed by hadd (e.g., stoning) or qisas are washed (not martyrs), as shown by the Prophet washing Ma'iz. Those killed in rebellion (baghi) or highway robbery by the state are not washed, but they are not martyrs either.
The passage clarifies that these categories are not in the category of Uhud martyrs.

Commentary
This passage explains the conditions under which a person killed is considered a shahid (martyr) in the Hanafi school, focusing on three factors: place (city vs. desert), weapon (iron/edged vs. other), and knowledge of the killer. The matn states: 'or killed in the city and it is not known that he was killed with an iron weapon unjustly.' The commentator clarifies that 'unjustly' means the kill is not legally justified (like qisas or hadd). In a city, if the killer is unknown, qasama (oaths) and diya (blood money) are obligatory, so the victim is not a martyr because the obligation of monetary compensation lessens the gravity of the oppression. In a desert, there is no qasama or diya, so if a wound is found, the person is not washed (i.e., considered a martyr). The key distinction is whether qisas (retribution) applies: if the killer is known and the weapon is edged, qisas is due, making the victim a martyr; if unknown or the weapon is not edged, diya is due, and the victim is not a martyr. Abu Hanifa's position is clear: being killed with a non-edged weapon (e.g., a heavy object) and having a known killer means no martyrdom; being killed with an edged weapon but unknown killer also means no martyrdom. However, exceptions exist for those killed by robbers in certain contexts, as detailed in al-Bada'i'. Additionally, those executed by hadd (fixed punishment) or qisas are washed because they submitted to the law, and the Prophet himself washed Ma'iz. Those killed in rebellion (baghi) or highway robbery are not washed, as they are not martyrs. The commentary also warns about a practical scenario: if robbers kill someone in his house but flee, no qasama or diya is due because the killer is known collectively, even if individuals are not identified.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): A martyr who is killed unjustly in jihad with an edged weapon by a known enemy, for whom ghusl and prayer are not performed.
قسامة (qasama): An oath-taking procedure by the community when a dead body is found and the killer is unknown. It applies only in settled areas.
دية (diya): Blood money paid to the heirs of a victim of homicide, either as compensation or when qisas is not applicable.

Rikaz: The Obligation of Khums on Minerals and Treasure
Defines rikaz and explains the obligation of khums on minerals and treasure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of rikaz, explain the obligation of khums based on the hadith and qiyas, and identify the types of minerals and conditions that trigger the khums.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school obligates khums on both minerals and buried treasure, based on the hadith and analogy to booty.

Rikaz is a unique category in Islamic law covering wealth embedded in the earth. The Prophet (ﷺ) declared, 'In rikaz is the khums.' This lesson unpacks the Hanafi understanding of rikaz, the proof for khums, and the conditions for its application. We will also highlight how the Hanafi position differs from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Define rikaz linguistically and legally
Understand that rikaz literally means something fixed in the earth, and legally includes both mineral deposits (ma'din) and buried treasure (kanz). Note that Hanafis treat it as a univocal term applying to both.
The author refutes the claim that rikaz is only literal for minerals and metaphorical for treasure.
2
Identify the primary dalil (hadith) and its authority
The hadith 'In rikaz is the khums' is the primary proof. It is a hadith ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in fiqh. Hanafis derive wujub from the imperative implication.
The hadith is recorded in Bukhari, Muslim, and other collections.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command to pay khums on rikaz indicates an obligation. Hanafis hold that the default for commands is wujub unless there is evidence to the contrary.
The ruling is wajib, not fard, because the dalil is zanni.
4
Apply the secondary dalil (qiyas)
Rikaz is analogized to ghanimah (booty) because it is wealth acquired from disbelievers by force. Just as ghanimah requires khums, so does rikaz. However, the finder retains four-fifths due to actual possession.
This qiyas strengthens the obligation.
5
Note the conditions for khums
Khums is due when: i) the find is in non-owned kharaj or 'ushr land (or owned land per the preferred view), ii) the material is a solid that can be melted by fire (gold, silver, copper, iron, etc.), iii) the finder is a Muslim or dhimmi (harbi without permission gets nothing).
Liquids (oil, salt) and non-meltables (gypsum, gems) are excluded.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq, addresses the chapter of Rikaz (treasure trove/minerals). It opens with a definition: rikaz linguistically refers to something embedded in the earth, and legally it encompasses both mineral deposits (ma'din) and buried treasure (kanz). The author argues that the term is equally applicable to both, refuting the view that it is only literal for minerals and metaphorical for treasure. The central ruling is that khums (one-fifth) is obligatory on minerals that yield gold, silver, or similar metals that can be melted by fire, provided they are found in kharaj (tax) or tithe ('ushr) land. This is based on the prophetic hadith: 'In rikaz there is the khums.' The reasoning follows that since rikaz was previously in the hands of non-Muslims and came into Muslim possession by force, it is analogous to booty (ghanimah), which also requires khums. However, the finder retains four-fifths as a recognition of his actual possession, while the khums goes to the public treasury. The passage details that the finder can be free or slave, adult or child, Muslim or dhimmi, but a non-Muslim combatant (harbi) without the imam's permission gets nothing. Subsequent discussions cover cases of multiple finders, hired workers, and the permissibility for a needy finder to retain the khums for himself. The text also clarifies the restriction to non-owned land, meaning that if found in a person's property, the owner keeps four-fifths, but khums is still due according to a difference of opinion. The overall lesson synthesizes definitions, proofs, and practical rulings, adhering to the Hanafi school's methodology of integrating textual evidence (naqli) and rational analysis.

Key Terms
ركاز (rikaz): A legal term for items embedded in the earth, including both mineral deposits (ma'din) and buried treasure (kanz).
معدن (ma'din): Mineral deposits created by Allah in the earth, such as gold, silver, iron, etc., which are extracted by mining.
كنز (kanz): Buried treasure, humanly deposited wealth that is found.
خمس (khums): One-fifth; the obligatory share due on rikaz, resembling the share in booty.

Six Scenarios of Intention in a Vow and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Analyzes six intention scenarios and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the six cases of intention in a vow, state the ruling for each, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Intention determines whether a ambiguous vow becomes a vow, an oath, or both—and the Hanafi imams differ on combining them.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq analyzes the six possible intentions when someone utters a vow that could also be an oath. The default is vow; intention can shift it to oath or both. The key dispute is between Abu Hanifah and Muhammad (both apply) and Abu Yusuf (only one applies). Understanding this clarifies when qada' and/or kaffarah are obligatory.

Guided Path
1
List the six intentions
Identify the six cases: (1) no intention, (2) vow only, (3) vow negating oath, (4) oath negating vow, (5) both, (6) oath only.
The passage enumerates them explicitly.
2
Determine the default ruling
When no intention or only vow is intended, the utterance is a vow (nadhr) by default. When oath is intended and vow negated, it is an oath.
The literal meaning of nadhr prevails unless intention specifies otherwise.
3
Analyze the disputed case (both intended)
According to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, if both are intended, the utterance is both a vow and an oath. Abu Yusuf holds it is only a vow. This is the core intra-Hanafi dispute.
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether both qada' and kaffarah are wajib.
4
Trace the reasoning of each position
Abu Yusuf argues that 'nadhr' is literal and 'yamin' is metaphorical; a single word cannot combine both. Abu Hanifah and Muhammad argue no contradiction exists because both entail obligation—vow for itself, oath for the swearing—and they analogize to a gift with compensation.
The analogy (qiyas) with hibah bi shart al-'iwad is key.
5
Apply the practical consequence
If both intended (majority view), the person must perform qada' (from vow) and pay kaffarah (from oath) if he breaks it. If only one intended, only that obligation applies.
The mufta bihi is the view of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the six scenarios regarding the intention (niyyah) when someone utters a vow that could be interpreted as either a vow (nadhr) or an oath (yamin). The context is the earlier ruling: if one vows to fast on Yawm al-Nahr and intends an oath, he must both make up the fast and pay expiation. The author here explores the possible intentions and their legal effects. The six cases are: (1) He intends nothing specific: the utterance is considered a vow (nadhr) by default. (2) He intends only a vow: it is a vow. (3) He intends a vow and explicitly negates an oath: it is a vow. (4) He intends an oath and negates a vow: it is an oath. (5) He intends both a vow and an oath: Abu Hanifah and Muhammad say it is both, so he must both make up and pay expiation; Abu Yusuf says it is only a vow, so only makeup is required. (6) He intends only an oath (without specifying negation of vow): according to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, it is both; according to Abu Yusuf, it is only an oath. The reasoning of Abu Yusuf is that the word 'nadhr' has a literal meaning (vow) and 'yamin' is metaphorical. A single word cannot carry both literal and metaphorical meanings simultaneously, so the literal meaning prevails when both are intended. Abu Hanifah and Muhammad argue there is no contradiction because both entail obligation; the vow obligates the act for itself, the oath obligates it for something else (the swearing). They support this by analogy with a gift with a condition of compensation, which combines donation and compensation. The passage then includes a separate discussion about i'tikaf and its validity as a vowed act, which seems to be a response to an objection. Finally, there are remarks on the application: if he intended both, he must both make up (for the vow) and pay expiation (for the oath).

Key Terms
النذر (al-nadhr): A vow that obligates an act of worship for its own sake.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath that obligates an act or abstention due to swearing by Allah, with kaffarah upon breaking.
نية (niyyah): Intention that determines the legal nature of an ambiguous utterance.

Definition and conditions of i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of i'tikaf, identify its pillar and conditions, and apply the rules regarding permission for women and slaves.

Core Idea
I'tikaf's pillar is staying in a mosque with intention; purity from major impurity is for permissibility, not validity.

I'tikaf (spiritual retreat) is a highly recommended act in Ramadan, but its precise conditions and pillar are often misunderstood. In this passage, the author clearly distinguishes the linguistic origin, the legal definition, and the essential requirements. Pay close attention to which elements are pillars (arkan), which are conditions (shurut), and why purity from janabah is treated differently.

Distinguish the pillar (rukn) from conditions (shurut)

Intent is a condition, not a pillar.

Puberty, masculinity, and freedom are not conditions.

Analyze the permission scenarios for women and slaves

Ruling on Nights in I'tikaf Vows: Singular vs. Plural Wording
Expansion on linguistic implications of i'tikaf vows.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person vows i'tikaf for a day (singular) or for days (plural) and identify when nights are included.

Core Idea
In i'tikaf vows, dual or plural wording includes nights; singular does not.

The wording of an i'tikaf vow determines what is binding. The Hanafi school applies linguistic rules to decide whether nights are included. This lesson unpacks the rulings for singular, dual, and plural forms, along with the crucial role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wording used in the vow
Look at the exact expression: is it singular (e.g., 'a day'), dual (e.g., 'two days'), or plural (e.g., 'days' or 'a month')? The ruling changes accordingly.
Also note if the word is 'night' or 'day'.
2
Apply the linguistic rule for dual and plural
In Hanafi fiqh, dual forms are treated like plurals: both days and nights are included. If one vows 'two days', the two nights in between are binding.
This is based on the principle that al-muthanna ka al-jami'.
3
Analyze the singular form and the role of intention
If the vow is 'a day', only the daytime is binding unless the person explicitly intended the night as well. Vowing 'a night' alone is invalid because i'tikaf requires fasting during the day.
Intention can expand the scope only if the wording allows it.
4
Distinguish the intra-Hanafi dispute on vowing a night
Abu Yusuf held that vowing 'a night' with the intention of the day makes i'tikaf binding. The majority (Abu Hanifa, Muhammad) reject this because the word 'night' does not normally include the day.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.

Consecutiveness in I'tikaf: Ruling and Proof
Ruling on consecutiveness in i'tikaf vs fasting vows with qiyas proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on consecutiveness in i'tikaf and distinguish it from fasting vows by applying the principle of connected parts.

Core Idea
I'tikaf includes night and day, so it is consecutive by default; fasting does not, so consecutiveness must be specified.

In this lesson, we examine a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: whether a vowed act must be performed consecutively. The source passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that i'tikaf, because it spans both day and night, is presumed consecutive, while a vow to fast a month is not. The proof, drawn from al-Mabsut, uses qiyas to the obligations imposed by Allah. Understanding this ruling prevents common errors in fulfilling vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios for i'tikaf
Determine whether the i'tikaf includes nights. If it does, consecutiveness is required; if not, it may be split.
The default is that a vow of i'tikaf for a month includes both days and nights.
2
State the default ruling for i'tikaf and fasting
For i'tikaf with nights: wajib to perform consecutively. For a month-long fast: no default consecutiveness; only required if specified.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the qiyas proof
The proof analogizes to Allah's obligations (e.g., zihar) that are consecutive; if broken, restart is required. I'tikaf, being continuous, falls under this rule.
The illa is the continuity of the act across day and night.
4
Understand the difference between specified and unspecified months
If the month is specified (e.g., Rajab), breaking a day does not require restart—only qada. If unspecified, restart is required.
This applies to both fasting and i'tikaf after the rule of consecutiveness is established.
5
Apply the distinction in practice
When someone vows an i'tikaf, consecutiveness is always required if nights are included. For a fast, the vow must explicitly state 'consecutively' for it to be binding.
The burden of proof shifts based on the nature of the act.

Vowing I'tikaf on Eid Day: Validity and Compensation
Ruling on the validity of a vow to perform i'tikaf on Eid day

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vowing i'tikaf on Eid day and explain the requirements for makeup (qada) and expiation (kaffarah) when applicable.

Core Idea
A vow to perform i'tikaf on a day when fasting is forbidden is valid but must be fulfilled on a different permissible day.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies that vowing i'tikaf on Eid al-Fitr or Eid al-Adha does not invalidate the vow. Since i'tikaf requires fasting and fasting on Eid is prohibited, the vow creates a binding obligation that must be fulfilled at another time. The text also addresses that specifying a particular mosque in a vow is not binding, and that expediting the vow is permissible.

Guided Path
1

The act is vowing i'tikaf on the day of Eid. The ruling is that the vow is valid (mun'aqid) and binding.
2
Understand the reason for the prohibition
I'tikaf requires fasting, and fasting on Eid is haram. Therefore, performing i'tikaf on that day is not possible.
3
Determine the consequences
Since the vow is binding, the person must make up the i'tikaf on another day. If they performed it on Eid, it suffices but they have done wrong. Expiation may also be required if the vow was made as an oath.
4
Relate to expediting the vow
If one vows i'tikaf for a specific day or month, it is permissible to perform it before that time, as the cause for fulfillment exists.
5
Note the ruling on specifying a place
Designating a specific mosque, such as al-Masjid al-Haram, in a vow is not binding. Performing i'tikaf elsewhere fulfills the vow.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa holds Laylat al-Qadr rotates within Ramadan; the two students hold it is fixed. This changes the outcome of vows tied to that night.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi disagreement concerning the nature of Laylat al-Qadr. The dispute becomes practically significant when someone makes a conditional vow, such as freeing a slave on Laylat al-Qadr. Guided Path

State the dispute
The issue is whether Laylat al-Qadr rotates (tataqaddam wa tata'akhkhar) within Ramadan or remains fixed.

2
Examine the basis for each view
Abu Hanifa's view is based on the idea that the night 'rotates' (da'irah) as stated in al-Kafi. The students see no evidence for rotation.
3
Apply the dispute to a vow example
The text gives an example: a man says to his slave before Ramadan 'You are free on Laylat al-Qadr'. According to Abu Hanifa, the slave is freed at the end of Ramadan. According to the students, if said after one night of Ramadan, the slave is not freed until next Ramadan because the night could have passed.
4
Identify the fatwa
Al-Muhit states the fatwa is according to Abu Hanifa's view.
This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is the timing of emancipation or any other vow tied to Laylat al-Qadr depends on which view is followed.
