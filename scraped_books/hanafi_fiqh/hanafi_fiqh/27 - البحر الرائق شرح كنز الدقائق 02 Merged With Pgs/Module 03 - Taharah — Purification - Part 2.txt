The Guardian's Permission (Idhn) in Funeral Prayer
Explains the guardian's permission in funeral prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two meanings of the guardian's permission (idhn) in funeral prayer and evaluate the ruling on announcing death.

Core Idea
The guardian's permission covers leading the prayer and dismissing the congregation; announcing death is permissible if without wailing.

The text explains that the guardian's right to authorize others (idhn) has two aspects: allowing someone else to lead the prayer, and permitting the congregation to leave after the prayer before burial. It also addresses the controversial practice of announcing the deceased, distinguishing lawful notification from the pre-Islamic custom of wailing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The guardian's permission (idhn) regarding funeral prayer is being discussed. Recognize that this is a right belonging to the guardian.
2
Understand the two meanings
First meaning: permission for someone else to lead the prayer, because leading is the guardian's right and he can waive it. Second meaning: permission for people to leave after the prayer before burial, as a social courtesy.
The commentary also mentions a third meaning: announcing the death.
3
Analyze the ruling on announcing death
Some scholars disliked announcing death in the streets, comparing it to the pre-Islamic practice of wailing (na'i). However, the correct Hanafi view is that it is not makruh if it increases the congregation and serves as a reminder.
The karahah is only for the Jahili custom with wailing.
4
Note the disagreement on knowledge vs age
The text mentions that knowledge (alim) should take precedence over age in choosing the imam, citing al-Quduri's reasoning that giving precedence to a son over his father is disliked due to belittlement, implying obligation to prioritize the father.
Abu Yusuf held that the guardian can delegate because his guardianship remains.

Order of Relatives in Leading Funeral Prayer
Details priority rules for funeral prayer leadership among relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the priority order among relatives for leading funeral prayer according to the Hanafi school, and apply the rules to special cases such as absent guardians, sick persons, and slave owners.

Core Idea
The guardian's right to lead is based on closeness and respect; older and closer relatives take precedence, with exceptions for knowledge and ownership.

The passage details the priority among relatives when multiple are present, considering age, lineage, and special circumstances like absence or sickness. It also addresses the unique case of a mukatab (slave contracting freedom) and the owner's priority over a free son due to residual ownership.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
If someone other than the guardian or the Sultan prays over the deceased, the guardian must repeat the prayer; the other does not lead again.
The guardian's right is a personal right.
2
Apply the rule for multiple guardians
If two guardians each nominate a person, the one nominated by the older guardian takes precedence. This applies to sons and paternal uncles similarly.
Age is a factor when rights are equal.
3
Determine order among brothers
A younger uterine brother (same mother) is more entitled than an older agnatic brother (same father), as in inheritance.
Lineage through mother gives precedence in certain cases.
4
Handle absent guardians and special persons
An absent guardian's written permission can be blocked by an agnatic brother if the absence prevents timely attendance. A sick person in the city retains full authority to choose. For a deceased woman, the father is first, then the son; if the son is from the husband, the husband takes priority over the son but remains after the father.
Absence is defined as inability to attend in time.
5
Address cases of slave owners and mukatab
The owner of a slave is more entitled to pray over him than the slave's free son, due to residual ownership. For a mukatab (slave contracting freedom), if he dies insolvent, the owner is more entitled; if solvent, the son is more entitled.
Ownership creates a stronger connection in the context of funeral prayer.

Prayer on the Grave: Conditions and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on janazah prayer on a grave with conditions and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for performing the janazah prayer on a grave after burial and explain the Hanafi position regarding prior washing.

Core Idea
The funeral prayer on a grave is valid as long as the body is intact; prior washing is not required.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq provides the Hanafi ruling on performing the funeral prayer on a grave when the deceased was buried without it. The ruling is based on the Prophet's action and is subject to specific conditions. We will explore the primary evidence, the conditions for validity, and a notable intra-Hanafi dispute about prior washing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing janazah prayer on a grave after burial. The ruling is that it is wajib if the deceased was buried without prayer. The matn explicitly states: 'If buried without prayer, prayer is performed on his grave as long as he has not decomposed.'
The ruling is conditional on burial and non-decomposition.
2
Locate the primary evidence
The primary evidence is the hadith that the Prophet prayed on the grave of an Ansari woman. This is a sunnah ahad but with clear indication (dalala qati). The matn makes it absolute (mutlaq), applying to all cases regardless of prior washing.
The hadith is cited as 'أن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم صلى على قبر امرأة من الأنصار'.
3
Analyze the conditions
Two explicit conditions: the deceased must have been buried (soil poured on the grave) and the body must not have decomposed. There is also a dispute about whether prior washing is a condition. The majority holds it is not.
Decomposition is determined by predominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann), not a fixed time.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea

A hadith from Abu Dawud warns that whoever performs funeral prayer in a mosque has no reward. The Hanafi school takes this as a strong prohibition. Learning Objective

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Carrying the bier on four corners by hand is emphasized sunnah; other methods are disliked.

Honoring the deceased extends to the manner of carrying them to the grave. The Hanafi school prescribes a specific sunnah method that multiplies participants and ensures dignity. In this lesson, we examine the correct method and the actions that are considered makruh.

The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the precise conditions under which a Muslim killed by another Muslim is treated as a martyr, with special funeral rites: no washing, buried in bloodstained clothes. Understanding these conditions is crucial for distinguishing between cases that require full funeral rites and those that do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
What is the definition of a martyr killed by a Muslim? He is one killed unjustly, by a Muslim, in battle, with no blood money due, and a visible effect (athar).
This is the core definition from the matn.
2
Distinguish between direct and indirect causation
If the Muslims' action directly causes death (e.g., trampling mount, throwing from wall), the victim is a martyr. If the action severs attribution to the enemy (e.g., Muslim shoots into crowd and hits Muslim), he is not a martyr.
Key concept: 'attribution' (nisba) to the enemy.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa holds that severed attribution negates martyrdom; Abu Yusuf holds it does not. The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's.
This affects whether the deceased is washed or not.
4
Identify additional conditions
The killer must be a Muslim (not a disbeliever), the killing must be unjust (not by hadd or qisas), no blood money is due, and the 'effect' (athar) is strictly defined as a wound or blood from eyes/ears, not from natural orifices.
Blood money obligation automatically negates martyrdom.
5
Apply the condition of the battlefield
The killing must occur during actual battle (fi al-ma'raka). If found before encounter with the enemy, he is not a martyr; qasama and blood money apply.
This distinguishes battlefield martyrs from other killed persons.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is an expansion of the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq on the chapter of funerals, specifically the definition of a martyr (shahid). The author, Ibn Nujaym, explains the conditions for a person killed by a Muslim to be considered a martyr. The main ruling is that if a Muslim kills another Muslim unjustly in a battle-like situation, the deceased is a martyr provided no blood money is due and there is a visible sign of killing (athar). The Martyr's Washing and Prayer: Ruling and Proofs
Expansion on martyr's funeral rites and categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing, shrouding, and praying over the martyr, explain the evidence from the hadith of Uhud, and identify the additional category of the defender (mudafi').

Core Idea
A martyr is buried with his blood and clothes, not washed, but the funeral prayer is performed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the three funeral rites for the battlefield martyr: he is not washed, is shrouded, and is prayed over. The author provides scriptural proof from the Prophet's treatment of the Uhud martyrs. He also addresses a refinement: the defender killed with any weapon (not necessarily a blade) is also a martyr, and resolves a potential contradiction regarding blood money.

1
Identify the three actions
The matn states: 'He is shrouded and prayed over without washing.' Confirm which applies.
2
Examine the dalil for no washing
The hadith of Uhud: the Prophet ordered removal of iron and skins and burial with blood and clothes. This proves no washing.
The command to bury with blood implies no washing.
3
Analyze the rebuttal of al-Hasan's reasoning
Al-Sarakhsi argues that if wounds were the cause, tayammum would be prescribed, but it is not, so the hadith is the sole cause.
4
Explore the fourth cause of martyrdom: mudafi'
One killed defending property/self/dhimmi is a martyr regardless of weapon. This cannot be subsumed under 'killed by a Muslim unjustly' because that requires a blade.
Refer to al-Muhit.
5
Resolve the objection about blood money
The objection that killing with a non-blade mandates blood money is refuted by equating the killer to a highway robber, so no blood money.
Thus, the defender remains a martyr and is not washed.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on martyrs, addressing several issues. First: two Muslim groups mistakenly fight each other each thinking the other is disbelievers. Imam Muhammad says no blood money or expiation, and they must be washed because they were not killed unjustly (the killers were defending themselves). Fourth: the ruling on the martyr's funeral rites—no washing, shrouded, prayed over—is proven by the hadith of Uhud and the Prophet's prayer on Hamza. The author criticizes al-Hasan al-Basri's reasoning and prefers the hadith. Finally, the defender's case is discussed with a problematic comment from al-Nahr, which is resolved by equating such defenders to highway robbers, thus no blood money.

Core Idea
Martyrdom removes minor impurity by necessity, but not janabah—so a junub martyr must be washed.

One of the key rulings in the Book of Funerals is that a martyr (shahid) killed on the battlefield is not washed. However, this exemption has conditions. In this passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq, the commentator explains an important exception: a martyr who is in a state of major impurity (janabah) must be washed. Why the difference? The answer lies in the nature of the impurities and the ruling's underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is washing the body of a martyr. The general ruling is that a martyr is not washed, but the matn restricts this to one who is not junub. Identify the legal act and its default ruling.
2
Locate the restriction
The restriction is the word 'junub' (جنباً) in the matn. The commentator clarifies that if the martyr was killed in a state of minor impurity (hadath asghar), he is still not washed.
3
Understand the distinction between impurities
The commentator provides a rational proof (ta'lil): death inevitably involves loss of intellect, so minor impurity is always present. Therefore, martyrdom must remove it out of necessity. Janabah, however, is not necessarily present at death, so its obligation remains.
This is a unique Hanafi ta'lil based on necessity (darurah).
4
Apply the principle
Thus, a martyr in janabah must be washed because the cause (janabah) is still effective. A martyr in minor impurity is not washed because the impurity is removed by martyrdom.
5
Note the subsidiary issue
The author cites a case from al-Khabbaziyyah about a menstruating or postpartum woman killed in battle, reinforcing that the ruling depends on whether the impurity is established (3+ days of blood) or not.
This is a practical application of the principle.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, specifically the section on funerals, dealing with the conditions under which a martyr is exempted from the ritual washing (ghusl). The author explains that the basic ruling is that a martyr is not washed, but there are exceptions.

The commentary first discusses the restriction mentioned in the matn regarding a martyr being in a state of major impurity (junub). The author clarifies that if a martyr was in a state of minor impurity (hadath asghar), he is also not washed. The reason is that the state of minor impurity is inevitably present before death due to the loss of intellect, so martyrdom cancels it out of necessity. However, major impurity is not necessarily present at death, so its requirement of ghusl is not waived. Thus, a martyr who is junub must be washed, while one with minor impurity is not. The author then references al-Khabbaziyyah regarding a woman in postpartum or menstruation, stating that if she was killed while in postpartum, the ruling is general, but if she saw blood for only a day or two, she is not considered menstruating and thus not washed by consensus.

The passage then delves into the condition of irtithath, which is being carried from the battlefield alive and partaking in worldly acts. The author explains that irtithath means the martyr has obtained some worldly comfort or benefit, which nullifies his special status in worldly rulings, so he is washed. He quotes al-Bada'i defining the murtathth as one who leaves the state of the dead and enters a worldly state by having some worldly rulings applied or benefits reach him. Examples include eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving medical treatment, the passing of a prayer time while conscious, being moved from the battlefield (even out of fear of horses), or making a will. The author notes a difference of opinion between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding a will: Abu Yusuf holds that any will (worldly or otherworldly) makes him murtathth, while Muhammad distinguishes that a will concerning worldly matters makes him murtathth, but a will concerning afterlife matters does not, because the latter is an act of one who has despaired of life, resembling the actions of the dead. The author also mentions that a long will or speech is considered irtithath, but a brief one is not, with a possible harmonization.

Additionally, the text discusses the issue of a child or an insane person. It raises the question whether a child (who has no sins) is deserving of the honor of martyrdom and thus not washed. There is a dispute, and the text suggests that one who reaches puberty insane should be distinguished from one who became insane after puberty. The author notes that if an insane person continues in his insanity until death, he is not accountable for past sins, but if he had a chance to repent after sinning, the matter is under God's will. This is analogous to one who broke a fast due to an excuse and died without making it up.

Overall, the commentary provides detailed legal reasoning (ta'lil) for the conditions that remove the martyr's exemption from ghusl, emphasizing that any involvement with worldly life after receiving fatal wounds turns the martyr into a 'murtathth' who must be washed like an ordinary Muslim, though he still receives the reward of martyrdom in the hereafter.

The Meaning of Irtithath: When a Martyr Loses the Exemption from Washing
Defining irtithath and its indicators, with a focus on the will dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the indicators of irtithath and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding wills.

Core Idea
Any worldly act after injury—eating, drinking, moving, or making a worldly will—makes the martyr murtathth and thus washed.

The second major exception to the rule that martyrs are not washed is when the martyr engages in worldly life after receiving fatal wounds. The classical term for this is 'irtithath'. In this passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq, the commentator lists the indicators that nullify the martyr's special status and discusses a significant difference between Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad regarding wills. Understanding irtithath is crucial for correctly applying the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Define irtithath linguistically and legally
The author gives the linguistic origin (from rathth, meaning worn out) and the legal definition: the martyr obtains some worldly comfort or benefit, thus losing the worldly ruling of martyrdom.
The legal definition is from al-Bada'i: one who departs from the state of the dead and enters worldly affairs.
2
List the indicators of irtithath
The matn mentions: eating, drinking, sleeping, medical treatment, passing of a prayer time while conscious, being moved from the battlefield, and making a will. The commentator notes these are all signs of engaging with worldly life.
The text mentions that even a brief act like a short speech in a will may or may not count; length matters.
3
Analyze the dispute on wills
There is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad. Abu Yusuf holds that any will, worldly or otherworldly, indicates irtithath. Muhammad distinguishes: a will concerning worldly matters shows worldly engagement, but a will concerning the afterlife (e.g., bequests for shrouding, freeing slaves) is an act of the dying and does not nullify the exemption.
The commentator suggests the apparent view is that there is no real dispute; the difference is reconcilable by considering the content.
4
Apply the indicators to determine if washing is required
If any indicator is present, the martyr is murtathth and must be washed. If none, he retains the exemption.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, specifically the section on funerals, dealing with the conditions under which a martyr is exempted from the ritual washing (ghusl). The author explains that the basic ruling is that a martyr is not washed, but there are exceptions.

The commentary first discusses the restriction mentioned in the matn regarding a martyr being in a state of major impurity (junub). The author clarifies that if a martyr was in a state of minor impurity (hadath asghar), he is also not washed. The reason is that the state of minor impurity is inevitably present before death due to the loss of intellect, so martyrdom cancels it out of necessity. However, major impurity is not necessarily present at death, so its requirement of ghusl is not waived. Thus, a martyr who is junub must be washed, while one with minor impurity is not. The author then references al-Khabbaziyyah regarding a woman in postpartum or menstruation, stating that if she was killed while in postpartum, the ruling is general, but if she saw blood for only a day or two, she is not considered menstruating and thus not washed by consensus.

The passage then delves into the condition of irtithath, which is being carried from the battlefield alive and partaking in worldly acts. The author explains that irtithath means the martyr has obtained some worldly comfort or benefit, which nullifies his special status in worldly rulings, so he is washed. He quotes al-Bada'i defining the murtathth as one who leaves the state of the dead and enters a worldly state by having some worldly rulings applied or benefits reach him. Examples include eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving medical treatment, the passing of a prayer time while conscious, being moved from the battlefield (even out of fear of horses), or making a will. The author notes a difference of opinion between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding a will: Abu Yusuf holds that any will (worldly or otherworldly) makes him murtathth, while Muhammad distinguishes that a will concerning worldly matters makes him murtathth, but a will concerning afterlife matters does not, because the latter is an act of one who has despaired of life, resembling the actions of the dead. The author also mentions that a long will or speech is considered irtithath, but a brief one is not, with a possible harmonization.

Additionally, the text discusses the issue of a child or an insane person. It raises the question whether a child (who has no sins) is deserving of the honor of martyrdom and thus not washed. There is a dispute, and the text suggests that one who reaches puberty insane should be distinguished from one who became insane after puberty. The author notes that if an insane person continues in his insanity until death, he is not accountable for past sins, but if he had a chance to repent after sinning, the matter is under God's will. This is analogous to one who broke a fast due to an excuse and died without making it up.

Overall, the commentary provides detailed legal reasoning (ta'lil) for the conditions that remove the martyr's exemption from ghusl, emphasizing that any involvement with worldly life after receiving fatal wounds turns the martyr into a 'murtathth' who must be washed like an ordinary Muslim, though he still receives the reward of martyrdom in the hereafter.

Key Terms
الارتثاث (al-irtithath): The state of a wounded person who is carried from the battlefield alive and partakes in worldly acts, losing the special ruling of being unwashed.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A will made by the wounded person; its content determines whether it counts as irtithath.
المرافق الحياة (al-marafiq al-hayah): Worldly comforts or benefits that, if obtained after injury, nullify the martyr's exemption.

Special Cases: Child and Insane Martyrs and the Ruling on Moving from the Battlefield
Discussing child, insane, and moved-from-battlefield martyrs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the rulings for child and insane martyrs and explain the reasoning behind the ruling on moving from the battlefield.

Core Idea
A child martyr is not washed based on analogy to Uhud martyrs; an insane person after puberty may need washing for past sins.

The rules for martyrs are not uniform for all individuals. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq discusses special categories: children and the insane, and also clarifies the ruling for a martyr who is moved from the battlefield. The commentary shows how the Hanafi school applies its principles of analogy (qiyas) and necessity to these edge cases.

Guided Path
1
Understand the case of the child martyr
The text raises the question: is a child (who has no sins) deserving of the honor of not being washed? The analogy is made to the martyrs of Uhud, who were not washed because the sword purified them. A child has no sin, so the sword should suffice, but the text notes a dispute.
2
Analyze the case of the insane martyr
The commentary distinguishes between one who was born insane or became insane before puberty (not accountable) and one who became insane after sinning. In the latter case, if there was time to repent, the matter is under God's will; if not, perhaps no sin.
This is complex and involves the principle of accountability (taklif).
3
Examine the ruling on moving from the battlefield
If a wounded person is moved from the battlefield (even for safety), he is considered murtathth because the move increases his suffering and delays death, making his death less certain from the wound alone. Therefore, he is washed.
The text notes a rebuttal: some argue that moving can be a comfort, but the majority view is that it makes him murtathth.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq is a commentary (sharh) on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, specifically the section on funerals, dealing with the conditions under which a martyr is exempted from the ritual washing (ghusl). The author explains that the basic ruling is that a martyr is not washed, but there are exceptions.

The commentary first discusses the restriction mentioned in the matn regarding a martyr being in a state of major impurity (junub). The author clarifies that if a martyr was in a state of minor impurity (hadath asghar), he is also not washed. The reason is that the state of minor impurity is inevitably present before death due to the loss of intellect, so martyrdom cancels it out of necessity. However, major impurity is not necessarily present at death, so its requirement of ghusl is not waived. Thus, a martyr who is junub must be washed, while one with minor impurity is not. The author then references al-Khabbaziyyah regarding a woman in postpartum or menstruation, stating that if she was killed while in postpartum, the ruling is general, but if she saw blood for only a day or two, she is not considered menstruating and thus not washed by consensus.

The passage then delves into the condition of irtithath, which is being carried from the battlefield alive and partaking in worldly acts. The author explains that irtithath means the martyr has obtained some worldly comfort or benefit, which nullifies his special status in worldly rulings, so he is washed. He quotes al-Bada'i defining the murtathth as one who leaves the state of the dead and enters a worldly state by having some worldly rulings applied or benefits reach him. Examples include eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving medical treatment, the passing of a prayer time while conscious, being moved from the battlefield (even out of fear of horses), or making a will. The author notes a difference of opinion between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding a will: Abu Yusuf holds that any will (worldly or otherworldly) makes him murtathth, while Muhammad distinguishes that a will concerning worldly matters makes him murtathth, but a will concerning afterlife matters does not, because the latter is an act of one who has despaired of life, resembling the actions of the dead. The author also mentions that a long will or speech is considered irtithath, but a brief one is not, with a possible harmonization.

Additionally, the text discusses the issue of a child or an insane person. It raises the question whether a child (who has no sins) is deserving of the honor of martyrdom and thus not washed. There is a dispute, and the text suggests that one who reaches puberty insane should be distinguished from one who became insane after puberty. The author notes that if an insane person continues in his insanity until death, he is not accountable for past sins, but if he had a chance to repent after sinning, the matter is under God's will. This is analogous to one who broke a fast due to an excuse and died without making it up.

Overall, the commentary provides detailed legal reasoning (ta'lil) for the conditions that remove the martyr's exemption from ghusl, emphasizing that any involvement with worldly life after receiving fatal wounds turns the martyr into a 'murtathth' who must be washed like an ordinary Muslim, though he still receives the reward of martyrdom in the hereafter.

المجنون (al-majnun): An insane person; accountability for past sins determines whether he is washed.

Rulings on Rebels, Highway Robbers, and Other Categories Excluded from Funeral Rites
States fiqh rulings on who is not washed or prayed upon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing and praying over rebels and highway robbers, identify the distinction based on when they are killed, and apply the same ruling to people of faction and those who kill a parent.

Core Idea
Rebels and highway robbers killed in battle are not washed or prayed over; if killed after capture, funeral rites are given.

In this lesson, we explore a set of exceptional categories of deceased Muslims who are not given the full funeral rites of washing and prayer. The primary examples are rebels (bughat) and highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq), but the ruling extends to people of faction and those who kill a parent. The key nuance is the timing of death: those killed in the act of rebellion or robbery are denied rites, while those killed after capture may receive them. This distinction rests on a deeper purpose—punishment versus retaliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Read the opening sentence: Those killed for rebellion or highway robbery are not washed, and if not washed, no prayer is offered. This is the default rule.
The author uses the absolute pronoun 'those killed for rebellion or highway robbery'.
2
Analyze the proof for rebels
The proof is the action of Ali (RA) who did not pray over the Kharijites. Since no Companion objected, it is considered consensus (ijma).
The text directly cites this as the dalil.
3
Understand the detailed distinction
Al-Sadr al-Shahid distinguished between two scenarios: if killed during war (in the heat of battle) – no rites; if killed after capture – rites are given because it is retaliation (qisas) for highway robbers or to break the power of rebels.
This distinction is endorsed by senior scholars and is indicated by the phrase 'for rebellion' in the base ruling.
4
Extend the ruling to other categories
The text extends the ruling to those who overpower in the city with weapons at night, those who strangle repeatedly, people of faction (ahl al-asabiyya), and one who kills his parent. The rulings for these are similar: no funeral rites.
These extensions are based on analogy and public benefit.
5
State the consequence
The practical outcome: such deceased are not to be washed or prayed over, and this is a punishment and humiliation for their crime.
The underlying purpose is to deter such crimes.

Intra-Hanafi Disagreement on Praying over an Intentional Suicide
States intra-Hanafi disagreement on suicide.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on praying over an intentional suicide, and evaluate the textual evidence for each view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad pray over an intentional suicide; Abu Yusuf does not, and his view is supported by a hadith.

This lesson focuses on a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: whether to perform the funeral prayer for a person who intentionally commits suicide. While the standard ruling for a Muslim who dies is to pray over him, suicide raises a question of punishment. The text presents the disagreement between the two primary students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and their master Abu Hanifa, and provides the evidence each side relies upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the point of agreement
All agree that if the suicide is accidental (unintentional), the person is washed and prayed over without dispute. The disagreement is only over intentional suicide.

2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They say the funeral prayer should be performed for an intentional suicide because he is a sinner (fasiq) but not a spreader of corruption on earth. Thus he retains his Muslim status.

3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf says the prayer is not performed because the person is aggressive against himself (baghi 'ala nafsihi), analogous to rebels who are aggressive against others.
This view analogizes suicide to rebellion against oneself.
4

Abu Yusuf's position is supported by a hadith in Sahih Muslim: the Prophet (peace be upon him) did not pray over a man who killed himself with a sharp instrument.
This is a direct textual proof.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preference)
The text notes that the correction (tashih) differs: some say Abu Hanifa's view is asahh, others say Abu Yusuf's. The author leans toward Abu Yusuf's view being supported by the hadith, but leaves room for ikhtilaf.

Definition and Conditions of Sâ'imah for Zakat
Definition of sâ'imah with conditions, objections, and intra-Hanafi discussion on male herds and trade animals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sâ'imah and apply the conditions that determine whether zakat is due on grazing livestock.

Core Idea
Zakat on livestock requires grazing most of the year and intent for milk, offspring, or fattening—not for work, riding, or trade.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi legal definition of sâ'imah (grazing livestock) as explained in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. Understanding this definition is crucial because it determines which livestock are subject to zakat. We will explore the conditions of grazing duration, purpose, and the important distinction between different uses of animals, including an intra-Hanafi nuance on male herds.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the juristic definition of sâ'imah
Identify the core components: animals that suffice by grazing on mubah (ownerless) pasture for most of the year. Note that light feeding does not remove the status, but feeding for half the year does.
The Arabic root 's-w-m' relates to grazing. The juristic definition is more specific than the linguistic one.
2
Assess the condition of duration
Understand why grazing 'most of the year' is required. If animals are fed at home for half the year or more, they are not sâ'imah because doubt prevents obligation.
The principle: the cause (being sâ'imah) must be certain for the ruling to apply.
3
Examine the condition of purpose
Distinguish between animals for milk/offspring/fattening vs. for riding/work/trade. Only the former triggers zakat as sâ'imah; the latter either have no zakat or trade zakat.
The passage clarifies that trade animals have their own zakat rules, and mixing purposes affects the obligation.
4
Resolve the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on male herds
Understand the positions of al-Zayla'i (restricting to milk/offspring) and al-Bada'i'/al-Muhit (including males for increase). The author reconciles: the condition is to exclude work/riding, not milk/offspring, so males are included unless for meat.
This highlights the importance of intent (niyyah) in Hanafi fiqh.

He then explains that light supplementary feeding does not remove the status of being sâ'imah, and that if they are fed for half the year, they are not considered sâ'imah, so no zakat is due due to doubt. An objection from al-Nihayah is raised that the definition is broader (a'am) because it does not include the condition of intent (milk, offspring, fattening) and would include animals grazed for riding, which have no zakat. The author responds that this condition is omitted because it will be clarified later, and the later statements make it clear that animals for riding have no zakat, trade animals have trade zakat, and only those for milk and offspring have the zakat described in this chapter. Then the passage discusses the ruling from al-Muhit: if animals were bought for trade and later made sâ'imah, the zakat year starts anew from the time of making them sâ'imah, because the two types of zakat differ in amount and cause. The discussion then addresses an apparent contradiction: al-Zayla'i says sâ'imah are only those for milk and offspring (implying no zakat on all-male herds), while al-Bada'i' and al-Muhit say there is no difference between males and females. The author resolves this by stating that the condition is to negate riding/trade, not to require milk/offspring; males can be grazed for increase and fattening, so zakat is still due. However, al-Bada'i' says grazing for meat (fattening for slaughter) is like riding—no zakat. Al-Qunyah gives an example of working camels: if they work four months and graze the rest, the author suggests no zakat is due, but this is not a definitive ruling. Finally, the passage clarifies the word 'ra'y': it can be read with fatha (verbal noun: grazing) or kasra (the herbage itself). Here the appropriate meaning is the herbage (ra'y with kasra), since the animals graze on it. The herbage must be mubah (ownerless), as al-Shumnuni pointed out, because the word in language includes all plants, even those not permissible for grazing (e.g., on private land).

Key Terms
سائمة (sâ'imah): Grazing livestock that subsist on pasture for most of the year, with the intent of breeding, milk, fattening, or increase (not for riding, work, or trade).
الرعي (al-ra'y): With fatha: the act of grazing; with kasra: the herbage itself. Here the intended meaning is the herbage (kasra) because the animals feed on it.
الأسامة (al-isāmah): The act of sending livestock to pasture (grazing). It is the verbal noun from 'asāma'.

Ruling and Conditions of I'tikaf
Definition, ruling, and conditions of i'tikaf with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on i'tikaf, distinguish its levels (wajib, sunnah mu'akkadah, mustahabb), and identify the required conditions for its validity according to the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is sunnah originally, becomes wajib by vow; the Prophet's continuous practice proves its emphasis in the last ten days of Ramadan.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq provides the Hanafi fiqh of i'tikaf. It begins with linguistic and technical definitions, then states the ruling as sunnah mu'akkadah in the last ten days of Ramadan, resolving an apparent conflict among Hanafi authorities. The passage also lists the conditions for validity, including fasting and purity, with detailed discussions on the permissibility for women, slaves, and children.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is i'tikaf? The passage defines it linguistically and technically, stating its pillar (lubth) and conditions.
The rukn is staying in the mosque; conditions include intention, Islam, sanity, purity, and fasting.
2
Locate the ruling classification
The author states i'tikaf is sunnah, but explains a threefold division: wajib (by vow), sunnah mu'akkadah (last ten days of Ramadan), and mustahabb (other times).
Al-Quduri called it mustahabb, but al-Hidayah verified sunnah mu'akkadah.
3
Trace the dalil
The proof is the Prophet's continuous practice (muwazabah) without ever abandoning i'tikaf, as recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim, and the statement of al-Zuhri.
The dalil is Sunnah mutawatir in meaning (qati al-thubut).
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The continuous practice, coupled with the lack of rebuke from the Prophet towards those who did not do it, indicates sunniyah (recommendation) rather than wujub (obligation).
If it were obligatory, the Prophet would have commanded it, but he did not.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Within the Hanafi school, there is disagreement: some say i'tikaf becomes wajib by starting it (wujub bi al-shuru'), but this is weak. The relied-upon view is that it does not.
The weak view is based on requiring a specific time for voluntary i'tikaf, but the minimum is one hour.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq is an expansion (sharh) on the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq, covering the chapter of I'tikaf (seclusion in the mosque). The commentator begins with linguistic definitions, then provides the technical definition, followed by the ruling (hukm) and detailed conditions (shurut). The passage is a ruling with proof, as it provides evidence from the Prophet's practice. The author establishes that the fundamental ruling of i'tikaf is sunnah (recommended), but it can become wajib (obligatory) through a vow (nadhr). Within sunnah, there is a distinction: sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized) for the last ten days of Ramadan, and mustahabb (recommended) at other times. The conditions include: being in a mosque, intention (niyyah), fasting (sawm), Islam, sanity, and purity from major impurity (janabah), menstruation (hayd), and postpartum bleeding (nifas). Regarding purity, the commentator notes a difference: some require it for validity, while others only for permissibility. The passage also discusses the validity of i'tikaf for a minor child, woman, slave, and mukatab, highlighting the permission of the guardian or master. The proof for the sunnah status of i'tikaf in the last ten days is the continuous practice of the Prophet as recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim, and the statement of al-Zuhri that he never abandoned it from the time he entered Medina until his death. The commentator resolves apparent conflicts about the terminology (mustahabb vs. sunnah mu'akkadah) by explaining that al-Quduri used 'mustahabb' broadly to include the emphasized sunnah.

Key Terms
الاعتكاف (al-i'tikaf): Staying in the mosque with intention of worship, with the conditions of Islam, sanity, purity, and fasting.
السنية (al-sunniyah): The legal classification of i'tikaf as sunnah (recommended), which is the default ruling, and it becomes mu'akkadah in the last ten days of Ramadan.
الوجوب بالشروع (al-wujub bi al-shuru'): The view that once one begins a voluntary i'tikaf, it becomes obligatory to complete it, which is considered a weak position in the passage.
