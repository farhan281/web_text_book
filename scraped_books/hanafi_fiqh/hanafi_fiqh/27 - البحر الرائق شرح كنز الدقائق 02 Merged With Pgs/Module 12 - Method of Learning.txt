Hanafi Terminology: Ṣiḥḥah, Fasād, and Buṭlān in Worship vs. Transactions
Clarification of Hanafi terminology for sahih, fāsid, and bātil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ṣiḥḥah, fasād, and buṭlān according to the Hanafi school and distinguish their usage in acts of worship versus transactions.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, sahih and fasid are synonyms in worship but distinct in transactions.

This passage from al-Baḥr al-Rāʼiq clarifies a fundamental terminological distinction in Hanafi fiqh. In acts of worship (ʿibādāt), the terms ṣiḥḥah (validity) and fasād (corruption) are synonymous—both indicate that the act does not properly fulfill its conditions and pillars, triggering the need for repetition. In contracts and transactions (muʿāmalāt), however, they diverge: fasād refers to a defect that does not destroy the essence but requires rescission, while buṭlān means total nullity. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying Hanafi rulings in practical cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the domain
First, note that the passage explicitly differentiates between 'ibādāt' (worship) and mu'āmalāt (transactions). This is the key segmentation for the terminology.
The distinction is not universal in other schools; it is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
2
Define the terms in worship
From the passage, extract that saḥīḥ and fāsid in worship have one meaning: both indicate failure to fulfill conditions and pillars, requiring repetition. They are rational determinations, not positive legal rulings (aḥkām waḍ‘iyyah).
The author refutes the notion that these are shar‘ī rulings; rather, they are ‘aqlī (rational).
3
Define the terms in transactions
In transactions, fāsid is a contract valid in essence but with a defect that makes rescission (tafāsukh) required by law. Bāṭil is a contract that produces no legal effect at all. This is the standard Hanafi position, despite a textual ambiguity in the passage.
Al-Ramlī's commentary confirms the distinction: fāsid requires rescission, while bāṭil has no effect.
4
Understand the rational basis
The passage notes that these terms are rational (‘aqlī) judgments, not positive legal rulings (waḍ‘ī). This means they are determined by compliance with conditions and pillars, not by a separate divine command declaring 'this is valid/invalid'.
This is a point of uṣūl al-fiqh: the classification of an act as valid/invalid is based on its fulfillment of conditions, not on a separate ruling.
5
Apply the distinction
Given a scenario, identify whether it falls under worship or transactions, then apply the correct terminology. For example, a missed condition in prayer renders it fāsid (which is the same as bāṭil in worship), while a minor defect in a sale renders it fāsid (rescindable) but not bāṭil.
The practical consequence is that a fāsid sale may be rectified, whereas a bāṭil sale cannot.

الصحة، الفساد والبطلان في العبادات بمعنى واحد وهو عدم وهي عند الفقهاء اندفاع القضاء بالإتيان بالشرائط والأركان، وقد يظن أن الصحة والفساد في العبادات من أحكام الشرع الوضعية وقد أنكر ذلك. وإنما حكمنا به عقلي على ما عرف في تحرير الأصول بخلافهما في المعاملات فإن ترتب أثر المعاملة مطلوب التفاسخ شرعاً هو الفساد، قوله : (بخلافهما في (المعاملات قال الرملي : يعني الفساد والبطلان في المعاملات متساويان مطلوب التفاسخ هو الصحة وعدم ترتب الأثر أصلاً هو البطلان
Soundness, corruption and nullity in acts of worship are one meaning, and they are [the condition of] being not, and according to the jurists, they are the warding off of re‑doing by performing the conditions and pillars. It may be thought that validity and corruption in worship are from the positive rulings of the Law, but that is denied. Rather, we deem them rational as known in Tahrīr al-Usūl, contrary to them in transactions, for if the effect of a transaction is required to be rescinded by Law, that is corruption. His saying: ('contrary to them in transactions') – al-Ramlī said: he means that corruption and nullity in transactions are equivalent; what is required to be rescinded is validity, and the non‑occurrence of any effect at all is nullity.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the Hanafi position on three key terms: ṣiḥḥah (validity), fasād (corruption), and buṭlān (nullity). In acts of worship (ʿibādāt), ṣiḥḥah and fasād are synonymous: a valid act is one that fulfills its conditions and pillars so as to discharge the obligation, and a corrupted act is one that fails to do so; both terms indicate the same reality of non‑fulfillment. In transactions (muʿāmalāt), however, they differ: fasād applies to a contract that is valid in essence but has a defect that makes it rescindable (mutalābis al-tafāsukh), meaning the parties must undo it; buṭlān applies to a contract that never produced any legal effect. The author refutes the notion that ṣiḥḥah and fasād are sharʿī rulings; rather, they are rational (ʿaqlī) determinations based on compliance with conditions. This distinction is critical in Hanafi fiqh for understanding the different consequences of defective contracts versus invalid worship.

Key Terms
الصحة (al-ṣiḥḥah): In worship, it means the fulfillment of conditions and pillars such that the act discharges the obligation; synonymous with fasād in that both indicate non‑fulfillment.
الفساد (al-fasād): In worship, same as ṣiḥḥah; in transactions, it refers to a contract that is valid in essence but has a defect requiring rescission.
البطلان (al-buṭlān): In transactions, a contract that does not produce any legal effect at all, as if it never existed.
