Obligation of Nights in a Vow of Daytime I'tikaf
Ruling that vowing daytime i'tikaf also obligates the intervening nights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vowing daytime i'tikaf and explain how the nights are automatically included.

Core Idea
A vow of days for i'tikaf includes the nights; the Arabic collective usage binds both periods.

This lesson addresses a common point of confusion in i'tikaf vows. When someone vows to observe i'tikaf for a number of days, does that also obligate the nights? The Hanafi school, based on linguistic principles and Quranic precedent, rules that the nights are automatically included. Understanding this clarifies the actual obligation of the vow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the vow
Recognize that the act is a vow (nadhr) of i'tikaf, specifically mentioning a number of days (e.g., three days).
The vow uses the word 'days' collectively.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling is that the nights between the days are also obligated and must be spent in i'tikaf.
3
Explain the linguistic basis
In Arabic, when 'days' is used collectively, it normally includes the corresponding nights. For example, 'I have not seen you for days' means days and nights.
This is a rule of interpretation in vows.
4
Cite the Quranic evidence
The story of Zakariyya (AS) in Quran 19:10 mentions 'three nights' and implies the days were also included.
The verse: 'Your sign is that you will not speak to people for three nights, being sound.'
5
Note the exception
If the person intended to go to the mosque for learning hadith and not specifically for i'tikaf, the vow may not obligate i'tikaf.
The passage is cut off, but this nuance is indicated.

Commentary
This passage explains a rule about nadhr (vow) for i'tikaf. If someone vows to perform i'tikaf for a number of days (e.g., three days), the nights between those days are automatically included and must be spent in i'tikaf. This is based on linguistic usage: when Arabs say 'days' collectively, they often include the nights. Similarly, if one vows nights, the days are included. The commentator also mentions a nuance: if the person intended to go to the mosque for learning hadith and not specifically for i'tikaf, the vow may not apply. The passage cites the story of Prophet Zakariyya as evidence that mentioning one period includes the other.

Key Terms

أيام (ayyām): Days, but when used collectively can include the intervening nights.

Ruling on Praying Maghrib Before Muzdalifah: Necessity of Repetition
Ruling on delaying Maghrib to Muzdalifah and validity of early prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on praying Maghrib before Muzdalifah and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Maghrib prayed before Muzdalifah is invalid and must be repeated before dawn; after dawn it becomes valid.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific scenario during Hajj: a pilgrim who prays Maghrib on the way to Muzdalifah before reaching it. The Hanafi school distinguishes between validity (sihha) and legality (hill), and the ruling hinges on whether the obligation of order (tarteeb) has been dropped. We will explore the dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, the evidence, and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the difference of opinion
Clearly articulate the scenario: a pilgrim prays Maghrib before reaching Muzdalifah. Note that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it is not valid (la yujzihi) and must be repeated before dawn, while Abu Yusuf says it suffices (yujzihi) but is disliked.
The term 'la yujzihi' is key; it means it does not fulfill the obligation.
2
Identify the key terms: jawaz, i'adah, tarteeb
Understand that 'jawaz' can mean permissibility or validity; here the mashayikh use it to mean legality (hill). 'I'adah' is repetition; 'tarteeb' is the obligatory order of prayers.
The text clarifies that 'la yujzihi' does not mean the prayer is completely void but rather that it is not legally sufficient.
3
Understand the dalil: the hadith of combining prayers at Muzdalifah
The hadith indicates that Maghrib is to be delayed until the time of Isha at Muzdalifah for combination. This is a zanni (speculative) evidence that specifies a temporary change in the time of Maghrib for that night.
The hadith is a khabar wahid but is considered mashhur and acted upon by the early community.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues that since the prayer was performed in its original time, it should be valid. The Hanafi response is that the invalidity is suspended; if dawn passes without repetition, the prayer becomes valid because the condition of order is dropped.
This is analogous to the case of one who prays Zuhr while remembering a missed Zuhr – the later prayer is invalid due to the obligation of order.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require repetition before dawn; Abu Yusuf says it suffices without repetition but is disliked. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
This is the view in al-Hidayah and supported by the majority of later Hanafi scholars.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq deals with the ruling on someone who prays Maghrib on the way to Muzdalifah before reaching it. According to the relied-upon Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad), the Maghrib prayer is not valid and must be repeated later at Muzdalifah with the intention of ada (timely performance) as long as dawn has not risen. This is because the hadith indicates that the time for Maghrib on this night is delayed until Isha so that both prayers can be combined at Muzdalifah. The term used here is 'la yujzihi' (it does not satisfy the obligation), which some scholars interpret as 'invalid' but the text clarifies it means 'impermissible' (not legally allowed). If one does not repeat it until dawn, it becomes permissible (i.e., valid as ada) because the obligation of tarteeb (order) is dropped after dawn. There is a dispute: Imam Abu Yusuf holds that it does suffice (yujzihi) and he has done something disliked. The author refutes this by analogy with the case of one who prays Zuhr while remembering a missed Zuhr—the later prayer is invalid due to the obligation of order. The passage also addresses objections about the hadith being a khabar wahid (single-narrator report) conflicting with the Qur'anic verse that prayer is timed. The answer is that the hadith is mashhoor (well-known) and acted upon by the early community, and that the Qur'anic verse does not specify exact times qat'an. The super-commentator (al-Ramli) raises doubts about the meaning of 'jawaz' (permissibility) and whether it implies validity or legality, clarifying that it refers to legality, not validity. The final ruling is that if one prays Maghrib and Isha after passing Muzdalifah, it is valid, though this is against the majority view.

Key Terms
الجواز (al-jawaz): In this passage, it denotes permissibility/validity in a legal sense, but the author distinguishes between 'sihha' (validity) and 'hill' (legality). 'Jawaz' can mean either; here it is clarified as 'hill' (legality), not 'sihha'.
الإعادة (al-i'adah): Repetition of a prayer that was not validly performed; it is obligatory here before dawn to make it ada (timely).
الترتيب (al-tarteeb): The obligation to perform prayers in their prescribed order. In this context, the order between Maghrib and Isha at Muzdalifah is discussed.
