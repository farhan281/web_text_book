Binding Effect of Verbal Slips in Vows: The Hadith of Three Matters
Establishing binding nature of verbal slips with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadith proof and apply the Hanafi rule that verbal slips in divorce, emancipation, marriage, and vows are binding.

Core Idea
What the tongue utters in these four matters binds, even if the heart intended otherwise.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses a critical principle: in certain legal acts, the spoken word overrides inner intent. The author cites a prophetic hadith and applies it to vows, divorce, and emancipation, supported by a case from al-Walwaliji's Fatawa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its scope
Note that the passage states verbal slips in vows, divorce, and emancipation are binding. Identify the three matters from the hadith: divorce, emancipation, and marriage; vows are analogized.
The rule applies to any utterance of these specific legal acts, regardless of intent.
2
Locate the dalil (the hadith)
The author cites the hadith: 'Three things are serious in seriousness and in jest: divorce, emancipation, and marriage.' Identify its source (Sunan Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah) and classify it as a hadith ahad (zanni al-thubut).
The hadith is the primary proof for the Hanafi position.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit mention of 'jest' (hazl) and 'seriousness' (jidd) indicates that these matters are binding in both cases. The hadith covers all utterances, including slips. The Hanafi school extends this analogy to vows.
The key linguistic point is the inclusion of hazl, which negates any consideration of intent.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may differentiate between jest and seriousness in divorce, requiring intent for divorce to take effect. The Hanafi position holds the hadith as definitive, making intent irrelevant.
This ikhtilaf has practical consequences in real cases.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle that in legal matters such as divorce, emancipation (i'tāq), and vows, what is actually said takes effect regardless of the speaker's inner intention or whether he was joking. The author cites a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) that these three matters are binding whether said in seriousness or jest. Based on this, al-Walwaliji gives an example: if someone intends to vow fasting a day but mistakenly says 'a month,' he is bound to fast a month. Similarly, if he accidentally pronounces divorce or emancipation, it takes effect. The ruling is grounded in the idea that these actions have immediate legal consequences that cannot be reversed once uttered, unlike other statements where jest might be excused. This is a general Hanafi position, and the hadith serves as the primary proof (dalīl naqlī). The authority weight is from Fatawa al-Walwaliji, which is a later source but consistent with the founding texts.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or joking; statements made without serious intent.
الجد (al-jidd): Seriousness; statements made with full intent.
