Rulings on Permissible Images and Removing Them
States the permissibility of making images of inanimate objects and the ruling on erasing pictures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on images of trees and inanimate objects and explain when it is permissible to erase images found in another's house.

Core Idea
Images of trees and inanimate objects are permissible to make; erasing impermissible images in another's house is allowed.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between images of animate beings, which are forbidden, and images of inanimate objects, which are permissible. This lesson explores the ruling on making images of trees and things without a soul, as well as the permission to remove pictures seen in someone else's property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and note that it covers two separate rulings: (1) the permissibility of making images of trees and inanimate objects, and (2) the permissibility of erasing a picture in another person's house.
These are distinct rulings, but both relate to the theme of images.
2
Analyse the first ruling: permissible images
Note that the text explicitly allows making images of trees and things without a soul. It also clarifies that there is no distinction between fruit-bearing and non-fruit-bearing trees, and this is the majority view except Mujaahid.
Mujaahid's dissenting opinion on fruit-bearing trees is not adopted by the Hanafi school.
3
Analyse the second ruling: erasing images
The passage from al-Khulasa states that if one sees a picture in someone else's house, it is permissible to erase or change it. This implies a duty to remove impermissible images where possible.
This ruling applies to images that are unlawful, such as animate beings.
4
Apply the principles
Consider a scenario: you see a painting of a man on the wall of a friend's house. According to this lesson, you are permitted to erase it, even without the owner's explicit permission.
The permissibility to erase is derived from the general command to remove evil.

Commentary
This passage presents several rulings related to images and statues. It begins by stating that it is permissible to make images of trees and things without souls (i.e., inanimate objects). There is no distinction between fruit-bearing and non-fruit-bearing trees; all are permissible. This is the position of the majority of scholars, with the exception of Mujaahid, who disliked fruit-bearing trees. The text then cites al-Khulasa: if a person sees a picture in someone else's house, it is permissible for him to erase or change it, implying a duty to remove impermissible images. Next, from al-Nihaya, Muhammad al-Shaybani rules that a worker hired to make statues of men or to decorate them, with the dyes provided by the hirer, is not entitled to a wage because his work is a sin (ma'siyah) – making animate images is forbidden. Finally, from al-Tafariq: if a person demolishes a house that has images painted on it with dyes, he is liable for the value of the house and the dyes (since they are property), but not for the images themselves, as they have no value in Islamic law.

Key Terms
ما لا نفس له (ma la nafsa lahu): What has no soul, i.e., inanimate objects such as trees, mountains, etc.
تماثيل (tamathil): Statues or images of animate beings, specifically men, as mentioned in the context of hiring for making them.
محو (mahw): Erasing or removing an image.

Hire for Statue-Making and Liability for Demolishing Painted Houses
States rulings on hire for sinful work and liability for demolishing decorated property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a hire contract for sinful work is invalid and no wage is due, and determine liability when demolishing a house with pictures.

Core Idea
A hire for making statues of animate beings is void; demolishing a painted house requires compensation for property but not for images.

The Hanafi school rules that any contract for a sinful act is invalid, so a worker hired to make statues of men is not entitled to a wage. Additionally, if someone demolishes a house decorated with pictures, they are liable for the material value but not the images themselves. This lesson unpacks these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage presents two separate cases: (1) a hire contract for making or decorating statues of men, and (2) demolishing a house that has pictures painted with dyes.
Each ruling has different consequences.
2
Analyse the hire contract case
Imam Muhammad rules that the hired worker receives no wage because his work is a sin (ma'siyah). Making statues of men is haram, so the contract is invalid.
This follows the principle that contracts for prohibited acts are void.
3
Analyse the demolition case
If one demolishes a house with pictures, he is liable for the value of the house and the dyes (property), but not for the pictures because they have no value in Islamic law.
The images themselves are not considered property.
4
Apply the principles
Consider: A man hires someone to paint a mural of a man. After completion, he refuses to pay. According to this lesson, the painter is not entitled to any wage.
This is because the work was forbidden.
