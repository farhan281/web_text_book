Ruling and Timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr
Discussion on the legal status (wajib vs fard) and time of obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Sadaqat al-Fitr (wajib vs fard) and explain the evidence and reasoning behind the broad timing (muwassa').

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib, not fard, and its timing is broad—you can pay it anytime in your lifetime, not just on Eid.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on Sadaqat al-Fitr: its legal status and the time of obligation. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies why it is wajib rather than fard, and explains the dispute over whether it must be paid specifically on the day of Eid or anytime. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Sadaqat al-Fitr is identified as a charitable gift given at the end of Ramadan. The ruling is that it is obligatory (wajib), not fard.
Hanafi technical wujub means the obligation is established from zanni evidence.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary dalil is the hadith: 'The Messenger of Allah prescribed (farada) zakat al-fitr.' Additionally, there is ijma' on its obligation.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) and the ijma' is not transmitted tawatur.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
In Hanafi usul, the word 'farada' indicates a command, and a command from a zanni source yields wujub, not fard. Therefore, it is wajib and denial does not cause kufr.
Fard would require qat'i evidence (e.g., mutawatir hadith or Quranic text).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on timing
The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold that the obligation is broad (muwassa') and can be fulfilled anytime throughout life. Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad held it is restricted to the day of Fitr.
The basis for the broad view: the command is absolute (mutlaq) regarding time, so no restriction.
5
Evaluate the tarjih and identify mufta bihi
The majority view (broad timing) is corrected in al-Bada'i' and is the relied-upon position for fatwa. The obligation does not lapse if delayed past Eid.
Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad's view is considered a minority opinion within the school.

Chapter on Sadaqat al-Fitr
His statement: 'And al-fitr is an Islamic term...' Some scholars objected to him and said: Indeed, al-fitr in language is the opposite of... (2) Narrated by Abu Dawood in Kitab al-Zakat, Bab 23.

It is an Islamic term coined by the jurists, as if from al-fitrah meaning creation. And the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) commanded it in the year in which Ramadan was prescribed, before zakat on wealth was prescribed. He used to deliver a sermon two days before al-Fitr and command its payment. So in Sharh al-Niqayah. The discussion here is about its modality, quantity, condition, ruling, cause, pillar, time of obligation, and time of recommendation. The first is that it is obligatory (wajib) as in the book, and by this he intends the technical wajib according to us, even though the hadith states: 'The Messenger of Allah prescribed (farada) zakat al-fitr,' because its meaning is a command of obligation, and a command established by zanni evidence only indicates wujub. And the ijma' that it is obligatory is not qat'i to establish it as fard because it was not transmitted tawatur, and for this reason they said: whoever denies its obligation does not become a disbeliever. And they differed whether it is due immediately (ala al-fawr) or delayed (ala al-tarakhi)? Some said it is due in a restricted manner on the day of Fitr specifically; others said it is due broadly throughout one's life like zakat, and he corrected this in al-Bada'i' with the reasoning that the command to perform it is absolute regarding time, so there is no restriction except in fasting. He said in al-Qamus: 'Fitr of the fasting person means eating and drinking, like aftara.' And in the letter mim: 'Sawm is refraining from eating, drinking, and speech.' So look, what is the meaning of it being Islamic after its establishment in linguistic books? He replied: The intended meaning is that it is a shar'i reality that was made a name for the breaking of the fast, like salah, which only appeared in Islam even if it was used before. There is no doubt that in Islam it is used for every legally defined breaking of the fast, and that was not known before Islam, so it became Islamic. It is not meant that no one from the people of language spoke it, as the author's statement 'coined by the jurists' might suggest, because the Companions spoke it. And the phrase 'sadaqat al-fitr' occurs in several hadiths that he mentioned in al-Fath, including those the author will mention. And in al-Nahr: 'As for the word fitrah occurring in the speech of jurists and others, it is a coined term (muwallad) to the point that some considered it a popular expression (lahn al-'ammah).' So in Sharh al-Wiqayah. And the intended meaning is fitrah as a name for a specific charity; otherwise, the word fitrah in another sense is eloquent Arabic occurring in the Qur'an, as He said: 'The fitrah of Allah upon which He created people.' And in it that the author of al-Qamus said: 'al-Fitrah (with kasrah) is sadaqat al-fitr and the original disposition upon which the newborn is created in his mother's womb, and also religion.' And its apparent meaning is that it is Arabic in the intended sense here, but some objected to him as reported by Nuh Afandi, saying it is not, because that meaning (the specific charity on the day of Eid) is not known except from the Lawgiver, so the people of language are ignorant of it; how can it be attributed to them? So the author of al-Qamus mixed shar'i realities with linguistic realities, and this is frequent in his speech, and all of it is error that must be noted. And this supports what is in al-Nahr that it is muwallad. However, some transmitted from al-Mughrib that fitrah occurs in the speech of al-Shafi'i and others and it is correct linguistically, even though I did not find it in the sources I have. This is based on what we said that the intended meaning is the specific charity. But if we say it means 'creation' and we assume an idafah, i.e., charity of the creation, as some said in the sense of zakat of the body, then it is a linguistic reality definitively. His statement: 'And he corrected it in al-Bada'i'—I say: That is not explicitly stated in al-Bada'i'; rather it is understood from it. And the wording of al-Bada'i' is: 'As for the time of its performance, it is all of one's life according to the generality of our mashayikh, and it does not lapse by delaying past the day of Fitr. And al-Hasan ibn Ziyad said: The time of its performance is the day of Fitr from its beginning to its end; if one does not perform it until the day passes, it lapses, because this is a right known by the day of Fitr, so its performance is specific to it like the udhiyah. The evidence of the generality's view is that the command to perform it is absolute regarding time, so it is due in absolute time, and it is only specified...' Correct. And all of this is from him.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling and timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins by addressing a critique about the term 'fitr' being an Islamic term, then outlines the topics to be discussed. The first point is the ruling: it is wajib according to the Hanafis, not fard, because the hadith uses 'farada' but that is interpreted as a command, and since the evidence is zanni (ahad hadith), it yields wujub. The ijma' on its obligation is also not qat'i. Hence, denying it does not cause kufr. Then there is a difference of opinion on timing: some say it is due specifically on the day of Fitr (narrow), while others say it is due broadly throughout life like zakat. The author notes that al-Bada'i' corrects the broad view, using the reasoning that the command is unspecified in time. Then there is a linguistic discussion about the word 'fitrah' (with kasrah) being a coined term or Arabic. The passage concludes by stating the majority view that the time for performance is one's entire lifetime and does not expire on the day of Fitr, while al-Hasan ibn Ziyad held it is restricted to the day. The majority's evidence is that the command is absolute.

Key Terms
صدقة الفطر (sadaqat al-fitr): A charitable gift given at the end of Ramadan, required for every free Muslim who owns a specific amount above his basic needs.
الفطر (al-fitr): The breaking of the fast; here it refers to the day of Eid al-Fitr, the occasion for this charity.
الوجوب (al-wujub): Obligation at the level of wajib, meaning it is required but based on zanni evidence; its denial does not cause kufr.
الفور والتراخي (al-fawr wa al-tarakhi): Immediate versus delayed obligation. Here it refers to whether Sadaqat al-Fitr must be performed on the day of Eid or can be delayed.

The Basis of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Person, Not Property
Establishes the core sabab/shart distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the cause (sabab) and condition (shart) of Sadaqat al-Fitr and apply the ruling that payment may precede ownership of nisab.

Core Idea
The person (ra’s) is the cause of obligation; the nisab is merely a condition.

The Hanafi school uniquely identifies the basis of Sadaqat al-Fitr as the individual Muslim (the head), not his property. This fundamental principle leads to several practical consequences, including the permissibility of hastening payment before acquiring the required wealth threshold. In this lesson, we analyze the text from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq that articulates this distinction and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
Who is obligated? The text states: every free Muslim with a nisab surplus beyond basic needs, paying for himself and his dependents.
2
Locate the sabab/shart distinction
The addition 'al-Fitr' to 'Sadaqah' is an addition of the thing to its condition (shart); the real cause (sabab) is the head (ra's).
Key phrase: 'al-haqiqatu idafatu al-hukmi ila sababihi wa huwa al-ra's'.
3
Understand the proof: tada'ud bi tada'ud al-ra's
The obligation multiplies with multiple heads, confirming the cause is the person, not property.
4
Apply to the ruling on hastening
Because the cause is the head, one may pay Sadaqat al-Fitr before owning the nisab; the condition can be fulfilled later.
Text: 'law 'ajjala sadaqata al-fitri qabla milki al-nisabi...'

The obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr depends on having a surplus nisab beyond basic necessities. Its pillar is the actual delivery to the recipient, so it is ownership-transfer like zakat, so it is not performed by food of mere permission. Its ruling is acquitting the obligation in this world and attaining reward in the afterlife. The addition [in 'Sadaqat al-Fitr'] is of the addition of a thing to its condition, which is metaphorical, because the reality is addition of the ruling to its cause, which is the head, evidenced by multiplicity with multiplicity of heads. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school on the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It first states the ruling: it is obligatory on every free Muslim who has a nisab (minimum surplus wealth) beyond basic necessities like home, clothes, furniture, horse, weapon, and slaves. The obligation is for oneself, one's poor child, one's servant slave, mudabbar, and umm walad, but not for the wife. The time of obligation is the end of Ramadan (last day of fasting). The author then defends that this is a time-restricted obligation (mu'aqqat) by the hadith 'Make them independent of asking in this day', meaning the day of Eid, and if missed, it becomes a make-up (qada). The second part elaborates conditions: the nisab must be surplus over essential needs, including dependents' needs, and it is not required to be growing like zakat. The basis is the head (ra's), not property, so if one hastens to pay before owning nisab, it is valid if the head is present. The passage also discusses intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa obligates it on the guardian for the child and insane person, while Muhammad disagrees for the minor and insane. The relied-upon position is that it is obligatory on the guardian's wealth for those under his care. There is also a discussion about the cause (sabab) being the head with full guardianship and maintenance obligation, excluding those for whom maintenance is not obligatory (like wife and adult child). The passage ends with a debate about whether the hadith 'Pay for those you support' implies guardianship or maintenance obligation, and concludes that the operative rule is based on maintenance responsibility.

Key Terms
المؤنة (al-mu'na): Maintenance; Sadaqat al-Fitr has the meaning of maintenance because it is due to another.
الولاية (al-wilaya): Guardianship; complete and absolute authority over the person, required to include someone in the obligation.

Obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Who Must Pay and the Role of Guardianship
Commentary on the ruling of Sadaqat al-Fitr for different dependents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions of maintenance and complete guardianship to determine for whom Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib, and distinguish the Hanafi position from that of other schools.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on the head for himself and those he both maintains and has complete guardianship over.

Sadaqat al-Fitr is a wajib charity due at the end of Ramadan. The Hanafi school bases its obligation on the hadith 'Pay for those whom you maintain' and adds the condition of complete guardianship. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks who is included: oneself, poor minor child, slave for service, mudabbar, umm walad; and who is excluded: wife, adult child, mukatab, jointly owned slave, etc. Understanding the condition of guardianship is key to applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause (sabab) of obligation
The cause is the person himself (the head), not possession of nisab. This means one may pay before owning nisab.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.
2
Extract the two conditions from the hadith and addition
The hadith 'Pay for those whom you maintain' indicates maintenance as a condition. The Hanafi school adds complete guardianship (wilayah kamilah) based on consensus and reasoning.
Both conditions must be present for the obligation to apply.
3
Apply the conditions to each category
For a poor minor child: both conditions present → wajib. For a wife: maintenance present but no complete guardianship (she is independent) → not wajib. For an adult child: no complete guardianship → not wajib unless insane.
The passage explicitly lists who is included and excluded.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on the insane adult child
Imam Muhammad is reported to hold that if a child becomes insane after puberty, the father is not obligated. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that the father is obligated regardless.
The dispute hinges on whether guardianship remains after insanity acquired after majority.
5
Examine the grandfather case and cross-madhhab difference
The apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) does not obligate the grandfather. A narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad obligates him. The Shafi'i school obligates for poor parents and adult children in one opinion, while Hanafi does not due to lack of complete guardianship.
This shows the practical impact of the condition of guardianship.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq concerning Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins by discussing the obligation of the executor (wasi) to pay Sadaqat al-Fitr from the property of a minor or insane person; if he does not, it must be paid after they become sane or reach puberty. The guardian (wali) also pays for slaves who are for service. The passage then delves into the underlying cause (sabab) for the obligation: it is the person himself (the head) and not the possession of nisab, as stated in al-Bazzaziyya. This means one can pay before owning the nisab because the obligation is tied to the person, not wealth. The commentary then explains the statement of the matn: 'for himself, his poor child, his slave for service, his mudabbar, and his umm walad; not for his wife, his adult child, his mukatab, or a slave belonging to both.' The key conditions are (1) the person must be maintained by the payer, and (2) the payer must have complete (absolute) guardianship over them. The hadith 'Pay for those whom you maintain' is the proof. The commentator notes that some later scholars objected to adding the condition of guardianship, arguing that it has no textual basis and is not consensus. However, the prevailing Hanafi position holds that both maintenance and full guardianship are required. For example, one does not pay for a foreign orphan because there is no guardianship, even though maintenance might be required. The disagreement with Imam Muhammad regarding an adult who becomes insane is noted: the relied-upon view is that the father must pay regardless of whether the insanity was before or after puberty. The passage also discusses the case of a grandfather and grandchild: the grandfather is not obligated for his grandchild in the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) because his guardianship is incomplete (having passed from the father), similar to an executor. However, a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad obligates it, and this is chosen by some. The commentator further explains that if a minor daughter is married and living with her husband, the father need not pay her Sadaqat al-Fitr because he no longer maintains her. For a child between two parents, both must pay a complete sadaqah. The child who is rich (owns nisab) must pay from his own wealth. A slave for trade is not covered by the master's obligation because it would lead to double taxation (al-thani), meaning the slave's value already attracts zakat. If a slave for trade is worth less than nisab and has no other zakatable wealth, his sadaqah is not due even without double taxation, because the cause for zakat (the value) exists but is insufficient. The passage concludes with discussions on the distinction between grandfather and executor, and on a slave whose usufruct is bequeathed to one person and the corpus to another, where the sadaqah is due on the owner of the corpus.

الولاية (al-Wilayah): Legal guardianship giving authority over a person. Here it means complete and absolute authority, such as a father over his minor child, or a master over his slave.
السبب (al-Sabab): The effective cause for the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr, stated as being the person himself (the head) rather than possession of nisab.

The Grandfather's Obligation for Sadaqat al-Fitr: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on grandfather's obligation for sadaqat al-fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two positions within the Hanafi school regarding the grandfather's obligation for sadaqat al-fitr and identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling) with its basis.

Core Idea
The grandfather's guardianship is deficient when transferred from the father, but the relied-upon view obligates him for sadaqat al-fitr.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when a grandfather's grandchildren have an absent or poor father, must the grandfather pay sadaqat al-fitr on their behalf? The apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) says no, citing deficient guardianship. However, al-Hasan ibn Ziyad's narration says yes, based on the grandfather's maintenance obligation. We will trace the arguments, the rebuttal, and see why the later preferred view (mufta bihi) adopts al-Hasan's position.

Guided Path
1
State the Issue
Clearly define the legal question: Is a grandfather obligated to pay sadaqat al-fitr for his needy grandchildren when the biological father is absent or poor?
This applies only when the grandfather has actual care of the grandchildren.
2
Present the Apparent Narration Position
Explain the position of zahir al-riwaya: the grandfather is not obligated. Identify the key reason: his guardianship (wilaya) is deficient because it transfers from the father, making him akin to a wasi (executor).
The analogy to a wasi is central; a wasi is not obligated for sadaqat al-fitr.
3
Present al-Hasan's Narration Position
Explain the position of al-Hasan ibn Ziyad: the grandfather is obligated. The basis is that the grandfather owes maintenance (nafaqa) to the grandchildren, which indicates complete guardianship, unlike a wasi.
Maintenance is a strong indicator of full guardianship in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Analyze the Rebuttal in the Text
The text offers a response defending the apparent narration: the transfer of guardianship itself creates deficiency, regardless of maintenance. Additionally, the analogy to a buyer of a slave (where guardianship is entirely new, not transferred) shows that transferred guardianship is inherently limited.
This rebuttal is important for understanding the reasoning behind the apparent narration.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Recognize that the author of al-Ikhtiyar prefers al-Hasan's narration, making it the operative ruling for fatwa. Conclude that in binding practice, the grandfather is obligated.
Tarjih (preference) is based on the strength of the maintenance analogy and its consistency with the guardian's role.

Sadaqat al-Fitr for Slaves and Items Sold with Option
Rulings on animals, slaves, and items sold with option regarding fitrah obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for sadaqat al-fitr on slaves and apply the suspension rule for slaves sold with option.

Core Idea
A slave for service requires fitrah; a trade slave does not; fitrah is suspended when ownership is suspended by khiyar.

Sadaqat al-fitr is wajib on behalf of dependents, including slaves, but not all slaves are treated equally. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the distinction between slaves for service and slaves for trade, and introduces the crucial principle of 'thaniy' (double obligation). It also explains how khiyar (option) in a sale suspends the fitrah obligation, differing from maintenance (nafaqa) which must be paid immediately. Understanding these nuances helps avoid common errors in calculating fitrah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary condition for fitrah on a slave
The text states that fitrah is wajib for a slave owned for service, but not for a slave owned for trade. The reason given is 'thaniy' – avoiding double financial obligation (fitrah and zakat) on the same asset.
The rule is explicitly tied to the purpose of ownership.
2
List the exceptional cases where no fitrah is due
Runaway, usurped, captive slaves, and those without a specific owner (e.g., khums, booty) are exempt. Also, a slave bequeathed with usufruct: fitrah falls on the owner of the corpus, not the usufructuary.
These cases illustrate the principle that fitrah is tied to effective ownership and control.
3
Understand the suspension rule for mabi' bi khiyar
When a slave is sold with an option (khiyar), the obligation of fitrah is suspended until ownership is finalized. The text explains that ownership and authority are suspended, so what depends on them is also suspended.
This applies whether the option is for the buyer, seller, or both.
4
Distinguish fitrah from nafaqa in khiyar
Nafaqa (maintenance) is immediate and cannot be suspended because the dependent would starve. Hence, even if ownership is not yet final, the person who has possession (e.g., buyer in khiyar) must provide nafaqa. Fitrah, however, can wait.
This distinction is key: fitrah follows final ownership, nafaqa follows immediate need.
5
Apply the ruling to scenarios of delayed possession
If a slave is sold without khiyar but the buyer has not taken possession before Eid, the fitrah is suspended. If he later takes possession, he pays; if the sale is cancelled (e.g., due to defect), the seller pays; if the buyer dies before possession, no one pays.
This shows how fitrah is tied to stable, beneficial ownership.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the precise quantity of Sadaqat al-Fitr. The matn states: 'Half a sa' of wheat, or its flour, or its fine flour, or raisins, or a sa' of dates or barley, and it is eight ratls.' The commentator explains that this is the required amount, supported by the hadith from Sahihain that the Prophet (PBUH) obligated a sa' of dates or barley, and people later adopted two mudds (half a sa') of wheat. The commentary then discusses several nuances: (1) Flour and fine flour of wheat and barley are treated like their original grains; value is not considered for these because they are directly mentioned in texts, but precaution demands considering both quantity and value. (2) Bread is not permissible except by value because there is no textual prescription. (3) Raisins: there is a difference of narration—some say they are like wheat (half sa'), others like dates (a sa'). The preferred view is to consider both quantity and value. (4) The weight of the sa': The matn says it is 8 ratls. This is according to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf said it is 5 1/3 ratls using the Madinan ratl. The commentary explains that this is actually equivalent because the Madinan ratl is larger (30 ustars) than the Baghdadi ratl (20 ustars). The two weights are equal when converted. The commentator notes that Muhammad did not mention disagreement, implying it is the same. This resolution harmonizes the apparent conflict.

بر (burr): Wheat; half a sa' is required for Sadaqat al-Fitr if giving wheat or its derivatives.
رطل (ratl): A unit of weight. Baghdadi ratl = 20 ustars; Madinan ratl = 30 ustars. Used to determine the weight of the sa'.

Ruling on types and amounts of Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required types and amounts of Sadaqat al-Fitr according to the Hanafi school and explain the ruling on flour, sawiq, and raisins.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr: half a sa' of wheat/flour/sawiq/raisins, or a full sa' of dates/barley.

This lesson focuses on the specific food items prescribed for Sadaqat al-Fitr and their quantities. The Hanafi school distinguishes between items mentioned in the hadith and those derived by qiyas, each with its own measurement rule.

Identify the prescribed items
From the hadith: a sa' of dates or barley. The Companions substituted two mudds of wheat. The text also mentions flour, sawiq, and raisins.
Wheat (burr), dates (tamr), barley (sha'ir) are the core items.
2

Half a sa' for wheat, its flour, its sawiq, and raisins. A full sa' for dates and barley.
The sa' is defined as 8 Iraqi ratls later in the lesson.
3
Understand the treatment of flour and sawiq
Flour and sawiq are treated like their original grains: value is not considered because they are textually mentioned. However, caution suggests considering both quantity and value.
Bread is not included by text; only value is considered.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (a Hanafi commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq) covers three major aspects of Sadaqat al-Fitr: the types and amounts of food to be given, the time of obligation, and the ongoing dispute about the weight of a sa'.

The passage begins by specifying that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib in the amount of half a sa' of wheat (burr), or its flour or sawiq (parched barley flour), or raisins; or a full sa' of dates or barley. This is based on the hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim that the Prophet r obligated a sa' of dates or barley. The Companions later substituted two mudds of wheat. The text comments that flour and sawiq are treated like their original grains: value is not considered because these items are explicitly mentioned. However, for bread and other grains not specified in the texts, only value is considered. There is a difference of opinion on raisins: some Hanafi authorities treat raisins like wheat (half sa'), while others treat them like dates (full sa'), and the preferred view is to consider both quantity and value.

The text then discusses the time of obligation: 'Dawn of the day of Fitr' (subh yawm al-fitr). This means that Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at true dawn (fajr sadiq) on Eid al-Fitr. Anyone who dies before that dawn, or converts to Islam after it, or is born after it, is not obligated. According to Imam al-Shafi'i, the obligation begins at sunset of the last day of Ramadan. The Hanafi position is based on interpreting 'fitar' in the hadith as the particular break of fast on Eid morning, not the general daily iftar.

A major portion of the passage is devoted to the dispute about the weight of a sa'. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani say a sa' is 8 ratls (by the Iraqi ratl of 20 ustar). Abu Yusuf says it is 5 1/3 ratls (by the Madani ratl of 30 ustar). The text reports that some scholars see no real difference because 8 Iraqi ratls equal 5 1/3 Madani ratls. Time of Obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr
Ruling on the time of obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the time Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib and the practical implications of the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at dawn of Eid day, not at sunset of last Ramadan.

This lesson examines the timing of obligation. The Hanafi school holds that Sadaqat al-Fitr is due at subh sadiq on Eid, while Imam al-Shafi'i places the obligation at maghrib of the previous day. The difference has practical consequences for those who die before dawn.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi time
The text says: 'Dawn of the day of Fitr' (صبح يوم الفطر). This means the obligation begins at true dawn (fajr sadiq) on Eid al-Fitr.
The author uses the accusative as an adverb of time.
2
Consider the hadith interpretation
The phrase 'al-fitr' could mean the usual breaking of fast (iftaar) or the specific Eid breakfast. The Hanafi take it as the latter.
3
Present the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that obligation begins at sunset (maghrib) of the last day of Ramadan, because 'fitr' can also mean breaking the fast at iftar.
4
Analyze the thamarah (practical consequence)
If a person dies before dawn on Eid, Hanafis say no sadaqah is due, while Shafi'is say it becomes part of the estate.
5
Conclude with the preferred position
The Hanafi position is preferred (mufta bihi) because 'yawm al-fitr' clearly points to the day of Eid.
The text says 'wa rajjalna al-thani' (we preferred the second).

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (a Hanafi commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq) covers three major aspects of Sadaqat al-Fitr: the types and amounts of food to be given, the time of obligation, and the ongoing dispute about the weight of a sa'.

The passage begins by specifying that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib in the amount of half a sa' of wheat (burr), or its flour or sawiq (parched barley flour), or raisins; or a full sa' of dates or barley. This is based on the hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim that the Prophet r obligated a sa' of dates or barley. The Companions later substituted two mudds of wheat. The text comments that flour and sawiq are treated like their original grains: value is not considered because these items are explicitly mentioned. However, for bread and other grains not specified in the texts, only value is considered. There is a difference of opinion on raisins: some Hanafi authorities treat raisins like wheat (half sa'), while others treat them like dates (full sa'), and the preferred view is to consider both quantity and value.

The text then discusses the time of obligation: 'Dawn of the day of Fitr' (subh yawm al-fitr). This means that Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at true dawn (fajr sadiq) on Eid al-Fitr. Anyone who dies before that dawn, or converts to Islam after it, or is born after it, is not obligated. According to Imam al-Shafi'i, the obligation begins at sunset of the last day of Ramadan. The Hanafi position is based on interpreting 'fitar' in the hadith as the particular break of fast on Eid morning, not the general daily iftar.

However, the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq favours the view that the difference is real, and the sa' is measured by volume, not weight, as per the madhhab of Abu Hanifa. The fatwa is that one should give by volume unless certain of the weight corresponding to the required volume.

The commentary also addresses various scenarios involving slaves, dowries, and corrupt sales, as well as the permissibility of giving defective goods while making up the deficiency, and the preference between giving the specific food items or their cash value. The preferred fatwa is that cash value is superior because it better serves the poor's needs.

The Dispute over the Sa' Weight: Volume vs Weight
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sa' weight and measurement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on the weight of a sa' and evaluate whether the difference is real or apparent.

Core Idea
The sa' is 8 Iraqi ratls (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad) or 5.33 Madani ratls (Abu Yusuf), but the real difference is volume vs weight.

This lesson resolves the apparent contradiction between the Hanafi imams on the weight of a sa'. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it is 8 ratls by Iraqi measure; Abu Yusuf says 5 1/3 ratls by Madani measure. Some scholars say these are equivalent when converted, while others insist the difference is real and based on volume vs weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two numeric values
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: a sa' equals 8 Iraqi ratls. Abu Yusuf: a sa' equals 5 1/3 Madani ratls.
Iraqi ratl = 20 ustar; Madani ratl = 30 ustar.
2
Understand the different ratl standards
The Iraqi ratl is lighter: 20 ustar per ratl. The Madani ratl is heavier: 30 ustar per ratl. So 8 Iraqi ratls equal 160 ustar, while 5.33 Madani ratls equal 160 ustar as well (5.33 × 30 = 160). Thus, the two amounts are equal in weight.
This is the resolution argued by some scholars.
3
Explore the volume vs weight distinction
Abu Hanifa allows weight; Muhammad insists on volume (kayl) because the sa' is a measuring vessel. So even if the weight matches, if one gives by weight without knowing that the volume is correct, it may be invalid according to Muhammad.
The text mentions that according to Muhammad, if wheat is heavy, four ratls may not equal half a sa' by volume.
4
Consider the resolution whether the difference is real or apparent
Some say the difference is only apparent because the numbers convert equally. Others (including the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) say the difference is real because Abu Yusuf used volume and Abu Hanifa used weight.
The text in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq sides with the real difference view.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
The preferred position is to follow Abu Hanifa's view that weight is acceptable, but caution suggests using volume unless one is certain of the equivalence.
The fatwa allows giving the value (qimah) to avoid these measurement issues.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (a Hanafi commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq) covers three major aspects of Sadaqat al-Fitr: the types and amounts of food to be given, the time of obligation, and the ongoing dispute about the weight of a sa'.

The passage begins by specifying that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib in the amount of half a sa' of wheat (burr), or its flour or sawiq (parched barley flour), or raisins; or a full sa' of dates or barley. This is based on the hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim that the Prophet r obligated a sa' of dates or barley. The Companions later substituted two mudds of wheat. The text comments that flour and sawiq are treated like their original grains: value is not considered because these items are explicitly mentioned. However, for bread and other grains not specified in the texts, only value is considered. There is a difference of opinion on raisins: some Hanafi authorities treat raisins like wheat (half sa'), while others treat them like dates (full sa'), and the preferred view is to consider both quantity and value.

The text then discusses the time of obligation: 'Dawn of the day of Fitr' (subh yawm al-fitr). This means that Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at true dawn (fajr sadiq) on Eid al-Fitr. Anyone who dies before that dawn, or converts to Islam after it, or is born after it, is not obligated. According to Imam al-Shafi'i, the obligation begins at sunset of the last day of Ramadan. The Hanafi position is based on interpreting 'fitar' in the hadith as the particular break of fast on Eid morning, not the general daily iftar.

A major portion of the passage is devoted to the dispute about the weight of a sa'. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani say a sa' is 8 ratls (by the Iraqi ratl of 20 ustar). Abu Yusuf says it is 5 1/3 ratls (by the Madani ratl of 30 ustar). The text reports that some scholars see no real difference because 8 Iraqi ratls equal 5 1/3 Madani ratls. However, the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq favours the view that the difference is real, and the sa' is measured by volume, not weight, as per the madhhab of Abu Hanifa. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that volume is the criterion because the sa' is a measuring vessel. The fatwa is that one should give by volume unless certain of the weight corresponding to the required volume.

The commentary also addresses various scenarios involving slaves, dowries, and corrupt sales, as well as the permissibility of giving defective goods while making up the deficiency, and the preference between giving the specific food items or their cash value. The preferred fatwa is that cash value is superior because it better serves the poor's needs.

Key Terms
صاع (sa'): A unit of volume equivalent to 8 Iraqi ratls (approx. 3.06 kg) or 5 1/3 Madani ratls (same weight) according to the Hanafi school.
رطل (ratl): A unit of weight. Iraqi ratl = 20 ustar ≈ 406.25 g; Madani ratl = 30 ustar ≈ 609.375 g.
إستار (ustar): A unit of weight equal to 4.5 mithqals, approx 19.125 g.

Zakat al-Fitr: Timing and Early Payment
Commentary on timing of obligation, permissibility of early payment, and measurement of sa'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on when Zakat al-Fitr becomes wajib and explain the permissibility and limits of early payment based on the school's evidence and intra-school differences.

Core Idea
Zakat al-Fitr becomes wajib at dawn of Eid, not sunset; early payment is allowed from Ramadan.

The timing of Zakat al-Fitr's obligation is a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. This lesson unpacks that dispute, the Hanafi evidence, and the practical implications for when one must give. Additionally, the lesson clarifies the permissibility of giving before Eid, both in terms of the relied-upon view and the differences among Hanafi authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Zakat al-Fitr. What is the hukm level? Wajib (obligatory). The time of obligation is tied to dawn of Eid day.
The obligation arises at the break of dawn on the day of Eid al-Fitr.
2
Locate the dalil
The commentary cites the hadith 'Your fast is the day you fast, and your fitr is the day you break fast' (Tirmidhi) and the practice of Ibn 'Umar. Identify these as the primary evidence.
The hadith is classified as ahad, with zanni thubut and zanni dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the obligation at dawn? The term 'fitar' is interpreted as the specific day of Eid, not the daily breaking of fast. If it were daily, thirty fitras would be due. Therefore it refers to the day of Eid.
This logical inference is a key Hanafi argument.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Al-Shafi'i holds obligation at sunset of the last day of Ramadan, interpreting 'fitar' as the usual break of fast. The Hanafi response uses logic and the hadith to reject this.
The thamarah al-khilaf: death after sunset but before dawn – Hanafi says no obligation, Shafi'i says obligation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and early payment
The relied-upon position is that early payment is permissible from the beginning of Ramadan (mufta bihi), though some authorities allowed it absolutely. Delay after Eid does not make it qada but remains an expansive duty.
The fatwa is on the restriction to Ramadan as per Fatawa al-Zahiriya.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq on the chapter of Sadaqat al-Fitr from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It addresses two main issues: (1) the time of obligation and the permissibility of early or late payment, and (2) the measurement of the sa' in terms of weight versus volume, along with related intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab disputes. The author first states the ruling from the matn: if a person dies before the morning of Eid, or accepts Islam after that time, or a child is born after, Zakat al-Fitr is not obligatory. However, it is valid if one gives it early (before Eid) or delays it. The commentary then explains the timing: according to the Hanafi school, the obligation is tied to the dawn of Eid day, while Imam al-Shafi'i holds it becomes obligatory at sunset of the last day of Ramadan. The difference stems from interpreting the word 'fitar' (breaking fast) in the hadith of Ibn 'Umar – whether it refers to the daily break of fast (sunset) or the specific day of breaking the fast (Eid). The Hanafi school adopts the latter, supported by the hadith 'Your fast is the day you fast, and your fitr is the day you break fast.' The implication is that the obligation arises at fajr. Regarding early payment, it is permissible from the beginning of Ramadan according to the relied-upon view (mufta bihi), though there is some disagreement. The commentator cites various authorities and notes that the fatwa is upon the permissibility from Ramadan entry. Delay after Eid is also valid but does not become a make-up (qada) because it remains an expansive obligation like zakat. The second major topic is the measurement of the sa'. The text records an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa considers the sa' by weight, while Muhammad al-Shaybani considers it by measure (volume). Abu Yusuf permits weight if certainty exists, but his view is not the relied-upon. The disagreement is resolved by noting that for grains whose weight and volume are proportional (like lentils), eight ratls by Iraqi measure equal the sa'. The commentator clarifies that the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq left the sa' unrestricted regarding quality, but if poor quality or defective goods are given, the deficiency must be compensated. Regarding giving value (price) instead of grain, the fatwa supports it as better for the poor, though there is a difference of opinion. The passage also addresses mixing types of grains, which is permissible according to Hanafis but not according to al-Shafi'i who requires a single type. Finally, a note on the earlier disagreement about the sa' measure: some thought the difference among Hanafi scholars was merely in interpretation, but the correct view is that it is a real difference based on the Iraqi ratl.

Timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Preferred Time, Early and Late Payment
Explains definition, preferred time, and rulings on early/late payment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr, including the preferred time, permissibility of early and late payment, the intra-Hanafi dispute on early payment, and the measurement standard.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is valid before or after Eid, but the preferred time is before the prayer; early payment after Ramadan begins is the fatwa position.

This lesson explores the timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr (Zakat al-Fitr) according to the Hanafi school. The text from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the definition, preferred time, and rulings on early (taqdim) and late (ta'khir) payment. It also clarifies the measurement standard and addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute on the earliest permissible time for early payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib (obligatory). The term is derived from 'Fitr' meaning breaking the fast, indicating it is the charity given at the end of Ramadan.
The ruling is wajib in the Hanafi school, supported by strong Sunnah and consensus.
2
Determine the preferred time
The preferred (mustahabb) time is before going to the Eid prayer, specifically after dawn (fajr) on the day of Eid. This is based on the hadith commanding to give before the prayer and the practice of the Companions.
The matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) does not explicitly mention the preferred time, but al-Kafi clarifies it.
3
Analyze early payment (taqdim)
Early payment before the day of Fitr is valid. The proof is the hadith of Bukhari that the Companions used to give one or two days before. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute on the earliest permissible time: some allow absolutely anytime before, while others restrict it to after Ramadan begins. The fatwa is upon the restriction to Ramadan.
The issue is not mentioned in Zahir al-Riwayah; later authorities discuss it.
4
Analyze delayed payment (ta'khir)
Delaying payment after the day of Fitr is also valid, as it is a financial obligation that does not drop. However, if paid after the day, it is considered qada (make-up) rather than ada (performance) because the time is considered restricted by the hadith 'Make them independent on this day'.
The text notes a difference of opinion on whether delaying is sinful; the chosen view is that it is not disliked.
5
Examine the measurement standard
The amount of Sadaqat al-Fitr is based on volume (kayl), not weight. This is because the traditional measure (sa') is a volume unit. The text clarifies that giving by weight alone is not sufficient if the volume is not equivalent.
The discussion arises from a need to reconcile different practices.

Commentary
This passage addresses the timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins by defining the term: 'Sadaqat al-Fitr' is derived from 'Fitr' (breaking the fast), indicating it is the charity given at the end of Ramadan. The preferred time, though not explicitly stated in the Kanz, is mentioned in the Kafi: before going to the Eid prayer, specifically after dawn on Eid day. The author then discusses the ruling on early and late payment. The statement 'it is valid if given before or after' is from the matn. The commentary explains that early payment is permitted after the cause (head of Ramadan) exists, but Fitr is a condition for obligation; hence, analogies are drawn with a slave freed on Fitr. The early payment is supported by the Bukhari hadith of the Companions giving one or two days before. A dispute among later scholars arises: some restrict early payment to after Ramadan begins, while others permit it absolutely. The text notes that the fatwa is with the restriction to Ramadan, though the author of al-Hidayah considered absolute permissibility. The delay is also valid, as it is a financial obligation that does not drop; paying late is considered ada (performance) until the day passes, after which it becomes qada (make-up). The measurement standard for the fitr is also clarified: it is based on volume (kayl), not weight alone. The passage concludes that delaying without excuse may incur sin if it is considered time-bound, but according to the absolute view, there is no sin. This reflects the Hanafi position that the obligation is broad (muwassa'), though the preferred time is before Eid prayer.

Splitting Sadaqat al-Fitr Among Multiple Recipients
Ruling with proof: resolving intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on distributing fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on splitting Sadaqat al-Fitr among multiple poor and explain how later authorities established permissibility as the madhhab.

Core Idea
Splitting Sadaqat al-Fitr is permissible; later Hanafi authorities confirmed it, analogous to zakat.

This lesson clarifies a practical question: can a person split their Sadaqat al-Fitr among several needy individuals? The source text from Bahru al-Raiq reveals an intra-Hanafi development from prohibition to permissibility, ultimately relying on the later consensus of authorities like al-Walwalji and Qadikhan. Understanding this evolution strengthens your grasp of how the Hanafi school updates its positions through tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage discusses: (1) one person distributing their fitr among multiple poor, and (2) multiple persons pooling their fitr to give to one poor. Note the difference in consensus between the two.
2
Identify the earlier Hanafi view
Al-Tabyin and Fath al-Qadir suggest prohibition (mummani') for scenario 1, with only al-Karkhi permitting. Understand their likely basis: the hadith commanding 'ighna' (making independent).
This earlier view is not the mufta bihi.
3
Identify the later Hanafi view
Al-Walwalji, Qadikhan, al-Muhit, and al-Bada'i' explicitly permit splitting without noting any disagreement. This later consensus becomes the madhhab (mufta bihi).
The permissibility is likened to the permissibility of splitting zakat.
4
Analyze the hadith of ighna'
The hadith commands making the poor independent. The text states it indicates priority (awlawiyya) not prohibition or obligation. Thus, giving to one person is better but not required.
5
State the mufta bihi and the unanimous ruling
The relied-upon position: splitting one's fitr is permissible. Pooling multiple fitr to one poor is unanimously permissible without any dispute.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Mixing Grains for Sadaqat al-Fitr
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mixing grain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling when a wife mixes her husband's grain without permission and evaluate the two disciples' position.

Core Idea
Mixing without permission severs ownership in Abu Hanifa's view, affecting the husband's fitr validity.

The passage introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a wife who mixes her husband's wheat with hers without his permission while paying Sadaqat al-Fitr. The resolution hinges on whether unauthorized mixing constitutes consumption (istihlak) that severs ownership. This lesson analyzes the differing views of Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Dispute
Recognize the scenario: a husband orders his wife to pay fitr, she mixes his grain with hers without permission and gives it to the poor. The question is whether the husband's obligation is discharged.
The act is paying fitr on behalf of someone else.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's Reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that mixing without permission is istihlak (consumption), which severs the owner's right to the specific property. Thus, only the wife's fitr is valid.
istihlak here means the grain is no longer distinguishable as separate property.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's Reasoning
The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that mixing does not sever ownership; hence both fitrs are valid, especially if the husband later authorizes the act.
The later authorization reconciles the views.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Practical consequence: According to Abu Hanifa, the husband must pay again; according to the two, both are discharged if the husband agrees.
The difference affects whether the husband owes an additional fitr.

Giving Sadaqat al-Fitr to the Wife of One's Slave
Ruling on giving sadaqat al-fitr to the wife of one's slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to rule on the validity of giving sadaqat al-fitr to the wife of one's slave and explain the legal reasoning regarding her maintenance.

Core Idea
It is valid to give sadaqat al-fitr to a slave's wife even though her maintenance is on the slave, because the slave's property is ultimately the owner's.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: giving sadaqat al-fitr to the wife of one's own slave. At first glance, it might seem invalid because the owner already provides for the slave's wife indirectly through the slave. However, the Hanafi school rules it valid based on the legal principle that the maintenance is on the slave, and the slave's property is the owner's, so the giving is effectively from the owner to a third party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
In 'Umdat al-Fatawa: 'if one gives his sadaqat al-fitr to the wife of his slave, it is valid'.
2
Understand the apparent problem
Her maintenance (nafaqa) is on the slave (the husband). The owner might be seen as giving to a dependent of his own property.
3
Resolve via legal reasoning
The maintenance is on the slave. The slave can be sold to cover it. Since the slave is owned by the giver, when she sells the slave, she recovers maintenance from the slave's property (which is the owner's). Thus, the owner is not giving to his own direct dependent.
4
Note the condition
As with all sadaqat al-fitr, the recipient must be a Muslim (dhimmis excluded).
