Hajj, Umrah, and Confessions of the Safih
Rulings on safih's Hajj/Umrah and confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the safih's performance of Hajj and Umrah and identify which of his confessions are accepted, explaining the legal reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
A safih may perform Hajj and one Umrah, but his expenses are controlled; his confessions are accepted only for paternity, fatherhood, and marriage.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah addresses two important aspects of the safih's legal capacity: his ability to undertake acts of worship like Hajj and Umrah, and the scope of his confessions (iqrar). Understanding these rulings requires distinguishing between obligations that arise from his own actions versus those imposed by Allah, and between matters where he retains full legal capacity (such as marriage) and those where he does not. The Hanafi school balances protection of the safih's wealth with his religious duties and personal relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage covers two distinct areas: (1) the safih's performance of Hajj and Umrah, and (2) the validity of his confessions regarding lineage and marriage. Note the different legal principles applied in each.
2
Analyze the Hajj and Umrah ruling
Why is Hajj allowed but controlled? Because it is an obligation from Allah not caused by his action. Expenses go to a trustee because the spending itself is not an act of worship requiring intention (niyyah). Contrast with expiations for oaths, vows, and zihar, which are his own doing and thus he expiates by fasting, not spending.
The key distinction is between wajib bi'l-asalah (obligatory by origin) and wajib bi'l-sabab (obligatory due to his action).
3
Note the istihsan for Umrah
Umrah is not obligatory, but istihsan (juristic preference) allows one Umrah to avoid hardship, balancing the safih's religious benefit with financial control.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here; the general rule would prevent any discretionary spending, but leniency is granted for a single Umrah.
4
Examine the confessions rule
The safih's confession of a child or father is accepted without evidence because these relationships are fundamental and don't solely depend on wealth. Confession of other relatives requires evidence because they could be motivated by financial gain.
The same reasoning applies to marriage: since the safih can contract a valid marriage, his confession of marriage is also valid.
5
Understand the qiyas for marriage confession
The text uses an analogy: 'because if he were to initiate marriage, it would be valid, so likewise he may confess to it.' This is a clear qiyas from the permissibility of the act (marriage) to the permissibility of confessing to it.
This qiyas is valid because marriage is an area where the safih retains full legal capacity, unlike pure financial transactions.

Commentary
This passage covers additional rules for the safih. First, he may perform Hajj (the obligatory pilgrimage) because it is a divine obligation not caused by his action. However, the judge gives the travel expenses to a trustee, not directly to the safih, because the expenditure is not itself an act of worship requiring intention. This contrasts with expiations for oaths, vows, and zihar (a type of divorce), which the safih is not required to pay from his wealth because they arise from his own actions; instead, he may fast. The distinction prevents depletion of his wealth through self-imposed obligations. Istihsan allows one 'Umrah and qiran (combining Hajj and 'Umrah) because the safih is not prevented from separate journeys. Second, regarding confession (iqrar): the safih's confession of paternity (child) or being a son (father) is accepted, but for other relatives it requires evidence. His confession of marriage is accepted because he can validly contract marriage, so analogously he can confess to it.

Key Terms
الإقرار (al-iqrār): A declaration or confession by the safih that establishes certain legal relationships, accepted only in specific cases (child, father, spouse).
الاستحسان (al-istihsān): Juristic preference used to allow the safih to perform Umrah once, despite the general rule that his expenses are controlled.

The Hanafi school permits the safih to fulfill his religious obligations (Hajj, one Umrah) with controlled expenses, and accepts his confessions only in matters where he retains legal capacity (paternity, fatherhood, marriage).

The Interdicted Person's Right to Perform Hajj and Umrah
Expansion on Hajj and Umrah for the interdicted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an interdicted person performing Hajj and Umrah and explain why the judge entrusts maintenance to a trustee.

Core Idea
An interdicted person may perform Hajj because it is an obligation from Allah, not from his own action.

This lesson examines the first set of rulings in the commentary on legal interdiction. The key issue is whether the mahjur alayh, who is restricted from managing his wealth, can still undertake the obligation of Hajj. The text clarifies that he is not prevented, but the judge must handle his expenses through a trustee to prevent misuse of funds.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What specific act is being considered? (Hajj of Islam / Umrah). What is the ruling? (permitted).
The text says 'لم يمنع منها' – he is not prevented.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
What is the reason given for not preventing him? The text states 'لأنها واجبة عليه بإيجاب الله تعالى من غير صنعه' – because it is obligatory by Allah's command without his own doing.
3
Apply the principle of talil
The ruling is based on the cause (illah): obligations not caused by the person's action do not open the door to wasting wealth. Obligations caused by personal action (oaths, vows) are treated differently.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the mufta bihi.
4
Note the financial handling
Why does the judge give maintenance to a trustee and not directly? To prevent the interdicted person from misusing the money.
The commentary compares with the case of an expiatory fast.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Mukhtasar al-Quduri in the Book of Legal Interdiction (Kitab al-Hajr). It expands on the rulings regarding a person placed under financial interdiction (mahjur alayh) and his ability to perform Hajj and Umrah, as well as the acceptance of his acknowledgments (iqrar).

The text first states that if the interdicted person intends to perform the obligatory Hajj of Islam (Hajjat al-Islam), he is not prevented from doing so. This is because Hajj is a religious obligation imposed directly by Allah, not arising from the person's own action. However, the judge does not entrust the maintenance money to him directly, fearing he may misuse it; instead, the judge appoints a trustee or a trustworthy pilgrim to manage the expenses during the journey. The commentary draws a distinction between obligations that arise from the person's own actions (like oaths, vows, or dhihar) and those that are originally imposed by Allah. For the former, since the person caused the obligation, if the judge gave him money, he might squander it on such matters, so these are expiated by fasting instead of payment. For the latter, like Hajj, the obligation is from Allah, so the door for misuse is not opened.

There is a brief interjection about the interdicted person's acknowledgment (iqrar): he is believed regarding the parentage of a child or a parent, but not for other relatives without evidence. His acknowledgment of a valid marriage is accepted because he could have validly entered into marriage himself. This fragment ends abruptly but indicates the principle.

The commentary then elaborates on the earlier ruling: the judge pays the maintenance to a trustworthy person from among the pilgrims who will spend it on the interdicted person during the Hajj journey. This is a precaution against the interdicted person misusing the funds.

If the interdicted person invalidates his Hajj by having sexual intercourse before standing at Arafat, he must make up the Hajj (qada), and the judge must provide expenses for the return journey. However, the expiation (kaffarah, e.g., fasting three days) is not immediately required because he is unable to perform it while under interdiction; it is deferred until after the interdiction is lifted, similar to the case of a slave or an insolvent person who cannot afford it.

As for Umrah, if the interdicted person invalidates it, he is not required to make it up until after the removal of interdiction. The commentary explains that we allowed him to perform Umrah despite interdiction only because scholars disagreed on its obligation. Since he was unable to perform it at the time of commission (as he had no wealth), the make-up is postponed.

If the interdicted person is prevented (ihsar) from completing his Hajj by an enemy or illness, the person entrusted with his maintenance should send a sacrificial animal (hadi) so that he can exit ihram. This is because the prevention is not his fault, and he needs to free himself, just as he would use medicine if ill.

Finally, if the interdicted person hunts while in ihram, shaves his head due to harm, or commits similar violations of ihram, a penalty is required. Since he cannot pay a financial penalty (because he is under interdiction and has no wealth), his obligation becomes fasting, similar to an insolvent person (mu'sir).

Key Terms
محجور عليه (mahjur alayh): The person under legal interdiction; restricted from managing his own wealth.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; exception to qiyas based on equity or necessity.

Invalidation of Hajj/Umrah and Deferred Obligations for the Interdicted
Expansion on invalidation and ihram violations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the immediate and deferred penal obligations for an interdicted person who invalidates Hajj or Umrah or commits violations in ihram.

Core Idea
Kaffarah is deferred for the interdicted person because he cannot pay; instead, he fasts or postpones qada.

This lesson continues with the interdicted person's situation after he begins Hajj or Umrah. The text addresses what happens if he invalidates his pilgrimage by intercourse, is prevented (ihsar), or commits hunting/shaving violations. The key principle is that financial penalties are deferred or transformed into fasting due to his inability to pay.

1
Identify the invalidating act
What act invalidates Hajj? Sexual intercourse before standing at Arafat. What is required? Qada (make-up) and kaffarah?
Text says: 'فعليه نفقة القضاء' and 'ولا تلزمه الكفارة'.
2
Apply the rule of inability
Why is kaffarah deferred? Because he is unable to perform it (fast or pay) while under interdiction. Deferred until after interdiction is removed.
Compare with the case of a slave or insolvent person.
3
Differentiate Umrah invalidation
What if he invalidates Umrah? No qada required until after interdiction. Reason: he was allowed to perform Umrah despite inability due to ikhtilaf on its obligation.
This shows the legal principle that permissibility based on istihsan does not create an immediate obligation to make up.
4
Handle ihsar (prevention)
If prevented from completing Hajj by enemy or illness, the trustee sends a sacrificial animal to allow him to exit ihram. This is because the prevention is not his fault.
5
Resolve hunting/shaving violations
If he hunts or shaves due to harm, a penalty is required. Since he cannot pay, his obligation is fasting, similar to an insolvent person.
The text says: 'ففرضه الصوم'.
