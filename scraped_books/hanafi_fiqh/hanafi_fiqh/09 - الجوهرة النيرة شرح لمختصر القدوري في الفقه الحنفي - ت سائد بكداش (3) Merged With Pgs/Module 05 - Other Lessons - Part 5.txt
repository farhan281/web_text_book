Ruling on partial claim by present pre-emptor

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A present pre-emptor cannot claim only part; he must take all or leave all.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is an indivisible right. When one pre-emptor is absent, the present pre-emptor cannot opt to take only a fraction of the property proportionate to his share. The law requires him to either take the entire property if he wishes to exercise his right, or to abandon it completely. This lesson examines the ruling and a stricter opinion that nullifies the right entirely if a partial claim is made.

Guided Path

The absence of the other pre-emptor is key to the ruling.
2
Understand the nature of the demand
The present pre-emptor demands only part of the property (e.g., half or one-third), which he believes corresponds to his share.
This partial demand is the critical action.
3
Apply the general Hanafi ruling
According to the dominant view, such a partial demand is not permissible; the pre-emptor must either take the entire property or leave it completely.
The right of shuf'a is indivisible.
4
Note the stricter variant
The text cites al-Yanabi', which holds that even the act of demanding half voids the right of shuf'a entirely, regardless of intention.
This is a more stringent position within the Hanafi school.
5

In practice, a present pre-emptor should avoid any partial claim and must commit to the whole or forfeit his right.
This prevents fragmentation and disputes.

The right of pre-emption is personal and non-transferable; attempted assignment voids the assignor's right.

In Hanafi fiqh, shuf'a (pre-emption) is a personal right that arises when a co-owner sells his share to an outsider. This lesson examines what happens when a pre-emptor tries to assign his right to another pre-emptor rather than exercising or dropping it. The ruling is clear: such an assignment is invalid, and the assignor loses his right entirely.

Guided Path

The act is an attempted transfer of a personal right.
2
Identify the consequence for the assignor
The passage states that if the assignment is attempted, the assignor's right of pre-emption falls (yasqut haqq al-ja'il). He can no longer claim the property.
This is a penalty for trying to transfer a non-transferable right.
3
Identify the consequence for the remaining pre-emptors
The property is then divided among the remaining pre-emptors (yuqsam 'ala adad man baqiya) according to their own shares, ignoring the assignor.
The division is proportional among those who still hold rights.
4
Understand the rationale
Shuf'a is a right attached to the person, not a property interest. It cannot be sold or assigned independently; it can only be exercised or waived.
This distinguishes it from ownership rights which are transferable.

When a property is bought using goods measured by volume or weight, the price is not converted to cash for pre-emption. Instead, the pre-emptor must deliver the exact same quantity and type of that good. This ruling rests on the Hanafi classification of such items as mithliyyat — things that have standardized equivalents with negligible value differences.

The pre-emptor takes the property by giving the mithl (the exact like) of that payment item. The text says: 'he takes it with its like (mithl)'.
Mithl means the same type and quantity.
3
Understand the legal basis
Because the payment item is a mithli (fungible good), it can be precisely replaced without difference in value. The commentary explains: 'because it is from among things that have likes (mithliyyat)'.
This is an application of the Hanafi principle of mithliyyat.

If the purchase price were a unique item (e.g., a specific artwork or a car), the pre-emptor would pay its monetary value (qimah) instead of an exact replacement.
This distinction is implicit in the ruling.
5

This protects the seller's interest in receiving the contracted commodity.

Validity of Surrendering Shuf'ah to an Outsider's Request
Ruling on validity of responding to an outsider's request to surrender shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish valid from invalid verbal responses when a third party requests surrender of shuf'ah, and apply the Hanafi istihsan reasoning to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A shafi's surrender to an outsider's request is valid; an independent statement is not.

In Hanafi law, the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) can be surrendered through clear words. But what if a third party asks the shafi' to surrender to the buyer? Does the shafi's response to the third party count? The Hanafi school uses istihsan to treat certain phrases as valid surrender when uttered in response, while rejecting independent statements. This lesson explores the key contextual distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that an outsider (ajnabi) requests the shafi' to surrender the right to the buyer. The shafi' then responds with a phrase like 'I have surrendered it to you,' 'I have gifted it to you,' or 'I have relinquished it.'
The outsider is not the buyer, but acts as an intermediary.

By istihsan (juristic preference), the shafi's response to the outsider is interpreted as being on behalf of the buyer. The phrase 'to you' is understood as 'to him through you.' Therefore, the surrender is valid.
This overrides the strict qiyas requirement that surrender be addressed directly to the buyer.

If the shafi' initiates an independent statement without being prompted by the outsider, e.g., 'I surrender the pre-emption of this house to you,' it is not considered a valid surrender because it lacks the contextual linkage to the buyer.
The same phrase can be valid or invalid depending on the presence of a prior request.
4
Generalize the principle
The decisive factor is whether the shafi's utterance is responsive to a request made on behalf of the buyer. If so, the surrender is valid; if it is an independent statement, it is not.
This applies to any phrase that indicates surrender, gift, or relinquishment.

Rulings on Mudarib's Purchase and Manumission of Slaves
Explains legal effects of manumission by mudarib and rabb al-mal in various profit scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal effect of manumission by the mudarib and the rabb al-mal in three scenarios of a mudarabah contract, and identify when compensation is due.

Core Idea
Manumission by mudarib only frees his profit share; rabb al-mal's manumission may require compensation.

This lesson examines the rulings on manumission of slaves purchased with mudarabah capital. The key is to determine the mudarib's share in the asset based on profit. We will see three scenarios: profit, no profit, and two assets. Understanding these rulings is essential for correctly applying Hanafi partnership law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mudarib's profit share
Determine the profit ratio (default half) and compute the mudarib's share as a fraction of the asset's value.
In the first scenario, profit is 1000, so mudarib's half is 500, which is 1/4 of the slave's value.
2
Determine ownership ratio in purchased asset
The rabb al-mal owns the capital and the asset; the mudarib's share is only the profit portion. Thus, ownership is split proportionally.
If no profit, mudarib has no ownership share.
3
Apply manumission rule
Each party can free only their own share. Mudarib's manumission is effective only in his profit share; rabb al-mal's manumission covers his share plus compensation for mudarib's share if destroyed.
If rabb al-mal frees the entire slave, he must compensate the mudarib for his share if it exceeds his own ownership.
4
Check compensation liability
When rabb al-mal frees a slave and destroys the mudarib's share, he must pay compensation (daman itlaf) even if insolvent.
In the two-slave scenario, freeing both together requires compensating the mudarib 500.
5
Recognize sequential vs simultaneous manumission
If rabb al-mal frees one slave first, that slave counts as capital recovery; the second slave becomes profit asset, and only half is freed when he frees it.
The second slave is then treated like a co-owned slave between two partners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the liability of a mudarib who sub-contracts without permission and explain the well-known (mashhur) resolution giving the owner the option to sue either mudarib.

Core Idea
When a mudarib sub-contracts without permission, liability hinges on profit, action, or delivery—but the mashhur view gives the owner the option to sue either mudarib.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the consequences of a mudarib sub-contracting the mudarabah capital without the owner's permission. The commentators record multiple positions from the founding imams, then resolve the conflict with a well-known (mashhur) ruling. You will learn to differentiate these positions and understand the practical implications for the owner's recourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions on the first mudarib's liability
List the three distinct views on when the first mudarib becomes liable for the capital: (1) Hasan's narration: only after profit; (2) Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: upon action by the second mudarib; (3) Zafar: immediately upon delivery.
These are all within the Hanafi school; they differ on the threshold of transgression (ta'addi).
2
Analyze the basis of each position
Consider the reasoning: Profit indicates unauthorized use of capital; action constitutes trespass; delivery alone may be a breach of trust. Each imam weighs different aspects.
3
Examine the dispute regarding the second mudarib's liability
According to one interpretation from Abu Hanifa, the second mudarib is not liable; according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, he is liable, based on their analogous dispute on sub-deposit (mudi' al-mudi').
The text mentions an analogy to sub-deposit, where the second depositary benefits the first, so not liable; in mudarabah the second mudarib works for his own benefit, so liability can attach.

This resolution balances the positions and provides practical relief to the owner.

Definition and General Rule of Agency (Wakalah)
Opening of the Book of Agency: definition and foundational rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of wakalah and apply the rule that any contract one may personally conclude may be delegated, while identifying the exception of personal acts like qisas.

Core Idea
Any contract you can make yourself, you can appoint an agent—except personal acts like retaliation.

Agency (wakalah) is a facilitative mechanism in Islamic law, allowing someone unable to act personally to appoint another to conclude contracts on their behalf. This lesson introduces the linguistic and legal definitions of wakalah and the foundational rule that determines which matters can be delegated. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly applying agency in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the linguistic meaning from the legal definition
Begin by noting that linguistically 'wakalah' means preservation or protection. The legal definition is narrower: appointing another to perform a known disposition (tasarruf) on one's behalf.
The Quranic phrase 'Na'ma al-wakil' (best Disposer) supports the linguistic sense.
2
State the general rule
The rule: any contract a person may validly conclude by himself, he may delegate to an agent. This includes sales, rentals, marriages, etc.
The rule is based on need ('ajz) and facilitation.
3
Identify the exclusion (qisas)
The author explicitly excludes actions that are not contracts, such as exacting retaliation (qisas). Such personal acts cannot be delegated because they require personal involvement.
This exclusion is a key boundary: not every permissible action can be delegated.
4
Note the requirement for verbal expression
Agency is valid only with explicit verbal expressions like 'I appoint you to sell this my slave' or 'to buy such-and-such.'
Implied delegation or mere conduct may not suffice; clarity avoids disputes.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary (Al-Jawharat al-Nayyirah) expands on the condition of the principal (muwakkil) in agency (wakalah). It begins by stating the general principle: the agent transacts on behalf of the principal, so the principal must have the capacity to own and transfer ownership. Classification of agency contracts and liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of agency contracts (attributed to agent vs. attributed to principal) and determine where rights and liabilities attach in each case.

Core Idea
Contracts made by agents are of two types: those attributed to the agent (rights on agent) and those attributed to the principal (rights on principal).

This lesson focuses on the final part of the passage, which classifies agency contracts into two broad categories. The classification hinges on whether the agent attributes the contract to himself or to the principal. This distinction determines who bears the rights and liabilities (huquq wa uhda) arising from the contract. The text gives examples such as sale (attributed to agent) and marriage (attributed to principal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of agency contracts
The text states: 'العقود التي يعقدها الوكلاء على ضربين'. List the two types: contracts the agent attributes to himself, and contracts he attributes to the principal.
The distinction is based on whose name the contract is concluded in.
2
Explain what it means for the agent to attribute the contract to himself
When the agent makes a contract in his own name (e.g., 'I sell this to you'), he is the apparent party. Therefore, all rights and liabilities of that contract attach to him personally.
The principal is not directly liable to the third party.
3
Give examples of each type
Sale (bay') and lease (ijara) are given as examples of contracts attributed to the agent. Marriage (nikah) and loan (qard) are contracts typically attributed to the principal.
The text does not explicitly list the second type, but it mentions that if the buyer swears that the principal owes him nothing, he is truthful if the agent acted in his own name.
4
Determine where rights attach in each case
In contracts attributed to the agent, rights attach to the agent; in contracts attributed to the principal, rights attach to the principal.
This determines who can be sued and who bears warranty.
5
Consider the consequence for a buyer's oath
The text gives a practical application: if the buyer swears that the principal (not the agent) owes him nothing, and the contract was attributed to the agent, the oath is truthful because the agent is the one liable.
This shows the importance of the classification in procedural matters.

Commentary
Passage type: Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute. This passage from the Book of Agency (al-Wakalah) explains the conditions for a valid agent and the classification of agency contracts. The author first states that the agent must be someone who understands the contract (ya'qil al-'aqd) and intends it (yaqsiduhu). This is because the agent stands in place of the principal in expressing the contract, so he must be legally capable of expression. Therefore, if a minor who does not understand buying and selling or an insane person is appointed, the agency is void. The condition of intention excludes the case of a joker (hazil) or one coerced (mukrah); if such a person acts as agent, his actions do not take effect. Next, the text discusses the appointment of a free adult or an authorized person (like a slave with permission to trade) appointing someone similar. The similarity is not limited to freedom and slavery; it can be higher or lower. The key is that if the agent is of a similar status, the rights of the contract attach to him; if of a lower status (like a minor or restricted slave), the rights attach to the principal. The text then specifically addresses appointing a restricted minor who has discernment (ya'qil al-bay' wa al-shira') or a restricted slave. Such appointments are valid, but the rights and liabilities (al-huquq wa al-uhda) do not attach to the minor or slave; they attach to the principals. This is because the minor, though capable of disposition with guardian's permission, lacks full capacity to bear liability; the slave is impeded by his master's right. The passage then reports an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: according to Abu Yusuf, if the buyer was unaware that the seller was a minor, he has the option to rescind (khiyar al-faskh) upon discovering it, similar to the option for a defect. The later part distinguishes between the minor and slave regarding liability (uhda): if the slave is freed, the liability becomes his own because the impediment (master's right) is removed; however, for the minor, even after reaching puberty, the liability does not attach because the impediment is his own deficiency. Finally, the text classifies contracts made by agents into two types: those the agent attributes to himself (like sale and lease) – rights attach to the agent; and those he attributes to the principal (like marriage, loan, etc.) – rights attach to the principal. This classification determines who bears the obligations of the contract.

Key Terms
الوكيل (al-wakīl): The agent who acts on behalf of the principal in concluding contracts.
يضيف العقد إلى نفسه (yudīfu al-'aqd ilā nafsihi): Attributes the contract to himself: acts in his own name, becoming the apparent party.

Rights Attach to the Agent in Agency Contracts
Explaining the rule and Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that in contracts where the agent attributes the contract to himself, the rights attach to the agent, and explain the practical difference from the Shafi'i position using the oath example.

Core Idea
The agent bears the rights and liabilities; the principal benefits from ownership.

In Hanafi fiqh, agency contracts are categorized by who the agent attributes the contract to. If the agent contracts in his own name, he becomes the direct counterparty. This lesson explores the rule, its evidence, and the dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
Read the opening sentence: contracts where the agent attributes to himself (e.g., sale, lease) – rights attach to the agent, not the principal.
This is the foundational Hanafi position.
2
Examine the Practical Consequence
Consider the oath scenario: if a buyer swears the principal has no claim, he is truthful; if he swears the agent has no claim, he is a perjurer. This demonstrates rights attach to the agent.
Example from al-Nihayah.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i Objection
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that rights attach to the principal. Understand why they differ.
The Shafi'i view is based on agency theory that the principal is the real party.
4
Trace the Hanafi Proof (Qiyas)
The Hanafi proof analogizes to a slave plundering or hunting for his master – the master gets ownership, but the slave is the one who acts and therefore rights attach to the slave. Similarly, the agent acts directly.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
5
Understand the Khilafah Concept
Ownership first vests in the agent momentarily then transfers to the principal as a deputyship (khilafah). This explains why rights stay with the agent.
This concept prevents the agent's actions from affecting his own relatives.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Wakalah (Book of Agency) of al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the legal effect of agency contracts. The author states that agency contracts are of two types: those where the agent attributes the contract to himself (e.g., sale, purchase, lease) and those where he attributes to the principal. For the former, the rights of the contract (like delivery, receipt of price, warranty) attach to the agent, not the principal. The text then addresses special cases: a minor and a slave. A minor can act with guardian’s permission, and a slave can act for himself but ownership goes to the master. For both, their contracts bind the principal regarding liability (al-‘uhda), not themselves, due to the minor’s deficient capacity and the slave’s master’s right. Abu Yusuf gives the purchaser an option if he was unaware the seller was a minor, analogizing to a defective sale. Qadhi Khan distinguishes between minor and slave for liability: if the slave is freed, liability binds him because the master’s right ceases; a minor’s liability does not bind him after puberty because the impediment is his own right (which persists). The text then quotes the matn and expands: if the purchaser swears the principal has no claim, he is truthful; if swears the agent has no claim, he is false. This is because rights attach to the agent. The Shafi’i position is then mentioned: rights attach to the principal, not the agent. The Hanafi response uses analogy: ownership is established for the principal as a deputyship from the agent, like a slave who plunders and hunts for his master—ownership goes to the master but the act is attributed to the slave. However, in Hanafi law, ownership first vests in the agent momentarily then transfers to the principal, which is why it does not affect the agent’s own relatives or marriage. The passage ends by clarifying that if the agent is an interdicted minor or slave, they are not addressed for delivery; if permitted (ma’dhun), rights attach to them.

العهدة (al-‘uhdah): Liability or responsibility for the obligations arising from a contract.
خلافة (khilāfah): Deputyship; ownership is established for the principal as a deputyship from the agent.

Agency by Minors and Slaves: Capacity and Liability
Explaining capacity rules for minor and slave agents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school distinguishes between a minor and a slave agent regarding liability ('uhdah), and identify when the liability binds the principal versus the agent.

Core Idea
A minor's deficient capacity and a slave's master's right prevent liability from binding them, so it falls on the principal.

When a minor or slave acts as an agent, their capacity affects who bears the liability (al-'uhdah). This lesson examines the Hanafi position, the option granted by Abu Yusuf, and the distinction drawn by Qadhi Khan regarding liability after emancipation or puberty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling on Minor and Slave Agents
The text states that when a minor or slave (interdicted) contracts as an agent, the liability (al-'uhdah) does not bind them but binds the principal, due to the minor's deficient capacity and the slave's master's right.
This is the default rule.
2
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Option
Abu Yusuf gives the buyer an option to rescind if he was unaware the seller was a minor, analogizing to a defective sale. Understand the reasoning.
This is intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Examine Qadhi Khan's Distinction
Qadhi Khan distinguishes between a minor and a slave regarding the binding of liability after their status changes: if a slave is freed, liability binds him because the master's right ceases; if a minor attains puberty, liability does not bind him because it is his own right that persists.
This shows the different impediments.
4
Apply the Rule to Interdicted vs. Permitted Agents
If the minor or slave is permitted (ma'dhun) by the guardian/master, they are addressed for delivery and rights attach to them. If interdicted, the principal is responsible.
Permission makes them capable.

General Rulings on Agency in Contracts: Principal's Liability
Explains the general principle and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general ruling that for contracts where the agent is a mere envoy, rights attach to the principal, and identify the exceptions and conditions that affect this ruling.

Core Idea
In contracts like marriage and khul', the agent is a mere envoy; the principal bears all rights and obligations.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in Hanafi law of agency: when an agent acts as a mere intermediary (safir mahd) in certain contracts, the legal effects attach to the principal, not the agent. We will explore the rule, its examples, and an exception involving fathers marrying minor children.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and its scope
Read the opening statement: 'Every contract that the agent adds to his principal... its rights pertain to the principal, not the agent.' Note that this applies to specific contracts: marriage, khul', and sulh for intentional homicide.
2
Understand the concept of safir mahd
The commentator explains that the agent in these contracts is a 'mere envoy' (safir mahd) who cannot dispense with adding the contract to the principal. If he adds it to himself, the contract becomes his own.
This contrasts with a wakil who has discretionary authority.
3
Examine the examples and their consequences
For marriage: the agent of the husband is not sued for mahr; the agent of the wife is not obliged to hand her over. For khul': the husband's agent cannot receive compensation; the wife's agent is not liable for the compensation unless he guarantees it.
4
Analyze the exception for father of minor children
When a father marries his minor son or daughter, the rules differ. If the father of the girl says 'I married my daughter to your son' and the boy's father says 'I accept', the marriage is valid for the son even without explicit 'for my son' because the offer was directed to the son. However, if the offer is ambiguous, the marriage may fall for the father.
The commentator emphasizes caution: the agent should explicitly say 'I accept on behalf of so-and-so'.
5
Note the recommended formulation for agents
For the agent in marriage, it is recommended to say 'Qabiltu al-nikaha li-ajli fulan' (I accept the marriage on behalf of so-and-so) to avoid any ambiguity.

The Validity of General Agency and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Defective Purchases
Expounds the ruling on general agency and requirements for valid purchase agency, including intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid purchase agency, distinguish between general and specific agency, and explain the Hanafi positions on the binding effect of buying a severely defective slave-girl.

Core Idea
General agency is valid; specific agency without type and price is void. Custom overrides literal compliance in severe defects.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, clarifies how an agency to purchase is structured. It first validates a general agency where the principal gives full discretion. It then voids a specific agency lacking essential details like price and type. Finally, it presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: if an agent appointed to buy a slave-girl of a certain type and price purchases one with a severe defect (blind, handless, paralyzed), is the principal bound? Abu Hanifa says yes because the term 'jariyah' covers defective; the Sahibayn say no because custom opposes such purchases. Understanding this reveals the role of 'urf in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of agency
Distinguish between general agency (وكالة عامة) where the principal says 'buy whatever you see' – valid because discretion is delegated; and specific agency where the principal says 'buy a sheep' without price/description – void because the subject matter is undefined.
The general agency is analogous to bida'ah and mudarabah in terms of discretion.
2
Examine the requirement for specifying type and price
When the principal says 'buy a garment for 10 dirhams,' he must also state the type (e.g., Herawi or Marwi) because 'garment' covers many types (cotton, wool, linen) and price alone does not clarify which type is intended. Without specification, the agency is void and the agent's purchase is for himself.
This rule ensures the subject matter is known (ma'lum) to avoid uncertainty (gharar).
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
The case: principal appoints agent to buy a slave-girl of a specified type and price. Agent buys a severely defective one (blind, handless, paralyzed). Abu Hanifa says the purchase binds the principal; the Sahibayn say it does not.
Severe defects are those that make the slave practically unusable.
4
Analyze each side's proof
Abu Hanifa's proof: the word 'jariyah' literally applies to both sound and defective; the agent complied technically. The Sahibayn's proof: custom ('urf) dictates that people do not buy such severely defective slaves; the agent acted contrary to the principal's implicit intent.
This is a clash of literal interpretation vs. customary understanding.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and consensus on minor defects
Later authorities prefer the Sahibayn's view, upholding 'urf in severe defects. But if the defect is minor (e.g., one-eyed, one-handed), there is consensus that the purchase binds the principal, because such defects are customary and people do buy them.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position for severe defects.

Lists rulings for agency with specific purposes: service, manumission, and intercourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each of the three specified purposes and determine whether a purchase binds the principal or the agent.

Core Idea
An agent who buys a slave unfit for the principal's stated purpose violates the agency — the purchase stands on the agent, not the principal.

Agency (wakala) often involves purchasing goods for a specific use. When a principal gives a clear purpose — service, manumission, or intercourse — the agent must ensure the purchased slave can fulfil that purpose. This lesson examines three classical cases from the matn: buying a slave for service, for manumission (raqabah), and for intercourse/istīlād. Understanding these rulings clarifies the binding nature of conditions in agency contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three specified purposes
Read the three cases: service (or bread-making), manumission (raqabah), and intercourse/istīlād. Note that the principal explicitly states the intended use.
2
Determine fitness for each purpose
For service, the slave must be able to work (e.g., have hands, sight). For raqabah, the slave must be validly manumissible in kaffarah — minor defects like one-eyedness are acceptable. For intercourse, the slave must be lawful for the principal — not a mahram, not a Magian, etc.
Know the definitions: ratqā' (closed vagina), mahram, milk-sister, Magian.
3
State the legal effect of unfitness
If the slave is completely unfit for the purpose, the purchase does NOT bind the principal. The agent is considered to have bought it for himself. If the slave is partially fit (as in raqabah with one-eyed), it binds the principal.
The passage says 'bi-ijma'' (by consensus) for the first two cases.
4
Recognize the scope of 'raqabah'
The term raqabah in agency for manumission implies a slave valid for expiation. Blindness and handlessness exclude, but one-eyed and one-handed are included.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
If a principal says 'buy me a slave for cooking' and the agent buys a deaf slave, does it bind? Use the principle: if the slave is unfit for the stated purpose, it does not bind. Practice with examples.

Agent's Right to Return Defective Goods
Ruling on agent's option to return defect and termination of that right after delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for an agent returning a defective item and identify when he loses the right to return without the principal's permission.

Core Idea
The agent may return a defective item only while it remains in his possession; after delivery, he needs the principal's permission.

When an agent purchases an item for a principal and later discovers a defect, does he have the right to return it? The answer depends on whether the item is still in his possession. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling on this issue and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the agent (wakil) purchasing an item, taking possession (qabd), then discovering a defect. The ruling is that he has the right (lahu) to return it due to the defect.
This is a right, not an obligation.
2
Locate the condition
The right is conditional: 'as long as the item is in his hand' (ma dam al-mabi' fi yadihi). Possession is key.
Once delivered to the principal, the condition changes.
3
Understand the proof
The text states: 'because it is from the rights of the contract, and all of them belong to him.' The agent is the contracting party, so he holds the rights, including the right of return for defect.
This is a rational proof based on agency.
4
Note the change after delivery
If the agent delivers the item to the principal, the agency relationship ends regarding that item. The agent can only return it with the principal's permission.
Three reasons given: agency ends, invalidating principal's possession, restriction on agent.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, the agent should return the defect while still in possession, or seek permission from the principal after delivery.

Core Idea
An agent's purchase binds only if the slave fits the implied purpose agreed.

In Hanafi agency, when a principal instructs an agent to buy a slave for a specific purpose, the contract binds the principal only if the slave is fit for that purpose. This lesson examines two scenarios: buying for service and buying for emancipation. The key is understanding the implied condition in the principal's instruction and the consensus (ijma) on these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principal's instruction
Determine what the principal said: did he specify a service (e.g., 'serve me', 'baking') or did he use the word 'raqaba' (slave body)? The wording determines the implied condition.

2
Assess the slave's capability
For service instructions, the slave must be physically capable of the specified work. For 'raqaba', the slave must be sound enough for emancipation in kaffara (free from major defects).
Total disability (blind, no hands) fails both; partial defects (one eye, one hand) may still allow emancipation.
3
Apply the ruling
If the slave's condition contradicts the implied purpose, the purchase does not bind the principal; otherwise, it binds by consensus.
The agent may bear the loss if he violated the condition.
4
Note the consensus
The Hanafi school holds ijma (consensus) on these rulings, meaning they are well-established and not subject to ikhtilaf within the school.

Agent's Right to Reimbursement and Retention
Ruling on agent's reimbursement and retention rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rights and obligations of an agent who pays the purchase price from his own funds, and apply the rulings on perishing of goods and the right of retention.

Core Idea
An agent paying from his own funds has a right to reimbursement and a lien on the goods until paid; loss before withholding falls on the principal.

When an agent appointed to purchase goods pays the price from his own pocket, he becomes entitled to reimbursement from the principal. He also has the right to retain the goods as security. But what if the goods are destroyed before he exercises that right? This passage clarifies the legal consequences, protecting both the agent's claim and the principal's ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the agent pays the price from his own wealth and takes possession of the goods. This triggers specific rights and responsibilities.
2
Determine the agent's right to reclaim
The passage states that the agent may reclaim the price from the principal. Why? Because the price was a debt on the agent's liability, and the principal involved him in this situation.
The agent's payment does not transfer ownership; the goods belong to the principal.
3
Analyze the case of loss before withholding
If the goods perish in the agent's hands before he withholds them, the loss is from the principal's wealth, and the agent's right to reclaim the price remains. Reason: the agent's possession is like the principal's possession.
Withholding (habs) is a key act that changes liability.
4
Understand the right to withhold
The agent may withhold the goods until he receives the price, regardless of whether he already paid or not. This is a lien to secure his claim.
This right exists even if the goods have not yet perished.

Two Agents Appointed Jointly: Default Rule and Exceptions
Explains when one of two agents may act alone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between joint and separate agency, identify the exceptions where one agent may act alone, and explain the Hanafi position on ratification when one agent acts without the other.

Core Idea
Two agents appointed jointly cannot act independently unless the action requires no deliberation.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on two agents appointed by a single statement. The default is that neither may act alone, but exceptions exist for certain actions. We also explore an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding ratification when one agent acts in the presence or absence of the other.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Read the matn: 'If a man appoints two men as agents, one of them is not permitted to act without the other.' This is the general rule when the appointment is made with a single utterance.
The ruling applies only to joint appointment; separate appointments allow independent action.
2
Recognize the exceptions
The text lists actions that do not require deliberation: litigation, divorce without compensation, manumission without compensation, returning a trust, and paying a debt. For these, one agent may act alone.
Exception applies only to actions where the agent's judgment is not needed.
3
Understand the ratification dispute
If one agent acts while the other is present, the action is invalid unless the other ratifies it. Al-Muntaqa holds it is valid even without ratification. If the other is absent, Abu Hanifa says subsequent ratification does not validate the action.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the relied-upon view requires ratification when present.
4
Apply to incapacity or death
If one agent is a minor, a restricted slave, dies, or loses sanity, the other cannot act alone. The principal's consent was to both together, so the remaining agent lacks authority.
This is consistent with the principle of joint delegation.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri discusses the permissibility of one of two appointed agents acting independently. The matn states the general rule: if a person appoints two agents with a single statement, neither may act without the other. The commentary clarifies that this applies only when the appointment is made in one utterance; if they are appointed separately (each by a distinct statement), either may act alone. A comparison is made with executors of a will (wasiyy), who—even if appointed separately—cannot act independently because at the moment of death they become joint executors. Exceptions to the general rule are then listed: litigation, divorce without compensation, manumission without compensation, returning a trust, and paying a debt—these actions do not require deliberation and thus one agent may perform them alone. However, in litigation, if it involves collecting money, both must be present for receipt. The text then addresses a dispute: if one agent acts in the presence of the other without his permission, the action is invalid unless ratified, according to the relied-upon view; but al-Muntaqa holds it is valid. If the other agent is absent, subsequent ratification does not validate the action according to Abu Hanifa. Additionally, if one agent is a minor or a slave under interdiction, the other cannot act alone because the principal did not consent to the opinion of only one capable person. Similarly, if one agent dies or loses sanity, the other cannot continue alone; this is stated in al-Nihaya.

Exceptions to Joint Agency: Acts Requiring No Opinion
Explains exceptions to joint agency rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which acts allow a single joint agent to act alone and explain the rationale (istihsan) based on the absence of need for judgment.

Core Idea
Joint agents need consensus for judgment-based acts, but not for pure expression (ta'bir mahd) or litigation.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira explains a critical exception to the general rule of joint agency. While the default is that both agents must act together, the Hanafi school carves out exceptions for actions that require no personal judgment. Understanding these exceptions reveals a deeper principle: the law does not demand impracticality.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Recognize that the default position in joint agency is that both agents must act together for any transaction.
2
Identify the exceptions
List the specific acts from the text: litigation, divorcing a specific wife without compensation, manumitting a specific slave without compensation, returning a deposit or borrowed item, paying a debt.
Also note that receiving money in litigation requires both agents.
3
Analyze the rationale
Understand why these acts are exceptions: they are either pure expression (ta'bir mahd) that does not require opinion, or in litigation joint action is impossible (would cause disorder). This is based on istihsan overriding the general rule.
The illa is lack of benefit or impossibility of joint action.
4
Distinguish similar cases
Contrast with cases where the principal delegates to the agents' opinion (e.g., 'Divorce her if you wish'). In those cases, both must agree because judgment is involved.
Also note that unspecified wife or slave requires agreement because selection involves judgment.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Recognize the disagreement: Abu Hanifa holds that if one agent acts alone while the other is absent and later ratifies, it is not valid; al-Muntaqa reports a view that it is valid. The mufta bihi is the default position requiring joint action except for the listed exceptions.
No explicit tarjih is given in this text.

General Rule on Sub-Agency: Permission and Full Discretion
Ruling with rational proofs and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on sub-agency and identify the conditions under which an agent may appoint a sub-agent.

Core Idea
An agent cannot delegate without permission; only explicit consent or full discretion permits sub-agency.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains the default prohibition on an agent appointing a sub-agent, rooted in the nature of agency delegation and the principal's reliance on the agent's personal judgment. You will learn the precise conditions that allow sub-agency, the rationale behind the rule, and the legal consequences when sub-agency is permitted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
Recognize that the act in question is an agent (wakīl) appointing another person (sub-agent) to perform the task he was hired for. The default ruling is prohibition: 'ليس للوكيل أن يوكل' – it is not permitted.
The ruling is established in the matn of al-Qudūrī.
2
Recognize the exceptions
Note the two exceptions: (1) the principal explicitly permits sub-agency (أذن له الموكل), or (2) the principal says 'act on your own judgment' (اعمل برأيك). Both give the agent authority to delegate.
The second exception implies full discretion; the agent can then use his best judgment, including delegation.
3
Examine the proofs (taʿlīl)
Understand the three reasons given: (a) The agent was entrusted with direct action, not delegation; (b) the contract does not imply sub-agency; (c) the principal chose this agent's opinion, and people's opinions differ. These rational proofs support the prohibition.
These are examples of istidlāl bi al-taʿlīl – legal reasoning based on effective cause.
4
Analyze the examples
Two examples illustrate the rule: (1) appointing two agents to manumit a slave for a price or to divorce by khulʿ – they can use discretion because compensation is involved; (2) appointing two agents to collect a debt – they must act jointly because the principal relied on both judgments.
The example of joint agency shows that when the principal trusts multiple opinions, each alone is insufficient.
5
Trace the legal consequences
If sub-agency is valid (by permission or discretion), the sub-agent becomes agent of the principal, not the first agent. The first agent cannot dismiss the sub-agent, and the sub-agent is not dismissed by the first agent's death. Both are dismissed by the principal's death.
This is a key distinction: the sub-agent's relationship is directly with the original principal.
