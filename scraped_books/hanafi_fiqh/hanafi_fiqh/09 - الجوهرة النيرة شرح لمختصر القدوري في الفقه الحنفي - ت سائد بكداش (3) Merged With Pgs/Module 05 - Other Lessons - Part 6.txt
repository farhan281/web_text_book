Sub-Agency Without Permission: Presence of the Principal and Liability
Ruling on sub-agency without permission in the principal's presence, with disputes and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a sub-agent's contract in the presence of the principal without permission, and explain the disputes on liability and ratification, as well as the distinction between contracts and divorce/manumission.

Core Idea
A sub-agent's contract in the principal's presence is valid, but liability and ratification are disputed; divorce and manumission are exceptions.

After establishing that a fully delegated agent may appoint a sub-agent, the text addresses a common scenario: the agent appoints a sub-agent without the principal's permission, and the sub-agent then contracts in the principal's presence. The ruling is that the contract is valid because the principal's implicit consent is present. However, two disputes arise: who bears liability (al-ʿuhda) for the contract's obligations, and whether the first agent's ratification is required. Finally, a crucial exception is made for acts like divorce and manumission, which are conditional on the specific agent's action and thus do not fall under the same rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The scenario: a principal fully delegates to an agent, who then appoints a sub-agent without permission. The sub-agent contracts in the principal's presence. The ruling: the contract is valid (jāz).
The key is the principal's presence; his opinion is considered obtained.
2
Understand the reasoning (talīl)
The reasoning given is that the purpose is the presence of the principal's opinion (ra'y), and that opinion was obtained because he was present and did not object.
This is a proof based on the principal's implicit consent.
3
Recognize the dispute on liability
The text asks: who bears the liability (al-ʿuhda) for the contract's rights and obligations? Two views: al-Baqqali says the first agent; al-ʿUyūn and Qāḍī Khān say the second agent.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference; no single settled position is given.
4
Analyze the dispute on ratification
Is the first agent's ratification of the sub-agent's contract required? Al-Aṣl (a Zahir al-Riwayah source) says no; the majority of later mashāyikh say yes. The absolute statement of validity is interpreted as conditional upon ratification.
The muftā bihī position is that ratification is required.
5
Apply the exception for divorce and manumission
If the agency was for divorce or manumission, and the sub-agent acts in the principal's presence without permission, the divorce/manumission does not take effect. Reason: the original appointment is like a condition; the action is suspended on the first agent's performance.
Divorce and manumission are 'azā'im (determinations) and can be conditional; contracts (sale etc.) are ithbātāt (confirmations) and cannot.

Commentary

Contracts like sale are different because they are 'confirmations' (ithbātāt) - they establish rights and are not conditional on the person of the agent.

Expansion on sub-agency and dismissal.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a sub-agent's contract is valid and distinguish between acts that can be delegated unconditionally and those that require the agent's own performance.

Core Idea
A sub-agent's contract is valid only if the first agent's oversight is present; divorce cannot be delegated as a condition.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar concerning agency. It clarifies when an agent may appoint a sub-agent without explicit permission, the role of the principal's presence or approval, and the critical distinction between commercial contracts and acts like divorce or manumission. It also addresses the principal's right to dismiss the agent and exceptions when third-party rights are attached.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar concerning the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wakalah). It expands on key rulings regarding the appointment of a sub-agent and the dismissal of an agent. The commentary opens by addressing a scenario where an agent (first wakil) appoints another agent (second wakil) without the explicit permission of the principal (muwakkil). If the second agent contracts in the presence of the first agent, the contract is valid because the first agent's judgment was effectively present. However, if the principal had instructed the first agent to appoint someone, the first agent does not have the right to dismiss that appointee without the principal's consent. The commentary then discusses a difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars regarding who bears the responsibility (al-'uhdah) for the contract: Al-Baqqali holds that it is on the first agent, while the authors of al-'Uyun and Qadi Khan say it is on the second agent. Another point of contention is whether the first agent's explicit approval is necessary for the second agent's contract that took place in his presence. According to al-Asl, it is not required, but the majority of mashayikh hold that it is required, and the absolute text is interpreted as when approval is given. The commentary then clarifies a restriction: the ruling of validity in the presence of the first agent applies only to contracts like sale, not to divorce (talaq) or manumission ('itaq). If the principal authorizes the first agent to divorce or manumit, and the first agent appoints a second agent who actually performs the divorce or manumission in the first agent's presence, the divorce or manumission does not take effect. The reason is that the delegation in such matters is treated as a condition (shart); it is as if the principal made the divorce conditional upon the first agent's own action. Since divorce and manumission can be suspended by conditions, they do not occur without the fulfillment of that condition. In contrast, sale and similar contracts are affirmative acts (ithbatat) that do not admit suspension by conditions, so they are valid if the first agent's presence or approval is obtained. Next, the commentary addresses what happens if the second agent contracts in the absence of the first agent. In that case, the contract is not valid unless the first agent later approves it. This applies to sales; if the second agent purchases something, the purchase is binding on the first agent (i.e., the first agent becomes responsible) unless he repudiates it. The Hidayah clarifies that if the first agent is absent at the time of the contract, it is invalid because his opinion was missing, but if he later learns of it and approves, it becomes valid. Similarly, if a non-agent sells and the principal approves, it becomes valid. Finally, the commentary discusses the principal's right to dismiss the agent. The principal may dismiss the agent at any time because agency is his right, and he may revoke it. However, an exception is made when a third party's right is attached to the agency, such as when a pledgor (rahn) authorizes a trustee to sell the pledged property at maturity; in that case, the pledgor cannot dismiss the trustee without the consent of the pledgee because the agency was stipulated in the pledge. The commentary ends by describing how dismissal of an absent agent is effected: either by a letter that reaches the agent and is understood, or by a messenger of any type (just or unjust, free or slave, young or old) who conveys the message of dismissal; upon receipt and understanding of the message, the agent is dismissed.

الوكيل (al-wakil): The agent who is appointed to act on behalf of the principal.
العهدة (al-'uhdah): The responsibility or liability for the contract, including rights and obligations arising from it.
الإجازة (al-ijazah): Approval or ratification of a contract made by another, often retroactively validating the contract.
الإثباتات (al-ithbatat): Affirmations; contracts like sale that establish ownership and cannot be suspended by conditions.

Notification of Dismissal: Evidence Rules and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dismissal notification evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on dismissal notification and distinguish the evidence rules that lead to the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
When a single unjust person informs of dismissal, Abu Hanifa trusts the agent's disbelief; the Sahibayn trust the eventual truth.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute on agency termination. It establishes the consensus cases first, then highlights the disagreement when the informant is a single fasiq. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how evidentiary principles shape practical outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the consensus cases
Locate the scenarios where all Hanafi imams agree the agent is dismissed: two just witnesses, two unjust witnesses, or one just witness, provided the news is true. Note that the agent's belief is irrelevant in these cases.
Consensus indicates the baseline rule: dismissal depends on the truth of the news, not the agent's belief, when the informant is trustworthy or multiple.
2
Locate the contested scenario
The dispute arises when the informant is a single unjust (fasiq) person. Note that both sides agree the agent is dismissed if he believes the informant; the contested element is his disbelief.
The fasiq's testimony is generally not accepted in legal proofs, creating the tension.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that the agent is not dismissed if he disbelieves the fasiq. His basis: the testimony of a fasiq is unreliable; the agent is not legally bound to accept it.
This prioritizes the agent's subjective knowledge and the evidentiary weakness of the informant.
4
State the Sahibayn's position and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the agent is dismissed if the news is later proven true, regardless of his initial disbelief. Their basis: the objective truth of the dismissal is determinative.
This prioritizes the objective reality over the agent's personal belief.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the agent disbelieves and continues to act (e.g., sell property), according to Abu Hanifa the sale is valid (he was still agent), while according to the Sahibayn the sale is invalid (he had already been dismissed).
Liability and validity of subsequent transactions depend on which view is followed.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) regarding the evidentiary value of informing the agent of dismissal. There is consensus that if the news comes from two just witnesses, two unjust witnesses, or one just witness, and the news is proven true, the agent is dismissed regardless of whether he personally believes the news. The dispute arises when the informant is a single unjust (fasiq) person. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the agent is not dismissed if he disbelieves the informant, because an unjust person's testimony is not reliable; the agent is not obliged to accept it. However, Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that even if the agent disbelieves, he is dismissed once the truth of the news becomes evident (i.e., if it is later confirmed). The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, if the agent continues to act after being told by a fasiq but not believing, his actions may be valid, whereas according to the Sahibayn, he would be acting without authority if the news was true.

Dismissal of Agent Requires Knowledge: Ruling and Liability
Explaining the rule that agent's dismissal is ineffective without knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an agent's dismissal is ineffective without knowledge, apply the principle to a scenario, and evaluate the rationale (ʿillah) based on the nature of prohibitions.

Core Idea
An agent remains authorised until he learns of his dismissal – knowledge is essential for a prohibition to bind.

Imagine you appoint someone to sell your property, then change your mind but forget to tell him. He sells it to a buyer. Who bears the loss? In Hanafi law, the key is knowledge: because dismissal is a prohibition, it only takes effect when the agent knows. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its juristic reasoning, and a concrete application involving a slave sale.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage introduces a subsidiary ruling (far') concerning a conditional appointment of an agent. If someone says, 'I appoint you to sell my slave tomorrow,' the agency becomes effective only from tomorrow onward, not before. The commentator then extracts a general principle: It is valid to suspend 'ilaqat (empowerments) like agency, permission, and the effects of contracts such as talaq and 'itaq, on a future condition. An agent is a trustee over the price; any condition making him a guarantor negates his trusteeship and is void.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, commented by al-Haddadi, addresses a common scenario: an agent appointed to sell guarantees the price on behalf of the buyer. When Buying the Remainder After Partial Purchase: Principal or Agent?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on who is bound by remainder purchase in agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on purchasing the remaining portion after a partial purchase in agency and apply it to scenarios with and without court intervention.

Core Idea
If the agent buys the remainder before court, it binds the principal; after court imposes liability, it binds the agent by consensus.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a nuanced ruling in agency (wakalah). When an agent purchases part of a property and later buys the remainder, who becomes liable? The Hanafi school holds the principal bound, except when a judge has already ruled against the agent. Understanding this ruling sharpens your grasp of how agency obligations interact with partial transactions and judicial decisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between buying the remainder before any court action and after court action. Identify which scenario is discussed first.
The key is timing in relation to judicial intervention.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad (the three imams), if the agent buys the remainder before any litigation, it binds the principal. Write down the rationale: buying part is a means to comply with the agency.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
Identify Zufar's opposing view
Zufar holds that the remainder binds the agent, not the principal. Consider why he might differ – possibly because the agent acted beyond explicit instructions.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Analyze the consensus scenario
When the agent and principal have already litigated before a judge and the judge imposes liability for the first purchase on the agent, then buying the remainder afterwards binds the agent by consensus (ittifāq).
Judicial intervention changes the binding.
5
Apply the 'tab'id' (splitting) principle
The passage states the ruling applies to all cases where splitting the purchase would cause harm (ḍarar). Understand that this principle limits the agent's ability to split purchases if it harms the principal.
This is a general rule for agency.

Agency for Goods Where Partial Purchase Causes No Harm
Ruling on partial purchase in agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's partial purchase when splitting causes no harm and identify the condition for validity.

Core Idea
If splitting the purchase causes no harm, the agent's partial purchase binds the principal by consensus.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in agency (wakalah): what happens when the agent buys only part of what he was instructed to purchase? The Hanafi school rules that if the purchased goods are homogeneous and splitting them causes no harm, the partial purchase is valid and enforceable against the principal. This ruling is agreed upon by all Hanafi authorities, as the text explicitly states 'by consensus' (إجماعاً).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the agent buying part of the goods he was authorized to purchase. The ruling: it binds the principal (لزم الأمر).
The binding is immediate, even if the remainder is never bought.
2
Identify the condition
The key condition is that splitting the goods into parts does not cause harm (لا ضرر في تبعيضه). This is the decisive factor.
Harm here means loss of value or usability; homogeneous goods like wheat or slaves of equal value meet this condition.
3
Review the examples
The text gives two examples: (1) buying half a kurr of wheat when authorized for a full kurr, and (2) buying one of two slaves, or one of a group of similar countable items.
The common feature is that the goods are uniform (العددي المتقارب) and splitting does not diminish their value or utility.
4
Note the consensus
The ruling is by consensus (إجماعاً) among Hanafi scholars, meaning there is no disagreement within the school.
This strengthens the authority of the ruling; it is drawn from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Agent Buys More Than Instructed: Meat Purchase Case
Explanation of Abu Hanifa's ruling on agent buying twenty pounds when instructed for ten

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Abu Hanifa's ruling when an agent buys more than instructed and evaluate the objection based on the divorce analogy.

Core Idea
An agent's excess purchase binds the principal proportionately only if the excess can be attributed to the agent.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in agency law: what happens when an agent buys more than instructed? The Hanafi school, through Abu Hanifa, provides a precise ruling that balances the principal's instruction with the agent's action. We will also explore an objection based on an analogy to divorce and see why the Hanafi response distinguishes the two contexts.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its conditions
Identify the case: principal instructs agent to buy 10 lbs meat for 1 dirham; agent buys 20 lbs for 1 dirham from meat normally sold at 10 lbs per dirham. State Abu Hanifa's ruling: principal is bound for 10 lbs at half a dirham.
The market price condition is crucial; if meat is cheaper, the entire purchase binds the agent by consensus.
2
Identify the objection and its basis
The objector argues that this ruling conflicts with Abu Hanifa's own principle in divorce: if told to divorce once and he divorces thrice, the one divorce does not occur because it is included in the three but not intended. Here, the ten pounds are included in the twenty, so they should not bind the principal.
The objection uses qiyas (analogy) from divorce to purchase.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa's response distinguishes divorce from purchase: in divorce, the included (one divorce) cannot be attributed to either the principal or the agent (the agent cannot divorce the principal's wife without specific authorization). In purchase, if the ten pounds do not bind the principal, they bind the agent (since the agent is the buyer). Thus the analogy fails.
Key difference: in purchase, the agent can be held liable for the excess; in divorce, no one can be held for the included.
4
Determine the practical outcome
Result: principal pays half a dirham for 10 lbs; agent is responsible for the other 10 lbs and the other half dirham. The purchase is valid overall.
This ruling applies when the market price matches the instruction.

Agent Buying a Specified Item for Himself
Ruling on agent buying specified item for himself

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an agent buys a specifically appointed item for himself and identify the conditions under which the purchase belongs to the agent.

Core Idea
A specific agency makes the agent's purchase for the principal, except when principal is present and agent declares otherwise.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi agency law: when an agent is appointed to buy a specific item, that item becomes ‘earmarked’ for the principal. Even if the agent tries to buy it for himself, the purchase is attributed to the principal. However, there is an exception if the principal is present and the agent explicitly revokes his agency. This ruling protects the principal's interest while allowing the agent to withdraw under certain conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an agent buying a specifically appointed item for himself. The ruling is that the purchase is not for the agent but for the principal, regardless of the agent's intention or statement.
The ruling applies automatically once the agent accepts the specific agency.
2
Examine the condition of the principal's presence
The ruling depends on whether the principal is absent or present. If absent, the purchase is for the principal no matter what. If present, and the agent explicitly declares buying for himself, then it becomes the agent's purchase.
Presence allows the agent to revoke the agency in front of the principal.
3
Consider the exception for deviation
If the agent deviates from the principal's instructions—by buying at a different price or a different type—the ruling may change. The text mentions this as an exception, implying the purchase may then be for the agent.
Deviation voids the specific agency, so the agent acts on his own behalf.
4
Apply the rule to a hypothetical
Test your understanding: A appoints agent B to buy a specific horse. B buys that horse and says 'I buy it for myself.' A is absent. The horse belongs to A. If A is present and B so declares, it belongs to B.
The key variable is presence/absence, not intention.

Default Ownership in Unspecified Agency Purchases
Ruling on agent buying unspecified slave and conditions for principal's ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling when an agent buys an unspecified item and identify the exceptions that transfer ownership to the principal.

When the absent principal returns, if he confirms the agency, the transaction is valid; otherwise, the debtor must pay the debt a second time to the principal because the original payment was not proven validly due to the denial of agency.

In Hanafi fiqh, the ruling on whether a depositor (or debtor) must hand over property to someone claiming to be an agent of the absent owner depends on the nature of the property.
